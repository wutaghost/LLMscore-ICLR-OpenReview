009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Existing image-to-3D creation methods typically split the task into multi-view
012            image generation and 3D reconstruction, leading to two main limitations: (1) multi-
013            view bias, where geometric inconsistencies arise because multi-view diffusion
014            models ensure image-level rather than 3D consistency; (2) misaligned reconstruc-
015            tion data, since reconstruction models trained on mostly synthetic data misalign
016            when processing generated multi-view images during inference. To address these
017            issues, we propose Ouroboros3D, a unified framework that integrates multi-view
018            generation and 3D reconstruction into a recursive diffusion process. By incorporat-
019
               ing a 3D-aware feedback mechanism, our multi-view diffusion model leverages
               the explicit 3D information from the reconstruction results of the previous denois-
020
               ing process as conditions, thus modeling consistency at the 3D geometric level.
021
               Furthermore, through joint training of both the multi-view diffusion and recon-
022            struction models, we alleviate reconstruction bias due to data misalignment and
023            enable mutual enhancement within the multi-step recursive process. Experimental
024            results demonstrate that Ouroboros3D outperforms methods that treat these stages
025            separately and those that combine them only during inference, achieving superior
026            multi-view consistency and producing 3D models with higher geometric realism.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Creating 3D content from a single image have achieved rapid progress in recent years with the
032   adoption of large-scale 3D datasets (Deitke et al., 2023; 2024; Wu et al., 2023) and diffusion
033   models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020). A body of research (Liu
034   et al., 2023b; Shi et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023c; Kwak et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023; Tang et al.,
035   2024b; Voleti et al., 2024; Long et al., 2023) has focused on multi-view diffusion models, fine-
036   tuning pretrained image or video diffusion models on 3D datasets to enable consistent multi-view
037   synthesis. These methods demonstrate generalizability and produce promising results. Another group
038   of works (Hong et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2024a; Xu et al., 2024b; Wang et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024a)
039
      propose generalizable reconstruction models, to generate 3D representation from one or few views in
      a feed-forward process, leading to efficient image-to-3D creation.
040
041   Since single-view reconstruction models (Hong et al., 2023) trained on 3D datasets (Deitke et al.,
042   2023; Yu et al., 2023) lack generalizability and often produce blurring at unseen viewpoints, several
043   works (Li et al., 2023a; Tang et al., 2024a; Wang et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024a) combine multi-view
044   diffusion models and feed-forward reconstruction models, so as to extend the reconstruction stage to
045
      sparse-view input, boosting the reconstruction quality. As shown in Fig. 1, these methods split 3D
      generation into two stages: multi-view synthesis and 3D reconstruction. By combining generalizable
046
      multi-view diffusion models and robust sparse-view reconstruction models, such pipelines achieve
047
      high-quality image to 3D generation. However, combining the two independently designed models
048   introduces a significant "data bias" to the reconstruction model. Data bias manifests primarily in two
049   aspects: (1) Multi-view bias. Multi-view diffusion models achieve consistency at the image level,
050   not in 3D space, complicating the assurance of geometric consistency. (2) Reconstruction data is
051   misaligned. Unlike multi-view diffusion models, reconstruction models are trained from scratch on
052   mostly synthetic data and limited real data. During inference, multi-view images generated by the
053   previous diffusion model lack geometric consistency and exhibit a more varied data distribution, both
      of which affect the reconstruction.

068   Recent works have explored several mechanisms to enhance multi-view consistency. Carve3D (Xie
069   et al., 2024) employs a RL-based fine-tuning algorithm (Black et al., 2023), applying a multi-view
070   consistency metric to enhance the multi-view image generation. However, the challenge of data
071   limitation has not been well-addressed, leading to poor reconstruction quality. On the other hand, IM-
072   3D (Melas-Kyriazi et al., 2024) and VideoMV (Zuo et al., 2024) aggregate the rendered views of the
073   reconstructed 3D model into multi-view synthesis during inference by adopting re-sampling strategy
074   in the denoising loop. However, on the overall image-to-3D pipeline, its (a) lacking joint training and
075
      (b) inability to use geometric information hinder its capacity to fully leverage 3D-aware knowledge
      and unify the two stages. Moreover, these methods fail to address the "data bias" between multi-view
076
      generation and 3D reconstruction, and the use of biased information from few-shot reconstructed 3D
077
      models can result in multi-view outputs misaligned with the input image (see Fig. 4).
078
079   In this paper, we introduce Ouroboros3D, a novel image-to-3D framework that seamlessly integrates
080   multi-view generation with 3D reconstruction within a recursive diffusion process, as depicted in
081
      Fig. 2. To facilitate the modeling of multi-view consistency, we propose a 3D-aware feedback
      mechanism, where our multi-view diffusion model utilizes 3D-aware maps rendered by the recon-
082
      struction module from the previous timestep as additional conditions during the denoising phase.
083
      Leveraging 3D information from reconstructed representations, our model produces images with
084   enhanced geometric consistency, thereby reducing the multi-view bias. To address the misaligned
085   distribution due to training the reconstruction model on mostly synthetic data and limited real data,
086   we involve joint training of the multi-view diffusion model and reconstruction model. During training,
087   the reconstruction model utilizes images restored by the diffusion process rather than original images.
088   This approach not only reduces the data bias of the reconstruction stage, increasing the diversity of the
089   reconstruction, but also enhances the diffusion model’s capability to generate images better suited for
090   few-shot reconstruction, making the two stages mutually beneficial in the multi-step iterative diffusion
091   process. The 3D-aware recursived diffusion, with the integration of the two stages, facilitates adaptive
092
      refinement of outputs through mutual feedback, enhancing inference stability and reducing data bias.
093   In our experiments, we use the Stable Video Diffusion(SVD) (Blattmann et al., 2023) as the multi-view
094   generator and the Large Multi-View Gaussian Model (LGM) (Tang et al., 2024a) as the reconstruction
095   module. Experimental results on the GSO dataset (Downs et al., 2022) show that our framework
096   outperforms separation of these stages and existing methods (Zuo et al., 2024) that combine the
097   stages at the inference phase.
098   Our key contributions are as follows:
099
100   • We introduce a image-to-3D creation framework Ouroboros3D, which integrates multi-view gener-
101     ation and 3D reconstruction into a recursive diffusion process. The framework is highly extensible
102     and can accommodate various multi-view generation networks and reconstruction networks.
103   • Ouroboros3D employs a self-conditioning mechanism with 3D-aware feedback, using rendered
104     maps to guide the multi-view generation, ensuring better geometric consistency and robustness.
105   • We conducte extensive experiments to demonstrate that Ouroboros3D significantly reduces data
106     bias and outperforms both the method that separates the two stages and the method that combines
107     them only at inference time. (Zuo et al., 2024) .

      Image/Video Diffusion for Multi-view Generation Diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022;
      Saharia et al., 2022; Podell et al., 2023; Sauer et al., 2024; Ho et al., 2022b;a; Singer et al., 2022;
127
      Blattmann et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Hu, 2024; Ma et al., 2024; Brooks et al., 2024) have
128
      demonstrated their powerful generative capabilities in image and video generation fields. Current
129   research (Liu et al., 2023b; Shi et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023c; Kwak et al., 2023; Huang et al.,
130   2023; Tang et al., 2024b; Voleti et al., 2024; Long et al., 2023; Zheng & Vedaldi, 2023) fine-tunes
131   pretrained image/video diffusion models on 3D datasets like Objaverse (Deitke et al., 2023) and
132   MVImageNet (Yu et al., 2023). Zero123 (Liu et al., 2023b) introduces relative view condition
133   to image diffusion models, enabling novel view synthesis from a single image and preserving
134   generalizability. Based on it, methods like SyncDreamer (Liu et al., 2023c), ConsistNet (Yang et al.,
135   2023) and EpiDiff (Huang et al., 2023) design attention modules to generate consistent multi-view
136   images. These methods fine-tuned from image diffusion models produce generally promising results.
137
      By considering multi-view images as consecutive frames of a video (e.g., orbiting camera views), it
      naturally leads to the idea of applying video generation models to 3D generation (Voleti et al., 2024).
138
      However, since the diffusion model is not explicitly modeled in 3D space, the generated multi-view
139
      images often struggle to achieve consistent and robust details.

      Image to 3D Reconstruction Recently, the task of reconstructing 3D objects has evolved from
142   traditional multi-view reconstruction methods (Mildenhall et al., 2021; Barron et al., 2021; Müller
143   et al., 2022; Kerbl et al., 2023) to feed-forward reconstruction models (Hong et al., 2023; Jiang et al.,
144   2023; Zou et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2024a; Xu et al., 2024b; Wang et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024a).
145   Ultilizing one or few shot as input, these highly generalizable reconstruction models synthesize
146   3D representation, enabling the rapid generation of 3D objects. LRM (Hong et al., 2023) proposes
147   a transformer-based model to effectively map image tokens to 3D triplanes. Instant3D (Li et al.,
148   2023a) further extends LRM to sparse-view input, significantly boosting the reconstruction quality.
149   LGM (Tang et al., 2024a) and GRM (Xu et al., 2024b) replace the triplane representation with 3D
150
      Gaussians (Kerbl et al., 2023) to enjoy its superior rendering efficiency. CRM (Wang et al., 2024)
      and InstantMesh (Xu et al., 2024a) optimize on the mesh representation for high-quality geometry
151
      and texture modeling. These reconstrucion models built upon convolutional network architecture or
152
      transformer backbone, have led to efficient image-to-3D creation.

159   image or video diffusion models, and training from scratch on 3D data limits its generalization.
160   Several works (Liu et al., 2023c; Huang et al., 2023; Long et al., 2023; Melas-Kyriazi et al., 2024)
161   fine-tune image diffusion models to generate multi-view images, which are then utilized for 3D
      shape and appearance recovery with traditional reconstruction methods (Wang et al., 2021; Kerbl

196   3     M ETHOD
197
198   Given a single image, Ouroboros3D aims to generate multiview-consistent images with a recon-
199
      structed 3D Gaussion model. To reduce the data bias and improve robustness of the generation, our
      framework integrates multi-view synthesis and 3D reconstruction in a recursive diffusion process.
200
      As illustrated in Fig. 3, the proposed framework involves a video diffusion model (SVD (Blattmann
201
      et al., 2023)) as multi-view generator (refer to Section 3.1) and a feed-forward reconstruction model
202   to recover a 3D Gaussian Splatting (refer to Section 3.2. Moreover, we introduce a self-conditioning
203   mechanism, feeding the 3D-aware information obtained from the reconstruction module back to
204   the multi-view generation process (refer to Section 3.3). The 3D-aware recursive diffusion strategy
205   iteratively refines the multi-view images and the 3d model, enhancing the final production.
206
207   3.1   V IDEO D IFFUSION M ODEL AS M ULTIVIEW G ENERATOR
208
209   Recent video diffusion models (Voleti et al., 2024; Brooks et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2024) have
210   demonstrated a remarkable capability to generate 3D-aware videos. We employs the well-known
211   Stable Video Diffusion (SVD) Model as our multi-view generator, which generates videos from an
212
      image input. Further details about SVD can be found in Appendix A.1. In our framework, we set the
      number of the generated frames f to 8.
213
214   We enhance the video diffusion model with camera control c to generate images from different
215   viewpoints. Traditional methods encode camera positions at the frame level, which results in all
      pixels within one view sharing the same positional encoding (Liu et al., 2023a; Voleti et al., 2024).

      Building on the innovations of previous work (Huang et al., 2023; Zheng & Vedaldi, 2023), we
217   integrate the camera condition c into the denoising network by parameterizing the rays r = (o, o × d).
218   Specifically, we use two-layered MLP to inject Plücker ray embeddings for each latent pixel, enabling
219   precise positional encoding at the pixel level. This approach allows for more detailed and accurate
220   3D rendering, as pixel-specific embedding enhances the model’s ability to handle complex variations
221   in depth and perspective across the video frames.
222

224

225   subsequent 3D reconstruction. The rendered maps are employed as conditions to guide the next
226   denoising step. At each sampling step,we reparameterize the output from the denoising network Fθ

235   3.2   F EED -F ORWARD R ECONSTRUCTION M ODEL
236
      In the Ouroboros3D framework, the feed-forward reconstruction model is designed to recover 3D
237
      models from pre-generated multi-view images, which can be images decoded from straightly predicted
238
      ef0 , or completely denoised images. We utilize Large Multi-View Gaussian Model (LGM) (Tang
      x

      et al., 2024a) G as our reconstruction module due to its real-time rendering capabilities that benefit
240   from 3D representation of Gaussian Splatting. This method integrates seamlessly with our jointly
241   training framework, allowing for quick adaptation and efficient processing.
242
243   We pass four specific views from the reparameterized output x  ef0 to the Large Gaussian Model (LGM)
244   for 3D Gaussian Splatting reconstruction. To enhance the performance of LGM, particularly its
245
      sensitivity to different noise levels cnoise (σ) and image details, we introduce a zero-initialized time
      embedding layer within the original U-Net structure of the LGM. This innovative modification
246
      enables the LGM to dynamically adapt to the diverse outputs that arise at different stages of the
247
      denoising process, thereby substantially improving its capacity to accurately reconstruct 3D content
248   from images that have undergone partial denoising.
249
250   The loss function employed for the fine-tuning of the LGM is articulated as follows:

252                    LG = Lrgb (x0 , G(x̃0 , cnoise (σ))) + λLLPIPS (x0 , G(x̃0 , cnoise (σ))). (2)

259   3.3   3D-AWARE F EEDBACK M ECHANISM
260
261   We use a 3D-aware feedback mechanism, as shown in Fig. 3, involving rendered color images
262   and geometric maps in a denoising loop to enhance multi-view consistency and facilitate cyclic
263   adaptation. Unlike integrating multi-view generation and 3D reconstruction at the inference stage
264   using re-sampling strategy (Melas-Kyriazi et al., 2024; Zuo et al., 2024), we jointly to train these
265   two modules to support more informative feedback. Specifically, in addition to the rendered color
266
      images, our flexible framework is able to derive additional geometric features to guide the generation
      process, which brings guidance of more explicit 3D information to multi-view generation. Moreover,
267
      the explicit geometric structure allows it to be adaptable across various network designs.
268
269   In practice, we obtain color images and canonical coordinates maps (CCM) (Li et al., 2023b) from the
      reconstructed 3D model, and utilize them as condition to guide the next denoising step of multi-view


                                                           5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      generation. We choose CCM over depth or normal maps because CCMs capture global vertex
271   coordinates normalized across the entire 3D model, unlike depth maps that normalize relative to the
272   self-view. This operation enables the rendered maps to be characterized as cross-view alignment,
273   providing the strong guidance of more explicit cross-view geometry relationship. The details of
274   canonical coordinates maps can be found in Appendix A.2.
275
      To encode color images and coordinates maps into the denoising network of multi-view generation
276
      module, we design two simple and lightweight encoders for color images and coordinates maps
277   using a series of convolutional neural networks, like T2I-Adapter (Mou et al., 2024). The encoders
278   are composed of four feature extraction blocks and three downsample blocks to change the feature
279   resolution, so that the dimension of the encoded features is the same as the intermediate feature in
280   the encoder of U-Net denoiser. The extracted features from the two conditional modalities are then
281   added to the U-Net encoder at each scale.
282
      We then introduce the proposed 3D-aware self-conditioning (Chen et al., 2022) strategy for both train-
283   ing and inference. The original multi-view denoising network Fθ (x; σ) is augmented with 3D-aware
284   feedback, formulated as Fθ (x; σ, G(x̃0 )), where G(x̃0 ) is the rendered maps of the reconstruction
285   module.
286
287   Training Strategy As illustrated in Algorithm 1 of Appendix A.3, the training of the 3D-aware
288   multi-view generation network involves a probabilistic self-conditioning mechanism. During each
289   training iteration, the network uses the rendered results from a feed-forward model as self-conditioning
290   input with a probability of 0.5. Specifically, if the 3D reconstruction result is not used, the input
291   G(x̃0 ) is set to 0. This approach ensures balanced learning and prevents the model from over-relying
292   on the 3D information.
293
294
      Inference/Sampling Strategy As illustrated in Algorithm 2 of Appendix A.3, the initial condition
      G(x̃0 ) is set to zero. At each subsequent timestep, this condition is updated based on the previous
295
      reconstruction result. This iterative updating process refines the 3D representation, enhancing the
296
      consistency of multi-view images and improving the quality of the reconstructed 3D models.

301   4.1   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
302
      Datasets We use a filtered subset of the Objaverse (Deitke et al., 2023) dataset to train our model.
303
      Following LGM (Tang et al., 2024a), we implemented a rigorous filtering process to remove bad
304
      models with bad captions or missing texture. It leads to a final set of around 80K 3D objects. We
305   render 2 16-frame RGBA orbits at 512×512. For each orbit, the cameras are positioned at a randomly
306   sampled elevation between [-5, 30] degrees. During training, we subsample any 8-frame orbit by
307   picking any frame in one orbit as the first frame (the conditioning image), and then choose every 2nd
308   frame after that.
309
      We evaluate the synthesized multi-view images and reconstructed 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS)
310
      on the unseen GSO (Downs et al., 2022) dataset. We filter 100 objects to reduce redundancy and
311   maintain diversity. For each object, we render ground truth orbit videos and pick the first frame as the
312   conditioning image.
313
314
      Experimental Settings Our Ouroboros3D is trained for 30,000 iterations using 8 A100 GPUs
      with a total batch size of 32. We clip the gradient with a maximum norm of 1.0. We use the AdamW
315
      optimizer with a learning rate of 1 × 10−5 and employ FP16 mixed precision with DeepSeed(Rasley
316
      et al., 2020) with Zero-2 for efficient training. At the inference stage, we set the number of sampling
317   steps as 25, which takes about 20 seconds to generate a 3d model.

356   4.2    C OMPARISON WITH E XISTING A LTERNATIVES
357
      Image-to-Multiview generation We compare our method with SyncDreamer (Liu et al., 2023c),
358
      SV3D (Voleti et al., 2024) and VideoMV (Zuo et al., 2024), as shown in Fig. 4. SyncDreamer and
359
      SV3D fine-tune image or video diffusion models on 3D datasets but lack explicit 3D information,
360   often resulting in blurry textures or inconsistent details. VideoMV aggregates rendered views
361   from reconstructed 3D models at the inference stage but fails to take into account the "data bias"
362   between two stages. Although VideoMV improves the multi-view consistency, it introduces biased
363   information from the reconstruction stage, leading to results that are unaligned with the input image.
364   Our Ouroboros3D uses joint training of the two stages and uses geometry and appearance feedback
365   for multi-view generation, generating consistent and high-quality multi-view images.
366   Image-to-3D generation We compare our method with TripoSR (Tochilkin et al., 2024),
367   VideoMV (Zuo et al., 2024), LGM (Tang et al., 2024a) and InstantMesh (Xu et al., 2024a), as
368   visualized in Fig. 5. TripoSR struggles with high-quality geometry and appearance due to lacking
369   large pre-trained generative models. VideoMV reconstructs 3DGS from its generated multi-view
370   images, but its inherent biases in multiview generation can lead to misaligned textures and distorted
371   geometries. Two-stage methods such as LGM and InstantMesh, which comprise an off-the-shelf
372   image-to-multiview generation method followed by reconstruction models for the image-to-3D gener-
373   ation process, often yield incomplete geometry due to the disparity between multiview generation and
374
      3D reconstruction. In contrast, our framework integrates multiview generation and 3D reconstruction,
      enhancing each module’s strengths to produce high-quality 3D assets.
375
376   Generalizability Ouroboros3D exhibits remarkable generalizability, adept at producing high-
377   quality 3D models from images that fall outside its training distribution, including real-world images.
      This capability is demonstrated in the results shown in Fig. 6 and Fig. 9.

531   5   C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this paper, we introduce Ouroboros3D, a unified framework for single image-to-3D creation that
534   integrates multi-view image generation and 3D reconstruction in a recursive diffusion process. We In
535   our framework, these two modules are jointly trained through a self-conditioning mechanism, which
536   allows them to adapt to the inherent characteristic of each stage, leading to more robust generation. By
537   establishing a recursive relationship between these two stages through a self-conditioning mechanism,
538   our approach effectively mitigates the data bias encountered in existing two-stage methods. Exper-
539   iments demonstrate that Ouroboros3D not only generates consistent and high-quality multi-view
      images, but also produces 3D objects with superior geometric consistency and details.

      A.2    C ANONICAL C OORDINATES M AP
828
      For control networks (Zhang et al., 2023; Mou et al., 2024) of image diffusion models, the
829   conditional maps like depth maps need to be normalized to [0, 1], typically using the formula:
830   (p − pmean )/(pmax − pmin ). For multi-view generation, each view performs a normalize operation
831   on itself, which results in a scale ambiguity. At the same time, the depth map is relative to a certain
832   view, and the correlation between the depth values is not significant across views.
833
      To avoid the above issues caused by self-normalization, we use canonical coordinate maps (CCM).
834
      Coordinate maps transform the depth value d to a common world coordinate system using the
835   camera’s intrinsic and extrinsic parameters, represented as (X, Y, Z). The transformation formula is:
836
                                              X                u
                                                !                !
837
838                                           Y = K −1 · v · d
839                                           Z                1
840
      where (u, v) are the pixel coordinates, d is the corresponding depth value, and K is the camera
841
      intrinsic matrix. Then the coordinate values of all views will be multiplied by a global scale and
842   added an offset value to convert to the range of 0 to 1. This representation makes the correlation
843   between different views more significant and is helpful for multi-view generation.
