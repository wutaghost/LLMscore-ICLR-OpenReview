011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            In real-world traffic networks, it is common to encounter a shortage of historical
014            data in the target region. Researchers often address this issue through transfer learn-
015            ing. However, transfer learning tasks in traffic prediction currently lack dedicated
016
               datasets and instead rely on datasets designed for non-transfer prediction tasks.
               The major drawback of these existing datasets is the adoption of a fixed network
017
               topology to model the real world’s road networks. This does not align with reality
018
               and limits the model’s transferability. To tackle this issue, we propose DynST, a
019            dataset specifically designed for transfer learning tasks in traffic prediction, with a
020            massive data volume of 20.35 billion, spanning 20 years and 9 regions. The key
021            feature of DynST is evolving dynamic road network topology, which reflects the
022            evolution of real road networks. Moreover, to address the shortcomings of the
023            distance-based adjacency generation algorithm, we introduce a novel tree-based
024            algorithm. Extensive experiments demonstrate that the adoption of DynST as
025            the source dataset can significantly enhance the performance of the target region.
026            The comparative experiment also validates that our adjacency matrix generation
027
               algorithm can lead to improved prediction accuracy. We believe that DynST, with
               rich spatial variation information, will facilitate research in the field of transfer
028
               traffic prediction.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Traffic forecasting plays a crucial role in urban management and travel planning, and relies heavily
034
      on historical data. In reality, the available data in the target areas are often inadequate. A feasible
035
      approach is to utilize the transfer learning technique, where generalizable knowledge learned from
036   a data-rich region is transferred to the target area. Although many transfer learning models have
037   been developed (Wang et al., 2021; Yin et al., 2022; Jin et al., 2022; Yao et al., 2019; Jin et al.,
038   2022), there is currently no dataset specifically designed for the transfer learning task. These models
039   typically leverage datasets proposed for non-transfer learning, which primarily suffer from the use of
040   an invariant topological structure to describe the real-world road network (Yu et al., 2018; Li et al.,
041   2017; Cui et al., 2019; Guo et al., 2019; Song et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2023b).
042   The existing datasets inherently assume the completeness of data and the invariance of the road
043   network. In the real world, due to factors like weather conditions and equipment failures, data
044   integrity is infeasible in large-scale datasets (Yu et al., 2024). Additionally, it is normal for regional
045   road construction to evolve as the dataset spans long periods (Chen et al., 2001). On the other hand,
046   the nature of transfer learning tasks requires knowledge to be transferred between two regions with
047   completely different road networks, but not the same network topology. Intuitively, increasing the
048   dynamism of the original region’s road network topology can enhance model generalizability. By
049   exposing the model to a more diverse topology of road networks, it can learn more robust patterns
050
      that are less tied to specific nodes’ characteristics. In contrast, a fixed road network topology in the
      source region may limit the model’s transferability to adapt to new network structures.
051
052   Motivated by this, we propose DynST, a large-scale spatial-temporal dataset featuring evolving
053   dynamic topology with data spanning up to 20 years, specifically designed for transfer learning tasks
      in traffic forecasting. The basic information is compared in Table 1. To construct DynST, we sourced

079   data from the California Department of Transportation Performance Measurement System (PEMS) 1 ,
080   aggregating 30-second granularity data to the standard 5-minute granularity, to align with prevailing
081   practices. The dataset includes traffic flow, vehicle speed, and road occupancy information. The
082   construction of DynST ensures the realities of real-world traffic data. Two primary considerations
083   are addressed: (1) Road Network Development and Dynamic Missing Data: The PEMS system
084
      irregularly adds new sensors to accommodate regional road development. Due to various unexpected
      events, missing data in the deployed sensors are inevitable. (2) Data Cleaning for Reliability: The
085
      cleaning process is supposed to ensure data reliability and maintain a sufficient volume for training
086
      purposes. We filter out any sensors with missing values throughout the day. Subsequently, we use
087   the latitude and longitude coordinates of the sensors, along with an adjacency matrix generation
088   algorithm, to create the road network topology. In other words, DynST ensures that the sensors
089   and the road network structure maintain consistency throughout the day, while also exhibiting some
090   variation from one day to the next. This strategy cleverly constructs a large-scale spatial-temporal
091   dataset with a dynamic road network structure. It is more reflective of reality while also capturing
092   regional road changes and various emergencies.
093   During the data construction, annotating the adjacency matrix is a crucial step. Manually annotating
094   large datasets is impractical, and the distance-based generation method proposed by Liu et al. (2023b)
095   has overconnected and disconnected issues. To obtain an adjacency matrix that closely reflects the
096   actual road network, we propose a tree-based generation method to address the shortcomings.
097
      Our research adheres to PEMS regulations 2 , and the dataset is provided under the CC BY-NC
098
      4.0 License. Our contributions can be summarized as follows: (1) We constructed a large-scale
099   dataset, DynST, specifically designed for transfer learning tasks in traffic forecasting, which features
100   a dynamic road network structure; (2) We proposed a tree-based adjacency matrix generation
101   algorithm that creates a topology more closely aligned with real-world road networks; (3) Extensive
102   experiments demonstrate that utilizing DynST can effectively enhance the predictive capability of
103   transfer learning models in target regions. Comparative experiments validate the effectiveness of the
104   proposed adjacency matrix generation algorithm.

110   2.1   N ON - TRANSFER LEARNING TRAFFIC FORECASTING
111
112   In the early days traffic forecasting tasks were viewed as multivariate time series forecasting tasks
113   and were modeled using statistical models like ARIMA (Williams & Hoel, 2003) and VAR (Lu
114   et al., 2016). They could not handle complex spatial-temporal correlation. In recent years, the
115   development of Spatial-temporal Graph Neural Networks (STGNNs) (Wu et al., 2020) has led to
116   significant progress in traffic prediction tasks. STGNNs use GNNs (Kipf & Welling, 2017) to
117
      model spatial information, and a variety of methods adopt sequential models to model the temporal
      information, such as RNN-based methods (Li et al., 2017; Bai et al., 2020; Pan et al., 2019), CNN-
118
      based methods (Yu et al., 2018; Wu et al., 2019; Song et al., 2020; Han et al., 2021), attention-based
119
      methods (Zheng et al., 2020; Cirstea et al., 2022; Jiang et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2019; 2022; Li
120   et al., 2022; Liang et al., 2018). The recent research hotspot lies in the utilization of Transformer
121   structure (Vaswani et al., 2017) to model spatial-temporal dependency, and the Transformer-based
122   methods achieve superior performance (Guo et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023a; Xu
123   et al., 2020). Beyond the aforementioned categories, there are also some methods (Fang et al., 2021;
124   Choi et al., 2022) focused on graph representation construction. These methods have facilitated the
125   development of traffic prediction tasks.
126   These above models require a lot of historical data to be trained from scratch, but unfortunately,
127   collecting comprehensive traffic data in a particular city is a time-consuming and expensive endeavor.
128   Therefore, transfer learning techniques become a promising direction to address this issue.
129
130   2.2   T RANSFER L EARNING IN T RAFFIC F ORECASTING
131
132   Transfer learning is the process of enhancing performance in a target domain by learning rich
133   generalized information from the source domain. This technology has been widely used in the
134   field of computer vision (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) and natural language processing (Devlin et al.,
135
      2019; Brown et al., 2020). The methods based on transfer learning in traffic prediction mainly adopt
      meta-learning (Yao et al., 2019; Jin et al., 2022) and pretrained-finetuning (Wang et al., 2021; Yin
136
      et al., 2022; Jin et al., 2022) strategies.
137
138   However, current transfer learning models do not have dedicated datasets; instead, they rely on
139   datasets designed for non-transfer learning tasks. These datasets model urban road networks using
140   fixed topology and do not explicitly provide the capability for the model to make predictions on
141
      changing road networks. However, the task inherently requires the models to transfer across different
      road networks. Therefore, we propose DynST, which adopts a novel evolutionary dynamic topology
142
      structure to describe real-world road networks.

      4.1   L IMITATIONS OF E XISTING DATASETS
165
166   Fixed Road Network Structure. As far as we know, in traffic prediction, there is no dedicated
167   dataset specifically designed for transfer learning; instead, transfer learning models treat the naive
168   datasets as the source region dataset. Transfer learning requires the model to make predictions across
169   different road networks, but these datasets adopt a fixed topological structure to portray the road
170   network, which cannot explicitly provide the model with transferability. In other words, since the
171   road network structure in the source region dataset does not change, the model tends to overly rely on
172
      certain features, which restrain its transferability. Therefore, we posit that the adoption of fixed road
      network structure in existing datasets hinders models’ generalizability.
173
174   Distance-based Adjacency Matrix Generation Method. Current datasets use the adjacency matrix
175   to represent the road network topological structure, which is the root for a bunch of prediction
176   methods (Wu et al., 2019; Bai et al., 2020; Yu et al., 2018; Guo et al., 2019). Recent studies, such
177   as Liu et al. (2023b), have explored automated methods for generating adjacency matrices in traffic
178
      forecasting. One common approach is the distance-based method (Liu et al., 2023b), which assumes
      that the nodes are neighborhoods within a hard threshold. However, it often happens that two nodes
179
      within a close distance are not connected, i.e., there is no straightforward path to reach each other;
180
      or two nodes are adjacent but the distance coincidentally exceeds the threshold, and they would be
181   determined as isolated nodes. So this distance-based adjacency matrix generation method would lead
182   the model to learn the wrong topological relationship, which can be summarized as overconnected
183   and disconnected issues.
184
185   4.2   DATASET C ONSTRUCTION
186
187   We obtain the raw data from the PEMS system (Chen et al., 2001). The PEMS system reports raw
188   data every 30 seconds. We aggregate it into 5-minute granularity, which means 12 data points an
189   hour and 288 data points a day. We collected and cleaned out 9 districts’ data. The dataset consists of
190   three types of traffic signals: traffic flow, vehicle speed, and occupancy.
191
             • Traffic flow is quantified as the number of vehicles detected by a sensor over five minutes.
192
193          • Vehicle speed is measured by the sensors as the average speed of vehicles during five minutes
194            expressed in miles per hour (mph).
195          • Occupancy refers to the percentage of time in which vehicles are passing over the sensors.
196
197   Metadata. We provide detailed metadata for each district, which includes 12 attributions, such as the
198
      Sensor ID, lane information, latitude, and longitude. The latitude and longitude data can be used to
      construct the adjacency matrix. This metadata is presented in a timeline format and its update period
199
      is aligned with the new road construction. Additional detailed information is available in Appendix D.
200
      We hope rich metadata can facilitate future research with finer granularity.
201
202   Data Cleaning. After collecting the daily raw data via PEMS, we processed it for each day. Given
203   abundant missing values (bigger than 50% in some cases) and the limitations of simple linear
204
      interpolation, which could distort the patterns, we grouped the raw data by Sensor ID and filtered out
      any data points with missing values in any of the three types of traffic signals. This strategy ensures
205
      the data accurately reflects real-world distributions. See more in Appendix A.

249        To address this limitation and follow the principles, we propose a tree-based adjacency matrix
250        generation algorithm, as its main part is described in Algorithm 1 and the rest are detailed in
251        Appendix E. The core idea is to denote the distances between nodes (sensors) as edge weights to

253

353   5     E XPERIMENT
354
355   To verify the necessity and effectiveness of DynST for transfer learning models, we design experi-
356   ments following mainstream setting (Wang et al., 2021; Yin et al., 2022; Jin et al., 2022; Yao et al.,
357   2019; Jin et al., 2022). A non-transfer learning experiment is supposed as a control group under data-
358   scarce scenarios, but this is challenging in DynST because non-transfer learning models disregard
359   changes in graph structures, while DynST directly uses dynamic topology to characterize regional
360
      road networks. Fortunately, over 20 years, there are still certain periods in DynST where the road
      network remains structurally consistent, meeting the requirements of non-transfer learning models.
361
      We refer to this period as the fixed road network version of DynST, with specific details provided
362
      in the appendix B. Then we conduct transfer learning experiments, and, specifically, according to
363   the source dataset version, categorize them into experiments conducted on the fixed road network
364   version and the dynamic road network version.
365
366
      Since the main variation across these three experimental groups is the dynamism of spatial topology,
      we need to align the time spans to ensure the rigor of our experiments. However, the time span of the
367
      first experimental group’s data is affected by unexpected real-world events, resulting in a monthly
368
      duration. To better validate the effectiveness of the dataset, we also include experiments using the
369   native Transformer model over a longer time span for transfer learning.

      5.1   BASIC S ETUP

443
444   Exp.1: Non-transfer learning with fixed road network. As a control group, the models in this
445   experiment are trained and tested in the same region of the fixed road network version. This setting
446   simulates a data-scarcity scenario where the available data grows over time, and the training size
447   increases from a 1-day to a 30-day time range. Table 3 presents the experimental results. As observed,
448   the performance of all models consistently improves with a gradual increase in the training set size.
449
      Exp.2: Transfer learning with a fixed road network. This experiment aligns with the traditional
450   setting of transfer learning tasks, where the model is trained on the source region with a fixed road
451   network, and then the model is transferred to the target region for prediction. Following previous
452   studies (Wang et al., 2021; Tang et al., 2022; Lu et al., 2022), we randomly choose one out of the two
453   districts as the source region, while the remaining district serves as the target region. To simulate
454   real-world data scarcity scenarios, we employ a limited training set size for the target region, 1-day
455   and 3-day. The source region leverages an extensive training set size of 30 days. The baseline models
456   chosen for this evaluation are DASTNet (Tang et al., 2022) and ST-GFSL (Lu et al., 2022). To provide
457   a comprehensive comparison, we show the best-performing results excerpted from Exp.1 in Table 3.
458   The results are shown in Table 4. DASTNet generally outperforms the best results of Exp.1, which
459   verifies the effectiveness of the transfer learning technique. While ST-GFSL demonstrates improve-
460   ments in part of evaluation metrics, its performance might be limited by the expressive power of the
461   backbone model GRU (Chung et al., 2014), which struggles with capturing complex dependencies
462   within traffic data sequences.
463   Exp.3: Transfer learning with a dynamic road network. This experiment is a brand new transfer
464   learning setting for dynamic road network topology. The training set of the source region is the
465   dynamic version of DynST. As far as we know, ST-GFSL (Lu et al., 2022) is the only model that can,
466   in principle, handle the infinite number of evolving road networks without modification to the model
467   architecture. We follow the default hyperparameters reported in the original paper (Lu et al., 2022)
468   and turn the size of the dynamic road network sampling pool to align with the time span of Exp.1 – 2.
469   The results are shown in Table 5. ST-GFSL boosts the performance compared to one of Exp.2 settings
470   in most scenarios, which inspires us to develop an elaborate design to better capture rich information
471   of the spatial dynamism in the source region.

518   6      C ONCLUSION
519
520   We present a large-scale dataset, DynST, designed for transfer learning tasks in traffic forecasting.
521
      The key distinction of DynST from existing datasets lies in its adoption of an evolving dynamic
      road network topology, which better captures the construction evolution of regional road networks
522
      and the impact of unexpected events. DynST encompasses 20.35 billion data points collected over
523
      20 years across 9 regions in California. In addition to providing traffic signal data and metadata,
524   we propose a tree-based adjacency matrix generation algorithm that better aligns with the upstream
525   and downstream connection relationships of real road networks. We conducted extensive statistical
526   analyses to aid in understanding the temporal and spatial characteristics of DynST. Experiments
527   demonstrate that the dynamic spatial topology provided in DynST facilitates better transfer and
528   generalization of models to the target region. Compared to the distance-based adjacency matrix
529   generation algorithm, our proposed tree-based method shows greater benefits for model prediction.
530   The pretraining experiment conducted over the full time span reveals that the baseline model exhibits
531   a certain degree of zero-shot capability. We hope that DynST will provide insights to the community
532
      and promote the emergence of foundational models in the spatial-temporal prediction field.

      A       D ETAILS OF DATA C LEANING
703
704
705   For data cleaning, there are many practical considerations. For dynamic missing values in raw data,
706   there are generally two handling strategies. The first is to discard the missing values. While some
707   sensors provide high-quality data and can operate continuously, many other sensors often experience
708   significant data loss over certain days. If we only retain high-quality points, it will drastically reduce
709
      the size of the road networks and the time span. The second strategy is interpolation, which inevitably
      disrupts data dependencies. Some studies (Guo et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2001) have pointed out that
710
      traffic data distribution exhibits specific abrupt changes, and the data produced by interpolation lacks
711
      such variation, which can severely impact model learning.
712
713   We adopt a daily processing granularity, ensuring that the road network topology remains the same
714   within a day. We discard points with missing values that occur on that day. This strategy ensures both
715
      data volume and data reliability.
716

718   B       F IXED AND DYNAMIC ROAD N ETWORK V ERSION
719
720
721
      DynST uses a dynamic road network topology to describe the real road network, which means that the
      road network structure in the dataset changes over time. Non-transfer learning models cannot handle
722
      this kind of change, but we still need Exp.1 for comparison. Fortunately, over 20 years, there have
723
      been instances of the road network remaining unchanged in DynST. We compared the road networks
724   for two consecutive days, and when they were completely identical, we merged them, ultimately
725   resulting in this fixed road network version of DynST.
726
727
      Thereby, we provide two versions of DynST, and the fixed one can be gained by running the merging
      script to process the dynamic version.

844       G      C HALLENGE AND F UTURE W ORK
845
846       DynST has greater potential for transfer learning, it also demands higher requirements for model
847       design. An interesting observation from our preliminary experiments is that the ST-GFSL exhibited
848       loss spikes on DynST of the same length, ultimately leading to model collapse. This further illustrates
849       the challenges posed by DynST for transfer learning, and subsequent research will need to design
850       new transfer learning approaches tailored for DynST. We believe potential approaches could include:
851
852               • More flexible node index encoding: STID (Shao et al., 2022) has demonstrated that distin-
853                 guishing between node indexes and timestamps is essential for the model. Previously, nodes
854                 had fixed indexes in the data matrix, which could be encoded using embedding techniques.
855
                    However, in DynST, due to the changing node, similar simple encoding methods are not
                    feasible. Furthermore, unseen nodes in downstream tasks will need to be encoded to align
856
                    with nodes from the pre-training phase for better transferring.
857
858               • Robust multi-scale network structures: As shown in the accompanying figure in the PDF
859                 materials, the number of nodes and edges varies significantly over the years. The model
860                 needs to better handle the dependencies between large and small graphs simultaneously.
861                 Another possible scenario is that the model relies on relevant points to make predictions
862                 for the target node, but at a certain moment, the data from those dependency points may
863                 dynamically be lost. This poses a greater requirement for the model’s robustness, as it
                    should not overly depend on specific points.

       I       L IMITATION
872
       With the dynamic evolution of traffic road networks over time, the patterns from 20 years ago may
873
       not directly benefit recent predictions. We deem that the routine setting of forecasting 12 steps based
874    on the previous 12 steps, ensures a strong correlation within the sample pair. While the extensive time
875    coverage enriches the dataset with diverse patterns, issues like pattern conflicts and useful information
876    selection are reserved for future research. Researchers have the flexibility to select specific time
877    ranges according to their particular needs.
878
       Based on the investigation, it is inferred that the existing datasets, apart from LargeST, have their
879
       adjacency matrices manually labeled by real maps. This approach is impractical for large datasets
880    like DynST. While tree-based adjacency matrix generation algorithms show significant improvements
881    compared to distance-based methods, there will always be discrepancies in real-world road network
882    conditions. We acknowledge this and welcome the emergence of better automatic labeling algorithms.
883
       PEMS is an ongoing project and our data collection work was completed by April 2024. We are
884
       considering implementing an automated data updating mechanism in the future.
