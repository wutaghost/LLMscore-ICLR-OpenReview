011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            A convenient approach to optimally solving combinatorial optimization tasks is
014            the Branch-and-Bound method. The branching heuristic in this method can be
015
               learned to solve a large set of similar tasks. The promising results here are
               achieved by the recently appeared on-policy reinforcement learning (RL) method
016
               based on the tree Markov Decision Process (MDP). To overcome its main dis-
017
               advantages, namely, very large training time and unstable training, we propose
018            TreeDQN, a sample-efficient off-policy RL method trained by optimizing the ge-
019            ometric mean of expected return. To theoretically support the training procedure
020            for our method, we prove the contraction property of the Bellman operator for
021            the tree MDP. As a result, our method requires up to 10 times less training data
022            and performs faster than known on-policy methods on synthetic tasks. Moreover,
023            TreeDQN significantly outperforms the state-of-the-art techniques on a challeng-
024            ing practical task from the ML4CO competition.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
      Industrial tasks in multiple areas such as logistics (Bertsimas & Van Ryzin, 1991), portfolio man-
029
      agement (Markowitz, 1952), manufacturing (Barahona et al., 1988), etc., can be formulated as
030
      combinatorial optimization problems, such as Mixed Integer Linear Programs (MILP), (Wolsey &
031   Nemhauser, 1999). Solving a large set of similar MILP tasks with varying parameters corresponding
032   to different numbers of vehicles, customer demands, locations, etc., is usually necessary. If each task
033   is solved independently, the optimal solution can be efficiently obtained with the Branch-and-Bound
034   algorithm (B&B) (Land & Doig, 2010). The B&B algorithm employs divide-and-conquer approach.
035   It iteratively builds a tree, where the root node corresponds to the initial problem, and child nodes
036   correspond to problems with the restricted domains. The performance of the B&B algorithm hardly
037   depends on two sequential decision-making processes: variable selection and node selection. Node
038   selection picks the next node in the B&B tree to evaluate, and variable selection chooses the next
039
      variable to split the domain (Linderoth & Savelsbergh, 1999). The efficiency of the solver depends
      on the number of the destination/leaf nodes of the tree. The variable selection method (branching
040
      rule) should produce trees of the smallest possible size. Although the optimal branching rule is
041
      unknown (Lodi & Zarpellon, 2017), modern solvers implement human-crafted heuristics designed
042   to perform well on a wide range of tasks (Gleixner et al., 2021).
043
044   Suppose a set of similar tasks with varying parameters is solved frequently. In that case, the B&B
045
      method can be significantly accelerated if a branching rule is adapted to the distribution of tasks by
      using the reinforcement learning (RL) paradigm (Etheve et al., 2020; Scavuzzo et al., 2022). To solve
046
      a MILP task, the B&B method generates a tree of nested subtasks. The typical RL algorithms are
047
      designed to process sequential data Sutton & Barto (2018). Hence, they cannot be directly applied
048   to the tree structure in the B&B algorithm. Indeed, the RL method, which learns optimal branching
049   decisions, should map this tree structure to an episode. A promising approach to such mapping is
050   to consider the decision-making process of a tree MDP instead of a temporal MDP Scavuzzo et al.
051   (2022). However, there exist several difficulties of known RL methods to the variable selection pro-
052   cess Etheve et al. (2020); Scavuzzo et al. (2022), namely: 1) the computational complexity of MILP
053   is very high, which limits the applicability of on-policy algorithms such as tMDP+DFS Scavuzzo
      et al. (2022); 2) it is challenging to accurately predict the resulting tree sizes, whose distributions

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      usually have extremely high variance; and 3) there is no theoretical evidence of convergence for tree
055   MDP of such methods as FMSTS Etheve et al. (2020).
056
057   To overcome these difficulties, this paper presents a novel RL method. In particular, our contribution
058
      is as follows:
059
            1. We present a novel sample efficient off-policy RL algorithm for the tree Markov Decision
060
               Process, TreeDQN, which is trained significantly faster than existing on-policy techniques
061
               for combinatorial optimization.
062
063         2. To overcome the issue with high variance of tree size distribution, we propose a loss func-
064
               tion that optimizes the geometric mean of expected return.
065         3. We provide a theoretical basis for using the Bellman operator to train the RL agent in our
066            TreeDQN by proving that the Bellman operator in the tree Markov Decision Process is
067            contracting in mean.
068         4. We demonstrate our method‚Äôs superior performance and learning efficiency on a set of
069            synthetic and practical MILP tasks. In particular, our TreeDQN is the first successful RL
070            algorithm to learn an efficient variable selection policy for the complex practical task from
071            the ML4CO competition (Gasse et al., 2022).

125   3     R ELATED WORK
126
127   3.1   H EURISTIC METHODS
128
      Practical implementations of the B&B algorithm in SCIP (Bestuzheva et al., 2021) and CPLEX
129
      (Cplex, 2009) solvers rely on handcrafted heuristics for node selection and variable selection. A
130
      straightforward strategy for node selection is Depth-First-Search (DFS), which aims to find any
131   integer feasible solution faster to prune branches that do not contain a better solution. The default
132   node selection heuristic in the SCIP tries to estimate the node with the lowest feasible solution. One
133   of the best-known heuristics for the variable selection is Strong Branching. It is a tree-size efficient
134   and computationally expensive branching rule (Achterberg, 2007). For each fractional variable with
135   integrality constraint, Strong Branching computes the lower bounds for the left and right child nodes
136   and uses them to choose the variable for splitting.
137
138   3.2   S UPERVISED LEARNING
139
140
      A statistical approach to learning a branching rule was applied in Khalil et al. (2016) for the first time.
      Authors used SVM (Cortes & Vapnik, 1995) to predict the variable ranking of an expert for a single
141
      task instance. Later works (Khalil et al., 2017) and (Selsam et al., 2018) proposed methods based
142
      on Graph Convolutional Networks (GCNN) (Kipf & Welling, 2017) to find an approximate solution
143   of combinatorial tasks. In Gasse et al. (2019), authors applied the same neural network architecture
144   to imitate the Strong Branching heuristic in sophisticated SCIP solver (Bestuzheva et al., 2021).
145   The imitation learning agent can not produce trees shorter than the expert. However, it solves the
146   variable selection task much faster, especially if running on GPU, thereby significantly speeding up
147   the whole B&B algorithm. In Gupta et al. (2020), the authors examined the choice of architectures
148   and proposed a hybrid model that combines the expressive power of GCNN with the computational
149   efficiency of multi-layer perceptrons. Despite the decrease in running time, imitation learning cannot
150   lead to better heuristics.
151
152   3.3   R EINFORCEMENT LEARNING
153
      RL is a promising direction to learn a variable selection rule for the B&B algorithm. It keeps
154
      the guarantees of the B&B method to find an optimal solution and can significantly accelerate the
155
      algorithm by optimal choices of branching variables. A natural minimization target for an agent
156   in the B&B algorithm is the size of the resulting tree. One of the main challenges here is to map
157   the variable selection process to the MDP and preserve the Markov property. In the B&B search
158   trees, probabilities of visiting descendant nodes p+ , p‚ąí depend on the global upper bound that
159   can be changed by the future branching decisions, which violate the Markov property. To learn a
160   branching rule, the FMSTS (Fitting for Minimizing the SubTree Size) algorithm was introduced in
161   Etheve et al. (2020). In their method, an agent plays an episode until termination, fitting the Q-
      function to the bootstrapped return. The authors used the DFS node selection strategy to enforce the


                                                             3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Markov property during training. This method is sample efficient since training data can be sampled
163   from a buffer of past experiences. However, it may not converge to the optimal policy because its
164   training data was obtained by older and less efficient versions of the Q-function. In Scavuzzo et al.
165   (2022), the authors proposed setting the global upper bound to the optimal solution for a MILP as
166   an alternative method to ensure the Markov property. They derived the policy gradient theorem for
167   the tree MDP (tMDP) and evaluated the REINFORCE-based agent on challenging tasks similar to
168   (Gasse et al., 2019). This method could learn an optimal policy because the agent uses only the latest
169   data. Still, it is sample inefficient since a single gradient step of the REINFORCE agent requires
170   solving a batch of MILP tasks. Both works (Etheve et al., 2020; Scavuzzo et al., 2022) use the
171
      cumulative return to update the agent. We improve their approaches in terms of sample efficiency
      and agent performance. We apply the tree Bellman operator to train the agent efficiently with the
172
      buffer of previous experiences. We use a novel learning objective that stabilizes the training in the
173
      presence of high-variance returns.

218   RL methods generally regress the expected return with the mean squared error (MSE) loss function,
219   thereby optimizing the prediction of the arithmetic mean. When solving a MILP problem, non-

      and execution time Gasse et al. (2019), we propose to optimize the geometric mean of the expected

515   6   C ONCLUSION
516
517   In this paper, we have presented a novel data-efficient deep RL method to learn a branching rule
518
      for the B&B algorithm (Alg. 1) and demonstrated its superiority over the existing RL-based tech-
      niques (Tab. 2, 3). We provided statistical tests (Tab. 2) which confirm the statistical significance
519
      of our experimental results. The synergy of the exact solving algorithm and data-driven heuristic
520
      takes advantage of both worlds: guarantees the computation of the optimal solution from the B&B
521   algorithm and the ability to adapt to specific tasks from the learned branching heuristic. In particu-
522   lar, we introduced the novel loss function (7), which stabilizes the training process in the presence
523   of high-variance returns and proves its superiority over the alternative approaches listed in Tab. 1.
524   As a typical RL application to MILP, our method is designed to perform well on the distribution of
525   similar MILP tasks (see formal task definition in Section 2). Thus, transferring the trained policy
526   to significantly different MILP tasks is out of the scope of the present paper and is considered a
527   promising direction for future research. We experimentally demonstrated the high performance of
528   our method on a set of synthetic and practical tasks compared to previously known solutions. Our
529
      TreeDQN method trains much faster than the previous RL methods, as shown in Fig. 1, it outper-
      forms state-of-the-art RL methods as demonstrated in Tab. 2 and can learn an efficient branching
530
      policy which outperforms imitation learning method in complex practical task when trained only on
531
      500 episodes which would not be possible with sample inefficient on-policy methods. The source
532   code is available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/treedqn-F72F.

849   D.2                  P ROBABILITY- PROBABILITY PLOTS
850
851   To further analyze the performance of our agent, we present distributions of tree sizes in the form of
852   probability-probability plots (P-P plots) in Fig. 6. P-P plot allows us to compare different cumulative
853   distribution functions (CDF). For a reference CDF F and a target CDF G P-P plot is constructed
854   similar to the ROC curve: we choose a threshold x, move it along the domain of F and draw
855
      points (F (x), G(x)). To show multiple distributions on the same plot, we use Strong Branching as
      reference CDF for all of them. If one curve is higher than another, the corresponding CDF is more
856
      extensive so that the associated agent can solve more tasks in x or nodes or less. All our baselines
857
      (except Strong Branching) have close complexity per call. So, if one curve is higher than another, the
858   corresponding agent can solve more tasks at the same time. This is related to winning rates, which
859   shows the number of instances solved in a specific time limit (see Appendix D.3, Tab. 9, 10, 9). From
860   the P-P plot for the Maximum Independent Set, we see that TreeDQN is good at solving simple
861   tasks where it performs better than Imitation Learning. When the tasks become more complex,
862   the performance of TreeDQN decreases. This behavior is the direct consequence of our learning
863   objective. We optimize the geometric mean of expected tree size, so complex task instances may
      have less influence on the learning process.
