009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent advancements in autonomous driving technologies involve the capability
012            to effectively process and learn from extensive real-world driving data. Current
013            imitation learning and offline reinforcement learning methods have shown remark-
014            able promise in autonomous systems, harnessing the power of offline datasets to
015            make informed decisions in open-loop (non-reactive agents) settings. However,
016
               learning-based agents face significant challenges when transferring knowledge
               from open-loop to closed-loop (reactive agents) environment. The performance is
017
               significantly impacted by data distribution shift, sample efficiency, the complex-
018
               ity of uncovering hidden world models and physics. To address these issues, we
019            propose Sample-efficient Imitative Multi-token Decision Transformer (SimDT).
020            SimDT introduces multi-token prediction, online imitative learning pipeline and
021            prioritized experience replay to sequence-modelling reinforcement learning. The
022            performance is evaluated through empirical experiments and results exceed popu-
023            lar imitation and reinforcement learning algorithms both in open-loop and closed-
024            loop settings on Waymax benchmark. SimDT exhibits 41% reduction in collision
025            rate and 18% improvement in reaching the destination compared with the baseline
026            method.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   The rapid advancement in machine learning has led to unprecedented achievements in the au-
031   tonomous systems domain. One of the critical methodologies in training these neural networks
032   involves leveraging large offline datasets. These datasets provide the foundational knowledge that
033   neural networks require to learn and make predictions or decisions. However, deploying these
034   offline-trained neural networks in real-world scenarios or simulators with reactive agents presents
035   challenges stemming from the distribution shift between the dataset and the application environment.
036   Learning from Demonstration (Dauner et al., 2023) (Cheng et al., 2023) (Huang et al., 2023) (Levine
037   et al., 2020) encounter hurdles when presented scenarios deviate from the training distribution, ex-
038   emplified by rare events like emergency braking for unforeseen obstacles. Similarly, these methods
039   grapple with long-tail distribution phenomena during closed-loop tests, such as navigating through
040   unexpected weather conditions or handling the erratic movements of jaywalking pedestrians.
041
      Interacting with environments allows for improved management of out-of-distribution issues in
042   closed-loop tests by learning policies focused on reward maximization, thus facilitating adaptation.
043   However, reinforcement learning (RL) faces challenges in overcoming the simulation-reality dis-
044   crepancy and suffers from low sampling efficiency (Kiran et al., 2022) (Liu et al., 2022). Traditional
045   reinforcement learning methods also grapple with the complexities of large state spaces, long-term
046   planning, and sparse rewards, mirroring the challenges of real-world driving scenarios. Decision
047   Transformer (Chen et al., 2021b) leverages a transformer-based architecture to learn policies for
048   decision-making in reinforcement learning tasks via sequence modeling. Despite its potential to
049   scale with large state space (Zhou et al., 2024), the original architecture and pipeline are designed
050
      for offline learning and are not enough for complex and dynamic autonomous driving tasks. Classic
      RL techniques like prioritized experience replay (Schaul et al., 2016) are designed for large-scale
051
      datasets but cannot be applied as the Decision Transformer does not compute temporal differences.
052
053   On the other hand, single-token prediction does not fully fit the nature of autonomous driving and
      has difficulty uncovering the hidden physical world model. Concentrating solely on single-token

      This paper seeks to address the challenges associated with closed-loop autonomous driving by in-
      troducing Sample-efficient Imitative Multi-token Decision Transformer. The proposed approach
074
      leverages real-world driving data and realistic simulators, providing scenarios that are both realistic
075
      and conducive to online adaptation to distribution shifts. By employing multi-token prediction via
076   sequence modeling of (state, action, return to go) pairs, the network is enabled to discern the quality
077   of various action sequences, thereby gaining a deeper insight into the underlying world model. Ex-
078   periment results indicate that our approach yields a substantial performance enhancement in terms
079   of policy robustness and sample efficiency both in open-loop and closed-loop settings. SimDT ex-
080   hibits 41% reduction in collision rate and 18% improvement in reaching the destination compared
081   with the baseline method. The work is conducted entirely with Jax, which facilitates highly efficient
082   training on large-scale data and real-time inference. The inference time is 1.63 milliseconds for
083   SimDT(median) on RTX 3090. The main contributions are as follows:
084          • We present an online imitative reinforcement learning pipeline designed for wide data dis-
085            tribution across a collaboration of real-world driving datasets and simulators.
086          • We propose multi-token Decision Transformer architecture for receding horizon control to
087            enhance long-horizon prediction and broaden the attention field.
088          • We introduce prioritized experience replay to sequence modeling-based reinforcement
089            learning and enable sample-efficient training for large-scale data.

093
      Reinforcement learning via sequence modeling. Trajectory Transformer (Janner et al., 2021) and
094
      Decision Transformer (DT) (Chen et al., 2021b) are pioneers in this area, leveraging transformer
095
      architectures to model sequences of state-action-reward trajectories and predicting future actions in
096   an offline manner. Following work (Lee et al., 2022) Meng et al. (2022) (Wu et al., 2023) (Badri-
097   nath et al., 2023) extends leverage the power of transformers for efficient and generalized decision-
098   making in RL. Online DT (Zheng et al., 2022) and Hyper DT (Xu et al., 2023) adapt the original
099   concept for online settings and interact with environments. However, previous work are done on
100   relatively simple environments compared to autonomous driving environments.
101
      Multi-token prediction. Transformers have significantly impacted NLP since their inception
102   (Vaswani et al., 2017), outperforming RNNs and LSTMs by processing sequences in parallel and
103   efficiently handling long-range dependencies. Subsequent models like GPT (Radford et al., 2018)
104   and BERT (Devlin et al., 2019) have refined the architecture, enhancing pre-training, fine-tuning,
105   and scalability. Recent studies explore multi-token prediction on semantic representation (Kitaev
106   et al., 2020), streamline computation (Wang & Cho, 2019), prediction technique (Qi et al., 2020)
107   and multilingual (Jiang et al., 2020). However, focusing only on single-token prediction makes the
      model too sensitive to the immediate context and overlooks the need for deeper analysis of longer

      sequences(Gloeckle et al., 2024). This paper extends the concept to Decision Transformer and ex-
109   plores the potential benefits of multi-token prediction for motion planning.

111   Learning with real-world driving data. Much work has been done to accommodate with real-
112
      world driving data (Sun et al., 2020)(H. Caesar, 2021)(Houston et al., 2020)(Phan-Minh et al., 2023)
      for generalizable driving policy. Lu et al. (2023) explores the cooperation between reinforcement

121   it inevitably has training and test distribution mismatch. Our approach differs as SimDT addi-

      designed for dynamic driving scenarios through sequence modeling. SimDT integrates a multi-

139

140

141
      cles with past 10 historical information are recorded in terms of [position x, position y, yaw, speed,

145

146

147
      specified goal position.

      The general idea of the proposed Algorithm 1 is to perform online imitative reinforcement learning
191
      with off-policy expert data for pre-training at the beginning. Subsequently, the model undergoes
192
      a mixed on-policy adaptation phase which is introduced at the mid-point of the training process.
193   The core concept behind this is to quickly shift the distribution towards the expert behavior at the
194   beginning and reduce environmental distribution shift with on-policy adaptation (OPA). Note on-
195   line policy adaptation and imitative reinforcement learning are performed concurrently after mid of
196   training, this helps the network not to fall into online local minimal.
197
      Imitative reinforcement learning is done by applying a similar concept as Shaped IL (Judah et al.,
198

199   lowing the same implementation in (Lu et al., 2023), expert data from real-world driving trajectory
200   was converted to expert agent actions with inverse kinematics. We also design negative rewards
201   for off-road and overlap (collision) behavior. The network learns good behavior through imitation
202   rewards and bad actions through online interaction with off-road and overlap rewards. The over-
203   all online imitative reinforcement pipeline is essential to achieve the greater data-distributed policy
204   described in Figure 1.

      The proposed imitative reinforcement learning method not only handles large volumes of offline
257
      demonstration data but also obtains an infinite amount of data through online interactions. The abil-
258   ity to utilize prioritized experience replay (PER) becomes critical for enhancing sample efficiency.
259   The original proposed PER(Schaul et al., 2016) selectively samples experiences with high temporal-
260   difference errors from the replay buffer for focusing on more informative experiences. However, DT
261   does not use temporal-difference errors and therefore precludes direct application of PER. Instead,
262   we adapt by using action loss to gauge transition importance within the Decision Transformer, which
263   assesses state-action-return relationships. The design concept is that if the model’s predicted actions
264   diverge from actual ones, it indicates a misinterpretation of the environment.
265   We develop prioritized experience replay for Decision Transformer on top of the previous online
266   imitative training pipeline(Algorithm 2). Extra replay buffers are designed to store prioritized sam-
267   pled trajectories based on action loss. The action loss represents the difference between the actions
268   predicted by the policy network and the actual actions taken. A low actor loss means that the policy
269   network’s predictions are close to the actual actions, while a high actor loss means that the pre-
      dictions are far from the actual actions. Different from bootstrapping RL algorithms which sample

271   of state-action-return pairs [[(si,j , ai,j , gi,j )]ti=t−c ]j for sequence modeling. Therefore, the replay
272   buffers designed for the decision transformer store a set of state-action-return pairs with a specified
273   context length. Our proposed prioritization rule takes advantage of these state-action-return pairs,
274   which try to analyze and understand either single scene or cumulative scenes. The proposed method
275   prioritizes trajectories based on the following criteria:
276
      Criterion 1: Preservation of transitions which contains single-step action discrepancy(Lsingle ):
277   This methodology concentrates on isolating the instances where the model’s prognostications man-
278   ifest the greatest deviation from expected accuracy. Such a strategy is instrumental in directing the
279   model’s learning efforts toward ameliorating its most significant errors. The replay buffer for single-
                                  per
280   step action discrepancy Dsingle  can generally hold unexpected collisions and out-of-line actions.
281

282
      This methodology is characterized by its emphasis on identifying and retaining sequences wherein

284   ors aimed at refining the model’s performance across a continuum of actions for context length of
285                                                                                      per

      Dataset, simulator and metrics. Training and experiments are conducted using the Waymo Open
298   Dataset and the Waymax simulator. Waymax provides embedded support for reinforcement learn-
299   ing and diverse scenarios drawn from real driving data. It integrates with the Waymo Open Motion
300   Dataset (WOMD), which offers 531,101 real-world driving scenarios for training and 44,096 sce-
301   narios for validation. Each scenario contains 90 frames of data. Specifically, WOMD v1.2 and the
302   exact same metrics (off-road rate, collision rate, kinematic infeasibility, average displacement error
303   (ADE)) from Waymax are used for benchmarking with the paper. Evaluations are conducted in both
304   open-loop and closed-loop settings for a wider range of performance comparisons.
305   Implementation Detail. Models of various sizes are developed to quickly conduct ablation studies
306   and assess final performance effectively. Raw observation takes ego vehicle, 15 nearest dynamic
307   obstacles, 300 of closest roadgraph elements, traffic signals and position goal as input. The total
308   size for each step observation is 8892 and feature extraction is applied to reduce the total size.
309   SimDT(small) has 384 tokens for each element of (s, a, g) pair, 10 blocks, 16 attention heads and
310   in total 22.6 million parameters. SimDT(small) has 512 tokens for each element of (s, a, g) pair, 15
311   blocks, 16 attention heads and in total 53.2 million parameters. Both models use context length with
312
      value 10, which means the causal transformer has access to past 10 (s, a, g) pairs.

414   A key aspect of the Decision Transformer is its ability to model the relationship between state,
415   action, and return-to-go (gt ). Given the same state observation, expert actions are associated with a
416   higher gt , and sub-optimal actions with a lower gt . Ideally, DT should learn to act differently (e.g.
417
      choose an expert action, avoid driving out of the lane, or prevent collisions) based on the gt value.
      When a predicted action significantly diverges from the ground truth action given state observation
418
      and specific gt , it indicates a misinterpretation of the environment. Intuitively, such misbehaved
419
      experiences need further reinforcement through the replay buffer.
420
421   From a high-level perspective, training on large-scale real-world data presents uneven scenario dis-
422   tributions. When the network begins to generalize common scenarios, rare scenarios like Figures
423
      4(a) and 4(b) are undertrained. At this point, the network performs relatively well on common sce-
      narios (low action loss) but not on rare scenarios (high action loss). Consequently, the replay buffer
424
      starts storing more of these rare scenarios. Criterion 2 is applied with accumulated action loss to
425
      prioritize such scenarios. Figure 4(c) illustrates a policy rollout during online adaptation where sub-
426   optimal actions cause changes in gt . What is important is the record of the corresponding gt with
427   respect to action, where expert actions and sub-optimal actions are respectively given positive and
428   negative rewards. eg. Figure 4(c) shows the ego vehicle driving out of the lane with a recorded de-
429   crease in gt value -2. It is crucial for the neural network to understand which actions cause a -2 value
430   drop in gt . These suboptimal state-action-return sequences are also important as expert driving data
431   doesn’t contain such behavior. The next time the agent encounters a similar scenario, the correct gt
      will help prevent out-of-lane actions.

      5   C ONCLUSION
476
477
      We present SmiDT, a novel sequence modeling-based reinforcement learning approach for closed-
478
      loop driving and complex real-world driving scenarios. Our fully online imitative Decision Trans-
479
      former pipeline is adept at handling diverse data distributions found within extensive driving
480   datasets, ensuring wide applicability and robustness. By implementing a multi-token Decision
481   Transformer that integrates receding horizon control, we improve the model’s ability to predict
482   over longer horizons and extend its attention span across broader contexts. Furthermore, the in-
483   corporation of prioritized experience replay within our framework enhances the sample efficiency
484   of training, allowing for more effective learning from large-scale datasets. Our work can also benefit
485   other real-world robotics tasks that demand sample-efficient learning from expert demonstration and
      online adaptation.

687   B. C HOICE FOR IMITATION REWARD LOG DIVERGENCE THRESHOLD
688
689   The log divergence is the distance between the logged position and the actual agent position. The
690   key here is to reward the system when the agent is close to expert human actions. When performing
691
      real-world human demonstration through inverse kinematics, the log divergence is close to zero.
      The reason to set it to 0.2 is to reward policy agents. However, we consider 0.2 to be the maximum
692
      that we can set as values bigger than 0.2 can lead to a collision and out-of-lane actions which should
693
      not be rewarded. In general, we do not see a significant difference when varying values from 0.05
694   to 0.2. The reason can be 1. Change of threshold values has no impact on reward functions during
695   the offline reinforcement learning phase. 2. Values under 0.2 are still relatively small for agents to
696   follow during the online exploration phase.
