011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            This paper introduces a novel benchmark for evaluating the logical reasoning ca-
014            pabilities of Large Language Models (LLMs), grounded in the polynomial ana-
015            logue of Gandy’s classical fixed point theorem. Since this theorem can be used to
016
               describe the P-complete HornSAT problem, and our benchmark is based on this
               theorem, our benchmark thus covers all problems from class P and shows that se-
017
               rious problems have already arisen in this class, not to mention those benchmarks
018
               whose complexity classes are NP-complete and NP-hard. Drawing on concepts
019            from mathematical logic, we design a parameterized set of recursively definable
020            problems where the objective is for LLMs to predict whether a problem belongs
021            to an inductively definable set of polynomial complexity. By varying the param-
022            eters, we generate problem instances of differing complexity. Our experiments
023            reveal that current state-of-the-art LLMs with zero-shots promts fail to reliably
024            solve even the most straightforward cases despite an effective deterministic al-
025            gorithm existing. Even advanced models like GPT-4 exhibit significant biases in
026            solving benchmark problems. These findings highlight the limitations of modern
027
               LLMs as code interpreters, even in basic scenarios, and underscore the necessity
               for hybrid LLM/interpreter systems. Furthermore, they emphasize the importance
028
               of developing quantitative tests for reasoning, given the increasing reliance on
029
               LLM-based systems in decision-making applications.

034   1.1   M OTIVATION
035
036   The question of whether large language models (LLMs) can think has been a subject of intense de-
037   bate for many years (Mirzadeht et al., 2024), and it is only getting hotter as large language models
038   solve more and more problems. To address this question, researchers are designing increasingly
039
      complex tests and challenges for LLMs, which allow us to assess the cognitive capabilities of these
      models more precisely. From a logical perspective, a key component of any intelligent system being
040
      able to think is the ability to reason and construct proofs from basic axioms, as well as the ability
041
      to work with recursive definitions (Goncharov & Nechesov, 2021b) If we look at the problems from
042   the other side, all problems can be divided into complexity classes P, NP-complete, NP-hard, etc.
043   Many benchmarks try to cover all of these classes at once(Lizhou Fan et al., 2024), but if LLMs
044   cannot solve problems from class P well, then it is pointless to try to study questions in the more
045   complex classes NP-complete and NP-hard. These findings are supported by the results obtained
046   from most studies that aim to tackle problems spanning across different complexity levels simulta-
047   neously. When we approach problems from the perspective of formalization, the majority of them
048   can be articulated through non-recursive descriptions with first-order logic formulas. However, when
049   a problem permits a recursive representation, its formulation in logical terms becomes significantly
050
      more compact. It is convenient to work with such recursive descriptions of problems and send them
      to the input of LLMs together with the objects for which these problems need to be solved.
051
052   In this paper, we propose to focus on a unique combination of problems that lie in the class P and
053   at the same time admit a recursive description. If LLMs cannot solve these problems, then most
      often it is pointless to move on to more complex problems from the NP-complete and NP-hard

      classes, since their complexity becomes higher and at the same time most of them also admit a
055   recursive description. To navigate through recursive structures while remaining within the confines
056   of class P, the PAG theorem (Appendix A) and FPAG theorem (Nechesov & Goncharov, 2024) prove
057   invaluable, enabling us to define sets and functions that can be described recursively with polynomial
058   complexity. In this investigation, we focus on the PAG theorem, demonstrating that even within this
059   framework, LLMs encounter significant difficulties in determining whether an object belongs to an
060   inductively defined collection of entities. The uniqueness of the benchmark is achieved due to its
061   unique properties - in essence, we are trying to solve recursively defined problems, but at the same
062   time do not jump out of the class P.
063   The HornSAT problem (Adebayo et al., 2022), which lies in the class P and is a simplification of
064   the NP-complete SAT problem, also falls under the recursive description. The HornSAT problem
065   can be solved using a modified PAG theorem, where we can to transfer the main concepts of the
066   PAG theorem from nested lists to the set of horn formulas, and also had to abandon generating
067   families in favor of the set of horn formulas. This was achieved by redefining the function γ, which
068   now takes some horn formula as input and produces another shorter horn formula according to a
069
      recursive P-computable algorithm for Horn satisfiability (hor). The operator ΓHF acts on the set
      of all subsets of horn formulas in the set of all subsets of horn formulas. In this case, the smallest
070
      fixed point Γ∗ of the operator ΓHF is P-computable. Without loss of generality, we can assume
071
      that the HornSAT problem is a special case of a modified polynomial analogue of Gandy’s theorem.
072   A similar approach can be used to describe the 2-SAT problem using a modified PAG theorem.
073   Since the 2-SAT problem reduces to a 2N×2N adjacency matrix, a transitive closure is iteratively
074   constructed for it using a modified version of the Floyd-Warshall algorithm. (2sa). Due to the fact
075   that the HornSAT problem is p-complete, we can assume that our benchmark based on the PAG
076   theorem covers all problems from the class P. This is its uniqueness and universality.
077
      Large language models have a transformative effect on various domains of science, including lan-
078   guage processing and understanding (Ouyang et al., 2022), music and speech processing (Agostinelli
079   et al., 2023), smart cities and digital twins of complex objects, and even drug and protein de-
080   sign (Madani et al., 2023). These models, such as GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023), Claude (cla) and
081   their open-source competitors like Llama (Dubey et al., 2024) and Mistral (Jiang et al., 2023), have
082   demonstrated remarkable capabilities in tasks ranging from simple text generation to complex math-
083   ematical problem-solving. The remarkable advancements of LLMs were enabled by the abundance
084   of unlabelled text data (Penedo et al., 2024) and the effective unsupervised training at scale (Kaplan
085   et al., 2020).
086   Despite these successes, there are notable limitations in the current methodologies for evaluating the
087   reasoning capabilities of LLMs. Standardized benchmarks (Hendrycks et al., 2020; Yue et al., 2024;
088   Zheng et al., 2023) have shown that LLMs excel in few-shot and zero-shot tasks, often outperforming
089   previous state-of-the-art models by large margins. However, these benchmarks may not fully capture
090   the models’ ability to generalize and reason logically, as evidenced by various studies highlighting
091   often unexpected failures Nezhurina et al. (2024) that contradict the claimed strong capabilities.
092   Addressing these limitations is crucial for applying LLMs in real-world scenarios, where accurate,
093   logical reasoning is essential. For instance, in fields like healthcare, legal services, and automated
094   decision-making, the reliability of AI models can have significant implications for outcomes and
095   trustworthiness. Therefore, there is an urgent need for new evaluation frameworks Gao et al. (2023);
096   Guha et al. (2024); Wang (2024) that can more accurately assess the logical capabilities of LLMs in
097   different settings.
098   In response to this need, we introduce a new benchmark designed to evaluate the reasoning abilities
099   of LLMs in recursive problems. Our approach involves generating [object, condition] pairs, where
100   objects are nested lists of elements (for example, numbers) and conditions are expressed with recur-
101   sive functions. The task of the LLM is to determine whether the object satisfies all conditions. This
102   setup, expressed in Python language, leverages the extension of fixed-point Gandy’s theorem (Gon-
103   charov & Nechesov, 2021b), ensuring that the check can be performed efficiently at most quadratic
104
      times relative to the object’s size. Despite an efficient verification algorithm, our findings indicate
      that all tested LLMs fail to solve even simple instances of these problems, highlighting a signif-
105
      icant gap in their logical reasoning capabilities. The source code of the benchmark is available
106
      at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/logic-lm-0B9C.

      2   R ELATED WORK
109
110
      As the capabilities of large language models themselves increase, so does the number of various
111
      benchmarks that test their capabilities in various areas including the ability to solve mathematical
112
      and logical problems. Most often, the problems that arise when testing LLMs can be divided into
113   complexity classes: P, NP-complete, and NP-hard.
114
115   In our research, we primarily focus on problems from the P class, as we rely on the PAG (Gon-
116
      charov & Nechesov, 2021b) and FPAG theorems (Nechesov & Goncharov, 2024) the powerful tools
      for constructing polynomially computable sets of inductively defined objects and also for defining
117
      recursive functions of polynomial complexity. This allows us to see how the larger language models
118
      handle these constructions.
119
120   The complexity arises from LLMs’ need to effectively handle recursive operations that inevitably
121   emerge when tackling such problems. Recursive definitions serve as a key factor in increasing
122
      complexity. By carefully managing recursion, we can either maintain operations within the P class
      or transition to NP-complete or NP-hard classes.
123
124   As our research shows, even within the complexity class P, there exist tasks that pose a challenge
125   for large language models, suggesting that higher-level classes such as NP-complete and NP-hard
126   become simply unsolvable in most cases. It is futile to engage in experimentation with issues in these
127   domains unless one possesses a comprehensive understanding of the root causes for tasks from class
128
      P.
129   In the work under consideration Lizhou Fan et al. (2024), attention is directed towards tasks span-
130   ning various complexity levels, encompassing P, NP-complete, and NP-hard classes. The research
131   aims to comprehensively explore these diverse complexity classes, rather than focusing narrowly on
132   a specific aspect. However as we noted earlier in the context of this work, where researchers try
133   to solve problems belonging to the NP-complete and NP-hard classes without having fully compre-
134
      hended the intricacies of problems within the class P.
135   In another work (Rishi Hazra et al., 2024) the solution of the 3-SAT problem using LLM occurred
136   with a small number of free variables (no more than 10). That is a 3-SAT problem in which it
137   is possible to find a solution manually by enumeration. It turned out that even with such a small
138   number of parameters, LLMs cope poorly with them. As expected, with an increase in parameters,
139   their capabilities sharply decrease.
140   If we look at benchmarks that have made a lot of noise in the LLM community, then of course this
141   is the Alice problem. (Nezhurina et al., 2024) This problem can be solved by an 8-year-old child,
142   but most of the leading LLMs could not do it. If we want to build trusted AI algorithms based on
143   LLMs, then it is very important to start with the simplest problems to understand where they make
144   mistakes and where they do not. The most acceptable complexity class for these problems is in class
145   P. That is why it is so important to study this complexity class in as much detail as possible, which
146
      is what we do in this work.

      4    M ETHODS
208
209
      Based on the results of the PAG theorem, we build a generator of problem instances with varying
210   complexity. Each problem has the form of a set of generating families F, a test object x ∈ X
211   and the equality Fi (x) == 1, Fi ∈ F. We call the set of generating functions a condition and the
212   test object x the probe. In the following sections, we will show how to express the condition as a
213   Python program and present a probe generator algorithm, which allows the building of probes of any
214   depth and any desired result when substituted in Eq. 7. The resulting Python code can be directly
215   supplied to LLMs, and the response can be evaluated without human intervention, thus providing an
      automatic benchmark system.

      6   D ISCUSSION
541
542
      The construction of benchmark systems is essential steps in the evaluation of LLMs performance.
543   Our reasoning benchmark is based on the results of the polynomial analog of Gandy’s classical fixed
544   point theorem and provides means for principled LLMs evaluation. We found that even smallest
545   problem instances are challenging for state of the art LLMs.
546
      The unexpected finding was a significant bias inherent to every tested LM. No model demonstrated
547
      an expected Bernoulli distribution-like behavior when tested on uniform positive or negative prob-
548
      lem instances, with GPT-3.5 coming closer to the expected distribution. This observation suggests
549   that LLMs may have difficulty with the generation of random data. As the most extreme case, biased
550   behavior renders GPT-4-turbo the worst performer in our test, suggesting that bias increases with
551   the model scale.
552
      Contrary to what may be expected, models trained to understand code demonstrated worse perfor-
553
      mance than general-purpose LLMs. We plan to adapt our benchmark to natural language formulation
554
      to make it even more natural for general purpose LLMs.
555
556   Overall, our experiments demonstrate an unusual behavior of LLMs for recursive code-
557   understanding tasks. None of the models can consistently solve even simple problem instances
558
      despite the existence of a deterministic algorithm with quadratic complexity (Depth-First search
      with backtracking). Our findings stipulate the revision of language model training procedures to
559
      eliminate biases we observed, particularly in contexts where high accuracy and reliability in reason-
560
      ing are required.
