011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent advancements in large multimodal models (LMMs) have significantly en-
014            hanced performance across diverse tasks, with ongoing efforts to further integrate
015            additional modalities such as video and audio. However, most existing LMMs
016            remain vulnerable to hallucinations, the discrepancy between the factual multi-
017            modal input and the generated textual output, which has limited their applicability
018            in various real-world scenarios. This paper presents the first systematic investi-
019            gation of hallucinations in LMMs involving the three most common modalities:
020
               language, visual, and audio. Our study reveals two key contributors to hallucina-
               tions: overreliance on unimodal priors and spurious inter-modality correlations.
021
               To address these challenges, we introduce the benchmark The Curse of Multi-
022
               Modalities (CMM), which comprehensively evaluates hallucinations in LMMs,
023            providing a detailed analysis of their underlying issues. Our findings highlight
024            key vulnerabilities, including imbalances in modality integration and biases from
025            training data, underscoring the need for balanced cross-modal learning and en-
026            hanced hallucination mitigation strategies. Based on our observations and find-
027            ings, we suggest potential research directions that could enhance the reliability of
028            LMMs. We will make our code and data publicly available.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Large Multi-modal Models (LMMs) have rapidly advanced, driving significant improvements across
034   a wide range of tasks by effectively integrating and processing diverse data modalities. These mod-
035   els (Li et al., 2024a; Zhang et al., 2024; Hong et al., 2024; Yao et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024a;
036   Achiam et al., 2023; Team et al., 2023; Ormazabal et al., 2024), leveraging multimodal inputs such
037   as image and text, have achieved notable performance gains, particularly in generating contextually
038   accurate textual outputs. As the field evolves, there is a growing trend toward incorporating addi-
039
      tional modalities, such as audio and video (Xu et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024a; Wang et al., 2024c;
      Zhang et al., 2023; Cheng et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024b; Kong et al., 2024; Tang et al., 2023; Ghosh
040
      et al., 2024; Chu et al., 2024), to enhance LMMs’ ability to understand and interact with complex
041
      real-world environments. However, despite these advancements, LMMs are prone to a critical is-
042   sue known as hallucination, where the generated outputs do not accurately reflect the multimodal
043   inputs (Liu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023a; 2024d; Nishimura et al., 2024). This issue can severely
044   undermine the reliability and applicability of LMMs in real-world scenarios, particularly in tasks
045   requiring precise and factual content generation.
046
      Hallucination, particularly object hallucination, has been a key focus in LMMs that handle image
047
      and text inputs. Object hallucination occurs when LMMs generate semantically coherent but factu-
048   ally unaligned contents with the actual objects present in the input images. Various benchmarks (Li
049   et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2023b; Lovenia et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023a) and mitigation techniques
050   have been proposed to address this issue by refining training processes (Liu et al., 2023), imple-
051   menting post-hoc correction (Leng et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2023), etc. However, accommodating
052   additional modalities like audio and video exacerbates alignment and fusion difficulties (Lahat et al.,
053   2015; Dimitri, 2022; Tong et al., 2024; Liang et al., 2024), which may lead to increased hallucina-
      tions.

      This study systematically examines how LMMs produce hallucinations while integrating language,
055   visual, and audio inputs, revealing the prevalence and causes of hallucinations under such multi-
056   modal scenarios. Two key contributors are identified: (1) Overreliance on unimodal priors: Models
057   over-rely on data from a single modality, neglecting others. This results in outputs that do not accu-
058   rately reflect the full range of input data, as models default to familiar patterns within one modality
059   despite contradictory signals from others. (2) Spurious inter-modality correlations: Models learn
060   erroneous cross-modal associations based on patterns that appear statistically significant but lack
061   meaningful or causal connections, leading to plausible but counterfactual outputs. We introduce
062   The Curse of Multi-Modalities (CMM), a comprehensive benchmark for assessing hallucinations in
063
      LMMs, covering a wide range of scenarios across visual, audio, and their joint contexts. CMM con-
      verts hallucination evaluation into a binary classification task through object-level and event-level
064
      probing. It comprises 1, 200 video/audio/video-audio samples across various multimodal contexts,
065
      ensuring balanced evaluation with 2, 400 probing questions evenly split between queries for exis-
066   tent and non-existent objects/events. LMMs are prompted with straightforward yes-or-no questions
067   regarding the presence of specific objects or events in the input modalities.
068
069
      CMM is the first benchmark to systematically investigate LMMs’ hallucinations in such comprehen-
      sive multimodal settings. Unlike prior benchmarks that broadly assess hallucination performance,
070
      CMM segments hallucinations into nuanced subcategories under two key contributors: spurious
071
      inter-modality correlations (e.g., Visual-Language, Audio-Language, Visual-Audio-Language) and
072   unimodal overreliance (e.g., Language Domianance, Visual Dominance, Audio Dominance), en-
073   abling precise diagnosis of LMM vulnerabilities and shedding light on possible improvements.
074   By introducing diagnostic metrics including perception accuracy (PA) and hallucination resistance
075   (HR), CMM offers a comprehensive framework for gauging both perception capabilities and hallu-
076   cination severity in LMMs. In summary, the contributions of this work are threefold:
077
078       • We conduct the first systematic investigation of hallucinations in LMMs across language, visual,
079
            and audio modalities, identifying their key contributors including unimodal prior overreliance
            and spurious inter-modality correlations.
080
081       • We introduce a novel and comprehensive benchmark, The Curse of Multi-Modalities (CMM),
082         which evaluates hallucinations using object-level and event-level probing within a binary classi-
083         fication framework. CMM defines hallucinations with nuanced subcategories and granularities,
084
            enabling comprehensive diagnosis of LMM vulnerabilities across various modalities.
085       • We evaluate a diverse set of state-of-the-art LMMs across visual, audio, and joint contexts, re-
086         vealing critical insights in model limitations and fundamental challenges in multimodal learn-
087         ing. Our thorough analysis and discussion pinpoint future directions for mitigating hallucina-
088
            tions and enhancing LMM reliability, providing a viable roadmap for improvements.

090   2     A NALYZING H ALLUCINATIONS ACROSS L ANGUAGE , V ISUAL , AND AUDIO
091
092   This section systematically investigates the underlying causes of hallucinations in Large Multi-
093   modal Models (LMMs). It includes qualitative demonstrations and comprehensive statistical analy-
094   sis from two key perspectives: Overreliance on Unimodal Priors and Spurious Inter-modality Cor-
095   relations. Our analysis provides empirical evidence and quantifies the extent to which these factors
096   influence LMMs’ reliability.
097   Notations. Consider an LMM parametrized by θ that processes inputs from three modalities: lan-
098   guage x, visual v, and audio a. The model generates textual output y autoregressively, where each
099   token yt is conditioned on all three modalities and the previously generated tokens y<t :
100
                                             yt ∼ pθ (yt | v, a, x, y<t ),
101
102   where yt represents the token at time step t, and y<t denotes the sequence of tokens generated up to
103   time step t − 1.

126   Language Dominance, also know as language biases (Rohrbach et al., 2018; Leng et al., 2024;
127   Guan et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024b), arises when a model excessively depends on pre-trained large
128   language models (LLMs), generating responses that adhere to linguistic patterns or prior knowl-
129
      edge from large language corpora, even when visual or audio inputs provide contradictory infor-
      mation. This issue is particularly prevalent in LMMs that integrate LLMs as their decoder base.
130
      These LLMs Chiang et al. (2023); Jiang et al. (2023); Yang et al. (2024), due to their proficiency
131
      in capturing linguistic structures and semantic relationships, often dominate the decision-making
132   process, overshadowing contributions from visual or audio modalities. As illustrated in Fig. 1a,
133   a video depicts finger skateboarding with shoes on fingers. When asked by the language-biased
134   question “Did you see shoes worn on feet?”—reflecting commonsense event that follows linguis-
135   tic priors—LMMs respond “yes,” contradicting the actual content and inducing hallucination. This
136   demonstrates LMMs’ tendency to rely on language priors over factual multimodal inputs.
137
      Visual Dominance occurs when a model over-relies on visual information, underutilizing or dis-
138   regarding linguistic and auditory cues. In such cases, the model-generated outputs are heavily in-
139   fluenced by visual context, often neglecting important information from the other modalities. As
140   illustrated in Fig. 1b, a video depicts a person planning a woodworking project with a hammer
141   in sight, while the audio track contains only the person speaking and bird chirping. Despite this,
142   advanced LMMs may over-rely on the visual presence of the “hammer” and incorrectly infer a
143   “hammer hitting” sound, ignoring the actual audio content where no such sound is present.
144   Audio Dominance arises when a model excessively relies on auditory input, disregarding visual or
145   linguistic information. As illustrated in Fig. 1c, a video captures a person recording a village view
146   through a window, showing dark clouds. The audio track contains evident thunderstorm sounds,
147   but no lightning is visible. Despite this, LMMs may over-rely on the audio cues, hallucinating that
148   lightning is visible in the scene, thereby disregarding the actual visual content.
149

150   Fig. 2, hypothesizing that altering information from a dominant modality would significantly affect
151   the model’s responses if hallucinations are primarily due to overreliance on that modality.

      2.2    S PURIOUS I NTER - MODALITY C ORRELATIONS
185
186   Spurious inter-modality correlations are a major contributor to hallucinations in LMMs, especially
187   when integrating multiple modalities. Learned during pretraining on large-scale multimodal datasets
188   (e.g., image-caption, video-caption, and audio-caption data (Lin et al., 2014; Kim et al., 2019; Bain
189   et al., 2021; Schuhmann et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023b; Sun et al., 2024)), these correlations involve
190   misleading associations between modalities that appear statistically significant but lack meaningful
191
      or causal connections. Two common sources of spurious correlations are: (1) Global occurrence
      frequency: The high overall occurrence of specific objects or events in the dataset leads LMMs to
192
      hallucinate these elements even when they are absent in the input. (2) Co-occurrence frequency:
193
      Frequent co-occurrence of objects or events during training causes the model to incorrectly predict
194   the presence of one of them when only the other is present. While spurious object-level correlations
195   between language and visual inputs have been extensively studied (Rohrbach et al., 2018; Li et al.,
196   2023; Zhou et al., 2023), integrating additional modalities like audio introduces new complexities,
197   resulting in increasingly intricate spurious correlations. We categorize them into three subtypes:
198
199    • Visual-Language (VL): The model hallucinates visual objects or events based on pre-training
200      patterns. For instance, if “phone” frequently co-occurs with “human” in captions, the model may
201      hallucinate a phone upon recognizing a human, even when no phone is present.
202    • Audio-Language (AL): The model links absent sound events to textual descriptions due to over-
203      represented patterns in pre-training data. For example, if “dog barking” frequently appears dur-
204      ing pre-training, the model may hallucinate this audio event even when the dog in the current
205      input simply whimpers 1 .
206    • Visual-Audio-Language (VAL): Spurious correlations arise from frequent co-occurrence of vi-
207      sual objects and audio events in video-audio joint training. For example, if “bird chirping” in
208      audio descriptions is often paired with “tree” in visual annotations, the model may hallucinate to
209      see trees when only hearing birds, or vice versa.
210
      To validate spurious inter-modality correlations, we curate 200 samples for each subtype, paired with
211
      probing questions that target non-existent objects or events based on learned co-occurrence patterns.
212

      3.2       DATA C ONSTRUCTION

272   3.2.1       C ONSTRUCTING Q UERIES FOR OVERRELIANCE ON U NIMODAL P RIORS
273
274   To assess overreliance on a single modality (visual, audio, or language), we construct targeted prob-
275   ing queries that test the model’s dependence on one modality while ignoring complementary signals.
276   Visual Dominance This subcategory tests whether LMMs hallucinate audio events based on visual
277   input, where we construct queries asking about the existence of specific audio event. While queries
278   with a “yes” answer are manually annotated, non-existent events are sourced from video-audio pairs
279   in the AudioCaps dataset (Kim et al., 2019), where visual objects that do not correspond to any audio
280   content are identified. These samples are manually verified to ensure accurate annotation, setting
281   the ground truth answer as “no.”
282   Audio Dominance We probe LMMs’ tendency to infer incorrect visual content from audio cues.
283   Queries ask about the presence of visual objects, with “yes” queries annotated manually. For “no”
284   queries, we filter video-audio pairs from AudioCaps where audio-indicated objects have no visual
285   representation, confirmed through manual review.
286
      Language Dominance To explore how language priors contribute to hallucinations, we define sets
287   of common-sense events (e.g., “fish swim in water”) and object attributes (e.g., “yellow banana”) to
288   reflect typical linguistic biases. Videos are manually sourced from YouTube to depict anti-common-
289   sense scenarios (e.g., “fish fly in the air,” “black banana”). For existence-probing queries, we ask
290   about the video’s anti-common-sense object/event, annotating the ground truth as “yes.” Conversely,
291   for non-existence probing queries, we test for the common-sense version of the object/event, setting
292   the ground truth as “no.”
293
294   3.2.2       C ONSTRUCTING Q UERIES FOR S PURIOUS I NTER - MODALITY C ORRELATIONS
295
296   We evaluate hallucinations arising from Spurious Inter-modality Correlations, constructing object-
297   level and event-level queries across visual, audio, and textual associations3 .
298   Visual-Language Hallucinations are assessed based on associations between visual content and
299   textual descriptions. Object-level queries are derived from (i) global appearance frequencies and (ii)
300   co-occurrence frequencies within the WebVid10M (Bain et al., 2021) video-caption dataset. Event-
301   level queries, however, are constructed based on (i) global appearance patterns and (ii) [subject]-
302   [action object] co-occurrence patterns. All probing samples are curated from WebVid10M.
303   Audio-Language Hallucinations derived from associations between audio and text are probed
304   through event-level queries, given the temporal nature of audio. Queries are formed from (i) global
305   appearance frequencies and (ii) subject-oriented co-occurrence patterns, based on data from the
306   Auto-acd (Sun et al., 2024).
307
      Visual-Audio-Language This subcategory explores hallucinations across visual and audio modali-
308   ties. Queries probe non-existent audio events based on existent co-occurred visual objects and vice
309   versa, with data sourced from AudioCaps (Kim et al., 2019), focusing on co-occurrence frequencies
310   between visual objects and audio events.

453   4.3      D ISCUSSIONS
454
455   Effects of Probing Granularities Our benchmark includes both object-level and event-level prob-
456
      ing questions across subcategories6 . As shown in Tab. 3, most models show lower PA scores for
      event-level queries than object-level ones, highlighting the challenge posed by temporal complex-
457
      ity and the limited availability of event-oriented training data. For Visual-Language (VL) spurious
458
      correlations, event-level probing yields higher HR scores than object-level probing. This may be
459   due to the scarcity of event-level annotations in visual-text pretraining data, while object-level an-
460   notations are more prevalent, fostering stronger spurious correlations. Conversely, within Language
461   Dominance under Unimodal Overreliance, HR scores are lower for event-level queries. This pat-
462   tern is likely due to the autoregressive nature of large language models, which increases reliance on
463   language priors as the length of processed sequences grows, heightening the risk of hallucinations,
464   especially when longer event-related common-sense knowledge is involved.
465   Effects of Probing Modalities The Visual-Audio-Language (VAL) subcategory examines spurious
466   correlations arising from the co-occurrence of visual objects and audio events. It includes two prob-
467   ing types: (1) object-level queries about non-existent visual objects when frequently co-occurring
468   audio events are present, and (2) event-level queries about non-existent audio events when frequently
469   co-occurring visual objects are present. Despite both probing types originating from similar co-
470   occurrence patterns, HR scores for event-level (audio) probing are significantly lower than those for
471   object-level (visual) probing across all models (Tab. 3). This finding aligns with Sec. 4.2.1’s analysis
472   of Visual and Audio Dominance under Unimodal Overreliance, suggesting a bias towards visual data
473
      due to its abundance in training and the visual-centric nature of joint visual-audio pretraining. As
      a result, models tend to over-rely on visual cues, leading to more pronounced hallucinations when
474
      predicting non-existent audio events.
475
476   Effects of LLM Sizes We analyzed the impact of LLM decoder sizes on two LMMs, PLLaVA
477   and LLaVA-OneVision7 . As shown in Tab. 5, increasing the LLM size has minimal influence on
478   HR scores for Visual-Language spurious correlations, supporting our claim that these correlations
479
      primarily arise from global appearance and co-occurrence patterns in training data. In contrast,
      larger LLM sizes consistently improve HR scores for Language Dominance. For example, LLaVA-
480
      OneVision’s HR score increases from 55.0 (0.5B LLM) to 75.5 (34B LLM), suggesting that larger
481
      LLMs are more adept at managing complex or contradictory multimodal inputs. Smaller LLMs,
482   however, are more susceptible to overfitting to linguistic priors, leading to higher hallucination rates
483   when faced with content that deviates from expected patterns.

      Future Directions Our analysis identifies key vulnerabilities in current LMMs, representing only
487   a subset of broader challenges. These include but are not limited to unbalanced cross-modal in-
488   tegration, often with visual dominance overshadowing audio or text cues; spurious inter-modality
489   correlations arising from training biases; overreliance on linguistic priors from large-scale LLM
490   pretraining; and divergent response tendencies—either overconfident approval or overly cautious
491   rejection. To address these challenges, we propose several potential directions for reference:
492
493    • Balanced Multi-modal Training Data: Creating datasets with balanced modality representation
494      and diverse temporal annotations to reduce visual biases and improve event-level understanding.
495    • Advanced Cross-modal Fusion: Implementing dynamic fusion strategies to adjust modality im-
496      portance based on context can improve multimodal integration and reduce hallucination.
497
       • Mitigating Linguistic Priors: Fine-tuning LMMs with contextually diverse prompts and incor-
498
         porating visual/audio fact-checking mechanisms can decrease overreliance on language priors.
499
500    • Refined Safety Alignment: Establishing balanced response strategies to avoid overconfidence or
501      excessive caution ensures accurate interpretation, even for ambiguous inputs.
502
503   5   R ELATED W ORKS
504
505
      Large Multimodal Models Recent advances in large multimodal models (LMMs) have focused
506   on leveraging large language models (LLMs) as decoder bases to process complex image-text inter-
507   actions. Models like LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024) and Flamingo (Alayrac et al., 2022) utilize transformer
508   architectures to enhance cross-modal understanding, enabling nuanced visual-text comprehension
509   for tasks such as visual question answering and image-based dialogue. Beyond static image-text
510   tasks, recent approaches have aimed to extend multimodal capabilities by incorporating additional
511   modalities like video and audio (Cheng et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024c; Chu et al., 2024; Tang
512   et al., 2023), fostering richer context and enhancing the model’s ability to handle a diverse range of
513   multimodal scenarios.
514
515   Hallucinations in LMMs Hallucination, particularly object hallucination, has been extensively
516   studied in LMMs that process image and text. This phenomenon arises when a model generates
517   content inconsistent with the actual objects present in the input image. Various benchmarks have
518
      been developed to assess hallucination in vision-language tasks (Li et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023a;
      Guan et al., 2024; Nie et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024b; Ye-Bin et al., 2024; YWang et al., 2024), and
519
      several mitigation techniques have been proposed (An et al., 2024; Leng et al., 2024; Huang et al.,
520
      2024; Yu et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2023b). However, research on hallucinations in LMMs beyond
521   image-text tasks is scarce, with limited investigation into hallucinations involving additional modal-
522   ities like audio and video (Wang et al., 2024d; Nishimura et al., 2024). Motivated by this gap, our
523   work introduces the Curse of Multi-modality (CMM) benchmark, the first to systematically evaluate
524   hallucinations across language, visual, and audio inputs. CMM provides a comprehensive evalua-
525   tion framework to explore how LMMs handle complex multimodal integration, offering insights
526   into model vulnerabilities and guiding the development of more reliable multimodal systems.
527

      6   C ONCLUSIONS
529
530
531
      To the best of our knowledge, this paper is the first to systematically investigate and verify the two
      key contributors to hallucinations in large multimodal models (LMMs) across language, visual, and
532
      audio modalities: overreliance on unimodal priors and spurious inter-modality correlations. We
533
      introduce the Curse of Multi-modality (CMM) benchmark, which features nuanced subcategories
534   and granularities along with diagnostic metrics, enabling precise diagnosis of model limitations and
535   guiding targeted improvements. By benchmarking various LMMs across diverse multimodal con-
536   texts, we identified key vulnerabilities in current models, such as unbalanced multimodal integration
537   and biases arising from pretraining datasets. Our analyses provide fundamental insights into multi-
538   modal learning, highlighting the need for improved alignment across multimodal inputs and offering
539   foundational guidance for developing more robust and reliable LMMs. We conclude by outlining
      potential future directions, hoping to inspire subsequent research in this area.

      A.4     B ENCHMARK DATA C ONSTRUCTION D ETAILS
875
876
      The benchmark is designed to evaluate hallucination scenarios across multiple modalities, targeting
877
      specific LMM tendencies such as overreliance on individual modalities and spurious inter-modality
878   correlations. It comprises video, audio, and textual inputs with probing questions aimed at assessing
879   the presence or absence of objects or events in these modalities. Precise annotation is employed to
880   ensure a thorough evaluation of LMM performance in multimodal contexts.
881
882
883   A.4.1    C ONSTRUCTING Q UERIES FOR OVERRELIANCE ON U NIMODAL P RIORS
884
885
      To assess how LMMs may excessively depend on a single modality (visual, audio, or language), we
      construct targeted probing queries that test this overreliance while potentially neglecting comple-
886
      mentary information.
887
888
889   Visual Dominance The Visual Dominance subcategory examines the extent to which LMMs over-
890   rely on visual content, potentially leading to hallucinated sound events that are often associated with
891   visual objects. All probing questions focus on audio events. For queries about existent sound events,
892   the ground truth “yes” is derived from direct human annotation. To identify non-existent sound
893   events, we use the AudioCaps dataset (Kim et al., 2019), which provides short captions describing
894
      the audio track. Objects associated with these audio events are extracted using LLaMA3 (Dubey
      et al., 2024) from the audio caption, while visual objects are identified from video frames using
895
      InternVL2 (Chen et al., 2023). Samples where visual objects do not correspond to any audio content
896
      are filtered and manually verified, with the ground truth set to “no.” All raw video-audio pairs are
897   sourced from AudioCaps.
898
899
900   Audio Dominance The Audio Dominance subcategory explores how LMMs may over-rely on
901   audio cues, leading to hallucinations of visual content. Here, questions probe the presence of visual
902   objects. For existent objects, the ground truth “yes” is annotated manually. To find non-existent
903   objects, we filter samples where the objects indicated by audio cues are not visually present in the
904
      video. These samples undergo manual review to ensure accurate annotation, with the ground truth
      as “no.” All raw video-audio pairs are also sourced from AudioCaps.
905
906
907   Language Dominance The Language Dominance subcategory targets hallucinations caused by
908   the LMMs’ dependence on language priors from pretraining corpora. This category focuses on
909   common-sense events and object attributes. We manually define sets of typical events (e.g., “fish
910   swim in water”) and object characteristics (e.g., “yellow banana”). Videos depicting anti-common-
911   sense scenarios (e.g., “fish fly in the air,” “black banana”) are then collected from YouTube. For
912   queries probing existent content, the ground truth “yes” corresponds to the anti-common-sense ob-
913   ject/event depicted in the video. Conversely, non-existent content queries, which are the common-
914
      sense versions that do not match the video, have the ground truth “no.”
915   Each subcategory includes 200 video-audio or video-only samples, each accompanied by two prob-
916   ing questions: one querying an existent object/event (“yes”), and another probing a non-existent one
917   (“no”). For subcategories containing both object- and event-level probing, the dataset is balanced
      with equal numbers of object- and event-level queries.

      A.4.2    C ONSTRUCTING Q UERIES FOR S PURIOUS I NTER - MODALITY C ORRELATIONS
919
920   This section outlines the construction of queries targeting Spurious Inter-modality Correlations,
921   where hallucinations arise from misleading associations between different modalities learned during
922   pretraining. These correlations are probed at both object- and event-level granularities.
923
924   Visual-Language Spurious Correlations Visual-Language spurious correlations occur when
925   LMMs hallucinate visual objects due to associations learned from patterns in video-caption pre-
926
      training data. Queries in this subcategory are developed based on two factors: global appearance
      frequencies and co-occurrence patterns within the data.
927
928   - Object-level queries are derived from two sources: (i) global appearance frequencies, where the
929   model is asked about frequent objects that are absent in the video (e.g., “Did you see a tree in the
930   video?” when no tree is present), and (ii) co-occurrence patterns, where queries target non-existent
931   objects that are often seen alongside other objects in the pretraining data (e.g., ”Did you see a phone
932
      in the video?” when a human is present but no phone).
933   - Event-level queries similarly explore global appearance frequencies by probing events that fre-
934   quently occur in pretraining data but are not present in the video. For co-occurrence patterns, event-
935   level queries are designed around subject-fixed action-object pairs, such as “Did you see a person
936   using a phone in the video?” when the person is engaged in a different action like walking.
937   Both global frequencies and co-occurrence data are extracted from the large-scale video-caption
938   pretraining dataset WebVid10M. Probing samples are curated accordingly from the same source.
939
940   Audio-Language Spurious Correlations This subcategory assesses correlations learned from
941   audio-caption pretraining, leading to potential hallucinations of audio events based on their ap-
942   pearance or co-occurrence in the training data. Due to the temporal nature of audio, all queries are
943   event-level.
944   - Event-level queries focus on global appearance frequencies, probing for hallucinated audio events
945   that are common in the pretraining data but absent from the audio track (e.g., “Did you hear a dog
946   barking?” when no such sound exists). Co-occurrence queries involve subject-fixed action-object
947   pairs, targeting frequently co-occurring events (e.g., ”Did you hear a dog barking?” when only dog
948   whimpering is present).
949
      The dataset Auto-acd is used for constructing these queries, ensuring a balanced representation of
950   global appearance and co-occurrence-based patterns.
951
952   Visual-Audio-Language Spurious Correlations The Visual-Audio-Language subcategory cap-
953   tures cross-modal hallucinations, where visual objects are hallucinated based on audio cues, and
954   vice versa.
955
      - Event-level queries test for non-existent audio events that are frequently co-occurred with visual
956
      objects in training data (e.g., “Did you hear car revving?” when a human is visible without any car
957
      sound).
958
959   - Object-level queries target visual objects that are hallucinated based on associated sound events
960   (e.g., “Did you see a tree in the video?” when bird chirping is present without any tree visible).
961   The co-occurrence frequencies between visual objects and audio events are computed using the
962   Auto-acd dataset, with the visual and audio content reviewed and annotated by human reviewers.
963   Queries are evenly split between probing audio events and visual objects.
964
      For all subcategories, there is a balance between object-level and event-level queries. Additionally,
965   the samples constructed from global appearance frequencies and co-occurrence patterns are evenly
966   distributed.
