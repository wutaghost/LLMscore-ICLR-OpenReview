009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Meta-learning enables learning systems to adapt quickly to new tasks, similar to
012            humans. To emulate this human-like rapid learning and enhance alignment and
013            discrimination abilities, we propose ConML, a universal meta-learning framework
014            that can be applied to various meta-learning algorithms without relying on specific
015            model architectures nor target models. The core of ConML is task-level contrastive
016            learning, which extends contrastive learning from the representation space in unsu-
017            pervised learning to the model space in meta-learning. By leveraging task identity
018
               as an additional supervision signal during meta-training, we contrast the outputs
               of the meta-learner in the model space, minimizing inner-task distance (between
019
               models trained on different subsets of the same task) and maximizing inter-task
020
               distance (between models from different tasks). We demonstrate that ConML
021            integrates seamlessly with optimization-based, metric-based, and amortization-
022            based meta-learning algorithms, as well as in-context learning, resulting in perfor-
023            mance improvements across diverse few-shot learning tasks. Code is provided at
024            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/conml_ano-3372.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Meta-learning, or "learning to learn" (Schmidhuber, 1987; Thrun & Pratt, 1998), is a powerful
030   paradigm designed to enable learning systems to adapt quickly to new tasks. During the meta-training
031   phase, a meta-learner simulates learning across a variety of relevant tasks to accumulate knowledge
032   on how to adapt effectively. In the meta-testing phase, this learned adaptation strategy is applied
033
      to previously unseen tasks. The adaptation is typically accomplished by the meta-learner, which,
      given a set of task-specific training examples, generates a predictive model tailored to that task.
034
      Meta-learning has been widely applied to important learning problems such as few-shot learning
035
      (Finn et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2020), reinforcement learning (Yu et al., 2020; Nagabandi et al., 2019),
036   and neural architecture search (Elsken et al., 2020; Shaw et al., 2019).
037
038   Despite the success of meta-learning, there remains room for improvement in how models gen-
039
      eralize to new tasks. Human learning leverages two key cognitive abilities—alignment and dis-
      crimination—which are essential for rapid adaptation (Hummel, 2013; Chen, 2012; Christie, 2021).
040
      Alignment refers to integrating different perspectives of an object to form a coherent understanding
041
      (Christian, 2021), while discrimination involves distinguishing between similar stimuli to respond
042   appropriately only to relevant inputs (Robbins, 1970). Incorporating these abilities into meta-learning
043   models could enhance their adaptability and precision.
044
045
      Several existing approaches attempt to leverage alignment and discrimination in meta-learning. Some
      methods assume that task-specific target models for meta-training tasks are available, allowing
046
      meta-training to be supervised by aligning the learned model with the target model, either through
047
      model weight alignment (Wang & Hebert, 2016; Wang et al., 2017) or knowledge distillation (Ye
048   et al., 2022). However, these methods have limited applicability, as learning target models can be
049   computationally expensive and, in many real-world problems, such target models are not readily
050   available. Fei et al. (2021) and Wang et al. (2023) introduce alignment into meta-learners by aligning
051   classifiers in few-shot classification. However, their reliance on a static pool of base classes for
052   meta-training limits their flexibility in more dynamic or diverse tasks. Similarly, Gondal et al. (2021)
053   and Mathieu et al. (2021) explore contrastive representations for neural processes, but their methods
      are tied to specific models, reducing their generalizability.

054
      In this paper, we propose ConML, a universal meta-learning framework that employs task-level
055   contrastive learning to enhance both alignment and discrimination abilities. Our approach is applicable
056   to various meta-learning algorithms without relying on specific model architectures, target models, or
057   extensive modifications. By treating tasks similarly to how contrastive learning handles unlabeled
058   samples, ConML contrasts the outputs of the meta-learner based on task identity. Positive pairs consist
059   of different subsets of the same task, while negative pairs come from different tasks, with the objective
060   of minimizing inner-task distance (alignment) and maximizing inter-task distance (discrimination).
061   ConML is efficient, requiring no additional data or retraining, and is learner-agnostic that can be
062   integrated into diverse representative meta-learning algorithms from different categories including
063
      optimization-based (e.g., MAML (Finn et al., 2017)), metric-based (e.g., ProtoNet (Snell et al.,
      2017)), and amortization-based (e.g., Simple CNAPS (Bateni et al., 2020)). We also demonstrate
064
      how ConML enhances in-context learning (Brown et al., 2020) within the meta-learning paradigm.
065
066   Our contributions can be summarized as below:
067
068   • We propose a universal meta-learning framework, ConML, that emulates cognitive alignment
069     and discrimination abilities in meta-learning, helping to bridge the gap between the fast learning
070     capabilities of humans and meta-learners.
071
072
      • We extend contrastive learning from the representation space in unsupervised learning to the model
        space in meta-learning. By introducing model representations for various types of meta-learners,
073
        ConML integrates efficiently into episodic training.
074
075   • We empirically show that ConML universally improves a wide range of meta-learning algorithms
076     with minimal implementation cost on diverse few-shot learning problems and in-context learning.
077
078   In the following sections, we first review related work and introduce the general learning to learn
079   process in Section 2, setting the foundation for how we incorporate alignment and discrimination
080   into meta-learning. In Section 3, we first present the general framework of learnig with ConML and
081   then provide the specifications of different types of meta-learning approaches. Section 4 presents
082   experimental results, demonstrating the effectiveness of ConML.

      Meta-learning, or "learning to learn," focuses on improving the learning algorithm itself (Schmidhuber,
088   1987). Popular meta-learning approaches can be broadly categorized into three types (Bronskill et al.,
089   2021): (i) Optimization-based approaches (Andrychowicz et al., 2016; Finn et al., 2017; Nichol
090   et al., 2018), which focus on learning better optimization strategies for adapting to new tasks; (ii)
091   Metric-based approaches (Vinyals et al., 2016; Snell et al., 2017; Sung et al., 2018), which leverage
092   learned similarity metrics; and (iii) Amortization-based approaches (Garnelo et al., 2018; Requeima
093   et al., 2019; Bateni et al., 2020), which aim to learn a shared representation across tasks, amortizing
094   the adaptation process by using neural networks to directly infer task-specific parameters from the
095   training set.
096   All of these meta-learning approaches utilize episodic training, where the meta-learner is trained
097   across multiple tasks (episodes). Recently, several works have focused on optimizing task sampling
098   strategies by adjusting the task schedule based on task difficulty and diversity (Agarwal & Singh,
099   2023; Han et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2020; Kumar et al., 2023). In contrast, ConML
100   does not alter the task sampling process. Instead, it incorporates task-level contrastive learning within
101   the episodic training framework to enhance the meta-learner’s alignment and discrimination abilities.
102
      Moreover, ConML can be used alongside task sampling strategies, further increasing its versatility.

150   3.1   A G ENERAL F RAMEWORK
151
152   To enhance the alignment and discrimination abilities of meta-learning, we draw inspiration from
153
      Contrastive Learning (CL) (Oord et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2020; Wang & Isola, 2020). CL focuses on
      learning representations that are invariant to irrelevant details while preserving essential information.
154
      This is achieved by maximizing alignment and discrimination (uniformity) in the representation space
155
      (Wang & Isola, 2020). While most existing studies focus on sample-wise contrastive learning in the
156   representation space via unsupervised learning (Wu et al., 2018; Hjelm et al., 2018; Bachman et al.,
157   2019; Tian et al., 2020; He et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2020; Khosla et al., 2020), we extend CL to the
158   model space in meta-learning. Specifically, we design a taks-level CL in the model space, where
159   alignment is achieved by minimizing the inner-task distance (i.e., the distance between models trained
160   on different subsets of the same task), and discrimination is achieved by maximizing the inter-task
161   distance (i.e., the distance between models from different tasks). The detailed procedures of ConML
      are introduced below.

164   the model h = g(D; θ) ∈ H as a fixed-length vector e ∈ Rd , and then compute the distances using
165   an explicit distance function ϕ(·, ·) (e.g., cosine distance). Note that H is learner-specific, so to
166   generalize across different learners to form a learner-agnostic framework, we introduce a projection
167   function ψ : H → Rd to obtain the model representations e = ψ(h), and then the proceedings are

      5   C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
526
527
      In this work, we propose ConML, a universal, learner-agnostic contrastive meta-learning framework
528
      that emulates the alignment and discrimination capabilities integral to human fast learning, achieved
529   through task-level contrastive learning in the model space. ConML can be seamlessly integrated
530   with conventional episodic meta-training, and we provide specific implementations across a wide
531   range of meta-learning algorithms. Empirical results show that ConML consistently and significantly
532   enhances meta-learning performance by improving the meta-learner’s fast-adaptation and task-level
533   generalization abilities. Additionally, we explore in-context learning by reformulating it within
534   the meta-learning paradigm, demonstrating how ConML can be effectively integrated to boost
535   performance. The primary contribution of ConML is offering a learner-agnostic and efficient
536   framework built on the most general meta-learning setting and training procedure. While the current
537
      implementation of ConML is relatively simple, it lays a foundation for general contrastive meta-
      learning and offers numerous opportunities for further improvement, such as optimizing sampling
538
      strategies or refining the contrastive loss function.

      B.1    I N -C ONTEXT L EARNING
921
922   In-context learning (ICL) is first proposed for large language models (Brown et al., 2020), where
923   examples in a task are integrated into the prompt (input-output pairs) and given a new query input,
924   the language model can generate the corresponding output. This approach allows pre-trained model
925   to address new tasks without fine-tuning the model. For example, given "happy->positive; sad-
926   >negative; blue->", the model can output "negative", while given "green->cool; yellow->warm;
927   blue->" the model can output "cool". ICL has the ability to learn from the prompt. Training ICL
928
      can be viewed as learning to learn, i.e., meta-learning (Min et al., 2022; Garg et al., 2022; Kirsch
      et al., 2022). More generally, the input and output are not necessarily to be natural language. In
929
      ICL, a sequence model Tθ (typically transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017)) is trained to map sequence
930
      [x1 , y1 , x2 , y2 , · · · , xm−1 , ym−1 , xm ] (prompt prefix) to prediction ym . Given distribution P of
931   training prompt t, then training ICL follows an auto-regressive manner:
