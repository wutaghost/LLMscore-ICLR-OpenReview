009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Class incremental learning (CIL) aims to learn a model that can not only incre-
012
               mentally accommodate new classes, but also maintain the learned knowledge of
013
               old classes. Out-of-distribution (OOD) detection in CIL is to retain this incremen-
014            tal learning ability, while being able to reject unknown samples that are drawn
015            from different distributions of the learned classes. This capability is crucial to
016            the safety of deploying CIL models in open worlds. However, despite remarkable
017            advancements in the respective CIL and OOD detection, there lacks a systematic
018            and large-scale benchmark to assess the capability of advanced CIL models in
019            detecting OOD samples. To fill this gap, in this study we design a comprehen-
020            sive empirical study to establish such a benchmark, named OpenCIL, offering
021            a unified protocol for enabling CIL models with different OOD detectors using
022
               two principled OOD detection frameworks. One key observation we find through
               our comprehensive evaluation is that the CIL models can be severely biased to-
023
               wards the OOD samples and newly added classes when they are exposed to open
024
               environments. Motivated by this, we further propose a novel approach for OOD
025            detection in CIL, namely Bi-directional Energy Regularization (BER), which is
026            specially designed to mitigate these two biases in different CIL models by having
027            energy regularization on both old and new classes. Extensive experiments show
028            that BER can substantially improve the OOD detection capability across a range of
029            CIL models, achieving state-of-the-art performance on the OpenCIL benchmark.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Training of deep neural networks (DNNs) heavily relies on large-scale data on a fixed set of classes
036   (Russakovsky et al., 2015; Krizhevsky et al., 2017), but data in real-world applications is constantly
037   changing, leading to continuous new classes in training data. Continual learning (CL) enables DNNs
038   to continuously learn a sequence of tasks, with each task consisting of a set of unique classes. The
039
      samples for the learned/old tasks are assumed to be not accessible in such dynamic environments.
      Class incremental learning (CIL) is one type of CL where task identifiers are not known at testing
040
      time. Compared to another type of CL, task-incremental learning, CIL is often considered a more
041
      practical setting in real applications (Rebuffi et al., 2017; Li & Hoiem, 2017). Thus, we focus on
042   CIL in this study.
043
044   Many CIL methods have been introduced over the years to overcome Catastrophic Forgetting (CF)
045
      of the knowledge learned on old classes, i.e., degraded classification accuracy on old classes due
      to model updating on new classes (Xiao et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2022a; Niu et al., 2024). They
046
      have shown remarkable performance on the in-distribution (ID) classes in incremental tasks, but lack
047
      the capability to recognize and reject out-of-distribution (OOD) samples that are drawn from non-
048   training datasets during incremental learning (Huang & Li, 2021; Wang et al., 2020) (i.e., no class
049   overlapping between ID and OOD samples). Such a capability is crucial to the safety of deploying
050   CIL models in open environments in real-world application systems such as autonomous systems
051   (Kendall & Gal, 2017; Leibig et al., 2017). For example, in Unmanned Aerial Vehicles (UAVs), the
052   DNNs are initially trained using a limited set of available trajectories and then continuously updated
053   based on newly available trajectories. Meanwhile, these DNNs need to be capable of recognizing
      OOD data to handle unexpected situations in every ongoing trajectory.

074   Numerous OOD detection methods have been proposed (Sun et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023b;
075   Li et al., 2024b; Miao et al., 2024), but these OOD methods are focused on static environments,
076   where the samples of all tasks are accessible during training, making them ineffective in dynamic
077   CIL environments. Therefore, it is non-trivial to combine the off-the-shelf CIL and OOD models.
078   To justify this difficulty, a plausible evaluation protocol is needed to assess the OOD detection
079   capability of different CIL models with the support of different types of OOD detectors. There are
080   such protocols on the respective CIL and OOD detection areas, e.g., OpenOOD (Yang et al., 2022;
081
      Zhang et al., 2023) for OOD detection and FACIL (Masana et al., 2022) for CIL, but no work has
      been done on a systematic and large-scale benchmarking study to evaluate the synergy of existing
082
      state-of-the-art (SOTA) CIL models and OOD detection methods.
083
084   To bridge this gap, we design a performance benchmark for OOD detection in CIL, called Open-
085   CIL, offering a unified protocol for different CIL models with diverse OOD detectors. To achieve
086   this, OpenCIL introduces two principled frameworks for incorporating diverse OOD detection meth-
087
      ods into CIL models and also introduces a new evaluation pipeline that enables fair comparison of
      not only the OOD detection capability for different CIL models but also the ability of different OOD
088
      detectors in the presence of CF. In particular, OpenCIL accommodates four representative CIL mod-
089
      els with 15 diverse OOD detection methods, resulting in 60 baseline models on two popular CIL
090   datasets and six commonly-used near/far OOD datasets. Based on the large-scale experiments on
091   OpenCIL, we provide a number of important observations, offering crucial insights into the design
092   of CIL models for open-world applications.
093
      One key observation we find is that compared to OOD detection in static environments, the dynamic
094
      environments in the CIL setting can lead to increasing biases towards OOD samples and newly
095
      added classes with the growth of incremental learning steps. The underlying reasons are two-fold.
096   One main reason is that due to the CF problem, CIL models often have lower prediction confidence
097   for samples of old ID classes (i.e., classes seen in the old tasks), compared to new ID classes, as
098   illustrated in Fig. 1a. This leads to a difficulty in distinguishing between old ID class samples and
099   OOD samples. Furthermore, as illustrated in Fig. 1b, DNN-based CIL models typically exhibit an
100   over-confident prediction on OOD samples, causing the misclassification of the OOD samples into
101   not only the old ID classes but also the new ID classes. Increasing the incremental learning steps
102   results in a larger classification semantic space and more severe CF, which continually amplifies the
103   biases towards the OOD samples and newly added classes. Motivated by these issues, we propose
104
      a new approach for OOD detection in CIL, namely Bi-directional Energy Regularization (BER),
      which jointly optimizes two energy regularization terms to modulate the energy prediction of OOD
105
      samples w.r.t. samples of old and new class samples, respectively. This effectively reduces these
106
      two biases, improving the OOD detection capability across a range of CIL models.
107
      In summary, our main contributions are as follows:

119   2   R ELATED W ORK
120
121   Out-of-distribution (OOD) Detection. The objective of this task is to determine whether a
122   given input sample belongs to the learned classes (in-distribution) or unknown classes (out-of-
123
      distribution). In recent years, OOD detection has been extensively developed, including Post-hoc-
      based methods (Sun et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023b; Zhang & Xiang, 2023) and fine-tuning-based
124
      methods (Liu et al., 2020; Wei et al., 2022; Tian et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Liu
125
      et al., 2023b; Miao et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024b;a). The post-hoc methods focus on devising new
126   OOD scoring functions in the inference stage. The fine-tuning-based methods focus on separating
127   OOD samples from ID samples by training a strong classifier as OOD detector. However, all these
128   methods are applied to non-CIL models. There lacks of exploration of their capability on CIL mod-
129   els, resulting in poor performance when there is catastrophic forgetting. Our BER is a fine-tuning
130   method that can be applied to different CIL models to improve their OOD detection performance.
131
132   Class Incremental Learning (CIL). CIL performs the learning procedure in an incremental man-
133   ner with growing data samples. It focuses on alleviating the catastrophic forgetting problem, in
134   which the CIL models are required to remember the knowledge of the learned classes from old tasks
135   while learning the discriminative information for the newly coming classes. There are three main
136
      lines of work in this area (Luo et al., 2023; Xiao et al., 2023). Regularization-based methods focus
      on applying discrepancy (between old and new models) as penalization terms in their objective func-
137
      tions (Liu et al., 2021; Rebuffi et al., 2017; Xiao et al., 2023). Parameter-isolation-based methods
138
      aim to increase the model parameters in each new incremental step to prevent knowledge forgetting
139   caused by parameter overwritten when learning new tasks (Xu & Zhu, 2018; Yan et al., 2021; Wang
140   et al., 2022a). Replay-based algorithms assume there is a memory budget allowing a handful of
141   old class examples in the memory. These memory examples can be used to re-train/fine-tune the
142   CIL model in each new incremental step (Rebuffi et al., 2017; Wu et al., 2019; Luo et al., 2023;
143   Niu et al., 2024). However, all these methods focus on tackling the CIL problem in a closed world,
144   failing to take into account distinguishing ID data from unknown samples (e.g., OOD data), Our
145   BER baselines can be applied to different pre-trained CIL models, in which all new class data and
146   old class memory are used for fine-tuning a new OOD detector for the CIL models. It effectively
147
      equips the CIL models with significantly improved capability for OOD detection.

149   3   E QUIPPING CIL MODELS WITH OOD D ETECTION C APABILITY
150
151   This section investigates the effectiveness of two principled OOD detection frameworks in enabling
152   CIL models to reject unknown samples.
153
154   Problem Statement. Our goal is to equip CIL models with the capability of rejecting OOD sam-
155   ples. In this setting, a model is required to learn a sequence of tasks during training. At testing time,
156   the model is used to classify samples into old/new classes while also rejecting unknown samples
157   at each incremental step. The model is evaluated based on its effectiveness in preventing the CF
158
      problem and distinguishing samples of old/new classes from OOD data.

200   3.1       O PEN CIL B ENCHMARK : E NABLING CIL M ODELS WITH E XISTING OOD D ETECTORS
201
202   It is challenging for CIL models to recognize OOD samples and to conduct a fair comparison of
203   OOD detection capabilities, given the diverse types of OOD detectors, the significant differences of
204   two settings, and the various incremental steps involved. Therefore, we introduce OpenCIL, the first
205
      large-scale and systematic benchmark designed to enable CIL models with existing OOD detectors.
      There are two types of OOD detection methods: post-hoc-based and fine-tuning-based methods.
206
      Post-hoc methods calculate OOD scores based on features/logits derived from the pre-trained model,
207
      which can be used for different pre-trained classification models. Fine-tuning methods require the
208   fine-tuning of part or all layers of the pre-trained models, and then calculating the OOD score based
209   on the fine-tuned models. Below we introduce two principled frameworks that OpenCIL uses to
210   incorporate these two types of OOD methods into CIL models, as also illustrated in Fig. 2 Left.
211
212   CIL models with post-hoc-based OOD detection methods. A SOTA CIL algorithm is first ap-
213   plied to learn the c-tasks stream of ID data in an incremental manner, resulting in the standard CIL
214   model θ(·) = {θ1 (·), θ2 (·), ..., θc (·)}. Subsequently, we directly perform the post-hoc OOD scoring
215   function on the features/logits extracted from the well-trained CIL model θt (·), t ∈ {1, 2, ..., c} at
      each incremental step to calculate the OOD score, without having any effect on the CIL models.


                                                                                                      4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      CIL models with fine-tuning-based OOD detection methods. Similar to the previous frame-
217   work, fine-tuning-based OOD methods also apply a SOTA CIL algorithm to obtain the standard CIL
218   model θ(·) = {θ1 (·), θ2 (·), ..., θc (·)} in the first place. As shown in Fig. 2, for each incremental
219   step, the well-trained CIL model θt (·), t ∈ {1, 2, ..., c} contains a feature extractor ϕt (·) and a clas-
220   sifier ht (·), i.e., θt (·) is a composition of ϕt (·) and ht (·). Then we freeze both ϕt (·) and ht (·), and
221   fine-tune an additional classifier ft (·) only on top of ϕt (·) to avoid intensifying the catastrophic for-
222   getting problem. This means that the fine-tuned CIL model contains the same feature extractor ϕt (·)
223   and classifier ht (·) as the standard pre-trained CIL model for incremental ID classification, but it
224   also has the additional fine-tuned classifier ft (·) for OOD detection at each incremental step t, with-
225
      out affecting any of the learning procedures of the CIL model θt (·) (i.e., ϕt (·) and ht (·)). Notably,
      the fine-tuning of ft (·) is applied to each incremental step only with training ID data Tttrain .
226
227   With these two principled frameworks, different OOD detection methods can be easily incorporated
228   into three different types of CIL models without impairing the incremental learning accuracy at all.
229   The overall algorithm of these two principled frameworks is provided in Appendix F.

      CIL Datasets. Following (Wang et al., 2022a; Wu et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2023a; Luo et al.,
233   2023), we use two popular CIL datasets as the ID data in our benchmark: CIFAR100 (Krizhevsky
234   et al., 2009) and large-scale ImageNet1k (Russakovsky et al., 2015). Besides, these two datasets are
235   also widely used as ID datasets in the area of OOD detection. Following (Rebuffi et al., 2017; Wang
236   et al., 2022a; Wu et al., 2019), the splits of the two ID datasets are as follows. 1) For CIFAR100,
237   we train the CIL model gradually with k classes per incremental step with a fixed memory size of
238   2,000 exemplars. We respectively evaluate the performance with the step size k ∈ {5, 10, 20}. 2)
239   For ImageNet1K, since it is a much larger dataset, we train the CIL model gradually with a larger
240   number of k classes per step that k ∈ {50, 100, 200} with a fixed memory size of 20,000 exemplars.
241
242
      OOD Datasets. Following the recent large-scale solely OOD detection benchmark OpenOOD
      (Yang et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023), we select six datasets as OOD data for each ID dataset
243
      respectively. 1) For the ID dataset CIFAR100, the OOD data includes two near OOD datasets
244
      – CIFAR10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009) and Tiny-ImageNet (TIN) (Le & Yang, 2015) – and four
245   far OOD datasets: MNIST (LeCun et al., 2010), Texture (Cimpoi et al., 2014), SVHN (Netzer
246   et al., 2011) and Places365 (Zhou et al., 2017). 2) For ImageNet1k, the OOD data includes four
247   near OOD datasets – ImageNet O (Hendrycks et al., 2021), iNaturalist (Van Horn et al., 2018),
248   OpenImage O (Wang et al., 2022b) and Species (Basart et al., 2022) – and two far OOD datasets:
249   MNIST (LeCun et al., 2010) and Texture (Cimpoi et al., 2014).

310   3.2     M AIN F INDINGS
311
312   We summarize our main findings and justification based on our OpenCIL benchmarking as follows.
313   OOD detectors suffer from catastrophic forgetting as well when applied to CIL models. This is
314   illustrated in Fig. 3a, where the performance of different OOD detection methods decreases signif-
315   icantly with increasing incremental steps. This is because, with more incremental steps, more old
316   class samples are wrongly detected as OOD samples, while at the same time, more OOD samples
317   are misclassified into new classes, leading to fast downgraded OOD detection performance.
318   However, catastrophic forgetting is more persistent in CIL models than OOD detection methods. As
319   shown in Fig. 3b, the AUC performance for OOD detection drops fast in early incremental steps, and
320   then it slows down and levers off, but the ACC performance for CIL has a continuous, fast decrease
321   throughout all incremental steps. This is because remembering the forgotten ID samples is more
322   difficult than distinguishing them from OOD data. Also, after the CIL models reach a certain level
323   of forgetting for some ID classes, the performance of distinguishing them from OOD data tends to be
      stable, resulting in relatively stable OOD detection performance at the end of incremental learning.


                                                       6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      Consequently, CIL models are prone to misclassifying OOD samples into new classes. This is also
325   shown in Fig. 1b, where the CIL models often yield significantly higher prediction confidence on
326   misclassifying the OOD samples into the new classes than the old classes, due to the presence of
327   large samples from these new classes at each incremental step. The reason is that due to more severe
328   catastrophic forgetting in CIL, the CIL models predict the ID samples as the new classes with higher
329   confidence than that for the old classes and the OOD samples.
330
      In addition to catastrophic forgetting, CIL models need to handle new issues in the presence of OOD
331   samples, since they exhibit stronger prediction confidence on OOD samples than Old class samples.
332   As shown in Figs. 1a and 1b, the ID samples from old classes/tasks often have lower prediction
333   confidence than different OOD samples, thereby being misclassified as the OOD samples. This
334   phenomenon becomes more severe with an increasing number of incremental steps. This is because
335   the CIL models tend to be less confident when predicting the samples of old classes due to the CF
336   problem, making their OOD scores lower than samples from the OOD data.
337   On the other hand, CIL models with fine-tuning-based OOD methods show to be more advantageous
338   than those with post-hoc-based methods. This can be observed by looking at the average AUC
339   and FPR results of each CIL model for the nine post-hoc-based and the five previous fine-tuning
340   methods in Tables 1, 2, and 3. The observation holds for both ID datasets. This demonstrates the
341   advantage of tuning an additional classifier for OOD detection, but note that it is at the expense
342   of some computational overhead. Besides, CIL models with higher CIL accuracy often gain better
343   OOD detection performance. This can be observed in Tables 1, 2, and 3, where, when averaged
344   over all the existing OOD methods used, the CIL models with higher CIL accuracy, e.g., WA and
345
      FOSTER, often achieve better AUC and FPR performance than the other two CIL models, especially
      on CIFAR100. This observation is consistent with the performance at each incremental step, as
346
      shown in Fig. 3c. This is because better CIL algorithms can keep more essential information about
347
      ID data to improve ID classification, which can also prevent OOD data from being misclassified into
348   ID classes.

351   4     O UR P ROPOSED A PPROACH BER
352
353   As summarized above, one key issue for the CIL models in the presence of OOD samples is the in-
354   creasing biases of the CIL models towards OOD samples and newly added classes with the growth
355   of incremental steps due to more severe catastrophic forgetting. Further, fine-tuning-based OOD
356   methods are generally more effective than the post-hoc methods. Therefore, we introduce the novel
357
      approach, Bi-directional Energy Regularization (BER), a fine-tuning-based OOD detection ap-
      proach, to tackle this bias issue, i.e., avoiding the classification of the old and new class samples as
358
      OOD samples. BER consists of two components, namely New Task Energy Regularization (NTER)
359
      and Old Task Energy Regularization (OTER). NTER is designed to distinguish OOD data from sam-
360   ples of new task classes, while OTER is designed to distinguish OOD data from samples of old task
361   classes. Below we introduce each component in detail.
362
363
      4.1   N EW TASK E NERGY R EGULARIZATION (NTER)
364
365   Due to the overwhelming presence of new class samples, CIL models typically demonstrate a
366   strongly biased prediction on the OOD samples towards the new classes, i.e., they have high predic-
367   tion confidence on classifying OOD samples into the classes in the new task, as illustrated in Fig.
368   1b. To address this issue, NTER synthesizes the pseudo OOD samples that are distributed on the de-
369   cision boundary of different new classes, and further utilizes them to enlarge the decision boundary
370   margin, as illustrated in Figure 2 Right. Specifically, NTER first randomly mixes up samples of dif-

      4.2   O LD TASK E NERGY R EGULARIZATION (OTER)
388
389   Due to the severe catastrophic forgetting problem, as illustrated in Fig. 1a, the CIL models exhibit
390   significantly lower prediction confidence on samples of old classes than new classes. As a result,
391   the CIL models often misclassify the old class samples as the OOD samples. To address this issue,
392   OTER performs a different mixup operation from NTER to generate more samples of old classes.
393   In particular, it randomly mixes up new class samples with old class samples to synthesize old
394
      class samples. This mixup operation not only increases the diversity of the small-sized old class

396

518

519
      the two components are combined. Since NTER is designed to alleviate the misclassification of
520   OOD samples into new classes while OTER is designed to reduce the misclassification of old class
521   samples as OOD samples, their combination results in a detection model that largely reduces both

      5     C ONCLUSION
526
      In this paper, we introduce OpenCIL, the first large-scale and systematic benchmark designed to
527
      enable CIL models with existing OOD detectors, regarding the CIL models in open world applica-
528
      tions. OpenCIL introduces two principled frameworks for incorporating diverse OOD detectors into
529   CIL models and a new evaluation pipeline for fairly evaluating the capability of OOD detectors in
530   incremental learning. In particular, OpenCIL accommodates four representative CIL models with
531   15 diverse OOD detection methods, resulting in 60 baseline models on two popular CIL datasets and
532   six commonly-used near/far OOD datasets. Based on our large-scale experiments on OpenCIL, we
533   offer several important insights into the design of CIL models for open-world applications. We fur-
534   ther propose a novel approach, namely Bi-directional Energy Regularization (BER), which utilizes
535   energy regularization based on two types of sample synthesis to effectively mitigate the increasing
536   bias of CIL Models towards OOD samples and newly added classes with the growth of incremen-
537
      tal steps. Extensive experiments demonstrate that BER achieves state-of-the-art performance on
      the OpenCIL benchmark under varying incremental step sizes on popular CIL and OOD datasets,
538
      improving the OOD detection capability of a wide range of CIL models.

      A.1     DATASETS
759
760   The in-distribution (ID) datasets for class incremental learning (CIL) include two datasets. 1) CI-
761   FAR100 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009) contains 50, 000 training images and 10, 000 test images of size
762   32 × 32 with 100 classes, and 2) ImageNet (Russakovsky et al., 2015) is a large-scale classification
763   dataset, which contains 1.28M training images and 50K testing images with 1, 000 classes sampled
764   from nature images.
765   We select six commonly used out-of-distribution (OOD) datasets for OOD detection on CIFRA100.
766   Following the recent large-scale OOD detection benchmark OpenOOD (Yang et al., 2022), our
767   OOD datasets include two near OOD datasets: CIFAR10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009) and Tiny-
768   ImageNet (TIN) (Le & Yang, 2015), and four far OOD datasets: MNIST (LeCun et al., 2010),
769   Texture (Cimpoi et al., 2014), Places365 (Zhou et al., 2017), and SVHN (Netzer et al., 2011).
770   CIFAR10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009) contains 10, 000 images with 10 classes. TIN (Le & Yang,
771   2015) contains 7, 498 images, which removes the 2, 502 images that have overlapping semantics
772
      (Yang et al., 2021) with CIFAR100 classes. MNIST (LeCun et al., 2010) contains 10, 000 images
      with 10 classes. Texture (Cimpoi et al., 2014) contains 5, 640 images with 47 classes. Places365
773
      (Zhou et al., 2017) contains 33, 773 images, which removes the 2, 727 images that have overlapping
774
      semantics (Yang et al., 2021) with CIFAR100 classes. SVHN (Netzer et al., 2011) contains 26, 032
775   images with 10 class.
776
777   We also select six commonly used OOD datasets for OOD detection on ImageNet1k. Following
778
      OpenOOD (Yang et al., 2022), these includes four near OOD datasets: ImageNet O (Hendrycks
      et al., 2021), iNaturalist (Van Horn et al., 2018), OpenImage O (Wang et al., 2022b) and Species
779
      (Basart et al., 2022), and two far OOD datasets: MNIST (LeCun et al., 2010) and Texture (Cimpoi
780
      et al., 2014). ImageNet O (Hendrycks et al., 2021) contains 2, 000 images from categories not
781   found in the ImageNet1k dataset. We use a 10, 000 image subset of iNaturalist (Van Horn et al.,
782   2018), which is based on 110 manually selected plant classes not present in ImageNet1k. The OOD
783   samples are randomly sampled images from these 110 classes (Huang & Li, 2021). All images are
784   resized to have a max dimension of 800 pixels. OpenImage O (Wang et al., 2022b) contains 15, 869
785   images with the support of a manual filter. We use a 10, 000 subset of 713K images Species (Basart
786   et al., 2022) with 10 classes. Two far OOD datasets are the same as CIFAR100. Near OOD datasets
787   mean that their OOD samples have small semantic shifts compared with the ID samples, while
788   far OOD datasets mean that their OOD samples are very different from the ID samples, typically
789
      containing obvious covariate (domain) shift (Yang et al., 2022). A summary of the CIL ID and

      A.2     P ERFORMANCE M ETRICS
805
806   This section provides more introduction to the performance measures used in our experiments. (1)
807   FPR is the false positive rate of OOD examples when the true positive rate of ID examples is at
808   95%. It measures the portion of falsely recognized OOD when most of the ID samples are recalled.
809   (2) AUC computes the area under the receiver operating characteristic curve of detecting OOD
      samples, evaluating the OOD detection performance. (3) AP measures the area under the precision-

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      recall curve, in which the OOD samples are treated as positive samples. (4) ACC calculates the
811   classification accuracy of the ID data for the CIL models. Among all these metrics, only FPR95 is
812   expected to have a lower value for a better model. Higher values indicate better performance for the
813   other three metrics.

      B    BASELINE L IBRARY.
817
      We include four different popular CIL models. iCaRL (Rebuffi et al., 2017) and WA (Zhao et al.,
818
      2020) are the regularization-based algorithms, BiC (Wu et al., 2019) is a replay-based algorithm, and
819
      FOSTER (Wang et al., 2022a) is a parameter-isolation-based method but it also uses data replay.
820
821   For OOD methods, there are two main categories, post-hoc-based and fine-tuning-based methods.
822   For post-hoc-based methods, we include nine OOD detection methods: MSP (Hendrycks & Gim-
823
      pel, 2017), ODIN (Liang et al., 2018), Energy (Liu et al., 2020), MaxLogit (Basart et al., 2022),
      and GEN (Liu et al., 2023a) use the statistic based on prediction output of each test samples without
824
      using any training ID information, while the other four methods: ReAct (Sun et al., 2021), KLM
825
      (Basart et al., 2022), Relation (Kim et al., 2023), and NNGuide (Park et al., 2023) need the posterior
826   information of training ID samples to obtain the OOD score. Therefore, if they need a whole label
827   space of training ID data, we feed Tttrain to them, which is the combination of memory data and cur-
828   rent task training data. For fine-tuning-based methods, we include five methods: LogitNorm (Wei
829   et al., 2022) and T2FNorm (Regmi et al., 2023) are the regularization-based methods that apply the
830   normalization to calibrate the loss function, AUGMIX (Hendrycks et al., 2020) and REGMIX (Pinto
831   et al., 2023) are the augmentation-based methods that apply the data augmentation to ID samples
832   for enhancing the ID data training, while VOS (Du et al., 2022) are synthesis-based methods that
833   generate pseudo-OOD samples to assist the detector training. Notably, VOS focuses on outlier syn-
834
      thesis, but applying their original training method to fine-tune the extra classifier is difficult since it
      was originally designed without the final linear classifier. Thus, to have a fair comparison, we apply
835
      the same energy regularization as our BER for them, which replaces our synthesized pseudo-OOD
836
      samples x̄t with their synthesized outlier samples.

842   original papers. For OOD fine-tuning, following (Yang et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023), we use
843
      the common setting with SGD optimizer, using a learning rate of 0.1, a momentum of 0.9, a weight
      decay of 0.0005, and adjusting the learning rate using a cosine annealing learning rate schedule.

      The batch size is fixed at 128 for all experiments. We freeze the feature extractor and original
845
      classifier, fine-tune the extra classifier for 10 epochs, and keep other hyperparameters the same as
