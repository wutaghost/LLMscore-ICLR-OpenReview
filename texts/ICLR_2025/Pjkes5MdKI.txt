013
014
                 Program synthesis methods, whether formal or neural-based, lack fine-grained
015
                 control and flexible modularity, which limits their adaptation to complex soft-
016
                 ware development. These limitations stem from rigid Domain-Specific Language
017              (DSL) frameworks and neural network incorrect predictions. To this end, we pro-
018              pose the Chain of Logic (CoL), which organizes synthesis stages into a chain and
019              provides precise heuristic control to guide the synthesis process. Furthermore,
020              by integrating neural networks with libraries and introducing a Neural Network
021              Feedback Control (NNFC) mechanism, our approach modularizes synthesis and
022              mitigates the impact of neural network mispredictions. Experiments on relational
023              and symbolic synthesis tasks show that CoL significantly enhances the efficiency
024              and reliability of DSL program synthesis across multiple metrics. Specifically,
025
                 CoL improves accuracy by 70% while reducing tree operations by 91% and time
                 by 95%. Additionally, NNFC further boosts accuracy by 6%, with a 64% reduc-
026
                 tion in tree operations under challenging conditions such as insufficient training
027
                 data, increased difficulty, and multidomain synthesis. These improvements con-
028              firm COOL as a highly efficient and reliable program synthesis framework.

      In this paper, following the principles of fine-grained control and flexible modularity, we present
      COOL (Chain-Oriented Objective Logic), a neural-symbolic framework for complex program
094
      synthesis. At the core of our approach, we introduce the Chain-of-Logic (CoL), which integrates
095
      the functions of the activity diagram to enable fine-grained control Gomaa (2011). As illustrated in
096   Figure 1, programmers can precisely organize rules into multiple stages and manage control flow

100   generated during synthesis and suppresses neural network incorrect predictions through filtering. To
101   ensure modularity, each neural network is bound with a specific CoL DSL, stored in separate library
102   files for clear isolation and easy reuse. Thus, through the combination of CoL and NNFC, COOL
103   achieves high efficiency and reliability when tackling complex synthesis tasks.

192   2.1       C HAIN - OF -L OGIC (C O L)
193
194   Activity diagrams, widely used in software engineering, effectively describe how an initial state
195   transitions to a final state through multiple activities. This feature aligns with the DSL-based pro-
196   gram synthesis process. A DSL, defined as a context-free grammar, converts partial programs with
197   nonterminal symbols into complete programs by applying given rules. However, as the rule set
198
      grows, DSL becomes inefficient in exploring partial programs. To enhance the efficiency of DSL,
      the Chain-of-Logic, drawing inspiration from activity diagrams, organizes the synthesis process into
199
      a sequence of manageable activities.
200
201   CoL improves the synthesis workflow of the DSL with two key features: heuristic vectors and
202   keywords. As shown in Figure 3, heuristic vectors decompose DSL into multiple sub-DSLs that are
203   consistent with the activity flow by dividing the rule applications scope. Within each activity, the
204
      DSL solver uses heuristic values to perform efficient program synthesis. For example, in Figure 1,
      a rule with the heuristic vector (0,7,3) belongs to sub-DSLs in activities 2 and 3 with heuristic
205
      values of 7 and 3, respectively. By dynamically pruning the search space and providing search
206
      guidance, heuristic vectors promote program synthesis efficiency.
207
208   Second, as illustrated in Figure 4, CoL introduces three keywords—return, logicjump(n),
209   and abort—to dynamically manage state transition within or between activities during synthesis:
210
211
                1. return: Ends the current rule, staying within current activity or advancing to following
                   activities.
212
213             2. logicjump(n): Jumps directly to the activity n, enabling branching and loops within activ-
214                ity flow.
215
                3. abort: Terminates the current synthesis branch, pruning the search space.

      2.2   N EURAL N ETWORK F EEDBACK C ONTROL (NNFC)
249
250
      While CoL enables programmers to fine-tune the synthesis process, the control flow may lack detail
251
      or vary by task. To this end, Neural Network Feedback Control (NNFC) dynamically refines con-
252   trol flow through feedback from neural networks, improving precision and adaptability. However,
253   neural networks present the risk of generating incorrect predictions, threatening reliability.
254
255
      Therefore, a robust control flow in NNFC is crucial to ensuring overall performance. As illustrated
      in Figure 5, NNFC enhances the CoL DSL in the following ways: In the forward flow, the Clipper
256
      prioritizes control signals aligned with DSNN guidance by capping any inconsistent signals, while
257
      the CoL DSL applies rules based on the adjusted heuristic values. Meanwhile, in the feedback loop,
258   the DSNN generates error signals from partial programs across domains. To suppress the impact of
259   mispredictions, the Filter refines these signals before they influence the forward flow.
260
261
      The quality of the signals generated in the feedback loop directly determines the effectiveness of
      NNFC. If the error signals are of poor quality, NNFC may not only fail to provide additional im-
262
      provements but also degrade CoL DSL performance. We ensure the error signal quality through an
263
      inner coupling structure within DSNN. As shown in Figure 5 (right), during synthesis tasks, DSNN
264   processes partial programs using a series of sequentially connected neural networks. Each neural
265   network takes both the partial programs and intermediate results from the preceding neural network
266   as input, generating its own predictions. When errors occur in earlier networks, they propagate
267   downstream as noise signals, amplifying at each stage. The difference in the outputs between these
268   neural networks is positively correlated with the accumulated error. To mitigate this, we set a thresh-
269   old to filter out signals with a significant difference in outputs. Finally, DSNN uses passed signals
      to generate multi-head outputs to fine-tune the forward flow:


                                                                  5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
            1. Task Detection Head (TDH): Improves modularity by determining whether the partial
271            program contains components that the CoL DSL can process.
272
            2. Search Space Prune Head (SSPH): (Active when TDH is true) Evaluate the feasibility of
273
               synthesizing the final complete program from the current partial program, and CoL DSL
274
               will avoid exploring infeasible spaces.
275
            3. Search Guidance Head (SGH): (Active when both TDH and SSPH are true) Guides the
276
               CoL DSL in applying the most promising rules to the partial program.
277
278   By adopting filtering and multi-head outputs, the feedback loop delivers high-quality error signals
279   to the forward path, ensuring that NNFC enhances the synthesis process on top of CoL.

304   Overhead is measured by the number of neural network invocations. (4) Time overhead is refer-
305   enced by the actual time spent on program synthesis tasks. (5) Filtering Performance is evaluated
306
      by the attenuation ratio of invalid to passed neural network predictions.

403   64% and transformation pairs by 46%. The results showcase that neural networks can further narrow
404   the search space for program synthesis beyond CoL. Importantly, the group with the inner coupling
405   structure outperforms non-neural groups in both tasks. In contrast, the group without it presents an
406
      accuracy decline in symbolic tasks, validating the structure’s role in improving reliability.
407   Third, the inner coupling structure is more effective when error tolerance is low. As indicated
408   in Figure 6, for symbolic tasks, CoL DSL-based groups with the inner coupling structure signifi-
409   cantly outperform those without it. However, for relational tasks and DSL-based groups (without
410   CoL or heuristic), those without such structure perform better. This difference indicates that the
411   filtering effect of the inner coupling structure comes at a cost: it filters out both incorrect and correct
412
      predictions. So, its effectiveness depends on the positive impact of eliminating incorrect predic-
      tions outweighing the loss of correct ones. Therefore, for relational tasks with a limited search
413
      space and DSL-based groups with higher error tolerance, the cost of filtering outweighs the benefit.
414
      However, in symbolic tasks, where avoiding errors is more critical, CoL DSL-based groups benefit
415   significantly from the inner coupling structure.

532   4                    C ONCLUSION
533
534   We explored fine-grained control and flexible modularity for complex program synthesis through the
535   Chain-Oriented Objective Logic (COOL) framework. Inspired by activity charts and control theory,
536   we developed Chain-of-Logic (CoL) and Neural Network Feedback Control (NNFC) to achieve
537   these goals. Static and dynamic experiments across relational, symbolic, and multidomain tasks
538   demonstrated that COOL offers strong efficiency and reliability. We believe that continued research
539   and refinement of CoL and NNFC will inspire advancements not only in program synthesis but also
      in broader areas of neural network reasoning.

799   A.3    T RADITIONAL DSL
800
801   A DSL, defined as a context-free grammar:
802                                           G = {V, Σ, R, S},                                         (1)
803
804   where V is the set of non-terminal symbols, Σ is the set of terminal symbols, R is the set of rules,
805   and S is the starting symbol (in this context, it is a partial program). The DSL’s derivation pro-
806
      cess converts partial programs with nonterminal symbols into complete programs by applying given
      rules.
807
808   The synthesis process for traditional DSLs involves iteratively transforming partial programs into
809   complete programs by applying a series of rules. Each partial program (p) and the corresponding
      rule (r) to be applied to it form a transformation pair (p, r). When a rule is applied, it modifies


                                                       15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      the syntax tree of the partial program through what we refer to as a tree operation. The synthesis
811   process consists of a series of transformation pairs connected by tree operations, which is known as
812   a synthesis path or trajectory. These paths are classified into three kinds:
813
814          • Feasible Path: Leads to a complete program.
815          • Infeasible Path: Proven to be unable to synthesize a complete program.
816
             • Unfinished Path: Still in progress.
817
818
      To clarify key concepts involved in the synthesis process, we provide the following definitions of
819   terms:
820
821          • Tree Operation/Manipulation: Refers to the modification of the syntax tree of a partial
822            program during the synthesis process. It is essential for transforming partial programs into
823            complete programs and has an associated CPU cost.
824          • Transformation Pair (p, r): A combination of a partial program and a rule to be applied. It
825            records the explored space and possible exploration directions, requiring memory storage.
826
             • Synthesis Path/Trajectory: A sequence of transformation pairs, {(p0 , r0 ), (p1 , r1 ), . . . },
827            representing the process of transforming a partial program into a complete one. Its func-
828            tion is to track the entire synthesis process, whether it leads to a feasible, infeasible, or
829            unfinished path.
830
831   A.4    C O L DSL
832
833   Compared with traditional DSLs that apply rules to input programs without a clear destination to
834   synthesize output programs, the Chain-of-Logic (CoL) allows the programmer to outline the flow
835   of activities to synthesize the complete program from an initial partial program. For example, in
836   Figure 3, the activity flow is:
837
838
        Start → 1 Separate Relations and Genders → 2 Reason Inverse Relations → 3 Reason Indirect
          Relations → 4 Recombine Relations and Genders, Eliminate Irrelevant Relations → End.
839
840
      Each activity in the synthesis process has a corresponding sub-DSL decomposed from the original
841   DSL for transforming the program from one state to another.
842
843   Therefore, a CoL DSL with n activities can be defined as multiple sub-DSLs in series:
844                                        CoL G = {G1 , G2 , . . . , Gn }                                  (2)
845
846
      A sub-DSL for activity i is defined as:
847
848                            Gi = {V, Σ, {(r, h, k) | h[n] ̸= 0 and r ∈ R}, S, f }                        (3)
849
850   where h = (h1 , h2 , . . . ) in (r, h, k) represents the heuristic vector bound with r, and the components
851   are termed as heuristic values. h[n] represents the n-th component of h, which is the effective
852   heuristic value of rule r in activity n. h[n] is a parameter of the sub-DSL’s program synthesis
853   algorithm f . It guides the direction of program synthesis by affecting the application decisions of
854
      the rules bound to it, thereby improving synthesis efficiency and accuracy. The specific role of the
      heuristic value is determined by the search algorithm f used by the DSL program synthesis. To
855
      conduct controlled variable experiments, we regard the heuristic value as a reward (negative cost)
856
      and use the A* algorithm as the search algorithm for all DSLs, which means that in activity n, under
857   the same circumstances, the rule with a larger h[n] will be applied first, and the derived program
858   will also be considered promising and will be explored further with priority.
859
860
      k represents keyword(s) in a rule’s specific logic, which controls the program state transition within
      an activity or between activities.
861
862   The Chain-of-Logic provides a comprehensive methodology for achieving fine-grained control over
863   DSL program synthesis. This approach allows programmers to explicitly break down the synthesis
      process into distinct phases or activities, with each activity corresponding to a specific sub-DSL.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      A.5   C O L DSL S YNTHESIS P ROCESS
865
866   The synthesis process in CoL DSLs is conducted through multiple stages, each corresponding to
867   a defined activity. These stages operate sequentially, gradually refining the program through well-
868   defined transformations. The key difference from traditional DSL synthesis is that, except for the
869   sub-DSL of the final activity, intermediate sub-DSLs are allowed to generate partial programs, which
870   are passed on to subsequent activities for further processing.
871   Each stage in the CoL DSL synthesis process focuses on a specific aspect of synthesis to transform
872   the program incrementally. For instance, in Figure 4:
873
874          • In the first activity, relations and genders are separated, breaking down the initial partial
875            program into simpler components for easier processing.
876          • The second activity reasons about inverse relationships, further structuring the intermediate
877            program by identifying and processing inverse connections.
878
             • The third activity deals with indirect relationships, providing additional context to relation-
879            ships identified in earlier stages.
880
881
             • The final activity recombines relations and genders while eliminating irrelevant relations to
               produce a fully synthesized and optimized program.
882
883
      During each activity, the synthesis process leverages heuristic values to prioritize rule application,
884   focusing on areas that are more likely to lead to successful outcomes. Additionally, in intermediate
885   activities, since we cannot judge whether the synthesis process is correct based on whether com-
886   plete programs are generated, guidance based on heuristic values and synthesis flow control using
887   keywords are pivotal.

896
897   The neural networks leverage the transformation pairs (p, r) in the synthesis paths to train various
898   heads.
899
      To train the neural networks in DSNN bound with a DSL for program synthesis tasks of type T ,
900
      COOL builds the dataset as follows:
901
902   Task Detection Head (TDH): This head distinguishes whether the input partial program belongs
903   to type T . This is a binary classification task. The partial programs from type T program synthe-
904   sis paths are collected as positive examples (proportion: 67%), while partial programs from other
905
      synthesis paths and built-in function calls are collected as negative examples (proportion: 33%).
906   Search Space Prune Head (SSPH): After determining that the program is of type T , this head
907   identifies whether the input partial program is feasible to synthesize into a complete program. This
908   is also a binary classification task. Programs from feasible synthesis paths are collected as posi-
909   tive examples (proportion: 67%), while programs from infeasible synthesis paths are collected as
910   negative examples (proportion: 33%).
911   Search Guidance Head (SGH): After determining that the input is a feasible type T partial pro-
912   gram, this head generates rule features to guide the DSL solver in applying rules to the partial
913   program. This includes a series of classification and regression tasks.

1230   heuristic search. This algorithm is renowned for its efficacy in discrete optimization tasks, utiliz-

1232   decision is associated with a specific cost in this context. By evaluating the cumulative cost of ac-
1233
       tions taken so far and the estimated costs of future actions, A* seeks to determine the path with

1234

1285   H    O PTIMIZATION STRATEGY
1286
1287   In practice, we observe that as the CoL length increases, the frequency of skipping stages rises.
1288
       While skipping can lead to shorter synthesis paths and improved efficiency, it may cause task failures
       by omitting necessary stages. To manage this, we propose two strategies:
1289
1290         1. Gradient-Based Regulation: We employ gradient-based regulation, a widely used strategy
1291            in program synthesis Cui & Zhu (2021); Liang et al. (2018); Chaudhuri et al. (2021). By
1292            evaluating the slope or rate of change between consecutive stages, gradients help us make
1293            dynamic adjustments to synthesis paths. In our approach, we regulate skipping by applying
1294            a gradient to the heuristic values at each stage in the CoL. We encourage skipping when
1295            the heuristic gradient from one stage to the next is positive. Conversely, if the gradient is
                negative, we suppress skipping.


                                                        24
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1296
            2. NNFC Regulation: Once we establish a feasible synthesis path, we can treat partial pro-
1297           grams derived through skipping as infeasible. Then, we will utilize the feedback loop to
1298           suppress unwarranted skipping actions. However, since these partial programs might still
1299           contain feasible solutions, we need further investigation to understand and fully leverage
1300           the potential impact of this data.
1301
1302   In our experiments, we prioritize accuracy by suppressing skipping behavior, ensuring essential
1303   stages are included in synthesis paths.

1644   Neural Search Optimization: Neural networks are key for optimizing search in program synthesis.
1645   Projects like Kalyan et al. (2018); Zhang et al. (2023) and Li et al. (2024) use neural networks to pro-
1646   vide oracle-like guidance, while Neo Feng et al. (2018), Flashmeta Polozov & Gulwani (2015), and
1647   Concord Chen et al. (2020) prune search spaces with infeasible partial programs. COOL employs
1648   both strategies to enhance efficiency.
1649   Multi-step Program Synthesis: Chain-of-Thought (CoT) Wei et al. (2022) enhances LLMs by
1650   breaking tasks into subtasks. Projects like Zhou et al. (2022); Shi et al. (2023b) and Zheng et al.
1651   (2023) use this in program synthesis. Compared to CoT, which directly decomposes tasks, CoL does
1652   so indirectly by constraining rule applications.
1653   Reinforcement Learning: Reinforcement learning improves neural agents in program synthesis
1654   through feedback, as seen in Eberhardinger et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2024); Bunel et al. (2018),
1655   Concord Chen et al. (2020), and Quiet-STaR Zelikman et al. (2024). NNFC similarly refines control
1656   flow but serves an auxiliary role for programmer strategies in synthesis rather than dominating it.
