009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent advancements in vision-language models have expanded their potential for
012            real-world applications, enabling these models to perform complex reasoning on
013            images. However, in the widely used fully autoregressive pipeline like LLaVA,
014            where projected visual tokens are prepended to textual tokens, the visual tokens
015            often number in the hundreds or thousands, making them much longer than the
016            input prompt. This large quantity of visual tokens introduces significant compu-
017            tational overhead, slowing down training and inference. In this paper, we propose
018
               Visual Compact Token Registers (Victor), a method that reduces the number of
               visual tokens by summarizing them into a smaller set of register tokens. Victor
019
               adds a few learnable register tokens after the visual tokens and summarizes the
020
               visual information into these registers using the first few layers in the language
021            tower. After these few layers, all visual tokens are discarded, significantly im-
022            proving computational efficiency for both training and inference. Notably, our
023            method is easy to implement and requires a small number of new trainable param-
024            eters with minimal impact on model performance. In our experiment, with merely
025            8 visual registers—about 1% of the original tokens—Victor shows less than
026            a 4% performance drop while reducing total training time by 43% and boosting
027            inference throughput by 3.36×.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Vision-language models (VLMs) have attracted considerable attention for their capability to process
033
      visual and textual information, enabling various real-world applications such as image captioning,
      visual question answering, and multimodal reasoning (OpenAI, 2023; Liu et al., 2024c). For exam-
034
      ple, GPT-4V (OpenAI, 2023) demonstrates the potential of these models in helping visually impaired
035
      individuals to “see” the world through cell phone cameras.
036
037   Recent vision-language models, such as LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024c), employ a pre-trained vision
038   encoder as the model’s “eye” to extract visual features and use a pre-trained language model as
039
      the “brain” to perform reasoning and text generation. This straightforward architecture is highly
      effective and requires only a small instruction dataset for fine-tuning. However, since the entire
040
      set of projected image features is fed as the input to the language model, it results in a significant
041
      computational overhead due to the large number of visual tokens. For instance, LLaVA-NeXT (Liu
042   et al., 2024b) utilizes 2, 880 tokens to represent a single image, which can be overly redundant
043   in many scenarios. In contrast, the average text instruction length across all benchmarks used in
044   LLaVA-NeXT is fewer than 70 tokens, as shown in Appendix A.1.
045
      Therefore, to improve the efficiency of vision-language models, it is clear that reducing the number
046
      of visual tokens is essential. The state-of-the-art method, FastV (Chen et al., 2024), achieves this by
047
      directly dropping unimportant visual tokens. This approach is highly effective when reducing the
048   number of tokens by up to half. However, the model’s performance drops significantly when more
049   than half of the tokens are removed. Meanwhile, FastV requires obtaining the attention scores from
050   the self-attention block. Efficient attention implementations, such as FlashAttention (Dao et al.,
051   2022; Dao, 2023), do not support this feature. Consequently, FastV relies on standard attention
052   implementations to retrieve the attention scores. This can limit its deployment on devices that do
053   not support such implementations. We also find that this constraint slows down the fine-tuning stage,
      as presented in Section 5.3.

080   On the other hand, another popular approach for token reduction is to use a transformer-based pro-
081
      jection model for condensing visual tokens into a smaller set of queries. Notable examples include
      the Perceiver Resampler (Jaegle et al., 2021; Alayrac et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2023) and Q-Former
082
      (Li et al., 2023b). These methods outperform FastV when the reduced set of visual tokens is much
083

084
085   In this paper, we introduce Visual Compact Token Registers (Victor), a simple yet effective early
086   visual token summarizing method that provides a superior efficiency-quality trade-off compared to
087
      the state-of-the-art techniques. For visual tokens, we observe that they often contain redundant
      information, with many tokens being highly similar, as discussed in Section 3.1. To address this,
088
      our approach leverages the language model to summarize these visual tokens into compact registers.
089
      Victor begins by appending a small subset of register tokens to the visual tokens and uses the
090   initial k layers of the language model to summarize visual information into these register tokens.
091   Notably, during training, no specific loss function or operation is applied to explicitly force the visual
092   information into the registers. Empirical results show that the language model naturally uses these
093   tokens to store visual information. Meanwhile, starting at layer k, all visual tokens are discarded,
094   leaving only the summarized registers and textual tokens for efficient inference in the subsequent
095   layers.
096   Our method does not rely on attention scores, making it compatible with the most efficient attention
097   implementations. Moreover, Victor introduces just 1.78M additional parameters, accounting for
098   only 0.03% of the total model size. In contrast to approaches like the Perceiver Resampler, which
099   incorporates a separate transformer with 252.86M parameters (3.61% of the total model), Victor
100   leverages the powerful language model itself to perform this task, achieving significantly better
101   performance.
102   As illustrated in Figure 1, Victor establishes a more favorable Pareto frontier than the state-of-the-
103   art method, particularly exhibiting a noticeably smaller performance drop as throughput increases.
104   For instance, when the number of visual registers is set to 8—approximately only 1% of the origi-
105   nal visual tokens—our model experiences a performance drop of less than 4% while reducing total
106   training time by 43% and achieving a 3.36× increase in inference throughput. Our extensive ex-
107   periments demonstrate the effectiveness of Victor in balancing both efficiency and performance,
      making it a promising solution for visual token reduction in vision-language models.

110   2.1 V ISION -L ANGUAGE M ODELS
111
      Modern vision-language models typically combine a pre-trained image encoder with a large lan-
112
      guage model to handle multimodal data (Li et al., 2023b). One popular approach, often referred to
113
      as the autoregressive or LLaVA-style model (Li et al., 2023b; Liu et al., 2024c), directly projects
114   visual features into the input embedding space of the language model, treating these features as
115   part of the input tokens. However, in this design, the number of visual tokens is large and of-
116   ten exceeds the number of textual tokens, leading to inefficiencies. Another common approach is
117   cross-attention-based fusion (Alayrac et al., 2022), where added cross-attention blocks inside of the
118   language transformer layers allow textual tokens to attend to visual tokens. More recently, early-
119   fusion models like Fuyu (Bavishi et al., 2023), MoMA (Lin et al., 2024), and Chameleon (Team,
120   2024) use a unified transformer that processes raw textual tokens and visual patches simultaneously.
121   Additionally, a key component of modern vision-language models recipe is instruction fine-tuning
122
      (Dai et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024a;c; Singla et al., 2024), which enables the model
      to function as a typical chatbot while also processing images, even with a small synthetic fine-tuning
123
      dataset. In this paper, we focus on LLaVA-style models.
124
125
      2.2 V ISUAL T OKEN R EDUCTION
126
127   To improve the efficiency of vision or vision-language models, pruning or distilling visual tokens
128   has been widely studied. Rao et al. (2021) introduce DynamicViT, which uses a small module to
129
      predict the importance of each visual token, dropping less important ones to enhance efficiency.
      Similarly, EViT (Liang et al., 2022) retains important tokens and fuses the less important ones
130
      within the model, using attention scores from the class token to the visual tokens. Further, PuMer
131
      (Cao et al., 2023) reduces the number of both textual and visual tokens by progressively pruning
132   and merging them throughout the cross-modal encoder. Another interesting approach by Saifullah
133   et al. (2023) involves discretizing visual features into textual tokens to reduce dimensionality. For
134   more recent vision-language models, Perceiver Resampler (Jaegle et al., 2021; Alayrac et al., 2022;
135   Bai et al., 2023) and Q-Former (Li et al., 2023b) are commonly used to pool visual tokens into
136   a smaller set of queries using a transformer-based model. Additionally, in the FastV paper (Chen
137   et al., 2024), the authors observe that in LLaVA-style models, the attention from textual tokens to
138   visual tokens significantly diminishes after the first few layers, with the attentiveness declining close
139   to zero after 10% of the transformer layers. Intuitively, their proposed state-of-the-art method drops
140
      the unimportant visual tokens accordingly after the first few layers.
141
142
      2.3 V ISUAL R EGISTERS
143   Burtsev et al. (2020) first introduce memory tokens, which function similarly to registers. These
144   tokens are used to store global information, enabling the model to effectively handle long-context
145   tasks. Darcet et al. (2023) apply the idea of registers to ViTs. In their work, the authors observe
146
      that vision transformer models implicitly use low-information tokens to store global information for
      internal computations. However, this leads to abnormally high norms for these tokens, making it
147
      difficult to interpret the attention maps. Therefore, to address this, they introduce additional reg-
148
      ister tokens at the end of the sequence to handle this task. This approach not only improves the
149   interpretability of attention maps but also boosts model performance. In our work, we show that
150   these register tokens also enhance the performance of vision-language models, as demonstrated in
151   Section 5.6. However, our primary focus in this paper is on using these registers for information dis-
152   tillation, enabling the model to condense visual information into the registers to improve efficiency.

      3.2   V ICTOR
202
203   We now formally introduce our method: Victor (Visual Compact Token Registers). A LLaVA-
204   style vision-language model consists of three main components: (1) the image tower I, which is a
205   pre-trained vision model, such as the CLIP image encoder (Radford et al., 2021); (2) the language
206   tower T , a pre-trained LLM, such as LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023); and (3) a projector P that
207   bridges the two, mapping image features into the input embedding space of the language model.
208   Given an image ximg , we first extract its features using the image tower I and produce a set of
                                                           −1
209   projected visual tokens xV = {x0V , x1V , . . . , xN

229   This input is processed through the language tower for the first k layers. At the start of layer k,
230   all visual tokens are discarded, and the model continues with the truncated hidden states for the
231   remaining layers.
232   During training, we do not explicitly force the language model to summarize the visual informa-
233   tion into the visual registers, but we empirically observe that it does so implicitly. In Section 5.7,
234   we provide an empirical analysis showing that the visual registers effectively summarize important
235   information from the visual tokens. Moreover, because Victor leverages the language model it-
236   self for this summarization, rather than relying on an external model, and the language model is
237   both powerful and knowing at identifying the most useful image features, our method experiences
238
      minimal performance drop compared to approaches like Perceiver Resampler while requiring much
      fewer additional model parameters.
239
240   In practice, we typically set k to 3, which is just roughly 10% of the language tower. This means the
241   language tower processes the full-length hidden states for only the first 10% of its layers. For the
242   remaining 90% layers, it operates on significantly shorter hidden states, thereby improving model ef-
243   ficiency. FastV (Chen et al., 2024), a state-of-the-art method, follows a similar approach by dropping
244
      unimportant tokens in the early layers and achieves a comparable theoretical reduction in FLOPs.
      However, we find that because FastV relies on attention scores to determine which tokens to drop, it
245
      cannot utilize efficient attention implementations like FlashAttention (Dao et al., 2022; Dao, 2023)
246
      or PyTorch High-Performance Scaled Dot Product Attention (SDPA). Consequently, FastV delivers
247   less throughput improvement than Victor when applying the same token-drop ratio in practical
248   scenarios, and also empirically experiences a greater performance degradation in high token-drop
249   ratio regimes.

      4.2      E VALUATION
280
281   For evaluation, we use 11 benchmarks from the LLaVA-v1.5 report (Liu et al., 2024a), supplemented
282   by MMMU (Yue et al., 2024), a widely-used benchmark for assessing modern vision-language
283   models. The benchmarks include: VQAv2 (Goyal et al., 2017), GQA (Hudson & Manning, 2019),
284   ScienceQA (Lu et al., 2022), TextVQA (Singh et al., 2019), VizWiz-VQA (Gurari et al., 2018),
285   POPE (Li et al., 2023c), MME-P (Yin et al., 2023), MMBench (Liu et al., 2023), SEED-Bench (Li
286   et al., 2023a), LLaVA-Bench-in-the-Wild (Liu et al., 2024c), MM-Vet (Yu et al., 2023), and MMMU
287   (Yue et al., 2024). These benchmarks provide a comprehensive assessment of models’ multi-modal
288
      reasoning capabilities, encompassing both academic-task-oriented and instruction-following tasks.
      We employ the LMMs-Eval framework4 (Zhang et al., 2024) for evaluation. For simplicity, we
289
      primarily report the average of the normalized benchmark scores. Specifically, for MME-P, the
290
      score is divided by 2, 000, which represents the full score, as their metric is calculated by summing
291   the accuracies of individual subtasks.
292
293   We evaluate efficiency by measuring the increase in throughput with the KV-cache on (Pope et al.,
294
      2023). After gathering statistics from all 12 benchmarks, presented in Appendix A.1, we evaluate
      two settings: 1) 2-token generation and 2) 128-token generation. The 2-token generation simu-
295
      lates scenarios where questions expect a single word, as in GQA (Hudson & Manning, 2019) and
296
      TextVQA (Singh et al., 2019). In contrast, the 128-token generation represents open-ended question
297   scenarios, such as in LLaVA-Bench-in-the-Wild (Liu et al., 2024c) and MM-Vet (Yu et al., 2023). In
298   both settings, we use a text prompt length of 64 and a batch size of 16. We choose a batch size of 16
299   because it is the largest batch size that fits in memory. All training is conducted on 8 NVIDIA A100
300   GPUs, with efficiency profiling performed on a single NVIDIA A100. By default, we set k to 3 and
301   vary the number of final visual tokens to 256, 128, 64, 32, 16, and 8 for all methods to thoroughly
302   assess the trade-off between efficiency and performance, where the number of visual tokens for the
303   original LLaVA-v1.5 model is 576.
304   We also compare our methods against two baselines: FastV (Chen et al., 2024) and Perceiver Re-
305   sampler (Jaegle et al., 2021). FastV is the state-of-the-art token reduction method that filters out
306   less important vision tokens based on attention scores, while the Perceiver Resampler is a compact,
307   transformer-based model designed to condense input tokens into a smaller query set.

      6    L IMITATION AND F UTURE W ORK
519
      While Victor is simple and effective, we identified some limitations and directions for future
520
      improvements. Currently, Victor is not a training-free method, and it must be incorporated at
521
      the training stage of the vision-language modeling. Developing a version of Victor that could
522   be applied post-training would be a valuable advancement. However, this might be challenging,
523   as the language tower may need to be specifically trained to learn to effectively utilize the visual
524   registers. Another limitation is the inflexibility of the number of visual registers. As discussed
525   in Appendix A.5, the performance degrades if the number of visual tokens is changed on the fly
526   without retraining. In future work, we believe incorporating certain auxiliary loss functions could
527   help make Victor more adaptable and flexible. Additionally, while this paper focuses on applying
528   Victor to vision-language models, we believe this technique could also benefit language models,
529   particularly in long-context tasks. We leave these for future exploration.
530
531   7    C ONCLUSION
532
533   In this paper, we introduce Victor, a novel visual token summarization method that significantly
534   enhances the efficiency-performance trade-off in vision-language models. Without explicit enforce-
535   ment, the language tower utilizes register tokens to summarize visual information within the first
536   10% of the layers. After summarization, Victor removes the need for visual tokens beyond these
537   layers. Our approach offers a superior balance in efficiency compared to state-of-the-art methods.
538   Moreover, with just up to 0.03% additional parameters, Victor is compatible with various atten-
539
      tion mechanisms, providing a user-friendly and efficient solution across different hardware environ-
      ments for future applications.

912   In this subsection, we conduct an ablation study to demonstrate the necessity of using visual registers
913   for summarization. Specifically, we compare our approach to an alternative method where instead of
914   prepending additional tokens to the visual tokens, we retain the last M visual tokens at layer 3. This
915   requires the model to summarize all visual information into these last existing M visual tokens. As
916   shown in Figure 15, while the ablated method results in a slight improvement in throughput, overall
917   the performance drops significantly. This highlights the importance of incorporating visual registers
      for effective summarization.

937   We also experiment with various types of visual registers. In addition to using learnable tokens,
938   we test three alternative methods for visual registers: 1) Pooled Image Feature: utilizing average-
