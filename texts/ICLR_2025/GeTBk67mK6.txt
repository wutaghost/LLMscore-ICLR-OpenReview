011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            As the field of Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) continues to evolve,
014            their potential to handle mathematical reasoning tasks is promising, as they can
015            handle multimodal questions via cross-modal understanding capabilities com-
016            pared to text-only LLMs. Current mathematical benchmarks predominantly fo-
017            cus on evaluating MLLMs’ problem-solving ability, yet there is a crucial gap in
018
               addressing more complex scenarios such as error detection, for enhancing rea-
               soning capability in complicated settings. To fill this gap, we formally formulate
019
               the new task — multimodal error detection, and introduce E RROR R ADAR, the
020
               first benchmark designed to assess MLLMs’ capabilities in such a task. E RROR -
021            R ADAR evaluates two sub-tasks: error step identification and error categoriza-
022            tion, providing a framework for evaluating MLLMs’ complex mathematical rea-
023            soning ability. It consists of 2,500 high-quality multimodal K-12 mathematical
024            problems, collected from real-world student interactions in an educational organi-
025            zation, with expert-based annotation and metadata such as problem type and error
026            category. Through extensive experiments, we evaluated both open-source and
027            closed-source representative MLLMs, benchmarking their performance against
028            educational expert evaluators. Results indicate challenges still remain, as GPT-4o
029
               with best model performance is still around 10% behind human evaluation.

076    Towards this end, addressing the aforementioned research gap, we aim to formulate the new task
077    of evaluating MLLMs in the context of error detection scenarios, and therefore introduce the corre-
078    sponding benchmark termed E RROR R ADAR. We have designed two sub-tasks to comprehensively
079
       assess the performance: error step identification and error categorization. To construct a rich and
       reliable dataset, we initially sourced a collection of multimodal K-12 level math problems from an
080
       educational organization and subsequently refined the dataset through rigorous manual annotation to
081
       ensure quality. In particular, we also collect real students’ answers for each multimodal question for
082    a relatively robust experimental setting, compared to other relevant benchmarks (as shown in Table
083    1). Furthermore, we categorized the dataset to better align with diverse needs as follows: Problem
084    types: plane geometry, solid geometry, diagram, algebra, and mathematical common sense; and
085    Error categories: visual perception errors, calculation errors, reasoning errors, knowledge errors,
086    and misinterpretation of the problem. In summary, the E RROR R ADAR comprises 2,500 high-quality
087    instances derived from real-life problem-solving data, providing a foundational dataset to enhance
088    the complex reasoning capabilities of MLLMs for the research community and industry.
089    For E RROR R ADAR, we carry out an extensive experimental analysis to determine the proficiency
090    in complex mathematical reasoning of various MLLMs. The evaluation encompasses both the lat-
091    est open-source MLLMs (e.g., InternVL2 (Chen et al., 2023b), LLaVA-NEXT (Liu et al., 2024a),
092    CogVLM2 (Wang et al., 2023a)), and closed-source MLLMs (e.g., GPT4-o (OpenAI, 2024a), Gem-
093    ini Pro 1.5 (Reid et al., 2024), Claude 3.5 (Anthropic, 2024b)). Our focus was on their error de-
094    tection capabilities, specifically the identification of the erroneous step and the classification of the
095    error type. To establish a comparative human performance standard, we involved expert human ed-
096
       ucators who possess a graduate-level degree or higher qualifications. The results demonstrate that
       E RROR R ADAR, covering cutting-edge topics such as MLLMs’ complex reasoning, poses a signifi-
097
       cant challenge, with human evaluation for two error detection tasks achieving less than 70%.
098
099    From in-depth evaluation of representative MLLMs, we obtain the following findings: ❶ Closed-
100    source MLLMs, particularly GPT-4o, consistently outperform open-source MLLMs in both sub-
101    tasks, and show more balanced accuracy across different error categories; ❷ Weaker MLLMs exhibit
102
       an over-reliance on simpler categories, while stronger models handle complex tasks better; ❸ Both
       MLLMs and humans perform better on error step identification compared to error categorization, as
103
       localizing specific errors is inherently simpler than categorizing errors.

       Benchmarks for Mathematical Reasoning. Recent advancements in mathematical reasoning
       benchmarks have led to the development of both pure text and multimodal assessments (Lu et al.,
120
       2022; Wang et al., 2024a; Zheng et al., 2024; Huo et al., 2024). While datasets like GSM8K (Cobbe
121
       et al., 2021), MATH (Hendrycks et al., 2021), SuperCLUE-Math (Xu et al., 2024), and MathBench
122    (Liu et al., 2024b) focus on text-based problems, the field has expanded to include multimodal
123    benchmarks that introduce visual elements, pushing the boundaries of AI’s mathematical under-
124    standing. For instance, MathVista (Lu et al., 2024b) evaluates AI’s performance on visual math
125    QA tasks; MATH-V (Wang et al., 2024a) focuses on multimodal mathematical understanding with
126    competition-derived questions; MathVerse (Zhang et al., 2024) assesses visual diagram compre-
127    hension using CoT strategies; CMMU (He et al., 2024b) tests multi-disciplinary, multimodal math
128    understanding with a broad range of Chinese-language questions; MathScape (Zhou et al., 2024)
129    further advances the field by presenting longer, more complex, and open-ended multimodal prob-
130
       lems; and MMMU(Yue et al., 2024) covers college-level knowledge including interleaved mathe-
       matical questions. The aforementioned benchmarks assess the mathematical reasoning capabilities
131
       of MLLMs by evaluating their problem-solving levels, but they overlook tasks based on the student’s
132
       perspective, such as error detection, and thus fail to comprehensively evaluate the more complex role
133    of current MLLMs. Therefore, we propose the E RROR R ADAR benchmark, which is entirely based
134    on real student response data to evaluate the proficiency of MLLMs in error detection tasks.
135
136
       Multimodal Large Language Models. Generative foundation models such as GPT-4 (OpenAI,
       2023), Claude (Anthropic, 2024b), and Gemini (Pal & Sankarasubbu, 2024) have significantly ad-
137
       vanced various task solutions without fine-tuning (Cui et al., 2024; Yan & Lee, 2024; Zou et al.,
138
       2025; Zhong et al., 2024). Similarly, current open-source MLLMs, built on top of powerful LLMs,
139    have also demonstrated promising potential in multimodal tasks such as image captioning (Yang
140    et al., 2024) and visual question answering (Fan et al., 2024). For instance, LLaVA-NEXT (Liu
141    et al., 2024a) proposed projecting visual embeddings, extracted by a pretrained vision encoder, into
142    the word space through a single MLP layer, where LLMs like LLaMA, Vicuna, and Mistral are fine-
143    tuned to understand these post-projection tokens. In a similar fashion, Phi3 (Abdin et al., 2024),
144    DeepSeek-VL (Lu et al., 2024a), MiniCPM-V (Yao et al., 2024), ChatGLM (GLM et al., 2024),
145    CogVLM (Wang et al., 2023a), Intern-VL (Chen et al., 2023b), Qwen-VL (Bai et al., 2023) and
146    Yi-VL (Young et al., 2024) also utilize a projector (or adapter, shared compression layer, etc.) to
147
       align the visual embeddings extracted from a vision encoder with text embeddings, which are then
       concatenated and fed into LLM. Therefore, we propose E RROR R ADAR, a comprehensive bench-
148
       mark on a fine-grained evaluation of MLLMs’ ability to detect errors based on students’ answers
149
       and reasoning steps, thereby advancing the development of complex multimodal system.

252   3.2 DATA S OURCE & A NNOTATION
253
      Following the roadmap shown in Figure 3, this section includes how we collect and annotate E R -
254
      ROR R ADAR dataset to ensure the overall data quality. Different from the conventional benchmarks
255
      that rely on public datasets or modified textbook collections (Lu et al., 2024b; Zhou et al., 2024),
256   E RROR R ADAR dataset is uniquely sourced from the question bank of a global educational organi-
257   zation. This repository encompasses a vast array of mathematical problems in K-12 levels, totaling
258   over a million entries. Initially, we curated approximately 180,000 math problems that feature a
259   single image-based question stem, aligning with our goal to target a multimodal assessment setup.
260   Subsequently, we refined our selection by evaluating the universality and articulation of the prob-
261   lem content. For each problem, we identified multiple incorrect answers. To ensure the dataset’s
262   relevance for error detection tasks, we selected the most frequently given incorrect answer as the
263   student’s response. Additionally, we scrutinized cases where the most common incorrect answer
264   was due to system input errors despite the answer being correct. In such instances, we amended the
265   dataset by incorporating the next most frequently incorrect answer.
266   Furthermore, since error detection tasks necessitate a step-by-step reasoning process, we enriched
267   our dataset with new content through manual annotation. Specifically, we provided professional
268   annotators with the original multimodal QA data, student’s incorrect answers, and the pedagogical
269   team’s analysis of correct answer process. Based on this initial data, annotators delineated the
      erroneous steps leading to the incorrect answers (More details in Appendix B.1 and B.2).

      Our team of annotators, consisting of around ten educational experts with domain expertise, con-
271   ducted two rounds of cross-checking to ensure the reliability of the annotations. In cases of incon-

      4.1   E VALUATION P ROTOCOLS
288
289   In E RROR R ADAR benchmark, we propose an evaluation strategy using template matching rules.
290   The evaluation process consists of three stages: response generation, answer extraction, and perfor-
291   mance calculation. Initially, the MLLMs generate responses given the inputs, which incorporates the
292   multimodal mathematical question, wrong answer, and its step-by-step reasoning, using the template
293   from Appendix C.2. Subsequently, the short answer text can be extracted from the detailed response.
294   Finally, the model performance is based on the detailed score calculation as shown in Section 3.1.
295   The final score will be calculated by averaging the scores from three rounds of assessment.

410   not fully encapsulate yet. Even human performance reflects this trend, with knowledge error scoring

423   achieve human-level performance in error detection tasks, we can further introduce a reinforcement
424   learning from human feedback (RLHF) approach, enabling the model to align with human thinking
425   mechanisms in understanding error causes (Liu et al., 2023).

446   4.3.3   R ELATION BETWEEN E RROR C ATEGORY AND E RROR S TEP
447
448
      Finding #1: There is a close relationship between different error category and their distribu-
      tion in the reasoning steps. As shown in Figure 8, VIS tends to occur in the earlier to mid-stages,
449
      accounting for a median proportion of 0.5 of total steps. In contrast, MIS, REAS, CAL, and KNOW
450
      are more likely to arise in the later stages, with their median proportions ranging from 0.7 to 0.9.
451   More analysis of this relationship across MLLMs can be seen in Appendix C.6.
452
453   Finding #2: VIS occurs in the earlier stages of problem-solving reasoning. This finding could be
454
      closely linked to the sequence in which students approach the task (Binz & Schulz, 2023; Kennedy
      & Romig, 2024). Since image content often serves as a key reference early on, any misinterpre-
455
      tation of this visual information directly impacts the subsequent problem-solving steps. Students
456
      typically first examine the image, and then integrate the information before proceeding to reasoning
457   or calculation. As a result, visual perception errors arise earlier compared to other types of errors.
458
459   Finding #3: Other error categories are primarily in later stages of problem-solving reasoning.
460
      This may be linked to the increasing cognitive load students encounter during problem-solving.
      Cognitive Load Theory posits that information complexity ranges from low to high interactivity
461
      (Paas et al., 2010; Binz & Schulz, 2023). While low-interactivity information can be understood
462
      independently, high-interactivity information requires simultaneous processing of related elements,
463   thus increasing cognitive load (Kennedy & Romig, 2024; Abbad-Andaloussi et al., 2023). In later
464   stages, students must integrate complex information from multiple sources. For instance, calculating
465   the distance between two points needs increasing interactivity heightens cognitive load, leading to
466   errors like forgetting to take the square root or miscalculating differences. Consequently, as cognitive
467   load rises, the frequency of errors in later steps also increases.

514   4.3.5   V ISUAL P ERCEPTION C ASE S TUDY
515
516
      Visual perception errors are critical in multimodal error detection tasks, as they impact the accurate
      comprehension of mathematical problems presented with both text and diagrams. As illustrated in
517
      Figure 10 and Appendix C.7, the five primary categories of visual errors observed in GPT-4o (the
518
      MLLM with best overall and VIS performance) include distance perception, diagram percep-
519   tion, spatial perception, flip/fold perception, and shape perception. These categories differ in
520   their cognitive demands: distance perception focuses on point identification; diagram perception on
521   quantitative estimation; spatial perception on geometric visualization; flip/fold perception on mental
522   rotation; and shape perception on object classification (Lu et al., 2024b; Zhang et al., 2024). Detect-
523   ing such errors is challenging because they often require both intricate visual processing and precise
524   interpretation of mathematical relations, which can be difficult to encode in current MLLMs. To
525   overcome these challenges, future MLLMs should incorporate more advanced visual reasoning ca-
526   pabilities, possibly through enhanced alignment between vision and language modalities, enabling
527
      better detection and correction of complex perception errors (Song et al., 2023). This could signifi-
      cantly improve the robustness of MLLMs in mathematical and other perception-heavy tasks.

530   5   C ONCLUSION
531   In conclusion, this work introduces E RROR R ADAR, the first multimodal benchmark designed specif-
532   ically for evaluating MLLMs’s reasoning in mathematical error detection scenarios. By focusing on
533   both error step identification and error categorization, E RROR R ADAR bridges a critical research
534   gap in assessing MLLMs’ capabilities in complex mathematical reasoning. The dataset’s construc-
535   tion, based on real-world student interactions, ensures a robust evaluation framework that reflects
536   genuine user needs. Our extensive experimental analysis, comparing leading open-source and pro-
537   prietary MLLMs, reveals significant challenges in error detection, highlighting the need for con-
538   tinued advancements in this domain. As MLLMs continue to evolve, E RROR R ADAR serves as an
539
      essential benchmark for driving improvements in the effectiveness of multimodal reasoning systems
      in real-world applications, on the path to Artificial General Intelligence.

1136   B.1   A NNOTATION D ETAILS
1137
1138   To ensure the quality and relevance of the E RROR R ADAR dataset for error detection tasks, we em-
1139   ployed a rigorous manual annotation process, involving professional educational experts as anno-
1140   tators. This section outlines the details of the annotation procedure, focusing on how the data was
1141   enriched with step-by-step reasoning processes, identification of erroneous steps, and error catego-
1142   rization.
1143   Annotator Selection and Training. Given the complexity of the task, we recruited a group of ten
1144   annotators with specialized knowledge in educational theory and mathematics, particularly in K-12
1145   pedagogy. These annotators were trained extensively to familiarize themselves with the structure
1146   and expectations of the task. The training covered the specifics of multimodal problem-solving in
1147   mathematics, typical student error patterns, and the need for precise identification of reasoning steps
1148   that led to incorrect answers. The annotators were also briefed on using the provided tools and the
1149
       quality assurance process.
1150   Annotation Process. Each mathematical problem in the dataset was annotated with a step-by-step
1151   reasoning process, capturing both correct and incorrect approaches to problem-solving. Annotators
1152   were provided with:
1153
1154
             1. The original question stem (comprising both text and image components).
1155         2. The student’s most frequent incorrect answer.
1156         3. The correct answer to the question.
1157         4. The pedagogical analysis of the correct reasoning process, prepared by educational experts.
1158
1159   Based on these inputs, annotators were tasked with:
1160
1161
             1. Step-by-Step Reasoning Annotation: For each problem, annotators mapped out the log-
                ical steps that students should ideally follow to arrive at the correct answer. This involved
1162
                identifying key stages in the problem-solving process, such as formula application, arith-
1163
                metic operations, or logical deductions.
1164
1165
             2. Error Step Identification: For problems where students provided incorrect answers, an-
                notators identified the exact steps where the reasoning went wrong. These error steps were
1166
                explicitly marked and linked to the incorrect responses, ensuring that they could be traced
1167
                back to specific problem-solving mistakes.
1168
             3. Error Categorization: Once the erroneous step was identified, annotators assigned an ap-
1169
                propriate error category based on a predefined schema. These categories included common
1170
                types of errors such as misinterpretation of the question (More details can be seen in Sec-
1171            tion 3.1). The categorization was designed to align with known student error patterns in
1172            mathematical learning.
1173
1174   Quality Control and Cross-Validation. To ensure annotation accuracy and consistency, each prob-
1175   lem underwent two rounds of cross-checking:
1176
             1. First Round of Cross-Validation: After the initial annotation, another annotator indepen-
1177
                dently reviewed the annotations. Any discrepancies between the first and second annotators
1178
                were flagged for further analysis.
1179
1180
             2. Second Round of Cross-Validation: In the second round, if the errors or discrepancies
                persisted, the problem was escalated to a senior educational expert who acted as the anno-
1181
                tation lead. The annotation lead adjudicated these contentious cases, ensuring that the final
1182
                decision was both pedagogically sound and aligned with the dataset’s goals.
1183
1184   Dataset Refinement. Throughout the annotation process, we worked closely with the educational
1185   organization from which the dataset originated. This collaboration ensured that the annotations were
1186   not only reliable but also adhered to the standards of the organization’s question bank. Addition-
1187   ally, ongoing feedback and updates from the organization helped refine the dataset, making it more
       accurate and relevant for multimodal error detection tasks.

       Annotation Duration and Effort. The annotation process for the E RROR R ADAR dataset spanned
1189   over a period of at least two months. During this time, the annotators, comprised of both profes-
1190   sional educational experts and domain specialists, worked meticulously through several stages of
1191   preparation, annotation, and validation. Each annotator dedicated significant time to understanding
1192   the dataset, reviewing the provided pedagogical analyses, and applying their domain knowledge to
1193   identify and categorize errors. The first phase, involving step-by-step reasoning annotation, took ap-
1194   proximately six weeks, while the subsequent cross-validation and quality control efforts accounted
1195   for the remaining two weeks. Given the complexity of the tasks and the necessity for high precision,
1196   the team’s sustained efforts ensured that the final dataset was of the highest quality.
1197   By incorporating these annotations, E RROR R ADAR provides a robust foundation for studying stu-
1198   dent errors in mathematical reasoning and enables the development of advanced models for error
1199   detection and correction.

1201   B.2     D ETAILS OF H ANDLING I NCONSISTENT A NNOTATIONS
1202
1203   To ensure the quality and reliability of our dataset for the multimodal mathematical error detection
1204   task, we established a systematic approach to resolve annotation inconsistencies. This process bal-
1205   ances annotator independence with rigorous quality control, ensuring that the dataset is both accurate
1206   and representative.
1207
1208   B.2.1    A NNOTATION AGREEMENT P RINCIPLES
1209
             1. Guided Consensus: Annotations must align with clear, predefined guidelines covering the
1210            five error categories. Annotators are trained extensively to reduce subjective biases.
1211
1212         2. Cross-Checking and Agreement Threshold: Each instance is annotated by at least three
1213            annotators. Disagreements are flagged for further review.
1214         3. Systematic Review Process: For inconsistent cases, a multi-step resolution process is ap-
1215            plied:
1216
                 (a) Initial Review: Annotators discuss disagreements, referencing annotation guidelines
1217
                     and the specific problem context.
1218
                 (b) Expert Arbitration: For unresolved cases, a domain expert (e.g., an educational pro-
1219
                     fessional) reviews and finalizes the annotation.
1220
1221
                 (c) Consensus-Driven Decisions: When possible, annotations are harmonized based on
                     majority opinion or shared agreement after discussions.
1222
1223
1224   B.2.2    C ASE R ESOLUTION F RAMEWORK
1225
       Case Example 1: Visual Perception vs. Reasoning Error
1226
1227           • Example: A problem presents a bar chart requiring students to determine the highest value.
1228             A student misidentifies the tallest bar and selects the wrong answer.
1229
1230
                   – Annotator A labels this as a Visual Perception Error, arguing the mistake stems from
                     misreading the chart.
1231
1232               – Annotator B classifies it as a Reasoning Error, interpreting the mistake as a failure to
1233
                     compare values logically.
1234           • Resolution: Annotators revisit the problem:
1235
                   – If evidence shows the student misunderstood the chart format (e.g., interpreting height
1236
                     as quantity but misjudging due to poor visualization), it is classified as a Visual Per-
1237                 ception Error.
1238
                   – If the student correctly interprets the chart but misapplies logical comparisons (e.g.,
1239
                     failing to compare values explicitly), it is categorized as a Reasoning Error.
1240
1241             For persistent disagreement, an expert examines the student’s work, including any notes or
                 intermediate steps, to determine the correct annotation.

1243
1244           • Example: A problem asks for the perimeter of a rectangle, but the student calculates the
1245             area instead.
1246               – Annotator A identifies this as a Knowledge Error, attributing the mistake to a lack of

1248               – Annotator B labels it as a Misinterpretation of the Question, asserting that the student

               • Resolution:
1251
1252               – Did the student’s work demonstrate understanding of the concept but apply it incor-

                     rectly (Misinterpretation of the Question)?
1254               – Did the mistake reveal a fundamental gap in knowledge about perimeter (Knowledge
1255
                     Error)?
1256             If disagreement persists, the annotators consult the expert, who may analyze additional

       If discrepancies persist despite review and arbitration, the affected data points are excluded from the

1262   retained samples maintain high reliability.

1265

1268
       enhances annotator alignment over time.

        B.3   D EFINITION OF P ROBLEM T YPE C ATEGORY
1297
1298    The E RROR R ADAR dataset distinguishes five primary types of multimodal mathematical problems,
1299    each characterized by unique features:
1300   ✯ Plane Geometry Problems: These involve two-dimensional shapes and figures, requiring knowl-
1301     edge of properties such as angles, lines, and polygons. Solving these problems often depends on
1302     understanding basic geometric principles and theorems about plane figures.
1303
       ✯ Solid Geometry Problems: In contrast to plane geometry, solid geometry involves three-
1304     dimensional objects, such as cubes, cylinders, and spheres. These problems require spatial vi-
1305     sualization and understanding of volume, surface area, and the relationships between different
1306     three-dimensional shapes.
1307
       ✯ Diagram-Based Problems: These require analysis of provided visual information, such as
1308     graphs, charts, or diagrams, to solve mathematical queries. Interpreting visual data correctly is
1309     crucial, as these problems test the ability to extract and analyze quantitative information from
1310     visual aids.
1311
       ✯ Algebra Problems: Algebra problems focus on abstract symbols and variables to represent num-
1312     bers and relationships. These include tasks like solving equations, manipulating algebraic ex-
1313     pressions, and understanding functions. The problem-solving process typically involves logical
1314     reasoning and manipulation of mathematical symbols.
1315
       ✯ Math Commonsense Questions: These encompass a variety of problem types, including time
1316     judgment, direction judgment, counting, and pattern recognition. Unlike the other categories,
1317     math commonsense challenges rely on everyday mathematical reasoning and problem-solving
1318     strategies that do not necessarily require formal mathematical knowledge, testing intuitive under-
1319     standing rather than procedural skills.
1320
1321    These problem types highlight the E RROR R ADAR dataset’s diverse nature, with each category pre-
1322    senting distinct challenges and requiring specific reasoning abilities.

       C.1   H UMAN P ERFORMANCE E VALUATION
1461
1462   In the Human Performance section, the evaluation involved three educational expert evaluators, each
1463   independently performing the two subtasks — error step identification and error categorization —
1464   on a set of multimodal math problems. To ensure the validity of their assessments, a rigorous cross-
1465   checking procedure was implemented. After the initial independent evaluations, the results from all
1466   three experts were compared for both the identification of error steps and the categorization of those
1467
       errors. When discrepancies arose, particularly in cases where the experts disagreed on which step
       contained an error or how an error should be classified, a structured conflict resolution process was
1468
       followed.
1469
1470   The cross-check process began with identifying areas of disagreement between the evaluators. These
1471   conflicts were discussed in a series of consensus meetings, where the evaluators would review
1472   the conflicting steps or categorizations in detail. Each expert provided their rationale, referenc-
1473
       ing the mathematical principles involved as well as the multimodal representations of the problems.
       Through open dialogue, the evaluators aimed to reach a consensus on the correct interpretation of
1474
       the error.
1475
1476   In cases where consensus could not be easily achieved, a majority-vote system was employed. How-
1477   ever, for particularly complex or ambiguous cases, an additional adjudicator — who did not par-
1478   ticipate in the initial evaluations but had equivalent expertise — was consulted to provide a final
1479
       judgment. This adjudicator reviewed the contentious cases along with the evaluators’ justifications,
       ensuring an unbiased final decision. The outcome of this process was the creation of a refined
1480
       ground truth dataset that balanced expert knowledge with the goal of consistent and reliable error
1481
       identification and categorization.

1837
1838   In analyzing the error step distribution for the multimodal error detection task using InternVL2-
1839   76B (see Figure 21) and GPT-4o (see Figure 22), we observe a consistency in the pattern of error
1840   category distribution across both MLLM’s predictions and those in E RROR R ADAR (see Figure 8). In
1841   particular, VIS tends to occur in the earlier stages of problem-solving for both MLLMs, which aligns
1842   with the sequence in which students typically approach tasks. Since visual content often serves
1843   as a key reference at the outset, any misinterpretation of this information can significantly impact
1844
       subsequent steps. Students generally examine the image first and then integrate the information
       before proceeding to reasoning or calculation, leading to visual perception errors arising earlier
1845
       compared to other types of errors.
1846
1847   Other error categories, such as REAS, CAL, MIS, and KNOW, are more likely to emerge in the later
1848   stages of problem-solving. This pattern is linked to the increasing cognitive load students encounter
1849   as they progress. According to Cognitive Load Theory, information complexity ranges from low
1850
       to high interactivity. Low-interactivity information can be understood independently, whereas high-
       interactivity information requires the simultaneous processing of related elements, thereby increas-
1851
       ing cognitive load. In the later stages, students must integrate complex information from multiple
1852
       sources, which can lead to errors like forgetting to take the square root or miscalculating differ-
1853   ences when calculating distances, for example. Consequently, the frequency of errors in later steps
1854   increases with the rising cognitive load.
1855
1856
       Despite the overall pattern being consistent, there may be subtle differences between InternVL2-
       76B and GPT-4o in terms of error step distribution, especially for MIS category. These differences
1857
       could be attributed to the models’ distinct architectures and training data, which might influence
1858
       their approaches to error detection. As an open-source MLLM, InternVL2-76B might not have
1859   been optimized for specific types of questions or educational contexts, which could lead to a higher
1860   variability in MIS.
