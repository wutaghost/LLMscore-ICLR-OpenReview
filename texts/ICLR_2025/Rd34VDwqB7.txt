009                                                          A BSTRACT
010
011            Low-cost cameras have recently become widely used to monitor environmental ecosystems. This
012            paper focuses on scene prediction for monitoring small streams, which is critical for ensuring water
013            supply and informing early actions for floods and droughts. In contrast to traditional stream models
014            that typically rely on coarse-resolution weather data, stream images provide detailed information
015            about water properties and local environment at a higher temporal frequency. This paper presents a
016
               multi-modal generative framework designed for frequent temporal stream imagery datasets, aimed
               at generating the subsequent stream images. This task is challenging due to the variability of stream
017
               images caused by changes in time and local environmental conditions. Our method captures scene
018
               changes in both stream and surrounding environment by incorporating temporal context of weather,
019            water flow, and time information. We also introduce a domain-discriminative learning approach to
020            enforce the learning of domain-specific information in generating images. Our experiments demon-
021            strate the superior performance of the proposed method in preserving semantics of water and envi-
022            ronmental properties, using real data from the West Brook area in western Massachusetts, USA.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Effective observation over environmental ecosystems is critical for the sustainability of our planet, especially given the
      increasing pressures from environmental degradation, climate change, population growth, and urbanization. Remote
028
      sensing observations have been widely used to monitor environmental changes at large scale, but they are not suitable
029
      for monitoring small local regions due to limited spatial resolution and occlusions of various sources. More recently,
030   low-cost cameras have been deployed in many environmental applications to consistently monitor target small regions
031   at a high temporal frequency. These data sources provide rich information to facilitate the understanding of the
032   underlying processes and timely decision making on managing natural resources.
033
      This paper focuses on monitoring small headwater streams at a sub-hourly scale. Effective monitoring of these streams
034
      are critical for the estimation of multiple water quantity and water quality variables (e.g., streamflow, water depth, algal
035
      blooms), which are needed for ensuring drinking water supply for large population and suitable habitats for aquatic
036   life. Because of the importance of this problem, scientists have built many different machine learning (ML)-based
037   stream models (Feng et al., 2020; Cigizoglu, 2005; Jia et al., 2021; Karpatne et al., 2017). However, existing models
038   highly rely on meteorological data and remote sensing data as input features, but such data sources are only available
039   at a coarse resolution that is insufficient for predicting small streams. Besides, these data sources do not contain
040   the information of local catchment conditions, which are critical for understanding the variability of water dynamics
041   across different stream sites.
042   To enhance small stream monitoring, scientists have started using low-cost cameras to capture images at frequent
043   intervals, such as sub-hourly, at ecologically important stream locations (Cam; Geo). In this paper, we introduce
044   a new dataset containing stream images that are frequently collected from multiple sites in the West Brook area,
045   western Massachusetts, USA. This dataset provides new opportunities for building ML models to extract detailed
046   water-related information and the surrounding environmental conditions. Moreover, we propose Environment-Aware
047   Latent Diffusion Model (EALDM), which is a new scene-predicting method to to generate subsequent stream images
048   from previous stream images. This method can facilitate the forecasting of water and environmental conditions in
049   future time, the imputation and restoration of noisy or blocked images in history for retrospective analysis, as well as
050
      many downstream prediction tasks for water properties (e.g., streamflow). However, this generative task is challenging
      as it requires the model to effectively capture not only varying backgrounds and views in different sites, but also the
051
      strong temporal variation in images due to changes in time and environmental conditions.
052
053   Here we summarize the contributions made by the proposed EALDM in addressing these challenges. In particular,
      EALDM extends the latent diffusion model by incorporating the temporal information and introduces three key in-


                                                         1

054
      novations. First, EALDM integrates weather data, measured flow data, and camera images, which are from different
055   sources and of different modalities, into the image generative process. The goal is to ensure that the changes observed
056   in the generated images relative to the previous images are consistent with local weather conditions and water proper-
057   ties. Second, the image generative model explicitly takes into account time features, such as time of day and day of
058   year, to learn from sub-hourly images taken over long periods. Once trained, EALDM can be used to generate images
059   at specific times and capture seasonal and daily variations. Finally, we introduce a domain-discriminative learning

062   editing by predicting future changes in the environment and generating corresponding images, effectively providing a
063
      forward-looking view of the scene.
064   Our implementation can be accessed through the link https://github.com/Anonymous/EALDM. Our eval-

066   specific images at target times, which precisely capture water flows and environmental changes. This innovative
067   approach has potential to significantly enhance our ability to monitor and predict streamflow, contributing to improved
068   water management and ecological studies.

      2   R ELATED WORK
072
073   Stream modeling enables the prediction of water quantity and water quality variables, and thus can facilitate a range
074   of decision making processes in managing water resources. With the success of ML over the past two decades, there
075
      is a growing interest in using data-driven ML techniques for water dynamics prediction (Feng et al., 2020; Cigizoglu,
      2005; Jia et al., 2021; Karpatne et al., 2017; Ghosh et al., 2022). These methods typically use weather drivers (e.g.,
076
      precipitation, air temperature, solar radiation) as input features, which are often available at a coarse resolution and
077
      insufficient for predicting small streams. Image-based streamflow prediction methods emerge as promising alternatives
078   due to their ability to leverage visual data directly. The paper (Zhao et al., 2024) introduces a model combining
079   Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) with Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) networks to capture both spatial
080   and temporal dynamics in sequential stream image. This approach allows CNNs to extract spatial features from
081   images while RNNs model the temporal dependencies, enhancing the accuracy of streamflow prediction.
082
      Scene prediction for environmental monitoring aims to provide more comprehensive and detailed information by
083
      leveraging the temporal and spatial characteristics of environmental data. Diffusion models (Nichol et al., 2021;
084   Ramesh et al., 2022; Saharia et al., 2022; Song et al., 2020; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Jia et al., 2023) are a class
085   of generative models that gradually transform a simple distribution (usually Gaussian noise) into a complex data
086   distribution (such as images) through a process that iteratively denoises the data. This process is modeled as a Markov
087   chain and involves hundreds to thousands of steps, each slightly denoising the input towards the data distribution.
088
      Latent diffusion model (LDM) (Rombach et al., 2022) enhances the efficiency of traditional diffusion models by
089
      operating in a latent space instead of the pixel space. This is achieved by first encoding the data (images) into a lower-
090   dimensional latent representation using an encoder (part of an autoencoder). The diffusion process is then applied in
091   this latent space, and finally, a decoder transforms the denoised latent representation back into the image space. This
092   approach significantly reduces the computational cost and can speed up the generation process without sacrificing the
093   quality of the generated images. Classifier-Free Diffusion Guidance Model (CFDG) (Ho & Salimans, 2022) extends
094   LDMs by enabling conditional generation without relying on an external classifier. Instead, the model conditions itself
095   on given information (like text, class labels, etc.) during the training process, making it more efficient and easier to
096   use for conditional generation tasks.
097   Recent advancements in controlled image diffusion models have enhanced the personalization and precision of im-
098   age generation. Various techniques have emerged, offering diverse methods to manipulate these models for specific
099   outputs. For instance, DreamBooth (Ruiz et al., 2023) fine-tunes the diffusion model based on small, user-specific
100   datasets to personalize content. Text-guided methods modify prompts, CLIP features, and cross-attention (Brooks
101   et al., 2023; Hertz et al., 2022; Kawar et al., 2023), enabling more detailed and context-sensitive image generation.
102   Controlling the diffusion process allows adjustments over features such as inpainting (Avrahami et al., 2023) and color
103   variation (Meng et al., 2021). Other techniques, such as GLIGEN (Li et al., 2023b), train attention layers to better
104
      guide grounded image creation.
105   ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023) further extends the potential for conditional control in large pre-trained models, such
106   as Stable Diffusion, by freezing the original model’s parameters and training a separate copy to learn conditional
107   controls without sacrificing image quality. This copy is used for learning diverse conditional controls, with the two
      networks connected via zero convolution layers whose weights are initialized to zero, allowing them to gradually

      adjust during training. Similarly, BLIP-Diffusion (Li et al., 2024) introduces subject-driven generation, integrating a
109   vision-language encoder (BLIP-2 (Li et al., 2023a)) with Stable Diffusion to guide the model using both image and
110   text inputs, improving subject-driven generation and editing capabilities.

114

116
      The objective of EALDM is to generate the image at the next time step n + 1 conditioned on the available images,
      current weather information, and water flow data. This is formulated as an image generation problem where the goal is
117
      to accurately predict future scenes that incorporate critical environmental variables. Specifically, at each time step n,
118
      we represent the current image as xn , the weather data as wn , and the streamflow measurement as y n . The objective is
119   to learn a model f capable of generating the subsequent image f : {xn , wn , y n } → xn+1 . These data sources are also
120   collected over different stream sites (i.e., domains). We train the diffusion model using the collection of samples from
121   different sites and omit the domain index when it does not cause ambiguity. We will use the subscript i to represent
122   each stream site when we introduce the domain-discriminative learning method (Section 4.2).
123
      Our proposed EALDM method is based on the latent diffusion model (LDM), which utilizes an encode-decoder
124
      architecture to covert images to the latent space and recover the images, while involving a UNet (Ronneberger
125   et al., 2015)-based conditional diffusion model for conditional generation in the latent space. First, a given image
126   x0 is encoded into a latent representation z0 = E(x0 ) using the encoder E employed in latent diffusion model.
127   Noise is progressively added to the latent representation through a √      forward process, and the obtained noisy data at
128   tth diffusion step model can be represented as q(zt |z0 ) = N (zt ; ᾱt z0 , (1 − ᾱt )I), where ᾱt controls the noise
129   schedule. The reverse process gradually recovers the latent representation by following an iterative denoising pro-
130   cess pθ (zt−1 |zt ) = N (zt−1 ; µθ (zt , t), Σθ (zt , t)), for t = T to 1. The training process aims to minimize the loss:
131   L = Et,z0 ,ϵ ∥ϵ − ϵθ (zt , t)∥2 , where ϵθ (zt , t) is the predicted noise, and ϵ is the actual noise. After denoising, z0 is
132   decoded back to the image x0 = D(z0 ) using the decoder D.

135   4     E NVIRONMENT-AWARE L ATENT D IFFUSION M ODEL (EALDM)
136
137   The Environment-Aware Latent Diffusion Model (EALDM) consists of three key stages: encoding, conditioning, and
138   generation. In particular, the encoding process is similar to that in the standard LDM. The encoder E processes the
139   input data xn at time n and maps it to a latent space representation zn . The intuition is for this latent representation zn
140   to embed the critical features and dependencies of the input data, which are essential for the generation task. Next, we
141   introduce a UNet-based conditional diffusion model on the obtained latent representation zn . In particular, The UNet
142   model conditions the generation process for xn+1 on the sequential contextual data until time n, i.e., the temporal
143   context cn derived from weather, images, streamflow data, and other time-aware features (e.g., time of day and day
144   of the year). These conditioning factors provide crucial contextual information, which can help effectively guide the
145
      generation of future images. Finally, after we obtain the latent representation for the next time step, zn+1 , the decoder
      D then reconstructs the image xn+1 from zn+1 . In the following, we will provide details for two major components
146
      in the diffusion model: (i) how we incorporate the temporal context as the model condition and (ii) how we leverage
147
      the spatial domain information in the diffusion model.
148
149
150   4.1   E NVIRONMENT- AWARE C ONDITIONING
151
152   Temporal changes in stream images could be determined by weather, water conditions, as well as the time when images
153   are taken. Therefore, it is important to incorporate these influential factors into the image generative process, which
154   essentially captures the transition of image embeddings from each time n to the next time n + 1. Specifically, we
155   employ the same encoder from the Latent Diffusion Model to embed the previous image xn , as zn = E(xn ). To cap-
156   ture the temporal information, we apply a long-short term memory (LSTM) model to embed sequential weather data
157   {w1 , w2 , ..., wn } and streamflow data {y 1 , y 2 , ..., y n }, as w̃n = LSTM(w1 , . . . , wn ) and ỹn = LSTM(y 1 , . . . , y n ).
158   Additionally, the background and appearance of stream images reflect both seasonal patterns (e.g., green foliage in
159   summer and snow-covered landscapes in winter, as shown in Figure 3) and short-term variations throughout the day
160   (e.g., daytime versus nighttime images). To capture temporal variations in the images, we introduce two variables:
161   td , representing the time of day, and ty , representing the day of the year. The incorporation of these variables into
      the generative process helps capture both long-term and short-term temporal changes of the environment. Inspired by

      5.3   Q UANTITATIVE A NALYSIS OF M ODEL P ERFORMANCE
299
300
      We chose Latent Diffusion Models (LDMs), Classifier-Free Diffusion Guidance (CFDG), BLIP-Diffusion (Li et al.,
301   2024), and ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023) as baselines due to their complementary strengths in image generation
302   tasks conditioned on time and environmental factors. LDMs operate efficiently in the latent space of pre-trained
303   autoencoders, which allows for high-quality image generation while maintaining computational efficiency. This is
304   crucial for handling complex conditioning inputs like weather and time. Additionally, CFDG improves control over
305   the generation process by leveraging both the latent representation and conditioning input, providing sharper and more
306   accurate results that reflect the specific climate conditions being modeled. Moreover, BLIP-Diffusion enhances the

424   6   C ONCLUSION
425
426   In this work, we introduced the Environment-Aware Latent Diffusion Model (EALDM), which integrates multimodal
427   data such as weather, streamflow values, and temporal information into the generative process to produce realistic,
428   environment-consistent images over time. EALDM significantly expands the capabilities of latent diffusion models
429   by conditioning the generation process on temporal and environmental context. New model architectures have been
430   developed to effectively embed contextual information from different sources. Our method not only predicts future
431   visual changes based on past conditions but also ensures that the generated images precisely reflect realistic weather,
      time of day, and seasonal variations for the target stream site.

434   improves the adaptability of our model to specific sites, enabling more accurate image generation at different locations.
435   The results highlight the potential of EALDM for applications in climate-aware image generation, environmental

523   7   L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE WORKS
524
525
      While the proposed EALDM provides significant advancements in temporally conditioned image generation, there are
526
      several areas for future exploration. First, incorporating higher spatial-temporal resolutions could enhance the accu-
527
      racy of environment-dependent predictions, especially in regions where small-scale variations, such as microclimates,
528   play a significant role. Additionally, integrating more diverse data sources, such as satellite imagery or real-time IoT
529   sensor data, would further enrich the conditioning process, enabling more precise and real-time predictive capabili-
530   ties. Another promising avenue is the extension of this work to 3D or video-based temporal generation, which could
531   better capture dynamic environmental changes. Finally, exploring more sophisticated learning frameworks, such as
532   reinforcement learning or adversarial training techniques, could further refine the model’s predictive capabilities.
533
      Despite its effectiveness, EALDM has some limitations. First, the model’s reliance on multi-modal data sources
534   introduces challenges related to data availability, quality, and synchronization. Inconsistent or missing environmental
535   data could degrade model performance, particularly in real-world applications. Additionally, the current framework
536   assumes static relationships between environmental variables, which may not fully capture complex interdependencies
537   like feedback loops or long-term climatic shifts. Another limitation is the computational overhead, as the training
538   process for high-resolution, environment-aware diffusion models can be resource-intensive, making scaling to larger
539   datasets or more complex scenarios costly. Finally, the model’s capacity to generalize to unseen environments remains
      a challenge, particularly in regions where the conditions are drastically different from the training data.
