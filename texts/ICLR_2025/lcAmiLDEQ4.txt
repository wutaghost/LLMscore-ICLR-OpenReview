011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            We study the problem of minimizing the expectation of smooth nonconvex func-
014
               tions with the help of several parallel workers whose role is to compute stochastic
               gradients. In particular, we focus on the challenging situation where the workersтАЩ
015
               compute times are arbitrarily heterogeneous and random. In the simpler regime
016
               characterized by arbitrarily heterogeneous but deterministic compute times, Tyurin
017            & Richt├бrik (2024) recently proposed the first optimal asynchronous SGD method,
018            called Rennala SGD, in terms of a novel complexity notion called time complexity.
019            The starting point of our work is the observation that Rennala SGD can have arbi-
020            trarily bad performance in the presence of random compute times тАУ a setting it was
021            not designed to handle. To advance our understanding of stochastic optimization in
022            this challenging regime, we propose a new asynchronous SGD method, for which
023            we coin the name MindFlayer SGD. Our theory and empirical results demonstrate
024            the superiority of MindFlayer SGD over existing baselines, including Rennala SGD,
025
               in cases when the noise is heavy tailed.

      In real systems, each workerтАЩs computational power may differ from the others, leading to varying
055   completion times of gradient computation. A notable drawback of Minibatch SGD is its failure
056   to account for these differences in compute times across workers. The duration of each step is
057   determined by the slowest workerтАЩs computation time. As a result, all other workers remain idle
058   after completing their tasks, waiting for the slowest device to finish. Meanwhile, this idle time could
059   potentially be used in a more efficient way to improve the overall time complexity. Clearly, a redesign
060   of the algorithm is necessary.
061
062   Asynchronous SGD. As a result, a new generation of algorithms emerged, known as asynchronous
063   stochastic gradient descent (ASGD) methods, designed to fully utilize all available computational
064   resources (Recht et al., 2011; Feyzmahdavian et al., 2016; Nguyen et al., 2018; Arjevani et al., 2020;
065   Cohen et al., 2021; Mishchenko et al., 2022; Koloskova et al., 2022; Islamov et al., 2023).
066
      Here, the server performs a gradient-type update immediately after receiving a stochastic gradient
067
      from any worker, without waiting for the others. The updated model is then sent back to the worker,
068   which immediately begins computing a new stochastic gradient based on the updated model. By the
069   time the worker finishes computing this gradient, the model may have already been updated multiple
070   times on the server due to gradients received from other workers. This creates a delay in the model
071   update, denoted as ╬┤k . The algorithm can be described as follows:
072
073         1. receive a stochastic gradient тИЗf (xkтИТ╬┤k ; ╬╛ kтИТ╬┤k ) from any worker,
074
            2. update the model via xk+1 = xk тИТ ╬│тИЗf (xkтИТ╬┤k ; ╬╛ kтИТ╬┤k ),
075
076         3. send new xk+1 to the worker so the worker computes тИЗf (xk+1 ; ╬╛ k+1 ).
077
078   Cohen et al. (2021); Mishchenko et al. (2022); Koloskova et al. (2022) showed that ASGD is provably
079   faster in terms of time complexity then Minibatch SGD.
080
      However, it turns out that this untamed and wild asynchrony can be detrimental. The drawback
081   of ASGD lies in the assumption that all workersтАЩ computations are beneficial. It suffers from the
082   issue of updating the model with potentially significantly delayed gradients, which ultimately harms
083   convergence and, consequently, the overall time complexity, as discussed in the work of Tyurin &
084   Richt├бrik (2024). To address this issue, there was a need to introduce a method that ignores outdated
085   gradients while maintaining the philosophy of maximizing the utilization of available computational
086   resources.
087
088   Rennala SGD. Such a method was proposed in a recent breakthrough by Tyurin & Richt├бrik (2024).
089   Their method which can be viewed as a modification of the Minibatch SGD method. At each iteration
090   the server collects a batch of gradients, but it allows workers to send as many gradients as they can on
091   the same point xk . Then, using this batch, Rennala SGD proceeds with a gradient-type update using
092   this batch as in Minibatch SGD:

      it sends. For the struggling workers, it may happen that they are completely ignored.
099
100   Their approach considers a setting where each worker i requires a fixed ╧Дi > 0 seconds to compute a
101   stochastic gradient. For the first time lower bounds on time complexity were obtained for first order

105   While it may seem that the story is over, we want to question the fixed time assumption, arguing

      The deterministic compute time setup considered by Tyurin & Richt├бrik (2024), where Rennala
111   SGD is optimal, fails to capture the complexities of real-world distributed learning environments. In
112   practice, compute times are often uncertain due to various factors such as failing hardware, preemption
113   by other jobs, delays in GPU computation, and inconsistencies in network communications (Chen
114   et al., 2016; Dutta et al., 2018). This uncertainty is even more pronounced in federated learning
115   scenarios, where client unreliability can lead to unpredictable computation times or even incomplete
116   tasks (Kairouz et al., 2021).
117   To address these real-world challenges, we propose a more practical setup that incorporates ran-
118   domness into compute times. Specifically, we consider a scenario where the stochastic gradient
119   computation time of worker i is given by:
120

121
122   where ╧Дi > 0 is a constant representing the minimum time for client i to complete the gradient
123   computation, and ╬╖i is a non-negative random variable drawn from some distribution Ji , modeling
124   the aforementioned uncertainties.
125   In this more realistic setting, existing methods like Rennala SGD and ASGD can perform poorly or
126   even fail to converge. We can illustrate this with a simple example:
127
      Consider a scenario where each time we request a device to compute a stochastic gradient, one of
128
      two outcomes occurs. Either the device completes the computation exactly after the minimum time ╧Д
129
      without any delays, or something goes wrong and the computation is never completed. This situation
130   can be modeled using a random time ╬╖ as follows:
131

135
      each iteration to perform a step runs the risk of never receiving the required batch and getting
136   stuck. This includes methods like Rennala SGD or ASGD. Specifically, if the algorithm waits for a
137   single stochastic gradient on each iteration, there is a probability q n that it will never receive it and
138   consequently never proceed.

140

141

142   time, the partial computation is discarded, and a new computation is initiated. Our main contributions
143   are as follows.

768   B    C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
769
770
      In this paper, we address the problem of minimizing the expectation of nonconvex functions with
771
      Lipschitz gradients, with the use of parallel workers computing stochastic gradients. Our focus lies
772   on the challenging scenario where worker compute times are heterogeneous and random, expanding
773   on recent developments in ASGD methods like Rennala SGD. We observe that while Rennala SGD
774   performs optimally in environments with deterministic compute times, its effectiveness diminishes
775   under random compute conditions.
776
      To better understand and improve stochastic optimization in these conditions, we introduce a novel
777
      asynchronous SGD method named MindFlayer SGD. This method adjusts to the randomness in
778   computation times by not adhering to a fixed batch size but rather setting specific times for computing
779   single stochastic gradients. If a client fails to deliver within this time frame, the computation is
780   discarded, and the process restarts. This flexibility allows MindFlayer SGD to perform robustly across
781   various conditions, notably outperforming both Rennala SGD and standard Asynchronous SGD
782   (ASGD) in our theoretical and empirical analysis.
783
      Our results demonstrate that MindFlayer SGD significantly reduces time complexity, particularly in
784   environments characterized by positively skewed distribution of computation times. We empirically
785   validate this in simulations with several distributions conditions where MindFlayer SGD consistently
786   outperforms the other methods, particularly in high-variance scenarios. This showcases its superiority
787   in adapting to the unpredictable duration of gradient computations typical in real-world applications
788   such as federated learning environments.
789
      In this study, our analysis was confined to computation times, with no consideration given to
790   communication times. Future research will extend our investigation to include communication times.
791   Moreover, we plan to explore the application of gradient estimators with varying variance bounds
792   across different clients. We hypothesize that controlling these variance bounds could yield further
793   benefits in the optimization process.

      E    S IMPLIFYING M IND F LAYER FOR P RACTICAL U SE
929
930
931   The version of MindFlayer SGD presented in this paper aims to be as general as possible, with
932   the primary objective of providing theoretical insight, which is the focus of this work. Allowing
933   for significant variability in the distributions of worker compute times intuitively necessitates the
934   introduction of multiple hyperparameters, such as Bi (batch sizes) and ti (clipping times), to ensure
935   effective optimization under diverse scenarios. While these hyperparameters enable the algorithm to
936
      adapt to heterogeneous and random conditions, they also introduce additional complexity, which may
      complicate implementation in practical settings.
937
938   We propose Mod MindFlayer SGD, a practical variant that replaces Bi and ti with two global
939   parameters: a probabilistic threshold p, which reflects the likelihood of completing a gradient
940   computation, and a global batch size B, specifying the total number of trials across all workers. This
941   reformulation simplifies hyperparameter tuning while retaining robustness.
942   The parameter p captures system reliability. For reliable systems, p approaches 1, recovering Rennala
943   SGD, while for less reliable systems, lower p values leverage MindFlayer SGD тАЩs robustness. The
944   choice of ti can be guided by historical data via the inverse cumulative distribution function of p, or
945   adjusted dynamically using the Robbins-Monro stochastic approximation, as such:
946   We update the clipping time ti at each iteration using the Robbins-Monro stochastic approximation
947   (Robbins & Monro, 1951):
