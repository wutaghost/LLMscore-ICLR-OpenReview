009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We study the problem of evaluating the predictive uncertainty of large language
012            models (LLMs). We assign an uncertainty measure to the correctness of outputs
013            from an LLM conditioned on a query using a form of entropy that applies to
014            semantic objects (concepts). Unlike prior works, the notion of meaning used to
015            define concepts is derived from the LLM, rather than from an external model.
016            Our method measures an uncertainty over concept structures by drawing from
017            ideas in Formal Concept Analysis (FCA) and lattice/order theory, and can be used
018
               to estimate correctness in closed- and open-ended scenarios. Our method has a
               relative improvement of up to 4.8% on average across five standard benchmarks as
019
               well as improves over comparable baselines on datasets consisting of both closed-
020
               and open-ended questions.

023   1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Large language models (LLMs) produce factually incorrect output, colloquially referred to as hal-
026   lucination, which is a serious concern for their widespread use. For instance, LLM hallucination
027   has already been the center of multiple scandals such as naming academics as terrorists (Hsu, 2023),
028   inaccurately summarizing a federal court case using non-existent legal precedence (Belanger, 2023),
029   and citing imaginary scientific articles (Heaven, 2022).
030   Recent work shows that hallucinations often occur when an LLM has high uncertainty (Farquhar
031   et al., 2024). However, measuring a robust notion of LLM uncertainty for detecting hallucination
032   is challenging (Abbasi-Yadkori et al., 2024; Hou et al., 2024). This difficulty stems from the fact
033   that the LLM’s output logits reflect syntactic uncertainty — the probability of individual tokens —
034   while hallucination is a property of the meaning of the output. As a solution to this issue, Kuhn et al.
035   (2023) proposed clustering sampled outputs based on their meaning before computing an entropy
036   measure (dubbed ‘semantic entropy’). In their approach, meaning is defined using an external model
037   which serves as an arbiter of semantic equivalence.
038   Semantic entropy improves over naive entropy, but semantic variation of an LLM’s output does not
039   always align with its uncertainty. For instance, as noted by Abbasi-Yadkori et al. (2024), seman-
040   tic entropy becomes large in open-ended scenarios even when an LLM responds correctly. These
041   scenarios are common and can arise together with closed-ended questions. For instance, the query
042   “Name a city in the 31st US state” introduces uncertainty on two levels: first, determining which
043   state is the 31st — a question with a definite answer — and second, identifying an appropriate city
044
      within that state — a question that is inherently open-ended.
045   Another challenge comes from defining semantic equivalence. Existing methods often use an ex-
046   ternal model of semantics, but a semantic grouping which differs from a model’s own notion of
047   semantics incorporates external (lack of) knowledge into the uncertainty measure. For instance, the
048   model used by Kuhn et al. (2023) designates “Kitzbuehel” and “Kitzbuehel, Austria” as distinct
049   even though a relatively small LLM correctly outputs both as answers to “Where does the infamous
050
      ‘Streif’ downhill ski race take place?”. In addition, the external model limits the applicable domains
      to its training distribution which is often much smaller than the LLM’s training distribution.
051
052   We address these problems with Conceptual Uncertainty, a framework for measuring LLM uncer-
053   tainty, which applies to both open- and closed-ended scenarios without using external knowledge.
      Our key insight is to measure uncertainty over an LLM’s likelihood of different answer truth as-

      2     A G ENERAL F RAMEWORK FOR LLM U NCERTAINTY
109
110
      In this section, we give some background on existing methods for LLM uncertainty estimation. We
111   write A for a vocabulary of tokens and A∗ for the set of variable-length strings with tokens in A. A
112   general uncertainty metric is a function which takes an LLM M and a query q ∈ A∗ and outputs a
113   score U (M, q) ∈ R. Informally, the metric should provide information on whether M is likely to
114   produce incorrect outputs given the query q. We measure an uncertainty metric’s usefulness by
115
                     Pr U (M, q1 ) < U (M, q2 )| M answers q1 correctly and q2 incorrectly ,
116
117   where the queries q1 and q2 are sampled from an annotated dataset, and a probability closer to 1 rep-
118
      resents a stronger uncertainty metric. Indeed, this evaluation corresponds to the ROC-AUC metric
      commonly used in prior work on LLM uncertainty (Galil et al., 2023) as well as work on metacog-
119
      nition (Hosseini & Ferrell, 1982). Note that, following Lin et al. (2024), we use the term uncertainty
120
      as opposed to confidence as we view uncertainty as independent of the selected output to the query.
121
122
      2.1   C OMPONENTS OF LLM U NCERTAINTY
123
124   Most existing works quantify LLM uncertainty with two main steps: 1) generating multiple answers
125   to a query, and 2) measuring some form of answer diversity. The model is considered uncertain when
126   answers are diverse. The measure of answer diversity is central to an uncertainty metric and should
127   reflect semantic rather than surface-level syntactic differences between the answers. The exception
128   to this description are methods operating on the hidden activations of the model (before producing
129   an answer) to derive an uncertainty score (Kadavath et al., 2022; Yin et al., 2024).
130   We further decompose existing diversity measures (step 2) into three key components. In the fol-
131   lowing, we assume we have used a model M and some sampling method to generate n answers
132   A = {a1 , . . . , an } ⊂ A∗ to a query q ∈ A∗ .
133
      Output Grouping: The first component is a strategy for grouping the model’s answers into (disjoint)
134   subsets G = {g1 , . . . , gk }, gi ⊂ A, each consisting of semantically equivalent answers. Prior works
135   group using an external entailment model (Kuhn et al., 2023), prompting (Farquhar et al., 2024), or
136   a token-level heuristic (Abbasi-Yadkori et al., 2024) such as the ROUGE (Lin, 2004). Some work
137   also considers a soft grouping with a similarity metric between all answers (Chen et al., 2024).
138
      Group Weighting: The second component assigns a likelihood score P (gi ) ∈ [0, 1] to each of
139
      the semantic groups gi ∈ G. The simplest        approach is to sum the model’s raw likelihoods for
140   each output in the group, P (gi ) =

      In this section, we present our framework for addressing the above challenges. First, we introduce
195
      the structure of internal concepts based on entailment orderings. We then define the truth-assignment
196   likelihood function and our proposed Conceptual Entropy measure to measure uncertainty of the
197   truth-assignment distribution. Finally, we describe our practical implementation of these elements.
198
199   3.1   I NTERNAL C ONCEPTS
200
201   Given a set of candidate answers A = {a1 , . . . , an } ⊂ A∗ for a query q, we view the corresponding
202   “concept structure” as determined by a partial ordering where ai ≤ aj indicates that if ai is a valid
203   output for q, then aj is also a valid output. We discuss shortly how this partial ordering can be
204   obtained from the language model, but for the moment we assume that such an order is given. For
205
      any set S ⊂ A, the corresponding concept can be associated with the set S ▷ = {a : a′ ≤ a ∀a′ ∈
      S} ⊂ A. That is, S ▷ is the smallest set of answers containing S that is closed under forward
206
      implication, or the set of “consequents” of S. Dually, we can also consider the set S ◁ = {a : a ≤
207
      a′ ∀a′ ∈ S} ⊂ A of all valid “premises” for S. The sets S ▷ and S ◁ determine each other, and either
208   one can be viewed as a representation of the concept spanned by S. The set of all concepts is itself
209   partially ordered and forms a lattice, technically known as the “Dedekind-MacNeille” completion
210   of (A, ≤) (Davey, 2002). A simple example of a concept lattice is shown in Figure 2. We refer to
211   Appendix A for further discussion and general definitions.
212
      In our approach, we aim to recover an approximate partial ordering among outputs from the LLM’s
213
      probabilities. Specifically, we can consider a “clipped” likelihood ratio given by

286
      contribution to the overall probability. Note that, in contrast, traditional entropy composes additively
287   (without weights) for statistically independent groups, rather than for mutually exclusive ones.
288
289   Finally, we observe that if P describes mutually exclusive outcomes A (or a partition of outcomes
290
      into mutually exclusive groups), then Equation 3 can be seen as traditional entropy where only
      the probability outside of the logarithm is normalized. This coincides with partially normalized
291
      entropy used by Aichberger et al. (2024), which was adopted based on empirical motivations. Our
292
      experiments confirm that Conceptual Entropy is significantly more effective than traditional entropy.

      3.4   P RACTICAL I MPLEMENTATION
295
296   Computing the full truth-assignment function is unfortunately computationally expensive. To make
297   Conceptual Uncertainty practical, we leverage the weighted composition property of Conceptual
298   Entropy. This allows us to simplify the computation in Equation 3 by replacing the sum over all
299   possible answer truth assignments, a total of 2|A| , with a composition of sums over truth assignments
300   for smaller subsets of answers. The method is detailed in Algorithm 1. We derive approximately
301   disjoint answer groups by clustering answers with an L∞ -norm in a constructed concept space
302   shown on lines 2-4 in Algorithm 1. Then we compute the Conceptual Entropy in lines 5-8.

471
      LLM Uncertainty Estimation. There is now a large body of work on LLM uncertainty estima-
      tion. We categorize this work into sampling-based, probing-based, and verbalization-based methods.
472
      Sampling-based methods compute an uncertainty metric, such as entropy, over multiple sampled
473
      outputs (Malinin & Gales, 2021; Manakul et al., 2023; Chen & Mueller, 2024). The output distri-
474   bution can be modified by grouping semantically equivalent outputs (Kuhn et al., 2023) or sampling
475   more diverse outputs (Aichberger et al., 2024). Some work also measures uncertainty using variance
476   in the LLM’s activations (Chen et al., 2024). Finally, there are ensembling-based sampling methods
477   (Sun et al., 2022). Probing methods predict uncertainty from the LLM’s activations before sampling
478   (CH-Wang et al., 2024; Kadavath et al., 2022; Ahdritz et al., 2024). Finally, verbalization methods
479   have the LLM directly output its uncertainty in words or as a numeric value (Xiong et al., 2024;
480   Kadavath et al., 2022; Amayuelas et al., 2024). Verbalization methods are often the least performant
481   without finetuning the model. See Gawlikowski et al. (2023) for a survey on traditional uncertainty
482
      quantification. Our method is a sampling-based uncertainty method.
483   Semantic Information. There is an existing line of work redefining the tools from information
484   theory, such as entropy, to apply to semantic objects rather than raw data representations (Xin et al.,
485   2024; Chattopadhyay et al., 2021). We share the same motivation as this line of work while extract-
      ing a notion of meaning from a pretrained model without specialized training.

520   6          D ISCUSSION
521
522   We proposed Conceptual Uncertainty as a general framework for measuring LLM uncertainty in
523   open- and closed-ended scenarios without an external model. By leveraging a model’s internal
524   concept structure, Conceptual Uncertainty defines a distribution over possible truth assignments.
525   Using this, we propose Conceptual Entropy, a modified entropy measure to quantify uncertainty
526   over truth assignments. Our approach outperforms state-of-the-art baselines across diverse datasets,
527   effectively handling both question types.
528   Our method requires sampling 10–20 outputs per query, making it resource-intensive. It also detects
529   only uncertainty-driven hallucinations, missing those with high confidence—limitations shared by
530   similar methods like semantic entropy. We also note that measuring uncertainty from internal con-
531   cept structure has the concern of circularity. However, uncertainty relating to the structure of internal
532   concepts is not necessarily related to the model’s output uncertainty, breaking the circularity. For
533   example, a model may not know the answer to the question “When was Lincoln born?”, but still
534   know that if the answer is “February 12, 1809” then “February 12” is also correct.
535   In the future, we hope to find more efficient ways to compute and leverage the full truth-assignment
536   function for the model, which captures the model’s interpretation of conceptual structures. More
537   broadly, we plan to develop ways of integrating our framework directly into LLM-based systems.
538   For example, our uncertainty can guide tasks like Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG), allowing
539   the model to better gauge when it needs additional information. We hope this can lead to improve-
      ments in the reliability and safety of LLMs deployed in real-world applications.

      A     M ATHEMATICAL BACKGROUND
757
758   We give a brief overview of basic notions in order theory and Formal Concept Analysis. For more
759   details, we refer to Davey (2002) and Ganter et al. (1997).
760
761   A.1   O RDERS AND L ATTICES
762
763   A partial order (or simply order) on a set Q is a relation ≤ that is reflexive, antisymmetric, and
764   transitive. A relation that is reflexive and transitive but not necessarily antisymmetric is often called
765   a pre-order. A partial ordering on a finite set Q can be represented pictorially using a Hasse diagram.
766   This is a diagram like the one in Figure 2, where nodes represent elements of the set and lines indicate
767
      the order relation, with greater elements appearing higher in the diagram.
                                                           W
768   If S ⊂ Q is a subsetVof an order, then we write S for the least upper bound       Wof S, when
                                                                                                 V it exists.
769   Similarly, we write S for the greatest lower bound of S, when it exists. If S and S exist for
770   all sets S ⊂ Q, then Q is called a complete lattice. Not all partial orders are a lattices: for example,
771   a set S may have no upper/lower bounds, or a minimal/maximal bound might not exist. A lattice
772   can also be defined algebraically as a set L equipped with two binary operations ∨, ∧ (“join” and
773
      “meet”) satisfying certain algebraic conditions.
774   A particular example of a lattice is a powerset algebra P(A), where elements are subsets of a set A
775   ordered by inclusion. Given any partialy ordered set Q, the collection of down-sets O(Q) := {S ⊂
776   Q : x ∈ S, y ≤ x ⇒ y ∈ S} ordered by inclusions is always a lattice.
777
778   A.2   F ORMAL C ONCEPT A NALYSIS
779
780   Formal Concept Analysis (FCA) is a very general mathematical framework for defining a concept
781
      lattice from a simple “pairing” between two sets. The point of departure is a triple (G, M, I) where
      G and M are sets and I ⊂ G × M is an arbitrary relation. The elements G and M are traditionally
782
      interpreted as objects and attributes respectively, and the pairing I describes which objects have
783
      which attributes. The triple (G, M, I) is called a context.
784
785   For any sets X ⊂ G and Y ⊂ M , we define
786         X ▷ := {m ∈ M : ∀g ∈ X, (g, m) ∈ I},               Y ◁ := {g ∈ G : ∀m ∈ X, (g, m) ∈ I}.
787   In words, X ▷ is the set of all common attributes of the objects in X, and Y ▷ is the set of all objects
788   that have all attributes in Y . The maps X 7→ X ▷ and Y 7→ Y ◁ are sometimes called the polars
789   of the relation I. A concept in the context (G, M, I) is defined as a pair (X, Y ) with X ⊂ G and
790   Y ⊂ M such that X ▷ = Y and X = Y ◁ . The set of all concepts in in the context is denoted by
791   B(G, M, I) (from the German ‘Begriff’). The two sets X, Y are called the extent and the intent of
792   the concept respectively, and each of the two uniquely determines the concept.
793   Concepts are partially ordered according to
794
                                 (X1 , Y1 ) ≤ (X2 , Y2 ) ⇔ X1 ⊂ X2 ⇔ Y1 ⊃ Y2 .
795
796   With this partial ordering, B(G, M, I) is a complete lattice called the concept lattice
797   These ideas are extremely general and find applications in very diverse settings. For example, one
798   can consider a partial order Q, and consider the context where M = G = Q and (x, y) ∈ I if and
799   only if x ≤ y. This leads to a lattice called the Dedekind-MacNeille completion of Q.
800

      “fuzzy” binary relation with values in an appropriate set L (a residuated lattice). In the following we

1126
1127
       While the uncertainty produced by our method, and similar approaches, represents an entropy, it
1128   can be hard to interpret in practice. We show that it is possible to post-hoc calibrate the uncertainty
1129   value produced by our approach into a confidence that the greedy answer is correct. We use Platt
1130   scaling (Platt et al., 1999), which trains a logistic regression classifier to map the uncertainty score
1131   for each question to a value in [0, 1] representing the likelihood that the greedy answer is correct.
1132   This classifiers is trained on 20% of the evaluation set, or 80 samples, and we evaluate calibration
1133   on the rest of the test set. The calibration curve for our Conceptual Uncertainty method on TriviaQA
       using Mistral-7B is shown in Figure 10.
