009                                             A BSTRACT
010
011           Large Language Model (LLM)-based agents have shown effectiveness across many
012           applications. However, their use in data science scenarios requiring solving long-
013           term interconnected tasks, dynamic data adjustments and domain expertise remains
014           challenging. Previous approaches primarily focus on individual tasks, making it
015
              difficult to assess the complete data science workflow. Moreover, they struggle
              to handle real-time changes in intermediate data and fail to adapt dynamically
016
              to evolving task dependencies inherent to data science problems. In this paper,
017
              we present Data Interpreter, an LLM-based agent designed to automatically
018           solve various data science problems end-to-end. Our Data Interpreter incorporates
019           two key modules: 1) Hierarchical Graph Modeling, which breaks down complex
020           problems into manageable subproblems, enabling dynamic node generation and
021           graph optimization; and 2) Programmable Node Generation, a technique that
022           refines and verifies each subproblem to iteratively improve code generation results
023           and robustness. Extensive experiments consistently demonstrate the superiority of
024           Data Interpreter. On InfiAgent-DABench, it achieves a 25% performance boost,
025           raising accuracy from 75.9% to 94.9%. For machine learning and open-ended tasks,
026
              it improves performance from 88% to 95%, and from 60% to 97%, respectively.
              Moreover, on the MATH dataset, Data Interpreter achieves remarkable performance
027
              with a 26% improvement compared to state-of-the-art baselines. Code will be open-
028
              sourced upon publication.

070   Furthermore, these methods often struggle to handle real-time changes in intermediate data and adapt
071   dynamically to evolving task dependencies. While recent works (Wu et al., 2023b; Zhang et al.,
072   2023) have improved performance in data-related tasks, they remain inadequate for machine learning
073   or comprehensive data transformation tasks, involving intricate task interdependencies that require
074   continuous updates and dynamic global planning (Zhang et al., 2024c).
075   To address these challenges, we present Data Interpreter, an LLM agent that reframes the data
076   science workflows as a Hierarchical Graph Modeling problem, where interconnected tasks are
077   represented as nodes, and their dependencies as edges within the graph. This structured representation
078   enables dynamic and flexible task management, allowing the system to adjust to evolving data and
079   task requirements in real-time, and thus efficiently manages the complex, interdependent steps of data
080   science. Another core of Data Interpreter is Programmable Node Generation, a key innovation that
081
      automates the real-time generation, refinement, and verification of nodes in the graph. This ensures
      that each subproblem is accurately defined and executed, improving the robustness and precision of
082
      the workflow. Leveraging the coding capabilities of LLMs, the system dynamically synthesizes and
083
      optimizes the graph structure, making it highly adaptable to the demands of complex, evolving data
084   science tasks.
085

      several benchmarks, achieving a 25% performance boost on the public dataset InfiAgent-DABench,

096   LLMs as Data Science Agents Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated expert-level
097   knowledge in machine learning and have made significant progress in automating data science tasks.
098   Early research focused on using LLMs to write code, aiming to simplify complex computations
099
      involved in reasoning processes (Gao et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2022). Subsequent work introduced
      code interpreters that leverage function-calling mechanisms, offering greater flexibility in solving
100
      complex problems (Zhou et al., 2023; Gou et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024a). This interpreter-based
101
      approach has now become a mainstream method for enabling LLMs to handle complex reasoning
102   and scientific tasks (Huang et al., 2023b; Hassan et al., 2023; Qiao et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024b).
103   Recently, Zhang et al. (2023) introduces an LLM-based agent for data analysis, demonstrating
104   capabilities in data processing and exploration within a code-centric framework, but does not evaluate
105   its performance on predictive tasks such as machine learning pipelines. Guo et al. (2024) harness
106   LLMs and case-based reasoning to solve data science tasks, leveraging human expertise to enhance
107   the efficiency of LLM-based agents in data science, which is complementary to our work. Liu et al.
      (2024) uses LLMs to perform hyperparameter tuning to automate machine learning tasks focusing on


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      single task rather than full pipeline construction and evaluation. Therefore, end-to-end evaluation
109   frameworks specifically designed for data science tasks remain insufficiently developed. To address
110   this gap, we propose a unified, general framework specifically designed for data science tasks. Our
111   framework has been rigorously benchmarked across diverse tasks and settings, offering valuable
112   insights into the application and effectiveness of LLMs in data science.
113
114   Enhancing LLM with Tools Recent research has focused on enhancing LLM capabilities by
115   integrating external tools (Schick et al., 2024; Paranjape et al., 2023). Zhuge et al. (2023); Shen et al.
116   (2024) introduced multi-agent systems to tackle multimodal tasks, while Yuan et al. (2023); Liu et al.
117   (2023) proposed frameworks for retrieval and automatic tool selection, eliminating the need to assign
118   tools for specific tasks statically. Recent efforts have increasingly focused on integrating tool-using
119   abilities into a structured pipeline, enabling sophisticated task planning, tool invocation (Wu et al.,
120
      2023a; Shen et al., 2024; Liang et al., 2024). Qian et al. (2023); Yuan et al. (2024) discuss the
      creation and instruction of the tool from code-form or lengthy tool documentation to enhance tool
121
      utilization efficiency. In this paper, we further advance these ideas by enabling LLMs to dynamic
122
      orchestration and combination of multiple tools. Our approach improves practicality by leveraging
123   execution experience, allowing LLMs to select and combine tools as needed independently.
124
125   Graph-Based Planning for LLM Agents Planning is a critical capability of LLM-based agents,
126   focusing on generating logically structured action or thought roadmaps for specific problems (Huang
127   et al., 2024b; Chen et al., 2024). Earlier works like CoT (Wei et al., 2022; Yao et al., 2022)
128   decompose complex tasks into subtasks and perform sequential planning. However, due to the
129   complexity of certain problems, a single plan generated by an LLM-based agent is often insufficient.
130   To address this, ToT (Yao et al., 2024) and GoT (Besta et al., 2023) introduce automatic tree or graph
131   structures that refine node-level LLM prompts, optimizing connectivity to improve performance.
132   Similarly, DSPy (Khattab et al., 2023) abstracts LLM pipelines as text transformation graphs, while
133
      PRODIGY (Huang et al., 2023a) applies graph-based in-context learning and pre-training methods.
      Further, Zhuge et al. (2024) enhance node prompts and agent coordination via graph connectivity
134
      adjustments, and Vierling et al. (2024) develop a learnable model to dynamically generate edges
135
      between agents in a graph, facilitating internal communication. While these planning approaches
136   excel in various domains, they often struggle with multi-step, task-dependent problems commonly
137   encountered in data science. In this paper, we explore the potential of integrating graph structures with
138   LLM-based agents for data science tasks—an area that remains largely untapped despite emerging
139   related work.

      data science problems, defining the task graph and action graph in Section 3.1. Next, we detail the

      3.1   H IERARCHICAL G RAPH M ODELING FOR C OMPLEX TASK D ECOMPOSITION
150
151   Data science problems, particularly those involving machine learning, encompass extensive detailing
152   and long-range workflows, including data pre-processing, feature engineering, and model training.
153   This long-term planning complicates the direct planning of all detailed tasks and coding. Drawing
154   inspiration from the application of hierarchical planning in automated machine learning tasks (Mohr
155   et al., 2018; Mubarak & Koeshidayatullah, 2023), we organize the data science workflow via
156   hierarchical structure, which initially decomposes the intricate data science problem into manageable
157   tasks and further break down each task into specific actions executed through code (see Figure 2).
158   Therefore, solving a data science problem can be formulated as follows: given a task-oriented input
159   x, we seek to apply a series of operators, unified as a function P, to produce an output ŷ = P(x). Our
160   goal is for P to generate solutions that closely approximate or match the anticipated y. However, due
161   to the complexity of P, which may involve various operations and intermediate data, fully automating
      the solution to a task is typically challenging.

      the solving process of P into a series of sub-processes p1 , p2 , p3 , . . . that can be directly solved and
      verified. The primary challenge lies in determining the relationships r = ⟨pi , pj ⟩ ∈ R between these
201
      sub-processes. Our framework represents all subprocesses as nodes within P, ultimately forming a

209   planning (Moerland et al., 2023; Schmidhuber, 2003), which often require a substantial number of

211   of LLMs. This training-free nature allows our method more adaptable and efficient for general task

      decomposition.

226   the project requirement as the goal without relying on pre-defined steps, tasks and relation-
227   ships. As shown in Figure 2, an example workflow decomposed by Data Interpreter for a ma-

248   3.2   TASK GRAPH : I TERATIVE G RAPH R EFINEMENT
249
250   Task Graph Generation and Execution. A key advantage of our approach is its ability to dynami-
251   cally adjust the task graph in response to changing environments, unlike prior methods (Wei et al.,
252   2022; Besta et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2022) and frameworks such as OpenInterpreter (Lucas, 2023)
253   and AutoGen (Wu et al., 2023b), which generate static plans for one-time execution. Our method
254
      introduces iterative graph optimization, allowing it to adapt to a dynamic environment through
      continuous updates.
255
256   As shown in Figure 2, Data Interpreter uses a task graph generator to initialize the task graph as
257   discussed in Section 3.1. Each task is then translated into executable code by the action graph
258   generator, which takes into account the outcomes of prior tasks to ensure contextual consistency. The
259   generation process is detailed in Algorithm 1.
260   To ensure runtime verification and provide real-time feedback during execution, Data Interpreter
261   incorporates a stateful graph executor that manages both execution and debugging using reflection
262   mechanisms (Shinn et al., 2024). Specifically, if the execution encounters exceptions or fails a
263   verification check, the action graph generator dynamically reflects on the execution results, and then
264   regenerates the code to resolve the issue or optimize the output, providing data-driven feedback. This
265   process is collectively conducted by action graph generator and graph executor.
266   Task Graph Refinement. The task graph generator manages tasks, monitors their statuses and
267   dependencies, and dynamically adjusts the task graph by adding, removing, or modifying tasks as
268   needed. Each task is further decomposed into an action graph, which consists of one or several action
269   nodes. Each action graph can be executed and evaluated independently, allowing for granular control
      and flexibility in the execution process.

312   For failed tasks, Data Interpreter regenerates the task graph based on current episodic memory and the
313   execution context, as depicted in Figure 3. Given the task dependencies, the regenerated task graph
314   is sorted topologically and compared to the original using a prefix matching algorithm (Waldvogel,
315   2000) to identify differences in task descriptions. This comparison helps identify divergence points
316   (forks), and the final output includes all unchanged tasks before the fork, along with any new or
317   modified tasks after the fork. This approach allows Data Interpreter to efficiently locate the parent
318   node of the failed task and seamlessly integrate the newly generated task and its subsequent tasks
319   into the original graph. It directly leverages the completed memory of all dependent tasks during
320
      re-execution, avoiding unnecessary code regeneration or redundant executions.
321   By employing continuous monitoring and iterative updates, Data Interpreter avoids the inefficiencies
322   associated with generating all tasks upfront. This dynamic adjustment of both the code and planning
323   levels based on task outcomes enables modifications at varying levels of granularity, significantly
      improving overall efficiency.

      3.3   ACTION G RAPH : P ROGRAMMABLE N ODE G ENERATION
325
326
      Action Node. An action node, as introduced in Section 3.1, represents an executable code snippet
327   that encapsulates the computational logic required for task execution. Each action node can encompass
328   data transformations, function calls, or other relevant operations, making it the fundamental unit
329   of execution within the action graph. It integrates both external functions and operators invoked
330   from various tools, as well as non-tool logic derived from libraries such as Pandas and NumPy. By
331   combining tool-based operations and library functions into a single executable code snippet, action
332   nodes ensure uniform and flexible execution across different tasks.
333   Tool Selection. Effective tool selection and integration, particularly in the context of task-specific
334   requirements, play a crucial role in the success of task execution, as noted in prior research (Qian
335   et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024a; Liu et al., 2023). In Data Interpreter, we leverage
336   task dependencies to enrich the task-specific context, thereby enhancing the decision-making process
337   for tool selection and code generation.
338
      During the execution of each task pi ∈ G, where G represents the task graph, Data Interpreter first
339   retrieves suitable tools before generating the associated code. The task metadata q(pi ), which includes
340   textual information such as task descriptions and types as well as graph-structured task dependencies,
341   is used as a query to retrieve a list of candidate tools from the available toolset T = {t1 , t2 , . . . , tn }.
342   The model ranks these tools by evaluating their semantic relevance to the task using their functionality
343   schemas S(tj ). This produces a ranked list R(pi , T ) = {r1 , r2 , . . . , rn }, where each tool tj is ranked
344   according to its suitability for the task. From this ranked list, Data Interpreter selects the top-k tools,
345   denoted as Tk (pi ) ⊆ T , to assist in executing task pi . Importantly, Data Interpreter can bypass tool
346   selection when no suitable tools are found, relying solely on the LLM to generate appropriate code.
347
      This flexibility ensures that the system can adapt to a wide range of task requirements without being
      restricted by tool availability.
348
349   Programmable Node Generation.               Unlike conventional LLM-based agent frameworks that
350   invoke tools through isolated function calls, Data Interpreter generates comprehensive code snippets
351   that seamlessly integrate selected tools within the broader logic of the task. Based on the tools
352   selected from Tk (pi ), Data Interpreter dynamically incorporates them into the code, aligning their
353
      functionality with the specific task context. This approach allows tools to function in the same manner
      as standard libraries like NumPy, enabling adaptive tool usage that adjusts to evolving task conditions.
354
      For example, in the deployment workflow, the CatCount tool dynamically utilizes its fit and transform
355
      functions depending on the task context, as illustrated in Figure 6 in the Appendix.
356
357   Our programmable node generation approach not only ensures that tools are used in a context-aware
358   and task-specific manner but also facilitates the seamless integration of domain-specific expertise. By
359
      allowing real-time adaptability and optimization of tool usage, Data Interpreter significantly enhances
      the efficiency and robustness of task execution, representing a novel contribution to LLM-based task
360
      automation.

531
      In this work, we present the Data Interpreter, an LLM-based agent designed to tackle data science
532
      challenges via hierarchical graph representation. Our framework continuously monitors data changes
533
      and adapts to dynamic environments through iterative task refinement and graph optimization. It
534   enhances data analysis and machine learning performance, and improves reasoning capabilities
535   through hierarchical decomposition, fine-grained execution, validation, and iterative modifications.
536   Combined with the LLM’s planning and coding abilities, this approach effectively solves tasks
537   requiring complex multi-step reasoning. Extensive evaluations demonstrate that our Data Interpreter
538   outperforms various open-source frameworks in machine learning tasks, mathematical problems, and
539   real-world applications, marking a significant advancement in the capabilities of LLM-based agents
      for data science.

      A    L IMITATIONS
757
758
      Insufficient diversity and complexity. Our novel framework Data Interpreter outperforms other
759   open-source frameworks on machine learning problems, yet are limited to entry-level Kaggle datasets
760   and benchmarked against the capabilities of a junior human data scientist. These datasets are relatively
761   small (under 500MB), with a limited number of columns (in the hundreds) and rows (in the tens of
762   thousands), and mainly involve classification and regression tasks (as described in Appendix F.2).
763   However, we have not yet evaluated our Data Interpreter on more challenging datasets involving
764   large-scale data or complex tasks such as time series analysis, multi-label classification, or multi-
765   table problems. In our future work, we plan to expand our dataset collection to include these types
766   of problems to thoroughly evaluate our framework’s performance and capabilities. Precise self-
767
      improvement. Human data scientists usually perform multiple experiments on a dataset, focusing on
      pipeline optimization and hyperparameter tuning Liu et al. (2021); Hutter et al. (2019). Our Data
768
      Interpreter integrates experience to enhance the node generation quality. The experience primarily
769
      involves tracking the progress of tasks and code. However, it does not use numerical feedback
770   from multiple experiences to develop and refine specific strategies, such as increasing the learning
771   rate or using an ensemble technique, to improve the performance continuously for a given dataset,
772   thus lacking the capability for automatic self-improvement. In the future, we aim to address this
773   limitation by developing mechanisms that allow our model to conduct multiple experiments and
774   derive insights from the numerical feedback for a given dataset on its own. DAG constraint detection
775   mechanism. Our current implementation does not include an explicit DAG constraint detection
776   mechanism, we rely on the LLM’s inherent ability to avoid cycles during task planning, as observed
777   in our experiments. However, such mechanisms could enhance robustness in handling less structured
778
      domains or highly complex dependencies. Incorporating cycle detection and resolution strategies in
      future iterations would ensure improved reliability and adaptability across diverse applications. Full-
779
      scale evaluation on mathematical problems. For the MATH problem, our experiments are limited
780
      to level-5 problems, primarily due to the budget constraints, we will explore more cost-effective
781   strategies for evaluating our Data Interpreter on a wider range of mathematical problems in future
782   studies.

       D.2   E VALUATION METRICS
1081
1082   In the MATH benchmark Hendrycks et al. (2021), accuracy served as the chosen evaluation metric,
1083   aligning with the setting proposed in Wu et al. (2023c); Hendrycks et al. (2021).
1084
       For the ML-Benchmark, three evaluation metrics were utilized: completion rate (CR), normalized
1085
       performance score (NPS), and comprehensive score (CS). These metrics provided comprehensive
1086   insights into the model’s performance and were defined as follows:
1087
1088   Completion rate (CR): In the task requirements description, there were T steps, and the task comple-
1089
       tion status of each step was denoted by a score st , with a maximum score smax of 2 and a minimum
       score smin of 0. The task completion status categories were defined as follows: missing (score of 0),
1090
       fail (score of 0), success - non-compliant (score of 1), success-compliant (score of 2), and optional
1091
       step (not involved in scoring). To measure the completion level, we proposed a completion ratio
1092   where the numerator was the sum of scores st for each step, and the denominator was the sum of the
1093   maximum possible scores for all steps (smax × T ):
1094                                                     PT
1095                                                         t=1 st
                                                CR =                .                                    (3)
1096                                                    smax × T
1097
1098   Normalized performance score (NPS): In our ML-Benchmark, each task was associated with its
1099   evaluation metric, which may vary between tasks, including metrics such as accuracy, F1, AUC and
1100   RMSLE, etc. For metrics such as accuracy, F1, and AUC, we presented the raw values to facilitate
1101
       comparison across identical data tasks. We normalize all performance values s:
                                           
1102                                        1
1103
                                                   , if s is smaller the better
                                  NPS = 1 + s                                                        (4)
1104                                       s,       otherwise.
1105
1106   This transformation ensured that loss-based metrics like RMSLE are scaled from 0 to 1, with higher
1107
       normalized performance score values indicating better performance.
1108   Comprehensive score (CS): To simultaneously assess both the completion rate of task requirements
1109   and the performance of generated machine learning models, we calculated the weighted sum of CR
1110   and NPS as follows:
1111
                                         CS = 0.5 × CR + 0.5 × NPS.                                     (5)
1112

1114   and directly equate CS to CR.
