011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            In this paper, we develop SE3Set, an SE(3) equivariant hypergraph neural network
014            architecture tailored for advanced molecular representation learning. Hypergraphs
015            are not merely an extension of traditional graphs; they are pivotal for modeling
016            high-order relationships, a capability that conventional equivariant graph-based
017            methods lack due to their inherent limitations in representing intricate many-body
018
               interactions. To achieve this, we first construct hypergraphs via proposing a new
               fragmentation method that considers both chemical and three-dimensional spatial
019
               information of molecular system. We then design SE3Set, which incorporates
020
               equivariance into the hypergragh neural network. This ensures that the learned
021            molecular representations are invariant to spatial transformations, thereby provid-
022            ing robustness essential for accurate prediction of molecular properties. SE3Set has
023            shown performance on par with state-of-the-art (SOTA) models for small molecule
024            datasets like QM9 and MD17. It excels on the MD22 dataset, achieving a notable
025            improvement of approximately 20% in accuracy across all molecules, which high-
026            lights the prevalence of complex many-body interactions in larger molecules. This
027            exceptional performance of SE3Set across diverse molecular structures underscores
028            its transformative potential in computational chemistry, offering a route to more
029
               accurate and physically nuanced modeling.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Molecular representation (Mathews & Chaffee, 2012; David et al., 2020; Wigh et al., 2022) is pivotal
035   for cheminformatics (Fourches et al., 2010), impacting the prediction of molecular properties in drug
036   discovery and material science. Traditional descriptors like fingerprints capture basic structural and
037   energetic aspects of molecules by considering mainly one- and two-body interactions. However,
038   they often miss complex electronic correlations and collective behaviors important for understanding
039
      phenomena such as chemical reactivity and protein folding. To address this, advanced methods that
      include many-body interactions are crucial for a more comprehensive molecular characterization.
040
      These methods enhance the predictive capabilities of computational models by more accurately
041
      reflecting the intricate dynamics and properties of molecules, which are essential for a deeper
042   understanding of their functionality and reactivity in cheminformatics.
043
044   Graph neural networks (GNNs) (Zhou et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2020) a foundational tool for representing
045
      structured data in molecular sciences molecular sciences with atoms as nodes and chemical bonds
      as edges, respectively. GNN models excel in tasks ranging from property prediction to reaction
046
      simulation (Do et al., 2019; Xiong et al., 2021; Reiser et al., 2022). GNNs can capture higher-
047
      order molecular interactions through message passing (Gilmer et al., 2017) but face overfitting
048   and inefficiency challenges (Godwin et al., 2021; Rusch et al., 2023). Architectural improvements
049   in GNNs facilitate the modeling of complex interactions, overcoming some limitations of deep
050   networks (Gasteiger et al., 2019; Schütt et al., 2021; Batzner et al., 2022). Advances demonstrate
051   the potential of architectural enhancements in GNNs to represent complex interactions (Gasteiger
052   et al., 2020; 2021; Thölke & De Fabritiis, 2021; Batatia et al., 2022; Musaelian et al., 2023; Wang
053   et al., 2024), but efficiently integrating many-body interactions into these networks is an ongoing
      challenge (Wang et al., 2023).

054
      To address the complexities of many-body interactions in molecular systems, hypergraphs offer a
055   compelling alternative to complex GNN architectures. Hypergraphs, with hyperedges connecting
056   multiple vertices, can naturally represent many-body phenomena like electronic delocalization and
057   collective vibrations. This allows for a more accurate modeling of molecular intricacies beyond the
058   limitations of traditional graphs. Integrating hypergraphs with machine learning, particularly through
059   Hypergraph Neural Networks (HGNNs), is an emerging research area. HGNNs manage the flow of
060   information across hyperedges, capturing complex multi-atom interactions and enriching molecular
061   representations. This technique promises to balance model expressiveness with computational
062   efficiency. By innately encoding many-body interactions, HGNNs stand to significantly advance
063
      cheminformatics, offering a new approach to molecular property prediction and simulation that
      resonates with the actual behavior of chemical systems.
064
065   In this work, we introduce SE3Set, an innovative approach that enhances traditional GNNs by
066   exploiting hypergraphs for modeling many-body interactions, while ensuring SE(3) equivariant
067   representations that remain consistent regardless of molecular orientation. Our key contributions are:
068
             • A new fragmentation method for hypergraph construction that seamlessly integrates 2D
069
               chemical and 3D spatial information, enriching the molecular structure representation.
070
071          • The deployment of hypergraph neural networks to capture many-body interactions, providing
072
               a deeper insight into molecular behavior that surpasses conventional pairwise modeling.
073          • The incorporation of SE(3) equivariance within our hypergraph framework, guaranteeing
074            orientation-independent molecular representations.
075          • SE3Set underwent a comprehensive benchmarking process, exhibiting comparable outcomes
076            to state-of-the-art (SOTA) models on small molecule datasets QM9 and MD17. It demon-
077            strated exceptional performance on the larger molecule dataset MD22, where higher-order
078            interactions are more evident, surpassing SOTA models with a significant reduction in mean
079            absolute errors (MAEs) by an average of roughly 20%. This confirms SE3Set’s efficacy in
080
               capturing the complexity of molecular representations.
081
      These advances establish SE3Set as a formidable tool for molecular representation learning, with
082   implications for computational chemistry and beyond.

      2.1   G RAPH NEURAL NETWORKS
088
      Message passing neural networks (MPNNs), a class of graph neural networks, are essential for
089   learning node features by transmitting information along graph edges, a process crucial for interpreting
090   structured data like molecules (Gilmer et al., 2017). Equivariant GNNs are especially important
091   for molecular modeling. They adopt either group representation methods, aligning architectures
092   to symmetry groups for improved interaction modeling (Thomas et al., 2018; Anderson et al.,
093   2019; Fuchs et al., 2020; Batzner et al., 2022; Liao & Smidt, 2022; Liao et al., 2023; Musaelian
094   et al., 2023), or direction-based methods that incorporate spatial information for accurate molecular
095   representations (Schütt et al., 2017; Kindermans & Müller, 2018; Coors et al., 2018; Gasteiger et al.,
096   2019; 2020; Schütt et al., 2021; Thölke & De Fabritiis, 2021; Gasteiger et al., 2021; Wang et al.,
097
      2022; Du et al., 2024; Aykent & Xia, 2024; Wang et al., 2024) and have been engineered to handle
      intricate up to five-body interactions (Wang et al., 2023).
098
099
100
      2.2   H YPERGRAPH NEURAL NETWORKS
101
      Hypergraph Neural Networks (HGNNs) enhance GNNs by incorporating multi-node hyperedges,
102   better capturing complexity in data from various domains. They advance GNNs’ binary interactions
103   with methods like clique expansion for compatibility with existing algorithms (Agarwal et al., 2005;
104   Zhou et al., 2006) and employ tensor techniques for improved hypergraph-based feature learning (Li
105   et al., 2013; Pearson & Zhang, 2014; Benson et al., 2017; Chien et al., 2021a; Tudisco et al., 2021).
106   While equivariant HGNNs adeptly handle node permutations, preserving data symmetries (Kim et al.,
107   2021; 2022a), they often miss 3D spatial transformations, crucial for physical system modeling. In
      computational chemistry, hypergraph algorithms simulate complex behaviors and optimize molecules

108
      through hypergraph grammar (Cui et al., 2023; Tavakoli et al., 2022; Kajino, 2019), providing
109   multidimensional insights into molecular structures (Nachmani & Wolf, 2020; Chen et al., 2021;
110   Chen & Schwaller, 2023). Despite their promise, these methods still face hurdles in integrating
111   spatial information effectively.
112
113   2.3   F RAGMENTATION METHODS
114
115   Fragmentation methods break down complex molecules for simpler ab initio QM computations of
116   properties, later combining these for a holistic view (Gordon et al., 2012; Collins & Bettens, 2015).
117   Leveraging the localized nature of chemical reactions, these techniques aim for scalable algorithms
118   suitable for large molecule analysis. While instrumental in computational pretraining (Du et al.,
119
      2021; Kim et al., 2022b; Luong & Singh, 2023), they typically neglect the fusion of 2D structural
      with 3D spatial data. Hence, we advocate for a refined fragmentation approach that merges chemical
120
      properties with spatial context, potentially advancing hypergraph-based chemical modeling.

126
      Consider a function L that maps inputs from space X to outputs in space Y. L is called G-equivariant
127
      if it preserves the symmetry of a group G across mappings, meaning for each g ∈ G, we have:
128
129                                        L ◦ DX (g) = DY (g) ◦ L,                                    (1)
130
      where DX represents the group G’s action on X . This ensures that the function L reflects changes
131
      made to inputs by G in its outputs.

      3.2   H YPERGRAPH
134
135   Hypergraphs elegantly capture the essence of higher-order interactions among multiple entities,
136   making them an invaluable tool for representing complex relational data. Let G = (V, E) be a
137   hypergraph with N vertices and M hyperedges, where V represents a set of nodes and E is a set
138   of hyperedges. Distinguishing itself from a traditional graph, a hyperedge can encompass multiple
139   nodes, not limited to two, i.e. each hyperedge e ∈ E is a non-empty subset of V .

188   4.1 F RAGMENTATION ALGORITHM
189
      To harness the power of hypergraph neural networks for molecular representations, we need to
190
      map molecules onto hypergraph structures through a refined fragmentation algorithm. Our strategy
191
      intertwines molecular topology and spatial geometry to create hyperedges that capture groups of
192   atoms, reflecting their functional and spatial characteristics. In crafting this fragmentation approach
193   for hypergraph-based molecular representation, the methodology must adhere to a set of fundamental
194   principles:
195
196            1. The design should merge topological chemistry with 3D structural data into a unified
197               hypergraph representation, ensuring hyperedges accurately embody the molecule’s chemical
198
                  and spatial properties.
199            2. Controlling fragment size is vital for the SE3Set model to balance capturing meaning-
200               ful interactions and computational efficiency. Optimal fragment sizes are key for model
201
                  performance and learning capabilities.
202            3. The fragmentation could only selectively break single bonds and must maintain functional
203               groups and ring integrity to preserving key chemical information critical for the molecule’s
204               properties and behavior.
205            4. Fragment overlap is essential to maintain functional group effects on local charge distribution
206               and to ensure hyperedge interaction within the SE3Set model for improved molecular
207               learning.
208
      Before delving deeper into the specifics of our fragmentation method, it’s important to establish a
209   foundational understanding through key definitions and concepts,
210
      Definition 4.1. The bond order represents the multiplicity or the number of shared electron pairs that
211
      constitute a covalent bond between two atoms.
212
213   The bond order matrix B is an N × N representation of bond strength between atoms in a molecule,
214   with higher bond order values indicating stronger bonds. This symmetric matrix (Bij = Bji ) is
215   crucial for studying molecular structure and reactivity, capturing bond nuances including delocalized
      and resonance bonds in computational chemistry.


                                                             4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      Our fragmentation algorithm improves molecular representations by combining bond order, functional
217   groups, and substructures, including SMARTS-identified smaller rings and merged adjacent groups.
218   Overcoming the drawbacks of non-overlapping fragmentation, it uses 3D spatial data and allows
219   overlaps, preserving local effects for precise charge distribution and enhancing hypergraph neural
220   network learning of molecular interactions.
221   Definition 4.2. A molecular fragment, denoted as F, is defined as a specific subset of atoms within
222   a molecule, characterized by being a cohesive assembly of predefined substructures linked in a
223   sequential concatenation.
224
225   Our fragmentation method meticulously dissects a given molecule into meaningful subsets of atoms,
226   and this process unfolds through four steps (corresponding to the pipeline in Fig. 1):
227
228           1. Pre-processing by analyzing the molecule’s bond order matrix to mask high-order bonds
229
                 and those within functional groups or rings, and merging adjacent functional groups for a
                 streamlined structural representation.
230
231           2. Core substructures are delineated from the remaining bonds using a Breadth-First Search
232              algorithm, establishing the basic units of the molecular framework.
233           3. These substructures are then aggregated into larger molecular fragments according to
234              predefined rules that maintain a minimum atom count within each fragment. This step could
235              be optional.
236           4. To enhance fragment connectivity, we expand each by incorporating adjacent groups, using
237              interaction strength metrics based on interatomic distances to guide this process. Here we
238              set the cutoff value denoted as cw of an interaction strength metrics to intercept the extended
239              fragment.
240
241   Furthermore, step 4 leads to a substantial computational overhead for hypergraph neural networks
242
      when processing larger molecular systems. To enhance the efficiency of our model for such expansive
      molecular systems, we introduce a revised strategy for step 4:
243
244
            4* For each atom i, identify the neighboring atoms Ni that fall within a specified radial cutoff
245            rc . A fragment F is considered to be adjacent to atom i if there is an overlap of at least one
246            atom between F and Ni . For ease of reference, the set of fragments adjacent to atom i is
247            represented as NiF , which implies that NiF = {F|F ∩ Ni ̸= ∅}.
248
249   We designate the application of step 4 as an explicit overlap and the application of step 4* as
250   an implicit overlap. These approaches introduce nuanced variations in the mathematical expressions
251   of our model, as reflected in the Eq. 8, Eq. 9, and Eq. 16. For the detailed step-by-step methodology,
252   please refer to Appendix A.

405   The SE3Set model employs node features to generate predictions, using a feed-forward network to

506   6   C ONCLUSION
507
508   In conclusion, this study demonstrate the efficacy of SE3Set, a cutting-edge hypergraph neural
509   network architecture, in the realm of molecular representation learning. By meticulously crafting a
510   fragmentation method that coalesces two-dimensional chemical knowledge with three-dimensional
511
      spatial information, we establish a robust foundation for constructing hypergraphs that faithfully
      capture the complex nature of molecular structures. The SE3Set architecture, drawing inspiration
512
      from the AllSet framework and the Equiformer, adeptly processing these hypergraphs and preserving
513
      the essential invariances and symmetries. SE3Set demonstrates performance comparable to SOTA
514   models in small molecular systems and significantly outperforms SOTA models in large molecular
515   systems where higher-order many-body interactions are pronounced. The results of our research
516   affirm the potential of SE3Set to model high-order many-body interactions, providing a powerful tool
517   for molecular representation.

      A    D ETAILS OF FRAGMENTATION STEPS
760
      Based on the design principles in Sec. 4.1, the detailed step-by-step methodology of explicit overlap
761
      fragmentation method is shown as follows,
762
763        1. The pre-processing step begins by analyzing the given molecule through its bond order
764           matrix, denoted as B. Identify and mask bonds that are part of functional groups or rings,
765           as well as those with a bond order of Bij ⩾ 2. Functional groups are then identified
766           using predefined SMARTS patterns for accurate matching. To achieve a more generalized
767           representation of functional groups, topologically adjacent functional groups are merged into
768           a single entity. This aggregation allows to focus on specific subfunctional groups that are
769           of particular interest, simplifying the complexity of the molecular structure for subsequent
770
              analysis.
771        2. Following the masking of selected bonds, the Breadth-First Search (BFS) algorithm is
772           employed to reconstruct the substructures, denoted as {S}, from the remaining unmasked
773           bonds. These groups represent the core structural units of the molecule as discussed at the
774
              outset of this section.
775        3. Consolidate the previously identified groups {S} into larger molecular fragments, applying
776           a set of predefined rules to guide the merging process. These rules are meticulously designed
777
              to ensure that each resulting fragment, now denoted as {F}, contains at least a minimum
              specified number of atoms. For a comprehensive understanding of the merging criteria, one
778
              can refer to the detailed rules outlined in C.
779
780        4. Extend each fragment {F} by incorporating adjacent groups from {S} to enrich the connec-
781
              tivity between molecular fragments, thus intentionally creating regions of overlap among
              the fragments. This expansion is controlled by a cutoff threshold, denoted as cw , which
782
              is typically a function based on interatomic distances. the fragment bond order (Lendvay,

784           interaction strength between a fragment Fi and an adjacent substructure Sj . This method
785           reflects the interaction strength based on the proximity of atoms in different fragments,

842   C    M ERGE PROCESS OF FRAGMENTATION
843
844
      During the third step of our fragmentation method, we introduce a strategy to enlarge substructures,
845   ensuring that each initial fragment contains at least nmin atoms, with nmin being a predefined integer.
846   To maintain permutation invariance for a molecule, we incorporate weights, Wf s , to guide the
847   sequence of merging. The process is outlined in the pseudocode (Algorithm 1). The calculation
848   of W is based on either Eq. 18 or Eq. 19. By considering the sum of bond orders to other groups,
849   we assess each group’s centrality. The groups are then ordered first by the number of atoms they
850   contain, followed by the summation of their bond orders, ensuring that the fragmentation merge
851   process is permutation invariant when following this specified sequence. The algorithm then assists
852   smaller groups in merging with others to achieve a size of at least nmin atoms. Initially, we consider
853
      topologically adjacent groups with the fewest atoms. If a target group lacks topological neighbors, we
      proceed to merge based on the bond order from W . We introduce a threshold cis that allows a group
854
      to remain isolated if it is significantly distant from others. It should be noted that isolated groups may
855
      not meet the minimum atom number requirement; however, they could be further expanded in the
856   subsequent fragmentation step, depending on the chosen thresholds for cis and cw (refer to Sec. 4.1).
857   Overall, this algorithm ensures a permutation invariant merging process.

1099   overlap method, if the system has m fragments with average nexp atoms in one fragment, the V2E
1100   module’s complexity will be O(mn2exp ) according to the previous analysis. For E2V module, the
1101   number of fragments to which each atom belongs on average can be represented as mnexp /N . Thus
1102   the complexity will be O(mnexp ) when considering N atoms.
1103   Similarly, for the implicit overlap method, the complexity of V2E module will be O(nF nimp N ),
1104   where nF represents the average number of neighbor fragments within cutoff per atom and nimp
1105   denotes the average atoms in one fragment. And the complexity of E2V module is O(nF N ).
1106
       Compare to the explicit overlap method, the implicit overlap method has lower computational
1107
       complexity. As we can approximate that nF nimp ≈ nexp (overlap degree is similar) and determine
1108
       mnimp = N (implicit overlap method has same fragment number m but without extra count of atoms
1109   in nimp ). On the other hand we can determine mnexp ⩾ N as the explicit overlap will count atoms
1110   more times. Hence the complexity of explicit overlap method has higher complexity than implicit
1111   overlap on V2E Module according to the deduction below
1112
                                           mn2exp ⩾ N nexp ≈ nF nimp N                                           (40)
1113
1114                                        mnexp ≈ mnF nimp ≈ nF N                                              (41)
