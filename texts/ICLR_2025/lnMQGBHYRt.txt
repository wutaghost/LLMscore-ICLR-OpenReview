009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Among explainability techniques, SHAP stands out as one of the most popular, but
012            often overlooks the causal structure of the problem. While do-SHAP uses inter-
013            ventional causal queries, its reliance on estimands hinders scalability. To address
014            this problem, we propose employing estimand-agnostic Causal Inference, which
015            allows for the estimation of any identifiable query with a single model, making
016
               do-SHAP feasible on arbitrarily complex graphs. We also develop a novel algo-
               rithm to significantly accelerate its computation at a negligible cost with a marked
017
               improvement in computational speed, as well as a method to explain inaccessible
018
               Data Generating Processes. We validate our approach on two real-world datasets,
019            highlighting its potential in obtaining reliable explanations.

024   The widespread adoption of Machine Learning (ML) systems has
025
      raised concerns about their limitations: models can replicate human
      biases (Angwin et al., 2016), base their outcomes on spurious cor-               E
026
      relations (Neuhaus et al., 2023), or be vulnerable to malicious ad-
027
      versarial attacks (Szegedy et al., 2014). Since most of these systems
028   are black-boxes, there is an ever-increasing need for explainability
029
                                                                             A         S         Y
      techniques to make sense of the model. This is especially relevant
030   w.r.t. fairness (i.e., protecting certain groups against discrimina-
031   tion), the right to explanation (European Commission, 2016) (e.g., Figure 1: Salary causal graph.
032   “What would I need to do for my loan to be approved?”), debug-
033   ging, auditing, and fostering user trust in the system.
034   As a response, the field of explainability has steadily gained traction, resulting in several approaches
035   (Zhang et al., 2021) to explain model predictions. Among them, the Shapley value (SV, or SHAP)
036   (Štrumbelj & Kononenko, 2014) is one of the most popular, since it is the only attribution strategy
037   fulfilling a set of axioms that correspond to human intuition (see Appendix A). SVs are derived from
038   a value function ν, used to measure the effect of a subset (coalition) S of features X when applied
039   on the model’s prediction. Different definitions of ν result in different kinds of Shapley values, the
040   most common being marginal and conditional SHAP (Chen et al., 2023).
041
      However, both of these options ignore the causal structure underlying the data; for instance, Fig-
042   ure 1 represents the salary Y of an employee of age A with a certain education level E and seniority
043   level S. Let f be a ML model f (X) ≈ E [Y | X], learning Y given inputs X = {A, E, S}. Consider
044   ν({E}). In marginal SHAP, ν assigns values {E = e} and marginalizes the complementary set
045   (a, s) ∼ P (A, S) regardless of how the coalition’s values causally affect them (E → S). Condi-
046   tional SHAP does consider these effects, but conditionally, (a, s) ∼ P (A, S | E = e), producing
047   anti-causal effects to A (i.e., we cannot change age by granting them a degree). Please refer to
048   Appendix G for an extended discussion on this example.
049   Several works (Frye et al., 2020; Heskes et al., 2020; Lauritzen & Richardson, 2002; Janzing et al.,
050   2020) discuss the limitations of non-causal SHAP and propose approaches with a causal interpreta-
051   tion under certain limitations, finally resulting in do-SHAP (Jung et al., 2022), whose value function
052   is defined as ν(S) = E [f (X) | do(S = s)], where do(S = s) represents a causal intervention on
053   S with values s. Thanks to Causal Inference, we can transform this query into a probabilistic for-
      mula (the estimand) only containing terms from the observational distribution P (X); hence, it is


                                                         1

054
      possible to train ML models on each of these terms and apply them back into the formula to ob-
055   tain an estimation for the query. The main drawback of estimand-based estimation is that do-SVs
056   require computing 2|X| causal queries, one for each subset S ⊆ X, each with a different estimand
057   formula and several ML models to estimate its terms, making it infeasible for complex graphs. In
058   fact, do-SHAP’s authors stated they “are not aware of any general causal effect estimators suitable
059   for estimating the expression”.
060
      Here lies our first contribution: by employing the estimand-agnostic approach (Parafita & Vitrià,
061
      2022) —based on Structural Causal Models (SCMs)—, we can compute any of the required causal
062   effects with a single model. We do so by following a general procedure instead of query-specific
063   estimands, thereby enabling the computation of do-SVs in a general, scalable way; see Sections 3.2
064   and 4.1. Secondly, we demonstrate new do-SHAP properties that result in the novel Frontier-
065   Reducibility Algorithm (FRA), which optimizes do-SHAP significantly at virtually no cost; see
066   Section 4.2. Next, we devise a explainability strategy with do-SVs, not only for accessible ML sys-
067   tems, but also for natural, inaccessible Data Generating Processes; see Section 4.3. Finally, we test
068   the estimation capabilities of the estimand-agnostic approach on do-SVs, demonstrate the speedup
069   of FRA, and showcase the application of these techniques on two real-world datasets to demonstrate
070
      the power of do-SHAP in providing reliable explanations; see Section 5 and Appendix F. We include
      an Impact Statement of our work in Appendix I.

075   2   R ELATED WORK
076
077
078
      Among many different kinds of explainability techniques (see (Zhang et al., 2021) for a recent
079   survey), we are particularly interested in Shapley values (Lundberg & Lee, 2017), which provide
080   an attribution for each input feature of the system to explain. There is a vast literature on SHAP
081   estimation, discussing the choice of value function ν and tractable estimation strategies for the actual
082   Shapley value (e.g., permutation sampling, adaptive sampling or model-specific strategies); refer to
083   (Chen et al., 2023) for an extensive survey on the topic.
084
      Our main interest is in SHAP approaches that leverage the underlying causal structure of the data.
085   Asymmetric Shapley values (Frye et al., 2020) employ a topological order of the graph to restrict
086   which permutations are considered in the computation of Conditional SHAP, thereby granting more
087   attribution to ancestors of other features. Causal Shapley values (Heskes et al., 2020) properly
088   considers the impact of causal interventions on Shapley attributions, but assumes a partial causal
089   ordering of the graph to define causal chain graphs (Lauritzen & Richardson, 2002) in order to avoid
090   the impact of causal confounders on identifiability. do-SHAP (Jung et al., 2022) does require a
091   full causal graph, but provides a full method to compute attribution on all variables, as long as an
092   estimand can be found for every causal query. Finally, in a different direction, Shapley flow (Wang
093
      et al., 2021) studies causal attributions on the causal graph’s edges instead of its nodes/variables.
094   In order to avoid do-SHAP’s scalability problems, we propose estimand-agnostic methods, which
095   train SCMs modelling the data distribution and estimate causal queries. This approach is explored
096   in the Neural Causal Models framework (Xia et al., 2021). In this line, recent contributions em-
097   ploy Deep Learning for SCM modeling: CausalGAN (Kocaoglu et al., 2018) uses Generative Ad-
098   versarial Networks (Goodfellow et al., 2020) to model images in an SCM containing descriptive
099
      factors of the image; Parafita & Vitrià (Parafita & Vitrià, 2019) propose the Distributional Causal
      Node as a way to model mixed-type distributions (i.e., with discrete and continuous random vari-
100
      ables) and expand their framework with Deep Causal Graphs (Parafita & Vitrià, 2022); Pawlowski et
101
      al. (Pawlowski et al., 2020) propose Normalizing Flows (Papamakarios et al., 2021) and Variational
102   AutoEncoders (Kingma & Welling, 2014) for SCMs with medical image nodes; and Diffusion-based
103   Causal Models (Chao et al., 2023) uses Diffusion Models (Ho et al., 2020) to train their SCMs on
104   high-dimensional data. A promising alternative models SCMs not node by node as all previous
105   works, but the graph as a whole with a single function of its noise signals, thereby avoiding error
106   propagation when sampling. Two of these approaches are VACA (Sánchez-Martın et al., 2022),
107   which uses Graph Neural Networks (Zhou et al., 2020), and Causal Normalizing Flows (Javaloy
      et al., 2024), with a single Normalizing Flow for the whole graph.

175

176

177   θ so that the associated distribution PMθ (V) = P(V) (in an infinite data setting). If that is the case,

179   can be estimated as if we were employing the underlying SCM M, without ever using the estimand.
180

181
      formula of the estimand, it depends exclusively on observational terms resulting from the joint dis-
182
      tribution PM (V), which we assume is correctly represented by our trained proxy Mθ . Therefore,
183   as long as we derive its value from the distribution entailed by the proxy, we will necessarily ar-
184   rive at the same result as with M; otherwise, PMθ (V) ̸= PM (V). In other words, even though
185   its latent priors and functional assignments may be different, we can still compute the causal query
186   through the proxy SCM because there is an estimand for Q in GM . Hence, this results in an al-
187   ternative approach for causal query estimation, the estimand-agnostic approach (Parafita & Vitrià,
188   2022): define a trainable SCM M′ with the underlying SCM G, train it to learn the observational
189   distribution PM (V) and compute any identifiable queries from that single model using the SCM’s
190   procedures, not the specific estimand for each query. This will become essential for the computation
191
      of do-Shapley values.

302   4.3   DO -S HAPLEY EXPLANATIONS
303
304   So far, we have been talking about do-SHAP values w.r.t. a variable Y ∈ V in a certain SCM M, but
305   there are two use cases to consider in practice: either we want to explain a ML model that models Y
306   given some inputs Z ⊆ V \ {Y } or we want to explain the original variable Y directly.
307   If we want to explain a ML model f (Z) := E [Y | Z], we can replace Y with Y ′ := f (Z) (EY will
308   have no effect on fY ′ since f is deterministic) and then work on the projected SCM M[An(Y ′ )]
309   considering P aY = Z. Note that this subgraph may contain variables other than those in Z, since
310   any X ∈ X \ Z may have an effect on some Z ∈ Z, and are therefore ancestors of Y . With this
311   SCM, we can apply estimand-agnostic procedures to estimate do-SHAP. We exemplify this case in
312   the experiment in Section 5.1.

512   6    C ONCLUSION
513
514   In this work, we have introduced a practical and scalable method to estimate do-SVs using the
515   estimand-agnostic approach, with which we can estimate any identifiable query—in particular, do-
516   SVs—using general procedures agnostic to the query’s estimand. This flexibility is essential to make
517   these techniques accessible to practitioners, who may not necessarily be experts in Causal Inference.
518   We have tested our approach on multiple SCM architectures, showcasing the relationship between
519   distribution modeling and do-SHAP estimation performance, which paves the way for future re-
520   search. We have demonstrated several do-SHAP properties along with the proposal of the Frontier-
521
      Reducibility Algorithm to speed up do-SHAP significantly. Finally, we have applied our method on
      two real-world datasets (see Appendix F) showcasing the applicability of these techniques to obtain
522
      reliable explanations, either from a ML model or an inaccessible DGP.
523
524   Further work could propose new SCM architectures to better model the data distribution, along with
525   more efficient estimators (ideally with doubly-robust guarantees) for the causal queries underlying
526   do-SHAP. A general graphical criterion for do-SV identifiability is also a worthwhile new direction.
527
      Finally, do-SVs are based on interventional queries, but these are inherently population-based mea-
      sures, and therefore not really appropriate for individual, local explanations; alternative definitions
528
      of the value function could try to offer causal local explanations.

767   C.1   d- SEPARABILITY AND do- CALCULUS
768
769   In the following, we will define the concept of d-separability, its connection to independence, and
770   the three rules of do-calculus. Please refer to (Pearl, 2009) for more details.
771
      Definition C.1. d-separability.
772
      Given a DAG G = (V, E), a path p is d-separated (blocked) by a set Z ⊆ V (possibly empty) if and
773
      only if either is true:
774
775
            1. p contains a chain A → B → C or a fork A ← B → C such that B is in Z.
776
777         2. p contains a collider A → B ← C such that no descendant of B (including B) is in Z.
778
779   Given disjoint sets X, Y, Z ⊆ V, we say that Z d-separates X from Y in G if Z d-separates every path
780   p from a node X ∈ X to a node Y ∈ Y. We denote this by (X ⊥    ⊥ Y | Z)G .
781
782
      Definition C.2. Markov Compatibility.
      We say that a distribution P(V) on a set of variables
                                                      Q V = (V1 , · · · , VK ) is (Markov) compatible
783
      with a DAG G with V as vertices in G if P (V) = k∈[K] P(Vk | P aG (Vk )).
784
785   Theorem C.3. Independence and d-separability.
786   Given an SCM M = (V, W, P, F) compatible with a DAG GM and disjoint sets X, Y, Z ⊆ V, if
787   (X ⊥⊥ Y | Z)GM then (X ⊥    ⊥ Y | Z)P . Conversely, if (X ̸⊥ ⊥ Y | Z)GM , there exists at least one
788   distribution P ′ compatible with GM (in fact, almost all) such that (X ̸⊥
                                                                              ⊥ Y | Z)P ′ .
789   Remark C.4. The second statement comes from the fact that precise parameter choices θ of distri-
790   butions PΘ might result in independence in an otherwise unblocked path in G. Fortunately, such
791   specific tuning of Θ rarely occurs in practice.
792   Remark C.5. If we need to determine independence relationships (X ⊥⊥ Y | Z)P (Z possibly empty),
793   we simply verify that all paths connecting X and Y are blocked by Z, using d-separability.
794
795   Next, we introduce the three rules of do-calculus, with which we can transform causal queries step
796   by step, until we reach the desired estimand.
797
      Theorem C.6. Rules of do-calculus.
798   Given an SCM M = (V, W, P, F) compatible with a DAG GM , for any disjoint sets X, Y, Z, W, ⊆
799   V (X and W possibly empty):

801         1. Insertion/deletion of observations (R1):
802            Px (Y | Z, W) = Px (Y | W) if (Y ⊥ ⊥ Z | X, W)GX .

804         2. Exchange of interventions/observations (R2):

                                                  ⊥ Z | X, W)GXZ .

      Frontier, which is populated as FRA processes more sets S. On the other hand, when checking
960   if P ∈ F rG (X, Y ), we check instead for P’ := P ∩ DeG (X), which is equivalent; this is so that we
961   can better employ the Frontier cache, collapsing different P ∪{X} sets into the same evaluation,
962   with the added benefit that a lower number of parents when testing if a path is blocked by P’ will be
963   faster. As a result, for any given set S ⊆ X, Algorithm 2 requires |S| iterations (one per element of
964   S), some of them skipped because X ∈ P aG (Y ), some already cached in Frontier. Finally, this
965   cache can be reused between explanations of do-SHAP for the same graph; only the ν cache must
966   be reset every time. This speeds up further explanations with virtually zero cost from the FRA.
967   The next step is how to determine if a set S ⊆ X \ {X} is a frontier between X and Y . Naively,
968   we could check if all directed paths between X and Y are blocked by (intersect with) S; we could
969   precompute all paths and store them for faster access, but the number of paths grows exponentially
970   (in the worst case scenario, i.e., a complete graph, there are 2K − 1 directed paths), which would
971   in turn require an exponential number of iterations per frontier check. Instead, we devise a more
      efficient method, described in the I S -F R O N T I E R procedure in Algorithm 2.

1290   We chose several SCM architectures for the experiment; here we justify these choices. Regarding
1291   the classic approach of modeling an SCM with each of its functions fk ∈ F separately, some of the
1292   approaches mentioned in Section 2 focus on how to model complex multivariate r.v.s (e.g., images),
1293   but the remaining univariate r.v.s are modelled by specifying which probability distribution family
1294   they belong to. Since our DGP consists exclusively of univariate continuous r.v.s, these proposals
1295   are equivalent. Instead, we will employ Deep Causal Graph (DCG) (Parafita & Vitrià, 2022), a gen-
       eral framework for all sorts of implementations of SCMs. In particular, with DCGs, we can train


                                                          24
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1296
       three different kinds of SCM: 1) a linear SCM with Normal distributions for each variable, used
1297   as a baseline; 2) the Distributional Causal Node (Parafita & Vitrià, 2019) architecture, where every
1298   node is modeled after a probability distribution family with a feed-forward network for the com-
1299   putation of its parameters (DCN); and 3) DCGs powered with its most flexible implementation for
1300   continuous nodes, based on Normalizing Flows. Finally, in order to test the alternative approach of
1301   modeling SCMs not node-wise, but the graph as a whole, we could use Variational Causal Autoen-
1302   coder (VACA) (Sánchez-Martın et al., 2022) or Causal Normalizing Flows (CNF) (Javaloy et al.,
1303   2024). However, as stated by the authors of CNF based on their experiments, “VACA shows poor
1304   performance, and is considerably slower due to the complexity of GNNs”. For this reason, we opt
1305
       for CNFs as a representative of this alternative approach for SCM modeling.
1306   Regarding the definition of the synthetic DGP, we employ a set of non-linear functions along with
1307   exogenous samples from diverse continuous r.v.s for the generation of new samples. Let χ2 (k) be

1666   On the one hand, marginal-SHAP sets ϕA = 0, since A does not appear in Y ’s structural equation;

1668   on Y . As for ϕE , it disregards the effect of E on S while also confounding the back-door effect
1669   E ← A → S → Y . Finally, for ϕS , it is closer to an intervention with do-SHAP, since S does not
1670

1673   fact that {A}, {A, E} and {A, E, S} have identical ν(.) values for conditional-SHAP and do-SHAP.

       difference with do-SHAP. This is more apparent in ϕE and ϕS , where the anti-causal flow inevitably
1675   affects the estimations.
1676
1677   In summary, both marginal-SHAP and conditional-SHAP misrepresent the underlying DGP, given
1678
       that they ignore its underlying causal structure. This is the reason why do-SHAP results in more
       reliable explanations than the alternatives.

       H.5    F INAL CONSIDERATIONS
1811
       Putting all of this together amounts to a seemingly complex method to estimate feature attribution:
1812   from finding the assumed Causal Graph G, defining the appropriate SCM architecture, training such
1813   a model, estimating the ν values, running FRA to avoid computations, to finally arriving at the do-SV
1814   estimations. However, given already-implemented SCM architectures with appropriate training and
1815   estimation procedures ready for use, estimand-agnostic do-SHAP is made practical. For this reason,
1816   we advocate for an open-source library bringing together different SCM architectures for easier
1817   switching between approaches, facilitating the general applicability of estimand-agnostic methods,
1818   and as a result, of do-SHAP explanations.

1820   I     I MPACT STATEMENT
1821
1822   The goal and byproduct of this work is a tool for the estimation of do-SVs on an arbitrary black-
1823   box system we wish to explain, natural or artificial. As an explainability tool, the core impact of
1824   this research is positive, as it will push towards several desirable goals: on top of being a useful
1825   tool both for science and business, as it allows to understand underlying systems in the data and
1826   apply that knowledge with informed decisions, explanations answer to several concerns about the
1827   trustworthiness of AI systems; e.g., the right to explanation on human-facing decision systems,
1828   debugging complex black-box systems, and better transparency and accountability. Additionally,
1829
       since it constitutes a tool for auditing these systems, explainability paves the way for AI regulation,
       necessary to protect human rights against the blind application of powerful but opaque AI systems.
1830
1831   One potential negative application of these techniques is in terms of willingly or unwillingly obfus-
1832   cating harmful behaviour in black-box models. Given the complexity of these techniques and the
1833   subsequent analysis required to derive conclusions from its outputs, explainability techniques could
1834   be used to provide a superficial layer of supervision and result in misleading conclusions about the
1835
       behaviour of the system. Great care with respect to the assumptions and outputs of these tools must
       be taken in their application.
