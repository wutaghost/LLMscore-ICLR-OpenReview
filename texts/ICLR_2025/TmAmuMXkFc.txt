009                                           Abstract
010
011           Generative diffusion processes are state-of-the-art machine learning models
012           deeply connected with fundamental concepts in statistical physics. Depend-
013           ing on the dataset size and the capacity of the network, their behavior is
014           known to transition from an associative memory regime to a generalization
015           phase in a phenomenon that has been described as a glassy phase transi-
016           tion. Here, using statistical physics techniques, we extend the theory of
017           memorization in generative diffusion to manifold-supported data. Our theo-
018
              retical and experimental findings indicate that different tangent subspaces
              are lost due to memorization effects at different critical times and dataset
019
              sizes, which depend on the local variance of the data along their directions.
020
              Perhaps counterintuitively, we find that, under some conditions, subspaces
021           of higher variance are lost first due to memorization effects. This leads to a
022           selective loss of dimensionality where some prominent features of the data
023           are memorized without a full collapse on any individual training point. We
024           validate our theory with a comprehensive set of experiments on networks
025           trained both in image datasets and on linear manifolds, which result in a
026           remarkable qualitative agreement with the theoretical predictions.

029   1   Introduction
030
031   Generative diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015) have achieved spectacular perfor-
032   mance in image (Ho et al., 2020; Song and Ermon, 2019; S. et al., 2021) and video (Ho
033   et al., 2022; Singer et al., 2022; Blattmann et al., 2023; B. et al., 2024b) generation and
034
      currently form the backbone of most state-of-the-art image generation software (Betker
      et al., 2023; Esser et al., 2024). The defining feature of these models is their remarkable
035
      ability to generalize on complex high-dimensional data distributions. However, diffusion
036
      models are known to be capable of fully memorizing the training set in the low-data regime
037   (Somepalli et al., 2023a;b; Kadkhodaie et al., 2024), and in this regime have been shown
038   (Ambrogioni, 2024; Hoover et al., 2023) to be mathematically equivalent to Dense Associative
039   Memory networks, which are modern variants of the celebrated Hopfield model admitting
040   very large memory storage capacity (Krotov and Hopfield, 2016). The ability of the models
041   to memorize has widespread societal implications, as in case of memorization their adoption
042   would likely violate existing copyright laws. Therefore, understanding the factors that lead
043   to generalization and memorization has great practical and theoretical value and drives
044   future developments. It is often conjectured that natural images and other high-dimensional
045
      natural data have most of their variability confined on a relatively small sub-space of the
      ambient space of all possible pixel-values (Peyré, 2009; Fefferman et al., 2016). This latent
046
      manifold charts the space of possible images, which is embedded in a significantly larger
047
      space of meaningless configurations of pixels. The dimensionality of the latent manifold
048   provides an estimate of the richness of generalizations since it quantifies the number of
049   (linearly independent) ways in which an image can be altered while remaining in the space of
050   possible generations. From this geometric perspective, a network that fully memorized the
051   training set corresponds to a zero-dimensional latent manifold (i.e., a collection of points),
052   because the individual “memories” cannot be altered without leaving the space of possible
053   generated images. Generative diffusion models have a rich mathematical structure that
      closely mirrors systems that have been heavily studied in statistical physics (Montanari,

079   2    Related work
080
081   Diffusion Models (DMs) are the current state-of-the-art generative models in several domains,
082   and works such as (De Bortoli, 2022; Pidstrigach, 2022) show that they are capable of
083   learning the manifold structure of the data. There are several methods that, given a trained
084   DM, can then estimate the Local Intrinsic Dimensionality (LID) at individual datapoints,
085   defined as the dimension of the manifold in the component corresponding to the datapoint
086   (Stanczuk et al., 2022; Horvat and Pfister, 2024; Kamkari et al., 2024b). Our analysis makes
087   use of the method from (Stanczuk et al., 2022) to extend the analysis of manifold learning
088
      by DMs. The idea that memories can be detected as datapoints with lower LID is formalized
      in (Kamkari et al., 2024a). While our work shares similar conclusions, our focus is on
089
      the interplay between memorization and generalization for diffusion models, and provide
090
      a theoretical explanation of such phenomenon. Furthermore, (Kadkhodaie et al., 2024)
091   show, both theoretically and empirically, how generalization arises in diffusion models as a
092   function of the number of datapoints as well as the way the manifold dimension varies along
093   the diffusion trajectory. While their findings are related to our results, our analysis differs
094   as we analyze the nonparametric empirical score and we provide for a statistical physics
095   perspective similar to the work of (Biroli and Mézard, 2023) on mixture of Gaussian data.
096   This differs from (W. et al., 2024) which considers parameterized low-rank score functions.
097   The advantage of the non-parametric empirical formulation is that it provides insight on
098   the large capacity regime, which can cast insight on the behavior of modern large scale
099
      architectures. Regarding the usage of statistical mechanics, our work takes inspiration from
      the analysis conducted by (B. et al., 2024a; Raya and Ambrogioni, 2024; Ambrogioni, 2023)
100
      to describe the backward process of DMs as a series of phase transitions similar to those
101
      ones occurring in disordered systems.

      4    Local geometry and latent manifolds
150
151
      Here, we assume that the data distribution p0 (x) is supported on a d-dimensional manifold
152
      M0 embedded in Rn . We study the family of latent manifolds Mt for different values of the
153   diffusion time t. When the distribution inside the manifold is uniform, these manifolds can
154   be defined as sets of stable fixed-points of the score function:
155
156                           Mt = {x∗ | s(x∗ , t) = 0, with s(x∗ , t) n.s.d.}                           (3)
157   where the negative semi-definiteness (n.s.d.) condition refers to the Jacobian of the score
158   function at x∗ . Due to the Gaussian annulus theorem, during high-dimensional generative
159   diffusion the samples “orbit” a shell around the manifold. However, while the manifold itself
160   is not usually visited by the particles, its shape reflects the shape of the potential around it,
161   which guides the generated trajectories. In order to understand the generative process, it is
      therefore important to study how the manifold emerges and changes during generation, and

735   B      Experimental methodology: measuring the intrinsic
736          dimension of the data manifold
737
738   The method used to geometrically visualize the intrinsic dimension of the data manifold
739   is the same as in (Stanczuk et al., 2022): the score function is measured across several
740   independent positions in the vicinity of the manifold and ordered as the columns of a
741   rectangular matrix S; the singular values of the matrix S are computed and collected; the
742
      intrinsic dimension of the manifold is given by the d − ker(S), with the kernel is estimated
      directly from the spectrum of the singular values of S.
743
744
      On the other hand, we propose a new method to geometrically estimate the in-
745   trinsic dimension of the data manifold. The procedure is based on empirically computing
746   the absolute value of the second derivative of the singular values, selecting the first bigger
747   value with respect to the median multiplied by a threshold factor. We further discard the
748   initial singular value as it tends to be large, resulting in instabilities. We found this method
749   to be more robust than the one proposed in (Stanczuk et al., 2022), especially for high
750   dimensional datasets where there is no sharp drop in the spectrum of the singular values.
751

       E                          Additional experimental results on trained networks
1243
1244
       In addition to the experiments described in section 6 we report here some tests on synthetic
1245   data generated on the linear manifold introduced in section 5.
1246
1247   First, we report numerical results from experiments in an ambient space of dimension d = 100,
1248
       while the manifold lives in a space of dimension m = 40. On the same manifold, through the
       choice of a diagonal F matrix, we define subspaces of different variances, which will result in
1249
       the opening of gaps at different times in the spectrum of the singular values of the matrix
1250
       obtained by sampling the score functions. In the specific, we choose the case of two subspaces
1251   associated to two variances of the data and the particular scenario of m different variances
1252   sampled uniformly at random. In Fig. 15 we plot the spectra at the smallest diffusion time ϵ,
1253   for models trained on datasets with different amount of training samples. With few training
1254   samples, we cannot see any gap opening. However, as the training samples increase, the
1255   model starts generalizing to the sub-spaces with higher variance, indicating both a smooth
1256   transition between generalization and memorization, and that the subspaces with higher
1257   variance are learned first by the model. As we shall see, as predicted by the theory the
1258   network will instead generalize to subspaces of low variance for parameter settings where σ22
1259
       is lower than σ12 but not negligible. Furthermore, in Fig. 16 report a similar experiment with
       a smaller ambient dimension, i.e. d = 30. Now the geometric memorization phenomenon
1260
       is more evident for medium data-set sizes at small times. Moreover, the phenomenology
1261
       emerging from the trained model is fully consistent with the one resulting from the empirical
1262   score, obtained through the same choice of the parameters, as showed in figures 12, 13 and
1263   14. This conclusion suggests two powerful insights about diffusion models: the network
1264   behaviour is consistent with our theory of memorization derived from the physics of Random
1265   Energy Models; the trained score function behaves consistently with the empirical score for
1266   a certain choice of the parameters.
