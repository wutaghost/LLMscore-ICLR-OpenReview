009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Mamba has recently emerged as a promising alternative to Transformers, offering
012
               near-linear complexity in processing sequential data. However, while channels in
               time series (TS) data have no specific order in general, recent studies have adopted
013
               Mamba to capture channel dependencies (CD) in TS, introducing a sequential order
014
               bias. To address this issue, we propose SOR-Mamba, a TS forecasting method
015            that 1) incorporates a regularization strategy to minimize the discrepancy between
016            two embedding vectors generated from data with reversed channel orders, thereby
017            enhancing robustness to channel order, and 2) eliminates the 1D-convolution
018            originally designed to capture local information in sequential data. Furthermore,
019            we introduce channel correlation modeling (CCM), a pretraining task aimed at
020            preserving correlations between channels from the data space to the latent space in
021            order to enhance the ability to capture CD. Extensive experiments demonstrate the
022            efficacy of the proposed method across standard and transfer learning scenarios.
023
024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
      Time series (TS) forecasting is prevalent in various fields, including weather (Angryk et al., 2020),
026   traffic (Cirstea et al., 2022), and energy (Dudek et al., 2021). While Transformers (Vaswani et al.,
027   2017) have been widely employed for this task due to their ability to capture long-term dependencies
028   in sequences (Wen et al., 2022), their quadratic computational complexity causes substantial compu-
029   tational overhead, limiting their practicality in real-world applications. Several attempts have been
030   made to reduce the complexity of Transformers (Zhang & Yan, 2023; Zhou et al., 2022); however,
031   they often result in performance degradations (Wang et al., 2024).
032   To tackle the computational challenges of Transformers, alternatives such as state-space models
033   (SSMs) (Gu et al., 2022) have been considered, employing convolutional operations to process
034   sequences with linear complexity. Recently, Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023) enhanced SSMs by incorpo-
035   rating a selective mechanism to prioritize important information efficiently. Due to its strong balance
036   between performance and computational efficiency (Wang et al., 2024), Mamba has been widely
037   adopted across various domains (Zhu et al., 2024; Schiff et al., 2024). In the TS domain, Mamba is
038
      utilized to capture temporal dependencies (TD) by processing input TS along the temporal dimension
      (Ahamed & Cheng, 2024), channel dependencies (CD) along the channel dimension (Wang et al.,
039
      2024), or both (Cai et al., 2024). In this paper, we focus on Mamba capturing CD, in line with the
040
      recent work (Liu et al., 2024a) that advocates for the use of complex attention mechanisms for CD
041   while employing simple multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs) for TD.

064   To this end, we introduce Sequential Order-Robust Mamba for TS forecasting (SOR-Mamba), a
065   TS forecasting method that handles the sequential order bias by 1) incorporating a regularization
066   strategy to minimize the distance between two embedding vectors generated from data with reversed

      channel orders to enhance robustness to the order, and 2) removing the 1D-convolution (1D-conv)
      originally designed to capture local information in sequential inputs. Additionally, we propose
068

069
      capture CD by preserving the correlation between channels from the data space to the latent space.
070   The main contributions of this work are summarized as follows:
071
072   • We propose SOR-Mamba, a TS forecasting method that handles the sequential order bias by
073     1) regularizing the unidirectional Mamba to minimize the distance between two embedding vectors
074     generated from data with reversed channel orders for robustness to channel order and 2) removing
075
        the 1D-conv from the original Mamba block, as channels lack an inherent sequential order.
076   • We introduce CCM, a novel pretraining task that preserves the correlation between channels from
077     the data space to the latent space, thereby enhancing the model’s ability to capture CD.
078   • We conduct extensive experiments with 13 datasets in both standard and transfer learning settings,
079     demonstrating that our method achieves state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance with greater efficiency
080     compared to previous SOTA methods by utilizing the unidirectional Mamba.

082   2   R ELATED W ORKS
083   TS forecasting with Transformer. Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) are commonly employed for
084   long-term TS forecasting (LTSF) tasks due to their ability to handle long-range dependencies through
085   attention mechanisms. However, their quadratic complexity has led to the development of various
086   methods aimed at improving efficiency, such as modifying the Transformer architecture (Zhang & Yan,
087   2023; Zhou et al., 2022), patchifying the TS (Nie et al., 2023) or using MLP-based models (Chen et al.,
088   2023; Zeng et al., 2023). While MLP-based models offer simpler structures and reduced complexity
089   compared to Transformers, they tend to be less effective at capturing global dependencies (Wang et al.,
090   2024). Recently, iTransformer (Liu et al., 2024a) inverts the conventional Transformer framework in
091
      the TS domain by treating each channel as a token rather than each patch, shifting the focus from
      capturing TD to CD. This framework has led to significant performance gains and has become widely
092
      adopted as the backbone for TS models (Liu et al., 2024b; Dong et al., 2024).
093
094   State-space models. To overcome the limitations of Transformer-based models, state-space models
095
      have been integrated with deep learning to tackle the challenge of long-range dependencies (Rangapu-
      ram et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023). However, these methods are unable to adapt
096
      their internal parameters to varying inputs, which limits their performance. Recently, Mamba (Gu &
097
      Dao, 2023) introduces a selective scan mechanism that efficiently filters specific inputs and captures
098   long-range context by incorporating time-varying parameters into the SSM. Due to its linear-time
099   efficiency for modeling long sequences, it has been widely adopted in various domains, includ-
100   ing computer vision (Ma et al., 2024a; Huang et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2024) and natural language
101   processing (Pióro et al., 2024; Anthony et al., 2024; He et al., 2024).
102   TS forecasting with Mamba. Due to its balance between performance and computational efficiency,
103   Mamba has also been applied in the TS domain. TimeMachine (Ahamed & Cheng, 2024) utilizes
104   multi-scale quadruple-Mamba to capture either TD alone or both TD and CD, with its architecture
105   relying on the statistics of the dataset. CMamba (Zeng et al., 2024) captures TD with patch-wise
106   Mamba and CD with an MLP. FMamba (Ma et al., 2024b) integrates fast-attention with Mamba
107   to capture CD, and SST (Xu et al., 2024) captures global and local patterns in TS with Mamba
      and Transformer, respectively. S-Mamba (Wang et al., 2024), Bi-Mamba+ (Liang et al., 2024), and

130   Problem definition. This paper addresses the multivariate TS forecasting task, where the model uses
131   a lookback window x = (x1 , x2 , · · · , xL ) to predict future values y = (xL+1 , · · · , xL+H ) with
132   xi ∈ RC representing the values at each time step. Here, L, H, and C denote the size of the lookback
133
      window, the forecast horizon, and the number of channels, respectively.
134   State-space models. SSM transforms the continuous input signals x(t) into corresponding outputs
135   y(t) via a state representation h(t). This state space represents how the state evolves over time, which
136   can be expressed using ordinary differential equations as follows:
137                                         h′ (t) = Ah(t) + Bx(t),
                                                                                                               (1)
138                                          y(t) = Ch(t) + Dx(t),

      where h′ (t) = dh(t)
                      dt , and A, B, C, and D are learnable parameters of the SSMs.
141   Due to the continuous nature of SSMs, discretization is commonly used to approximate continuous-
142   time representations into discrete-time representations by sampling input signals at fixed intervals.
143   This results in the discrete-time SSMs being represented as:
144                                           hk = Ahk−1 + Bxk ,

148   B = (∆A)−1 (exp(∆A) − I) · ∆B are the discrete-time matrices obtained from the A and B.
149   Recently, Mamba introduces selective SSMs that enables the model to capture contextual information
150   in long sequences using time-varying parameters (Gu & Dao, 2023). Its near-linear complexity makes
151   it an efficient alternative to the quadratic complexity of the attention mechanism in Transformers.

      4          M ETHODOLOGY
154   In this paper, we introduce SOR-Mamba, a TS forecasting method designed to address the sequential
155   order bias by 1) regularizing Mamba to minimize the distance between two embedding vectors
156   generated from data with reversed channel orders and 2) removing the 1D-conv from the original
157   Mamba block. The overall framework of SOR-Mamba is illustrated in Figure 2, which consists of
158   four components: the embedding layer for tokenization, Mamba for capturing CD, MLP for capturing
159   TD, and the prediction head for predicting the future output.
160   Furthermore, we introduce a novel pretraining task, CCM, where the model is pretrained to preserve
161   the correlation between channels from the data space to the latent space, aligning with the recent TS
      models that focus on capturing CD over TD. The overall framework of CCM is illustrated in Figure 3.

      4.1   A RCHITECTURE OF SOR-M AMBA
163
164   1) Embedding layer. To tokenize the TS in a channel-wise manner, we use an embedding layer that
165   treats each channel as a token, following the approach in iTransformer (Liu et al., 2024a). Specifically,
166   we transform x ∈ RL×C into z ∈ RC×D using a single linear layer.
167   2) Mamba for CD. The original Mamba block combines the H3 block (Fu et al., 2023) with a gated
168   MLP, where the H3 block incorporates a 1D-conv before the SSM layer to capture local information
169   from adjacent steps. However, since channels in TS do not possess any inherent sequential order, we
170   find this convolution unnecessary for capturing CD. Accordingly, we remove the convolution from
171   the original Mamba block, resulting in the proposed CD-Mamba block, as illustrated in Figure 2(b).
172
      Note that this differs from the previous work (Cai et al., 2024) which replaces the 1D-conv with a
      dropout in the Mamba block, as it is designed to capture TD. Using the CD-Mamba block, we obtain
173
      z1 and z2 , which are two embedding vectors with reversed channel orders that are employed for
174
      regularization to address the sequential order bias. These vectors are then added element-wise and
175   combined with a residual connection from z. Further analysis regarding the removal of the 1D-conv

      4.3   C HANNEL C ORRELATION M ODELING
209
210   Previous pretraining tasks for TS have primarily focused on TD, such as masked modeling (Zerveas
211   et al., 2021) and reconstruction (Lee et al., 2024), to pretrain an encoder. However, we argue for the
212   necessity of a new task that emphasizes CD over TD to align with recent TS models that focus on
213   capturing CD with complex model architectures (Liu et al., 2024a; Wang et al., 2024). To this end,
214   we propose CCM, which aims to preserve the (Pearson) correlation between channels from the data
215
      space to the latent space, as correlation is a simple yet effective way to measure channel relationships
      and has been utilized in prior studies to analyze CD (Yang et al., 2024; Zhao & Shen, 2024).

      Tasks and evaluation metrics. We demonstrate the effectiveness of SOR-Mamba on TS forecasting
250
      tasks with 13 datasets under standard and transfer learning settings. For evaluation, we follow the
251
      standard self-supervised learning (SSL) framework, which involves pretraining and fine-tuning (FT)
252   or linear probing (LP) on the same dataset. Additionally, we consider in-domain and cross-domain
253   transfer learning settings, with the domains defined in the previous work (Dong et al., 2023). For
254   evaluation metrics, we employ mean squared error (MSE) and mean absolute error (MAE).
255
      Datasets. For the forecasting tasks, we use 13 datasets: four ETT datasets (ETTh1, ETTh2, ETTm1,
256   ETTm2) (Zhou et al., 2021), four PEMS datasets (PEMS03, PEMS04, PEMS07, PEMS08) (Chen
257   et al., 2001), Exchange, Weather, Traffic, Electricity (ECL) (Wu et al., 2021), and Solar-Energy
258   (Solar) (Lai et al., 2018). Details of the dataset statistics are provided in Appendix A.
259
      Baseline methods. We follow the baseline methods and results from S-Mamba (Wang et al., 2024).
260   For the baseline methods, we consider Transformer-based models, including iTransformer (Liu
261   et al., 2024a), PatchTST (Nie et al., 2023), and Crossformer (Zhang & Yan, 2023), as well as
262   linear/MLP models, including TimesNet (Wu et al., 2023), DLinear (Zeng et al., 2023), and RLinear
263   (Li et al., 2023). Additionally, we include S-Mamba (Wang et al., 2024), which is a Mamba-based TS
264   forecasting model. Details of the baseline methods are provided in Appendix B.
265   Experimental setups. We follow the experimental setups from iTransformer and S-Mamba. Note
266   that we do not tune any hyperparameters except for λ, which is related to the proposed regularization,
267   while adhering to the values used in S-Mamba for all other hyperparameters concerning the model
268   architecture and optimization. For dataset splitting, we adhere to the standard protocol of dividing
269   all datasets into training, validation, and test sets in chronological order. Details of the experimental
      setups, including the size of the input window and the forecast horizon, are provided in Appendix A.

480
      In this work, we introduce SOR-Mamba, a TS forecasting method that addresses the sequential order
      bias by incorporating a regularization strategy and removing the 1D-conv from Mamba. Additionally,
481
      we propose a novel pretraining task, CCM, to improve the model’s ability to capture CD. Our results
482
      demonstrate that the proposed method is robust to variations in channel order, leading to superior
483   performance and greater efficiency in both standard and transfer learning scenarios. We hope that our
484   work motivates further research on sequential order-robust Mamba in domains where a sequential
485   order is not inherent, such as in tabular data.
