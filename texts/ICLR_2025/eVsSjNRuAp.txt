009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            This paper centers around a novel concept proposed recently by researchers from
012            the control community where the training process of a deep neural network can
013            be considered a nonlinear dynamical system acting upon the high-dimensional
014            weight space. Koopman operator theory, a data-driven dynamical system analysis
015            framework, can then be deployed to discover the otherwise non-intuitive training
016            dynamics. Taking advantage of the predictive power of the Koopman operator
017            theory, the time-consuming Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) iterations can be
018
               bypassed by directly predicting network weights a few epochs later. This novel
               predictive training framework, however, often suffers from gradient explosion
019
               especially for more extensive and complex models. In this paper, we incorporate
020
               the idea of differential learning, where different parts of the network can undergo
021            different learning rates during training, into the predictive training framework and
022            propose the so-called “predictive differential training” (PDT) to sustain robust
023            performance for accelerated learning even for complex network structures. The
024            key contribution is the design of an effective masking strategy based on Koopman
025            analysis of training dynamics of each parameter in order to select the subset
026            of parameters that exhibits “good” prediction performance. PDT also includes
027            the design of an acceleration scheduler to keep track of the prediction error so
028            that the training process can roll back to the traditional GD-based approaches
029
               to “correct” deviations from off-predictions. We demonstrate that PDT can be
               seamlessly integrated as a plug-in with existing optimizers, including, for example,
030
               SGD, momentum, and Adam. The experimental results have shown consistent
031
               performance improvement in terms of faster convergence, lower training/testing
032            loss, and fewer number of epochs to achieve the best loss of Baseline.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   The advent of cutting-edge hardware (Li et al., 2014) and the development of parallel processing
038   techniques (Li et al., 2020) have greatly accelerated the training process of the Deep Neural Network
039   (DNN). However, enhancing the fundamental techniques of DNN training continues to be a significant
040   challenge. From the inception of SGD (Robbins & Monro, 1951), which has since become a mainstay
041   in DNN training, numerous techniques have been proposed to increase the efficiency of the underlying
042   optimization task, including, for example, learning rate annealing and momentum (Sutskever et al.,
043   2013), RMSprop (Tieleman & Hinton, 2012), and Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2014). In addition to these
044
      first-order optimizers, second-order alternatives (Martens, 2010) utilizing curvature information or
      second-order derivatives of the loss function have been explored to potentially enable more efficient
045
      convergence.
046
047   Notably, the Adam optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2014) has been a significant advancement and belongs
048   to the family of differential learning, where different parts of the network can exhibit different
049   learning rates during training. The different parts can be, for example, layer-specific (Devlin et al.,
050
      2019; He et al., 2019a) or parameter-specific (Tieleman & Hinton, 2012; Kingma & Ba, 2014; Duchi
      et al., 2011a). This is particularly useful in large-scale models where different layers or parameters
051
      might require different levels of adjustment during training.
052
053   Very recently, a novel interpretation of the DNN training process has been proposed, mainly by
      researchers from the control community (Redman et al., 2022; Dogra & Redman, 2020; Manojlovic


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      et al., 2020; Tano et al., 2020) – If it is intuitive to consider a pre-trained DNN as an inherently
055   nonlinear static system acting upon the high-dimensional inputs, then the DNN “training process”
056   itself is a “nonlinear” dynamical system acting upon the high-dimensional “weight space”! It
057   is a discrete dynamical system since the weights of a DNN evolve over each iteration (or epoch)
058   according to the optimization process adopted. This drastically different interpretation has led
059   to the establishment of a novel mathematical framework for learning. Koopman operator theory
060   (Mezić, 2005), a powerful data-driven dynamical system analysis tool, is often adopted to exploit the
061   underlying dynamics in the seemingly non-intuitive training process of a DNN. Taking advantage of
062   the predictive power of the Koopman operator theory, the time-consuming SGD iterations can be
063
      bypassed by directly predicting network weights a few epochs later (Dogra, 2020; Dogra & Redman,
      2020; Tano et al., 2020). We refer to these approaches as predictive training.
064
065   However, practical challenges quickly emerge. The absence of actual gradient descent means that
066   convergence cannot be guaranteed, and the framework is sensitive to disturbances in the weight
067   space, leading to error accumulation across iterations. As the network scales, the Koopman-based
068   prediction training framework becomes increasingly ineffective. This issue is mostly due to the lack
069
      of adaptive mechanisms when applying prediction-based acceleration. That is, existing predictive
      training approaches tend to apply the predicted weights to all parameters without considering the
070
      different dynamics they might exhibit during the training process. This often leads to gradient
071
      explosion, especially for more extensive and complex models.

094   Adam through a visual comparison of the training trajectory and loss landscape. The contribution of
095   the proposed PDT is three-fold:
096

097

098
               performance.
099
             • We design a scheduler to keep track of the prediction error so that the training process can
100

101
102
             • We demonstrate that PDT can be seamlessly integrated as a plug-in with existing optimizers,
               including, for example, SGD, momentum, and Adam.

205   In fact, the correlation between quality of prediction and training dynamics has been heavily studied.
206   From neuroscience perspective, the quality of predictions made by neurons is intricately linked to
207   their learning dynamics (Schultz et al., 1997; Friston, 2010). Accurate predictions lead to more stable
208

214

243   3.2   ACCELERATION S CHEDULE
244
245   The acceleration schedule concerns the problem of “when” to enable prediction. As illustrated in
246   Fig. 3, the “prediction” block is strategically placed among the regular SGD optimization blocks,
247   acting as a plug-in enhancement within the existing optimization framework. The placement of
248   the “prediction” block is solely determined by the masking strategy designed in Sec. 3.1. If no
249   element in the mask is qualified as “good” prediction, then standard SGD-based optimization takes
250   place; otherwise, qualified predicted weights will be incorporated to accelerate learning. This
251
      approach is analogous to the “one-step-ahead” strategy employed by NAG (Nesterov, 1983), where
      the subsequent step of standard optimization acts to correct any small errors that may arise from the
252
      Koopman prediction.
253
254   Let us use a toy example to demonstrate the effect of accelerating the learning of a subset of variables
255   to further motivate the concept of differential learning. Consider the function,
256
             f (x, y, z, u, v, w) = x2 + y 2 + sin(z) + u2 − cos(v) + w2 + xy + y sin(z) + uvw,
257
258   which involves six variables: x, y, z, u, v, w. To find the minimum of this function, we employ a sim-
259   ple gradient descent optimization process. Starting from the initial point [2.0, 2.0, 1.0, 0.5, −0.5, 1.5]
260   with a learning rate of 0.01, it takes 67 steps to converge to a minimum.
261
      We then explore an alternative optimization strategy where the variables x, y, z undergo an opti-
262
      mization process that is three times faster than the standard process, while u, v, w are optimized at
263   the normal rate but employing the updated values of x, y, z. See Fig. 10 in Appendix A.1 for the
264   acceleration trajectory, where the trajectory maintains the same direction for x and y but achieves
265   convergence in just 27 steps.
266
      This example shows that by strategically identifying a subset of variables and simply increasing their
267
      learning rate, the training can be accelerated by about 60%.

284   3.3   C OMPUTATIONAL C OMPLEXITY A NALYSIS
285
286   To facilitate our discussion, we consider a DNN with N parameters. The computational load for
287   processing each batch is directly proportional to both the batch size (B) and the number of parameters
288   (N ), resulting in a complexity of O(B × N ) per batch. When extended to the entire dataset with S
289
      samples across one epoch, the complexity scales to O(S × N ).
290   Integrating Koopman operator predictions into the DNN training process entails constructing a data
291   matrix from h past epochs of the parameter trajectories, with the matrix dimensions being N × h.
292   The primary computational burden arises from performing SVD on this matrix with a complexity
293   of O(N × h2 ). Given that N significantly exceeds h — with h usually being a small number like 5
294   to 10, and N potentially reaching the millions or even billions—the quadratic impact of h remains
295
      manageable relative to N .
296   Since Koopman predictions are integrated at much less frequent intervals than standard batch process-
297   ing—potentially at epoch-level intervals—this approach can lead to significant computational savings
298   and efficiency enhancements in the training of large-scale neural networks. A detailed analysis of
299   computational efficiency with experimental results is provided in Sec. A.4.

302
303   We conduct four sets of experiments to evaluate the effectiveness of the proposed PDT framework in
304   accelerating learning. The first set of experiments implements PDT across a variety of popular neural
305   network architectures using a range of popular optimizers and evaluate the savings in run-time. The
306   second set investigates the effectiveness of the proposed masking strategy. The third set evaluates the
307   proposed masking strategy against other potential metrics for prediction quality, like validation loss.
308   The final set of experiments studies the effect of some important hyperparameters.

310   4.1   G ENERALIZATION S TUDY OF PDT
311
312   We implement the proposed Koopman-based PDT process across a variety of popular neural network
313   architectures, including Fully-Convolutional-Network (FCN), AlexNet, ResNet, and ViT-Base. We
314
      also use a range of optimizers, including the SGD, SGD with momentum, and Adam.
315   In all experiments, we use the past five epochs to form the snapshot with a one-epoch interval to
316   predict weights in the next five steps. Prediction is initiated starting from the 5th epoch. As elaborated
317   in Sec. 3, the computational load of the Koopman-related calculations is comparable to that of
318   batch-level updates. However, since we apply these calculations at the epoch level, the overhead
319   introduced by the DMD is effectively compensated by the acceleration in loss reduction. We observe
320
      from both Table 1 and Fig. 5 that the proposed PDT consistently achieves the best training loss of the
      Baseline in fewer number of epochs without sacrificing performance. All experiments were repeated
321
      with five random seeds (0, 100, 200, 300, 400) to ensure reliability.
322
323   The last column in Fig. 5 illustrates a so-called “masked ratio curve” unique to PDT, where it tracks
      the percentage of predictions accepted according to the masking strategy described in Sec. 3.1. We

      observe that the masked ratio always starts with higher values in the early stage of the training
325   process, then generally decreases as training progresses. More interestingly, we observe that smaller
326   networks on simpler tasks (FCN/AlexNet on CIFAR-10) show a relatively more gradual reduction in

511   5    D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
512
513
      This paper proposed a novel predictive differential training (PDT) framework based on the study of
514
      training dynamics, where we consider the training process as a dynamical system acting upon the
515
      weight space. PDT presents stable performance in accelerating training even for complex network
516   structures due to its selective incorporation of predicted weights.
517
518   Future Work and Challenges. Despite these advancements, considerable work remains. First,
519
      further studies into different predictive methods beyond DMD is necessary. Innovative approaches,
      such as streaming DMD (Hemati et al., 2014; Liew et al., 2022), can not only reduce the memory
520
      footprint of constructing trajectory matrices, but also improve computational efficiency.
521
522   Second, investigating the impact of PDT on the properties of the learned function, such as loss surface
523   sharpness or smoothness, is highly valuable (Humayun et al., 2024; Foret et al., 2020). These metrics
524   provide a deeper understanding of the model’s robustness and generalization capabilities. Based on
525
      the current experimental results, we hypothesize that the selective application of predictions may
      help avoid sharp local minima by allowing more exploration in the weight space. In future work, we
526
      intend to incorporate these measures into our analysis to provide a more comprehensive evaluation of
527
      PDT, and further explore how these properties influence the efficacy of PDT.
528
529   Third, we observe from the masked ratio vs. epoch curves in Fig. 5 that as training prolongs and as
530   training loss converges to a stable value, we should expect the training dynamics to be less complex or
531
      easier to predict, which should have resulted in a higher masked ratio. However, in reality, except for
      the ResNet-50 on ImageNet-1K curve where a small bouncing back on the masked ratio is observed
532
      toward the end of the training process, all the rest scenarios exhibit a stable masked ratio, much lower
533
      as compared to that at the beginning of the training process. In addition, we would have expected the
534   epoch number, where the masked ratio starts turning flat, to be consistent with that when the training
535   loss enters a plateau, but this is only observed in the complex network scenarios Fig. 5(c) and (d), but
536   not the simple network cases Fig. 5(a) and (b). This seems to indicate that the masked ratio curves
537   can have the potential of indicating when the network overfits, that when the marked ratio starts to
538   drastically decrease again after the initial reduction. This would serve as a potential indicator for
539   early stopping conditions. Although this is out of the scope of the current paper, the potential impact
      warrants further investigation.

876   example, when the batch is too small for a diverse dataset like ImageNet, the weight updates could be
877   chaotic since each consecutive batch is no longer an identical distribution. There are two experimental
878   designs that can test this: 1) test PDT on a very large dataset like ImageNet-22K and 2) design a
879   batching scheme to intentionally violate the i.i.d. assumption of mini-batches using a smaller dataset
880   such as CIFAR-10. In the second design, we maintain the normal batch size, but only put samples of
881   the same class in the batch. We also randomize the batch sequence instead of using any fixed order so
882   that there is no regular training set dynamics that DMD might pick up on.
