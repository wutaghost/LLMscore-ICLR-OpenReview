007                                                  ABSTRACT
008
009            With the introduction of transformer-based models for vision and language tasks,
010            such as LLaVA and Chameleon, there has been renewed interest in the discrete
011            tokenized representation of images. These models often treat image patches as discrete
012            tokens, analogous to words in natural language, learning joint alignments between
013            visual and human languages. However, little is known about the statistical behavior
014            of these visual languages—whether they follow similar frequency distributions,
015            grammatical structures, or topologies as natural languages. In this paper, we take a
016
               natural-language-centric approach to analyzing discrete visual languages and uncover
               striking similarities and fundamental differences. We demonstrate that, although visual
017
               languages adhere to Zipfian distributions, higher token innovation drives greater entropy
018
               and lower compression, with tokens predominantly representing object parts, indicating
019            intermediate granularity. We also show that visual languages lack cohesive grammatical
020            structures, leading to higher perplexity and weaker hierarchical organization compared
021            to natural languages. Finally, we demonstrate that, while vision models align more
022            closely with natural languages than other models, this alignment remains significantly
023            weaker than the cohesion found within natural languages. Through these experiments,
024            we demonstrate how understanding the statistical properties of discrete visual languages
025            can inform the design of more effective computer vision models.
026
027
      1    INTRODUCTION
028
029   Transformer-based models have not just advanced, but fundamentally reshaped how we approach both
030   vision and language processing, merging these domains in shared sequential representation spaces. Indeed,
031   most recent multi-modal models including DALL-E (Ramesh et al., 2022), LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024) and
032   Chameleon (Team, 2024) operate over joint tokenized representations of images and language, where
033   models decompose images into “visual languages”: linearized discrete patches or tokens analogous
034   to words in a sentence. This process, shown in Figure 1, enables seamless integration of images into
035   transformer architectures and allows models to solve multimodal tasks, ranging from image generation
036   and image captioning to visual question answering and translation.
037   Despite the success of such shared-structure models, current research lacks an in-depth understanding of
038   whether the internal structure of visual tokens mirrors the principles governing natural languages. Specif-
039   ically, the question arises: do languages formed of visual tokens follow the same statistical patterns, such as
040   frequency distributions, grammatical rules, or semantic dependencies, that human languages exhibit? Inves-
041
      tigating such statistical behavior of discrete visual tokens extends beyond theoretical curiosity; it has broad
      implications for practical machine learning applications. While in linguistic theory, phenomena like Zipf’s
042
      law and entropy shape natural languages’ structure and shape the design of machine learning algorithms,
043
      no such rules exist for visual languages. Such rules, if they exist, have the potential to motivate modality-
044   specific models and procedures to capture the unique statistical properties of the underlying visual data.
045
      In pursuit of such rules, in this paper we inspect the equivalence of visual and natural languages through
046
      an empirical analysis of token distributions, segmentation granularity, and syntactic and semantic structures.
047
      We start by investigating the frequency statistics of visual words and compare them to natural languages.
048   Our analysis reveals that although visual languages can follow power-law (Zipfian) distributions, they use
049   more tokens more uniformly. This leads to languages with greater per-token entropy and lower compression
050   ratios, and implies that vision models may require more attention heads, larger embeddings, and longer
051   training times with more diverse data compared to natural language models (subsection 2.2, subsection 2.3,
052   subsection 2.4, subsection 2.5). Noting in these experiments that visual languages have coarser granularity
053   than patches, we demonstrate through correlation analysis that visual tokens operate at an intermediate
      level of granularity, and typically represent object parts rather than whole objects or sub-parts in images.

078   2     DO VISUAL TOKENS ACT LIKE WORDS?
079
080   The first question that we examine is: Do visual tokens themselves (i.e. the patches of an image) act like
081
      words? While we often treat these tokens as either a word (or subword), as each token forms a single
      input sequence element in a transformer, it seems unintuitive that there would be a one to one statistical
082
      correlation between the two concepts. In this section, we look at several statistical properties of individual
083
      tokens, comparing those observed in natural language to those in visual systems.
084
085
      2.1   PRELIMINARIES
086
087   What, explicitly, is a visual language? In this work, we consider a visual language to be a language induced
088   over “visual tokens” by first converting images in a dataset to a discrete set of symbols using a visual
089   tokenizer (often a VQ-VAE), and then linearizing those tokens into one-dimensional sequences (See
090   Figure 1). Such a definition parallels efforts in both text-to-image diffusion and large vision and language
091
      models which have both explored using discrete visual tokens for vision-language model alignment (Team,
      2024; Ramesh et al., 2022; Gu et al., 2022; Razavi et al., 2019), as well as in uni-modal models such as
092
      LVM (Bai et al., 2024) and LLamaGen (Sun et al., 2024).
093
094   We primarily focus on common tokenizers used for recent vision and language models, and our selection
095
      of tokenizers is overviewed in Table 1. These tokenizers are all VQ-VAE-based, trained on varying
      datasets, and with various methods. While some recent models such as Transfusion (Zhou et al., 2024)
096
      and LLaVA Liu et al. (2024) leverage continuous-valued tokens instead of discrete vocabularies, there
097
      is still considerable uncertainty about whether discrete or continuous-valued tokens are more effective
098   (Mao et al., 2021). While many of our methods in this paper could apply to continuous tokens through
099   a discrete quantization of those tokens, we leave such continuous extensions to future work. For more
100   details on the tokenizers, see Appendix A.
101
      We ground our empirical experiments in several common multi-modal datasets, including Conceptual
102   Captions (12M) (Sharma et al., 2018), MS-COCO (Lin et al., 2014), ILSVRC (ImageNet) (Russakovsky
103   et al., 2015) and XM-3600 (Thapliyal et al., 2022). Each of these datasets has a set of images, and (except
104   ILSVRC) paired text in one or more languages. For more information on the datasets, see Appendix B.
105   An example visual sentence from MS-COCO (Image ID: 399655) is given in Figure 1. In all of the
106   experiments in this paper, we linearize the tokens using a row-wise scan order (for a detailed discussion
107   on scan-order, see Appendix C). Such linearization is the de facto standard for turning spatial visual tokens
      into sequences of discrete tokens for use in learning applications.

      2.2    TOKEN FREQUENCY AND ZIPF’S LAW
118
      The statistics of natural language token distributions have long been studied, beginning with Dewey (1921),
119   who first plotted the frequency of English words. A key principle that emerged from this research is Zipf’s
120   Law (Kingsley Zipf, 1932), which describes a power-law relationship between the frequency of words
121   and their rank in a language where a small number of high-frequency words dominate natural language,
122   while the majority of words occur infrequently. Formally, Zipf’s law states that:
123                                                    f(r)∝rα+σZ                                             (1)
124
      where f(r) is the frequency of the element with rank r and α/σ parameterize a learned Gaussian
125   distribution (close to 1/0 in many natural languages).
126
      Zipf’s law has been observed across many languages (Gelbukh & Sidorov, 2001; Yu et al., 2018) and
127
      non-human communication systems (such as dolphins (McCowan et al., 1999)). As Mandelbrot pointed
128
      out, adherence to Zipf-like distributions ensures that communication systems—whether natural or
129   artificial—operate efficiently (Mandelbrot, 1953). Language models, especially large language models
130   (LLMs), have been shown to follow this same pattern, with token distributions that obey Zipf’s law
131   (Patwary et al., 2019). This statistical regularity in language extends beyond word frequency - Zipf’s law
132   has also been observed in images themselves: Ruderman (1997) showed that the distribution of object sizes
133   and spatial frequencies in natural scenes follows power-law distributions, and Crosier & Griffin (2007)
134   showed that there was Zipfian behavior in image coding schemes such as JPEG.
135   Thus, we first ask the question - Do “visual languages” follow Zipf’s law? To do this, we tokenize the
136   image datasets according to subsection 2.1 and compute the empirical token-rank frequency distributions
137   on each of the datasets (See Appendix D for details). We show the empirical distributions in Figure 2. If the
138   plots were Zipfian, we would expect them to be linear in the log-log space; while this is the case for natural
139   languages, visual languages do not seem to generally conform to a linear curve, instead, for one and two
140   grams, the plots follow a lognormal distribution, and for higher level N-grams are more convex in nature.
141   For one/two-grams, this indicates that token utilization is fairly uniform, with most tokens occurring in
142   equal proportion, and the heavier tails of the distribution indicate that “rare” are, in practice, not so rare,
143   occurring with much higher frequency than expected under a power-law distribution. Whereas natural
144   languages are often structured with a clear core vocabulary and then more specialized words, it seems like
145
      visual features seem to be more evenly distributed, with many features or combinations being equally likely.
      At higher n-grams, for visual languages there is more convex behavior, suggesting that there are very few
146
      common n-grams, instead, n-grams are often unique, and composed in ways that appear very infrequently
147
      within the datasets. Such an implication implies that visual languages are highly context-dependent (which
148   is sensible, as visual scenes are quite complex).
149
      To confirm these details, we fit a Zipf’s distribution to each of the models, with the results of the fit shown
150
      in Table 2. Interestingly, the α values have opposite behaviors for visual and natural languages in the
151
      light of increasing N . In natural languages, the fact that α increases with N means that higher-order
152   N-grams follow steeper power-law distributions, and the distribution of N-gram frequencies becomes
153   more concentrated around a few common combinations, while the frequency of rare combinations
154   decreases rapidly. In visual languages, on the other hand, the decrease in α with increasing N suggests
155   that higher-order combinations of visual features follow flatter distributions: as visual N-grams increase
156   in complexity, there is more diversity in the combinations of features and patterns, leading to richer and
157   more distributed sets of higher-order feature combinations.
158   These phenomena together suggest that VQ-VAEs are “spreading” information between the independent
159   tokens, rather than building compressive and compositional structures, which we explore further in
160   subsection 2.2 (token innovation) and subsection 2.5 (compression). Indeed, since Zipf’s Law reflects a
161   (theoretically optimal) balance between redundancy and information, it suggests that visual languages are
      more data-driven, and reflect the underlying complexity and variability of visual scenes, rather than focusing

307     2.4                     NATURALITY
308
309    Benford’s Law describes the frequency distribution of leading digits in naturally occurring datasets, where
310    smaller digits like 1 and 2 appear disproportionately more often than larger digits like 8 and 9 (Benford,
311
       1938). Originally observed in domains such as physics (Sambridge et al., 2010), economics (Tödter,
       2009), and demographics (Miller, 2015), recently, there has been growing interest in extending this
312
       statistical principle to linguistic data (Golbeck, 2023; Melián et al., 2017; Hong, 2010). One of the primary
313
       applications of Benford’s law is the detection of anomalies in data: datasets that do not follow Benford’s
314    law are likely to be unnatural in nature - here, we ask the question, do visual language token frequencies
315    naturally follow Benford’s law? We follow a similar tokenization process to subsection 2.2, and plot the
316    occurrence of leading digits in the token frequency distribution (See Appendix G for more details).
317
        Our results are shown in Figure 5 for the MS-COCO dataset, and in Appendix G on other datasets.
318     Interestingly, for single tokens, the distribution is unique-token-heavy, with the remaining tokens having
319     a Gaussian distribution around six. Two-grams are the most natural, with Chameleon following Benford’s
320     law almost exactly, with three-grams significantly dominated by low/unique frequency tokens. Interestingly,
321     the highest quality tokenizer, the Chameleon tokenizer, is by far the most natural in Figure 5a, suggesting
322     that tokenizations performing well for vision-text tasks might have more natural distributions. Beyond
323     this effect, Figure 5b shows that distributions of visual-token bi-grams have the most natural distribution
        curves, implying a potential correspondence in statistics between visual bi-grams and text uni-grams,

       2.5              ENTROPY AND REDUNDANCY
352
       Building on the foundational work of Shannon (1951), entropy and redundancy have long been
353    understood as key characteristics of natural language, providing insight into its inherent predictability
354    and compressibility. While natural languages, like English, exhibit high redundancy that enables efficient
355    encoding, it is unclear if visual languages might have similar coding behaviors. To evaluate the efficiency
356    of encoding, we use a similar setup to subsection 2.2 and extract token streams for each of the target
357    datasets. We then compute the entropy of the token streams, as well as compute a simple Huffman
358    code/compression (Huffman, 1952) for each of the resulting streams. Such a hierarchical compression code
359    allows us to estimate the overall “compressibility” of the stream (See Appendix H for background/details).
360    The results are summarized in Table 3. We can see that in general, the average code length, entropy,
361    and bits of information/sample are higher for visual languages. This suggests that visual languages have
362    more variability and are inherently more complex to predict and encode than natural language. This is
363    unsurprising, given the complexity and richness of the visual world, compared to the sparsity of natural
364    language, however, it is somewhat surprising that the entropy is not massively different from natural
365
       languages, suggesting that visual tokenizers are capable of reducing the richness of natural language
       to suitably sparse representations for reasoning. Notably different is the “compressibility” of the token
366
       streams. While natural language tokens are highly compressible using Huffman encoding, visual languages
367
       are almost incompressible, suggesting that information is highly distributed amongst the tokens and that
368    there is very little structural reuse between the different images. While we explore grammars further in
369    section 3, this experiment indicates that it is unlikely that models have non-trivial grammars of tokens,
370    instead, these tokens are more local, and particularly high-variance.
371
       These experiments have several potential implications for model design. First, since visual tokens have
372    significantly higher entropy and lower compressibility, it may be necessary to use more attention heads,
373    deeper models, and more dense embeddings, in visual-based models in order to capture a sufficient number
374    of relationships and higher-level representations of visual information. Models like LLaVA (Liu et al.,
375    2024) with simple projection layers between the visual token and text token spaces may not perform
376    as well on downstream visual tasks as models such as mPlug (Ye et al., 2024) which have more dense
377    transformer-based adapters (a result which is empirically verified by Tong et al. (2024), who leverage
       a spatially aware dense connector to achieve significant performance improvements).

391   2.6    TOKEN SEGMENTATION GRANULARITY
392
393   One common question for many vision researchers is: “do visual tokens represent objects?” Indeed, while
394   visual tokens are spatially fixed to patches in the image, because of the VQ-VAE training process, it is un-
395
      clear if they take on additional non-spatial semantic meaning. Recently, Hsu et al. (2021) demonstrated that
      in audio domains, HuBERT tokens (audio-tokens) have relatively high mutual information with phoneme
396
      representations of audio, suggesting that self-supervised models are capable of learning natural structures
397
      despite being segmented to fixed-width patches. Can we answer this question for visual languages as well?
398
399
      Recently, Myers-Dean et al. (2024) introduced the SPIN dataset, a new labeled dataset of hierarchically
      segmented objects, where the objects are labeled at the whole, part, and sub-part levels. This gives us
400
      per-image annotations of the existence of wholes, parts, and sub-parts. From this, we compute several
401
      measures of natural correlation between these part-annotations and the visual token languages, inspired
402   by Hsu et al. (2021) (For more details, see Appendix I): Part Purity, a metric that measures the average
403   accuracy of assigning a visual-token to its most likely part label, reflecting image-level part consistency
404   within a particular visual token, Visual-Token Purity, a metric that assesses how well images containing
405   the same part label are consistently assigned to the same visual-tokens and Part-Normalized Mutual
406   Information, an information-theoretic metric which measures the percentage of uncertainty about the
407   part-label eliminated after observing a particular visual token.
408   The results are summarized in Table 4. In general, tokenizers appear to be most effective at capturing
409   part-level representations, as evidenced by consistently higher Part Purity (PP) values for parts compared
410   to wholes or sub-parts across all models. This suggests that tokenizers are better aligned with mid-level
411   structures (parts), rather than whole objects or fine-grained sub-parts. However, Visual-Token Purity
412   (VTP) remains low across all models and levels of granularity, indicating that images containing the
413   same part-label are not consistently assigned to the same visual tokens, reflecting fragmentation in the
414
      clustering. PNMI values are generally higher for sub-parts than for parts or wholes, particularly in models
      like llamagen-vq-ds16-c2i, which shows the highest PNMI across all levels. This implies that
415
      tokenizers can capture more fine-grained information at the sub-part level, though the corresponding
416
      decrease in part purity for sub-parts suggests that while they can reduce uncertainty about part labels, their
417   actual clustering of sub-parts is inconsistent.

      3     ARE VISUAL LANGUAGES STRUCTURED LIKE NATURAL LANGUAGES?
421
      In subsection 2.5 we showed that visual languages are not very compressible using Huffman encodings,
422   suggesting that visual languages may not have hierarchical structures similar to those of natural languages.
423   To inquire further into this question, we test whether Context-free Grammars (Chomsky & Schützenberger,
424   1959) can approximate the structure of visual languages as well as they can natural languages by fitting
425   grammars to each modality using unsupervised grammar induction techniques.
426   Particularly, we use Compound Probabilistic Context-Free Grammars (C-PCFG) (Kim et al., 2019) as the
427   grammar formalism for our experiments. C-PCFGs are a type of neural PCFG, where grammar production
428   rules are modeled as compound probability distributions (Robbins, 1956) – every production depends
429   on both the set of symbols in the grammar as well as a global latent variable z. This formulation, trained
430   with variational methods, allows for global sentence information to flow through all parsing decisions
431   in a sentence while remaining compatible with efficient inference methods which standard PCFGs enjoy
      (Baker, 1979). For more details on C-PCFGs see Appendix J.2.

501   3.1   TOPOLOGICAL SIMILARITY
502
      To expand our discussion on structural similarity, we further investigate how similar the topological
503
      structures of visual and textual tokens are, and whether these similarities can reveal meaningful insights
504   about the underlying representations, i.e. can we observe strong structural alignment points between the
505   natural and visual latent spaces, or are there notable deviations?
506
      We begin by training GloVe embeddings (Pennington et al., 2014) on co-occurrence matrices derived from
507
      visual tokens and textual tokens present in the captions (details in Appendix J). This gives us a continuous
508   topology of similar dimension within which we can explore potential alignment. We then explore two
509   pairwise distance matrices between the two GloVe vector spaces: Procrustes alignment (Gower, 1975)
510   and directed Haussdorf distance (Bowen, 1979).
511
      Figure J.2 gives the Procrustes similarity and Figure J.3 gives the directed Haussdorf distance between
512   the models, with some key aggregates summarized in Table 5. While there are few clear trends, a key
513   finding is that vision models are largely more aligned with natural language models than they are with
514   each other, with Chameleon being slightly more central than other models (perhaps due to its training
515   process). Overall, the lack of strong alignment trends between different vision models highlights that their
516   latent spaces are more fragmented, suggesting that visual token representations are often model-specific
517   or task-dependent, rather than universally structured. Notably, however, some languages align much better
518   with visual models than others (such as Korean to the Chameleon tokenizers, or Hungarian/Polish in
519   general), suggesting that some tokenizers may be significantly stronger when aligning to specific languages.
520
      Another interesting observation is that the directed Hausdorff distance shows that the natural language
      to vision model alignment is significantly further than the vision model to natural language alignment.
521
      This results implies that generation of images from text is much harder than the generation of text from
522
      images - something often observed in practice.
523
524
      Given the overall distances between these structural representations, our experiments suggest that future
      model architectures should focus on reducing this asymmetry. Specialized models that effectively encode
525
      multimodal information - and perhaps aligned tokenization methods (such as CLIP), represent promising
526
      future directions for research.

529   4     CONCLUSION
530
531
      This paper takes a first look at visual languages from the angle of empirical statistics. While there are
      similarities between how we currently treat visual and natural languages/sentences - the experiments in
532
      this paper show that, at least statistically, visual tokens and natural languages are far from trivially aligned.
533
      Such poor statistical alignments motivate both unique model architectures and training procedures for
534   visual transformers (summarized tabularly in Appendix K) - and we hope that this work inspires further
535   research into novel architectures, designs, and hyper-parameters for vision-token based models. Indeed,
536   while some of the hypotheses that we outlined in this paper have already been demonstrated, many of the
537   suggestions (such as increasing frequency penalties when decoding visual languages) remain untested in
538   practice - and it is interesting and necessary future work to close the loop on such potential modifications.
539   We hope, as a whole, that this work inspires additional research into fundamental statistics as a motivation
      for new architectural decisions and directions.

795

      A.1    DEFINING N-GRAMS FOR VISION TOKENIZERS
835
836   To define N-grams, we follow the procedure indicated in Figure 1 of the paper: tokens are first linearized
837   using a row-wise linearization scheme (as is done in traditional transformer approaches), giving a 1-D
838   sequence of tokens (x1,x2,...,xn). N-grams are then defined analogously to natural language, with 2-grams
839   being a sequence of all pairs of tokens (i.e. (x1,x2), (x2,x3), (x3,x4), etc.), 3-grams being a sequence of
840   all triplets of tokens (i.e. (x1,x2,x3), (x2,x3,x4), etc.) and other N-grams being defined similarly.

842   B     DATASETS
843
844   In this work, we explore the effects of tokenization across several datasets:
845   Conceptual Captions (12M) (Sharma et al., 2018): Conceptual captions (12M, CC12M) is a dataset with
846   approximately 12 million image-text pairs soruce from web alt-text, traditionally used for vision-language
847   pre-training.
848   MS-COCO (Lin et al., 2014): The MS-COCO dataset is a dataset for image description containing 328K
849   images, each with 5 ground truth descriptions in English. In addition to the standard annotations, we also
850   leverage translated annotations from Thapliyal et al. (2022), which provide machine translations into 36
851   languages for each of the MS-COCO images.
852   ImageNet (ILSVRC) (Deng et al., 2009): ImageNet contains approximately 1.2M images which are
853   manually annotated to indicate the objects present in each image. These annotations are linked to the
854   WordNet hierarchy, providing a rich set of object categories. The dataset covers 1,000 object classes for
855   the classification task, including common objects like animals, vehicles, and household items.
856   XM-3600 (Thapliyal et al., 2022): The Crossmodal-3600/XM3600 dataset is a multilingual multimodal
857   evaluation dataset designed to support image captioning tasks across 36 languages. It consists of 3600
858   geographically diverse images, each annotated with human-generated captions that are consistent across
859   languages but not derived from direct translations, ensuring linguistic naturalness and cultural relevance.
860   The images were selected from regions where these languages are spoken, drawn from the Open Images
861   Dataset using a careful algorithm to ensure regional diversity.
862   SPIN (Myers-Dean et al., 2024): The SPIN (SubPartImageNet) dataset is a hierarchical semantic segmen-
863   tation dataset designed to provide detailed annotations for natural images at multiple levels of granularity,
      specifically focusing on objects, parts, and subparts. SPIN builds on the PartImageNet dataset, expanding

865   categories from diverse objects such as animals, vehicles, and human figures. The dataset contains 10,387

      The datasets explored in this work—Conceptual Captions (12M), MS-COCO, ImageNet (ILSVRC),
868
      XM-3600, and SPIN—present unique challenges and opportunities for token-based analysis in
869   vision-language models. Their diversity in scale, annotations, and contextual richness impacts the statistical
870   properties of the visual languages induced by tokenization. These properties are helpful for understanding
871   the analyses in this work, which directly influence the performance of models on multimodal tasks.
872

877   tokenization. ImageNet (ILSVRC), on the other hand, offers a well-structured dataset for studying token
878   representations in object-centric tasks. SPIN’s emphasis on hierarchical segmentation of images into
879   objects, parts, and sub-parts allows for detailed analysis of how tokenization captures different levels of

      to varying linguistic and cultural contexts, shedding light on its impact on model generalization.

885   C    LIMITATIONS
886
887   While this paper does have significant empirical results, we want to recognize the several potential
888   limitations/opportunities for future work:
889   Tokenizer Selection: While the paper does focus on a fairly wide range of common (and modern) visual
890   tokenizers, there is a fairly large potential selection of additional tokenizers that could be compared.
891   Indeed, a key limiting factor is that all of the tokenizers explored in this work are VQ-VAE based. As
892   discussed in subsection 2.1, a detailed analysis of continuous tokenizers (such as auto-encoders which
893   are KL-regularized, CLIP-style encoders, or BERT-style encoders) would provide significant additional
894   information. Directly applying natural language statistics to these continuous embeddings, however, is
895   non-trival, as to understand ideas of “token frequency” or “grammar”, such analyses would have to either (a)
896   be extended to the continuous domain, or (b) the tokens themselves would have to be quantized to discrete
897
      representations. For example, for entropy, continuous domain generalizations exist (such as differential
      entropy), however are challenging to quantify in higher dimensional spaces, and it remains unclear if such
898
      entropy values are comparable to those in the discrete domain. For Benford’s law, no such continuous
899
      domain generalization exists, and would have to be derived from first-principles. While it appears that there
900   is some intuition as to the underlying foundational principles behind Benford’s law (Becker et al., 2018),
901   simply deriving (and demonstrating) such a continuous generalization would be a significant undertaking.
902   Similar techniques would have to be derived for other methods such Yule-Simon laws or C-PCFGs.
903
      While is possible to perform analyses on quantized spaces of the continuous domain, treating the quantized
904   states as continuous variables, however doing so introduces significant quantization bias that can impact
905   the outcomes. For example when analyzing entropy in quantized spaces, the resolution of quantization
906   directly impacts the calculated entropy. Coarse quantization tends to underestimate the entropy by failing
907   to capture the full variability of the continuous domain, while fine quantization can overfit noise in the
908   data. Similarly, for Yule-Simon distributions, the observed frequencies of quantized states would have the
909   potential to reflect artifacts of binning rather than true reflections of the underlying continuous distribution.
910   Thus, the resulting power-law exponent might be systematically distorted, either attenuated or exaggerated,
911   based on the quantization scheme used.
912   Overall, we believe that such extensions are highly interesting, but are worthy of detailed analysis and
913   discussion which is outside the scope of this initial work.
914   Dataset Coverage: Another limiting factor of this research is the dataset coverage. While it is impossible
915   to analyze all data, visual information is highly diverse, and domains such as medical imaging, geospatial
916   imaging, or autonomous driving may have entirely different statistics. In general, however, we found that
917   across the datasets that we did use (which represent a fairly general slice of traditional training data), the
      statistical representations were similar. For example, it is fairly challenging to distinguish any dataset-level


                                                            17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      patterns in Figure D.1, which shows a per-dataset breakdown of the empirical token frequency distributions,
919   or Figure F.2 which shows the Yule-Simon fits for emprical token frequencies.
920
      Scan Order of Images: One of the notable limitations of this work is that we primarily investigate a linear
921
      row-wise scan order of the images. We primarily limit our experiments to this scan order as (1) this is the de-
922
      facto scan order used in all existing transformer-based tokenization schemes and (2) we do not want to intro-
923   duce further confounding analytical axes in this work. Exploring non-row-wise scan orders is, however, an
924   extremely interesting question. In our limited experimentation, we found that a row-wise scan order does not
925   significantly impact the explorations in the paper, as the majority of the analyses are scan-order independent.
926
      Token Granularity and Semantic Understanding: Although granularity analysis is insightful, a deeper
927   examination of how well visual tokens capture complex semantic meaning in images (e.g., context, object
928   relationships, or scene understanding) remains future research. We strongly believe that future research
929   should explore how visual tokens represent not just parts of objects but also their roles in broader scenes
930   or tasks requiring semantic understanding (e.g., visual reasoning, narrative generation), however such
931   explorations would require signficant new labeled data, or novel statistical approaches.
932   Visual Tokens in Video Data: In tasks like video understanding or motion tracking, the temporal
933   relationships between visual tokens might reveal additional complexities not captured in static image
934   analysis. Future research could explore how the behavior of visual tokens changes in sequential or temporal
935   data settings and whether current statistical patterns hold when accounting for time.

952   E    HEAPS’/HERDAN’S LAW
953
954   Heaps’ law (also referred to as Herdan’s law) is an empirical rule that describes the relationship between the
955   size of a corpus and the number of unique word in the corpus (Heaps, 1978; Herdan, 1964). Specifically,
956   it predicts that as the size of a text grows, the number of unique words increases, but at a decreasing rate.
957   Mathematically, the law is described by:
958
                                                      V (N)=kN β                                          (E.1)
959
      where V(N) is the number of distinct words (the vocabulary size), N is the total number of words, and k
960
      and β are parameters, 0<β <1. Heaps’ law reflects the fact that even as new text is added to a corpus, the
961
      frequency of newly introduced words diminishes, meaning a large corpus doesn’t proportionally expand
962   its vocabulary.

1017   F.1    EXPERIMENTAL DESIGN
1018
       For each dataset and tokenizer configuration on the COCO and XM-3600 datasets, we fit the Yule-Simon dis-
1019
       tribution to the observed token frequency distributions by minimizing the negative log-likelihood using the
1020   L-BFGS-B optimization algorithm. This method is selected due to its ability to handle the bound constraints
1021   placed on the parameter α, ensuring that α>0. The optimization starts with an initial guess of α=1.0, and
1022   the negative log-likelihood is computed based on the observed token frequencies. The optimization process
1023   continues until convergence, with the final α value corresponding to the best-fit parameter for the Yule-
1024   Simon distribution. Invalid α values are penalized by assigning an infinite log-likelihood to ensure feasible
1025   solutions. Once the optimal α is found, we compute the empirical PMF from the frequency distributions by
       normalizing the observed token counts. In parallel, the theoretical PMF is computed using the fitted α value.

         G              BENEFORDS LAW
1065
1066
         Benford’s Law (Benford, 1938) describes the distribution of leading digits in many naturally occurring
         datasets, where smaller digits are more likely to appear as the first digit. Specifically, the probability P (d)
1067
         of a digit d (where d is between 1 and 9) being the leading digit is given by:
1068
1069
1070
                                                                                         1
                                                                          P (d)=log10 1+                                                                        (G.1)
1071
                                                                                         d
1072     According to this law, the number 1 appears as the first digit around 30% of the time, while larger digits
1073     like 9 appear less frequently, around 5% of the time.
1074     For each dataset and tokenization configuration, we extract n-grams (with n = 1, 2, and 3) from tokenized
1075     text and image data. We aggregate the token frequencies by computing the distribution of the first digits
1076     of these counts. Specifically, the first digits of each token frequency are extracted, and their occurrences
1077     are counted to form a first-digit distribution. In cases where natural language data is available, we also
1078     compute aggregate distributions across multiple locales for text-based tokenizations. The aggregated text
1079     distributions include the mean, standard deviation, minimum, and maximum values for each first-digit
         count across different locales.

1115   H                                         HUFFMAN ENCODING / ENTROPY
1116
1117
1118   Huffman encoding is a widely-used algorithm for lossless data compression, which assigns variable-length
1119   codes to tokens based on their frequencies. The core idea is to minimize the total number of bits required
1120
       to represent the token stream by assigning shorter codes to more frequent tokens and longer codes to less
       frequent ones. This is achieved by constructing a binary tree where each token is a leaf, and its depth
1121
       (or code length) corresponds to its frequency. The encoding process ensures that the total number of bits,
1122
       LHuffman , needed to encode a stream of tokens is reduced compared to fixed-length encoding, where each
1123   token would require ⌈log2(n)⌉ bits, with n being the number of unique tokens.
1124
       Entropy, denoted as H(X), represents the theoretical limit on the average number of bits needed to encode
1125
       the token stream, and is calculated using Shannon’s entropy formula:
1126
1127                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                   X
1128                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                    H(X)=−                                                                 P (x)log2 P (x)                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                     (H.1)
                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                     x∈X
1129
1130   where P (x) is the empirical probability of token x in the stream. In this experiment, entropy serves as
1131   a benchmark for comparing the performance of Huffman encoding. The closer the average code length of
1132   the Huffman encoding is to the entropy, the more efficient the compression. By evaluating the compression
1133   rate and percentage reduction, we can quantify how effectively Huffman encoding reduces the bit length
       compared to the fixed-length encoding, with the goal of approaching the entropy limit.

1177   H.1                                    EXPERIMENTAL DESIGN
1178
       In our experiments, for each dataset/tokenizer combination (both N =1 (unigrams) and N =2 (bigrams)),
1179
       we extract the first 500,000 tokens as a token stream, and then apply Huffman encoding to compress this
1180
       token stream. We extract several key metrics from the resulting encoding:
1181
1182                                           • Average Code Length: The weighted average of the lengths of Huffman codes for all tokens.
1183                                           • Entropy: The theoretical minimum average code length for the specified token distribution (See
1184                                             Equation H.1).
1185                                           • Fixed Code Length: The length of the fixed-length codes used for comparison.
1186
                                               • Original Bits: The number of bits required for fixed-length encoding of the token stream.
1187
                                               • Huffman bits: The number of bits required after applying Huffman encoding.

1441   Part Purity: Part purity describes the average probability of the most likely part-label for each visual-token,
1442   representing how accurately parts are assigned to the corresponding visual tokens. It is computed as:
1443                                         Part Purity (PP) =Ez [p(y∗(z)|z)]                                    (I.1)
1444   where z is a visual token cluster, y∗(z) denotes the most likely part-label for a given visual-token z,
1445   p(y∗ (z)|z) is the conditional probability of the most likely part-label y∗ (z) given the visual-token z, and
1446   Ez is the expectation over all visual tokens. In practice, we draw these probabilities from the normalized
1447   empirical co-occurrence matrix.
1448   Visual Token Purity: Visual token purity measures how well images containing the same part-label are
1449   consistently assigned to the same visual tokens. It is computed as:
1450                                   Visual Token Purity (VTP) =Ey [p(z∗(y)|y)]                               (I.2)

1453   expectation over all part-labels. Similar to part-purity, these probabilities are derived from the normalized
1454   empirical co-occurrence matrix.

1481
1482   In order to get continuous representations of the vision and language token spaces, we employ GloVe
1483
       embeddings Pennington et al. (2014). GloVe (Global Vectors for Word Representation) is a word
       embedding technique that captures semantic relationships between words by training on global word
1484
       co-occurrence statistics. Unlike local context methods like Word2Vec (Church, 2017), GloVe constructs
1485
       a matrix from word co-occurrence counts in a corpus and factorizes this matrix to generate dense vector
1486   representations. These embeddings reflect the relative meanings of words, allowing similar words to
1487   have similar vectors in the latent space. GloVe aims to learn word embeddings by factorizing a token
1488   co-occurrence matrix. The model minimizes a weighted least squares objective function:

1494   discount frequent co-occurrences.

1550   J.3     PROCRUSTES ANALYSIS
1551
1552   Procrustes Analysis is a statistical method used to compare the shapes or structures of two datasets by
1553   finding an optimal transformation (including translation, scaling, and rotation) that minimizes the distance
1554   between corresponding points in the datasets. The resulting transformation provides insight into how
1555   closely the datasets align in their geometry. Procrustes Analysis minimizes the distance between two
1556
       matrices X and Y by finding the optimal translation, scaling, and rotation. The goal is to solve:
1557
1558                                               min ∥bXR+c−Y ∥F                                             (J.3)
                                                   R,b,c
1559
1560   where: X and Y are the two point sets (matrices) being compared, R is the optimal rotation matrix, b
1561   is a scaling factor, c is the translation vector, and |·∥F is the Frobenius norm.
1562   For Procrustes analysis, it is required that the two matrices to be aligned have identical shape. Because
1563   the number of tokens is different in the vision and language cases, in our experiments we use K-means
1564   to quantize the different token embedding spaces to 256 centers, which we then compare topologically.
1565   This has the downside of reducing the topological comparisons to more global structure comparisons,
       however means that we can run experiments on point-to-point coherence.

1644   J.4                DIRECTED HAUSDORFF DISTANCE
1645   Directed Hausdorff Distance is a measure used to quantify the degree of mismatch between two point sets by
1646   calculating the greatest distance from a point in one set to the nearest point in the other set. It considers only
1647   the largest such deviation in one direction, making it useful for determining the extent to which one set is
1648   contained within or approximates another. The directed Hausdorff distance from set A to set B is defined as:
1649
1650
                                                                                                                                                                  dH (A,B)=maxmin∥a−b∥                                                                                                                                                                                (J.4)
1651                                                                                                                                                                                            a∈A b∈B

1652   where A and B are two point sets, and ∥a−b∥ is the Euclidean distance between point a∈A and point b∈B.
1653
       Similar to Figure J.2, for simplicity and clarity, in Figure J.3 we replace the distance with 1 − distance
1654
       to get a similarity measure, and set the diagonal to 0 (even though the diagonal similarity is naturally 1),
1655   in order to avoid contrast issues on the off-diagonals.
