009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011             While Large Language Models (LLMs) have exhibited impressive performance in
012             long-form question-answering tasks, they frequently present a hazard of produc-
013             ing factual inaccuracies or hallucinations. An effective strategy to mitigate this
014             hazard is to leverage off-the-shelf LLMs to detect hallucinations after the gen-
015
                eration. The primary challenge resides in the comprehensive elicitation of the
                intrinsic knowledge acquired during their pre-training phase. However, existing
016
                methods that employ complex reasoning chains predominantly fall short of ad-
017
                dressing this issue. Moreover, since existing methods for hallucination detection
018             tend to decompose the text into isolated statements, they are unable to understand
019             the inherent in-context semantics in long-form content. In this paper, we pro-
020             pose a novel framework, SelfElicit, which synergizes the self-elicitation of intrin-
021             sic knowledge of large language models and long-form continuity understanding.
022             Specifically, we leverage self-generated thoughts derived from prior statements as
023             catalysts to elicit the expression of intrinsic knowledge, which is integrated with
024             graph structures to alleviate induced hallucinations and guide the factual evalua-
025             tion by effectively organizing the elicited knowledge. Extensive experiments on
026
                real-world QA datasets demonstrate the effectiveness of self-elicitation and the
                superiority of our proposed method.

      1       I NTRODUCTION
030
031
      Large Language Models (LLMs) pre-trained on massive text corpora and fine-tuned to follow hu-
032
      man instructions have shown remarkable performance in various neutral language tasks (Bai et al.,
033   2023; Touvron et al., 2023; GLM et al., 2024). However, there remains a concern regarding their
034   tendency to generate hallucinations (Bang et al., 2023), producing sentences with plausible looking
035   yet factually unsupported content (Huang et al., 2023)1 and hurting their reliability in real-world
036   scenarios expecting factually-accurate responses (Wei et al., 2024). For example, a model-generated
037   non-factual statement “Gliclazide can be taken at any time of the day×, regardless of whether it is
038   on an empty stomach or after meals×” might mislead patients into taking medication at incorrect
039   times since this medication is recommended to be taken with the meal (NHS, 2024). An important
040   strategy to alleviate hallucinations is to detect hallucinations after the generation (Lee et al., 2023;
041
      Manakul et al., 2023; Mishra et al., 2024; Guan et al., 2024).
042   Numerous methods have been proposed for the hallucination detection task. Several methods rely
043   on retrieval (Min et al., 2023; Xia et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023b; Wei et al., 2024; Yue et al., 2024;
044   Sansford et al., 2024) or probes (Li et al., 2023a; Zhang et al., 2024a; Chuang et al., 2024; Wang
045   et al., 2024), but external databases or probe training corpus are not always available in all scenarios.
046   Therefore, many studies focus on using the intrinsic capabilities of off-the-shelf LLMs acquired
047
      through pre-training, where the key challenge is how to effectively elicit the intrinsic knowledge
      from the models. Some methods prompt to model to implicitly utilize their knowledge to identify
048
      hallucinations by assessing confidence levels (Kadavath et al., 2022; Mahaut et al., 2024; Zhao et al.,
049
      2024). In contrast, other methods explicitly elicit intrinsic knowledge to enhance detection accuracy.
050   For example, some works (Manakul et al., 2023; Mündler et al., 2024; Miao et al., 2024) prompt
051   the model to generate statements from various perspectives and contrast these statements to quantify

069   increase the risk of accumulated inaccuracies and hallucinations.
070
      Additionally, an inherent characteristic of long-form content is the in-context semantics among sen-
071
      tences, a logical and consistent relationship between different elements of meaning, such as coher-
072
      ence, comparison, and causality. For instance, the preceding statement, “Gliclazide is an oral hypo-
073   glycemic medication” and the subsequent statement, “It is suitable for adult type 2 diabetes patients
074   whose blood sugar cannot be adequately controlled by diet alone” demonstrate logical coherence
075   and progression. The first statement identifies the category and function of the medication while the
076   second statement further elaborates on its medical application. However, existing long-form hallu-
077   cination detection methods (Zhang et al., 2020; Min et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024a)
078   generally decompose the long-form text into isolated statements that each is fact-checked individ-
079   ually (Figure 1 (a)), overlooking such semantic continuity and limiting their reasoning capabilities.
080   Providing prior contextual information to models (Figure 1 (b)) can present a more natural chain of
081
      meanings, thereby benefiting both the understanding and evaluation of subsequent statements.
082   In this work, we present SelfElicit, an integrated framework designed to effectively elicit a model’s
083   intrinsic knowledge and utilize semantic continuity to improve hallucination detection in long-form
084   content. Specifically, it follows an iterative process in which the model first evaluates the factuality
085   of statements conditioned on prior contextual information. It then engages in reflection to elicit the
086   intrinsic knowledge and finally incorporates these reflections as context to enhance subsequent eval-
087
      uations (Figure 1 (c)). To mitigate hallucinations arising during the self-elicit process, we integrate a
      knowledge hypergraph into the iterative framework, which facilitates knowledge retention, dedupli-
088
      cation, and resolution of inconsistencies. Our extensive experiments demonstrate that self-eliciting
089
      can act as an effective catalyst to improve both the factuality and diversity of models’ knowledge
090   expression and our method outperforms existing methods for long-form hallucination detection. To
091   sum up, our contributions include:
092
093
               • We study a novel concept of self-eliciting large language models for hallucination detection.
                 We show that using self-generated thoughts from prior statements as catalysts prompts the
094
                 models to effectively express intrinsic knowledge and facilitates hallucination detection.
095
096            • We propose a new framework, SelfElicit, for long-form hallucination detection, which syn-
097
                 ergizes the self-eliciting mechanism with semantic continuity understanding. We design a
                 knowledge hypergraph to carefully organize the elicited knowledge and effectively alleviate
098
                 hallucination snowballing.
099
100
               • SelfElicit framework consistently demonstrates superior performance in long-form halluci-
                 nation detection using real-world datasets with modern language models. We further show
101
                 that self-eliciting enhances knowledge expression with better factuality and diversity.

122   2.2   K NOWLEDGE H YPERGRAPH
123
124   A knowledge hypergraph is used to store and describe the relationships of knowledge statements
125   with a graph structure. Each vertice v refers to an entity. Each hyperedge e connecting any number
126   of vertices refers to a knowledge relating to these entities, which are denoted as e.nodes. For
127   example, edge “The mechanism of Gliclazide is to lower blood glucose by stimulating pancreatic
128   β-cells to secrete insulin“ connects vertices “Gliclazide”, “blood glucose”, “pancreatic β-cells”,
129
      and “insulin” as the statement is directly related to these concepts. We denote a graph as:
130                                                      G = (V, E),
131
132   where V and E respectively refer to the vertice set and the edge set. Compared with vanilla knowl-
133   edge graphs constructed by triples symbolizing knowledge regarding only two entities, a hyper-
134
      edge interconnects any number of entities and thus is more suitable for describing complex knowl-
      edge (Chen et al., 2024).

137   3     M ETHODOLOGY
138
139   Figure 2 provides an overview of our framework. Given long-form content to be fact-checked, we
140   first extract important entities and statements representing the knowledge to be checked. We then
141   present the framework along with a knowledge hypergraph to iteratively evaluate the factuality of
142   each statement via (1) sampling on the graph to acquire contextual information, (2) evaluating the
143   factuality of each statement and eliciting the intrinsic knowledge by reflection, and (3) updating the
144   graph to retain the elicited thoughts and resolving inconsistencies that might suggest fabrication or
145
      induced hallucinations.

185
186   Knowledge Sampling. Given graph Gi−1 = (V, Ei−1 ) retaining self-generated knowledge dur-
187   ing prior evaluation of statements {s1 , s2 , · · · , si−1 }, a graph sampling procedure is conducted to
188   provide contextual information and intermediate thoughts to the evaluation of current statement si .
189   Specifically, we extract sub-graphs from Gi−1 that are most relevant to si . A set of relative entities
190   Vi is first identified by word matching, i.e. Vi = {vj |vj in si , vj ∈ V}. Then, sub-graphs with
191
      multiple granularities are extracted using the combinations of the relative entities as queries:
192
                                             V̂i (k) = Combine(Vi , k),                                                                                      (3)
193
194                                          Êi (k) = {e|e.nodes == V̂i (k), e ∈ Ei−1 },                                                                    (4)
195                                             Êi = ∪{Êi (k)|α ≤ k ≤ β},                                                                                  (5)
196
197   where Combine(·) refers to k-length combinations of elements Vi . α and β are hyperparameters
198
      balancing the relevance and scope. Lower α refers to a more relaxed matching strategy for a wider
      sampling scope, while higher α refers to a stricter matching strategy for contextual information with
199
200
      stronger relevance. Finally, all sampled edges Êi are linearized to obtain contextual statements Ci .
201   Fact-Evaluation. Following (Kadavath et al., 2022; Manakul et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2024; Tian
202   et al., 2024), we prompt the models to evaluate the correctness of a given statement si by asking
203   whether the statement is True, False, or Not Sure. This straightforward prompt has shown
204   relatively stable and competitive performance (Zhao et al., 2024; Mahaut et al., 2024). We prepend
205   the sampled contextual statements Ci to the prompt to leverage semantic continuity for better un-
206
      derstanding and reasoning.
207   The probabilities of True and False tokens are obtained at the first output token position and
208   normalized. The latter is regarded as the final hallucination score of the statement si , denoting as p̂i .
209
      Intrinsic Knowledge Elicitation. Language models pre-trained on a large corpus abstract the fac-
210   tual knowledge in their weights, i.e. intrinsic knowledge (Petroni et al., 2019). Efforts have been
211   made to elicit the intrinsic knowledge to facilitate fact-checking (Weller et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024b;
212   Manakul et al., 2023; Miao et al., 2024; Mündler et al., 2024; Dhuliawala et al., 2024; Zhao et al.,
213   2024). Nevertheless, we conclude that these methods mostly have complicated reasoning chains,
214   and tend to suffer from induced hallucinations or accumulated inaccuracy, limiting their overall ca-
215   pacity for elicitation. Moreover, we argue that prompting the model to provide reflections on the
      evaluation is a more convenient method, which guides the model to provide elaborations on its judg-

242   Conflicts Resolving. However, LLMs might produce hallucinations during the reflection pro-
243
      cess, especially when reflecting on ambiguous or unfamiliar statements. Similar to previous
      works (Mündler et al., 2024; Yehuda et al., 2024), we notice that in such cases, the generated state-
244
      ments tend to be inconsistent with each other, appearing to have identical entities yet contradictory
245
      meanings. The phenomenon of inconsistency can also be found when two sentences from the origi-
246   nal response contradict each other, which might indicate faithfulness hallucinations.
247
248   To this end, it is crucial to carefully resolve the inconsistencies to avoid the propagation of hal-
249
      lucinations (Zhang et al., 2023a). Specifically, we predict the semantic relationship between the
      conflictive statements, enew ∈ Enew  i    and eorig ∈ Ei−1 , that share identical vertice sets, i.e.
250
      enew .nodes == eorig .nodes. A Natural Language Inference (NLI) method is utilized to predict
251
      their semantic relationship and resolve conflicts:
252
253          • Neutral: The two statements describe different entities, or different aspects of the same
254            entities, and can coexist. We keep both statements in the updated graph.
255          • Entail: The content of the two statements is identical, describing the same aspect of the
256            same entities, with consistent meaning. We replace the original statement with the new one
257            to avoid duplication.
258          • Contradict. The two statements describe the same aspect of the same entities, but their
259            meanings are directly opposite, presenting a contradiction. In this case, we ask the model
260            to contrast these statements and revise them for a final resolution.
261
      In practice, we can either use a pre-trained NLI model or prompt LLMs to predict the semantic
262
      relationships (see Appendix D.3). The resolving process between two conflictive edges is conducted
263
      iteratively until all candidate edges in Enew
                                                i   have been incorporated into the graph.
264

267
      porated into the graph, facilitating subsequent evaluation and elicitation.

294   4.1     E XPERIMENTAL S ETUPS
295
      We conduct long-form hallucination detection experiments on two medical datasets (MedHallu-
296
      ZH and MedHallu-EN dataset, see Appendix C.2), and a biography dataset (WikiBio (Manakul
297   et al., 2023)) with the following off-the-shelf language models: Qwen1.5-7B-chat (Qwen (Bai et al.,
298   2023)), Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct (Qwen2 (Bai et al., 2023)), ChatGLM3-6B (GLM (GLM et al., 2024)),
299   Llama2-7B-chat (Llama2 (Touvron et al., 2023)), Llama-3-8B-Instruct (Llama3 (AI@Meta,
300   2024)), and GPT4o-mini. All language models use greedy decoding (temperature=0) during text
301   generation for stable outputs. All experiments are conducted with transformers (Wolf et al., 2020)
302   4.43.0 on a Centos machine with Nvidia A800-80G GPUs.
303   Baselines. We compare our method with the following baselines, including classic self-eval (IO (Ka-
304   davath et al., 2022; Mahaut et al., 2024)), long-form enhanced methods (ContextIO, HistoryIO),
305   and methods with various elicitation approaches: chain-of-thought (CoT (Wei et al., 2022) and
306   FaR (Zhao et al., 2024)), self-ask (CoVE (Dhuliawala et al., 2024)), and self-consistency (Self-
307   CheckGPT (Manakul et al., 2023), ChatProtect (Mündler et al., 2024)). For all methods, we use
308   an identical IO prompt after their original procedures to obtain the hallucination score for a fair
309   comparison (i.e. only elicitation approaches are different). The details are listed in Appendix C.1.
310   Metrics. Hallucination detection is a classification task, where positive labels refer to non-factual
311   statements. We use F 1 and AU ROC as metrics, for sentence-wise and response-wise predictions.
312   Since the threshold variance affects the metrics (Huang et al., 2024), we search for the best threshold
313   values with the highest sentence/response F1 metrics independently on the validation set and regard
314   non-factual scores larger than the thresholds as positive predictions on the test set.

316   4.2     M AIN R ESULTS
317
      Table 1 shows the overall detection results. Methods requiring multi-step reasoning (CoT, CoVE,
318   SelfCheckGPT, and ChatProtect) generally have inferior performance compared with other meth-
319   ods. This observation is partial because the primary benefit of multi-step reasoning comes in the
320   ability to execute symbolic steps and track the output (Sprague et al., 2024), rather than directly

      5.2   C ONNECTION WITH RAG
506
507   SelfElicit and retrieval-argument generation (RAG) (Jin et al., 2024; Luo et al., 2024; Sun et al.,
508   2024) share some similarities in their schemas: sampling relative knowledge from a knowledge
509   graph to facilitate the down-streaming tasks. Recent works (Sansford et al., 2024; Yuan et al., 2024;
510   Niu et al., 2024) have demonstrated the performance gain to incorporate external knowledge graphs
511   for hallucination detection. Differently, our work organizes a knowledge graph elicited from the
512
      model itself, rather than relying on external databases. Moreover, compared with RAG methods
      where databases are stand-alone, the self-elicited knowledge hypergraph in our framework is depen-
513
      dent on the model and evolves in parallel with the evaluation process. Theoretically, our method is
514
      orthogonal to these RAG methods and can be integrated with these methods into a unified design,
515   which might further benefit both the elicitation and hallucination detection.
516
517
518   5.3   L IMITATIONS
519
520   Some of the limitations are: (1) this paper primarily focuses on technological methods to elicit the
521   intrinsic knowledge of models, leaving the question of whether LLMs either abstract knowledge
522
      over linguistic forms or merely memorize statements (Carlini et al., 2022) to future works. (2)
      Due to the lack of large-scale, long-form datasets with domain expertise, we focus on two medical
523
      datasets collected from an online QA platform and a biography dataset. Although the models are
524
      not specialized for specific domains, conducting experiments on other domains would provide more
525   comprehensive and credible conclusions. (3) Since the capacity of the models theoretically restricts
526   the performance upper bound, methods for continual improvements remain an open question. (4)
527   The sampling and conflict detection strategies will fail in some specific cases and such failure might
528   accumulate during the iteration.

      6     C ONCLUSION
532
      In this paper, we have investigated the task of detecting hallucinations from long-form content. Ex-
533
      isting methods predominantly fall short of comprehensively elicitating the intrinsic knowledge of
534   models and overlook the semantic continuity within long-form content. To address these issues,
535   we present a novel framework, SelfElicit, that uses self-generated thoughts from prior statements
536   to elicit the models’ intrinsic knowledge. It is integrated with a knowledge hypergraph to enable
537   effective knowledge organization via retention, deduplication, and inconsistency mitigation, there-
538   fore synergizing self-elicitation and contextual understanding in a unified diagram. Experiments on
539   real-world, multilingual datasets with modern large language models have shown the effectiveness
      of self-elicitating and demonstrated the superiority of the proposed framework.

759   A.1   H ALLUCINATION D ETECTION
760
761
      Retrieval-argument methods. Extracting relevant knowledge from external authentic database and
      incorporating it with the query is a common way of detecting hallucination (Min et al., 2023; Tian
762
      et al., 2024; Gou et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024b; Xia et al., 2024). (Li et al., 2023b; Yu et al., 2023;
763
      Wei et al., 2024) proposed to update the retrieval results with LLM until the retrieved documents
764   adequately support answering the questions. (Kamoi et al., 2024), (Yuan et al., 2024) and (Sansford
765   et al., 2024) extracted keywords as entities and knowledge as triples and retrieved reference triples
766   from knowledge graphs or texts. Additionally, (Yue et al., 2024) contrasted the supportive arguments
767   and refuting arguments derived from retrieval evidence.
768
      Innerstate-based methods. Innerstate-based methods aim to understand the hallucination within
769
      the hidden activations of deeper model layers (Azaria & Mitchell, 2023; Zhang et al., 2024b; Wang
770   et al., 2024). They usually required probes pre-trained on a specific dataset to detect the hallucina-
771   tions (Li et al., 2023a; Zhang et al., 2024a).
772
773
      Uncertainty-based methods. We categorize existing uncertainty-based hallucination detection
      methods into three categories. (1) Some methods focus on the token probabilities of white-box
774
      LLMs. (Kadavath et al., 2022; Tian et al., 2024) proposed a calibration-based method to evaluate
775
      the correctness of the content with multiple-choice questions. Extending the token entropy estima-
776   tion (Manakul et al., 2023) with keyword focusing, (Zhang et al., 2023b) proposed to penalize the
777   attention score of the hallucinated token to avoid snowballing (Zhang et al., 2023a). FaR (Zhao
778   et al., 2024) elicited the intrinsic knowledge relevant to the query and reflected on the knowledge
779   to improve the calibration. (2) Some methods propose to ask LLMs to express their uncertainty
780   verbally (Mahaut et al., 2024). (Tao et al., 2024) leverages reinforcement learning guided by a tai-
781   lored dual-component reward function. (3) Other methods aim at the semantic consistency over
782   sentences (Kuhn et al., 2023; Manakul et al., 2023; Mündler et al., 2024; Miao et al., 2024). Self-
783   CheckGPT (Manakul et al., 2023) and (Kuhn et al., 2023) and (Farquhar et al., 2024) estimated
784
      the variance of the meaning of generated content. (Cohen et al., 2023) discovered the inconsis-
      tencies with the interaction between LLMs. InterrogateLLM (Yehuda et al., 2024) reversed the
785
      query-response pair and estimated the variation of reconstructed queries for semantic uncertainty.
786
      ChatProtect (Mündler et al., 2024) and SelfCheck (Miao et al., 2024) detected hallucinations by
787   comparing the original content and the regenerated one. EVER (Kang et al., 2023), CoVE (Dhu-
788   liawala et al., 2024), (Zhang et al., 2024c), (Farquhar et al., 2024), and QuestGen (Setty & Setty,
789   2024) generated questions corresponding to each fact within the content, answered the generated
790   question, and measured the coherence between the answer and the original content.
791
      Compared with the above works, our method uses self-generated thoughts as a catalyst to elicit
792
      intrinsic knowledge, without external databases, finetuning, or complex multi-step reasoning, while
793   the iterative schema can capture the semantic continuity of long-form content.
794

796   A.2   L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS WITH K NOWLEDGE G RAPHS
797
798   Efforts have been made to facilitate large language models for reasoning or factuality with knowl-
799   edge graphs. GoT (Besta et al., 2024) used a graph structure to guide the reasoning of LLMs.
800   (Yuan et al., 2024) proposed to extract knowledge graphs from external text databases and regarded
801
      fact-checking as a task of NLI. GraphRAG (Edge et al., 2024) built a graph-based text index by
      deriving entity knowledge graphs from the source documents and generating summaries for hi-
802
      erarchical graph communities. RoG (Luo et al., 2024) synergized LLMs reasoning with KGs to
803
      improve the ability of knowledge traceability and knowledge correctability. ToG (Sun et al., 2024)
804   and Graph-CoT (Jin et al., 2024) treated the LLM as an agent to interactively explore related entities
805   and relations on KGs and perform reasoning based on the retrieved knowledge. Re-KGR (Kamoi
806   et al., 2024) and StructGPT (Jiang et al., 2023) leveraged knowledge graphs as external databases
807   and directly retrieved reference information for factual QA. (Sansford et al., 2024) converted the
808   response into a candidate knowledge graph and fact-checked each individual triple in the graph.
809   Compared with the above methods, our method does not rely on external knowledge graphs but uses
      self-elicited knowledge to construct the graph to facilitate hallucination detection.

892
893   We have collected a substantial dataset, namely MedHallu, by collecting genuine user queries
894   and the corresponding responses generated by LLMs from an online healthcare QA platform. This
895   corpus mainly encompasses chronic diseases, cancer, and psoriasis and includes a Chinese version
896   (with postfix zh) and an English version (with postfix en). The query-response pairs are prepro-
897
      cessed with the following steps to obtain response-wise and sentence-wise hallucination labels.
898   Step 1: Parsing. The long-form response is first segmented into sentences by punctuation. Then,
899   following (Wei et al., 2024), GPT-4 is used to split sentences into atomic claims, which refers to a
900   fundamental unit for a piece of information.
901   Step 2: Labeling. We ask medical experts to label whether each LLM-generated response includes
902   any factual error or misunderstands the user query. Then, GPT-4 is used to label each sentence given
903   the response-wise human labels to obtain sentence-wise labels and claim-wise labels. The labeling
904   prompt is shown in Figure 13. We carefully check every positive sentence/claim labeled by GPT-4
905   such that they actually include factual errors.
906
      Step 3: Multi-language. We use GPT4 to translate the original Chinese QA pairs, sentences, and
907   claims into English. The prompt is constituted of the original user query and the LLM-generated
908   response and includes instructions to ask the LLM to take special care of the medical terminologies.
909   The translation prompt is shown in Figure 12.
910
      Step 4: Remove Duplicate & Split. Samples with duplicated queries and responses are re-
911
      moved. Then all samples are randomly shuffled and split into train/validation/test sets with a ratio
