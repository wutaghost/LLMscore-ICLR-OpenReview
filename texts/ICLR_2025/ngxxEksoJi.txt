009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We study quantile-optimal policy learning where the goal is to find a policy whose
012            reward distribution has the largest α-th quantile for some α ∈ (0, 1). We focus
013            on the offline setting whose generating process involves unobserved confounders.
014
               Such a problem suffers from three main challenges: (i) nonlinearity of the quantile
               objective as a functional of the reward distribution, (ii) unobserved confounding
015
               issue, and (iii) insufficient coverage of the offline dataset. To address these chal-
016            lenges, we propose a suite of causal-assisted policy learning methods that provably
017            enjoy strong theoretical guarantees under mild conditions. In particular, to address
018            (i) and (ii), using causal inference tools such as instrumental variables and nega-
019            tive controls, we propose to estimate the quantile objectives by solving nonlinear
020            functional integral equations. Then we adopt a minimax estimation approach with
021            nonparametric models to solve these integral equations, and propose to construct
022
               conservative policy estimates that address (iii). The final policy is the one that
               maximizes these pessimistic estimates. In addition, we propose a novel regular-

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Offline reinforcement learning (RL) (Levine et al., 2020; Prudencio et al., 2023) aims to learn the op-
033   timal decision-making policies from pre-collected datasets, where the learner has no control over the
034
      data collection process. This lack of control is further complicated by the fact that many real-world
      datasets suffer from incomplete information due to unmeasured confounders (Rubin, 1974)—factors
035
      influencing both the actions and rewards, yet not observed by the learner. These unobserved con-
036   founders naturally arise in the partially observable setting, where the behavior policy used to gener-
037   ate the actions is contingent on the state, while only noisy observations of the state are recorded in
038   the data. A prime example can be found in healthcare, where electronic health records (EHR) may
039   lack key details about a patient’s condition or treatment environment (Hernán & Robins, 2016), but
040   decisions about treatment still need to be optimized based on the available data.
041   In many decision-making scenarios, rather than expected rewards, we are interested in optimizing
042   more nuanced objectives involving quantiles of the reward distribution. For instance, to measure the
043   efficacy of job training programs, it may be more meaningful to optimize for the median income
044   increase, as the mean could be skewed by a few extreme cases. Moreover, quantile-based objectives
045   naturally arise when some notion of fairness is of interest (Yang et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2022).
046   In this paper, we tackle the problem of quantile-optimal policy learning in offline settings where
047   unmeasured confounders play a critical role. Our goal is to find a policy that maximizes the α-th
048   quantile of the reward distribution, conditioning on the context. Such a policy is learned on a pre-
049   collected offline dataset that involves unobserved confounders, which are some hidden variables that
050   simultaneously affect the rewards and actions stored in the data.
051   This problem is particularly challenging due to three core issues: (i) the quantile objective is a
052   nonlinear functional of the reward distribution, making statistical estimation more difficult; (ii) un-
053   measured confounders introduce bias into the estimation problem, which can lead to misleading
      results if not properly accounted for; and (iii) the offline dataset often lacks full coverage, meaning


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      that the distribution of the collected data might have insufficient overlap with that induced by some
055   candidate policy, making it challenging to evaluate the performance of that policy.
056
057
      To address these challenges, we propose a suite of novel, causal-assisted policy learning approaches.
      Our methods leverage powerful causal inference techniques, such as instrumental variables (IV)
058
      (Angrist et al., 1996; Baiocchi et al., 2014) and negative controls (NC) (Lipsitch et al., 2010; Tchet-
059   gen et al., 2020), to account for unmeasured confounding. Leveraging these causal inference tools,
060   we frame the quantile-based estimation problem as solving nonlinear functional integral equa-
061   tions, which can be solved using a minimax estimation approach with nonparametric models. This
062   enables us to address Challenges (i) and (ii). Furthermore, to handle Challenge (iii), we propose to
063   adopt the pessimism principle (Jin et al., 2021; Xie et al., 2021; Rashidinejad et al., 2021; Buckman
064   et al., 2020; Lu et al., 2022), which enables us to relax the requirement on data coverage. We in-
065   troduce two versions of the algorithms based on the pessimism idea – a constrained version and a
      regularized version. In particular, our method works as long as the offline data has sufficient cover-
066
      age over the optimal policy, and it does not matter whether the data contains sufficient information
067
      about the suboptimal policies — pessimism ignores policies with large uncertainty and enables us
068   to only focus on the policies that are covered by the dataset.
069
070   We prove that our algorithms achieve sample efficient O(n  e −1/2 ) suboptimality bounds, where n
071   is the sample size. Compared to existing works that study offline policy learning with confounded
      datasets aiming to maximize expected rewards, our analysis is more complicated due to the nonlinear
072
      nature of the quantile-based objective. In particular, to get the suboptimality bounds, we leverage
073
      the local curvature of the nonlinear operator in the functional estimating equation. We examine the
074   statistical error of the estimated quantile-based objective in terms of a local norm, defined using
075   the first-order Taylor expansion of the nonlinear operator. To our best knowledge, we establish the
076   first provably sample-efficient algorithms for quantile-optimal policy learning under unmeasured
077   confounding and insufficient support.

079   1.1   R ELATED W ORKS
080
081
      Offline Decision Making Our work is built upon the general framework of offline reinforcement
      learning (Yin et al., 2022; Uehara et al., 2021; Jin et al., 2021; Xie et al., 2021; Rashidinejad et al.,
082
      2021) and offline contextual bandits (Li et al., 2012; Lee et al., 2021; Metevier et al., 2019). In
083   particular, Cassel et al. (2023); Zhu & Tan (2020); Prashanth et al. (2020) study nonlinear objectives
084   in contextual bandits without confounding bias. To address the challenge of insufficient coverage
085   in the offline dataset, we incorporate the principle of pessimism (Jin et al., 2021; Xie et al., 2021;
086   Rashidinejad et al., 2021; Buckman et al., 2020; Lu et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023). Our algorithm
087   leverages the idea of pessimism to handle the challenge of insufficient data coverage. Our problem
088   has two additional challenges due to the nonlinearity of the quantile objective and the unobserved
089
      confounders, which are not addressed in most of these works.
090
      Causal Inference A substantial body of work in causal inference has focused on addressing con-
091
      founding bias through covariate adjustment (Rubin, 1974; Rosenbaum & Rubin, 1983; Lee et al.,
092
      2010; Liu et al., 2024), instrumental variables (Angrist et al., 1996; Ai & Chen, 2003; Newey &
093   Powell, 2003; Chen et al., 2003; Chernozhukov & Hansen, 2008; Chen & Pouzo, 2012; Baiocchi
094   et al., 2014; Hartford et al., 2017), and negative controls (Miao et al., 2018; Kallus et al., 2021).
095   Our estimation procedure leverages tools from nonlinear nonparametric instrumental variables, e.g.,
096   (Chen & Pouzo, 2012) and negative controls (Miao et al., 2018; Kallus et al., 2021). We need to ad-
097   ditionally incorporate pessimism for policy learning, which leads to a more complicated algorithm
098   and analysis.
099
100
      Pessimism + Causal Inference There are existing works that combine pessimism with causal
      inference tools to establish algorithms for confounded offline decision making (Dong et al., 2023;
101
      Chen et al., 2023). Our work is particularly relevant to Chen et al. (2023), which studies a similar
102   setting, with the goal of maximizing the expected return. In contrast, we study the quantile objective,
103   which involves the nonlinear estimating equation, and thus requires a more sophisticated analysis.
104
105   Quantile Policy Learning Quantile treatment effect has been extensively studied in Econometrics
106   (Abadie et al., 2002; Chernozhukov & Hansen, 2005; Horowitz & Lee, 2007; Chernozhukov &
107   Hansen, 2008; Chen & Pouzo, 2012; Gagliardini & Scaillet, 2012). However, limited research has
      explored quantile policy learning. For instance, Wang et al. (2018) investigates quantile-optimal

      treatment regimes, while Linn et al. (2017) proposes quantile regression methods to indirectly and
109   approximately optimize the quantile of outcomes within specific decision rule classes. Additionally,
110   Fang et al. (2023) incorporates the quantile of the reward as a regularization term in their objective
111   to maximize the average reward.

      2    O FFLINE D ECISION M AKING P ROBLEM S ETUP
114
115   In this section, we formalize the problem of offline decision making under the confounded contextual
116   bandit model. Let A ∈ A be the action and ∆(A) denote the set of all distributions over A. Let
117   X ∈ X be the context, and Y ∈ R be the reward. Denote O as the side observations that can assist
118   in decision making. In this paper, O can be either the Instrumental Variables (IVs) Z (Angrist et al.,
119   1996) or the negative control exposure-outcome pair (E, V )(Lipsitch et al., 2010). Informally, IVs
120   are random variables that affect the reward only through the actions. The Negative Control Exposure
121
      (NCE), denoted by E, are random variables known not to causally affect the reward Y. The Negative
      Control Outcomes (NCO), denoted as V are random variables known to be causally unaffected by
122
      either the action A or E. We will elaborate on these side observations in detail later. Let U ∈ U
123   denote the unmeasured confounders that causally affects A and Y simultaneously. We use the lower-
124   case letter to indicate a realization of the corresponding random variable. The decision-making
125   process involves two steps: first, collecting an offline dataset, and then learning a policy from the
126   offline data to apply in an interventional process.
127
128   The offline data collection process The offline data collection process (ODCP) describes the pro-
129   cess by which the offline dataset is collected. ODCP generates n samples, {ui , xi , oi , ai , yi }ni=1 . We
130
      assume tuples of random variables are independent and identically distributed. As U is unmeasured,
      the offline dataset only includes {xi , ai , oi , yi }ni=1 . A motivating example is the dataset of job train-
131
      ing programs aimed at improving workers’ income distributions (Abadie et al., 2002; Chernozhukov
132   & Hansen, 2008). Here, Yi represents the utility for the i-th worker, defined as the difference be-
133   tween the increase in wage and the cost of participating in the program. Xi captures the worker’s
134   pre-intervention covariates. Ai denotes whether the i-th worker participates in the program. Ui
135   could represent unmeasured factors such as the worker’s motivation, which influence both their par-
136   ticipation decision Ai and their utility outcomes Yi . Oi serves as an instrumental variable Angrist
137   et al. (1996), which could be whether the worker was invited to participate in the program.
138
139
      The Interventional Procecss A policy π : X → ∆(A) is defined as a mapping from the context
      to a distribution over the action space, specifying the decision-making rule. From the offline dataset,
140
      we learn a policy π̂, which is subsequently applied in an interventional process. In this process, we
141   no longer observe the side observations. Moreover, the context X follows a different but known
142   distribution from that in the ODCP. We denote the new distribution as pe(x). The environment first
143   generates the context X ∼ pe(x). The action a is then selected by the agent using the learned policy
144   π̂, based solely on the newly generated X. In the job training program example, this corresponds
145   to the government using the learned policy to decide whether to allow a new worker to join the
146   program based on their pre-intervention covariates. When O represents the IV, Z, Figure 1 illustrates

      We provide the details of the ODCP, interventional process, and the motivated examples for the case

187   In this section, we identify key challenges that arise in quantile policy learning and propose algo-

190   3.1   C AUSAL I DENTIFICATION
191
192   Optimizing the average quantile function Epπin [h∗α (A, X)] over π hinges on first estimating
193   h∗α (A, X). While a naive approach involves a quantile regression of Y on A and X, the unmea-
194   sured confounders U bring the endogeneity into the ODCP, making this approach inappropriate for
195   the structural quantile function (Chernozhukov & Hansen, 2008; Chen et al., 2014; Horowitz & Lee,
196
      2007). To address the confounding bias, we propose leveraging the side observations observed in
      ODCP to transform the estimation of h∗α (A, X) into solving an integral equation T (h) = 0 for h,
197
      where T is a nonlinear operator, and h∗α (A, X) is a solution of the equation.
198
199   For instance, denote D := (Y, X, A, O). When the side observations are instrumental variables Z,
200   we write 1{Y ≤ h(A, X)} − α as W (D; h). Let h ∈ H ⊆ (X, Z) → R where H is a functional
201
      space that we will determine later. Chen et al. (2014) shows that, under certain regularity conditions,
      for the nonlinear operator T IV : H → L2 (pX,Z ) defined as T IV h := E [W (D; h) | X, Z] , we
202
      have T IV h∗α (A, X) = 0 almost surely (See details of the derivation of this in §A). When the side
203
      observations is the negative control exposure-outcome pair (E, V ), we derive a novel set of integral
204   equations that h∗α satisfies. Denote

206                         T1 (h1 , h2 ) := E [W (D; h1 ) − h2 (V, A, X) | E, A, X] ,
207                         T2 (h1 , h2 ) := E [h2 (V, A′ , X) | A′ , X] ,

      5   E XPERIMENT
487
488
      In this section, we evaluate the performance of the regularized version of the algorithm through sim-
489   ulation experiments. We consider the case where instrumental variables are observed in a synthetic
490   ODCP. To investigate the algorithm’s robustness, we consider offline datasets that contain p% of
491   action distributed as random noise, independent of (X, Z, U ), and affecting only Y. We set p to be
492   20, 50 and 70. We take this approach to deliberately contaminate the offline dataset, enabling us to
493   evaluate the robustness of our algorithm. One of the key strengths of pessimism Jin et al. (2021) is
494   its ability to handle suboptimal actions that could mislead the learned policy due to undercoverage
      in the offline dataset. Through the simulation experiment, we aim to demonstrate this advantage.
495
      For implementation, we let both H and Θ be some linear function classes. Given a fix h ∈ H and
496
      π, the optimization procedure over Θ can be carried out by deriving the closed-form solution of a
497   standard nonparametric regression problem. We then optimize over H by substituting the closed-
498   form solution over Θ and performing the gradient descent. Finally, we update the optimal policy
499   π̂R by solving a linear programming problem. The complete details regarding the data generating
500   process for the synthetic dataset and the implementation of the algorithm can be found in §I. Figure
501   2 characterizes the regret plotted against the sample size n, illustrating the empirical convergence
502   rate of the algorithms. We can see that as n increases, the values and variability of the regrets for
      the pessimistic algorithm decrease rapidly. In addition, the pessimistic algorithm is able to achieve
503
      low regrets for large sample sizes even if 70% of the dataset is contaminated.

      6   D ISCUSSION
522
      In this work, we propose a policy learning algorithm where the goal is to find a policy whose reward
523
      distribution has the largest α-th quantile for some α ∈ (0, 1). To our best knowledge, this is the first
524
      sample-efficient policy learning algorithm for estimating the quantile-optimal policy under unmea-
525   sured confounding. Our work takes the IVs and NCs as examples of the side observations to tackle
526   the confounding bias. We demonstrate that the proposed algorithm is equally effective when other
527   side observations with different underlying causal structures are observed in the ODCP, as long as
528   the conditional moment restrictions can be established.

      We describe our approach to tackling the problem of confounding bias when we observe IV as the
718
      side observation from the ODCP. We introduce the nonparametric quantile IV model (NPQIV) in
719
      the ODCP and the conditional moment restrictions it follows.
720
721   A standard nonparametric instrumental variables model requires the instrumental variables Z to
722
      satisfy three conditions: (i) relevance: the distribution of action is not constant in the instrumental
      variables, (ii) exclusion: the instrumental variables affect the reward only through action, and (iii)
723
      unconfoundedness: the instrumental variables is conditionally independent of the error term (Newey
724   & Powell, 2003; Hartford et al., 2017). The general NPQIV model, however, necessitates a more
725   stringent set of assumptions (Chen et al., 2014). Following the treatment of Section 6 in Chen &
726   Pouzo (2012), we employ a simplified version of the NPQIV model incorporating the additive error
727   assumption and conditional moment restriction:
728
729   Assumption A.1 (Model Assumption for Quantile IV) We assume that the following conditions
730
      hold for the IV model in the ODCP:
731                              Y = h∗α (A, X) + ϵ and P(ϵ ≤ 0 | X, Z) = α.
732
733   By Chen et al. (2014), with some regularity conditions, Assumption A.1 holds by the underlying
734   causal structure of the ODCP in the IV case, as depicted in Figure 1. In contrast to a standard regres-
735   sion model where the mean of the error term is assumed to be 0, we define ϵ as the structural error.
736   This error term adheres to a distinct conditional distribution assumption and is responsible for the
737   confounding effect induced by the unmeasured confounders U. Consequently, ϵ is not independent
738
      of (A, X) and the condition P(ϵ ≤ 0 | X, A) = α does not hold. This subtlety renders the conven-
      tional quantile regression of Y on A and X erroneous. Fortunately, the existence of the IV allows
739
      us to write Assumption A.1 as
