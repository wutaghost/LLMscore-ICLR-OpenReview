009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Visual grounding aims to localize the target object in an input image according to
012            a language expression. To achieve this purpose, existing methods either extract
013            the visual and linguistic features independently or utilize language information
014            to guide visual feature extraction. However, the former strategy generates iden-
015            tical, general-purpose visual representations for different text queries, which are
016            redundant and sub-optimal for visual grounding tasks. Other methods that adopt
017            the latter scheme typically construct sophisticated modules based on linguistic
018
               features, implicitly guiding the visual feature extraction through end-to-end train-
               ing. But they often overlook fine-grained visual-linguistic alignment information,
019
               resulting in less discriminative visual features, and thus limiting overall model
020
               performance. In this paper, we propose a simple-yet-effective module named
021            Highlighter, which explicitly calculates pixel-word correlations between visual
022            and linguistic features, and then uses this correlation information to calibrate and
023            enhance the visual representations. Based on the proposed module, we further
024            introduce a cross-layer regularization loss, designed to maintain the consistency
025            of fine-grained alignment information across different layers and to facilitate the
026            transmission of supervision signals to shallow layers of the visual encoder. Ex-
027            tensive experiments demonstrate that our method achieves state-of-the-art perfor-
028            mance on five widely used visual grounding datasets. And ablation studies also
029
               verify the effectiveness and efficiency of our method.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Visual grounding is a general object detection task (Yao et al., 2024a), which aims to localize the
034
      target object or region in an input image that is most relevant to a natural language description.
035
      Due to its potential in bridging the gap between visual perception and language expression, visual
036   grounding has emerged as a central problem in many multi-modal reasoning researches (Kamath
037   et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022; Zou et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2022).
038
039
      To achieve the above purpose, inspired by DETR (Carion et al., 2020) based detectors, the previous
      methods like TransVG (Deng et al., 2021) and MDETR (Kamath et al., 2021) re-formulate the
040
      visual grounding as an end-to-end coordinates regression problem. They typically design a two-
041
      branch network by leveraging the transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017), in which two
042   parallel encoders are employed to extract the visual and linguistic features from the input images
043   and language expressions, respectively. However, a major problem with this parallel-encoder design
044   is that the extraction processes of visual and linguistic features are completely independent. As a
045   result, given an input image with multiple objects, the visual encoder will output identical, general-
046   purpose feature representations for different text queries (Ye et al., 2022). But actually, the multi-
047   modal fusion decoder only requires the foreground visual features that associated with the input
048   language expression to localize the target object. This inconsistency will decrease their performance
049   in solving the visual grounding task.
050   Some recently proposed works have noticed this problem and introduced various language-guided
051   visual encoder structures to deal with it. These methods can be roughly divided into two categories:
052   (1) The feature-based methods directly manipulate the intermediate visual features based on the
053   language information (Ye et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2022a) or integrate such information into the
      visual representations through the cross-attention mechanism (Yang et al., 2022b; Deng et al., 2023).

093

094
      similar semantic concepts. This will reduce the discriminability of the extracted visual features and
095
      eventually decrease the detection accuracy.
096
097   To alleviate these problems, we propose the Highlighter, a simple-yet-effective module which ex-
098   plicitly calculates a correlation map between visual and linguistic features at the pixel-word level,
099
      and then utilizes it to spatially calibrate and enhance the visual representations. Specifically, the
      Highlighter first projects the visual features produced at each intermediate encoder layer and the
100
      extracted linguistic features into a common space to align their dimensions. The matrix multiplica-
101
      tion operation is then used to calculate the pixel-word correlation map between these two projected
102   embeddings. After that, we generate word-wise weights based on the linguistic features to per-
103   form attentive pooling over the correlation values, such that the pixel-wise responses of the visual
104   features can be obtained. Finally, the Highlighter spatially recalibrates the original visual features
105   according to their corresponding responses, and feeds the calibrated representations back into the
106   visual encoder to make it more focused on language-relevant image regions. As shown in Fig-
107   ure 1, by exploiting fine-grained pixel and word alignment information, our Highlighter can more
      accurately partition the pixels of the target object and other image regions into different clusters,

109   the same input text will highlight similar areas of the visual features in different encoder layers,

118
      contributions are three-folds:
119
120   (1) We propose a Highlighter module, which explicitly calculates and exploits the fine-grained pixel-
121   word correlation information to improve the visual feature extraction process, such that more atten-
122   tion can be paid to language-relevant image regions. We also introduce a cross-layer regularization
123
      loss to promote the consistency of focused areas across the Highlighter modules in different layers.
124   (2) By integrating the proposed Highlighter modules, we establish a visual grounding framework.
125   Since the visual representations extracted by our encoder are sufficiently effective, further feature
126   enhancement is not required during the decoding phase. This allows our framework to utilize a more
127   computationally efficient decoder for final predictions.
128   (3) Extensive experiments and ablation studies are conducted on five widely used datasets to evaluate
129   the performance and efficiency of our method. The results indicate that our method can promote the
130   visual encoder to better focus on the region referred to by the input expression and significantly
131   improve the detection accuracy.

136   Visual Grounding. Early visual grounding methods typically extend the general object detection
137   framework and can be roughly divided into two categories: (1) Two-stage approaches (Yang et al.,
138   2019a; Yu et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2019a; Chen et al., 2021; Hong et al., 2019)
139   first utilize a pre-trained detector to generate a set of object proposals, and then select the most text-
140   relevant one by matching them with the input referring expression. (2) One-stage methods (Chen
141
      et al., 2018; Yang et al., 2019b; Liao et al., 2020; Qiu et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2020) make a dense
      prediction at each spatial position of a feature map, which is generated by fusing the visual and
142
      linguistic features.
143
144   Recently, with the success of Vision Transformer (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020; Vaswani et al., 2017) in
145   object detection and vision-language tasks, a series of transformer-based visual grounding models
146   have been proposed. Referring Transformer (Li & Sigal, 2021) proposes to generate contextualized
147
      lingual queries from image-text joint embeddings, and produces detection and segmentation predic-
      tions based on these queries. TransVG (Deng et al., 2021) reformulates the visual grounding as a
148
      coordinates regression problem. It feeds a learnable query token along with visual and linguistic
149
      features into a multi-modal fusion module, and uses it for target coordinates prediction. MDETR
150   (Kamath et al., 2021) tackles visual grounding tasks through a text-modulated detection framework
151   derived from the DETR detector (Carion et al., 2020). Dynamic MDETR (Shi et al., 2023) further
152   introduces a 2D adaptive sampling module to select more informative image patches, which reduces
153   the spatial redundancy and speeds up the visual grounding process. Although improved perfor-
154   mance have been achieved, the visual feature extraction process of these methods are completely
155   independent from the language expression, which will eventually limit their effectiveness in solving
156   the visual grounding task.
157   Language-guided Visual Encoder. Several approaches have noticed the above issue and attempt to

160   features at intermediate layers of the vision transformer encoder. VLTVG (Yang et al., 2022a) incor-
161   porates a visual-linguistic verification module to explicitly model the relationships between visual
      and linguistic features. LG-FPN(Suo et al., 2022) further performs the attention operation on image

197   3.1        A RCHITECTURE OVERVIEW
198
      In this section, we present the overall architecture of our framework, which is established following
199
      a typical end-to-end encoder-decoder paradigm (Deng et al., 2021; Ye et al., 2022), as illustrated
200   in Figure 2. More specifically, the encoder part of our framework utilizes a visual encoder and
201   a linguistic encoder to extract the corresponding features from the input images and language ex-
202   pressions, respectively. The proposed Highlighter module is hierarchically embedded into the visual
203   encoder to make the extracted features more focused on language-relevant image regions, promoting
204   its effectiveness in the visual grounding task. For the decoder part, a multi-stage cascade decoder
205   is employed. It introduces a learnable query token ([REG] token) as an additional input and then
206   performs the cross-attention on it with the linguistic and visual features alternatively, such that the
207   language-relevant target object information can be effectively aggregated. Finally, similar to (Yang
208
      et al., 2022a), based on the output representations of [REG] token, a regression head predicts the
209
      bounding box coordinates b̂i = (x̂i , ŷi , ŵi , ĥi ) of the text-referred object at each decoder stage.
      Based on these predictions, the training loss for optimizing our framework is formulated as:

      3.2   T HE V ISUAL AND L INGUISTIC E NCODERS
221
222   For feature extraction, we build the visual and linguistic encoders as follows:
223
      Visual Encoder: Following the setup in previous works (Deng et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2022a), we
224
      adopt a ResNet network (He et al., 2016) (e.g., ResNet-50 or ResNet-101) followed by 6 transformer
225
      layers as the visual encoder. Given an input image, we flatten its feature map produced by the ResNet
226   along the spatial dimension. The resulted features are then added with the positional embeddings
227   and fed into the following transformer layers to generate the final visual representations.
228
229
      Linguistic Encoder: We employ a basic BERT (Devlin et al., 2018) network with 12 layers as the
      linguistic encoder. For each input language expression, we process the sentence text as in (Deng
230
      et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2022a; Ye et al., 2022) and feed it into the BERT to extract its linguistic
231
      features Fl ∈ RNl ×Cl . Here, Nl is the length of the input expression and Cl denotes the feature
232   dimension of each word token. As our main objective is to highlight the language-relevant regions
233   in the visual features, we do not introduce any specific design for this encoder.
234

      3.4   T HE C ROSS -L AYER R EGULARIZATION LOSS
293
294   The visual features Fv extracted by the different encoder layers typically vary considerably in terms
295   of receptive field size, degree of information abstraction and some other factors. Hence, the response
296   matrix U e that is calculated from Fv can also vary greatly across Highlighter modules. However, for
297   the same input expression, it is expected to highlight similar spatial areas for different features Fv .
298   To explicitly achieve this consistency, we propose the cross-layer regularization loss Lcr , which
299   applies distribution constrains to U e generated by each layer’s Highlighter module. Specifically, we

      4.1   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
335
      Datasets. To evaluate the detection performance and computational efficiency of our entire frame-
336
      work, as well as to validate the effectiveness of our proposed Highlighter module, we conduct
337   extensive experiments and a series of ablation studies on five commonly used datasets, including
338   RefCOCO (Yu et al., 2016), RefCOCO+ (Yu et al., 2016), RefCOCOg (Mao et al., 2016), Refer-
339   ItGame (Kazemzadeh et al., 2014) and Flickr30k Entities (Plummer et al., 2015). We provide a
340   more detailed introduction of these datasets in Appendix A.2.
341
      For all these datasets, we resize each image sample to make its longer edge equal to 640 and then
342
      pad its shorter edge to 640 as well. So all the input images in our experiments have a spatial size
343   of 640 × 640. We also set the maximum length of each language expression to 40 and truncate the
344   words that exceed this length. The special [CLS] and [SEP] tokens are appended to the beginning
345   and end of each expression before it is input into the linguistic encoder.
346
      Training Details. To train our proposed framework, we employ the AdamW optimizer (Loshchilov
347
      & Hutter, 2017) with batch size of 64 and weight decay of 10−4 . Similar to (Deng et al., 2023;
348
      Yang et al., 2022a), our visual and linguistic encoders are initialized by well pre-trained DETR
349   (Carion et al., 2020) and BERT (Devlin et al., 2018) models, respectively, and then optimized with
350   an initial learning rate of 10−5 . As for our cascade decoder, we randomly initialize its parameters
351   by the Xavier (Glorot & Bengio, 2010) scheme and set its initial learning rate to 10−4 . To make a
352   fair comparison with other methods, we similarly train our model for 90 epochs and decrease the
353   learning rate by a factor of 10 after 60 epochs. In addition, we also adopt the data augmentation
354   strategy used in the previous works (Yang et al., 2019b; 2020; Deng et al., 2021; Ye et al., 2022)
355   during the training phase. The common space dimension value Cs is set to Cs = 256. For the loss
356   function in Equation (1), the hyperparameters are as λL1 = 5, λgiou = 2, λcr = 1 and the number
357
      of decoder stages L is set to L = 6.
358   Experimental Environment. All experiments in this section are performed on a server with an
359   Intel(R) Xeon(R) Gold 6133 @2.50GHz CPU and the Ubuntu 20.04.4 LTS operating system. Our
360   model is trained by using two NVIDIA A100 Tensor Core GPUs (32 samples per GPU for each
361   mini-batch with a size of 64) and tested on a single NVIDIA GeForce RTX 3090 GPU.
362   Evaluation Metric. We follow the standard ACC@0.5 protocol (Yang et al., 2019b; 2020; Deng
363   et al., 2021; Ye et al., 2022) to evaluate the detection performance of our framework for the visual
364   grounding task. Specifically, given an image-expression pair as input, the predicted bounding box is
365   considered as correct only if the IoU value calculated between it and the corresponding ground-truth
366   target is greater than 0.5.

      5   C ONCLUSION
532
      This work proposes a Highlighter module for improving visual grounding performance. It only
533
      consists of several simple operations, but can effectively leverage the pixel-word correlation infor-
534
      mation to calibrate and enhance the visual representations. In this way, the extracted visual features
535   will pay more attention to the language-relevant regions, so that the visual-linguistic alignment can
536   be explicitly achieved. In addition, a multi-stage cascade decoder is also employed to further im-
537   prove the effectiveness and efficiency of our framework. Experiments indicate that our method
538   achieves new state-of-the-art performance on five datasets, surpassing recently proposed VLTVG,
539   LADS, LUNA, QRNet, VG-LAW and MMCA.

794   A.2    I NTRODUCTION OF THE USED DATASETS
795
796   RefCOCO (Yu et al., 2016) includes 19,994 images with 50,000 referred objects, where each image
797   may contain multiple instances from the same object categories. There are 142,210 referring ex-
798   pressions in total, so each instance may get more than one text description. Following the standard
799   setup, the image samples in RefCOCO are officially split into train/validation/testA/testB subsets
800
      that have 120,624/10,834/5,657/5,095 expressions, respectively.
801   RefCOCO+ (Yu et al., 2016) consists of 19,992 images with 49,856 referred objects and 141,564
802   referring expressions. The usage of location-related words (e.g., “left” or “right”) is strictly disal-
803   lowed in the expressions from this dataset. RefCOCO+ is also officially split into train, validation,
804   testA and testB sets with 120,191, 10,758, 5,726 and 4,889 expressions, respectively.
805   RefCOCOg (Mao et al., 2016) has 95,010 long expressions collected on Amazon Mechanical Turk
806   for 49,856 referred objects in 25,799 images. Among them, 85,474 expression-referent pairs are
807   selected for model training, and the remaining 9,536 expressions are separated following two differ-
808   ent strategies, namely RefCOCOg-google (Mao et al., 2016) (val-g) and RefCOCOg-umd (Nagaraja
809   et al., 2016) (val-u and test-u). We conduct experiments on these two partitions to make comprehen-
      sive comparisons.
