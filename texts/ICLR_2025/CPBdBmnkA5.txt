011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013
               The pervasiveness of proprietary language models has raised privacy concerns
014            for users’ sensitive data, emphasizing the need for private inference (PI), where
015            inference is performed directly on encrypted inputs. However, current PI methods
016            face prohibitively higher communication and latency overheads, primarily due
017            to nonlinear operations. In this paper, we present a comprehensive analysis to
018            understand the role of nonlinearities in transformer-based decoder-only language
019            models. We introduce AERO, a four-step architectural optimization framework that
020            refines the existing LLM architecture for efficient PI by systematically removing
021            nonlinearities such as LayerNorm and GELU and reducing FLOPs counts. For the
022
               first time, we propose a Softmax-only architecture with significantly fewer FLOPs
               tailored for efficient PI. Furthermore, we devise a novel entropy regularization tech-
023
               nique to improve the performance of Softmax-only models. AERO achieves up to
024
               4.23× communication and 1.94× latency reduction. We validate the effectiveness
025            of AERO by benchmarking it against the state-of-the-art.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Motivation. The widespread adoption of proprietary models like ChatGPT Achiam et al. (2023)
032
      significantly raised the privacy concerns to protect the users’ sensitive (prompt) data Staab et al.
      (2024); Mireshghallah et al. (2024); Priyanshu et al. (2023); Lauren & Knight (2023), while also
033
      preventing the attacks aimed at extracting model weights Carlini et al. (2024); Jovanović et al. (2024).
034
035   This emphasizes the need for private inference (PI) where a user sends the encrypted queries to the
036   service provider without revealing their actual inputs, and the inference is performed directly on
037   encrypted inputs, assuring the privacy of input and protection of the model’s weight.
038   Despite their promises, current PI methods remain impractical due to their prohibitive latency and
039   communication overheads—generating a single output token with GPT-2 model (125M parameters)
040   on 128 input tokens takes 8.2 minutes and requires 25.3 GBs communication (Figure 1), scaling to
041   30.7 minutes and 145.2 GBs for context size of 512 (Table 7). These overheads stem largely from the
042   nonlinear operations, crucial for model performance, in a transformer-based large language model
043   (LLM), such as GELU, LayerNorm, and Softmax Hou et al. (2023); Lu et al. (2025).
044   Challenges. Current PI solutions for transformer-based models (e.g., ViT, BERT) either neglect the
045   cost of LayerNorm (Li et al., 2023a; Zeng et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023) or
046   approximate nonlinear operations using polynomial functions Zimerman et al. (2024); Dhyani et al.
047   (2024). Nonetheless, polynomial approximation methods have their limitations: their accuracy is
048   highly sensitive to data-specific initial guesses Knott et al. (2021), and their effectiveness is confined
049   to narrow input ranges Zimerman et al. (2024). Moreover, networks employing higher-degree
050
      polynomials for improved approximation precision are notoriously difficult to train and optimize.
051   Meanwhile, the nonlinearity reduction methods, used for improving plaintext speed, offer very-limited
052   potential to improve the PI efficiency. For instance, (He et al., 2023; Noci et al., 2023; He & Hofmann,
053   2024) has explored architectural heuristics to design LayerNorm-free LLMs; however, their broader
      implications on the choices of activation function, a key bottleneck in PI, remains largely unexamined.

068     Our techniques and insights. We conducted an in-depth analysis of the role of non-linearities in
069     transformer-based LLMs. Our key findings are: (1) LayerNorm-free models exhibit a preference for
070     ReLU over GELU in feed-forward network (FFN), making them more PI-friendly; and (2) training
071     instability, as entropy collapse in deeper layers, in the Softmax-only model can be prevented by
072
        normalizing FFN weights, avoiding the nonlinear computations (unlike LayerNorm) at inference.
073    We observed a phenomenon we term entropic overload, where a disproportionately larger fraction
074    of attention heads stuck at higher, close to their maximum, entropy values in LN-free with GELU,
075    and Softmax-only models. We hypothesize that the entropic overload causes a lack of diversity and
076    specialization in attention heads, squandering the representational capacity of attention heads. This
077    leads to performance degradation, indicated by a higher perplexity.
078    To mitigate the entropic overload, we propose a novel entropy regularization technique that penalizes
079    the extreme entropy values at training and avoids the deviation from well-behaved entropy distribution.
080
        Results and implications. As shown in Figure 1, substituting GELU with ReLUs in the baseline GPT-
081
        2 model alone reduces the communication and latency overheads by 2.68× and 1.35×, respectively.
082     Eliminating LayerNroms further improves these savings to 3.41× and 1.42×. Similar improvements
083     are observed with the Pythia model (see Figure15).
084
085
       Since the FFN in the Softmax-only model is performing only the linear transformations, merging the
       linear layers into a single linear layer reduces the FFN FLOPs by 8× and gains significant speedup
086
       without increasing the perplexity (see Figure 1). Furthermore, our analysis reveals that the linear
087
       transformations performed by early FFNs are crucial for training stability in the Softmax-only model,
088    while deeper FFNs can be pruned. This provides additional opportunities for FLOPs reduction.

090     Contributions. Our key contributions are follows:
091     1. We thoroughly characterize the role of GELU and LayerNorm nonlinearities in transformer-based
092        LLMs by examining their impact on the attention score distribution using Shannon’s entropy,
093
           offering insights for tailoring existing LLM architectures for efficient PI.
        2. We introduced AERO, a four-stage optimization framework, and designed a Softmax-only model
094
           with fewer FLOPs, achieving up to 1.94× speedup and 4.23× communication reduction.
095
        3. We introduce a novel entropy regularization technique to boost the performance of the Softmax-
096        only model, which achieves 6% - 8% improvement in perplexity.
097     4. We conducted extensive experiments across various context sizes (128, 256, 512) and model

114   The Multi-Head Attention (MHA) sub-block enables input contextualization by sharing information
115   between individual tokens. MHA employs the self-attention mechanism to compute the similarity
116   score of each token with respect to all other tokens in the sequence. In particular, self-attention
117   mechanism transform the input sequence X into Attn(X) as follows:
                                               1
118                   Attn(X) = Softmax √ (XWQ )(XWK )⊤ + M XWV .                                      (2)

      Here, each token generates query(Q), key(K), and value(V ) vectors through the linear transformations
121   WQ , WK , and WV ∈ Rd×dh , respectively. Then, similarity scores are computed by taking the dot
122   product of the Q and K vectors, scaled by the inverse square root of the K dimension, and passed
123   through a softmax function to obtain the attention weights. These weights are then used to compute
124   a weighted sum of the V vectors, producing the output for each token. For auto-regressive models
125   (e.g., GPT), mask M ∈ RT ×T , which has values in {0, −∞} with Mi,j = 0 iff i ≥ j, is deployed to
126   prevent the tokens from obtaining information from future tokens.
127   The MHA sub-block employs a self-attention mechanism across all the heads, each with its own
128   sets of Q, K, and V . This allows the attention heads to focus on different parts of the input
129   sequence, capturing various aspects of the input data simultaneously. The outputs from all heads are
130   concatenated and linearly transformed (WO ∈ Rd×d ) to produce the final MHA output as follows:
131              MHA(X) = Concat Attn1 (X), Attn2 (X), Attn3 (X), . . . , AttnH (X) WO .

198        Entropic overload We observed that in certain nonlinearity configurations, a disproportionately large
199        fraction of the attention heads exhibit higher entropy values (between 3max

200        phenomenon as entropic overload and hypothesize that this imbalance results in under-utilization

202        model from effectively leveraging the diversity of attention heads.
203       To investigate further, we examined how entropy values evolve during training. Typically, all heads
204       start with higher entropy values, indicating an initial exploration phase, and gradually adapt to balance
205       exploration and exploitation in baseline networks (see Figure 12). However, in the absence of certain
206       nonlinearities, this balance is disrupted, preventing attention heads from specializing and refining
207       their focus on critical aspects of the input, thereby diminishing overall performance.

      4.1                         D ESIGNING S OFTMAX - ONLY A RCHITECTURE
338
339
      To eliminate nonlinearities in existing LLM architectures, we first remove normalization layers,
340   creating an LN-free design. Our approach extends previous work on LN-free design (He et al., 2023;
341   Noci et al., 2023; He & Hofmann, 2024) by also carefully selecting FFN activation functions, opting
342   for ReLU due to its superior PI efficiency and ability to mitigate entropic overload in LN-free models.
343
      We then remove ReLU, leading to a full normalization and activation-free, or Softmax-only, archi-
344
      tecture. Training this architecture, however, poses challenges, such as entropy collapse in deeper
345
      layers. To address this, we introduce learnable scaling factors, α and β, in the FFN sub-block, which
346   stabilize training more effectively than weight or spectral normalization.
347
348
349   4.2                         FLOP S R EDUCTION IN S OFTMAX - ONLY A RCHITECTURE
350
      To develop an effective FLOPs reduction strategy, we begin by analyzing the distribution of FLOPs
351
      between the attention and FFN sub-blocks across varying context lengths.
352
353   FFN FLOPs dominates in shorter context length regimes (T < 38 d). While prior work on LN-free
354   architectures (He & Hofmann, 2024) has emphasized reducing attention FLOPs, we find that the
355   network’s FLOPs are dominated by FFN FLOPs during inference with shorter context lengths (when
356
      T < 83 d, Eq. 13). For instance, when T ≤1K, FFN FLOPs constitute 60%-65% of the total FLOPs
      in models like GPT-2 (Figure 20) and Pythia (Figure 21) variants.
357
358   Given that current research on 2PC PI primarily focuses on smaller context lengths (Zhang et al.,
359   2025; Lu et al., 2025; Zimerman et al., 2024; Pang et al., 2024; Gupta et al., 2024; Hou et al., 2023),
360   we strategically target reducing FFN FLOPs. First, we merge the two linear layers in FFN of Softmax-
361   only architecture—Win    ffn
                                   ∈ Rd×4d and Wout
                                                  ffn
                                                      ∈ R4d×d —into a single linear layer, Wffn ∈ Rd×d , as
362
      they effectively perform linear transformation in the absence of intervening nonlinearity. This reduces
      FFN FLOPs by a 8× without any performance degradation, which is not achievable in polynomial
363
      transformers, where GELU is approximated by polynomials (Zimerman et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023a).
364
365   To reduce FFN FLOPs even further, we ask the following questions: What functional role do FFNs
366   serve when they are purely linear? Do all FFNs contribute equally, or can some of them be pruned?
367   Early FFNs in Softmax-only architecture are critical, while deeper ones can be pruned. We
368   observe that early FFNs, despite being purely linear, are crucial for training stability, as their removal
369   leads to entropy collapses (Fig. 16 and Fig. 17). Deeper FFNs, however, exhibit redundancy, allowing
370   additional FLOPs reduction without degrading performance. This observation resonates with findings
371   on the significance of early-to-mid (conventional non-linear) FFNs (Nanda et al., 2023; Sharma et al.,
372   2024; Jin et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2024a; Stolfo et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Haviv et al., 2023;
373   Meng et al., 2022) and the redundant FFN computations (Kobayashi et al., 2024; Pires et al., 2023).
374   This enables an additional opportunity to reduce FFN FLOPs by selectively removing deeper FFNs.
375   In Softmax-only GPT-2-small architecture, we successfully remove up to six deeper FFNs, achieving
376   an additional 6× FLOPs reduction in FFN. We refer to this simplified model as SM + ScFuFFNix ,
377   where x represents the number of deeper FFNs that are replaced with identity functions, while the
      remaining FFNs have one (fused) linear layer. When x=0, we represent the model as SM + ScFuFFN.

      4.3         E NTROPY R EGULARIZATION
401
402   Challenges in designing entropy regularization schemes to prevent entropic overload. Previous
403   entropy regularization approaches have primarily aimed at penalizing low-entropy predictions (Setlur
404   et al., 2022; Pereyra et al., 2017), based on the principle of maximum entropy (Jaynes, 1982). Recently,
405   (He et al., 2024) introduced entropy regularization to prevent entropy collapses, by addressing
406   extremely low entropy values, in LLMs.
407   However, our goal is to regularize higher entropy values, which presents two-fold challenges: (1) Head
408   specialization: Since each attention head captures different aspects of the input, the regularization
409   strength needs to be adjusted for each head individually. (2) Preventing over-regularization: Some
410   heads naturally exhibit higher entropy even in well-behaved entropy distributions, thus, penalizing all
411   high-entropy values without distinction could be harmful, requiring a more flexible approach.
412   Key design principles for entropy regularization. Followings are the key design principles for our
413   entropy regularization scheme (see Algorithm 1), addressing the aforementioned challenges:
414   • Balanced entropy distribution with parameterized attention matrix: Inspired by Miller et al. (1996),
415     which used temperature parameter as a Lagrangian multiplier to control the entropy of a stochastic
416     system, we parameterized the attention matrix by a learnable temperature t ∈ RH×T for each
417     softmax operation, allowing the model to adjust the sharpness of the attention scores (see Appendix
418     A.3). A higher temperature value (t > 1) diffuses the attention scores and increases the entropy,
419     while a lower temperature value (t < 1) sharpens the attention scores and reduces the entropy.
420   • Dynamic thresholds with head-specific adaptation: To adapt the regularization strength based
421
        on the characteristics of each attention head (Voita et al., 2019), we use headwise learnable
        threshold parameter reg_threshold_weights ∈ RH . Consequently, the threshold for each head
422
        is computed as a learnable fraction of the maximum value of entropy (reg_threshold_weights×
423
        Emax ), providing the fine-grained control (see Algorithm 1, line #11).
424   • Tolerance margin to prevent over-regularization: To prevent over-regularization, we allow small
425     deviations from the respective thresholds. Thus, a penalty is imposed only if the deviation from the
426     threshold exceeds the tolerance margin, which is set as a fraction of Emax using the hyper-parameter
427     γ (see Algorithm1, line #3). (                       2
428                               (l,h)      deviation(l,h)    if deviation(l,h) > γEmax
                         penalty        =

        is reg_threshold_weights. The hyper-parameter γ ensures that the model is not excessively

434     improves the model’s robustness while maintaining its adaptability to various input distributions.
435   • Maximum entropy reference: We set Emax = log(T ) as a reference point for computing thresholds

      4.4    P UTTING IT A LL T OGETHER
441
442   We developed the AERO framework (Figure 8) to systematically eliminate non-linearities and reduce
443   FFN FLOPs from the existing transformer-based LLMs. Given an input baseline LLM, the first two
444   steps, Step1 and Step2, attempt to address the overheads associated with non-linear operations in
445   PI, resulting in a softmax-only architecture. The next step, Step3, aims at reducing FFN FLOPs by
446   fusing the adjacent linear layers, and then selectively pruning deeper FFNs by replacing them with
447   identity functions, resulting in a substantial reduction in FLOPs without destabilizing the model.
448   Further, to mitigate the entropic overload, and improve the utilization of attention heads, Step4
449   introduces entropy regularization, keeping the balanced attention distributions by penalizing extreme
450   entropy values. This step plays a crucial role in boosting the performance of the softmax-only model.

531   6                                  C ONCLUSION
532
533   In this work, we introduce AERO, a four-stage design framework to streamline the existing LLM
534   architecture for efficient private inference. We design Softmax-only architecture with significantly
535   lower FLOPs and propose entropy regularization to boost their performance.
536   Limitations. This study mainly focuses on pre-training performance, with perplexity as the primary
537   metric, and does not include experiments to evaluate other capabilities such as transfer learning or
538   few-shot learning. Additionally, the efficacy of the proposed Softmax-only models has been validated
539   on models with fewer than 1B parameters. Future work will explore broader experimental evaluations,
      including the adaption of AERO for large-scale models (see Appendix H).

       A.1   W HY U SE E NTROPY TO E VALUATE THE I MPACT OF N ONLINEARITIES ?
1029
1030   We use entropy as a metric to study the impact of nonlinearities on the transformer-based LLMs for
1031   the following reasons:
1032
       • Quantifying attention distribution: The attention mechanism lies at the core of transformer ar-
1033     chitectures, and by computing the entropy of attention score distributions, we can observe how
1034     nonlinearities influence the spread (or the concentration) of attention scores. Higher entropy implies
1035     a more exploratory behavior, while lower entropy suggests a more focused attention distribution.
1036   • Feature selection: Nonlinearities, such as ReLU, enhance feature selectivity by amplifying im-
1037     portant features and suppressing less relevant ones (Alleman et al., 2024; Maas et al., 2013).
1038     Entropy can measure this selectivity across layers and heads, providing insights into the model’s
1039     prioritization of information. Previous studies have used entropy to capture layer-wise information
1040     flow in neural networks (Peer et al., 2022).
1041
       • Exploration vs. exploitation: Nonlinear operators like the self-attention mechanism, LayerNorm,
         and GELU balance exploration and exploitation by selecting relevant features while considering
1042
         a broader context. For instance, heads in the first layer focus on exploration, while those in the
1043
         second layer focus on exploitation. (see Figures 5a, 5b, 12a and 12b).
1044   • Systematic assessment: Prior work Zhang et al. (2024); Nahshan et al. (2024); Zhai et al. (2023);
1045     Vig & Belinkov (2019); Ghader & Monz (2017) also used entropy to analyze the behavior of
1046     transformer-based models; thus, enhancing validity and comparability of our findings.

1234   Perplexity (Jelinek et al., 1977) is a widely adopted metric to evaluate the predictive performance of
1235   auto-regressive language models, reflecting the model’s ability to predict the next token in a sequence.
1236   However, for perplexity to serve as a meaningful comparative metric across different architectures,
1237   it is critical to ensure consistency in the tokenizer, and vocabulary size and quality (Hutchins et al.,
1238
       2022). Any variation in these components can potentially skew the results by inflating or deflating
       perplexity scores; thus, obfuscating the true effects of architectural changes.
1239
1240   In our work, we maintain tokenization schemes and vocabulary attributes as invariant factors across all
1241   experiments within a dataset. This isolation of architectural modifications ensures that any observed
       variations in perplexity are directly attributable to changes in the model design. Thus, by enforcing a

       C.2   W HY T RAINING FROM S CRATCH TO S TUDY N ONLINEARITIES ?
1247
1248
       Understanding the intricate roles of architectural components and nonlinearities—such as activation
1249   functions (e.g., GELU, ReLU) in FFN, normalization layers (e.g., LayerNorm), etc.—in transformer-
1250   based language models necessitates a methodical and detailed investigative approach. Training
1251   models from scratch is essential for this purpose, as it allows us to delve into the internal mechanisms
1252   of the model using quantitative measures like entropy. Below, we present a justification for our
1253   methodology:
1254
        • Nonlinearities’ impact on the fundamental learning dynamics: Nonlinearities significantly influ-
1255      ence the optimization landscape by affecting gradient flow and the model’s ability to navigate
1256      non-convex loss surfaces. Training models from scratch allow us to observe the fundamental
1257      learning dynamics that emerge during the initial stages of training. Thus, constructing models
1258      with controlled variations, such as substituting or excluding specific nonlinearities, enables us to
1259      isolate their direct effects impact on convergence behavior and training stability.
1260    • Understanding internal mechanisms through entropy analysis: Training from scratch enables
1261      us to navigate the evolution of entropy values across the layers and assess how architectural
1262      components influence information flow within the model. This analysis provides deep insights
1263
          into the internal workings of models that may not be accessible when starting from pre-trained
          checkpoints.
1264
        • Limitations of fine-tuning approaches: The aforementioned granular level of analysis is unattain-
1265
          able when starting from pre-trained models, where the optimization trajectory has already been
1266      largely determined. In contrast, training models from scratch eliminates confounding variables
1267      that could arise from pre-existing weights and learned representations, ensuring that any observed
1268      effects are solely due to the architectural modifications introduced.
1269

       C.3   C RYPTOGRAPHIC P ROTOCOLS FOR L INEAR AND N ONLINEAR O PERATIONS
1271
1272   Linear Operations (MatMul) For privacy-preserving matrix multiplication operations, BumbleBee
1273   leverages homomorphic encryption with a novel ciphertext compression strategy Lu et al. (2025).
1274   The protocol implements Oblivious Linear Transform (OLT) with efficient packing techniques that
1275   reduce communication costs by 80-90% compared to previous approaches. Testing on BERT-base
1276   model showed 92% less communication compared to IRON Hao et al. (2022) and 90% less than
1277   BOLT Pang et al. (2024). The protocol seamlessly handles both scenarios where one matrix is in
1278   plaintext and another is secret-shared, as well as when both matrices are secret-shared, adapting its
1279   compression strategy based on matrix dimensions.
1280   GELU To efficiently compute GELU activation, the protocol employs strategic polynomial approxi-
1281   mations (with degree 3 and degree 6) across different input ranges. The implementation optimizes
1282   branch selection through batched comparisons and leverages mixed bitwidth arithmetic, achieving
1283   89% reduction in communication costs compared to previous approaches. This optimization main-
1284   tains model accuracy within 1% of plaintext evaluation while being 35% faster in computation time
1285   compared to BOLT’s implementation.
1286   ReLU The private ReLU is implemented through a composition of secure comparison and multiplexer
1287   operations. Using optimized OT(oblivious transfer)-based comparison protocols and Boolean-to-
1288   arithmetic conversions, achieving significant efficiency gains. The protocol leverages Ferret OT
1289   Yang et al. (2020) instead of traditional IKNP OT Ishai et al. (2003), contributing to the overall
1290   improvement in communication efficiency while maintaining the simplicity of ReLU computation.
1291   LayerNorm The LayerNorm computation breaks down into optimized sub-components: secure
1292   mean calculation, variance computation using an efficient square protocol, and normalization using
1293   reciprocal square root. The implementation takes advantage of squaring operations costing half of
1294   general multiplication in secure computation. This optimization, combined with efficient reciprocal
1295   computation, shows significant improvement over IRON’s approach Hao et al. (2022), contributing to
       the overall 13× speedup in end-to-end inference time.

       Softmax For Softmax computation, the protocol implements a numerically stable approach using max
1297   normalization. The exponential function computation is optimized specifically for negative inputs
1298   using Taylor approximation, reducing communication by 80% compared to previous approaches. The
1299   division operation is restructured to use reciprocal followed by multiplication, resulting in over 80%
1300   reduction in communication costs while maintaining numerical precision.

1323

       The Pitfalls of LayerNorm in LLM While LayerNorm is a critical source of non-linearity in both
1893
       CNNs (Ni et al., 2024) and transformer-based models (Wu et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2024; Joudaki
1894
       et al., 2023), it causes several challenges in the transformer-based LLMs that extend well-beyond PI.
1895
1896   First, in PI with hybrid protocols, the inverse-square root computation poses significant challenges
1897   for its precise computation. Also, in HE-only PI, the polynomial approximation of LayerNorm is
1898
       quite challenging because of the unusually wide range of its variance values Zimerman et al. (2024).
1899   Furthermore, the trainable parameters in LayerNorm are shown to be associated with the outlier
1900   features in LLMs He et al. (2024); Bondarenko et al. (2023); Wei et al. (2022); Kovaleva et al. (2021),
1901   posing significant issues in LLM quantization. Specifically, the scaling parameters in LayerNorm
1902   amplify outlier features, which in turn makes low-precision training challenging Wei et al. (2022).
1903   Moreover, LayerNorm introduces difficulties in mechanistic interpretability, as it tends to make the
1904   residual stream more complex and harder to analyze Nanda (2023).

       Also, from the perspective of signal propagation theories, LayerNorm negatively impacts the train-

1926   G.1    L INEAR VS . N ON - LINEAR FFN S : P RIVILEGED BASIS AND ROTATIONAL I NVARIANCE
1927
1928   In transformer-based architectures, there is a crucial distinction between linear and non-linear
1929   transformations, particularly when analyzing the privileged basis phenomenon (Elhage et al., 2023).
1930   A privileged basis occurs when certain directions in the network’s activation space are favored due
1931   to the network’s architecture. Specifically, non-linear activations (e.g., ReLU, GELU) encourage
1932   features to align with specific directions in the hidden space, breaking the rotational invariance of the
1933   network. This alignment forces neurons to prioritize specific directions or features, leading to a basis
1934   that is more interpretable but inherently dependent on the chosen non-linear function.
1935   Non-linear FFN In a conventional FFN, the architecture includes two linear transformations with a
1936   non-linear activation between them. This non-linearity breaks the rotational invariance of the hidden
1937   space, as the activation function behaves differently depending on the input direction. Mathematically,
1938   this can be described as:
1939
1940
                                                           ffn        ffn
1941                               FFNnon-linear (x) = σ(xWin  + b1 )Wout + b2
1942
1943   Where σ(·) represents the elementwise non-linearity, which restricts the model from treating all
       directions in the hidden space equally, thereby establishing a privileged basis.

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1944
       Linear FFN Removing the non-linearity from the FFN results in a purely linear transformation,
1945   which preserves rotational invariance in the hidden space. The linear FFN operates as:
1946
1947
                                                            ffn   ffn
1948                                     FFNlinear (x) = (xWin  )Wout +b
1949
       Without the activation function, the representations remain invariant under rotations, meaning that no
1950
       specific direction is favored over others. In a linear FFN, the model retains its flexibility in how it
1951   represents features, without forcing any particular alignment of neurons. In effect, the model operates
1952   in a non-privileged basis, allowing the representation to rotate freely in the vector space.

1955
1956   Early FFNs in transformer models are particularly critical due to the presence of polysemantic
1957   neurons(Ferrando et al., 2024), which can respond to multiple, unrelated features simultaneously.
1958   These neurons enable early layers to detect broad and diverse patterns, such as linguistic structures,
1959   n-grams, and other foundational features of the input. This general-purpose functionality allows the
1960   early FFNs to play a vital role in the initial stages of context formation, capturing a wide range of
1961   information necessary for effective processing in later stages.
1962   Additionally, research highlights that early layers play a pivotal role in the memorization and recall
1963   of factual information (Haviv et al., 2023; Meng et al., 2022). These layers not only capture general
1964   patterns but also store and retrieve memorized knowledge, promoting the predicted token to the
1965   top of the output distribution. This suggests that early FFNs are integral for accessing learned
1966   information, making them crucial for both context formation and recalling facts essential to the
1967   model’s predictions.
1968   In contrast, deeper FFNs become more specialized, focusing on refining the information extracted by
1969   the earlier layers. They process the data with a narrower scope, tailoring it for task-specific outputs.
1970   While these deeper layers are essential for fine-tuning the model’s predictions, the early FFNs are key
1971   to generalizing over complex and varied input patterns, establishing the groundwork for the model’s
1972   overall performance.
