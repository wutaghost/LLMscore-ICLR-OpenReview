009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011             We introduce GATE, a framework for improving the estimation of conditional
012             average treatment effects (CATE) from observational data. Our framework lever-
013             ages generative models to selectively augment datasets with synthetic potential
014             outcomes, thus addressing the covariate shift problem inherent in CATE estimation.
015             Crucially, GATE enables the integration of external knowledge into downstream
016             CATE models, by leveraging generative models trained on external data sources,
017             such as large language models (LLMs). These models utilize rich contextual
018             information, such as dataset metadata, to generate synthetic potential outcomes
019
                grounded in real-world contexts. While generative models can introduce bias
                when imperfect, we theoretically demonstrate that restricting augmentation to a
020
                carefully chosen subsets of the covariate space can allow to achieve performance
021
                gains despite these imperfections. Empirically, GATE instantiated with LLMs
022             consistently improves a wide range of CATE estimators, narrowing performance
023             gaps between learners and underscoring the advantages of incorporating external
024             knowledge through generative augmentation, particularly in small-sample regimes.

027   1       I NTRODUCTION
028
      Motivation. Conditional Average Treatment Effect (CATE) estimation has received a lot of attention
029   from the machine learning (ML) literature (Shalit et al., 2017; Künzel et al., 2019; Kennedy, 2023).
030   This should not be a surprise: tools allowing to leverage observational data to estimate the person-
031   alised effects of interventions are an invaluable asset in domains varying from healthcare (Gershon
032   et al., 2021) to economics (Baum-Snow & Ferreira, 2015) or marketing (Hill et al., 2015). However,
033   despite its immense benefits, estimating CATE from observational data remains challenging.
034
      In observational data, especially in high-stakes domains, treatments are assigned non-randomly. This
035   non-random treatment assignment, particularly when guided by expert judgment (Hüyük et al., 2024),
036   leads to a covariate shift, where covariate distributions differ between treated and control groups,
037   leading to unreliable or high-variance treatment effect estimates (Johansson et al., 2022). Covariate
038   shift can have especially negative consequences in small-sample regimes (Alaa & van der Schaar,
039   2018), prevalent in case of novel treatments, rare diseases or data collected at the country level.
040
      Existing approaches address the covariate shift through model specification, employing inverse-
041   propensity weighting (Abrevaya et al., 2015), representation learning (Johansson et al., 2016; Shalit
042   et al., 2017), or both (Assaad et al., 2021; Hassanpour & Greiner, 2019). In this work, we explore
043   a complementary solution to the covariate shift problem, which can be used alongside any CATE
044   learner: addressing covariate shift by manipulating the dataset rather than the model.
045
      Generative Augmentation for CATE. To this end, we introduce GATE (Generative Augmentation
046
      for Treatment Effect estimation), a flexible data augmentation framework that leverages diverse
047   generative models to supercharge the performance of existing CATE estimators. By augmenting1 the
048   observational dataset with missing potential outcomes sampled from a generative model, GATE not
049   only increases the effective sample size (as is typical in data augmentation) but also directly mitigates
050   the covariate shift inherent in CATE estimation. Importantly, as we demonstrate both theoretically
051   and empirically, the reduction in covariate shift can counterbalance biases introduced by a potentially
052

055   inaccuracies in the generative model can easily degrade performance (Manousakas & Aydöre, 2023).

057   To maximize effectiveness, GATE restricts augmentation to a carefully selected subset of the covariate

      This principled selection process, informed by the properties of the generative model, ensures robust

060
061   Leveraging External Knowledge Through Generative Models. Beyond mitigating covariate
062   shift, data augmentation offers a unique opportunity to inject external knowledge into downstream
063   CATE models. GATE achieves this by leveraging generative models trained on external data sources,
064
      contrasting imputation methods limited to local regression modules or GANs (Aloui et al., 2023;
      Yoon et al., 2018). In particular, we propose to instantiate GATE with Large Language Models
065
      (LLMs), which benefit from extensive pretraining and have been shown to improve performance of
066
      downstream models across diverse tasks (Choi et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2023; Seedat et al., 2024).
067
068   The key advantage of using LLMs as generative models lies in their ability to leverage rich metadata
069   present in observational datasets, such as textual descriptions of covariates or contextual information.
070
      By utilizing this metadata, LLMs can generate potential outcomes that align with common-sense
      reasoning, grounding downstream CATE models in real-world contexts. Recent studies also highlight
071
      LLMs’ ability to uncover causal structures in real-world data (Richens & Everitt, 2024; Zečević et al.,
072
      2023; Long et al., 2024), further validating their use in this context.
073
074   However, there are valid concerns regarding the safety and robustness of employing LLMs in the
075   causal setting, particularly given their propensity for hallucinations. As such, we approach the use
076
      of LLMs with a critical and cautious stance, acknowledging the uncertainties and potential pitfalls.
      Through empirical experiments, we investigate under which circumstances LLMs outperform other
077
      generative models within our framework. Our results indicate that LLMs can be particularly beneficial
078
      in low-data regimes, and under a strong covariate shift.

233   4.1   L ARGE LANGUAGE MODELS AS POTENTIAL OUTCOME GENERATORS
234                                                                                                  (gen)

238   While GATE can be be instantiated with generative models trained exclusively on observational
239          (obs)
      data Dt      – such as local regression models (Aloui et al., 2023)) – the utility of such solutions
240   is inherently limited since such generative models ultimately utilise the same information as the

242   foundational models, such as large language models (LLMs) (Bommasani et al., 2021).
243
      Why LLMs? LLMs bring several unique properties that make them particularly well-suited for this
244   task. Due to extensive pretraining they encode rich domain knowledge. As such, they can effectively
245   utilize dataset metadata, such as descriptions of covariates, study populations, or the context of
246   data collection, to align their outputs with the specific problem domain and integrate contextual
247                                                (obs)
      relationships that may not be apparent in Dt       . This is particularly advantageous in small-sample
248   regimes. Additionally, LLMs excel at few-shot learning, allowing them to adapt to a given task when
249                               (obs)
      conditioned on subsets of Dt      . By grounding their predictions in observed data while incorporating
250
      broader priors, LLMs can address shortcomings of D(obs) and improve alignment with real-world
251   distributions, thus allowing the downstream CATE models to transcendent observational data.
252
253   To harness the capabilities of LLMs, we employ two complementary prompting strategies, which –
254
      as we show in our empirical experiments (Section 6) – allow GATE to significantly improve CATE

259   about the data collection technique, the population of the study or more general context of the dataset.
260   (II) Conditioning the LLM on the observational dataset. We exploit the few-shot learning
261   capabilities of the LLM by conditioning the generation on a randomly chosen subset of samples
262   from the observational dataset D(obs) , presented in natural language, hence exploiting its in-context
263   learning abilities.

      5     R ELATED W ORKS
306   CATE Meta-Learners. Model-agnostic approaches to CATE estimation, known as meta-learners,
307   are widely studied for their competitive performance and strong theoretical foundations (Künzel et al.,
308
      2019; Kennedy, 2023). Among these, two-step learners (Kennedy et al., 2017; Nie & Wager, 2021;
      Curth & Van der Schaar, 2021b) implicitly leverage data augmentation by estimating all missing
309
      potential outcomes in the first step. Unlike two-step learners, GATE selectively imputes outcomes
310
      based on the generative model’s properties. This flexibility allows GATE to act as a pre-processing
311   step in the meta-learning pipeline, offering performance gains (e.g., by regularization or incorporating
312   external knowledge) without altering the standard meta-learners. We empirically demonstrate GATE’s
313   benefits also to two-step learners in Section 6, with further discussion in Appendix A.
314

315

316

317   with local regression models. Unlike GATE, these methods do not provide a principled way to utilize
318   generative models trained on external datasets, which is critical in small-sample or high covariate
319   shift settings. We compare GATE against GANITE and COCOA in Section 6.3 and Appendix F.1,
320   showing improved performance across datasets.

327
328   Data. Evaluating CATE models using observational data is challenging due to the lack of ground-
329   truth CATE values. Standard benchmarks like IHDP (Hill, 2011) or News (Johansson et al., 2016)
330   address this by designing artificial potential outcome functions. However, since we aim to compare
331   generative models trained on D(obs) with those trained on external (real-world) datasets, the outcome’s
332   relationship with treatment and covariates must be reality-grounded. Consequently, we utilize the
333
      following datasets: Lalonde CPS1 (LaLonde, 1986) (n = 7279), where synthetic counterfactual
      outcomes are obtained using a pre-trained generative model from RealCause (Neal et al., 2020); STAR
334
      Project (Achilles et al., 2008) (n = 1429), obtained by subsampling an RCT dataset using the method
335
      from (Gentzel et al., 2021) to induce covariate shift, with ‘ground-truth’ CATE estimates derived
336   from the full RCT; and Hillstrom (Hillstrom, 2008) (n = 9639), obtained similarly. Additional
337   experiments on the IHDP dataset are in Appendix F.2. We partition these datasets into training D(obs)
338   and test Dtest sets of equal sizes, and standardize both covariates and continuous outcomes before
339   inputting them into the CATE learners.
340
      CATE Models. We evaluate GATE by comparing the performance of downstream CATE models
341
      when trained on the original dataset D(obs) vs. when trained on the augmented D̃(obs) . From the
342
      portfolio of CATE meta-learners, we use the S-, T-, X-, R-, IPW- and DR-learner (Künzel et al., 2019;
343
      Curth & Van der Schaar, 2021b). We also consider the CFR-Wass and CFR-MMD algorithms (Shalit
344   et al., 2017) relying on balanced representations, designed specifically to tackle the covariate shift
345   (Johansson et al., 2022). We further complete the list of considered models with TARNet (Shalit
346   et al., 2017), DragonNet (Shi et al., 2019) and BART (Athey & Imbens, 2016).
347
      Instantiating GATE. To test the potential of instantiating GATEwith LLMs, we use GPT-3.5 Turbo
348
      (Achiam et al., 2023) – a widely used and reliable model, well-suited for robust evaluations of our
349
      framework. Further, we define the admissible set Xt with the variance-based criterion (Section 4.2),
350   using a fixed threshold α = 0.5 unless otherwise stated (note that this value is not tuned between
351   datasets or experiments, to allow for fair evaluation). We also use 100 in-context samples in each
352   prompt unless otherwise stated. In experiments in section Section 6.3, we compare the performance
353   of the LLM-instantiated GATE against a diverse set of models trained on D(obs) , which include:
354   mean model, 1-nearest neighbour (1-NN), random forest (RF) and GAN (following the approach of
355   GANITE (Yoon et al., 2018)) (detailed description of these models can be found in Appendix D.3).
356
      Takeaways. The takeaways from the experiments are organised as follows:
357
      ▶ Section 6.2: LLM-instantiated GATE improves performance across diverse datasets and benchmark
358   meta-learners.
359   ▶ Section 6.3: Although multiple generative models used within GATE provide performance benefits
360   for CATE estimation, leveraging LLMs provides substantial performance gains over models trained
361   solely on D(obs) , particularly in low-sample regimes.
362   ▶ Section 6.4: Prompting the LLM with meta-data and contextual information is crucial to elicit its
363   prior knowledge. In-context samples further allow to improve its performance.
364   ▶ Section 6.5.1: GATE is particularly advantageous in problems with strong covariate shift.
365   ▶ Section 6.5.2: Adjusting the admissible set Xt through the hyperparameter α enables balancing the
                            (gen)
366   bias introduced by Pt,x against the reduction in covariate shift, optimizing overall performance.
367   More detailed descriptions of the experiments can be found in Appendix D. Anonymised code to
368   reproduce the experiments can be found here.

524    7                         D ISCUSSION AND F UTURE W ORKS
525
       Model Selection. Within the proposed implementation of GATE (see Section 3.1), using GATE re-
526    quires making two key choices: selecting the generative model and choosing the value of the
527    hyperparameter α. We provide principles to guide the selection of the generative model in Section 4.1
528    and Section 6.3. Once the generative model is selected, the value of α can be tuned using standard
529    model selection approaches in CATE estimation, noting that setting α = 0 corresponds to the ’no
530    augmentation’ baseline. This means that if the generative model is not sufficiently accurate and might
531    degrade downstream performance, using GATE can recover the baseline performance. We also note
532    that while incorporating GATE introduces one additional hyperparameter, as we show in Section 6.2,
533    it can reduce the performance gap between different CATE learners, thus potentially simplifying the
534
       model selection process.
535    Broader Impact. In Section 6 we learn that GATE may enable practical adoption of CATE estimation
536    in low-sample settings, possibly yielding a positive impact in fields where data is costly. Furthermore,
537    GATE helps address problems such as covariate shift (particularly in low-sample regimes), further
538    aiding the adoption of CATE inference in practice. However, extra care should be taken before relying
539    on the LLM to guide decision-making in high-stakes domains.

      Methods for CATE estimation. Machine learning methods for CATE estimation can be broadly di-
813   vided into two categories: model-specific and model-agnostic methods. Method-specific approaches
814   rely on adjusting specific machine learning methods to the treatment effect setting. This gives rise to
815   solutions based on neural networks (Shalit et al., 2017; Shi et al., 2019; Johansson et al., 2016; Curth
816   & Van der Schaar, 2021a), Gaussian processes (Alaa & Van Der Schaar, 2017) or random forests and
817   regression trees (Athey & Imbens, 2016; Wager & Athey, 2018; Hahn et al., 2020; Hill, 2011).
818
      In contrast, model-agnostic methods (so-called ’meta-learners’ (Künzel et al., 2019; Curth & Van der
819   Schaar, 2021b)) are general learning strategies which can be instantiated with any base learner (e.g.,
820   neural network, random forest). Within the model-agnostic strategies we can distinguish the one-step
821   learners, which directly estimate the potential outcome surfaces, µ̂0 and µ̂1 , and then obtain the
822   CATE as: τ̂ = µ̂1 − µ̂0 . The alternative two-step learners (Kennedy et al., 2017; Nie & Wager, 2021;
823   Curth & Van der Schaar, 2021b) implicitly rely on ideas from data imputation. In their first step,
824   two-step learners obtain pseudo outcomes Ỹϕ , which are “proximal" target treatment effect values,
825   composed from nuisance parameters ϕ = (π, µ0 , µ1 ) estimated from the given observational dataset
826   D(obs) (or a subset of it). In the second step, the final CATE model is obtained by regressing the
827   pseudo-outcomes Ỹϕ on the covariates X. The pseudo-outcomes can be obtained using strategies
828   relying on propensity weighting (IPW-learner (Horvitz & Thompson, 1952)), regression-adjustment
829   (X-learner (Künzel et al., 2019)) or both of these combined (DR-learner (Kennedy, 2023)). We note
830   that our framework GATE is a strategy which is complementary to these standard two-step learners.
831
832   How is GATE different from a standard two-step learner?
833

838            where Xt ̸= X , GATE does not allow to explicitly obtain treatment effect proxies for all

859   instantiation of the two-step learning strategy, relying for example on obtaining the pseudo-outcome
860   using a GAN model (Yoon et al., 2018), or local regression methods (Aloui et al., 2023). However,
861   these imputation approaches are constrained by the amount of information present in the observational
862   datasets. As a result, they are particularly vulnerable to scenarios with covariate shift, where there
863   are significant differences between the distributions of the control and treated groups. Furthermore,
      these imputation methods (GAN and local regression) require large amounts of data to be accurate,

      LLMs as sources of prior knowledge for downstream tasks. As large language models (LLMs)
870
      have increased in parameter count and training set size, it has become clear that they are able to
871
      act as knowledge bases, showing great performance across a variety of knowledge-retrieval tasks
872   (Brown et al., 2020; Nori et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024; He et al., 2024). As such, LLMs have been
873   proposed as tools for extracting prior knowledge about the world, which can be used to ground
874   standard data-driven ML models in real-world contexts and encourage their outputs to be consistent
875   with common-sense reasoning based on the meta-data (Choi et al., 2022). Relying on the inductive
876   biases generated by the LLMs from the task-specific metadata has been demonstrated to improve
877   performance on tasks as diverse as reinforcement learning (Choi et al., 2022; Du et al., 2023), tabular
878   learning (Zhu et al., 2023; Seedat et al., 2024) as well as causal discovery (Choi et al., 2022; Takayama
879   et al., 2024; Jiralerspong et al., 2024; Ban et al., 2023). Furthermore, recent studies demonstrate
880
      that LLMs are in principle capable of gaining knowledge about the underlying causal structure
      of real-world data generating processes, despite not being explicitly trained to reason ‘causally’
881
      (Richens & Everitt, 2024; Zečević et al., 2023). This finding is further supported by empirical
882
      research, demonstrating LLMs potential for answering causal queries (Long et al., 2024; Willig et al.,
883   2022; Zečević et al., 2023). This further motivates the use of LLMs as sources of prior knowledge
884   and relevant inductive biases in causal inference tasks in particular.

      B.2    T HE QUESTION OF MODEL SELECTION AND HYPERPARAMETER TUNING
960
961   The fundamental problem of causal inference makes the standard approaches to model selection and
962   hyperparameter tuning not applicable in CATE estimation. Because the ground truth CATE value
963   is unobserved, one cannot simply choose a model which performs best on a held-out validation set.
964   Instead, model selection for CATE estimation has to rely on heuristics, assumptions on the data
965   generating process and general prior knowledge of the problem at hand (Curth & Van Der Schaar,
966   2023). Model selection procedures for causal inference models remain an active area of research
967   (Saito & Yasui, 2020; Schuler et al., 2018; Lan & Syrgkanis, 2024; Curth & Van Der Schaar, 2023;
968
      Mahajan et al., 2022).
969   The challenges of model selection in causal inference also apply to GATE. Deciding which of the
970   available generative models should be used to augment the observational dataset at hand is non-trivial,
971   and neither is the question of choosing the admissible set Xt (e.g. by specifying the value of α in our
      proposed instantiation) for a given generative model.

       Choosing the generative model. We provide the following insights which might guide the selection
973    of the generative model within the GATE framework:
974

976             on D(obs) seems to depend on the size of the dataset (with LLMs providing particular
977             performance improvements in smaller datasets, cf. Figure 3), as well as on the variability
978             in the (standardized) potential outcomes (the Hillstrom dataset – where the performance
979             gap is particularly small – has V ar(Y (1)) = 0.04 and V ar(Y (0)) = 0.02, while in the
980             STAR dataset – where the performance gap is particularly large – V ar(Y (1)) = 1.05 and

                V ar(Y (0)) = 0.93). In scenarios with low outcome heterogeneity and/or large sample
                sizes, a simple model such as mean imputation can already perform well.
982

984
                the use of LLMs with GATE permits to make the generation process more transparent.

985
                generations. Indeed, alternative prompting strategies can be employed to elicit explicit
986

987             in-the-loop applications of GATE, where domain experts can evaluate the generated data by
988             examining these reasoning traces against their domain knowledge. As such, we view the use
989             prompting techniques for explicit reasoning, such as chain-of-thought (Wei et al., 2022) or

       Sources of variance. We acknowledge that the variance in the outcomes generated by the LLM,
1019
       which we use as a proxy to evaluate the LLM’s uncertainty and hence guide the selection of the
1020   admissible set Xt , might capture different types of uncertainty. Firstly, it might reflect the aleatoric
1021   uncertainty, which refers to the irreducible uncertainty of the outcome distributions. Secondly, it
1022   also captures the epistemic uncertainty, which accounts for both the insufficiency of observational
1023   data in some regions of the covariate space and insufficient semantic knowledge of the LLM. Our
1024   variance-based selection mechanism relies on the implicit assumption that the aleatoric uncertainty
1025   does not vary significantly across the covariate space X . This implies that choosing the admissible
       set Xt based on the variance allows to capture the differences in the epistemic uncertainty of the LLM
