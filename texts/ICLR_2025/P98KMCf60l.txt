011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Large Language Models (LLMs), built on Transformer architectures, exhibit re-
014            markable generalization across a wide range of tasks. However, fine-tuning these
015
               models for specific tasks remains resource-intensive due to their extensive param-
               eterization. In this paper, we investigate two remarkable phenomena related to
016
               the attention mechanism during the fine-tuning of LLMs. The first phenomenon,
017
               termed “Unequal Importance of Attention Matrices,” highlights the impact of fine-
018            tuning different weight matrices. It shows that optimizing the Wv matrix yields
019            significantly better performance than optimizing the Wk matrix. Fine-tuning only
020            the Wq and Wv matrices is computationally efficient while delivering results
021            comparable to, or even better than fine-tuning all three matrices (Wq , Wk , and
022            Wv ). The second phenomenon, “Attention Matrices with Customized Learning
023            Rate Leads to Better Convergence,” emphasizes the importance of assigning dis-
024            tinct learning rates to these matrices. Specifically, a higher learning rate for the
025            Wv matrix compared to Wq and Wk accelerates convergence and improves per-
026
               formance. Building on these insights, we propose a new strategy that improves
               fine-tuning efficiency in terms of both storage and time. Experimental results on
027
               benchmark datasets validate the effectiveness of this approach, supporting our the-
028
               oretical findings. Our analysis lays the theoretical groundwork for configuring and
029            improving lightweight algorithms in LLMs fine-tuning.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Large Language Models (LLMs) are often built on Transformer architectures [43] and possess a
035   large number of parameters, enabling them to generalize across a broad range of general tasks [43,
036   27, 42, 8, 31]. However, achieving optimal performance on specific tasks typically necessitates
037   fine-tuning these pre-trained models. Despite the formidable capabilities of LLMs, the fine-tuning
038   process is resource-intensive, requiring significant computational power, storage, and time due to
039
      the large scale of model parameters involved. Fine-tuning all the parameters of a large language
      model, known as full fine-tuning, is highly computationally expensive. To reduce the computational
040
      cost, various parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods have been proposed [7, 19, 23, 24, 20],
041
      which only fine-tune a small number of (extra) model parameters.
042
043   A fundamental component of transformers is the attention mechanism, particularly the interactions
044   among the query matrix Wq , the key matrix Wk , and the value matrix Wv . During the fine-tuning
045
      of LLMs involving the attention mechanism, two interesting phenomena have been observed: (1)
      Unequal Importance of Attention Matrices—optimizing the Wv matrix is pivotal for enhancing per-
046
      formance, significantly more so than adjustments to the Wk matrix, which exhibit limited impact
047
      on the outcomes. Additionally, fine-tuning only the Wq and Wv matrices often yields results that
048   are comparable to or surpass those achieved by fine-tuning all three matrices Wq , Wk , and Wv ,
049   which also reduces the number of tunable parameters by approximately 1/3, offering computational
050   benefits (Section 3). (2) Attention Matrices with Customized Learning Rate Leads to Better Conver-
051   gence—using the same learning rate for Wq &Wk and Wv is not optimal for efficient convergence.
052   In fact, it is essential to apply distinct learning rates for the Wq , Wk , and Wv components to ensure
053   optimal fine-tuning performance. Specifically, the learning rate for Wv should generally be higher
      than that for Wq and Wk to facilitate efficient convergence (Section 4).

054
      While certain empirical guidelines, such as the original Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) [20], explore
055   which weight matrices in transformers are suitable for the application of LoRA, comprehensive the-
056   oretical analyses of these phenomena are still limited. This includes aspects such as selecting appro-
057   priate weight types for fine-tuning and optimizing learning rate settings. Reflecting on the attention
058   equation itself (Section 2): (1) In linear algebra, two matrices multiplied without an intermediate
059   activation can be equivalent to a single matrix. Some studies [33, 40, 4] often treat Wq and Wk as
060   a single unit (Wqk = Wq WkT ), however, the benefits of fine-tuning Wq &Wv alone have yet to be
061   further clarified. (2) Cosidering the scenario where the values of Wq , Wk , and Wv approach zero,
062   the gradients of Wq &Wk tend to diminish towards zero. In contrast, the gradient of Wv remains
063   non-zero due to the influence of softmax normalization. Driven by the above motivations, this paper
064
      delves into the issue from the following two perspectives.
065
               • Generalization: advantages of fine-tuning Wq &Wv over Wq , Wk , Wv together. We
066
                 perform a thorough theoretical analysis to demonstrate the advantages. To be more specific,
067              we employ information-theoretic approaches [46, 34, 45, 53] to establish the generalization
068              bounds of fine-tuning pre-trained models with attention mechanism (See Theorem 1 for
069              details). This indicates that fine-tuning Wq &Wv instead of Wq , Wk , Wv reduces the
070              number of parameters, while improving generalization bounds and potentially providing
071              memory benefits.
072            • Optimization: convergence analysis of attention mechanism with varying learning
073              rate settings. To further investigate the aforementioned phenomena, we examine the op-
074              timization process of the attention mechanism. First, we discuss the learning dynamics in
075              transformers in Case 1, suggest that Wv may experience instances of inefficient learning
076              during fine-tuning process for downstream tasks. This naturally leads to the hypothesis
077              that accelerating the learning of Wv in the early stages could potentially induce Wk and
078              Wq to begin learning earlier. Additionally, by using scaling arguments for large width-n
079              networks [49, 13], we illustrate (Theorem 2) that the feature learning of attention mecha-
080
                 nism is efficient when the learning rate for Wv should be generally much larger than that
                 of Wq &Wk in fine-tuning.
081
082   Building on our experimental and theoretical insights, one can develop new algorithms to improve
083   the effectiveness (e.g., storage, and time) of fine-tuning. Experimental results for our strategy (in
084   Section 5) on benchmark datasets [44] and open source pre-trained models [29, 2] verify that the
085   method can visibly influence fine-tuning efficiency1 . We do not make direct comparisons with var-
086   ious parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods, as our strategy is primarily intended to demonstrate
087   how theoretical analysis can effectively guide experimental procedures.

280
281   In Section 3, we have already demonstrated the generalization performance of the attention mecha-
282   nism during fine-tuning. Our focus will now shift toward optimizing convergence efficiency. Some
283   optimization observations have also been reported in previous works [20, 25, 15], such as: Li et al.
284   [25] provide theoretical analyses of learning dynamics in transformers and observes a roughly two-
285   stage process of self-attention. Meanwhile, He et al. [15] empirically show that the attention mech-
286   anism, particularly the value vector, stores the largest amount of memories and has the greatest
287
      influence during fine-tuning. However, there is not yet a satisfactory explanation for why this phe-
      nomenon occurs or how it can be effectively leveraged. In this section, we will explore these ques-
288
      tions in more depth.

      4.1   A N I NSIGHT INTO I NEFFICIENT L EARNING FOR VALUE M ATRIX
291
292   We first discuss the optimization process of attention mechanism in the following simple case.
293
      Case 1. Omitting the scale factor for qualitative analysis in Eq.(1), we obtain:
294

295
296   Intuitively, if Wq , Wk , Wv are initialized as random matrices close to zero and trained simultane-
297   ously, then in the initial step, ∇Wk L(∇Wq L) contains the term Wq (Wk ), which is close to 0. By
298   contrast, ∇Wv L contains the softmax-normalized attention weights. Therefore, during the initial
299   steps (in training), Wv intuitively grows at a much faster rate than Wk (Wq ).
300
301   The work of [25] empirically exhibits Case 1 with an approximately two-stage phenomenon: (1) In
302   stage 1 (initial steps), the norms of Wk and Wq remain close to zero across all layers, while the
303
      norm of Wv increases significantly, accompanied by rapid changes in its orientation. (2) In stage
      2, the norms of Wk and Wq begin to grow significantly, though much later than the Wv matrices.
304
      Briefly, in this case, Wv reaches a certain level of learning during training before Wk and Wq
305
      begin to learn. This suggests that when fine-tuning the model for downstream tasks, there may also
306   be instances of inefficient learning in Wv . Additionally, is there a fine-tuning strategy that could
307   facilitate more effective learning for downstream tasks? For instance, accelerating the learning of
308   Wv in the early stages could potentially induce earlier learning in Wk and Wq .

      Remark 4. In practice, Theorem 2 implies that the learning rate for Wv should be generally much
438
      larger than that of Wq &Wk in fine-tuning. We verify that this scaling is valid for general neural
439   network models in Section 4.1. Naturally, the optimal ratio λ depends on the architecture and the
440   fine-tuning task through the constants in ‘Θ’. This represents a limitation of the asymptotic results,
441   as they do not provide insights into how the task and neural architecture influence these constants.
442   We will further address this issue in our future work.

444   A summary of the main theoretical analyses. According to the traditional statistical learning view-
445   point, performance can be defined by the sum of optimization error and generalization error. Our
446   theoretical analyses in Sections 3 and 4 correspond to generalization and optimization, respectively.
447
      In Section 3 (generalization, storage-friendly), we give Theorem 1 (Information-theoretic genral-
      ization bounds), showing that with the same r value, fine-tuning Wq &Wv consistently achieves
448
      results comparable to or even surpassing those of fine-tuning Wq , Wk , Wv . This reduces the num-
449
      ber of parameters for the same r, while improving generalization bounds and potentially providing
450   memory benefits. In Section 4 (optimization, time-friendly), we discuss the learning dynamics in
451   fine-tuning attention mechanism, and we illustrate (Theorem 2) that the feature learning of attention
452   mechanism is efficient when the learning rate for Wv should be generally much larger than that of
453   Wq &Wk in fine-tuning. Building on our experimental and theoretical insights, one can develop
454   new algorithms to improve the effectiveness (e.g., storage, and time) of fine-tuning (Example in

516   6    C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATION
517
518   In this paper, we present our key findings in fine-tuning attention mechanism: Unequal Importance
519   of Attention Matrices—optimizing the Wv matrix significantly improves performance compared to
520   the Wk matrix. Fine-tuning only the Wq and Wv matrices is computationally efficient and can
521   yield results that match or surpass fine-tuning all three matrices Wq , Wk , and Wv . Attention Ma-
522   trices with Customized Learning Rate Leads to Better Convergence—using distinct learning rates
523
      for these matrices is essential for optimal performance, with a higher learning rate for Wv speed-
      ing up convergence. While theoretical analysis of these phenomena is limited, this paper provides
524
      insights from two angles: Generalization—fine-tuning only Wq and Wv improves generalization
525
      and memory efficiency, and Optimization—using different learning rates enhances the efficiency of
526   feature learning in the attention mechanism, leading to more effective fine-tuning. Our analysis pro-
527   vides a theoretical foundation for the configuration and improvement of lightweight algorithms in
528   LLMs fine-tuning. However, further studies are required on (i) how the task and neural architecture
529   influence the optimal ratio λ and (ii) whether the results about attention hold true for tasks beyond
530   natural language processing. These studies will further deepen our understanding of attention-based
531   fine-tuning in LLMs.

      A     M ORE R ELATED W ORKS
703
704
      Attention mechanism analysis. A key component of transformers is the attention mechanism,
705
      which dates back to [9]. Initially designed to capture long-range signals in sequential inputs by mix-
706   ing individual tokens, it has also been utilized to capture general structures in input data. After the
707   fully-attention-based language model has appeared [43, 5], the research community gets interested
708   in the functionality and benefits of the attention. For instance, transformers implicitly favor hierar-
709   chical interpretations of input sequences [21], the computational graphs tend to be tree-structured
710   [30, 32]. Theoretical analysis of training dynamics sheds light on how to identify key tokens [41],
711   select a few relevant tokens only (which is called localized attention) or select many tokens uni-
712   formly [4], and learn topic structure [25]. Besides, considerable works [36, 1, 51] try to understand
713   in-context learning capabilities from the perspective of gradient descent with attention.
714   Scaling for neural networks. Scaling refers to the process of enlarging a specific ingredient of a
715   model to enhance its overall performance [18]. The method is straightforward: extend the width or
716   depth of a neural network towards infinity, analyze how this limit is influenced by hyperparameters
717   like the learning rate and initialization variance during training, and then establish well-founded
718   choices for these hyperparameters to achieve a specific objective [17, 38, 11, 47, 49, 14, 10, 50, 13].
719   In the theory of scaling of neural networks, one usually tracks the asymptotic behaviour of key
720
      quantities as we scale some model ingredient, it is a standard approach used to derive scaling rules
      for initialization [38], activation function [11], network parametrization [50]. In this paper, we are
721
      interested in scaling model capacity via the width n for the fact that most state-of-the-art pre-trained
722
      models have large width. Examples of the infinite-width limit can be found in studies focused on
723   initialization methods [17, 47], or more comprehensive approaches to network parameterization.
724   For instance, Yang et al. [49] introduced µP, a parameterization technique for neural networks that
725   guarantees feature learning in the infinite-width limit, providing specific scaling rules for both ar-
726   chitecture and learning rates to optimize feature learning [48, 49].
727
      Parameter-efficient fine-tuning. Fine-tuning all the parameters of a large language models, known
728
      as full fine-tuning, is highly computationally expensive. To reduce the computational cost, various
729   parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods have been proposed [7], which only fine-tune a
730   small number of (extra) model parameters. PEFT methods can be divided into two categories from
731   the perspective of whether extra parameters are involved: (1) extra-parameter methods, freeze all of
732   the original parameters of an LLM and insert a set of learnable parameters to optimize the model
733   input or model layers suach as adapter tuning [19] , prompt tuning [23] and prefix tuning [24]; (2)
734   intra-parameter methods freeze most of the original parameters of an LLM and only tune a small
735   number of parameters of the LLM such as LoRA [20]. Furthermore, He et al. [16] present a unified
736   framework that establishes connections between PEFT methods and Zhu et al. [53] formally identify
737
      and investigate asymmetry in the roles of low-rank adapter matrices in LoRA fine-tuning.
