011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Unlearning techniques have been proposed as a cost-effective post-training way
014            to remove undesired knowledge learned by large language models (LLMs). How-
015            ever, existing methods often fail to effectively unlearn the targeted information
016
               or cause a significant drop in model performance. In this paper, we frame ma-
               chine unlearning as a multi-task optimization problem to balance this tradeoff –
017
               one task maximizes forgetting loss, while the other minimizes retaining loss. We
018
               introduce a novel unlearning method, Normalized Gradient Difference (NGDiff),
019            which guarantees Pareto optimality upon convergence. Specifically, NGDiff dy-
020            namically normalizes task gradients, enabling the model to unlearn targeted for-
021            getting data while preserving utility on the retaining set. We also identified that
022            unlearning methods are sensitive to learning rate and integrate an automatic learn-
023            ing rate scheduler that selects the locally optimal learning rate to stabilize and
024            accelerate the convergence. Experiments with various LLMs demonstrate that
025            NGDiff outperforms state-of-the-art unlearning methods on the TOFU and MUSE
026            datasets.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Large language models (LLMs) are trained on a huge collection of data from various sources, such
032   as books and websites. For example, LLAMA3 is pre-trained on over 15T tokens Dubey et al.
033
      (2024), while Falcon Penedo et al. (2023b), OLMo Groeneveld et al. (2024) are each pre-trained
      on over 1T tokens. However, this extensive data pre-training raises serious concerns about data
034
      risks for the following reasons: (1) Certain data sources, despite being publicly available, contain
035
      potentially harmful or sensitive content, such as nudity, personal information, copyright-protected
036   information, etc. (2) LLMs can memorize these data during training, and then re-generate them.
037   For example, Exhibit J from The New York Times Times (2023) shows examples of outputs from
038   GPT-4 that “contain large spans that are identical to the actual text of the article from The New
039   York Times,” which are copyrighted; some attacks can extract an individual’s name, email address,
040   phone number, and physical address from LLMs Carlini et al. (2021). Furthermore, the risk of
041   memorization increases with the size of the model (see Figure 1 and 3 in Carlini et al. (2022)),
042   exposing bigger models with higher utility to greater risks of data memorization.
043   Although retraining the models by removing the problematic data can resolve this issue, this ap-
044   proach is not feasible given that LLMs cost millions of dollars and take months to train, and the cost
045   escalates each time new data are identified as problematic. Recently, researchers have developed
046   a number of machine unlearning methods, which are applied after the models have completed the
047   training and memorized the data. Specifically, we divide the data into two classes: the retaining
048   set (R), on which the model can memorize and have high performance, and the forgetting set (F),
049   which the model should not memorize. The goal of unlearning is to continue training the model in a
050
      way, so that knowledge from the forgetting set is effectively removed, and that the unlearned model
      behaves similarly to one that is retrained solely on the retaining set.
051
052   Existing machine unlearning methods are formulated primarily as optimization techniques aimed
053   at minimizing memorization through the language model loss Liu et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2024);
      Liu et al. (2024b). For instance, the Gradient Ascent (GA) method seeks to maximize the language

      In this paper, we formulate the unlearning as a multi-task optimization (MTO) problem Chen et al.
100
      (2021); Xin et al. (2022): we aim to minimize the loss (or maximize the utility) on the retaining
101
      set and maximize the loss on the forgetting set, simultaneously. To solve this two-task problem, we
102   leverage the rich literature of multi-task methods to achieve the Pareto optimality of two tasks, e.g.
103   IMTL Liu et al. (2021), GradNorm Chen et al. (2018a), RLW Lin et al., PCGrad Yu et al. (2020),
104   and scalarization Boyd & Vandenberghe (2004). In particular, we leverage the linear scalarization as
105   a simple and strong candidate among all multi-task methods, which minimizes a linearly weighted
106   average of the task losses (see (2)). This is motivated by two reasons: theoretically, linear scalariza-
107   tion is guaranteed to be Pareto optimal (see Lemma 2); empirically, it outperforms or at least is on
      par with other MTO methods in a variety of language and vision experiments Xin et al. (2022).

      Contributions We focus on and extend the linear scalarization for machine unlearning as follows:
109
110          • We formally formulate machine unlearning as a multi-task optimization problem and es-
111            tablish the Pareto optimality for scalarization-based unlearning methods (including ours),
112            as demonstrated in Theorem 3.
113          • Through the lens of multi-task optimization (two-task specifically), we propose a novel
114            unlearning method NGDiff, which leverages the gradient norms to dynamically balance the
115            forgetting and retaining tasks. In particular, NGDiff improves both tasks simultaneously
116            and monotonically under the proper learning rate, as demonstrated in Theorem 5.
117          • We integrate the automatic learning rate from GeN Bu & Xu (2024), which adaptively
118            and dynamically selects the learning rate based on the Hessian information, and thereby
119            achieving stable convergence.
120          • We empirically showcase the effectiveness of our method through extensive experiments on
121            multiple datasets, different LLMs and vision models. For example, on the TOFU dataset,
122            our method achieves 40% higher model utility while maintaining comparable unlearning
123            performance with the Llama2-7B model.

      To optimize two tasks, it is critical to consider the Pareto optimality in Definition 1 as MTO may
138   have infinitely many solutions.
139
      Definition 1 (Pareto optimality in unlearning). For two models θ and θ ′ , if LR (θ) ≥ LR (θ ′ ) and
140
      LF (θ) ≤ LF (θ ′ ) with at least one inequality being strict, then θ is dominated by θ ′ . The model θ is
141
      Pareto optimal if it is not dominated by any other models.
142
143   In practice, the machine unlearning solutions generally exhibit a trade-off between the performance
144   on R and F (see Figure 1): without the unlearning, both R and F have high performance and high
145   memorization; in order to forget F, one may unlearn other general knowledge such as grammar rules,
146   which oftentimes sacrifices the performance on R.
147   We will illustrate several MTO methods and show that the Pareto optimality is guaranteed upon the
148   convergence of these methods.

      3     U NLEARNING WITH NORMALIZED GRADIENT DIFFERENCE
217
218   While Theorem 3 shows the Pareto optimality of θ ∗ as t → ∞, it does not shed insight on the
219   convergence through intermediate steps θt . Put differently, although many MTO and unlearning
220   methods are all Pareto optimal upon convergence, they may converge to different Pareto points at
221   different convergence speed. Therefore, it is important to understand and control their algorithm
222   dynamics in order to maintain high performance for R throughout the training. To be specific, the
223   dynamics is determined by their choices of gUN ∈ Rd and ηt ∈ R in (3).
224   In this section, we propose to use gradient normalization for gUN and automatic learning rate for ηt ,
225   so as to achieve stable convergence, effective unlearning, high retaining utility, without manually
226   tuning the learning rate.

385   4.4   E VALUATION M ETRICS
386
387   Following the existing work (Shi et al., 2024), we evaluate the unlearning performance based on
388   model’s output quality. We expect a good performance should satisfy the following requirements:
389   No verbatim memorization. After the unlearning, the model should no longer remember any
390   verbatim copies of the texts in the forgetting data. To evaluate this, we prompt the model with the
391   first k tokens in F and compare the model’s continuation outputs with the ground truth continuations.
392   We use ROUGE-L recall scores for this comparison, where a lower score indicates more effective
393   unlearning.
394   No knowledge memorization. After the unlearning, the model should not only forget verbatim
395   texts, but also the knowledge in the forgetting set. For the MUSE-NEWS dataset, we evaluate knowl-
396   edge memorization using the KnowmemF split, which consists of generated question-answer pairs
397   based on the forgetting data. Similar to verbatim memorization, we use ROUGE-L recall scores for
398   the evaluation.
399   Maintained model utility. An effective unlearning method must maintain the model’s performance
400   on the retaining set. We prompt the model with the question from R and compare the generated
401   answer to the ground truth. We use ROUGE-L recall scores for these comparisons. Additionally,
402   we evaluate the model using the Truth Ratio metric. We use the Retain10-perturbed split from
403   the TOFU dataset, which consists of five perturbed answers created by modifying the facts in each
404   original answer from R. The Truth Ratio metric computes how likely the model generates a correct
405   answer versus an incorrect one, where a higher value indicates better model utility.

532   5   D ISCUSSION
533
534   We have formulated the machine unlearning problem as a two-task optimization problem, which
535   can be provably (under Pareto optimality) and effectively solved by our novel unlearning method
536   NGDiff. We also adapt GeN to set an automatic and adaptive learning rate scheduler but we believe
537   other learning-rate-free methods can serve as alternatives, maybe after some modifications. While
538   NGDiff is applicable to general problems, most experiments in this work focus on LLMs except
539   one in Appendix C. It would be desirable to test NGDiff on more modalities, and test more MTO
      methods for machine unlearning (see a list of methods in Appendix D).

      We compare the GradNorm algorithm Chen et al. (2018b) with our proposed method, NGDiff. We
732
      highlight some steps of GradNorm in red to indicate the differences than NGDiff:
733
734          • NGDiff sets the scalaring coefficient as 1/||gR || and 1/||gF ||, while GradNorm uses gradi-
735            ent descent to learn these coefficients as wR and wF .
736          • NGDiff is model-agnostic while GradNorm contains specific designs for multi-task archi-
737            tecture. In unlearning, there are 2 data splits (i.e. F and R) and each data split defines one
738            task. Hence all model parameters are shared. However, in the original form of GradNorm,
739            there is 1 data split on which multiple tasks are defined (can be more than 2). Hence the
740            model parameters are partitioned into [shared layers, task 1 specific layers, task 2 specific

825   Machine unlearning Machine unlearning is oftentimes viewed as a continual learning approach,
826   that removes specific data points after a model has been trained to memorize them. Such removal
827   is light-weighted in contrast to re-training, especially when the forgetting set is much smaller than
828   the retaining. In addition to the methods already introduced in Section 2.2 (namely GA, GDiff and
829   NPO), other methods include SISA Bourtoule et al. (2021), influence functions Ullah et al. (2021),
830
      differential privacy Gupta et al. (2021) and so on. However, these methods could be difficult to scale
      on large models and large datasets due to the algorithmic complexity. To our best knowledge, this is
831
      the first work that formulate the unlearning problem as a two-task problem, which can be solved by
832
      a number of well-known MTO methods.
833
834
835
836
      Multi-task optimization MTO is a paradigm where one model is trained to perform multiple tasks
      simultaneously, so as to significantly improve the efficiency in contrast to training multiple models,
837
      one for each task. The key challenge of MTO is the performance trade-off among tasks, where the
838
      multi-task model is worse than single-task model if trained on each task separately. Therefore, the
839   core idea is to balance different tasks by modifying the per-task gradients, e.g. with normalization
840   (LossNorm and NGDiff), PCGrad Yu et al. (2020), RLW Lin et al., IMTL Liu et al. (2021), MGDA
841   Désidéri (2012), CAGrad Liu et al. (2024a), GradVaccine Wang et al. (2020), GradDrop Chen et al.
842   (2020), RotoGrad Javaloy & Valera (2022), etc.
843
844
845   Learning-rate-free methods Parameter-free or learning-rate-free methods automatically set the
846   learning rate scheduler without the hyperparameter tuning, which is computationally infeasible for
847   LLMs, e.g. LLAMA2 pre-training uses 3 hyperparameters just for the learning rate: warmup steps,
848   peak learning rate, and minimum learning rate. At high level, there are two approaches to learning-
849   rate-free methods.
850   On one hand, GeN Bu & Xu (2024) leverages the Taylor expansion and convex-like landscape of
851   deep learning, which is applicable for the general purpose, even if the gradient is modified like in
852   the unlearning.
853

854
