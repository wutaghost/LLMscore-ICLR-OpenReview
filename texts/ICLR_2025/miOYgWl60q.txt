011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013              Clinical interviews are the gold standard for detecting depression, and previ-
014              ous work using multimodal features from participants’ audio, transcribed text,
015              and video have shown promising results. Recent approaches further improve
016              performance by incorporating an additional textual modality—the interviewer’s
017              prompts—during training. However, these approaches risk introducing biases, as
018              models may over-rely on specific prompt-response pairs, which may not always
019              be present in real-world settings. This leads to models exploiting these cues as
020              shortcuts for detecting depression rather than learning the language and behaviors
021
                 that genuinely indicate the subject’s mental health, ultimately undermining consis-
                 tency and objectivity. To address this, we propose a novel approach that combines
022
                 Contextual Position Encoding (CoPE) and Latent Space Regularization (LSR),
023
                 leveraging both subjects’ responses (audio) and the interviewer’s prompts (text).
024              CoPE captures the evolving context of the interview, ensuring that the model uti-
025              lizes insights from the entire conversation, preventing over-reliance on isolated
026              or late-stage cues. This helps the model understand interactions holistically and
027              more accurately reflect mental health indicators. LSR introduces constraints to
028              enforce consistency in the model’s learned representations, reducing overfitting to
029              superficial cues and guiding the model toward more generalizable patterns. By
030              smoothing the latent space, LSR helps the model focus on meaningful, high-level
031              representations of both audio and text. Our approach yields competitive results
032
                 on the DAIC-WOZ benchmark and surpasses the state-of-the-art on the EATD
                 benchmark. The code is released1 .

      1       I NTRODUCTION
037
038
039   Depression is a widespread condition with severe consequences, such as emotional distress, so-
040
      cial withdrawal, and even suicide. According to the World Health Organization (WHO)2 , over 300
      million people worldwide suffer from depression, impacting individuals, families, and society pro-
041
      foundly (World Health Organization, 2017). Unfortunately, in many communities, lack of awareness
042
      and stigma around mental health problems result in underdiagnosis and undertreatment. This em-
043   phasizes the urgent need for practical depression screening tools (Kessler, 2012; Craft & Landers,
044   1998).
045
046
      At present, clinical interviews (Figure 1) are still the standard method for evaluating depressive
      symptoms(He et al., 2022). These interviews mainly consist of four main components: the inter-
047
      viewer’s questions, and the participant’s responses in text, audio, and video formats, which serve
048
      as the basis for supervised learning in automatic depression assessment. Typically, these interviews
049   are semi-structured, multi-round question-and-answer conversations where the interviewer uses a
050   flexible outline to dig into the participant’s personal experiences and mental states.

      Multimodal approaches that incorporate multiple data sources, such as participants’ audio, text, and
070   video collected during interviews, have shown promising results in depression detection. These
071   approaches can be broadly categorized into two types (Al Hanai et al., 2018). The first type models
072   a participant’s outcome based on responses to specific questions (e.g., What makes you sad?’ or Do
073   you have a history of depression?’), while the second type models outcomes based on responses that
074   are independent of the questions asked (e.g., loudness). In the first approach, (Arroll et al., 2005)
075   and (Burdisso et al., 2024) explored the use of key question sets to optimize screening accuracy
076   while minimizing time. (Yang et al., 2017) combined multiple modalities conditioned on manually
077   selected questions for depression detection. The second approach attempts to exploit global and
078
      time-varying statistics, independent of the question that prompted the response, by treating the entire
      interview (e.g., a waveform file) as a single input data point. For example, (Du et al., 2023) used
079
      MFCCs extracted from audio as the primary input, while (Xue et al., 2024) utilized both textual and
080
      audio information in their multimodal fusion module.
081
082   Recent studies have shown that incorporating the interviewer’s prompts as additional textual input,
083   in addition to using participants’ audio, video, and transcribed text, can further improve perfor-
084
      mance (Milintsevich et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024; Agarwal et al., 2024; Burdisso et al., 2024).
      However, these methods, along with the first approach discussed above, face two significant chal-
085
      lenges. First, focusing on a participant’s response to specific mental-state probing questions during
086
      an interview may optimize accuracy and reduce screening time, but it may not be practical in real-
087   world scenarios, where participants might try to conceal their true emotional state when asked direct
088   questions (Pretorius et al., 2019; Wilson et al., 2011). Second, conducting clinical interviews con-
089   sisting of numerous question-answer dialogues, from general questions gradually leading to mental-
090   state-specific questions, is not always feasible. In shorter interviews with random, less predictive
091   questions, previous models tend to be less robust and generalizable.
092
      Given the question-answer format of depression screening tests (Figure 1), we use the interviewer’s
093   prompt (text) and the participant’s response (audio) as input data for the task of depression detec-
094   tion, framing it as a binary classification problem to predict whether the participant is depressed or
095   non-depressed. We model depression using contextual position encoding (CoPE) (Vaswani, 2017;
096   Shaw et al., 2018; Golovneva et al., 2024), which captures the position of interactions within the in-
097   terview. This allows the model to consider the evolving context of the conversation and ensure that
098   comprehensive information from the entire interview is effectively utilized. This prevents the model
099   from relying on isolated cues that only appear in specific regions of the interview. After combining
100   the data from these two modalities, we apply regularization on the latent space (LSR) (Kingma,
101
      2013; LeCun et al., 2015; Higgins et al., 2017) to smooth it by reducing overfitting caused by biases
      from specific question-answer pairs. This ensures that the model learns consistent patterns.
102
103   We evaluated our model on two datasets: DAIC-WOZ (Gratch et al., 2014) and EATD (Shen et al.,
104   2022). Our approach has the potential to enhance automated healthcare decision-making and offer
105   better support for clinicians in practical settings.

      2     R ELATED WORK
109
110
111   2.1   S INGLE -M ODALITY D EPRESSION D ETECTION
112
113
       (Zhang et al., 2021) proposed DEPA, a self-supervised, pre-trained audio embedding model for
114   depression detection using a traditional encoder-decoder framework. (Nguyen et al., 2022) utilized
115   Reddit data, manually defining PHQ-9-based patterns for classification and introducing a BERT-
116   based model to bypass explicit questionnaires. (Liu et al., 2024) combined contrastive learning
117   and a capsule network to classify depressed and control groups based on PHQ-9 responses. (Du
118   et al., 2023) introduced MSCDR, which used features like MFCC and linear predictive coding with
119   LSTMs to capture dynamic depressive features across speech data. (Burdisso et al., 2023) applied
120   Graph Convolutional Networks with self-connection nodes to capture long-distance semantics in
121   participants’ textual responses.
122
123
124   2.2   M ULTIMODAL D EPRESSION D ETECTION
125
126   Multimodal approaches combine features from text, audio, and video to enhance depression detec-
127   tion. (Gong & Poellabauer, 2017) used an ensemble of audio, video, and semantic features, with
128   topic modeling to identify relevant interview topics. (Al Hanai et al., 2018) employed LSTMs to
129   model interactions between audio and text features, demonstrating that sequential modeling can de-
130   tect depression even with minimal structural information. (Wu et al., 2023) leveraged pre-trained
131   foundational models with self-supervised learning, fine-tuning representations from ASR-based
132   emotion and text for classification. The MMPF model(Yang et al., 2024) refined text and audio
133   features using expert knowledge before applying late fusion. (Iyortsuun et al., 2024) introduced a
134
      cross-modal attention network (ACMA) with BiLSTMs to capture interactions between audio and
      text. (Zhang et al., 2024) integrated acoustic landmarks into an LLM framework, converting speech
135
      signals into linguistically meaningful tokens for multimodal depression detection.
136
137
138
139   2.3   I NCORPORATING I NTERVIEWER ’ S P ROMPTS FOR D EPRESSION D ETECTION
140
141   The Hierarchical Context-Aware Graph (HCAG) attention model proposed by (Niu et al., 2021)
142   applies a Graph Attention Network to integrate contextual information from relational interview
143   questions across audio and text modalities for depression detection. (Dai et al., 2021) extracted top-
144   ics from interviewer questions and constructed feature vectors combining audio, video, and semantic
145   features for context-aware analysis. To capture interactions between prompts and audio responses,
146   (Shen et al., 2022) utilized GRU and BiLSTM models with an attention layer to summarize mul-
147
      timodal representations. (Milintsevich et al., 2023) framed depression detection as a symptom
      profile prediction task, training a hierarchical regression model on patient-therapist interview tran-
148
      scripts. (Agarwal et al., 2024) leveraged the interview structure using a multi-view architecture,
149
      separating transcripts into patient and therapist views based on sentence type to exploit symmetrical
150   discourse. (Chen et al., 2024) introduced the Structural Element Graph (SEGA), representing clini-
151   cal interviews—including audio, text, and video—within a directed acyclic graph inspired by expert
152   knowledge, augmented with LLM-based data. (Xue et al., 2024) designed the Channel Attention-
153   based Multimodal Fusion Module (CAMFM), which integrates low-level audio features, wav2vec
154   representations, and BERT-derived sentence embeddings for comprehensive fusion.
155
       (Burdisso et al., 2024) demonstrated that introducing interviewer prompts can bias models to con-
156   centrate on certain question-answer pairs. Although some methods take advantage of this by manu-
157   ally selecting predictive pairs, they often lack generalizability, particularly in conversational contexts
158   where specific questions might not be available. To tackle this issue, we propose CoPE, a method
159   that dynamically encodes the position of each interaction within the entire conversation, allowing the
160   model to capture the participant’s changing emotional state. Additionally, we regularize the latent
161   space after data fusion to minimize overfitting and ensure that the model learns robust, generalizable
      patterns.

184   3.1   OVERALL FRAMEWORK AND PROBLEM FORMULATION
185
186   In a clinical interview I from the corpus C, the interviewer asks the participant a series of m ques-
187   tions (recorded as text) Q = {q1 , . . . , qm }, typically probing into their feelings, experiences, and
188
      mental states. The participant’s responses are recorded in audios A = {a1 , . . . , am } capturing tone
      and prosody. Each question (qi ) and its corresponding audio response (ai ) form a single question-
189
      response pair.
190
191   Given a dataset D = {I1 , . . . , In } consisting of n interviews, each interview Ik consists of a set of
192   question-response pairs {(q1 , a1 ), . . . , (qm , am )}, and the task is to predict whether the participant
193   is depressed (denoted by the binary label yk ∈ {0, 1}). Our proposed overall framework is shown in
194
      Figure 2.
195   Contextual Position Encoding (CoPE) combines both absolute position information and contextual
196   information within a sequence to provide a semantically meaningful representation of each interac-
197   tion (Vaswani, 2017; Golovneva et al., 2024). Unlike absolute position encoding, which treats each
198   position independently, CoPE integrates the surrounding context into the position encoding, allow-
199   ing the model to simultaneously capture both token-level and sentence-level positions. This ensures
200
      that responses are interpreted not only by their standalone content but also by their position relative
      to earlier interactions, making it especially suited for tasks like depression detection, where earlier
201
      responses may shape later ones.
202
203   In our task, CoPE ensures that the model accounts for the evolving nature of the conversation,
204   attending to earlier emotionally charged interactions that may influence subsequent responses. This
205   avoids over-reliance on isolated cues appearing late in the conversation.
206   We compute the positional encoding PEi for each interaction (qi , ai ) as:

224   Latent Space Regularization (LSR) (Kingma, 2013; LeCun et al., 2015; Higgins et al., 2017) is a set
225   of techniques that can be used to reduce domain shift. These techniques have been applied in various
226
      semantic segmentation tasks, such as continual learning (Michieli & Zanuttigh, 2021) and few-shot
      learning (Dong & Xing, 2018; Wang et al., 2019). These strategies generally involve imposing
227
      additional constraints on the feature vectors, effectively reducing the space each can occupy. This
228
      reduction has promoted more overlap between the source and target distributions, thus decreasing
229   the domain shift (Toldo et al., 2021; Barbato et al., 2021). Unlike traditional weight-based regular-
230   ization techniques such as L1 or L2 , which control model complexity by penalizing large weights,
231   LSR directly constrains the latent feature space. This enables the fused text and audio representations
232   to capture more robust patterns and mitigates overfitting to noise or superficial features, ultimately
233
      enhancing model generalization. Without regularization, models can overfit to noisy or superficial
234   patterns, such as specific question-response pairs or keywords that may only appear in certain parts
235   of the interview.

265   3.2   AUDIO FEATURE EXTRACTION
266
267   We use two types of audio features: Low-Level Descriptors (LLDs) and wav2vec 2.0 embeddings.
268   LLDs (Eyben et al., 2015) are manually crafted features derived from the raw waveform. They cap-
269   ture both time-domain and frequency-domain properties, such as short-time energy, zero-crossing
      rate, and spectral centroid, providing a detailed audio signal representation. For LLD extraction,


                                                          5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      we use OpenSMILE (Eyben et al., 2010) to generate a 23-dimensional feature set (excluding the
271   bandwidth of the second and third formant peaks), with a frame size of 0.025 seconds and a frame
272   step of 0.01 seconds. Wav2vec 2.0 (Baevski et al., 2020) is a pre-trained model that learns audio
273   representations through multiple convolutional layers. It captures features across varying time and
274   frequency scales, producing high-level embeddings of the raw audio signal.
275
276   3.3     T EXT FEATURE EXTRACTION
277
278   We use BERT (Devlin et al., 2018), a pre-trained transformer model, to generate contextualized
279   text embeddings. Unlike traditional word embeddings, BERT-based sentence embeddings capture
280   intricate word dependencies and contextual nuances, providing a deeper understanding of the re-
281
      lationships within text sequences. This feature makes BERT well-suited for tasks involving rich
      semantic and syntactic context, such as identifying mental health indicators in interview data (Senn
282
      et al., 2022). In our methodology, we employ BERT base3 to extract sentence-level embeddings
283
      from the interview prompts in the DAIC-WOZ dataset. For the EATD dataset, we utilize a pre-
284   trained Chinese BERT model4 with the same number of parameters for consistency.

353   4.1      DATASETS AND IMPLEMENTATION
354
355   The DAIC-WOZ dataset (Gratch et al., 2014) is a widely used English dataset consisting of inter-
356   views from 189 participants, recorded in transcripts, audio, and video formats. Each participant is
357   labeled with a PHQ-8 score (Kroenke et al., 2009) (ranging from 0 to 24), with participants scor-
358   ing 10 or higher classified as depressed (D) and those scoring below 10 labeled as control (C). The
359   training set contains data from 107 participants (30 depressed and 77 control), the development set
360
      includes 35 participants (12 depressed and 23 control), and the test set has 47 participants. Since the
      labels for the test set are not publicly available, we follow prior studies and use the development set
361
      for evaluation. The dataset is publicly available upon request5 .
362
363   The EATD dataset (Shen et al., 2022) is a relatively new Chinese dataset that consists of inter-
364   views with 162 student volunteers. These interviews were recorded in both transcript and audio
365   formats. Participants were asked to respond to three randomly selected questions and also complete
366
      a Self-rating Depression Scale (SDS) questionnaire. Each participant was then assigned an SDS
      score (Zung, 1965), with scores of 53 or higher indicating depression. Out of the 162 participants,
367
      132 were found to be non-depressed, while 30 were found to be depressed. The authors have made
368
      the dataset publicly available6 .
369

374
      question-answer pairs {(q1 , a1 ), . . . , (q10 , a10 )} from each of the 30 depressed samples to form the
375   first set. However, unlike these approaches, we maintained the original order of responses when

412   sets using 3-fold cross-validation, with a 2:1 ratio between the training and development data.
413   After resampling, we have 154 and 208 audio samples for training from the DAIC-WOZ and EATD
414   datasets, respectively. Since the current version of DAIC-WOZ lacks interview videos, and the
415   EATD does not contain video at all, visual cues are not utilized in this study. Please find detailed
416   statistics of the two datasets in Appendix A.2, Table 4, and our discussion on the limitations of
417   these datasets in Appendix A.10. To ensure consistency, each question-answer pair within an audio
418   sample—10 pairs per sample for DAIC-WOZ and 3 pairs per sample for EATD—is adjusted to
419   fixed durations of 3 seconds and 10 seconds, respectively. This standardization ensures that all
420   audio samples from both datasets have a consistent length of 30 seconds.

      In line with most previous works, we use the F1 score, accuracy, and precision as evaluation metrics

      for our binary classification task. The rationale and further details on these metrics can be found

      in Appendix A.7. Our analysis and training were executed on a PC running Windows 10 (version

      5      C ONCLUSIONS
525
526
      This paper presents a method for identifying depression through interviews by combining partici-
527
      pants’ audio and interviewers’ text prompts. To overcome the limitations of prior methods reliant
528   on specific question-answer pairs, we introduce two components: Contextual Position Encoding
529   (CoPE), which captures the evolving nature of interactions, and Latent Space Regularization (LSR),
530   which promotes robust, generalizable representations while reducing overfitting.
531
      Our method achieved competitive performance on all metrics on the DAIC-WOZ dataset and state-
532
      of-the-art performance on the EATD dataset. By focusing on contextually relevant interactions and
533
      capturing the flow of the conversation, we significantly improved the detection of subtle emotional
534   patterns associated with depression. Our ablation study demonstrated that our approach is more
535   resilient when faced with datasets containing random, non-conversational style data. Future work
536   could explore additional modalities, such as visual cues or physiological signals, to enhance the
537   model’s predictive power. Additionally, extending the framework to other mental health conditions
538   could provide valuable insights into how different features contribute to various mental states. Our
539   approach offers a promising direction for building more consistent and generalizable systems for
      clinical applications. The limitations of our work are discussed in Appendix A.10.

788
      wav2vec 2.0 features, the input dimension is 768, and for the OpenSMILE features, it is 23. The
      BERT embeddings also have an input dimension of 768. Each BiLSTM has a hidden dimension
789
      of 256, with 2 layers and a bidirectional configuration. Dropout of 0.3 is applied, and the activa-
790
      tion function used is ReLU. The learning rate for the BiLSTMs is set to 1 × 10−4 with the Adam

794
      input dimension of 768. The positional encoding dimension is set to 512. Learnable weights α and

795
      absolute and contextual information. The learning rate is set to 5 × 10−5 with a dropout of 0.2, and
796
      the optimizer used is AdamW.

799   attention mechanism. The input dimension for the fused audio and text features is 768, with 8
800
      attention heads. Dropout is set to 0.1, and the learning rate is 2 × 10−5 . The output dimension after
      fusion is 768. AdamW is used as the optimizer.
801

803   a strength of 0.01 to the fused latent space, which has a dimension of 768. Dropout of 0.3 is applied
804   to the latent space. The batch size is set to 32, with a learning rate of 1 × 10−4 , and the optimizer
805   used is Adam.
806   Fully Connected (FC) Layer: The final fused representations are passed through a fully connected
807   layer for binary classification. The input dimension is 768, with a hidden layer of 128 dimensions.
808   The activation function is ReLU, and dropout is set to 0.2. The output dimension is 1 (binary:
809   depressed vs. non-depressed). The learning rate for the FC layer is 1 × 10−4 , and AdamW is used
      for optimization.

1051   A.10    L IMITATIONS
1052
1053   Our work has several limitations. First, although we integrate audio and text modalities through
1054   an attention-based fusion mechanism, we do not include other potentially useful modalities such as
1055   visual cues (e.g., facial expressions or gestures), which have been shown to provide relevant signals
1056
       in assessing mental health conditions. There are two main reasons for not including visual cues
1057
       or physiological data in this study. The first reason is the availability of such data in real-world
1058
       settings. Labeled speech data is limited, and clinically labeled speech data is even scarcer. There-
1059
       fore, to make a framework that can be integrated into real-world clinical scenarios, we aimed to
1060   develop a framework that operates efficiently with easily accessible data, specifically the patient au-
1061   dio recordings and the questions posed by the clinician or interviewer. The second reason is that we
1062   used the DAIC-WOZ and EATD datasets in this study because both of them are interview-based.
1063   However, the EATD dataset does not include video recordings, preventing us from utilizing visual
1064   cues in our analysis. We acknowledge that incorporating visual or physiological data could further
1065   improve the accuracy and robustness of the model in future work, provided that more datasets with
1066   comprehensive modalities of data become available.
1067   Several critical factors must be considered to implement our model in real-world clinical settings.
1068
       First, even when data is collected through interviews, variations such as language differences, the
1069
       types of speech tasks (e.g., reading scripted sentences, engaging in spontaneous speech, or recall
1070
       tasks), and the recording environments (e.g., laboratory settings, natural settings, one-on-one inter-
1071
       actions, or group interactions) can significantly affect the downstream task of depression detection.
1072
       Second, when data is not collected through interviews, capturing and modeling the complex patterns
1073
       between an interviewer’s prompts and a participant’s responses becomes more challenging. There-
1074
       fore, in our future work, we aim to develop a more generalizable and reliable approach to ensure our
1075
       model is better suited for real-world applications.
1076
1077   Additionally, while effective at handling imbalanced datasets through LSR, our approach still heav-
1078   ily relies on the availability of annotated data, which can be costly and time-consuming to collect in
1079   clinical settings. The use of semi-supervised or unsupervised techniques could alleviate this reliance
       on labeled data and further enhance the model’s generalizability.
