009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The success of clinical trials of longevity drugs relies heavily on identifying in-
012            tegrative health and aging biomarkers, such as biological age. Epigenetic aging
013            clocks predict the biological age of an individual using their DNA methylation
014            profiles, commonly retrieved from blood samples. However, there is no standard-
015            ized methodology to validate and compare epigenetic clock models as yet. We
016
               propose ComputAgeBench, a unifying framework that comprises such a method-
               ology and a dataset for comprehensive benchmarking of different clinically rel-
017
               evant aging clocks. Our methodology exploits the core idea that reliable aging
018
               clocks must be able to distinguish between healthy individuals and those with
019            aging-accelerating conditions. Specifically, we collected and harmonized 66 pub-
020            lic datasets of blood DNA methylation, covering 19 such conditions across differ-
021            ent ages and tested 13 published clock models. We believe our work will bring
022            the fields of aging biology and machine learning closer together for the research
023            on reliable biomarkers of health and aging.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
      Longevity drugs (a.k.a., geroprotectors) appear to be on the brink of entering clinical practice to
028
      slow down or reverse the features of aging (Moqri et al., 2024; Justice et al., 2018). The research
029   community is yet to identify proper biomarkers of aging and rejuvenation that could be used as
030   clinical trial endpoints instead of or in combination with observations on patient lifespans (Schork
031   et al., 2022). Biological age (BA) has been proposed as one of such surrogate biomarkers of aging,
032   defined as a generalized measure of human health compared to the average health of individuals at a
033   given age within a population (Yousefi et al., 2022; Jylhävä et al., 2017). Thus, if an individual has a
034   biological age of 40 at the chronological age of 30, it is assumed that their overall health corresponds
035   to that of an average 40-year-old in the population. This relationship can be concisely expressed as
036                                                B = C + ∆,                                                (1)
037   where B represents biological age, C denotes chronological age (i.e., time since birth), and ∆
038   symbolizes BA acceleration (or deceleration, if negative).
039
      In general, BA can be estimated from a set of biomarkers X with a model (algorithm) f : X →
040
      B, also called an aging clock. However, BA is latent: it has no ground truth value that can be
041
      measured directly and then used to train an aging clock model f in a classical supervised fashion,
042   making clock validation a nontrivial task (Sluiskes et al., 2024). This obstacle forces researchers to
043   introduce various additional assumptions about the aging clock behavior (Klemera & Doubal, 2006;
044   Horvath, 2013; Pierson et al., 2019; Rutledge et al., 2022), as well as to experiment with different
045   machine learning models (including penalized linear regressions, such as ElasticNet, support vector
046   machines, decision trees, transfomer-based neural networks, etc. (Rutledge et al., 2022; Urban
047   et al., 2023)) and underlying types of data X (Putin et al., 2016; Xia et al., 2020; Holzscheck et al.,
048   2021). The vast majority of aging clocks, though, rely primarily on DNA methylation data, also
049   called epigenetic aging clocks (Hannum et al., 2013; Levine et al., 2018; Lu et al., 2019; Galkin
050
      et al., 2021; Ying et al., 2024). Summarizing abundant discussions about a “good” mathematical
      description of BA in the literature (Moskalev, 2019; Rutledge et al., 2022; Moqri et al., 2024), we
051
      elicited four of its defining properties, formalized as follows.
052

      acceleration ∆ = B − C, the following four properties hold:

      Garnered together, these properties motivated us to construct a benchmarking methodology for val-
077
      idating the potential biological age predictors. In property #1, the model f should output age values
078
      in a biologically meaningful range, comparable with a typical lifespan, e.g., from 0 to 120 years
079   for the humans. To investigate if a model f satisfies the 2nd property, we can define a panel of
080   aging-accelerating (or decelerating) conditions and test if the predicted ∆ allows distinguishing the
081   individuals with an AAC/ADC from a control group, according to an appropriate statistical test. To
082   validate the compliance with the 3rd and the 4th properties, one also needs data on mortality and
083   multi-morbidity. That is, the information about the timing of death or the onset of chronic age-
084   related diseases, along with a prior measurement of a set of relevant biomarkers. It is important to
085   note that such data are highly sensitive and are generally not publicly available.
086   DNA methylation (DNAm) is the most prevalent measurement employed in the construction of aging
087   clocks (Xia et al., 2021). From a chemical point of view, DNA methylation refers to a covalent
088   modification of DNA nucleotides by the methyl groups (Greenberg & Bourc’his, 2019). Specifically,
089   cytosine nucleotides (C) followed by guanine nucleotides (G), also referred to as cytosines in a CpG
090   context or simply CpG sites (CpGs), are methylated most often in the mammalian cells, making it the
091   most well-studied type of DNA methylation (Seale et al., 2022) (refer to Fig. 1 for visualization of
092   the DNA and CpGs). This epigenetic modification plays a crucial role in regulating gene expression
093
      and is engaged in a variety of cellular events, varying significantly across different species, tissues,
      and the lifespan. DNA methylation levels per site are usually reported quantitatively as beta values
094
      that represent the methylation proportion at a specific CpG site in the range from 0 to 1, where 0
095
      indicates no methylation, and 1 indicates complete methylation across all the cells in the sample
096   (Fig. 1).
097
098   Importantly, despite the numerous recent publications of various aging clocks (Xia et al., 2021; Rut-
099
      ledge et al., 2022; Yousefi et al., 2022), including the ones built on DNA methylation, no systematic
      open access benchmark, which would include standardized panel of datasets, diseases, interven-
100
      tions, or other conditions, has been proposed to date to validate the aforementioned properties. In
101
      this paper, we introduce such a benchmark to validate the 1st and the 2nd properties in epigenetic
102   aging clocks. To do this, we developed a methodology for identifying aging-accelerating conditions,
103   which relies on simple, yet strict and evidence-based principles for defining and selecting a panel of
104   aging-accelerating conditions. We collected an unprecedented number of DNA methylation datasets
105   for the respective conditions from dozens of published studies. We also developed a cumulative
106   benchmarking score that aggregates two error-based tasks and two simple, but informative tasks
107   based on common statistical tests. Ultimately, this cumulative score enables comparing aging clock
      ability to satisfy the 1st and the 2nd properties.

      2     R ELATED WORK AND BACKGROUND
116
      2.1   AGING CLOCK CONSTRUCTION METHODOLOGY
117
118   Because the BA ground truth values cannot be measured, and, therefore, a direct validation of ag-
119   ing clocks is problematic, previous studies introduced various approaches to construct aging clocks
120   with different underlying assumptions. The most widespread one, belonging to the so-called “first-
121   generation aging clocks”, uses an assumption that a model f can be trained to predict chronological
122   age, i.e., C = Ĉ + ε = f (X) + ε, and its predictions will correspond to BA: B = Ĉ. The simplicity
123   of this approach has made it attractive for decades, and it is still used today to train new aging clocks
124   on new types of data (Hollingsworth et al., 1965; Voitenko & Tokar, 1983; Duggirala et al., 2002;
125   Varshavsky et al., 2023; Prosz et al., 2024). In fact, BA obtained by this approach can satisfy the
126   2nd (Horvath, 2013) and the 3rd (Kuiper et al., 2023) properties from our definition. However, using
127
      this assumption in Eq. (1) leads us to the conclusion that ε = −∆. It then turns out that the perfect
      solution of the chronological age prediction problem, i.e., minimizing the prediction error so that
128
      ε → 0, leads to the inability of a clock to identify any aging acceleration or deceleration. Namely,
129
      it implies that ∆ → −0, which is also known as the biomarkers paradox (Hochschild, 1989; Kle-
130   mera & Doubal, 2006). Supporting this concept, it has been shown that the clocks featuring strong
131   correlation with the chronological age poorly correlate with the population mortality (Zhang et al.,
132   2019) (hence they fail to satisfy the 3rd property). As a consequence, validating clock performance
133   in terms of accuracy of chronological age prediction becomes meaningless, because high accuracy
134   may not necessarily correspond to a biologically relevant clock. Despite the obvious methodological
135   challenges of this approach, it is worth noting that the vast majority of aging clocks belong to the
136   first generation (Sluiskes et al., 2024).
137   Seeking for a better solution, researchers experimented with survival models, which led to the de-
138   velopment of ”second-generation aging clocks”. In this approach, models are trained to predict
139   time to death (Levine et al., 2018; Lu et al., 2019; Hertel et al., 2016), and the resulting predic-
140   tion is rescaled to age units to represent BA, therefore addressing the 3rd and the 4th properties of
141   a ”good” BA estimator. However, there is no open large-scale DNA methylation data containing
142   time-to-death or multi-morbidity measurements, with existing studies being either available upon an
143   authorized request or being held completely private (see Appendix A.7).
144
145   2.2   ATTEMPTS TO COMPARE EPIGENETIC AGING CLOCKS
146
147
      Despite reported attempts to compare the performance of different aging clocks, a benchmark with
      a standardized panel of datasets, diseases, interventions, or other conditions has not been proposed
148
      yet. As a result, different comparative studies employ widely varying validation data and approaches
149
      (Moqri et al., 2024; Ying et al., 2024; Kuiper et al., 2023; Mei et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2021; Huan
150   et al., 2022; Chervova et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2020; Maddock et al., 2020; McCrory et al., 2021).
151   As highlighted in a recent review on biomarker validation by Moqri et al. (2024), “for a reliable
152   comparison across studies, . . . biomarker formulations should be established ‘a priori’ and not be
153   further modified during validation”. In the same line of thought, we propose to define a standardized
154   and a justified procedure for clock benchmarking before constructing any predictive model.
155
      Two approaches we propose as essential tasks in our benchmark entail related prior art. For example,
156   Porter et al. (2021) and Mei et al. (2023) used one-sample or two-sample aging acceleration tests for
157   clock validation. Ying et al. (2024) employed two-sample tests across multiple aging clocks. These
158   authors implicitly tested the 2nd property of “good” aging clocks discussed above. Likewise, there
159   were also attempts to test the 3rd and the 4th properties separately. In other works, including the
160   recently updated pre-print of Biolearn (Ying et al., 2023), a Python-based framework for aging clock
161   training and testing in ongoing development, authors performed Cox Proportional Hazards analysis
      and calculated hazard ratios with statistical significance to test if BA estimates of selected clocks are

162
      capable of predicting all-cause mortality or the onset of age-related diseases (e.g., cardiovascular
163   events) (Kuiper et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2021; McCrory et al., 2021; Huan et al., 2022; Chervova
164   et al., 2022; Ying et al., 2023). However, these prior studies are either small-scale (Ying et al., 2024),
165   limited to predicting the chronological age (Liu et al., 2020), or miss standardized datasets and
166   compare only a small number of models (Porter et al., 2021; Mei et al., 2023), or rely on mortality
167   and disease data that are under restricted access (Ying et al., 2023). Therefore, while developing our
168   methodology, we attempted to mitigate all mentioned drawbacks.

175   3.1       C RITERIA FOR SELECTING AGING - ACCELERATING CONDITIONS
176
177   In the context of clock benchmarking, we propose to define an aging-accelerating condition (AAC)
178   as a biological condition that satisfies the following three criteria (Fig. 2B). First, having an AAC
179   must lead to decreased life expectancy (LE) compared to the general population, even when treated
180   with existing therapies. Second, an AAC must be chronic (to safely assume that it has sufficient
181   time to drive observable changes in DNAm). And third, an AAC must manifest itself systemically,
182   so that it can be expected to affect DNAm in blood, saliva, and buccal cells (hereafter referred to as
183
      BSB).
184   Importantly, the decrease in LE and the corresponding increase in mortality must result mainly from
185   intrinsic organismal causes rather than from socioeconomic factors and self-destructive behaviors
186   related to a given condition. The second criterion is aimed at excluding short-term conditions such
187   as acute infectious diseases, stressful events, and other confounding DNAm-alternating accidents,
188   whose effects might not induce significant changes in DNAm data obtained from BSB, or, on the
189
      contrary, might last too briefly to be reliably detected. The third part of the AAC definition pre-
      cludes us from considering events with long-lasting and life-threatening consequences that might be
190
      difficult to observe in BSB-derived data. For instance, a bone fracture (unless it is a critical bone
191
      marrow reserve) or some types of malignancies.
192
193   Conversely, an aging-decelerating condition (ADC) is defined as a condition that increases LE,
194   compared to the general population, and features the same second and third criteria as an AAC.
195
      With human data, however, the ADCs are difficult to determine, as the human lifespan-increasing
      interventions are yet to emerge. To avoid ambiguous interpretation, we omitted such conditions in
196
      our benchmarking of human aging clocks (see Appendix A.5 and Table A2 for more details).

199   3.2       C RITERIA FOR DATASET SELECTION
200
201   Aiming to provide a comprehensive, easily accessible, and clinically relevant toolbox for the ongo-
202
      ing research on human epigenetic clocks, we relied on the following five criteria while performing
      the datasets aggregation (Fig. 2C). First, all datasets in the benchmark must feature open access
203
      to pre-processed data, without any data access requests or raw data processing required. Second,
204
      we only used data obtained from the BSB samples. Third, chronological ages must be annotated
205   with, at most, one year intervals (e.g., without age binning by decades), including only samples
206   from the age range of 18–90 years1 . The only exception to this requirement are the individuals
207   with certain progeroid conditions, such as the Hutchinson-Gilford progeria syndrome, who survive
208   approximately 12 to 13 years on average: these conditions resemble premature aging so strikingly
209   (Schnabel et al., 2021) that we included patients aged under 18 years into the benchmark. Fourth, we
210   employ data obtained only with the Illumina Infinium BeadChip (27K, 450K, and 850K) methyla-
211   tion microarrays, as they remain to be the most popular technologies for human DNAm profiling and
212   clock construction. Fifth, we applied thresholds of at least 10 samples per dataset, 5 samples with
213
      an AAC per dataset, and 10 samples with an AAC across all datasets to attain sufficient statistical
      power.

      3.4   E PIGENETIC AGE PREDICTORS
296
297   Any blood-based epigenetic aging clock that predicts BA in age units (or can be re-scaled to them)
298   can be validated in our benchmark. We tested 13 publicly available epigenetic clock models trained
299   on adult human data to evaluate sample age (Table A4), with the model coefficients retrieved from
300   the corresponding studies. Among the collected first-generation clocks, 6 were trained purely on
301   blood samples (Hannum et al., 2013; Ying et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2016; Vidal-Bralo et al., 2016),
302   and 3 models were trained on multiple tissues (Horvath, 2013; Zhang et al., 2019; Horvath et al.,

304   CpG sites as predictive features (Levine et al., 2018; Higgins-Chen et al., 2022), while the other 2

      required additional information about gender and chronological age as inputs (Lu et al., 2019; 2022).

307

      3.5   B ENCHMARKING TASKS FOR EVALUATING AGING CLOCKS
313
314   To benchmark aging clock models, we propose four tasks: relative aging acceleration prediction
315   (Fig. 3A), absolute aging acceleration prediction (Fig. 3B), chronological age prediction accuracy
316   (Fig. 3C), and systematic chronological age prediction bias (Fig. 3D). In the first two tasks, the
317   clocks are tested if they can correctly predict aging acceleration in the predefined panel of AAC
318   datasets.
319   In the relative aging acceleration prediction task (AA2 task), we test aging clock ability to distin-
320   guish AAC from healthy control (HC) samples in a dataset containing both sample groups. After
321   predicting ages in each dataset corresponding to this task using various clock models, we apply a
322   two-sample Welch’s test per dataset and calculate a one-sided P-value (i.e., HA : ∆AAC > ∆HC ) to
323   determine if mean aging acceleration in the AAC cohort is significantly greater than that in the HC
      cohort (Fig. 3A). Next, we apply the Benjamini-Hochberg correction procedure for controlling the


                                                         6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      false discovery rate (FDR) of predictions across all datasets, with an adjusted P-value less than 0.05
325   considered indicative of statistical significance. We selected a parametric test due to the assump-
326   tion of normal distribution of ∆, a fundamental trait of the multivariate linear regression models
327   commonly used in aging clock construction.
328
      In the absolute aging acceleration prediction task (AA1 task), we test clock ability to correctly
329
      predict positive aging acceleration for an AAC in the absence of the HC cohort. For each dataset
330
      in this task, we predict ages using various clock models, apply a one-sample Student’s t-test and
331   calculate a one-sided P-value (i.e., HA : ∆AAC > 0) to determine if mean aging acceleration in
332   the AAC cohort is significantly greater than zero (Fig. 3B). As before, we apply the Benjamini-
333   Hochberg correction procedure for controlling FDR with the same adjusted P-value threshold.
334
      Clearly, the first task (AA2) provides a more rigorous way to test aging clocks compared to AA1,
335
      because it helps to control potential covariate shifts, but the second task (AA1) deserves its place
336
      in the list, as it allows including more data into the panel to overcome data scarcity. The third
337   task is aimed at distinguishing good predictors of chronological age from predictors of biological
338   age. Due to the paradox of biomarkers mentioned above, it is highly unlikely that the same model
339   could combine both these properties. Yet, the good predictors of chronological age are believed to be
340   useful in forensics (Paparazzo et al., 2023) or data labeling, where the chronological age information
341   is lacking. We chose median absolute aging acceleration (M ed(|∆|)), a full equivalent of median
342   absolute error, for testing clock performance. We calculate it across HC samples from the whole
343   dataset panel and report it as a single number expressed in years.
344   We introduced the fourth task, a prediction bias task, to evaluate the robustness of a given aging
345   clock model to covariate shift between the original clock training dataset and the datasets from the
346   proposed benchmark. Covariate shift, also referred to as batch effect in bioinformatics, denotes the
347   shift between covariate distributions in two datasets. For instance, the distribution of methylation
348   values for a given CpG site could be centered around 0.45 in one dataset and around 0.55 in the
349   other one—a common scenario in DNAm and other omics data. Because each clock is trained on
350   healthy controls, we expect age deviation of HC samples to be zero on average (i.e., E(∆HC ) = 0).
351
      In practice, however, due to the presence of a covariate shift between the training and testing data,
      a clock might produce biased predictions, resulting in a systemic bias and adding or subtracting
352
      extra years for a healthy individual coming from an external dataset. The goal of the fourth task
353
      is to control for such systemic bias in clock predictions. Therefore, as a benchmarking metric for
354   this task, we calculated median aging acceleration (M ed(∆)) across HC samples from the entire
355   dataset panel, which reflects the systematic shift in clock predictions caused by differences between
356   datasets.

470   5   D ISCUSSION
471
472
      Biological age is an elusive concept that cannot be measured and validated directly, which necessi-
473
      tates careful choice of model assumptions to avoid methodological errors and false discoveries while
474   estimating it. While maintaining some degree of correlation between predicted and chronological
475   age is desirable, the biomarkers paradox (Klemera & Doubal, 2006) precludes one from automati-
476   cally accepting a BA estimation as acceptable (via the classic performance metrics of chronological
477   age prediction accuracy). From a methodological perspective, training BA predictors to estimate
478   time to death or a disease onset remains the most rigorous approach to aging clock validation, as
479   these events can be measured directly. However, obtaining such data is challenging due to various
480   ethical and financial constraints. At present, no open access data of DNA methylation with mortality
481   labels are available for public clock benchmarking (see Appendix A.7).
482   While mortality data remain unavailable, we propose to validate clocks by their ability to demon-
483   strate BA acceleration in a fixed pre-determined panel of datasets for established aging-accelerating
484   diseases or predict decelerated aging in the datasets of lifespan-prolonging interventions. For that,
485   we developed our benchmark, where each aging clock could be tested across 4 distinct tasks. We
      gathered an unprecedented number of DNA methylation datasets from more than 50 studies,


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      covering 19 putative aging-accelerating conditions. Notably, no aging-decelerating conditions
487
      have been confirmed for the benchmark study (see Appendix A.5). It should be taken into account
488   that in vitro cell reprogramming cannot serve as validation data for the deceleration effect, because,
489   as has previously been shown (Kriukov et al., 2023), such data are essentially out-of-domain with
490   regard to blood DNA methylation across aging.
491
      To showcase our benchmark, we tested 13 different published models and revealed that the second-
492
      generation aging clocks, namely, PhenoAge (Levine et al., 2018), GrimAge (Lu et al., 2019), and
493
      their upgraded variants (Higgins-Chen et al., 2022; Lu et al., 2022), were the most successful, ac-
494   cording to the cumulative benchmarking score. As these clocks had initially been designed to predict
495   all-cause mortality, they were expected to be robust in distinguishing aging-accelerating conditions.
496   Yet, our findings reinforce the growing trends in training BA predictors based on mortality rather
497   than chronological age (Yousefi et al., 2022; Moqri et al., 2024).
498
      As blood DNA methylation generally comes from the immune cells, which would be directly af-
499
      fected by the HIV, it is not surprising that the majority of clocks managed to discern accelerated
500   aging in the immune system-related conditions (featured predominantly by the HIV infection in our
501   dataset). This result supports the notion that the blood-based clocks might be implicitly attuned to
502   such conditions, while only a few clocks are capable of successfully capturing accelerated aging in
503   the other disease classes.
504
      Remarkably, some datasets were evaluated incorrectly by all models, which may have several pos-
505
      sible explanations apart from the poor clock performance. First, a strong covariate shift between
506   these data and the training data might impede model performance on some datasets. Second, some
507   selected conditions might not induce accelerated aging in blood, either by itself or by the design of
508   the original study (see Limitations in Appendix A.1). Third, the multidimensionality of aging as a
509   biological phenomenon might not allow for correct prediction of all aging-accelerating conditions
510   by such univariate measures as the blood-based epigenetic clocks. In favor of this notion, it has
511   recently been shown that different organ systems have different aging trajectories (Schaum et al.,
512   2020; Oh et al., 2023), suggesting several directions for the future research, outlined in Appendix
513   A.2.
514
515   6   C ONCLUSION
516
517   In this work, we developed the first systematic benchmark for evaluating blood-based epigenetic
518   aging clocks. We believe it will help longevity researchers and data scientists to better gauge the
519   power of existing biomarkers of aging, quantitatively assessing their role, limitations, and reliability.
520   We anticipate that, as a result of such computational paradigm, rapid and reliable clinical trials of
521
      lifespan-extending therapies will become an attainable reality in a not-so-distant future.

      A.1   L IMITATIONS
921
922   The current version of our benchmark harbors several important limitations. First, some selected
923   conditions might not actually fulfill the suggested criteria, especially regarding their effect on blood
924   DNA methylation, although we did our best to identify the most unambiguous ones. From the other
925   hand, some conditions that fit our criteria might have escaped our attention. Second, the conditions
926   are not represented uniformly, with some being featured in 10+ datasets (HIV, rheumatoid arthritis),
927   and some present in a single dataset with few samples (ischemic heart disease, chronic obstruc-
928   tive pulmonary disease, congenital generalized lipodystrophy). The third limitation arises from the
929
      known issue of hidden subgroups of patients and mislabeled instances (Varoquaux & Cheplygina,
      2022). For the AAC cohorts, having hidden co-morbidities is acceptable, as they would supposedly
930
      exaggerate aging acceleration even stronger. Conversely, having severe, but unlabeled diseases in
931
      the HC cohort would likely substantially alter the findings of our benchmark. Unfortunately, we can
932   neither expand our dataset to cover all conditions equally, nor explicitly confirm if all studies at hand
933   comply with our requirements.

      A.2   F UTURE WORK
936
937   We plan to further extend our benchmarking dataset by incorporating open access data of additional
938   modalities, such as clinical biochemistry, transcriptomics, proteomics, metabolomics, etc. To over-
939   come the aforementioned limitations, we strongly urge an open discussion on developing a panel of
940   conditions and datasets that would serve as the gold standard for reliable and comprehensive valida-
941   tion of emerging biomarkers of aging. We also believe that it is important to expand the benchmark
942   to animal models, since collecting the required data and developing preclinical biomarkers of ag-
943   ing in some animals is associated with fewer ethical and financial challenges. Hopefully, all these
944
      issues and developments will be addressed by the efforts of a recently established Biomarkers of
      Aging Consortium (https://www.agingconsortium.org/). Ultimately, the “correct” BA
945
      estimator should satisfy all four properties we defined in the Introduction. Regardless of the clock
946
      generation or data modality, reliable aging clock models must also be able to assess the uncertainty
947   of their own predictions before being integrated in clinical trials (Chua et al., 2023; Kriukov et al.,
948   2023). And indeed, an example of uncertainty-aware aging clocks has recently been proposed (Var-
949   shavsky et al., 2023). We also aim to upgrade our package to facilitate the interaction with other
950   clock-related resources, including Biolearn (Ying et al., 2023) and pyaging (de Lima Camillo, 2024).

       A.5   M OTIVATION BEHIND INCLUDING OR EXCLUDING PARTICULAR CONDITIONS
993
994
       Our first criterion for selecting aging-accelerating conditions (AACs) was that having an AAC must
995
       lead to decreased life expectancy (LE) compared to the general population, even when treated with
996
       existing therapies. As we have mentioned earlier, this decrease in LE and the corresponding increase
997    in mortality must result mainly from intrinsic organismal causes rather than from socioeconomic fac-
998    tors and self-destructive behaviors related to a given condition. Thus, while Down syndrome (DS) is
999    associated with elevated prevalence of multiple chronic diseases (O’Leary et al., 2018; Landes et al.,
1000   2020; Baksh et al., 2023), LE of DS individuals has grown dramatically by over 450% from 1960
1001   to 2007 (Presson et al., 2013), even though no cure for DS has been developed, suggesting strong
1002   non-biological confounding factors at play. Additionally, while some authors expect DS to display
1003   accelerated epigenetic aging (Horvath, 2013), others anticipate deceleration when applying epige-
1004   netic clocks to DS blood samples, as DS individuals are hypothesized to feature juvenile blood (Mei
1005
       et al., 2023). Schizophrenia (SZ) is another example of a controversial condition: while we can find
       increased incidence of age-related comorbidities such as cardiovascular diseases, cancers, or chronic
1006
       obstructive pulmonary disease (Olfson et al., 2015; Oakley et al., 2018; Yung et al., 2021), the rates
1007
       of suicide and substance-induced death are also increased in people with SZ (Olfson et al., 2015).
1008   We therefore suggest excluding such ambiguous conditions from robust clock benchmarking, as it
1009   is currently difficult to disentangle functional organismal deterioration from external and behavioral
1010   condition-related confounders and evaluate the degree to which the latter influence LE.
1011
       Regarding cancers in general, it is difficult to formulate a pre-hoc hypothesis about the directionality
1012
       of epigenetic age changes. Even though we know that DNAm can be used to create signatures
1013
       of various cancers, and that changes in some DNAm sites are shared between aging and cancers
1014   (Yu et al., 2020), we cannot be certain that an aging clock would indicate accelerated aging in
1015   cancerous samples, as some cancer-specific and stem cell-like features such as telomere maintenance
1016   might prompt a clock model to treat it as a marker of partial rejuvenation. In support of these
1017   considerations, epigenetic age predictions were found to exhibit no correlation with multiple TCGA
1018   cancer types (Lin & Wagner, 2015). To avoid possible speculation as far as possible, we recommend
1019   excluding cancer from clock benchmarking, as it is difficult to hypothesize about clock performance
1020   in such complex phenomena.
1021   Aging-decelerating condition (ADC) is defined as a condition that increases LE compared to the
1022   general population and features the same second and third criteria as an AAC. With respect to hu-
1023   man data, however, the ADCs are difficult to determine, as human lifespan-increasing interventions
1024   are yet to emerge. There are genetic mutations, such as Laron syndrome (growth hormone insen-
1025   sitivity) or isolated growth hormone deficiency (growth hormone releasing hormone insensitivity),
       that appear to protect against some age-related pathologies, but they do not feature a prolonged lifes-


                                                         19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
       pan (Aguiar-Oliveira & Bartke, 2019). To avoid dubious interpretation, we recommend omitting the
1027   inclusion of any condition into the ADC category when benchmarking human aging clocks.

1317     A.9                            DNA METHYLATION DATA PROCESSING
1318
        After pre-processing raw output from microarrays or sequencing machines, DNA methylation levels
1319
        per site are reported quantitatively either as beta values, or as M values. Briefly, beta values represent
1320
        the ratio of methylated signal (probe intensity or sequencing read counts) to total signal per site (sum
1321    of methylated and unmethylated probe intensities or sequencing read counts), while M value is the
1322    log2 ratio of the methylated signal versus an unmethylated signal. A more thorough comparison of
1323    the two measures can be found in Du et al. (2010). In the original datasets deposited on GEO, DNA
1324    methylation values were represented either as a beta fraction (ranging from 0 to 1), beta percentages
1325    (ranging from 0 to 100), or M-values (can be both negative and positive, equals 0 when beta equals
1326    0.5). We converted all data to the beta-value fractions ranging from 0 to 1. The values outside
1327    this range were treated as missing values (NaNs), as they are not biological. In each dataset, only
1328    samples that were relevant for benchmarking (that is, were annotated by age, tissue, and condition)
1329
        were retained.
1330    The resulting datasets meta-data contains the following fields: DatasetID (datasets GEO ID), Plat-
1331    formID (GEO ID of a DNA methylation profiling platform), Tissue (sample source tissue: “Blood”
1332    stands for peripheral blood samples, “Saliva”—for saliva samples, and “Buccal”—for buccal swab
1333    samples), CellType (sample cell type: either a specific cell population, e.g., immune cell subtypes
1334    with cell type-specific molecular markers, or broader categories such as whole blood, buffy coat,
1335
        peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMC), or peripheral blood leukocytes (PBL); some samples
        lack this annotation), Gender (abbreviated sample donor gender: M = Male, F = Female, U = Un-
1336
        known), Age (sample donor chronological age in years; in the original datasets deposited on GEO,
1337
        it can be either rounded by the researchers to full years, or converted from months, weeks, or days;
1338    where available, we calculated years from the smaller units), Condition (one of AACs or HC), and
1339    Class.
