011                                                  A BSTRACT
012
013            E(3)-equivariant neural networks have demonstrated success across a wide range
014            of 3D modelling tasks. A fundamental operation in these networks is the tensor
015            product, which interacts two geometric features in an equivariant manner to create
016
               new features. Due to the high computational complexity of the tensor product,
               significant effort has been invested to optimize the runtime of this operation. Luo
017
               et al. (2024) recently proposed the Gaunt tensor product (GTP) which promises a
018
               significant speedup over the naive implementation of the tensor product. However,
019            this method is unable to perform antisymmetric operations which are crucial for
020            tasks involving chirality. In this work, we introduce vector signal tensor product
021            (VSTP) to solve this issue and show how it generalizes to a class of irrep signal
022            tensor products (ISTPs). Finally, we investigate why these tensor products are
023            faster. We find most of the speedup comes at the price of expressivity. Further,
024            we microbenchmarked the various tensor products and find that the theoretical
025            runtime guarantees may differ wildly from empirical performance, demonstrating
026            the need for careful application-specific benchmarking. Our code is linked here.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031
      Many complex physical systems possess inherent spatial symmetries, and incorporating these sym-
      metries into models has been shown to significantly improve both learning efficiency and robustness
032
      Batzner et al. (2022); Rackers et al. (2023); Frey et al. (2023); Owen et al. (2024). To address the spe-
033
      cific symmetries present in 3D systems, considerable effort has been dedicated to the development
034   of E(3)-equivariant neural networks (E(3)NNs) (Thomas et al., 2018; Weiler et al., 2018; Kondor,
035   2018; Kondor et al., 2018). E(3)NNs have delivered strong performance across a wide range of
036   scientific applications, including molecular force fields (Batzner et al., 2022; Musaelian et al., 2023;
037   Batatia et al., 2022), catalyst discovery (Liao & Smidt, 2023), generative models (Hoogeboom et al.,
038   2022), charge density prediction (Fu et al., 2024), and protein structure prediction (Lee et al., 2022;
039   Jumper et al., 2021).
040   The group E(3) consists of all rotations, translations and reflections in 3 dimensions; we say a model
041   is E(3)-equivariant if it satisfies:
042
043                                   f (g · x) = g · f (x) ∀g ∈ E(3), x ∈ X                                    (1)
044
045   E(3)-equivariant neural networks work with features that transform as irreducible representations
046   of O(3), termed ‘irreps’, as described in Appendix A. How these irreps transform under 3D rota-
047
      tion, SO(3) is defined by a positive integer L, which can intuitively be thought of as an angular
      frequency. To interact these irreps, a special ‘tensor product’ operation is performed, which replaces
048
      how features are traditionally multiplied with each other in a typical neural network. As described
049
      in Section 2, the well-studied Clebsch-Gordan (Varshalovich et al., 1988) coefficients can be used to
050   define a tensor product. The most general Clebsch-Gordan tensor product (CGTP) has a time com-
051   plexity1 of O(L5 ) as we show in Appendix D, which can quickly become expensive for larger L.
052

088
      In Section 4, we give a measure of expressivity of the different tensor products and demonstrate
      that by normalizing against expressivity, the original GTP algorithm, MTP, and the sparse version of
089
      CGTP all have the same asymptotic runtime. Therefore the speed up come at the price of freedom
090
      in expressivity.
091
092   Based on rigorous benchmarking, GTP seems to scale better than MTP. Further, we identify that
093   there exists asymptotically faster spherical harmonic transform algorithms (Healy et al., 2003) which
094
      allows the asymptotic runtime of GTP to be faster than the others even when normalized for expres-
      sivity. However, GTP is the only tensor product which suffers from an inability to perform anti-
095
      symmetric operations. In fact we demonstrate this prevents identification of chiral tetris pieces in
096
      Section 5.2. This motivates us to propose a new tensor product which leverages the ideas of GTP to
097   retain the speed yet allows antisymmetry.
098
099   Therefore in Section 3.2 we propose vector signal tensor products (VSTP). Rather than using scalar
100
      signals and scalar products, VSTP uses vector signals and cross product interactions. We prove that
      the selection rules for VSTP only eliminate the trivial scalar 0 ⊗ 0 interaction. In addition, we show
101
      that VSTP has the same asymptotic runtime as GTP. Further, we describe in Section 3.4 how our
102
      approach generalizes to arbitrary irrep signal types to form an entire class of tensor products we call
103   irrep signal tensor products (ISTPs).
104
105   We summarize our core contributions as follows:
106
             • Proposal of VSTP (and generalization of ISTPs) solving the antisymmetry problem of GTP

118   2     T ENSOR P RODUCTS
119
120   We begin by motivating why tensor products between irreps are an important operation. First, irreps
121   are a key component of many equivariant architectures Geiger & Smidt (2022); Unke & Maennel
122   (2024). In particular, Schur’s lemma states that equivariant linear maps between 2 irreps consist
123   of either scaling if the irreps are the same, or must be 0 (Dresselhaus et al., 2007). Hence if the
124   input and outputs of a linear layer are irreps, Schur’s lemma directly gives the constraints needed
125   for the layer to be equivariant. This is why identifying features in terms of irreps is so prevalent in
126   equivariant architectures.
127   However, Schur’s lemma prevents interactions between different irreps in linear layers. This is the
128   motivation for tensor product operations. Given two spaces V and W and a desired output space
129   Z, the most natural interaction is a bilinear mapping. This can also be viewed as a linear mapping
130   from the tensor product space V ⊗ W → Z. Since we are trying to construct equivariant networks,
131   if there are actions of group G on spaces V, W, Z, we would like our bilinear map to be equivariant.
132   Further, because irreps are so useful for constructing linear layers, we want the input and output
133
      reps of our bilinear maps to explicitly be written as direct sums of irreps. Note that requiring the
      output to explictly be written as a sum of irreps is precisely what makes tensor products expensive.
134
      In the context of this paper, a tensor product operation refers to a fixed equivariant bilinear map
135
      V × W → Z where V, W, Z have been explicitly decomposed into a direct sum of irreps.

      2.2   G AUNT T ENSOR P RODUCT
173
      The Gaunt tensor product (GTP) as introduced by Luo et al. (2024) uses the intimate connection
174   between spherical harmonics, irreps, and spherical signals. We define this relation more precisely in
175   Appendix B. The key idea is that any rep of form (0, 1, . . . , L) can be interpreted as coefficients for
176   the spherical harmonics and hence corresponds to a function on S 2 .

195   such as [ℓ1 , ℓ2 , ℓ2 ] and [ℓ′1 , ℓ′2 , ℓ2 ] and loses information. In addition, note that GTP is a symmetric
196   operation. Hence, antisymmetric paths cannot appear. For example, GTP cannot be used to compute
197   cross products, because u × v = −v × u. We can characterize this with selection rules for GTP
198
      derived from the Gaunt coefficients (Gaunt, 1929).

206   we show that this implies that the Gaunt tensor product is incapable of solving a simple task of
207   classifying chiral 3D structures.

      3     V ECTOR SIGNAL TENSOR PRODUCT
235
      The key idea of GTP is the connection between irreps and scalar spherical signals, which can lead
236
      to improvements in asymptotic runtime. However, the symmetry of GTP introduces an additional
237   selection rule which eliminates antisymmetric tensor products. Here, we introduce vector signal
238   tensor products (VSTP) which solves the antisymmetry issue while retaining the asymptotic benefits.
239   We derive the selection rules for VSTP and prove they do not eliminate any paths except for the
240   trivial 0 ⊗ 0. In addition, we note that in principle we can generalize GTP and VSTP to using
241   arbitrary tensor signals which we call irreps signal tensor product (ISTP).

517   6   C ONCLUSION
518
519
520   In this work, we investigate the distinction between different O(3) equivariant tensor products that
521   exist in the literature and analyze their asymptotic behavior, empirical runtimes, and expressivity.
522   While there is much focus on improving how runtimes scale with L, we show that this speedup
523   comes at the cost of expressivity.
524   This broader investigation was inspired by the observation that specific antisymmetric paths were
525   missing in GTP and that paths which result in the same output irrep type are merged together. We
526   identify selection rules to characterize whether certain paths are missing and formulate a measure for
527   how merging of paths can affect expressivity. This framing lets us more easily evaluate the balance
528   between expressivity and efficiency of new versions tensor products, which we hope others find
529   useful. We use our framing to evaluate the asymptotic runtime and expressivity of various tensor
530
      product algorithms, including our new VSTP and the more general class of ISTPs.
531   Finally, we microbenchmarked different implementations of CGTP, GTP, MTP, and VSTP. As ex-
532   pected, the absolute walltimes of GTP, MTP, and VSTP are faster than CGTP past some Lmax , but
533   when normalized for expressivity CGTP is the fastest.
534   However, more empirical work is needed to understand how much the loss of expressivity affects
535   actual performance of E(3)NNs. If we need the extra expressivity then clearly CGTP is the better
536   choice, but if not, we may benefit from the absolute speed up of the other tensor products. Our
537   work highlights the need to carefully analyze the tradeoffs between expressivity and walltime when
538   deciding which tensor product operation to use in practice. There are many opportunities for creative
539   design of equivariant operations. The best solution may depend heavily on the task and dataset at
      hand.

      A    I RREDUCIBLE R EPRESENTATIONS OF E(3)
649
650   A representation ρ of a group G maps each group element g to a bijective linear transformation
651   ρ(g) ∈ GL(V ), where V is some vector space. Representations must preserve the group multipli-
652   cation property:
653                                    ρ(g · h) = ρ(g) ◦ ρ(h) ∀g, h ∈ G                               (7)
654   Thus, the representation ρ defines a group action on a vector space V . The dimension of the repre-
655   sentation ρ is simply defined as the dimension of the vector space V .
656
657
      There may be subspaces W ⊂ V which are left invariant under actions of ρ(g) for all g ∈ G. If
      this is the case, then restricting to W also gives a representation ρ|W (g) ∈ GL(W ). If there is no
658
      nontrivial W , then we say the representation ρ is an irreducible representation (irrep).
659
660   To build E(3)-equivariant neural networks, the irreducible representations of E(3) play a key role.
661   Because E(3) is not a compact group, the usual approach has been to consider irreducible repre-
662   sentations of the group SO(3) of 3D rotations, and compose them with the representation in which
663
      translations act as the identity:
664                                           ρ(R, T ) = ρ′ (R)                                       (8)
665   This is why translations are often handled in E(3)-equivariant neural networks by centering the
666   system or only using relative vectors.
667   The ‘scalar’ representation ρscalar representation of SO(3) is defined as:
668                                        ρscalar (R) = id ∀R ∈ SO(3)                               (9)
669
      and is of dimension 1 over V = R. Elements of R are unchanged by any rotation R. We call such
670   elements ‘scalars’ to indicate that they transform under the ‘scalar’ representation of SO(3). An
671   example of a ‘scalar’ element could be mass of an object, which does not change under rotation of
672   coordinate frames.
673
      Let T (R) ∈ R3×3 be the rotation matrix corresponding to a rotation R ∈ SO(3). Then, the ‘vector’
674
      representation of SO(3) is defined as:
675
                                       ρvector (R) = T (R) ∀R ∈ SO(3)                                 (10)
676
677   and is of dimension 3 over V = R3 . The name arises from the way vectors in R3 transform under
678
      a rotation of the coordinate frame. We call such elements ‘vectors’ to indicate that they transform
      under the ‘vector’ representation of SO(3). For example, the velocity and position of an object in a
679
      certain coordinate frame are ‘vectors’.
680
681   Weyl’s theorem for the Lie group SO(3) states that all finite-dimensional representations of SO(3)
682   are equivalent to direct sums of irreducible representations. The irreducible representations of
683   SO(3) are indexed by an integer ℓ ≥ 0, with dimension 2ℓ + 1. ℓ = 0 corresponds to the ‘scalar’
684
      representation, while ℓ = 1 corresponds to the ‘vector’ representation above. We will often use m,
      where −ℓ ≤ m ≤ ℓ, to index of each of the 2ℓ + 1 components.
685
686   We say that a quantity x ∈ R2ℓ+1 is a ℓ irrep, if it transforms as the irreducible representation
687   (‘irrep’) of SO(3) indexed by ℓ. If x1 is a ℓ1 irrep and x2 is an ℓ2 irrep, we say that (x1 , x2 ) is a
688   direct sum of ℓ1 and ℓ2 irreps, which we call a (ℓ1 , ℓ2 ) ‘rep’. Weyl’s theorem states that all reps are
689   a direct sum of ℓi irreps, possibly with repeats over ℓi : x = ⊕ℓi x(ℓi ) . The multiplicity of an irrep in
690   a rep is exactly the number of repeats.
691   An important lemma for constructing equivariant linear layer is Schur’s lemma (Dresselhaus et al.,
692   2007).
693   Lemma A.1 (Schur’s Lemma). Suppose V1 , V2 are irreps of a Lie group over any algebraically
694   closed field (such as SO(3)). Let ϕ : V1 → V2 be an equivariant linear map.
695
      Then ϕ is either 0 or an isomorphism.
696
697   Further, if V1 = V2 then ϕ is a multiple of identity.
698
      Finally for any two ϕ1 , ϕ2 : V1 → V2 we must have ϕ1 = λϕ2 .
699
700   This tells us that to construct equivariant linear layers between reps written as a direct sum of irreps,
701   we can only have weights between input and output irreps of the same type and that those weights
      must be tied together so they give multiples of the identity transformation.

757   scalar functions on a sphere. Hence to form an orthonormal basis for a tensor function of irrep s, we
758   can use 2s + 1 copies of the spherical harmonics, one for each component of the 2s + 1 dimensional
759   output. Hence we have a total of (2ℓ + 1) × (2s + 1) coefficients for the degree ℓ harmonics which
760   we can naturally form into an 2D array.

766   array of coefficients would transform as a ℓ ⊗ s tensor product representation.
