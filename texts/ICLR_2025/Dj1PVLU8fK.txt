009                                                      A BSTRACT
010
011                Traditional fixed test datasets fall short in quantifying the open-ended potential
012                of foundation models. In this work, we propose ∞-benchmarks, a new testing
013                paradigm that combines individual evaluation datasets into a single, uniform, ever-
014                expanding sample pool from which custom evaluations can be flexibly generated.
015                ∞-benchmarks allows users to dynamically select a collection of sample-level
016                evaluations that correspond to specific capabilities. By aggregating and reusing
017
                   samples across various test sets, it enables the assessment of diverse capabilities
                   beyond those covered by the original test sets, while mitigating overfitting and
018
                   dataset bias. Most importantly, it frames model evaluation as a collective process
019                of aggregation and selection of sample-level tests. The shift from multi-task bench-
020                marks to ∞-benchmarks introduces two key challenges: (1) heterogeneity and (2)
021                incompleteness. Heterogeneity refers to aggregating diverse metrics, including
022                binary, numeric, and ordinal data, while incompleteness describes comparing mod-
023                els evaluated on different subsets of testing data. To address these challenges, we
024                explore algorithms to aggregate sparse, unequal measurements into reliable model
025                scores. Our aggregation algorithm ensures identifiability (asymptotically recovering
                   ground-truth scores) and rapid convergence, enabling accurate model comparisons
026
                   with relatively little data. Our algorithm recovers ground-truth rankings with high
027
                   correlations when compared to standard aggregation on homogeneous metrics, even
028                with up to 95% of measurements missing. This approach reduces evaluation cost by
029                up to 20× with little to no change on model rankings. We introduce ∞-LLMBench
030                for language models and ∞-LMMBench for vision-language models, unifying
031                evaluations across diverse test-beds in these domains, and showcasing targeted
032                testing of models over a wide-range of capabilities. Overall, we present the first
033                large-scale ∞-benchmarks for lifelong, efficient evaluation of foundation models,
034                which can aggregate over open-ended heterogeneous sample-level testing to evolve
                   alongside the rapid development of these foundation models.

037   1       I NTRODUCTION
038
039       Machine learning has arrived in the post-dataset era1 . With the rapidly growing range of zero-shot
040
      capabilities of foundation models, the focus of model evaluation has moved beyond singular, dataset-
      specific performance measurements obtained by splitting a fixed collection of data into training and
041
      test sets. Instead, foundation models are employed as general knowledge and reasoning engines across
042   the broad suite of domains for which they prove to be useful. There is consequently a pressing need
043   to characterize their open-ended set of capabilities across various metrics in zero-shot settings (Ge
044   et al., 2024). Traditional static benchmarks, however, which test generalization on fixed test splits, are
045   unable to probe the ever-evolving set of capabilities of foundation models. This raises an important
046   question: How can benchmarking adapt to measure an open-ended set of capabilities?
047   We propose a solution based on dynamic sample-level evaluation, which we call ∞-benchmarks,
048   where test sets for particular capabilities are generated ad hoc from a large pool of individual
049   annotated data samples. These sample-level evaluations act as atomic units of measurement that
050   can be combined into an exponential variety of aggregations. Due to this flexibility, the sample
051   pool and corresponding annotation metrics can be continuously updated to include new evaluations.
052   Additionally, this can reduce dataset bias—systematic quirks in the data arising from the acquisition
053

      procedures used during dataset collection (Torralba & Efros, 2011; Liu & He, 2024). By combining
055   samples across test sets, ∞-benchmarks can better capture real-world diversity (Ni et al., 2024).
056
057   The most important feature of ∞-benchmarks is their ability to efficiently democratize evaluation.
      Unlike traditional benchmarks, typically created by individual groups arbitrarily deciding on spe-
058
      cific data collection and evaluation procedures (Dhar & Shamir, 2021), ∞-benchmarks allow the
059
      integration of test sets from many diverse sources reflecting a wide range of perspectives, use-cases,
060   and objectives. This flexibility allows different interest groups with varying needs to collaboratively
061   define their own evaluations selecting the most appropriate combination of tests to suit their specific
062   requirements. Moreover, the design of ∞-benchmarks challenges the dominant approach of chasing
063   single benchmark scores in favour of a plurality of rankings and dynamic, multi-faceted evaluation.
064
      Challenges in ∞-Benchmarks. To build effective ∞-benchmarks, we must address two main
065   challenges: (a) Heterogeneity and (b) Incompleteness. Heterogeneity refers to aggregating samples
066   over an ever-expanding set of metrics, which span different measurement types—including binary,
067   numeric, and ordinal data. This diversity makes it difficult to standardize comparisons across different
068   models. Incompleteness, on the other hand, arises from models being evaluated on different, unequal
069   subsets of testing data, rendering direct aggregation unfair. Traditional benchmarks typically use a
070   multi-task benchmarking setting, where each component benchmark still evaluates models over an
071
      equal, fixed sample set across a homogeneous metric, completely sidestepping both these issues.
072   Solution and Theoretical Guarantees. To tackle these challenges, we apply social choice theory,
073   viewing samples as voters expressing preferences among models. By converting all measurements into
074   ordinal rankings, we leverage well-established principles to develop a sound model for aggregating
075   over diverse and incomplete data. We assume a random utility model, generated by the Plackett-Luce
076
      framework, which provides guarantees on recovering ground-truth model utility scores from input
      samples. This approach ensures that our model rankings are both theoretically sound and practical,
077
      with rapid convergence guarantees enabling accurate rankings from relatively small amounts of data.
078
079   Empirical Validation. We develop two instantiations of ∞-benchmarks: ∞-LLMBench for language
080   models and ∞-LMMBench for multimodal models. These benchmarks unify evaluations across their
081   respective domains by aggregating data from diverse sources, from arena-style human preference
082
      data (Chiang et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024b) to heterogeneous multi-task leaderboards (Beeching et al.,
      2023; Liang et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024b; CRFM, 2024). Our empirical results demonstrate
083
      that the Plackett-Luce model (Plackett, 1975; Luce, 1959) is a good fit for aggregating real-world
084
      benchmarks, showing high correlations with ground-truth rankings over homogeneous leaderboards.
085   Importantly, we demonstrate that this strong correlation holds even when up to 95% of the data is
086   missing. Conversely, this robustness allows us to reduce costs by 20× with little loss in performance.
087   We observe that the simple strategy of randomly selecting a subset of samples achieves comparable
088   performance to more sophisticated sample selection strategies. Finally, we compare Plackett-Luce
089   rankings with widely adopted ranking metrics like ELO Elo (1967) and Bradley-Terry (Bradley &
090   Terry, 1952) and outperform them on overall accuracy and robustness to missing information.
091   Personalized Aggregation. Consider this scenario: you are a scientist in a Biochemistry lab and
092   require an LLM to assist with designing experiments related to antibodies. ∞-benchmarks allow
093   users to input a query, “biochemistry”/“antibodies”, and receive dynamically constructed
094   benchmarks. This benchmark ranks models based on their performance on this specific capability.
095   While optimal selection of personalized capability sets is an emerging research field, we provide a
096   proof of concept by categorizing capabilities into tasks (e.g., reading comprehension) and concepts
097
      (e.g., Clostridium Bacteria), and showcasing targeted capability evaluation and model rankings.
098   In essence, ∞-benchmarks are a democratized, open-source collection of diverse evaluation samples
099   and model measurements, with detailed metadata. Users can conduct semantic searches and apply
100   structured query filters to dynamically generate a benchmark tailored to their specific use case.
101   Sample-level model measurements can be instantly aggregated, producing personalized rankings.

106   lens (Brandt et al., 2016)—samples are voters, models are candidates, and the aggregation algorithm
107   is the voting mechanism. Using established methods, we aggregate ordinal comparisons with partial
      data to produce a global ranking and analyze properties of this resultant ranking.

136   2.1            T HEORETICAL F OUNDATIONS : W HY T HIS W ORKS ?
137
138   We begin by postulating a ground-truth statistical model generating the data, which is converted into
139
      ordinal comparisons (S)2 . Specifically, we use a random-utility model (Thurstone, 1927), where
      each model fi is associated with a utility distribution Ufi . Preferences between models fi and fj
140
      are based on comparing sampled utilities, i.e., fi ≺ fj := u(fi ) < u(fj ), where uf ∼ Uf . Since
141
      computing maximum likelihood estimates over general random-utility models is computationally
142   hard (Xia, 2019), we focus on the Plackett–Luce model (Plackett, 1975; Luce, 1977), the only known
143   exception that allows for tractable maximum likelihood estimates (MLE).
144
      P1. Identifiability. We first ask: Are the utility distributions across models Ufi ∀fi recoverable? The
145
      Plackett-Luce model allows identifying the utility distribution (up to arbitrary additive constant) if all
146   models are compared via a directed path (Xia, 2019; Hunter, 2004)3 . Consistency and asymptotic
147   normality hold under specific assumptions about the comparison graph (Han & Xu, 2023).
148
149
      P2. Sample-Efficient Convergence from Sparse Data. Identifiability is asymptotic, but we also ask:
      How sample-efficient are algorithms for recovering the utility distribution? With partial rankings of
150
      size k, the MLE is surprisingly sample efficient while being minmax-optimal (Hajek et al., 2014;
151   Maystre & Grossglauser, 2015). Specifically, sampling k model comparisons from the model set
152   |F| independently and uniformly at random for |D| samples induces an expander graph with high
153   probability, which provides guarantees on sample-efficiency of recovery, with |D| = Ω(|F |)/k samples
154   being necessary, and |D| = Ω(|F | log |F |)/k samples being sufficient. Efficient algorithms like those
155   in Agarwal et al. (2018); Maystre & Grossglauser (2015) achieve these bounds. Rank-breaking
156   techniques, used in our empirical evaluation, also offer near-optimal solutions (Soufiani et al., 2014).
157   P3. Active Aggregation. In ∞-benchmarks, we can strategically select model comparisons, by
158   framing selection as an online multi-armed bandit problem. One can provide significantly more
159

163   Ren et al., 2018), significantly outperforming random comparisons (Maystre & Grossglauser, 2017).
164
165   P4. Social Properties. The Plackett-Luce model ensures computational efficiency and recoverability
      of the underlying ranking. However, to design democratic systems for decision-making, it is essential
166
      to also have fair aggregation. However, ensuring fairness involves tradeoffs (Zhang & Hardt, 2024)
167
      because different notions of fairness often conflict, and agents may have differing, even opposing
168   preferences (Garman & Kamien, 1968; Arrow, 1950; Benoıt, 2000). We, however, can state that
169   Plackett-Luce model is procedurally fair (List, 2022) (Section 2.2), i.e. it satisfies:
170
171           • Anonymity. All voters (samples) are treated equally, ensuring the system does not rely on a
172             single vote. The rankings unchanged even if the input sample set is permuted.
173           • Neutrality. The ranking is invariant to the identities of the models, ensuring fairness among
174             alternatives. This means permuting the models similarly permutes the new ranking.
175           • Independence from Irrelevant Alternatives (IIA). The relative ranking of two models is un-
176             affected by other alternatives in a given sample, as guaranteed by Luce’s axiom of choice
177             (Luce, 1959). This provides grounding for incomplete model evaluations.

180   We now empirically validate our framework, aiming to show that: (i) the Plackett-Luce model fits
181   real-world data well, (ii) our aggregation method is sample-efficient, and (iii) it handles high levels of
182   incompleteness. Furthermore, we discuss practical strategies for reducing evaluation costs in Sec. 2.3.
183   Below, we describe our setup and address these points.

      2.2.1    S ETUP
186   Benchmarks. We conduct experiments using four popular leaderboards with established ground truth
187   model rankings: HELM (Liang et al., 2023) and Open-LLM Leaderboard (Beeching et al., 2023) for
188   LLMs, and VHELM (CRFM, 2024) and LMMs-Eval (Zhang et al., 2024b) for LMMs. We fix our
189   sample pool as all samples from the constituent datasets of a given leaderboard and compare rankings
190   obtained by our aggregation strategy. These leaderboards evaluate foundation models across varied
191   tasks with different metrics, serving as good indicators of real-world performance.
192   Methods. We evaluate three model ranking methods:
193
      (i) Elo Score: (Elo, 1967) A competitive game rating system adapted to rank models through pairwise
194
      comparisons, adjusting scores based on wins or losses to reflect win-rate reliability.
195
196   (ii) LMArena Ranking: (Chiang et al., 2024) A method for LLM ranking using the Bradley-Terry
197   model (Bradley & Terry, 1952), which estimates model rankings through Maximum Likelihood
198   Estimation (MLE) based on pairwise comparisons using an underlying ELO model.
199   (iii) Our Method: Our approach leverages the Plackett-Luce model (Maystre & Grossglauser, 2015)
200   to aggregate pairwise comparisons using partial rank breaking (Soufiani et al., 2014).
201
      Metrics. We compare the rankings generated by each method to the ground-truth from the leader-
202   boards using Kendall’s τ , a standard correlation metric for rankings. Each method is tested thrice, and
203   we report the mean and variance. We additionally check that the top-k models are reliably recovered.

      before. As shown in Fig. 3, our method performs well even with 95% fewer model comparisons,

267
      models are evaluated on different samples.

296   Setup. We leverage the sample-level design of ∞-Benchmarks to inspect the distribution of samples
297   in standard benchmarks. We investigate efficient model evaluation strategies by selecting a small
298   subset of the total pool—we aggregate model accuracies to identify easy and difficult data samples.
299   Insight 1: Many Samples Provide No Signal. The histograms in Fig. 4 show that a large portion
300   of samples result in identical model scores (all 0s or all 1s), resulting in ties when converted to
301   ordinal ranking and contributing no useful information for model comparison. Excluding these
302   samples could reduce dataset size by up to 50% for benchmarks like Open LLM Leaderboard and
303   LMMs-Eval(comprising close to 150K samples which no model can answer correctly).
304   Insight 2: Efficient Sampling from Central Bins. By analyzing rank correlation (Kendall’s τ ) in
305   Fig. 4, we found that sampling from central bins of the data histogram—where models differ
306   in evaluation performance—maintains a higher rank correlation than the edge bins, even with fewer
307   than 400 data points. This indicates that effective sampling can be achieved in ∞-benchmarks. While
308   prior studies suggest sampling informative instances (Vivek et al., 2024; Perlitz et al., 2024), others,
309   like (Prabhu et al., 2024), show random sampling can yield strong results.
310   Insight 3: Random Sampling Matches Informative Sampling. Comparing informative sampling
311   (based on the fraction of models solving a data instance) with random sampling, we found no
312   significant difference in sample efficiency (Fig. 4). This suggests that random sampling is an equally
313   effective and simpler approach for reducing benchmarking costs.
314
      Conclusion. Benchmarking large models is resource-intensive, but we demonstrate that excluding
315   low-signal data and relying on random sampling can significantly reduce costs without compromising
316   accuracy. This strategy is effective for large-scale, sample-wise evaluations in ∞-benchmarks.

397   3.2     C APABILITIES AND C ONCEPT P ROBING
398   Here, we present empirical results on generating arbitrary test sets and rankings. Our goal is to
399   enable users to make targeted queries within ∞-benchmarks, helping them identify the best LLMs
400   and LMMs for their specific needs. To achieve this, we extend our system with a flexible mechanism
401   for personalized aggregation, allowing users to (1) retrieve relevant data instances through semantic
402   search, and (2) dynamically generate rankings based on the retrieved samples.
403   Setup. The user submits a query, and we retrieve relevant data samples using semantic
404   search. This concept querying mechanism provides a personalized comparison of foundation
405   model capabilities. We use two querying mechanisms: (i) Semantic search, where we use
406   all-MiniLM-L6-v2 (Reimers & Gurevych, 2019) for language tasks and SigLIP-B16 (Zhai
407   et al., 2023) for vision-language tasks, employing cosine similarity for retrieval. We retrieve top-k
408   samples for a given concept with a well-tuned cut-off similarity score of 0.3 and 0.7 for ∞-LLMBench
      and ∞-LMMBench respectively. (ii) Metadata search: We search metadata to match querying. With
409
      this, we gather representative samples for the query, and aggregate the ordinal model rankings per
410
      sample using the Plackett-Luce model to produce final model rankings, for that particular query.
411
412   Concepts Tested. We curated a diverse set of 50 concepts to test the breadth and versatility of our
413
      ∞-benchmarks, ranging from domain-specific knowledge, such as the Coriolis Effect, to broader
      academic disciplines like Neuroscience, and everyday consumer goods like the Apple iPad. We
414
      showcase 6 of them in the main paper, and present the rest in Appx. E.

481
482
      While recent benchmarks have tested broad capabilities of foundation models, viewing benchmarking
      as a science ((Hardt & Recht, 2022)) is understudied. We provide a short overview of recent efforts,
483
      highlighting the intersectional nature of our work We include a detailed version in Appx. B.
484
485   Multi-task Benchmarks. Multi-task leaderboards, e.g., GLUE (Wang et al., 2019b), Super-
      GLUE (Wang et al., 2019a), and BigBench (Srivastava et al., 2023), are standard for evaluating


                                                                                                            9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      foundation models across tasks. However, concerns about dataset selection and saturation have
487   emerged (Ethayarajh & Jurafsky, 2020; Dehghani et al., 2021; Liu & He, 2024). Our ∞-benchmarks
488   address these by enabling extensive reuse of samples, avoiding task selection bias (Torralba & Efros,
489   2011; Dominguez-Olmedo et al., 2024), and supporting open-ended evaluations through querying for
490   diverse concepts across a broad range of input metrics and incomplete set of model comparisons.
491
      On Aggregation across Benchmarks. Traditional benchmarks use arithmetic mean for task aggrega-
492   tion (Beeching et al., 2023) which can distort rankings (Benavoli et al., 2016; Zhang & Hardt, 2024;
493   Colombo et al., 2022a) and unusually depend on outliers (Agarwal et al., 2021) and missing scores
494   (Himmi et al., 2023). Inspired by non-parametric statistics and social choice theory, we employ
495   ordinal rankings and the Plackett-Luce model (Plackett, 1975) for task aggregation, which is robust
496   to irrelevant alternatives and outliers, providing more accurate and efficient evaluations.
497   Efficient Evaluation and Democratization. As benchmarks grow, so do inference costs, leading to
498   compressed subsets (Varshney et al., 2022; Polo et al., 2024; Vivek et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2024;
499   Perlitz et al., 2024) and evolving lifelong benchmarks (Prabhu et al., 2024). Our approach, for the first
500   time, enables past work to handle incomplete data and ordinal rankings. Further, by allowing diverse
501   contributors to add samples and preferences, along with arbitrary queries, we hope ∞-benchmarks
502   can bemore inclusive than traditional benchmarks dominated by well-funded labs following recent
503
      progress (Pistilli et al., 2024; Pouget et al., 2024; Nguyen et al., 2024; Luccioni & Rolnick, 2023).
504

      5   C ONCLUSIONS AND O PEN P ROBLEMS
506   This work tackled scalable benchmarking of arbitrary capabilities of foundation models, requiring
507   a shift from traditional fixed training and test splits, by introducing ∞-benchmarks, a lifelong
508   benchmarking framework for foundation models. Our open-source, democratized benchmarking
509   methodology allows diverse evaluation samples and model measurements with detailed metadata.
510   This affords creating customized benchmarks and testing arbitrary capabilities, including using
511   semantic and structured searches. We provide a principled aggregation mechanism, that is both
      theoretically grounded and empirically validated to be robust to incomplete data and heterogenous
512
      measurements across evaluations. We demonstrate the utility of ∞-benchmarks in two domains:
513
      ∞-LLMBench and ∞-LMMBench, showing how dynamic probing reveals new insights into model
514   performance on specific tasks, domains, or concepts. This combination of theoretical rigour, empirical
515   results, and practical flexibility makes ∞-benchmarks a valuable tool for comprehensively evaluating
516   foundation models. we provide some promising directions for improvement below:
517
518   1. Testing Limits and Scaling Up ∞-Benchmarks: Currently, our prototype demonstrates the core
519      methodology of ∞-benchmarks, with less than 100K samples in ∞-LLMBench and under
520
         1M in ∞-LMMBench. These pools can be greatly expanded and diversified by expanding to
         incorporating all existing LLM and LMM benchmarks. Our retrieval mechanisms are designed to
521
         scale efficiently as the test pool grows in size and diversity.
522
523
      2. Exploring Aggregation Algorithms from Computational Social Choice: While we currently use
524
         the Plackett-Luce model for aggregating diverse measurements, there exist other algorithms from
         computational social choice theory with different trade-offs. A comprehensive evaluation of these
525
         alternatives could offer new insight for aggregating model performance.
526
      3. Structured Querying and Enhanced Retrieval: One can improve retrieval by better querying mech-
527
         anisms using models like ColBERT (Khattab & Zaharia, 2020) and ColPALI (Faysse et al., 2024)
528
         and optimization using DSPy (Khattab et al., 2023). A particularly interesting direction is allowing
529
         compositional queries, where users combine multiple queries to test behaviour in foundation
530      models, similar to works like ConceptMix (Wu et al., 2024) and SkillMix (Yu et al., 2023).
531
      4. On the Limits of Capability Probing: While we currently allow broad, open-ended inputs to probe
532
         capabilities, some are easier to assess than others (Madvil et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024b). As
533      foundation models become more generalizable, a thorough analysis identifying which capabilities
534      can be easily, reliably evaluated, which are possible to evaluate but challenging, and which are in
535      principle “impossible to evaluate” is needed—this will help improve benchmarking effectiveness.

       A     ∞-B ENCHMARKS : F ORMULATION
1135
1136
       At the heart of ∞-benchmarking is the idea to homogenize performance evaluation across benchmarks
1137   by replacing their benchmark-specific metrics with rankings. Importantly, this can be done at the level
1138   of individual data samples. In the following, we describe the process of construction and evaluation
1139   in detail, together with specific mathematical guarantees.
1140
1141   A.1    C OMPONENTS
1142
1143   The goal of building an ∞-benchmark (B∞ ) from a growing set of benchmarks (Bk )N   k=1 is to evaluate
1144   a collection of models (M) using an ever-growing test pool of data instances (D) which may be
1145
       annotated with additional meta-data specifying the capabilities (C) tested. To cope with the diversity
       of data originating from diverse benchmarks, sample-level rankings (S) are created for all data
1146
       instances in the test pool. We provide a schematic overview of ∞-benchmarks in Fig. 1 and describe
1147
       each component below:
1148
1149   i) Pool of Data. D=((x1 , y1 ), . . . , (xn , yn )) denotes an ordered collection of test data instances xi
1150
       with annotation yi . An example of a data instance xk is the question ‘What was the dominant strain of
       Flu Virus in 2010? Select among the four choices.’ with the reference answer ‘H1N1/09’ represented
1151
       by yk . In addition, information about capabilities can be provided as meta data for example as a list of
1152
       keywords such as ‘temporal Q&A, pandemics, history, biology, virology, multiple-choice Q&A, etc’.,
1153   beyond the specific dataset it originates from. Typically, the data samples are obtained via pooling
1154   from N different benchmarks (Bk )N       k=1 and we refer to the subset of data instances obtained from
1155   benchmark Bk as Dk ⊆ D.
1156
       ii) Models. M=(fbase , f1 , . . . , fm ) is a set of m models, whose capabilities are evaluated with
1157
       respect to a baseline fbase . Example of fbase is a random model. The original benchmarks (Bk )Nk=1
1158   will likely cover different sets of models MBk ⊆ M for their evaluations.
1159
1160   iii) Sample-level Rankings. For each data instance (xj , yj ) ∈ D a sample level ranking sj ∈ S is
1161
       created for the subsets of models Mj = M ∩ MBk(j) where k(j) denotes the index of the benchmark
1162   from which the data instance (xj , yj ) was collected. Importantly, sample-level rankings are a function
       of the metrics used by the different benchmarks that discards any information about the specifics of
1163
       the metrics. This is the key of our approach to enable the aggregation across heterogeneous evaluation
1164
       paradigm and metrics. More specifically, sj ∈ S represents an ordinal ranking over the models
1165   Mj for sample (xj , yj ) represented by a permutation σj such that fσj (1) ⪰ · · · ⪰ fσj (mj ) where
1166   mj = |Mj | is the number of models compared in the j-th sample-level ranking. In addition, for each
1167   k we distinguish the case fσ(k−1) ≻ fσ(k) if fσ(k−1) performs better than fσ(k) and fσ(k−1) ∼ fσ(k)
1168   in case of indistinguishable performance. Thus, each sample-level ranking sj ∈ S can be uniquely
1169   determined by a mapping σj : {1, . . . , mj } → {1, . . . , m} with σj (k) providing the index of
1170   the model in M that is on the k-th place in the ordering for the j-th sample-level ranking and
1171   πj ∈ {≻, ∼}mj −1 defining the corresponding binary sequence of pairwise performance relations.
1172   Ordinal Rankings and Information Loss. Using ordinal measurements leads to information loss,
1173   which can hinder downstream aggregation algorithms due to the data processing inequality ((Thomas
1174   & Joy, 2012), Section 2.8). This principle states that estimation from manipulated data cannot
1175   outperform estimation from the original data. However, cardinal measurements often face calibration
1176   issues, even within a single metric (Shah et al., 2014). As a result, in practice, ordinal measurements
1177   can paradoxically outperform cardinal ones despite the inherent information loss.
1178   iv) Capabilities. To support the selective retrieval of all relevant sample-level rankings in B based
1179   on varying interests of evaluators in different capabilities, it is possible to endow the sample-level
1180   rankings with additional capability c ∈ C. Of course, modeling the range of capabilities that different
1181   evaluators are interested in is a research challenge in itself. Here, we only provide a proof of concept,
1182   for which we define two categories of capabilities, including tasks, like multiple-choice question
1183
       answering, captioning, translation, to concepts like makeup, dogs, π, tarantula. The reason behind
       the broad interpretation is for ∞-benchmarks is to test which capabilities can be reliably tested
1184
       dynamically. Note that since the capability set is open-ended, we do not append capabilities per
1185
       sample as meta-data, but rather select relevant samples at test-time.
1186
1187   Continual Expansion of ∞-Benchmarks. The data instance pool (D), and model names (M) are
       stored as a table while sample-level testings (S) are stored as a relational database between these two

       tables. Construction of a lifelong heterogenous benchmark augments D, M and S with three opera-
1189   tions: B=(D, M, S, insertD , insertM , insertS ). Operations insertD and insertM for
1190   expanding the data pool are straightforward: add new samples and new models to the corresponding
1191   table. The insertS operation adds a new sample-level ranking, each of which corresponds to one
1192   sample and a ranking of models. Additional measurement metadata is saved to enable retrieval over
1193   database rows with the same metadata, such as ‘BLEU score’, or ‘exact match’.

       B    R ELATED W ORKS
1243
1244   Multi-task Benchmarks as Broad Capability Evaluators. Multi-task leaderboards have been the
1245   standard for benchmarking foundation models that generalize across various situations and solve
1246
       complex tasks. Examples include GLUE (Wang et al., 2019b), decaNLP (McCann et al., 2018), Super-
       GLUE (Wang et al., 2019a), BigBench (Srivastava et al., 2023), OpenLLM-Leaderboard (Beeching
1247
       et al., 2023), CLIP-Benchmark (LAION-AI, 2024), ELEVATOR (Li et al., 2022) and DataComp-
1248   Evals (Gadre et al., 2023) as well as massive multitask benchmarks like XTREME (Siddhant et al.,
1249   2020) and ExT5 (Aribandi et al., 2021). However, concerns have arisen regarding the limitations of
1250   multi-task benchmarks (Bowman & Dahl, 2021). Issues include saturation and subsequent discarding
1251   of samples (Liao et al., 2021; Beyer et al., 2021; Ott et al., 2022; Ethayarajh & Jurafsky, 2020),
1252   susceptibility to dataset selection (Dehghani et al., 2021), obscuring progress by evaluation metrics
1253   (Schaeffer et al., 2023; Colombo et al., 2022b), training on test tasks (Udandarao et al., 2024), and data
1254   contamination (Elangovan et al., 2021; Magar & Schwartz, 2022; Golchin & Surdeanu, 2024; Deng
1255
       et al., 2023; Sainz et al., 2023; Golchin & Surdeanu, 2023; Sainz et al., 2024). ∞-benchmarks helps
       tackle these challenges by enabling the extensive reuse of samples for broader model comparisons,
1256
       avoiding task selection bias through democratized sourcing of samples, and using ordinal rankings to
1257   avoid evaluation minutia. Sample-level evaluations with sparse inputs also allow selective removal of
1258   contaminated data from targeted models for fairer comparisons and make it harder to train on all test
1259   tasks by supporting open-ended evaluations, compared to leaderboards with fixed test sets.
1260
       On Aggregation across Benchmarks. Since the current dominant form of benchmark was multi-task
1261
       benchmarks, the dominant aggregation strategy was arithmetic mean over scores across individual
1262   benchmarks. However, mean-scores inherently assumes different scoring metrics are homogeneous,
1263   scaled correctly and treats treating tasks of different complexity equally (Mishra & Arunkumar, 2021;
1264   Pikuliak & Šimko, 2023). In consequence, simple normalization preprocessing changing rankings
1265   (Colombo et al., 2022a), and the rankings nearly entirely dependent on outlier tasks (Agarwal
1266   et al., 2021), change rankings even with simple alternate aggregations like geometric/harmonic
1267   mean (Shavrina & Malykh, 2021) and including irrelevant alternative models can change statistical
1268   significance or even change the ranking entirely (Benavoli et al., 2016; Zhang & Hardt, 2024). Mean-
1269   aggregation also has significant failure modes in handling missing scores in benchmarks (Himmi
1270
       et al., 2023). The benchmarking paradigm is hence shifting towards adopting evaluation principles
       from other fields, such as non-parametric statistics and social choice theory (Brandt et al., 2016;
1271
       Rofin et al., 2022). We use ordinal rankings instead of scores similar to ChatBot Arena. However,
1272   Arena systems use Elo-based scoring systems, well-established to be a poor metric (Boubdir et al.,
1273   2023), and our work confirms that. The pairwise variant of the Plackett-Luce model has been shown
1274   to have advantages both theoretically and empirically (Peyrard et al., 2021), allows us to inherit some
1275   of their theoretical properties like identifiability, sample-efficient convergence, provable robustness to
1276   irrelevant alternatives, non-dominance of outliers and empirical robustness properties across a wide
1277   range of real-world factors which affect ranking. In contrast, we do not aggregate over benchmarks,
1278   our primary proposal is avoid monolithic benchmarks and consider aggregation on a samplewise-level,
1279
       needing to tackle incomplete and heterogeneous measurements.
1280   Efficient Evaluation. As evaluation suites have grown in size, associated inference costs have also
1281   increased. Recent research has focused on creating compressed subsets of traditional benchmarks
1282   to address this issue (Varshney et al., 2022; Polo et al., 2024; Vivek et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2024;
1283   Perlitz et al., 2024). Popular extensions include subsampling benchmarks to preserve correlations
       with an external source like ChatBot-Arena (Ni et al., 2024), or designing evolving sample-level
1284
       benchmarks (Prabhu et al., 2024) similar in principle to our work. However, Prabhu et al. (2024) do
1285
       not handle incomplete input matrices, which is necessary for aggregation over multiple timesteps and
1286   requires binary 0/1 evaluation metrics as input. We precisely address these limitations by showing
1287   efficient evaluation while accommodating for incomplete data and extending it to ordinal ranks in our
1288   work.
1289
       Democratizing Evaluation. Most standard image classification and retrieval benchmarks are col-
1290
       lected from platforms like Flickr, which are predominantly Western-centric (Ananthram et al., 2024;
1291   Shankar et al., 2017). This has raised the important question: “Progress for whom?”, with many
1292   seminal works showcasing large disparities in model performance on concepts (Nguyen et al., 2024;
1293   Hemmat et al., 2024), tasks (Hall et al., 2024; 2023b;a), and even input samples (Pouget et al.,
1294   2024; Sureddy et al., 2024; Gustafson et al., 2024) from the Global South. In response, works
1295   have developed benchmarks tailored to diverse cultures and demographics to include their voice
       in measuring progress (Pistilli et al., 2024; Pouget et al., 2024; Nguyen et al., 2024; Luccioni &


                                                           4
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1296
       Rolnick, 2023). We take a different approach by creating flexible benchmarks where individuals, and
1297   contributing labs being able to add their own samples and preferences. During capability testing,
1298   users can select similar preferences, making ∞-benchmarks more inclusive than traditional test sets
1299   created by well-funded labs in wealthier countries.
