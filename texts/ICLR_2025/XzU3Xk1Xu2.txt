009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Long-context inference of Large Language Models (LLMs) is known to be chal-
012            lenging due to the excessive Key-Value(KV) cache accesses. This paper introduces
013            “Double Sparsity,” a novel post-training sparse attention technique designed to
014            alleviate this bottleneck by reducing KV cache access. Double Sparsity combines
015            token sparsity, which focuses on using only the important tokens for computing
016            self-attention, with channel sparsity, an approach that uses important feature chan-
017            nels for identifying important tokens. Our key insight is that the pattern of channel
018            sparsity is highly static, allowing us to use offline calibration to make it efficient at
019
               runtime, thereby enabling accurate and efficient identification of important tokens.
               Moreover, this method can be combined with offloading to achieve significant
020
               memory usage reduction. Experimental results demonstrate that Double Sparsity
021                          1
               can achieve 16   sparsity with minimal impact on accuracy across various tasks with
022            different architectures including MHA, GQA, MoE and vision language model. It
023            brings up to a 14.1× acceleration in attention operations and a 1.9× improvement
024            in end-to-end inference on GPUs with various batch sizes. With CPU offloading
025            under extremely long-context settings (e.g., 256K), it achieves a decoding speed
026            acceleration of 16.3× compared to state-of-the-art solutions. Our code is integrated
027            into a widely-used framework SGLang and deployed in real-world workloads.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Large Language Models (LLMs) have significantly advanced machine learning capabilities, en-
033   abling a wide range of applications from natural language processing to complex problem-solving
034   tasks (OpenAI, 2023; Touvron et al., 2023; Google, 2023). Recent progress has dramatically extended
035   the context window of LLMs from 2K to 1M, enabling more long-context applications (Dubey
036   et al., 2024). However, long-context inference is costly and slow due to its token-by-token gener-
037   ation scheme. This auto-regressive process requires loading the entire previous Key-Value (KV)
038   cache to generate the next token, making the self-attention layers extremely memory-intensive and
039
      time-consuming (Williams et al., 2009; Liu et al., 2024b). Furthermore, simultaneously serving
      multiple long-context requests within a single batch greatly increases the size of KV cache, making
040
      the inference more memory-intensive (Zhao et al., 2024). For example, serving 16 requests of 8k
041
      length with a Llama-2-7B model, a 64 GB KV Cache requires 44 ms, which accounts for more than
042   80% of the total serving time.
043
044   In this paper, we explore methods to reduce access to the KV cache during inference, thereby making
045
      attention computation more bandwidth-efficient and accelerating its execution. Our focus is on
      post-training methods that can be directly applied to a pre-trained model to provide wall-clock
046
      acceleration without requiring excessive additional training or fine-tuning overhead. Prior work has
047
      attempted to leverage quantization (Hooper et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b), compression (Nawrot
048   et al., 2024), and sparsity (Zhang et al., 2024; Anagnostidis et al., 2024; Ge et al., 2024; Ribar et al.,
049   2023) to achieve these goals. Among them, sparsity holds significant potential if a high sparsity
050   ratio can be achieved. The intuition of sparsification is that not every token is equally important for
051   decoding the next token. Therefore, during the decoding process, we can rely on a small subset of
052   important tokens to compute the self-attention, achieving nearly the same results. While the approach
053   of sparse attention seems intuitive, previous research has struggled to find a post-training sparse
      attention method that maintains high accuracy while being runtime-efficient.

054
      The primary challenge in post-training sparse attention lies in accurately and efficiently identifying
055   important tokens. A naive approach entails calculating the entire attention weight matrices and
056   then sorting the tokens based on the accumulated attention weights. Although this method can
057   precisely identify important tokens, it fails to offer a runtime speedup, as it requires computing the
058   full attention weight matrices, which is precisely the step we aim to avoid. Previous studies have
059   proposed various methods for selecting important tokens; however, these methods either lead to
060   significant accuracy losses or fail to achieve practical wall-clock acceleration. Notably, H2O (Zhang
061   et al., 2024) employs a dynamic strategy that maintains a small, fixed-size cache of important tokens
062   during the decoding steps. Due to its limited size, it must evict tokens during these steps, and once
063
      evicted, these tokens cannot be reinstated if they become important later. This approach can lead to
      significant accuracy degradation since it cannot predict which tokens will be important in the future.
064
      Quest (Tang et al., 2024) and SparQ (Ribar et al., 2023), in contrast, retain all tokens and dynamically
065
      select important ones at each decoding step, with different importance estimation methods. Quest
066   proposes a page-based estimation method, which is efficient but fails to accurately identify important
067   tokens. SparQ, while being more accurate, falls short of achieving the desired speedup and incurs
068   considerable memory overhead. Therefore, designing a both efficient and accurate estimation method
069   remains a significant challenge.

083   We propose “Double Sparsity,” a method that leverages both token sparsity and channel sparsity
084   to achieve accurate and efficient post-training sparse attention. Token sparsity refers to the sparse
085   attention method mentioned above (Zhang et al., 2024), which uses only important tokens to compute
086   self-attention. Channel sparsity estimates the importance of tokens at runtime using heavy channels in
087
      embedding. Our key insight is that while token sparsity is highly dynamic, channel sparsity exhibits
      static patterns, enabling us to identify and select important channels through offline calibration.
088
      This static channel sparsity thus provides an efficient way to achieve dynamic token sparsity at
089
      runtime. Furthermore, once we can quickly identify important tokens for the current layer, we extend
090   this process by predicting the important tokens of the next layer. We achieve this by utilizing the
091   embedding similarity between adjacent layers. This approach enables us to offload the entire KV
092   cache to host memory and prefetch only the important tokens to GPU memory, significantly reducing
093   GPU memory footprint.
094                                                              1                         1
      We demonstrate that “Double Sparsity” can achieve an 16       token sparsity and an 16  channel sparsity
095
      simultaneously while incurring only a negligible accuracy loss across a broad array of benchmarks,
096   including language modeling, question answering, and retrieval tasks. The sparsity directly leads to
097   the reduction of memory access and runtime speedup. “Double Sparsity” accelerates the attention
098                                                         1
      operation by up to 14.1× at a sparsity level of 16       on NVIDIA A10G and A100 GPUs, closely
099   approaching the theoretical acceleration upper bound. It accelerates end-to-end inference in our
100   evaluated workloads by up to 1.9×. With offloading and prefetch, it achieves a decoding throughput
101   that is 16.3× higher than the state-of-the-art offloading-based solutions at a sequence length of 256K.

      2.2   P OST- TRAINING S PARSE ATTENTION
118
119   In this work, we introduce the term “post-training sparse attention,” analogous to “post-training
120   quantization.” Post-training sparse attention refers to techniques that exploit inherent model sparsity,
121   such as token-level sparsity, to accelerate attention calculations without requiring additional training.
122   In the field of LLMs, many works have utilized post-training sparse attention, including H2O,
123   StreamingLLM (Xiao et al., 2024), SparQ and Quest. However, these methods come with significant
124   limitations on either maintaining good accuracy or achieving fast inference (details see Section 3),
125   presenting serious challenges for post-training sparse attention.
126
127   3     C HALLENGES IN P OST-T RAINING S PARSE ATTENTION
128
129   In this section, we discuss prior research on post-training sparse attention, identifying the challenges
130   and shortcomings that have prevented these approaches from achieving their full potential. More
131   related works are included in Section 7.
132
133   3.1   R ETRIEVAL ACCURACY
134
135   One of the most challenging issues for post-training sparse attention is maintaining retrieval accuracy.
136   For instance, StreamingLLM discards earlier tokens except for a few tokens at the beginning, which
137
      are called attention sinks, while H2O selectively drops tokens based on previous attention scores.
      Although discarding tokens can accelerate computations, this exclusion leads to the loss of critical
138
      information, potentially compromising the model’s retrieval accuracy. As highlighted in Jelassi et al.
139
      (2024), this issue is inherent to techniques that rely on discarding tokens, prompting the exploration
140   of sparse attention methods that preserve access to the complete KV cache.
141
142
      3.2   BANDWIDTH F RIENDLINESS
143
144   Achieving wall-clock speedup poses a greater challenge while maintaining model retrieval accuracy,
145   particularly because some post-training sparse attention techniques are not hardware-friendly. For
146   instance, SparQ retains the complete KV cache and computes attention selectively on a subset of the
147   KV cache based on the estimation with query. This approach theoretically allows for acceleration
148   while maintaining accuracy. However, SparQ’s estimation method uses heavy channels to approximate
149   attention scores, which causes non-contiguous memory access at the granularity of 2B for FP16.
150
      Such irregular access can substantially waste memory bandwidth, as GPU is designed to efficiently
      deal with continuous 16B memory loading (NVIDIA, 2020). As a result, despite being designed to
151
      accelerate processing, SparQ achieves only a modest 1.3 × speed increase in attention computations.
152
      We further quantify this effect in Appendix B. Therefore, it is crucial to develop an algorithm that
153   ensures continuous memory access patterns to accelerate attention while preserving accuracy.
154
155
      3.3   M EMORY F OOTPRINT
156
157   Besides the efficiency of memory bandwidth, long-context inference is still challenging because of
158   the substantial memory capacity required to store the KV cache, as post-training sparse attention can’t
159   prune tokens to assure great accuracy. E.g., with Llama2-7B and a context length of 128K, 64GB is
160   still needed even with 1/16 token sparsity. To mitigate this large memory footprint, FlexGen (Sheng
161   et al., 2023b) offloads the entire KV cache on the CPU and loads it to the GPU layer-by-layer during
      the decoding process. However, the communication overhead of loading the KV cache for all previous


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      tokens within a single layer is still large enough to affect overall inference efficiency. In sparse
163   attention, considering that important tokens constitute just a small fraction of all tokens, the time
164   taken to load these specific tokens to the GPU is considerably less than the time required for loading
165   the entire KV cache. By accurately predicting the important tokens and efficiently managing when
166   and how data is transferred and processed, it’s possible to significantly reduce both the time and
167   memory overhead typically associated with maintaining a full KV cache.
168
      To address these challenges, we propose two post-training sparse attention techniques. In Section 4,
169   we introduce Double Sparsity, which accelerates attention by up to 16 × with minimal additional
170   memory consumption. In Section 5, we present Double Sparsity-Offload, which reduces memory

194   4.1   O FFLINE C ALIBRATION
195
      Offline calibration is a commonly used technique to identify channel sparsity, particularly effective
196
      for pinpointing outlier channels. For example, AWQ (Lin et al., 2023) utilizes offline calibration
197
      to identify salient weight channels that significantly impact model performance. Inspired by this
198   approach, we employ offline calibration to pre-determine the channels that most influence attention
199   scores. Attention computation can be expressed as A = Q · K T , which can be broken down into
200        Pd
      A = i h Si where Si = Qi ∗ Ki . Due to channel sparsity, only a few Si have a significant impact on
201   A. Therefore, by conducting offline calibration on a small validation set, we can efficiently identify
202   these critical channels by computing the arg maxi Si . Figure 7a in Appendix A illustrates the outlier
203   channels identified by AWQ and Double Sparsity.
204
      To validate the efficacy of outlier channels identified through offline calibration, we conducted a
205
      comparison in Appendix A between the outlier channel indices derived from offline calibration and
206   those determined during the online decoding process. A significant overlap between the two sets
207   underscores the reliability of offline-calibrated outliers. Figure 7b illustrates this relationship. An
208   observation from the comparison is that when the ratio surpasses 0.25, the overlap reaches 0.95.
209
210   4.2   F ORWARDING WITH L ABEL C ACHE
211
212   After identifying the outlier channel indices, it becomes crucial to access them efficiently. Reading
213   these channels directly from the Key cache can lead to non-contiguous memory accesses, which
214   significantly under-utilized the bandwidth. To alleviate non-contiguous memory accesses, we leverage
215   a label cache to store pre-determined heavy channel values. This label cache allows for continuous
      memory access when computing approximate attention, avoiding the need to retrieve non-contiguous


                                                        4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      segments from the Key cache. During the prefilling stage, all heavy channel values from the Key
217   cache are stored in the label cache; in the decoding phase, only the heavy channel values of new
218   tokens are added. Since approximate attention is not sensitive to precision, we can store the label
219                                                                                     1
      cache in 4-bit. This approach enables us to maintain a label cache that is only 16   the size of the K
220   cache, facilitating contiguous memory access and significantly improving the hit rate of L1/L2 caches,
221   thereby optimizing inference speed and efficiency. In Appendix B, an ablation study was conducted to
222   evaluate the impact of label caches. The results demonstrated that a label cache accelerates decoding
223   speeds by 2 to 4 times compared to configurations without a label cache.

      Building upon Double Sparsity, we further propose the Double Sparsity-Offload technique to reduce
228
      the large GPU memory footprint introduced by long-context inference. This approach significantly
229                                                   1

230   footprint, Double Sparsity-Offload enables efficient decoding under extremely long-context settings
231   with limited GPU memory resources.

      5.1   P REFETCHING T OKENS WITH D OUBLE B UFFER
234
235   The Double Sparsity-Offload algorithm introduces a prefetching technique with a double buffer
236   during for decoding process, following the design principles of prior works (Lee et al., 2024; Shi
237   et al., 2024). Our key idea is to utilize inter-layer similarity to predict and prefetch the heavy tokens
238   for the next layer and overlap the computation with memory transfer. The complete KV cache is
239   offloaded to the CPU, while the GPU maintains only the label cache and a double buffer. During
240   the decoding process, each layer processes its embeddings through the next layer’s query projection
241   to generate an approximate query for the subsequent layer. This approximate query is then used to
242   compute the next layer’s approximate attention. While the current layer’s attention and feed-forward
243
      network computations are being performed, the tokens corresponding to the approximate attention
      results for the next layer are loaded to the GPU. This use of double buffering allows for a smooth and
244
      efficient overlap of computation and memory transfer.
245
246
247   5.2   E MPIRICAL A NALYSIS : E MBEDDING S IMILARITY B ETWEEN L AYERS
248
      The feasibility of the Double Sparsity-Offload algorithm is based on the high degree of similarity
249
      between embeddings across consecutive layers. To empirically validate this assumption, we conducted
250
      an analysis using the Pile validation dataset, applied to the Llama-2-7B model. We measured the
251   cosine similarity of embeddings between every two consecutive layers throughout the model. The
252   results show that apart from the first two layers, the second and third layers, and the very last layers
253   (30 and 31), all other layer pairs exhibited a cosine similarity exceeding 90%, with the majority of
254   layers showing similarities above 95%, as illustrated in Figure 2. These high similarity scores support
255   the viability of utilizing prior layer embeddings to predict queries for subsequent layers in Double
256   Sparsity-Offload.

283   5.3     C OMPLEXITY A NALYSIS
284
285   To understand the potential speedup of Double Sparsity, we need to analyze its Cache IO-Complexity
286   since attention mechanisms are bandwidth-bounded. Most sparse attention techniques can be simpli-
287   fied into two steps: calculating approximate attention and computing attention over k tokens. We
288   denote α as the ratio of memory access in the first step relative to the K cache size, and β as the ratio
289   of memory access in the second step relative to the KV cache size. Memory-wise, the total access of
290
      Double Sparsity comprises O(d) bytes for Q, O(S × r) for the label cache, O(2 × k × d) for the KV
      cache, leading to a total of O(S × r + 2 × k × d) = O(α × S × d + 2 × β × S × d). Given that the
291
      approximate attention phase of Double Sparsity does not involve softmax operations, it allows for
292
      high parallelism compared to the following step. Therefore, the overall IO complexity of Double
293   Sparsity primarily depends on the latter step, which can be approximated as O(2 × β × S × d). This
294   analysis reveals that Double Sparsity’s time complexity is linearly dependent on β, and the extra
295   memory overhead is linearly proportional to α. Table 1 summarizes all the sparsity works discussed,
296   specifying their overhead, complexity, and speedup.

      7    R ELATED W ORK
487
488
      Sparse Attention Inference Due to the significant memory-intensive nature of self-attention, many
489
      studies have focused on exploiting sparsity to accelerate the inference process. These efforts can
490   be categorized under three main criteria: 1) static or dynamic sparse patterns; 2) the presence of
491   token eviction; 3) accelerating prefilling or decoding. StreamingLLM (Xiao et al., 2024) and LM-
492   Infinite (Han et al., 2023) utilize static sparse patterns with token eviction to accelerate decoding.
493   These approaches achieve inference acceleration by preserving only a small and fixed number of
494   initial tokens along with local tokens. H2O (Zhang et al., 2024) and Scissorhands (Liu et al., 2024a)
495   employ dynamic sparse patterns with token eviction for decoding, preserving only a small fraction of
496   the KV cache called heavy hitters according to accumulated attention scores, while FastGen (Ge et al.,
497   2024) uses adaptive sparse attention patterns for different attention heads. MInference (Jiang et al.,
498
      2024) serves as a prefilling acceleration method that retains all tokens. It first identifies sparse patterns
      within the model via offline calibration, and then leverages these identified patterns to accelerate the
499
      pre-filling stage. SparQ (Ribar et al., 2023) and Quest (Tang et al., 2024) implement dynamic sparse
500
      decoding while preserving all tokens without eviction. SparQ filters the important tokens using heavy
501   channels of incoming query and Key cache. Quest segments token into multiple pages and proposes
502   a page-based estimation method to utilize sparsity in self-attention.
503
504   Sparse Attention Training There are also many efforts to reduce attention complexity through
505   training (Qiu et al., 2020; Ding et al., 2023; Tay et al., 2020; Chen et√al., 2021). For example,
506   Sparse transformer (Child et al., 2019) reduces the complexity to O(n n) by introducing sparse
507   factorization of the attention matrix. Reformer (Kitaev et al., 2019) achieves O(n log n) complexity
508   via locality-sensitive hashing. Longformer (Beltagy et al., 2020), BigBard (Zaheer et al., 2020), and
509   Linformer (Wang et al., 2020) further reduce the complexity to linear. Linear attention architectures
510   have also been proposed in Katharopoulos et al. (2020).
511
512   Other Attention and Inference Optimizations Despite efforts to sparsify the self-attention com-
513   putation, there are many other optimizations for attention efficiency. Common techniques include
514
      quantization and compression (Hooper et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b; Kang et al., 2024; Nawrot et al.,
      2024), efficient attention architecture like multi-query attention (Shazeer, 2019) and group-query
515
      attention (Ainslie et al., 2023), and memory-efficient attention algorithms (Rabe & Staats, 2021;
516
      Dao et al., 2022). Alternatives to transformers include using the state space model to remove the
517   attention mechanism (Gu et al., 2021). Other common inference optimizations for LLMs include
518   batching Yu et al. (2022), memory optimizations Sheng et al. (2023b); Kwon et al. (2023); Aminabadi
519   et al. (2022), parameter sharing Sheng et al. (2023a); Chen et al. (2023), speculative decoding Stern
520   et al. (2018); Leviathan et al. (2023); Miao et al. (2023), scheduling Han et al. (2022); Agrawal et al.
521   (2023); Patel et al. (2023); Zhong et al. (2024), quantization Xiao et al. (2023); Lin et al. (2023);
522   Dettmers et al. (2022); Frantar et al. (2022), and sparsification Frantar & Alistarh (2023).
523

      8    F UTURE D IRECTIONS AND C ONCLUSION
525
526
      Future Directions. Double Sparsity can be integrated with other sparse attention techniques. For
527
      instance, Quest could harness Double Sparsity using heavy channels instead of max-min representa-
528   tions to reduce total memory access. Similarly, MInference could employ heavy channels in place of
529   pooling when computing block sparse patterns to increase accuracy. Despite the progress made with
530   Double Sparsity, several limitations remain that reveal promising directions for future research. It is
531   challenging to perfectly overlap communication with computation. Enhancing asynchronous capa-
532   bilities to mask communication overheads presents a promising direction that allows for significant
533   acceleration with a minimal memory footprint.
534   Conclusion. In this work, we introduced Double Sparsity and Double Sparsity-Offload, innovative
535   post-training sparse attention techniques. Double Sparsity leverages offline calibration and label
536   cache to achieve nearly lossless performance across various benchmarks at a 1/16 sparsity level.
537   Performance tests showed that Double Sparsity could accelerate attention computations by up to
538   16× and achieve an end-to-end speedup of 1.9×. Double Sparsity-Offload significantly reduced KV
539   Cache memory usage to 1/16, outperforming the throughput of previous SOTA offloading techniques
      by 16 times.
