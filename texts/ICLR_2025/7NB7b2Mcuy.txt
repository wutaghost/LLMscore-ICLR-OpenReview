009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent research on backdoor attacks mainly focuses on invisible triggers in in-
012            put space and inseparable backdoor representations in feature space to increase
013            the backdoor stealthiness against defenses. We examine common backdoor attack
014            practices that look at input-space or feature-space stealthiness and show that state-
015            of-the-art stealthy input-space and feature-space backdoor attacks can be easily
016            spotted by examining the parameter space of the backdoored model. Leverag-
017            ing our observations on the behavior of the defenses in the parameter space, we
018
               propose a novel clean-label backdoor attack called Grond. We present extensive
               experiments showing that Grond outperforms state-of-the-art backdoor attacks
019
               on CIFAR-10, GTSRB, and a subset of ImageNet. Our attack limits the param-
020
               eter changes through Adversarial Backdoor Injection, adaptively increasing the
021            parameter-space stealthiness. Finally, we show how combining Grond’s Adver-
022            sarial Backdoor Injection with commonly used attacks can consistently improve
023            their effectiveness. Our code is available at https://anonymous.4open.
024            science/r/grond-557F.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   While deep neural networks (DNNs) have achieved excellent performance on various tasks, they are
030   known to be vulnerable to backdoor attacks. Backdoor attacks insert a secret functionality into a
031   model that is activated when malicious inputs containing a specific trigger are provided to the model
032   during inference. Backdoored DNNs can be crafted by training with poisoned data (Gu et al., 2019;
033
      Chen et al., 2017), controlling the training process (Bagdasaryan & Shmatikov, 2021; Nguyen &
      Tran, 2021; 2020), or directly modifying the weights of the victim model (Hong et al., 2022).
034
035   In early backdoor attacks (Gu et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2018b), triggers could in-
036   duce noticeable changes that human inspectors or anomaly detectors can easily spot. To enhance
037   input space stealthiness, smaller or more semantic-informative (to be mixed with clean samples)
038   triggers are designed. However, most input-space stealthy backdoor attacks need to change labels
039
      of poisoned samples to the target class, i.e., dirty-label, which is also easy to detect (Chen et al.,
      2018). To this end, another line of backdoor attacks poisons the training data without changing
040
      the labels (Turner et al., 2019; Zeng et al., 2023), i.e., clean-label, which is more stealthy during
041
      poisoning but requires large trigger sizes. For example, (Zeng et al., 2023) uses l∞ norm of trig-
042   gers to be upper bound by 16/255 for backdoor training and 48/255 at inference. In addition,
043   despite the stealthiness concerning input images and labels, it has been widely observed that exist-
044   ing attacks introduce separability in the feature space, which can be exploited to develop backdoor
045   defenses (Wang et al., 2022a; Xu et al., 2024b).
046
      In response to feature-space defenses, state-of-the-art (SOTA) backdoor attacks focus on eliminating
047
      the separability in the feature space (Mo et al., 2024; Qi et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2024; Shokri et al.,
048   2020). However, our analysis shows that these attacks still require significant modifications to the
049   model’s parameters, and there is a lack of systematic evaluations against the latest parameter-space
050   backdoor defenses. To this end, we evaluate ten attacks against nine parameter-space backdoor
051   defenses, including four pruning-based and five fine-tuning-based defenses (See Section 4.2). Sur-
052   prisingly, our experiments demonstrate that state-of-the-art backdoor attacks can be easily mitigated
053   by parameter-space defenses, such as ANP (Wu & Wang, 2021), RNP (Li et al., 2023a), or FT-
      SAM (Zhu et al., 2023). More importantly, our systematic analysis reveals that even though some

078
      To resolve these shortcomings, we propose a novel clean-label attack called Grond that consid-
      ers comprehensive stealthiness to remain stealthy in the input, the feature, and the parameter space
079
      of the model. Grond achieves the input space stealthiness by using targeted universal adversar-
080
      ial perturbation (TUAP) (Moosavi-Dezfooli et al., 2017) as the trigger, where we only poison the
081   target class to build a clean-label attack. To inject the backdoor, we propose a novel Adversarial
082   Backdoor Injection mechanism that adaptively injects the backdoor during poisoning to achieve pa-
083   rameter space stealthiness. Specifically, we leverage the Lipschitz continuity of neuron activations to
084   find backdoor-related suspicious and sensitive neurons in each poisoning epoch. Then, we conduct
085   pruning on these neurons to eliminate the backdoor effect. As a result, after Adversarial Backdoor
086   Injection, the backdoor is associated with neurons throughout the DNN rather than just focusing on
087   a few prominent neurons, as illustrated in Figure 1.
088   We make the following contributions:
089
090
             • We revisit state-of-the-art backdoor attacks regarding their stealthiness, showing that most
               attacks only aim for input space invisibility and feature space inseparability. Based on
091
               this finding, we examine the latest backdoor attacks and show that state-of-the-art stealthy
092
               input-space and feature-space backdoor attacks are vulnerable to parameter-space defenses.
093          • We propose a novel backdoor attack, Grond, that considers comprehensive stealthiness,
094            taking input-, feature-, and parameter-space defenses into account. Extensive experiments
095            demonstrate that Grond outperforms SOTA backdoor attacks against four pruning- and

098          • We further verify our approach by binding Grond’s Adversarial Backdoor Injection with
099            other attacks. Experimental results demonstrate that our Adversarial Backdoor Injection
100            could substantially improve the parameter space robustness of most backdoor attacks.

120   2.2   BACKDOOR ATTACKS
121
122   Backdoor attacks compromise the integrity of the victim model so that the model performs natu-
123   rally on benign inputs but is misled to the target class by inputs containing the backdoor trigger.
124   The trigger can be a visible pattern inserted into the model’s input in input space or a property
125   that affects the feature representation of the model’s input in feature space. Eventually, however,
126   the backdoored model’s parameters in the parameter space will be altered regardless of the exact
127
      backdoor attack. To insert a backdoor, the attacker is assumed to control a small portion of the
      training data (Gu et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2021) or also control the training
128
      process (Shokri et al., 2020; Nguyen & Tran, 2020; Bagdasaryan & Shmatikov, 2021; Nguyen &
129
      Tran, 2021; Wang et al., 2022b). Moreover, the backdoor can also be created by directly modifying
130   the model’s weights (Liu et al., 2017; Hong et al., 2022; Qi et al., 2022).
131
132   Input-space attacks. Traditional backdoor attacks typically use simple patterns as their triggers.
133
      For example, BadNets (Gu et al., 2019) uses a fixed patch, and Blend (Chen et al., 2017) mixes a
      Hello Kitty pattern into the images as the trigger. These non-stealthy triggers introduce abnormal
134
      data into training data and can be easily detected by human inspectors or defenses (Chen et al., 2018;
135
      Wang et al., 2019). To improve the stealthiness, various triggers are proposed to achieve invisibility
136   in the input space. IAD (Nguyen & Tran, 2020) designed a dynamic solution in which the triggers
137   vary among different inputs. WaNet (Nguyen & Tran, 2021) proposed the warping-based trigger,
138   which is invisible to human inspection. Bpp (Wang et al., 2022b) used image quantization and
139   dithering as the trigger, which makes imperceptible changes to images. Although these methods
140   successfully build invisible triggers and bypass traditional defenses (Wang et al., 2019), they still
141   introduce separable features and can be detected by feature-space defenses (Wang et al., 2022a; Xu
142   et al., 2024b). These input-invisible attacks can be even more noticable than input-visible attacks
143   (BadNet, Blend) in the feature space (Xu et al., 2024a).
144   Feature-space attacks. Knowing the vulnerability of input-space attacks against feature-space de-
145   fenses, backdoor attacks are improved for feature-space stealthiness. A common threat model of
146   this attack type is to assume additional control over the training process. For example, (Shokri et al.,
147   2020; Zhao et al., 2022; Zhong et al., 2022) directly designed loss functions to minimize the dif-
148   ference between the backdoor and benign features. Aside from design loss penalties, TACT (Tang
149   et al., 2021) and SSDT (Mo et al., 2024) point out that source-specific (poison only the specified
150
      source classes) attack helps obscuring the difference in features between benign and backdoor sam-
      ples. In addition, (Qi et al., 2023) proposed Adap-blend and Adap-patch, which obscures benign and
151
      backdoor features by 1) including poisoned samples with the correct label, 2) asymmetric triggers
152
      (using a stronger trigger at inference time), and 3) trigger diversification (using diverse variants of
153   the trigger during training). Unfortunately, existing attacks lack systematic evaluation against the
154   latest defenses. For example, Adap-blend can be thoroughly mitigated by recent works (Zhu et al.,
155   2023; Xu et al., 2024b;a).
156
157   2.3   BACKDOOR D EFENSES
158
159   Backdoor defenses can be classified into detection and mitigation. Detection refers to determining
160   whether a model is backdoored (model detection) (Wang et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2019; Zhao et al.,
161   2022; Wang et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024b) or a given input is applied with a trigger (input detec-
      tion) (Gao et al., 2019; Guo et al., 2023; Mo et al., 2024). Model detection by trigger inversion is


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      considered one of the most general defenses against backdoors (Wang et al., 2022a; 2023; Xu et al.,
163   2024b; Zhu et al., 2024). The inversed trigger could determine whether the model is backdoored and
164   be used for backdoor unlearning. For example, NC (Wang et al., 2019) inverses input space triggers
165   and determines the backdoor by selecting abnormally smaller triggers.
166
      Mitigation refers to erasing the backdoor effect from the victim model by pruning the backdoor-
167
      related neurons (pruning-based defenses) (Liu et al., 2018a; Wu & Wang, 2021; Zheng et al., 2022;
168
      Li et al., 2023a) or unlearning the backdoor trigger (fine-tuning-based defenses) (Zhu et al., 2023;
169   Zeng et al., 2022; Min et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024b). These methods attempt to remove the neurons
170   associated with backdoors. For example, ANP (Wu & Wang, 2021) prunes neurons that are more
171   sensitive to adversarial neuron noise, and FT-SAM (Zhu et al., 2023) combines sharpness-aware
172   minimization with fine-tuning to decrease the norms of backdoor neurons.

177   3.1   T HREAT M ODEL
178
      Attacker’s goal. The attacker provides pre-trained models to users. The aim is to inject backdoors
179
      into the pre-trained model so that the model performs well on clean inputs but predicts the attacker-
180
      chosen target label when receiving inputs with a backdoor trigger, i.e., an all-to-one attack.
181
182   Attacker’s knowledge. The attacker has white-box access to the training processes, the training
183   data, and the model weights. During training, poisoned images do not contain visible patterns for
184
      human inspectors, so the labels of poisoned images are the same as the images’ original class, i.e.,
      clean-label. During inference, the backdoor trigger is invisible to human inspectors.
185
186   Attacker’s capabilities. The attacker can train a well-performed surrogate model to generate TUAP,
187   which is used by the attacker to perturb the victim model’s input. Additionally, the attacker can alter
188   the model’s weights during training. Table 7 in Appendix A.2 shows that the threat model of Grond
189   is aligned with baseline attacks.
190
191   3.2   BACKDOOR ATTACKS M UST C ONSIDER PARAMETER S PACE D EFENSES
192
193   Early backdoor attacks that introduce noticeable changes in either input (Gu et al., 2019; Chen
194   et al., 2017) or feature space (Nguyen & Tran, 2021; 2020) have been empirically shown powerful,
195
      even with very low poisoning rates (Gu et al., 2019; Zeng et al., 2023). Focusing on the backdoor-
      introduced noticeable changes, backdoor defenses are improved to distinguish backdoor patterns in
196
      either input or feature space (Wang et al., 2022a; Lin et al., 2024). Meanwhile, backdoor attacks are
197
      optimized to increase stealthiness in input (Nguyen & Tran, 2021) or feature space (Qi et al., 2023).
198   However, we point out that, regardless of the implementations in the input of feature spaces, the
199   backdoor behaviors are eventually embedded and reflected in the backdoored model parameters (Liu
200   et al., 2019; Wu & Wang, 2021; Li et al., 2023a). As seen in Table 1, our experiments demonstrate
201   that existing attacks (including both input- and feature-space) are not robust against parameter-space
202   backdoor defenses, such as ANP (Wu & Wang, 2021) (pruning backdoor-related parameters) and FT-
203   SAM (Zhu et al., 2023) (shrinking the norms of backdoor-related neurons). This finding indicates
204   that stronger backdoor attacks should consider the parameter-space defenses, besides input- and
205   feature-space defenses, to achieve comprehensive stealthiness.
206
207   3.3   G R O N D FOR C OMPREHENSIVE S TEALTHINESS
208
209   We propose a stealthy backdoor attack, Grond, that considers comprehensive stealthiness, i.e.,
210   stealthiness in input, feature, and parameter space. Grond includes two key parts: Backdoor gener-
211   ation and Adversarial Backdoor Injection.

213   to generate invisible backdoor triggers, inspired by adversarial example studies (Moosavi-Dezfooli
214   et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2021). Specifically, we use Targeted Universal Adversarial Perturbations
215   (TUAP) as the imperceptible perturbations. TUAP contains non-robust but generalizable semantic
      information (Tsipras et al., 2019), which correlates with the benign functions of the victim model and

238   following the PGD (Madry et al., 2018) algorithm to decrease the surrogate model’s cross-entropy
239   loss that takes as inputs the adversarial examples (the poisoned samples in our case) and the target
240   class samples. This procedure is described formally as follows:

      Adversarial Backdoor Injection for parameter-space stealthiness. Backdoor neurons (i.e.,
257
      trigger-related neurons) regularly show higher activation values for inputs that contain the trigger,
258   which results in powerful performance (Liu et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2022a; Lin et al., 2024). To
259   this end, backdoor training needs to substantially increase the magnitude of parameters of backdoor
260   neurons (Wu & Wang, 2021; Li et al., 2023a; Zheng et al., 2022), which harms the parameter-space
261   stealthiness of backdoor attacks.
262
      One way to find the sensitive neurons with higher activation values is to analyze the Lipschitz con-
263
      tinuity of the network. Leveraging this fact, we introduce a novel backdoor training mechanism,
264   Adversarial Backdoor Injection, to increase the parameter-space backdoor stealthiness. Specifically,
265   each neuron’s Upper bound of Channel Lipschitz Condition (UCLC (Zheng et al., 2022)) is calcu-
266   lated, based on which the weights of these suspicious neurons are set to the mean of all neurons’
267   weights in the corresponding layer after every training epoch. In our implementation, we use the
268   weights before every batch normalization as the neuron weights, which corresponds to the channel
269   setting in UCLC. We prune neurons by substituting their weights with the mean ones because prun-
      ing to zeros makes the training unable to converge. Formally, the kth parameter of the lth layer,

460   4.4   A DVERSARIAL BACKDOOR I NJECTION I MPROVES OTHER BACKDOOR ATTACKS
461
462   To evaluate the generalizability of Grond, we combine our Adversarial Backdoor Injection with
463   all baseline attacks in order to improve their resistance against parameter-space defenses. Figure 3
464   demonstrates that Adversarial Backdoor Injection is effective for all attacks when evaluating against
465   the parameter-space defense ANP, where ASRs increase after adversarial injection, especially for
466   BadNets, Blend, AdvDoor, Narcissus, and Adap-Blend. The improvement for feature space attacks
467
      (WaNet, IAD, and Bpp) is incremental. We conjecture that feature space attacks rely too much on
      prominent features as their modification in the input space is minor. To activate the backdoor with
468
      such minor input modifications, the prominent features are required in the feature space. In addition,
469
      Figure 5 in Appendix B.2 shows the results of Adversarial Backdoor Injection without defense,
470   demonstrating that it does not harm in general the BA and ASR when no defense is applied.

473
474   This section provides an oracle experimental analysis utilizing the trigger information. Specifically,
475   we use the TAC values (Zheng et al., 2022) to quantify the relevance of a neuron to the backdoor

      5              C ONCLUSIONS AND L IMITATIONS
526
527
      This paper examines input-space and feature-space backdoor attacks in the parameter space, show-
528   ing that state-of-the-art backdoor attacks are surprisingly vulnerable to parameter-space defenses. To
529   overcome this shortcoming, we propose a novel clean-label backdoor attack, Grond, that considers
530   comprehensive stealthiness, including input-, feature-, and parameter-space stealthiness. Grond
531   achieves state-of-the-art performance by leveraging adversarial examples and adaptively limiting
532   the backdoored model’s parameter changes during the backdoor injection to improve the backdoor
533   stealthiness. We also show that Grond’s Adversarial Backdoor Injection can consistently improve
534   other backdoor attacks against parameter space defenses.
535   While current backdoor defenses are ineffective against Grond, we anticipate defenses considering
536   the design of Grond could mitigate Grond in the future. Moreover, we consider only one adversar-
537   ial perturbation method, TUAP, and one Adversarial Backdoor Injection method, modified UCLC.
538   As we pointed out in the oracle analysis in Section 4.5, different adversarial injection and adversarial
539   perturbation methods are also promising under the Grond framework. We leave the exploration to
      future work.

857   related to backdoor behaviors. Specifically, TAC is defined as:

994    C    OTHER L IMITATIONS
995
996    Dirty-label Grond. Grond is a clean-label backdoor attack that uses an invisible trigger and does
997    not change the original labels of the poisoned samples. Since in our threat model, the attacker,
998    having access to the training data, provides a poisoned model to the user, we could also explore
999    the effect of a dirty-label backdoor attack to insert an all-to-one backdoor. Theoretically, a dirty-
1000   label attack could simplify the backdoor insertion and require fewer poisoned samples, which could
1001   potentially reduce our attack’s overhead and improve its performance.
