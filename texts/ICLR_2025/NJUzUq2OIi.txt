010
011
                    Long document question answering is challenging due to the quadratic compu-
012
                    tational cost of transformer-based LLMs. In resource-constrained environments,
013                 Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) uses document chunking to maintain lin-
014                 ear computational costs but often loses sight of the global context. We introduce
015                 the Mamba retriever 130M and Mamba retriever 1.3B retrievers, capable of pro-
016                 cessing entire long documents in linear time and integrating earlier context to
017                 retrieve relevant sentences for answering questions. Mamba retrievers outperform
018                 state-of-the-art embedding models across 41 long-document Q&A benchmarks
019                 while maintaining speed and computational efficiency. Their performance is com-
020                 parable to GPT-4o on long documents over 256k tokens while using significantly
021
                    fewer tokens. Mamba retrievers are trained on synthetic data generated from our
                    novel link-based method, which enhances the retrievers’ ability to leverage long-
022
                    range document connections. We further demonstrate the effectiveness of our link-
023
                    based method over baseline synthetic data methods. All code, datasets, and model
024                 checkpoints are available at https://github.com/MambaRetriever/MambaRetriever

028   1       I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Long document question answering remains a significant challenge in natural language processing
031   due to the quadratic computational cost associated with using transformer-based Large Language
032   Models (LLMs) (Vaswani et al., 2017). Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) (Asai et al., 2024)
033
      addresses this issue by processing long documents in shorter chunks with transformer-based em-
      bedding models, thereby maintaining an approximately linear computational cost relative to context
034
      size. These embedding models then retrieve relevant chunks to serve as input for an LLM to gen-
035
      erate an answer. However, embedding models that only process shorter chunks may lose important
036   global and contextual information. This limitation has spurred ongoing research into context-aware
037   embedding models (Morris & Rush, 2024).
038
039
      In this paper, we introduce a new class of retrievers. Mamba retriever 130M and Mamba retriever
      1.3B process long documents in their entirety with linear scaling in sequence length. They use their
040
      understanding of the entire preceding context to retrieve relevant sentences, as illustrated in Figure1.
041
042   The Mamba retriever is the first successful transformation of a state-space model (Dao & Gu, 2024)
043   into a fine-grained, sentence-level retriever for RAG pipelines. It outperforms state-of-the-art em-
044   bedding models across 41 long-document Q&A benchmarks (see Table 1). The Mamba retriever
045
      also maintains faster speed at processing documents and uses fewer FLOPS than embedding models
      while achieving higher accuracy (see Table 2). The Mamba retriever generalizes well on very long
046
      documents, achieving performance close to GPT-4o’s full-context capabilities on documents longer
047
      than 256k tokens (see Figure 3), while using 160 times fewer tokens1 .
048
049   A key contribution of this paper is our novel link-based synthetic data generation method, which
050   discovers real connections within a document and transforms these connections into coherent and
051
      realistic questions that require using different parts of the document to answer. When trained with
      our synthetic data, Mamba retrievers can leverage long-range connections within documents to more

058   2       R ELATED W ORK
059
060   Long-context Language Models: Transformer models are inefficient when processing long-context
061   documents because they suffer from quadratic scaling in both training and inference (Liu et al.,
062   2024a). Many works are dedicated to reducing transformer’s quadratic complexity while improving
063
      global reasoning in long documents. Sparse-attention models such as Longformer (Beltagy et al.,
      2020) and LongT5 (Guo et al., 2022) achieve linear scaling at the expense of some performance
064
      degradation. Other work focuses on using customized synthetic data and architectures to effectively
065
      extend the context window size of language models (Zhang et al., 2024c; An et al., 2024b; Luo et al.,
066   2024; Xiong et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2024). For the task of long-document summarization, several
067   techniques are developed to semantically segment documents for summarization (Moro & Ragazzi,
068   2022; 2023), including iterative (Zhang et al., 2022), recursive summarization (Wu et al., 2021) and
069   memory-enhanced segmentation (Moro et al., 2023).
070
      State Space Models: Meanwhile, State Space Models (SSMs) emerge as an alternative to process
071
      long sequences (Fu et al., 2024; Gu et al., 2022; Peng et al., 2023; Arora et al., 2024), as they have
072   linear scaling during training and inference. Dao & Gu (2024) incorporated input-dependent param-
073   eters into SSMs and integrate efficient parallelizable training and efficient autoregressive inference.
074   Glorioso et al. (2024) and Waleffe et al. (2024) proposed a hybrid Mamba that combines Mamba
075   with attention. Arora et al. (2024) used non-causal prefix-linear-attention to improve model under-
076   standing of the global context. Some recent works turn SSMs into embedding models (Hwang et al.,
077   2024; Zhang et al., 2024a). In particular, Saad-Falcon et al. (2024) built a retrieval embedding model
078   from the Monarch Mixer architecture (Fu et al., 2024). This line of work is tangential to our paper
079   because we formulate Mamba retriever as a sentence-level retriever and not an embedding model.
080   Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG): The approach of retrieving information using an em-
081   bedding model followed by generating an answer has been fundamental for processing long texts
082   that exceed the context limits of language models (Nakano et al., 2021; Borgeaud et al., 2022; Wang
083   et al., 2023a; Izacard & Grave, 2020; Huang et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024c; Xu et al., 2024; Yu et al.,
084   2024). For example, RAG systems have been successfully applied in long-document summarization
085   (Mao et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2024b) and query-centric multi-document retrieval and summarization
086
      (Gianluca Moro & Molfetta, 2023; Moro et al., 2022).
087   Embedding Models: Transformer-based embedding models are typically used as retrievers for
088   RAG systems. Previous embedding models focus on semantic understanding and instruction-
089   following (OpenAI, 2024; Wang et al., 2023b; Izacard et al., 2021; Lin et al., 2023; BehnamGhader
090   et al., 2024). Embedding models NV-Embed-v2-7B (Lee et al., 2024), GTE-Qwen2 (Li et al., 2023),
091   Stella (Zhang, 2024) and GritLM (Muennighoff et al., 2024) excel at identifying semantically simi-
092
      lar sentences within localized contexts (Muennighoff et al., 2023). However, sentence-level retrieval
      in long-context tasks requires a more comprehensive approach that needs a global understanding of
093
      the entire document. Our proposed Mamba retriever can naturally process the full document and
094
      perform sentence-level retrieval based on the global understanding of the document.

097   3       M ODEL A RCHITECTURE OF M AMBA RETRIEVER
098
099   The Mamba retriever is built using the Mamba-2 architecture (Dao & Gu, 2024), a state space model
100   with linear complexity relative to sequence length. Starting with a pretrained Mamba-2 checkpoint,
101   we remove the language modeling head and replace it with a classification head. It is important to
102   distinguish between Mamba retrievers, which are our trained discriminative retrievers, and Mamba-2
103   models, which are pretrained language models.
104   During training, each data point is created by prepending a synthetic query to a document. A binary
105   label, serving as a supervision signal, is assigned to the last token of each sentence in the document.
106   This label indicates whether the preceding sentence can help answer the question. Note that we do

133
      After labeling, we input the entire list of tokens into the model. For the last token of each sentence,
134
      the model’s classification head produces a logit value. If a sentence is important and can help answer
135
      the query, we want the logit value of its last token to be high; otherwise, it should be low. We then
136   compute the average binary cross-entropy loss using the labels and logit values of these tokens.
137
138   During the testing phase, the question is prepended to the document, and the combined text is pro-
139
      cessed by the model. Based on the logit value of the last token in each sentence, we select the top-k
      sentences to input into a generator model, such as GPT-4o, for final answer generation (see Figure
140
      1). Due to the causal structure of the model, retrieval decisions for each sentence are conditioned on
141
      all prior tokens in the document. This approach contrasts with standard embedding-based retrievers,
142   which divide documents into relatively short chunks and process these chunks independently.
143
144   In this paper, we train Mamba retrievers to operate at sentence-level resolution and retrieve sen-
145
      tences. However, our model architecture is flexible and can be adapted to other levels of granularity.
      For example, when using paragraph-level resolution, the model would focus on the final token of
146
      each paragraph in the document. See a formal formulation of Mamba retriever in Appendix F.

149   4    S YNTHETIC DATA G ENERATION
150
151   As mentioned in Section 3, the dataset for training Mamba retrievers must have labeled documents,
152   where each sentence is labeled for its relevance to a query. While long-context documents are avail-
153   able, their sentences lack the relevance labels needed for training Mamba retrievers. To address this,
154   we propose a synthetic data generation method that creates queries from existing long documents
155   and labels sentences. In this section, we introduce our novel link-based synthetic generation strategy
156   and compare it with two baseline approaches: chunk-based and pair-based generation.
157   Chunk-based generation creates a question based on a single chunk of a document. Specifically,
158   we randomly select a chunk consisting of 20 sentences from a real document and use an LLM to
159   synthetically generate a question about the selected chunk. The LLM also labels each sentence in the
160   chunk. Models trained with chunk-based synthetic data can perform localized sentence retrieval but
161   do not learn to integrate information from various parts of the document. See Figure 5 in Appendix
      B.1 for prompts used in chunk-based generation.

184   Pair-based generation is a method for synthetically creating questions that require combining in-
185
      formation from two distinct parts of a document. The process begins by dividing a document into
      non-overlapping chunks of 20 sentences each, which are then embedded using GritLM-7B. For
186
      each generation, we first randomly select one chunk and identify a second chunk that has the highest
187
      cosine similarity to the first. An LLM is then prompted to generate a question that requires in-
188   formation from both chunks to answer correctly. However, we found that even semantically similar
189   chunks often lack the necessary logical or contextual dependencies to generate realistic and coherent
190   questions. For detailed prompts used in this pair-based generation approach, please refer to Figure
191   6 in Appendix B.2.

193   4.1   L INK - BASED G ENERATION
194
195   The link-based generation strategy improves upon pair-based strategy and produces more realistic
196   and coherent questions by leveraging natural connections between different sections of a document.
197   Since the pair-based strategy fails to identify suitable chunks to form synthetic questions, we directly
198   employ an LLM to explore natural connections between chunks within a long document. Beginning
199   with a randomly selected initial chunk of 20 sentences, the LLM is provided with a context of 200
200   surrounding sentences and is tasked with identifying any sentences or chunks that have a natural
201   connection to the initial chunk. The LLM then outputs a list of chunks along with their connections
202   to the initial chunk. As illustrated in Figure 2, a synthetic question is generated based on a natural
203   connection, and relevant sentences are labeled. Unlike pair-based generation, link-based generation
204
      explores and leverages the actual structure of the document. See Appendix B.3 for prompts used.
205   GPT-4o-mini-0718 is the LLM used for synthetic data generation. See Table 4 for the computational
206   (i.e. financial) cost of each strategy. See Appendix B.4 for examples of synthetic data generated from
207   each strategy and human evaluation of these synthetic examples.

      4.3   D ECONTAMINATION FROM T EST S ETS :
217
218   To prevent contamination between our training and testing data, we implemented the following
219   procedure. First, we divided all documents from our 41 test sets into individual sentences, yielding
220   a set of 2.4 million test sentences. Next, we split the document from each synthetic data point
221   into sentences and calculate the overlap with the test set using string matching. We removed any
222   synthetic data point where more than 1% of its sentences matched those in the set of 2.4 million test
223   sentences. This process effectively eliminated textual overlap between the test and training sets.

      5.4   S LIDING W INDOW
271
272
      LLMs like GPT-4o have a context length limit of 128k tokens. To standardize evaluation on full-
273
      context LLMs, we employ a sliding window approach for documents exceeding 120k tokens. This
274   approach uses a window size of 120k tokens and a stride of 60k tokens. The answers from different
275   windows are then aggregated by the same LLM, which then produces a final answer.
276
277
      Our Mamba retriever can generalize beyond its training context length of 10k tokens (see Section
      7.2). For instance, the Mamba retriever 1.3B can handle up to 256k tokens without memory errors
278
      on a single node with 8× 80GB H100. To ensure a fair comparison with GPT-4o, we use the same
279
      sliding window approach for Mamba retrievers when documents exceed 120k tokens. This allows
280   both models to operate within the same effective context window. Sentences scored twice have their
281   scores (i.e., logit values) averaged.

303   5.6   D OCUMENT P ROCESSING S PEED AND E FFICIENCY
304
305   We evaluate the performance of various retrieval systems using our test sets. Specifically, we mea-
306   sure the average time it takes for a retriever to process a single long document (already on GPU de-
307   vices), excluding any pre-processing, post-processing, or host-to-device transfer time. For retrieval
308
      systems utilizing embedding models, a long document is processed either in batches of sentences
      or in chunks of 300 words, depending on the retrieval setting. For the embedding models, batches
309
      consist of sentences or chunks from the same document. The batch size for these models is selected
310
      to maximize token throughput. Sentences and chunks of similar lengths are batched together in
311   order to minimize the number of padding tokens. For the Mamba retrievers, a batch size of 1 is
312   consistently used, as the entire long document must be processed at once.
313
314
      When embedding models process input in batches larger than size 1, padding is necessary. Since
      embedding models require larger batch sizes for faster processing, the additional padding results
315
      in higher FLOPS. To provide a more informative comparison, we calculate FLOPS for embedding
316
      models both with and without padding. Note that the Mamba retriever does not use padding, so
317   the FLOPS remain the same regardless of padding. FLOPS are calculated using standard formulas
318   provided by Kaplan et al. (2020); Dao & Gu (2024).

528   8     C ONCLUSION & L IMITATION
529
530   We introduce the Mamba retriever, which excels in long document sentence-retrieval for RAG sys-
531
      tems and approaches GPT-4o’s performance for documents over 256k tokens despite being much
      smaller. The Mamba retriever outperforms all state-of-the-art embedding models, such as NV-
532
      Embed-v2, while using fewer FLOPS and processing documents faster. By taking into account
533
      all prior document context, the model efficiently leverages long-range dependencies for answering
534   questions about long and complex documents. Our approach eliminates the need for document
535   chunking, a common limitation in current retrieval systems that often results in loss of context and
536   reduced accuracy. Additionally, we propose a novel link-based synthetic data generation strategy
537   that proves most effective for training, helping Mamba retrievers capture long-distance dependen-
538   cies more effectively. A limitation of our approach is the cost of generating synthetic data points
539   - more than $1000 for one million examples. Finding more efficient methods for producing high-
      quality training data is essential for future research.

      A.1   T EST S ETS & VALIDATION S ETS OVERVIEW
867
868   For testing, evaluation is done on 41 QA benchmark tasks, which are collected from the following
869   long-document understanding benchmarks: Longbench (Bai et al., 2024), ∞ bench (Zhang et al.,
870   2024b), L-eval (An et al., 2024a), LVeval (Yuan et al., 2024), Bamboo (Dong et al., 2024), ELITR
871   bench (Thonet et al., 2024), docfinQA (Reddy et al., 2024), MuLD (Hudson & Al Moubayed, 2022).
872   The tasks are freeform and multiple-choice questions on long documents, which range from 1,000
873
      to 780,000 tokens.
874   Note, for clarity of presentation in the main paper, we categorize all data points from these 41
875   datasets into 4 categories based on the type of long documents reported by their original documen-
876   tation. Each data point can belong to only one category, but within a dataset, data points can be
877   distributed across multiple categories. Benchmark task statistics based on these 4 categories are pro-
878   vided in Table 7. Details of each dataset and the categories it belongs to can be found in Appendix
879
      A.2.
880
             • Educational: wikipedia, college exams, English tests, scientific papers, etc.
881
882
             • Creative: movie scripts, novels, screenplays, etc.
883          • Official: legal contract, financial documents, etc.
884          • Conversational: meeting transcripts, dialogues, etc.
885
886   We also record model performance averaged across all data points (not averaged by the 4 categories)
887   in the “Average” column in tables. Model performance on each dataset is in Appendix A.4.
888   For validation sets, we take 100 data points each from the train sets of 8 benchmark tasks. We en-
889   sured and verified no document or question from our validation sets are in any test set. Validation
890   sets and test sets are completely disjoint. We did not use the validation sets for training any models
891   or for guiding any synthetic data generation. The sole purpose of our validation sets is for hyperpa-
892   rameter tuning of our Mamba retrievers and embedding models. See full details of validation sets in
893   Appendix A.3.

       B.4   S YNTHETIC DATA Q UALITY E VALUATION
1675
1676   We provide and evaluate a few synthetic data examples generated from different synthetic data
1677   strategies in Tables 20, 21, and 22.
1678
       Link-based data (20) typically generates questions that are more coherent because these questions
1679
       arise from natural connections searched within the document. The labeled sentences are implicitly
1680   linked to the question through these connections, with sentences from different parts of the document
1681   collectively forming the answer. When a model is trained on such data, identifying the first chunk
1682   can guide it to locate the second chunk. This training process teaches the model to use information
1683   from previously encountered content when evaluating the relevance of each new sentence. By train-
1684   ing Mamba retriever in this way, it learns to use its global understanding of the entire document to
1685   determine which sentences are important for answering the question.
1686   Human Evaluation: The first example in Table 20 explores the significance of the little things in
1687   a marriage. In both contexts, the highlighted sentences offer intriguing insights into this topic. In
1688   the first context, a young girl asks her mother about things that might not matter in a marriage. The
1689   mother responds by emphasizing that these small details, which can take the edge off, do indeed
1690   matter. In the second context, the sentence about a husband and wife working together highlights
1691   the importance of collaboration in a marriage, i.e. pulling together. Thus, both contexts provide
1692   valuable information to address the question. Without the first context, the “simple secret” would
1693   not resonate as strongly, as it refers to the seemingly trivial yet significant details discussed by Marie
1694
       and her mother. Interestingly, the female character in the second context is also the same Marie.
       Therefore, the first context sets the stage for the Mamba retriever to identify the second context.
1695
       This ability to utilize long-range connections is crucial for a deeper understanding of subsequent
1696
       contexts.
1697
1698   The second example in Table 20 examines the tension between Lester’s internal conflict with his
1699   father and his struggle to navigate societal rejection. Both contexts illuminate distinct yet inter-
1700
       connected aspects of this conflict. In the first context, Lester grapples with his father’s disapproval
       and his own hesitancy to act decisively to mend their relationship. His introspection reveals his
1701
       uncertainty about standing alone in the face of societal judgment. The second context depicts the
1702
       external consequences of Lester’s actions. Together, the two contexts demonstrate the layered na-
1703   ture of Lester’s struggle, where his need for personal reform and decisive action is tied to both his
1704   father’s approval and his standing in society. By establishing Lester’s introspective conflict in the
1705   first context, Mamba retriever learns to recognize and leverage this psychological groundwork when
1706   identifying relevant connections in the second context.
1707
       For chunk-based data (21), GPT-4o-mini processes each text chunk in its entirety and directly gener-
1708
       ates questions based on the information within that chunk. This approach ensures that the generated
1709   questions are highly relevant to the content, as the model can focus on the specific details present in
1710   each text segment. However, this method may lead to issues with superficial textual overlap when
1711   training the Mamba retriever. The retriever might learn to search for semantically similar sentences
1712   rather than identifying deeper connections between individual sentences and the given query.
1713
       Pair-based synthetic data (22) are generated from two chunks of a long document that have high
1714
       cosine similarity. High cosine similarity indicates significant textual overlap between the chunks
1715   but does not ensure logical or contextual dependencies, as demonstrated in Table 22. Consequently,
1716   the questions generated may appear unnatural. Additionally, creating questions directly from these
1717   chunks can result in questions that either consist of two merged smaller questions or are unrelated
1718   to both chunks.
1719
       The first example in 22 involves a question about an event that prompted an inquiry regarding a
1720
       specific time during the group’s evening activities. Context 1 effectively answers this question by
1721   discussing these activities. However, Context 2, which frequently uses keywords like “evening” and
1722   “I,” creates a high semantic similarity with Context 1. Despite this similarity, Context 2 does not
1723   talk about the same event as Context 1 and does not contribute to answering the question in any
1724   sense. Similarly, the second example inquires about Thomas’s motivation for confessing. Context
1725   1 clearly explains that Thomas confessed because he felt sorry for Mary. In contrast, Context 2 is
1726   unrelated to the question; it only contains negative words that might have some semantic similarity
1727   to the question.

2048   E.3   A RE M AMBA RETRIEVERS L OST IN THE M IDDLE ?
2049
2050   “Lost in the Middle” is a phenomenon identified by Liu et al. (2024a), where large language models
2051   (LLMs) tend to lose track of information in the middle of a long document, favoring information
       at the beginning and end. To investigate the behavior of Mamba retrievers when processing long


                                                           38
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


2052
       documents, we first identify useful and important information within a document and record their
2053   positions. We then examine whether Mamba retrievers are more likely to forget or ignore important
2054   information from specific locations within long documents.
2055
2056
       We designed an LLM-powered pipeline that scans through a long document using a sliding window
       of 200 sentences, with a stride of 100 sentences. The goal is to identify all sentences potentially
2057
       relevant to providing the ground-truth answer to a given question. We use GPT-4o, supplying it with
2058
       both the question and the reference ground-truth answer for all data points with document lengths
2059   up to 120k tokens. Since the main paper employs a sliding window approach to aggregate logits
2060   produced by Mamba retrievers, it is not practical to investigate potential “lost in the middle” issues
2061   for documents exceeding 120k tokens.
2062
       With knowledge of both the question and the ground-truth answer, GPT-4o is better equipped to
2063
       identify relevant sentences within a 200-sentence window. The sliding window approach is designed
2064
       to mitigate potential long-context issues with GPT-4o.
2065
2066   After GPT-4o identifies relevant sentences in each window, we aggregate these sentences from dif-
2067   ferent windows and present them to GPT-4o for a final selection. Once GPT-4o selects a final list
2068
       of sentences, we ask it again whether these sentences can yield the correct ground-truth answer to
       the question. This step serves as a filtering process. After filtering, we have 3,067 data points with
2069
       documents under 120k tokens. We manually reviewed a random subset of 200 data points to validate
2070
       the quality of this pipeline.
2071
2072   We now have a set of 3,067 data points with documents annotated for relevant sentences. We also
2073   have Mamba retriever 1.3B top 50 retrieved sentences for each of these data points. For each relative
2074
       position, we calculate the number of relevant sentences retrieved by Mamba retriever 1.3B, divided
       by the total number of relevant sentences found in that position. This metric is known as sentence
2075
       recall at a certain relative position. Note that relative position is used because documents vary in
2076
       length.
2077
2078   In Figure 12, we observed an interesting pattern. Mamba retriever’s recall performance is noticeably
2079   better for smaller relative positions (i.e., the beginning of the document). Mamba retriever’s recall
2080
       performance drops to its lowest for the last 10% of relative positions (i.e., the end of the document).
       We also observed a general decreasing trend in Mamba retriever’s recall as the relative position
2081
       increases. This suggests that the Mamba retriever is less effective at retrieving relevant sentences
2082
       when they are located farther from the beginning of the document (i.e., where the query is). While
2083   there is no discernible “lost in the middle” pattern in Figure 12, we did find that Mamba retriever
2084   tends to lose track at the end of the document.
