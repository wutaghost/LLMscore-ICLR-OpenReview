009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            In this work, we demonstrate the integration of a score-matching diffusion model
012            into a deterministic architecture for time-domain musical source extraction, result-
013            ing in enhanced audio quality. To address the typically slow iterative sampling
014            process of diffusion models, we apply consistency distillation and reduce the
015            sampling process to a single step, achieving performance comparable to that of
016            diffusion models, and with two or more steps, even surpassing them. Trained on the
017            Slakh2100 dataset for four instruments (bass, drums, guitar, and piano), our model
018
               shows significant improvements across objective metrics compared to baseline
               methods. Sound examples are available at https://consistency-separation.github.io/.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
022
023
      An audio source extraction refers to the process of isolating or extracting a specific sound source
024
      from a mixture of audio signals. The source extraction task is closely related to source separation;
025   while extraction focuses on isolating a specific target source, source separation involves separating
026   all components. These techniques are crucial in various fields, including speech separation, noise
027   reduction, music processing, music analysis, transcription, and more. Different from other fields
028   in audio, music is characterized by a strong interdependence between the sources (stems) in a
029   mixture. Stems interact not only in terms of timing and rhythm but also through their spectral and
030   harmonic content. In addition, masking effects frequently occur in audio mixing (Moore, 2012),
031   where louder sounds can obscure quieter ones, and overlapping frequencies—particularly in low-
032   frequency instruments like bass and kick drum—can interfere with each other, reducing perceptual
033
      clarity. These challenges often make isolating individual sounds particularly difficult. However,
      humans have a natural ability to focus on specific sound sources, as illustrated by the ”cocktail
034
      party effect” in speech (Cherry, 1953), and similarly in music, where listeners can focus on specific
035
      instruments (Bregman, 1984; McAdams & Bregman, 1979), discerning what each one is playing.
036   The latter is especially well-developed in professional musicians and composers, who often extract,
037   transcribe, or analyze individual musical elements as part of their creative process.
038
039
      Recent advances in deep learning technology have significantly impacted the field of audio source
      extraction and separation, leading to substantial improvements in its quality. There are two primary
040
      approaches to the field using machine learning. The first approach involves deterministic discrimina-
041
      tive models (Choi et al., 2021; Défossez, 2021; Lluı́s et al., 2019; Gusó et al., 2022; Luo & Mesgarani,
042   2019; Défossez et al., 2019; Takahashi et al., 2018), which typically use mixtures for conditioning
043   in the training and inference process and learn how to derive one or more sources from mixtures.
044   On the other hand, generative models (Zhu et al., 2022; Subakan & Smaragdis, 2018; Kong et al.,
045   2019; Narayanaswamy et al., 2020; Jayaram & Thickstun, 2021; Postolache et al., 2023a; Manilow
046   et al., 2022; Postolache et al., 2023b; Kavalerov et al., 2019; Wisdom et al., 2020) generally learn
047   a prior distribution of sources and use the mixture during inference to generate separate sources.
048   Recently, there have been several attempts to apply diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015;
049   Ho et al., 2020) to audio source separation and extraction tasks (Scheibler et al., 2023; Lutati et al.,
050
      2023; Huang et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023; Hirano et al., 2023; Plaja-Roglans et al., 2022; Mariani
      et al., 2024).
051
052   We propose an extension of the deterministic mixture-conditional musical source extraction model by
053   incorporating a generative methods. First, we train a deterministic model. However, deterministic
      models relying solely on the mixture to regressively derive the sources often struggle with imperfect


                                                          1

054
      separation and source leakage, caused by the complex relationships between musical sound sources
055   and masking effects within the mixture. This creates an upper performance limit for deterministic
056   models. To address this limitation, we introduce a generative element to our system: a denoising
057   score-matching diffusion model (Song & Ermon, 2019) that enhances the extracted sources, providing
058   a generative final touch. Since generative models can synthesize data from scratch, we hypothesize
059   that incorporating a generative component would help model to reconstruct missing information,
060   reduce source leakage and farther improve the quality of source extraction. The introduction of
061   diffusion models into our system required an iterative sampling procedure, which increased the
062   inference time for the overall model. To speed up the generation process, we adapted methodologies
063
      presented in Consistency Models (CM) (Song et al., 2023) and Consistency Trajectory Models (CTM)
      (Kim et al., 2024), applying Consistency Distillation (CD) to our diffusion model, thereby reducing
064
      the number of denoising steps.
065
066   We trained our model on the Slakh2100 (Manilow et al., 2019) dataset, focusing on four instruments:
067   bass, drums, guitar, and piano. In our experiments, we compared our model with baseline models
068   Demucs (Défossez et al., 2019), Demucs+Gibbs (Manilow et al., 2022), ans MSDM (Mariani et al.,
069
      2024) and demonstrated significant improvements in the objective metrics of music separation. Our
      diffusion model showed enhanced extraction quality over the deterministic model. Furthermore, our
070
      CD model achieved accelerated single-step denoising without any loss of quality. The consistency
071
      model also provided flexibility in trading off between quality and speed, delivering improved results
072   over the diffusion model with only 2-4 steps and a minimal increase in time compared to the
073   deterministic model. Notably, our CD model outperformed the diffusion model without the use of
074   adversarial training, which contrasts with CTM, where superior performance in the image domain was
075   achieved by incorporating the GAN (Goodfellow et al., 2014) component into the training procedure
076   along with the denoising loss.
077
078
      Contributions Our work presents three main contributions: (i) We bridge the gap between deter-
079   ministic and generative source extraction methods, showing they can complement each other and
080   improve overall model performance. (ii) To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to introduce a
081   consistency framework to the raw audio domain and demonstrate a student CD model outperforming
082   the teacher diffusion model without the use of additional GAN loss. (iii) We achieve significant
083   improvements in musical source extraction and set a new benchmark on the Slakh2100 dataset.

086   2   R ELATED W ORK
087
088   Audio source extraction and separation fields have seen significant advances, driven by the develop-
089   ment of deep learning techniques and the availability of large-scale datasets. A significant body of
090   work in the field focuses on time-frequency representations, such as the Short-Time Fourier Transform
091   (STFT). Initially, STFT models concentrated on speech extraction (Grais et al., 2014), later expanding
092   to include music (Uhlich et al., 2015; 2017; Liu & Yang, 2018; Takahashi & Mitsufuji, 2017; Nugraha
093
      et al., 2016). State-of-the-art models for music source separation in the spectrogram domain include
      MMDenseLSTM (Takahashi et al., 2018), which combines convolutional and recurrent networks,
094
      D3Net (Takahashi & Mitsufuji, 2020), which uses dilated convolutions, and Spleeter (Hennequin
095
      et al., 2020), based on a U-Net architecture.
096
097   In recent years, there has been a shift in focus towards models that operate directly on raw waveforms.
098   The pioneering waveform-based audio separation model from Lluı́s et al. (2019), built on the WaveNet
099
      architecture (van den Oord et al., 2016), was followed by Wave-U-Net (Stoller et al., 2018), based on
      the U-Net architecture, with both models underperforming compared to spectrogram-based models
100
      at the time. Conv-TasNet (Luo & Mesgarani, 2019), utilizing stacked dilated 1-D convolutional
101
      blocks, was the first waveform-based model to surpass spectrogram-based approaches in speech
102   source separation. Demucs (Défossez et al., 2019) extended the U-Net architecture by incorporating
103   bidirectional LSTM layers between the encoder and decoder, surpassing all existing state-of-the-
104   art architectures at the time. Building on Demucs, the source separation problem was reframed
105   as an Orderless Neural Autoregressive Density Estimator (NADE) by Demucs+Gibbs (Manilow
106   et al., 2022), using a Gibbs sampling procedure to iteratively improve separation performance by
107   conditioning each source estimate on those from previous steps. These latest two works serve as the
      primary baselines for our approach.

200   4.1   DATASET
201
      For our training and evaluation experiments, we used the Slakh2100 dataset (Manilow et al., 2019), a
202
      widely recognized and extensively used benchmark for music source separation. Our choice of dataset
203
      was driven by the high data requirements of diffusion models, making Slakh an ideal candidate for
204   training. Slakh consists of 2100 tracks, with 1500 allocated for training, 375 for validation, and 225
205   for testing. It is a synthetically generated multi-track audio waveform dataset created from MIDI
206   files using virtual instruments. The dataset contains 31 instrument classes. We downsampled the
207   audio to 22kHz and used a duration of N = 262144 samples, which approximately corresponds
208   to 11.9 seconds of audio file length. We focused on four instruments—Bass, Drums, Guitar, and
209   Piano—due to their prevalence in the dataset and to ensure direct comparability with baseline models.
210   The presence of these instruments in the dataset is 94.7% for Bass, 99.3% for Drums, 100.0% for
211   Guitar, and 99.3% for Piano.
212   In addition to Slakh2100, we also trained and evaluated our models using the MUSDB18 (Rafii et al.,
213   2017) dataset, which is another widely used data for music source separation. The dataset consists of
214   150 tracks, with 100 designated for training and 50 for testing, totaling approximately 10 hours of
215   professional-grade audio. Each track is divided into stems: Bass, Drums, Vocals, and Other. For the
      results on MUSDB18 dataset please refer to Appendix C.

233   4.2     M ODEL A RCHITECTURE AND T RAINING OVERVIEW
234
235   All of our models—Deterministic, Diffusion, and Consistency—are based on a U-Net backbone. Our
236
      U-Net design follows the structure of generative U-Net models from Moûsai (Schneider et al., 2024)
      and MSDM (Mariani et al., 2024), with modifications.
237
238   The U-Net for the deterministic model was modified to accept a one-hot encoded instrument label as
239   a conditioning input, with a size of S = 4. Our hyperparameter settings for the deterministic model
240   mostly follow those used in MSDM, with the exception of using a single channel instead of four. We
241   trained the deterministic model using the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 1 × 10−4 for 170
242
      epochs, until convergence.
243   For the diffusion model, we used the same backbone U-Net architecture as in the deterministic
244   model, wrapping it within the diffusion model framework. We added additional conditioning by
245   incorporating the deterministic model as a feature extractor, integrating the extracted features into the
246   diffusion model layer by layer. The preconditioning and training procedures for the diffusion model
247   largely followed the method introduced in the EDM framework (Karras et al., 2022). We trained
248
      the diffusion model using the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 1 × 10−4 for 280 epochs until
      convergence.
249
250   The consistency model is an exact replica of the diffusion model and, for effective distillation, is
251   initialized using the pre-trained weights of the diffusion model. For the Consistency model, we
252   applied consistency distillation as described in the original work (Song et al., 2023), but with some
253   modifications. Specifically, we did not use schedule functions for T or µ, the total number of steps,
254
      and the update rate of the EMA for the stop-gradient student model, and instead kept these values
      fixed at T = 18 and µ = 0.999 throughout the experiments. We increased the number of ODE steps
255
      to h ≤ 17, compared to h = 1 in the original. We incorporated settings from the CTM framework
256
      (Kim et al., 2024) to balance the losses in equation 6. The consistency model was trained using
257   the Rectified Adam optimizer (Liu et al., 2020), without applying learning rate decay, warm-up, or
258   weight decay, with a fixed learning rate of 1 × 10−5 for 50 epochs.
259
260
      For more detailed technical account of our models, please first refer to Appendix A, which introduces
      preliminary foundational concepts, notations, algorithms, and mathematical formulations of score-
261
      based models and consistency models, and then refer to Appendix B for more detailed implementation,
262
      hyperparameters, training, and inference settings.

      time, and real-time factor (RTF) for separating a 11.9-second audio mixture. Unlike Demucs,
352
      Demucs+Gibbs, and MSDM, which are separation models and extract all 4 tracks simultaneously,
353   our models extracts one source at a time, thus we multiply inference times by 4. We additionally
354   report the number of parameters of the models for the efficiency comparison.
355
356
      Despite need for four iterations, our deterministic model shows the best speed, with an inference time
      of 0.114 seconds and an RTF of 0.009, comparable in performance with Demucs. This is expected,
357
      as Demucs and our deterministic model both are simple discriminative models with relatively similar
358
      model sizes. It is worth noting that, in the table, we report Demucs without the shift trick, which is the
359   fastest but not the best-performing configuration for Demucs. The shift trick, as described in Défossez
360   et al. (2019), involves running inference multiple times with different input shifts, which slows down
361   the process by a factor proportional to the number of shifts used. In practice, this can potentially
362   make inference 2 to 10 times slower, highlighting the advantageous edge of our deterministic model.
363
      Our diffusion model, while slower than our deterministic model with 1.399 seconds and an RTF of
364
      0.117, still outperforms generative baselines like ISDM and MSDM, which exhibit significantly longer
365   inference times, the ISDM model, in particular, being both parameter-heavy and time-consuming.
366   Another semi-generative method, Demucs+Gibbs, requiring many inference steps, also shows signifi-
367   cantly slower performance than our diffusion models.
368
      Our CD models, especially at T = 1, achieve an RTF of 0.023, only twice as slow as the deterministic
369
      models and comparable with double deterministic model, while providing significant audio quality
370
      improvement. Notably, our best-performing CD model with 4 steps, though 5 times slower than the
371   deterministic model, remains still significantly faster than other diffusion-based models (including
372   ours) and far below the real-time generation benchmark.

375   6      C ONCLUSION
376
377   In this work, we introduced a framework that integrates discriminative and generative models for
      time-domain musical source extraction by combining a score-matching diffusion model with a


                                                               7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      deterministic model to enhance extraction quality. Our experiments confirmed the hypothesis that
379   incorporating a generative model enhances extraction quality by reconstructing information lost in
380   the mixture due to masking and by reducing source leakage—issues that are common in deterministic
381   approaches.
382
      We overcame the typically slow sampling process of diffusion models by applying Consistency
383
      Distillation (CD), which accelerated sampling to speeds comparable to the deterministic model
384
      without compromising quality, while also offering significant improvements in exchange for a slight
385   speed trade-off. Notably, this work not only represents the first application of consistency models
386   in the audio waveform domain, showing CD beating diffusion model, but also achieves significant
387   improvements across objective metrics, establishing new state-of-the-art source extraction/separation
388   results on the Slakh2100 dataset.
389
      Our findings highlight the strong potential of mixed deterministic and generative models to advance
390
      the field of source extraction and separation, paving the way for future developments in audio
391   processing and related applications. Future work should explore the possibilities of reducing audio
392   segment lengths along with model size of our model, potentially farther decreasing inference times
393   and enabling lightweight, fast, real-time applications for audio source extraction with high-quality
394   results.

587   Score-based diffusion models (Song & Ermon, 2019) are the generative models designed to learn
588   data representations by introducing controlled noise and learning to iteratively remove it from the
589   data. Score-based models are a class of generative models that rely on learning the gradient of the
590
      data distribution, known as the score function of the target distribution p(x), namely ∇x log p(x).

593   represents Gaussian noise, and σt defines the amount of noise added at time t. In score-based

      errors in the diffusion process. The EDM sampler includes R iterations at each time step, resulting in

696   Consistency models (Song et al., 2023) are a novel class of generative models closely related to
697   diffusion models, designed for few-step or even one-step generation. The core idea of consistency
698   models is the concept of self-consistency. Consistency function g directly connects any timestep point
699   of the diffusion trajectory to the trajectory’s starting point g(xt , t) → xδ , where δ → 0+ represents
700   an infinitesimally small positive value, indicating the very low noise step at the start of the trajectory.
701   This property of the consistency function can also be written as g(xt , t) = g(xt′ , t′ ), ∀t, t′ ∈ [δ, T ],
      indicating that any noise level on the given trajectory results in the same output.

780

783   maintaining 1024 channels. The decoder mirrors the encoder, following a symmetric structure in
784   reverse.

      Reported in the Table 3, we compared the performance of our model with MSDM, Demucs v2, and
840   Demucs + Gibbs on the MUSDB18 (Rafii et al., 2017) test set, reporting SI-SDRI values (dB). First,
841   we evaluated our model trained on Slakh2100 without any training or fine-tuning on MUSDB18,
842   reporting results only for bass and drums, as our model is not trained on the other and vocals categories
843   in this setting. We found that our model, when evaluated on the MUSDB18 test set, outperformed
844   MSDM trained on Slakh2100 and even MSDM trained on MUSDB18 (as reported in their paper).
845   However, we observed that the diffusion and CD extensions of our model did not show any additional
846   improvement over the deterministic model on the MUSDB dataset.
847   Next, we trained our deterministic model on the MUSDB18 dataset and found that it outperformed
848   both Demucs and Demucs + Gibbs as reported in Manilow et al. (2022). However, our deterministic
849   model did not outperform Demucs v2, as reported in the MSDM paper. Additionally, we did not
850   observe any improvement from the diffusion and CD methods over the deterministic model when
851   further trained on the MUSDB dataset. The results were similar to those of the deterministic model,
852   and thus we do not report them here.
853   While our model demonstrated reasonable performance on the MUSDB18 dataset, the limited size
854   of MUSDB18 (10 hours) compared to the significantly larger Slakh2100 dataset (145 hours) likely
855   hindered the full potential of our diffusion-based approach. We believe the smaller dataset size was
856   insufficient to fully leverage the capabilities of our diffusion based method and plan to investigate
857   possibilities for adapting our approach to better accommodate smaller datasets in future work.
