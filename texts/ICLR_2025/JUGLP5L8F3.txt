009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Federated Learning (FL) provides a flexible distributed platform where numerous
012            clients with high degrees of heterogeneity in data and system can collaborate
013            to learn a model jointly. Previous research has shown that FL is effective in
014            handling diverse data but often assumes idealized conditions. Specifically, client
015            participation is often simplified in these studies, while real-world factors make it
016            difficult to predict or design individual client participation. This complexity often
017            diverges from the ideal client participation assumption, rendering an unknown
018
               pattern of client participation, referred to as arbitrary client participation (ACP).
               Hence, it is an important open problem to explore the impact of client participation
019
               and find a lightweight mechanism to enable ACP in FL. In this paper, we first
020
               empirically investigate the influence of client participation on FL, revealing that
021            FL algorithms are significantly impacted by ACP. To alleviate the influence, we
022            propose a lightweight solution, Federated Average with Snapshot (FAST), to
023            unleash almost ACP for FL. It can seamlessly integrate with other classic FL
024            algorithms. Specifically, FAST enforces the clients to take a snapshot once in
025            a while and facilitates ACP for the majority of the training process. We show
026            the convergence rates of FAST in non-convex and strongly-convex cases, which
027            match the rates with those in ideal client participation. Furthermore, we empirically
028            introduce an adaptive strategy for dynamically configuring the snapshot frequency,
029
               tailored to accommodate diverse FL systems. Our extensive numerical results
               demonstrate that our FAST attains significant improvements under the conditions
030
               of ACP and highly heterogeneous data.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Federated Learning (FL) stands out as an emerging distributed machine learning framework where a
036   large number of clients (i.e., computing nodes or devices) collaborate together to train a machine
037   learning model under the coordination of a central server (McMahan et al., 2017; Kairouz et al.,
038   2021). FL establishes itself as a powerful and flexible distributed platform, fostering collaboration
039
      among diverse clients characterized by substantial heterogeneity in data and system while preserving
      the privacy of raw data residing within each client. Hence, previous research endeavors have yielded
040
      a spectrum of efficient algorithms capable of achieving optimal convergence rates in theory and
041
      delivering great performance in some practical cases, in the presence of varying degrees of data
042   heterogeneity (Kairouz et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2018; Li et al., 2019; Karimireddy et al., 2020; Yang
043   et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2021).
044
045
      Nevertheless, realizing these favorable outcomes often hinges on the assumption of an ideal system
      condition (i.e., ideal client participation). Specifically, most FL algorithms presume that client
046
      participation can be fully known, controlled, predicted, or tracked. For example, some works assume
047
      partial client participation, where participation follows a known or controllable random process,
048   such as ergodic, mixing, or independent processes (McMahan et al., 2017; Acar et al., 2021; Cho
049   et al., 2023). Others assume that each client participates at least once within certain rounds (Yang
050   et al., 2022b; Gu et al., 2021; Yan et al., 2024). In practice, however, each client’s participation is
051   highly dynamic, unknown and unpredictable (Bonawitz et al., 2019; Soltani et al., 2022) since
052   clients frequently exhibit a spectrum of heterogeneous and dynamically shifting attributes, including
053   computational power, communication capacity, and availability (Kairouz et al., 2021; Bonawitz et al.,
      2019; Yang et al., 2021). These variations stem from the unique characteristics of each individual

      client and the dynamics of distributed learning systems. The intricacies of client participation, marked
055   by their dynamic, unknown, and unpredictable nature, make it challenging and even impossible to
056   ascertain a priori beforehand. Moreover, in some FL systems, such as cross-device FL, tracking client
057   participation is either infeasible or not permitted Kairouz et al. (2021). We refer to this complex and
058   unpredictable pattern as arbitrary client participation (ACP), reflecting its dependence on various
059   system factors and the absence of explicit client tracking. Clearly, it leaves a substantial gap between
060   algorithmic designs built on the premise of ideal client participation and the real-world applications
061   of FL in the face of ACP. On the other hand, without any conditions on client participation, a constant
062   error arises for ACP as identified by the lower bound Cho et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2020); Yang
063
      et al. (2022b), implying that no algorithm can achieve stationary point convergence in such case. This
      observation motivates us to pose the following fundamental question:
064
065    (Q1): Is it possible to design a lightweight mechanism for FL that can accommodate arbitrary
066    client participation with theoretical guarantees?
067
068   In this paper, we show an affirmative answer to this question by proposing a new client participation
069   mechanism for FL, denoted as Federated Averaging with SnapshoT (FAST). In contrast to most FL
070   algorithms that necessitate ideal client participation in each communication round, FAST imposes a
071   minimal requirement for client participation by intermittently implementing a snapshot step. This
072
      approach significantly diminishes the requirement for individual client participation, enabling ACP
      for the majority of the training process. We highlight our contributions as follows:
073
074   • Through extensive experiments, we revealed that the mismatch between ideal client participation
075     in algorithm design and arbitrary client participation in practice leads to severe performance
076     degradation, especially in highly heterogeneous data scenarios. These phenomena are universal
077     and extend beyond specific algorithms, as observed across multiple FL algorithms.
078   • To address this issue, we introduce FAST, a lightweight FL framework that requires only intermittent
079     snapshot steps, enforcing fully random client participation during these steps while accommodating
080     arbitrary client participation within the system at all other times. This requirement applies to
081     the client cohort rather than individual clients, allowing the participating group to be statistically
082     representative. This is a milder condition compared to existing works (see Table 1), as it eliminates
083     the need to track each client individually.
084   • Theoretically,

103   Ideal Client Participation: full client participation and uniformly random client participation.
104   In FL, client participation can be seen as a proxy for system heterogeneity. Due to the inherent
105   complexity of real-world FL systems, explicitly modeling client participation proves challeng-
106   ing (Bonawitz et al., 2019; Yang et al., 2021). Most existing FL algorithms often make an assumption
107   about ideal client participation, typically relying on either full client participation (Gorbunov et al.,
      2021; Haddadpour et al., 2019; Lin et al., 2018; Wang & Joshi, 2019; 2021; Yu et al., 2019) or


                                                                                  2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      uniformly random client participation (McMahan et al., 2017; Li et al., 2019; Karimireddy et al.,
109   2020; Yang et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2021;
110   Jhunjhunwala et al., 2022; Grudzień et al., 2023). This assumption requires that the server can
111   force all clients or at least uniformly and randomly sample a subset of clients to participate in each
112   communication round. However, it is crucial to acknowledge that each client in FL is not entirely
113   under the server’s control. While the server may sample a client for a specific round, the client
114   is highly likely not to participate due to various system factors such as drop-out, communication
115   congestion, and other unpredictable factors (Kairouz et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2021). It is worth
116   noting that the server can invest additional resources to enforce uniform client participation, such
117
      as sampling more clients and extending the waiting time in each round. Yet, this approach leads to
      prolonged training times due to significant communication and computation overhead (Zhou et al.,
118
      2022). As shown in (Luo et al., 2022), enforcing uniform client participation in every round by the
119
      server results in slow wall-clock time for FL training.
120
121   Controllable Client Participation. In addition to uniform client participation, another approach
122   in the field involves modeling client participation as a controllable random process. One line of
123
      works utilizes predefined patterns or probabilities as the model of client participation (Chen et al.,
      2022; Yang et al., 2022b; Fraboni et al., 2021; Ruan et al., 2021; Gu et al., 2021; Avdiukhin &
124
      Kasiviswanathan, 2021; Wang & Ji, 2022; Koloskova et al., 2022). The main idea is to allow
125
      asynchronous communication or fixed participation patterns (e.g., given probability) for clients to
126   participate flexibly in training. However, existing works in this area often require extra assumptions,
127   such as bounded delay and extra memory (Yang et al., 2022b; Ruan et al., 2021; Gu et al., 2021;
128   Koloskova et al., 2022) and identical computation rate (Avdiukhin & Kasiviswanathan, 2021).
129   Moreover, several works explore some unique scenarios of client participation. For instance, (Chen
130   et al., 2022) introduced a novel client subsampling scheme considering the importance of updates,
131   relying solely on the norm of the update. The studies by (Malinovsky et al., 2023) and (Cho et al.,
132   2023) investigated cyclic client participation. (Wang & Ji, 2022) provided a unified analysis for
133   various client participation, including regularized, ergodic, independent, and mixing participation.
134
      The implicit assumption in these studies is that client participation is either known, largely controllable
      or adheres to predefined patterns. It is also noteworthy to mention a related work (Wang & Ji, 2023),
135
      wherein the estimated probability of each client’s participation was used for a re-weighting process
136
      under unknown participation statistics. However, estimating such probabilities can be challenging in
137   practical scenarios, such as cross-device FL (Kairouz et al., 2021).
138
139   Each of these approaches contributes to the diverse client participation strategies employed in FL.
140
      However, these strategies often necessitate adherence to specific patterns, which may not align
      seamlessly with practical FL scenarios characterized by highly dynamic, unknown and unpredictable
141
      nature. In this paper, we introduce a more general and practical approach, referred to as arbitrary
142
      client participation (ACP). This implies that we do not impose any assumptions on client participation
143   for the majority of the training round. Our aim is to offer a flexible and realistic framework that
144   accommodates various client participation scenarios in real-world FL applications.
145   Comparison of Related Work. We compare some related work about ACP in Table 1. Except for
146   the difference in participation patterns and convergence rate, there are still some important points
147   that we need to compare. For FedAmplify (Wang & Ji, 2022), it can achieve the convergence rate

149   participation frequency for each client. For MIFA (Gu et al., 2021), each client needs to participate
150   in training at least once in the one-time window. For Anarchic Federated Learning (AFL) (Yang
151   et al., 2022b), the server needs to identify and store local models, and each client needs to participate
152   in training at least once in the one-time window. In contrast, our FAST framework has no extra
153   assumptions for client participation and can achieve the ideal convergence rate. In addition, regular
154
      FAST does not have any demand to store extra information.

202   3.2   T HE I MPACT OF C LIENT PARTICIPATION IN FL
203
      Simulation of Arbitrary Client Participation. We delve into FedAvg’s performance across four
204   client participations characterized by distinct distributions: uniform, Beta, Gamma, and Weibull.
205   Uniform client participation entails the random client selection from the entire client set, which is
206   an assumption representing the ideal scenario in current FL algorithms. The Beta distribution is
207   commonly employed to model events constrained within an interval. The Gamma distribution finds
208   application in characterizing the frequency of a sequence of events associated with time or distance,
209   while the Weibull distribution is widely utilized in reliability or survival analysis (Lai et al., 2006). In
210   FL, the server often receives returns from clients within a given time window. Hence, it is reasonable
211   to use uniform distribution as a baseline for ideal client participation. The latter three distributions
212
      are utilized to approximate different real-world scenarios, serving as representatives of ACP.
213   It is important to emphasize that our primary goal is not to precisely model client participation in
214   FL but to explore the impact of different potential client participation scenarios. Also, we aim to
215   highlight the adverse effects resulting from the mismatch between the ideal client participation used
      in the current algorithm design and ACP observed in practical FL.

231   Observations. We have three key observations. First, the performance of FedAvg is significantly
232
      influenced by client participation. As shown in Table 2, the model accuracy varies across different
      client participation cases, with uniform participation yielding the best performance among these four
233
      cases. This performance difference is substantial, ranging from 3% to 18%. These results align with
234
      practical FL simulations, where uncontrolled client participation induced by system heterogeneity
235   leads to non-trivial model performance degradation (Yang et al., 2021). Second, this performance
236   degradation strongly correlates with the degree of Non-IID data. In our setting, we adopt the common
237   approach of generating Non-IID data using the Dirichlet distribution (Acar et al., 2021), with the
238   parameter α controlling the Non-IID degree. A smaller α corresponds to a higher Non-IID degree.
239   For datasets with a higher degree of Non-IID data (smaller α), the model accuracy gap between
240   uniform and other cases becomes more pronounced. For instance, on the Fashion-MNIST dataset, the
241   model behaves similarly for different client participation cases with less Non-IID data (i.e., α = 1).
242   However, as the degree of Non-IID gets higher, such as α = 0.05, the accuracy gap between uniform
243
      and other participation cases could be as large as 18%. Third, the performance degradation for ACP
      (in the latter three cases) is a universal phenomenon. This extends beyond FedAvg, as evidenced by
244
      consistent observations across other FL algorithms such as FedProx and FedAvgM.
245
246   It is essential to note that occasional enforcement of uniform client participation in FL is feasible.
247   For instance, the server can sample a larger number of clients and allocate sufficient time for each
248   communication round, allowing ample clients to complete local computations. However, this strategy
249
      inevitably demands more resources and significantly extends the training time due to longer waiting
      time. Therefore, it becomes unrealistic to enforce uniform client participation in every round. On
250
      the other hand, without imposing any constraints on client participation, FedAvg is theoretically
251
      incapable of asymptotically converging to a stationary point (Yang et al., 2022b;a) and experiences
252   non-trivial performance degradation in practice, as shown above. This realization motivates us to
253   develop a lightweight client participation mechanism. This mechanism aims to achieve performance
254   similar to that of uniform participation while imposing fewer constraints on FL systems.

384   4.3   A DAPTIVE FAST
385
      As shown in Algorithm 1, FAST introduces an extra hyper-parameter, q (or I), representing the
386
      snapshot probability (or frequency). Obviously, the effective performance of our FAST is evidently
387   contingent on the selection of an appropriate q, as indicated by the ablation study on q in Sec. 5. In
388   practice, obtaining prior knowledge to consistently set the optimal q poses a challenge. To address
389   this issue, we propose an adaptive strategy to dynamically update q as shown in Algorithm 2.
390
391   Algorithm 2 Adaptive q in FAST
392    1: Initialize q0 = 0, ∆ = 0, λ(default = 1).
393    2: for round r = 0, 1, ..., R − 1 do
394    3:    Obtain accr
395    4:    ∆ ← ∆ − accr
396    5:    qr+1 ← min(1, max(0, qr + λ∆))
397
       6:    ∆ ← accr
       7: end for
398
399
400   In more detail, we initiate with q = 0 to refrain from enforcing client participation at the beginning of
401
      training procedure. Meanwhile, q is adjusted in each round based on the training accuracy difference
      ∆ between the current and previous rounds. When ∆ > 0, indicating a decrease in training accuracy
402
      compared to the last round, we increase q by λ∆. This adjustment aims to increase the probability
403
      of uniform client participation, improving performance. Conversely, when ∆ < 0, signifying an
404   increase in training accuracy in the current round, we decrease q by λ∆. This reduction aims to
405   diminish the probability of uniform client participation, ensuring a more substantial contribution from
406   arbitrary participation in the training process. Line 5 ensures that the frequency q stays within the
407   range of [0,1]. For the selection of λ, we conduct a series of experiments to assess the performance
408   under different λ. Our results show that the adaptive FAST is less sensitive to the choice of λ, and
409   choosing a default λ = 1 works well under different settings provided in Sec. 5 and Appendix B.2.3.

531   6     C ONCLUSION
532
      In this paper, we explored the impact of ACP on FL, characterized by an unknown pattern of client
533   participation. We first empirically showed that FL algorithms are significantly impacted by ACP.
534   Afterward, we proposed a lightweight solution, Federated Average with Snapshot (FAST), to unleash
535   the almost ACP for FL. In strongly-convex and non-convex cases, we proved the convergence rates
536   of FAST, which match the rates with those in ideal client participation. Besides, we introduced an
537   adaptive strategy for dynamically configuring the snapshot frequency, tailored to accommodate diverse
538   FL systems. Extensive numerical results showed that our FAST attains significant improvements
539   under the conditions of ACP and can seamlessly integrate with other classic FL algorithms.

1248   We design different kinds of models for different tasks and datasets. All details about the models we

1263   We use Dirichlet distribution to simulate the Non-IID data on Fashion-MNIST and CIFAR-10 datasets.
1264   The Shakespeare dataset is naturally Non-IID, so there is no demand to use Dirichlet distribution

1267   Dirichlet distribution) to control the degree of Non-IID in data distribution. For a clear display, we
1268   visualize it in Figure 1. In each figure below, each color represents a class of label, meaning that there
1269   are 10 kinds of colors in the figures below.

1271   types of labels, and data distribution varies greatly among clients, which is shown in Figure 1 (a).
1272   When α = 1, each client owns data with all labels, and data distributions on every client are less
1273   Non-IID, as shown in Figure 1 (b).

1336   B.2.1               A DDITIONAL R ESULTS OF I MPACT OF A RBITRARY C LIENT PARTICIPATION
1337
1338   In our main paper, we have demonstrated that arbitrary client participation can degrade the perfor-
1339   mance of FL when using the classic FedAvg algorithm. To further verify the ubiquity of adverse
1340   effects of arbitrary client participation, we conducted extra related experiments to verify the impact
1341
       of arbitrary client participation on other FL optimization algorithms. Table 4 shows the performance
       degradation resulting from arbitrary client participation when the FedProx Li et al. (2020) algorithm
1342
       is used. Under the less Non-IID circumstances (e.g., α ≥ 0.5), the accuracy of the four types of
1343
       participation is concentrated on around 90%. However, in highly heterogeneous situations, compared
1344   to uniform participation, the accuracy of the three types of arbitrary participation patterns suffers
1345   from a more severe decline. More specifically, when α = 0.05, uniform participation suffers from 8%
1346   accuracy degradation. However, the accuracy of Beta, Gamma and Weibull on the Fashion-MNIST
1347   dataset falls by around 15%, 16% and 11%, respectively, which are more severe than the one under
1348   uniform client participation. In another heterogeneous situation with α = 0.1, the accuracy of Beta,
1349   Gamma and Weibull on the CIFAR-10 dataset decreases by about 4%, 14% and 5% separately, but
       the accuracy of uniform client participation is just roughly reduced by 2%.
