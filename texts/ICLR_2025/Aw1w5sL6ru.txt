009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Detecting out-of-distribution (OOD) data is crucial in real-world machine learning
012            applications to prevent severe errors, particularly in safety-critical domains. Ex-
013            isting methods often leverage language information from vision-language models
014            (VLMs) to enhance OOD detection by improving confidence estimation through
015            rich class-wise text information. However, these methods primarily focus on obtain-
016            ing OOD scores based on the similarity of the new sample to each in-distribution
017            (ID) class, overlooking the OOD scores to a group of similar classes. We assume
018
               that an ID sample should consistently receive a high similarity score across sim-
               ilar ID classes. This paper investigates the ability of image-text comprehension
019
               among different semantic-related ID labels in VLMs and proposes a novel post-
020
               hoc strategy called SimLabel. SimLabel enhances the separability between ID
021            and OOD samples by establishing a more robust image-class similarity metric
022            that considers consistency over a set of similar class labels. Extensive experi-
023            ments demonstrate the superior performance of SimLabel on various zero-shot
024            OOD detection benchmarks, underscoring its efficacy in achieving robust OOD
025            detection.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Handling out-of-distribution (OOD) data is a critical challenge in real-world machine learning
031   applications, particularly in safety-related domains such as autonomous driving systems and medical
032   diagnosis Hendrycks & Gimpel (2016). Traditional image domain OOD detection methods primarily
033
      focus on visual inputs Hendrycks et al. (2020); Hsu et al. (2020); Jin et al. (2022); Shen et al. (2021);
      Xu et al. (2021) and develop various scoring functions Wang et al. (2022); Hendrycks & Gimpel
034
      (2016) to distinguish OOD data from in-distribution (ID) classes. Due to the unimodal nature of these
035
      approaches, they rely solely on visual information, limiting their ability to leverage rich semantic
036   information inherent in text labels.
037
038   The emergence of Vision-Language Models (VLMs), notably CLIP Radford et al. (2021), has opened
039
      new opportunities to leverage paired image and text information for OOD detection. For instance,
      ZOC Esmaeilpour et al. (2022) utilizes a trainable captioner to generate OOD labels and introduces
040
      the task of Zero-Shot OOD detection, which does not require training on ID samples. Maximum
041
      Concept Matching (MCM) Ming et al. (2022) proposes a distance-based zero-shot OOD detection
042   method where the fundamental assumption is that images are more likely to be ID if their embeddings
043   are closer to ID text embeddings, and vice versa. However, the naive textual prompt construction in
044   this method neglects the rich semantic textual information of the ID classes, leading to less effective
045   ID/OOD separation.
046
      To address these limitations, variants of the MCM score have been presented. For instance, Dai
047
      et al. (2023) introduces class-wise attributes to enhance the confidence score between ID images and
048   labels, providing more accurate and expressive descriptions for improved performance. Similarly,
049   Wang et al. (2023) introduces and trains a negative prompt for each ID class using an external dataset,
050   performing OOD detection by combining scores from both negative and traditional prompts. However,
051   these methods primarily focus on learning individual class-wise textual information, overlooking the
052   semantic information existing among different classes. In Fig. 1 (a), we show OOD detection results
053   for ID (top) and OOD (bottom) samples, respectively, without considering intra-class similarity. The
      inaccurate predictions over OOD samples motivate our investigation into aggregating OOD scores

078   for similar classes to determine OOD. Our basic assumption is that an ID sample should consistently
079   have high similarity scores across similar ID classes. This assumption motivates us to devise a new
080   method for detecting OOD samples based on measuring the consistency among semantically related
081   labels from the ID classes.
082   To further illustrate our motivation, for every ID label, we introduce a set of accompanying or similar
083   labels from either the ID labels or external knowledge that enable the model to detect OOD samples
084   in a second direction: consistency between image and similar classes. Specifically, as demonstrated
085   in Fig. 1 (b), given ID (top) and OOD (bottom) images which are predicted as the same class by the
086   baseline method, ID images show consistent higher similarity to the set of semantically similar ID
087   classes than OOD images. Based on this observation, we propose SimLabel, a post-hoc method with
088
      a well-designed OOD score to detect OOD images by examining the consistency of high-similarity
      over similar classes. For instance, Fig. 1 (b) illustrates the detection on the OOD sample as it receives
089
      diverse similarity scores for similar classes, namely Tiger Cat, Tabby Cat, Siamese Cat etc. The
090
      proposed OOD score combines knowledge from the prediction and its similar classes (see example in
091   Appendix D), thus better leveraging the VLMs’ capabilities of comprehending class prototypes.
092
093   Additionally, we design several algorithms for selecting high-quality similar labels from the ID
094
      class or external knowledge. The choice of similar classes can be generated from three directions:
      text-hierarchy, world knowledge, and pseudo-image-label alignment. Extensive experiments validate
095
      that our proposed method, SimLabel, achieves superior performance across various zero-shot OOD
096
      detection benchmarks.
097
098   We summarize our main contributions as follows:
099
100          • We propose a novel post-hoc framework, SimLabel (see Sec. 3.2), that constructs the affinity
101            between images and class prototypes with semantic-related labels for robust OOD detection.
102
             • We introduce different and comprehensive strategies (see Sec. 3.1) for selecting similar labels
103
               from the various perspectives and illustrate the influence on OOD detection performance
104            with the different choices of similar classes (see Sec. 4).
105
106          • We present in-depth empirical analysis, offering insights into the effectiveness of the SimLa-
107            bel score (see Sec. 5) and show that SimLabel learns a robust and discriminative image-class
               matching score, potentially improving visual classification ability.

139   3     M ETHODOLOGY
140
141   In estimating confidence for determining whether images are ID or OOD, prior works such as the
142   MCM score and Ming et al. (2022); Dai et al. (2023) are confined to their predicted label li . However,
143   VLMs are trained on extensive datasets Radford et al. (2021) and have demonstrated their image-text
144   comprehension ability by measuring the similarity between images and various text embedding. Thus,
145
      as illustrated in Fig. 1, this confinement to a single label prevents the model from fully capturing the
      image-text comprehension capabilities of VLMs, ultimately compromising the model’s confidence
146
      estimation on ID samples.
147
148   The motivation behind our method is to enhance the model’s robustness by extending the label
149   set for each class, effectively utilizing the VLMs’ image-text comprehension capabilities. Thus,
150   we demonstrate several intuitive algorithms for generating similar classes to extend each ID class
151
      (Sec. 3.1). We then develop a post-hoc SimLabel score for OOD detection by measuring the
      consistency between images & similar-class-labels similarity (Sec. 3.2).The pipeline of estimating
152
      SimLabel score in detecting OOD samples can be seen in Fig. 2.

157   have higher affinity/similarity to ID samples compared with OOD samples. We then refer these labels

159   similar classes: 1. exploring the text hierarchy among class labels and select the labels under the
160   same super-class as similar classes (see Sec. 3.1.1); 2. using the external large language models/world
161   knowledge in generating similar classes (see Sec. 3.1.2); 3. utilizing the similarity between ID images
      and ID labels for selecting the similar classes (see Sec. 3.1.3).

185   3.1.1      S IMILAR CLASSES BASED ON TEXT HIERARCHY
186
187   Utilizing inherent hierarchical information among different class labels is a natural idea in clustering
188   ID classes into groups with high semantic correlation and Novack et al. (2023) has shown a precise
189   construction of tree-structured hierarchical label sets over various datasets. Here, we follow the
190
      pipeline shown in Novack et al. (2023) to build hierarchical label sets among ID labels where the
      subsets of the hierarchy can be seen in Appendix B. For each class li , we select the class labels under
191
      the same super-class as the set of similar classes D(li ). We denote the SimLabel score with these
192
      hierarchical similar classes as SimLabel-H.
193
194
      3.1.2      S IMILAR CLASSES FROM LARGE LANGUAGE MODELS
195
196   Nevertheless, the above method may face with the problem of unbalanced ID label space which
197   result in the lack of similar labels for some rare ID classes. Large language models (LLMs), such as
198   GPT-3 Brown et al. (2020), possess extensive world knowledge across various domains, making us a
199   direct approach for generating similar classes. We prompt LLMs to generate similar classes D(li )
200   where they share similar visual features. We randomly select several visual categories and manually
201
      compose similar classes using a one-shot in-context example, and the details of the templates for
      prompting LLMs have been shown in Appendix A. An example of generated similar classes is listed
202
      in Fig. 3.
203
204   Additionally, although the LLM generates semantically correlated similar classes from world knowl-
205   edge, we find that some classes are more semantic-related and potentially affect the OOD detection
206   performance. We measure the semantic textual similarity between li and every similar label d ∈ D(li )
207
      with cosine similarity in feature space, and selecting the labels with top-k similarities as the final set
      of similar classes. We denote the SimLabel score with prompting LLMs as SimLabel-L.

      3.1.3      S IMILAR CLASSES WITH IMAGE - TEXT ALIGNMENT
210
211   Selection of similar classes is to find labels whose ID samples have high affinity. The above two
212   selection methods are limited to the textual modality, where the sensitivity of the text encoder Miyai
213   et al. (2023) may result in the incorrect selection of similar classes. In this section, we introduce a
214   new strategy for selecting similar classes with consideration of ID image-text alignment. For each ID
215   image, we can first perform zero-shot visual classification to assign it to a pseudo ID class and select
      the labels with top similarities as similar classes.

      4.1     E XPERIMENT SETUP

      Datasets and benchmarks. We evaluate our method on the ImageNet-1k OOD benchmark Huang
293   et al. (2021) and primarily compare it with the MCM method Ming et al. (2022) due to its promising
294   and consistent performance in the zero-shot OOD detection task. The ImageNet-1k OOD benchmark
295   is a widely used performance validation method that uses the large-scale visual dataset ImageNet-1k
296   as ID data and iNaturalist Horn et al. (2018), SUN Xiao et al. (2010), Places Zhou et al. (2016), and
297   Texture Cimpoi et al. (2013) as OOD data, covering a diverse range of scenes and semantics. Each
298   OOD dataset has no classes that overlap with the ID dataset.
299   Implement details. In our experiments, we adopt CLIP Radford et al. (2021) as the target pre-trained
300   model, which is one of the most popular and publicly available VLMs. Note that our method is
301   not limited in CLIP; it can be applicable to other vision-language pre-trained models that enable
302   multi-modal feature alignment. Our experiments are primarily conducted using the CLIP-B/16
303   model, which consists of a ViT-B/16 Transformer as the image encoder and a masked self-attention
304   Transformer Vaswani et al. (2017) as the text encoder. For selecting similar classes in SimLabel-H,
305   we follow the construction of hierarchical label sets in Novack et al. (2023) to obtain accurate super-
306   classes for the ImageNet labels and generate similar classes. The LLMs we prompt for SimLabel-L is
307
      GPT-4 Achiam et al. (2023). In generating similar classes for SimLabel-L and SimLabel-I score, we
      select the quantity of similar classes k = 6. Additionally, following the theoretical analysis and setting
308
      in Ming et al. (2022), we set temperature τ = 1. We set the weight of image & similar-classes-label
309
      α = 1. All experiments are conducted on a single NVIDIA 4090 GPU.
310
311   Metric. For evaluation, we mainly use two metrics: (1) the false positive rate (FPR@95) of OOD
312   samples when the true positive rate of in-distribution samples is 95%.(2) the area under the receiver
313
      operating characteristic curve (AUROC).

455
456   Our method, namely the SimLabel score, assumes that when measuring similarity between ID samples
457   and ID labels set, other ID labels also show high similarity beyond the ground truth class label li .
458   Ideally, the affinity with only the image & similar-classes-label should still be representative of an
459   image-class pairing even without the class label li . We verify this assumption by reformulating the
460   image-class P prototype similarity in Eq. 5 to include only image and similar-classes-label matching:
461   A(x, li ) =      d∈D(li ) M(x, d)/|D(li )|. If the assumption does not hold, the similarity between
462   the ID image and other ID labels would be identical, rendering the design of image-class affinity
463   meaningless.
464   Here, we use this affinity to conduct OOD detection with similar classes generated for SimLabel-I
465   on ImageNet benchmark. The results are shown in Table 7 (denoted as SimLabel-S). Although its
466   performance is not as good as SimLabel-I, its OOD performance still demonstrates the ability to
467   represent a class prototype without a class label. The result verifies the assumption in Fig. 1 and
468   provides strong support for the design of SimLabel.

471   5.3   L IMITATION AND FUTURE WORK
472
473   The proposed method relies on a “similar class pool” to decide robust OOD score. One of the
474   basic requirements of our method is that the distribution of ID class should be relatively uniform, or
475   balanced. For the long-tailed scenarios, it is harder to select informative similar classes for the tail
476   classes than for the head ones, posing challenges for the proposed consistency-guided OOD detection
477
      method. Thus, the non-uniform quantity of class-wise similar classes hinders the measurement of
      image-class affinity in terms of consistency, explaining the failure cases when using SimLabel-H
478
      on zero-shot OOD detection. One potential solution is extending the tail class by introducing extra
479
      sibling or child classes. Notably, in expanding the classes pool, a well-designed classes selection is
480   needed for its potential problem of bring noiseDai et al. (2023) explaining the failure of SimLabel-L.
481   Another limitation of our work stems from the design of image & similar-classes-label similarity
482   in Eq. 4 where we assume the equal contribution for each similar class d ∈ D(li ). In real-world
483   scenarios, the semantic similarity between two classes varies so the uniform weights on every similar
484   class result in the limited utilization of semantic information across the similar classes. An extension
485   of Eq. 4 can be investigated by accurately measuring class-wise similarity within the similar class
      pool.

      6    R ELATED WORKS
497
498
499   Out-of-Distribution Detection. Conventionally, the objective of OOD detection is to derive a binary
500   ID-OOD classifier to detect OOD images within the test dataset and designing the OOD score is
501   one of the most important tasks in OOD detection. The design of OOD score can be divided into
502   three main categories: probability-based, logit-based, and feature-based. MSP Hendrycks & Gimpel
503   (2016) uses the maximum predicted probability as the score and Liang et al. (2020) aims to get rid of
504
      the over-confidence through perturbing the inputs and re-scaling the logits. MaxLogits Hendrycks
      et al. (2022) utilizes the maximum of logits as the score and Energy Liu et al. (2021) defines the
505
      energy-function as the OOD score. ReAct Sun et al. (2021) and DICE Sun & Li (2022) further
506
      investigate the improvement of energy score through the feature clipping and discarding. For the
507   feature-based method, Lee Lee et al. (2018) propose the score via the measurement of minimum
508   Mahalanobis distance between the feature and the class-wise centroids as the OOD score. KNN Sun
509   et al. (2022) investigates the effectiveness of non-parametric nearest-neighbor distance for detecting
510   OOD samples.
511
      OOD Detection with Vision-Language Representations. With the rise of large-scale pre-trained
512
      VLMs, there are various works focusing on utilizing textual information for visual OOD detection.
513   Fort et al.Fort et al. (2021) firstly propose the utilization of VLM for OOD detection through the
514   generation of the candidate OOD labels. MCM Ming et al. (2022) is a conventional post-hoc zero-shot
515   method that uses the maximum predicted softmax value as the OOD score for OOD detection. Based
516   on MCM, NPOS Tao et al. (2023) conducts the OOD data synthesis and fine-tunes the image encoder
517   to find a decision boundary. Dai et al. Dai et al. (2023) introduce class-wise attributes to enhance
518   the confidence score between ID images and labels while overlooking the semantic information
519   among different ID labels. CLIPEN Dai et al. (2023) introduces the positive and negation-semantic
520   prompts in separating ID and OOD domain and NegLabel Jiang et al. (2024) introduces external
521
      negative labels in enhance ID/OOD separation. Both CLIPEN and NegLabel utilize the external text
      knowledge in boosting OOD detection tasks, while our method aims in building a more reasonable
522
      and robustness ID image-text pairing. Our method, SimLabel, detecting samples through measuring
523
      consistency between images and similar classes, enables the model to build affinity from images to
524   various ID labels in an interpretable and robust manner.

530
531
      This paper introduces a simple and effective post-hoc method for multi-modal zero-shot OOD
      detection called SimLabel. It generates a set of class-wise similar labels that exhibit semantic
532
      relation to each class. Different from the naive textual prompt construction strategy in the existing
533
      VLMs-based solutions that decide OOD score based only on similarity to one ID label, we decide
534   OOD based on similarity to a group of similar classes. Our basic assumption is that an ID sample
535   should consistently have high similarity scores across similar ID classes, leading to the proposed
536   consistency-guided OOD detection. Particularly, the proposed method determines whether an image
537   is ID or OOD by measuring and comparing its affinity towards ID labels and corresponding similar
538   classes, where we provide three different strategies to select high-quality similar classes, namely
539   selecting similar classes through text hierarchy, prompting LLMs and pseudo-image-text pairing.
      Extensive experiments on various OOD detection tasks demonstrate the effectiveness of our method.

803
804   SimLabel Proposes a Novel, Straightforward Method in OOD Detection In our work, we propose
805   a straightforward idea for the task of OOD detection: an image that is correctly correlated with one
806   class should also show high similarity to similar classes. Based on this simple motivation, we propose
807   several direct and comprehensive approaches for selecting similar classes to use in OOD detection
808   tasks. The effectiveness of our method, SimLabel-I, stemming from the potential of CLIP’s text
809   comprehension ability, has been verified in various experiments as shown in the experiments section
      and general rebuttal section.

      SimLabel Offers a New Direction for Enhancing ID/OOD Separation In enhancing ID/OOD
811   separation through modeling ID image-class labels, existing works Wang et al. (2023); Dai et al.
812   (2023) either focus on a single class prototype or build an image-whole ID label setJiang et al.
813   (2024). NegLabelJiang et al. (2024) introduces a new score utilizing synthesized negative text labels,
814   suggesting that ID images should have higher affinity to ID text labels while showing low affinity
815   to other texts. Building the similarity between ID images and the full-label space, low-semantic
816   correlation may hinder understanding Jiang et al. (2024) because ID labels may vary significantly, as
817   seen in the ImageNet-1K dataset.
818   Our paper proposes a novel and potential direction for building ID image-label prototypes, suggesting
819   that ID images should show high similarity to similar labels, acting in a more interpretable way
820   compared to Jiang et al. (2024). Therefore, our idea can be a further direction to cooperate with
821   existing works such as CLIPEN or NegLabel, providing a more interpretable affinity between ID
822   images and various label pools.
823   SimLabel Works as a Novel Uncertainty Estimation Method Our main method (SimLabel-I)
824   introduces a novel strategy for uncertainty estimation over ID class prototypes and enhances ID/OOD
825   separation through connections among ID classes.Thus, our main comparing baseline is MCM Ming
