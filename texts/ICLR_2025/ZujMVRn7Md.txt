                                                  A BSTRACT
011
012
               Accurately identifying the underlying physical laws in complex systems is vital
013
               for effective control and interpretation. However, many systems are governed
014
               by a combination of known physical principles and unobservable or poorly un-
015            derstood components. Traditional model-based methods like Kalman filters and
016            state-space models often rely on oversimplified assumptions, while modern data-
017            driven approaches, such as physics-informed neural networks (PINNs), can suffer
018            from overfitting or lack theoretical guarantees in recovering true physical dynam-
019            ics. We propose the Orthogonal Deep Neural Network (ODNN) architecture to ad-
020            dress these limitations. ODNN disentangles known physical components from un-
021            observable or poorly understood components by imposing orthogonal constraints
022            on the deep neural network. Unlike additive regularization methods, ODNN con-
023
               verts the physical constraints directly into the network structure, ensuring that
               the DNN focuses on capturing the unknown or complex dynamics without over-
024
               fitting. This novel approach leverages both explicit orthogonality (e.g., zero in-
025
               ner product) and implicit orthogonality (e.g., contrasting convexity, periodicity, or
026            symmetry) between physical laws and disturbance components. Theoretically, we
027            prove that ODNN provides strong guarantees for accurate system identification
028            under mild orthogonality assumptions, building on the universal approximation
029            theorem. Empirically, ODNN is evaluated across seven synthetic and real-world
030            datasets, showcasing its ability to recover governing physical equations with high
031            accuracy and interpretability. Our results demonstrate that ODNN offers signifi-
032            cant advantages in terms of generalizability and robustness, making it a valuable
033            framework for physics-based model identification in complex systems.

091   non-periodic growth patterns influenced by periodic environmental factors (Egilmez et al., 2021).
092   In power grids, non-convex power flow equations coexist with quadratic, convex disturbance terms

100   push the constrained DNN to only capture the disturbance given the orthogonality condition. This
101   theoretical derivation offers a solid foundation for understanding and applying ODNN, as well as an
102   analysis tool for any learning algorithm in digital twin (Pattanaik & Mohanty, 2024) structure.

      Traditional System Identification Methods Classical system identification methods, such as
111   Kalman filters (Kwasniok, 2012), state-space modeling (Haber & Verhaegen, 2020), and Bayesian
112   inference (Huang et al., 2019), have been widely used for estimating physical systems’ dynamics.
113   These approaches have played a crucial role in early physics-based modeling by leveraging statisti-
114   cal properties like Gaussian noise to estimate system equations. Despite their success, such methods
115   often struggle in dealing with complex, nonlinear systems and unobservable dynamics, limiting
116   their applicability to real-world scenarios (Pillonetto et al., 2025). For instance, in power systems,
117   disturbances like unmeasured third-party devices (Singh, 2021) introduce challenges that classical
118   methods are not equipped to handle. Consequently, these approaches need to be supplemented or
119
      replaced by more flexible data-driven methods.
120   Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) Recent advances have introduced Physics-Informed
121   Neural Networks (PINNs) (Stiasny et al., 2021), which embed physical laws directly into neural net-
122   work architectures. PINNs offer improved data efficiency and interpretability by integrating known
123   differential equations into their training processes. For instance, in the study of low-inertia systems
124   in power grids, PINNs have been used to model frequency dynamics in the presence of significant
125
      nonlinearities and limited data (Nagel & Huber, 2022). However, while PINNs excel in situations
      where physical laws are well understood, they fall short when unobservable dynamics or unknown
126
      system components are present. These limitations highlight the need for models that can balance
127
      physical constraints with the discovery of unobservable dynamics.
128
129   Neural-Symbolic Integration and Sparse System Identification Sparse system identification
130   methods, such as Sparse Identification of Nonlinear Dynamical Systems (SINDy) (Brunton et al.,
131
      2016) and symbolic regression techniques (Chen et al., 2021; Quade et al., 2016), aim to discover
      governing equations from data while minimizing the number of terms required. These approaches
132
      are powerful in their ability to capture essential dynamics but are often hampered by noise and
133
      data scarcity. Neural-symbolic integration methods have emerged to address these challenges by
134   combining the expressiveness of deep neural networks with the interpretability of symbolic repre-
135   sentations (Tian et al., 2021). While symbolic methods like sparse regression work well for simple
136   systems, they struggle in complex, nonlinear scenarios, necessitating approaches like ours, which
137   are designed to disentangle physical and non-physical components explicitly.
138
      Orthogonality and Disentanglement in Deep Learning Disentangling physical components from
139
      non-physical disturbances is critical for accurate system identification and generalization. In deep
140   learning, orthogonality has been widely adopted to disentangle factors of variation, both in latent
141   space and network parameters (Wang et al., 2020a). Methods like Variational Autoencoders (VAEs)
142   (Kingma, 2013) and orthogonalization of network weights aim to reduce redundancy and improve
143   the interpretability of learned representations. However, these approaches mainly focus on disen-
144   tangling features for representation learning and do not address the explicit separation of physical
145   dynamics from disturbances. Our work builds on this by introducing Orthogonal Deep Neural Net-
146   works (ODNNs), which embed orthogonality constraints tailored for identifying physical parameters
147   in complex, partially observable systems. This approach mitigates overfitting and enhances gener-
148
      alization by ensuring that physical equations are disentangled from disturbances.

194   3.3   O RTHOGONAL D EEP N EURAL N ETWORK FOR G UARANTEED P HYSICS I DENTIFICATION
195
      To overcome the overfitting issue inherent in regular DNNs, we propose the Orthogonal Deep Neu-
196
      ral Network (ODNN) framework. ODNN is designed to enforce a disentanglement between the
197
      known physical dynamics and unstructured disturbances by constraining the DNN to operate in an
198   orthogonal space relative to the physical components. The term “unstructured” is used to suggest
199   that while the form of g(x) is not known, certain properties of g(x) can be inferred to enable our
200   disentanglement of f (x) from g(x). This approach also aligns with the broader framework of disen-
201   tangled representation learning, which seeks to develop representations that separate a system into
202   independent components for improved interpretation and control (Wang et al., 2022).
203
      Motivation. Our idea is motivated by the following reasoning. Traditionally, one common method
204   to mitigate the overfitting issue of regular DNNs is to add a regularization term to the loss func-
205   tion. This approach has led to the development of various techniques within the domain of Physics-
206   Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) (Kaheman et al., 2020), which incorporate physics-based con-
207   straints by adding penalty terms to the objective function. However, while these methods provide a
208   useful way to include domain knowledge, they often lack formal guarantees for accurate parameter
209   estimation as they rely on additive regularization with hyperparameter tuning. For a guaranteed es-
210   timation, we propose a novel form of regularization: rather than adding penalty terms, we directly
211   constrain the representational capacity of the DNN. By doing so, we ensure that the DNN’s univer-
212
      sal approximation power is focused solely on the disturbance components, eventually disentangling
      the physics from disturbance. This constrained approach prevents overfitting and provides a more
213
      principled and theoretically sound solution for accurate system identification.
214
215   In order to formalize the requirements for effective disentanglement, we introduce Assumption 1,
      which outlines the conditions necessary for ODNN to separate physical and disturbance components.

224   desired precision, while at the same time being unable to approximate the physical component f (x).

      From theory to practice. A natural question that arises is: what characteristics must a constrained
      DNN have to satisfy Assumption 1? Intuitively, it can be achieved by ensuring that the output space
237
      of the DNN is orthogonal to the functional basis of the physical components. The core idea here
238
      is to constrain the DNN to operate within a subspace that is orthogonal to f (x). This allows for
239   a natural separation between the known physics and the disturbances. This design fundamentally
240   differentiates ODNN from traditional approaches, such as those in PINNs, which use additive regu-
241   larizations rather than modifying the architecture itself.
242
      To illustrate, consider an example from reinforcement learning in the Humanoid Standup environ-
243
      ment (Brockman, 2016). In this scenario, an agent is rewarded for upward movement, which is cap-
244
      tured by the true reward function f (x). However, in practice, the reward signal is often corrupted by
245   disturbances g(x) arising from sensor drift, calibration errors, adversarial manipulation, or ground
246   reaction force variability. Typical RL algorithms, such as Proximal Policy Optimization (Schulman
247   et al., 2017) or Soft Actor-Critic (Haarnoja et al., 2018), assume that the reward signal is clean and
248   reliable. When misleading factors are introduced, these algorithms struggle to distinguish between
249   meaningful reward contributions and misleading signals, often resulting in suboptimal policies or
250   convergence to incorrect behaviors. To handle these disturbances, ODNN is designed to disentan-
251   gle the disturbances from the true reward signal. Since the true reward for upward movement is
252   expected to be a monotonic function, the disturbance g(x), which may introduce non-monotonic or
253
      oscillatory behavior, must be captured by a network constrained to avoid monotonic patterns. By
      designing a DNN that operates “orthogonally” to the monotonic characteristics of g(x), we ensure
254
      that only the disturbances are captured, leaving the true reward function unaffected. This allows the
255
      agent to learn from a clean reward signal that accurately reflects its desired upward movement.
256
257   Based on this intuition, we name our framework the Orthogonal Deep Neural Network (ODNN),
258   emphasizing its use of orthogonality to disentangle the components of the system.

299
      that rely heavily on specific patterns learned from training data, ODNN is a self-supervised learn-
      ing framework designed to generalize effectively to real-world scenarios. Many existing models,
300
      such as DCCRN (Hu et al., 2020) and Demucs (Défossez, 2021) for audio enhancement, perform
301   well under controlled conditions. However, they could struggle with real-time disturbances that do
302   not match training conditions, leading to issues with overfitting and poor adaptability. ODNN, by
303   contrast, utilizes a self-supervised learning framework that requires only knowledge of the underly-
304   ing physical equations and assumes an orthogonality condition between the physical system and the
305   disturbances. These requirements are reasonable and applicable in many practical settings.

      5   C ONCLUSION
516
517
      This paper presents a novel approach to system identification that integrates orthogonality and dis-
518   entanglement into deep neural networks, enabling accurate recovery of physical equations while
519   handling unobservable disturbances. Unlike traditional physics-informed models, which often over-
520   fit to known physical laws, our Orthogonal Deep Neural Network (ODNN) framework introduces
521   explicit constraints that ensure the separation of physical dynamics from non-physical disturbances.
522   By leveraging orthogonality properties within the network architecture, we demonstrate improved
523   system interpretability, reduced overfitting, and enhanced generalizability. Through comparative
524   analysis with state-of-the-art methods—including classical system identification, physics-informed
525   neural networks, and hybrid neural-symbolic models—our approach outperforms these techniques
526
      in scenarios where systems are governed by partially observable or unknown dynamics. Specifically,
      ODNN achieves a more robust and consistent disentanglement of physical and non-physical com-
527
      ponents across various domains, ranging from power systems to mechanical and biological systems.
528
529   Relationship to existing machine learning society. Our method contributes to the growing body
530   of research in self-supervised disentangled representation learning, which focuses on decomposing
531   systems into independent, interpretable components for improved control and understanding. While
532
      previous work in disentangled representation learning, such as (Tran et al., 2017) and (Wang et al.,
      2022), primarily relied on heuristic methods with limited formal guarantees, our approach advances
533
      the field by providing theoretical guarantees for accurate disentanglement. This is achieved through
534
      the use of orthogonality between known physical laws and unstructured disturbances. Furthermore,
535   our approach operates in a self-supervised setting, requiring only known physical equations, making
536   it robust for real-world applications without needing explicit label information.

      A    S PECIFIC I LLUSTRATIONS OF I MPLICIT O RTHOGONALITY C ASE
811
812
      Besides explicit orthogonality, we recognize another category termed implicit orthogonality. This
813   scenario represents many engineering systems where g(·) and f (·) exhibit contrasting properties in
814   a more qualitative sense. These contrasting properties include pairs such as convexity versus non-
815   convexity, periodicity versus non-periodicity, symmetry versus asymmetry, and monotonicity versus
816   non-monotonicity.
817
      For instance, physical systems typically exhibit complex, non-convex dynamics, whereas distur-
818
      bances are often well-approximated by convex functions (Astolfi et al., 2021). Disturbances also
819   tend to include periodic components, such as oscillatory noise or seasonal variations (e.g., periodic
820   excitations in damping systems), while physical behaviors are frequently non-periodic (Spitas et al.,
821   2020). Similarly, physical systems are often asymmetric due to material properties or boundary
822   conditions, whereas disturbances generally exhibit symmetric characteristics. Furthermore, while
823   physical systems may involve complex, non-monotonic relationships, disturbances are often ap-
824   proximated effectively by monotonic functions.
825
826   • Convex v.s. Non-Convex. While physical systems often exhibit complex, non-convex behaviors,
827
        the characteristics of disturbances frequently lend themselves to approximation by convex func-
        tions (Astolfi et al., 2021). For instance, disturbances can often be simplified as square functions
828
        (Astolfi et al., 2021). The sensor noise in power systems are often modeled as Gaussian variables,
829
        whose square are then utilized in security analysis (Xiao et al., 2024). To capture exclusively
830     convex functions, we leverage the off-the-shelf input convex neural network (ICNN) architecture
831     (Amos et al., 2017), which has demonstrated significant success in various inference tasks related
832     to convex optimization. ICNNs are designed to output convex functions by imposing non-negative
833     constraints on network weights. As demonstrated in (Chen et al., 2020), ICNNs satisfy the uni-
834     versal approximation theorem for convex functions, ensuring they adhere to the Assumption 1.
835   • Periodic v.s. Non-Periodic. Disturbances frequently contain periodic components, while physical
836     systems often exhibit non-periodic behaviors. For instance, oscillatory noise, such as periodic
837     excitation in the damping system or seasonal variations in environmental factors, can be effectively
838     modeled using sinusoidal functions (Spitas et al., 2020). To exclusively capture periodic functions,
839     we employ Hopfield networks (Deng et al., 2024), capable of exhibiting periodic attractor states
840     through weight sharing. These networks produce periodic outputs upon convergence.
841   • Symmetric v.s. Asymmetric. While physical systems often exhibit asymmetric behaviors due to
842     factors such as material properties or boundary conditions, disturbances frequently possess sym-
843     metric characteristics. For example, random noise, a common disturbance, is often symmetrically
844     distributed around zero. Symmetric functions, such as Gaussian distributions, are commonly used
845     to model these types of disturbances. To capture exclusively symmetric functions, we leverage
846
        the off-the-shelf Siamese networks (Ilina et al., 2022). By adapting this architecture to induce
        symmetry, we aim to create a model that specifically captures symmetric disturbances g(·).
847
848   • Monotonic v.s. Non-Monotonic. While physical systems often exhibit complex and non-
849     monotonic relationships between variables, disturbances can frequently be approximated by
850
        monotonic functions. For instance, gradual changes in environmental conditions or systematic
        measurement errors might introduce monotonic trends into the data. To exclusively capture sym-
851
        metric functions, we leverage Deep Lattice Networks (You et al., 2017; Yanagisawa et al., 2022),
852
        which enforce monotonicity w.r.t. specified inputs through alternating layers of linear embeddings
853     and lattice ensembles.

1193   experiment, the oscillator is initialized with an unknown velocity, and the simulated dataset is

1195   mx′′ (t) + cx′ (t) + kx(t) = 0, where x(t) is the displacement at time t, m is the mass, c is the
1196   damping coefficient, and k is the spring constant. The dataset provides the parameters m, c, and
1197   k, which satisfy the underdamped condition (c2 < 4mk). Under this condition, the displacement
                                                                                               q

                                                                                      and ω = m    k

1200                                ′

       C.6    WATERMARK R EMOVAL IN I MAGE P ROCESSING
1243
1244   For the watermark removal experiment, we use images from ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009) with
1245   human-embedded watermarks. The watermark, visually perceptible and symbolically interpretable,
1246   serves as the target signal f (·) to be identified, while the host image is treated as the disturbance g(·).
1247   The objective is to accurately identify and remove the watermark while preserving the integrity of
1248   the host image. Users were given two options for specifying the watermark content: direct text
1249   input or manual area selection. This information was used to generate a reference watermark image,
1250
       serving as the symbolic target f (·) for our ODNN model, as illustrated in Figure 13.
1251   Watermark removal is traditionally challenging due to the significant overlap between watermark
1252   features and underlying image content, which often makes conventional methods prone to either
1253   over-removing host content or leaving residual watermark traces. Our ODNN approach is designed
1254   to overcome these challenges by leveraging orthogonality. Specifically, watermarks and host images
1255   tend to exhibit different frequency characteristics: watermarks are often designed with repetitive pat-
1256
       terns or high-contrast features that manifest prominently in specific frequency bands, whereas natu-
       ral images typically contain broader, non-repetitive frequency content (Gonzalez & Woods, 2008).
1257
       This approximate orthogonality allows ODNN to effectively disentangle the watermark from the
1258
       underlying image.
1259
1260   Experimental results demonstrate that ODNN can achieve high accuracy in identifying and remov-
1261   ing watermarks, preserving the underlying image’s quality even in complex cases. Moreover, our
1262
       method is computationally efficient, allowing for real-time applications. The success of ODNN
       in watermark removal showcases its broader applicability to tasks involving symbolic versus non-
1263
       symbolic content separation, which aligns well with ICLR’s interest in advancing representation
1264
       learning methods that incorporate disentanglement and interpretability.

1352   In this section, we describe our experiments conducted on a simulated heat transfer dataset to eval-
1353   uate the performance of the proposed ODNN method for identifying true heat sources in a noisy 2D
1354   temperature distribution. We consider two heat source cases in our experiments. Case f. It repre-
1355   sents the true heat transfer system, generated by four heat sources located symmetrically along the
1356   edges of the domain: the left center, upper center, right center, and bottom center. The resulting tem-
1357   perature distribution from Case f is symmetric about both axes, forming a consistent and balanced
1358
       heat map. The temperature at any point (x, y, t) in Case f can be represented by:
1359                                         X4

1360                          Tf (x, y, t) =          exp −

1363   of the four symmetric sources:
1364
1365          • (x1 , y1 ) = (0, H/2): Located at the left center edge of the domain.
1366          • (x2 , y2 ) = (L/2, H): Positioned at the upper center edge of the domain.
1367
              • (x3 , y3 ) = (L, H/2): Located at the right center edge of the domain.
1368
1369          • (x4 , y4 ) = (L/2, 0): Positioned at the bottom center edge of the domain.

       Here, L and H represent the length and height of the rectangular domain, respectively. The choice
1371

1372   the four edge lines, resulting in an inherently symmetric temperature distribution across the domain.
1373   α is the thermal diffusivity. The symmetry of Case f implies that the contributions from the four
1374   sources are balanced and reflected across both axes.

1387   The goal of our experiment is to determine whether the proposed ODNN method can accurately
1388   disentangle the contributions of the true sources (Case f) from the noisy influence introduced by Case
1389   g, and subsequently reconstruct the 2D temperature heatmap. An essential aspect of this experiment
1390   is the observation that the heat transfer equation for Case f is inherently symmetric, while the heat
1391   transfer from Case g, being a single point source in the upper-left corner, is asymmetric. This
1392   difference creates an implicit orthogonality between the symmetric and asymmetric components of
1393   the temperature distribution, which our ODNN approach exploits to disentangle the physics-based
1394
       temperature distribution from the noise. By leveraging this implicit orthogonality, ODNN is capable
       of identifying the structural symmetries in the observed data, allowing it to accurately separate the
1395
       true heat distribution from the noise contribution.

1460   This subsection describes the experiment on Biology and Pendulum Datasets. Driven pendulum
1461   dataset. This system represents a classic mechanical system characterized by oscillatory motion.
                                                                                          f
1462   The movement θ(t) of driven pendulum can be expressed as θ(t) = θ0 cos(ωt)+ 2ω       t sin(ωt), where
1463   ω is the oscillator frequency and f the unknown driven force. These two functions exhibit the “con-
1464   trasting property” in terms of symmetry. We simulate this dataset using the underlying equations of
1465   pendulum motion. Biology growth dataset. This system describes bacterial population dynamics
1466   and represents a canonical population growth model. Following the Aiba-Edward model (Muloiwa
                                                                                  S
1467
       et al., 2020), the population over time µ(t) can be modeled as µ(t) = S+K     S
                                                                                       (exp KSS + cos(2πt))
1468   where KS is the half saturation constant and S is the unknown substrate concentration. This model
1469
       incorporates an underlying exponential growth component coupled with a sinusoidal disturbance
       simulating daily temperature fluctuations, thus satisfying the “contrasting property” requirement as
1470
       periodicity. We simulate the time-series data using an Escherichia coli dataset following studies in
1471
       (Paula et al., 2020; Aida et al., 2022).
1472
1473   In Figure 16, we present the identified physical equations for both the training and testing datasets, il-
1474   lustrating the superior generalization capability of the proposed ODNN compared to a regular DNN.
1475
       This enhanced generalization is crucial for effective system control and robust decision-making in
       complex environments. Specifically, ODNN accurately identifies the physical dynamics even in the
1476
       testing set, demonstrating its ability to learn and generalize the underlying physics beyond the train-

       C.10   E XTENDING TO M ULTIPLICATIVE DYNAMICS : P OPULATION G ROWTH C ASE S TUDY
1513
1514   In addition to the additive setting F (x) = f (x) + g(x), this section explores the multiplicative
1515   setting F (x) = f (x) · g(x) and demonstrates how the proposed ODNN method can adapt to such
1516   scenarios. We consider  a synthetic time-series dataset modeling population growth as: F (t) =
1517   (1+r)t · 1 + 21 sin(k0 t)  where (1+r)t represents the known physics basis function of exponential
1518   growth, 1 + 21 sin(k0 t) models seasonal effects on population growth with k0 as the frequency
1519   parameter, and t is the time. In this model, r is the unknown growth rate we aim to learn. This
1520   synthetic case captures characteristics of many real-world population growth scenarios, such as those
1521   influenced by both exponential trends and periodic fluctuations.
1522   To handle the multiplicative setting, we apply a logarithmic transformation to convert it into an
1523   additive form:
1524                                                                    1
                         F̄ (t) = log F (t) = t · log(1 + r) + log 1 + sin(k0 t) .
1525                                                                    2
1526   The transformed data F̄ (t) is then fed into the ODNN model, where:
1527
1528          • f (t) = t represents the linear term associated with the growth rate,
1529
              • g(t) = log 1 + 21 sin(k0 t) encodes the seasonal fluctuations and is distinct from f (t) due
1530            to its periodic nature.
1531
1532   By leveraging the separability of f (t) and g(t) in terms of their periodic and non-periodic charac-
1533   teristics, ODNN is able to disentangle the contributions of exponential growth and seasonal effects,
1534   enabling accurate recovery of the unknown growth rate r. The learning trajectory of r is illustrated
1535   in Figure 17. The results demonstrate that ODNN successfully converges to the true value of r,
1536   highlighting its capability to adapt to more complex scenarios beyond the additive setting. This
1537
       showcases the flexibility and robustness of ODNN in handling multiplicative dynamics, effectively
       disentangling the underlying components even in challenging settings.

       D    L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE D IRECTIONS
1567
1568
       While the ODNN framework demonstrates strong performance in disentangling physical compo-
1569   nents from disturbances and achieving accurate system identification, several potential limitations
1570   warrant further exploration. One key limitation is the reliance on an implicit or explicit assump-
1571   tion of orthogonality between physical dynamics and disturbance components. In complex systems
1572   where these conditions are not well-defined or where orthogonality is not easily identifiable, the
1573   performance of ODNN may be constrained. Additionally, the need for careful architectural design
1574   to ensure orthogonality might limit scalability when extending ODNN to more diverse or higher-
1575   dimensional systems. Another consideration is the computational cost associated with training con-
1576   strained DNN architectures, which may be higher compared to unconstrained models, particularly
1577
       for large-scale problems. Future research could focus on relaxing the orthogonality requirements,
       making the method applicable to a broader class of systems, as well as improving computational
1578
       efficiency through more advanced optimization techniques. Exploring adaptive mechanisms to au-
1579
       tomatically identify suitable constraints could further enhance the applicability and robustness of
1580   ODNN across different real-world domains.
