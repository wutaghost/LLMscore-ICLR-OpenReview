011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent advances in text-to-image (T2I) diffusion models have significantly im-
014            proved the quality of generated images. However, providing efficient control over
015            individual subjects, particularly the attributes characterizing them, remains a key
016
               challenge. While existing methods have introduced mechanisms to modulate at-
               tribute expression, they typically provide either detailed, object-specific localiza-
017
               tion of such a modification or fine-grained, nuanced control of attributes. No cur-
018
               rent approach offers both simultaneously, resulting in a gap when trying to achieve
019            precise continuous and subject-specific attribute modulation in image generation.
020            In this work, we demonstrate that token-level directions exist within commonly
021            used CLIP text embeddings that enable fine-grained, subject-specific control of
022            high-level attributes in T2I models. We introduce two methods to identify these di-
023            rections: a simple, optimization-free technique and a learning-based approach that
024            utilizes the T2I model to characterize semantic concepts more specifically. Our
025            methods allow the augmentation of the prompt text input, enabling fine-grained
026            control over multiple attributes of individual subjects simultaneously, without re-
027
               quiring any modifications to the diffusion model itself. This approach offers a
               unified solution that fills the gap between global and localized control, providing
028
               competitive flexibility and precision in text-guided image generation.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Text-to-image (T2I) diffusion models have rapidly advanced, achieving remarkable quality in gener-
034   ating visually stunning images (Rombach et al., 2022; Imagen-Team, 2024). However, as the quality
035   of generated images improves, the need for precise control over the generation process becomes in-
036   creasingly crucial. This control should extend beyond simply adjusting what is depicted in the scene.
037   It must also provide nuanced control of the attributes describing how these objects are characterized.
038   Attributes, such as a person’s age, are not binary or static – they often span a continuum, requiring
039   models to capture fine-grained variations to produce results that align with user intent.
040   Currently, a fundamental gap exists: no method provides fine-grained modulation and subject-
041   specific localization simultaneously. Recent works like Prompt-to-Prompt (P2P) (Hertz et al., 2023)
042   and Concept Sliders (Gandikota et al., 2024) have made significant strides in introducing control into
043   T2I models. P2P enables localized expression changes, allowing adjustments to specific aspects of
044   a given image based on text modifications, while Concept Sliders facilitate fine-grained modulation
045   over global attributes across all subject instances. This limitation means that while we can tweak at-
046
      tributes globally or localize changes to subjects, we still lack a unified, generalized approach capable
      of concurrently achieving fine-grained control for both aspects.
047
048   This work aims to bridge this gap by introducing a method that enables unified, subject-specific, fine-
049   grained control over attributes within T2I diffusion models. Unlike existing methods that provide
050   either localized coarse control or global fine-grained control, our approach offers precise modulation
051   of attributes that can be directed at specific subjects within the generated image (see Figure 1). This
052
      results in an unprecedented level of intuitive control, allowing users to fine-tune not just what appears
      in an image but how it appears, down to the smallest level of attribute expression.

073   We summarize our main contributions as follows:
074
075
                  • We show that token-level edit directions exist within common CLIP embeddings, enabling
                    fine-grained control of subject-specific attributes, and show that diffusion models can ef-
076
                    fectively interpret these directions.
077
078               • We introduce a simple, optimization-free approach to identify attribute-specific directions
079
                    by contrasting text prompts that describe the desired attributes or concepts.
080               • We introduce a second, learning-based method that identifies more robust directions
081                 through backpropagation of high-level semantic concepts to the text embedding input, us-
082
                    ing a reconstruction loss objective.
083               • We show that these token-level edit directions enable fine-grained, subject-specific, com-
084                 positional control of attributes and concepts in generated images.

      2       R ELATED W ORK

088
      The rapid advancements in generative models for image and video synthesis, particularly diffusion
089   models like Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022), have spurred efforts to develop techniques
090   for fine-grained editing and control of specific attributes in generated content. Our work focuses
091   on enabling precise, subject-specific control in images by targeting individual characteristics in a
092   controlled and continuous manner.
093
      Existing methods for controlled generation and image editing can be broadly categorized based on
094
      the underlying generative models – primarily Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) (Goodfel-
095   low et al., 2014) and Diffusion Models (Ho et al., 2020) –, and the mechanisms they use for control
096   – typically latent space manipulations or textual descriptions.
097
098   T2I Diffusion Model Preliminaries T2I Diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022; Podell et al.,
099   2024) simulate a reverse diffusion process pθ (x0:T |P ) that enables sampling from the distribution
100   of images pθ (x0 |P ) given a text conditioning P and a Gaussian noise sample xT . They iteratively
101   denoise xT using a diffusion model ϵ̂θ (xt |P, t). This is typically done by learning to predict the
102   noise content ϵ in the sample xt = αt x0 + σt ϵ using the following loss function:

      2.1   GAN- BASED I MAGE E DITING AND CLIP-BASED DIRECTIONS
109
110   GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2014; Radford et al., 2016), particularly StyleGANs (Karras et al., 2019),
111   are popular for image editing due to their generative power and disentangled latent space. Methods
112   like InterFaceGAN (Shen et al., 2020) manipulate attributes by identifying latent space directions.
113   Approaches such as StyleCLIP (Patashnik et al., 2021), CLIP2StyleGAN (Abdal et al., 2022), and
114   TediGAN (Xia et al., 2021) use CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) for text-based guidance in latent space
115   editing. Despite these advancements, these methods inherit the limitations of StyleGAN and struggle
116
      to generalize to complex, multi-subject images.
117
118   2.2   S TEERING THE G ENERATION P ROCESS OF D IFFUSION M ODELS
119
      Direction-based Control Similar to GAN-based editing, approaches like DiffusionCLIP (Kim
120
      et al., 2022) use CLIP for editing with unconditional closed-domain diffusion models. Recent meth-
121
      ods, such as Asyrp (Kwon et al., 2023), InterpretDiffusion (Li et al., 2024a), LFM (Hu et al., 2024),
122   and BoundaryDiffusion (Zhu et al., 2023), modulate learned directions in the diffusion backbone
123   or noise space, similar to StyleGAN. Concept Sliders (Gandikota et al., 2024) achieve disentangled
124   attribute modulation by training attribute-specific LoRAs (Hu et al., 2022), however these methods
125   typically lack subject specificity, as they perform global modulations . Mask-based approaches like
126   MAG (Mao et al., 2023) allow more targeted control but require significant user input to define the
127   masks.
128
129   Attention Map-based Control Building on the observation by Hertz et al. (2023) that fixing at-
130   tention maps during generation while changing the text prompt enables generating variations of
131   images, a range of control methods utilizing this mechanism have been introduced. Methods like
132   Prompt-to-Prompt (Hertz et al., 2023), MasaCtrl (Cao et al., 2023), AdapEdit (Ma et al., 2024), and
133
      many others (Brooks et al., 2023; Simsar et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024) leverage attention control
      combined with prompt editing to allow for subject-specific manipulations via text interfaces. These
134
      methods provide intuitive control and subject-specificity but suffer from the inherent discreteness of
135
      text inputs and struggle with fine-grained control over the magnitude of changes.
136
137
      From Controlled Generation to Editing For editing real images, inversion techniques are em-
138
      ployed to map images back into a model’s latent space. In GAN-based methods, Image2StyleGAN
139   (Abdal et al., 2019) and In-Domain GAN Inversion (Zhu et al., 2020) are commonly used. Similarly,
140   for diffusion models, DDIM Inversion (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021), Null-Text Inversion (Mokady
141   et al., 2023), and ReNoise (Garibi et al., 2024) enable mapping images to the latent noise space,
142   allowing editing of real images via re-generation with controlled generation methods.

      Let M denote the number of attributes we consider in our work and let N denote the number of
      subjects mentioned in a prompt P , A = {Ai | i ∈ J1, M K} denote the set of attributes Ai and
148
      SP = {Sj | j ∈ J1, N K} denote the set of subjects Sj mentioned in the prompt. We aim to influence
149
      the generation process to enable control over the expression expr(Ai ) of specific attributes Ai ∈ A
150   of specific subjects Sj ∈ SP . As an example, consider the prompt “a portrait of a man and woman
151   sharing a laugh”. If the man should be younger, one can change “man” to “young man”, but this
152   does not offer continuous control over how young the man is supposed to be. Instead, we aim to
153   provide the same subject-specificity that changing the prompt offers, but without the limitations
154   of the non-continuousness of language. Unlike previous works, we wish to provide control that is
155   simultaneously i) continuous, ii) subject-specific, and iii) does not require manual image masks or
156   reference images.
157   Our key observation is that the diffusion model’s interpretation of the tokenwise CLIP text embed-
158   ding vector e = ECLIP (P ) = (e<SOS> , e1 , . . . , ek , e<EOS> ), which is typically used to condition
159   the model, is locally smooth and enables subject-specific semantic modulations (Section 3.1). Using
160   this property, we can continuously modulate semantic attributes of specific subject instances in the
161   prompt P . To enable targeted modulation of specific attributes, we introduce methods to identify
      latent space directions corresponding to attributes A (e.g., “old”, “happy”, “expensive”).

178   3.1    I NTERPRETATION OF TOKENWISE CLIP T EXT E MBEDDINGS IN D IFFUSION M ODELS
179
180   Global v.s. Local Behavior Unlike the pooled text embedding space of CLIP (Radford et al.,
181
      2021) models, which has been explored extensively in previous works (Patashnik et al., 2021; Wang
      et al., 2023; Ramesh et al., 2022), the tokenwise text embedding space has not been investigated as
182
      much. Previous methods (Chefer et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024b; Wang et al., 2024) typically interpret
183
      this space globally, applying projections onto subspaces to decompose concepts or eliminate them
184   from the generated images. Conversely, we find two distinct local behaviors in the tokenwise CLIP
185   embedding space as interpreted by diffusion models (Podell et al., 2024). We can observe strong
186   local phase changes when interpolating between substantially different subjects (see Figure 2, top
187   row). Here minor changes in the embedding cause drastic changes in the generated images. At the
188   same time, the space shows smooth, semantically interpretable changes in the vicinity of the original
189   embeddings and when interpolating between similar subjects (see Figure 2, bottom row).
190
191   Subject-Specificity The CLIP tokenizer typically maps individual words to single tokens. Diffu-
192   sion models also directly attend to adjectives added to subjects in the prompt to determine details
193   of the subjects’ appearance (Hertz et al., 2023; Rassin et al., 2023). Despite this direct connection,
194
      additional information is also stored in other tokens, especially the following tokens describing the
      subject, and is interpreted by the diffusion model (Li et al., 2024b). Our key observation here is
195
      that we can exploit this semantic aggregation in the subject tokens to perform targeted interventions:
196
      modulating the token embedding e[Sj ] of a specific subject Sj primarily affects only that subject in
197   the generated image (see Figure 3), without the need for adding new tokens.
198
199   3.2    I DENTIFYING S EMANTIC D IRECTIONS FROM C ONTRASTIVE P ROMPTS
200
201   To use the key observations in Section 3.1 for subject-specific control, we have to identify which
202   directions enable modulating specific attributes. We previously found that interpolation of the to-
203   kenwise text embeddings leads to locally smooth changes around the original embeddings (c.f. Fig-
204   ure 2). Motivated by this finding, we propose identifying semantic directions in the tokenwise
205   embedding space by comparing embeddings of contrastive prompts.
206   Formally, given a target attribute Ai , defined via an adjective (e.g., “old”), we want to identify a
207   direction vector ∆eAi ∈ RdCLIP that can be added to the embedding of a target subject token e[Sj ]
208   to modulate the expression of that attribute exprSj (Ai ) in the generated image. To identify this
209   direction, we first obtain the tokenwise CLIP embeddings for two prompts: a neutral prompt P
210   describing a single subject S and a positive prompt P+ , which prepends the adjective to the subject.
211   Then, we compute the difference between the subject token embeddings e[S] :
212                                          ∆eAi = (ECLIP (P+ ) − ECLIP (P ))[S] .                                                  (2)
213
214   This directly yields a direction ∆eAi that captures the change induced by prepending the adjective
215   to the subject noun in the text prompt. To obtain more robust estimates of this direction, we average
      it over a multitude of prompt pairs which describe the same target attribute Ai .

243
      Although the simple difference-based method introduced in Section 3.2 is effective in many scenar-
      ios, it has several limitations. In practice, it often leads to unintended side effects (see Figure 4)
244
      and is limited to attributes Ai expressible as prefixes to the subject noun, due to the causality of
245
      the CLIP text encoder. To address these issues, we propose a substantially more robust approach
246   for identifying such directions. To obtain more robust directions, we use a T2I diffusion model to
247   identify associations of adjectives to directions in the tokenwise embedding space. This effectively
248   inverts the typical relation, where language models are used to augment the T2I model, such as
249   with prompt augmentation (Betker et al., 2023). We use the diffusion model to identify sample-
250   specific directions corresponding to modulations of the target attribute in the noise prediction space
251   and backpropagate them through the diffusion model to discover generalizable, fine-grained local
252   modulation directions ∆eAi within the tokenwise CLIP embedding space. Specifically, we aim to
253   apply the modulation and change the image similarly to adding an adjective to the prompt, but with-
254
      out adding additional tokens or affecting the rest of the embedding, and while enabling fine-grained
      modulations.
255

      Generalization We further investigate the generalizability of our method. Generally, any learned
501
      modulation direction ∆eAi will have only been trained on a closed set of nouns describing the target
502   subject S. To verify that they generalize beyond this set, we apply directions that have been trained
503   on a very small set of generic nouns (e.g., “person”, “woman”, and “man” for people) to more
504   specific nouns (see Appendix A.2). We find that our directions generalize to this setting as expected.
505   We also find that our learned modulation directions ∆eAi can generalize to other models that use
506   the same text encoders in a zero-shot manner. By learning a direction on one model, in this case,
507   SDXL (Podell et al., 2024), we can directly transfer it to models that use the same text encoders (see
508   Figure 10), such as SDXL Turbo (Sauer et al., 2023), or a subset of them, as with SD 1.5 (Rombach
509   et al., 2022) or the image+depth model LDM3D (Stan et al., 2023). Our learned directions even
510
      generalize to non-diffusion models such as aMUSEd (Patil et al., 2024).

512   5    C ONCLUSION
513
514   This work uncovers the powerful capabilities of the tokenwise CLIP Radford et al. (2021) text em-
515   bedding for exerting control over the image generation process in T2I diffusion models. Instead of
516   just acting as a discrete space of embeddings of words, we find that diffusion models are capable of
517   interpreting local deviations in the tokenwise CLIP text embedding space in semantically meaning-
518
      ful ways. We use this insight to augment the typically rather coarse prompt with fine-grained, con-
      tinuous control over the attribute expression of specific subjects by identifying semantic directions
519
      that correspond to specific attributes. Since we only modify the tokenwise CLIP text embedding
520
      along pre-identified directions, we enable more fine-grained manipulation at no additional cost in
521   the generation process.
522
523   Limitations and Future Work This work is a step towards revealing the hidden capabilities of the
524   text embedding input to common large-scale diffusion models and making them usable in straight-
525   forward ways. While our approach works for different off-the-shelf models without modifying them,
526   it is also inherently limited by their capabilities. Specifically, our method inherits the limitation that
527   diffusion models sometimes mix up attributes between different subjects. Complementary meth-
528   ods (Chefer et al., 2023; Rassin et al., 2023) reduce these problems substantially, and future work
529   could investigate their combination with our method in depth. Our approach also uses linear mod-
530   ulations along semantic directions in CLIP’s tokenwise embedding space. In GANs, where similar
531
      linear modulations are often used, previous works (Balakrishnan et al., 2022) found that more disen-
      tangled changes can be achieved using nonlinear modulations. The tokenwise CLIP text embedding
532
      space might share this property and could benefit from applying similar strategies to further improve
533
      disentanglement.

      E THICS S TATEMENT
541
542
      This work aims to improve the capabilities of text-to-image diffusion models by enabling more fine-
543   grained control over generated content, with applications to controlled generation and image editing.
544   Text-to-image models can generally be used to create misleading or inappropriate content and may
545   inherit biases from training data, including gender, race, and cultural stereotypes. Our method offers
546   a potential mitigation strategy for some of these issues, helping to counteract biases by providing
547   users with more precise control over generated images instead of purely relying on the pre-trained
548   model to determine appropriate attribute combinations. We encourage responsible use and further
549   research into mitigating biases in text-to-image generation.

      A.2          S UBJECT N OUN T RANSFERABILITY
865
866   We investigate how much our learned attribute modulations can generalize across different nouns
867   that describe the same subject. We generally learn them on a set of different nouns that describe
868   a subject of a specific category (e.g., for people with the words “man”, “woman”, and “person”).
869   However, these words typically do not cover the whole range of possible nouns that can be used to
870   describe subjects of a general category. Ideally, one could learn one modulation for one concept,
871   such as age, on a small set of nouns and generalize across all nouns of a category or even to subjects
872
      of other categories.
873   First, we test the generalization of modulations learned for people on “man”, “woman”, and “person”
874   and apply them to increasingly more specific nouns that describe people. Results are shown in
875   Figures 15 and 16, and all prompts are “a photo of a beautiful <noun>”. As a baseline, we apply
876   them to “child”, “mother”, and “father”, three words that are previously unseen but still describe very
877   high-level sub-categories of people. We find that the learned modulations still work as expected.
878
      Similarly, for categories of jobs such as “doctor”, “barista”, or “firefighter”, which are substantially
      more specific and also substantially affect their clothing and the rest of the image, we find that
879
      they also work well. Finally, applying these learned modulations to very specific nouns such as the
880
      names “John” and “Jane” also works as expected. This demonstrates that our learned modulations
881   can generalize well across a wide range of unseen nouns describing instances of a specific category,
882   even if they were only learned on a small set of high-level, potential nouns.

1520   most costly part) and that values for λi very close to zero were not particularly useful for the training
1521   process. Empirically, we find that most of our learned directions are already close to convergence
1522   after five optimization steps, but we keep training for the full time for simplicity.

1526   In Section 4, we combine our attribute control method with other off-the-shelf controlled generation
1527   methods.
1528
1529   Combination with Prompt-to-Prompt (Hertz et al., 2023) To combine our method with Prompt-
1530   to-Prompt, we apply the standard Prompt-to-Prompt method. We use the same adaptation mode and
1531
       hyperparameters as used for adding adjectives in the text prompt, but add our modulations on the
       text prompt embedding instead. To modulate the change, we scale our directions as usual.
1532
1533
       Combination with AdapEdit (Ma et al., 2024) AdapEdit uses the same general external interface
1534
       as Prompt-to-Prompt. Here, we apply our modulations in the exact same way as previously described
1535   for Prompt-to-Prompt. As AdapEdit is not available for SDXL (Podell et al., 2024), we use zero-shot
1536   adaptation of our semantic directions obtained on SDXL to SD1.5, as described in Section 4.2.
1537
1538   Combination with ReNoise (Garibi et al., 2024) To apply our controlled generation approach to
1539   editing, we combine it with ReNoise, a standard inversion approach. We use their official reference
1540   implementation based on SDXL Turbo (Sauer et al., 2023) and apply our modulations learned on
1541   SDXL there. We perform inversion purely with ReNoise with default settings and an image de-
1542   scription prompt to obtain a starting latent xT , and then perform controlled generation purely with
1543   our method with standard settings. This could optionally be combined further with other methods
1544   during inference, such as Prompt-to-Prompt (Hertz et al., 2023) and AdapEdit (Ma et al., 2024).
