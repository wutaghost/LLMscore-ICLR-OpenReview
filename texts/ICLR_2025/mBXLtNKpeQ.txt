                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               We propose Pullback Flow Matching (PFM), a novel framework for generative
               modeling on data manifolds. Unlike existing methods that assume or learn re-
011
               strictive closed-form manifold mappings for training Riemannian Flow Matching
012
               (RFM) models, PFM leverages pullback geometry and isometric learning to pre-
013            serve the underlying manifold’s geometry while enabling efficient generation and
014            precise interpolation in latent space. This approach not only facilitates closed-
015            form mappings on the data manifold but also allows for designable latent spaces,
016            using assumed metrics on both data and latent manifolds. By enhancing isomet-
017            ric learning through Neural ODEs and proposing a scalable training objective,
018            we achieve a latent space more suitable for interpolation, leading to improved
019            manifold learning and generative performance. We demonstrate PFM’s effective-
020            ness through applications in synthetic data, protein dynamics and protein sequence
021
               data, generating novel proteins with specific properties. This method shows strong
               potential for drug discovery and materials science, where generating novel sam-
022
               ples with specific properties is of great interest.

      We consider modeling the data under the manifold hypothesis, which states that high-dimensional
048   data lies on a lower dimensional manifold. This has been successfully applied to several downstream
049   tasks in various fields across the scientific domain (Vanderplas & Connolly, 2009; Dsilva et al., 2016;
050   Noé & Clementi, 2017). Modeling the data in its intrinsic dimension allows for efficient analysis
051   Diepeveen et al. (2024) and generation Rombach et al. (2022). Furthermore, accurately capturing the
052   geometry of the data manifold in the learning problem has shown to improve several down-stream
053   tasks such as clustering (Ghojogh et al., 2022), classification (Kaya & Bilge, 2019; Hauberg et al.,
      2012) and generation (Arvanitidis et al., 2020; Sun et al., 2024).

      One way to achieve a latent manifold that supports interpolation is to have a structured Riemannian
055   geometry, such as the one from pullback geometry, which provides closed-form manifold mappings
056   (Diepeveen et al., 2024). This requires constructing an invertible and differentiable mapping—
057   diffeomorphism—between the data manifold and the latent manifold. Interpolation on manifolds is
058   then performed through geodesics, shortest paths, and thus to achieve our goal we require geodesics
059   on the data manifold to match geodesics on the latent manifold. This motivates our consideration of
060   isometries, that is, metric-preserving diffeomorphisms φ. These mappings preserve the distances of
061   points on the data manifold on the latent manifold, and thereby ensure proper interpolation.
062   Related-Work. In the literature, low-dimensional generation and generation on manifolds have typ-
063   ically been addressed as separate problems. Low-dimensional approaches, such as latent diffusion
064   (Rombach et al., 2022) or latent flow-matching (Dao et al., 2023), often overlook the geometric
065   structure of the data, leading to inaccuracies in tasks requiring a faithful representation of the un-
066   derlying manifold. Conversely, manifold generation methods either assume geodesics on the data
067   manifold for simulation-free training (Chen & Lipman, 2024)—an approach flawed when closed-
068   form mappings are unavailable—or attempt to learn a metric that forces the generative trajectories
069
      to have data support (Kapusniak et al., 2024).
070   Using a pullback framework presents challenges, such as task-specific learning problems that limit
071   generality and prevent the learning of isometries across broader data manifolds Cuzzolin (2008);
072   Gruffaz et al. (2021); Lebanon (2006). Geometrically regularized latent space methods, like Lee
073   et al. (2022) and Duque et al. (2022), work in practice but lack solid mathematical grounding in
074   isometries, particularly guaranteeing diffeomorphism in architectural design. Diepeveen (2024) ad-
075
      dresses isometry challenges with a more general mathematically grounded framework, but its learn-
      ing objective’s expressivity and computational feasibility limit its application to high-dimensional
076
      real-world datasets.
077
078   Our approach bridges these gaps by modeling data on a lower-dimensional latent manifold with
079   known geometry through a diffeomorphisms parameterized and trained in a scalable and expressive
080   way. By doing so we preserve the intrinsic properties of the data manifold and enable accurate and
081
      efficient generation through simulation-free training.
082   Contributions. We propose Pullback Flow Matching (PFM), a novel framework for latent manifold
083   learning and generation through isometries. This method respects the geometry of the data manifold,
084   even when closed-form manifold mappings are not available. Second, learning can be performed in
085   the intrinsic dimension of the data manifold resulting in efficient and effective learning of the gener-
086   ative model with fewer parameters. Building on Diepeveen (2024), we leverage pullback geometry
087
      to define a new metric on the entire ambient space, Rd , by learning an isometry φ that preserves the
      geometric structure of the data manifold D on the latent manifold M. We use the corresponding
088
      metric of the assumed latent manifold M to perform Riemannian Flow Matching (RFM) on the
089
      latent manifold that supports interpolation. Our contributions are as follows:
090
091            1. We introduce PFM, a novel framework that enables accurate and efficient data generation
092               on manifolds. PFM leverages the pullback geometry to preserve the underlying geometric
093               structure of the data manifold within the latent space, facilitating precise interpolation and
094               generation.
095            2. We improve the parameterization of diffeomorphisms, used to learn isometries, in both ex-
096               pressiveness and training efficiency through neural ordinary differential equations (Neural
097               ODEs).
098            3. We introduce a scalable and stable isometric learning objective. This objective relies solely
099               on a distance measure on the data manifold, simplifying the training process compared to
100               Diepeveen (2024) while maintaining geometric fidelity.
101            4. We demonstrate our methods’ effectiveness through experiments on synthetic data, high-
102               dimensional molecular dynamics data, and experimental peptide sequences. Our frame-
103               work utilizes designable latent spaces to generate novel proteins with specific properties
104               closely matching reference samples. This directed generation showcases the significant ap-
105               plicability of isometric learning and PFM in accurate physical modeling and interpolation,
106               advancing generative modeling techniques in drug discovery and materials science. 1

      We give a brief summary of the notation used in the paper, and give a more extensive background
111   on Riemannian and pullback geometry in Appendix A.
112
113   A manifold M is a topological space that locally resembles Euclidean space. A d-dimensional
114
      manifold M around a point p ∈ M is described by a chart ψ : U → Rd , where U ⊆ M is a
      neighborhood of p. The chart provides a local coordinate system for the manifold. The tangent
115
      space at a point p ∈ M, denoted Tp M, is the vector space of all tangent vectors at that point.
116
117   A smooth manifold M equipped with a Riemannian metric is called a Riemannian manifold and
118   is denoted by (M, (·, ·)M ). The Riemannian metric (·, ·)M is a smoothly varying inner product
119   defined on the tangent spaces Tp M for all points p ∈ M, and it defines lengths and angles on the
120
      manifold. A geodesic, γp,q (t) is the shortest path between two points p, q ∈ M, generalizing the
      notion of a straight line in Euclidean space.
121
122   The exponential map expp : Tp M → M maps a tangent vector Ξp to a point on the manifold by
123   following the geodesic in the direction of Ξp starting from p. The inverse of the exponential map is
124   the logarithmic map, denoted by logp : M → Tp M, which returns the tangent vector corresponding
125   to a given point on the manifold.

      4     L EARNING I SOMETRIES
234
      The motivation for learning isometries φθ —metric-preserving diffeomorphisms—is to enable a la-
235
      tent (sub)manifold that supports interpolation with closed-form geometric mappings, facilitating
236   simulation-free training of PFM. Building on the framework of Diepeveen (2024), summarized
237   in Appendix A, we propose a more expressive parameterization of learnable diffeomorphisms φθ
238   through Neural ODEs and enhance the objective for scalable isometric learning on data manifolds.
239
240   4.1   PARAMETERIZING D IFFEOMORPHISMS
241
242   We parameterize diffeomorphisms, invertible and differentiable functions between two manifolds,
243   specifically φ : Rd → M. In practice, we construct the latent manifold as a product manifold,
                         ′
244   M = Md′ × Rd−d and the diffeomorphism φ as,
245                                   φ := [ψ −1 , Id−d′ ] ◦ ϕ ◦ Tµ ,                            (11)

      The goal of this paper is to learn interpolatable latent (sub)manifolds for generation on data man-
310   ifolds. We achieve this through isometric learning in the framework of pullback geometry. In this
311   section we validate our methods on synthetic, simulated and experimental datasets, for full descrip-

316   we compare (latent) interpolation methods with interpolation on the latent manifold M, (·, ·)M -
317   interpolation, and on the latent submanifold Md′ , (·, ·)Md′ -interpolation. We demonstrate that we
318   can accurately interpolate on the data manifold by interpolating on the latent (sub)manifold 2 . Third,
319   we validate PFM as a generative model on data manifolds and discuss how sample generation is im-
320   proved by generating on the submanifold Md′ . Finally, we inspect the designability of the latent
321   manifold through the choice of metric (·, ·)D in the task of small protein design.

      We introduce Pullback Flow Matching (PFM), a novel framework for simulation-free training of
534   generative models on data manifolds. By leveraging pullback geometry and isometric learning, PFM
535   allows for closed-form mappings on data manifolds while enabling precise interpolation and efficient
536   generation. We demonstrated the effectiveness of PFM through applications in synthetic protein dy-
537   namics and small protein generation, showcasing its potential in generating novel, property-specific
538   samples through designable latent spaces. This approach holds significant promise for advancing
539   generative modeling in fields like drug discovery and materials science, where precise and efficient
      sample generation is critical.

865
866   To achieve the goal of accurate generative modeling on data manifolds through isometric learning,
867   we first need to understand generative modeling on Euclidean spaces. We do this through summa-
868   rizing CFM Lipman et al. (2022), a commonly used and effective framework for learning CNFs for
869   generative modeling for Euclidean data Chen et al. (2018). CFM is a method designed to map a
870   simple base distribution to a target data distribution by learning a time-dependent vector field. The
871   fundamental goal of Flow Matching (FM) is to align a target probability path pt (x) with a vector
872
      field ut (x), which generates the desired distribution. The FM objective is defined as follows:
873
874                                    LFM (η) = Et,pt (x) ∥vt (x; η) − ut (x)∥2 ,                              (36)
875   where η represents the learnable parameters of the neural network that parameterizes the vector
876   field vt (x; η), and t ∼ U(0, 1) is uniformly sampled. However, a significant challenge in FM is the
877   intractability of constructing the exact path pt (x) and the corresponding vector field ut (x).
878
      To address this Lipman et al. (2022) introduce CFM, a more practical approach by constructing the
879
      probability path and vector fields in a conditional manner. The CFM objective is then formulated by
880   marginalizing over the data distribution q(x1 ) and considering the conditional probability paths:
881
882
883
                               LCFM (η) = Et,q(x1 ),pt (x|x1 ) ∥vt (x; η) − ut (x|x1 )∥2 .                      (37)
884   A key result, as established in Theorem 2 of Chen & Lipman (2024), is that the gradients of the
885   CFM objective with respect to the parameters η are identical to those of the original FM objective,
886   i.e.,
887
888                                            ∇η LFM (η) = ∇η LCFM (η),                                        (38)
889   ensuring that optimizing the CFM objective yields the same result as the original FM objective. This
890   enables effective train of the neural network without needing direct access to the intractable marginal
891   probability paths or vector fields.

1039   C    M ANIFOLD AND M ETRIC S ELECTION
1040
1041   Isometric learning requires three key choices to be made, first one needs to choose the Riemannian
1042
       metric of the data manifold (·, ·)D , second one needs to choose both the latent (sub)manifold and its
       Riemannian metric Md′ , (·, ·)Md′ and finally one needs to choose the dimensionality d′ . Techni-

1043                                                      ′
1044   cally one also needs to assume a metric on Rd−d , but in this work we assume a Euclidean metric
1045   (·, ·)2 throughout all our experiments.
1046   There are several options when selecting the metric on the data manifold (·, ·)D . One can choose for
1047   example a locally euclidean approximation through Isomap Tenenbaum et al. (2000) or a more noise-
1048   robust geodesic approximation Little et al. (2022). One can also design a metric to create a latent
1049   space 4 structured based on properties of the data one cares about, we show how in subsection 5.4.
1050   In this work, we focus on using a proper metric and defer the exploration of learning with pseudo-
1051   metrics to future research.
1052   When selecting a latent Riemannian (sub)manifold and metric it is crucial to select Md′ such that
                           ′
1053   M = Md′ × Rd−d it is diffeomorphic to the data manifold D. This ensures that the latent space of
1054   the RAE can effectively capture the intrinsic structure of the data. The manifold should be chosen
1055   based on its ability to accommodate the data’s periodicity, curvature, and dimensionality. This
1056   alignment is essential for accurately representing the data manifold within the latent space. Unless
                                                ′
1057   otherwise stated we   assume Md′ = Rd . Additionally, one should select the Riemannian geometry
       of Md′ , (·, ·)Md′ such that geometric mappings can be explicitly defined in closed form. A list of

1058
1059   manifolds with closed form geometric mappings can be found in the appendix of Chen & Lipman
1060   (2024). Unless otherwise states we select (·, ·)Md′ = (·, ·)2 .
1061   Finally, d′ , the dimensionality of the latent space, is a hyperparameter that could be tuned through
1062   iterative testing. Techniques such as Isomap Tenenbaum et al. (2000) or equivalents on other mani-
1063   folds such as hyperbolic space Cvetkovski & Crovella (2011) can be employed to evaluate various
1064   dimensional and Riemannian geometric settings and determine the optimal d′ that balances model
1065   complexity with the ability to accurately capture the data manifold’s structure.
