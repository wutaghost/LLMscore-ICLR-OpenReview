009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            High-dynamic scene optical flow is a challenging task, which suffers large dis-
012            placement. Limited by frame imaging, large displacement causes potential spatial
013            blurry textures due to long exposure and temporal discontinuous motion due to low
014            frame rate, thus deteriorating the spatiotemporal feature of optical flow. Typically,
015            existing methods mainly introduce event camera with high temporal resolution to
016
               directly fuse the spatiotemporal features between the two modalities. However,
               this direct fusion is ineffective, since there exists a large gap due to the heteroge-
017
               neous data representation between frame and event modalities. To address this
018
               issue, we explore a common-latent space as an intermediate bridge to mitigate the
019            modality gap. In this work, we propose a novel common spatiotemporal fusion
020            between frame and event modalities for high-dynamic scene optical flow, including
021            visual boundary localization and motion correlation fusion. Specifically, in visual
022            boundary localization, we figure out that frame and event can be derived into
023            the spatiotemporal gradient maps with the same data representation, where the
024            similarity distribution between the two modalities is consistent with the extracted
025            boundary distribution. This motivates us to design the common spatiotemporal
026            gradient to constrain the localization of the reference boundary as a template. In
027
               motion correlation fusion, we discover that the frame-based motion possesses
               spatially dense but temporally discontinuous correlation, while the event-based
028
               motion has spatially sparse but temporally continuous correlation. This inspires us
029
               to take the reference boundary template to guide the fusion of the complementary
030            motion knowledge between the two modalities. Moreover, common spatiotemporal
031            fusion can not only relieve the cross-modal feature discrepancy, but also make the
032            fusion process interpretable to achieve dense and continuous optical flow. Extensive
033            experiments have been performed to verify the superiority of the proposed method.

036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Optical flow is a task of modeling the pixel-level temporal correspondence from the spatial features
039
      between adjacent frames. Optical flow has made great progress in conventional dynamic scenes with
      ideal imaging, but paid little attention to the challenging high-dynamic scenes, i.e., fast motion. The
040
      main reason is that fast motion in the world space brings in the degradation of large displacement to
041
      the frame-based imaging space, thus breaking the basic spatiotemporal assumption of optical flow in
042   Fig. 1 (a). On the one hand, large displacement leads to the spatial blur of visual textures due to long
043   exposure, violating the photometric constancy assumption. On the other hand, large displacement
044   causes the temporal discontinuity of motion trajectory due to low frame rate, breaking the gradient
045   continuity assumption. The two degradations further deteriorate the spatiotemporal features of optical
046   flow in the feature space. In this work, we focus on modeling the spatiotemporal characteristic of
047   high-dynamic scene motion, thus achieving spatial-dense and temporal-continuous optical flow.
048   From the perspective of imaging mechanism, event camera (Gallego et al., 2020) is a neuromorphic
049   vision sensor with asynchronous trigger, which has the advantage of high temporal resolution to make
050   up for the limitation of frame camera. Regarding the issue of high-dynamic scene optical flow, several
051   methods (Wan et al., 2022; Gehrig et al., 2024) introduce event camera, and formulate this problem
052   as a task of multimodal fusion, which directly fuses the spatiotemporal features between frame and
053   event modalities to achieve dense and continuous optical flow in Fig. 1 (b). For example, Wan et al.
      (2022) encoded the frame images and event stream into the spatial features and the temporal features,

132   2    R ELATED W ORK
133
134   Optical Flow. In recent years, learning-based methods have been proposed to model the motion
135   features of optical flow, including supervised and unsupervised methods. Supervised methods
136
      (Dosovitskiy et al., 2015; Luo et al., 2022; Sun et al., 2018; Ilg et al., 2017; Hur & Roth, 2019; Teed
      & Deng, 2020; Lu et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2021) usually design different deep networks to learn
137
      motion field using optical flow labels. Furthermore, to relieve the dependency on motion labels,
138
      unsupervised methods (Jonschkowski et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2019a;b; Meister et al., 2018; Ren
139   et al., 2017; Yu et al., 2016) mainly employ the basic spatiotemporal assumption (i.e., photometric
140   constancy and gradient continuity) of optical flow as the prior to design the training losses for motion
141   feature representation. However, despite these methods could satisfy conventional dynamic scenes
142   with ideal imaging, they are still limited by the degradation caused by large displacement in frame
143   imaging under high-dynamic conditions. The main reason is that large displacement leads to the
144   spatial blur of visual textures and the temporal discontinuity of motion trajectory, thus breaking the
145   basic spatiotemporal assumption of optical flow. In this work, we aim to improve the spatiotemporal
146   feature representation, thus achieving spatial-dense and temporal-continuous optical flow.
147   High-Dynamic Scene Optical Flow. In frame imaging, large displacement may cause spatial blur
148   of visual textures and temporal discontinuity of motion trajectory in high-dynamic scenes. An
149   intuitive solution is image preprocessing, such as image deblur (Ruan et al., 2022; Kupyn et al.,
150   2019) and frame interpolation (Plack et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023). Image deblur does indeed
151   improve the spatial visualization quality of frame imaging, but cannot guarantee the valid motion
152   feature matching in the temporal dimension. Frame interpolation is indeed beneficial for estimating
153
      temporally continuous optical flow, but it is difficult to model the temporally non-linear motion
      pattern of dynamic targets from the frames with low frame rate. Another novel solution is to introduce
154
      event camera with high temporal resolution to assist frame camera in optical flow, including unimodal
155
      and multimodal methods. Unimodal methods (Zhu et al., 2018; Paredes-Vallés & de Croon, 2021;
156   Gehrig et al., 2021b) input event stream to learn the temporal continuous optical flow, while is limited
157   by the spatial sparsity of event data. Multimodal methods (Wan et al., 2022; Gehrig et al., 2024)
158   formulate this problem as a fusion task, which directly fuse the spatiotemporal motion features
159   between frame and event modalities for dense and continuous optical flow. However, these direct
160   fusion methods neglect the large gap due to the heterogeneous data representation between frame and
161   event modalities. To address above issue, we explore an intermediate bridge to close the modality
      gap, thus improving the cross-modal spatiotemporal fusion of motion features.

180   Multimodal Fusion. Multimodal fusion aims to exploit the inter-modal complementary knowledge
181   for the target vision task. To our knowledge, most existing multimodal fusion methods (Hori et al.,
182   2017; Hu et al., 2017; Wei et al., 2020; Sun et al., 2022; Cai et al., 2023) construct the feature-level
183   attention to fuse the cross-modal complementary knowledge. For example, Sun et al. (2022) built
184
      a cross-modal attention module to fuse the relevant features between frame and event modalities
      for motion deblurring using several residual convolution blocks. However, these multimodal fusion
185
      methods face two problems. First, there exists a large gap between different modalities, exacerbating
186
      the discrepancy of the cross-modal feature distribution, thus limiting the fusion performance. Second,
187   the complementary features lack the physical meanings, and the entire fusion process is too implicit,
188   causing uncontrollable fusion results. In this work, we design a common-latent space with physical
189   meanings to bridge the frame-event gap, as a guidance to fuse the complementary spatiotemporal
190   correlation for motion representation, thus controllably achieving dense and continuous optical flow.

195   3.1          OVERALL F RAMEWORK
196
      High-dynamic scene optical flow suffers the degradations caused by large displacement of fast
197
      motion in the spatial and temporal dimensions. The key of solving this issue is how to improve
198
      the spatiotemporal feature representation of the motion. To this end, we rethink this problem from
199   the perspective of multimodal fusion, and introduce event camera to assist frame camera in the
200   spatiotemporal fusion of motion features, thus better representing optical flow. Considering that
201   there exists a large gap due to the heterogeneous data representation between frame and event
202   modalities, we further explore a common-latent space to bridge the modality gap, thus alleviating
203   the feature discrepancy between the two modalities. In this work, we propose a novel commonality-
204   guided spatiotemporal fusion framework between frame and event modalities for high-dynamic scene
205   optical flow. As shown in Fig. 2, the whole framework looks complex but is simplified into two
206   sub-modules, i.e., visual boundary localization and motion correlation fusion. In visual boundary
207
      localization module, we first build the common spatiotemporal gradient space, where we can represent
      both frame and event modalities using the same data form. Next, we utilize the common space to
208
      constrain the reference point localization of the boundary features fused from the two modalities.
209
      In motion correlation fusion module, we take the located reference points of the boundary as the
210   template to guide the feature clustering within the frame-based correlation for the density of motion
211   appearance in the spatial dimension, and guide the feature tracking within the event-based correlation
212   for the continuity of motion trajectory in the temporal dimension. Finally, we build the cross-modal
213   transformer architecture to fuse the spatiotemporal correlation features. Under this unified framework,
214   visual boundary localization constructs common spatiotemporal gradient space to bridge frame and
215   event, while motion correlation fusion fuses the complementary spatiotemporal motion knowledge
      under the guidance of the common space, thus achieving dense and continuous optical flow.

229   Estimating optical flow in high-dynamic scenes is challenging, since large displacement of fast motion
230   causes spatiotemporal degradations due to the limitation of frame imaging. Therefore, we introduce
231   event camera with high temporal resolution to assist frame camera in fusing the complementary
232   knowledge for dense and continuous optical flow. However, there are two difficulties: pixel-level
233   alignment and large modality gap between frame and event cameras.
234   Pixel-Aligned Frame and Event. The pixel-level alignment between frame and event cameras
235   heavily relies on spatial calibration (e.g., hardware and software) and time synchronization (see
236   supplementary for details). Hardware calibration sets up a physically coaxial optical device with
237   a beam splitter for frame and event cameras, which allows the same light to pass through the
238   same lens and enter different cameras. Software calibration further performs a standard stereo
239   rectification between frame data and event data, and then refines the slight calibration errors via pixel
240   offset (Tulyakov et al., 2022). Moreover, time synchronization is achieved by external trigger from
241   microcontroller. In this way, we can obtain the spatiotemporal pixel-aligned frames and event stream.

304   3.3         M OTION C ORRELATION F USION
305
306   Although visual boundary localization can bridge the large modality gap and provide the visual
307   boundary points as the reference template, selecting which feature to use for fusion and how to utilize
308   the template to guide this fusion for optical flow is a challenge.
309   Complementary Frame-Event Spatiotemporal Correlation. As for the choice of the features,
310   the motion features that optical flow relies on mainly include two categories: correlation feature
311   and decoded feature. Correlation feature is derived from visual features via warping and pixel-wise
312   multiplication, namely correlation volume, which represents the temporal correspondence of visual
313   features. Decoded feature is updated by recurrent network (e.g., GRU (Cho et al., 2014)). Therefore,
314   correlation feature has a physical meaning, which could serve as the key motion feature to fuse.
315
      As for the frame-event complementary nature, frame reflects dense appearance while event senses
      sparse boundary in the spatial dimension, and frame captures discontinuous displacement while
316
      event triggers continuous displacement in the temporal dimension. This naturally makes us wonder
317
      whether these two modalities also have similar complementary knowledge at the level of motion
318   features. To demonstrate this insight, we first extract the boundary corner as the template, and then
319   transform frames and event stream to correlation volume space via warping, where we sample the
320   correlation features to match this template using K-Means clustering (Likas et al., 2003). As shown
321   in Fig. 4, the frame-based corelation features are x, y-axis spatially dense but t-axis temporally
322   discontinuous, while the event-based correlation features are x, y-axis spatially sparse but t-axis
323   temporally continuous. Therefore, we choose correlation feature as the motion feature, and fuse the
      frame-based spatially dense correlation and the event-based temporally continuous correlation.

509   Role of Common Space. We analyze the role of common spatiotemporal gradient space in the feature
510   alignment and fusion via feature visualization in Fig. 8. In visual feature distribution, frame-based and
511   event-based features are mixed discrete, while common spatiotemporal gradient can bridge the two

      bridge, which further makes the motion features between the two modalities close.
514   Choice of Clustering Strategies. In Table 5, we discuss the impact of various clustering strategies
515   (e.g., DBSCAN (Ester et al., 1996), K-Means (Likas et al., 2003) and GMM (Reynolds et al., 2009))

517   than those with clustering. Second, there is no difference between the impacts of various clustering
518   strategies on the final optical flow. This reveals that correlation feature possesses the discriminative
519
      properties for motion patterns, which can be distinguished by simple clustering strategies.
520   Why to Temporally Match Correlation. We compare the effects of different tracking solutions
521   (e.g., event signal, visual feature and correlation feature) on the temporal continuity of optical flow in
522   Table 6. First, tracking feature is significantly better than tracking event signal. This is because event
523   signals are simple data with only positive and negative polarities, which is difficult to represent scene
524   knowledge. Second, tracking correlation feature performs better than tracking visual feature. The
525
      reason is that correlation feature can directly reflect the temporal correspondence of optical flow.
526   Limitation The proposed method may suffer challenges for the radial moving object relative to the
527   camera. This is because frame and event cameras can only capture x,y-axis motion patterns, while
528   the radial moving object moves along the z-axis, making the two cameras ineffective for this special
529   motion pattern. In the future, we will introduce LiDAR to assist in modeling the radial motion.

531   5          C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this work, we propose a novel common spatiotemporal fusion framework between frame and event
534   modalities to achieve dense and continuous optical flow. We construct a common spatiotemporal
535   gradient space as an intermediate bridge to mitigate the heterogeneous data representation between
536   frame and event modalities. The common space locates the visual boundary templates, which is
537   used to explicitly guide the fusion of the frame-based spatially dense correlation and the event-based
538   temporally continuous correlation. Moreover, we build a pixel-aligned frame-event dataset, and
539   demonstrate that the proposed method significantly outperforms the state-of-the-art methods. We
      believe that our work could facilitate the development of high-dynamic scene optical flow.
