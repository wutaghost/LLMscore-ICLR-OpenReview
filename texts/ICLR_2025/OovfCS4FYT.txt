009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) embody priors about the visual world:
012            locality, stationary statistics, translation invariance, and compositionality. Sim-
013            ilarly, CNNs implement the retinotopy of visual cortex—nearby pixels are pro-
014            cessed by nearby neurons. A common cortical computation not usually included
015            in CNNs is divisive normalization. It has been shown that divisive normalization
016            of Gabor filters results in more statistically independent responses (Simoncelli
017            & Heeger, 1998). In this paper, we model divisive normalization as a simple
018
               computationally-efficient layer that can be inserted at any stage within deep arti-
               ficial neural networks. Divisive normalization acts on neuronal sub-populations,
019
               whose parameters are initialized from a multivariate Gaussian distribution. This
020
               leads to the emergence of learned competition between both orientation-preferring
021            and color-opponent cell types. Divisive normalization improves categorization
022            performance, as well as robustness to perturbed images. Interestingly, in smaller
023            networks, divisive normalization as a non-linear operation eliminates the need for
024            a non-linear activation function like ReLU to drive performance.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Deep Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) have had much success as categorization models of
030   real world visual stimuli, in particular on the ImageNet dataset (Deng et al., 2009; Krizhevsky et al.,
031   2012; Simonyan & Zisserman, 2015; Szegedy et al., 2015; He et al., 2016). Moreover, they have
032   been shown to be good at predicting neural responses to images in non-human and human ventral vi-
033
      sual cortex (Yamins et al., 2014; Nayebi et al., 2018; Kubilius et al., 2019; Schrimpf et al., 2020). The
      superiority of CNNs over fully-connected networks can be attributed to their biologically-inspired
034
      properties of shared filter weights, translation invariance, and larger receptive fields with depth.
035
      These built-in priors reflect properties of the visual world: locality, stationary statistics, and the
036   invariance of object identity with respect to translation.
037
038   Today, almost every CNN architecture makes use of some form of rectification, often ReLU, fol-
039
      lowing its introduction by Glorot et al. (2011). While ReLU is not as biologically plausible as
      saturating nonlinearities, empirically, it works better in deep networks. ReLU models, however, do
040
      not account for one important nonlinearity—the common cortical computation of cross-feature inhi-
041
      bition (Simoncelli & Heeger, 1998). Carandini and Heeger (Carandini & Heeger, 2012) suggest that
042   divisive normalization (DN) is a good model of this neural computation, and show that it can explain
043   nonlinear properties of neurons in the primary visual cortex. Their model computes a ratio between
044   the activity of an individual neuron and the summed activity of a local neighborhood of neurons. The
045   activity of an individual neuron is produced after its summed input is halfwave-rectified and squared
046   (Heeger, 1992; Wainwright et al., 2002). The reasons for performing DN include, but are not limited
047   to, removing statistical dependence between pairs of filter responses obtained by projecting natural
048   images onto linear basis functions at nearby spatial positions, orientations, and scales (Wainwright
049   et al., 2002), light adaptation in the retina and contrast normalization (Boynton & Whitten, 1970),
050
      attention in the primary visual cortex (Reynolds & Heeger, 2009), and surround suppression through
      lateral excitation and inhibition (Cavanaugh et al., 2002; Carandini & Heeger, 2012).
051
052   As described above, divisive normalization in the VVC is often implemented in the form of local
053   competition between sub-populations of neurons. This competition has been extensively observed
      between Gabor-like orientation selective cell types (Beck et al., 2011; Bonds, 1989; Busse et al.,


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      2009; Carandini et al., 1997; DeAngelis et al., 1992; Morrone et al., 1982). Recently, Cirincione
055   et al. (2022) and Miller et al. (2022) implement divisive normalization in CNNs to demonstrate
056   robustness to image corruptions and activation sparsity. However, they limit their analyses to either
057   a single architecture, to competition only across V1 neurons, or to the introduction of an expensive
058   divisive normalization computation.
059
      In this paper, we propose a simple divisive normalization formulation inspired by Carandini &
060
      Heeger (2012) and a parameter initialization scheme based on the circular multivariate Gaussian dis-
061   tribution to drive local competition (section 3). This formulation of divisive normalization requires
062   less computation than Miller et al. (2022), while still making the model robust to image corruptions
063   to a large extent (sections 4.2 and 4.4). For shallower networks, we find that the use of divisive nor-
064   malization as a squaring operator to turn the responses of neurons positive (making both excitatory
065   and inhibitory signals excitatory) seems to be sufficient, as well as better, in terms of performance
066   gains over using a half-wave rectifier before (section 4.1). We use various model architectures to
067   robustly conclude the effectiveness of our divisive normalization formulation. And finally, we not
068   only observe learned competition across Gabor-like orientation selective filters (as most works have
069
      previously shown), but also have this competition emerge between different color-opponency cell
      types (section 4.3).

073   2   R ELATED W ORK
074
075
076   There are three different normalization techniques worth thinking about that enforce some form of
077   “competition” among neurons in some local neighborhood. The first is Local Response Normaliza-
078   tion (Krizhevsky et al., 2012) that was proposed by the authors of AlexNet. This formulation uses a
079   linear numerator and norms the responses of neurons by neighbors in some neighborhood over the
080   channel space. Miller et al. (2022) then come up with a new formulation that is similar to LRN, but
081   instead with a squared numerator and exponentially decaying weights attributed to neighbors away
082
      from the center in a neighborhood. They claim that their formulation is better than LRN in perfor-
      mance. Both of these normalization schemes use overlapping neighborhoods. Group normalization
083
      (Wu & He, 2018) is similar to our formulation of divisive normalization in the sense that it breaks
084
      down channels into mutually-exclusive (non-overlapping) neighborhoods, but then the computation
085   is simply z-scoring the activations, rather than putting them in competition. Our approach differs
086   from group normalization in the sense that it enforces local competition among neurons, as opposed
087   to standardizing their responses.
088
      There have been many prior works that have incorporated divisive normalization in deep networks:
089
      Jarrett et al. (2009) show that using non-linearities that include rectification and local contrast nor-
090
      malization is the single most important ingredient for good accuracy on object recognition bench-
091   marks, but they only consider two layers. Ren et al. (2016) modified batch normalization and layer
092   normalization by adding the additive constant in the denominator of the calculation like divisive
093   normalization and explore how their version, which is also a canonical normalization, improves the
094   performance of recurrent and convolutional neural networks for image classification. The numerator
095   of their normalization formula is still first order, so it cannot remove the second order dependency
096   as our formulation of divisive normalization can, whose numerator is squared (see equation 2). Pan
097   et al. (2023) observes that CNN neurons are most suppressed when the surround matches the center
098   and less suppressed when the surround differs from the center. Burg et al. (2021) shows that a single
099
      layer model with divisive normalization can predict the V1 response to natural signals well. This
      additionally also supports our claim that our model with CH divnorm is more biologically plau-
100
      sible. Coen-Cagli et al. (2012) provides an account of orientation-based contextual modulation in
101
      early vision by introducing a model of natural images that includes grouping and segmentation of
102   spatially neighboring features, which is different from our channel-wise normalization solution. Cir-
103   incione et al. (2022) add divisive normalization to the front-end of VOneNet (Dapello et al., 2020) to
104   show robustness to image corruptions; however, VOneNet uses a fixed-weight Gabor filter-bank as
105   opposed to learning V1 feature representations. Veerabadran et al. (2022) introduced DivNormEI,
106   which performs divisive normalization within the spatial neighborhood of each channel and applies
107   lateral inhibition and excitation by weighted sum across channels, demonstrating improved perfor-
      mance in large-scale object recognition tasks.

      3.3   A SYMPTOTIC C OMPUTATIONAL C OMPLEXITY
178
179   For Miller et al. (2022)’s formulation of divisive normalization, a sliding window technique is used
180   (i.e., neighborhoods are overlapping). Each neighborhood has a size of 8λ, where λ is learned
181   during training. This means that, in the worst case, λ = N/8, with N being the number of neurons
182   in that layer. There are (N − 8λ + 1) total neighborhoods. If, for every neighborhood, divisive
183   normalization needs to take into account all 8λ neurons in that neighborhood, then the total number
184   of computations becomes (N − 8λ + 1) · 8λ = O(N 2 ) in the worst case (i.e., λ = N/8).
185   On the other hand, our formulation of divisive normalization subscribes neurons to non-overlapping
186   neighborhoods, which means that every neuron is visited only once, making the total number of
187   computations O(N ).
188
189   3.4   M ULTIVARIATE G AUSSIAN W EIGHT I NITIALIZATION
190
191   While all of our models implement Kaiming initialization for model parameters (He et al., 2015)
192   as the one resulting in highest categorization performance, we also implement a parameter initial-
193   ization scheme to explore how local competition gets enforced among neuronal sub-populations.
194
      Local competition is usually seen between groups of neurons that perform similar computation;
      for example, between cell types preferring slightly varying orientations. Thus, in CH divnorm, to
195
      encourage similarity between neurons within a neighborhood while allowing for differences be-
196
      tween neurons across separate neighborhoods, we implement a multivariate Gaussian parameter
197   initialization. We first randomly sample a mean vector µp from the Kaiming distribution for each
198   neighborhood. We then initialize filter weights within a neighborhood by sampling from a circular
199   multivariate Gaussian distribution, with µp serving as the mean vector and the standard deviation
200   set to gp × the standard deviation of the Kaiming distribution, where gp is a hyperparameter. This
201   initialization promotes greater similarity within each neighborhood while ensuring more diversity
202   between neighborhoods, encouraging neurons in the same neighborhood to learn similar yet com-
203   petitive features.

301   4.2   D IVISIVE N ORMALIZATION IMPROVES CATEGORIZATION PERFORMANCE
302
303
      We demonstrated in the previous section that divisive normalization leads to improved categoriza-
      tion performance for two different deep neural networks, on two separate datasets. Next, we more
304
      rigorously quantify the improvement by evaluating on the ImageNet dataset (Deng et al., 2009).
305
306   Overall, categorization performance improves for all model variants that use divisive normalization
307   (see Tables 2, 3). For AlexNet, Miller et al. (2022) use four parameters (λ, α, β, and κ) for divisive
308   normalization that are learned for every layer of the network. Due to the way that they implement
309
      this computation, they incur a significantly large runtime that is approximately three times that of the
      base model. There is also an approximately twice GPU memory requirement over the base model.
310
      These overheads come with an improved performance on categorization. For our formulation of di-
311
      visive normalization, we experiment with the number of divisive normalization parameters per layer
312   of the network. Any parameter choice results in an improved performance over the base model. If
313   we fix λ and σ to the same value for all neighborhoods within a layer, we observe slightly, but not
314   significantly, drop in performance compared to Miller et al. (2022). Learning these two parameters
315   for each neighborhood in a layer improves categorization performance, while incurring a modest in-
316   crease in the runtime and GPU memory. Learning separate parameters for each neighborhood helps
317   the divisive normalization computation to capture heterogeneous neural dynamics across neuronal
318   sub-populations.
319   To assure the generalization of our formulation of divisive normalization to deeper models—an im-
320   portant criterion that Miller et al. (2022) do not explore—we applied it to the VGG-16 (Simonyan &

322   16 with Miller et al. (2022)’s formulation of divisive normalization with or without ReLU because
323   it turns out to be very computationally expensive and does not converge successfully. As shown in
      Table 3, the use of divisive normalization outperforms the baseline on ImageNet. However, given

417   geNet test set to evaluate model robustness. We visualize performance on this dataset in Figure 4.
418   Across a variety of different perturbations and noise levels, divisive normalization helps in statis-
419   tically significant reduction in performance drop from the clean accuracy on unperturbed images
420   compared to the base model. However, for some perturbation classes like defocus and zoom blur-
421   ring, divisive normalization does not significantly help under high noise levels. Overall, divisive
422   normalization seems to help significantly when facing color or pixel-wise variations in images (such
423   as brightness, saturation, and gaussian noise) but not for spatially-induced distortions (such as blur-
424   ring and pixel compression). This makes sense, since divisive normalization primarily focuses on
425
      normalizing neuronal responses within local receptive fields, why may not adequately address spa-
      tially global distortions.

428   5     D ISCUSSION
429
430   In this work, we presented a lightweight, memory and runtime efficient implementation of divi-
431   sive normalization that is inspired by Carandini & Heeger (2012). We analyzed its role in learning
      unsupervised local competition between neuronal sub-populations—orientation-selective and color-


                                                                8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      opponent cell types. Divisive normalization leads to improved performance on categorization, and
433   makes the model more robust to localized parametric distortions. Finally, we show that divisive nor-
434   malization can be used as a module that acts as both an activation function and modulates responses,
435   not requiring the use of a half-wave rectifier in shallower networks.
436
      We acknowledge certain limitations in the work that we present. First, we limit our analyses to
437
      AlexNet, VGG-16, and a two-layer CNN. While these are more anatomically consistent than very
438
      deep networks like ResNet-50, since there are somewhere on the order of 15-20 visual areas in hu-
439   mans (Van Essen, 2003), it will still be interesting to understand how very deep networks perform
440   with divisive normalization. In this work, we were constrained by computational power from per-
441   forming this more demanding experiment. Another avenue for improvement is quantifying learned
442   filter similarity for models trained with divisive normalization. Although we visually examined the
443   grouping patterns of different cell types (orientation-selective and color-opponent) in the first con-
444   volutional layer of AlexNet (in section 4.3), we lacked a robust metric to quantify this similarity.
445   Thirdly, using Brain-Score (Schrimpf et al., 2018; 2020) as a set of metrics to analyze the capabili-
446   ties of different layers of a model trained with divisive normalization to predict real neuron responses
447
      in the biological ventral visual cortex’s V1, V2, V4, and the inferior temporal cortex (IT) would be
      a strong test of such a canonical neural computation.
448
449   Finally, there are some shortcomings in the way we implement divisive normalization. Miller et al.
450   (2022) define overlapping neighborhoods for neurons to be divisively normed over (with exponen-
451   tially decaying weights away from the center), while we define neighborhoods to be non-overlapping
452   (neurons only affect other neurons performing similar computation and in their neighborhood). Both
453
      of these formulations are of value in their own right (similar to requiring both functional segregation
      and coherent perception). We could create a more biologically plausible formulation of divisive
454
      normalization that would incorporate the contribution of recurrent amplification from higher visual
455
      areas, i.e., amplifying weak inputs more than strong inputs (Heeger & Zemlianova, 2020). Ad-
456   ditionally, it would be interesting to understand what effects divisive normalization has in vision
457   transformers (Vaswani, 2017), which have become state-of-the-art for image categorization tasks.
458   More specifically, divisive normalization can be used to explain how responses in the visual cortex
459   are modulated by attention, wherein attention multiplicatively enhances the stimulus drive before
460   normalization (Carandini & Heeger, 2012). Performing divisive normalization over the attention
461   weights of the transformer encoding layers might reveal new insights and serve as a good direction
462   for future work.
