009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011            Text-to-SQL enables users to interact with databases using natural language, sim-
012            plifying information retrieval. However, its widespread adoption remains limited
013            for two main reasons: (1) existing benchmarks focus solely on feasible questions
014            that can always be mapped to SQL queries, overlooking infeasible questions that
015            cannot, and (2) current models lack abstention mechanisms, posing the risk of
016            providing incorrect answers. To address these gaps, we introduce TrustSQL, a
017            new benchmark designed to evaluate text-to-SQL reliability. At its core is the
018            proposed Reliability Score (RS), which quantifies a model’s helpfulness (correct
019
               answers) relative to its harmfulness (incorrect answers weighted by a user-defined
               penalty). TrustSQL is constructed by re-annotating three datasets—ATIS, Advis-
020
               ing, and EHRSQL—while incorporating infeasible questions to enable compre-
021
               hensive evaluations across diverse model inputs. We evaluate text-to-SQL mod-
022            els integrated with various abstention mechanisms, leveraging classification and
023            uncertainty estimation methods. Our experiments reveal that only a few models
024            achieve positive scores (i.e., helpfulness outweighing harmfulness) under high-
025            penalty settings, indicating that most models are unsuitable for deployment in
026            safety-critical scenarios. This underscores the need to develop models that not
027            only improve SQL generation but also guarantee a certain degree of reliability,
028            ensuring safe deployment.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Relational databases store vast amounts of information and are fundamental to decision-making
034   in various domains such as healthcare (Hillestad et al., 2005), finance (Beck et al., 2000), and e-
035   commerce (Akter & Wamba, 2016). Text-to-SQL, the task of converting natural language questions
036   into SQL queries, has emerged as a powerful tool for enabling database question-answering (DBQA)
037   systems (Androutsopoulos et al., 1995; Zhong et al., 2017; Yu et al., 2018; Li et al., 2023). However,
038
      deploying such models in real-world settings requires assessing both the potential helpfulness and
      harmfulness they may introduce1 , in line with specific user safety requirements. The helpfulness of
039
      these models lies in their ability to handle input questions accurately—either by providing correct
040
      answers to questions that have ground-truth (GT) answers or by abstaining from answering ques-
041   tions with no GT answer (Rajpurkar et al., 2018). Conversely, harmfulness arises from inaccurate
042   handling—either by providing incorrect answers to questions that have GT answers or by failing to
043   abstain from answering questions that have no GT answer. Given this context, we consider a model
044   more reliable if the difference between its helpfulness and harmfulness exceeds that of other models,
045   making it more suitable for deployment.
046
      Current text-to-SQL benchmarks, however, fall short in assessing these aspects for two main rea-
047   sons: (1) Limited Scope of Questions: Most text-to-SQL models and benchmarks focus on ques-
048   tions that can be directly mapped to SQL queries (i.e., feasible questions). However, this limited
049   scope overlooks the variety of questions users may pose in real-world scenarios (see Figure 1a).
050   For example, questions that reference non-existent columns (e.g., “Which physician treated patient
051   1001?” when the physician column is missing) or contain ambiguous phrasing (e.g., “What’s the

142   Uncertainty Estimation in NLP Uncertainty estimation enhances AI system safety by quantify-
143   ing model confidence and identifying potential errors (Hendrycks et al., 2021). In natural language
144
      processing (NLP), uncertainty estimation methods can be categorized into four broad approaches:
      (1) Probability-based methods, which quantify uncertainty through probabilistic outputs, such as
145
      entropy or maximum softmax probabilities, providing a measure of confidence in model predictions
146
      (Xiao & Wang, 2021; Kim et al., 2022; Stengel-Eskin & Van Durme, 2023); (2) Distance-based
147   methods, which assess uncertainty by calculating the deviation of new inputs from the training data
148   distribution, often using metrics like the Mahalanobis distance between features to identify outliers
149   (Lee et al., 2018; Ren et al., 2021; 2022); (3) Sampling-based methods, which utilize techniques
150   such as Monte Carlo Dropout or Self-Consistency to generate multiple predictions for the same
151   input, quantifying uncertainty through the variance in model outputs (Gal & Ghahramani, 2016;
152   Wang et al., 2022); and (4) Text-based methods, which employ language models to generate both
153   task outputs and their corresponding confidence levels in natural language (Lin et al., 2022; Kada-
154   vath et al., 2022). While significant research has focused on quantifying uncertainty, its application
155
      to text-to-SQL has been less explored due to the absence of benchmarks addressing safety and model
      abstention. TrustSQL aims to bridge this gap by providing a concrete benchmark, enabling the ex-
156
      ploration and evaluation of various uncertainty estimation methods applied to text-to-SQL.

161   Evaluating the reliability of text-to-SQL models is essential to ensure their safe and effective use
      in real-world DBQA systems. These models can be potentially helpful or harmful, depending on

163   correct decisions. This includes generating correct SQL queries for feasible questions (denoted as
164   Q), returning results matching the ground-truth (GT) SQL query (denoted as S), or abstaining from
165   answering infeasible questions (denoted as Qc ) where no valid SQL query exists (represented by ϕ).
166   Conversely, a system becomes harmful when it makes incorrect decisions, such as generating incor-
167   rect SQL for feasible questions or attempting to answer infeasible questions where SQL generation

170   model reliability to answer if it is deployable or not. To this end, we introduce the Reliability Score

172   correct decisions) and its harmfulness (defined as the number of incorrect decisions, weighted by a
173   penalty factor c). The penalty factor c reflects how many correct decisions are needed to offset one
174   incorrect decision. The RS is calculated as follows:

      Model selection with RS A model can be considered potentially more harmful than helpful de-
189
      pending on the contexts, as reflected in the penalty term c. In fact, the choice of the penalty c is
190
      user-defined and depends on the safety requirements for model deployment, varying possibly based
191   on user preferences, SQL proficiency, or organizational policies. We provide a few practical starting
192   points to set the penalty and model selection guidelines:
193
194
      • Lenient scenario (c = 1): A single mistake carries the same weight as one correct decision. A
        positive RS here indicates the model makes more correct decisions than incorrect ones.
195
      • Moderate scenario (c = 10): Every 10 correct decisions carry the same weight as one incorrect
196
        decision, reflecting a moderate tolerance for errors.
197
      • Strict scenario (c = N/2): Two mistakes result in a negative RS, even if all other decisions (N –2
198
        out of N total cases) are correct. This scenario emphasizes high reliability.
199
      • Most strict scenario (c = N ): A single mistake results in a negative RS, even if all other decisions
200
        (N –1 cases) are correct. Models must avoid any mistakes to achieve a positive RS.
201
202   Once the safety requirement for model deployment is set, the best model can be selected based on
203   its RS performance. Below are key guidelines for model selection using the RS:
204   • Model comparison: A model’s superior performance under a specific penalty does not imply it
205     outperforms others across different penalty settings. Models should be compared within the same
206     penalty setting.
207   • Interpreting the score: Models with positive RS values are preferable for deployment, as they
208     meet safety requirements and demonstrate greater helpfulness than harmfulness. A model that
209     ranks the highest but achieves a negative RS should be reconsidered for deployment

220   4.1   DATABASE SELECTION
221
222   To construct TrustSQL, we first select databases based on two main criteria: (1) the presence of
223   complex schemas that mirror the intricacies of real-world databases, and (2) the availability of asso-
224   ciated question intents that reflect genuine user intents regarding their complex database. Based on
225   these criteria, we choose three domain-specific text-to-SQL datasets: ATIS (airline travel) (Hemphill
226   et al., 1990), Advising (education) (Finegan-Dollak et al., 2018), and EHRSQL (healthcare) (Lee
227
      et al., 2022). These datasets feature complex database schemas (ATIS: 25 tables; Advising: 12
      tables; EHRSQL: 17 tables) and capture real user needs that a DBQA system is likely to encounter.
228
229   We focus on these domain-specific datasets rather than including additional databases and question-
230   SQL pairs from large-scale, cross-domain datasets like Spider (Yu et al., 2018) or BIRD (Li et al.,
231   2023) for two main reasons: First, the nature of our task and the penalty-based scoring—which
232   amplifies the impact of incorrect model decisions—necessitates high-quality annotations. Template-
233
      free SQL annotations obtained through crowdsourcing cannot be scalably maintained in terms of
      data quality. Second, these large-scale datasets have other limitations for inclusion in our task.
234
      For example, Spider is largely solved by models like GPT-4o, with remaining errors often due to
235
      annotation issues rather than challenges in SQL generation. BIRD relies on ‘sample-level’ evidence,
236   which is incompatible with TrustSQL’s setup where not all input questions are text-to-SQL feasible.
237   TrustSQL uses ‘database-level’ evidence (i.e., SQL assumption text in Appendices A.1.1, A.1.2, and
238   A.1.3) shared across samples, removing the assumption that all input questions are feasible while
239   still enabling the text-to-SQL task by using evidence only when necessary.
240
241   4.2   F EASIBLE QUESTIONS
242
243   Constructing high-quality data is critical for accurately evaluating the reliability of text-to-SQL mod-
244   els in TrustSQL. To ensure the highest quality of annotations, the three authors (all proficient in SQL)
245   serve as annotators for creating the benchmark data, without relying on crowdsourcing. The annota-
246
      tion process involves the following steps: Template Review and Modification. One annotator thor-
      oughly reviews all question templates and SQL structure annotations across the three datasets (one
247
      question template corresponds to one unique SQL structure). Templates that are semantically identi-
248
      cal are merged, resulting in approximately 550 templates. Paraphrase Generation. Two annotators
249   generate new natural language questions for each question template using GPT-4o and review them
250   to ensure they coherently reflect the original question template. Additionally, the annotators check
251   whether the paraphrases contain any implicit assumptions related to their corresponding SQL struc-
252   tures and draft a SQL assumption text if dataset-level assumptions exist. Pair Construction. One
253   annotator merges the paraphrases with the question templates. This process involves inserting values
254   into both the question paraphrases and the SQL queries derived from the SQL structures. Review
255   and Quality Control. After merging question templates and paraphrases, all annotators engage in
256   real-time discussions to resolve any disagreements regarding the created question-SQL pairs and
257
      SQL assumption text (Appendices A.1.1, A.1.2, and A.1.3). Discussions continue until a consensus
      is reached. Further details are provided in Appendix A.2.

      4.4   DATA OVERVIEW
299
300   This section provides an overview of data in TrustSQL, detailing the distribution of feasible and
301   infeasible questions across different data splits.
302
303   Feasible Data Text-to-SQL model performance is influenced by several factors, including the
304   complexity of SQL queries (query difficulty) (Yu et al., 2018) and the presence of the same
305   SQL structures (but with differently paraphrased questions) in the training set (query familiarity)
306   (Finegan-Dollak et al., 2018). To better understand these factors, we tag each sample for both diffi-
307   culty and familiarity. Query difficulty. We classify SQL queries into three categories based on their
308   complexity, following the criteria introduced in DIN-SQL (Pourreza & Rafiei, 2023): easy (single-
309   table queries with no joins, nesting, or set operations), medium (queries with joins but no nesting
310   or set operations), and hard (queries with joins, subqueries, or set operations). Query familiarity.
311
      First, we split the data into training, validation, and test set evenly based on SQL structures, with
      each set containing disjoint structures. Since each structure is associated with multiple questions,
312
      we sample data from the training set without replacement and allocate it to both the validation and
313
      test sets. We refer to these sampled questions as familiar, because their corresponding structures can
314   be available to the model during fine-tuning or few-shot prompting. The questions whose structures
315   are not present in the training set are referred to as unfamiliar. Since feasible data questions are
316   created in a template-based manner, this process is possible and enables maintaining control over
317   the semantic distribution of questions across data splits.
318
319   Infeasible Data Infeasible questions are designed to simulate real-world text-to-SQL deployment
320   scenarios where user questions often do not correspond to any valid SQL queries. To ensure balanced
321   evaluation, we set the number of infeasible questions to match the number of feasible ones in the test
322   set. In task-oriented dialog systems, out-of-scope intents (i.e., infeasible questions) are deliberately
323   excluded from the training set Zheng et al. (2020); Marek et al. (2021). Following this practice, we
      include these questions only in the test set, reflecting unexpected encounters during deployment.

      5.1     M ODELS
344   To illustrate how reliable models can be constructed to tackle this benchmark, we implement text-to-
345   SQL models using both fine-tuning (FT) and in-context learning (ICL) with different model architec-
346   tures. Each architecture is integrated with two distinct styles of abstention mechanisms—Classifier-
347   based and Uncertainty-estimation-based—depending on their compatibility. The implementation
348   details are reported in Appendix C. Note that we treat any SQL generation that lacks a SELECT
349   clause as an abstention, even if no explicit abstention mechanisms are in place.
350
      • Classifier (CL)-based: These models operate sequentially, with task-specific sub-models func-
351     tioning in the following order: infeasible question detection, SQL generation, and SQL error
352     detection. Each sub-model is designed to be plug-and-play, offering high interpretability but also
353     posing the risk of error propagation between sub-models.
354   • Uncertainty-estimation (UE)-based: These models use a single model framework to determine
355     abstention decisions based on uncertainty estimation, resulting in a more streamlined implemen-
356     tation. However, this simplicity comes at the cost of interpretability and control, as the SQL
357     generation and abstention mechanisms are closely integrated.

402   5.2     E VALUATION M ETRICS
403
404   We apply three penalties to the RS: Φ1 , ΦN/2 , and ΦN . Additionally, we incorporate three metrics
405   from the selective prediction literature (El-Yaniv et al., 2010), adapted to our framework. These
406   metrics include: coverage on feasible questions (CQ ), the proportion of feasible questions that the
407
      model chooses to answer; risk on feasible questions (RQ ), the proportion of incorrect answers
      among the answered feasible questions; and risk on infeasible questions (RQc ), the proportion of
408
      answered infeasible questions. These metrics reflect the model’s performance in terms of the ratio
409
      of correctness, while the RS directly measures user-defined text-to-SQL reliability.

514   Q2: What is the advantage of using the RS? While coverage and risk provide an intuitive view of
515   a model’s performance in terms of the ratio of correctness, they alone may not be adequate measures
516   of text-to-SQL reliability. For example, in scenarios where occasional mistakes are acceptable,
517   models with higher coverage and some level of risk may be preferred. In this context, users can
518   apply a lower penalty, such as c = 1, and select models with higher scores in Φ1 (GPT-4 O +VOTE -
519   ANS). In contrast, high-stakes scenarios like healthcare demand much stricter control over incorrect
520   decisions. In such cases, models that show extremely low risk are prioritized, as even a single
521   incorrect decision could lead to dire consequences. By applying a stricter penalty, such as c = N ,
522
      the RS allows users to identify models that are more conservative but accurate in providing answers
      (GPT-4 O +I NF‡ +E RR‡ or GPT-4 O +VOTE -SQL). As a result, the coverage and risk required for a
523
      model to be helpful vary according to the user’s safety requirements, but the RS provides users with
524
      a consistent measure that is positive if the model is helpful.

527   7    C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this work, we introduce TrustSQL, a benchmark designed to evaluate the reliability of text-to-
530   SQL models, prioritizing models that demonstrate helpfulness while minimizing harmfulness. To
531
      quantify this, we also propose a new metric, the Reliability Score (RS), which assigns positive
      scores for correct decisions to represent helpfulness and penalizes incorrect ones according to a user-
532
      defined safety penalty that accounts for harmfulness. TrustSQL is constructed by re-annotating three
533
      datasets—ATIS, Advising, and EHRSQL—and augmenting them with critical types of infeasible
534   questions that may arise during deployment, enabling a comprehensive evaluation across diverse
535   user inputs. We evaluate text-to-SQL models integrated with abstention mechanisms using both
536   classifier-based and uncertainty estimation methods. Our experimental results show that only a few
537   models achieve positive scores in the RS across all penalty settings, underscoring the need to not
538   only improve SQL generation but also ensure a certain degree of reliability. We hope TrustSQL can
539   shed light on developing more reliable text-to-SQL models, thus accelerating their adoption across
      vast industry areas in need.
