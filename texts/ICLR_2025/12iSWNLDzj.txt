                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               Recent studies have demonstrated that modern facial recognition systems, which
               are based on deep neural networks, are vulnerable to adversarial attacks, includ-
011
               ing the use of accessories, makeup patterns, or precision lighting. However, de-
012
               veloping attacks that are both robust (resilient to changes in viewing angles and
013            environmental conditions) and stealthy (do not attract suspicion by, for example,
014            incorporating obvious facial features) remains a significant challenge. In this con-
015            text, we introduce a novel diffusion-based method (DAFR) capable of generating
016            robust and stealthy face masks for dodging recognition systems (where the system
017            fails to identify the attacker). Specifically our approach is capable of producing
018            high-fidelity printable textures using the guidance of textual prompts to determine
019            the style. This method can also be adapted for impersonation purposes, where
020            the system misidentifies the attacker as a specific other individual. Finally, we
021
               address a gap in the existing literature by presenting a comprehensive benchmark
               (FAAB) for evaluating adversarial accessories in three dimensions, assessing their
022
               robustness and stealthiness.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Facial recognition systems have increasing prominence, with applications in a range of environ-
028
      ments. Importantly, these systems aim to accurately classify an individual when presented with an
029
      image of them, hence, adversarial attacks against such systems are important to identify and explore.
030   Deep learning facial recognition systems, the state of the art technique for biometric identification
031   (Vakhshiteh et al., 2021), have a history of said attacks, causing the systems to behave in an unin-
032   tended manner when presented with images that have been carefully modified by attacks.
033
      Previous studies on the matter have used a plethora of both attack surfaces and techniques to mis-
034
      direct these systems into misclassifying individuals. Some explore attacks by digitally perturbing
035
      images of faces (Lin et al., 2023), whilst others use makeup (Yin et al., 2021; Sun et al., 2024) or
036   accessories (Sharif et al., 2019; Komkov & Petiushko, 2021; Zolfi et al., 2022; Gong et al., 2024;
037   Pautov et al., 2019; Xiao et al., 2021). Traditionally, gradient descent based approaches have been
038   employed to generate accessories, to much success (Zolfi et al., 2022); however, whilst these achieve
039   robustness to changes in viewing angles and environmental conditions, they lack in stealthiness –
040   the need for the attacks to be undetectable by human observers.
041
      Many developments have been made to this regard in order to balance the adversarial strength of
042   an attack with the style and realism of the perturbations. Various loss functions have been explored
043   such as total variation loss (Mahendran & Vedaldi, 2015) which makes the perturbations smoother,
044   making an attack more stable, realistic and robust to interpolation techniques (Komkov & Petiushko,
045   2021; Zolfi et al., 2022). Other work has used style extractors, L1 losses with a reference style, to
046   make a style adapt to an attack in order to encourage the generation of a stealthy accessory that would
047   not raise suspicion in the real world (Gong et al., 2024). A common struggle with these approaches
048   is generating perturbations that look stealthy consistently, especially against larger facial recognition
049   models such as those based on ResNet (He et al., 2016). When attacks are not attempting to max-
050
      imize stealthiness, the final perturbations often contain facial features and noise-like perturbations.
      On the other hand, when attacks prioritize stealthiness, their efficacy is significantly reduced.
051
052   Recent literature for general adversarial attacks have propagated towards the use of generative mod-
053   els to support the generation of realistic adversarial examples and perturbations. These methods
      use a pretrained model to produce or manipulate an adversarial sample towards a given style. Song

055   examples than were possible with perturbation based methods. Alternatively, diffusion models have

057   Dai et al., 2023) and have several desirable properties for this task, such as greater interpretability,
058   controllability and visual fidelity in the produced samples (Dai et al., 2023).

080   In summary, our main contributions are:
081
082       • A novel diffusion-based stealthy adversarial face mask generation method, titled DAFR, which
083         uses adversarial guidance to produce adversarial textures that retain the content of the reference
084         images and that can be styled using text prompts. The resulting generated face masks are stealthy,
085
            robust to environmental changes, and comparable to previous work.
086       • A robust benchmarking framework, called FAAB, that includes a set of standardized tests and
087         procedures to evaluate the performance of accessories. The framework supports frequently used
088         statistics like cosine distances, success rates, and a new metric that we discuss later that is based

            the modular design of the benchmark allows each component to be easily interchanged in order
090
            to suit each attack’s objective.

093   2      DAFR: D IFFUSION ATTACK AGAINST FACIAL R ECOGNITION
094
095   Facial Recognition: Modern facial recognition networks are often Siamese networks (Bromley
096   et al., 1993) that are designed to work with a large number of classes and with potentially unseen
097   identities during testing (Wen et al., 2016). These models can be split into two components: the
098   backbone and head. The backbone takes in an image and outputs the embedding of that image in
099
      the learnt feature space, which can then be fed into a head for final classification. The embedding
      spaces are trained to be discriminative and to be effective for multiple different heads for different
100
      recognition problems. Recent adversarial work focuses on attacking the backbone directly rather
101
      than the head (Vakhshiteh et al., 2021; Zolfi et al., 2022; Gong et al., 2024) and we follow suit. A
102   further discussion of facial recognition systems can be found in appendix B.
103
104   DAFR: The objective of DAFR is to produce textures for face masks that are not only adversarial,
105   but stealthy as well. To do this, the reverse diffusion process of a diffusion model can be manipulated
106   such that the final output, ma , looks like the output if the reverse process was not manipulated (i.e, is
107   stealthy) and is adversarial. For a texture to be adversarial, it must have a low cosine similarity with
      the anchor embedding, ea , of the attacker and preferably be under a recognition threshold such that


                                                           2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      the network would not recognize the masked image as the attacker, this is called dodging. If the
109   similarity is maximized with the anchor of a specific other identity, then it is called impersonation.
110   We focus on dodging, but our attack can be adapted for impersonation too.
111
112
      It is well documented that adversarial patches (and thus adversarial accessories too) must consider
      different real world transformations during generation so that the resulting accessories would exhibit
113
      robustness to these transformations when they do occur (Athalye et al., 2018). To achieve this, we
114
      generate our face masks using a set of images of the attacker, H, and optimize over these. Moreover,
115   since face masks are 3D objects, to generate physically realizable 3D masks, the generated textures
116   must be rendered onto the face during generation so the generation conditions match the real world.
117
118
      One way to control the generation of a diffusion model is classifier guidance where the scores (the
      gradient of the log of a function) of a classifier are used during generation to perform conditional
119
      generation (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021). AdvDiff is a recent diffusion-based adversarial attack that
120
      uses adversarial guidance (Dai et al., 2023), based on classifier guidance, to control the generation
121   of a class conditional latent diffusion model (LDM, Rombach et al. (2022)) to generate unrestricted
122   adversarial examples for ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009). We fuse together adversarial guidance and
123   3D rendering to allow for a more advanced procedure to generate samples that can act as textures
124   for stealthy adversarial masks, as demonstrated in algorithm 1. Since we use LDM’s, adversarial
125   guidance is applied to latents, not on the pixel level. f is defined in equation (1).

151   Additionally, we needed to have a 3D differentiable rendering pipeline so that the texture could not
152   only be rendered onto a 3D face mask, but also have gradients from the target network backpropagate
153   through it. Zolfi et al. (2022) developed such a pipeline as long as the texture could be fit into a 2D
154   UV mask, shown in figure 2, which we use in DAFR. We find that optimal performance occurs
155   when the texture is resized to fit most of the content within the UV mask, allowing the perturbation
156   to manifest across the majority of the area in the sample.
157   Generating images that follow text prompts that are also adversarial to facial recognition networks
158   is more abstract (and thus more challenging) than generating samples that use class conditionals of
159   the target network to make it look like another one of the classes – this is without considering the
160   challenges relating to the generated image being a texture applied to a variety of different images,
161   rather than being the final example itself with no concerns for any other image. By optimizing for

      multiple distinct images, the aim is so that when the texture is applied to a mask on an unknown
163   image, the mask will be robust to environmental conditions and remain adversarial.
164
165   This led to the introduction of multiple mechanisms to control the adversarial elements of genera-
166
      tion which emphasize different aspects of texture generation. Firstly, we introduced an inner loop
      which increased the number of steps of adversarial guidance done per time step (controlled by k
167
      in algorithm 1). Having a sufficient number of adversarial steps is important due to the importance
168
      of optimizing the texture to work in different conditions. Secondly, we manipulate how early in
169   the reverse process we begin adversarial guidance (controlled by l in algorithm 1) which allows for
170   more adversarial steps, thus better robustness and adversarial strength.
171
172
      Thirdly, we use DDIM sampling (Song et al., 2021) which allows for a variable number of sampling
      steps in the time schedule (controlled by T in algorithm 1). Finally, the step size of each adver-
173
      sarial step (controlled by s in algorithm 1) can be changed to influence generation. We find that a
174
      constant step size throughout the entire generation leads to adverse perturbations in the later steps
175   of generation, where the noise schedule varies significantly less. We introduce a scaling function in
176   equation (1) to slowly decrease the step size based on the proportion of the time schedule left.
177

      Baselines: To evaluate our accessories, we compare them to recent adversarial face mask attacks.
190
      Adversarial Mask (Zolfi et al., 2022), shortened to AdvMask for brevity, generates face masks for
191   dodging by using a 3D differentiable pipeline and optimizing the mask to be adversarial while main-
192   taining a low TV loss. SASMask (Gong et al., 2024) generates face masks for impersonation so that
193   given content is included (e.g., flowers); however, uses a style transfer network to change the style
194   to be optimal (e.g., by changing the colour). AdvMask does not attempt to be faithful to a style so
195   we do not report our stealthiness measure for those masks, while SASMask does so we do for them.
196   We also test a white non-adversarial face mask to act as a non-adversarial baseline for comparison.
197

      a variety of celebrities, and is where the identities for the dodging benchmark come from, and
199
      VGGFACE2-HQ (Chen et al., 2024), which contains GAN upscaled images of the VGGFACE2
200
      dataset (Cao et al., 2018). We randomly choose 100 identities from VGGFACE2-HQ to form part

      Results: The results in table 3 show that DAFR is consistently outperforming previous work in
      stealthiness, reflected by having a lower M-CMMD than previous stealthy mask attacks, which can
300
      be visually confirmed in figure 4. In the majority of cases, especially for FT100 in table 3 and
301
      MobileFaceNet in table 3, DAFR has managed to accomplish the dual goal of creating adversarial
302   masks that are effective and stealthy. This is due to the adversarial generation process being able to
303   find adversarial textures that do not deviate substantially from the original generation. We believe
304   this is significant progress as no other work to date has managed to preserve the style and content of
305   a given reference image for adversarial accessories as effectively as ours. This can be seen further
306   in the appendices D and E where the style generation capabilities are tested further.
307
      When attacks do not consider stealthiness (such as AdvMask in table 3), then the adversarial strength
308   of the masks is strong, but still not 100% against some of the networks like FT100 and R100. This
309   demonstrates the challenging threat model of adversarial accessories, where critical facial features
310   for recognition can not be manipulated, therefore on unseen images it is difficult to cover every
311   possible transformation. Figure 4 presents some of the masks generated by AdvMask which do not
312   achieve a perfect success rate despite focusing on such.
313
      FT100 was able to achieve the highest TPR on the 100 class problem while achieiving a FAR of 0.01
314   on unmasked images (table 1). Despite this, FT100 (table 3) is vulnerable to DAFR’s capability to
315   produce very stealthy masks while not sacrificing much adversarial strength, compared to AdvMask.
316   This should inform real world decisions to avoid reckless use of such technology as it can appear on
317   the surface to be outstanding while being incredibly vulnerable to adversaries.

361   4             FAAB: FACE ACCESSORY ATTACK B ENCHMARK
362
363
364   For an adversarial accessory to be considered robust, it is necessary that it is effective in various
365   environmental conditions including lighting, backgrounds and angles. This is not just an important
366   factor during generation, but also when evaluating an accessory’s performance. As the results in sec-
367   tion 3 demonstrate, the balance between adversarial strength and stealth has a significant impact on
368
      the attack success and so it is crucial to evaluate these factors in order to fairly compare approaches.
369   To date, there is no standardized framework for testing adversarial face accessories (Vakhshiteh
370   et al., 2021), in part due to the varying threat models and attack objectives for work in the field.
371   Whilst benchmarking frameworks exist for neighboring fields, such as GREAT score (Li et al.,
372   2023) for evaluating general adversarial perturbations using generative models, within the realm
373   of adversarial accessories there are inconsistent experimental frameworks that create hard to com-
374
      pare results. Therefore, we propose a highly adaptable benchmarking framework, titled the Face
      Accessory Attack Benchmark (FAAB), that is capable of consistent and systematic comparison of
375
      different attack methods.
376
377   To achieve this feat, FAAB uses a systematic procedure for evaluating accessories, with interchange-
      able components, detailed below:


                                                                       7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
          • Firstly, an accessory must be generated by the attack being tested for a given individual. It is
379         important at this stage that images used to generate the accessory are not those that will be later
380         used to evaluate, as a strong adversarial accessory should be effective in unseen conditions.
381
          • Once the accessory has been generated, the testing phase begins. This consists of loading in
382
            dataset images, placing the accessory on the images using the augmentation method specified by
383         the attack and computing the output of the recognition system.
384
          • Next, we calculate statistics based on adversarial strength and the accessory itself. The statistics
385
            calculated are interchangeable and new statistics can be added. By default, we compute the
386         embedding of the backbone network as well as recording the angle of the face within each
387         picture – figure 5 demonstrates the variety of poses tested. Alongside computing statistics based
388         on the attack, we compute statistics on the accessory.
389
          • In order to get a holistic view of the performance of an attack method as a whole, we repeat the
390         above steps over multiple individuals. To further expand analysis, FAAB supports benchmark
391         variations which can modify how images are augmented. For example, we can ensure that the
392         accessory brightness matches that of the image to test different lighting conditions.
393       • Once all the accessories have been tested, the final stage of the benchmark is to group statis-
394         tics together based on the properties recorded throughout. This allows us to view the statistics
395         recorded, for example, for each individual, or for images where the individual is looking straight
396         on. How statistics are grouped is entirely customizable and can help discover links between
397         various properties that have been accumulated in the benchmark.
398
399   As alluded to above, it is necessary to quantize how stealthy an accessory is as it is infeasible to
400
      construct a manner of evaluating stealthiness through the means of a user survey in such a way as
      to not introduce bias to one attack and to be fair, especially when the definition of stealthiness itself
401
      is often subjective. In section 5 we outline why we believe that CMMD is an effective measure of
402
      stealthiness and the resulting values are discussed in section 3. Further principles to evaluating style
403   that could be applied to other work are provided in appendix C.

421   5      R ELATED W ORKS
422
423
      Here we discuss the closest previous works; we comparatively review further literature across several
      areas in appendix B.
424
425
      Patch-based Adversarial Attacks On Facial Recognition: Adversarial accessories are small
426   wearables that contain patterns that when placed within an image cause malicious behavior. Pre-
427   vious adversarial accessories have varied significantly in the generation process and in the type of
428   accessory, including glasses (Sharif et al., 2019), hats (Komkov & Petiushko, 2021), face patches
429   (Pautov et al., 2019), eye patches (Xiao et al., 2021) and face masks (Zolfi et al., 2022; Gong et al.,
430   2024). As mentioned in section 1, face masks have seen an increase in usage within the general
431   public and are a prime adversarial accessory as they cover a substantial area of the face (Zolfi et al.,
      2022; Gong et al., 2024), hence they are our chosen accessory type.


                                                           8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      Most adversarial accessory attacks primarily focus on the accessory being adversarial (Sharif et al.,
433   2019; Pautov et al., 2019; Komkov & Petiushko, 2021) or focus on emphasizing additional proper-
434   ties such as transferability (Xiao et al., 2021; Gong et al., 2024; Zolfi et al., 2022). However, the
435   generated accessories from these works do not look like “normal” attire and would arouse suspicion
436   if worn in the real world – we would consider these to not be stealthy. Gong et al. (2024) attempts
437   to explicitly generate stealthy face masks using adaptive styles and style losses, but these cause the
438   texture to depart from a reference image significantly. We focus primarily on stealthiness and argue
439   that for a mask to be stealthy, it must look similar to a reference image. To the best of our knowledge,
440   we are the first work to utilize diffusion models to generate adversarial accessories.
441
      Quantitative Measures Of Style: Within adversarial attacks on facial recognition, some style
442   measures that have been used before in makeup attacks (Sun et al., 2024) include SSIM (Wang et al.,
443   2004), PSNR and FID (Heusel et al., 2017). Gong et al. (2024) used SSIM in a setup specific to
444   their face mask which is hard to transfer to other attacks. Creating metrics to evaluate the quality of
445   generated images is a problem faced by generative models and stealthy adversarial accessories that
446
      generate textures can be seen as generators that have a baseline style (a “real” set) which can generate
      multiple adversarial textures (a “generated” set). CLIP Maximum Mean Discrepancy (CMMD)
447   (Jayasumana et al., 2024) is a recent metric proposed to measure the quality of generated images
448   by finding the maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) (Gretton et al., 2006; 2012) between CLIP
449   (Radford et al., 2021) embeddings of a real and generated set of images. An unbiased estimator of
450   MMD on two sets of CLIP embeddings, X = {x1 , x2 , ..., xm } and Y = {y1 , y2 , ..., yn }, and kernel
451
      k (for which we use a RBF kernel) can be given by the equation below. For the results in this paper,
      we scaled the output of CMMD for display purposes, using the same values as in the original paper
452   (Jayasumana et al., 2024).
453

454                              1     X                      1     X                    2 XX
       dist2M M D (X, Y ) =                  k(xi , xj ) +                k(yi , yj ) −            k(xi , yj )
455                           m(m − 1) i=1                 n(n − 1) i=1                 mn i=1 j=1
                                            j̸=i                          j̸=i
456
457   By using CLIP embeddings, CMMD is able to provide a more holistic evaluation of style and has
458   been found to outperform FID and other common metrics when compared to human raters (Jaya-
459   sumana et al., 2024) which resonates back to the user surveys in previous accessory work (Sharif
460   et al., 2019). We believe future work should also use CMMD as a metric to evaluate the quality
461   (thus stealthiness) of their generated textures and so having an evaluation that is similar to how
462   image generators are evaluated. More details about how we use CMMD are found in section 4.

464   6   C ONCLUSION
465
466   We propose a novel diffusion-based attack, DAFR, for adversarial mask generation that generates
467   masks that are both adversarial and stealthy. We demonstrate the effectiveness of the attack on a
468   range of architectures and threat models, and highlight the challenges in attacking these models.
469   Moreover, we propose a robust standardized benchmarking framework, FAAB, for evaluating the
470   strength and stealthiness of these attacks such that comparisons between future work can be quicker,
471   robust, and fair. This further invites future work to use this framework to create strong and stealthy
472
      adversarial accessories.
473
      Limitations: We have tested different attacks on a variety of networks and have found that the be-
474   havior of the attacks can vary significantly between networks. This unfortunately means that DAFR
475   can struggle to produce stealthy adversarial masks on the strongest networks. Additionally, DAFR
476   uses adversarial guidance (Dai et al., 2023) and is sensitive to the hyperparameters highlighted in
477   table 2. Small changes can lead to significant variation in the output and their values must be ad-
478   justed for different target networks. This requires manual testing to balance the stealthiness of the
479   generated mask and its stealthiness.
480
481   Future Work: Generating stealthy masks on stronger networks is difficult and future work could
482
      expand the number of networks these masks are stealthy for. Finally, all adversarial face mask
      attacks are inherently vulnerable to removal by generative based defenses. Given the weights of a
483
      face mask removal network, future work could generate masks that are adversarial to the recognition
484
      network and the removal network to mitigate this weakness.

      7   E THICS S TATEMENT
541
542
      Deep learning based facial recognition and verification systems are becoming more prominent
543   around the world. On one hand, DAFR highlights new security risks to existing face recognition
544   and verification systems by creating masks that are indistinguishable from more colorful masks peo-
545   ple wear; which could undermine their efficacy and the trust put in them. However, by demonstrating
546   these capabilities, future defenses and adversarial training schemes will have to consider these types
547   of accessories thus allowing future work to defend against DAFR or a more advanced version of
548   it. On the other hand, these powerful systems can be misused by different institutions and the exis-
549   tence of these accessories demonstrate that these systems are not flawless and can be manipulated in
550   certain circumstances.

      Table 1 demonstrates the performance of the different target networks. Pretrained backbones have
867   been trained to have a highly discriminative embedding space across a wide range of datasets, rather
868   than a separable one across one dataset. This leads them to perform incredibly well on unseen
869   faces and to have the highest TPR in table 1. FT100 was trained for the 100 class scenario and
870   therefore performs well, but then struggles on 1000 classes. This threat model has not been explicitly
871   explored before (with previous work performing further training on their models (Gong et al., 2024))
872   and highlights vulnerabilities to these models if deployed recklessly. FaRL is a general purpose
873   face encoding and so has not been explicitly trained for recognition, explaining the lower TPR. On
874   the other hand, MFN has been trained for recognition but we expect its smaller size may limit its
875
      performance. Despite this, MFN performs the best out of all the networks tested at not being fooled
      by the non-adversarial mask when using the 100 class threshold and second best when using the
876
      1000 class threshold, demonstrating that it is still an effective network, shown in table 3.
877
878
879   B    E XTENDED R ELATED W ORKS
880
881   Facial Recognition: Facial recognition systems have evolved significantly over the last couple of
882   decades, with the state of the art approaches using deep learning models that are able to achieve
883   high accuracy in both large and small class sizes. Traditionally, for a small number of classes in a
884   closed-set environment (that is the test set consists only of identities from within the training set),
885
      softmax-based approaches that are used in general object recognition can be effective. However,
      softmax losses encourage the learned features to be separable, but not necessarily discriminative
886
      (Wen et al., 2016), leading to worse performance when there are lots of classes of data or in an
887
      open-set environment, where the test set includes identities not in the training set (Liu et al., 2017).
888
889   Focus has moved to using Siamese networks (Bromley et al., 1993) where the backbone learns a
890   discriminative, rather than separable, embedding space through different losses such as center loss
891
      (Wen et al., 2016) or triplet loss (Schroff et al., 2015). More recent work has focused on maximizing
      the angular margins of learnt class centers in a learnt embedding space, such that embeddings from
892
      a given class’ center point are in a similar direction and that embeddings not from that center’s class,
893
      point in a different direction (Liu et al., 2017). Several works have aimed for intra-class compactness
894   and inter-class discrepancy with the aim of learning a discriminative embedding space (Liu et al.,
895   2017; Wang et al., 2018; Deng et al., 2019a).
896
897   Adversarial Examples Using Generative Models: Traditionally, adversarial examples were gen-
898   erated using gradient based methods such that the perturbation has a small matrix norm; one example
899   is using projected gradient descent (PGD) (Madry et al., 2018). However, Song et al. (2018) used
900   generative models (specifically generative adversarial networks) to construct unrestricted adversarial
901   examples that exhibit greater realism. This has progressed so that recently there have been several
902
      works proposing diffusion model based adversarial attacks using different techniques (Xue et al.,
      2023; Chen et al., 2023; Dai et al., 2023). Using diffusion models for this task has several benefits,
903
      most notably, greater controllability and visual fidelity of generated samples (Dai et al., 2023), areas
904
      in which previous adversarial accessories struggled. Our attack, therefore, leverages these properties
905   through the use of a textually controlled diffusion model.
906
907   Patch-based Adversarial Attacks And Defenses: Adversarial patches (Brown et al., 2017) are
908   small patterns that when placed within an input image, cause unintentional behavior in a network.
909   To improve the robustness of these patches to real world conditions, past work has shown that it
910   is necessary to incorporate real world transformations into the generation process (Athalye et al.,
911   2018). In the context of adversarial accessories, this translates to generating on different images of
912   a person, for example at different poses, such that different transformations are considered during
913   accessory generation.
914   From the perspective of defending adversarial attacks, recent defenses have utilized diffusion models
915   (Nie et al., 2022) for purification of adversarial examples, removing the perturbation while maintain-
916   ing the original content. For adversarial patches, these techniques have been found to be inadequate,
917   therefore, specific adversarial patch defenses have been developed (Kang et al., 2023). These de-
      fenses use diffusion models to locate the patch and then replace it using inpainting, which could be


                                                        17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      used to replace a face mask with an estimated face. Another defense would be to remove face masks
919   from images using generative models trained to do so (Kumar et al., 2023). All current adversarial
920   face masks are vulnerable to these last two defenses and so creating robustness to these defenses is
921   not within the scope of our work and could be the goal of future work.
922
923
      Other Attacks On Facial Recognition: There have also been other attacks on these systems such
924
      as adversarial makeup (Yin et al., 2021) which has been used so that the attack can access a wider
925
      area of the face rather than just a local patch. Recent work has used diffusion models to enhance
926   this approach further (Sun et al., 2024) creating highly realistic makeup to fool these models. These
927   attacks have a significantly larger area of the face to attack and may be difficult to physically realize
928   compared to face masks.
929
      Another channel of attack is using visible light (Shen et al., 2019; Nguyen et al., 2020; Li et al.,
930
      2020) where perturbations are projected onto the face. These attacks offer a different representation
931
      to their perturbations which presents unique challenges which could also be explored with diffusion
932   models in a similar fashion to our work.
933
934   Backdoor attacks on face recognition have also been developed, where the system behaves as ex-
935
      pected on clean input but then has been modified to behave erroneously on malicious input. These
      attacks can be split into three components: the attack channel (the attacker’s knowledge and access
936
      to the victim model), the injection method (how the manipulation can occur) and the trigger method
937
      (what triggers the corruption) (Roux et al., 2024). One such example work has directly manipu-
938   lated weights such that only certain identities are misclassified, but the rest are unaffected (Zehavi
939   & Shamir, 2023).
940
941
      Another type of attack are those that poison the training set of a victim model such that when the
      attacker wears a physical accessory, the model erroneously classifies them (Chen et al., 2017).
942
943   These methods have a different threat model to our work, but are a different avenue of work that
944   could be expanded using diffusion models.

      C    E VALUATING S TYLES
948
949   Previous Style Metrics: As previously discussed, most adversarial accessory work has not fo-
950
      cused on stealth and so there is a limited range of quantitative measures for stealthiness. Previous
      work has used TV loss to ensure accessories are color smooth, making them easier to physically
951
      realize and less noise like, but often stealthiness is not explicitly measured after generation (Zolfi
952
      et al., 2022; Komkov & Petiushko, 2021; Pautov et al., 2019).
953
954   Stealthiness is a subjective measure so an ideal method would be to collect user surveys, as has been
955   done before (Sharif et al., 2019) where participants were asked to identity whether given images
956
      of glasses were “real” or generated. While this does gather valuable user opinion, these surveys
      are time consuming to run, potentially hard to reproduce when ran on a small scale and may not
957
      accurately measure stealthiness (as the concept is abstract to the general public). Some measures
958
      that have been used before in makeup attacks (Sun et al., 2024) are SSIM (Wang et al., 2004), PSNR
959   and FID (Heusel et al., 2017). In recent stealthy mask work, Gong et al. (2024) measure the SSIM
960   of masked faces with the mask texture being the original pattern in the style of their adversarial
961   pattern and then comparing these images to masked faces with the adversarial pattern. Whilst these
962   measures are able to yield valuable statistics about a generated accessory, we believe these do not
963   capture the true essence of stealthiness – a better metric would be one which determines the quality
964   of generated images. This can be achieved by treating the adversarial attack as an image generator
965   and using similar metrics to measure its performance such as CMMD.
966
967   Proper Use Of CMMD: When choosing images for CMMD evaluation for general accessory
968   evaluation in future work, we recommend trying to evaluate on as close of a representation as the
969   texture in the final accessory while avoiding any faces being in the images (such as the images in
970   figure 4). Furthermore, the reference set for the style should be one image representing the style
971   the textures in the generated set are attempting to create. The generated set should contain multiple
      textures from different attacks (i.e different attackers/targets) of the same style.
