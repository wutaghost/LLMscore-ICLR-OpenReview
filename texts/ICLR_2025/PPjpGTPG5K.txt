011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013               The Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) paradigm has emerged as a promising approach
014
                  for scaling transformer-based large language models (LLMs) with improved re-
                  source utilization. However, efficiently fine-tuning MoE LLMs remains largely
015
                  underexplored. Inspired by recent works on Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning
016
                  (PEFT), we present a unified framework for integrating PEFT modules into MoE
017
                  LLMs. Our framework, aligned with the core mechanisms of MoE, encompasses
018
                  a comprehensive set of design dimensions including various functional and com-
019               position strategies. By combining the key design choices within our framework,
020               we introduce Parameter-Efficient Routed Fine-Tuning (PERFT) as a flexible and
021               scalable family of PEFT strategies tailored for MoE LLMs.1 Extensive experi-
022               ments adapting OLMoE-1B-7B and Mixtral-8×7B for various commonsense and
023               arithmetic reasoning tasks demonstrate the effectiveness, scalability, and intrigu-
024               ing dynamics of PERFT. Additionally, we provide empirical findings for each
025               specific design choice to facilitate better application of MoE and PEFT.
026
027   1       I NTRODUCTION
028
029   As modern transformer-based Vaswani et al. (2017) large language models (LLMs) continue to scale
030   up, Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) (Shazeer et al., 2017) has emerged in recent years as a promising
031   solution to the trade-off between performance and cost, yielding notable results in a series of frontier
032   models (Jiang et al., 2024; Reid et al., 2024; Dai et al., 2024; Qwen, 2024; Grok, 2024). With
033   so many new MoE LLMs available, how to effectively fine-tune them for downstream tasks has be-
034   come an area of considerable value. The advancements of MoE do not directly translate to efficiency
035
      in their fine-tuning, and full fine-tuning these models remains prohibitively expensive due to their
      immense number of expert parameters. Besides, the routing mechanism among sparsely-activated
036
      experts poses unique challenges unseen in conventional dense architectures (Wang et al., 2024).
037
      This necessitates exploring solutions specially-designed for efficiently adapting sparse MoE models,
038   without incurring the full cost of fine-tuning all parameters.
039
040   Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) techniques, such as adapters (Houlsby et al., 2019) and
041
      LoRA (low-rank adaptation; Hu et al., 2022), have gained considerable attention on conventional
      dense models. Combining hybrid elements from different PEFT methods have also shown promising
042
      results (He et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023). With the rise of MoE architectures,
043
      recent studies have explored PEFT solutions for dense models with MoE-inspired designs (Zadouri
044   et al., 2023; Dou et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024).
045   However, designing PEFT strategies tailored for MoE models remains largely underexplored.
046
047
      To this end, we present the first unified framework for incorporating diverse PEFT modules di-
      rectly into the MoE mechanism. Different from previous PEFT solutions that operate in isolation
048
      from the underlying MoE architecture, our framework is designed closely around the unique routing
049
      mechanisms among experts in MoE models. We introduce two key design dimensions. Functional
050
      strategies define the internal mechanisms of the introduced PEFT module, including the architec-
051   ture inside individual PEFT modules, the multiplicity of PEFT modules, and the routing mechanism
052   among them. Compositional strategies describe how PEFT modules interact with the original MoE

      Extensive experiments are conducted on OLMoE-1B-7B (Muennighoff et al., 2024) and Mixtral-
      8×7B (Jiang et al., 2024) for commonsense and math reasoning tasks. Our results demonstrate that
082
      PERFT enables different levels of efficient adaptation of MoE LLMs while maintaining competitive
083
      performance. With an equivalent level of activated trainable parameters in OLMoE-1B-7B, PERFT-
084   R achieves improvements of up to 17.2% and 12.3% over the average performance of MoE-agnostic
085   baseline methods in each domain. We also demonstrate and empirically analyze our observations
086   for the optimal scaling, sparsity, and routing configurations that generalize across settings. We hope
087   to provide practical insights for improving future MoE and PEFT approaches, and contribute to the
088   understanding of adaptation strategies for modern large-scale LLMs.
089
      The primary contributions of our work are as follows:
090
091       1. We introduce a unified framework of PEFT techniques tailored for MoE LLMs. This encom-
092          passes multiple dimensions of design strategies, offering a novel perspective.
093       2. By combining the design choices within this unified framework, we propose PERFT as a
094          flexible and scalable family of strategies for adapting MoE LLMs.
095       3. Extensive experiments adapting OLMoE-1B-7B and Mixtral-8×7B for commonsense and
096          arithmetic reasoning tasks validate the effectiveness, scalability, and intriguing dynamics of
097          PERFT. We provide empirical findings and analysis for each specific design choice.

      Mixture-of-Experts. As a viable solution to the computational challenges in scaling models and
      improving specialization, early forms of MoE were introduced (Jacobs et al., 1991; Jordan & Jacobs,
113
      1994; Eigen et al., 2013; Shazeer et al., 2017). In the era of transformers, studies have revealed that
114
      FFNs, with two-thirds of the model parameter, encapsulate a substantial amount of knowledge (Geva
115   et al., 2021; Dai et al., 2022) that can be attributed to sparsely represented features (Dalvi et al.,
116   2019; Durrani et al., 2020; Gurnee et al., 2023). Leveraging this internal sparsity, MoE architectures
117   can achieve better resource utilization by activating only a subset of effective parameters for each
118   input (Liu et al., 2023b), which has since been successfully applied to transformer-based language
119   models (Lepikhin et al., 2020; Du et al., 2022; Fedus et al., 2022; Zoph et al., 2022a; Komatsuzaki
120   et al., 2022; Rajbhandari et al., 2022; Jiang et al., 2024; Dai et al., 2024; Qwen, 2024; Grok, 2024).
121   Modern MoE architectures employ token-wise gating network (router) G(·), which dynamically
122   assigns each token to K of top-activated experts among N FFN experts Ei (·):

      be interpreted as a set of D-dimensional column vectors {gi |i ∈ 1, · · · , N }, each corresponding to a

129   (Zhou et al., 2022; Dikkala et al., 2023; Lo et al., 2024), which are subsequently top-K thresholded
130   to yield expert selection results for each token. Notably, recent works (Gou et al., 2023; Dai et al.,
131   2024; Qwen, 2024) have explored shared experts that structurally mirror routed experts, working in
132   parallel with them and always remaining activated for capturing common knowledge.

134   2.2   PARAMETER -E FFICIENT F INE - TUNING FOR T RANSFORMER - BASED M ODEL
135   Vanilla PEFT. Classical full fine-tuning approaches for downstream tasks (Devlin et al., 2019; Qiu
136   et al., 2020) have become increasingly impractical as transformers continue scaling up. Recent work
137   has introduced diverse PEFT methods offering comparable performance to full fine-tuning with sig-
138   nificantly reduced computational demands. He et al. (2022) present a unified view for PEFT, where
139   any PEFT method can be viewed as a combination of several design dimensions. For instance, given
140   the adapted module’s input h and output x, LoRA (Hu et al., 2022), which approximates weight
141   updates using low-rank matrices, can be described as a parallel operation ∆(h) = hWdown Wup and
142   x ← x + s · ∆(h). This framework facilitates hybrid design for better PEFT variants. They find
143
      that parallel PEFT modules generally outperform sequential adaptations, and modifying FFN yields
      better results than modifying attention, which are further supported by Hu et al. (2023), Zhang et al.
144
      (2023), Dettmers et al. (2024) and Hao et al. (2024).
145
146   PEFT with MoE-like Structures. The success of MoE transformers has inspired MoE-structured
147   adaptations. Much recent work has focused on developing such modules for dense models, including
148   inserting multiple LoRA experts with routers at attention layers (Liu et al., 2023a; Luo et al., 2024)
149
      and alongside dense FFN layer (Zadouri et al., 2023; Dou et al., 2023; Page-Caccia et al., 2024; Chen
      et al., 2024; Hao et al., 2024). Gao et al. (2024) find that allocating more LoRA experts to higher
150
      layers leads to better performance. Li et al. (2024) propose up-cycled a mixture of LoRA-adapted
151
      frozen FFN experts from dense models. Wu et al. (2024) explore methods for composing multiple
152   trained LoRAs in a MoE style. Notably, all these methods primarily focus on adapting dense models,
153   leaving the application of PEFT to inherently sparse MoE models largely underexplored. Recently
154   Wang et al. (2024) propose an expert-specialized fine-tuning approach, which comes closest to this
155   research gap by selectively fine-tuning the most relevant experts for downstream tasks, though no
156   PEFT techniques are involved. Our work, in contrast, directly addresses this area by introducing
157   PEFT modules into the MoE mechanism, which offers a more flexible and efficient solution for
158   adapting MoE models while preserving their original weights untouched.

241   Based on findings from previous research (He et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2024; Hao

243   exclusively on parallel insertion methods, i.e., PEFT receiving the same input as the module it is
244   adapting and combining its output with that of the same module. This consideration aligns with
245   the parallel nature of MoE architectures, where FFN experts operate concurrently rather than in a
246   stacked configuration. Here we identify three main categories of insertion strategies:
247   Shared PEFT Experts. The PEFT module can operate in parallel with the entire MoE module,
248   functioning as shared PEFT experts. Given a input hidden state sequence h1:T , we have:

308   4.1   E XPERIMENT S ETUP
309
      Benchmarks. Our experiments follow the settings provided by Hu et al. (2023), encompassing
310
      8 benchmarks for commonsense reasoning and 6 for arithmetic reasoning. We utilize their amal-
311   gamated training sets Commonsense170K and Math50K to fine-tune models respectively for each
312   domain. Evaluations are conducted correspondingly across all individual benchmark test sets.
313
314
      LLM Backbones. Two state-of-the-art open-source MoE LLMs serve as the backbone models for
      our experiment: OLMoE-1B-7B (Muennighoff et al., 2024) and Mixtral-8×7B (Jiang et al., 2024).
315
      They are selected among publicly available MoE models based on their outstanding performance in
316
      the 1B and 13B activated parameter ranges. We use the model weights of their pretrained versions.
317
318   Baselines. Since there is little previous work on applying PEFT to MoE, we primarily experiment
319   with applying LoRA to attention matrices Wq and Wv , the versatile and popular PEFT solution that
320
      provides optimal performance under limited parameter budgets (Hu et al., 2022). This serves as our
      baseline across all scales and tasks. For the smaller OLMoE-1B-7B model, we also include results
321
      of applying LoRA to the router matrix Wg , as reported in Table 4 in appendix.
322
323   Training. In our experiments, we maintain consistency with the original training process of each
      LLM by incorporating their respective auxiliary losses alongside the cross-entropy loss for token

324
      outputs. The models we investigate all include the load balancing loss (Shazeer et al., 2017), which
325   aims to distribute tokens equally among experts. OLMoE-1B-7B additionally incorporates a router
326   z-loss (Zoph et al., 2022b) to penalize large logits in the router for better training stability. To
327   ensure a fair comparison, we keep all auxiliary losses active during fine-tuning for baseline and all
328   PERFT variants. For PERFT-R, we extend this approach with the load balancing loss for the PEFT

514   5   C ONCLUSION
515
516
      In this paper, we introduce a unified framework for integrating PEFT techniques into MoE mod-
      els, addressing the challenges of efficiently adapting these large, sparse architectures to downstream
517
      tasks. Our framework, encompassing both functional and compositional strategies, bridges the gap
518
      between existing PEFT methods for dense models and the unique sparsity characteristics of MoE ar-
519   chitectures. Building upon this framework, we propose PERFT, a flexible family of PEFT strategies
520   specifically tailored for MoE modules. Through extensive experiments on adapting several state-
521   of-the-art MoE models (OLMoE and Mixtral) for various commonsense and arithmetic reasoning
522   tasks, we demonstrated the effectiveness and scalability of PERFT. Our results showed significant
523   performance improvements over MoE-agnostic baseline methods. We provide an analysis of our
524   findings for each specific design choice from our study, contributing to a deeper understanding of
525   the dynamics between PEFT adaptation strategies and the MoE architecture.

871   B.2    B OTTLENECK S IZE OF PEFT E XPERTS
872
873
      We provide a detailed empirical analysis about the inefficient parameter utilization when always-
      activated shared experts are employed without an effective routing mechanism. This symbolizes a
874
      mismatch between effective dimensionality and adapter capacity: if the adapter’s dimensions sig-
875
      nificantly exceed the task’s intrinsic dimensionality, surplus dimensions may introduce useless or
876   harmful adaptations. Larger random-initialized bottlenecks in PERFT-S and PERFT-D can intro-
877   duce unnecessary noise in the additional adapted spaces due to insufficient information, interfering
878   with useful representations in the original pretrained model. With the perspective viewing hidden
879   states on the residual stream as bandwidths for modules to communicate on (Elhage et al., 2021), in
880   our PEFT scenario where most parameters remain unchanged, only a relatively small subspace of
881   each layer’s hidden state requires task-specific adaptation. Any over-parameterized adaptation can
882   unnecessarily disrupt normal functioning on the residual stream’s bandwidths, potentially destabi-
883   lizing the original gradient flow in the transformer and leading to unstable training or sub-optimal
884
      solutions (Aghajanyan et al., 2021). Simultaneously, in the PEFT context with limited adaptation
      information compared to model pretraining, an excessively large parameter space without gating
885
      control can easily result in over-fitting on fine-tuning data, which is exacerbated by the sparse nature
886
      of the MoE module we are adapting. As the MoE module hosts multiple different patterns on vari-
887   ous combinations of activated FFN experts that dynamically interact with each other on the residual
888   stream, the always-activated PERFT-S and PERFT-D variants may learn unnecessary adaptations
889   during the training process, further aggravating the disrupted functionality and over-fitting problems.
890
      It is also worth noting that since FFN tends to learn task-specific textual patterns (Geva et al., 2021)
891
      and attention learns more about positional interactions (Elhage et al., 2021), the nature of different
892
      components to which PEFT is introduced also contributes to different phenomena. For the baseline
893   LoRA operating on attention matrices, individual attention heads are already operating on relatively
894   smaller subspaces and can easily write outputs to disjoint subspaces without interaction. The spaces
895   they read and write are relatively more fixed due to the low rank property (Dhead < D of hidden
896   space) of multi-head attention matrices. Consequently, additional parameters introduced by scaling
897   the bottleneck of attention LoRA may not interfere with information from other components as
898   severely as adapting the MoE FFN module.
