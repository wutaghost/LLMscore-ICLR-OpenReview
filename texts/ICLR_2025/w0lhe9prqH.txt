009                                                                                                              A BSTRACT
010
011                     Recent advancements in human preference optimization, originally developed for
012                     Large Language Models (LLMs), have shown significant potential in improving
013                     text-to-image diffusion models. These methods aim to learn the distribution of
014                     preferred samples while distinguishing them from less preferred ones. However,
015                     existing preference datasets often exhibit overlap between these distributions,
016
                        leading to a conflict distribution. Additionally, we identified that input prompts
                        contain irrelevant information for less preferred images, limiting the denoising
017
                        network’s ability to accurately predict noise in preference optimization methods,
018
                        known as the irrelevant prompt issue. To address these challenges, we propose
019                     Dual Caption Preference Optimization (DCPO), a novel approach that utilizes
020                     two distinct captions to mitigate irrelevant prompts. To tackle conflict distribution,
021                     we introduce the Pick-Double Caption dataset, a modified version of Pick-a-Pic
022                     v2 with separate captions for preferred and less preferred images. We further
023                     propose three different strategies for generating distinct captions: captioning, per-
024                     turbation, and hybrid methods. Our experiments show that DCPO significantly
025                     improves image quality and relevance to prompts, outperforming Stable Diffusion
026                     (SD) 2.1, SFTChosen , Diffusion-DPO and MaPO across multiple metrics, includ-
027
                        ing Pickscore, HPSv2.1, GenEval, CLIPscore, and ImageReward, fine-tuned on
                        SD 2.1 as the backbone.

082   1      I NTRODUCTION
083
084   Image synthesis models (Rombach et al., 2022; Esser et al., 2024) have achieved remarkable ad-
085   vancements in generating photo-realistic and high-quality images. Text-conditioned diffusion (Song
086   et al., 2020a) models have led this progress due to their strong generalization abilities and profi-
087
      ciency in modeling high-dimensional data distributions. As a result, they have found wide range of
      applications in image editing (Brooks et al., 2023), video generation (Wu et al., 2023a) and robotics
088
      (Carvalho et al., 2023). Consequently, efforts have focused on aligning them with human prefer-
089
      ences, targeting specific attributes like safety (Liu et al., 2024b), style (Everaert et al., 2023), and
090   personalization (Ruiz et al., 2023), thereby improving their usability and adaptability.
091
092   Similar to the alignment process of Large Language Models (LLMs), aligning diffusion models
093
      involves two main steps: 1. Pre-training and 2. Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT). Recent fine-tuning
      based methods have been introduced to optimize diffusion models according to human preferences
094
      by leveraging Reinforcement Learning with Human Feedback (RLHF) (Ouyang et al., 2022), the
095
      aim of which is to maximize an explicit reward. However, challenges such as fine-tuning a separate
096   reward model and reward hacking have led to the adoption of Direct Preference Optimization (DPO)
097   (Rafailov et al., 2024) techniques like Diffusion-DPO (Wallace et al., 2024). Intuitively, Diffusion-
098   DPO involves maximizing the difference between a preferred image and a less preferred image for
099   a given prompt.
100
      Although DPO-based methods are effective in comparison to SFT-based approaches, applying direct
101
      optimization in multi-modal settings presents certain challenges. Current preference optimization
102   datasets consist of a preferred (xw ) and a less preferred (xl ) image for a given prompt (c). Ideally,
103   xw should show a higher correlation with c compared to xl . However, we find that in current
104   datasets, both the images share the same distribution for the given prompt c, which we refer to as
105   conflict distribution in the data. Additionally, irrelative information in c restricts the U-Net’s ability
106   to predict noises from xl in the diffusion reverse process, which we refer to as irrelevant prompts.
107   This entails that there is a lack of sufficient distinguishing features between the two pairs (xw , c),
      (xl , c), thereby increasing the complexity of the optimization process.

108
      Overall, we identify two key challenges: 1. conflict distribution in data, which contradicts the core
109   idea of direct optimization, and 2. irrelevant prompts for less preferred images, which can hinder
110   the learning process of the diffusion model during optimization.
111
112
      To address the aforementioned bottleneck, we propose DCPO: Dual Caption Preference Opti-
      mization, a novel preference optimization technique designed to align diffusion models by utilizing
113
      two distinct captions corresponding to the preferred and less preferred image. DCPO broadly con-
114
      sists of two steps - a text generation framework that develops better aligned captions and a novel
115   objective function that utilizes these captions as part of the training process.
116
117   The text generation framework seeks to alleviate the conflict distribution present in existing datasets.
118
      We hypothesize that c does not serve as the optimal signal for optimization because they do not con-
      vey the reasons why an image is preferred or dis-preferred; based on the above, we devise the
119
      following techniques to generate better aligned captions. The first method involves using a caption-
120
      ing model Qϕ (z i |xi , c); which generates a new prompt z i based on an image xi and the original
121   prompt c, where i ∈ (w, l). The second method introduces perturbation techniques f , such that
122   c = z w , z l = f (c); i.e. generating z l , to represent the less preferred image, considering the original
123   prompt c as the prompt aligned with the preferred image. We investigate multiple semantic variants
124   of f , where each variant differs in the degree of perturbation applied to the original caption c. Fi-
125   nally, we also explore a hybrid combination of the above methods, where we combine the strong
126   prior of the captioning model and the efficient nature of the perturbation method. All the above
127   methods are designed to generate captions that effectively discriminate between the preferred and
128   less preferred images.
129   We introduce a novel objective function that allows DCPO to incorporate z w and z l into its opti-
130   mization process. Specifically, during optimization, the policy model pθ increases the likelihood of
131   the preferred image xw conditioned on the prompt z w , while simultaneously decreasing the like-
132   lihood of the less preferred image xl conditioned on the prompt z l . The results in Tables 1 and
133   2 demonstrate that DCPO consistently outperforms other methods, with notable improvements of
134   +0.21 in Pickscore, +0.45 in HPSv2.1, +1.8 in normalized ImageReward, +0.15 in CLIPscore, and
135
      +3% in GenEval. Additionally, DCPO achieved 58% in general preference and 66% in visual appeal
      compared to Diffusion-DPO on the PartiPrompts dataset, as evaluated by GPT-4o (see Figure 8).
136
137   In summary, our contributions are as follows :
138
139          • Double Caption Generation: We introduce the Captioning and Perturbation methods to
140            address the conflict distribution issue, as illustrated in Figure 3. In the Captioning method,
141            we employ state-of-the-art models like LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024a) and Emu2 (Sun et al.,
142
               2024) to generate a caption z based on the image x and prompt c. Additionally, we use
               DIPPER (Krishna et al., 2024), a paraphrase generation model built by fine-tuning the T5-
143
               XXL model to create three levels of perturbation from the prompt c.
144
145          • Dual Caption Preference Optimization (DCPO): We propose DCPO, a modified version
146            of Diffusion-DPO, that leverages the U-Net encoder embedding space for preference op-
147
               timization. This method enhances diffusion models by aligning them more closely with
               human preferences, using two distinct captions for the preferred and less preferred images
148
               during optimization.
149
150          • Improved Model Performance: We demonstrate that our approach significantly outper-
151            forms SD 2.1, SFT, Diffusion-DPO, and MaPO across metrics such as Pickscore, HPSv2.1,
152            GenEval, CLIPscore, normalized ImageReward, and GPT-4o (Achiam et al., 2023) evalu-
153
               ations.

157   In this section, we present the conflict distribution issue in preference datasets, where preferred and
158   less-preferred images generated from the same prompt c exhibit significant overlap. We also explain
159   the irrelevant prompt issue found in previous direct preference optimization methods. To address
160   these challenges, we propose Dual Caption Preference Optimization (DCPO), a method that uses
161   distinct captions for preferred and less preferred images to improve diffusion model alignment.

187   refined version of Diffusion-DPO designed to address these challenges.1

      To optimize a diffusion model using DCPO, a dataset D = {z w , z l , xw        l

238   and image pairs without captions. To address this, we propose three methods for generating captions
239   z and introduce a new high-quality dataset, Pick-Double Caption, which provides specific captions

242   2.2.1       DCPO- C : C APTIONING M ETHOD
243
244   In this method, the captioning model Qϕ (z|c, x) generates the caption z based on the image x and the
245   original prompt c. As a result, we obtain a preferred caption z w ∼ Qϕ (z w |c, xw ) for the preferred
246   image and a less preferred caption z l ∼ Qϕ (z l |c, xl ) for the less preferred image, as illustrated in a
247   sample in Figure 2. Thus, based on the generated captions z w and z l , we can optimize a diffusion
248   model using the DCPO method.
249   In the experiment section, we evaluate the performance of DCPO-c and demonstrate that this method
250   effectively mitigates the conflict distribution by creating two differentiable distributions. However,
251   the question of how much divergence is needed between the two distributions remains. To investigate
252   this, we propose Hypothesis 1.
253
      Hyphothesis 1. Let d(z, x) represent the semantic distribution between a caption z and an image
254
      x, with µ being the mean of the distribution d, and ∆µ = µ(d(z0w , xw               l    l
                                                                              0 )) − µ(d(z0 , x0 )) as the
255   difference between the two distributions. Increasing ∆µ between the preferred and less-preferred
256   image distributions in a preference dataset beyond a threshold t (i.e., ∆µ > t), can improve the
257   performance of the model pθ .
258
      Our hypothesis suggests that increasing the distance between the two distributions up to a certain
259
      threshold t can improve alignment performance. To examine this, we propose the perturbation
260
      method to control the distance between the two distributions, represented by ∆µ.

      2.2.2       DCPO- P : P ERTURBATION M ETHOD
263
264   While using a captioning model is an effective way to address the conflict distribution, it risks devi-
265   ating from the original distribution of prompt c, and the distributions of preferred and less preferred
266   images may still remain close. To tackle these issues, we propose a perturbation method. In this
267   approach, we assume that prompt c is highly relevant to the preferred image xw      0 and aim to gen-
268   erate a less relevant caption, denoted as cp , based on prompt c. To achieve this, we use the model
269   Wϕ (cp |c), which generates a perturbed version of prompt c, altering its semantic meaning. In this
      framework, prompt c corresponds to the preferred caption z w (c = z w ), while the perturbed prompt

270
      cp represents the less-preferred caption z l (cp = z l ). For the perturbation model Wϕ , we utilized
271   the DIPPER model (Krishna et al., 2024) built by fine-tuning the T5-XXL (Chung et al., 2022) to
272   produce a degraded version of the prompt c.
273
274
      We define three levels of perturbation: 1) Weak: where prompt cp has high semantic similarity to
      prompt c, with minimal differences. 2) Medium: where the semantic difference between prompt cp
275
      and c is more pronounced than in the weak level. 3) Strong: where the majority of the semantics
276
      in prompt cp differ significantly from prompt c. For further details on the perturbation method, see
277   Appendix C.
278
279   The main advantage of DCPO-p is to reduce the captioning process cost while staying closer to
280
      the original data distribution by using prompt c as the preferred caption. However, we observe that
      the quality of captions in DCPO-c outperforms that of the original prompt c, as shown in Table 5
281
      in Appendix B. Based on this observation, we propose a hybrid method to improve the alignment
282
      performance by combining captioning and perturbation techniques.

      2.2.3    DCPO- H : H YBRID M ETHOD
285
286   In this method, instead of perturbing the prompt c, we perturb the caption z generated by the model
287   Qϕ (z|x, c) based on the image x and prompt c. As discussed in Section 2.2.1, the goal of the per-
288   turbation method is to increase the distance between the two distributions. However, the correlation
289   between the image x0 and prompt c significantly impacts alignment performance. Therefore, we
290   propose Hypothesis 2.
291
292   Hypothesis 2. Let S(c, x) represent the correlation score between prompt c and image x, and
293   P (pθ (c1 , c2 )) denote the performance of model pθ optimized on captions c1 and c2 with DCPO,
294   where Wϕ is the perturbation model. If S(z, x) > S(c, x), then P (pθ (z w , zpw ∼ Wϕ (zpw |z w ))) >
295   P (pθ (c, cp ∼ Wϕ (cp |c))).
296   In Section 3.3, we provide experimental evidence supporting Hypothesis 2 and investigate the po-
297   tential of using zpl ∼ Wϕ (zpl |z l ) as the less-preferred caption z l , instead of zpw ∼ Wϕ (zpw |z w ) as
298   originally proposed in Hypothesis 2.

343   captions were significantly longer (see Table 5 in Appendix B). Models fine-tuned on captions from
344   the conditional prompt strategy outperformed those using the non-conditional approach, though
345
      the conditional prompt captions were twice as long. Interestingly, despite Emu2 generating much
      shorter captions, the models fine-tuned on Emu2 were comparable to those fine-tuned on the original
346
      prompts from Pick-a-Pic v2.
347
348   A key challenge is generating captions for the less preferred images using the captioning method.
349   We observed that in both prompting strategies, the captions for the preferred images are more aligned
350   with the original prompt c distribution. However, the non-conditional prompt strategy often produces
351
      captions for less preferred images that are out-of-distribution (OOD) from the original prompt c in
      most cases. We will explore this further in Section 3.3.
352
353   Finally, we observe that the key advantage of the Pick-Double Caption dataset is the greater dif-
354   ference in CLIPscore (∆µ) between preferred and less preferred images compared to the original
355   prompts. Specifically, while the original prompt has a ∆µ of 1.3, LLaVA shows a much larger differ-
356   ence at 4.3, and Emu2 at 2.8. This increased gap reflects improved alignment performance in models
357
      fine-tuned on this dataset, indicating that the captioning method mitigates the conflict distribution.

413

414

416   increasing the distance ∆µ between the two distributions enhances performance. However, this
417   distance must be controlled and kept below a threshold t, a hyperparameter that may vary depending
418   on the task. These findings support our hypothesis.

501   guage Models (LLMs), Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024) reformulate
502   the RLHF objective in a closed-form manner and introduce it as an implicit reward model with a
503   simple classification objective. Diffusion-DPO (Wallace et al., 2024) directly adopts DPO method
504   into text-to-image diffusion models, utilizing pairwise preference datasets consisting of text and im-
505   ages to guide alignment. Diffusion-KTO (Li et al., 2024) incorporates Kahneman & Tversky model
506   of human utility to align these models, simplifying the process by using images with binary feedback
507   signals, i.e., likes or dislikes instead of pairwise preference data. To enhance flexibility, Hong et al.
508
      (2024) introduce MaPO, an alignment technique independent of a reference model previously used
      by other methods, enabling greater control over stylistic adaptations. However, previous alignment
509
      methods optimize diffusion models based on a single prompt for a pair of images, which supports
510
      the irrelevant prompts issue explored in Section 2.1.
511
512   Text-to-image Preference Datasets. Text-to-image image preference datasets commonly involve
513   the text prompt to generate the images, and two or more images are ranked according to human
514   preference. HPS (Wu et al., 2023c) and HPSv2 (Wu et al., 2023b) create multiple images using a
515   series of image generation models for a single prompt, and the images are ranked according to real-
516   world human preferences. Moreover, a classifier is trained using the gathered preference dataset,
517   which can be used as a metric for image-aligning tasks. Also, Pick-a-Pic v2 (Kirstain et al., 2023)
518   follows a similar structure to create a pairwise preference dataset along with their CLIP (Radford
519   et al., 2021) based scoring function, Pickscore. While these datasets are carefully created, having
520
      only one prompt for both or all the images introduces conflict distribution, which will be further
      discussed in Section 2.1. For this reason, we modified the Pick-a-Pic v2 dataset using recaptioning
521
      and perturbation methods to improve image alignment performance.
522
523
524   5   C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
525
526   In this paper, we present a novel preference optimization method for aligning text-to-image diffusion
527   models called Dual Caption Preference Optimization (DCPO). We tackle two major challenges in
528   previous preference datasets and optimization algorithms: the conflict distribution and irrelevant
529
      prompt. To overcome these issues, we introduce the Pick-Double Caption dataset, a modified version
      of the Pick-a-Pic v2 dataset. We also identify difficulties in generating captions, particularly the risk
530
      of out-of-distribution captions for images, and propose three approaches: 1) captioning (DCPO-
531
      c), 2) perturbation (DCPO-p), and 3) a hybrid method (DCPO-h). Our results show that DCPO-h
532   significantly enhances alignment performance, outperforming methods like MaPO and Diffusion-
533   DPO across multiple metrics.
534
535   Limitations. Although DCPO shows strong performance across various metrics, the captioning
536   and perturbation methods are resource-intensive. We encourage future research to explore cost-
537   effective alternatives to these methods. Additionally, the potential of using different backbones,
538   such as Stable Diffusion XL (SDXL) (Rombach et al., 2022), has not been explored in the context
539   of DCPO. We also invite researchers to investigate DCPO’s effectiveness on other tasks, such as
      safety. We believe our work will have a significant impact on the alignment research community.
