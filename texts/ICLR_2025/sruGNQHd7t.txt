009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
012            In the era of cloud-based deep learning (DL) services, data privacy has become a
013
               critical concern, prompting some organizations to restrict the use of online AI ser-
               vices. This work addresses this issue by introducing a privacy-preserving method
014
               for DL model queries through domain shifting in the input space. We develop
015
               an encoder that strategically transforms inputs into a different domain within the
016            same space, ensuring that the original inputs remain private by presenting only the
017            obfuscated versions to the DL model. A decoder then recovers the correct output
018            from the model’s predictions. Our method keeps the authentic input and output
019            data secure on the local system, preventing unauthorized access by third parties
020            who only encounter the obfuscated data. Comprehensive evaluations across var-
021            ious oracle models and datasets demonstrate that our approach preserves privacy
022            with minimal impact on classification performance.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Cloud-based deep learning services offer powerful capabilities but also introduce significant privacy
028
      risks related to user data. Uploading data for analysis or model training exposes sensitive infor-
029
      mation to third-party cloud providers, increasing the risk of data breaches, unauthorized access, or
030   misuse of personal, proprietary, or confidential information. Additionally, service providers may
031   retain user data and potentially re-purpose it for activities beyond the original analysis (He et al.,
032   2022). These concerns are especially critical in fields such as healthcare, finance, and personal
033   communications, where data protection is paramount.
034
      There are several approaches to address the privacy issues, each with its own advantages and limi-
035
      tations. One common approach is to perturb the data before sharing it with model providers, using
036   Differential Privacy (Dwork et al., 2006) to control the level of perturbation. Differential privacy
037   has been effectively applied during the training process to ensure data privacy in deep learning(Wei
038   et al., 2020). However, this approach is less suitable for protecting privacy during the inference
039   stage, as the added noise significantly degrades model accuracy and reduces utility.
040
      Another prominent method is Homomorphic Encryption (Rivest & Dertouzos, 1978; Gentry, 2009),
041
      which allows inference operations to be performed directly on encrypted data. Only the final re-
042   sults are decrypted by users. Despite its potential, fully homomorphic encryption (FHE) poses
043   substantial performance challenges, with operations on encrypted data being thousands to millions
044   of times slower than operations on plaintext (Akram et al., 2024; Jiang & Ju, 2022). This makes
045   FHE impractical for many real-world applications. To mitigate these performance issues, multi-
046   party computation (MPC) has been combined with homomorphic encryption to reduce DL model
047   inference latency (Liu et al., 2017; Juvekar et al., 2018). These approaches, however, all require
048   the model providers or third parties to implement the privacy protection mechanisms, limiting user
049   control over the privacy of their data.
050   In this work, we propose a novel privacy-preserving strategy that users can implement independently,
051   without requiring any modification to the deep learning models provided by service providers. Our
052   approach centers on input domain shifting, where the user encodes their input by shifting it to another
053   part of the input domain. Only the obfuscated input is sent to the model, protecting the privacy of
      the original data from the model service provider.

054
      We define the significant input domain as the subset of the input space containing all real-world
055   samples. The remainder of the input space, which includes inputs that are unlikely to occur naturally,
056   constitutes the non-significant input domain.
057
058
      In-place domain shifting maps inputs within the significant input domain to other inputs also within
      this domain and can be applied by encoders with only blackbox access to the deep learning (DL)
059
      model. Out-of-place domain shifting, on the other hand, encodes inputs from the significant input
060
      domain to inputs within the non-significant input domain. This approach is designed for users with
061   whitebox access to the DL model, providing highly obfuscated inputs tailored specifically to the
062   model. In both approaches, the DL model processes the obfuscated inputs and produces obfuscated
063   outputs, which the user then decodes to retrieve the useful inference results.
064
      This paper encompasses the following aspects:
065
             • An evaluation of the feasibility of applying domain shifting as an input obfuscation method,
066
               and a demonstration of domain shifting theory on pre-trained DNN models.
067
             • A proposal of two transform training: model-specific transform training by out-of-place
068
               domain shifting, and model-agnostic transform training by in-place domain shifting.
069          • A comprehensive evaluation of both obfuscation methods, including comparisons across
070            state-of-the-art pre-trained models and datasets.

      2 BACKGROUND
072
073   With the advancement of deep learning (DL), the associated risks can be categorized into three main
074
      types: those related to the training dataset, the trained model (including its structure and parameters),
      and the inputs/results of predictions (He et al., 2022). This paper focuses on privacy-preserving
075
      inference in DL models, where the goal is to protect users’ input data and prediction results.
076
077   Various privacy-preserving DL inference methods have been proposed in the literature, based on
078   concepts such as Homomorphic Encryption (HE) (Gentry, 2009) and Multi-Party Computation
079   (MPC) (Yao, 1982). CryptoNets (Gilad-Bachrach et al., 2016) introduced the first fully HE-based
080
      neural network capable of operating on encrypted data. However, this approach incurs significant
      computational overhead, leading to substantial inference latency. To reduce this latency, MPC has
081
      been combined with HE to create hybrid neural network designs that preserve privacy (Liu et al.,
082
      2017; Juvekar et al., 2018).Delphi (Mishra et al., 2020) further reduces online latency by offloading
083   most of the heavy cryptographic computations to offline processing. Additional efforts to decrease
084   latency by combining DL techniques and MPC have been proposed in (Song et al., 2023; Nie et al.,
085   2024).
086
      These cryptography-based private DL inference schemes require modifications to the DL model
087
      implementation, which must be carried out by the service provider. The MPC/HE hybrid schemes
088
      also involve frequent communication between the DL model and users during the inference process.
089   In contrast, this paper explores an alternative scenario where users do not depend on the service
090   provider to implement privacy-preserving measures. The DL model implementation remains un-
091   modified. Instead, users encode their data through a domain shift strategy that obfuscates private
092   information while preserving essential features, enabling the continued use of the DL classifier ser-
093   vice.

      3   T HREAT M ODEL
096   In our work, we investigate a scenario where users employ deep learning services provided by a
097   service provider who acts as an honest-but-curious adversary, meaning they collect data uploaded
098   by the users and may use these data for other purposes. The users aim to maintain the confidentiality
099
      of their data while still being able to perform inference using the service’s DNN classifier.
100   The users have no control over the DNN model and cannot modify it in any way. Their only inter-
101   action with the model is through querying it by sending inputs and receiving output classification
102   results.
103   To protect their privacy, users avoid directly submitting their real data to the DNN. Instead, they
104   send modified data to the DNN service and rely on the resulting classification outputs. We examine
105   two levels of model access for users within this context:
106
107         1. Whitebox Access: Users have access to the internal model parameters, including detailed
               logit scores for each class, and can calculate gradients.


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
            2. Blackbox Access: Users have no access to the internal workings of the model and receive
109            only the output class label for the most likely class corresponding to the input data.
110
111   In the blackbox scenario, the DNN classifier takes an input xin and outputs to users only the most
112   likely class label y out for xin . The DNN classifier, provided by the commercial service, is assumed
113   to be highly accurate—referred to as an oracle model. This means that when xin represents real data
114   for the intended application, y out corresponds to the true class label y with a very high probability.
115
      In the whitebox scenario, users also receive the classification scores sout , typically the logit values
116
      for each class corresponding to xin . The predicted class label y out from the oracle model corre-
117   sponds to the class with the highest score. We denote y out = f (xin ) and sout = f¯(xin ), where and
118   f and f¯ are functions of the oracle model that output the classification label and the classification
119   scores, respectively. Here, f is a composite of the softmax function and f¯. In the whitebox scenario,
120   the parameters of f¯ are also known to the users, enabling them to calculate gradients.
121
122   4   T HE F RAMEWORK OF P RIVATE Q UERIES
123
124   In our framework, users protect their sensitive information by not directly providing their data to
125   the DNN model. Instead, they first employ an input encoder EN to transform their real input
126
      data x into an obfuscated form xob = EN (x). This obfuscated input xob is then uploaded to the
      cloud service, ensuring that service providers only have access to the obfuscated data and cannot
127
      discern the original information. The obfuscated input xob is processed by the service provider’s
128
      DNN model (referred to as the oracle model), which generates an output as either y ob = f¯(xob ) or
129   y ob = f (xob ). Users then apply an output decoder DE to convert the obfuscated output y ob back
130   into meaningful information DE(y ob ), allowing them to utilize advanced deep learning services
131   while maintaining control over their sensitive data throughout the process.
132
      The input encoder and output decoder are a pair of data mapping models that handle the transfor-
133
      mation of input and output data. These models are trained using easily accessible public datasets,
134
      which may not be as high-quality as the datasets used to train the commercial service provider’s
135   DNN model. Although this may lead to a slight performance degradation when combined with the
136   commercial oracle model, the minor reduction in accuracy is a trade-off for the enhanced privacy
137   and accessibility that this method provides.
138
      When building the encoder/decoder pair, we consider two levels of applicability. For one level, we
139
      build the pair that is specifically aimed at the oracle model at hand. This encoder/decoder pair is
140
      thus model-specific. For another level, we propose a model-agnostic method, constructing encoders
141   and decoders solely based on the dataset, independent of the oracle model.
142
143   To address these considerations, we introduce two fundamental approaches: Model-specific Trans-
144
      form Training and Model-agnostic Transform Training by Domain Shifting.
145
      4.1 D OMAIN S HIFTING
146
147
      In a Deep Neural Network (DNN) model, the input space is the set of all possible inputs that the
      model can accept. This could be the space of all possible pixel values for images of a specific size
148
      and color depth. The input domain, on the other hand, narrows down this space to the specific subset
149
      of inputs that are relevant and meaningful for the task at hand. It encompasses the range of values
150   and types of data that the model is designed to process and that are expected to occur in real-world
151   applications.
152
153
      Basically, it contains two basic shifting methods:
154          • Out-of-place Domain Shifting: In this approach, inputs are transformed into an alternative
155            domain distinct from their original domain. This resulting domain may be unrecognizable
156            and not meaningful to human observers. This approach requires the whitebox access to the
157
               oracle model to build the Model-specific Transform Training upon Out-of-place Domain
               Shifting.
158
159          • In-place Domain Shifting: This method involves shifting inputs only within their original
160            domain. The shift can occur either within the same class or across different classes. We
161            build the Model-agnostic Transform Training based on In-place Domain Shifting which
               only requires the blackbox access.

220   4.3   M ODEL - AGNOSTIC T RANSFORM T RAINING UNDER B LACKBOX ACCESS
221
222   We now focus on developing an encoder/decoder pair that is model-agnostic, meaning it can be
223   utilized with any oracle model (a highly accurate commercial classification model) for privacy pro-
224   tection.
225   We observe that a model-agnostic approach necessitates the encoder to perform in-place domain
226   shifting. To understand why, consider the scenario where the obfuscated input xob = EN (xin )
227   falls outside the original domain. In this case, two different oracle models, f1 and f2 , are unlikely
228   to produce consistent classification results on xob because it lacks intrinsic relevance to the classi-
229   fication task, as illustrated by the example in Figure 1. Consequently, we cannot rely on the same
230   model-agnostic decoder DE to accurately decode these varying results.
231   A model-agnostic decoder can only exist if different oracle models produce very similar obfuscated
232   outputs y ob for the same obfuscated input xob . The key commonality across various oracle models
233   is that they all produce the same (real) class label with high probability for inputs within the original
234   domain. Therefore, the decoder should operate on the class label output y ob = f (xob ) rather than
235   on the logit output sob = f¯(xob ).
236
      We can summarize the above discussion into two necessary conditions for a model-agnostic ap-
237   proach: (1) the model-agnostic encoder should transform any input x from the original domain
238   into another data point xob = EN (xin ) that also remains within the original domain; and (2) the
239   model-agnostic decoder should take as input the class label output y ob = f (xob ) from the oracle
240   model f . This implies that the model-agnostic encoder/decoder pair can be constructed with black-
241   box access to the oracle model, as additional internal information, such as logits available through
242   white-box access, does not provide any advantage. This is to be expected, since model internals
243   vary among different oracle models, and thus the model-agnostic encoder/decoder should not rely
244   on these internals.
245   For our implementation, we build our encoder on top of basic sub-encoders. Notice that the inputs
246   in the original input domain is transformed to other data points within the same domain. Assume
247   that there are total of M classes in the input domain, a basic sub-encoder transforms the data among
248   the M classes according to a permutation plan. Specifically, the basic sub-encoder ENi transforms
249   input in the j-th class to another data point in j ′ -th class with
250                                           j′ = j + i       mod M.                                      (3)
251
252
      That is, for the basic sub-encoder EN0 , all inputs in the original domain are transformed to another
      data points within the same class. The basic sub-encoder ENi transforms inputs in the original
253
      domain to data points of another class according to a permutation plan of shifting i. For example,
254
      if there are 5 classes {0, 1, 2, 3, 4}, the permutation plan for EN1 is {0 → 1, 1 → 2, 2 → 3, 3 →
255   4, 4 → 0}, the permutation plan for EN2 is {0 → 2, 1 → 3, 2 → 4, 3 → 0, 4 → 1}, etc.
256
257   Our encoder first generates a random number i from the set 0, 1, . . . , M − 1 with equal probability,
258
      and then applies the i-th basic sub-encoder to produce the obfuscated output xob = ENi (x). As
      a result, for any input x in the original domain, xob randomly belongs to any class with equal
259
      probability. This ensures the privacy of x, as no class information about x can be inferred from xob
260
      alone.
261
262   The obfuscated input xob is then passed to the oracle model f , and the resulting obfuscated output
263   y ob = f (xob ) is returned to the user. Since the user knows the random number i, they can decode
264
      y ob to obtain the original class label using the formula:
265                                          y ′ = y ob − i    mod M.                                      (4)

273   4.3.1   GAN M ULTI - CLASS T RANSFORMATION
274
275   A Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) is a tool used to generate data within a specific domain.
276   The generator G takes a random input z sampled from a probability distribution pz and produces
277   an output G(z). To ensure that G(z) follows the target data distribution pdata , the generator is set
278
      up to compete with a discriminator D, whose goal is to distinguish real data x ∼ pdata from the
      generator’s output. In this setup, the discriminator and the generator act as two players in a min-max
279
      game, where the objective is to solve minG maxD V (G, D) with the following value function:
280
281                       V (G, D) = Ex∼pdata log D(x) + Ez∼pz log[1 − D(G(z))].                         (5)
282
283   Both the discriminator and the generator improve through their competition, ultimately leading the
284   generator’s output G(z) to closely follow the target distribution pdata .
285   However, for our purposes, we require a data transformer rather than a data generator. GANs can be
286   adapted to transform data from a source distribution pdata to a target distribution pdata′ by modifying
287   the generator G to include an additional input argument x ∼ pdata and adjusting the discriminator
288   to distinguish between the generator’s outputs and data x′ ∼ pdata′ . In this setup, we train G(x, z)
289   in the min-max game using the following value function:
290                 V (G, D) = Ex′ ∼pdata′ log D(x′ ) + Ex∼pdata ,z∼pz log[1 − D(G(x, z))].              (6)
291
292   Next, we consider how to implement the basic sub-encoders using GAN-based transformers. Each
293   basic sub-encoder corresponds to a specific permutation plan: for an input x in class y, the sub-
294   encoder outputs xob in class per(y). Specifically, for ENi , the permutation plan is defined as
295   per(y) = (y + i) mod M , as given in (3).
296   We assume that users have access to some training data x with corresponding class labels y to train
297   the basic sub-encoders. Given a class y, the data distribution is denoted as pdata|y .
298
299
      For a single class y0 , we define pdata = pdata|y0 as the distribution of data in class y0 and set
300
      pdata′ = pdata|per(y0 ) as the distribution of data in class per(y0 ). A class-y0 to class-per(y0 )
      GAN can then be trained with the objective function (6). However, this class-specific GAN does
301
      not yet constitute the desired basic sub-encoder ENi , as it only transforms data from class y0 to
302
      class per(y0 ), whereas we need it to simultaneously transform data across all classes y to their
303   corresponding classes per(y).
304
305   To achieve this multi-class transformation, we condition the competing discriminator on the class
306
      label y. This leads to the following objective function for the min-max game:
307      V (G, D) = Ex′ ∼pdata|per(y) log D(x′ |per(y))+
308
309
                                                        Ex∼pdata|y ,z∼pz log[1 − D(G(x, z)|per(y))].     (7)
310
      For training a multi-class transformation GAN, we condition on the class label y using the same
311
      concept as in conditional GANs (cGANs) (Mirza & Osindero, 2014). However, our approach differs
312
      from cGANs in a key aspect: while cGANs condition the generator also on the class label, our
313   method conditions only the discriminator on the class label. The class label is used solely during the
314   training of the GAN to create the basic sub-encoders.
315
316
      Unlike cGANs, which generate data with an explicit class label, our encoder users do not have access
      to the class label when encoding a data point x to feed to the oracle model. Instead, they rely on the
317
      commercial oracle model to recover the class label from the encoded data.

      4.3.2   GAN + DDPM- BASED G ENERATIVE M ODEL
320
321    The GAN-based encoder described above performed well on the MNIST handwritten digits dataset
322   in the later experimental section 5. However, for more complex image datasets, the quality of the
323   images generated by the encoder was low, which hindered accurate classification when these images
      were fed to oracle models. To improve the quality of the generated images, we turn to Denoising


                                                        6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPM) (Ho et al., 2020) , a generative tool that has recently gained
325   popularity for producing high-quality images.
326
327   In DDPM training, the first step involves augmenting the training data from the target distribution
328
      pdata by adding varying levels of noise. The diffusion models are then trained on this noisy data,
      producing a generator with parameter settings corresponding to different noise levels. For denoising,
329
      the noise level parameter is set to zero, resulting in the desired DDPM generator that produces data
330
      from the distribution pdata without noise.
331
332   To create a multi-class transformation encoder that outputs high-quality images, we combine GANs
333   with DDPMs. We begin by building a conditional DDPM that generates images from class y when
334
      provided with the corresponding class label y as input. The first layer of the conditional DDPM is
      an embedding layer, where each class y is represented by a latent embedding vector emy . We then
335
      train our multi-class transformation GAN as before, but with the goal of having the GAN output
336
      emper(y) instead of an image in class per(y). Specifically, for an input image x, it first passes
337   through the trained GAN, which outputs an embedding emx . This embedding is then fed into the
338   trained DDPM, which generates an obfuscated image xob = ENi (x). For an image x in class y,
339   this GAN+DDPM implementation of the basic encoder ENi produces a high-quality image xob in
340   class per(y) with high probability.
341
      It is important to note that the training of this model-agnostic encoder does not involve the oracle
342
      model.

509   5.3    E VALUATION ON OVERHEAD
510
511   The additional overhead arises from the inference execution times of both the encoder and decoder,
512   for both out-of-place and in-place domain shifting methods. In Table 2 and Table 4, we present two
513
      key timing measurements. The Oracle Inference Time indicates the duration required to process a
      single input query using the oracle model. The Encoder+Decoder Inference Time represents the
514
      additional time incurred when implementing our domain shifting method.
515
516   In the out-of-place domain shifting scenario, our protection method achieves significantly faster in-
517   ference times on MNIST dataset, our method requires approximately 0.5 ms, which is substantially
518   lower than the 0.481s reported previously in (Juvekar et al., 2018) and 3.58s in (Liu et al., 2017).
519
      Similarly, for the CIFAR-10 dataset, our method completes inference in just 1.2 ms, a marked im-
      provement over the 472s reported in (Liu et al., 2017) and 9.74s in (Juvekar et al., 2018).
520
521   For in-place domain shifting, the GAN inference time is about 0.3 times the oracle inference time.
522   However, the DDPM generation process takes longer, with the generation of a single image requir-
523   ing around 4 seconds. Consequently, the extra time incurred after applying protection measures
524   is approximately 4.12 seconds. Despite this, it remains much quicker than the overhead seen in
525
      previous works Liu et al. (2017); Juvekar et al. (2018). Unlike approaches that involve significant
      communication overhead due to layer-by-layer processing in MPC, our in-place shifting technique
526
      introduces only 4.12s of latency when querying the CIFAR-10 and ImageNet datasets, which is still
527
      faster than the 5s inference time for CIFAR-10 reported in Nie et al. (2024).

530   6     C ONCLUSION
531
532   This paper presents a novel privacy-preserving approach that can be implemented by users, without
533   requiring modifications to the deep learning (DL) models provided by service providers. The core
534
      strategy involves the development of a domain-shifting encoder by the user. Depending on their
      access level to the DL model, users can create either in-place shifting encoders (for model-agnostic
535
      schemes) or out-of-place shifting encoders (for model-specific schemes). These approaches enable
536
