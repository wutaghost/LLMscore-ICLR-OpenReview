009                                                             A BSTRACT
010
011            This contribution proposes adiabatic reinforcement learning (ARL), a new method
012            for continual reinforcement learning (CRL). In CRL, we assume a non-stationary
013            environment partitioned into tasks. To avoid catastrophic forgetting (CF), RL re-
014            quires the use of large replay buffers, which leads to very slow learning and high
015            memory requirements. To remedy this, we propose adiabatic reinforcement learn-
016            ing (ARL), a wake-sleep method that performs slow learning of internal represen-
017            tations from high-error transitions during sleep phases. Wake phases are used for
018
               the fast learning of policies, i.e., mappings from representations to actions, and to
               collect new high-error transitions. Representation learning is performed by adia-
019
               batic replay (AR), a recent CL technique we adapted to the RL setting. AR uses
020
               selective, internal replay of samples that are likely to be affected by forgetting.
021            Since this process is conditioned on incoming samples only, its has constant time-
022            complexity w.r.t. tasks. Other benefits include fast adaptation to new tasks, and a
023            very low memory footprint due to the complete absence of replay buffers.

026   1     I NTRODUCTION
027
028   This article is in the context of continual reinforcement learning (CRL), a branch of reinforcement
029   learning (RL) where a non-stationary environment is assumed in addition to non-stationary observa-
030   tions due to ongoing exploration and model adaptation. Non-stationary data distributions cause the
031   well-known catastrophic forgetting (CF) effect McCloskey & Cohen (1989) when employing DNN
032   learners. The study of machine learning from non-stationary data distribution is the objective of con-
033   tinual learning (CL), a particular goal being the mitigation or avoidance of catastrophic forgetting.
034
      Both in CL and in CRL, a common simplification is to assume the existence of tasks, i.e., phases of
      stationary data distribution or environment, with non-stationarities (or shifts) occurring only at task
035
      onsets, see fig. 1 for a visualization.
036
037
038

039

040   large replay buffers for storing past samples and replaying them for to the learner. This simulates a
041   stationary distribution, but comes at a significant memory overhead and slows down learning of new

055   have an impact. Techniques like prioritized experience replay (PER, Schaul et al. (2015)) attempt
056   to accelerate convergence by focusing on high-error samples, but this requires extensive parameter

059
      A CL technique to replace replay buffers in CRL is generative replay (GR), for which new sam-
060
      ples are immediately used for training, and which avoids forgetting by mixing them with generated
061   samples from past tasks. This improves learning speed for new tasks, but the replay of samples
062   conditioned on past tasks leads to an ever-increasing number of replayed samples as the number
063   of tasks grows (see Krawczyk & Gepperth (2024b) for a discussion of why such balanced replay
064   is required). Furthermore, this strategy requires that task onsets be known with a high degree of
065   certainty, which is a highly artificial assumption, just as the assumption of distinct, crisply defined
066   RL tasks is in general.
067
068   1.2   C ONTRIBUTION
069
070   The contribution of this article is the method of adiabatic reinforcement learning (ARL), which is
071
      validated on task-based CRL benchmarks (see fig. 1) without being informed about task onsets at any
      point. The core of ARL is a replay-based CL method termed adiabatic replay (AR, see Krawczyk
072
      & Gepperth (2024a)), which performs generative replay not conditioned on a task, but on incoming
073
      samples themselves (selective replay) and therefore does not depend on the number of previously
074   encountered tasks. The internal representation of the learner employed here is a fully probabilistic
075   Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) that supports selective updating exclusively with high-error sam-
076   ples. This decomposition of the learner into representation and (policy) readout blocks is typical of
077   state representation learning, see section 1.3. A wake-sleep training algorithm is applied so that no
078   interference can occur between these blocks.
079
      In particular, this article proposes the following contributions to the field of CRL:
080
081          • fast learning of new tasks
082          • extremely small replay buffers and low memory footprint
083
             • generative replay approach focusing on high-error transitions without forgetting
084
085          • constant time complexity w.r.t. the number of CRL tasks
086          • plastic representation over time, arbitrary number of tasks
087
088   1.3   R ELATED WORK
089
090   When data distributions are not stationary or samples are not i.i.d. Lesort et al. (2021) catastrophic
091   forgetting (CF, McCloskey & Cohen (1989); Ratcliff (1990)) will occur. Especially deep neural
092   networks (DNNs) are highly susceptible to that rapid performance degeneration Pfülb & Gepperth
093
      (2019). Various approaches have been introduced for addressing CF, see Shaheen et al. (2021); Qu
      et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2023a;b) for extensive surveys. The “default” CL scenario for supervised
094
      CL Bagus & Gepperth (2022), also known as class-incremental learning (CIL, van de Ven & Tolias
095
      (2019); Masana et al. (2022); Zhou et al. (2023)) is adopted in the majority of recent publications.
096   Essentially, it is posited that non-stationary data streams can be partitioned into a subset of non-
097   contradictory, non-overlapping tasks with stationary statistics, whose onset and duration are known.
098   However, not all CL methods are suitable for RL, as it differs considerably from the common CIL
099   setting Lesort et al. (2020); Khetarpal et al. (2020); Bagus & Gepperth (2022). We will therefore
100   focus on rehearsal or experience replay (ER, see Rolnick et al. (2019)) and pseudo-rehearsal or
101   generative replay (GR, see Shin et al. (2017); Kamra et al. (2017); Atkinson et al. (2018a)), which
102   either store samples from previous tasks or use a generator to obtain them in unlimited quantities.
103   Such samples are then merged with current samples to avoid forgetting. ER and GR in particular
104
      have become strong baselines Balaji et al. (2020); Zhang et al. (2022) in CL and work in a variety
      of scenarios Verwimp et al. (2021); Hayes et al. (2021).
105
106   Continual reinforcement learning (CRL) is studied for a variety of algorithms, of which the most
107   commonly used is deep Q-learning (DQN) with experience replay, see, e.g., Mnih et al. (2013).
      However, more sophisticated variants like soft actor-critic (SAC) are studied as well Wolczyk et al.


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      (2022b). An overview concerning the field of CRL is given in, e.g., Lesort et al. (2020); Khetarpal
109   et al. (2020); Shaheen et al. (2021). There are several works on generalizing CL methods to CRL: a
110   straightforward adaptation of pseudo-rehearsal is described in Atkinson et al. (2018b), where stan-
111   dard double DQN with experience replay is employed for training an short-term memory (STM)
112   instance on the current task. The trained STM instance, together with the (large) replay buffer from
113   the current task, are then used to train a long-term memory (LTM) instance about the current and
114   past tasks. This is convincingly demonstrated for a sequence of three Atari games. A drawback of
115   this work is the need to know about task onsets, and of course that this will scale linearly with the
116   number of tasks, at least for a large number of tasks. A similar approach is adopted in the S-Trigger
117
      model Caselles-Dupré et al. (2019), which, in addition, detects task onsets by using the VAE as an
      outlier detector and thus no longer needs to be informed about task onsets. Linear scaling behavior
118
      w.r.t. time will still be an issue, and new tasks may not be associated with a change in observa-
119
      tions but in the optimal policy, thus limiting applicability to a subset of possible CRL problems.
120   S-trigger belongs to the class of state representation learning models (see Lesort et al. (2018) for
121   an overview), which decompose RL into learning a representation for observations and for policies.
122   Similar in this respect is DARLA Higgins et al. (2017), which uses VAEs for this purpose as well and
123   shows that such an approach can provide TL with invariance to unforeseen variations. DARLA is
124   not intended for CRL, although it is unclear how invariance can be controlled and restricted. Finally,
125   the DisCoRL model Traoré et al. (2019) employs a babbling phase (purely random exploration) for
126   learning a model encoding representational states with some degree of invariance. This is a very
127   significant contribution, albeit limited to cases where random exploration will reach all possible
128
      observational states, which is unlikely to be the case in general settings.
129   Little consensus exists concerning the benchmarks on which to evaluate CRL. Commonly used
130   benchmarks are Atari gamesAtkinson et al. (2018b), Continual World Wołczyk et al. (2021), but
131   mostly self-defined benchmarks Traoré et al. (2019); Daniels et al. (2022); Tomilin et al. (2024).

199
200   In this benchmark, the robot is placed in front of one out of several colored cubes (see fig. 2) and
201   ends with the robot losing visual contact, pushing the cube or reaching the maximum number of 30
202   actions. Pushing or stopping should depend on a cube’s mass (tied to color): massive cubes must
203   not be touched/pushed, whereas massless cubes should be pushed. The robot is initially tilted ±15
204   degrees away from the cube it is facing, so a purely random walk will not bring it, on average,
205   near the cube. This benchmark consists of four tasks PO1 - PO4 separated by an environment
206   shift that modifies cube colors (red, blue, green, then yellow) and masses (20kg,0,0, then 20kg).
207
      Observations ⃗ot are 100 × 100 RGB images downsampled to 20 × 20 size obtained by the robot’s

208
      A(⃗ot , at ) is given continuously, and B(t) only for a terminal state. Terminal states are reached either
209

      2.2     BASELINES
247
      One set of evaluation baselines relies on vanilla deep Q-learning (DQN) and double deep Q-learning
248   (DDQN) with experience replay (ER) using a buffer of size M . We test several values for M for
249   each benchmark such that the buffer is either much larger than one task’s worth of samples, or
250   much smaller. If the buffer is large, then we should expect that it can mitigate CF, and the reverse
251   should be the case for a small buffer. Conversely, small buffer sizes should enable fast learning.
252   Sampling from the buffer is performed either uniformly Vitter (1985) or via prioritized experience
253   replay (PER) Schaul et al. (2015). All DQN methods are realized by a three-hidden-layer DNN with
254   256 units in each layer. Exploration is performed in an ϵ-greedy fashion, where ϵ is decreased from
255   an initial value of ϵ0 by the equation ϵt = ϵt−1 − ∆ϵ . We tune ∆ϵ such that ϵ = 0.2 at the end
256
      of each task, and set ϵ0 to 1.0 before the first task, and to 0.5 at the start of tasks n > 1 in order
      to re-use existing knowledge where possible and feasible. For prioritized experience replay, we use
257
      consensus parameter values α = 0.6 and β = 0.6 and perform linear annealing of β such that its
258
      value reaches 1 at the end of each task. All DNNs are trained using the Adam optimizer with a
259   learning rate of 0.001 and a mini-batch size of 32. Update frequencies for DDQN are always 200
260   iterations. The discount factor for Q-learning is always set to γ = 0.8.
261
262
      For completeness, we also investigate sequential fine-tuning (SFT) to adapt to new tasks, using no
      replay buffer but rather a DNN learning rate reduced by a factor of 10.

294
295   ARL architecture An ARL experiment is subdivided into tasks of length T , at the onset of which
296   environment statistics change. Generally, an ARL agent is not informed about this. ARL learning
297   is conducted in alternating wake-sleep phases, which together form a learning cycle. An ARL agent
298
      consists of an AR learner S which models the transitions of the current cycle c, Dc , while retaining
      previous knowledge D1:c−1 due to selective sampling. In wake phases of length C < T , the
299
      agent explores its environment according to the chosen exploration strategy and stores transitions
300
      (⃗ot , ⃗ot−1 , at , rt ) in a buffer together with their TD error et measured by S, which is measured by
301   the learner S. This is visualized in fig. 4. At the same time, the solver (not the generator) of the AR
302   learner S is updated with transitions from the buffer irrespectively of TD error. Before sleep phases,
303   the initial adaptation radius σ0 is set to a pre-determined value, a percentage χ of highest-error
304   transitions is selected from the buffer and the learner is copied to a long-term memory L such that
305   the long-term memory represents past-cycle data: L ∼ D1:c−1 . Then, the generator of S is trained
306   with high-error transitions from the buffer until convergence, i.e., when the adaptation radius σ(t)
307   (see Gepperth & Pfülb (2021) for details) reaches a predetermined value. Subsequently, the buffer
308   is cleared. This is visualized in fig. 5.
309
      Reasoning behind ARL design choices A common assumption in CL literature, see section 1,
310
      is that data Dn for the current task n is immediately available , meaning that selective sampling
311   can be performed before adaptation takes place. For ARL, cycles take the place of tasks, and data
312   acquisition is sequential. If generator adaptation were performed in parallel to selective sampling, at
313   some point generated samples would no longer reflect the statistics of past cycles, but of the current
314   cycle c as well. Selective sampling using the generator of the frozen L instead of the plastic S
315   ensures sampling from the right distribution: q ∼ D1:c−1 . If L is to generate samples that S will
316   likely confuse with incoming ones, these two instances should not diverge too much during a sleep
317   phase. Therefore, wake phases should not collect too many examples and therefore be rather short:
318   C << T .

414   In this set of experiments on ARL, we will visualize the samples with highest TD errors collected

416   will be done for the pushing-objects benchmark only, since its samples (20x20x3 RGB images)
417
      have the easiest visual interpretation. An useful property of the GMMs employed for representation
      learning is that their component centroids "live" in the space of the data they model. This means
418
      they can themselves be interpreted and visualized as images, thus allowing to understand what has
419
      been learned. Indeed, this explainability property is an important advantage of GMMs over DNNs.
420   In fig. 6, we show the centroids for a ratio of generated to real samples of 1 (easier for visualization
421   since new samples have a stronger impact) and the usual AR parameters, at the end of the babbling
422   phase as well as the end of tasks 1-4. For all tasks, we observe a gradual integration of new-
423   task centroids into existing ones, in a way that shifts and redistributes existing centroids instead of
424   simply replacing them. This is due to the selective replay mechanism, see section 2.3. In particular,
425   we observe a shift towards larger blocks after processing task 1 w.r.t. the babbling phase, purely
426   random motor babbling will, in general, not lead the robot close to a red block. Consequently, when
427   large blocks are encountered, their associated TD error is high, leading to inclusion into the learning
428
      set for the sleep phase.

430   associated TD error. We generally observe that observations close to a block have the highest TD-

      and thus to large errors if this is not anticipated. We observe that task 4 blocks have a color similar

445   2.8   A BLATION STUDY FOR ARL
446
      In order to understand how the various ARL parameter contribute to the method’s performance,
447
      we vary each relevant one individually while leaving the others constant. Obviously, we can do
448   this only for the most relevant parameters, which we identified to be (see also section 2.3): the
449   number of wake phases per task T /C, the percentage χ of highest-error transitions to be used for
450   representation learning in sleep phases, the ratio of generated to real samples in ARL, and the initial
451   adaptation radius σ0 for the generator. Similarly to the preceding section, we present the detailed
452   performance measure Pmn for each benchmark and parameter setting. The observed picture is not
453   very clear. Some parameter variations universally result in inferior performance, e.g., the number
454   of sleep phases, for which the best value seems to be 1. Setting σ0 too large also seems to be
455   problematic, as is too high a ratio of generated to observed transitions, This parameter governs,
456
      among others, how important it is to preserve past knowledge w.r.t. acquiring new knowledge and
      is always hard to tune, even for generative replay models in supervised CL.
457
458   Give the observed variability of experimental outcomes, we may therefore conclude that most pa-
459   rameter variations have impact on performance although these, in general, do not result in a catas-
460   trophic loss of acquired policies.

462   2.9   D ISCUSSION
463
464   Choice of evaluation benchmarks The chosen benchmarks were chosen because they include a
465   potentially large number of tasks which have comparable difficulty, so the ordering of tasks does not
466   impact the results. Furthermore, they allow expressive visualizations of the learned representations
467   as shown in section 2.7. And lastly, their intrinsic difficulty is relatively low, so we can be sure that
468   negative results are not caused by insufficient model complexity. Inspirations for these benchmarks
469
      were taken from other works on representation learning, see, e.g., Traoré et al. (2019). For establish-
      ing the basic capacity for continuous RL, these benchmarks therefore seem more appropriate to us
470
      than, e.g., Atari Games which, besides, contain a significant number of parameters on which results
471
      may critically depend (initialization for example).
472
473   Choice of baselines We concentrated on variants of Q-learning here because we wished to demon-
474   strate a very basic effect, i.e, the strong reliance on large replay buffers with the associated affects
475   on learning speed. Since more advanced RL variants like soft actor-critic (Haarnoja et al. (2018);
476   Wolczyk et al. (2022a)) contain additional functions to be approximated by neural networks like
477   the state-value function in SAC, the issue of catastrophic forgetting will be even more pronounced,
478
      require large buffers again, with the demonstrated consequences.
479
      CL methods for CRL: adiabatic -vs- experience replay We experimentally verified that experi-
480   ence replay (ER) with large buffers is capable of dealing with the presented benchmarks. However,
481   this implies a replay buffer large enough to store all transitions seen so far. It follows that the buffer
482   size must scale linearly with the number of CRL tasks. While this might be acceptable when con-
483   sidering memory only, there is also time complexity to be considered: as shown in section 2.6, the
484   learning of new tasks is slowed down by large buffers. Thus, the time until a new task has been prop-
485   erly acquired increases linearly with the number of previous tasks (i.e., buffer size), which might be
      considered unacceptable. In contrast, ARL just requires a single frozen model that is created on-

528   3       C ONCLUSIONS , GENERALITY OF RESULTS
529
530
531
      A principal conclusion from this article is that RL can be conducted completely without replay
      buffers, and with high-TD-error samples only. This entails a fast reaction to changes in the envi-
532
      ronment which we model by CRL tasks, see section 2.1. CF is mitigated by using adiabatic replay,
533
      which, although requiring computational resources, can be performed at constant time complexity, a
534   significant improvement w.r.t. existing CL approaches like generative replay. All of these advantages
535   are obtained by replacing DNNs by adiabatic replay based on GMMs, i.e., by trading computational
536   power of the employed models for the ability to learn continuously. Foundation models Verwimp
537   et al. (2023) may be used to increase the computational power, or else deep hierarchical variants of
538   AR based on Gepperth (2022). We believe that the ability to learn continuously is a very important
539   ingredient when it comes to large-scale and long-term learning for truly human-level performance
      in future intelligent systems.
