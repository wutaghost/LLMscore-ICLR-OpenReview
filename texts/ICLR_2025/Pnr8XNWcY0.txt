009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011             The success of large language models (LLMs) has prompted efforts to integrate
012             speech and audio data, aiming to create general foundation models capable of
013             processing both textual and non-textual inputs. Recent advances, such as GPT-
014             4o, highlight the potential for end-to-end speech LLMs, which preserves non-
015             semantic information and world knowledge for deeper speech understanding. To
016             guide the development of speech LLMs, we propose a five-level roadmap, ranging
017             from basic automatic speech recognition (ASR) to advanced superhuman mod-
018             els capable of integrating non-semantic information with abstract acoustic knowl-
019
                edge for complex tasks. Moreover, we design a benchmark, SAGI Bechmark,
                that standardizes critical aspects across various tasks in these five levels, uncover-
020
                ing challenges in using abstract acoustic knowledge and completeness of capabil-
021
                ity. Our findings reveal gaps in handling paralinguistic cues and abstract acoustic
022             knowledge, and we offer future directions. This paper outlines a roadmap for ad-
023             vancing speech LLMs, introduces a benchmark for evaluation, and provides key
024             insights into their current limitations and potential.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Paradigms to process language have been reshaped due to large language model (LLM) and its scal-
030   ing law. Given the success of LLMs, one may expect to integrate extensive data in speech and audio
031   modality into LLMs (similar to visual language models Liu et al. (2023); Li et al. (2023) 1 ), result-
032   ing in a more general foundation model. Towards this path, the exploration on speech foundation
033   models recently brings new research insights from the perspectives of multi-task and multi-lingual
034   processing (Radford et al., 2023; Bapna et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023c; Seamless Communication
035
      et al., 2023; Pratap et al., 2024). A remarkable event is the release of GPT-4o, which is notable
      for its ability in open-ended speech-to-speech dialogue. Its performance in speech understanding,
036
      speech synthesis, and system latency has reached new levels, leading to a wave of studies on speech
037
      LLMs. The next question is, where are we now and where should we go? To answer this, we begin
038   by introducing the potential of using LLMs to understand speech.
039
040   Processing Speech using LLMs Compared to the traditional approach of feeding ASR-transcribed
041
      text into text-only language models, unified speech-language models process raw audio or speech di-
      rectly in an end-to-end fashion. The benefits for using LLMs to process speech are mainly two-fold.
042
      I) Preservation of non-semantic information: Processing raw speech directly through language
043
      models allows for the preservation of non-semantic information, such as emphasis, speaker identity,
044   background sounds, emotions, and feelings, to the greatest extent possible. II) World knowledge
045   inherited in LLMs: LLMs have superior language understanding capabilities compared to tradi-
046   tional models and store vast amounts of world knowledge. Therefore, starting with an LLM as the
047   foundation for speech processing allows for the natural inheritance of this embedded knowledge,
048   which might benefit at speech recognition level.
049
      Five-level Speech Understanding The two benefits highlight the potential of speech LLMs, achiev-
050   ing of which requires the models to perceive complete speech information and achieve abstraction

150   2.2   T HE P HILOSOPHY OF THE ROADMAP
151
152
      With the rise of large language models (LLMs), there is an increasing demand to understand infor-
      mation beyond text, particularly speech. The core idea is that speech conveys richer information
153
      than text alone, positioning ASR (Automatic Speech Recognition) as a foundational level. End-to-
154
      end speech LLMs can begin with ASR capabilities to directly leverage the capabilities of text LLMs.
155   And then, it progressively incorporate more advanced comprehension of non-semantic features. Fi-
156   nally it contains the ability to retain and apply abstract acoustic knowledge. This progress can be
157   described as evolving through the following five levels:
158   Level 1. Speech Recognition Level At the most basic level, a speech language model should be
159   capable of recognizing text.
160
161   These tasks form the most fundamental requirements for interacting with large models using speech.
      However, even at Level 1, the model offers limited advantages over a traditional cascade approach

      at the next level, with the ability to capture non-semantic features such as paralinguistic information.
183   Level 2. Basic Paralinguistic Perception Level At this level, Speech LLMs gain the ability to
184   perceive basic paralinguistic features in speech, such as tone, pitch, volume, rhythm, and speech
185   rate.
186
      These elements are essential to speech comprehension and provide distinct advantages over pure
187   text-based models (or Speech LLMs at Level 1). While this lays the foundation for more advanced
188   capabilities, the insights derived at this level are still relatively shallow. For deeper understanding,
189   we must move to Level 3, where a model comprehends a broader range of non-semantic information.
190
      Level 3. Non-semantic Comprehension Level At this stage, the Speech LLM extends beyond ba-
191   sic paralinguistic features and is capable of comprehending and interpreting more complex non-
192   semantic information, such as emotions, sarcasm, and heightened states like pride.
193
194   For example, emotions are higher-level human experiences that involve cognitive functions, dis-
195   tinguishing them from basic paralinguistic information. Interestingly, even some higher animals,
196   like pet dogs, can perceive these types of non-semantic information. To fundamentally distinguish
197
      humans from animals, we designed Level 4 by leveraging the human strengths in higher-level cog-
      nitive capabilities.
198
199   Level 4. Speech Specialist Level At this advanced level, Speech LLMs integrate expert-level ab-
200   stract acoustic knowledge to handle a few specific, complex tasks.
201
      This requires integrating abstract acoustic knowledge which are advanced knowledge derived from
202   acoustic information. This goes beyond mere recognition and comprehension at Level 1 and Level
203   2, requiring the model to apply higher-order thinking skills (such as analysis, evaluation, and cre-
204   ation) based on acoustic information 2 , according to Bloom’s cognitive taxonomy Krathwohl (2002).
205   Despite these abilities, the model at this level remains domain-specific, which leads to the need for
206   a fully generalized Speech LLM, as defined by Level 5.
207   Level 5. Speech AGI level The ultimate level, Speech Artificial General Intelligence (SAGI), repre-
208   sents a comprehensive speech model that functions as a generalist. It can integrate knowledge from
209   various domains and perform both general and specialized tasks, potentially surpassing human ex-
210   perts.
211
212
      This vision of SAGI represents the culmination of speech understanding, combining domain exper-
      tise, adaptability, and the capacity to exceed human performance in speech-based tasks. SAGI’s

258   Philosophy of Benchmark The SAGI Benchmark is structured to align with the five levels of
259   speech understanding3 , and the overview of the benchmark is shown in Tab. 2. The tasks are or-
260   ganized into five levels: Level 1 focuses on testing the recognition capabilities of speech LLMs,
261   including ASR, lyrics transcription, and term recognition tasks. Level 2 evaluates foundational per-
262
      ception abilities, such as pitch and volume perception for tasks like age, gender, and emotion recog-

263
      translation, environment perception, and emotional intensity recognition. Level 4 explores the appli-
264
      cation of abstract acoustic knowledge, specifically focusing on medical-related contexts. Finally,
265   Level 5 envisions the capabilities of Speech AGI (SAGI), highlighting tasks that promote creativity
266   and diverse thinking, such as appreciating artwork, with a strong foundation in earlier levels.

498   5   R ELATED W ORK
499
500   Speech language models have seen a surge in development following the advent of LLMs. Currently,
501   most work integrates pre-trained acoustic models with LLMs using an alignment module. There
502   are two main strategies to bridge the gap between the two models: 1) adapters and 2) attention
503
      mechanisms.
504   Adapter The former method adds modules (usually convolutional networks and MLPs) between
505   the acoustic model and LLMs. Convolutional networks can compress sequence length (Wang et al.,
506   2023a), while MLPs are used to align acoustic tokens with text embeddings (Su et al., 2023).
507   Attention Mechanisms Regarding the attention method, Kong et al. (2024) implemented cross-
508   attention to filter information from the output of the speech encoder. Li et al. (2023) proposed
509   the Q-former as an intermediate extractor based on cross-attention. Similarly, Pan et al. (2023)
510   applied the Q-former to extract useful acoustic information for LLMs. Some works directly treat the
511   acoustic codec as tokens and do not rely on alignment strategies (Zhang et al., 2023a; Rubenstein
512   et al., 2023).
513   Categorization of speech LLMs We have introduced that acoustic models can generally be di-
514   vided into four types. Some works aim to build universal multi-modal LLMs (Su et al., 2023;
515   Zhan et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2023b; Lyu et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023b; Shukor et al., 2023). Sev-
516   eral studies focus on enhancing music understanding, an important area that has not yet received
517   enough attention (Deshmukh et al., 2023; Zhan et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024a). Most speech LLMs
518   aim to improve speech-to-text tasks and multi-turn dialogue capabilities (Fathullah et al., 2024;
519   Shu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023b; Pan et al., 2023; Rubenstein et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a;
520   Bai et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2023a; Maiti et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023a; Chu et al., 2024; Dubey
521
      et al., 2024). Some works utilize audio codec models to enhance audio processing performance
      (Chen et al., 2023; Kong et al., 2024; Nguyen et al., 2024; Das et al., 2024; Gong et al., 2023).
522
      Inspired by these efforts, several studies (Tang et al., 2023; Ghosh et al., 2024a; Hu et al., 2024)
523
      combine acoustic and semantic codecs to integrate audio and speech processing capabilities into a
524   single model.

527   6   C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this paper, we explored the evolving landscape of large language models (LLMs) in the realm
530
      of speech processing. We introduced a five-level roadmap to guide the development of human-level
      speech understanding, from basic ASR capabilities to advanced generalist models that integrate non-
531
      semantic information with general abstract acoustic knowledge for complex tasks. To assess the
532
      current state of speech LLMs, we designed a comprehensive benchmark that standardizes critical
533   aspects across various tasks, ensuring consistency and reliability in performance evaluation. Our
534   research reveals the current stage and deficiencies in understanding speech by both humans and
535   speech LLMs. We evaluate the advanced speech mode of GPT-4o and find that following speech
536   instructions is very challenging. Further analysis has uncovered structural flaws in existing speech
537   LLMs. Reveals that current speech LLMs face issues in both Acoustic Information Transfer and
538   Foundation LLMs’ Potentiality. The contributions of this paper provide a structured approach to
539   advancing speech LLMs, offering valuable insights for future innovations in this field.

      L IMITATION
757
758   Artificial intelligence should not be confined to overly narrow domains, as such a focus can lead to
759   frequent model switching when handling diverse tasks.This requires SAGI, a speech AGI, to be a
760   powerful assistant capable of completing all kinds of tasks. However, during our primary testing,
761   most speech LLMs remain at levels 1 and 2, indicating there is still a long way to go in terms of
762   understanding speech.
763
      To advance further, we conclude some important directions for improving speech LLMs toward
764   higher level:
765
766         • Requiring more diverse speech data to handle complex tasks.
767         • Enhancing the ability of text LLMs to process speech-related tasks.
768
            • Ensuring that LLMs can receive complete acoustic information.
769
770   We advocate for the development of more powerful acoustic models, consideration of cross-domain
771   compatibility when constructing datasets, and a deepening of expertise in specific research areas.
772   This approach will enhance the generalization and adaptability of the models.

1081
1082   We believe that translations should reflect different expressions based on the emotional context.
1083   For example, the phrase “What are you doing?” can convey various meanings depending on the
1084   emotion—whether it’s anger, surprise, sadness, or neutrality. In an angry context, it expresses strong
1085   disapproval or questioning of the person’s actions; in a surprised context, it conveys disbelief about
1086   what the other person is doing; and in a sad context, it should reflect disappointment. Therefore,
1087
       translations should be adjusted accordingly to better capture these nuances.
1088   We observed that cosyVoice (SpeechTeam, 2024) demonstrates excellent zero-shot capabilities,
1089   effectively mimicking the tone and style of the input speech prompt. Therefore, we used cosyVoice
1090   to emulate the sentences with strong emotions from the RAVDESS (Livingstone & Russo, 2018)
1091   dataset to generate speech with corresponding emotions. After synthesis, we had five native speakers
1092   review the generated speech. If any of the native speakers felt that the synthesized speech did not
1093
       convey the intended emotion, that segment was discarded. Ultimately, we obtained xxx valid speech
       samples.and the task was set as: “Please translate the following sentence into the most appropriate
1094
       Chinese, based on the emotion and content of this audio segment.”.

1335   D.4.1    WER FOR ASR
1336
1337   The Word Error Rate (WER), a key metric for gauging the effectiveness of Automatic Speech Recog-
1338   nition (ASR) systems, quantifies the divergence between an ASR system’s output and a reference
1339   transcript. It assesses the total error rate by tallying the number of insertion, deletion, and substitu-
1340
       tion operations needed to align the ASR output with the true reference text.
1341   While computing the WER, certain variances in word usage, like ”I am” compared to ”I’m,” may
1342   be seen as semantically equivalent by human standards but are flagged as errors by computational
1343   algorithms. Thus, a standardization process is essential prior to WER calculation to make both texts
1344   directly comparable. The methodology for this standardization, akin to what is employed in the
1345   Whisper (Radford et al., 2023) framework, has been detailed in a related research paper. It has been
1346
       demonstrated that this approach exerts negligible influence on the assessment of WER outcomes
       when tested against the LibriSpeech (Panayotov et al., 2015) dataset, which was utilized in our
1347
       paper.
1348
1349   For cases where the error rate exceeds 100% (i.e., WER is over 1), we mark them in our experimental
       records as having significant recognition errors. Such data will not be included in the calculation


                                                          25
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1350
       of the final average WER. In the final record of the experiment, we will focus on two key metrics:
1351   first, the ASR completion rate, which is the percentage of data with a WER less than 1; second, the
1352   mean WER of the completed portion, which is the average WER of data with a WER less than 1. If
1353   the mean WER of the completed portion does not decrease to below 0.8, we will conclude that the
1354   model lacks effective automatic speech recognition (ASR) capabilities and document this finding in
1355   detail in the experimental results.
1356
       The implementation details regarding WER (Word Error Rate) can be found in our publicly available
1357   code.
