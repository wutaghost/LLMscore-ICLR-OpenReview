                                                A BSTRACT
034
              Despite its potential, 3D generation lags behind 2D generation in quality and util-
035
              ity, primarily due to the vast gap in the scale and diversity of training data—high-
036           quality 2D data is abundant, while high-quality 3D assets remain limited by orders
037           of magnitude. Existing methods use 2D generative priors for 3D asset creation
038           via distillation or generate-and-reconstruct schemes, both of which suffer from
039           quality loss during optimization. In this paper, we propose a novel scheme to
040           exploit 2D diffusion prior to 3d generation by integrating a lightweight adapter
041           into the decoder of a frozen 2D diffusion model, allowing it to generate RGB
042           images, Gaussian splats, and physics-based rendering material maps simultane-
043           ously. Once trained, the proposed Lightweight Image Splats Adaptation (LISA)
044
              directly produces relightable Gaussian splats in feed-forward manner, which can
              be converted into high-quality, relightable 3D meshes through an inverse render-
045
              ing framework. Quantitative and qualitative results demonstrate that our method
046
              outperforms state-of-the-art approaches with a significantly lower computational
047           budget for both training and sampling. More results can be found at https://LISA-
048           3dgen.github.io.

052
053   AI-generated content(AIGC) has achieved remarkable progress in recent years, especially in 2D im-
      age generation Rombach et al. (2022). However, 3D generation has been left far behind with 3D

      datasets of limited scale and quality. Though suffering from time-consuming per-prompt optimiza-
055   tion and 3D inconsistency, Dreamfusion Poole et al. (2022) and its follow-up works Wang et al.
056   (2023); Chen et al. (2023); Lin et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2024a); Chen et al. (2024b); Tang et al.
057   (2023) achieve zero-shot 3D generation via score distillation from pre-trained 2D diffusion priors.
058   To better leverage 2D priors, pioneer works Shi et al. (2023a); Long et al. (2024); Liu et al. (2023a);
059   Shi et al. (2023b) fine-tune stable diffusion models on the large-scale open-world 3D dataset Deitke
060   et al. (2023; 2024); Yu et al. (2023a) to inject 3D consistency prior into 2D diffusion models, known
061   as multi-view(MV) diffusion models. Large reconstruction models(LRMs) Li et al. (2023a); Hong
062   et al. (2023); Xu et al. (2023b; 2024a); Tang et al. (2024); Xu et al. (2024b) lift MV images to
063
      3D representation Mildenhall et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2021a); Shen et al. (2023); Kerbl et al.
      (2023) via a large feed-forward model. Although the latter diagram reconstructs 3D representation
064
      from multi-view images, the LRMs are trained on 3D datasets with limited scale and have no direct
065
      access to 2D diffusion priors, failing to explore 2D diffusion priors encoded in the network fully.
066
067   Does a 2D diffusion model already encode 3D information? We conduct a toy experiment to in-
068   vestigate this. We take an off-the-shelf multi-view latent diffusion model and attach a lightweight
069
      decoder, allowing it to output a splatter image Szymanowicz et al. (2024) (where each pixel has a
      Gaussian splat’s mean, opacity, and covariance features instead of RGB color). We only fine-tune
070
      this decoder on a toy dataset that contains multiple 3D plants, while freezing all other learnable
071
      components. Surprisingly, we find that this new model is able to generate complete 3D Gaussian
072   assets, even for input prompts that are not plants, such as animals and robots. This suggests that (1)
073   multi-view latent diffusion might already encode 3D knowledge; we just need a lightweight adapta-
074   tion to unleash it, allowing it to output 3D, and (2) such 3D generation can retain the rich knowledge
075   learned in 2D diffusion and transfer it to 3D without forgetting, thereby enabling us to leverage
076   widely available 2D data.
077
      Inspired by the findings above, we present the Lightweight Image Splats Adaptation (LISA) to
078   repurpose 2D diffusion for end-to-end realistic, relightable, and generalizable 3D asset generation,
079   as shown in Figure 1. LISA achieves efficient end-to-end 3D generation by directly adapting pre-
080   trained layers from 2D diffusion models to output Gaussian splat images via additional learnable
081   zero convolution layers. We can easily fuse the Gaussian splats from multiple views and ensemble
082   a complete 3D Gaussian asset. This lightweight adaptation approach maximizes the preservation of
083   rich generative priors learned from 2D data while generalizing for 3D creation. Moreover, it benefits
084   from low 3D training data requirements as well as fast sampling and rendering speeds. To produce
085   realistic and user-ready 3D assets, we further apply test-time inverse rendering to convert our 3D
086
      Gaussians into high-quality, UV-mapped, relightable 3D meshes.
087   Our experiments show that LISA achieves superior performance over prior 3D generation ap-
088   proaches in terms of both geometry quality and rendering quality, demonstrating the efficacy of
089   the proposed framework. Notably, LISA achieves this with significantly less 3D training data and
090   faster generation times. Specifically, with a subset of 46K high-quality multi-view instances from
091   G-Objaverse Qiu et al. (2024), LISA can be efficiently fine-tuned to generate 2D Gaussian splats
092
      with coarse PBR materials, based on which we further achieve high-quality mesh generation with
      PBR materials in under 30 seconds on a single RTX 3090 GPU.

193   3.2    L IGHTWEIGHT I MAGE S PLATS A DAPTATION
194
195
      Except for current ways to leverage 2D diffusion priors for 3D generation via distillation or generate-
      and-reconstruct scheme, we opt to exploit another way to achieve end-to-end 3D generation by
196
      modifying multi-view diffusion models to output 3D representation. However, the challenge mainly
197
      lies in the entirely different organization of primitive data. To mitigate the gap, we construct the
198   generation part using the Gaussian Splatting organized in multi-view Gaussian attribute maps as
199   mentioned in Section 3.1, and for simplicity, we use 3DGS instead of 2DGS in this section.
200
201
      With proper 3D representation, we propose to extract 3D information from 2D diffusion models
      without violating pre-trained priors. ControlNet Zhang et al. (2023) paves an efficient way to inject
202
      control information into a large pre-rained diffusion model via a new branch adapted by zero con-
203
      volution layers, inspired by which we present our Lightweight Image Splats Adaptation (LISA) to
204   achieve direct 3D generation, except that ControlNet is designed to modulate extra information into
205   the diffusion model while LISA is to demodulate extra information from the model.
206
207
      We introduce our LISA model from the basic building block, which we name the LISA block and
      LISA switcher, as in Figure 3. For simplicity, we follow the notation from Zhang et al. (2023),
208
      and refer network block in diffusion models as commonly combined network blocks, such as resnet
209
      block, conv-bn-relu block, transformer block, et al. The LISA block clones and freezes the pre-
210   trained layer from 2D diffusion models, adapts the information flow from the previous layer, skip
211   connection, and intermediate information from frozen 2D diffusion models with zero convolution
212   layers. With such a design, the LISA block outputs the same information with the same inputs given
213   at the start of training. Therefore, the constructed model maximizes the preservation of rich gener-
214   ative priors learned from 2D data while generalizing for 3D creation. As we unleash 2D diffusion
215   priors to output extra information as 3D representation, we construct LISA with the decoder part of
      the 2D diffusion models.

342   3.5   G EOMETRY AND T EXTURE R EFINEMENT
343
344   Based on the generated 2DGS from our feed-forward network, we first extract meshes from 2DGS
345   via TSDF Fusion and refine them through continuous remeshing Palfinger (2022), then initialize
346   texture maps for the 3D assets and leverage differentiable ray-tracer to refine the PBR materials.
347
      Geometry extraction and refinement. To extract meshes from 2DGS, we render albedo and depth
348   along circle camera paths at elevations of [10, 15, 20] around the instance plus a top view and a
349   bottom view, and we use the ScalableTSDFVolume from open3d Zhou et al. (2018) with the voxel
350   size of 0.004 and the truncation threshold of 0.02 to perform TSDF Fusion to extract the initial
351   mesh. Then, we extract the convex hull of the initial mesh to fill all the holes in the original mesh.
352   Finally, we render normal maps and alpha maps from the 2DGS along circle paths at elevations
353   of [−40, −30, −20, −10, 0, 10, 20, 30, 40] around the instance as the target views and perform 100
354   iterations of continuous remeshing Palfinger (2022) to transform the convex hull into high-quality,
355   smooth meshes.
356
      Texture initialization and refinement. After mesh extraction, we use the smart uv project from
357
      Blender Community (2018) to generate UV maps for the mesh, and we unproject the rendered
358   albedo, metallic, and roughness from 2DGS onto the mesh to initialize the PBR materials. Then
359   following Ummenhofer et al. (2024), we use differentiable ray-tracer from Jakob et al. (2022b;a) to
360   align the four orthogonal images with the generated four views from MVDream.

531   socre distillation or generation-and-reconstruct scheme. We propose a novel LISA model that modu-
