      3.1   A DVANTAGES OF THE C HINESE V ERSION OF THE P ERIODIC TABLE
156
157   In 1869, Russian chemist Dmitri Mendeleev revolutionized chemistry and atomic science with the
158   periodic table. For phonetic languages like English and Portuguese, translating the periodic table
159   was relatively straightforward. However, adapting it into Chinese presented significant challenges.
160   Early Sino-cultural regions, such as Japan, relied on phonetic transliterations, leading to confusion
161   due to inconsistent usage. Some elements were represented by single characters, while others re-
      quired multiple characters.

162
      The Chinese periodic table not only addresses these issues of localization but also leverages the
163   unique advantages of Chinese characters, which encapsulate visual, phonetic, and semantic fea-
164   tures. For instance, the element “At” is symbolized by “砹”, where the “石” (stone) radical intu-
165   itively suggests a non-metal, while “艾” (Ai) provides the phonetic hint. Similarly, “Li” is denoted
166   by “锂”, where “钅” indicates metallic properties, and “里” contributes the phonetic element. Such
167   phono-semantic combinations simplify the representation and understanding of complex chemical
168   elements, making the Chinese version more efficient for both human learning and machine process-
169   ing.
170   Furthermore, the standardized rules for creating new element characters support the generation of
171   symbols for newly discovered elements. For example, on November 30, 2016, the International
172   Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry (IUPAC) announced the English names and symbols for four
173   new elements, completing the seventh period of the periodic table. On May 9, 2017, the Chinese
174   Academy of Sciences, the National Language Committee, and the National Science and Technology
175   Terminology Committee jointly released their Chinese names: “钅尔” (nı̌), “镆” (mò), “石田” (tián),
176   and “气奥” (ào).
177   Figure 2 presents examples of four elements (fluorine, neon, chlorine, and argon) from the periodic
178   tables of English, Chinese, Japanese, and Portuguese. In comparison, the English and Portuguese
179   versions are relatively concise. The Chinese version, however, presents a wealth of information
180   in a more concise and eye-catching manner within the same space. The Japanese version is more
181   complex, with elements represented by either 1-2 kanji characters (e.g., “塩素” for chlorine), a
182   combination of kanji and kana (e.g., “弗(フッ)素” for fluorine), or entirely phonetic kana (e.g., “ア
183
      ルゴン” for argon). Preliminary statistics indicate that English element names average 7.82 letters in

184
      (and bytes). For Latin language, this average increases slightly to 8.07 letters and bytes, also with a
185
      maximum of 13. In contrast, Japanese element names average 4.58 characters in length, occupying
186   approximately 13.73 bytes in Unicode/UTF-8 encoding, with a maximum of 9 characters, taking
187   up to 27 bytes. Additionally, 55% of the element names exceed the median value of 5 characters.
188   Meanwhile, each Chinese element name is represented by a single character, requiring only 3 bytes
189   in Unicode/UTF-8 encoding—making it significantly more concise.

      3.2   G ENERATING C OMPOUND NAMES BASED ON A FFIX AND ROOT C HARACTERS
217
218
      The naming of Chinese chemical element symbols marks only the beginning of a great endeavor.
219
      On this foundation, the naming of organic and inorganic chemical compounds unfolds its vast po-
220
      tential (Wang, 2010). The Chinese Chemical Society introduced the “Nomenclature of Organic
221   Compounds-2017,” (Society, 2017) establishing principles such as systematization, standardization,
222   simplification, and consistency, which have fostered the orderly and scientific development of chem-
223   ical research and education (Ma et al., 2019).
224
      The naming of organic compounds in Chinese is far more intricate than that of elements or inor-
225
      ganic compounds. Prior to the 20th century, organic compound names were phonetically transliter-
226
      ated from Western languages, leading to cumbersome terms (He, 2016). To resolve this, chemists
227   introduced single-character names like “烷” (alkane), “烯” (alkene), and “炔” (alkyne) based on
228   semantic translation. These concise terms convey the core meaning of the compounds and lay the
229   groundwork for naming their derivatives. For example, the Chinese character for “烃” (hydrocar-
230   bon) integrates elements from “碳” (carbon) and “氢” (hydrogen), with a “火” radical symbolizing
231   combustion. Hydrocarbons, made of carbon and hydrogen, are the basis for many organic com-
232   pounds such as alkanes, alkenes, and alkynes. The IUPAC nomenclature system (Favre & Powell,
233   2014) is often applied, with single bonds indicating alkanes, double bonds for alkenes, and triple
234   bonds for alkynes. Cyclic structures are named as cycloalkanes, and those with benzene rings as
235
      aromatic hydrocarbons (He, 2016). This alignment with chemical “semantics” demonstrates the
      evolution from element names to more complex compound terms.
236
237   In discussing word formation, prefixes, suffixes, connectors, and roots in Chinese compound words
238   are crucial. The “Nomenclature of Organic Compounds-2017” provides detailed naming rules for
239   organic compounds, which cannot be fully covered here. However, the use of Chinese numerals
240   and ordinal terms like the Heavenly Stems gives an insight into this system. The terms like “伯”
241
      (primary), “仲” (secondary), “叔” (tertiary), and “季” (quaternary) denote the substitution level of
      hydrogen atoms in hydrides. For example, hydrocarbons have primary, secondary, tertiary, and
242
      quaternary carbons, while amines correspond to primary, secondary, tertiary amines, and quaternary
243
      ammonium salts. These terms parallel their English counterparts and align Chinese and English
244   naming conventions (Society, 2017).
245
246   The Chinese character element symbols go beyond simple chemical notation and represent a cultural
247
      phenomenon, creatively merging phonetic and semantic elements with scientific knowledge. This
      method makes complex scientific ideas more tangible and supports research and education, while
248
      also enriching the cultural heritage of Chinese characters.
249
250   Moreover, applying Chinese characters in chemical nomenclature, especially in constructing com-
251   pound words, offers valuable insights for CNLP. This is a key point of this article. The efficiency of
252   Chinese characters in expressing information reduces computational complexity, making language
253
      models more effective. For cross-lingual robots, the high information density of Chinese charac-
      ters offers unique advantages. When processing the periodic table or compound names, humanoid
254
      robots, with their advanced multilingual processing capabilities, are able to efficiently acquire and
255
      utilize multiple languages, serving as a bridge across cultural and linguistic barriers.

      4.1   U SING SWPC TO PRESENT C HINESE CHARACTER AND WORD
271
272   Many large language models use Unicode/UTF-8 encoding to represent different language symbols
273   and achieve tokenization Wang et al. (2020). In this system, English letters are encoded using
274   ASCII, with each letter occupying one byte, while Chinese characters typically occupy 3-4 bytes.
275   This encoding system effectively addresses the problem of unified multilingual representation and
276   performs well in handling out-of-vocabulary (OOV) words. However, the processing of Chinese
277   characters still faces challenges in efficiency and in the adequate representation of linguistic features.
278   Particularly in natural language processing tasks, accurately expressing the phono-semantic structure
279   of Chinese characters remains a technical bottleneck that needs to be overcome.
280   In the past, researchers have attempted to use systems such as the Four-Corner Code, Wubi, and
281   Cangjie to aid natural language processing tasks through phonetic and structural encoding (Cao
282   et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2020; Sun et al., 2021; Jin et al., 2021; Lv et al., 2022). However, these
283   methods often encounter representation threshold issues in both encoding and image processing ap-
284   plications (Weigang et al., 2024b). For instance, among the 8,105 of the list of Commonly Used
285   Standard Chinese Characters (Commission, 2013), the Wubi encoding has a threshold of 14 strokes,
286
      covering 83.54% of the characters, while the Cangjie encoding has a threshold of 16 strokes, cov-
      ering 92.39%. Against this backdrop, SWPC method was developed, providing a foundational tool
287
      for multimodal Chinese character processing (Weigang et al., 2024a).

      Unlike fixed encoding schemes such as Wubi or Cangjie, which encounter difficulties when handling
311
      characters that exceed a certain complexity threshold, SWPC employs a dynamic encoding strategy.
312   This flexibility enables SWPC to represent a broader range of Chinese characters, making it more
313   suitable for multimodal and cross-lingual applications (Weigang et al., 2024a). The SWPC technol-
314   ogy encodes Chinese character radicals and components using two-letter combinations, resulting in
315   approximately 676 possibilities (26 x 26 letters). Based on this foundation, the SWPC code for most
316   Chinese characters typically consists of 4 letters, while more complex characters may require 6 to
317   12 letters.
318   Although English letters are represented by a single byte in the Unicode/UTF-8 system, 99% of
319   English words are composed of at least four letters. The SWPC model leverages this by using
320   English letters as symbolic representations of Chinese radicals and components, taking advantage
321   of the fact that one letter corresponds to one byte in Unicode/UTF-8 encoding. At the same time, it
322   preserves the grammatical rules of Chinese (semantic properties), which is beneficial since 99% of
323   Chinese words consist of no more than four characters. This combination of symbolic and semantic
      encoding is the core of the SWPC approach, see Figure 3.

413
414   This research integrates SWPC with advanced image processing techniques to develop multimodal
415
      algorithms for character and word recognition. We have constructed an SWPC database containing
      3,981 Chinese characters (Weigang et al., 2024a) and an image library featuring 33,950 distinct
416
      character representations (Weigang et al., 2024b). The Scale-Invariant Feature Transform (SIFT)
417
      technology (Lowe, 1999) is applied to optimize these algorithms, allowing for effective recognition
418   of Chinese characters and words in various contexts (Hu et al., 2013). While some state-of-the-art
419   (SOTA) deep learning algorithms, like MA-CRNN (Tong et al., 2020) and MaskOCR (Lyu et al.,
420   2024), can achieve higher accuracy in certain scenarios, SIFT’s interpretability and compatibility
421   with SWPC make it an ideal choice for providing a comprehensive understanding of the processing
422   and recognition of Chinese characters and words.
423
      Figure 6 illustrates how SIFT recognizes Chinese character images representing various chemical
424   elements. Each image has a resolution of 96x96 pixels, and SIFT analyzes the radicals and compo-
425   nents to identify the entire character. The recognition process involves the following steps:

509   5   C ONCLUSIONS
510
511   This paper emphasizes the unique advantages of Chinese characters in multimodal language process-
512   ing, particularly for humanoid robots. As a language system that intricately integrates pictographic
513   and phonetic elements, Chinese characters offer distinct benefits in expressing complex scientific
514   concepts and conveying cultural nuances. By examining the localization of the periodic table in Chi-
515   nese and the naming conventions for organic compounds, this study demonstrates how the structural
516   properties of Chinese characters open new opportunities for natural language processing (NLP).The
517   main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:
518
519
             • Advocating for the adoption of the systematic approach observed in Chinese chemical
               nomenclature to advance research in Chinese natural language processing (CNLP).
520
521          • Demonstrating the efficacy of Six-Writings Pictophonetic Coding (SWPC) technology in
522            encoding and recognizing Chinese characters by utilizing both visual and phonetic features.
523          • Proposing a novel multimodal processing framework that integrates SWPC with the Scale-
524            Invariant Feature Transform (SIFT) method to enhance character recognition.
525
             • Establishing the feasibility of leveraging Chinese characters as cross-lingual carriers in
526
               humanoid robot language processing.
527
528   As a cultural treasure and a linguistic heritage of Chinese civilization, Chinese characters have
529   gained new significance in the era of artificial intelligence. By further exploring the phonosemantic
530   encoding mechanisms and the hierarchical structure of Chinese character components, researchers
531   can better harness the strengths of Chinese characters, thereby enhancing language comprehension
532   and generation capabilities in humanoid robots. These developments will contribute to more intelli-
533   gent and harmonious human-robot interactions in diverse linguistic and cultural contexts.
534   Future research should focus on two key areas: (1) enhancing the SWPC concept and expanding the
535   SWPC library and image database to cover a broader range of Chinese characters, including both
536   simplified and traditional forms, and (2) developing intelligent algorithms to facilitate the efficient
537   search and combination of Chinese characters using radicals and components, as well as constructing
538   words through affixes and roots for sentence-level analysis. These advancements will pave the way
539   for more effective language processing in humanoid robots, enabling them to explore additional
      languages and create more sophisticated human-machine interaction scenarios.
