011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            With the advancement of large pre-trained vision-language models, effectively
014            transferring the knowledge embedded within these foundational models to down-
015            stream tasks has become a pivotal topic, particularly in data-scarce environments.
016
               Recently, parameter-efficient fine-tuning approaches, especially prompt tuning,
               have garnered considerable attention. To better understand the nature of prompt
017
               tuning, we propose the concept of “Information Density” (ID) to indicate whether
018
               a matrix strongly belongs to certain feature spaces rather than being evenly dis-
019            tributed across various feature spaces. We suppose a higher ID with strong bias
020            across some feature spaces naturally leads to excellent robustness and stability.
021            Our research, inspired by the observation that generalizability is closely linked to
022            the information density of the prompt embedding, introduces the Dense Informa-
023            tion Prompt (DIP). DIP aims to enhance information density to improve gener-
024            alization. Several alternative algorithms to increase ID are proposed and verified
025            effective. With further help of proper initialization and regularization, compre-
026            hensive experiments substantiate the superiority of DIP. Notably, DIP surpasses
027
               the latest state-of-the-art methods by a substantial margin with an exceptionally
               small parameter count and no extra inference overhead. Across a range of tasks
028
               spanning 11 datasets, DIP improves the average downstream accuracy of classic
029
               prompt tuning by up to 5.76%.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      In recent years, vision-languages models Radford et al. (2021); Jia et al. (2021) have achieved
035
      tremendous success. Representative models like CLIP Radford et al. (2021) are first pre-trained
036   on a huge number of text-image pairs on the web to align textual and visual features, and then can
037   be tuned and used for various downstream tasks.
038
039
      However, traditional fine-tuning is not a good choice to adapt vision-language models. Simply
      fine-tuning all the parameters can easily cause the model to overfit because the huge number of pa-
040
      rameters bring redundant non-essential information. The huge training and storage cost is also an
041
      intractable problem. In the context of our study, we introduce the concept of “information density”.
042   Much like the rank of a matrix in linear algebra, which represents the maximum number of linearly
043   independent rows or columns in the matrix, “information density” represents the maximum amount
044   of essential and non-redundant information that the model can extract from the downstream task.
045   Just as a matrix with a higher rank possesses more unique information, a model with high informa-
046   tion density can acquire more essential and general information from the downstream task by fewer
047   parameters, even with a smaller dataset. Our goal is increasing the information density and thus
048   using the fewest but most essential parameters to finish generalization, without causing catastrophic
049   forgetting or overfitting to the small dataset.
050   As the concept of information density can be functionally analogous to the rank of a matrix, we
051   decided to use properties related to rank to quantify information density. Specifically, we take a
052   full-rank matrix and decompose it using Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) to obtain a matrix
053   of singular values, using the properties of these singular values to define and quantize information
      density. We found that this definition of information density is highly correlated with the model’s


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      generalization performance (Spearman correlation coefficient > 0.9). Therefore, we propose DIP,
055   aiming to enhance the model’s generalization ability by increasing information density. Addition-
056   ally, due to the increased information carried by each parameter unit, our approach can significantly
057   reduce the required number of parameters.
058
      In Section 3, we will show our finding about the strong correlation between generalization capability
059
      and information density of the prompt matrix in Fig. 1. Inspired by such observation, we propose
060
      Dense Information Prompt (DIP) for effective and efficient adaptation, well adapting models under
061   an extremely small number of parameters where nobody has explored before. There are several good
062   advantages of DIP:
063
064          • Efficiency and Effectiveness By operating on lightweight prompts, we can reach compa-
065            rable or even better performance with state-of-the-art methods using very few parameters.
066          • Simplicity Replacing the classic prompt by DIP just needs to modify several lines of code.
067
             • Robustness DIP is relatively capable of anti-disturbance. As in Tab. 3, DIP could maxi-
068
               mally reserve its knowledge from domain shift.
069
070
             • Plug and Play For any classic model, DIP only replaces the prompts, enabling us to plug
               DIP into most of the existing methods fruitfully.
071
072
      In summary, we conclude our contributions as follows:
073
074          • We propose a new concept “information density”, give its definition and well-quantize the
075            concept. We further find the strong correlation between generalizability and information
076            density in Fig. 1, and thus propose DIP to increase information density for better general-
077            ization capability.
078
             • We design three effective implementations for DIP and prove that our motivation could be
               correctly verified through controlled experiments, i.e. improving ID significantly promotes
079
               generalizability.
080
             • We propose a novel initialization technique and integrate a lightweight regularization mod-
081            ule to further improve the performance of Dense Information Prompt tuning without intro-
082            ducing any extra parameters and inference cost.
083          • We conduct extensive experiments and show the fantastic effectiveness and efficiency of
084            DIP. In base-to-new generalization, domain generalization, cross-dataset transfer and few-
085            shot learning settings, DIP consistently reaches very competitive results, surpassing state-
086            of-the-art tuning methods.

090   2.1   V ISION -L ANGUAGE M ODELS
091
092   Recently, large-scale vision-language models have shown very competitive performance in various
093   tasks. Classic works Radford et al. (2021); Jia et al. (2021); Zhai et al. (2022); Yao et al. (2022); Yuan
094   et al. (2021) learn the multi-modal representation by a self-supervised manner on a large amount of
095   image-text pairs. The representative work CLIP Radford et al. (2021) is a milestone, which aligns
096   the vision representation and language representation by contrastive learning and shows excellent
097
      performance. A well-trained vision-language model is a great treasure, which could largely facilitate
      the development of many fields. There have been successful applications of such strong models on
098
      few-shot recognition Zhou et al. (2022b;a), detection Rasheed et al. (2022); Maaz et al. (2022);
099
      Feng et al. (2022); Zang et al. (2022) and segmentation Li et al. (2022); Rao et al. (2022); Ding et al.
100   (2022); Lüddecke & Ecker (2022). For video data, there are also works on video classification Qian
101   et al. (2022) and video understanding Ju et al. (2022).

103   2.2   P ROMPT T UNING
104
105   Prompt tuning is one of the most popular methods to tune models in downstream tasks with excel-
106   lent efficiency. Originating from natural language processing, prompts are first introduced as a fixed
107   template Schick & Schütze (2020), e.g. a photo of a , which is hand-crafted and fixed. Later, a series
      of methods Li & Liang (2021); Lester et al. (2021); Liu et al. (2021b); Shin et al. (2020); Liu et al.


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      (2021a); Jiang et al. (2021) are proposed to make such prompts tunable and be optimized during
109   adaptation. Prompt tuning could adaptively narrow the gap between pre-trained representations and
110   downstream tasks, significantly facilitating the fine-tuning process. Representative prompt tuning
111   methods would add tunable virtual tokens, i.e. prompts, along with the semantic tokens as inputs
112   of the model. All of the tokens are processed together to get text embeddings first and then sent
113   to the feature encoder. Witnessing the success of prompting language models, researchers design
114   prompts Jia et al. (2022); Zhang et al. (2022) for visual models in a similar way. In vision-language
115   field, there are several explorations as well. Bahng et al. Bahng et al. (2022) adopts prompt tuning
116   merely on the image encoder. CoOp Zhou et al. (2022a) uses tunable text prompts to replace the
117
      fixed template in CLIP Radford et al. (2021). CoCoOp Zhou et al. (2022b) utilizes image feature
      to instruct the optimization of the tunable text prompts in CoOp. Khattak et al. (2023a); Lee et al.
118
      (2023) simultaneously optimize image and text prompts and establish extra connections between
119
      different modals. Khattak et al. (2023b); Yao et al. (2023); Bulat & Tzimiropoulos (2023); Zheng
120   et al. (2023); Hao et al. (2024) integrate strong regularization modules or losses into prompt tuning
121   to diminish the overfitting and catastrophic forgetting problem. For better downstream accuracy,
122   researchers design more and more complicated methods, accompanied by inefficiency. To solve the
123   problem, we propose Dense Information Prompt (DIP) to take the place of classic prompts, which
124   can largely decrease the number of tunable parameters and further enhance the model’s general-
125   ization ability. Notice that though becoming more complex, existing methods are still refined on a
126   common fundamental basis, i.e. prompt tuning. Such a common basis guarantees that DIP could be
127   easily and smoothly integrated into most of the off-the-shelf methods besides individually applied.
128
      Besides prompt-based methods, there are also many other works to acquire storage efficiency Hao
      et al. (2023a); Houlsby et al. (2019); Hu et al. (2021); Lian et al. (2022); Zhang et al. (2022); Chen
129
      et al. (2022a), inference efficiency Chen et al. (2022b); Wang et al. (2023a); Bolya et al. (2023); Hao
130
      et al. (2023b); Ding et al. (2021; 2019); Chen et al. (2023); Shen et al. (2024); Xiong et al. (2024)
131   and data efficiency Wang et al. (2023b); Lyu et al. (2024) during downstream tuning.

193   3.2   I NFORMATION D ENSITY IN P ROMPT T UNING
194
195   Here, we first provide precise definitions for “information density” to clearly convey our motivations.
196   For typical parameter matrix like prompts P ∈ Rn×d (assume n < d), we can always rewrite
197
      such matrix into a combination of several orthogonal bases with different weights by singular value
                                                             Pd
198   decomposition (SVD). Formally, P = U ΣV T =                          T            T
                                                                i=1 σi ui vi . Each ui vi can span a unique
199
      feature space, and P is a linear combination of these features. Typically, {σi }ni=1 are arranged in
      descending order.
200
201   To help readers better understand the concept of information density, let’s draw an analogy using
202   images from nature. A real image always has one or a few very prominent features and almost
203   never contains an even mix of various odd features. In an extreme case, if an image truly exhibits
204   isotropic characteristics, it would simply mean that its content is almost entirely noise and lacks
205
      clear meaning. Reflecting this back to the matrix decomposition we discussed earlier, a good image
      tends to have several significantly larger singular values. The features of the image can largely be
206
      expressed by the feature space behind these prominent singular values. Therefore, from the matrix
207
      decomposition expression, the differences among the singular values {σi }ni=1 are significant, indi-
208   cating that the information is concentrated in a few feature spaces. In other words, the information
209   density is higher. Thus, to quantize such property, we define k-th order “Information Density (ID)”
                                                             k
210
                           P
                                                                 σ
      as follows: IDk = Pni=1 σii . In other words, IDk is the proportion of the largest k singular values
211                         i=1

212
      among all the singular values. Greater information density represents more robust and stable intrin-
      sic features, meaning they are less likely to be affected by external disturbances and have stronger
213
      anti-interference capabilities.

215   Returning to our initial discussion, a core contribution of this paper is the hypothesis and verifica-
      tion that the parameter matrices, like prompts, follow the same ID-related laws during fine-tuning

216
      in downstream CLIP models. As is well-known, in transfer learning, the transfer of knowledge in
217   a model depends on the updating of parameters. For CLIP models, the optimal solution is prompt
218   tuning Zhou et al. (2022a;b); Khattak et al. (2023a); Zhu et al. (2023). We first hypothesize that
219   the information density of the prompt matrix also represents its robustness and the strength of its
220   intrinsic features. Therefore, greater information density should theoretically result in better gener-
221   alizability throughout the prompt tuning process.
222
      To verify this hypothesis, we conducted an experiment using a classic method, CoOp Zhou et al.
223   (2022a). During training, we masked half of the classes, using data from only the other half for
224   training, and performed singular value decomposition on the prompt matrices during the process.
225   As shown in Fig. 1, for better visualization, ID1 is scaled up to 2x to be put under the same right
226   axis with ID2. Unseen classes accuracy is improved in the first few iterations, but it starts dropping
227   later, indicating overfitting and catastrophic forgetting. Importantly, the fluctuation trend of unseen
228   classes accuracy is highly consistent with ID. We compute the Spearman correlation coefficient
229   between unseen classes accuracy and ID1/ID2 in Fig. 1. Clearly, the first-order and second-order
230   information densities of CoOp on the SUN397 Xiao et al. (2010) and DTD Cimpoi et al. (2014)
231
      datasets exhibit a very strong correlation with the accuracy on unseen classes (i.e., generalizability),
      with Spearman correlation coefficients greater than 0.9. This demonstrates that our hypothesis is
232
      correct.

307   4.1.2   I NITIALIZATION
308
309   In the field of tuning vision-language models, existing works have confirmed that the initialization
310   method of prompts is quite important. For example, Zhou et al. (2022b;a) adopt a hand-crafted
311   template as the initial point of the text prompts, and Lee et al. (2023) copies the parameters in
312   the text or image class token to initialize the prompts of the corresponding branch. If we just do a
313   random initialization, the overall performance of such existing methods would drop severely. See
314
      Section 5.2 for more details. In other words, it would be helpful if we could take advantage of a
      good initial point.
315
316   The problem lies in the fact that directly applying dense information losses or structures on artifi-
317   cially designed initialization Pinit is not a good idea. Violet jitter might cause sever performance
318   drop for DIP-DO and DIP-PP. As for DIP-LRA, since k < min(n, d), it is impossible to directly
319   initialize PA and PB by a given Pinit .
320   To solve such a problem, we add a concurrent branch of normal frozen prompts Pinit ∈ Rn×d
321   along with the proposed DIP as shown in Fig. 2b. For DIP-DO and DIP-PP, an additional random
322   initialized prompt branch is used for update. For DIP-LRA, we randomly sample PA /PB from a
323   Gaussian distribution in which µ = 0 and σ → 0. A small σ here could avoid constant initialization
      and enrich the update paths. Notably, Pinit is kept frozen during the whole adaptation.

347   4.1.3    R EGULARIZATION
348
349
      As discussed in Section 2, existing works have shown that proper regularization would significantly
      improve the generalization ability. Therefore, to alleviate overfitting and catastrophic forgetting, we
350
      put a Dropout layer with drop ratio p after the DIP branch as displayed in Fig. 2b.
351
352   Therefore, the input prompt of the feature encoder is P = Pf r + Dropout(Plr , p). Finally, we have
353   P = Pinit + Dropout(PA PB , p).
354
355
      4.2     E FFICIENCY A NALYSIS
356
357   The whole fine-tuning process of a vision-language model can be divided into three parts: training,
358   storage and inference. We separately analyze the efficiency of DIP in each part here.
359   Training In the training phase, all of DIP-DO, DIP-PP, DIP-LRA lead to slight training cost increase
360   which is negligible. See Tab. 12 for more details.
361   Storage After training, DIP-DO and DIP-PP save the prompt parameters with the same size, i.e. nd,
362   as the original prompt onto disk. DIP-LRA merely saves k(n + d) parameters, less than nd of the
363   original prompt tuning.
364   Inference Before inference, we first load Pinit and PDIP from disk to memory. Noticing that
365
      Dropout is exactly an identity layer in the inference mode, we could pre-calculate the equivalent
      P by P = Pinit + PDIP and just keep P in the memory. For inference, we directly use P as
366
      the input prompts, and thus the inference cost is the same as classic prompt tuning. Some existing
367
      methods add complex bridges between the isolated parameters to earn extra improvements, e.g.
368   CoCoOp Zhou et al. (2022b). There ain’t no such thing as a free lunch. They would face slower
369   speed and huge memory occupation in inference time.

      5.2                   A NALYSIS
462
463   5.2.1                  A BLATION STUDY
464
465   We first show the impact of components of DIP step by step.
466   We start from transforming the text encoder. We first replace the tunable prompt tokens with our
467   decomposed small prompt matrices, denoted as “Decomposition”, for the CoOp method. After such
468   replacement, the average Harmonic accuracy over 11 datasets directly improved from 71.66% to
469   75.46%. The accuracy on the base classes decreases by 2.78% and on the new classes increases by
470   8.26%. Although the adaptation ability slightly drops, the generalization ability raises quite a lot.
471   This phenomenon proves that our dense information design is fairly beneficial for the model’s gen-
472
      eralization ability once again. Then we integrate the special initialization into CoOp. The harmonic
      mean accuracy improves by 1.27%. Finally, we add a lightweight regularization layer, Dropout. It
473
      helps us alleviate overfitting and catastrophic forgetting, resulting in further improvement.
474
475
      5.2.2                  A DDITIONAL EXPERIMENTAL RESULTS
476
477   Effect of different training epochs In this paragraph, we investigate that how the total training
478   epoch could influence the adaptation result. Shown in Fig. 4, we run experiments for the given
479   epochs separately. As the training epoch increases, the accuracy on base classes continues decreas-
480   ing while the accuracy on new classes continues increasing. It is reasonable because as the training
481   continues, the model has a higher risk of forgetting its original knowledge and overfitting.
482   Effect of different dropout ratios In this paragraph, we will show the influence of different dropout
483   ratios in DIP on ImageNet. Seen from Fig. 4, as the dropout ratio increases, the base accuracy starts
484   decreasing while the new accuracy starts increasing mostly. The harmonic mean first increases
485   and then decreases. Dropout iss a kind of regularization, only proper regularization can help avoid
      overfitting and catastrophic forgetting.

      6   C ONCLUSION
516
      With the development of huge vision-language models, how to effectively and efficiently adapt such
517
      huge models to downstream tasks becomes a challenging problem. Much effort has been made
518   to leverage the potential of prompt tuning in adapting vision-language models. However, existing
519   methods suffer from inefficiency. To reach extremely efficient generalization, we propose Dense
520   Information Prompt (DIP) based on the observation about the strong correlation between Informa-
521   tion Density (ID) and generalizability. Moreover, we propose a novel initialization method and a
522   lightweight regularization module to further improve the dense information design without adding
523   any extra inference cost. Besides efficiency and effectiveness, DIP has many valuable advantages
524   such as simplicity and robustness. Extensive experiments and analyses adequately show the superi-
525   ority of DIP.

      Following previous work Zhou et al. (2022a;b), we leverage 11 image recognition datasets to ver-
769
      ify the effectiveness of the proposed method for both the base-to-new generalization task. These
770
      datasets include two datasets for the generic object classification, i.e., ImageNet Deng et al. (2009)
771   and Caltech101 Fei-Fei et al. (2004), five datasets for the fine-grained classification, i.e., Oxford-
772   Pets Parkhi et al. (2012), StanfordCars Krause et al. (2013), Flowers102 Nilsback & Zisserman
773   (2008), Food101 Bossard et al. (2014) and FGVCAircraft Maji et al. (2013), one dataset for the
774   scene recognition, i.e., SUN397 Xiao et al. (2010), one dataset for the action recognition, i.e.,
775   UCF101 Soomro et al. (2012), one dataset for the texture classification, i.e., DTD Cimpoi et al.
776   (2014), and one dataset for the satellite imagery recognition, i.e., EuroSAT Helber et al. (2019). Fol-
777   lowing previous works Zhou et al. (2022b;a), for each dataset, we split its classes equally into two
778   non-overlapping groups, i.e., one as base classes and the other as new classes. We train all models
779
      on the base classes and perform a base/new evaluation on the base/new classes.
780   For the domain generalization task, we utilize ImageNet-A Hendrycks et al. (2021b), ImageNet-
781   R Hendrycks et al. (2021a), ImageNetv2 Recht et al. (2019) and ImageNet-S Wang et al. (2019) to
782   verify the robustness of the model. In this setting, we need to first train the model using ImageNet,
783   and then directly use images from other four datasets to do inference.
784   For the cross-dataset transfer task, the datasets are the same as those of the base-to-new generaliza-
785   tion task. Similar to domain generalization, the model will be first trained on ImageNet and then do
786   inference on the other 10 different datasets.
787
      For the few-shot learning task, the datasets are the same as those of the base-to-new generalization
788
      task. The model will be trained and evaluated with 1, 2, 4, 8 and 16 shots separately.
789
790   The dataset splitting is exactly the same as previous works Zhou et al. (2022a;b). We report the
791   averaged model performance over three runs with different random seeds for fair comparisons.

825   C    C OMPETITORS
826
827
           1. CLIP Radford et al. (2021): CLIP is a strong baseline vision-language model that is pre-
828           trained on a large number of image-text pairs from the web by learning a contrastive ob-
829           jective. CLIP enables strong zero-shot adaptation ability on various downstream tasks by
830           using fixed text prompts, i.e. a photo of a.
831
832        2. CoOp Zhou et al. (2022a): CoOp replaces the fixed text prompts in CLIP with tunable
833           text prompts to improve the adaptation ability of the vision-language model. CoOp shows
834           excellent performance in few-shot situations.
835
           3. CoCoOp Zhou et al. (2022b): CoCoOp replaces the isolated tunable text prompts in CoOp
836           with conditional text prompts, which receive extra gradients from the image features be-
837           sides text features. CoCoOp largely improves the generalization ability of the vision-
838           language model, getting good results on base-to-new generalization and domain adapta-
839           tion.
840
841        4. CLIP-Adapter Gao et al. (2021): CLIP-Adapter adopts the thoughts of classic
842
              Adapter Houlsby et al. (2019) to use serial linear layers and activation functions to adapt
              for downstream tasks. It is simple yet effective in few-shot learning.
843
844        5. LoRA Hu et al. (2021): LoRA adopts low-rank decomposition for weights in FC layers. It
845           is efficient and earns good results in NLP field.
846
847        6. MaPLe Khattak et al. (2023a): MaPLe simultaneously adds prompts to the image encoder
848           and text encoder of CLIP. To trigger more information exchange between the image side
849           and text side, MaPLe generate image prompts from the projection of text prompts. Though
850
              effective, such design brings quite heavy cost.
851        7. ProGrad Zhu et al. (2023): ProGrad only updates the text and image prompts whose gra-
852           dient are aligned (or non-conflicting) to the general knowledge, which is represented as the
853           optimization direction offered by the pre-defined prompt predictions. Such regularization
854           helps it finish good adaptation and generalization.
