009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011            Contrastive Learning (CL) is among the most popular methods for self-supervised learning
012            (SSL). However, CL requires a large memory and sample size and careful hyperparameter
013            tuning. These factors make it difficult to learn high-quality representations with limited
014            amount of memory. In this work, we theoretically analyze a recently proposed supervised
015            approach, DIET, for SSL. DIET labels every example by its datum index and trains on
016            the labeled data with a supervised loss. DIET does not require a large sample size or
017            hyperparameter tuning. However, it does not scale to larger datasets due to the massive
018            classifier head and does not always match the performance of existing methods. Given its
019
               remarkable simplicity and inconsistent results, it is not obvious whether DIET can achieve
               the performance of CL methods, which explicitly model pairwise interactions between
020
               augmented examples. We prove that, perhaps surprisingly, for a linear encoder DIET with
021
               MSE loss is equivalent to spectral contrastive loss. Then, we prove that DIET is prone to
022            learning less-noisy features and may not learn all features from the training data. We show
023            feature normalization can provably address this shortcoming and use of a projection head
024            can further boost the performance. Finally, we address the scalability issue of DIET by
025            reducing its memory footprint. The modified approach, namely S CALED -DIET (S-DIET),
026            substantially improves on the linear probe accuracy of DIET across a variety of datasets
027            and models and outperforms other SSL methods, all with limited memory and without
028            extensive hyperparameter tuning. This makes S-DIET a promising alternative for simple,
029            effective, and memory-efficient representation learning.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Contrastive Learning (CL) has emerged as one of the most successful methods to learn generalizable features
034
      without the need for labels. CL trains an encoder by aligning augmented views of the same example, and
035
      pushing augmented views of different examples apart (Chen et al., 2020; Zbontar et al., 2021; Chen &
036   He, 2021; Grill et al., 2020). However, CL has a complicated pairwise loss function that requires large
037   memory and sample size to effectively align representations of similar examples (Huang et al., 2022), and
038   needs careful hyperparameter tuning (Khosla et al., 2020). These factors make it difficult to learn high-
039   quality representations with CL. This raises a key question: are there simpler ways to learn high-quality
040   representations with small memory?
041
      Recently, Balestriero (2023) proposed a supervised alternative for representation learning, namely DIET,
042   which labels every example by its datum index and trains on the labeled data with Cross Entropy loss. DIET
043   obtains state-of-the-art generalization performance when learning representations from small datasets, and
044   does not require extensive hyperparameter tuning. However, it does not scale to larger datasets as the huge
045   classifier head cannot be fit into the memory, and does not achieve competitive performance on all benchmarks.
046   This raises key questions about the theoretical and practical viability of DIET as an alternative for SSL:

                                                       1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


047
             • Theoretically, how do the solutions learned by DIET and CL compare?
048
049          • Why might DIET fail to achieve good performance on some benchmarks?
050          • Can DIET be implemented in an efficient, scalable manner?
051
052   In this work, we address each of these questions. First, by studying a linear encoder we prove that, perhaps
053   surprisingly, DIET with MSE loss is equivalent to the spectral contrastive loss, Then, we show that DIET
054
      is highly prone to learning the less-noisy and easier to learn features instead of all task-relevant features.
      To address this, we prove that normalizing the features before the classification head enhances the feature
055
      learning ability of DIET. We also show that the use of projection head can further boost the performance.
056
      Finally, we propose a modified loss function and parameter update step to reduce the memory requirements
057   of DIET. In doing so, our modified DIET, namely S CALED -DIET (S-DIET), state-of-the-art performance
058   with limited memory on a variety of datasets and model architectures without extensive hyperparameter
059   tuning, providing a promising alternative for memory-efficient SSL.
060
      We conduct extensive experiments on CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009), ImageNet-100 (Deng
061
      et al., 2009), and TinyImageNet (Le & Yang, 2015), and show that S-DIET significantly improves the
062
      performance of DIET and outperforms CL and other self-supervised learning methods under limited memory
063   requirements. We also conduct an ablation study to confirm the effectiveness of feature normalization and
064   projection head.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
067
068   Self-Supervised Contrastive Learning. Self supervised learning (SSL) methods broadly aim to learn
069   representations that capture semantically meaningful features of the data. Several works such as SimCLR
070   (Chen et al., 2020) and MoCo (He et al., 2020) demonstrated the effectiveness of the contrastive or InfoNCE
071   loss (Oord et al., 2018), which aims to maximize the similarity of so-called positive pairs, while minimizing
072   the similarity of all other pairs to avoid representation collapse. Since then, the general framework of
073   designing pairwise losses that compare different views of the data has proven a popular and effective approach
074
      in SSL. BYOL (Grill et al., 2020) showed that the use of negative pairs in the contrastive loss is unnecessary,
      instead using an online and target network to avoid collapse. SimSiam (Chen & He, 2021) developed a
075
      method based on siamese networks which also does not require negative pairs. Barlow Twins (Zbontar et al.,
076
      2021) proposed a new loss function which includes a redundancy reduction term to avoid representational
077   collapse. The introduction of more advanced data augmentations (Peng et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2022) and
078   new methods for selecting positive pairs (Dwibedi et al., 2021) have also boosted performance. But these
079   SSL methods have complicated loss functions which often require maintaining multiple views of the same
080   example, large batch sizes, and careful hyperparameter tuning. These factors increase memory requirements
081   and make it difficult to apply to new tasks.
082
      DIET. Recently, Balestriero (2023) proposed a supervised alternative for representation learning, namely
083   DIET, which assigns labels to every example by its datum index and trains on the labeled data with Cross
084   Entropy Loss. DIET does not require a large sample size or careful data augmentation or hyperparameter
085   tuning. However, it falls short on some benchmarks and is memory intensive due to the massive classifier
086   head, a fatal limitation when scaling to larger datasets. In our work, we will address these shortcomings.
087
      Theory on Contrastive Learning. There has been much progress on theoretically understanding CL. Wang
088
      & Isola (2020); Graf et al. (2021) study the clustering structure of learned embeddings. Arora et al. (2019);
089   HaoChen et al. (2021); Lee et al. (2021); Tosh et al. (2021) provide provable guarantees for downstream task
090   performance. Wen & Li (2021); Ji et al. (2021) analyze the feature learning power of contrastive learning.
091   Saunshi et al. (2022); HaoChen & Ma (2022); Xue et al. (2023) analyze the role of inductive biases in
092   the successes and failures of CL. Xue et al. (2024) investigates the benefits of using a projection head, a
093   common technique in CL. Other works relate CL to different methods such as generalized multi-dimensional

                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


094
      scaling (Balestriero, 2023), a conditional energy based model (Murphy, 2022), or kernel learning (Johnson
095
      et al., 2023). But these works do not provide a precise comparison between the representations learned by
096   supervised and contrastive learning methods. Our work provides the first rigorous theoretical connection
097   between CL and supervised learning by defining a precise correspondence between global minima of a
098   supervised and a contrastive loss.

345
346   In this section, we validate the effectiveness and efficiency of S-DIET on a variety of datasets and models.
347   First, we perform an in depth dive into the role of normalization in DIET through toy, synthetic, and real-world
348   examples, bridging the gap between theory and practice. We also confirm the effectiveness of our Batch Cross
349   Entropy, and the benefits of a projection head. Finally, we compare the performance of S-DIET with several

458   7     C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
459
460   Contrastive learning is among the most popular methods for self-supervised representation learning. Here,
461   we rigorously analyzed the learning mechanism of an alternative approach, DIET. At first, it may seem that
462
      DIET’s simple supervised architecture should not be able to match the performance of contrastive learning,
      which explicitly models pairwise interactions between examples. However, we derived a correspondence
463
      between the minima of DIET with MSE loss and the spectral contrastive loss. Then, we showed that
464
      normalizing the embeddings before applying the classifier head during training can prevent a failure mode
465   where the learning of one less noisy feature suppresses the learning of another, noisier feature. We leveraged
466   these observations, as well as improvements to DIET’s memory consumption, to improve the performance
467   of DIET to be on par with other SSL methods across a variety of datasets. Consequently, our modified
468   DIET (S-DIET) presents a simple, effective, and memory-efficient solution for self-supervised representation
469   learning. We believe that this inspires future work on this novel approach for representation learning.
