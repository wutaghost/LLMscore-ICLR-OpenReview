024                                               A BSTRACT
025
026
               We introduce Neural Lighting Priors, a learned surface emission model for in-
027            door scenes. Given multi-view observations as well as the geometry of a scene,
028            we decouple spatially varying lighting and material parameters. Existing inverse
029            rendering methods typically use hand-crafted emission models or require a large
030            number of views to better constrain the highly ambiguous appearance decompo-
031            sition task. We aim to overcome these limitations by introducing an expressive
032            learned parametric emission model and utilizing semantic information to suffi-
033            ciently constrain the optimization, thus allowing us to infer light sources, even if
034            they are not visible in the observations. We model the emitted radiance with a
035
               neural field parameterized by the emitting direction and a local latent code stored
               in a voxel grid. At test time, we fit the local latent codes to the scene using dif-
036
               ferentiable path tracing, optimizing the reconstruction loss. Our reconstruction
037
               allows us to insert virtual objects in a scene and gives us control over the emitters
038            to change their emission color and intensity. Thanks to the learned 3D prior, our
039            method requires fewer views than state-of-the-art relighting methods, gives more
040            control, and also improves the relighting quality.

042   1   I NTRODUCTION
043
044   Precise estimation of lighting conditions holds paramount significance in a multitude of subsequent
045
      applications, notably within the realms of virtual and augmented reality (AR). Image observations
      contain the interaction of lighting and material. Our goal is to decouple the lighting from a sparse
046
      set of images given pre-scanned geometry. One possible application is an AR meeting room, where
047
      virtual participants need to be inserted into the scene photo realistically. While the room’s geom-
048   etry can be scanned and reconstructed once in advance, the lighting may change across sessions,
049   motivating sparse view lighting estimation with known geometry. Previous methods directly op-
050   timize for emission parameters using inverse rendering (Maier et al., 2017; Azinovic et al., 2019;
051   Nimier-David et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022; Barron & Malik, 2015), which gives explicit control over
052   the scene lighting but mostly rely on hand-crafted priors for the lighting to constrain this highly
053   underdetermined optimization problem. Recent methods achieve impressive relighting results using
      a large number of views (Philip et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023).


                                                        1
054
      Recently, learning-based methods have been applied to directly estimate complex lighting conditions
055   (Gardner et al., 2017; 2019; Li et al., 2020; 2022; Zhu et al., 2022; Weber et al., 2022). Such methods
056   are able to reconstruct high-quality incident illumination models to allow convincing virtual object
057   insertion. However, they cannot provide consistent control over the scene’s lighting and often require
058   training on synthetic imagery, causing a domain gap (Wang et al., 2021; Li et al., 2020; Philip et al.,
059   2021; Li et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2022; Weber et al., 2022).
060
      In this work, we combine an explicit inverse rendering method with the expressiveness of neural
061   networks. We learn a neural parametric emission model from synthetic data, which can be fit to real
062   scenes to facilitate both relighting and virtual object insertion. Our model utilizes semantic infor-
063   mation to further constrain the optimization, which allows us to reconstruct high-quality emissions,
064   and also infer light sources that are completely unobserved across all of the input images.
065
      Specifically, we model the surface emission with a locally conditioned neural field (Xie et al., 2022).
066
      We render views from a large set of synthetic scenes with photo-realistic lighting conditions and
067   train a generic emission model to represent various realistic emitters. At test time, we use a differen-
068   tiable path tracer to reconstruct the observations and optimize the local lighting features. To enable
069   reconstruction from sparse or even incomplete observations, we further condition on local features
070   predicted from the scene’s geometry via a 3D convolutional neural network indicating the likelihood
071   that a certain piece of geometry is an emitter.
072
      Our approach benefits from the advantages of physically-based inverse rendering and neural repre-
073   sentations. First, explicit surface emission reconstruction enables lighting editing and virtual object
074   insertion with consistent global illumination. Second, our learned emission model is capable of rep-
075   resenting complex emission profiles with fine details and it is not limited by hand-crafted definitions.
076   By leveraging a learned semantic prior, we additionally constrain the optimization to significantly
077   reduce the required number of views and even infer light sources that are not directly observed in
078   any of the input images. In summary, our main contributions are:
079
080
             • We propose a neural-field-based lighting representation to model emitted radiance of sur-
               face points in conjunction with a voxel-based emitter sampling technique to efficiently
081
               render our neural representation.
082
083          • We introduce a learned prior for complex indoor lighting conditions leveraging semantical
084
               information to sufficiently constrain the highly ill-posed appearance decomposition task.
085          • We introduce high-quality textured mesh light sources to the 3D-Front dataset (Fu et al.,
086            2021) and render 976 train 100 test scenes.

088   2    R ELATED WORK
089
090   Lighting Reconstruction. Earlier light estimation methods for room-scale scenes focused on
091   predicting the incident illumination from single images. Gardner et al. (2017); Wang et al. (2022a);
092   LeGendre et al. (2019); Weber et al. (2022) predict global spherical environment maps. Gardner
093   et al. (2019) proposed to approximate the environment map with Spherical Gaussians (SG) to reduce
094   the task’s complexity and achieve better generalization. Since these approaches use a global lighting
095   representation, they are not able to reconstruct spatially varying (SV) lighting, which is crucial for
096   room-scale scenes.
097   Srinivasan et al. (2020); Wang et al. (2021); Maier et al. (2017); Philip et al. (2021); Li et al. (2020)
098   use local incident lighting representation to predict pixel, patch-wise or global environment maps
099   approximated by Spherical Harmonics (SVSH) (Maier et al., 2017), SVSG (Li et al., 2020), irradi-
100   ance maps (Philip et al., 2021), incident light fields (Yao et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023; Wang et al.,
101   2023), or volumetric lighting (Choi et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2022b). They excel in reconstructing
102   the lighting of a scene, but they cannot model consistent light transport prohibiting light editing.
103   Recent methods aim at decomposing the scene in a physically-based way using inverse path tracing
104   (Azinovic et al., 2019; Nimier-David et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022; Whelan et al., 2016; Wu et al.,
105   2023; Lin et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2023). They model the emitted radiance and provide globally
106   consistent lighting with scene editing capabilities. Nevertheless, they rely on hand-crafted priors and
107   emission models, such as mesh lighting with cosine emission profile (Azinovic et al., 2019; Nimier-
      David et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2023) or Spherical Gaussians (SG) (Li et al., 2022) to constrain the

152   to constrain reconstruction from a sparse set of views (Sitzmann et al., 2019). Our method further
153
      uses explicit inverse rendering to reconstruct the lighting of a scene using learned priors.

184   3.1   BACKGROUND
185
186   Our goal is to perform inverse graphics, i.e., reconstruct materials and lighting of the scene from im-
187   age observations. On a high level, we achieve this by inverting the forward imaging process, which
188   is given via the rendering equation (Kajiya, 1986) (Eq. (1)). We consider only surface emissions and
189   surface scatterings without any subsurface interactions. Since this integral is intractable to solve, we
190
      approximate it with path tracing using Monte Carlo estimation (Kajiya, 1986). To render a single
      pixel of the image, we need to estimate the incoming radiance Li towards the camera. Given a
191
      starting position x0 , we shoot a ray in direction ω, which hits the scene at position x1 . We evaluate
192
      the emission Le at the hit position towards the starting position. To approximate the integral part,
193   path-tracing uses a single sample, i.e., we shoot a single new ray and calculate the scattered radiance
194   given the reflectance fr and the incident angle θi . Then, we estimate the incident radiance recur-
195   sively. We use Mitsuba 2 (Nimier-David et al., 2019) to implement our path tracer. To generate the
196   camera rays, we use uniform sampling over the pixel area. To sample bounce rays, we use BRDF
197   and emitter multiple importance sampling (Veach & Guibas, 1995), as described in § 3.6. Our work
198   focuses on the emission Le reconstruction using learned priors given sparse-view observation.

254   3.3                    DATASET
255
      We aim to learn a generic model of indoor lighting emissions. Learning a prior requires a large
256
      dataset. However, to the best of our knowledge, only OpenRooms (Li et al., 2021) provides material
257   and lighting annotations with unbounded HDR renderings of indoor scenes with spatially-varying
258   lighting. Still, we found that their emitters lack complexity for our task. Therefore, we train our
259   model on synthetically rendered observations from the 3D-Front dataset (Fu et al., 2021).
260
      We extend the 3D-Front dataset (Fu et al., 2021) with photo-realistic, physically based lighting
261
      descriptions. We found that the defined emitters are not suitable for our task since they are often
262
      point light sources and are not defined for all lamps. To get realistic lighting conditions, we define
263   an emission texture for lamp objects, as described in our supplement.
264
265   We focus on internal light sources; thus, we do not consider the illumination coming from windows.
266
      Therefore, we close the rooms in our dataset by replacing the wall of windows and doors with closed
      planes. We match the texture of the closed wall to the original. Even though not specifically trained
267
      for windows, their emission can be approximated with directional emission profiles; see supplement.
268
269   The materials in the dataset are defined as albedo textures with object-specific specularity and rough-
      ness values. Our dataset uses the GGX (Walter et al., 2007) microfacet distribution.

      For our training set, we prepare 976 rooms, 10 views each. Our test set contains 100 rooms. We

277   3.4   T RAINING
278
279   Semantical prior. Decomposing the appearance into lighting and material parameters is a highly
280   ambiguous problem. To better constrain this task, we introduce a semantical prior for lighting re-
281
      construction. Given the reconstructed geometry of a scene, we predict a voxel grid of semantical
      features Gs at the same resolution as our lighting embedding grid Gl . We use the same semantical
282
      embedding zs for every point in a voxel.
283
284   Our semantical prediction model is a binary segmentation network. We use the ScanNet-pretrained
285   Res16UNet34D feature extractor network from (Rozenberszki et al., 2022). We fine-tune the net-
286   work on our training dataset (§ 3.3) for the downstream task of light source segmentation. We
287
      keep the encoder frozen and optimize the decoder and classifier. Our model does not use any color
      information. Based on the binary prediction, we choose between two learnable codes to get our
288
      semantical embeddings.
289
290   Emission prior. In contrast to any other solutions, our approach does not rely on hand-crafted
291   models but learns a parametric emission model of reasonable lighting conditions directly from ob-
292   servations. We train our neural field in auto-decoder fashion (Park et al., 2019) on views rendered
293   under a large corpus of lighting conditions.
294   Our network is trained to reconstruct the surface emission of the training scenes. We use ground truth
295   geometry, semantics, and lighting description during training. In each step, we randomly select 8192
296   pixels from 10 randomly selected views. We shoot one ray uniformly selected from the pixel area.
297   Our network Θl is shared across all voxels and scenes. In each optimization step, we update the
298   network parameters as well as the local lighting features.
299
      Our objective consists of two parts. First, we supervise our network with an emission loss, which
300
      is the L2 distance between the predicted Lˆe and ground truth surface emission Le at the hit points ,
301
      which is available in our dataset. Second, we use an L2 regularizer on our lighting features fl with
302   a weight of wlf = 1e−1.
303
                                        L_{train} = \|\hat {L_e} - L_e\|_2^2 + w_{lf} \cdot \|\bm {f}_l\|_2^2  (7)
304
305   We used the Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2015) optimizer with learning rate 1e−2, betas (0.9, 0.99), and
306   weight decay 5e−4. Similarly to the work of Nimier-David et al. (2021), we update only those local
307   latent codes, which were used in the current iteration to avoid unnecessary updates caused by the

356   3.6     E MITTER S AMPLING
357
358   Monte Carlo approximation of the rendering equation yields noisy estimates. To reduce the noise,
359   we apply BRDF and emission multiple importance sampling (Veach & Guibas, 1995). However,
360   emitter sampling requires exact knowledge of the light sources, which we lack. We thus introduce a
361   voxel-based emitter sampling strategy.
362   We show our sampling strategy in Fig. 3. We store an additional proxy value in our lighting voxel
363   grid Gl , which is optimized for the average emission value coming from that particular voxel. In
364   each path tracing iteration, we first sample a voxel V with the weighted probability of the voxel’s
365   emission proxy (pV ). Second, we uniformly sample a position S inside the voxel. Finally, we trace
366   a ray from our starting point B through the sampled point S. If the hit point H is inside the sampled
367   voxel V , we keep the ray; otherwise, we discard it. This way, every point along the ray inside the
368

      5 C ONCLUSION
531
      We introduce Neural Lighting Priors, a learned parametric emission model to better constrain the
532
      indoor scene appearance decomposition task given multi-view observations. We have presented an
533
      expressive learned lighting representation, which gives control over the reconstructed light sources
534   yet can be fit to unseen scenes with differentiable path tracing. We have also developed a voxel-based
535   emission sampling technique to reduce rendering noise. We have rendered a large-scale synthetic
536   dataset with annotated textured surface emissions and unbounded HDR images to train and test
537   our method. Thanks to our learned priors utilizing semantical information, our model can be fit
538   to a sparse set of views. High-fidelity lighting reconstruction is a key component of virtual and
539   augmented reality applications. We believe that our work takes an important step using learned
      priors to constrain the ill-posed problem of inverse rendering.

832
      We compare our voxel-based emitter sampling method against pure BRDF sampling in Fig. 11.
833   Using the same number of paths, our approach helps to reduce the rendering noise.
834
835   Emitter sampling for Monte Carlo integration requires determining the path probability. We now
836
      provide a detailed derivation of the sampling probability described in our paper. Given a bounce
      point B, our goal is to sample a ray r to an emitter and determine the sampling probability (p(r|B)).
837
      The ray can be determined by its starting position and direction r = [B, ωi ]. Since we need only
838
      the closest hit point along the ray, the ray can be reparametrized as the starting and end position
839   r = [B, H]. Thus,
840
841
842
                                                   p(r|B) = p(H|B)                                    (10)
843   Our method samples points in space and not on the surface. Therefore, the ray sampling probability
844   is the marginal probability over all spatial positions along the way, which requires integrating over
845   the whole ray. To simplify the calculations, we apply our fourth step, which rejects every sample
846   outside the sampled voxel. This way, we need to consider only samples inside the voxel of the
847   surface hit point.
848
849
850
                                     p(H|B) = \int _{S\in V} p(H|S,B) \cdot p(S|B) dS                 (11)
851
852   The position sampling probability p(S|B) can be determined as pV · pS . Since we used uniform
853   sampling inside the voxel, the position sampling probability p(S|B) does not depend on the position
854   and can be pulled out of the integral. The hit point probability p(H|S, B) is 0 if point S does not
855
      lie on the ray and 1 otherwise. Therefore, integrating over the voxel boils down to calculating the
      voxel-ray intersection (l), i.e.,
856
857
858                                              p(H|B) = p_V \cdot p_S \cdot l                       (12)

1072   to the original view, but we mask the pixel values corresponding to the inserted objects and use the
1073   rerendered pixels there.

1078   our quantitative comparisons. We rerender the view with the virtual objects using a lower number
