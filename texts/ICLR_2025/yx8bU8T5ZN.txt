009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Post-training has emerged as a crucial paradigm for adapting large-scale pre-
012            trained models to various tasks, whose effects are fully reflected by delta pa-
013            rameters (i.e., the disparity between post-trained and pre-trained parameters).
014            While numerous studies have explored delta parameter properties via operations
015            like pruning, quantization, low-rank approximation, and extrapolation, a unified
016            framework for systematically examining these characteristics has been lacking. In
017            this paper, we propose a novel perspective based on Riemann sum approximation
018
               of the loss function to elucidate delta parameter editing operations. Our analysis
               categorizes existing methods into three classes based on their post-editing perfor-
019
               mance: competitive, decreased, and improved, explaining how they are expressed
020
               by the Riemann sum approximation term and how they alter the model perfor-
021            mance. Extensive experiments on both visual and language models, including
022            ViT, LLaMA 3, and Mistral, corroborate our theoretical findings. Furthermore, we
023            introduce extensions to existing techniques like DARE and BitDelta, highlighting
024            their limitations in leveraging the properties of delta parameters and reorganizing
025            them into general expressions to enhance the applicability and effectiveness of
026            delta parameter editing in post-trained models.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   With the remarkable success of large-scale pre-trained models, post-training has emerged as the
032   de facto standard paradigm for effective adaptations to various tasks (Han et al., 2024; Xin et al.,
033
      2024; Dodge et al., 2020; Zhao et al., 2023). Conceptually, post-training optimizes the parameters
      of pre-trained backbone on task-specific data, endowing models with diverse abilities like visual
034
      recognition (Chen et al., 2022; Sandler et al., 2022), instruction following (Rafailov et al., 2023;
035
      Ethayarajh et al., 2024), and mathematical reasoning (Luo et al., 2023; Tong et al., 2024). It has
036   been noted that the impact of post-training is fully manifested in the delta parameters, which are
037   defined as the difference between parameters of pre-trained and post-trained models (Ilharco et al.,
038   2023; Yu et al., 2024).
039
      Due to the inherent correlations between delta parameters and post-training, significant efforts have
040
      been made to investigate the properties of delta parameters through various editing operations in
041
      recent years. For instance, studies like DARE (Yu et al., 2024) and DELLA-Merging (Deep et al.,
042   2024) showed that models can achieve comparable performance with only a small fraction of delta
043   parameters, highlighting their extreme redundancy. BitDelta (Liu et al., 2024) demonstrated that
044   delta parameters could be quantized to 1 bit with modest performance compromise. Twin-Merging
045   (Lu et al., 2024) and TIES-Merging (Yadav et al., 2023) discovered that most of the benefits of post-
046   training can be retained after executing singular value decomposition and magnitude-based pruning
047   on delta parameters. EXPO (Zheng et al., 2024) observed that cheaply extrapolating delta param-
048   eters with a suitable scaling factor can even enhance the performance. However, a comprehensive
049   framework for systematically discussing delta parameter characteristics and theoretically explaining
050
      how different operations impact model performance remains lacking.
051   In this work, we make a pioneering effort to provide a unified view of delta parameter editing in
052   post-trained large-scale models. We formulate the editing operations of delta parameters based on
053   Riemann sum approximation of the loss of the edited model. By mathematically representing ex-
      isting editing operations with the approximation term, we elucidate why certain operations result in


                                                       1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      competitive, decreased, or improved performance. Specifically, we verify that: 1) methods such as
055   DARE and DELLA-Merging can well keep the approximation term to zero through the random drop
056   and rescale processes, ensuring equal loss between the edited and post-trained models and achiev-
057   ing competitive performance. 2) techniques including BitDelta, Twin-Merging, and TIES-Merging
058   often result in decreased performance due to a positive approximation term introduced by quanti-
059   zation, low-rank approximation, and magnitude-based pruning; 3) EXPO-like methods can restrict
060   the loss of the edited model to be less than that of the post-trained model by yielding a negative
061   approximation term. To validate our theoretical analysis, extensive experiments are conducted on
062   large-scale visual models (ViT (Radford et al., 2021)) and language models (LLaMA 3 (Dubey et al.,
063
      2024), and Mistral (Jiang et al., 2023)), and the results strongly support our analysis.
064   Besides understanding existing delta parameter editing techniques in the proposed view, we further
065   present several extensions to provide more general formats. Firstly, we introduce a factor to handle
066   the dropped parameters in DARE, effectively expanding methods like DARE. Secondly, we extend
067   the scope of quantification-based methods like BitDelta, identifying a broader area for effective
068   quantification beyond solely utilizing the average sum of delta parameters. Finally, we identify that
069
      extrapolation is not the key to the success of EXPO-like methods. Instead, we should determine
      whether to use extrapolation or interpolation based on the direction of the approximation term.
070
      Experimental results also demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed extensions.

      2.1   P OST- TRAINING OF L ARGE -S CALE M ODELS
077
      In recent years, with the rapid development of large-scale models, post-training has become an
078
      essential process for adapting the pre-trained backbone to a variety of tasks (Xin et al., 2024; Dodge
079
      et al., 2020; Zhao et al., 2023). Post-training realizes the adaptation via adjusting the pre-trained
080   backbone’s parameters through full fine-tuning (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2021; Devlin
081   et al., 2019; Radford et al., 2018) or parameter-efficient fine-tuning (He et al., 2023; Houlsby et al.,
082   2019; Li & Liang, 2021; Hu et al., 2022; Han et al., 2024) algorithms. It is straightforward to
083   conclude that the effectiveness of post-training can be perfectly denoted by the delta parameters,
084   which represent the difference between post-trained and pre-trained parameters (Ilharco et al., 2023;
085   Yu et al., 2024). Given the close correlations between delta parameters and the post-training process,
086   investigating the properties of delta parameters becomes particularly important. In this paper, we
087   present a novel perspective to illustrate delta parameter characteristics of post-trained models.
088
089   2.2   D ELTA PARAMETER E DITING FOR P OST-T RAINED M ODELS
090
091   Existing delta parameter editing techniques can be generally categorized as three aspects according
092   to their post-editing performance, including competitive, decreased, and improved performance.
093
      Delta Parameter Editing with Competitive Performance. DARE (Yu et al., 2024) is a widely
094
      used approach to edit delta parameters without compromising the model performance. Technically,
095
      DARE can eliminate most (90% or even 99%) of the delta parameters with the random drop and
096   rescale operations. Inspired by DARE, DELLA-Merging (Deep et al., 2024) presented a magnitude-
097   aware drop to replace the random drop for achieving better performance, which ranks delta param-
098   eters by their magnitude and assigns higher dropout probabilities to those with lower ranks (i.e.,
099   corresponding to lower magnitudes). Yu et al. (2024) and Deep et al. (2024) explained that DARE
100   and DELLA-Merging can work because they are able to approximate the original embeddings based
101   on only a small fraction of delta parameters, thus maintaining the model performance.
102   Delta Parameter Editing with Decreased Performance. BitDelta (Liu et al., 2024) quantized delta
103   parameters to only 1 bit according to the average magnitude scalar and sign bits. Twin-Merging (Lu
104   et al., 2024) applied singular value decomposition (Klema & Laub, 1980) on delta parameters to
105   extract exclusive knowledge for each specific task. TIES-Merging (Yadav et al., 2023) retained
106   delta parameters with the largest magnitudes for reducing redundancy. All the above methods yield
107   slightly worse results after executing the corresponding quantization, low-rank approximation, or
      pruning operations.


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Delta Parameter Editing with Improved Performance. EXPO (Zheng et al., 2024) extrapolated
109   delta parameters calculated by two relatively weaker models with an appropriate scaling factor to
110   construct a stronger model, which can enhance the model performance.
111
112
      It can be concluded that current approaches utilizes distinct operations for editing delta parameter,
      lacking a comprehensive analysis of whether these editing operations are suitable and why different
113
      operations cause various influence on the model performance. In this work, we make the first attempt
114
      to introduce a unified view of delta parameter editing in post-training, which is supported both
115   theoretically and empirically.

505   7   C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSIONS
506
507   Post-training is a core step in the training of large models. In recent years, significant efforts have
508   been directed towards editing the delta parameters of post-training to achieve improvements in either
509   performance or efficiency. However, while previous work has shown some effectiveness, the com-
510   plexity of large model parameters has led to a fragmented understanding of delta parameter editing,
511   with different studies focusing on different aspects of its effectiveness, lacking a unified perspective.
512
      In this paper, we provide a unified perspective on the previous work related to post-training delta
513   parameter editing using Riemann sum approximation. We find that the changes in model capability
514   after altering the delta parameters essentially depend on the changes in the approximation term of
515   Riemann sum approximation. Specifically, when the approximation term remains unchanged, the
516   overall loss of the model remains stable, and thus the overall performance of the model also remains
517   largely unchanged. When the approximation term decreases, the model’s performance improves,
518   and when the approximation term increases, the model’s performance degrades.
519   Our work offers a concise, unified, and powerful explanation for almost all previous work in the field
520   of post-training delta parameter editing. We validate our hypothesis through numerical experiments.
521   From our conclusions, several potential applications emerge for future work in this direction: (1)
522   Model Quantization: By finding an edit that sets the approximation term to zero while using lower
523   precision, we can achieve nearly lossless compression of the model. (2) Model Enhancement: By
524   directly controlling the approximation term, we can enhance the model’s capabilities without ad-
525   ditional training data. (3) Post-training Mechanism Analysis: Since the model’s capability remains
526   almost unchanged when the approximation term is zero, we can construct more concise post-training
527
      delta parameters. This simplifies the parameter changes during the post-training phase, enabling a
      more effective analysis of the parameter mechanisms in this stage.
528
529   Additionally, our work highlights a critical observation: the analysis of parameter changes during
530   the post-training phase should not be limited to specific parameters, such as knowledge neurons,
531   but should consider the overall distribution of parameters. This is because the key constraint of
532   the approximation term being zero does not depend on the changes in a specific parameter during
533
      post-training but requires a comprehensive consideration of all parameter deltas. This suggests that
      trying to infer the impact on the global model parameters from changes in a single or a few local
534
      parameters is likely futile.
