009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We introduce LMCD, a novel framework for semantic clustering and multi-set
012            entity matching problems, in which we employ graph community detection algo-
013            rithms to prune spurious edges from match graphs constructed using embedding
014            and language models. We construct these match graphs by retrieving nearest em-
015            bedding neighbors for each entity, then querying a language model to remove false
016
               positive pairs. Across a variety of cluster size distributions, and for tasks ranging
               from sentiment and topic categorization to deduplication of product databases,
017
               our approach outperforms existing methods without requiring any finetuning or
018
               labeled data beyond few-shot examples, and without needing to select the de-
019            sired number of clusters in advance. Our embedding and inference stages are
020            fully parallelizable, with query and computational costs which scale near-linearly
021            in the number of entities. Our post-processing stage is bottlenecked only by the
022            runtime of community detection algorithms on discrete graphs, which are often
023            near-linear, with no explicit dependence on embedding dimension or numbers of
024            clusters. This is in stark contrast to existing methods relying on high-dimensional
025            clustering algorithms that are difficult to apply at scale; for entity matching our
026            approach also ensures consistency constraints across matches regardless of group
027
               sizes, a desirable practical feature which is absent from all prior approaches other
               than vector clustering. Our improvements over previous techniques are most stark
028
               when clusters are numerous and heterogenously-sized, a regime which captures
029
               many clustering and matching problems of widespread practical importance.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Modern language models have proven useful for a broad variety of “data wrangling” tasks, from data
035   imputation and error detection (Narayan et al., 2022) to reference resolution (Moniz et al., 2024)
036   log parsing (Xiao et al., 2024), named entity recognition (Wang et al., 2023), and pairwise entity
037   matching (Peeters & Bizer, 2024). We study two such classes of problems — semantic clustering
038   and (multi-set) entity matching — from a unifying perspective which leads us to consider LLM-
039
      based pipelines as end-to-end systems rather than granular decision engines. Indeed, as standard
      question-answer benchmarks have become increasingly saturated by frontier models (Taghanaki
040
      et al., 2024; McIntosh et al., 2024), it is also widely observed that LLM “agents” often struggle to
041
      complete complex multi-step tasks, particularly when the degree of sequential dependence is high
042   (Chen et al., 2024; Han et al., 2024; Xing et al., 2024). Circuit complexity analysis has provided
043   a partial explanation for this, showing that Transformers (Li et al., 2024b) cannot natively solve
044   highly sequential tasks in zero-shot settings; further, periodic “hallucinations” of LLMs (Kalai &
045   Vempala, 2024) may be inevitable if we hope to apply them to tasks which are ambiguous or out-
046   of-distribution. As such, this motivates the careful design of algorithms for language model-based
047   processing pipelines which are robust to these failure modes, yet which still produce outputs that
048   “type-check” with the constraints of a given problem (i.e. a partition into disjoint sets).
049   We adopt this approach for semantic clustering and entity matching problems, employing language
050   and embedding models to construct a “match graph” which can then be partitioned via community
051   detection algorithms, avoiding several drawbacks of existing methods while also improving upon
052   their experimental results. While these two tasks have historically been studied independently, with
053   parallel sets of techniques, we note that they can both be viewed as instances of a general problem
      (which we refer to as “multiset matching”): there is a universe of n entities, which must be parti-

      tioned into disjoint subsets along a particular semantic axis. Typical modern approaches to semantic
055   clustering involve applying high-dimensional clustering algorithms (e.g. KMeans(++) (MacQueen,
056   1967; Arthur & Vassilvitskii, 2007) or (H)DBSCAN (Ester et al., 1996; Campello et al., 2013)) to
057   embedding vectors for each entity (Zhang et al., 2023; Viswanathan et al., 2023; Petukhova et al.,
058   2024), generally targeting tasks where most clusters are large and the number of clusters is known
059   (and small relative to the number of entities). In contrast, the bulk of recent literature on entity
060   matching with language models (Li et al., 2020; Narayan et al., 2022; Peeters & Bizer, 2024; Wang
061   et al., 2024) considers only the pairwise variant of the problem involving a semantic join between
062   two databases, where a “blocking” stage (e.g. embedding or keyword similarity) first produces a set
063
      of candidate pairs to be matched. In fact, many standard benchmarks consist of “pre-blocked” pairs
      (Köpcke et al., 2010; Wang et al., 2021), where matching methods are then evaluated primarily via
064
      binary classification metrics. However, as noted by Peeters et al. (2023), the pairwise variant of the
065
      problem fails to capture many matching tasks of large practical importance, such as deduplication
066   and linkage of parsed, scraped, or manually entered entities. The benchmark suite they introduce,
067   WDC Products, contains several splits in which the ground-truth group sizes range from 2 to 10+.
068   We focus primarily on this variant, as pairwise matching is largely “solved” by frontier LLMs with
069   minimal prompting; yet, we are unaware of any prior methods for multiset matching which enforce
070   group consistency constraints on outputs without relying on high-dimensional clustering. As such,
071   we believe that our LMCD framework represents the most effective approach to date for address-
072   ing multi-set matching problems in practice; in addition to its simplicity of implementation, it also
073   yields interpretable outputs in the form of a match graph, which may be further post-processed via
074
      human-in-the-loop approaches.
075   Our interest in avoiding explicit vector clustering is two-fold. Our first consideration is runtime scal-
076   ing: when employing an embedding model with dimension d for a set n entities, running just a single
077   step of K-means takes O(n2 d) time when the number of groups is O(n), whereas most instantia-
                                                     2 1
078   tions of our framework run in time O(dn+n
                                           e          ) regardless of the number of groups. Our framework
079   makes O(n) queries to embedding and language models across three stages, each of which can be
080   straightforwardly parallelized, after which we can “throw away” all embedding vectors, leaving
081   only a size-O(n) discrete graph for postprocessing via community detection. The second factor is
082
      with regards to the curse of dimensionality. Accurate clustering is widely observed to be difficult
      in high dimensions, as conventional principles related to distance and shape begin to break down
083
      (Radovanović et al., 2010; Peng et al., 2024). This creates a tension with leveraging benefits from
084
      state-of-the-art pretrained embedding models, which often have embedding dimensions as large as
085   4,096 (Xiao et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2024). This is exacerbated when the number of clusters is large
086   and cluster sizes are non-uniform, as clustering algorithms such as KMeans (MacQueen, 1967) or
087   HDBSCAN (Campello et al., 2013) may interpret small clusters as “noise”, yielding poor perfor-
088   mance. Despite this, modern data structures for approximate nearest neighbor search such as HNSW
089   (Malkov & Yashunin, 2018) remain effective in many dimensions, and are widely used in perfor-
090   mant vector databases for ranking and retrieval tasks (Lewis et al., 2021; Muennighoff et al., 2023) in
091   conjunction with high-dimensional embeddings. Our approach demonstrates that nearest-neighbor
092   queries are indeed all we need from embeddings in order to reconstruct high-fidelity matchings,
093
      even for heterogeneous cluster size distributions, when applying appropriate post-processing with
      language models and graph community detection algorithms.
094
095   Across our experiments, we find that pretrained embedding models in conjunction with vector
096   databases are effective for efficient retrieval of candidate pairs with nontrivial precision, that and
097   pretrained language models exhibit both high precision and recall when matching candidate pairs.
098   Adopting these observations as primitives, our approach has a simple and intuitive interpretation.
099
      The “ground truth” match graph for a set of entities consists of disconnected components which are
      each cliques, and embedding-based retrieval yields a set of edges which is “dense” with respect to
100
      the true edges (though which may have many false positives). Language model queries then filter
101
      most of the false positives while retaining most of the true positives, yet some remaining false pos-
102   itive edges are inevitable. At this point, the “shape” of the ground truth match graph is sufficiently
103   defined such that community detection algorithms can find cuts in the match graph which elimi-
104   nate remaining false positive edges. Our results in Section 5 explore a number of tradeoffs related
105   to dataset and cluster sizes, as well as algorithmic choices for community detection. Our method
106   outperforms existing approaches for semantic clustering across a broad variety of datasets, and we

129   2.1   C LUSTERING AND M ATCHING VIA E MBEDDING S IMILARITY
130
131
      Following a line of work in which pretrained embedding models (e.g. BERT (Devlin et al., 2019))
      were finetuned for clustering and matching tasks via contrastive loss objectives (Shi & Wang, 2021;
132
      Li et al., 2020), requiring large amounts of labeled data for training purposes, more recent works
133
      (Zhang et al., 2023; Viswanathan et al., 2023; Petukhova et al., 2024) have begun incorporating
134   self-supervised data augmentation, such as concatenation of LLM-extracted keyphrase embeddings
135   or finetuning on LLM-generated labels, to supplant the dependence on labeled data. This issue is
136   also partially addressed by employing “promptable” embedding models such as Instructor (Su et al.,
137   2023), as is done by Zhang et al. (2023). We leverage this capability as well, via the bge-en-icl
138   model from BAAI (Xiao et al., 2023); however, as we only use embedding vectors for nearest-
139   neighbor retrieval rather than explicit clustering, we find that our methods are competitive without
140   any finetuning, and we instead rely on few-shot prompting in our LLM-querying stage to inject
141   precise distributional knowledge.
142   Though these prior methods most commonly employ Kmeans(++) (MacQueen, 1967; Arthur &
143   Vassilvitskii, 2007) for vector clustering, (H)DBSCAN (Ester et al., 1996; Campello et al., 2013)
144   presents a relevant alternative particularly when the number of clusters is unknown or expensive to
145   tune. However, we find that retrieval and querying with modern models is sufficiently precise to ren-
146   der high-dimensional clustering unnecessary, as it can be substituted for much more computationally
147   lightweight community detection algorithms running on discrete match graphs.
148
149   2.2   G RAPH C OMMUNITY D ETECTION A LGORITHMS
150
151   While originally developed for network analysis applications, community detection algorithms have
152
      found abundant applications across countless domains; see Li et al. (2024a) for a recent overview.
      Notably, community detection has proven useful for uncovering latent structure in knowledge graphs
153
      (Vahdati et al., 2018; Rollo & Po, 2022); our work falls within this line of inquiry.
154
155   While language models have been employed in conjunction with graph structures for retrieval and
156   reasoning techniques Pan et al. (2023); Besta et al. (2024), our LMCD framework is the first ap-
157   proach to our knowledge which directly applies graph algorithms to the outputs of LLMs in order to
158
      filter and aggregate granular responses.

160   graph topology as well as computational considerations; we summarize the theoretical runtimes sev-
161   eral community detection algorithms in Table 1, alongside popular algorithms for high-dimensional

254   4.1      B LOCKING VIA E MBEDDING S IMILARITY
255
      We begin by computing embedding vectors for string representations of each entity, using a pre-
256
      trained embedding model (namely, BAAI/bge-en-icl (Xiao et al., 2023)) in conjunction with a con-
257
      textual prompt which illustrates the semantic focus for retrieval (see Appendix A for prompt and
258   representation details). These vectors are then inserted into a vector database indexed by the Hi-
259   erarchical Navigable Small Worlds (HNSW) data structure, which supports approximate k-nearest-
260   neighbor retrieval in O(log n) time (Malkov & Yashunin, 2018).
261
      For each entity, we then use HNSW to retrieve its k nearest neighbors in terms of cosine similarity of
262
      embedding vectors, yielding a set of nk “candidate matches” to be processed in subsequent stages.
263
      While the parameter k can be tuned as desired, we find that selecting k = 10 is sufficient across
264   all of our experiments to obtain strong results. The primary goal of this stage is to ensure that
265   every entity is connected via candidate pairs to several others in its ground-truth group, yet the
266   total degree of interconnection within each group may be sparse while still enabling a high-fidelity
267   clustering (as long as it is sufficiently more dense than between-group candidate connections after
268   the querying stage from Section 4.2 is applied). As such, selecting too large of a value for k may
269   present challenges in subsequent stages, as there may become a higher proportion of false positive
      connections which must be filtered downstream.

      4.2   G RAPH C ONSTRUCTION VIA PAIRWISE Q UERIES
271
272
      Given a set of candidate pairs, we construct a “match graph” by querying a language model (Llama-
273   3.1-70B-Instruct in our experiments (Dubey et al., 2024)) as to whether each pair constitutes a true
274   match, guided by a domain-specific prompt and a set of few-shot examples. The prompts we use are
275   presented in Appendix A, and we discuss our procedure for choosing few-shot examples in Section
276   5.1. The intention of this stage, as illustrated by our numerical results in Tables 2 and 3, is to prune
277   the bulk of the false positive candidate matches introduced by the previous stage, while retaining the
278   bulk of the true positive pairs. When these two stages succeed, we are left with a graph which may
279   not yet constitute a compelling partition of the entities into disjoint clusters, but where there are still
280   clearly defined “communities” in terms of degree of interconnection.
281
282
      4.3   G RAPH PARTITIONING VIA C OMMUNITY D ETECTION
283
284
      The final step of our approach is to simply apply a community detection algorithm (such as those dis-
285
      cussed in Section 2.2) to our match graph, and return the generated partition as our clustering. Sev-
286
      eral community detection algorithms (notably, Walktrap) return vertex dendrograms (corresponding
287   to sequences of cuts or merges) rather than only a single cut; while these algoriths return a default
288   split according to numerical metrics, the easily interpretable nature of a sequence of splits (paired
289   with text descriptions of entities) enables a straightforward interactive process for selecting a final
290   partitioning of the entities. Further, given the broad variety of community detection algorithms with
291   performant implementations, this provides a powerful toolkit for practitioners to experiment with
292   various post-processing choices via inspection, removed from the computational burden of manipu-
293   lating high-dimensional embedding vectors.

      5.1   S ETUP
299
300
      For each entity, we retrieve the top k = 10 approximate nearest neighbors in embedding cosine
301   similarity (excluding the entity itself) as candidate pairs, using HNSW (Malkov & Yashunin, 2018)
302   via ChromaDB. Duplicate retrieved pairs are retained for the query stage, though each (undirected)
303   edge is included at most once in our graph construction stage. For all experiments we use the
304   bge-en-icl embedding model from BAAI (Xiao et al., 2023), an encoder-only model derived from
305   Mistral-7B (Jiang et al., 2023) which led the MTEB Leaderboard (Muennighoff et al., 2023) at the
306   time of our experiments, as well as Llama-3.1-70B-Instruct (Dubey et al., 2024) from Meta AI,
307   hosted locally via vLLM (Kwon et al., 2023). We remark that the highly parallelizable nature of our
308   approach is particularly amenable to leveraging prefix caching for prompts and few-shot examples
309
      (as is supported by vLLM). Additionally, we employ structured generation via Outlines (Willard
      & Louf, 2023) to ensure that queries only return either “Yes” or “No” as a response. While we
310
      include edges in our graph greedily according to maximum likelihood (e.g. if Pr[Yes] > 0.5), we
311
      can also capture logprobs for each response to use for optional post-processing. Our prompts and
312   representation formats are fairly generic, and similar to those from prior works (see Appendix A).
313   We use the community detection implementations from the igraph library (Csardi & Nepusz, 2006).
314
315
      To create few-shot examples, we select 6 clusters at random among those with at least 5 entities.
      From each of these clusters, we select two entities at random to form a positive example, and then
316
      a third as a negative example, paired with a random choice among the top 200 entities belonging to
317
      other clusters in embedding similarity. Each of these 24 example entities is then removed from the
318   dataset. We did not substantially attempt to tune the number of examples or their selection strategy.
319   We expect that further improvements could be obtained via more delicate example selection; how-
320   ever, our primary aim with LMCD is to demonstrate a method which can be applied to multi-set
321   matching problems in a largely black-box fashion, without requiring model finetuning or manual
322   optimization of prompts and hyperparameters. We note that this selection strategy can be efficiently
323   approximated without access to any ground-truth labelings, via human inspection of top-k retrieved
      nearest neighbors for a small number of randomly chosen entities.

      6     C ONCLUSION
423
424
      We have introduced LMCD, a novel framework for semantic clustering and multi-set entity match-
425
      ing, which offers improved performance over existing methods while simultaneously being easier to
426   scale. LMCD is agnostic to the distribution of cluster sizes, exhibiting strong performance across
427   regimes, and does not require finetuning or extensive advance parameter selection or finetuning. Our
428   framework produces match graph artifacts which are easily interpretable and amenable to computa-
429   tionally lightweight postprocessing, via testing a suite of graph algorithms or interactively selecting
430   cuts. For practical matching challenges involving large volumes of entities, with unknown demands
431   for deduplication and linkage, our results present a powerful approach for efficiently extracting latent
      semantic structure, while sidestepping the curse of dimensionality in its entirety.
