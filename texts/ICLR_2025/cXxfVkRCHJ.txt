009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Offline-to-online Reinforcement Learning (O2O RL) aims to perform online fine-
012            tuning on an offline pre-trained policy to minimize costly online interactions.
013            Existing methods have used offline data or online data to generate new data for
014            data augmentation, which has led to performance improvement during online
015            fine-tuning. However, they have not fully analyzed and utilized both types of
016            data simultaneously. Offline data helps prevent agents from settling too early on
017            suboptimal policies by providing diverse data, while online data improves training
018
               stability and speeds up convergence. In this paper, we propose a data augmentation
               approach, Classifier-Free Diffusion Generation (CFDG). Considering the differ-
019
               ences between offline data and online data, we use conditional diffusion to generate
020
               both types of data for augmentation in the online phase, aiming to improve the
021            quality of sample generation. Experimental results show that CFDG outperforms
022            replaying the two data types or using a standard diffusion model to generate new
023            data. Our method is versatile and can be integrated with existing offline-to-online
024            RL algorithms. By implementing CFDG to popular methods IQL, PEX and APL,
025            we achieve a notable 15% average improvement in empirical performance on the
026            D4RL benchmark like MuJoCo and AntMaze.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Traditionally, Reinforcement Learning (RL) (Haarnoja et al., 2018) is considered as a paradigm for
032   online learning, where agents learn from online interactions with the environment. Due to costly
033
      online interactions in some real-world applications, offline RL (Levine et al., 2020) is proposed
      where agents learn from a static dataset which is pre-collected by arbitrary policies. Current research
034
      in offline RL focuses primarily on addressing the challenge of distribution mismatch or out-of-
035
      distribution (OOD) actions through the implementation of a pessimistic update scheme (Kumar et al.,
036   2020) or in combination with imitation learning (Kumar et al., 2019). However, when dealing with a
037   fixed and suboptimal dataset, it becomes exceedingly challenging for offline RL to attain the optimal
038   policy (Kidambi et al., 2020).
039
      Some recent work addresses the above issues employing an offline-to-online setting. Such methods
040
      (Lee et al., 2022; Nair et al., 2020) focus on pre-training a policy using the offline dataset and
041
      fine-tuning the policy through further online interactions. The main task of O2O RL is to improve
042   sample efficiency and performance. In addition, current methods (Zheng et al., 2023; Nakamoto
043   et al., 2024) have considered the different advantages of offline and online data and utilised both well.
044   In particular, offline data can deter agents from prematurely converging to suboptimal policies due to
045   the diversity of available data, while online data can contribute to training stability and accelerate
046   convergence (Nair et al., 2020; Thrun & Littman, 2000).
047
      However, online data is often limited in the traditional O2O RL setting, which will restrict the
048   exploration of agent in fine-tuning stage. We consider using generative models for data augmentation,
049   rather than simply reusing offline and online data. Prior work has considered upsampling online data
050   with VAEs or GANs (Huang et al., 2017; Imre, 2021). Synthetic Experience Replay (SynthER) (Lu
051   et al., 2024) employs diffusion for data generation, augmenting offline data in offline RL and online
052   data in online RL. When applied directly to O2O RL settings, SynthER can effectively augment
053   online data during the online phase, but overlooks the crucial role of offline data in O2O RL. To
      fully leverage offline data, Energy-guided Diffusion Sampling (EDIS) (Liu et al., 2024) generates

054
      data aligned with the online policy based on the offline dataset to address the issue of distribution
055   shift. However, online data better aligns with the online policy, yet EDIS uses the offline dataset as
056   input for the diffusion model, which is counterintuitive. Therefore, studying the relationship between
057   offline and online data and determining how to augment these two types of data are crucial problems
058   that need to be addressed.
059
      To fully leverage offline and online data, we analyzed the distributions of these two types of data
060
      and applied data augmentation to each separately. We propose our method, Classifier-Free Dffusion
061   Generation (CFDG). Our conditional diffusion model requires only a single training session to
062   simultaneously sample new offline and online data, greatly reducing time costs and improving
063   sampling efficiency. To summarize, the contributions of this paper are:
064
065          • We analyzed the distributions of offline and online data used in O2O RL and also studied
066            the data distribution generated by the existing method EDIS. We found that performing data
067            augmentation separately for online and offline data yields better results.
068          • We use conditional diffusion with classifier-free guidance, taking online data and offline data
069            as inputs to generate new samples of both types. This approach avoids overlap between the
070            distributions of the two data types and enhances sample quality without incurring additional
071
               training time costs.
072          • We conducted experiments on the Locomotion and AntMaze tasks, demonstrating that our
073            data augmentation method significantly improves multiple O2O RL algorithms, including
074            IQL, PEX, and APL. Furthermore, our approach outperforms existing data augmentation
075
               methods such as SynthER and EDIS.

112   2.2   O FFLINE - TO - ONLINE R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING
113
114   Building upon the concepts of offline RL, offline-to-online RL aims aims at enhancing performance
115   by fine-tuning pre-trained offline policy, which contains two phases: (i) offline pre-training, where
116   offline datasets are used to pre-train the policy, and (ii) online fine-tuning, where online interactions
117   are used to refine the pre-trained policy.

145   2.4   C LASSIFIER - FREE G UIDANCE
146
147   In practical scenarios, there is a growing demand to condition the generation on a label c. For example,
148   in image synthesis, diffusion models can generate images consistent with input prompts. To address
149   this requirement, classifier guidance (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021) incorporates an auxiliary classifier
150   pϕ (c|xt ) to guide the sampling in each reverse denoising step, thereby increasing the likelihood
151   of c given xt . While this method has demonstrated some performance improvements, training a
152
      robust classifier for all reverse steps, particularly for the highly noisy input at the initial step, poses a
      significant challenge and incurs additional training costs.
153

155   c as another input of the denoising neural network to model the conditional diffusion score, i.e.,

157   randomly dropping the text prompt with a certain probability at each training iteration. Then the
158
      gradients for the classifier pϕ (c|xt ) can be estimated as:

      3.2   C LASSIFIER - FREE G UIDANCE S AMPLING
209
      There are currently two main types of conditional diffusion. One is the classifier guidance (Dhariwal
210   & Nichol, 2021), which involves training a separate classifier to guide the diffusion sampling process.
211   The other is classifier-free guidance (Ho & Salimans, 2022), which does not require an additional
212   classifier but instead trains the model using both conditional and unconditional inputs based on labels.
213   For the first method, the classifier is trained using noise-corrupted data produced by the forward
214   process of conditional diffusion model. Consequently, training an additional classifier can be difficult
215   especially when a significant amount of noise is added to the clean data. Classifier-free guidance is
      currently the more mainstream approach for diffusion models because it can address the above issues.

      We built on the original code implementation of classifier-free guidance, incorporating it into the
217   Elucidated Diffusion Model Karras et al. (2022). We choose to train an unconditional diffusion model
218   pθ (z) parameterized through a score estimator ϵθ (zλ ) together with the conditional model pθ (z|c)
219   parameterized through ϵθ (zλ , c). We use a single neural network to parameterize both models, where
220   for the unconditional model we can simply input a null token ∅ for the class identifier c when
221   predicting the score, i.e. ϵθ (zλ ) = ϵθ (zλ , c = ∅). We jointly train the unconditional and conditional
222   models simply by randomly setting c to the unconditional class identifier ∅ with some probability
223   puncond , set as a hyperparameter. We then perform sampling using the following linear combination of
224   conditional and unconditional score estimates:
225
226                                 ϵ̃θ (zλ , c) = (1 + w)ϵθ (zλ , c) − wϵθ (zλ ),                            (7)

228   where w is a parameter that controls the strength of the classifier guidance. ϵ̃θ is constructed from
229   score estimates that are non-conservative vector fields due to the use of unconstrained neural networks,
230   so there in general cannot exist a scalar potential such as a classifier log likelihood for which ϵ̃θ is the
231   classifier-guided score.
232   To apply it in the O2O RL setting, we use the classifier-free diffusion model to augment both online
233   data and offline data during the online phase. Algorithm 1 describes the classifier-free guidance
234   sampling process in detail. To reduce the high time cost of the construction of diffusion model and
235   data generation, a parameter Tdiff is set to perform data augmentation every a certain period of time.
236   First, we will update our diffusion model M using samples from the offline buffer Doff and online
237   buffer Don . Then the diffusion model M will sample synthetic data and add them to synthetic buffer

415   5                R ELATED W ORK
416
417   Offline-to-online Reinforcement Learning Offline-to-online RL aims to improve suboptimal
418   offline policies through online fine-tuning. Prior work usually improve the agent by adding regularizer
419   to mitigate the distribution shift problem or leverage both offline data or online data in online fine-
420   tuning. Kostrikov, Nair, and Levine (Kostrikov et al., 2021) introduce IQL, which incorporates a
421   weighted behavioral cloning step to enhance online policy improvement and is applicable in both
422   online and offline-to-online scenarios. When online interactions are available, such conservative
423
      designs may adversely affect the performance. OFF2ON (Lee et al., 2022) employs a balanced replay
      scheme to address the distribution shift issue. It uses offline data by only selecting near-on-policy
424
      samples. However, practical scenarios may involve agents pretrained by various offline RL algorithms,
425
      highlighting the necessity for developing a generic offline-to-online RL framework. Recent studies
426   place a growing emphasis on adaptability. PEX (Zhang et al., 2023) freezes the pre-trained policy
427   and initializes a random policy to enhance exploration. PROTO (Li et al., 2023) gradually evolves
428   the regularization term to relax the constraint strength. From a data-centric perspective, APL (Zheng
429   et al., 2023) and SUNG (Guo et al., 2023) impose constraints exclusively on data from offline datasets
430   and data with high uncertainty, respectively. Our method also focuses on data utilization, using
431   data augmentation to expand the available data, thereby enabling more comprehensive learning and
      improving the agent’s performance.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      Diffusion Models in RL Pearce et al. (Pearce et al., 2023) propose using diffusion model to better
433   imitate human behaviors due to their expressiveness and stability. Diffuser (Janner et al., 2022)
434   applies a diffusion model as a trajectory generator, where the full trajectory of state-action pairs form
435   a single sample for the diffusion model. Additionally, a separate return model is trained to predict
436   the cumulative rewards of each trajectory sample, and its guidance is incorporated into the reverse
437   sampling stage. This approach is similar to Decision Transformer (Chen et al., 2021), which also
438   learns a trajectory generator through GPT2 with the help of the true trajectory returns. However,
439   when used online, sequence models can no longer predict actions from states autoregressively since
440   the states are an outcome of the environment. Consequently, during evaluation, a whole trajectory is
441
      predicted for each state but only the first action is applied, leading to high computational cost. Our
      approach employs diffusion models to do data augmentation for RL in a distinct manner.
442
443
444   6    C ONCLUSION
445
446   In this paper, we analyze the distributions of offline and online data in the O2O RL setting and
447   improve upon existing data augmentation methods by addressing their limitations. We use a diffusion
448   model guided by classifier-free guidance to simultaneously augment both types of data. In the online
449   phase, we input offline data and online data as two distinct labels into the diffusion model. With a
450   single round of training, we can sample both types of data. Our method, CFDG, is simple and can be
451
      easily integrated with existing O2O RL algorithms, significantly boosting their performance while
      surpassing other data augmentation methods.
452
453   Although our method achieved the expected performance, there are still some limitations. One of
454   these is the setting of the data ratio. In our experiments, we used a fixed parameter for this, which
455   produced good results. However, according to some of our tests, the ratio of offline to online data can
456   significantly impact performance in different environments. Additionally, determining the optimal
457
      ratio for the three types of data, including the generated data, remains an open challenge.
