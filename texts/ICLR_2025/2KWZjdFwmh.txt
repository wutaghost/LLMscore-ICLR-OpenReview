009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Adaptive optimization algorithms such as Adam see widespread use in Deep
012            Learning. However, these methods rely on diagonal approximations of the pre-
013            conditioner, losing much information about the curvature of the loss surface and
014            potentially leading to prolonged training times. We introduce S T EVE (Stochas-
015            tic Eigenbasis-adaptive Variance Estimation), a novel optimization algorithm that
016            estimates lower order moments in the Kronecker-Factored Eigenbasis (KFE).
017            By combining the advantages of Adam over other adaptive methods with the
018
               curvature-aware transformations of methods like KFAC and EKFAC, S T EVE
               leverages second-order information while remaining computationally efficient.
019
               Our experiments demonstrate that EVE achieves faster convergence both in step-
020
               count and in wall-clock time compared to Adam, EKFAC, and KFAC for a variety
021            of deep neural network architectures.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Deep neural networks have shown state-of-the-art performance across a variety of tasks, including
027   computer vision, natural language processing, and speech recognition. Despite their success, train-
028   ing modern models with large parameter counts often requires extensive computational resources
029   and prolonged training times on high-end specialized hardware. This challenge has spurred sig-
030   nificant interest in developing more efficient optimization algorithms so as to reduce training time
031   without sacrificing performance.
032   Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) and its variants are the traditional choice of optimization algo-
033   rithm for training deep neural networks and remain a dominant choice for many model architectures.
034   SGD optimizes the model parameters θ by computing the gradient of empirical risk (calculated over
035   a mini-batch of training examples) and moving the model parameters by a small step in that direc-
036   tion. Formally, the t-th step is θt+1 = θt − η∇θ R(θt ) where θt represents the model parameters at
037   the tth step, η is a positive learning rate, and ∇θ R(θt ) is the gradient of the empirical risk R(θ).
038   Despite its simplicity and scalability, SGD struggles with the non-convex and ill-conditioned curva-
039   ture common to deep neural network loss surfaces. As a typical example, the loss surface may have
040   directions with very different curvatures, and thus the impact of the update in one direction may be
041   much larger than in other directions. This imbalance can raise the number of steps until convergence
042   considerably leading to longer training times.
043   To correct for these limitations, there have been attempts to design optimization algorithms for deep
044   neural networks which employ second-order information such as the curvature. The general form
045   of these methods is to use an update of the form θt+1 = θt − ηP −1 ∇θ R(θt ) where P , referred to
046   as the preconditioner, is some matrix that captures local curvature or similar information about the
047   loss surface such as the Hessian used in Newton-Raphson, the Fisher Information Matrix as used
048   in Natural Gradient Descent (Amari, 1998), Generalized Gauss Newton Matrices, or closely related
049   matrices.
050   The problem with this form of update is that modern deep neural networks have millions or billions
051   of parameters. Thus, while these methods require fewer updates to train, this advantage is overshad-
052   owed by the enormous cost of storing and inverting a fully maintained preconditioner which scale
053   quadratically and cubically respectively with the number of parameters. To overcome these issues it
      becomes necessary to approximate the preconditioner in a way that allows for faster inversion.

054
      By far the most common approximation is to take the preconditioner to be diagonal. This reduces
055   inversion to pure element-wise computations and also greatly reduces storage cost. Several popular
056   optimization algorithms use this strategy in some form.
057
058         1. Adagrad (Duchi et al., 2011) keeps a simple moving average of the elementwise squares
059            of the gradients and elementwise scales the gradients by the inverse square root of this
060            average. In essence, this approach is using a diagonal approximation of the square root of
061            the empirical Fisher
062         2. RMSProp (Tieleman & Hinton, 2012) uses a similar strategy but uses an exponential mov-
063            ing average of squared gradients.
064
            3. Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2015) introduces bias correction on the exponential moving average
065
               and use a different moving average for the gradients themselves.
066
067   While these methods have been shown to be more effective in a variety of tasks (Savarese et al.,
068   2021), they only capture curvature information along parameter axes and ignore interactions be-
069   tween different parameters. Consequently, these methods lose much of the second-order information
070   and do not fully correct for poor curvature in the loss surface.
071
      More sophisticated methods avoid diagonal approximations and instead approximate the precondi-
072
      tioner in ways that account for parameter correlations as encoded in the non-diagonal entries of the
073   preconditioner. These approaches vary, although common themes include low rank updates to the
074   preconditioner (Ollivier, 2015; 2017; Mu et al., 2022), using block approximations of the precondi-
075   tioner or of its inverse (Martens & Grosse, 2015; Desjardins et al., 2015; Fujimoto & Ohira, 2018;
076   Soori et al., 2022), quasi-Newton methods to estimate either the entire preconditioner or its block
077   approximations (Liu & Nocedal, 1989; Goldfarb et al., 2020) and Bayesian inverse-free approaches
078   (Lin et al., 2023; 2024).
079   Perhaps the most common non-diagonal concept for use in second-order optimization algorithms
080   for deep learning is Kronecker-Factored Approximate Curvature (KFAC). Originally developed for
081   fully-connected layers in Martens & Grosse (2015), KFAC approximates the preconditioning matrix
082   as block diagonal with blocks for each layer and then further approximates each block as a Kro-
083   necker product of two smaller matrices. Since inversion commutes with the Kronecker product, this
084   allows for a faster computation of the inverse for each update. This approach has been expanded to
085   convolutional layers in Grosse & Martens (2016) and to weight-sharing layers in Eschenhagen et al.
086   (2023).
087   Of particular interest is a further refinement of KFAC, Eigenvalue-corrected Kronecker Factored
088   Approximate Curvature (EKFAC) George et al. (2018), which more accurately captures the curva-
089   ture in different directions by correcting the eigenvalues in KFAC. This is done by diagonalizing
090   the Kronecker factors of the preconditioner blocks and replacing the diagonal with variances in the
091   Kronecker-Factored Eigenbasis (KFE). Due to the expensive nature of the computing KFE, EK-
092   FAC amortizes this computation by updating it infrequently while still being able to compute cheap
093
      updates to the diagonal variances every iteration. Despite its advantages, EKFAC, even when aug-
      mented with momentum, still underperforms Adam in convergence speed for some tasks.
094
095   Motivated by the strength of Adam within the scope of diagonal approximations and the curvature-
096   aware properties of EKFAC, we propose S T EVE (Stochastic Eigenbasis-adaptive Variance Estima-
097   tion) which combines the moment estimation of Adam with the curvature corrections of EKFAC.
098   Similar to EKFAC, S T EVE transforms the gradients into the KFE but instead of keeping a simple
099
      average of second moments S T EVE keeps bias-corrected exponential moving averages of the first
      and second moment in the same way as is done in Adam.

194   An instructive perspective on the diagonal approximation of the preconditioner is to view the pre-
195   conditioner as a diagonal rescaling of the parameter axis as viewed in the parameter basis. Natural
196   Gradient Descent which uses the Fisher A as a preconditioner can also be viewed as a diagonal
197   rescaling. If we diagonalize the positive semi-definite A as A = U SU T , the update becomes
198   θt+1 = θt − U S −1 U T ∇θ L which is to say converting the gradient A’s Eigenbasis, doing a diago-
199   nal rescaling by the eigenvalues of the Fisher, and then switching back to the parameter basis. This
200   perspective poses a challenge to the KFAC approximation as the critically important eigenvalues of
201
      the Fisher Blocks are not preserved by the approximation.
202   EKFAC (George et al., 2018) addresses this issue by correcting the eigenvalues of the KFAC approx-
203   imation. They do this by diagonalizing A(l) = B (l) ⊗ C (l) = (UB ⊗ UC )(SB ⊗ SC )(UB ⊗ UC )T
204   and then replacing (SB ⊗ SC ) with diag(E[(UB ⊗ UC )T ∇θ R2 ]) which is the matrix with diag-
205   onal equal to the vector of second moments in Kronecker-Factored Eigenbasis (KFE) defined by
206   applying the transformation (UB ⊗ UC )T . This replacement yields a provably closer approxima-
207   tion to the Fisher (as measured by the Froebenius Norm) and the optimal diagonal scaling in the
208
      KFE. Additionally, this approximation lends itself well to amortizing the expensive curvature esti-
      mation as the KFE does not have to updated with every step while the diagonal matrix of eigenvalues
209
      can cheaply be updated every step. Unfortunately, even when augemented with running averages
210
      EKFAC struggles to compete with Adam in practice.

      in the realm of diagonal adaptive optimizers. Viewing EKFAC from the perspective of diagonal

      5   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
379
380
      In this paper, we introduced S T EVE, a novel optimization algorithm that synergizes the moment es-
381   timation of Adam with the curvature-aware preconditioning of EKFAC. By transforming gradients
382   into a Kronecker-Factored Eigenbasis (KFE) of the Fisher and maintaining bias-corrected exponen-
383   tial moving averages of the first and second moments, S T EVE leverages second-order information
384   while retaining computational efficiency. Our empirical evaluations across various datasets and ar-
385   chitectures demonstrate that S T EVE significantly accelerates training, achieving substantial reduc-
386   tions in both wall-clock time and number of epochs compared to existing optimization algorithms
387   such as Adam, EKFAC, and KFAC.
388   Despite promising results, there are avenus for future exploration and improvement. One direction
389   to take is to improve the KFE by attempting to use other common preconditioners instead of the
390   Empirical Fisher such as the true Fisher Information Matrix. Other directions to take the work are
391   to investigate the potential of the improvements that have been made over Adam in the KFE such as
392   proper weight decay or Nesterov momentum.
