009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            This paper proposes a new theoretical lens to view Wasserstein generative adver-
012            sarial networks (WGANs). To minimize the Wasserstein-1 distance between the
013            true data distribution and our estimate of it, we derive a distribution-dependent
014            ordinary differential equation (ODE), which represents the gradient flow of the
015            Wasserstein-1 loss, and show that a forward Euler discretization of the ODE con-
016
               verges. This inspires a new class of generative models that naturally integrates
               persistent training (which we call W1-FE). When persistent training is turned off,
017
               we prove that W1-FE reduces to WGAN. When we intensify persistent training
018
               appropriately, W1-FE is shown to outperform WGAN in training experiments from
019            low to high dimensions, in terms of both convergence speed and training results.

022   1    I NTRODUCTION
023
024   Recently, Huang and Zhang (2023) have shown that the original generative adversarial network
025   (GAN) algorithm (Goodfellow et al., 2014) in fact follows the dynamics of an ordinary differential
026   equation (ODE), which represents the gradient flow induced by the Jensen-Shannon divergence
027
      (Huang and Zhang, 2023, Proposition 8). Huang and Malik (2024) characterize the gradient flow
      induced by the Wasserstein-2 distance and propose a class of generative models, called W2-FE, that
028
      follows the dynamics of the corresponding ODE (Huang and Malik, 2024, Algorithm 1). Notably,
029
      while W2-FE covers W2-GAN in Leygonie et al. (2019) as a special case, it allows for a modification
030   to the generator training that may lead to substantial numerical improvements over W2-GAN (Huang
031   and Malik, 2024, Section 7).
032
033
      This paper is motivated by the question: can we obtain analogous results for Wasserstein generative
      adversarial networks (WGANs)? As a mainstream class of GAN algorithms known for its enhanced
034
      stability, WGAN (Arjovsky et al., 2017; Gulrajani et al., 2017; Petzka et al., 2018) attempts to generate
035
      samples whose distribution is similar to the true data distribution (measured by the Wasserstein-1
036   distance) using adversarial training motivated by Goodfellow et al. (2014). It is natural to expect that,
037   by slightly modifying the arguments in Huang and Malik (2024), one can similarly derive an ODE
038   that represents the gradient flow induced by the Wasserstein-1 distance and correspondingly propose
039   a new class of generative models that covers and potentially improves WGAN.
040
      Mathematical challenges abound, however, under the the Wasserstein-1 distance. First and foremost,
041
      while subdifferential calculus is full-fledged under the Wasserstein-p distance for all p > 1 (Ambrosio
042   et al., 2008, Chapter 10), the same construction breaks down when p = 1. In particular, “Fréchet
043   differential” (Ambrosio et al., 2008, Corollary 10.2.7) or “Wasserstein gradient” (Carmona and
044   Delarue, 2018, Definition 5.62) is no longer well-defined for p = 1. Simply put, it is not even clear
045   how “gradient” should be defined under the Wasserstein-1 distance. On top of this, when showing that
046   a discretization of the gradient-flow ODE converges (as the time step tends to 0), Huang and Malik
047   (2024) crucially rely on an interpolation result from optimal transport: the most cost-efficient path to
048   move one probability measure to another (measured by the Wasserstein-p distance for any p > 1) can
049   be interpolated recursively into smaller segments between intermediate measures (Ambrosio et al.,
050
      2008, Lemma 7.2.1), where each segment is most cost-efficient in itself and represents one time step
      in the ODE discretization. Such an interpolation result, again, fails when p = 1.
051
052   To overcome these challenges, we first recall “linear functional derivative” from the mean field game
053   literature (Carmona and Delarue, 2018) and observe that the “Euclidean gradient of a linear functional
      derivative” can generally serve as the proper gradient notion in the space of probability measures,

054
      independently of any subdifferential calculus; see the discussion below Definition 3.1. Our first
055   main theoretical result, Proposition 3.1, shows that the linear function derivative of the Wasserstein-1
056   distance exists and it coincides with the Kantorovich potential in optimal transport (i.e., a 1-Lipschitz
057   function that maximizes the duality formula of the Wasserstein-1 distance; see Definition 2.1). The
058   gradient flow induced by the Wasserstein-1 distance thus takes the form of an ODE that evolves
059   along the negative Euclidean gradient of the Kantorovich potential (i.e., (3.6) below) and a forward
060   Euler discretization can be correspondingly devised (i.e., (4.1) below). As mentioned above, the
061   interpolation result in Ambrosio et al. (2008) can no longer be used to show the convergence of
062   the discretization. Our second main theoretical result, Theorem 4.1, instead relies on the uniform
063
      boundedness of the Euclidean gradient of the Kantorovich potential (see Remark 2.1), which is unique
      to the p = 1 case. Such boundedness allows us to show appropriate compactness and equicontinuity
064
      of the flow of measures induced by the discretization, so that a refined Arzela-Ascoli argument can
065
      be applied to give the convergence of the discretization; see the discussion below Theorem 4.1.
066
067   Algorithm 1 (called W1-FE) is designed to simulate the discretization of the gradient-flow ODE. It
068   first computes an estimate of the Kantorovich potential, by following the discriminator training in a
069
      typical WGAN algorithm. With the Kantorovich potential estimated, a generator is trained to move
      along the ODE discretization. As the generator’s task is to learn the new distribution at the next time
070
      point, persistent training techniques (Fischetti et al., 2018) can be naturally incorporated—namely,
071
      given a set of samples from the new distribution, the generator’s loss descends K ∈ N consecutive
072   times to better represent these samples (and thus the new distribution). For the case K = 1 (which
073   means no persistent training), Proposition 4.1 shows that W1-FE reduces to WGAN. It is interesting
074   to note that, despite the coincidence under K = 1, W1-FE and WGAN are fundamentally different.
075   Specifically, if we also incorporate persistent training into WGAN, the generator updates in W1-FE
076   and WGAN can be quite different under any K > 1. That is, K = 1 is the only case where they
077   agree; see the discussion below Proposition 4.1 and Remark 4.2 for details.
078   We train W1-FE with diverse persistency levels K = 1, 3, 5, 10 in three experiments that involve
079   datasets from low to high dimensions, including synthetic two-dimensional mixtures of Gaussians
080   and real datasets of USPS, MNIST, and CIFAR-10. Across the three experiments, W1-FE with
081   K > 1 converges significantly faster and achieves better training results than the baseline K = 1 case
082   (which is WGAN). Intriguingly, while a larger K generally implies faster convergence, we observe
083   numerically a threshold of K beyond which the training results start to deteriorate, possibly due to
084   overfitting. This suggests that taking K to be at that threshold can likely best balance the benefits of
085   persistent training against potential overfitting; see the last paragraph in Section 6 for details.
086   Many recently developed generative models also feature “Wasserstein gradient flows” (e.g., Fan
087   et al. (2022), Choi et al. (2024), Zhang and Katsoulakis (2023), and Onken et al. (2020)), and the
088   common theme is to minimize a loss function under the geometry induced by the Wasserstein-2
089   distance. This means that “Wasserstein gradient flows” in the literature should be more precisely
090   referred to as “Wasserstein-2 gradient flows.” In other words, simply because subdifferential calculus
091   under the Wasserstein-2 distance and the resulting gradient flows are widely studied (Ambrosio et al.,
092
      2008, Chapter 10 and Section 11.2), much of the recent literature has leveraged on this to design
      new generative models. Our study is distinct from all this, as we right away tackle Wasserstein-1
093
      gradient flows, which are much less understood. As explained before, this focus on Wasserstein-1
094
      gradient flows allows us to recover and even improve WGAN, which by construction minimizes the
095   Wasserstein-1 distance and cannot be easily analyzed by the standard Wasserstein-2 framework.
096
097   The rest of this paper is organized as follows. Section 2 introduces the mathematical framework and
098
      notation to be used. Section 3 discusses how the “gradient descent” idea can be applied to minimizing
      the Wasserstein-1 distance and formulates the corresponding gradient-flow ODE. Section 4 defines
099
      a forward Euler discretization of the ODE and designs an algorithm (i.e., W1-FE) to simulate this
100
      discretization. Section 5 trains W1-FE in three experiments and demonstrates its superiority over
101   WGAN. Section 6 discusses the limitations of our study on both the theoretical and numerical sides.
102   Section 7 concludes our findings.

471   standard theory in Ambrosio et al. (2008) does not provide concrete results or guidance, as it covers
472   the case Pp (Rd ) for all p > 1, but excludes our present case P1 (Rd ).
473
      Numerically, while we have shown that persistent training can markedly improve training results in
474   several experiments, it is not without restrictions. Recall that SGD is performed K ∈ N consecutive
475   times with the same minibatch {ζi } in Algorithm 1, with ζi = Gθ (zi ) −  ∇ϕ(Gθ (zi )). There are
476   two issues one has to confront. First, any inaccuracy in the estimation of the Kantorovich potential ϕ
477   will be amplified by persistent training. As a larger K ∈ N demands our algorithm to more closely
478   fits the data points {ζi }, even when {ζi } are of low quality due to the inaccuracy of ϕ, the issue of
479   “garbage in, garbage out” will be exacerbated. Second, even if ϕ is perfectly estimated, such that {ζi }
480   are of high quality, an excessive K ∈ N will certainly lead to overfitting.

528   7   C ONCLUSION
529
530   By performing “gradient descent” in the space P1 (Rd ), we introduce a distribution-dependent ODE
531
      for the purpose of generative modeling. A forward Euler discretization of the ODE converges to a
      curve of probability measures, suggesting that numerical implementation of the discretization is stable
532
      for small time steps. This inspires a class of new algorithms (called W1-FE) that naturally involves
533
      persistent training. If we (artificially) choose not to implement persistent training, our algorithms
534   recover existing WGAN algorithms. By increasing the level of persistent training suitably (to better
535   simulate the ODE), our algorithms outperform existing WGAN algorithms in numerical examples.
