008
009
               The growing ubiquity of relational data structured as graphs has underscored the
010            need for graph learning models with exceptional generalization capabilities. How-
011            ever, current approaches often struggle to effectively extract generalizable insights,
012            frequently requiring extensive fine-tuning and limiting their versatility. Graph
013            foundation models offer a transformative solution, with the potential to learn ro-
014            bust, generalizable representations from graph data. This enables more effective
015            and adaptable applications across a wide spectrum of tasks and domains. In this
016            work, we investigate a unified graph model, AnyGraph, designed to handle key
017            challenges: i) Structure Heterogenity. Addressing distribution shift in graph
018
               structural information; ii) Feature Heterogenity. Handling diverse feature repre-
               sentation spaces across graph datasets; iii) Fast Adaptation. Efficiently adapting
019
               the model to new graph domains; iv) Scaling Law Emergence. Enabling the
020
               model to exhibit scaling law behavior, where its performance scales favorably
021            with the amount of data and parameter sizes. To tackle these critical challenges,
022            we build the AnyGraph upon a Graph Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architecture.
023            This approach empowers the model to effectively manage both the in-domain and
024            cross-domain distribution shift concerning structure-level and feature-level hetero-
025            geneity. Furthermore, a lightweight graph expert routing mechanism is proposed
026            to facilitate AnyGraph’s fast adaptability to new data and domains. Our extensive
027            experiments on diverse 38 graph datasets have demonstrated the strong zero-shot
028            learning performance of AnyGraph across diverse graph domains with significant
029
               distribution shift. Furthermore, we have validated the model’s fast adaptation
               ability and scaling law emergence, showcasing its versatility. We have anony-
030
               mously released our open-sourced AnyGraph implementation at the following link:
031
               https://anonymous.4open.science/r/AnyGraph-FECD.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   The growing ubiquity of relational data in the form of graphs has underscored the pressing need
037   for advanced graph learning models that excel at generalization (Fey et al., 2024; Jin et al., 2020).
038   As real-world applications of graph-structured data continue to proliferate across diverse domains,
039   including social networks, academic networks, transportation systems, and biological networks, the
040   ability of graph learning models to effectively handle distribution shifts and adapt to new graph
041   domains has become increasingly crucial (Zhang et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2024; Mao et al., 2024).
042   Developing models with robust zero-shot learning performance and fast adaptation capabilities can
043   unlock transformative opportunities for leveraging the rich insights encoded within graph data.
044   The field of graph learning has seen significant advancements in recent years, largely driven by
045   the power of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) (Liu et al., 2022; Xiao et al., 2021; Li et al., 2021).
046   However, the state-of-the-art models often fall short when it comes to truly generalizable performance.
047   Existing approaches are heavily reliant on arduous fine-tuning processes, making them ill-equipped to
048   handle the diverse array of graph structures and distributions encountered in real-world applications.
049   This inability to adapt swiftly and seamlessly to novel graph domains poses a critical barrier to the
050
      widespread adoption of graph learning technologies. Therefore, addressing this challenge is of high
      importance if we are to fully harness the transformative potential of graph-based insights.
051
052   Inspired by the principles that have driven the development of successful foundation models in
053   understanding vision and language data (Wang et al., 2022; 2023), the concept of a versatile graph
      foundation model holds immense potential to unlock new frontiers in graph learning. By learning

      rich, transferable representations from diverse graph-structured data, such a model can be efficiently
055   adapted to a wide array of graph domains and tasks. However, building an effective and adaptive
056   graph foundation model is not a trivial endeavor. Several key challenges must be overcome, including:

058
      (i) Structure Heterogeneity. The development of versatile graph models faces the challenge of
      accommodating diverse structural properties and data distributions in various graph datasets. For
059
      instance, graphs can exhibit substantial heterogeneity in node degree distributions, ranging from
060
      homogeneous to highly skewed patterns. Similarly, graph structures can vary greatly in complexity,
061   from simple topologies to intricate, hierarchical arrangements. These structural variations can
062   significantly impact the performance and generalization of graph models. Effectively addressing this
063   heterogeneity is critical for developing unified models that can thrive across diverse graph data.
064
      (ii) Feature Heterogeneity. Graphs exhibit substantial heterogeneity in their node and edge features,
065
      which can span categorical attributes, continuous numerical data, and multi-modal content. Further-
066
      more, the dimensionality and semantics of these features often vary dramatically across different
067   graph domains. For instance, a social interaction graph may include textual content and demographic
068   information associated with its nodes, while a molecular graph may feature atomic compositions and
069   bond types. Effectively handling this feature heterogeneity is crucial for building a versatile graph
070   model capable of generalizing across diverse graph domains.
071
      (iii) Fast Adaptation for Broad Applicability. A key capability for graph foundation models is
072
      the ability to efficiently adapt to new graph dataset and domains. Rather than requiring extensive
073   retraining or fine-tuning, the ideal model should be able to quickly adjust its parameters and learning
074   strategies to handle the structural and distributional characteristics of previously unseen graph datasets.
075   By seamlessly generalizing and performing well across a diverse range of real-world scenarios – from
076   user behavior graphs to transportation networks and biological systems – these adaptable models can
077   unlock transformative insights across an ever-expanding universe of graph-structured data.
078
      (iv) Scaling Laws for Transformative Graph Capabilities. A key characteristic of successful
079   foundation models in domains like CV (Cherti et al., 2023) and NLP (Muennighoff et al., 2024) is
080   their ability to exhibit scaling laws - where performance systematically improves as the model size
081   or training dataset increases. By harnessing this emergent scaling phenomenon, graph foundation
082   models can unlock unprecedented levels of capability and generalization, far surpassing the limitations
083   of fixed-capacity architectures. As the size of graph datasets and model complexity grow, these
084   scaling-aware designs can continue delivering transformative performance gains.

      2     P RELIMINARIES
126
      Graph-Structured Data. A graph G consists of a set of nodes V = {vi } and a set of edges
127   E = {(vi , vj )}. In many cases, each node vi is associated with a feature vector fi ∈ Rd0 . To
128   efficiently utilize such graph-structured data, the link information is typically recorded using an
129   adjacency matrix A ∈ R|V|×|V| . Each element ai,j of A is either 1 or 0, inddicating whether there is
130   an edge from node vi to vj . Additionally, the feature vectors of the nodes are usually represented by
131   a feature matrix F ∈ R|V|×d0 , where each row corresponds to a node’s feature vector.
132
133
      Graph Foundation Models (GFMs). The essence of GFMs lies in their strong generalization
      capabilities. Specifically, a graph foundation model should be able to handle unseen graph data
134
      that exhibits significant discrepancies from its training graph datasets. These discrepancies may
135
      include differences in feature spaces, as well as variations in node and edge semantics across datasets.
136   Formally, let’s denote the training graphs as S = {Gs }, where each graph Gs is associated with a label
137   set Ys . Similarly, the set of test graphs is denoted as T = {Gt }, with labels Yt . With a differentiable
138   training objective L and an evaluation criterion C to measure the prediction accuracy of downstream
139   tasks, building a graph foundation model fΘ with trainable parameters Θ can be formalized as:
140                                 X                                    X
141                       arg max        C (fΘ (Gt ), Yt ) , Θ = arg min    L (fΘ (Gs ), Ys )                (1)
                           f,L                                    Θ
142                                Gt                                   Gs

143   The above formulation reveals that the key to building GFMs are: i) the model architecture design
144   (f ), which must have the capacity to encode diverse feature spaces and structural patterns, and ii)
145   the model training process (L), which must effectively traverse such diverse data to find an optimal
146   solution Θ for the model f . In light of this, our AnyGraph employs a mixture-of-experts architecture
147   with an automated expert routing method, to seamlessly integrate powerful prediction models for
148   highly diverse graph data. AnyGraph is extensively trained on graphs from various applications using
149   multiple featuring methods, with a graph augmentation technique to further enhance data diversity.

152
153   AnyGraph aims to address graph heterogeneity in both structures and node features, while enabling
154   fast adaptation to new data. The proposed graph MoE paradigm enables AnyGraph to learn a diverse
155
      ensemble of graph experts, each tailored to specific characteristics. The lightweight expert routing
      mechanism allows AnyGraph to quickly identify and activate the most relevant experts for a given
156
      input graph, without extensive retraining or fine-tuning. Its overall framework is depicted in Fig. 2.

494   5   R ELATED W ORKS
495
496   Graph Neural Models. Graph learning has garnered significant interest for its broad applicability
497
      across various fields such as user behavior modeling and biology/chemistry applications (Chang
      et al., 2021; Hao et al., 2020). Graph neural networks (GNNs) learn node representation vectors
498
      for downstream tasks like node classification and link prediction. The core mechanism involves
499
      iterative message passing, refining node embeddings to capture both node-specific information and
500   higher-order topological structures. This process ensures that the final node embeddings effectively
501   encapsulate both node-specific information and higher-order topological structures. Notable tech-
502   niques include Graph Convolutional Networks (GCNs) (Jin et al., 2021), Graph Attention Networks
503   (GATs) (Brody et al., 2022), Graph Isomorphism Network (GIN) (Xu et al., 2018), and Graph Trans-
504   former (Hu et al., 2020), which improves the graph modeling abilities. Despite the advancements,
505   these methods remain reliable on high-quality training data and often struggle with generalization.
506   Self-Supervised Graph Learning. Given the challenges with the generalizability of GNNs, consid-
507   erable research efforts (Xie et al., 2022) have focused on enhancing GNNs through self-supervised
508   learning objectives, aiming to capture invariant graph features. Specifically, GraphCL (You et al.,
509   2020) introduced a contrastive pre-training approach for graph data, designed to learn authentic graph
510   characteristics that are robust to structural and feature perturbations. Building on this, JOAO (You
511   et al., 2021) and GCA (Zhu et al., 2021) have developed adaptive augmentation strategies for self-
512   supervised tasks, effectively mitigating the adverse effects of random augmentations. Subsequent
513   works have sought to quickly adapt these pre-trained models to downstream tasks and evolving graph
514
      data, as demonstrated by GPF (Fang et al., 2023) and GraphPrompt (Liu et al., 2023). Despite the
      success, the generalizability of these methods remains confined to graph data with similar structural
515
      and feature patterns, overlooking the cross-domain generalization challenge highlighted in our paper.
516
517   Large-scale Graph Pre-training. Recent advances in graph modeling have seen efforts to pre-
518   train large-scale graph models across multiple datasets to improve their generalizability, drawing
519   inspiration from the strong generalization capabilities of large language models (LLMs). For instance,
520
      OFA (Liu et al., 2024) and ZeroG (Li et al., 2024) utilize text embeddings to standardize the feature
      spaces across various graph datasets and tasks, facilitating cross-dataset training of graph models.
521
      Models like InstructGLM (Ye et al., 2024) GraphGPT (Tang et al., 2024a) and LLaGA (Chen et al.,
522
      2024) synchronize graph representation spaces with the hidden spaces of LLMs, thus enabling the
523   application of general language models for graph prediction tasks. Furthermore, HiGPT (Tang et al.,
524   2024b) expands the capabilities of LLMs to accommodate heterogeneous graph data.
525
526
      Despite these advancements, most generalized graph models require substantial access to and inte-
      gration of text features, which confines their use primarily to text-abundant environments such as
527
      academic networks. Additionally, these methods are typically trained within specific application
528
      realms, failing to address the significant variances between datasets from diverse domains.

      6   C ONCLUSION
531
532   In this work, we present the AnyGraph framework, an effective and efficient graph foundation model
533   designed to address the multifaceted challenges of structure and feature heterogeneity across diverse
534   graph datasets. AnyGraph’s innovative Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architecture, coupled with its
535   dynamic expert routing mechanism, positions it at the state-of-the-art of cross-domain generalization
536   capabilities. Extensive experiments on 38 varied graph datasets have not only underscored Any-
537   Graph’s superior zero-shot learning performance but also its robustness to distribution shifts and its
538
      adherence to scaling laws, thereby enhancing its predictive accuracy with increased model size and
      data volume. The model’s efficiency in training and inference, validated through comparison with
539
      existing methods, further cements its practical applicability.

743

746   standard configuration of our AnyGraph includes 512 hidden units and 8 graph expert models. Each
747   expert model comprises 8 fully-connected layers. These layers utilize a ReLU activation function and
748   incorporate a dropout layer with a dropout probability of 0.1. Algorithm Hyperparameters. The
749   frequency regularization of our routing mechanism is set with an adjustment range of ρ = 0.2. The

      A.4   BASELINE M ETHODS
757
758   This section provides detailed descriptions of the baseline models used in our analysis. We employ
759   seven different baseline models across four distinct categories.
760   Training-from-scratch Graph Neural Networks.
761
      • GAT (Veličković et al., 2018). Graph Attention Networks (GAT) leverage an attention mechanism
762
        to dynamically weight node-to-node connections, enhancing the model’s ability to adaptively
763
        propagate and aggregate information across the graph.
764
765   • GIN (Xu et al., 2018). The Graph Isomorphism Network (GIN) significantly boosts the expressive
766
        power of Graph Neural Networks by introducing a unique graph encoding technique aimed at
        effectively distinguishing between non-isomorphic graphs.
767
768   Graph Pre-training Models.
769
      • GraphCL (Zhu et al., 2021). It enhances the pre-training of graph models via self-discriminative
770     contrastive learning, which is applied to learned node embeddings. The method employs various
771     graph augmentation techniques such as node dropping, edge permutation, random walks, and
772     feature masking to improve robustness.
773
774   Graph Prompt Tuning Methods.
775   • GraphPrompt (Liu et al., 2023). It proposes a unified approach that integrates pre-training and
776     prompt tuning for graph models. It features a learnable prompt layer designed to automatically
777     extract crucial information from the pre-trained model to enhance downstream performance.
778   • GPF (Fang et al., 2023). The Graph Prompt Framework (GPF) is a versatile graph prompt tuning
779     framework compatible with various graph pre-training methods. It offers two variants of a learnable
780     graph prompt layer, tailored to different application needs.
781
      Graph Foundation Models.
782
783   • GraphGPT (Tang et al., 2024a). This approach proposes representation alignment and instruction
784     tuning techniques to align graph representation spaces with text encoding spaces, empowring large
785
        language models with the capabilities of zero-shot graph encoding and inference.
786   • OpenGraph (Xia et al., 2024). This method introduces a unified graph tokenizer, a scalable graph
787     transformer to improve the model’s performance and generalization ability. An LLM-enhanced
788     data augmentation mechanism is proposed to address domain-specific data scarcity.
