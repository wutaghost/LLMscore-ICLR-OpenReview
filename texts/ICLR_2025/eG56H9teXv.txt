010                                              A BSTRACT
011
012            Deep learning models for image classification have become standard tools in re-
013            cent years. However, a well known vulnerability of these models is their suscep-
014            tibility to adversarial examples. Adversarial examples are generated by slightly
015            altering an image of a certain class in a way that is imperceptible to humans but
016            causes the model to classify it wrongly as another class. Many algorithms have
017
               been proposed to address this problem, falling generally into one of two cate-
               gories: (i) building robust classifiers (ii) directly detecting attacked images. De-
018
               spite the very good performance of the proposed detectors, we argue that in a
019
               white-box setting, where the attacker knows the configuration and weights of the
020            network and the detector, the attacker can overcome the detector by running many
021            examples on a local copy, and sending only examples that were not detected to the
022            actual model. This problem of addressing complete knowledge of the attacker is
023            common in security applications where even a very good model is not sufficient
024            to ensure safety. In this paper we propose to overcome this inherent limitation of
025            any static defence with randomization. To do so, one must generate a very large
026            family of detectors with consistent performance, and select one or more of them
027            randomly for each input. For the individual detectors, we suggest the method of
028
               neural fingerprints. In the training phase, for each class we repeatedly sample a
               tiny random subset of neurons from certain layers of the network, and if their av-
029
               erage is sufficiently different between clean and attacked images of the focal class
030
               they are considered a fingerprint and added to the detector bank. During test time,
031            we sample fingerprints from the bank associated with the label predicted by the
032            model, and detect attacks using a likelihood ratio test. We evaluate our detectors
033            on ImageNet with different attack methods and model architectures, and show
034            near-perfect detection with low rates of false detection.

037   1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039   In recent years, deep learning models have become ubiquitous across a wide range of applications,
040   from image classification and natural language processing to speech recognition and transportation.
041   However, these models have been shown to be vulnerable to adversarial attacks - small, impercep-
042   tible perturbations to inputs that cause models to make incorrect predictions (Szegedy et al., 2014;
043   Goodfellow et al., 2014). These attacks pose serious concerns regarding the security and reliability
044
      of deep learning systems, especially in critical domains (Kurakin et al., 2016). A growing body
      of research has focused on developing adversarial attacks that can reliably fool models as well as
045
      defences to mitigate these threats (Madry et al., 2017; Song et al., 2017; Gowal et al., 2020).
046
047   The main defence approaches can be divided into the broad categories of robust classification and
      adversarial attack detection. As the name suggests, robust models aim to mitigate the threat by being


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


048
      robust to such inputs. This is often achieved by introducing training schemes that include adversarial
049
      examples, or by alterations of the inputs aiming to negate the adversarial perturbation. Alternatively,
050   in adversarial attack detection, the main model is used as-is, but a parallel model performs the binary
051   classification of the input as clean or attacked. This is done for instance by adding and training an
052   additional output head from one of the layers of the network, or via statistical models that consider
053   network activations or the final output layer. A short introduction to the main adversarial attack and
054   protection methods is given in Section (2).
055
      When considering the truly white-box threat model – that is, assuming that the attacker has complete
056   knowledge of the system – even near-perfect detection will not suffice. Knowing the structure and
057   parameters used both for the main classifier and for the detector model, the attacker need only run
058   many attack attempts on an offline copy of the system, and present the actual system only with inputs
059   that were already verified to be successful adversarial attacks. If the detector model is differentiable,
060   as is the case when adding an extra binary output head, the attacker can feasibly bypass the defence
061   even more directly, by adding the desired (negative) response of the detector model to the objective
062   when computing the perturbation for the adversarial attack.
063   Imagine however if we could have multiple detector networks, each providing a consistent and
064   acceptable detection level. During inference, we randomly choose one of these detectors to apply
065   to the input. This randomized strategy prevents users from crafting an input that could compromise
066   both the network and the detector, as they won’t know which detector will be selected. For this
067   method to be effective, we need: (i) a large pool of detectors to choose from, and (ii) detectors that
068   are not highly correlated, so that attacking one does not affect many others. Additionally, the entire
069   process must be computationally efficient to ensure practicality.
070   In this paper, we introduce the concept of Neural Fingerprints. A neural fingerprint consists of a
071   subset of neurons with a known distributions given a specific class. We demonstrate that grouping
072   just a few dozen neurons into a fingerprint can achieve considerable detection rates, and by using
073   many fingerprints together we can achieve near-perfect detection with a negligible false alarm rate.
074   Additionally, we present an efficient method to prepare a large bank of fingerprints that share very
075   few neurons, allowing for the selection of an uncorrelated random subset of fingerprints at test
076
      time. Our method is validated on the ImageNet dataset, where we systematically created adversarial
      attacks across classes. This extensive experimentation surpasses that of most studies in adversarial
077
      detection methods, suggesting the practical effectiveness and scalability of our method in real-world
078
      scenarios.
079
080   The intuition behind the use of neural fingerprints for adversarial attack detection relies on several
081   facts to hypothesize that such detectors would exist in many cases. First, from the lottery ticket
082
      hypothesis (Frankle & Carbin, 2018), we know that most neurons are not actively driving the clas-
      sification result. Moreover, the various adversarial attacks try to make as little change as possible,
083
      and hence will mostly change the value of the activations that do affect the classification. From this
084
      we conclude that most neurons in a random set of neurons will not be significantly impacted by
085   an adversarial attack. Finally, due to the way that networks are trained (e.g., small gradient steps,
086   dropout) many neurons that are not currently important for the classification do carry some infor-
087   mation about the class that was gathered during training. In total, we hypothesise that information
088   about the identity of the true class is distributed among a large population of neurons, most of which
089   are not highly influential in the classification output and thus they will not be targeted by adversarial
090   attacks. We attempt to extract and exploit this information in our detectors.
091   The main contribution of this paper is twofold. First, to the best of our knowledge this paper is the
092   first to address the insufficiency of deterministic adversarial attack detectors in the truly white-box
093   setting when the attacker is assumed to have full information of the methods used. Second, we
094   propose and demonstrate the Neural Fingerprint approach for the creation of large detector banks,
095   and application of randomized attack detection.

      In this section we briefly review the most common and effective methods to create adversarial im-

      ages, and the state of the art in protecting from such attacks.

      2.1   A DVERSARIAL ATTACKS
109
110   We begin by setting the stage for both attack and defence methods. We assume that the attacked
111   model f (·; θ) is a classifier that gets an image x and returns a probability vector ŷf (x) over a pre-
112   defined set of labels L. We will denote the output of the classifier by ĉf (x), namely the class for
113   which arg max ŷf (x) is obtained. A white-box attack is the setting in which the network parameters
114   θ are known to the attacker, whereas in a black-box attack the attacker has only oracle-access to the
115   model.
116   An adversarial attack is comprised of the following steps: First, a clean image x is selected with the
117   corresponding label c. In the targeted case, the attacker has a specific desired output c′ , whereas
118   in the untargeted case the objective of the adversary is simply to change the output to be anything
119   other than c. The adversary generates an altered image by introducing a minor perturbation η:
120
121                                                   x′ = x + η                                                (1)
122
123   Finally, the attack is deemed successful if the perturbation η is imperceptible for the human eye,
124   but the classification ĉf (x′ ) gives the desired output. That is, if ĉf (x′ ) = c′ in the targeted case, or
125   simply ĉf (x′ ) ̸= c in the untargeted case.
126   The perturbation η used to create the adversarial image x′ must be imperceptible to humans. This
127   constraint is typically operationlized by limiting the magnitude of the perturbation η under some
128   metric, to ensure that the difference between the original input x and the perturbed input x′ remains
129   below a certain threshold d. Often, this distance d is measured using an Lp norm so as to emphasize
130   pixels with large deviations.
131
      The most direct way to obtain an adversarial example is through gradient-based methods. This broad
132   category utilizes gradient information to determine the direction of perturbation that maximizes the
133   desired output of the model. Let l be a loss function for the model as a function of the input image.
134   For example, the standard cross entropy loss with respect to the desired target class c′ :
135
136
                                                l(x) = −log ŷf (x)[c′ ]                                        (2)
137
138   where ŷ[c′ ] is used to denote the c′ -th element of the output ŷ. The gradient −∇x l(x) gives the
139   direction of movement in pixel space needed to produce a targeted adversarial example. Essentially,
140   this is the same procedure as when training the model, except that the input and parameters switch
141   roles. During training, the training data is fixed and model parameters are updated according to the
142   gradient of the loss function with respect to the parameters, whereas when computing the adversarial
143   perturbation the parameters are fixed and the input image is updated according to the gradient with
      respect to the pixels. Most adversarial attack methods follow this logic either explicitly or via various


                                                            3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


144
      workarounds (which are required for instance when direct access to to the parameters, and hence the
145
      gradients, is not available).
146
147   The Fast Gradient Sign Method (Goodfellow et al., 2014) is a technique for generating adversarial
148   examples by conducting a single gradient step:
149
150                                         η = −α · sign (∇x l(x))                                      (3)
151
152   Where, α is a small constant controlling the magnitude of the perturbation, and sign(·) computes the
153
      sign elementwise. This technique has been found to reliably cause various neural network models
      to misclassify their input data.
154
155   Iterative FGSM (IFGSM) (Dong et al., 2018) applies the FGSM step multiple times within an L∞
156   bound ϵ on the total perturbation. That is, it repeats the following update:
157
158                                 xi+1 = Clipϵ (xi − α · sign(∇x l(x)))                                (4)
159
160   where Clipϵ (x) = min(max(x, x0 − ϵ), x0 + ϵ). The step size α is in this case typically smaller
161   than the total budget ϵ so that IFGSM can make multiple steps without exceeding the constraint.
162   The iterative application of FGSM allows IFGSM to account for gradient directions that may not be
163
      directly toward the decision boundary from the starting point. By accumulating these gradient steps,
      it can find adversarial examples that FGSM would not acheive in a single step. More generally,
164
      projected Gradient Descent (PGD) attacks (Madry et al., 2017) take multiple small gradient steps
165
      while projecting back onto the allowed perturbation set after each step. The different approaches in
166   this family differ mostly by the metric used in the projection.
167
168   Black box attacks mostly follow the same general logic, except that the direct computation of gra-
169
      dients is no longer possible. The Substitute Blackbox Attack (SBA) (Papernot et al., 2016) method
      starts by querying the model on a set of inputs and training a substitute model on this dataset. Next,
170
      a white-box attack is performed with the substitute model. Other methods use numeric estimates of
171
      gradients (Spall, 1992; Chen et al., 2017) which are plugged into the standard whitebox methods.

      2.2   D EFENCES
174
175   We now turn to discuss methods for defence against adversarial attacks. Broadly speaking, adver-
176   sarial defences are grouped into two main approaches: improving model robustness and detecting
177   adversarial inputs. Adversarially-robust methods aim to produce the correct output whether pre-
178   sented with clean or attacked inputs. Detection methods are applied in parallel (or prior to) the main
179   deep learning model, and aim to classify the input into clean versus attacked.
180   Adversarial training aims to improve robustness by incorporating adversarial examples into the train-
181   ing data. The model is trained on original examples plus versions of those examples perturbed with
182   adversarial attacks. This exposes the model to adversarial inputs during training. To generate these
183   adversarial examples, one can adopt various techniques such as the Fast Gradient Sign Method
184   (FGSM) (Goodfellow et al., 2015).
185
      One drawback of this method is that models trained in this manner remain susceptible to other forms
186   of adversarial examples not encountered during the training process. Another approach is the use
187   of a Barrage of Random Transforms (BaRT), as proposed in the study by Tramer et al. (Tramer
188   et al., 2017). The key idea behind BaRT is to combine dozens of individually weak defences into
189   a single strong defence that is robust to attacks. Specifically, BaRT applies many random image
190   transformations like color reduction, noise injection, and FFT perturbation to each input image
191   before classification. While each transform alone can be defeated, together they provide a boost in
      adversarial robustness.


                                                        4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


192
      A key consideration when applying adversarial training or other adversarial defences is the potential
193
      impact on accuracy of clean, unperturbed inputs. Hardening a model against adversarial attacks
194   requires some trade-off with performance on the original task. As Kurakin et al. (Kurakin et al.,
195   2016) found a moderate decrease in clean image accuracy when adversarial training was applied,
196   with more robust models generally exhibiting a larger drop. In general, adversarial training induces
197   some minor accuracy drop in order to gain improved robustness, typically around 1%, as reported
198   by Kurakin et al. (Kurakin et al., 2016).
199
      Adversarial detection defences aim to detect adversarial examples by training networks to distin-
200   guish between legitimate and adversarial inputs. A key advantage of adversarial classification is
201   that it can be applied to any pretrained model without needing to modify the model architecture or
202   training process. However, a core challenge is enabling the detector to generalize across diverse
203   perturbation types and datasets.
204
      A common approach for adversarial classification is to augment a classifier network f (x) with a
205
      detector network D(x) that outputs a prediction yadv or yclean indicating whether the input x is
206   adversarial or clean (Metzen et al., 2017). The detector network D(x) is trained on a dataset con-
207   taining a mix of clean examples from the original training data and adversarial examples specifically
208   crafted to try to evade detection by the network.
209
      Several works have proposed training feedforward neural networks as detector models. The detector
210
      network D(h(x)) typically takes the activations of an intermediate layer h of the classifier network
211
      f (x) as input rather than the raw input data (Metzen et al., 2017).

      3     T HE METHOD OF N EURAL F INGERPRINTS
216
217   In the pure white-box setting even a near-perfect deterministic adversarial attack detector is insuf-
218   ficient. By pure white-box we mean that the attacker has full knowledge and access to the model
219   and trained parameters, as well as the detector model that will be employed to detect the attack. By
220   deterministic we mean that the defence model is constant, so the attacker is able to check offline
221
      weather or not each adversarial input created will be detected. Consider for instance a near-perfect
      deterministic adversarial attack detector with a detection rate of 99.9%. The attacker generates a
222
      few thousand unrelated adversarial inputs, finds on average a few that pass the defence, and discards
223
      all others. Then, the online system is only fed these few inputs that are already known to fool the
224   defence.
225
226   What is needed for an effective defence in this case is a large family of detectors from which one
227
      (or more) is sampled at random in real time for each input. The size of the family should be large
      enough so that an attacker is not able to find inputs that fool them all (even if such inputs exist). This
228
      randomization assures us that the attacker is not able to work offline to find inputs that are known to
229
      fool the detector. For the defender on the other hand the computation needed to generate a sufficient
230   number of detectors must be feasible. Finally, the detectors should have adequately good detection
231   properties.
232

      Two final remaining element are the estimation of the density functions Pclean (FSi (x)|y),

297
      the average of many neuron activations from different parts of the network, and it stands to reason

299   sufficient to approximate the individual fingerprint density functions using a Gaussian approxima-
300   tion. This was verified empirically (See figure 1).

302
      For efficient computation of fingerprints we suggest the following preprocessing. For a specific class
      c, we begin with a set of m images from the class, and m′ random images of other classes, which
303
      underwent a targeted adversarial attack and are now classified as class c. All images are then fed
304
      through the model, and the N activations that are considered for fingerprint membership are stored
305   for each one. In total this produces a table of size N × m for the clean images, and N × m′ for the
306   attacked images. The remaining computation requires only these tables (the images and model are
307   no longer used).
308
      The procedure we use to generate effective fingerprints is based on sampling and filtering. At each
309
      step a fingerprint (that is k out of the total N activations in the tables) is sampled. Next, the pa-
310
      rameters (mean and variance) for the Gaussian approximation of the distribution of the fingerprint
311   value is computed for clean and attacked images. Finally, a Cohen’s d (Cohen, 2013) effect size is
312   calculated to determine the usefulness of the fingerprint in separating the clean and attacked inputs.
313   If the fingerprint’s effect size is above a pre-determined threshold it is added to the fingerprint bank.
314   We note that in the anomaly detection variant the filtering step is not possible, as we are only using
315   the clean images, and hence all fingerprints are used.

      4    R ELATED W ORK
319
320   Neural probing is a method originally developed to ascertain how suitable the representation in
321
      each layer of a deep neural network is for the purpose of the learned task (Alain & Bengio, 2016),
      and has since become a fundamental tool for understanding models in natural language processing
322
      (Belinkov, 2022). In this method, entire representations (hidden layers) are used as features for a
323
      predictor of some aspect of the input, and the success of the predictor is understood to measure how
324   well the aspect is encoded in the representation. Using this framework, each fingerprint in our work
325   can be described as a random sparse linear readout for the binary prediction of adversarial attacks.
326   The success of these predictors points to the idea that the presence of adversarial attacks is encoded
327   in the representation of the final layers of the model. However, clearly using a straightforward probe
328   from these layers is insufficient for protection, as an attacker could simply add this additional output
329   to the objective of the adversarial attack, or find inputs that are not detected via offline trial and error.
330   It is the combinatorical size of the fingerprint space as they are formulated here, that provides some
331   additional protection from straightforward workarounds (see Section 3).
332   Several methods have previously utilized hidden layer activations for detection of adversarial attacks
333   or for robust classification. For example, in (Zheng & Hong, 2018) entire hidden layers are modeled
334   using Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs), and an adversarial attack is declared if the likelihood of
335   an image in the fitted GMM is below a threshold. In (Feinman et al., 2017) a similar method is used
      based on a Kernel Density Estimate (KDE) model of the last hidden layer.

      (iter=40), and the step size (step size=0.01). Here also we stop whenever reaching at least 70%
407   confidence in the target class. For each iteration, 100, 000 fingerprints of size d = 50 were sampled,
408   and the top 20 were selected based on the training data.

410
      We first consider the individual fingerprints. Figure (1) shows an illustrative example of fingerprints
      generated for class toucan4 in the Inception V3 model and IFGSM attack setting. The general
411
      fingerprints sampled (left panel) mostly show high overlap in distribution between the clean and
412
      attacked images, with a few exceptions. When sampling based on effect size (Cohen’s d > 1) 3
413   (right panel) we are able to obtain fingerprints with high individual separating power. With these
414   in mind, it is easy to see how combining many random fingerprints of this sort (either by voting or
415   likelihood model) will result in good detection performance.
416
      The main results are presented as test data detection rate when setting the false detection rate to 1, 3
417
      or 5% (Table 1). First, detection rates are relatively high for all combinations of deep learning model,
418
      attack method, and detection rule, ranging from 93.6% to 99.9%. As expected, the likelihood ratio
419   detection rule offers the best overall performance from among the three tested approaches, followed
420   by the voting decision rule, with the anomaly detection approach trailing behind. When considering
421   the effect of number of fingerprints used for each input (Figure 2), we see a saturation of the detection
422   AUC at 20 − 40 fingerprints. Furthermore, the detection performance for the ViT model saturates
423   higher but later than for Inception V3, suggesting that slightly increasing the number of fingerprints
424   used in the main results Table (1) beyond 20 could be beneficial for ViT adversarial attack detection.
