011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Prior research shows that differences in the early period of neural network training
014            significantly impact the performance of in-distribution (ID) data of tasks. Yet, the
015            implications of early learning dynamics on out-of-distribution (OOD) generaliza-
016
               tion remain poorly understood, primarily due to the complexities and limitations
               of existing analytical techniques. In this work, we investigate the relationship
017
               between learning dynamics, OOD generalization under covariate shift and the early
018
               period of neural network training. We utilize the trace of Fisher Information and
019            sharpness, focusing on gradual unfreezing (i.e., progressively unfreezing param-
020            eters during training) as our methodology for investigation. Through a series of
021            empirical experiments, we show that 1) changing the number of trainable param-
022            eters during the early period of training via gradual unfreezing can significantly
023            improve OOD results; 2) the trace of Fisher Information and sharpness can be
024            used as indicators for the removal of gradual unfreezing during the early period of
025            training for better OOD generalization. Our experiments on both image and text
026            data show that the early period of training is a general phenomenon that can provide
027
               Pareto improvements in ID and OOD performance with minimal complexity. Our
               work represents a first step towards understanding how early learning dynamics
028
               affect neural network OOD generalization under covariate shift and suggests a new
029
               avenue to improve and study this problem.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Deep neural networks have achieved remarkable results on in-distribution (ID) data of a task they
035
      trained on but often performed poorly on out-of-distribution (OOD) data under input distribution
036   shifts. OOD performance is critical for real-world applications, such as training on clean images
037   or text but inferencing on noise-corrupted data (Hendrycks & Dietterich, 2019; Michel & Neubig,
038   2018), data obtained from different time periods (Lazaridou et al., 2021; Yao et al., 2022), across
039   languages or domains (Wang et al., 2021; Talman & Chatzikyriakidis, 2019; Liu et al., 2022; Koh
040   et al., 2021; Gulrajani & Lopez-Paz, 2021). Inadequate generalization to OOD settings is a key issue
041   limiting the robustness and reliability of these models.
042   Prior research observed that variations in the early period of training have a significant impact on
043   the model’s ID performance (Golatkar et al., 2019; Achille et al., 2019; Mosbach et al., 2021; Fort
044   et al., 2020) across scenarios including unimodal and multimodal settings when training from scratch,
045   performing parameter-efficient fine-tuning, or using federated learning. The observation of such a
046   period in diverse applications suggests that the early period of learning is generally important for
047   neural network training (Kleinman et al., 2024), drawing parallels to biological phenomena like the
048   critical learning period in animals (Achille et al., 2019; Kleinman et al., 2023).
049   In particular, intervening during the early period of training can significantly impact ID generalization
050   at the end of training. Training techniques, such as adjusting optimization hyperparameters (e.g.,
051   weight decay, learning rate, or dropout; Golatkar et al. 2019; Jastrzebski et al. 2021; Mosbach et al.
052   2021; Liu et al. 2023b), using data augmentation (Golatkar et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2023c), or adding
053   noise to weights (Frankle et al., 2020), impact learning dynamics early on and can significantly
      improve or degrade ID results depending on when they are applied or removed. Despite extensive

070   the early training period on OOD generalization remains unexplored.
071   In this work, we focus on the impact of the early period of training on OOD generalization, specifically
072   under the common input distribution shift (i.e., covariate shift, encompassing clean to noisy inputs,
073   language, and domain shifts, etc.). We conduct a series of empirical investigations to explore
074   this effect from a previously unexplored perspective – trainable parameters – by using gradual
075   unfreezing (Howard & Ruder, 2018) to intervene in the early period of training (Figure 1). This
076   method is a simple instance of broader training approaches with selective trainable parameters (Kumar
077   et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2023), proven effective in adaptation for OOD generalization. We investigate
078
      changes in commonly used metrics to study generalization, namely Fisher Information and loss
      sharpness (Jastrzebski et al., 2021; Foret et al., 2021; Kwon et al., 2021; Zheng et al., 2021) during
079
      the early period of training, exploring their roles in shaping OOD generalization. Through targeted
080
      case studies, we demonstrate how leveraging the dynamics of the early period in training can be an
081   effective strategy for “seizing” the moment for generalization across various scenarios.
082
083   To the best of our knowledge, we show for the first time that intervening through trainable parameters
084
      (i.e., gradual unfreezing) in the early period of training, can significantly enhance OOD generalization
      under covariate shift in various settings. Our results indicate that sharpness and Fisher Information
085
      metrics, though they may not be directly predictive of OOD generalization, can be used as indicators
086
      to optimize the timing of intervention removal for better OOD results. We validate this finding in
087   both vision and language tasks, showing its ability to achieve Pareto improvements with minimal
088   complexity. Our analysis and empirical evidence reveal new insights into how early learning dynamics
089   impact neural network generalization, particularly under covariate shift, and suggest new avenues for
090   studying OOD generalization.

      2      R ELATED W ORK
093
094
      Early period of neural network training. Under the standard usage of the term generalization (in-
095
      distribution, where training and testing data are assumed to be from the same distribution), prior work
096   (Golatkar et al., 2019; Achille et al., 2019) shows that the early period of training of neural networks
097   exhibits a “critical learning period” when trained from scratch. Regularization and interventions
098   applied in this critical period affect final task results.
099
      Jastrzebski et al. (2021) indicates that when learning with a lower learning rate, Fisher Information
100
      exhibits an “explosion” in the early period of training which impedes ID generalization. Applying
101
      regularization to the trace of Fisher Information alleviates the negative impact of the high Fisher
102   Information. Liu et al. (2023c) shows the termination of MixUp (Zhang et al., 2018) early in training
103   and switching to standard empirical risk minimization helps with better ID generalization. You et al.
104   (2020); Frankle et al. (2020) shows that even winning “lottery tickets” emerge in the early period of
105   training with large learning rates. The critical learning period is also found in many other settings,
106   such as in multimodal models (Kleinman et al., 2023), in linear models (Kleinman et al., 2024), in
107   transformers (Mosbach et al., 2021) and federated learning (Yan et al., 2022). However, these works
      only focus on ID generalization, neglecting the challenges of OOD generalization.

108
      Prior work (Yang et al., 2024; Qiu et al., 2024) investigates into how models adapt to spurious
109   correlations (which is a distinct form of OOD problem compared to the covariate shift examined in
110   our work). These studies focus on the early formation of distinct spurious features during training
111   and the mitigation strategies. However, the impact of trainable parameters (an increasingly important
112   area in light of recent advancements in parameter efficiency and dynamic architectures) remains
113   underexplored.
114
      Kumar et al. (2022); Lee et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2023a) find that training different parts of a model
115   at different times can alter learning dynamics and achieve better OOD results. Encouraged by
116   these findings, we use gradual unfreezing (Howard & Ruder, 2018, a very simple form of training
117   parts of a model at different times) as the main investigative tool in this paper. We focus on two
118   key advancements: 1) more general settings (e.g., training from scratch, fine-tuning), and 2) the
119   characterization of the early period of training and its relationship to OOD generalization.
120
      Fisher Information, sharpness and generalization. Fisher Information has been studied in many
121   prior works such as Chaudhari et al. (2017); Martens & Grosse (2015) to investigate and improve
122   optimization behaviour. Similarly, sharpness is another popular metric used to study optimization
123   behaviour and its relationship to generalization.
124
      Jastrzebski et al. (2017) found a correlation between sharpness and the ratio of learning rate to
125
      batch size, which impacts generalization. Jiang et al. (2020); Dziugaite & Roy (2017); Neyshabur
126
      et al. (2017) provide theoretical backing for generalization error using sharpness-related measures
127   and empirically show a correlation with generalization. While prior work believes that flatter (less
128   sharp) minima in the loss landscape lead to better generalization in neural networks (Hochreiter &
129   Schmidhuber, 1997; Keskar et al., 2017; Izmailov et al., 2018; Cha et al., 2021), there have been
130   debates on whether sharp minima (such as a high largest eigenvalue of the training Hessian, λmax )
131   imply poor generalization (Dinh et al., 2017) and demonstrate the limits of λmax in explaining ID
132   generalization (Kaur et al., 2023). Andriushchenko et al. (2023) demonstrate that adaptive sharpness
133   is an unreliable metric for OOD generalization in the final solution.
134   Current research primarily examines the loss landscape at convergence to understand ID generaliza-
135   tion. However, the role of Fisher information and sharpness metrics during early training and their
136   relationship to final OOD generalization remains unclear.

181   3.3       G RADUAL U NFREEZING
182
183
      Gradual unfreezing (Howard & Ruder, 2018) progressively increases the number of trainable parame-
      ters (i.e., unfreeze, layer-by-layer) of a neural network from the top to the bottom of a network at
184
      a fixed interval of training steps, k (i.e., the unfreezing interval). In this paper, we use a modified
185
      formulation of gradual unfreezing (Liu et al., 2023a), where we progressively unfreeze “blocks” of
186   parameters during the early period of training top-down (a block of parameters can range from a single
187   layer to several consecutive layers). In our experiments, we use the namespace of the parameters used
188   in standard implementations to determine blocks. See Appendix B for the algorithm. This method,
189   along with the top-down unfreezing, is chosen as the analysis tool due to its proven effectiveness in
190   achieving state-of-the-art performance across various transfer learning settings (Howard & Ruder,
191   2018; Kumar et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023a; Reinhardt et al., 2024).

      5.1       T IMING IS CRITICAL FOR REMOVING INTERVENTIONS
237
      Here, we investigate how unfreezing interval k affects ID and OOD results. For all experiments in this
238   section, the smallest k is 1 (unfreeze a block of parameters every one batch update) and the largest k
239   is determined by equally dividing the total training steps among all blocks (Howard & Ruder, 2018;
240   Raffel et al., 2022; Kumar et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2023). We show the relative change in the test
241   results (by subtracting the test results using gradual unfreezing to the results using standard training).
242
      Training from scratch, noise-corrupted input shift. Figure 2 shows the relative change in test
243
      results compared to standard training. Gradual unfreezing of trainable parameters significantly
244   impacts OOD generalization (i.e., noise-corrupted images) as early as after a single batch of data,
245   and this effect is particularly pronounced in simpler datasets like MNIST. Extending the unfreezing
246   interval during training initially has minimal impact on ID performance, but later leads to a significant
247   decline, especially at a faster rate with CIFAR. The observed deterioration in ID performance over
248   extended unfreezing intervals mirrors trends from early-stage training interventions and aligns with
249   previous findings (Golatkar et al., 2019; Achille et al., 2019) using other interventions.
250   The influence of gradual unfreezing on OOD results serves as evidence for the importance of early
251   training periods for OOD generalization. Notably, gradual unfreezing reveals a trade-off between
252   ID and OOD performance for CIFAR datasets, with a brief window where OOD results improve
253   before a sharp decline in ID performance. These results suggest that there may be a critical range
254   of k during early training where ID performance remains stable and OOD performance improves.
255   This time-sensitive observation persists over different learning rates (Figure 2(b)) and model depths
256   (Figure 7 in Appendix F).
257   Fine-tuning from a pre-trained model, domain shift. Figure 3(a) presents the relative change in
258   domain generalization performance when fine-tuning a pre-trained ViT on single source-domain data.
259   Consistent with previous observations on noise-corrupted input images, results on DomainNet and
260   Office-Home both exhibit a time-sensitive nature in parameter training. 2 Notably, there is also a
261   specific period during training where domain generalization results improve significantly (+2.72%
262   points in accuracy for DomainNet and +4.30% points for Office-Home) with minimal impact on ID
263   evaluation results (DomainNet).
264   PEFT with a pre-trained model, language shift. We continue to observe a consistent pattern in

320   shifts. Intervening during this period with gradual unfreezing leads to a time-sensitive trade-off
321   between OOD and ID performance. While factors like data quality or sophisticated learning objectives

      6.2   D O LEARNING DYNAMICS SIGNAL THE RIGHT TIME FOR INTERVENTION REMOVAL ?
433
434
435
436   Learning dynamics criteria for improved OOD generalization. Here, we investigate the learning
437   dynamics of ResNet18 on MNIST when training from scratch. We combined our findings in §5.1 and
438   §6.1 to arrive at the hypothesis that the optimal range of k for achieving the best overall ID and OOD
439   performance is after the initial rapid change of sharpness and tr(F) but not too long afterward. For
440   instance, the ID results deteriorated rapidly after 800-1000 training steps for the CIFAR datasets in
441   Figure 2, while OOD results were still improving.
442   This observation suggests that the optimal time to remove intervention (i.e., unfreeze parameters in
443   our case) while maintaining ID results (less than 0.5 points decrease in accuracy) and achieving better
444   OOD results should meet two specific criteria: 1) after the initial rapid change of sharpness or tr(F),
445   and 2) before the stabilization phase progresses too far.
446
      Criterion 2) is evident across all figures in our prior experiments in §5, as larger values of k consistently
447   degrade both ID and OOD performance. To assess the criterion 1), we focus on the MNIST dataset
448   and identify k̂ as the earliest ending step of the initial rapid-changing phase among the three metrics
449     ρ         ρ
      (Sworst  , Savg  and tr(F)). We then experiment with 10 different k values, spaced 10 steps apart,
450
      both less and greater than k̂. For k < k̂, we obtained median OOD (ID) accuracies of 52.72 (98.93).
451
      For k̂ < k, we obtained median OOD (ID) accuracies of 53.54 (98.91). This result helps to validate
452
      the first criterion since the median OOD accuracy is lower for k < k̂ with minimal change in ID
453
      accuracy. Together, this analysis suggests that the stabilization of metrics after the initial phase could
454
      be a useful signal to determine the optimal time to introduce new trainable parameters.
455
456   Hypothesis validation with a heuristic algorithm. To further validates our hypothesis, we use a
457   heuristic algorithm that satisfies the above-mentioned criteria to determine the stabilization time of
458
      the three metrics (we first detect a significant change in metrics, then detect the stabilization point
      of the metrics, the algorithm is given in Appendix E). The OOD results are then compared with ten
459
      random sampled k values per dataset to determine the winning rate (i.e., the percentage of times
460
      when the value picked by the algorithm is better than a randomly sampled value).
461
462   Training from scratch, noise-corrupted input shift. As shown in Table 1, using a heuristic algorithm

514   7    C ONCLUSIONS
515
516   In this work, we investigate the early period of training and its impact on OOD generalization
517   under covariate shift. We show that interventions by altering trainable parameters (i.e., progressively
518   changing the number of trainable parameters through gradual unfreezing) during the early period of
519   training improve OOD generalization. This is validated across various vision and language tasks,
520
      achieving Pareto improvements with minimal complexity. We emphasize the overlooked role of
      trainable parameters during the early period of training. Unlike prior work on ID generalization,
521
      we empirically observed that sharpness and tr(F) during the early period of training may not be
522
      indicative of the OOD generalization, but can be indicative of “when” to remove interventions.
523
524   In light of these findings, it is also essential to consider the broader context of the training strategy
525   studied in this work. The significance of methods that modify only parts of the final model, along with
526
      the growing focus on efficient training and fine-tuning — such as freezing parameters (e.g., Adapters,
      Houlsby et al. 2019; Pfeiffer et al. 2020; Hu et al. 2022) or dynamic architectures (Yoon et al., 2018;
527
      Evci et al., 2022; Gu et al., 2021) — cannot be overstated. Our findings contribute to a deeper
528
      understanding of the early period of training and OOD generalization, and suggest new research
529   directions, including the development of theoretical metrics to better predict OOD generalization.

      A      L IMITATIONS
919
920
      There are several limitations to our work. We empirically evaluate three model architectures, and
921   three types of covariate shift, and our selection may not encompass all possibilities and real-world
922   applications. However, we believe our insights have generalizable value. Our empirical observations
923   reveal correlations between changes in training dynamics and OOD generalization. Future research
924   could explore causal interventions to better understand and enhance this relationship.
925
      In our work, we utilize a hand-crafted algorithm to determine metric stabilization time, aiming to show
926
      that sharpness stabilization could indicate the time needed for the removal of interventions. However,
927   leveraging training dynamics during the early period of training requires additional computation
928   compared to standard training. While this is not a concern for our study, as our focus is on uncovering
929   insights, more efficient, theory-driven algorithms and metrics should be explored for future practical
930   applications.

       We hypothesize that the effectiveness of gradual unfreezing
1287
       on OOD generalization stems from implicitly regularizing learning from possible spurious fea-

1293

1294   gradual unfreezing. This result confirms that gradual unfreezing achieves better OOD results by
1295   implicitly regularizing spurious correlations.
