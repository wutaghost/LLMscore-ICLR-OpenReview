011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            While the rapid increase in the number of model parameters poses significant
014            benefits to the development of large language models (LLMs), computational
015
               costs are also raised. In order to tackle this difficulty, the sparse mixture-of-
               experts(SMoE) model was introduced to tackle LLM scaling by activating a subset
016
               of experts per input. Therefore, how to leverage the knowledge of multiple experts
017
               will be an important topic. Normally, in the most extreme scenario, employing a
018            balanced expert allocation system will result in a time-saving of n times compared
019            to utilizing only a single expert. Thus, in this paper we (1) systematically ana-
020            lyzed the performance and functionality of each expert. (2) Introduced a metric to
021            fill the blank of evaluating load balance for the sparse mixture-of-experts(SMoE)
022            model, based on the observation. (3) Proposed a dynamic plug-and-play strategy
023            that is both trainingless and near-lossless, effectively resolving the load balancing
024            problem, in contrast to previous works that focused on training strategies.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Large-scale language models (LLMs) have become a cornerstone for advancing natural language
029
      processing (NLP) tasks ranging from machine translation to mathematical reasoning, owing to their
030   numerous model parameters Wang et al. (2023) Yuan et al. (2023) Imani et al. (2023) Huang &
031   Chang (2022). In face of the computational cost caused by an increasing amount of model parame-
032   ters, sparse mixture-of-experts (SMoE) architectures Chen et al. (2023) Riquelme et al. (2021) Zhao
033   et al. (2023) have arosed significant attention due to their empirical success in scaling model capac-
034   ity efficiently. The core idea behind SMoE is its sparse routing strategy, which enables the model to
035   selectively activate a subset of experts (specialized sub-models) for each input. This selective activa-
036   tion mechanism allows SMoE to increase the overall model capacity without a proportional increase
037   in computational cost. As a result, SMoE is considered as an attractive solution for deploying LLMs
038
      and becomes widely adopted in the state-of-the-art LLMs Jiang et al. (2024); Dai et al. (2024); Bai
      et al. (2023).
039
040   Load Imbalance of SMoE. Despite these advantages, SMoE architectures face a critical challenge:
041   load imbalance among experts Zhou et al. (2022) Fedus et al. (2022), i.e., some experts are overbur-
042   dened with a disproportionate amount of work while others remain underutilized. This issue affects
043   the inference speed and resource utilization. Therefore, there are some current SMoE model focuses
044
      primarily on the routing mechanism’s adjustment during the training stage, but it’s unpredictable
      in the inference period Shazeer et al. (2017); Fedus et al. (2022); Lepikhin et al. (2020b); Zhou
045
      et al. (2022). Moreover, this imbalance issue will be more clearly demonstrated within a fixed time
046
      window in streaming scenarios, which are more common in real-world applications.
047
048   Load Imbalance Score. Previous works focused only on optimizing the loss of load balance during
049   SMoE model training, with the aim of enhancing the model’s overall performance. However, there is
050
      no specific metric target to evaluate the routing strategy in the SMoE model. Based on this situation,
      we propose a metric for evaluating this phenomenon inside the SMoE model.
051
052   Our Observation: Heavy-Hitter vs Important Experts in SMoE. During the inference period, we
053   can easily observe which experts are activated more than others, a phenomenon we called ”heavy-
      hitter experts.” However, are those experts the most important and unique to the task? Does the

102     gap in evaluating the SMoE model’s routing strategy.

126   optimising model architecture can be an effective method. For example, quantization Gong et al.

      In this section, we present our plug-and-play strategy for load balancing in sparse mixture-of-experts
139
      LLMs. We begin by demonstrating the difficulty of traditional learning-based load balancing strate-
140
      gies in the setting of pre-trained LLMs. Next, we demonstrate how to identify the heavy-hitter
141   experts and the least important experts in SMoE model. Then, we show how an expert’s workload
142   and importance shape our two perspectives of view. Finally, we introduce our proposed replicate

179
      tuning strategies: (1) Full Finetune: All the parameters in this model participate in the fine-tune,
      (2) Tune Expert: Only the experts’ weights should be tuned. (3) Tune Router: Only the router’s
180
      weights should be tuned. (4) Freeze Router 1ep: Only freeze the router weights in the first epoch,
181
      and then release them to finish the full fine-tuning. (5) Tune Both: Only tune the router and experts’
182   weights. (6) Tune Router 10ep: During each epoch, tune the router weights only in the 10% step
183   while freezing the remaining weights. For the remaining steps, finish fine-tuning the entire model.
184   (7) Freeze Router: In this fine-tuning strategy, we only freeze the router weights and tune all of the
185   others’ weights. (8) Tune Router 2nd: Before the last two epochs, we fully fine-tuned the model,
186   and in the last two epochs, we only adjusted the router weights, freezing the other weights.
187
      This table illustrates the performance of the fine-tuned model. Except for the ”Tune Router” strat-
188   egy, the others all have obvious improvements in accuracy, as demonstrated by the results. However,
189   when we used those fine-tuning strategies, almost all of the load imbalance scores (see Defini-
190   tion 3.1) for each dataset in each strategy increased. This suggests that despite having sufficient
191   computing resources and time to refine the model in various ways, achieving the load balance in
192   token allocation remains challenging.

194   3.2   T HE H EAVY-H ITTER E XPERT O RACLE IN SM O E
195
196   We argue that due to the load imbalance of SMoE. There exists a heavy-hitter expert. Formally, we
197   introduce the following oracle.
198   Oracle 3.2 (Heavy-Hitter Expert). Let M denote a sparse mixture-of-expert (SMoE) model with p
199   MoE blocks. In each SMoE block, there are m expert network modules. Each input token selects
200   k < m expert in each SMoE block for computation. Given a dataset, X with n tokens, the expert
201   j at a layer at MoE block i that has the maximum ni,j among block i is viewed as the heavy-hitter
202   expert in block i.
203
      Next, we describe how to identify heavy-hitters using input data. As shown in Algorithm 1, given an
204
      input set of tokens X, we keep track of the expert choices for each input x ∈ X. We then quantify
205
      an expert’s workload by counting the number of input tokens that have selected this expert. For each
206   MoE block, we designate the expert with the heaviest workload as the heavy-hitter for that block.
207   According to our study, we can select up to 10% of the tokens and estimate the heavy-hitter. As a
208   result, we can use Algorithm 1 to retrieve the heavy-hitter expert as suggested in Oracle 3.2.

469   5   C ONCLUSION
470
471   In conclusion, large language models with sparse mixture-of-experts (SMoE) architectures have
472   shown empirical success across various tasks. This architecture allows SMoEs to scale up the num-
473   ber of experts without the need for a proportional increase in computational resources, offering an
474   efficient way to improve performance on diverse tasks. However, despite these advantages, SMoE’s
475   sparse routing can lead to significant load imbalances among experts, some may become overloaded
476   with too many tasks while others remain underutilized, causing efficiency issues during deployment.
477   Our paper presents a plug-and-play strategy to address this load imbalance. We propose a low-cost
478
      method that identifies and replicates the heaviest expert using a lower-bit quantized version while
      also quantizing the least important expert to maintain the memory budget. We conducted thorough
479
      empirical evaluations to validate the effectiveness of this approach. The results indicate that our
480
      strategy successfully alleviates the load imbalance issue in SMoE architectures. Furthermore, the
481   R&Q strategy we used resulted in minimal loss of performance, making our method a practical and
482   efficient improvement for the deployment of SMoE models in large-scale systems.
