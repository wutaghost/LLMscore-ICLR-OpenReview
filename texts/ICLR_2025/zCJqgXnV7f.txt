009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               We study the problem of best-item identification from relative feedback where a
012
               learner adaptively plays subsets of items and receives stochastic feedback in the
013            form of the best item in the set. We propose an algorithm - Dynamic Elimination
014            (DE) - that dynamically prunes sub-optimal items from contention to efficiently
015            identify the best item and show a strong sample complexity upper bound for it. We
016            further formalize the notion of inferred updates to obtain estimates on item win
017            rates without directly playing them by leveraging item correlation information. We
018            propose the Dynamic Elimination by Correlation (DEBC) algorithm as an extension
019            to DE with inferred updates. We show through extensive experiments that DE and
020            DEBC vastly outperforms all existing baselines across multiple datasets in various
021
               settings.

024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026
027
      Learning to rank from feedback about a set of items is an important problem in machine learning with
      applications in many areas including sociology (Vieira et al., 2007; Zareie & Sheikhahmadi, 2018),
028
      information retrieval (Hofmann et al., 2013; Grotov & De Rijke, 2016; Guo et al., 2020), search
029
      engine optimization (Kakkar et al., 2015; Krrabaj et al., 2017), recommender systems (Balakrishnan
030   & Chopra, 2012; Tang & Wang, 2018; Bałchanowski & Boryczka, 2023), and, more recently, natural
031   language generation (Hofstätter et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023; Chuang et al., 2023). An important
032   sub-problem is learning to rank from relative feedback (Chen et al., 2018; Saha & Gopalan, 2019c;
033   Haddenhorst et al., 2021). In this setting, a set of items are played and stochastic relative feedback is
034   received in the form of the best item or a full or partial ranking of the items.
035
      We consider the problem where we play fixed-sized item subsets and receive relative feedback
036   modelled by the Plackett-Luce (PL) model with the aim of PAC-learning the best item. Existing
037   works in this setting (Saha & Gopalan, 2019a;b) including instance-optimal algorithms (Saha &
038   Gopalan, 2020b; Haddenhorst et al., 2021) typically evaluate a static item subset and retain only the
039   set winner before moving on to the next. However, subset plays are wasted on items in the subset that
040   are already known to be suboptimal before the set winner is determined. We investigate if flexible
041   item elimination is feasible to alleviate this inefficiency.
042   Furthermore, no assumption is usually made about the underlying feedback distribution beyond some
043   random utility model. However, we argue that information about the entities to be ranked (e.g. items
044   in recommender systems, documents in the information retrieval setting, nodes in a social network,
045   etc.) is often readily available. Motivated by this, we investigate the question: Given what we know
046   about items i, j and k, if item i is ranked above/below item k, how likely is it that item j is ranked
047   above/below item k?
048   Latent embedding models are commonly used in many domains, including natural language pro-
049   cessing (Pennington et al., 2014; Church, 2017), information retrieval (Zuccon et al., 2015; Palangi
050   et al., 2016) and recommender systems (Chen et al., 2019; Huang et al., 2020), to flexibly represent
051   unstructured information as vectors in a latent space such that the vectors of closely related items are
052   highly similar. We apply the latent embedding model to the PL model such that item latent scores
053   are given by query-item vector cosine similarity and aim to learn a PAC-best item from stochastic
      relative feedback. Our contributions are fourfold:

069   2    R ELATED W ORK
070
071   Reward maximization from sampling an unknown reward distribution has been extensively studied
072   in the classical multi-armed bandit setting where an absolute stochastic reward is observed (Even-
073   Dar et al., 2006; Scott, 2010; Agrawal & Goyal, 2012). This was extended to relative feedback
074   in the duelling bandit problem (Yue et al., 2012) which has been the object of a large body of
075   work (Dudík et al., 2015; Chen & Frazier, 2017; Jamieson et al., 2015), including extensions to
076   multiwise comparisons (Brost et al., 2016; Sui et al., 2017; Saha & Gopalan, 2019b). Beyond regret
077   minimization in the bandit setting, active arm ranking or learning of the best arm has been studied
078   both in the exact (Jamieson & Nowak, 2011; Maystre & Grossglauser, 2017; Ren et al., 2019; 2021)
079
      and PAC setting (Saha & Gopalan, 2019a; Agarwal et al., 2022). In particular, Saha & Gopalan
      (2019c) and Saha & Gopalan (2019a) present algorithms for obtaining the PAC best item and full
080
      ranking respectively under a PL model assumption with fixed sized subsets which is identical to our
081
      setting. (Saha & Gopalan, 2020b) and (Haddenhorst et al., 2021) propose instance optimal algorithms
082   which outperform the former in empirical trials. More recently, Yang & Feng (2023) proposed an
083   algorithm in a setting where subsets of variable size can be played.
084
085
      However, these algorithms often require up to millions of samples to rank only a few items. The
      inefficiency lies in statically evaluating a subset to determine the winner before moving on to a new
086
      subset. This means that a set containing two closely matched items can be "stuck" for many turns,
087
      wasting item subset plays on the other clearly suboptimal items in the subset. We propose dynamic
088   item elimination to solve this problem.
089
090   Furthermore, ranking algorithms typically do not leverage additional information about the underlying
091
      reward distribution to improve performance. The body of work in this area is surprisingly relatively
      small. Sui et al. (2017); Saha & Ghoshal (2022) consider arms with correlated rewards while (Gopalan
092
      et al., 2016) considers a contextual bandit setting where user preferences are latent mixtures of a set of
093
      reward distributions. While learning to rank items by assuming latent vector representations has been
094   widely studied across many domains (Balakrishnan & Chopra, 2012; Palangi et al., 2016; Zuccon
095   et al., 2015), it is very limited in this setting. To this end, (Chen & Frazier, 2016; Mesaoudi-Paul et al.,
096   2020) assume random utility models where the latent scores are derived from the item vectors and
097   an unknown context vector. Jamieson & Nowak (2011); Chen & Frazier (2016) suggest algorithms
098   for precise ranking based on pairwise feedback assuming a latent reward given by query vector-item
099   vector Euclidean distance. However, the algorithms are heavily reliant on complete knowledge of the
100   exact vector representations, which can be limiting in real-world scenarios. In comparison, we utilize
101   cosine similarity as a vector distance which is widely used across all machine learning domains and
102
      only require the item correlation matrix as an input instead of the exact item vectors.

131   4     E STIMATING PAIRWISE WIN RATIOS FROM RELATIVE FEEDBACK
132
133   A common approach to item-ranking with relative feedback is to employ rank breaking and maintain
134   a preference matrix that tracks the empirical win ratios, i.e. the rate at which an item is selected over
135   the other. In rank breaking, partial rankings are decomposed into pairwise comparisons and pairwise
136   win ratios are estimated independently (Saha & Gopalan, 2019c). The IIA property of the PL model
137   allows the use of rank breaking. We use the term empirical updates to refer to preference matrix
138   updates arising directly from user feedback as opposed to inferred updates which will be covered in
139
      Section 6.
140   Formally, let us denote the preference matrix at iteration t by P(t) ∈ Rn×n , and the number
141   of times an item i has won a set containing S as a subset as ni|S (t). Then, we have Pij (t) =
             ni|{i,j}(t)
142
      ni|{i,j} (t)+nj|{i,j} (t) . Given a sequence of sets that have been played by the learner up to timestep
143   t S(t) = {G(τ ) : τ = 1, 2, ..., t} and a sequence of winning items ι(t) = {iτ : τ = 1, 2, ..., t}, let
144   us consider for some item pair i, j the subsequence of winners ιij (t) = {1(iτ = i) : τ ∈ [1, t], iτ ∈
145   {i, j}} for which the winner is either i or j. As shown in (Saha & Gopalan, 2019c;b;a; Saha &
146   Gaillard, 2022), we can treat this binary subsequence as a sequence of iid Bernoulli random variables
147   with success parameter pij due to the IIA property. Consequently, Pij (t) is an unbiased estimator for
148   pij with bounded deviation according to Hoeffding’s Inequality.

      Dynamic item elimination Existing PAC algorithms typically play a set of items for a certain
217   number of rounds before keeping the winning item and eliminating the rest (Ailon et al., 2012; Ailon,
218   2012; Saha & Gopalan, 2019c; 2020a; Haddenhorst et al., 2021). In contrast, DE eliminates an item
219   once it is no longer a potential Condorcet winner (with high probability) and avoids the redundancy
220   of playing an item that is known to be sub-optimal. We show that introducing this flexibility improves
221   the worst case sample complexity (Theorem 1) and leads to vastly lower sample complexity in
222   practice (Section 8).
223
224   Running winner inheritance A challenge in accommodating flexible item elimination is that a
225   running winner can potentially be eliminated before items that have received updates from it can be
226   eliminated with certainty. This renders existing updates redundant since the items need to accumulate
227
      pairwise interactions with the new running winner. To avoid this, we allow the new running winner
      to inherit the pairwise interactions of previous running winners. We show in Lemma 10 that this
228
      constitutes a conservative estimate (i.e. the win ratio of the new running winner exceeds that implied
229
      by the inherited interactions with high probability).

347   7.1   A LGORITHM OVERVIEW
348
349   We propose Dynamic Elimination by Correlation (DEBC) as an extension to DE that takes in an
350   item vector correlation matrix as an input which it leverages for inferred updates (Section 6) to the
351   preference matrix as well as item selection. The complete algorithm is in Appendix D.2.
352
353   Item selection The main idea is to construct an initial set of items that are poorly correlated with
354   each other to yield higher conditional probabilities (given items i, j, k, Eqn. 1 shows that pjk|ik , pkj,ki
355   increases for some fixed vi · vj as vi/j · vk decreases) and to maximize inferred updates by covering
356   the largest possible item space. For the latter reason, we also select the item that is the most correlated
357   with other items as the first running winner. This concept is extended to the replacement of eliminated
358
      items - items that are least correlated to items that have already been played are selected. This allows
      DEBC to sweep the largest item space in the fewest number of plays.

707   B.1    F URTHER DISCUSSION ON COMBINING INFERRED AND EMPIRICAL UPDATES
708
709   As mentioned in Section 6.3, jointly considering empirical and inferred updates breaks the identically
710    distributed condition. More precisely, given that pjk is being estimated, empirical updates follow a
711
       Bernoulli distribution with mean pjk whereas inferred updates from the conditional probability pjk|ik
712
       follow a Bernoulli distribution with mean pik rescaled according to pdf(pik ) - an approximation for
       pdf(pjk ) given partial information. In fact, we can observe that the predictive posterior distribution
713
       is independent of the order in which the updates are applied and view the update sequence in 2 stages
714
      - applying all empirical updates in the first stage and inferred updates in the second. Then, each stage
715    is a valid Lévy process, and the posterior distribution from the first stage is supplied as the prior
716    distribution of the second stage.
717
718
      B.2    C OMBINING INFERRED UPDATES FROM MULTIPLE ITEMS
719
720
      Consequently, incorporating inferred updates from multiple items can be viewed as a multi-stage
721   update, where each item yields a sequence of iid. updates constituting a single stage. The sequence is
722   independent across all stages - each random variable is only dependent on the underlying distribution
723   it is drawn from. It is trivial to extend Theorem 3 to the multi-item case to show that the sample mean
724   across multiple stages is still an unbiased estimator for pij .
725
      However, in doing so, we are considering the evidence inferred from observations of other
726
      item pairs separately instead of jointly, i.e. given ιik and ιhk , the inferred updates are de-
727   rived using the first-order conditional  probabilities pjk|ik and pjk|hk instead of pjk|ik∩hk =
728
      Pq pjk > 12 | phk > 12 ∩ pik > 21 . While considering evidence from all item pairs jointly clearly

729   leads to an optimal estimate, computing higher-order probabilities is intractable.
730
731   We analyze the feasibility of only considering first-order conditional probabilities via two approaches.
732
      Firstly, we derive a lower bound on second order conditional probabilities (Lemma 3) and show that
      it only deviates slightly from the mean of the constituent first order conditional probabilities when
733
      the first order probabilities are close to 1 (Figure 4 (left)). Secondly, for higher order conditional
734
      probabilities, we perform Monte Carlo simulations to estimate the average multi-order conditional
735   probability given multiple constituent first-order conditional probabilities (Figure 4 (right)).
736
737   Both analyses show that taking the mean of the first order conditional probabilities by treating
738
      inferred updates from multiple items independently is a reasonably conservative estimate of the
      high-order conditional probability when the constituent first order probabilities are sufficiently high.
739
      We thus employ the heuristic of weighting the updates by their information content to assign higher
740
      importance to probabilities close to 1. Details are found in Appendix B.4.

775   B.4   A NALYSIS OF HIGH ORDER CONDITIONAL PROBABILITIES
776
777   As discussed in Appendix B.2, inferred updates from multiple items is viewed as a multi-stage
778   Bayesian update sequence, and Theorem 3 is used to show the validity of using the sample mean across

      D.1   DYNAMIC E LIMINATION (DE)
883
884
      The complete algorithm is given as Algorithm 1 with a subroutine given in Algorithm 2 for updating

886
887   Remarks The algorithm draws inspiration from Trace-the-Best in (Saha & Gopalan, 2019c) and
888   maintains a prevailing winner that we term the running winner that is at least pairwise ϵ-optimal to
889   all items that have been played so far. Each item pair is played for the required number of times to
890   establish the winner with sufficient certainty before it is removed permanently. However, Trace-the-
891   Best removes an entire set (except the winner) only when the set winner is established instead of
892   removing items once they are no longer potential winners. We improve on this and implement flexible
893   item elimination while achieving an improved worst case sample complexity. A crucial component
894   of this is running winner inheritance in which the incoming running winner inherits the pairwise
895
      interactions of the outgoing winner during running winner replacement. Additionally, while DE is
      a superior algorithm in its own right as we show in Section 8, its ability to dynamically eliminate
896
      items facilitates straightforward accommodation of inferred updates. Firstly, without dynamic item
897
      elimination, inferred updates can only be eliminated outside of set plays. Otherwise, once items are
898   added into a set, their previously accumulated inferred updates are redundant since they can only be
899   removed together with other items in the set. Secondly, the importance weighting of inferred updates
900   means that more inferred updates are required for item elimination. This means that a running winner
901   can potentially be replaced before the items that have accumulated inferred updates from it have been
902   eliminated. Consequently, these updates are redundant since those items will have to accumulate
903   updates with the new running winner. Running winner inheritance effectively solves this problem
904   with theoretical correctness guarantees.

1928   Trace-the-Best (TTB) and Divide-and-Battle (DAB) Both of these algorithms were proposed in
1929   (Saha & Gopalan, 2019c) for (ϵ, δ)-PAC best-item identification and thus directly applicable to our
1930   setting.
1931   TTB is based on randomly selecting item sets and maintaining a prevailing winner. Each set is played
1932   for the required number of rounds to determine the set winner before all losing items are eliminated
1933   from contention and a new set is selected from the remaining items to play  against the prevailing
1934   winner. The sample complexity is not instance-dependent and is O( ϵn2 ) ln nδ .
1935
       Like TTB, DAB similarly plays each set for a required number of times and eliminates all items except
1936   the winner. However, the sets are formed in a hierarchical fashion. It pre-divides the item set into
1937   subsets and plays each to obtain the winner, before dividing the winners into subsets and playing
1938   them against each other. The process is repeated until
                                                             only one winner remains. (Saha & Gopalan,
1939   2019c) proved an instance independent O( ϵn2 ) ln kδ sample complexity which is superior to that of
1940   TTB. However, when the constants are included, DAB has a significantly worse sample complexity
1941   than TTB.

2039   Discussion on the validity of inferred updates in DEBC While we see that DE and DKWT fulfil
2040
       the (ϵ, δ) PAC condition across all trials (in agreement with Theorem 1 which guarantees this for DE),
       DEBC fails to meet the (1 − δ) success rate in some experiments due to the probabilistic nature of the
2041
       inferred updates. While preliminary analysis about the reliability of inferred updates can be found
2042
       in Section 6, we leave more detailed analysis to future work. In particular, the results suggests that
2043   the reliability of inferred updates is dependent on the distribution of vectors in the datasets and their
2044   correlation characteristics. A more detailed study would ideally lead to methods to assign importance
2045   weights/thresholds to inferred updates in a dataset dependent manner. Nevertheless, we show that
2046   inferred updates in its current form can be directly used in scenarios where high accuracy is not the
2047   primary concern. In particular, we propose that inferred updates will be necessary in a sample-limited
2048   setting where the objective (ranking, best-item, etc.) has to be achieved with a limited number of
2049   samples.
