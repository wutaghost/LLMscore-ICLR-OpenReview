010                                               A BSTRACT
011
012            Despite the transformative impact of deep learning across multiple domains, the
013            inherent opacity of these models has driven the development of Explainable Artifi-
014            cial Intelligence (XAI). Among these efforts, Concept Bottleneck Models (CBMs)
015
               have emerged as a key approach to improve interpretability by leveraging high-
               level semantic information. However, CBMs, like other machine learning mod-
016
               els, are susceptible to security threats, particularly backdoor attacks, which can
017
               covertly manipulate model behaviors. Understanding that the community has not
018            yet studied the concept level backdoor attack of CBM, because of ”Better the devil
019            you know than the devil you don’t know.”, we introduce CAT (Concept-level
020            Backdoor ATtacks), a methodology that leverages the conceptual representations
021            within CBMs to embed triggers during training, enabling controlled manipulation
022            of model predictions at inference time. An enhanced attack pattern, CAT+, in-
023            corporates a correlation function to systematically select the most effective and
024            stealthy concept triggers, thereby optimizing the attack’s impact. Our compre-
025            hensive evaluation framework assesses both the attack success rate and stealthi-
026
               ness, demonstrating that CAT and CAT+ maintain high performance on clean data
               while achieving significant targeted effects on backdoored datasets. This work un-
027
               derscores the potential security risks associated with CBMs and provides a robust
028
               testing methodology for future security assessments.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
035
      In recent years, deep learning technologies have witnessed tremendous advancements, permeating
036   numerous fields from healthcare Kaul et al. (2022); Ahmad et al. (2018) to autonomous driving
037   Muhammad et al. (2020); Lai et al. (2024) and beyond. Despite the remarkable performance of
038   these models, their inherent black-box nature poses a significant challenge to transparency and in-
039   terpretability Hassija et al. (2024). As the reliance on these systems grows, so does the necessity for
040   understanding their decision-making processes.
041
      To address this limitation, Explainable Artificial Intelligence (XAI) Doshi-Velez & Kim (2017) has
042   emerged as a critical research area, aiming to make machine learning models more interpretable
043   and understandable to human users. XAI encompasses methods designed to provide insights into
044   how complex models arrive at their decisions, thereby fostering trust and enabling effective human
045   oversight. Among various XAI approaches, Concept Bottleneck Models (CBMs) Koh et al. (2020)
046   stand out as a promising methodology designed to enhance model transparency by capturing high-
047   level semantic information.
048   The pivotal component enabling CBMs to function effectively is the concept bottleneck layer, which
049   integrates human-interpretable concepts directly related to the prediction task. This design allows
050   human experts to perform targeted backward fine-tuning on incorrect predictions made by the model,
051   mitigating the blind spots commonly found in traditional DNNs when performing. CBMs represent
052   a practical and user-friendly model, particularly in risk-sensitive application domains such as au-
053   tonomous driving, digital finance, and intelligent healthcare. Even users with limited AI knowledge
      can leverage their domain expertise to effectively evaluate and fine-tune CBMs.

      Although CBMs offer strong interpretability and can be fine-tuned by human experts, they are still
069
      vulnerable to certain types of attacks Lv et al. (2023). Backdoor attacks which directly poison the
070
      training dataset, are typically difficult to detect. Moreover, the erroneous outputs caused by such
071   attacks can be highly perplexing to human experts, further complicating their mitigation.
072
073   Specifically, in image classification tasks, some existing image-level backdoor attacks have become
074
      relatively easy to detect and defend against, and we compare them to ours in Figure 1. Consequently,
      we shift our focus to the unique concept bottleneck layer of CBMs, we propose CAT: Concept-level
075
      Backdoor ATtacks. While users can inspect the concept layer and fine-tune it based on erroneous
076
      predictions, handling all the labels in the concept layer at once remains a challenging task. CAT
077   achieves stealthy attacks by modifying a small number of concept values within the concept layer,
078   embedding triggers among numerous concepts, and injecting them into the training dataset. In
079   addition, the concept layer manipulated by CAT is implicit in the use of CBMs, while users tend to
080   find the problems out in inputs, however, there’s no falsify on concept layer in CAT.
081
      The stealthiness of CAT is also reflected in the confounding nature of the dataset, which makes it
082
      difficult for existing backdoor detection methods to effectively defend against CAT. CBMs typically
083   integrate the attributes labeled in the training and testing datasets (we primarily use CUB and AwA)
084   into the concept layer of the model. However, the labeled attributes in any dataset are often problem-
085   atic, such as having a large number of attributes, incomplete or inaccurate annotations, and strong
086   subjectivity. Additionally, due to the sparsity in the concept layer, both humans and models will find
087   it difficult to discern whether seemingly ”incorrect” concepts have been distorted by a CAT, making
088   it challenging for existing backdoor defense mechanisms to address attacks.
089   The characteristics of CAT can be summarized as follows: Embed triggers during training to ma-
090   nipulate the model’s predictions when these triggers are encountered during inference, all while
091   maintaining a low probability of detection. This stealthiness is particularly pronounced in datasets
092   like CUB, which contain hundreds of concepts, making it difficult to identify the few that have been
093   tampered with. The stealthy nature of CAT has also been validated in evaluations involving hu-
094   man assessors (See in Appendix L.2). One AI researcher likened this type of attack to “pouring
095   Coca-Cola into Pepsi”.
096   Building upon CAT, we developed an enhanced version, CAT+, which introduces a function to
097   calculate the correlation between concepts and target classes. This allows for a step-by-step greedy
098   selection of the most effective and stealthy concept triggers, optimizing the backdoor attack’s impact.
099   CAT+ employs iterative poisoning to corrupt the training data, selecting a concept as the trigger
100   concept and determining its trigger operation in each poisoning step, thus iteratively updating and
101   corrupting the training data.
102   Our contributions are as follows:
103

      systematic exploration of concept-level backdoor attack, marking a significant milestone in the field

      of AI security.
106
107   (ii) Enhancing CAT to CAT+ with a more sophisticated trigger selection mechanism. CAT+
      introduces an iterative poisoning approach that systematically selects and updates concept triggers,

      2     R ELATED W ORK
115
116   Concept Bottleneck Models are a family of XAI techniques that enhance interpretability by em-
117   ploying high-level concepts as intermediate representations. CBMs encompass various forms: Orig-
118   inal CBMs Koh et al. (2020) prioritize interpretability through concept-based layers; Interactive
119   CBMs Chauhan et al. (2023) improve prediction accuracy in interactive scenarios with strategic
120   concept selection; Post-hoc CBMs (PCBMs) Yuksekgonul et al. (2022) integrate interpretability into
121   any neural network without performance loss; Label-free CBMs Oikarinen et al. (2023) enable unsu-
122
      pervised learning sans concept annotations while maintaining accuracy; and Hybrid CBMs Sawada
      & Nakamura (2022) combine both supervised and unsupervised concepts within self-explaining
123
      networks. Despite their interpretability and accuracy benefits, CBMs’ security, especially against
124
      backdoor attacks, remains an understudied area. Current research tends to focus on functionality
125   and interpretability, neglecting potential security vulnerabilities unique to CBMs’ reliance on high-
126   level concepts, necessitating a systematic examination of their resilience against backdoor threats.
127
128
      Backdoor Attacks in Machine Learning have emerged as a critical research domain, with exten-
      sive exploration of both attack vectors and countermeasures across diverse areas, such as Computer
129
      Vision (CV) Jha et al. (2023); Yu et al. (2023), Large Language Models and Natural Language Pro-
130
      cessing (NLP) tasks Wan et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2021), graph-based models Xu & Picek (2022);
131   Zhang et al. (2021), Reinforcement Learning (RL) Wang et al. (2021), diffusion models Chou et al.
132   (2024), and multimodal models Han et al. (2024). These attacks exploit hidden triggers embedded
133   in the training data or modifications to the model’s feature space or parameters to control model
134   predictions. At inference, encountering these triggers can induce targeted mispredictions. More-
135   over, the manipulation of the model’s internal state can lead to unintended behavior even without
136   the presence of explicit triggers. Despite the extensive research, backdoor attacks on CBMs remain
137   uncharted territory, leaving a gap in understanding and a lack of formal or provably effective defense
138   strategies for these interpretable models.

142   over a predefined concept set C = {c1 , . . . , cL } and a training dataset D = {(xi , ci , yi )}ni=1 , where
143   for each i ∈ [n], xi ∈ Rd denotes the feature vector, yi ∈ R represents the label of the class, and
144
      ci ∈ RL signifies the concept vector, with its k-th entry ck indicating the k-th concept in the vector.
      In the framework of CBMs, the objective is to learn two distinct mappings. The first mapping,
145
      denoted by g : Rd → RL , serves to transform the input feature space into the concept space. The
146
      second mapping, f : RL → R, operates on the concept space to produce predictions in the output
147   space. For any given input x, the model aims to generate a predicted concept vector ĉ = g(x) and a
148   final prediction ŷ = f (g(x)), such that both are as close as possible to their respective ground truth
149   values c and y. Let Lcj : R × R → R+ be the loss function measures the discrepancy between
150   the predicted and ground truth of j-th concept, and Ly : R × R be the loss function measures the
151   discrepancy between the predicted and truth targets. We consider joint bottleneck training, which
152   minimizes the weighted sum fˆ, ĝ = arg minf,g Σi [Ly (f (g(x(i) )); y (i) ) + Σj λLcj (g(x(i) ); c(i) )] for
153   some λ > 0

      4.2.1 T RIGGER C ONCEPTS S ELECTION AND O PTIMIZATION
192
193   In the training pipeline, we assume that the attacker has full access to the training dataset but is only
194   permitted to alter the data through a poisoning function, Te , with a certain injection rate. To obtain
195
      the poisoning function, we propose a two-step method to determine an optimal trigger, c̃, which
      considers both invisibility and effectiveness.
196
197   Concept Filter (Invisibility). Given a target class ytc , the first step is to search for the trigger
198   concepts under a specified trigger size |e|, where e = {ck1 , ck2 , · · · , ck|e| }. In this process, our
199   goal is to identify the concepts that are least relevant to ytc . By selecting concepts with minimal
200   relevance to the target class, the CAT attack becomes more covert, as the model is typically less
201
      sensitive to modifications in low-weight predictive concepts, making these alterations more difficult
      to detect. To achieve this, we first construct a subset, Dcache , from the training dataset, D. Let Dtc
202
      denote the subset containing all instances labeled with ytc . Then, we randomly select instances not
203
      labeled with ytc to form another subset, Dntc , such that |Dtc | = |Dntc |. Consequently, we obtain
204   Dcache = Dtc ∪ Dntc . We refer to instances from Dtc as positive instances and instances from
205   Dntc as negative instances. Next, we fit a regressor (e.g., logistic regression) on Dcache using only c
206   and y. The absolute values of the coefficients corresponding to each concept indicate the concept’s
207   importance in the final prediction of y (positive or negative). By doing so, we identify the concepts
208   with minimal relevance to the target class ytc , while also ensuring that these concepts have relatively
209   low relevance to the remaining classes.
210   Data-Driven Attack Pattern. (Effectiveness) In concept bottleneck model (CBM) tasks, many
211   datasets exhibit sparse concept activations at the bottleneck layer. Specifically, in a given concept
212   vector c, most concepts ck tend to be either predominantly positive (ck = 1) or predominantly neg-
213   ative (ck = 0). The degree of sparsity varies across datasets: some are skewed towards positive
214   activations, while others are skewed towards negative activations. We categorize datasets with a
215   higher proportion of positive activations as positive datasets, and those with more negative activa-
      tions as negative datasets. To attack positive datasets, we set the filtered concept vector c̃ to all

314   attack. The key idea is to iteratively select a concept and apply a poisoning operation to maximize
315   the impact on the target class during training. Let D denote the training dataset, and Pc be the set of
316   possible operations on a concept, which includes setting the concept to zero or one.
317   We define the set of candidate trigger concepts as c, and for each iteration, we choose a concept
318   cselect ∈ c and a poisoning operation Pselect ∈ Pc . The objective is to maximize the deviation in the
319   label distribution after applying the trigger. This is quantified by the function Z(D; cselect ; Pselect ),
320   which measures the change in the probability of the target class after the poisoning operation.
321

322
323   (i) Let n be the total number of training samples, and ntarget be the number of samples from the

363   6.2    E XPERIMENTAL R ESULTS AND A NALYSIS
364
365   Before delving into the details of our proposed attack experiments, it is crucial to clarify the distinc-
366   tion between the overall attack assumption and the evaluation setup used in this paper.
367   Attack Assumption: In a real-world scenario, the attacker does not have the capability to directly
368   manipulate the concept vector during testing time. Instead, the attack is designed to embed triggers
369   during the training phase. During inference, these triggers must be present in the input data to
370   manipulate the model’s behavior. To achieve this, we assume that there are methods, such as an
371   image-to-image model (e.g., Image2Trigger c), that can inject concept-level triggers into the input
372   images.
373   Evaluation Setup: For the purpose of evaluating the effectiveness and stealthiness of our attack,
374   we assume that the attacker can add triggers to the concept vector during testing. This setup allows
375   us to systematically assess the attack’s impact and the model’s response to backdoored data. It is
376   important to note that this evaluation setup is a controlled environment and does not fully reflect the
377   real-world constraints of the attack. In practice, the triggers would need to be embedded in the input
      images using techniques like Image2Trigger c, as discussed in the Section 7.

      7   L IMITATION AND A NALYSIS
487
488
      Our proposed concept-level backdoor attack, CAT, has demonstrated exceptional stealthiness and
489
      effectiveness in manipulating the predictions of CBMs. However, we acknowledge that our attack
490   has limitations and potential avenues for improvement.
491
492   One of the primary challenges in launching a successful concept-level backdoor attack lies in the
493
      difficulty of triggering the backdoor in the test phase. Unlike traditional backdoor attacks, where the
      trigger is injected into the input data during both training and testing phases, concept-level attacks
494
      require the trigger to be injected into the concept space during training. However, during testing,
495
      we can only input the image, without direct access to the concept space, but we can still achieve our
496   attack goals by mixing poisonous datasets embedded with triggers into the training set, without the
497   need to directly manipulate the concept layer.
498
499
      To address this issue, we propose a new problem definition: Image2Trigger c. The goal of Im-
      age2Trigger c is to develop an image-to-image model that can transform an input image into a new
500
      image that, when passed through the backbone model, produces a concept vector that has been suc-
501
      cessfully triggered. In other words, the model should be able to generate an image that, when fed
502   into the backbone, will produce a concept vector that has been manipulated to activate the backdoor.
503
504   Formally, we define the Image2Trigger c problem as follows:
505   Given an input image x, a backbone model g, and a target concept vector ctc , the goal is to find an
506   image-to-image model F that can generate an image x′ such that g(x′ ) = ctc where ctc is the target
507   concept vector that has been manipulated to activate the backdoor.
508
      To evaluate the performance of the Image2Trigger c model, we propose the following metrics:
509
510   1) Trigger Success Rate (TSR): The percentage of images that, when passed through the backbone
511   model, produce a concept vector that has been successfully triggered.
512   2) Image Similarity (IS): A measure of the similarity between the original image x and the gener-
513   ated image x′ . This metric is essential to ensure that the generated image is visually similar to the
514   original image, making it harder to detect.
515
      We believe that the Image2Trigger c problem is a crucial step towards developing more effective
516   and stealthy concept-level backdoor attacks. We hope that our work will inspire the community to
517   explore this new problem and develop innovative solutions.
518
519
      It should be stated that, our paper has introduced the concept of concept-level backdoor attacks,
      which have the potential to be more stealthy and effective than traditional backdoor attacks. How-
520
      ever, we acknowledge the limitations of our approach and propose a new problem definition, Im-
521
      age2Trigger c, to address the challenges of triggering the backdoor in the test phase. We hope that
522   our work will contribute to the advancement of the field and inspire further research in this area.
523
524   As we have demonstrated, concept-level backdoor attacks can be incredibly powerful and difficult to
525
      detect. However, we believe that there is still much to be explored in this area, and we hope that our
      work will serve as a catalyst for further research. The concept space is a rich and complex domain,
526
      full of hidden patterns and relationships waiting to be uncovered. We hope that our work will inspire
527
      the community to continue exploring this space and developing new, innovative attacks that can help
528   us better understand the vulnerabilities of machine learning models.

      The iterative poisoning strategy in CAT+ is grounded in the concept of maximizing the impact of the
867   backdoor trigger while maintaining stealthiness. To formalize this, we first introduce the concept
868   of information gain to quantify the change in the model’s understanding of the target class after
869   applying the trigger.
870
      Information Gain: The information gain I(cselect , Pselect ) is a measure of the additional informa-
871
      tion the model gains about the target class ytc when the concept cselect is perturbed using operation
872
      Pselect . It can be defined as the mutual information between the target class and the perturbed
873   concept, given by:
874
875
876
                              I(cselect , Pselect ) = H(ytc ) − H(ytc |cselect , Pselect ),                (28)
877
      where H(ytc ) is the entropy of the target class distribution and H(ytc |cselect , Pselect ) is the condi-
878   tional entropy of the target class given the perturbed concept.
879
880   Optimal Concept Selection: In each iteration, we aim to maximize the information gain to ensure
881
      that the trigger has the most significant impact on the model’s prediction. To achieve this, we define
      the information gain ratio as:
882

                                     R(cselect , Pselect ) =                          ,                    (29)
885                                                                  H(ytc )

      which represents the relative increase in information about the target class due to the perturbation.
887
888   Z-score Revisited: The Z-score Z(cselect , Pselect ) introduced earlier is closely related to the infor-
889   mation gain ratio. In fact, we can show that the Z-score is a monotonic function of the information
890
      gain ratio, such that a higher Z-score corresponds to a higher information gain ratio. This relation-
      ship allows us to use the Z-score as a proxy for selecting the optimal concept and operation in each
891

899
      the preprocessing steps outlined by Koh et al. (2020). They first applied majority voting to resolve
      concept disparities across instances from the same class, then selected attributes that appeared in at
