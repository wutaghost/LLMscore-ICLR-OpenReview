009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Modeling global geometric context while maintaining equivariance is crucial for
012            accurate predictions in many fields such as biology, chemistry, or when modeling
013            physical systems. Yet, this is challenging due to the computational demands of
014            processing high-dimensional data at scale. Existing approaches such as equivariant
015            self-attention or distance-based message passing, suffer from quadratic complexity
016            with respect to sequence length, while localized methods sacrifice global informa-
017            tion. Inspired by the recent success of state-space and long-convolutional models,
018
               in this work, we introduce the SE(3)-Hyena operator, the first equivariant network
               that adopts a long-convolutional framework for geometric systems. SE(3)-Hyena
019
               captures global geometric context at sub-quadratic complexity while maintaining
020
               equivariance to rotations and translations. Evaluated on the task of all-atom prop-
021            erty prediction of large RNA molecules, SE(3)-Hyena matches or outperforms
022            equivariant self-attention while requiring significantly less memory and compute
023            for long geometric sequences. Additionally, we propose equivariant associative
024            recall as a new mechanistic interpretability task for studying the contextual learning
025            capabilities of equivariant models. Notably, our model processes the geometric
026            context of 30k tokens 20× faster than the equivariant transformer and allows 72×
027            longer context within the same memory budget. The code will be released upon
028            the acceptance.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Modeling global geometric context while preserving equivariance is crucial in many real-world tasks.
      The properties of a protein depend on the global interaction of its residues (Baker & Sali, 2001).
034
      Similarly, the global geometry of DNA and RNA dictates their functional properties (Leontis &
035
      Westhof, 2001; Sato et al., 2021). In computer vision, modeling global geometric context is crucial
036   when working with point clouds or meshes (Thomas et al., 2018; De Haan et al., 2020). In all these
037   tasks, maintaining equivariance while capturing global context is essential for robust modeling and
038   prediction.
039
      Processing global geometric context with equivariance is challenging due to the computational
040
      demands of processing high-dimensional data at scale. Existing methods either rely on global all-
041
      to-all operators such as self-attention (Liao & Smidt, 2023; de Haan et al., 2024; Brehmer et al.,
042   2023), which do not scale well due to their quadratic complexity, or they restrict processing to local
043   neighborhoods (Thomas et al., 2018; Köhler et al., 2020; Fuchs et al., 2020), losing valuable global
044   information. This limitation is a significant practical bottleneck, necessitating more efficient solutions
045   for scalable equivariant modeling with a global geometric context.
046
      An efficient algorithm for modeling global context should support parallelization during training
047
      while maintaining bounded computational costs relative to sequence length during inference. One
048   approach involves recurrent operators (Orvieto et al., 2023; De et al., 2024), which provide bounded
049   compute but lack easy parallelization. Another family of methods relies on self-attention (Vaswani
050   et al., 2017) allowing parallel processing at the cost of quadratic computational complexity. The
051   most recent advances leverage state-space (Gu et al., 2021b; Fu et al., 2022; Gu & Dao, 2023) and
052   long-convolutional (Romero et al., 2021; Poli et al., 2023) frameworks, enabling global context
053   reasoning in sub-quadratic time with easy parallelization. Extending these models to accommodate
      equivariance remains an unexplored direction.

077   Inspired by the success of state-space and long-convolutional methods, in this work, we propose an
078   SE(3)-Hyena operator that efficiently models global geometric context in sub-quadratic time while
079
      preserving equivariance to rotations and translations. The core of our method is an equivariant vector
      long convolution that utilizes cross products between equivariant queries and keys, as shown in
080
      Figure 1. The vector long convolution is implemented in the Fourier domain, achieving O(N log N )
081
      computational complexity. We then demonstrate how to further extend the vector long convolution
082   to geometric long convolution by accommodating interaction between invariant and equivariant
083   subspaces to extract more complex geometric relations. The SE(3)-Hyena operator integrates
084   the geometric long convolution with equivariant projection layer, selective gating, and key-value
085   normalization that, as we demonstrate, is essential for stability.
086
      We evaluate SE(3)-Hyena against other local and global equivariant methods on the tasks of dynamical
087
      system modeling and all-atom large molecule property prediction. In addition, we test the proposed
088   model on the equivariant associative recall task, a geometric counterpart of mechanistic interpretability
089   inductive heads problem (Olsson et al., 2022) that we introduce to study contextual learning
090   capabilities of equivariant models. Our results suggest that SE(3)-Hyena outperforms local methods
091   and performs on par with other global methods while requiring significantly less memory and compute
092   for long sequences. In particular, for a sequence of 30k tokens, the equivariant Hyena runs 20×
093   faster than equivariant self-attention methods. Notably, when the equivariant transformer runs out of
094   memory on sequences over 37k tokens, our model can handle up to 2.7M million tokens on a single
095   GPU, providing up to 72× longer context length within the same computational budget.
096   To sum up, we make the following contributions:
097
098          • We propose the SE(3)-equivariant Hyena operator which enables modeling global geometric
099            context in sub-quadratic time. Through the ablation study, we identify several key insights
100            necessary to scale the model for large-scale problems.
101
102
             • We demonstrate that SE(3)-Hyena outperforms local equivariant methods and matches or
               outperforms equivariant self-attention on several large-scale molecular property prediction
103
               and dynamical system modeling tasks while requiring significantly less memory and compute
104
               for long sequences.
105
106          • We propose a novel equivariant associative recall task as a geometric variant of the mecha-
107            nistic interpretability of equivariant models.

      The SE(3)-Hyena operator is designed for tasks that require modeling invariant and equivariant
133
      features in geometric graphs. A geometric graph of N nodes is represented by a set of features

174   2.2   G EOMETRIC LONG CONVOLUTION
175
176
      Geometric long convolution serves as the global context aggregation module in the SE(3)-Hyena
      operator, analogous to the self-attention mechanism in transformer architectures. Its primary function
177
      is to encode global relations between query and key tokens. The geometric long convolution comprises
178
      two key components: scalar long convolution that accommodates global relations between scalar
179   invariant tokens and equivariant vector long convolution that handles global relations between vector
180   tokens. The scalar and vector convolutions can be used as is for global context aggregation, or as
181   we demonstrate, they can be combined into the geometric long convolution enabling the interaction
182   between invariant and equivariant subspaces.
183

251   2.3   S ELECTIVE GATING
252
253
      The SE(3)-Hyena model incorporates a selective gating mechanism to dynamically control infor-
      mation flow, similar to the softmax operation in self-attention. This mechanism enables the model
254
      to emphasize relevant tokens while suppressing less informative ones. The gating is implemented
255
      through the gating function γ : R3 × Rd → [0, 1] that predicts soft masking value between 0 and
256   1 for each token. Practically, we implement the gating employing a projection layer (Section 2.1)
257   followed by a linear layer Rd → R with sigmoid activation. The gating function emits a masking
258   value mi for i-th token in a sequence. The masking is applied to the output of the geometric long
259   convolution as mi uinv
                          i   and mi ueqv
                                       i  for scalar and vector tokens.
260
      Finally, the resulting gated tokens are then integrated with value tokens viinv and vieqv using the
261
      element-wise product for scalar tokens and cross product for vector tokens. The SE(3)-Hyena operator
262
      is thus composed of input projection, QKV-projection, geometric long convolution, and gating. We
263   provide the ablation study on SE(3)-Hyena operations in Appendix 6.4.

306   Key-Value normalization Key-Value normalization is crucial for maintaining numerical stability
307   in the SE(3)-Hyena operator. To understand its necessity, let M be the maximum magnitude of
308
      an element in the query q, key k, or value v tokens. With this, the output magnitude after the
      long convolution can be bounded as ∥u∥2 ≤ ∥q∥2 ∥k∥2 ≤ M 2 . When further multiplied with the
309
      values, the final output magnitude is bounded as ∥y∥2 ≤ ∥u∥2 ∥v∥2 ≤ M 3 . This cubic growth with
310
      respect to input magnitudes leads to numerical instability and data type overflows in the SE(3)-Hyena
311   operator. Consequently, training with this naive implementation requires extremely small learning
312   rates and gradient clipping, resulting in very slow convergence. To address this issue, we employ
313   key-value normalization, ensuring unit norm for keys and values: ∥k∥2 = ∥v∥2 = 1. This bounds
314   the output magnitude as ∥y∥2 ≤ ∥q∥2 ≤ M removing the unstable cubic bound. In practice, we
315   observed using key-value normalization to be a critical step to ensure convergence and numerical
316   stability of the SE(3)-Hyena operator.

      To adapt this for geometric contexts, we modify the standard associative recall to accommodate 3D
344
      vectors. In this version, the tokens within a bigram (key and value) relate to each other by a rotation
345   matrix. The model processes a sequence of N vector tokens concluding with a query token and must
346   predict a vector associated with this query seen earlier in the sequence, as illustrated in Figure 4. The
347   task ensures that rotating the entire sequence affects only the orientation of the predicted vector, not
348   the key-value relationship within a bigram, thereby making the task equivariant to rotations. The
349   task complexity depends on the number of tokens in a sequence and on vocabulary size where the
350   vocabulary items are unique key-value vector bigrams.
351   We propose two versions of the equivariant associative recall task: random and fixed vocabulary
352   versions. In the random vocabulary version, sequences are sampled from a randomly generated
353   vocabulary at each training iteration while validation and test sets are fixed. Thus, an only way for a
354   model to solve this task is to learn an equivariant retrieval mechanism that can associate a given query
355   with a corresponding value vector. In the fixed vocabulary version, the vocabulary is fixed and shared
356   among training, test and validation sets, and the sequences are randomly rotated during the training.
357   This way, the fixed version tests the model’s ability to learn underlying vocabulary and generalize it
358   for various orientations.

360   Implementation details We evaluate models on sequences of 3D vector tokens. Sequences lengths
361
      vary from 27 to 210 vector tokens. We use 2600 sequences for training and 200 sequences for each
      test and validation. We compare equivariant models that permit global context: Vector Neuron
362
      Transformer VNT (Deng et al., 2021; Assaad et al., 2022), Equiformer (Liao & Smidt, 2023), SE(3)-
363
      transformer (Appendix 6.7) and proposed SE(3)-Hyena. We use 3 layers for all models. We use
364   hidden dimensions of 80 for SE(3)-Hyena, and for other methods, the hidden dimension is set to
365   equate a number of parameters across the models. All models are trained for 400 epochs using Adam
366   optimizer. Detailed setup is in Appendix 6.3. Note that we run Equiformer only on sequence lengths
367   27 and 28 as it runs out of memory on longer contexts.

      4.2 A LL - ATOM RNA PROPERTY PREDICTION
406
407   We evaluate SE(3)-Hyena on large molecule molecule property prediction, focusing on RNA
408   molecules that come with rotational and translational symmetries of atom coordinates. This ap-
409
      plication is crucial given RNA’s rapidly growing therapeutic importance, where accurate in-silico
      modeling can significantly reduce costly and time-consuming in-vitro/in-vivo measurements. RNA
410
      molecules present unique challenges: their high dimensionality (hundreds of nucleotides, thousands
411
      of atoms) and the lack of established backbone extraction methods often necessitate processing large-
412   scale all-atom systems. In addition, RNA molecules form complex secondary and tertiary structures,
413   with properties influenced by both short- and long-range structural interactions. Consequently, an
414   effective model must combine equivariance, multi-scale context processing, and scalability to handle
415   these intricate, high-dimensional structures.
416
      Datasets We use two real-world RNA datasets: Open Vaccine Covid-19 (Rhiju Das, 2020) and
417
      Ribonanza-2k (He et al., 2024). Open Vaccine (3000 sequences) provides stability profiles (reactivity,
418
      degradation at pH10, and degradation at Mg pH10) at various nucleotides, and contains sequences
419   up to 7800 nodes in all-atom resolution. Ribonanza-2k (2260 sequences) offers reactivity profiles for
420   DMS and 2A3 chemical modifiers (Low & Weeks, 2010). These profiles are crucial for understanding
421   RNA behavior in different environments and essential for RNA-based therapeutics design. The
422   Ribinanza-2k dataset contains mRNA sequences of up to 11300 atoms. We generate 3D structures
423   using RhoFold (Shen et al., 2022), and we focus on all-atom and on eta-theta pseudotorsional
424   backbone (Wadley et al., 2007) representations. Detailed dataset preparation is in Appendix 6.3.
425

      Results Table 1 shows average and individual label RMSE for each dataset and property. For
467
      backbone representation models, we did not observe any major differences between evaluated local
468   context models, with EGNN and LEFTNet slightly outperforming the rest of the local context models,
469   but still significantly underperformed compared to global context methods. This indicates the crucial
470   role of global geometric context. Also, we observed that EGNN performed on par or slightly better
471   than its modified version FastEGNN which relies on virtual nodes. We hypothesize this may step
472   from the center of mass initialization of virtual nodes in FastEGNN that may be suboptimal for
473   molecules with a strong sequence prior such as RNA, and more advanced virtual node initialization
474   strategies may alleviate this issue.
475   Among global context methods, SE(3)-Hyena performs the best, considerably outperforming the
476   second-best Vector Neuron Transformer on both datasets. We hypothesize that such significant
477   improvement is due to the mechanism of alternating global and local context used in the SE(3)-Hyena
478   operator. Our ablation study (Appendix 6.4) further confirms the critical role of alternating global and
479   local context in the SE(3)-Hyena operator. In addition, long convolutions are naturally well suited for
480   molecules with a strong sequence prior as they inherently discriminate different token orderings due
481   to the permutation symmetry breaking of a convolution.
482   In the all-atom representation case, the SE(3)-Hyena demonstrates its key advantage of improved
483   computational efficiency, being the only global context method that does not run out of memory on
484   large RNA atomic systems. Notably, for Open Vaccine datasets, the all-atom representation performs
485   considerably better than if only the backbone is used. This further highlights the necessity of all-atom
      representation for accurate in-silico modeling of biological systems.

503     4.3                 RUNTIME AND MEMORY BENCHMARKS
504
505    We benchmark the runtime and peak memory consumption for a forward pass of SE(3)-Hyena
506    against other equivariant global context models: Vector Neuron Transformer (Assaad et al., 2022),
507    Equiformer (Liao & Smidt, 2023) and SE(3)-Transformer (Appendix 6.7). Similar to Dao et al.
508
       (2022); Poli et al. (2023), we use sample sequences, and we test a one-layer model for the runtime
       and peak memory consumption. We record all runtimes on NVIDIA A10G GPU with CUDA 12.2.
509
510    The comparison is reported in Figure 6, showing forward pass time in milliseconds and peak GPU
511    memory utilization in megabytes. SE(3)-Hyena easily scales to longer sequences whereas VNT,
512    Equiformer, and SE(3)-Transformer are from 20× to 120× slower for a sequence length of 30k
513    tokens. Regarding memory usage, our SE(3)-Hyena requires 50× less GPU memory than self-
514
       attention-based methods for sequence length of 30k tokens. Moreover, we observed that when the
       Vector Neuron Transformer runs out of memory on > 37k tokens, our model supports up to 2.7M
515
       tokens on a single GPU allowing for 72× longer geometric context. This memory efficiency is
516
       attributed to the FFT long convolution that avoids materializing a quadratic self-attention matrix.
517
518     5                 C ONCLUSIONS
519
520    We introduced the SE(3)-Hyena operator being, to the best of our knowledge, the first equivariant
521    long-convolutional model with sub-quadratic complexity for global geometric context. Through
522
       experiments on the dynamical system, novel equivariant associative recall task and all-atom RNA
       property prediction, we demonstrated that equivariant long-convolutional models can perform com-
523
       petitively to the equivariant self-attention while requiring a fraction of the computational and memory
524
       cost of transformers for long context modeling. Our scalable equivariant model efficiently captures
525    the global context, highlighting its potential for a multitude of future applications in various domains.
526
527    Limitations and Future work This work introduces a novel approach for equivariant modeling
528    of global geometric context at scale, with initial experiments designed to validate the fundamental
529    principles of our method. While these experiments confirm the key advantages of our approach, they
530    represent the first step of a comprehensive experimental analysis that is necessary to uncover the
531
       model’s capabilities across a wider range of real-world tasks. On a technical side, an interesting
       direction for future improvement is to adapt the vector convolution to function across arbitrary dimen-
532
       sions as it currently relies on a cross product, which is only feasible in 3 and 7 dimensions (Massey,
533
       1983). Also, our method relies on the FFT convolution that is not permutation equivariant. While
534    this is not a significant limitation in domains such as molecular systems, where canonical ordering
535    is typically available (Jochum & Gasteiger, 1977), it becomes critical in fields like point cloud
536    processing, where establishing a canonical order is challenging. Enhancing the long-convolutional
537    framework to incorporate permutation equivariance or learning this geometric constraint from the
538    data could offer substantial advantages in these areas. Also, as the first step, in this work, we have
539    only considered EGNN-like equivariant network as a projection function. Using more advanced
       equivariant models as the projection layer could further improve the performance.

      6.1   R ELATED WORK
813
814   Equivariance Equivariance to group transformations, particularly rotations and translations in 3D,
815   is crucial for modeling physical systems Zhang et al. (2023). Schütt et al. (2017) condition continuous
816   convolutional filters on relative distances to build model invariant to rotations and translations.
817   Thomas et al. (2018); Fuchs et al. (2020); Brandstetter et al. (2021); Liao & Smidt (2023); Bekkers
818   et al. (2023) utilize spherical harmonics as a steerable basis which enables equivariance between
819   higher-order representations. Since computing spherical harmonics can be expensive, Jing et al.
820
      (2021b;a); Satorras et al. (2021); Deng et al. (2021) focus on directly updating vector-valued features
      to maintain equivariance, while Zhdanov et al. (2024a) employ another equivariant network to
821
      implicitly parameterize steerable kernels. Another recent line of work Ruhe et al. (2023a;b); Brehmer
822
      et al. (2023); Zhdanov et al. (2024b) employs geometric algebra representation which natively
823   provides a flexible framework for processing symmetries in the data Dorst et al. (2009).
824
825   While these works focus on how to build equivariance into a neural network, in this paper we focus
826
      on efficient equivariance to model global geometric contexts at scale.
827
828   Modeling geometric context Various strategies are employed to process context information in
829
      geometric data. Convolutional methods aggregate context linearly within a local neighborhood, guided
      by either a graph topology Kipf & Welling (2016) or spatial relations in geometric graphs Schütt
830
      et al. (2017); Wu et al. (2019); Thomas et al. (2018). Message-passing framework Gilmer et al.
831
      (2017) generalizes convolutions, facilitating the exchange of nonlinear messages between nodes
832   with learnable message functions. These approaches are favored for their simplicity, balanced
833   computational demands, and expressiveness Wu et al. (2020). However, they are limited to local
834   interactions and are known to suffer from oversmoothing Rusch et al. (2023). This hinders building
835   deep message-passing networks capable of encompassing a global geometric context in a receptive
836   field. To address these limitations, a recent line of work focuses on architectural modifications in
837   form of virtual nodes (Zhang et al., 2024) or using node-to-mesh message passing (Wang et al.,
838   2024b) to enhance the capability of a model to handle long-range interactions. Being effective in a
839   certain scenarios, these methods are constrained by their use of low-rank approximations for global
840
      interactions, limiting their expressivity when the interactions do to adhere to low-rank behavior.
      Another line of work adopts self-attention mechanisms for graph Yang et al. (2021); Kreuzer et al.
841
      (2021); Kim et al. (2022); Rampášek et al. (2022) and geometric graph Fuchs et al. (2020); Liao
842
      & Smidt (2023); Brehmer et al. (2023) data, outperforming convolutional and message-passing
843   approaches. Yet, the quadratic computational cost of self-attention poses significant challenges when
844   modeling large-scale physical systems. Along the same lines,
845
846
      In this work, we aim to develop a method for global geometric context processing with sub-quadratic
      computational complexity.
847
848
849
      State-space and long-convolutional models The quadratic computational complexity of self-
      attention has driven the exploration of alternatives for modeling long context. Structured state-
850
      space models Gu et al. (2021b) have emerged as a promising alternative, integrating recurrent and
851
      convolutional mechanisms within a single framework. These models enable parallelized training in a
852   convolutional mode and maintain linear complexity with respect to sequence length in a recurrent
853   mode. Models like S4 Gu et al. (2021a), H3 Fu et al. (2022), and Mamba Gu & Dao (2023); Li
854   et al. (2024) have consistently matched or exceeded transformer performance in diverse tasks such as
855   genomics Schiff et al. (2024), long-range language Wang et al. (2024a), and vision tasks Zhu et al.
856   (2024). Concurrently, another line of work integrates long-convolutional framework with implicit
857   filters Sitzmann et al. (2020); Romero et al. (2021); Zhdanov et al. (2024a) to capture global sequence
858   context. The implicit filter formulation allows for data-controlled filtering similar to transformers,
859   while FFT-based long convolution enables global context aggregation in sub-quadratic time Poli et al.
860
      (2023). Such models have shown competitive performance comparable to state-space and transformer
      architectures in time-series modeling Romero et al. (2021), genomics Nguyen et al. (2024), and
861
      vision tasks Poli et al. (2023).
862
863   Although state-space and long-convolutional methods dramatically reduced the computational costs
      associated with processing long sequences, their application to geometric data requiring equivariance


                                                        16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      remains unexplored. In this work, we adapt the recently proposed Hyena operator Poli et al. (2023)
865   to incorporate SE(3) equivariance. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first equivariant long-
866   convolutional model that can process global geometric contexts with sub-quadratic memory and time
867   requirements.

       6.6   E XTENSION TO HIGHER - ORDER REPRESENTATION
1120
1121   To further encourage the research towards equivariant long-convolutional models for geometric
1122   systems, we outline a blueprint of the potential extension of SE(3)-Hyena-like architecture to ac-
1123   commodate higher-order representations. We consider two possibilities: (i) employing spherical
1124   harmonics-based steerable representation with the tensor product, and (ii) interaction with higher-
1125   order features by scalarization trick Satorras et al. (2021); Cen et al. (2024); Anonymous (2024a).
1126   Extending the SE(3)-Hyena to higher-order steerable features requires working out steerable vector
1127   long convolution. Conceptually, the same mathematical approach can be applied but instead of
1128   vectors in and cross product, we will have steerable vectors and tensor product. Then, the long
1129   convolution can be built with tensor product and reduced to a series of FFT scalar convolutions by
1130   factoring out CG coefficients. This is the overall blueprint of one of the possible approaches. In this
1131   approach, various types of steerable vectors will interact via standard or parametrized tensor product.
1132

1171   framework by introducing higher-order components into the feature representations while simplifying
1172   computations by selectively considering interactions.
1173

1174

1175

1176   two such feature tuples, we drop the higher-order to higher-order interaction terms. With this, we
1177   can focus on interactions that involve higher-order to low-order order and low-order to low-order
1178   interactions only, effectively scalarizing the processing of higher-order features.
1179
       By excluding higher-order to higher-order interactions, we significantly reduce computational com-
1180
       plexity. The higher-order features still contribute to the model through their interactions with scalar
1181
       parts, allowing the network to capture important geometric information without the full computational
1182   overhead associated with processing all higher-order interactions.

1234        Since SE(3)-Hyena utilizes long convolutions based on cross products, we design cross product
1235        equivariant self-attention to align the global geometric context aggregation for Hyena and transformer
1236        models for more direct comparison. With this, we use a similar architecture to SE(3)-Hyena (i.e.
1237        input projection, QKV-projection, output-projection) but instead of long-convolution, we utilize cross
