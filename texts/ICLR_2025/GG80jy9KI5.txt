                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
               In this paper we introduce a method for improving the signal to noise ratio of fi-
010
               nancial data. The approach relies on combining a target variable with different
011
               context variables and using auto-encoders (AEs) to learn reconstructions of the
012            combined inputs. The idea is to seek agreement among multiple AEs which are
013            trained on related but different inputs for which they are forced to find common
014            ground. The training process is set up as a conversation where models take turns at
015            producing a prediction (speaking) or reconciling own predictions with the output
016            of the other AE (listening), until an agreement is reached. This leads to “mutual
017            regularization” among the AEs. Unlike standard regularization which relies on
018            including a complexity penalty into the loss function, the proposed method uses
019            the partner network to detect and amend the lack of generality in the data repre-
020
               sentation. As only true regularities can be agreed upon by the AEs, the replication
               of noise is costly and will therefore be avoided.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
025
      Financial data is characterized by an extremely poor signal-to-noise ration and hence poor pre-
026
      dictability AQR (2024). Even while some logical explanation of market moves may be given ex-post,
027
      future prices appear to be largely random especially when considering short (¡ one year) investment
028   horizons. Financial markets are social systems in which multiple mechanisms continuously compete
029   for the attention of investors. It is common to think of the market as operating in different “regimes”
030   each having its own set of rules on how real-world observations (news) are converted into prices.
031   Auto-encoders (AEs) seem to be well suited to extract and represent these rules. Their design is
032   guided by the manifold hypothesis which states that many datasets of practical interest concentrate
033   around a low-dimensional manifold Meilă & Zhang (2023). The geometry of the manifold reflects
034   structural constraints obeyed by the data which, in turn, are due to some mechanism that prepares the
035   data. AEs project inputs onto the manifold in the encoding stage and “lift” points on the manifold
036
      back into the high–dimensional input space during the decoding stage.
037   The key is to make the encoder as insensitive to variations of the input as possible. Only variations
038   that give rise to a new input pattern (instead of being a noisy version of a given pattern) must be
039   encoded. These variations define the tangent directions of the manifold. Learning is unsupervised
040   so the difference between signal and noise is not known but determined by the auto-encoder or, more
041   precisely, its (in-)sensitivity to inputs. Insensitivity to input data is typically achieved by placing a
042
      bottleneck layer at the end of the encoding stage such that the code dimension is lower than the
      input dimension (undercomplete auto-encoder). Alternatively (or in addition), a regularization term
043
      is added to the loss function which limits the complexity of the code e.g., by encouraging zeros in the
044
      encoding layer. Yet another method pursues local insensitivity to input variations by penalizing the
045   derivative of the encoding function with respect to inputs Rifai et al. (2011). None of these methods
046   incorporate an explicit statement of the generalization capability of the model. They all approach
047   the problem indirectly by preventing the auto-encoder to represent arbitrary data sets.
048
049   C OMMON UNDERSTANDING REQUIRES GENERALITY
050
051   In this paper we approach the regularization problem in a different way. Every training step is
052   followed by a reconciliation step in which two networks try and understand each other. The idea
053   is that common understanding can only be obtained if the acquired knowledge (about the data) is
      general enough. If two AEs disagree on the representation of data they will be forced to alter the

054
      way they encode the data so that different aspects are extracted on which an agreement is possible.
055   The agreement is measured in terms of the recurrence of combined encoding patterns: two networks
056   are said to agree if a given encoding keeps appearing in conjunction with the same encoding by
057   the partner AE. The idea is that this only happens if both networks extract essential features from
058   the input data which itself keep reappearing. If spurious features are encoded then the probability of
059   recurrence of a specific pair of encodings is low. This means that the agreement level (AL) quantifies
060   the generality of the representation.
061   The principal idea in this paper is to have two networks create two differing vantage points from
062   which the data is examined. This is realized by a) choosing heterogeneous architectures i.e., net-
063   works that differ in the number and width of layers or activation functions and b) by combining
064   a target variable with different context variables i.e., to provide the inputs (y, x1 ) and (y, x2 ) to
065   the networks as shown in figure 1. x1 and x2 are different but related signals which help create
066   alternative perspectives on y. Agreement can only be achieved on an abstract level in the sense that
067   the networks agree on the occurrence of data configurations to which they assign codes that can be
068   translated between the networks using a stable dictionary. In the context of finance, these config-
069
      urations are often referred to as market regimes. The advantage over a single auto-encoder of all
      available signals (y, x1 , x2 ) is that our set-up allows the AEs to develop independent perspectives
070
      which are subsequently aligned. This retains the focus on the target y which would otherwise be
071
      dominated by the context variables when minimizing the reconstruction loss.

074   2   R ELATED W ORK
075
076
      The de-noising problem is well-known in finance and has traditionally been addressed using linear
077
      filter methods (such as moving averages, Bollinger bands, Kalman filtering). In a recent paper Liu
078   & Cheng (2024), mode decomposition and a wavelet-thresholding method are employed to classify
079   stock price movements. Other methods employ a generative model to reproduce known stylized
080   features of market prices e.g., based on Langevin dynamics Wang & Ventre (2024). The paper
081   by Bao et al. (2017) presents stacked autoencoders for hierarchically extracting deep features in
082   stock prices which are used as an input to an LSTM to forecast next day’s closing prices. The
083   estimation procedure in this paper is comparatively simpler and consists in a pair (or more) parallel
084   auto-encoders. The novelty here is that the AEs mutually regularize their learned representations.
085   As stated in the introduction, regularization has been an integral part of the development of autoen-
086   coders. Without it, they would merely produce a literal copy of the input without extracting valuable
087   features from data or be able to reduce the dimensionality of its representation. Classic approaches
088   involve L1 and L2 constraints on the network weights or robust training techniques like dropout
089   Baldi & Sadowski (2013) that avoid over-dependence on particular activation patterns. This paper
090   takes an interactive approach which similar in spirit to adversarial regularization Makhzani et al.
091   (2015), Zhao et al. (2018). The adversary sub-network encourages the main network to learn an
092
      unbiased representation. This has been shown to yield undesirable side-effects, including unstable
      gradients and reduced performance on in-domain examples Grand & Belinkov (2019). By contrast,
093
      our setting is a collaborative one in which the encoder outputs of two networks are compared given
094
      the same input data. Our idea is to reconcile these outputs in terms of the synchronicity of their
095   appearance.

098   3   R EGULARIZATION
099
100
      Autoencoders project the input data on a low dimensional manifold M whose coordinates are given
101
      by the codes z in the hidden layer. They are typically designed to be undercomplete, i.e. the
102   dimension of their encoder outputs is much smaller than the input dimension. This prevents them
103   from learning the identity function. Because of the bottleneck, some aspects of the input have to be
104   grouped into larger components from which the input is reconstructed while a literal reproduction
105   is avoided. These components represent the features that define the objects contained in the data.
106   However, the relationship between architectural constraints and simplification is quite loose and is
107   typically determined empirically. In this paper, we propose a scheme in which the simplification is
      the result of a negotiation among two networks.

127   The networks only have weak bottlenecks and may produce over-fitted predictions. The key point is
128   that the models are different in the sense that predictions are obtained by decoding two different sets
129   of codes z. If the networks assign codes to different perceived regularities in the data they can only
130   agree on their (posterior) distribution p(z|x) if the different codes repeatedly co-occur. If a code has
131
      been assigned to noise, the probability of its repeated co-occurrence with a code generated by the
      other network is very low. On codes for which an agreement on p(z|x) is possible on the other hand
132
      must refer to a true regularities in the data.
133
134   The more the two networks differ the more effective they are as mutual regularizers. To achieve
135   a different encoding behavior the networks can be of different complexity (e.g., by the number
136   and width of layers). Also, independent noise sources n1 , n2 can be added (as shown in figure 1)
137
      whose role is to encourage the networks to search in different directions. Notice that the noise is
      applied at the input and output during the training of the AE (unlike the denoising setup). (Very
138
      loosely speaking,) it can be seen as an additional source of entropy which helps explore the space of
139
      possible code words. The objective is to find a common denominator with the other network, which
140   may lie outside of the code regions explored by the networks individually. By mutual agreement we
141   expect not only the noise to be rejected, but also the predictions to be more standardized as they are
142   reproductions of the input data which are decoded from an agreed-upon posterior.

145   3.1   T RAINING S ETUP
146
147   The training algorithm simultaneously improves the data fit and the mutual understanding on how
148   to achieve it. Training is organized as a conversation among two AEs. The networks take turns at
149   talk and either speak (S) or listen (L) to the other network. The S/L phases are asynchronous as in
150   natural conversations. Translation layers T1 and T2 are placed between the two AEs to convert to
151   and from the encoder outputs z1 and z2 . In view of the desired dissimilarity the encoding layers may
152   have different output dimensions e.g., dimz1 > dimz2 (without loss of generality). The loop starts
153
      with a speak action (S) by one of the networks, say AE1 . Utterances are produced by sampling from
      a multivariate Gaussian with mean µ1 (x) using the familiar re-parametrization trick

258   4   R EGULARITIES IN FINANCIAL TIME - SERIES
259
260   Financial markets are in general very efficient at converting new information into prices of traded
261
      securities. This means that changes in prices are essentially driven by the random arrival of new
      information. As a consequence, ’noise’ dominates the evolution of financial time-series. Regular-
262
      ities arise when market participants irrationally disregard available information or have difficulties
263
      interpreting it. The regularity represents a market inefficiency which becomes a source of risk-free
264   return (alpha) if exploited by a trading strategy. For example, the prices of two related commodi-
265   ties may temporarily be out of sync which creates a price spread that eventually has to disappear
266   because the more expensive commodity may be substituted by the cheaper one creating demand for
267   the latter. Most systematic trading strategies available today are handcrafted, i.e. they start with an
268   inefficiency which is underpinned by an economic theory, e.g. behavioral finance. It is reasonable
269   to expect that many more regularities exist in financial times years which however are hidden be-
      hind high levels of noise. An auto-encoder with strong denoising capabilities opens the door to a

      tural constraint improve by adding the mutual regularization constraint. In particular we are inter-
310
      ested in emergent patterns among the context variables once the AEs interact with each other. The
311
      experimental setup consists in a target variable y being combined with different context variables
312   xi , i = 1, 2, . . . , N . The variables refer financial time-series where y corresponds to the returns
313   of a target market (to be traded) and xi to environment (e.g., macroeconomic) data. The returns of
314   the target market are shifted forward h time-steps during the training phase. The motivation behind
315   this set-up is that if a relationship between today’s environment and the h-steps forward returns of a
316   target market is found, then xi provides a trading signal for y. In such a case, h corresponds to the
317   investment horizon of the trading strategy based on xi .
318   For every pair (y, xi ), an autoencoder AEi is trained which produces a denoised version (y ′ , x′i )
319   of the input. The idea is that after denoising the relationship between xi and y is more stable
320   and apparent. The objective is to learn “typical” evolutions of xi that precede market up– or down–
321   moves. These typical evolutions are obtained by clustering (e.g., by standard K-means) the denoised
322   x′i conditional on y > 0 (up-market) or y < 0 (down-market). The evolutions are truncated at
323   a length that corresponds to the “depth” dimension of the convolution layers (32 timesteps in this
      case, see figure 4). The clustered patterns form a library of signals that can be used out-of-sample

                 The paper discusses collaborative regularization where two networks compare notes in the form of
473
                 encodings generated upon seeing the same input data. A useful analogy is to think of the codes as
474              words in different languages denoting the same object. The expressions might have different con-
475              notations which however have to be neglected in order make an agreement on their usage possible.
476              This implies a degree of standardization in what can be predicted using agreed-upon codes. The
477              codes correspond to recurring features in the input data, which are therefore also the defining ele-
478              ments of the objects contained in the data. Every input data point gives rise to a code and if this code
479              is of the “agreed-upon” type, it means that the data point is representative of the object or pattern to
480              be identified. This conclusion can be reached in an entirely unsupervised fashion. In some sense,
481              the networks mutually supervise themselves by trying to match their use of encodings. Agreed-upon
482
                 codes define equivalence classes of inputs (one for each code).
483              The idea opens up a to a vast strategy space which has yet to unfold entirely as more and more
484              stable relationships are identified and traded. This will contribute to making financial markets more
485              complete by providing liquidity and putting a price on more assets in more states of the world. Many
                 active strategies are based on well-known (stylized) market inefficiencies or they operate within

      significant constraints (e.g., within a tracking error limit vs. a benchmark). If all market participants
487   search along a similar dimension, alpha capture becomes a zero-sum game. A market with more
488   heterogeneous trade positions can make all participants better off –according to their own criteria–
489   without having to make someone else worse off (Pareto optimality with differential preferences).
