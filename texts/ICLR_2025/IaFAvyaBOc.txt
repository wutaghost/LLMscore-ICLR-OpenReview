009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011              Non-iid data is prevalent in real-world federated learning problems. Data hetero-
012              geneity can come in different types in terms of distribution shifts. In this work, we
013              are interested in the heterogeneity that comes from concept shifts, i.e., shifts in the
014              prediction across clients. In particular, we consider multi-task learning, where we
015              want the model to adapt to the task of the client. We propose a parameter-efficient
016              framework to tackle this issue, where each client learns to mix between parameter-
017
                 efficient adaptors according to its task. We use Low-Rank Adaptors (LoRAs) as the
                 backbone and extend its concept to other types of layers. We call our framework
018
                 Federated Low-Rank Adaptive Learning (FLoRAL). This framework is not an
019
                 algorithm but rather a model parameterization for a multi-task learning objective,
020              so it can work on top of any algorithm that optimizes this objective, which includes
021              many algorithms from the literature. FLoRAL is memory-efficient, and clients
022              are personalized with small states (e.g., one number per adaptor) as the adaptors
023              themselves are federated. Hence, personalization is–in this sense–federated as
024              well. Even though clients can personalize more freely by training an adaptor
025              locally, we show that collaborative and efficient training of adaptors is possible
026              and performs better. We also show that FLoRAL can outperform an ensemble
027              of full models with optimal cluster assignment, which demonstrates the benefits
028
                 of federated personalization and the robustness of FLoRAL to overfitting. We
                 show promising experimental results on synthetic datasets, real-world federated
029
                 multi-task problems such as MNIST, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100. We also provide
030
                 a theoretical analysis of local SGD on a relaxed objective and discuss the effects of
031              aggregation mismatch on convergence.1

      of as 1) statistical in terms of data (e.g., shifts in distributions) or structural in terms of model (e.g.,
055   structured differences in subsets of parameters). To learn these differences efficiently, we often
056   assume that they are low-complexity differences.

058
      Most approaches maintain that the personalized models are either close in distance to the global
      model via proximal regularization (Li et al., 2021a; Sadiev et al., 2022; Beznosikov et al., 2021) or
059
      meta-learning (Fallah et al., 2020), or that the personalized models belong to a cluster of models
060
      (Marfoq et al., 2021; Werner et al., 2023). Other approaches also assume model heterogeneity, where
061   clients might have a local subset of parameters that are not averaged (Pillutla et al., 2022; Mishchenko
062   et al., 2023) where it can personalize to the local task by construction (Almansoori et al., 2024). For
063   example, a specific subset of parameters can be chosen to be the last layer or some added adaptors.
064
      It has been shown that fine-tuning works particularly well for personalization (Cheng et al., 2021).
065
      One well-known example is using Low-Rank Adaptors (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021) for personalizing
066
      large language models to different tasks, where the fine-tuning is done on additive low-rank matrices.
067   Thus, the personalized models differ from the base model only in low-rank matrices. Inspired by
068   the efficiency of low-rank adaptors in multi-task learning for language models and the idea that fine-
069   tuning changes parameters along a low-dimensional intrinsic subspace (Li et al., 2018; Aghajanyan
070   et al., 2020), we use low-rank adaptors in the FL setting and show that they can offer significant
071   improvements with a relatively small memory budget.
072
      Thus, instead of regularizing the complexity of a personalized model by its proximity to a reference
073   solution or clustering full models, we explicitly parameterize the personalized models as having
074   low-rank differences from the global model. This is done by introducing a small number of low-
075   rank adaptors per layer and a mixture vector per client that mixes between those adaptors. Thus,
076   it implicitly regularizes the personal models through a weight-sharing mechanism that enforces a
077   low-rank difference from the global model. Our approach can be seen as a hybrid that 1) explicitly
078   constrains the complexity of the difference (per layer) between the global model and the personalized
079   model and 2) casts the problem of learning these differences as a multi-task learning problem via
080   the local mixture vectors. The main benefit of this approach is that low-rank adaptors can also be
081
      federated, i.e., collaboratively learned, and the number of local personalization parameters per client
      is minimal. This means our approach can be efficiently employed in the cross-device setting as well.

106   Multi-task Learning Our problem can be seen as a multi-task learning problem in which the
107   solutions share a base model. The closest work to ours in this respect is FedEM (Marfoq et al.,
      2021), which works by assigning to each client a personalized mixture vector that mixes between


                                                           2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      a small number of full models such that each model solves one task. FedEM then proceeds with
109   an algorithm based on expectation maximization. One problem is that their approach assumes that
110   the full models should be mixed. In contrast, we assume that the mixed components are only the
111   adaptors, which constitute a small fraction of the model and are thus much more efficient in terms
112   of memory. Other related works on clustering include IFCA (Ghosh et al., 2020), FedSoft (Li et al.,
113   2022), and Federated-Clustering (Werner et al., 2023). The main difference from our work is that we
114   only cluster a small component of the whole model, allowing the clients to benefit from having a
115   shared base model that is learned among all the clients.
116
117   Personalization Another approach to personalization is by introducing a proximal regularizer with
118
      respect to a reference model. Ditto (Li et al., 2021a) is a stateful algorithm that trains the local models
      by solving a proximal objective with respect to a reference model. The reference model is the FedAvg
119
      solution, which is attained concurrently by solving the non-regularized objective. Meta-learning
120
      approaches, inspired by Finn et al. (2017), can extend naturally to personalization. For example,
121   Fallah et al. (2020) propose to solve a local objective that is an approximate solution after one local
122   gradient step. Meta-learning also assumes that the local solutions are close to the FedAvg solution
123   as they mimic fine-tuning from the FedAvg solution in some sense. In our approach, we do not
124   assume that the clients are stateful nor that the FedAvg solution is meaningful or close to any of the
125   local solutions. We assume that the local models can benefit from collaboration but still allow for
126   personalization via different mixtures, which is much more memory efficient and can be managed by
127   the server.
128
129   LoRA Using mixture of LoRAs in FL is not new due to their popularity. The idea of mixing LoRAs
130   has been explored recently (Wu et al., 2024b) for language models. SLoRA (Babakniya et al., 2023)
131
      focuses on parameter-efficient fine-tuning after federated training and thus does not federate the
      adaptors. Both FedLoRA (Wu et al., 2024a) and pFedLoRA (Yi et al., 2024) assume that the LoRAs
132
      are not federated as well, where they both also introduce a specific two-stage algorithm to train those
133
      LoRAs. The federated mixture of experts (Reisser et al., 2021) trains an ensemble of specialized
134   models, but they specialize in input rather than prediction. FedJETs (Dun et al., 2023) uses whole
135   models as experts in addition to a pre-trained feature aggregator as a common expert that helps the
136   client choose the right expert. Other works explore mixture of LoRAs (Zhu et al., 2023; Yang et al.,
137   2024) for adaptation but in a different, non-collaborative context.
138
139   Representation Learning Other successful approaches in FL work by feature, prototype, or
140   representation aggregation (Tan et al., 2022; Nguyen et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023), which makes
141   them orthogonal to our work.

      3.2   PARAMETER -E FFICIENT A DAPTORS
219
220   Linear layer This LoRA was introduced in (Hu et al., 2021). Let W ∈ Rdout ×din be the base
221   linear layer. We introduce a low-rank adaptive layer L := UV⊤ with rank r, where U ∈ Rm×r and
222   V ∈ Rn×r . We initialize L such that U is random (or initialized similarly to W) and V is zero. The
223   low-rank adaptive layer W̃ is
224
225                                      W̃ := W + L = W + UV⊤ .                                         (1)
226
227   Relative parameter budget It is easy to see that the number of parameters in a linear LoRA is
228   (m + n)r, which can be much smaller than mn for small r. We can have a constraint on the number
229
      of parameters relative to the model size, i.e., (m + n)r ≤ ρmn, where ρ > 0 is the relative parameter
      budget per adaptor (e.g., ρ = 0.01 for a maximum of 1% increase in model size per adaptor). Given
230
      a specific ρ based on system capabilities, r can be automatically set to be the maximum such that
231
      r ≤ ρmn/(m + n), or just r = ⌊ρmn/(m + n)⌋. Note how r attains its largest values when m = n,
232   where the equation simply becomes r ≤ ρn/2. We hereafter refer to ρ as the budget (per adaptor),
233   and set it to either 1% or 10% in the experiments. Note that for certain models, it is impossible to
234   satisfy the budget if ρmn/(m + n) < 1, so we enforce a minimum rank of 1 (otherwise, there will
235   be no adaptors).
236
237   Convolution layer Consider a 2D convolution layer. Let W ∈ Rcout ×cin ×k1 ×k2 be the base
238   convolution layer. We similarly introduce a “low-rank” adaptive convolution layer L = U ∗ V
239   with rank r, such that the adaptive convolution W̃ becomes (W + L) ∗ x = W ∗ x + L ∗ x =
240   W ∗ x + U ∗ (V ∗ x), where ∗ is the convolution operator. We call these adaptive layers Convolution
241   LoRAs (ConvLoRAs), which are more general than a linear LoRA on a matricized convolution as is
242   often done in practice, e.g., in the official implementation (Hu et al., 2021).
243   We can have more than one way of defining U and V. Note that one of the convolutions in the
244   adaptor should share the same padding, stride, and dilation as the base convolution, while the other
245   should be such that it does not change the resolution of the input. Depending on what is meant by
246   “rank” for convolution layers, we can either reduce the rank channel-wise, filter-wise, both, or as a
247   linear layer by matricizing the convolution. We discuss these in detail in Appendix F.1 and show that
248   a novel channel+filter-wise implementation is more parameter-efficient and performs better. More
249
      details about low-rank constructions of convolution layers can be found in (Jaderberg et al., 2014;
      Khodak et al., 2022).
250
251
252
      Bias Biases are vectors, so a low-rank parameterization would not be possible, and there is no
      straightforward way to have a parameter-efficient adaptor except by considering weight-sharing or a
253
      single constant. Due to biases contributing a small percentage of the overall number of parameters in
254
      large models, we consider adaptive biases as b + Lb with extra biases Lb initialized to 0. Although
255   this adaptor is not parameter-efficient relative to b, the small impact on the overall parameter count
256   means that this is not a significant limitation. Moreover, as demonstrated in Appendix G.3, this
257   approach can be crucial for achieving optimal accuracy.

      In this section, we empirically show the performance of our lightweight framework. In particular,
449   we show that our FLoRAL framework, which can have as few as 4% more parameters, can possibly
450   outperform an ensemble of models, which can collectively have up to 400% extra parameters. It can
451   also outperform clients equipped with a local adaptor. This clearly shows the benefit of collaborative
452   learning and how personalization and adaptation can also be done efficiently in a collaborative setting.
453
      We consider datasets with known ground-truth clusters, for example, linear and MLP synthetic
454
      datasets, MNIST and CIFAR-10 with rotation or label shift Ghosh et al. (2020); Werner et al. (2023),
455   CIFAR-100 with 10 clusters, each having 10 unique labels (Werner et al., 2023). Further, we consider
456   the same datasets with only 5% data availability per client (with 10 being the minimum number of
457   samples per client). This is to demonstrate the benefits of our approach, which would be when a large
458   model might overfit the local datasets. The results can be seen in Table 1. Further ablation studies on
459   ρ and C, the adaptors, and the type of ConvLoRAs can be found in Table 2, Table 4, and Table 5,
460   respectively.
461   In general, we follow the experimental setup in (Werner et al., 2023) or (Pillutla et al., 2022) and
462   implement our experiments using PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2019) and Flower (Beutel et al., 2022).
463   We use the simplest setup possible without any tricks other than LoRA preconditioning, which is
464   explained in Appendix D. We discuss another trick called LoRA centering in Appendix E, which we
465   believe is potentially useful but is still in the experimental phase. The algorithm we use in practice is
466   shown in Algorithm 1. Further details can be found in Appendix G.
467   We emphasize that we do not aim to improve over the state-of-the-art. However, we can refer readers
468   to (Werner et al., 2023) for a relevant comparison, where we should note that the algorithm they use
469   has a quadratic running time in the number of clients. We implemented and tested their linear-time

      Discussion The results in Figure 8 show the robustness of FLoRAL with respect to its hyperpa-
511
      rameters, particularly when C is larger than the number of ground-truth clusters. As for Table 1,
512   we can see that FLoRAL is always competitive with the best baseline, which is ensembles with
513   optimal routers. A particularly interesting case is the reduced CIFAR-10-R experiments, in which
514   FLoRAL(1%) and FLoRAL(10%) surprisingly outperform ensembles even in the optimal routing
515   case, which seems slightly counter-intuitive. We believe this to be due to the variance reduction
516   shown in Appendix B. Note that FLoRAL(ρ) has Cρd extra parameters, whereas ensembles have
517   (C − 1)d. For example, when d = 1, 000 and C = 4, FLoRAL(1%) adds 40 parameters vs. 3, 000
518   for ensembles, and when C = 10, it is 100 vs. 9, 000. Local Adaptors require each client to have
519   its own adaptor (i.e., each client has a storage of size ρd). Regardless of its feasibility, FLoRAL is
520
      shown to leverage the power of collaboration when local adaptors fail to do so. We note that the
      low accuracies of FLoRAL with learned routing in reduced MNIST-LS can be alleviated with more
521
      training rounds, e.g., see Appendix G.5 for plots. Overall, these results demonstrate that FLoRAL is
522
      an efficient personalization method, and it can lead to better generalization in low-data regimes.

525   7    C ONCLUSION
526
527   In this work, we presented a parameter-efficient method for collaborative learning and personalization.
528   Here are some future directions we are interested in exploring:
529   • Is there a principled way to understand the trade-off between parameter-efficiency and the accuracy
530
        gains from increasing ρ or C and how to choose them in practice?
      • (FML) can be formulated as a “multimodal optimization” problem (Wong, 2015), which can also
531
        be described in terms of a model class that uses mixture-candidate distributions (Khan & Rue,
532
        2023). It would be interesting to explore the design of efficient algorithms under their framework,
533     e.g., with a mixture of structured distributions (Louizos & Welling, 2016).
534   • Our framework is suitable for federated fine-tuning of language models. We are interested in
535     exploring this direction.
536   • The router π can route based on its input, as in mixture of experts (Shazeer et al., 2017). It can also
537     be learned per layer. Preliminary experiments suggest that these tweaks provide marginal benefits,
538     but there is still room for exploration.
539   • We are interested in designing methods for zero-shot generalization to unseen clients based on
        FLoRAL. Is it possible to fine-tune the router without labels?
