009                                                     A BSTRACT
010
011                Large language models (LLMs) exhibit impressive in-context learning (ICL) ca-
012                pability, enabling them to generate relevant responses from a handful of task
013                demonstrations in the prompt. Prior studies have suggested two different explana-
014                tions for the mechanisms behind ICL: induction heads that find and copy relevant
015                tokens, and function vector (FV) heads whose activations compute a latent encod-
016                ing of the ICL task. To better understand which of the two distinct mechanisms
017
                   drives ICL, we study induction heads and FV heads in 12 language models.
018
                   Our study reveals that in all 12 models, few-shot ICL is driven primarily by FV
                   heads: ablating FV heads decreases few-shot ICL accuracy significantly more than
019
                   ablating induction heads, especially in larger models. We also find that FV and
020
                   induction heads are connected: many FV heads start as induction heads during
021                training before transitioning to the FV mechanism. This leads us to speculate that
022                induction heads facilitate the learning of the more complex FV mechanism for
023                ICL. Finally, the prevalence of FV and induction heads varies with architecture,
024                which questions strong versions of the “universality” hypothesis: findings from
025                interpretability research are not always generalizable across models1 .

028   1       I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      One of the most remarkable features of large language models (LLM) is their ability to perform
      in-context learning (ICL), where a handful of demonstrations provided during inference enables
031
      the model to perform various tasks. ICL is widely used and a crucial tool for steering pre-trained
032
      LLMs for specific tasks, and a growing body of work aims to understand the mechanisms behind ICL
033   (Olsson et al., 2022; Akyürek et al., 2023; Von Oswald et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023).
034
035   To date, two key mechanisms have been primarily associated with ICL, substantiated by different lines
036
      of evidence. First, induction circuits (Elhage et al., 2021) were hypothesized to be the mechanism
      behind ICL in LLMs (Olsson et al., 2022; Singh et al., 2024; Crosbie & Shutova, 2024; Dong et al.,
037
      2022). Induction circuits perform ICL by looking back in the prompt for previous instances of the
038
      current token, then copying the subsequent token. More recently, Todd et al. (2024) and Hendel et al.
039   (2023) propose the existence of function vectors (FV). FVs are a compact representation of a task
040   extracted from a subset of attention heads in LLMs, and they can be added to a model’s computation
041   to recover ICL behavior without in-context demonstrations.
042
      Are two seemingly distinct mechanisms both responsible for ICL in transformer LLMs? To better
043
      understand the role of induction circuits and FVs, we study the attention heads that implement
044
      induction and FVs (which we call induction heads and FV heads). We run a series of experiments on
045   12 decoder-only autoregressive transformer language models of parameter size between 70M and 7B
046   (Table 2) and 48 natural language ICL tasks (listed in Appendix A.7).
047
048
      We find that FV heads have the strongest causal effect on ICL performance on few-shot learning tasks.
      In Figure 1a, ablating FV heads leads to a significant drop in accuracy on ICL tasks, whereas ablating
049
      induction heads has a weak effect on ICL performance. This trend is consistent in all 12 models we
050
      studied, and FV heads are increasingly influential to ICL relative to induction heads as model scale
051   increases (§4). This leads us to conclude that FV heads are mainly responsible for few-shot ICL,
052   contrary to the prevailing belief that induction heads are a primary mechanism of few-shot ICL.

077    We identify two considerations that help explain the conflicting accounts in the literature (§2).
078
       Primarily, previous work connecting induction heads to ICL (Olsson et al., 2022) used a different
       metric for ICL, which we call token-loss difference. In our work, we measure ICL performance
079
       by computing accuracy on few-shot learning tasks, since this definition of ICL is more commonly
080
       adopted in the literature (Brown, 2020; Dong et al., 2022). We find that token-loss difference and
081    few-shot accuracy behave differently, and ablating induction heads do strongly affect token-loss
082    difference without similarly affecting few-shot accuracy.
083
084
       Secondarily, studies on induction heads did not consider the correlation between induction heads
       and FV heads. Therefore, ablation studies that do not control for the correlation cannot compare the
085
       contributions of induction heads and FV heads to ICL well. Once we ablate only induction heads that
086
       are not also FV heads, the effect on ICL performance becomes much weaker. Previous studies were
087    also often done on smaller models, since they required mechanistic analysis, but we find that FV
088    heads become progressively more important for ICL relative to induction heads as models get larger.
089
090
       We also further examine induction and FV heads in the context of each other, and find several
       relationships between them:
091
092
093                        • The set of induction heads and FV heads are distinct, but there is some correlation between
094                          them (§3).
095
                           • FV heads appear deeper in models than induction heads (§3).
096
097                        • FV heads appear later during training than induction heads (§5.1).
098
099                        • There are many instances of induction heads that transition to FV heads during training, but
100                          the reverse does not occur (§5.1).
101
102    We summarize our findings in Table 1. In §6, we propose working conjectures to unify our findings.
103    In one conjecture, we speculate that induction heads facilitate learning the more complex FV heads
104    for ICL – the FV mechanism is more effective at performing ICL, and therefore eventually replaces
105    the simpler induction mechanism. Our findings also question universality – the difference between
106    the importance of FV heads and induction heads increases with model scale, where in our smallest
107    model, the causal effect of FV and induction heads are similar. Our study underscores the variability
       of neural models and the importance of understanding the interplay between different mechanisms.

      2      BACKGROUND & RELATED WORK
119
120
      We simultaneously investigate induction heads (Elhage et al., 2021; Olsson et al., 2022) and FV heads
121   (Todd et al., 2024; Hendel et al., 2023) for a comparative analysis.

      2.1     I NDUCTION HEADS
124
125   Induction heads were first discovered by Elhage et al. (2021) and further investigated by Olsson et al.
126   (2022) as the mechanism behind ICL. They are attention heads that attend to the token immediately
127   after an earlier copy of the current token, and predict that the token attended to comes next.
128
      Olsson et al. (2022) focus most of their study on small attention-only models with 1-3 layers, and
129   finds co-occurrence between the emergence of induction during training and the emergence of ICL
130   abilities in models, which they define as the loss of the 500th token minus the loss of the 50th token
131   in the context. They also perform ablations and find that knocking out induction heads decreases ICL
132   performance.
133
      In our work, we analyze induction heads by computing the induction score of attention heads.
134

135   induction score on a sequence of uniformly sampled random tokens r1 r2 ...r50 that is repeated twice:

168   2.3    R ECONCILING DIVERGENT FINDINGS
169
170
      While both mechanisms have been proposed by their respective works as the mechanism behind ICL,
171
      we find that if we analyze induction heads and FV heads side-by-side, FV heads seem to primarily
172
      contribute to ICL performance. We believe that the main reason for the divergence between our result
173   and previous work lies in several intuitively related concepts in the literature that are assumed to be
174   the same. On one hand, ICL is often used synonymously with few-shot learning from the prompt
175   without parameter updates (Brown, 2020; Dong et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2023). We also adopt this
176   conceptualization of ICL in this paper since it is the most standard in the literature, and for clarity, we
177   will use “in-context learning” in this paper as equivalent to this definition. On the other hand, ICL
178   performance is measured in Olsson et al. (2022) by computing the difference between the model loss
179   of the 500th token in the context and the loss of the 50th token. This difference was previously called
180   “ICL score” but we recommend adopting distinct terminology to avoid confusion, and for the purpose
181
      of our work, we will call this token-loss difference.
182   The primary discrepancy between our findings and Olsson et al. (2022) is that FV heads affect
183   few-shot ICL accuracy strongly, but not token-loss difference, while induction heads affect token-loss
184   difference strongly, but not few-shot ICL accuracy (§4). Secondly, we find that induction heads and
185   FV heads are correlated (§3), and previous studies on few-shot ICL did not control for this correlation.
186   In §4, when we naively ablated induction heads, the model significantly drops in ICL accuracy, which
187
      has been similarly shown in Crosbie & Shutova (2024) and Bansal et al. (2023). However, once we
      only ablate induction heads that are not also FV heads, their effect on ICL accuracy becomes close
188
      to random, whereas ablating FV heads that are not also induction heads still leads to a significant
189
      decrease in ICL accuracy. Therefore, previous studies that identified induction heads as important
190   for ICL may be because they were observing signals from induction heads that are also FV heads.
191   Finally, previous work that proposed induction heads as the mechanism driving ICL Olsson et al.
192   (2022); Singh et al. (2024) focused on small model sizes to facilitate mechanistic analyses. However,
193   we find that FV heads become increasingly important to ICL performance relative to induction heads
194   as models increase in scale. In our smallest model with 70M parameters, induction and FV heads
195   have similar causal effects to few-shot ICL, therefore it is important to study ICL mechanisms across
196   a range of model scale.

231   3                  I NDUCTION HEADS AND FUNCTION VECTOR HEADS ARE DISTINCT BUT
232
                         CORRELATED
233
234
      Induction heads and FV heads implement two seemingly distinct mechanisms, and yet, they have
235   both been attributed to ICL. To investigate whether one, or both, types of attention heads drive ICL,
236   we start by studying the extent of the overlap between induction and FV heads.
237
238   3.1                       H EAD LOCATIONS
239
240   First, we inspect where the top induction and FV heads are located in models to understand whether
241   they appear in similar layers of the model. In Figure 2, we plot the layers where the top 2%2 induction
242   heads and FV heads appear in 3 Pythia models, with the induction score of induction heads on the
243   left y-axis and FV score of FV heads on the right y-axis. We plot the head locations for all 12 models
244
      in Appendix A.9.
245   In general, induction heads appear in early-middle layers and FV heads appear in slightly deeper
246   layers than induction heads. This suggests that induction and FV heads may not fully overlap, and
247   that FV heads may implement more complex or abstract computations than induction heads.
248

252
      contain both induction and FV heads where there could be potential overlap between the two types of
      heads. We therefore investigate the extent of overlap between the two types of heads in Figure 3.

284   4             F UNCTION VECTOR HEADS DRIVE IN - CONTEXT LEARNING
285
286   If induction heads and FV heads are distinct from one another, which type of attention heads matter
287   most for ICL? To investigate this, we measure the causal importance of different heads for ICL by
288   evaluating ICL performance after ablating induction and FV heads. We also control for the correlation
289   between induction and FV heads by ablating induction heads while preserving FV heads, and vice
290   versa. We measure ICL performance by evaluating model accuracy on few-shot ICL tasks. For
291   completeness, we also study the effects of ablation on token-loss difference.
292
293   4.1            M ETHOD
294
295   Ablation. To analyze the causal effect of specific attention heads on ICL performance, we study
296   how much ICL performance drops when we “erase” certain heads. To do so, we perform mean
297   ablation on groups of heads by replacing the output vector of each head we ablate by the mean of
298   this head’s outputs over all examples in our dataset of few-shot ICL tasks described in later sections.
299
      We choose to perform mean ablation instead of zero ablation (setting the output to 0) to avoid the
      out-of-distribution problem Hase et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2023); Zhang & Nanda (2024).
300
301   Since FV and induction scores are correlated, there may be overlap between the heads we ablate
302   for induction and FV mechanisms, especially in larger ablation quantities. This may obfuscate the
303   comparison of contributions between the two types of heads. We therefore also perform ablation
304   experiments with “exclusion”: when ablating n FV heads, we take the top n heads by FV score that
305
      do not appear in the top 2% heads by induction score, and similarly when ablating n induction heads,
      we take the top n heads by induction score that do not appear in the top 2% heads by FV score.
306
307   Few-shot ICL accuracy. We evaluate ICL performance by measuring model accuracy on a series
308   of few-shot ICL tasks. Each ICL task is defined by a set of input-output pairs (xi , yi ). The model
309   is prompted with 10 input-output exemplar pairs that demonstrate this task, and one query input
310   xq that corresponds to a target output yq that is not part of the model’s prompt. We compute the
311
      model’s accuracy in predicting the correct output yq . We describe the full set of ICL tasks we study
      in Appendix A.7.
312
313   To avoid leakage between ICL tasks used to identify FV heads and those used to evaluate FV head
314   ablations, we randomly split the 40 ICL tasks from Todd et al. (2024) into 29 tasks used to measure
315   FV scores of heads, and 11 tasks to evaluate ICL performance. We also add 8 new tasks for ICL
316   evaluation: 4 tasks are variations of tasks in Todd et al. (2024), and 4 are binding tasks from Feng &
317
      Steinhardt (2024). In total, we evaluate ICL accuracy on 19 natural language tasks, with 100 prompts
      per task.
318
319   Token-loss difference. To compare with previous work, we also study the effect of ablations on
320   token-loss difference used in Olsson et al. (2022). We measure token-loss difference by taking the
321   loss of the 50th token in the context minus the loss of the 500th token in the context4 , averaged over
322   10,000 examples from the Pile dataset (Gao et al., 2020).

        4.2                     R ESULTS
352
      For each model, we ablate the top 1-20% of attention heads based on induction or FV score. We
353
      also compute model performance with no ablation and with ablations of randomly sampled heads
354   as a baseline. In Figure 4, we plot how model ICL accuracy and token-loss difference evolve with
355   different quantities of ablations for a sample of 3 models, where ICL accuracy is averaged over the
356   19 tasks used for evaluation. We also plot ablations for all models, and ICL accuracy broken down by
357   task, in Appendix A.8.
358
       In the top row of Figure 4, we ablate induction and FV heads without excluding one from the other.
359
       Here, we find that in general, ablating FV heads leads to a bigger drop in ICL performance than
360    induction heads, and the gap between the effect of FV heads and induction heads is larger in bigger
361    models. We also find that ablating induction heads leads to worse ICL accuracy than ablating random
362    heads, and the effect of ablating induction heads converges to the effect of ablating FV heads when
363    we increase the number of heads ablated.
364
       However, the convergence noted above may be due to an increasing overlap in the set of heads ablated
365
       in the induction head and FV head ablations (Appendix A.10). We control for this overlap using
366    ablations with exclusions. The center row of Figure 4 shows that after ablating induction heads while
367    preserving FV heads, the effect of ablating induction heads on ICL is weaker. In fact, in most models
368    with parameter count over 1B, the effect of ablating induction heads is close to ablating random
369    heads. On the other hand, ablating FV heads while preserving induction heads still causes a large
370    drop in ICL performance. We also recover a similar trend as before across model scale: the larger the
371    model, the larger the gap between the effect of FV heads and induction heads. This suggests that the
372    contributions of induction heads to ICL in the top row of Figure 4 mostly come from heads that are
373    both induction and FV heads, and that FV heads matter the most for ICL: as long as the model
374
       preserves its top 2% FV heads, it is able to perform ICL with reasonable accuracy even if we ablate

        5                FV HEADS EVOLVE FROM INDUCTION HEADS
397
398
       Our previous findings on the correlation between induction and FV scores hint at some interplay
399
       between induction and FV heads. To better understand how the two types of heads may be related, we
400    examine how induction and FV heads evolve during training in 8 intermediate training checkpoints
401    of 7 Pythia models.
402
403
        5.1                I NDUCTION AND FV STRENGTH DURING TRAINING
404
405    To measure the general strength of induction and FV mechanisms over the course of training, we plot
406    the mean induction and FV scores of the top 2% induction and FV heads at each model checkpoint
407    (Figure 5). We include plots for all Pythia models in Appendix A.11.
408
       In all Pythia models, induction heads appear at around step 1000 out of 143000 during training,
409
       whereas FV heads appear later at around step 16000 out of 143000. Moreover, induction score rises
410    sharply at step 1000 and then plateaus or slightly decreases, whereas FV score gradually increases
411    from step 14000 until the end of training. This suggests that induction heads are easy for models to
412    learn and FV heads are harder to learn.
413
414
        5.2                E VOLUTION OF INDIVIDUAL HEADS DURING TRAINING
415
416    We further investigate the evolution of induction and FV scores of individual attention heads. In
417    Figure 6, we plot the induction scores (top row) and FV scores (bottom row) of the top 2% induction
418    and FV heads across training steps. In each model, we find that certain FV heads have high induction
419    score earlier in training, at around the same time as when induction heads form, and often matching
420    the induction score of induction heads. These FV heads then observe a decay in induction score while
421    increasing in FV score later in training. However, the reverse is not true: all induction heads have low
422
       FV scores throughout training. This suggests that many FV heads evolve from induction heads
       during training.

425     6                I NTERPRETATION AND DISCUSSION
426
427    To summarize our key findings, we verified that induction heads and FV heads are distinct, but
428    correlated (§3). FV heads appear in slightly deeper layers than induction heads, and emerge later
429    during training (§3,5.1). To the extent that these two types of heads are different, FV heads play a
430    large role in ICL, especially in bigger models, whereas ablating induction heads does not decrease
431    ICL performance to the same extent (§4). We also find several instances of induction heads that
       transition to FV heads during training, whereas the reverse does not occur (§5.1). We propose two

452   working conjectures to explain these findings more broadly, and consider arguments for and against
453   them.
454   Our first conjecture (C1) is that induction heads are an early version of FV heads. We believe
455   induction heads help the model learn the more complex FV heads. Once the model obtains FV heads,
456   the FV mechanism is more accurate for performing ICL and therefore it eventually replaces the
457   simpler, less effective induction mechanism.
458
      The most compelling argument for C1 is the existence of FV heads that are formerly induction heads
459
      during training. The decrease in induction score late in training suggests that induction is not needed
460   by the head in its final form. This is reinforced by the fact that the reverse never happens (FV heads
461   never become induction heads), and that induction heads with low FV scores contribute little to ICL
462   under ablations. To further verify this, future work could explore how removing induction heads
463   during training could impact the development of FV heads. However, C1 does not fully explain the
464   existence of FV heads with low induction score throughout training.
465
      C1 also correctly predicts that FV heads are more complex and harder to learn than induction heads
466   (they appear later in training and deeper in model architectures). This also explains why attention
467   heads with higher parameter count are needed to learn strong FV heads, and ablating FV heads affect
468   larger models more than smaller models relative to ablating induction heads.
469
      An alternative conjecture (C2) is that FV heads are a combination of induction and another
470
      mechanism. Under this conjecture, the induction heads that “become” FV heads are polysemantic
471
      heads that implement both induction and FVs, and possibly other mechanisms. The induction score
472   of these polysemantic heads drop because the attention patterns become split between the different
473   mechanisms the attention head carries out. Under C2, induction and FV heads are correlated because
474   they share an underlying mechanism that the model learns to re-use for both tasks during training.
475   However, C2 would predict that ablating monosemantic FV heads would not hurt ICL performance,
476   whereas we observe that ablating monosemantic FV heads while preserving polysemantic induction-
477   FV heads lowers ICL accuracy.

480   7               C ONCLUSION
481
482   Contrary to the prevailing consensus that few-shot ICL is largely driven by induction heads, we find
483   that this assumption does not hold in most of the models we study. Instead, we find that FV heads
484   have a more important causal contribution to few-shot ICL. We believe the main reason for this
485   misconception is due to conflating few-shot prompting and token-loss difference when we discuss
      ICL, as well as not accounting for the overlap between induction and FV heads.


                                                               9
486
      Remarkably, although induction and FV mechanisms appear to implement two distinct processes,
487   we also observe interesting interplay between the two types of heads: induction and FV scores
488   are correlated, and many FV heads are ‘former’ induction heads that have high induction scores
489   earlier in training. In §6, we present arguments for and against early conjectures to explain this
490   phenomenon. We believe that one possible explanation is induction heads act as a precursor to FV
491   heads: induction, being simpler to learn and implement, initially facilitates ICL, from which the FV
492   mechanism emerges to achieve a more accurate implementation of ICL.
493   Our study also illustrates an important takeaway for interpretability research more generally. In several
494   experiments, we find that certain models with different architecture, model family, or parameter size
495   behave qualitatively differently than others. For example, induction heads significantly contribute
496   to ICL in small Pythia models but not in larger Pythia models (§4). The invalidity of the strong
497   version of the universality hypothesis in interpretability could also explain why induction heads
498   were previously commonly attributed to ICL: early studies focused on small models to facilitate
499   mechanistic analysis, which may give an incomplete explanation of ICL mechanisms.
500   Our findings open up several avenues for future investigation. If C1 is true, why do FV heads need
501   induction as a precursor? What are remaining induction heads used for? Is there a third mechanism
502   that better explains ICL? If model mechanisms are not universal across size or architecture, what is
503   the best approach to generalizing findings from interpretability research?

      A.6       E VALUATING FUNCTION VECTORS ON TASK EXECUTION
901
902   To further inspect the prevalence of the FV mechanism in different models, we evaluate the efficacy
903   of FVs for ICL task execution. A successful FV triggers the model to execute the particular task the
904   FV encodes, even when the model sees no useful in-context demonstrations of the task. First, to
905   extract FVs, for each model we gather the top 2% attention heads with highest FV scores as the set A.
906   Then, for each ICL task t ∈ T , we sum
                                           P the average outputs of heads in A over prompts from t and
907   obtain the FV for the task t: F Vt = a∈A āt .
908   In Figure 14, we report model accuracy averaged over 40 ICL tasks where the model performs
909   inference on uncorrupted prompts (clean), prompts with shuffled labels (shuffled), shuffled prompts
910   with F Vt added to hidden states at layer |L|/3, and shuffled prompts with FV extracted from random
911   heads added to hidden states at layer |L|/3. We take 1000 examples per task that are previously
912   unseen during FV score computation.
913   In most models, adding the FV recovers model performance on uncorrupted prompts, with the
914   exception of Pythia 2.8B. One possible explanation for this is again due to head dimensionality:
915   Pythia 2.8B has head dimension 80, which is significantly smaller than other models with similar
916   parameter size that have head dimensions of 128. Together with our experiments in §A.5, results
917   provide preliminary evidence that high head dimensionality relative to model size is a predictor of
      FV strength (H6).
