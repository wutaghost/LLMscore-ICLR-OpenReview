009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Rapid advancements in Autonomous Driving (AD) tasks turned a significant shift
012            toward end-to-end fashion, particularly in the utilization of vision-language mod-
013            els (VLMs) that integrate robust logical reasoning and cognitive abilities to enable
014            comprehensive end-to-end planning. However, these VLM-based approaches tend
015            to integrate 2D vision tokenizers and a large language model (LLM) for ego-car
016            planning, which lack 3D geometric priors as a cornerstone of reliable planning.
017            Naturally, this observation raises a critical concern: Can a 2D-tokenized LLM
018
               accurately perceive the 3D environment? Our evaluation of current VLM-based
               methods across 3D object detection, vectorized map construction, and environ-
019
               mental caption suggests that the answer is, unfortunately, NO. In other words,
020
               2D-tokenized LLM fails to provide reliable autonomous driving. In response, we
021            introduce DETR-style 3D perceptrons as 3D tokenizers, which connect LLM with
022            a one-layer linear projector. This simple yet elegant strategy, termed Atlas, har-
023            nesses the inherent priors of the 3D physical world, enabling it to simultaneously
024            process high-resolution multi-view images and employ spatiotemporal modeling.
025            Despite its simplicity, Atlas demonstrates superior performance in both 3D detec-
026            tion and ego planning tasks on nuScenes dataset, proving that 3D-tokenized LLM
027            is the key to reliable autonomous driving. The code and datasets will be released.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Autonomous Driving (AD) is a sophisticated system that integrates perception, reasoning, and plan-
033
      ning (Janai et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2023). Perception serves as the initial stage, capturing details of
      the surrounding environment. This information then feeds into the reasoning component, facilitating
034
      a deeper understanding, and ultimately guiding decision-making through the planning process. Re-
035
      cently, the incorporation of perception, reasoning, and planning to construct end-to-end models has
036   become prevalent. It can be broadly categorized into two distinct methodologies: modular bird’s-eye
037   view (BEV) based approaches and large vision-language model (VLM) based methods.
038
039
      The modular BEV-based approaches are meticulously engineered, comprising custom-tailored mod-
      ules, including 3D perception, trajectory prediction, and ego-car planning (Liang et al., 2020; Casas
040
      et al., 2021; Chen & Krähenbühl, 2022; Zhang et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2022a; Gu et al., 2023; Hu
041
      et al., 2023), as shown in Figure 1(a). While BEV representation enhances environmental per-
042   ception, these methods may encounter difficulty stemming from their limited reasoning abilities.
043   Specifically, these models tend to mimic established expert trajectories and struggle to predict mul-
044   tiple potential motion trajectories when confronted with novel scenarios. To tackle this challenge,
045   VLM-based methods mark a significant turning point. They usually employ a 2D vision tokenizer
046   (e.g., ViT-CLIP (Radford et al., 2021)) with a Large Language Model (LLM) to interpret distorted
047   images and produce navigational commands (Xu et al., 2023; Tian et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023b;
048   Shao et al., 2024; Jia et al., 2023a; Sima et al., 2023). Benefiting from the robust logical reasoning
049   and cognitive abilities of the VLM agent, the model can generate rational decisions and dialogues.
050   Despite the success of VLM-based algorithms, the perceptual capabilities within this paradigm is
051   barely studied. While we argue that the perception sub-task may not be essential for end-to-end
052   driving, the capacity to perceive the environment remains a cornerstone of reliable planning. Since
053   VLM-based methods rely on 2D vision tokenizers for environmental perception without incorporat-
      ing 3D geometric priors, an intuitive question arises: Can a 2D-tokenized LLM accurately per-

080
      To address this issue, we wonder if 3D vision tokenizers hold the key to Pandora. We discover that
081   the existing DETR-style BEV framework can naturally serve as a 3D visual compression tokenizer.
082   Therefore, we opt for the advanced StreamPETR (Wang et al., 2023a) and TopoMLP (Wu et al.,
083   2024) as our 3D visual tokenizers, forgoing the traditional use of ViT-CLIP (Radford et al., 2021).
084   This strategy brings three advantages: 1) The innate priors of the 3D physical world are naturally
085   encoded within visual tokens by introducing the position encodings. 2) It is capable of handling
086   high-resolution images with any aspect ratio without the risk of distorting. 3) Video frames can be
087   processed in a streaming manner, benefiting from DETR-style query propagation. Through evalua-
088   tion of the nuScenes dataset, we demonstrate that our 3D-tokenized LLM approach achieves perfor-
089
      mance on par with specialized algorithms in tasks such as 3D object detection and lane detection.
090   Beyond that, we need to answer another question: Is a 3D-tokenized LLM the key to reliable
091   autonomous driving? Following BEV-Planner (Li et al., 2024), we extend our exploration to the
092   open-loop planning on the nuScenes dataset. By leveraging the 3D tokenizers for enhanced per-
093   ception capabilities, our model not only comprehends the environment around the vehicle but also
094   utilizes the LLM to formulate driving recommendations and plan the ego-car trajectory in an end-to-
095
      end manner. Remarkably, this approach eschews hand-crafted designs and achieves state-of-the-art
      performance on the nuScenes planning task.
096
097   In summary, our work highlights the importance of proper vision tokenizers in VLM-based AD and
098   introduces the 3D-tokenized LLM as a solution. We showcase its superiority in adeptly addressing
099   challenges across multiple tasks such as 3D perception, vectorized map construction, environmental
100   caption, and planning within autonomous driving systems. Our model demonstrates superior perfor-
101
      mance in both benchmark evaluations and practical downstream applications, proving its reliability
      and versatility. Furthermore, our framework paves the way for pioneering end-to-end LLM-driven
102
      solutions in autonomous driving, potentially transforming how these systems are developed.

136

137

138
139   Datasets. We design datasets tailored for VLM methods built upon popular multi-view benchmark
140   nuScenes (Caesar et al., 2020), as shown in Figure 2. For the 3D detection task, we construct
141
      question-and-answer (QA) pairs that focus on pinpointing the locations of objects surrounding the
      ego vehicle. Each question prompts the model to extract spatial information about the target objects
142
      from six views. The corresponding answers require the model to identify both the category and the
143
      3D coordinates of objects. Similarly, the dataset for 3D lane detection also comprises QA pairs,
144   whose answers are lane points borrowed from OpenLane-V2 subset-B (Wang et al., 2024). Here,
145   each road is depicted using four consecutive points describing the road centerline. More details can
146   be found in the supplementary.
147
      Models. All 2D-tokenized LLMs in our study adhere to a uniform architecture, which consists
148
      of three main components: 2D tokenizer, projector, and large language model. The 2D tokenizer
149
      follows ViT-CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) to extract visual features from multiple perspectives of
150   images. For the projection module, we incorporate a single convolutional layer to bridge the 2D
151   tokenizer and LLM. Besides, we utilize diverse pre-trained LLMs, such as LLaMA (Touvron et al.,
152   2023), LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024), Vicuna (Chiang et al., 2023), which are comprehensive processing
153   of complex visual information to generate the perception of the environment, to prove consistency
154   and fairness in our exploration. Additionally, another available VLM-based model pre-trained on
155   2D object detection Merlin (Yu et al., 2023) is also evaluated.

157   score is motivated by two primary considerations: First, VLMs cannot deliver the necessary predic-

159   metrics commonly encourage numerous redundant predictions, which can clutter the model output.
160   In contrast, VLMs are designed to generate more targeted and focused predictions, making the F1
161   score a better fit for assessing these models. In this work, for 3D detection, we choose threshold
      distances of 0.5, 1.0, 2.0, and 4.0 meters to define positive predictions, similar to the discrimina-

195   2.2      2D-T OKENIZED LLM FOR C APTIONING
196
197   In addition to basic environmental perception tasks, LLMs can be adapted to perform more complex
198   tasks like extracting and interpreting key features from visual for captioning environments. This
199   capability extends the utility of LLMs in practical applications, and leverages world knowledge and
200
      reasoning ability, particularly in scenarios requiring detailed environmental understanding.
201   To explore whether a 2D-tokenized LLM could serve as an effective perceptron, we develop a spe-
202   cialized version of the model for environmental captioning. This variant utilizes Vicuna (Chiang
203   et al., 2023) as its underlying LLM, tasked with capturing and describing the environment of ego
204   vehicle. This description includes various elements such as the location and quantity of nearby ve-
205   hicles and pedestrians, traffic dynamics, concerning surrounding lanes of pedestrian crossing and
206
      road.
207   Despite the advanced capabilities of VLMs in generating natural language descriptions, as illustrated
208   in Figure 3, our findings indicate that the 2D-tokenized LLM struggles with accurate environmental
209   perception. The model frequently produces erroneous or "hallucinated" descriptions, which suggests
210   that it still falls short of reliable perception in practical applications. This underscores the challenges
211   and limitations inherent in deploying LLMs for complex perceptual tasks in dynamic environments.
212   Remark. To sum up, the experiments above reveal a significant limitation in the perception capabil-
213   ities of LLMs that rely on 2D visual tokenizers. This limitation poses serious challenges for reliable
214   ego vehicle planning. We claim that the primary reason for this limitation lies in the inability of 2D
215   visual tokenizers to effectively integrate 3D spatial priors. To address the limitation, we introduce
      advanced pre-trained 3D perception models as 3D tokenizers in the following section.

246   3.1   3D-T OKENIZED LLM
247
      Distinct from 2D-tokenized LLM, we introduce 3D tokenizers founded upon a DETR-inspired archi-
248
      tecture into LLM, formulating a 3D-tokenized LLM framework, named Atlas. In specific, Altas con-
249   sists of three primary components. Initially, the model employs 3D tokenizers, StreamPETR (Wang
250   et al., 2023a) and TopoMLP (Wu et al., 2024), to process multi-view images into DETR-style query
251   representations. Following this, these queries are streamlined through a single linear layer, func-
252   tioning as a projector, to align with the LLM. The final component of Atlas is an LLM, designed as
253   Vicuna (Chiang et al., 2023). This approach brings significant benefits in incorporating 3D innate
254   prior, achieving high resolution, and facilitating temporal propagation, as previously elaborated.
255   3D Environment Perception. The performance of Atlas is evaluated on standard datasets tailored to
256   the tasks of 3D object detection and 3D lane detection, as reported in Table 1 and Table 2. The results
257   demonstrate that 3D-tokenized LLM achieves remarkable performance across both tasks. Besides,
258   3D-tokenized LLM performs better than 2D-tokenized LLM on driving environment captioning, as
259   shown in Figure 3, thereby affirming the significant advantages of utilizing 3D tokenizers. In addi-
260   tion to representing 3D environment, our ultimate goal is to achieve reliable autonomous driving. In
261   the following, we will evaluate the performance of 3D-tokenized LLM on ego-car planning.
262

305   3.3      M AIN R ESULTS
306
307   In this section, we evaluate the performance of Atlas, by comparing it against existing state-of-the-
308   art BEV-based planners, as detailed in Table 3. Our experimental results reveal that Atlas achieves
309   substantial improvements over the SoTA methods, reducing the average L2 metric by 40.0% and the
310   average Collision metric by 60.6%. These significant enhancements corroborate the effectiveness of
311   the 3D-tokenized LLMs, which we consider as the key to reliable autonomous driving.
312   Further, to ascertain whether the performance improvements are solely attributable to the inclu-
313   sion of ego state information—a frequent topic of discussion within the community—we conduct
314   additional experiments by removing the ego state data during both training and testing. In this exper-
315   imental setting, compared to the prevailing VLM-based methods, our Atlas demonstrates superior
316   performance and robustly validates the effectiveness of 3D tokenizers. Despite this, Atlas continues
317   to outperform other BEV-based methods in terms of collision rates. However, the performance on
318   the L2 metric is comparable to other methods. We hypothesize that this outcome may stem from the
319
      inherent capabilities of the LLM to predict multiple potential motion trajectories and make rational
      decisions, which, while confronted with novel scenarios, deviate from the ground truth.

429
430   DETR-style BEV Perception. DETR (Carion et al., 2020) is initially proposed to address the chal-
431   lenge of end-to-end detection, and further extensively applied in BEV perception (Liu et al., 2022;
      Li et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023a; Lin et al., 2022), thereby significantly advancing its development.


                                                             8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      DETR3D (Wang et al., 2022) is a pioneering work that introduces the concept of 3D object queries,
433   which interact with multi-view image features to produce sparse yet informative object representa-
434   tions. Further, PETR (Liu et al., 2022; 2023b) introduces the concept of 3D position encoding, and
435   BEVFormer (Li et al., 2022) brings BEV temporal modeling. StreamPETR (Wang et al., 2023a) and
436   Sparsev2 (Lin et al., 2023) use object queries as a vessel for temporal modeling, effectively prop-
437   agating temporal information while achieving SoTA performance with commendable efficiency. In
438   a notable finding within StreamPETR, the inclusion of additional multi-frame image feature inter-
439   actions does not enhance performance, suggesting that the highly compressed object queries are
440   sufficiently expressive to encapsulate all necessary information for BEV perception. Moreover, the
441
      application of DETR framework has been expanded to map queries by works such as MapTR (Liao
      et al., 2023), TopoNet (Li et al., 2023a) and TopoMLP (Wu et al., 2024), which are instrumental in
442
      the construction of vectorized map representations.
443
444   BEV-based End-to-end Driving. Traditional autonomous driving systems have often relied on
445   manual rules, which can be cumbersome and complex, struggling to cover the numerous corner
446   cases. In recent years, there has been a pronounced shift towards end-to-end autonomous driv-
447
      ing approaches, which have demonstrated significant progress in simplifying and streamlining the
      pipeline. UniAD (Hu et al., 2023) is a pioneering work that introduces an end-to-end framework
448
      encompassing tasks such as perception, prediction, and planning, with these tasks executed sequen-
449
      tially to ultimately produce control outputs. Building upon this framework, VAD (Alexanian et al.,
450   1990) further streamlines the pipeline, enhancing efficiency and reducing complexity. However,
451   AD-MLP (Zhai et al., 2023b) and BEV-Planner (Li et al., 2024) have observed that existing end-
452   to-end methods can achieve high performance on open-loop benchmarks like nuScenes (Caesar
453   et al., 2020) by simply fitting to the ego status of the autonomous vehicle. This finding suggests
454   that the integration of planning and control in these models may not yet fully capture the complex-
455   ities of real-world driving scenarios. Subsequent works, such as Think-Twice (Jia et al., 2023b)
456   and VADv2 (Chen et al., 2024), have made substantial advancements in closed-loop simulators like
457   Carla (Dosovitskiy et al., 2017). Following BEV-Planner (Li et al., 2024), we present results with
458
      and without the ego status to address the open-loop challenges on the nuScenes (Caesar et al., 2020).
459   VLM-Agent for Autonomous Driving. The visual-language model (VLM) domenstrates promis-
460   ing results in the fields of visual-language understanding and logical reasoning, and has been ex-
461   tended to autonomous driving (Xu et al., 2023; Tian et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023b; Shao et al.,
462   2024; Jia et al., 2023a; Xie et al., 2024). DriveGPT4 (Xu et al., 2023) employs a VLM model to
463   predict driving commands and provide rational explanations for its decisions. DriveLM (Sima et al.,
464
      2023) excels at conversing about environmental information, while ADriver-I (Jia et al., 2023a) fo-
      cuses on predicting low-level vehicle signals. Furthermore, DriveMLM (Wang et al., 2023b) and
465
      LMDrive (Shao et al., 2024) have implemented end-to-end autonomous driving solutions and val-
466
      idated effectiveness on CARLA (Dosovitskiy et al., 2017) closed-loop benchmarks, showcasing
467   the potential of VLM-based agents. Despite impressive progress, no work explores how the 3D-
468   tokenized LLM influences real-life autonomous driving.
469
470
471   5   C ONCLUSION
472
473
      In this paper, we explored VLM-based methods increasingly used in autonomous driving, focusing
474   first on perception. We found large gaps between task-specific and 2D tokenized LLM-based meth-
475   ods in environmental perception, which is essential for reliable autonomous driving. To address
476   these gaps, we introduced Atlas, a system combining DETR-style 3D perceptrons with LLMs. This
477   approach integrates 3D priors for better depth perception and supports high-resolution, multi-view
478   images, and temporal modeling through query propagation. Our evaluation of Atlas on nuScenes
479   dataset revealed substantial improvements in 3D detection and planning, surpassing established
480   methods. This confirms our belief that 3D-tokenized LLM is the key to reliable autonomous driving.
481   Limitations. This paper aims to demonstrate the effectiveness of the 3D tokenizer for VLM-based
482   autonomous driving. Although our method has demonstrated outstanding performance in open-loop
483   planning, it has not yet been tested on a closed-loop dataset. However, existing close-loop bench-
484   marks (e.g., CARLA (Dosovitskiy et al., 2017)) lack reality, which fails to verify our motivation.
485   Moreover, this paper lacks of performance comparison with VLM-based AD methods. This omis-
      sion is due to the proprietary codes for these methods.
