009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            We present an online planning framework for solving multi-object rearrangement
012            problems in partially observable, multi-room environments. Current object rear-
013            rangement solutions, primarily based on Reinforcement Learning or hand-coded
014            planning methods, often lack adaptability to diverse challenges. To address this
015            limitation, we introduce a novel Hierarchical Object-Oriented Partially Observed
016            Markov Decision Process (HOO-POMDP) planning approach. This approach
017            comprises of (a) an object-oriented POMDP planner generating sub-goals, (b) a
018
               set of low-level policies for sub-goal achievement, and (c) an abstraction system
               converting the continuous low-level world into a representation suitable for ab-
019
               stract planning. We evaluate our system on varying numbers of objects, rooms,
020
               and problem types in AI2-THOR simulated environments with promising results.

023   1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Multi-object rearrangement with egocentric vision in realistic home environments is a fundamental
026   challenge in embodied AI, encompassing complex tasks that require perception, planning, naviga-
027   tion, and manipulation. This problem becomes particularly demanding in multi-room settings with
028   partial observability, where large parts of the environment are not visible at any given time. Such
029   scenarios are ubiquitous in everyday life, from tidying up households to organizing groceries, mak-
030   ing them critical for the development of next-generation home assistant robots.
031   Existing approaches to multi-object rearrangement typically fall into two categories: Reinforcement
032   Learning (RL) methods and hand-coded planning systems. RL methods often struggle as the prob-
033   lem becomes increasingly complex and lengthy, making it difficult to scale to more challenging sce-
034   narios. To address this limitation, many researchers have adopted a modular approach, decomposing
035   the task into a series of subtasks (Gu et al., 2022) such as manipulation skills, navigation skills, or
036   exploration skills. These subtasks are then sequenced together in different ways to accomplish the
037   overall goal. However, current modular approaches have their own limitations. Some pre-determine
038   the sequence in which to apply skills, while others use greedy planners (Trabucco et al., 2022). This
039
      constrains their potential for full optimization in terms of determining the optimal order to interact
      with objects and handling new problems, such as when another object blocks the path to an object or
040
      location or if the goal location itself is obstructed. To overcome these challenges, a more general ap-
041
      proach that incorporates high-level planning based on the current state would be beneficial. Such an
042   approach would enable the system to handle novel problems without requiring extensive re-learning
043   from scratch, thus increasing its adaptability and efficiency in diverse environments. Solving these
044   new problems is particularly important in the context of household robots, where obstacles, blocked
045   paths, and obstructed goals are common occurrences that a robust system must be able to handle
046   effectively.
047
      While significant progress has been made in rearrangement, the majority of current research focuses
048   on single-room settings or assumes that a large number of objects are visible at the beginning of
049   the task, either through a third-person bird’s eye view (Ghosh et al., 2022) or a first-person view
050   where most of the room is visible. However, as we move towards the more practical version of
051   the problems, such as cleaning a house, the majority of the space and objects to be manipulated
052   are not initially visible, and existing solutions begin to falter. This scenario of partial observability
053   introduces several major challenges: 1) uncertainty over object locations, as the starting positions of
      objects are unknown; 2) execution efficiency of searching for objects while simultaneously moving

055   rooms; 4) extensibility to scenarios involving blocked goals or obstructed paths; and 5) graceful

100
101   Rearrangement: Rearrangement is the problem of manipulating the placement of objects by pick-
102   ing, moving, and placing them according to a goal configuration. In this work, we are mainly
103   concerned with the rearrangement of objects by mobile agents in simulated environments such as
104
      AI2Thor and Habitat (Kolve et al., 2017),(Szot et al., 2021) and (Gan et al., 2020). We hypothesize
      that successful rearrangement planning in simulators with noisy sensors and effectors will go a long
105
      way towards successful planning and execution in robotics.
106
107   There are many versions of the rearrangement problem in literature. In tabletop rearrangement, a
      robot hand with a fixed base moves objects around to achieve a certain configuration in a limited


                                                           2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      space (Zhai et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024). Many current approaches for rearrangement by mobile
109   agents work by finding the misplaced objects and then use greedy planners to decide what order to
110   move the objects in (Gadre et al., 2022; Trabucco et al., 2022; Sarch et al., 2022). This can lead
111   to a high traversal cost since it is not explicitly optimized. The above works and others such as
112   (Mirakhor et al., 2024a) are also limited to a single-room setting where most objects are visible to
113   the agent.
114
      Rearrangement has also been studied from the Task and Motion Planning (TAMP) perspective (Gar-
115   rett et al., 2020a; 2021; 2020b). perspective. Garrett et al. (2020b) is limited to a single-room kitchen
116   problem and assumes perfect detection of objects. Unlike most previous work, our proposed solu-
117   tion optimizes the traversal cost and addresses multi-room settings and imperfect object detection
118   in an integrated POMDP framework. Tekin et al. (2023) and Mirakhor et al. (2024b) address the
119   multi-room rearrangement problem. However, the decision-making process of Tekin et al. (2023)
120   about when to explore and when to move an object is fixed and assumes perfect object detection.
121   Our framework optimizes, naturally handles exploration and manipulation, and addresses object de-
122   tector failures in a unified framework. Mirakhor et al. (2024b) make the assumption that objects are
123
      always on top of or inside containers. This limits its extendability to handling new problems, such
      as blocked paths where the objects could be in the path of another object without any containers.
124
      Our framework naturally allows for these new possibilities.
125
126   Object Oriented POMDP (OOPOMDP) Planning: Our work is partly inspired by Wandzel et al.
127   (2019b), who OOPOMDP and perform a multi-object search in a 2D environment. Zheng et al.
128   (2023) and Zheng et al. (2022) extend this formulation to object search in 3D environments. How-
129
      ever, they are limited to the task of object search. In our work, we build on their formulation of
      OOPOMDP and extend it to include rearrangement actions and their corresponding belief updates.
130
      We further extend this rearrangement OO-POMDP to HOO-POMDP through action abstraction.

      Environment and Agent: Our agent is developed for the AI2Thor simulator environment (Kolve
137
      et al., 2017). It consists of a simulated house with a set of objects located in one or more rooms. The
138
      agent can take the following low-level actions: As = (M oveAhead, M oveBack, M oveRight,
139   M oveLef t, RotateLef t, RotateRight, LookU p, LookDown, P ickObjecti , P laceObject,
140   Startloc , Done). The M ove actions move the agent by a distance of 0.25m in the environment.
141   The Rotate actions rotate the agent pitch by 90 degrees. The Look actions rotate the agent yaw
142   by 30 degrees. Start action starts the simulator and places the agent at the given location, and the
143   Done action ends the simulation. After executing any of the above actions, the simulator outputs
144   the following information: a) RGB and Depth images, 2) the agent’s position (x, y, pitch, yaw),
145   and 3) whether the action was successful. There are two types of objects in the world - interactable
146   objects and receptacle objects. The interactables are the ones that can be picked and placed. The
147
      receptacles are objects that are not movable but can hold other objects.
148   Task Setup: Rearrangement is done in 2 phases. Walkthrough phase and rearrange phase. The
149   walkthrough phase is meant to get information about stationary objects. The 2D occupancy map is
150   generated in this phase, as well as the corresponding 3D Map. We get the size of the house(width
151   and length) from the environment and uniformly sample points in the environment. We then take
152   steps to reach these locations (if possible - some might be blocked). This simple algorithm ensures
153
      we explore the full house. At each of the steps, we receive the RGB and Depth. Using this, we
      create a 3D point cloud at each step and combine them all to get the overall 3D point cloud of the
154
      house with stationary objects. We then discretize this point cloud into 3D map voxels of size 0.25m,
155
      we further flatten this 3D map into a 2D map of grid cells (location in the 2D map is occupied if
156   there exists a point at that 2D location at any height in the 3D map). While doing this traversal,
157   we also get information about the receptacles by detector on the RGB images we receive during
158   this traversal. This ends the walkthrough phase. (this walkthrough process needs to be done only
159   once for any house configuration of stationary objects - walls, doors, tables, etc.). Then, objects
160   are placed at random locations (done using AI2Thor environment reinitialization). This is when the
161   rearrangement phase begins, with the planner taking the following as input - the map generated in
      the walkthrough phase, the set of object classes to move, and their goal locations.

286

287

291   starting at h. The algorithm samples a state from the belief space b for the current history, and if

          Belief Update: Algorithm 2 presents the belief update function for our HOO-POMDP. The
353       UpdateBelief function takes as input the current belief state b, the performed action a, and the
354       received observation z. For each object i in the belief state and each possible state sij (j = 1, . . . , L,
355       where L is the set of all its possible locations in the 2D Map) of that object, the algorithm updates
356       the belief based on the action type. For navigation actions, it applies a probabilistic update using the
357       observation model p(zi |sij ) (Line 6). For ‘place’ actions, it sets the belief to 1 if the object’s state
358       matches its desired goal location gi , and 0 otherwise (Line 8). For ‘pick’ actions, it assigns a belief
359       of 1 if the object’s state corresponds to the agent’s location and 0 otherwise (Line 10).
360       Generating Abstract State: We now have a belief state over the set of all possible locations for each
361       object. We need to generate the abstract object-wise state consisting of object location information
362       and their corresponding pick-and-place information. The information that needs to be computed for
363       each object is as follows: picki , placelocs , is held, at goal.
364
          The value for is held comes from the previous low-level action and previous state. If the previous
365       state had is held as false and low-level action was to pick the object of interest, is held is set to
366       true. If the previous action was not a pick or a pick action for a different object, then the variable
367       remains unchanged. If the previous action was place and is held is true, then it is set to False.
368
          The value for at goal is copied from the previous state if the last low-level action was not the place
369
          action. If it was, and if is held was true in the previous state, then at goal is set to true.
370
371       The value for placelocs are sampled from the object goal location and three nearby receptacles as
372       alternate goal locations for the object. For each of these goal locations, a location from where the
373       object can be placed is sampled.
374       The location picki is sampled based on the belief distribution of where the object could be and is
375       the location from which the object can be picked. If the distribution over location is spread out, we
376       sample multiple locations (by ensuring each sampled location is far from the other sampled locations
377       for the same object). For both the locations in placelocs and for the location picki , we then check if
          they are reachable. If they are not, those locations are discarded.


                                                               7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      This sampling method enables our system to handle scenarios involving blocked goals, object swaps,
379   and obstructed paths effectively. If an object’s path is blocked, the planner will receive information
380   indicating that there is no accessible location from which to pick up the object, necessitating the
381   relocation of other objects first. When placing objects, we provide alternative receptacle locations.
382   This approach allows us to move an object to another location if its goal position is blocked, thereby
383   freeing up its current location. This strategy addresses both blocked goal and swap scenarios. Fur-
384   thermore, this sampling process enhances our system’s extensibility. We can incorporate additional
385   constraints based on new object properties. For example, if opening an object requires interaction
386   from a distance, the sampler can ensure that the sampled location is sufficiently far to enable suc-
387
      cessful opening.
388   After creating this abstract state, it is sent to the abstract planner, and the cycle starts again.

452   6      R ESULTS AND D ISCUSSION
453
454   Methods comparison: The perfect knowledge system is the best performance our agent can have -
455   there is no uncertainty in the world, and hence, it boils down to a path-traversal length optimization
456   problem. We can see from Table 1 that the results of the perfect detector setting and the perfect
457   knowledge setting are similar in the overall scene success rate as well as the object success rate.
458   This shows that the PPOMDP planner is able to explore and find all the objects perfectly. The
459
      failures in both cases are due to the low-level policies failing to pick/place objects. The difference
      between the methods can be seen in the steps taken. The perfect detector system has to explore and
460
      rearrange and hence ends up taking more steps than the perfect knowledge system. The third set
461
      of results are for our system with an imperfect detector and partial observability. We can see that
462   the detector failure causes our success rate to fall by a fair amount - this is because if an object is
463   not detected more than once at its location, the planner’s belief update prevents it from going to
464   that region to pick that object up. Since our detector only has a 50-60% success rate (different for
465   different classes of objects), it misses a fair subset of the objects, and hence the drop. As a result
466   of needing to explore more due to detector failure, the number of steps taken is greater than that of
467   the other methods. The difference between OURS and OURS-HP clearly shows the importance of
468   hierarchy and abstraction. The performance drop is significant across all settings for OURS-HP.
469   Comparison Across Datasets: We can see that the results of the RoomR dataset of a single room
470   and the Proc dataset with multiple rooms are similar (in scene success rate and object success rate).
471   This indicates that our method effectively manages the exploration of multiple rooms as well as a
472   reduction in the number of objects visible initially. Initial object visibility decreases when scaling
473   from 2 to 4 rooms in MultiRoomR with ten objects, yet success rates remain relatively stable. The
474   performance drops in our custom dataset with a larger number of objects. In particular, the object
475   success rate drops slightly, but the scene success rate drops significantly. This is because, for a scene
476
      to succeed, we need all objects to be rearranged correctly, so even if 9/10 objects are rearranged
      correctly, it is still considered a failure. Hence, there is a bigger reduction in scene success rate.
477
478   Across Different Challenges: From the different rows for each dataset (the difference being the
479   existence of blocked paths), we can see that the results for the blocked path version of the problem
480   are lower. This is because the low-level policy is not as good at picking the objects on the floor
481   as it is at picking the objects from the top of other objects. Also, the number of PickPlace actions
482
      increases as we tackle more complex problems such as swaps.
483   Error Analysis: The majority of failures of our system are due to low-level policy failures - the
484   pick or the place action fails due to the imperfections of the low-level RL policy. The other type of
485   failure in our system is due to belief estimation errors caused by detector failures - if a detector is
      looking at a certain location and it fails to detect an object multiple times (due to partial occlusion


                                                                9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      or high distance), then our belief about the object being in that region reduces to a large extent. In
487   that case, we are unlikely to come back to that part of the house. If a false positive happens, an agent
488   might pick up the wrong object and place it and believe it has placed the correct object - leading to
489   object and scene failure. These are the main causes of our failures in imperfect detector settings.
490
      Comparison to existing baselines: It is important to note that our system addresses a variant of
491
      the multi-object rearrangement problem that differs in key aspects from those tackled by existing
492
      baselines. Unfortunately, this means that direct comparisons are not meaningful or informative. The
493   primary distinction lies in the prior knowledge available to our system: we are given information
494   about the classes of objects to be moved, whereas other systems Mirakhor et al. (2024a) (Mirakhor
495   et al., 2024b), (Gadre et al., 2022) operate without this advantage. The motivation for it is that
496   when this information is not provided, agents must perform a walkthrough phase for each new goal
497   configuration to identify movable objects. In contrast, our formulation requires only a single initial
498   walkthrough to map stationary objects in the environment. Subsequently, our system can efficiently
499   handle multiple goal configurations without additional walkthroughs.
500   However, it is worth highlighting that our problem formulation introduces its own set of challenges.
501   In particular, while existing systems report initial visibility of approximately 60% ((Mirakhor et al.,
502   2024b), table 1) of target objects at the outset of their tasks, only about 20% of the objects are initially
503   visible in our problem settings, necessitating more extensive and strategic exploration. This reduced
504   initial visibility significantly increases the complexity of our task in terms of efficient exploration
505   and belief management. It underscores the importance and effectiveness of our hierarchical planning
506   approach in handling partial observability and perception uncertainty. The other key difference is we
507
      use low-level policies for navigation and manipulation, whereas other works Mirakhor et al. (2024b)
      and Mirakhor et al. (2024a) assume perfect navigation and manipulation capabilities.
508
509   While the difficulties faced by our system and existing baselines are not directly comparable, we
510   believe our results demonstrate the efficacy of our approach in solving a challenging and practically
511   relevant variant of the multi-object rearrangement problem. The performance across various metrics
512   and scenarios, as detailed in the preceding sections, showcases the robustness and scalability of our
513
      HOO-POMDP framework in environments with high uncertainty and limited initial information.
514   Limitations: Our system makes an object independence assumption - that the state and observation
515   of one object are not dependent on the state of other objects. However, this assumption doesn’t
516   always hold in cluttered environments. In such cases, observing, picking up, or placing an object
517   might be affected by the positions of nearby objects. As a result, our object-oriented belief update
518   system would need to be modified to handle these object interactions in more complex scenarios.
519
      HOO-POMDP cannot handle an unknown class of objects. It could potentially be handled by cate-
      gorizing all of the known object types into a single ‘unknown’ class. The difficult part, however, is
520
      to plan to find an empty space to move the unknown object to. In the worst case, this could lead to
521
      complicated packing problems that are NP-hard.

524   7    C ONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
525
526   In this paper, we presented a novel Hierarchical Object-Oriented POMDP Planner (HOO-POMDP)
527   for solving multi-object rearrangement problems in partially observable, multi-room environments.
528
      Our approach decomposes the complex task into a high-level abstract POMDP planner for generat-
      ing sub-goals and low-level policies for execution. Key components include an object-oriented state
529
      representation, belief updating to handle perception uncertainty, and an abstraction system to bridge
530
      the gap between continuous and discrete planning. Experimental results across multiple datasets
531   demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach in handling challenging scenarios such as blocked
532   paths and goals. The HOO-POMDP framework showed robust performance in terms of success rate
533   and efficiency comparable to Oracle baselines with perfect knowledge or perfect detection. Notably,
534   our method scaled well to environments with more objects and rooms. One of the ways to expand
535   the scope is to relax the assumption of object independence partially. We can allow objects to be
536   dependent on a small number of objects (e.g., objects in their close vicinity). Belief updates can
537   now consider a small set of objects at any time. This relaxation helps maintain the efficient belief
538   update while accounting for more real-world situations such as object-object interaction. Another
539
      potential future work is to handle stacking of objects and more cramped spaces, where more careful
      reasoning about object interactions is needed to plan the actions and order them appropriately.

798        A.4   Q UALITATIVE R ESULTS
799
           We test our system with different depths for the MCTS planner to see how much look-ahead affects
800
           the performance of our system. MCTS search depth is one of the important factors determining the
801        amount of exploration and the time taken for search at step. Results are shown in table 3. The depth
802        for the system - OURS is 12, and the depth for OURS-MCTS 1 is 1. From the table, we can see
803        that the greedy approach of just looking ahead by 1 step is not enough to solve the rearrangement
804        problem. This is because, when we look ahead only 1 step, we do not get enough reward/feedback
805        from the world about what is a good path to take and hence makes it extremely hard to solve the
806        problem. We can also see that, in the problems it does solve, it takes significantly longer to solve.
807        We did not compute the results for 15 and 20 objects in this setting as it failed to solve any problems
808        in 3-4 rooms with ten objects.
