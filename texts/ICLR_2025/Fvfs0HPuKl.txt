                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
               It is impossible today to pretend that the practice of machine learning is compatible
010
               with the idea that training and testing data follow the same distribution. Several
011
               authors have recently used ensemble techniques to show how scenarios involving
012            multiple data distributions are best served by representations that are both richer
013            than those obtained by regularizing for the best in-distribution performance, and
014            richer than those obtained under the influence of the implicit sparsity bias of
015            common stochastic gradient procedures.
016            This contribution investigates the use of very high dropout rates instead of en-
017            sembles to obtain such rich representations. Although training a deep network
018            from scratch using such dropout rates is virtually impossible, fine-tuning a large
019            pre-trained model under such conditions is not only possible but also achieves
020            out-of-distribution performances that exceed those of both ensembles and weight
021            averaging methods such as model soups.
022            This result has practical significance because the importance of the fine-tuning sce-
023            nario has considerably grown in recent years. This result also provides interesting
024
               insights on the nature of rich representations and on the intrinsically linear nature
               of fine-tuning a large network using a comparatively small dataset.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   The practice of machine learning has been shaped by the assumption that training and testing examples
030   are independently drawn from the same unknown probability distribution. This is seldom the case
031   in modern settings, not only because this i.i.d. assumption breaks down for the problems of interest,
032   but also because it is often convenient to use multiple datasets that are known to follow different
033   distributions. For instance, we may pre-train a deep network on a large dataset, fine-tune it on a
034   smaller dataset specific to the task of interest, and test on a collection of tasks designed to benchmark
035   various aspects of the system.
036   Many of the tenets of machine learning should therefore be regarded with healthy suspicion. For
037   instance, under the i.i.d. assumption, favoring solutions with sparse representations has well-known
038   benefits on the generalization performance. Yet, several authors (Zhang et al., 2022; Zhang & Bottou,
039   2023; Chen et al., 2023) make the point that scenarios involving multiple distributions are best served
040   by “richer representations” that contain redundant features, that is, features that do not improve
041   the model performance on the training distribution, but could prove helpful when the distribution
042   changes.
043   It would be nice to construct such rich representations by merely optimizing the expectation of
044   a suitable loss function for a single training distribution, for instance using stochastic gradient
045   techniques. Alas, this hope is contradicted by the implicit sparsity bias of stochastic gradient
046   algorithms (Andriushchenko et al., 2023; Blanc et al., 2020). In a nutshell, a feature only survives
047   when it brings an incremental training error advantage relative to what can be achieved using all
048   the other features already present in the network. We slightly abuse the terminology and call them
049
      “strongly relevant”. However, features that are not strongly relevant might nevertheless
050        (a) be incrementally useful when the data follows a different distributions of interest, or
051        (b) be useful under the training distribution when added to certain subsets of the other existing
052            features instead of all of them (“weakly relevant”).
053

      It is therefore tempting to “enrich" the representation with features of type (b), which can be found
055   using the training data, and hope that some of these will turn out to also be features of type (a) whose
056   inclusion helps when the data distribution changes.
057
058
      The dropout technique (Srivastava et al., 2014) seems well suited to finding weakly relevant features
      because randomly masking units of a representation layer during training amounts to forming random
059
      subsets of all other available features. However, in order to form small subsets, one would have to
060
      use very high levels of dropout. Unfortunately, training a sizable deep network from scratch with
061   such a large dropout is practically impossible. Instead, computationally demanding methods, such as
062   adversarial sampling (Zhang et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023) and representation ensembles (Zhang &
063   Bottou, 2023), have been proposed to find weakly relevant features while training a network from
064   scratch.
065
      There is however a practically meaningful scenario in which we can use an extremely aggressive
066
      dropout: fine-tuning a pre-trained network using a comparatively small dataset. This is possible
067   because such a fine-tuning operation makes only modest changes to the network weights. For example,
068   several authors (Ramé et al., 2022b; Wortsman et al., 2022a) argue that fine-tuned networks remain
069   “linearly connected”, that is averaging the parameters of multiple fine-tuned networks approximate
070   the ensemble of these networks. Evci et al. (2022) even show that a linear classifier on top of the
071   union of internal-layer features of pre-trained residual networks can match or exceed the performance
072   of fine-tuning.
073   In the present work, we adopt the out-of-distribution fine-tuning setup (three-distributions) of Ramé
074   et al. (2022b). In this framework, we have access to a model pre-trained using a large dataset for
075   a task weakly related to the task of interest. This pre-trained model is then fine-tuned on datasets
076   that illustrate the task of interest, and then tested on a dataset for the same task but with a different
077   distribution. However, instead of enriching the representations by constructing ensembles (Zhang &
078   Bottou, 2023) or averaging weights (Ramé et al., 2022b;a; Wortsman et al., 2022b), we simply fine-
079   tune using very large dropout levels, randomly masking above 90% of the units in the representation
080   layer. We find that this simple approach exceeds the performance of both ensemble and weight-
081
      averaging methods. This result is not only practically meaningful, but also clarifies the idea of richer
      representation.
082
083
084   2    R ELATED W ORK
085
086   Constructing versatile representations Reusing or transferring features across related tasks has
087   been commonplace for more than one decade (Collobert et al., 2011; Bottou, 2011; Sharif Razavian
088   et al., 2014) and plays a fundamental role in the appeal of foundational models (Bommasani et al.,
089   2021a). However, once the optimization process has identified a set of features that is sufficient
090
      to achieve near-optimal performance on the training set, additional features are often discarded
      because they do not bring an incremental benefit to the training error, despite the fact that they may
091
      independently carry useful information (Zhang & Bottou, 2023).
092
093   Researchers have devised ways to obtain more versatile representations by engineering a diversity of
094   datasets, architectures, and even hyper-parameters (Chen et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2022; Dvornik
095   et al., 2020; Bilen & Vedaldi, 2017; Gontijo-Lopes et al., 2021; Li et al., 2021; 2022; Chowdhury
096
      et al., 2021), as an alternative to the most popular approach which consists of simply using ever larger
      datasets (Bommasani et al., 2021b).
097
098   Interesting results have also been obtained without engineering diversity and without increasing the
099   dataset sizes. Zhang et al. (2022) and Chen et al. (2023) propose to discover rich representation
100   through multiple training episodes that adversarially reweigh the training dataset to impede the use
101   of previously learned features. Zhang & Bottou (2023) show that surprisingly good results can be
102
      obtained by concatenating the representations of multiple networks that are trained in exactly the
      same way, save for the random seed used in the stochastic gradient process.
103
104
      Fine-tuning as a near-linear process Although modern deep residual networks feature highly
105
      complex nonconvex cost functions, several authors have shown that their final training phase remains
106
      mostly confined to a nearly-convex attraction basin (Izmailov et al., 2018; Li et al., 2018; Frankle
107   et al., 2020). The same observation holds when fine-tuning a large pre-trained network using a dataset


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      whose size is considerably smaller than the dataset size one would need to train such a large network
109   from scratch. As long as one starts from the same pre-trained model, Wortsman et al. (2022a) and
110   Ramé et al. (2022b;a) observe that averaging the weights of diverse fine-tuned models can reproduce
111   the i.i.d. and o.o.d. performances of the ensemble of these models, implying that fine-tuning is a
112   near-linear process.
113
      Maddox et al. (2021) and Mu et al. (2019) propose instead to approximate the fine-tuning process
114
      with a first-order Taylor expansion, obtaining a linear system operating on top of the NTK features.
115   Evci et al. (2022) match the performance of fine-tuning by merely learning a strongly regularized
116   linear model that takes all internal layer states as inputs. Meanwhile, Yu et al. (2023) efficiently
117   fine-tune large foundational language models by essentially restricting the weight updates to low
118   dimensional manifolds.
119
120   Fine-tuning with very large dropout Our contribution advocates using very large dropout in the
121   fine-tuning scenario in order to force the learning algorithm to create a redundant representation
122   without specifically engineering diversity. We do not seek to propose new dropout variations (Chu
123   et al., 2022), understand dropout from either an overfitting/underfitting perspective (Liu et al., 2023)
124   or from a Bayesian perspective (Gal & Ghahramani, 2016).

126   3       F INE - TUNING AND DROPOUT
127
128   3.1     T HE THREE - DISTRIBUTIONS SETUP
129
130   The two-distributions setup is a commonly used for transfer learning. In this setup, features Ψ are
131   obtained by pre-training a network on a large training set associated with a first distribution Tp . These
132
      features are then used to construct or initialize a new model ωd ◦ Ψ, which is then trained using a
      smaller training set associated with a second distribution Td . The question is to determine which
133
      pre-training approach is most likely to make the features Ψ useful for the transfer task Td .
134
135   The three-distributions setup (Ramé et al., 2022b) views the pre-trained model as a base model that is
136   assumed very rich but whose training process is beyond our control (e.g., a fundational model). The
137   features Ψ of the pre-trained model are then incorporated into a new model ωd ◦ Ψ that is fine-tuned
138   using a second distribution Td and eventually tested on a third distribution T̃d illustrating the same
139   general task as the second distribution (e.g., using the same classification labels.) The question is
140
      then to determine which fine-tuning approach is most likely to produce a model that will perform
141
      robustly under the eventual testing distribution T̃d .
142
143   3.2     M ETHOD
144
      The key results described later in this paper have been obtained with a very simple method. The base
145
      model is a deep learning network with residual connections trained on data Tp that is related to but
146   substantially larger than the datasets illustrating the task of interest. Some of these datasets (Td ) are
147   used to fine-tune the base model. Performance is reported on both held-out data from the fine-tuning
148   datasets (i.i.d. performance on Td ) and data from the remaining datasets (o.o.d. performance on T̃d ).
149
150
      We focus on residual networks because fine-tuning has been found to hardly change the inner layers
      of non-residual networks (Raghu et al., 2019, fig 2). In contrast, skip connections in residual networks
151
      expose the inner block features in such a manner that the fine-tuning process can utilize these features
152
      in a near-linear way (Evci et al., 2022).
153
154   Fine-tuning is carried out with a standard stochastic learning procedure (e.g. SGD or ADAM) after
155   applying a very large dropout to the penultimate layer representation Φ. Unlike (Srivastava et al.,
156
      2014), we only apply dropout on the penultimate layer representation Φ,1 because skip connections
      in residual networks expose many inner-layer features to the last linear layer, as illustrated by the

176   4       E XPERIMENTS
177
178   We perform most experiments using PACS (Li et al., 2017), VLCS (Fang et al., 2013), OFFICE HOME
179   (Venkateswara et al., 2017), and TERRA INCOGNITA (Beery et al., 2018) datasets. These datasets
180   are part of the D OMAIN B ED suite which has been widely used for this kind of experiments.2 With
181
      9, 991 to 24, 788 examples, they are substantially smaller than the pre-training dataset I MAGE N ET
      with 1.2M examples. We also use the larger DOMAINNET dataset (Peng et al., 2019), 0.58M examples,
182
      to show that linear connectivity breaks down when the fine-tuning dataset size becomes comparable
183
      with the pre-training dataset size and justifies carrying out many more fine-tuning iterations.
184
185   Each of these datasets is divided into four sub-datasets that share the same target label categories but
186   follow a different distribution. For example, one sub-dataset of PACS contains simple sketch images
187
      of ‘dog’ and ‘elephant’, while another sub-dataset contains real photos of ‘dog’ and ‘elephant’. This
      makes it possible to conveniently evaluate o.o.d. performance by fine-tuning on three sub-datasets
188
      and testing on the fourth one.
189
190   Fine-tuning experiments are carried out on two widely used residual architectures: convolutional
191   residual networks, R ES N ETs (He et al., 2016), and vision transformers, V I Ts (Dosovitskiy et al.,
192   2020). Unless otherwise stated, all convolutional residual network experiments are carried out using
193
      the R ES N ET 50 neural network pre-trained on I MAGE N ET with substantial data augmentations3 such
      as TRIVIALAUGMENT (Müller & Hutter, 2021), CUTMIX (Yun et al., 2019), and RANDOM ERASINGS
194
      (Zhong et al., 2020). These augmentations mimic the properties of large foundational models that
195
      learn substantial diverse features using very large and diverse pre-training data. We refer to this
196   network as R ES N ET 50 #2 as opposed to the original R ES N ET 50 #1 recipe described by He et al.
197   (2016). Similarly, all visual transformer fine-tuning experiments leverage a I MAGE N ET pre-trained
198   V I T-L-16 model4 with 304M parameters.

202   Using these same datasets, Gulrajani & Lopez-Paz (2020) argue that plain Empirical Risk Minimiza-

      that ensemble and weight averaging methods consistently outperform the o.o.d. performance of ERM.
      We now show that fine-tuning with very large dropout outperforms the o.o.d. performance of these
207
      state-of-the-art methods.5

451   4.3   R ICHER PRE - TRAINING BEATS SOPHISTICATED FINE - TUNING
452
453   We have demonstrated that the very-large dropout method delivers consistently better o.o.d. perfor-
454   mance than computing ensembles or weight-averages of models fine-tuned without dropout. However
455   we also have argued that fine-tuning does not create new representations but merely exploits the
456   representations already present in the pre-trained model. Therefore the final o.o.d. performance of
457
      this fine-tuning process must strongly depend on the quality and the diversity of the features present
      in the pre-trained network (richer representation), even if these features are not exploited by the
458
      pre-trained network but buried in its hidden layers.
459
460   To validate this assertion, we compare the i.i.d. and o.o.d. performance obtained by various methods
461   applied to R ES N ET 50 networks pre-trained using the same I MAGE N ET data but using different data
462   augmentation schemes. As explained in the first paragraphs of section 4, the results reported so far
463
      use a network pre-trained using a broad array of data augmentation techniques, termed R ES N ET #2.
      We now compare its fine-tuning properties with network termed R ES N ET #1 pre-trained using the
464
      simpler protocol described in He et al. (2016).
465
466   Figure 3 compares the o.o.d. performances of both networks after regular fine-tuning and after fine-
467   tuning with all the available tricks, that is, with dropout, with 10× larger last layer learning rate, and
468   after averaging the weights of checkpoints collected along the fine-tuning trajectory. This comparison
469
      makes clear that the quality of the pre-trained representation matters more than the sophistication of
      the fine-tuning techniques. This is consistent with the idea that fine-tuning only leverages the existing
470
      features of the pre-trained network and does not create new ones.
471
472
473   5     D ISCUSSION
474
475   The o.o.d. performance of fine-tuning with very large dropout consistently exceeds that achieved
476   by popular techniques such as ensemble and by more recent techniques such as weight averaging.
477
      Furthermore, ensemble and weight averaging techniques only bring a small incremental improvement
      when applied on top of fine-tuning with large dropout rates. This suggests that very large dropout
478
      implements a key factor that favors o.o.d. performance, which we believe is related to seeking features
479
      of type (a) among features of type (b) as explained in the introduction.
480
481   Both ensemble and weight-averaging techniques can be used for training a network from scratch or
482   for fine-tuning a pre-trained network. In contrast, very large dropout rates cannot be realistically used
483
      when training a network from scratch. We argue that they work for fine-tuning because fine-tuning
      is well approximated as a linear process that can leverage their existing or buried features of a
484
      pre-trained network but cannot create new ones. Using large dropout rates is akin to a form of L2
485
      regularization, expressing a richer set of features even if redundant.


                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      This result also illustrates how the i.i.d. and o.o.d. scenarios can call for very different techniques. It is
487   well known that sparse representations can be very helpful in the i.i.d. scenario, and it is increasingly
488   clear that rich representations are preferable in the o.o.d. scenario (Zhang et al., 2022; Zhang &
489   Bottou, 2023; Chen et al., 2023). There are no reasons to expect that the many techniques designed
490   for the i.i.d. scenarios will systematically help o.o.d. generalization. The very-large dropout case is
491   one of many such examples.

786   B.1   R ESIDUAL BLOCK DROPOUT ( STOCHASTIC DEPTH ) IN O . O . D . FINE - TUNING
787
      Huang et al. (2016) shows dropping a residual block at random with probability p helps train very
788
      deep residual neural networks from scratch. Dropping a residual block, for example f1 (x), in Φ(x)
789   blinds the feature ϕ1 (x) but also changes the input of successive layers:
790
791
792                              Φ′ (x) = x + f1 (x) + f2 (x + f1 (x)) + . . .
793                                      |{z} | {z } |       {z      }
                                          ϕ0 (x)   ϕ1 (x)      ϕ′2 (x)
794
795   So an aggressive residual block dropout heavily disturbs the input of high residual blocks, hurts the
796   fine-tuning process. Figure 6 showcase the effect of redisual block dropout in o.o.d. fine-tuning.
