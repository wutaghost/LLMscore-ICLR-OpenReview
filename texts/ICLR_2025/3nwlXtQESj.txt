009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Geometric deep learning (GDL) has demonstrated enormous power in molecular
012            data analysis. However, GDL faces challenges in achieving high efficiency and
013            expressivity in molecular representations when high-order terms of the atomic
014            force fields are not sufficiently learned. In this work, we introduce message pass-
015            ing on path complexes, called the Path Complex Message Passing, for molecu-
016            lar prediction. Path complexes represent the geometry of paths and can model
017            the chemical and non-chemical interactions of atoms in a molecule across vari-
018
               ous dimensions. Our model defines messages on path complexes and employs
               neural message passing to learn simplex features, enabling feature communica-
019
               tion within and between different dimensions. Since messages on high-order
020
               and low-order path complexes reflect different aspects of molecular energy, they
021            are updated sequentially according to their order. The higher the order of the
022            path complex, the richer the information it contains, and the higher its prior-
023            ity during inference. It can thus characterize various types of molecular in-
024            teractions specified in molecular dynamics (MD) force fields. Our model has
025            been extensively validated on benchmark datasets and achieves state-of-the-art
026            results. The code is available at https://anonymous.4open.science/
027            r/Path-Complex-Neural-Network-32D6

032   Accurate prediction of molecular properties is crucial in fields such as drug design Zhang et al.
033
      (2017); Chen et al. (2018); Mak & Pichika (2019); Chan et al. (2019), biology Townshend et al.
      (2021); Jamasb et al. (2022), chemistry Qiao et al. (2022), and materials science Vlassis et al.
034
      (2020). Geometric Deep Learning (GDL) has demonstrated significant potential in molecular sci-
035
      ences, leading to a surge in studies employing GDL models for effective molecular representation
036   learning Bronstein et al. (2017); Atz et al. (2021); Ingraham et al. (2023). Among the three types of
037   representations used in GDL models—topological, geometric, and functional—the molecular graph
038   has become the most popular due to its simplicity, flexibility, and efficiency Wieder et al. (2020);
039   Yu & Gao (2022); Atz et al. (2021); Li et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2022b). However, relying solely
040   on graph representations fails to capture the many-body interactions inherent in complex systems,
041   thereby limiting the expressiveness and predictive power of this approach Bodnar et al. (2021b).
042   This paper develops a path complex-based neural message passing for molecule prediction, where
043   the molecular energy of force field can be well represented.
044   In Graph Neural Networks (GNNs), the molecular graph is typically constructed based on covalent
045   bonds. Node features are usually derived from atomic properties and are updated by aggregating
046   information from neighboring nodes Huang et al. (2020); Shindo & Matsumoto (2019); Shui &
047   Karypis (2020a); Schütt et al. (2017); Unke & Meuwly (2019). To enhance GNN performance,
048   researchers have proposed several approaches. One major strategy is to design more complex
049   molecular graphs that incorporate non-covalent interactions. The most common method involves
050
      introducing edges between any two atoms within a specified cutoff distance, effectively capturing
      non-covalent interactions. Additionally, molecule-based line graph models have been developed,
051
      where nodes represent atomic bonds and edges represent bond angles Choudhary & DeCost (2021).
052
053   The second approach focuses on incorporating global physical features and local geometric infor-
      mation into GNN models. Global physical attributes such as temperature, pressure, and entropy

      In this work, we develop path complex-based molecular representation and path complex message
102   passing (PCNN) model for molecular property analysis. PCNN is a neural message passing Gilmer
103   et al. (2017) on path complices. A path is a sequence of points, and a path complex is a subset of all
104   possible paths. In the context of a molecule or molecular graph, a path corresponds to the geometry
105   defined by chemical or non-chemical bonds. Our path complexes are specifically designed — based
106   on molecular graphs that include both covalent and non-covalent bonds — to characterize different
107   types of energy specified in molecular dynamics (MD) force fields, as shown in Figure 1. The MD
      potential energy Mayo et al. (1990); González (2011); Leach (2001) comprises bond terms (EB ,


                                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      two-body interactions), bond-angle terms (EA , three-body interactions), and dihedral-angle terms
109   (ET , four-body interactions), which are effectively characterized by our 1-path, 2-path, and 3-path
110   features, respectively.
111
112
      Each path complex is assigned a message. Similar to classical neural message passing on graphs
      Gilmer et al. (2017) and simplicial complexes Bodnar et al. (2021a;b), the propagation of messages
113
      in a path complex is influenced by the messages of its “adjacent” path complexes at different orders.
114
      A higher-order path complex contains longer paths and includes the shorter paths of lower-order path
115   complexes. Therefore, we need to update messages according to the order of the path complexes:
116   messages in higher-order path complexes have priority in being updated. However, since path com-
117   plexes of adjacent orders are interconnected, we incorporate interactions between higher-order and
118   lower-order path complex information during message passing. Specifically, the higher-order mes-
119   sage first updates the lower-order one, and then the updated lower-order message exerts a reverse
120   effect on the higher-order information. Figure 2 illustrates a neural message passing process among
121   path complexes of different orders designed based on this principle.
122   PCNN thus enables information passing between path complex features, using the aggregated in-
123   formation to predict molecular properties. Testing on benchmark datasets demonstrates promising
124   performance. Our contributions are as follows:
125
126        1. We have developed a path complex-based molecular representation that explicitly charac-
127           terizes different terms in the molecular dynamics (MD) force field for molecular property
128           prediction.
129        2. We propose constructing path complexes using the unique topologies of graphs, simpli-
130           cial complexes, and hypergraphs. This method, based on path complex message passing,
131           enables the exchange of information across multiple dimensions.
132        3. Our PCMP model has been rigorously tested and validated on benchmark molecular tasks,
133           consistently achieving state-of-the-art results.

      3     PATH C OMPLEX M ESSAGE PASSING
186
187
      Path complex was originally developed on directed graph (or digraph) and set, by Grigoryan, Lin,
188   Muranov and Yau in 2012 Grigor’yan et al. (2012). They also proposed a new homology theory
189   for path complex, called path homology, and use it to explore topological invariant information of
190   digraphs Grigor’yan et al. (2014). Mathematically, path homology provides a novel framework to
191   systematically explore intrinsic topological information of more general structures Grigor’yan et al.
192   (2019); Grigor’yan et al. (2020). Details of path complex and path homology can be found in the
193   Appendix B.
194   Here we propose a generalized way to construct path complex based on undirected graph, simplicial
195   complex, and hypergraph. On undirected graph, we propose graph collapse and expansion oper-
196   ations, and use them to systematically study graph isomorphism by their path complex homology
197   groups. We found that the path complex homology is a graph weak isomorphism invariant. For
198   simplicial complex and hypergraph, we propose simplex- and hyperedge- based path complex.

      Definition 3.1 (Path). Given a simple undirected graph G = (V, E) over the verset set V , an n-path
      σn of G is defined as any sequence of n+1 vertices v0 v1 · · · vn (vi ∈ V ) such that every two vertices
206
      are distinct and every two adjacent vertices form an edge.
207
208   Note that for each n-path σn = v0 v1 · · · vn , σn′ = vn · · · v1 v0 is also an n-path, we identify these
209   two paths as the same one. For an n-path σn = v0 · · · vn , the (n − 1)-paths by removing the first
210   or last vertex, denoted by ∂σLn and ∂σRn respectively, are called the faces of σn . Two n-paths are
211   neighbors if they are faces of a common (n + 1)-path. Let N (σn ) be the set of neighbors of σn .
212   Definition 3.2 (Path complex from undirected graphs). Given a simple undirected graph G =
213   (V, E), all paths of G form a path complex PG . We call PG the path complex derived from G.
214   Theorem 3.3 (Path Complex Invariance). The graph neural network is invariant to the permutation
215   of the simplexes in the path complex PG , meaning the output of the network remains unchanged
      under any permutation π of the vertices.

      3.2   M OLECULAR PATH C OMPLEX R EPRESENTATION AND PATH F EATURES

277   Molecular Path Complex Representation Currently, covalent-bond molecular graphs serve as
278   the standard for molecular topological representations. These graphs underpin the molecular force
279   fields used in molecular dynamics simulations, incorporating terms for both covalent bonds—such
280   as bond lengths, angles, and dihedral angles—and non-covalent interactions like electrostatic and
281   van der Waals forces. To enhance molecular representations with comprehensive force field data,
282   we introduce the molecular path complex. This model utilizes path simplices across different di-
283   mensions to distinctly represent both covalent and non-covalent bond terms. As depicted in Figure
284
      5 (in Appendix A.1), the C2 H6 O molecule is illustrated alongside its corresponding path simplices.
      Specifically, our 1-path simplex captures bond lengths, the 2-path simplex details bond angles, and
285
      the 3-path simplex reflects dihedral angles.
286
287
288
      Path Features Our path (simplex) features are meticulously designed to encapsulate the various
      atomic properties and interactions detailed in molecular dynamics (MD) force fields. Specifically,
289
      our 0-path features—comprising atomic number, radius, and electronegativity—are derived using
290
      Rdkit, akin to the approach in CGCNN Xie & Grossman (2018). Table 7 (in Appendix A.1) presents
291   a comprehensive listing of our 1-path, 2-path, and 3-path features. Importantly, our model employs
292   detailed local geometric properties of the path complex as path features. This method allows us to
293   explicitly learn covalent bond terms defined in the MD force fields, while also implicitly capturing
294   non-bond interactions.

296   3.3   M OLECULAR PCMP MODEL
297
298   Path Complex Message Passing (PCMP) introduces a novel method for message passing in graphs
299   by leveraging path complexes, which are composed of paths of varying lengths. In contrast to
300   traditional graph neural networks (GNNs) that primarily aggregate information from local neighbors,
301   PCMP prioritizes message propagation along higher-order path complexes. By incorporating longer
302   paths, PCMP effectively captures long-range dependencies within the graph, enhancing its ability to
303
      model complex relationships.
304   A key feature of PCMP is the hierarchical message passing mechanism between different orders of
305   path complexes. First, messages in higher-order paths are updated, reflecting the broader structure
306   of the graph. These updated messages are then propagated to lower-order paths, ensuring that global
307   information from longer paths informs to lower-order paths. After this, a feedback mechanism is
308   employed, where updated messages from lower-order paths influence the higher-order paths, thus
309
      refining the representation at all levels. This bidirectional interaction between higher- and lower-
      order path allows PCMP to seamlessly integrate both global and local information effectively. The
310
      Path Message Passing Module can be found in Appendix A.2.

      4.2   R ESULTS
358
359   The comparison of our PCMP model with existing models on benchmark datasets is presented in Ta-
360   ble 1. Detailed parameter settings for PCMP can be found in Section A.4 (Appendix A). Our PCMP
361   model shows a significant performance advantage across datasets, mainly due to its advanced fea-
362   ture extraction capabilities and superior recognition of complex molecular structures. The message-
363   passing mechanism in PCMP is organized into two distinct layers: the upper embedding, which
364   considers upper adjacent neighbors, and the lower embedding, which incorporates both upper ad-
365   jacent and face neighbors. This dual-layered approach integrates path information from multiple
366
      perspectives, enhancing the model’s ability to capture both local and global graph structures. Each
      path is updated not only based on the features of its constituent nodes but also by incorporating in-
367
      formation from both higher-order and lower-order connected paths. This sophisticated mechanism
368
      enables the model to detect subtle structural variations within molecules that are often difficult to
369   distinguish. Compared to models that rely heavily on traditional pre-training, PCMP reduces com-
370   putational demands by bypassing extensive pre-training phases while achieving excellent results,
371   making it a promising solution for molecular property prediction.

      5   C ONCLUSION
487
488
      In this study, we introduced the path complex message passing, a novel model for molecular struc-
489   ture representation based on path complexes, designed to predict molecular properties. By inte-
490   grating force fields with path complexes, the model enhances our understanding of the relationship
491   between molecular structure and function, offering valuable insights for both theoretical research
492   and practical applications in molecular design and materials science. The PCMP model employs
493   0-paths for atomic properties, 1-paths for pairwise interactions, 2-paths for bond angle terms, and
494   3-paths for dihedral angle information. These paths are used to compute attention scores, enabling
495   efficient message propagation and feature integration across various levels of molecular information.
496   Validation on five benchmark datasets has demonstrated the PCMP’s superior predictive capabilities.
497
      Ablation studies further confirm that incorporating higher-order features significantly improves per-
      formance, pointing to promising directions for future research in molecular simulation and design.

867   passing module, where path features are updates based on path neighbors (same order paths), co-
868   faces (higher-order paths), and faces (lower-order paths). Mathematically, each n-path will always

       Definition B.2 (Path complex Grigor’yan et al. (2012)). A path complex P over the vertex set
1027   V is a collection of elementary paths of V such that ∀σn = v0 v1 · · · vn ∈ P , v1 · · · vn ∈
1028   P, v0 v1 · · · vn−1 ∈ P .
