011                                          Abstract
012
013           Foundation models have become a cornerstone in deep learning, with tech-
014           niques like Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) offering eﬀicient fine-tuning of
015           large models. Similarly, methods such as Retrieval-Augmented Genera-
016           tion (RAG), which leverage vectorized databases, have further improved
017
              model performance by grounding outputs in external information. While
              these approaches have demonstrated notable success, they often require
018
              extensive training or labeled data, which can limit their adaptability in
019
              resource-constrained environments. To address these challenges, we in-
020           troduce Retrieval-based Parameter Ensemble (RPE), a new method that
021           creates a vectorized database of LoRAs, enabling eﬀicient retrieval and ap-
022           plication of model adaptations to new tasks. RPE minimizes the need for
023           extensive training and eliminates the requirement for labeled data, making
024           it particularly effective for zero-shot learning. Additionally, RPE is well-
025           suited for privacy-sensitive domains like healthcare, as it modifies model
026           parameters without accessing raw data. When applied to tasks such as
027           medical report generation and image segmentation, RPE not only proved
028
              effective but also surpassed supervised fine-tuning methods in certain cases,
              highlighting its potential to enhance both computational eﬀiciency and pri-
029
              vacy in deep learning applications.

      1   Introduction
033
034
      In recent years, foundation models such as CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), LLaMA (Touvron
035
      et al., 2023) and SAM (Kirillov et al., 2023) have captured significant attention for their
036   ability to handle various tasks with minimal adaptation. Pre-trained on large datasets,
037   these models have been successfully applied in fields such as natural language processing,
038   computer vision, and healthcare, driving major advancements in artificial intelligence (Shu
039   et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2024; Rezayi et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024).
040
      However, fine-tuning these large models for specific tasks remains resource-intensive, of-
041
      ten requiring substantial computational power and large-scale data. Low-Rank Adaptation
042   (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021) offers a solution by freezing most of the model parameters and
043   fine-tuning only a small portion, significantly reducing the computational overhead while
044   maintaining high performance. This is especially valuable in resource-constrained environ-
045   ments. Nonetheless, LoRA and similar methods are still susceptible to hallucinations—
046   where the model generates plausible but inaccurate content—which can undermine the re-
047   liability of predictions. To address hallucination, Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG)
048   (Lewis et al., 2020) incorporates an external retrieval step, grounding model outputs in
049   factual data. Additionally, RAG excels at zero-shot learning, allowing models to handle
050
      tasks or categories without prior exposure. This is particularly important in healthcare,
      where models may need to recognize new diseases or interpret unfamiliar medical data with
051
      minimal labeled examples, accelerating diagnostic advancements.
052

115   2   Related Work
116
117   We review related work on RAG, parameter combination methods, and zero-shot learning,
118   highlighting key advancements and differences from our approach.
119
      RAG integrates external knowledge into large language models (LLMs) by retrieving rel-
120   evant information to enhance generation accuracy (Ma et al., 2023). Recent advancements
121   focus on optimizing query prompting, indexing structures, and retrieval mechanisms (Ma
122   et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2022), addressing limitations of naive RAG ap-
123   proaches. These improvements enhance retrieval precision and reduce hallucinations in gen-
124   erated outputs, especially in low-resource domains. For instance, (Seo et al., 2024) leverages
125   retrieved instances to generate new training samples with LLMs, mitigating data scarcity
126   in specialized areas. Similarly, (Parvez et al., 2022) expands positive examples in privacy
127   policy question-answering tasks through retriever models. However, reliance on external
128
      data introduces challenges related to privacy and computational constraints, limiting ap-
      plicability in certain scenarios. For instance, some RAG methods used in LLMs retrieve
129
      raw data for the input to improve prompt quality. Others retrieve data in the feature
130
      space, which can still pose significant data privacy concerns, particularly when dealing with
131   sensitive datasets such as those in the medical domain. In contrast, our method retrieves
132   representations of tasks rather than specific data, ensuring the preservation of data privacy
133   while still enabling effective task-specific adaptations.
134
      Parameter Combination Methods Various methods have been developed to combine
135
      model parameters to enhance performance, robustness, and generalization. However, most
136
      current methods still require additional data for fine-tuning or additional neural network
137   evaluations for optimization. A more detailed comparison can be found in Appendix A.1.
138
139   We aim to focus on parameter combination methods without labeled data and additional
140
      neural network evaluations. One such method is Model Soup (Wortsman et al., 2022), which
      simplifies model combination through parameter averaging. Another method is Federated
141
      Learning (FL) (McMahan et al., 2017), which focuses on distributed learning. In FL, mul-
142
      tiple devices train models locally on their own data, and only parameter updates are sent
143   to a central server, which aggregates them into a global model. This decentralized setup
144   preserves privacy, making FL ideal for privacy-sensitive applications. FL often incorporates
145   secure protocols and privacy-enhancing techniques, such as secret sharing (Cheng et al.,
146   2021), to ensure data security.
147
      Zero-shot Learning is a machine learning technique where a model is trained to recognize
148
      objects, categories, or concepts that it has not seen during training (Wang et al., 2019;
149   Xian et al., 2017; Fu et al., 2018). This technique relies on the transfer of knowledge from
150   known (seen) tasks to unknown (unseen) tasks by utilizing shared attributes or semantic
151   relationships. In the realm of zero-shot learning, a model must from familiar tasks, denoted
152   as Tiref with corresponding parameters θiref to a novel task T trg . This process requires a
153   specific task representation ziref , which is often extracted from prior knowledge sources such
154   as textual data or structured entities. Notable studies in this field have employed neural
155   networks to facilitate the mapping A from zi to θi . For instance, DeViSE (Frome et al., 2013)
156   used a linear mapping from image features to a joint embedding space. GCN-ZL (Wang
157   et al., 2018) utilized Graph Neural Networks to map from word embeddings to semantic
158
      embeddings. DGP-ZL (Kampffmeyer et al., 2019) introduced Dense Graph Propagation to
      learn mappings from word embeddings to semantic embeddings.
159
160   Our work leverages pretrained models to obtain representations zi , and replaces the tradi-
161   tional neural network approach with a retrieval and algorithm-based method to perform the
      mapping A. This not only simplifies the generalization process but also improves the adapt-

      ability of the model to new, unseen tasks. By combining advanced retrieval techniques with
163   pretrained models, our method offers a scalable and eﬀicient alternative to conventional
164   zero-shot learning approaches, particularly beneficial where acquiring labeled data for all
165   potential classes is impractical.

      3.1   Construction of LoRA-VecDB
193
194   The vectorized database, named LoRA-VecDB, stands as a central repository that catalogs
195   LoRAs {δθi } and their corresponding representations {zi } for various tasks. This database
196   not only facilitates accessibility but also encourages ongoing contributions from the com-
197   munity, maintaining a collaborative and up-to-date resource.
198   For each specific dataset Di , a LoRA δθi is trained using the foundation model F (·, θ0 ).
199   LoRA achieves this by freezing the pre-trained model weights and introducing trainable low-
200   rank matrices into each layer, significantly reducing the number of parameters required for
201   adaptation. This process also generates a representation zi , capturing the essential features
202   or transformations unique to Di . Typically, the representation zi is derived directly from
203   the feature map of F ’s encoder, maintaining a raw projection of data features. However,
204   for enhanced interpretability and to manage multiple adaptations, an additional encoder
205   can be employed to refine these features into a more contextually appropriate form. This
206
      strategy draws from techniques such as RAG, where specialized encoders are employed to
      effectively handle large datasets.
207
208   In our application, unless explicitly stated, we utilize the feature map output from the
209   encoder of F , denoted as E F (xj , θ0 ), for individual data items xj , which may represent an
210   image or a document. This approach aligns with the strategy used in the encoder component
211   of the MoE, where feature maps serve a pivotal role in the model architecture. It is crucial
212
      to emphasize that these feature maps are utilized in their original form, without any fine-
      tuning, ensuring that the integrity and the originality of the model’s initial pre-training are
213
      maintained.
214
215   For simplicity and practicality in representing dataset features, we initially explored us-
      ing various distribution distance metrics, such as the Chamfer distance (Borgefors, 1986),

217   to measure similarities between datasets. However, these metrics did not show significant

219   the features of dataset Di by averaging all associated data feature maps:

464   4.4.1    Nearest LoRA vs. Ensemble Methods
465
      A natural concern arises regarding whether it is more effective to use a model trained on
466
      the most similar dataset directly, or to employ a fusion of parameters. In this context,
467   we explore a boundary scenario where we select only the nearest dataset’s LoRA during
468   retrieval, effectively setting k = 1 in a k-NN search.
469
470
      Results from different datasets displayed in Table 5 reveal that relying solely on the most
      similar training set exhibit highly variable outcomes. Compared to the ensemble approach,
471
      using a single model tends to result in overfitting to the specific dataset it was trained on.
472
      For a more detailed discussion and numerical analysis, please refer to Table 15 in Appendix
473   A.3. This suggests that integrating multiple models might provide a more robust and stable
474   performance across diverse datasets.

513
514
      From the experiments, it is evident that our approach yields promising results. An overall
515
      analysis based on the experimental section reveals that the RPE model significantly enhances
516   the adaptability and eﬀiciency of foundational models in tasks where labeled data is scarce
517   or unavailable.
518
519
      However, there are still some limitations to consider. Due to the limited number of LoRAs
      available, some aspects of our architecture merit further discussion. One such aspect is
520
      the potential for improving the encoder used to derive the representation z. This could
521
      involve utilizing a pre-trained model or specifically training an encoder to optimize weight
522   determination. Another challenge arises when there is a large pool of LoRAs: how to
523   eﬀiciently retrieve and compute weights. This may necessitate further compression of both
524   z and the LoRAs themselves, although such explorations exceed the scope of this paper.
525   This issue presents a valuable direction for future work, where enhancing the scalability and
526   eﬀiciency of retrieval processes could open new avenues for the application of retrieval-based
527   machine learning models.
528   These insights pave the way for improving the model’s robustness and applicability, particu-
529   larly in privacy-sensitive or resource-constrained environments. Future research could focus
530   on refining these aspects to fully leverage the potential of retrieval-based learning systems
531   in broader and more diverse settings.

536   We have introduced a RPE model that achieves zero-shot learning without the need for

539   the redundant computational resource consumption of community groups and holds the
      potential to become an important framework in the future.

      A.1   Compare with Parameter Ensemble Methods
705
706   Parameter Ensemble Methods, particularly LoRA ensembles, can be categorized into three
707   distinct types based on the requirement for labeled data and neural network evaluation:
708   Fine-tuning, zero-shot with Neural Network Evaluation (NNE), and zero-shot without NNE.
709
      Fine-tuning is applicable where labeled data are available for new tasks. In such scenarios,
710   it is possible to learn coeﬀicients for parameter combinations. For example, Zoo-Tuning
711   (Shu et al., 2021) adapts the parameters of pretrained models to target tasks adaptively.
712   Similarly, Mixture of Experts (MoE) methods (Xue et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2024) determine
713   the combination weights of sub-models, termed as ”routers”. MoE architectures utilize a
714   gating network to direct inputs to specialized sub-models, or ”experts”, that are tailored for
715   specific tasks.
716   Zero-shot learning is pertinent when no labels are available for new tasks. Within this cate-
717   gory, some methods still necessitate extensive neural network evaluations, often employing
718   consistency regularization to enhance network performance. For instance, AdaMix (Wang
719   et al., 2022) uses stochastic routing and consistency regularization during the training phase.
720   UP-RLHF (Zhai et al., 2023) optimizes weights using reinforcement learning, and IOP-FL
721   (Jiang et al., 2023) employs a consistency loss for weight optimization.
722   Particularly, as the computational costs of foundation models increase, zero-shot learning
723   without NNE becomes essential in contexts lacking both labels and computational resources.
724   Model Soup (Wortsman et al., 2022) simplifies model combination through parameter aver-
725   aging. While AdaMix (Wang et al., 2022) and LoRA-Ensemble (Halbheer et al., 2024) also
726   employ averaging during the inference phase, their contributions are often focused on other
727   aspects. In contrast, our proposed RPE model doesn’t require fine-tuning but achieve com-
728   petitive performance. Unlike zero-shot with NNE, RPE doesn’t optimize sub-networks or
729   object functions. We are a zero-shot method without NNE. However, our model appliesad-
730
      vanced algorithms instead of simple average. Notice that our model utilizes several datasets
      for ensemble while Model Soup, AdaMix and LoRA-Ensemble focus on one dataset. Our
731
      research specifically addresses the ensemble weights among different models, highlighting a
732
      unique perspective on model integration.
