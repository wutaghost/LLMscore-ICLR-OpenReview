009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
012            Diffusion models have made significant advancements in recent years. However,
013
               their performance often deteriorates when trained or fine-tuned on imbalanced
               datasets. This degradation is largely due to the disproportionate representation of
014
               majority and minority data in image-text pairs. In this paper, we propose a general
015
               fine-tuning approach, dubbed PoGDiff, to address this challenge. Rather than
016            directly minimizing the KL divergence between the predicted and ground-truth
017            distributions, PoGDiff replaces the ground-truth distribution with a Product of
018            Gaussians (PoG), which is constructed by combining the original ground-truth
019            targets with the predicted distribution conditioned on a neighboring text embed-
020            ding. Experiments on real-world datasets demonstrate that our method effectively
021            addresses the imbalance problem in diffusion models, improving both generation
022            accuracy and quality.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      The development of diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020b) and their subsequent exten-
029
      sions (Song et al., 2020a; Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021; Huang et al., 2023) has significantly advanced the
030   learning of complex probability distributions across various data types, including images (Ho et al.,
031   2022; Rombach et al., 2022; Saharia et al., 2022; Ho & Salimans, 2022), audio (Kong et al., 2020),
032   and 3D biomedical imaging data (Luo & Hu, 2021; Poole et al., 2022; Shi et al., 2023; Pinaya et al.,
033   2022). For these generative models, the amount of training data plays a critical role in determining
034   both the accuracy of probability estimation and the model’s ability to generalize, which enables
035   effective extrapolation within the probability space.
036   Data diversity and abundance are key to improving the generative capabilities of large-scale models,
037   enabling them to capture intricate details within a vast probability space. However, many data-driven
038   modeling tasks often rely on small, imbalanced real-world datasets, leading to poor generation quality,
039   particularly for minority groups. For example, when training and fine-tuning a diffusion model with
040   an imbalanced dataset of individuals, existing models often struggle to generate accurate images for
041   those who appear less frequently (i.e., minorities) in the training data (Fig. 1).
042   This limitation is true even for finetuning large diffusion models pretrained on large-scale datasets
043   like LAION-5B (Schuhmann et al., 2022), e.g., Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022). Imagine
044   an imbalanced dataset consisting of employees in a small company, senior employees might have
045   more photos available, while new employees only have a very limited number of them. Since none of
046   the employees appear in the LAION-5B dataset, generating photos of them require finetuning the
047   Stable Diffusion model. Unfortunately, finetuning the model on such an imbalanced dataset might
048   enable the model to generate accurate images for the majority group (i.e., senior employees), but it
049   will perform poorly for the minority group (i.e., new employees).
050   To address this challenge, we propose a general fine-tuning approach, dubbed PoGDiff. Rather than
051   directly minimizing the KL divergence between the predicted and ground-truth distributions, PoGDiff
052   replaces the ground-truth distribution with a Product of Gaussians (PoG), which is constructed
053   by combining the original ground-truth targets with the predicted distribution conditioned on a
      neighboring text embedding. Our contributions are as follows:

084

086   Long-Tailed Recognition. Addressing the challenges posed by long-tailed data distributions has
087
      been a critical area of research in machine learning, for both classification and regression problems.
      Traditional methods, such as re-sampling and re-weighting techniques, have been used to mitigate
088
      class imbalances by either over-sampling minority classes or assigning higher weights to them during
089
      training (Chawla et al., 2002; He & Garcia, 2009; Torgo et al., 2013; Branco et al., 2017; 2018).
090   Such algorithms fail to measure the distance in continuous label space and fall short in handling
091   high-dimensional data (e.g., images and text). Deep imbalanced regression methods (Yang et al.,
092   2021; Ren et al., 2022; Gong et al., 2022; Keramati et al., 2023; Wang & Wang, 2024) address this
093   challenge by reweighting the data using the effective label density during representation learning.
094   However, all these methods above are designed for recognition tasks such as classification and
095   regression, and are therefore not applicable to our generation task.
096   Diffusion Models Related to Long-Tailed Data. There are also works that related to both diffusion
097   models and long-tailed data. They aim at improving generation robustness using noisy label (Na
098   et al., 2024), improving fairness in image generation (Shen et al., 2023), and improving classification
099   accuracy using diffusion models (Zhang et al., 2024). However, these works have different goals and
100   therefore are not applicable to our setting.
101
      Most relevant to our work is Class Balancing Diffusion Model (CBDM) (Qin et al., 2023), which
102   uses a distribution adjustment regularizer that enhances tail-class generation based on the model’s
103   predictions for the head class. It improves the quality of long-tailed generation by assuming one-hot
104   conditional labels (i.e., classification-based settings). However, this assumption does not generalize
105   to the modern setting where image generation is usually conditioned on free-form text prompts. As a
106   result, when adapted to the free-form setting, they often fail to model the similarity among different
107   text prompts, leading to suboptimal generation performance in minority data (as verified by empirical
      results in Sec. 4).

      Evaluation Protocols and Metrics. We use two types of evaluation metrics: generation quality
      and generation accuracy. For general text-to-image generation performance, we report the widely
418
      used Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) score (Heusel et al., 2017). Unlike the traditional FID score,
419
      which uses Inception-v3 (Szegedy et al., 2016) as the feature extractor, we used a pre-trained face
420   recognition model instead; since our goal is to evaluate the ability to recognize humans, we need
421   to capture facial features rather than general features. For each identity, we collect all images from
422   the original AgeDB or DigiFace datasets as the true image set. Then, In all-shot evaluation, for
423   AgeDB-IT2I-S and AgeDB-IT2I-M, we generate 100 images per identity as the fake image set, and
424   for AgeDB-IT2I-L and DigiFace-IT2I-L, we generate 20 images per identity as the fake image set. In
425   few-shot evaluation, we generate 500 images per identity as the fake image set. For all generations,
426   we employ the DDIM sampling technique (Song et al., 2020a) with 50 steps. The prompt used during
427   generation is “An image of {p}.” where “p” is the name of the identity (e.g., Albert Einstein).
428   To assess generation accuracy, we use 10 different seeds to sample 10 images for each minority class.
429   We then gather feedback from both the GPT-4o model (Achiam et al., 2023) and human evaluators to
430   score the accuracy of identity recognition. Additionally, we employ a pre-trained DINO model (Caron
431   et al., 2021) for calculating DINO score for image similarities. More details about the evaluation
      process including prompts we used are in the Appendix.

      We report the performance of different methods in terms of FID score, human evaluation score,
445   GPT-4o score, and DINO score in Table 1, Table 2, Table 3 and Table 4, respectively5 . Across
446   all tables, we observe that our PoGDiff consistently outperforms all baselines. Notably, PoGDiff
447   demonstrates significant improvements, especially in few-shot scenarios (i.e., for minority classes).
448   It is also worth noting that CBDM (Qin et al., 2023) performs extremely poorly on AgeDB-IT2I-S
449   and AgeDB-IT2I-M datasets. This is because their method samples text conditions from the entire
450   space, which may work in one-hot class settings, but in our context (natural text conditions), this
451   sampling technique misguides the model during training. In addition, for each method, we report the
452   performance on low-density classes in AgeDB-IT2I-L in Fig. 5. Across each column, the individual
453
      names are Albert Einstein, JW Marriott, J.P. Morgan, Edward G. Robinson, Larry Ellison, and Luise
      Rainer, respectively. The results show that our PoGDiff achieves significantly better accuracy and
454
      quality for tail classes.
455
456   Note that one of our primary objectives is to generate accurate images of the same individual while
457   ensuring facial consistency. Therefore diversity can sometimes be harmful. For example, given a
458   text input of “Einstein”, generated images with high diversity would generate both male and females
459
      images; this is obviously incorrect. Therefore it is important to strike a balance between diversity
      and accuracy, a goal that our PoGDiff achieves.
460
461   Specifically, as shown in Fig. 5:
462
        • First Three Columns of SDv1.5, CBDM, and PoGDiff: In these cases, the training dataset
463       contains only two images per person6 . With such limited data, it is impossible to introduce
464       meaningful diversity.
465        – SDv1.5 fails to generate accurate images altogether in this scenario.
466        – While CBDM might appear to produce the “diversity” you mentioned, it does so incorrectly,
467          as it generates an image of a woman when the target is Einstein.
468        – In contrast, our PoGDiff can successfully generate accurate images (e.g., Einstein images in
469          Column 1) while still enjoying sufficient diversity.
470     • Fourth and Fifth Columns: Here, the training dataset contains a medium number of images per
471
          person (5–7 images). Under these conditions:
           – SDv1.5 can generate accurate representations of individuals, but its outputs lack diversity.
472

473

477        – CBDM still fails to generate accurate depictions of the individual.

509   Models. Our method is a general fine-tuning approach designed for datasets that the Stable Diffusion
510   (SD) model has not encountered during pre-training. As shown in Fig. 1, color deviation is very
511
      common and is a known issue when one fine-tunes diffusion models (as also mentioned in (Song
      et al., 2020b)); for example, we can observe similar color deviation in both baselines (e.g., CBDM
512
      and Stable Diffusion v1.5) and our PoGDiff. This can be mitigated using the exponential moving
513
      average (EMA) technique (Song et al., 2020b); however, this is orthogonal to our method and is
514   outside the scope of our paper. Moreover, as shown in Fig. 5, the baseline Stable Diffusion also
515   suffers from this issue. Besides, exploring PoGDiff’s performance when training from scratch is also
516   an interesting direction for future work.
517
      Methodology. The distance between the current text embedding y and the sampled y′ impacts
518
      the final generated results, therefore in our paper, we introduced a more sophisticated approach for
519
      computing the weight ψ, which depends on the quality of the image pre-trained model and our trained
520   VAE. These mechanisms ensure that data points with smaller distances are assigned higher effective
521   weights. Effectively producing ψ for any new, arbitrary dataset remains an open question and is an
522   interesting avenue for future work, as it could further enhance the method’s performance.
523
      Evaluation. Our goal is to adapt the pretrained diffusion model to a specific dataset; therefore the
524
      evaluation should focus on the target dataset rather than the original dataset used during pre-training.
525
      For example, when a user fine-tunes a model on a dataset of employee faces, s/he is not interested in
526   how well the fine-tuned model can generate images of “tables” and “chairs”. Evaluating the model’s
527   performance on the original dataset used during pre-training would be an intriguing direction for
528   future work, but it is orthogonal to our proposed PoGDiff and out of the scope of our paper.

      7    C ONCLUSIONS
531
532
      In this paper, we propose a general fine-tuning approach called PoGDiff to address the performance
533
      drop that occurs when fine-tuning on imbalanced datasets. Instead of directly minimizing the KL
534   divergence between the predicted and ground-truth distributions, PoGDiff replaces the ground-truth
535   distribution with a Product of Gaussians (PoG), constructed by combining the original ground-
536   truth targets with the predicted distribution conditioned on a neighboring text embedding. Looking
537   ahead, an interesting avenue for future research would be to explore more innovative techniques
538   for re-weighting minority classes (as discussed in Sec. 6), particularly within the constraints of: (1)
539   long-tailed generation settings, as opposed to recognition tasks, and (2) natural text prompts rather
      than one-hot class labels.

770   B     D ETAILS FOR EVALUATION
771
772   In this section, we provide details on our evaluation procedures.
773   FID Score. For each identity, we collect all images from the original AgeDB or DigiFace datasets
774   as the true image set. Then, In all-shot evaluation, for AgeDB-IT2I-S and AgeDB-IT2I-M, we
775   generate 100 images per identity as the fake image set, and for AgeDB-IT2I-L and DigiFace-IT2I-L,
776   we generate 20 images per identity as the fake image set. In few-shot evaluation, we we generate
777   500 images per identity as the fake image set. For all generations, we employ the DDIM sampling
778   technique (Song et al., 2020a) with 50 steps. The prompt used during generation is “An image of
779   { p }.” where “p” is the name of the identity (e.g., Albert Einstein).
780   Human & GPT-4o Feedback. For each minority identity, we generate 5 images using DDIM
781   sampling (Song et al., 2020a) with 50 steps. We then ask 10 people to evaluate whether the images
782   depict the same person (scored as 1.0) or not (scored as 0.0). Additionally, for each image, we ask
783   the GPT-4o model to rate the similarity on a scale from 1 to 10. The prompt used during generation
784   is “An image of { p }.” where “p” is the name of the identity. The text prompt using for GPT-4o
785   model is “It is mandatory to give a score that how close the person in the image to a well-known
786
      individual. A score of 10.0 means they are exactly the same person, while a score of 0.0 means they
      are definitely not the same person. How close you think the person in the image is to ‘p-true’.” where
787
      “p-true” denotes the real name (well-known name) in AgeDB. Note that the GPT-4o model might
788
      occasionally refuse to provide a score, and you may need to repeat and compel it to give a rating. For
789   each image, we collect 10 scores from the GPT-4o model and report the average rating.
790
791   Evaluating Image Similarities. We collect samples that are outside our training dataset (e.g.,
792
      AgeDB-T2I-L) but belong to the original dataset (e.g., AgeDB). Using the same prompt, we generate
      the corresponding images. A pre-trained DINOv2 model (Caron et al., 2021) is then applied to extract
793
      latent features, and cosine similarities are calculated.

917                       • Case 3: y′ is very close to y. When y′ is close to y, the reweighting can be approximated

1025          • Recall in the Context of Image Generation: “Correct Image” and “Covered Image”.
                For each generated image, we classify it as a “correct image” if its distance to at least one

               ground-truth (GT) image is below a predefined threshold. For instance, suppose we have two
1027           training-set images for Einstein, denoted as x1 and x2 . A generated image xg is a “correct
1028           image” if the cosine similarity between xg and either x1 or x2 is above some threshold (e.g.,
1029           we set to 0.9 here). For example, if the cosine similarity xg and x1 is larger than 0.9, we
1030           say that xg is a “correct image”, and that x1 is a “covered image”. Intuitively, a training-set
1031           image (e.g., x1 ) is covered if a diffusion model is capable of generating a similar image.
