009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Physics-Informed Machine Learning (PIML) methods, such as Physics-Informed
012            Neural Networks (PINNs), are notoriously difficult to optimize. Recent advances
013            utilizing second-order optimization techniques, including natural gradient and
014            Gauss-Newton methods, have significantly improved training accuracy over first-
015            order methods. However, these approaches are computationally prohibitive, as
016
               they require evaluating, storing, and inverting large curvature matrices, limiting
               scalability to small networks. In this work, we propose a Hessian-Free Natural
017
               Gradient Descent frameworks that employs a matrix-free approximation of the
018
               Hessian-vector product. This approach circumvents the need for explicitly con-
019            structing the Hessian matrix and incorporates a novel preconditioning scheme that
020            significantly enhances convergence rates. Our method enables scaling to large
021            neural networks and complex PDEs with up to a millions parameters. Empiri-
022            cally, we demonstrate that our approach outperforms state-of-the-art optimizers,
023            such as LBFGS and Adam, achieving orders-of-magnitude speedup and superior
024            accuracy across various benchmark PDE problems.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Partial Differential Equations (PDEs) Partial Differential Equations (PDEs) are central to the
029   mathematical modeling of complex physical systems, including fluid dynamics, thermodynamics,
030   and material sciences. Traditional numerical methods, such as finite element and spectral methods,
031   often require fine discretization of the physical domain to achieve high accuracy. These approaches
032   can become computationally expensive, particularly in engineering applications where systems must
033   be solved repeatedly under varying parameters or initial conditions. Recent advances in machine
034   learning (ML) have shown promise in addressing these challenges by leveraging neural networks
035   (NNs) as potential alternatives or enhancements to traditional numerical solvers Kovachki et al.
036   (2021); Li et al. (2020).
037
038   Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) PINNs are a machine learning tool to solve for-
039
      ward and inverse problems involving partial differential equations (PDEs) using a neural network
      ansatz. They have been proposed as early as Dissanayake & Phan-Thien (1994) and were later
040
      popularized by the works Raissi et al. (2019); Karniadakis et al. (2021). PINNs are a meshfree
041
      method designed for the seamless integration of data and physics. Applications include fluid dy-
042   namics Cai et al. (2021), solid mechanics Haghighat et al. (2021) and high-dimensional PDEs Hu
043   et al. (2023) to name but a few areas of ongoing research. Despite their popularity, PINNs are
044   notoriously difficult to optimize Wang et al. (2020) and fail to provide satisfactory accuracy when
045   trained with first-order methods, even for simple problems Zeng et al. (2022); Müller & Zeinhofer
046   (2023). Recently, second-order methods that use the function space geometry to design gradient pre-
047   conditioners have shown remarkable promise in addressing the training difficulties of PINNs Zeng
048   et al. (2022); Müller & Zeinhofer (2023); Ryck et al. (2024); Jnini et al. (2024); Müller & Zeinhofer
049   (2024). However, these methods require solving a linear system in the network’s high-dimensional
050
      parameter space at cubic computational iteration cost, which prohibits scaling such approaches and
      limits their applicability to small-scale neural-networks.
051
052   A promising solution to alleviate this computational burden is the use of matrix-free methods. These
053   methods avoid the need for explicitly forming or inverting the Hessian matrix and instead approxi-
      mate the curvature matrix implicitly via iterative procedures Schraudolph (2002). While matrix-free

055   (2024); Zampini et al. (2024), their success is often limited in scale due to the ill-conditioning of
056   the underlying optimization problems and the absence of straightforward scalable preconditioning
057   scheme, resulting in prohibitively high iteration counts.

059   Main Contributions Our main contributions can be summarized as follows:
060
061          • Hessian-Free Natural Gradient Descent (HF-NGD): We introduce a novel HF-NGD
062            framework that leverages a matrix-free approximation of the Hessian. Our method incor-
063            porates a Matrix-Free low-rank preconditioning strategy based on the truncated eigenvalue
064            decomposition of the Gauss-Newton Hessian to address the issue of ill-conditioning by re-
065
               ducing large gaps between eigenvalues. This significantly enhances the convergence rates
               of iterative solvers like Conjugate Gradient (CG).
066
067          • Scalability to Large Networks and Complex PDEs: Our approach scales second-order
068            optimizers that respect the underlying function space geometry, enabling us to train neu-
069            ral networks with up to a million parameters and optimize neural operators. This results
070
               in state-of-the-art performance, achieving more than a 1-order-of-magnitude improvement
               compared to LBFGS across benchmarks and over a 2-orders-of-magnitude improvement
071
               on neural operators.

073
      Related Works Improving the training of PINNs has been the focus of extensive research. Early
074
      approaches explored adaptive re-weighting of loss terms for PDE residuals, data terms, and bound-
075   ary conditions Wang et al. (2021), and adaptive sampling methods to optimize collocation points
076   based on error indicators like the PDE residual Wu et al. (2023). While these techniques enhanced
077   PINN training, they often failed to achieve relative L2 errors below 10−4 for even simple problems.
078
      Recent work has shown that second-order optimization methods, including LBFGS, significantly im-
079
      prove PINN accuracy. Notably, methods adopting an infinite-dimensional perspective have achieved
080
      near-single-precision accuracy for PINNs Zeng et al. (2022); Müller & Zeinhofer (2023); Ryck
081   et al. (2024); Jnini et al. (2024); Zampini et al. (2024). However, their high per-iteration cubic
082   cost—solving a large linear system in the network’s parameter space—limits their scalability to
083   small networks.
084
      Matrix-free methods have been proposed to compute Gauss-Newton directions without explicitly
085
      forming the Hessian Martens (2010); Schraudolph (2002); Zeng et al. (2022); Jnini et al. (2024).
086
      Despite reducing computational costs, these methods suffer from ill-conditioning, leading to slow
087   convergence for large networks without efficient preconditioners. Our algorithm addresses this by
088   leveraging the structure of the curvature matrix to design a function space-based preconditioner,
089   significantly improving inner solver convergence.
090
      An alternative approach uses Kronecker-Factored Approximate Curvature (KFAC) Dangel et al.
091
      (2024) to scale second-order optimizers but requires problem-specific adaptations. Our method, in
092
      contrast, is both PDE- and architecture-agnostic.
093
094   Our idea of cutting large gaps within the leading eigenvalues of the Hessian spectrum is also aligned
095   with recent advances in preconditioning techniques, such as volume sampling Rodomanov & Kropo-
096
      tov (2020), polynomial preconditioning Doikov & Rodomanov (2023), and spectral preconditioning
      Doikov et al. (2024). While these methods have shown promise for structured convex objectives,
097
      ours is the first to apply low-rank preconditioning in Gauss-Newton methods for PINN optimization.

186   In practice, the eigenvalue distribution of the Gauss-Newton Hessian frequently exhibits a small

      3.3   O NLINE C ONDITION N UMBER C ONTROL A LGORITHM
271
272   We propose an algorithm that controls the condition number of the Gauss-Newton Hessian H online
273   by dynamically adjusting the rank of its low-rank decomposition.
274
      To efficiently approximate the low-rank structure of the Gauss-Newton Hessian Hk , we employ
275
      a hybrid approach combining Lanczos decomposition with orthogonal iteration. Lanczos captures
276   dominant eigenvalues, while orthogonal iteration refines the subspace between Lanczos updates. For
277   details of the Lanczos and Orthogonal Iteration algorithms, refer to Appendix 3 and Appendix 4,
278   respectively.
279
      Lanczos Decomposition: Lanczos is performed periodically (every NL iterations) to generate a
280
      Krylov subspace that approximates the largest eigenvalues and eigenvectors of Hk . Due to its opti-
281
      mal convergence behavior, especially for the largest eigenvalues, Lanczos often exhibits superlinear
282   convergence Saad (2003).
283
284   Orthogonal Iteration: Orthogonal iteration refines the subspace V̂k−1 between Lanczos updates,
285   adapting to changes in Hk as parameters are updated. Its linear convergence is offset by its low com-
286   putational cost, making it suitable for maintaining subspace accuracy without incurring the higher
287
      cost of a full Lanczos decomposition at each step.
288   Adaptive Low-Rank Decomposition and condition number control: Our algorithm adaptively
289   alternates between full Lanczos decomposition and orthogonal iteration, depending on the epoch
290   and the residual norm. The rank of the subspace is dynamically adjusted by monitoring the smallest
291   eigenvalue of the previous Ritz values. When this eigenvalue surpasses a threshold tied to the damp-
292   ing factor—which serves as an approximation of the condition number—the rank is incremented.

527   C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
528
529   We introduced a Hessian-Free Natural Gradient Descent (HF-NGD) framework for Physics-
530   Informed Machine Learning, leveraging a matrix-free Gauss-Newton Hessian approximation to sig-
531   nificantly reduce computational overhead. Our low-rank preconditioning scheme based on truncated
532   eigenvalue decomposition mitigates Hessian ill-conditioning and accelerates convergence. Empiri-
533   cal results on diverse PDE benchmarks—including Allen-Cahn, Klein-Gordon, Navier-Stokes, and
534   high-dimensional Poisson equations—demonstrate that HF-NGD outperforms optimizers such as
535   Adam and LBFGS, providing faster convergence with superior accuracy, achieving up to a 2-order-
536
      of-magnitude accuracy improvement in benchmark performance.
537   While our approach scales to large neural networks with up to a million parameters and neural
538   operators, it is still limited by the memory complexity of O(kn), where k is the rank and n is the
539   number of parameters. Scaling to evel larger networks may require exploring stochastic methods to
      estimate spectra or other efficient approximation techniques, which we consider as future work.
