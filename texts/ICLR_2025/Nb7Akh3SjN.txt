009                                                         A BSTRACT
010
011              Federated Learning (FL) facilitates collaborative training of a global model whose
012              performance is boosted by private data owned by distributed clients, without com-
013              promising data privacy. Yet the wide applicability of FL is hindered by entan-
014              glement of data distributions across different clients. This paper demonstrates for
015              the first time that by disentangling data distributions FL can in principle achieve
016              efficiencies comparable to those of distributed systems, requiring only one round
017              of communication. To this end, we propose a novel FedDistr algorithm, which
018              employs stable diffusion models to decouple and recover data distributions. Em-
019
                 pirical results on the CIFAR100 and DomainNet datasets show that FedDistr sig-
                 nificantly enhances model utility and efficiency in both disentangled and near-
020
                 disentangled scenarios while ensuring privacy, outperforming traditional federated
021
                 learning methods.

054
      In order to take full advantage of the described disentangled and near–disentangled cases (see Fig.
055   1), we propose to first disentangle data distributions into distinct components such that each client
056   can launch their respective learning task independently. This disentanglement will lead to significant
057   reduction of required communication rounds as revealed by Theorem 1. Technology-wise, there is
058   a large variety of methods that can be utilized to decompose data distribution, ranging from sub-
059   space decomposition (Abdi & Williams, 2010; Von Luxburg, 2007) to dictionary learning (Tošić &
060   Frossard, 2011) etc. (see Section 2.2 for detailed review). In this work, we propose an algorithm,
061   called FedDistr, which leverages the stable diffusion model technique (Croitoru et al., 2023) due to
062   its robust ability to extract and generate data distributions effectively. In our approach, clients locally
063
      extract data distributions via stable diffusion model, and then upload these decoupled distributions
      to the server. The server actively identifies the orthogonal or parallel between the base distributions
064
      uploaded by clients and aggregate the orthogonal distribution once.
065
066   Previous work (Zhang et al., 2022) has shown that it is impossible to achieve the optimal results
067   among the utility, privacy and efficiency. The proposed FedDistr offers a superior balance between
068   model utility and efficiency under the disentangled and near-disentangled scenarios, while still en-
069
      suring privacy-preserving federated learning: (1) By decoupling client data distributions into the dif-
      ferent base distributions, the server actively aligns the base distribution across different client, while
070
      FedAvg does not perform this decoupling, leading to a decline in global performance, especially for
071
      the disentangled case; (2) The proposed FedDistr requires only one round of communication, and
072   the amount of transmitted distribution parameters is much smaller than that of model gradients; (3)
073   The proposed FedDistr transmits a minimal amount of data distribution parameters, thereby miti-
074   gating the risk of individual data privacy leakage to some extent. Furthermore, privacy mechanisms
075   such as differential privacy (DP) can be integrated into FedDistr, offering additional protection for
076   data privacy.

078   2     R ELATED W ORK
079
080   2.1   C OMMUNICATION E FFICIENT F EDERATED L EARNING
081
082   One of the earliest approaches to reducing communication overhead is the FedAvg algorithm
083   (McMahan et al., 2017). FedAvg enables multiple local updates at each client before averaging
084   the models at the server, thereby significantly decreasing the communication frequency. To further
085   mitigate communication costs, compression techniques such as quantization and sparsification (Rei-
086
      sizadeh et al., 2020) and client selection strategies (Lian et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2023) have been
      implemented in federated learning. However, all of these methods are less effective in Non-IID
087
      scenarios, where the model accuracy is significantly impacted.
088
089   To address the Non-IID problem, numerous methods have been proposed (Li et al., 2020b; Ari-
090   vazhagan et al., 2019), which introduce constraints on local training to ensure that models trained
091   on heterogeneous data do not diverge excessively from the global model. However, most of these
092
      methods require numerous communication rounds, which is impractical, particularly in wide-area
      network settings.
093
094   Furthermore, some methods propose a one-shot federated learning approach (Guha et al., 2019; Li
095   et al., 2020a), which requires only a single communication round by leveraging techniques such
096   as knowledge distillation or consistent voting. However, significant challenges arise in this context
097   under Non-IID settings (Diao et al., 2023).
098
099   2.2   D ISTRIBUTION G ENERATION
100
      This paper focus on transferring distribution instead of models. There are several distribution gener-
101
      ation methods: Parametric Methods (Reynolds et al., 2009) assume that the data follows a specific
102   distribution with parameters that can be estimated from the data. Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM)
103   represents a distribution as a weighted sum of multiple Gaussian components. Generative Models
104   (Goodfellow et al., 2020; Croitoru et al., 2023) aim to learn the underlying distribution of the data so
105   that new samples can be generated from the learned distribution. For example, GANs Goodfellow
106   et al. (2020) consist of two networks, a generator G and a discriminator D. The generator learns
107   to produce data similar to the training data, while the discriminator tries to distinguish between real
      and generated data. Principal Component Analysis (PCA) (Abdi & Williams, 2010) is a linear


                                                          2
108
      method for reducing dimensionality and capturing the directions of maximum variance. It decom-
109   poses the data covariance matrix Σ into eigenvectors and eigenvalues. Dictionary Learning and
110   Sparse Coding (Tošić & Frossard, 2011) seeks to represent data as a sparse linear combination of
111   basis vectors (dictionary atoms).

      6     D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
493
494   In this study, we have addressed the critical inefficiencies inherent in Federated Learning (FL) due
495   to the entanglement of client data distributions. By analyzing the distribution entanglement, we
496   demonstrated that achieving a disentangled data structure significantly enhances both model utility
497   and communication efficiency. Our theoretical analysis shows that under near-disentangled condi-
498   tions, FL can achieve optimal performance with a single communication round, thus aligning closely
499   with the efficiency of traditional distributed systems.
500   Furthermore, we propose the FedDistr algorithm by leveraging the diffusion model. The integration
501   of stable diffusion models for data decoupling proves to be a robust solution, paving the way for
502   future explorations in privacy-preserving machine learning. Ultimately, this work contributes to
503   the advancement of FL by providing a clear pathway toward more efficient and effective federated
504   learning.
505
      With advancements in computational capabilities during the era of large language models (LLMs)
506   (), the time consumption for local training has significantly decreased. Consequently, our focus
507   is on enhancing communication efficiency. Moreover, the communication efficiency is especially
508   important particularly in Wide Area Network scenarios (McMahan et al., 2017; Palmieri & Pardi,
509   2010).
510
      Whether transferring data distribution leaks privacy is also an intriguing problem. Some studies
511
      (Xiao & Devadas, 2023) regard the underlying data distribution as a non-sensitive attribute, assum-
512   ing that sharing insights into the distribution of data across users does not compromise individual
513   privacy. However, other work indicates that using generative models to estimate the distribution still
514   poses a risk of privacy leakage (Wu et al., 2021).
