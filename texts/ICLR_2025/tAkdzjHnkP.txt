                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011            Fluorescence microscopy is ubiquitously used in cell biology research to charac-
012            terize the cellular role of a protein. To help elucidate the relationship between the
013            amino acid sequence of a protein and its cellular function, we introduce CELL-
014            Diff, a unified diffusion model facilitating bidirectional transformations between
015            protein sequences and their corresponding microscopy images. Utilizing reference
016            cell morphology images and a protein sequence, CELL-Diff efficiently generates
017            corresponding protein images. Conversely, given a protein image, the model out-
018
               puts protein sequences. CELL-Diff integrates continuous and diffusion models
               within a unified framework and is implemented using a transformer-based net-
019
               work. We train CELL-Diff on the Human Protein Atlas (HPA) dataset and fine-
020
               tune it on the OpenCell dataset. Experimental results demonstrate that CELL-Diff
021            outperforms existing methods in generating high-fidelity protein images, making
022            it a practical tool for investigating subcellular protein localization and interactions.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Protein sequences inherently encode their functions, and predicting these functions solely from se-
028
      quence information has become a critical area of research. With the development of artificial in-
      telligence, learning-based methods are increasingly employed to predict a wide range of protein
029
      properties, including structural conformation (Jumper et al., 2021; Baek et al., 2021), interaction
030
      partners (Evans et al., 2021), subcellular localization (Almagro Armenteros et al., 2017; Khwaja
031   et al., 2024b), and binding affinity (Rube et al., 2022). Concurrently, the rapid development of gen-
032   erative models has enabled researchers to design functional proteins (Madani et al., 2023; Dauparas
033   et al., 2022) and drug-like molecules (Isigkeit et al., 2024). These computational methods allow for
034   large-scale virtual screening, significantly reducing the costs and resources associated with experi-
035   mental validation. The advent of those technologies presents significant opportunities for biomedical
036   research, potentially accelerating advancements in therapeutic target identification, drug discovery,
037   and the investigation of biochemical pathways (Palma et al., 2012).
038   In this work, we focus on the relationship between protein sequences and their cellular functions
039   as characterized by microscopy images. Specifically, we focus on fluorescence microscopy which
040   is ubiquitously used in nearly all cell biology research. Fluorescence microscopy images provide
041   extremely rich information for proteins of interest in the cellular context, such as their expression
042   level, subcellular distribution, and molecular interactions as can be measured by spatial colocaliza-
043   tion. Such information characterizes protein functional activities as well as the physiological and
044   pathological state of cells. Disease-causing genetic mutations can alter the amino acid sequence
045
      of proteins, resulting in changes in image phenotypes by modifying gene expression patterns, re-
      shaping molecular interaction profiles, or globally perturbing cellular states. As a first step towards
046
      building a model that connects the sequence of proteins and their cellular images, recently, Khwaja
047
      et al. (2024b) proposed CELL-E, a text-to-image transformer that predicts fluorescence protein im-
048   ages from sequence input and cell morphology condition. Furthermore, CELL-E2 (Khwaja et al.,
049   2024a) was developed to enhance the image generation speed of CELL-E by utilizing the idea from
050   MaskGIT (Chang et al., 2022). Additionally, CELL-E2 facilitates the bidirectional transformation
051   between sequences and images. However, their image model only allowed output of highly blurred
052   images lacking fine details to discern any of the subcellular structures other than the most promi-
053   nent one (i.e. the nucleus), restricting their applicability only to the study of a very limited set of
      sequences features (i.e. the nuclear localization signal).

090   2    R ELATED WORKS
091
092   Multi-modal generative modeling can be formalized as learning the conditional or joint distribution
093   between modalities. Representative applications include text-to-image generation (Ramesh et al.,
094   2021; Ding et al., 2021; Nichol et al., 2022), image-to-text generation (image captioning) (Mokady
095   et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2023), text-to-video generation (Ho et al., 2022), and text-to-speech (Chen
096   et al., 2021; Popov et al., 2021). Most of these approaches rely on diffusion models or auto-
097   regressive models for the generation and typically focus on unidirectional transformation. However,
098   our goal is to achieve bidirectional generation, which requires the learning of joint distributions.
099
      To achieve this, Hu et al. (2023) proposed a discrete diffusion-based model for learning the joint
      distribution between images and text, though its scalability remains unexplored. Bao et al. (2023)
100
      introduced Unidiffuser, a unified diffusion model capable of unconditional, conditional, and joint
101
      generation. The key observation of Unidiffuser is that the learning objective of the diffusion score
102   function can be unified in a general framework with multiple diffusion time steps. Furthermore,
103   Zhou et al. (2024) developed Transfusion, which integrates auto-regressive and diffusion models
104   for both single and cross-modality generation. Transfusion combines the auto-regressive loss with
105   diffusion, training a single transformer model using an extended causal mask. These methods gen-
106   erally depend on large pre-trained encoders for images and text, such as CLIP (Radford et al., 2021)
107   and VQGAN (Esser et al., 2021). However, for microscopy images, the variability in equipment
      and experimental conditions limits the availability of such robust image encoders, making the direct

      application of these models challenging. Indeed, the two previous protein-sequence-to-microscopy
109   generators, CELL-E (Khwaja et al., 2024b) and CELL-E2 (Khwaja et al., 2024a), which both used
110   VQGAN, only produce coarse-grain images that have too much blur to distinguish fine-scale sub-
111   cellular structures such as cytoskeleton. As for CELL-Diff, we combine continuous and discrete
112   diffusion to enable bidirectional transformation between protein images and sequences. The model
113   is trained on the pixel space, offering a straightforward and efficient approach.

      4     M ETHODOLOGY
205
      In this section, we introduce our unified diffusion model for generating microscopy images and
206
      protein sequences. Let Iprot represents the protein image, Icell represents the cell morphology image,
207
      and S represents the protein sequence. The task of protein image prediction involves sampling from
208   the conditional distribution p(Iprot |S, Icell ), while the task of sequence generation involves sampling
209   from p(S|Iprot , Icell ). To achieve these goals within a unified diffusion model, we choose to estimate
210   the joint distribution p(Iprot , S|Icell )), which involves model a continues variable Iprot and a discrete
211   variable S.

301   Network architecture. As shown in Equation 11 and Equation 10, the network model fθ takes four
302   inputs: the protein sequence S, the protein image Iport , the cell image Icell , and the diffusion time step
303
      tI . To process the protein and cell images, we first concatenate them and then apply the commonly

304
      downsampling blocks, transforming into image embeddings. The protein sequences are embedded
305
      using a pre-trained ESM2 model Lin et al. (2022). Then, the image and protein embeddings are
306   concatenated and processed using an encoder-only transformer model. After passing through the
307   transformer module, the concatenated feature tensors are split into image and sequence feature ten-
308   sors. The image feature tensors are then upsampled and combined with the downsampling features
309   to output the noise from the protein image. The sequence feature tensor is processed using a linear
310   projector to predict the masked values. The upsampling and downsampling blocks in the U-Net
311   consist of residual and attention blocks. To enhance the integration of sequence information within
312   the image processing component, we utilize cross-attention mechanisms with the attention blocks.

372   5.2        I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
373
374   We first train CELL-Diff models on the HPA dataset and then fine-tune on the OpenCell dataset.
375   Both pre-training and fine-tuning are conducted for 100,000 iterations using the Adam opti-

530   7     C ONCLUSION
531
532
      This paper proposes CELL-Diff, a unified diffusion model that facilitates the transformation between
533
      protein sequences and microscopy images. Given cell morphology images as conditional inputs,
534   CELL-Diff generates protein images from protein sequences. Conversely, it can generate protein
535   sequences based on microscopy images. The objective function of CELL-Diff is constructed by in-
536   tegrating continuous and discrete diffusion models. Experimental results on the HPA and OpenCell
537   datasets demonstrate that CELL-Diff produces accurate protein images with higher resolvability
538   than previous methods. Potential applications, including virtual screening of protein localization
539   signals, virtual staining, and protein localization signal generation, make CELL-Diff a valuable tool
      for investigating subcellular protein localization and interactions.
