011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            In Open-Set Domain Adaptation (OSDA) we wish to perform classification in a
014            target domain which contains a novel class along with k non-novel classes. This
015
               work formally studies OSDA under the assumption that classes are separable, and
               the supports of source and target domains coincide, while other aspects of the
016
               distribution may change. We term such a distribution shift as background shift. We
017
               develop a simple and scalable OSDA method that attains robustness to background
018            shift and is guaranteed to solve the problem, while showing that it cannot be solved
019            under weaker conditions for OSDA studied in the past, particularly in the presence
020            of covariate shift. We formally define the realistic assumptions of background
021            shift within the scope of OSDA problem that the previous literature has either
022            overlooked or not explicitly addressed. In a thorough empirical evaluation on both
023            image and text data, we observe that existing OSDA methods are not robust to the
024            distribution shifts we consider. Our proposed solution jointly learns representations
025            via concurrently learning to classify known categories and detect novel ones using
026
               methods with formal guarantees. The results demonstrate that optimizing these two
               objectives in unison leads to mutual performance improvements contrary to what
027
               might be expected when objectives are considered independently. Our rigorous
028
               empirical study also examines how OSDA performance under distribution shift is
029            affected by parameters of the problem such as the novel class size. Taken together,
030            our observations emphasize the importance of formalizing assumptions under
031            which OSDA methods operate and to develop appropriate methodology that is
032            capable of scaling with large datasets and models for different scenarios of OSDA.

035   1    I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Adapting Machine Learning models to shifts in the data distribution is pivotal to ensuring their
038   robustness and safety in real world applications (Quinonero-Candela et al., 2008; Koh et al., 2021)
039
      for fields like healthcare (Finlayson et al., 2021), autonomous driving (Filos et al., 2020; Wong et al.,
      2020) and more broadly in computer vision (Bendale & Boult, 2016). Maintaining robustness to
040
      distribution shift in classification problems includes both identifying familiar objects under new
041
      conditions, while detecting the long tail of object categories that has not been observed in the past.
042   In this work we study the problem of adaptation when these two types of distribution shift occur
043   concurrently. Namely, the appearance of novel categories, known as Open-Set Domain Adaptation
044   (OSDA) (Panareda Busto & Gall, 2017), and shifting distributions of known classes.
045
      Let our training data be SS . The core task here is to detect the novel classes in target data ST
046
      collected under some conditions different from those observed at training time (Panareda Busto &
047
      Gall, 2017) while maintaining good performance over the existing classes. The emergence of a novel
048   class in itself constitutes a shift between SS and ST , however, in real world scenarios it is likely not
049   the only distribution shift that occurs (Garg et al., 2022; Wald & Saria, 2023). Our study concentrates
050   on scenarios where the novel class exhibit a notable degree of separability from the other known
051   classes, and a distribution shift with overlapping supports between source and target distributions
052   exists within the known classes, which we refer to as ’background shift’. This is arguably the most
053   common case in practice like medical applications, e.g. classifying histopathology slides for known
      and novel tumor cells.

        It is crucial to highlight the understudied aspects of OSDA in the existing literature as a motivation:
055
056     The scenario of concurrent shift in known classes, and the appearance of novel ones, is challenging
057     as they require making clear assumptions on the nature of shift or novelty (David et al., 2010; Fang
058
        et al., 2022). However, methods that are explicit about the assumptions under which they are
        guaranteed to solve these problems, are often not those that obtain competitive performance in
059
        practice, especially with large scale data and models. Therefore in this work we seek to study and
060
        improve the applicability of algorithms that have formal guarantees under distribution shift to large
061     scale problems, under realistic conditions.
062
063    Most research on OSDA deals with settings where the distribution of known classes does not shift Ge
064
       et al. (2017); Neal et al. (2018); Lin & Xu (2019); Chen et al. (2021); Zeng et al. (2021); Vaze et al.
       (2022); Esmaeilpour et al. (2022) while some methods like Liang et al. (2021); Li et al. (2021) focus
065
       on OSDA under a broad umbrella of "domain shifts". Office-Home Venkateswara et al. (2017) is a
066
       popular benchmark used to evaluate methods that adapt to a target domain like image sketches (ST )
067    while SS consists of real images. Hence, OSDA methods that work in practice often lack clear
068    technical assumptions about the distribution shift leading to inconsistent performances across
069    the varying nature of shifts. Commonly studied shifts like in Office-Home dataset are qualitatively
070    different from shifts we study here, which include shifts in histopathology slides due to changes in
071    certain patient subpopulation or demographics where the support of the source domain must overlap
072    with that of the target domain, see assumption 1 in Section 3. Although this assumption may restrict
073    the shifts we study to a form of domain shift, our empirical observations reveal that methods effective
074    for domain shift do not necessarily perform well under the specific shifts considered in our assumption
075
       1. Therefore, our study aims to deepen understanding of how such shifts affect our capability to
       identify novel classes and accurately classify known classes in ST by addressing correlated tasks
076
       while learning a shared representation (Caruana, 1997; Maurer et al., 2016; Baxter, 1997; 2000).
077
078     Successful monitoring of novel classes facilitates making critical decisions like updating models to
079     accommodate these new classes, or defer classifications to humans when objects are suspected to be
080     unknown to a classifier to increase their reliability (Amodei et al., 2016; Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2016).
081
        Existing OSDA methods do not sufficiently address the impact of varying proportions of novel
        samples on OSDA performance. Hence, we empirically validate our method in such under-explored
082
        situations where the novel class is present in very small proportions, which posits greater challenges
083
        than dealing with large quantities of unknown classes compared to the size of known classes.
084
085     Large foundation models rely on large image-text pretraining datasets, which are often unavailable
086     in specialized fields like medical applications. For example, missing captions of histopathology
087
        slides or detailed disease descriptions may restrict training or fine-tuning of such models. Moreover,
        novel tumors or diseases might not appear in the pretraining data, making validation on natural
088
        images benchmark insufficient for open-set problems. This underscores the need for a fair OSDA
089
        benchmark that excludes novel classes from pretraining dataset to validate the legitimacy
090     of large foundation model based solutions for OSDA. Building such a dataset would require
091     significant efforts and involve challenges beyond the scope of this study.
092
093
        Our contributions to these aspects of OSDA are thus the following:
094   • Mitigate the impact of understudied aspects of OSDA problem: Existing literature in OSDA
095     under distribution shift within the non-novel instances is either very limited or primarily focuses on
096     specific scenario of label shift Garg et al. (2022). We observe that conditions deemed sufficient for
097     OSDA under label shift may not suffice under other shifts like covariate shift (see Lemma 1). We also
098     determine that the condition of separability (Assumption 1) along with the definition of background
099     shift is applicable widely in real world scenarios. Furthermore, our experiments primarily focus on
100     long-tail setting of novel objects characterized by low proportions of novel class samples.
101   • Leverage and improve principled methods for novelty detection: We improve the existing methods
102     having guarantees for novelty detection to scale effectively with large models and for complex, high-
103     dimensional datasets such as images and text. To mitigate distribution shift within non-novel classes,
104     we further optimize and extend the constrained learning rule proposed in (Wald & Saria, 2023).
105     Finally, we adapt these existing PU learning/novel subpopulation detection methods for the task of
106
        OSDA under background shift by learning shared representations.
107   • Rigorous analysis across diverse data modalities and datasets: Following these improvements,
        we present strong empirical results and rigorous analysis, demonstrating that, when combined with

118   The OSDA problem lies at the intersection of novelty detection and domain adaptation, while our
119   work also leverages and builds upon Multitask learning.
120   Open Set Domain Adaptation: OSDA has recently garnered significant research attention Wen
121   & Brbic (2024); Qu et al. (2023); Ge et al. (2017); Vaze et al. (2022); Liu et al. (2018); Xu et al.
122   (2019); Saito et al. (2018); Panareda Busto & Gall (2017); Choe et al. (2024); Hur et al. (2023); Zhu
123   et al. (2023). However, none of these studies extend beyond the traditional OSDA setting to address
124   the background shift with the separability assumption. Bendale & Boult (2016) initially proposed
125
      Openmax algorithm deriving from Extreme Value Theory followed by data augmentation based
      G-Openmax method proposed in Ge et al. (2017) which uses generative models to generate unknown
126
      samples. There is good line of work based on generative models like Neal et al. (2018), OpenGAN
127
      using real open-set images for selecting efficient models and was proposed in Kong & Ramanan
128   (2021). Vaze et al. (2022) leverages well trained closed set classifier for open-set recognition task.
129   Furthermore, there has been OSDA literature that use trained image caption generator such as in
130   Esmaeilpour et al. (2022) which proposes to employ zero-shot classification through pretrained
131   multi-modal representation learning (CLIP). Finally, Qu et al. (2023) propose multi-modal large
132   foundation models (LFMs) like ChatGPT, DALL-E and CLIP for OSDA. However, note that there are
133   several implicit assumptions and limitations that one is subject to when using such LFMs, such as, no
134   access to the pretraining data. This makes it infeasible to curate novel classes and design distribution
135   shift for OSDA benchmark that accommodates such LFM based solutions.
136   Multi-Task Learning: Multi-Task Learning (MTL) harnesses knowledge from related tasks to
137   enhance the generalization of machine learning models Caruana (1997). It involves acquiring shared
138   representations through simultaneous learning from these tasks. Previous research has extensively
139   explored MTL using hard/soft parameter sharing and has provided Bayesian or formal models within
140   the PAC setting Baxter (1997; 2000).
141   Out-of-Distribution (OOD) Detection: Most of the literature on OOD detection deals with the
142
      setting where a learner is shown ST only at test time (Ruff et al., 2021; Esmaeilpour et al., 2022)
      and a OOD/anomaly score (i.e. a score that ranks examples as more or less likely to be OOD) must
143
      be determined prior to observing them. Previous work find significant differences in performance
144
      when scores are calculated on different representations (e.g. pre-trained features, self-supervised, or
145   obtained from a classifier/auto-encoder) which play a crucial role here Shen et al. (2022).
146   Novel Class Discovery: Theoretical guarantees for this problem rely on distributional assumptions,
147   and making assumptions is necessary in order to learn a classifier with non-trivial guarantees in this
148   scenario (Bekker et al., 2019; Wald & Saria, 2023). While the majority of methods for this task are
149   heuristic and lack theoretical guarantees, positive and unlabelled (PU) learning based are well studied
150   providing effective solutions to novelty detection (Blanchard et al., 2010; Garg et al., 2021; du Plessis
151   et al., 2014; Elkan & Noto, 2008). Garg et al. (2022) proposed a k-way PU learning based solution
152   under a label-shift assumption, i.e. PS (x | Y = y) = PT ,[k] (x | Y = y) for all y ∈ [k] where [k] is
153   the set of known classes. 1 .
154   PU-Learning Under Distribution Shift: Under no distribution shift, solutions based on adjustments
155
      to a “Domain Discriminator" (i.e. models that are trained to distinguish PS and PT ) are rather effective
      (Garg et al., 2021; du Plessis et al., 2014; Elkan & Noto, 2008), however they can underperform
156
      when PS ̸= PT ,[k] even when assumptions such as separability (see 1) holds. Under separability
157
      the problem can be solved given infinite data Gerych et al. (2022), and Wald & Saria (2023) give
158   an algorithm with finite-sample guarantees that we will extend here to the case of OSDA under
159   background shift.

339     5     E XPERIMENTS
340
341     We are now in place to empirically evaluate CoLOR against variety of baselines across background
342     shifts and novel classes that we create in real data. Here, the main questions we wish to answer are:
343
       1. Does background shift within non-novel instances affect OSDA performance?
344
345
       2. Can shared representations mitigate the impact of background shift by enhancing robustness in
          classifying samples from ST ,[k] thereby improving overall OSDA performance?
346
347    3. What is the effect of novel class ratio α particularly w.r.t. detecting novel classes?
348
349     5.1   E XPERIMENTAL SETTING
350
        The experiments to examine these questions are devised over image and text datasets as follows.
351
        We randomly draw a class from the set of classes Y and assign that as the k + 1-th novel class.
352
        Denoting the instances of known classes by Sk = {x : y ∈ [k]} ∀ (x, y) ∈ S and novel ones
353     as Sk+1 = {x : y = k + 1} ∀ (x, y) ∈ S, we create a background shift by further splitting Sk
354     into SS and ST ,[k] . We use semantic attributes that are annotated in the metadata of each dataset
355     to create this shift between SS and ST ,[k] . The attributes used for each dataset are specified in
356     section 5.2 and elaborated further in section A.3.1. At training time the learner is provided with
357     a labelled dataset SS and the unlabelled dataset ST = ST ,[k] ∪ Sk+1 . The mixture proportion
358     α = |Sk+1 |/(|Sk+1 | + |ST ,[k] |) is set by adjusting the sizes of the selected novel classes in ST ,[k] .
359
360     5.2   DATASETS
361
362     Most of the large scale image classification models are trained on ImageNet dataset. Hence we employ
363
        a similar large scale dataset called SUN397 Xiao et al. (2010) having completely different categories
        than ImageNet. We also include a Amazon Reviews text dataset Ni et al. (2019) to demonstrate
364
        the versatility of the method across multiple modalities. Finally, we include small scale CIFAR100
365
        Krizhevsky (2009) dataset to evaluate the adaptive methods (like DD, nnPU, uPU, BODA, OSDA)
366     particularly in scenarios when their feature extractors are trained from scratch.Table table 1 provides
367     a summary of characteristics and further details on each dataset are in appendix A.3.1.
368
369   • SUN397. Popular scene understanding dataset with a three-level hierarchy of scene categories Xiao
370     et al. (2010), exploiting this structure to create distribution shifts by varying subtype (y) proportions
371     within primary class labels Y. Indoor scenes (e.g., shopping/dining places, workplaces) serve as
372
        known (in-distribution) classes, while outdoor scenes are randomly selected as novel classes. Varying
        subtype proportions in indoor categories causes a background shift since Supp(PT ,[k] ) ⊆ Supp(PS ).
373
374   • Amazon Product Reviews. Classes are different product categories (prime pantry, musical instru-
375     ments, etc.), and induce background/covariate shift in known classes based on positive/negative
376
        sentiment in the review. The novel class is an unknown product category.
377   • CIFAR100. Similar to the SUN397 dataset, we leverage the inherent hierarchies of CIFAR100
        classes to create a natural background shift based on subtypes (e.g. dolphins) of known primary

469
470   Based on our observations from the experiments, we address the three critical questions below:
471
472   Background shift within non-novel instances causes a significant drop in the OSDA performance
473
      of all the methods that involve fine-tuning using the source data.
      From tables 3b, 6 and 7 we observe that all the methods that involve training a closed-set classifier
474
      suffer from background shift. Hence, background shift is harming the the performance of the closed

476   was more robust to the background shift, then we probably would’ve ended up with better OSDA
477   performance too. But CoLOR seems to primarily help when standard techniques like long training,
478   label smoothing, and other techniques used in Vaze et al. are insufficient to train a model that’s robust
479   to shift. This is not the case for ZOC as it performs zero-shot open-set classification using pretrained

      6   F UTURE W ORK
523
524
      Future research could focus on refining the search criteria for the optimal model head by selecting
525
      an appropriate β threshold, as the bounds provided in Theorem 1 are loose, like most bounds in
526
      learning theory, and may be suboptimal. Additionally, a robust theoretical underpinning is needed to
527   explain why shared representations improve the classification of known classes under distribution
528   shifts. Integrating test-time or model-free domain adaptation methods, such as those by Wang et al.
529   (2020), Zhang et al. (2023), or Saito et al. (2018), could further enhance OSDA performance. It
530   would be valuable to compare our approach with large foundation models. Although curating such a
531   benchmark would require access to publicly available pretraining datasets like LAION, which would
532   require significant effort and present unforeseen challenges.

806
807   It is a common argument in the machine learning literature that AUPRC scores are more suitable
808   for evaluating methodologies in class-imbalanced scenarios. However, this stance is nuanced, as
809   some research, such as McDermott et al. (2024) suggests favoring AUROC over AUPRC in certain
      imbalanced conditions. McDermott et al. (2024) further notes that AUPRC inherently emphasizes

      the performance on samples with higher scores. Given our experimental focus on assessing models’
811   ability to assign higher scores for novelty detection, AUPRC emerges as the most relevant metric for
812   our analysis, especially when the proportions of positive class (novel class in our case) is very low.

844   A.3.1    DATASET
845
846   One important factor to consider while creating shifts is that SS and ST ,k+1 should be distinguishable.
847   As dataset separability is a difficult quantity to measure we train a classifier (oracle) for each dataset
848   to distinguish novel groups from samples belonging to known categories. We then use the learnability
849
      of the oracle as a criterion to ensure the separability of novel classes. This means that we calculate
      the AUROC and AUPRC scores of the oracle for the task of supervised novelty detection. Higher
850
      AUROC and AUPRC would correspond to higher separability. We consider an AUROC and AUPRC
851
      higher than 0.98 as ideal to ensure separability between novel class identities and known classes. It is
852   difficult to ensure such high separability for all the datasets, particularly for Amazon Reviews dataset
853   which does not perfectly satisfy the separability. Yet we observe that CoLOR is robust to background
854   shift in such settings outperforming all the baselines as observed in Tables 2, 10 and 12.
855   We conduct 5 repetitions of an experiment for each dataset and for every identity of the novel class.
856   Each repetition uses a unique random seed, representing a distinct background shift setting. These
857   settings are generated by randomly varying the subtype proportions of the known categories within
858   the SUN397 dataset.
859   SUN397: It consists of images of scenes/places from various locations. The dataset is provided
860   with 3 levels of hierarchy where Level-1 is grouped as indoor, natural outdoor and man-made
861   outdoor scenes. We use indoor classes as in-distribution classes while we choose novel classes
862   from natural outdoor scenes. Level-2 hierarchy has shopping, workplace, homes/hotels, etc. under
863   indoor category while outdoor natural contains classes like water/ice/snow, mountains/hills desert/sky,
      forest, etc. Each of these level-2 categories further has subcategories (level-3 classes) that form these


                                                         16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      level-2 groupings. We randomly select 8 level-3 subtypes (like bakery shop or banquet hall) per
865   level-2 category (shopping/dining places) and vary the subtype proportions to create background
866   shift between source and target. Furthermore, novel classes are randomly selected from the level-2
867   categories of outdoor natural group.
868   CIFAR100: It consists of 60,000 32x32 colour images in 20 primary classes (superclasses) each of
869   which have 5 subcategories composing a total of 100 classes Krizhevsky et al. (2009). The training
870   set has 50,000 images (i.e. 500 images per subcategory and 2500 images per primary class) while the
871   test set has 10000 images (i.e. 100 images per subcategory and 500 images per primary class). We
872   retain these splits for our experiments. We use 4 primary categories as aquatic mammals, flowers,
873
      fishes and birds. The subcategories of the aquatic mammals are beaver, dolphin, otter, seal are whale
      while that of flowers are orchid, poppy, rose, sunflower and tulip. Similarly fishes and birds have 5
874
      subcategories each. We vary the marginal distribution of these subcategories to create a subpopulation
875
      shift leading to a background shift between source and target data while maintaining no label shift
876   w.r.t. primary categories i.e. aquatic mammals and flowers. The novel category is randomly selected
877   from the remaining unseen categories.
878   Amazon Reviews The dataset is heavily skewed with respect to sentiments and product categories.
879   Hence, we select 6 product categories having similar orders of magnitude of the number of reviews
880   namely ’Digital Music’, ’Industrial & Scientific’, ’Luxury Beauty’, ’Musical Intstruments’, ’Prime
881   Pantry’ and ’Software’. To prevent further skewness in the dataset due to sentiments, we restrict
882   the sample size per sentiment per category to 500 reviews in the training set and 125 reviews per
883   sentiment per product category. We induce a background shift based on sentiments. Reviews with
884
      rating strictly below 3.0 (out of 5.0) are considered negative sentiments and those with rating strictly
      above 3.0 are considered positive sentiments whereas reviews having a rating of exactly 3.0 are
885
      discarded from the dataset. The minimum rating is 1.0 while the maximum is 5.0.

904   owing to diverse target recall constraints. These multipliers are initialized to 1.0, while the dual
905
