009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Model reliability is paramount for critical real-world applications. To enhance re-
012            liability, it is essential to quantify uncertainty in model predictions, as achieved
013            through Confidence Calibration and Selective Classification. Confidence Cali-
014            bration ensures prediction confidences accurately reflect the actual likelihood of
015            correctness, while Selective Classification allows a model to abstain from making
016
               predictions when uncertain. Although related, existing methods address each as-
               pect separately, or both through post-hoc methods. Only one method, Confidence-
017
               aware Contrastive Learning for Selective Classification (CCL-SC), combines both
018
               in an ad-hoc manner. Despite being a powerful calibrator, CCL-SC has some
019            drawbacks, including the absence of an additional unknown class, the use of two
020            different losses (detrimental for calibration), and its cumbersome implementation.
021            In the pursuit of reliable models and motivated by the idea of creating an ad-hoc
022            calibrated selective classifier with an unknown class, we first empirically analyze
023            the Self-Adaptive Training (SAT) method, a leading method in ad-hoc selective
024            classification. We identify that while SAT excels in selective classification, it falls
025            short in confidence calibration, especially when training for a small number of
026            epochs (e.g., ≤ 100). To address this, we introduce an original method that uses an
027
               unknown class and a unique novel loss, Socrates loss, which serves as a classifier
               and a calibrator with a unified optimization goal. This method mitigates overfitting
028
               and ensures theoretically well-calibrated predictions across all epochs, addressing
029
               the drawbacks of both CCL-SC and SAT, without the need for post-hoc process-
030            ing or additional data. We integrate our method into the SAT implementation and
031            extend it to provide selective classification and confidence calibration metrics. We
032            show empirically that our method matches or improves the selective classifica-
033            tion error rate of SAT and CCL-SC, while producing well-calibrated models in an
034            ad-hoc manner through the evaluation on 6 image benchmark datasets across two
035            architectures, VGG-16 and ResNet-34.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
039
      Reliability, the ability of a model to consistently operate in real-world environments (Tran et al.,
040
      2022), becomes particularly important in critical real-world scenarios, including but not limited to
041
      medical diagnosis (Gireesh & Gurupur, 2023), nuclear security (Ayodeji et al., 2022), and biosecu-
042   rity (McEwen et al., 2021). A reliable model should not only achieve strong predictive performance
043   but also excel in the representation of its own uncertainty. To quantify uncertainty, different meth-
044   ods measure distinct aspects of the predictive uncertainty stemming from reliability, such as Con-
045   fidence Calibration and Selective Classification. Selective classification allows models to abstain
046   from making predictions when uncertain, ensuring cautious decision-making in high-risk applica-
047   tions. On the other hand, confidence calibration ensures that predictive confidence accurately reflects
048   the likelihood of correctness. Although both strategies aim to enhance reliability, they are typically
049   approached and studied independently (Zhang et al., 2023).
050   In critical high-risk applications, where trust in predictions is essential, integrating confidence cali-
051   bration with selective classification is crucial. Recent work has highlighted this need and proposed
052   new post-hoc methods (Fisch et al., 2022; Galil et al., 2023; Moon et al., 2020), and, to the extent
053   of our knowledge, only one ad-hoc method, Confidence-aware Contrastive Learning for Selective
      Classification (CCL-SC) (Wu et al., 2024). Despite the fact that CCL-SC is able to output cal-

054
      ibrated models, it has several drawbacks. Firstly, following the work of Feng et al. (2023), the
055   extra unknown class was not added. The use of an extra unknown class is related to adaptations
056   aiming to address Open-Set Recognition (OSR) in deep neural networks (Bendale & Boult, 2016;
057   Patrick Schlachter & Yang, 2019). In the search for reliable models, we argue that mechanisms for
058   OSR should be integrated with calibration and selective classification to enhance model reliability
059   and adaptability. A reliable model should not only adapt its predictions to new scenarios but also be
060   flexible enough to handle different use cases. Incorporating an unknown class provides more flexi-
061   bility, allowing the model to function as a selective classifier or a traditional classifier with or without
062   an extra unknown class. Secondly, the CCL-SC method features two losses: Confidence-aware Su-
063
      pervised Contrastive (CSC) loss for calibration and cross-entropy (c.e.) loss for classification. We
      have identified that this method miscalibrates the model once it reaches a certain calibration point
064
      which is due to the c.e. effect (see Section 4.1.2). Therefore, having two losses can be detrimental
065
      if one of the losses is not specifically focused on calibration, highlighting the need for a unified loss
066   with the same optimization goal. Thirdly, CCL-SC exhibits variable behavior in terms of Expected
067   Calibration Error (ECE), depending on the architecture and dataset. The loss function across epochs
068   also shows varied trends, including spikes, which could suggest training instability. Finally, the
069   implementation of the CCL-SC method requires extensive modifications to the training code.
070
      Motivated by these drawback and the idea of creating an ad-hoc calibrated selective classifier with
071
      a capability to estimate the probability for an unknown class, we first empirically analyzed the
072   calibration capability of the Self-Adaptive Training (SAT) (Huang et al., 2020), the state-of-the-art
073   for end-to-end selective classification with an extra unknown class. This analysis showed that SAT
074   loss does not seem to be a calibration loss, as in the case of training for a smaller number of epochs
075   (e.g., ≤ 100) or using hard-to-classify datasets like CIFAR-100 and Food-101.
076
      We propose a method to train calibrated selective classifiers by introducing an extra unknown class
077
      and using a novel unified loss, Socrates loss. Socrates loss owes its name to the famous quote
078   of the philosopher Socrates: I know that I know nothing; which reflects the power of the loss to
079   train a model to be aware of its own uncertainty. This loss integrates classification and calibration
080   into a single optimization problem, optimizing a single loss function, and does need several losses,
081   switching to a different loss during training, or post-hoc processing. The loss uses its knowledge
082   about when it does not know, and dynamically utilizes model predictions to guide training, by giving
083   more attention to hard-to-classify instances.
084   We empirically evaluated our method to SAT and CCL-SC on the CIFAR-10, CIFAR-10C, CIFAR-
085   100, CIFAR-100C, SVHN, and Food-101 datasets with VGG-16 and ResNet-34 architectures. In
086   terms of calibration across epochs, our method outperforms SAT and is comparable to CCL-SC,
087   while effectively addressing the previously discussed drawbacks of both methods. Moreover, our
088   method achieves similar or lower Selective Classification error rates compared to CCL-SC and SAT,
089   with notable improvements observed over SAT on the hard-to-classify CIFAR-100, CIFAR-100C
090   and Food101 datasets. For instance, using the Food-101 dataset, the VGG-16 architecture, and
091   100% of coverage, we achieve Selective Classification error rates (%) of 26.93 for Socrates, 68.23
092
      for SAT and 27.18 for CCL-SC.
093   To summarise, our contributions are as follows:
094
095          • An easy-to-implement ad-hoc method that uses an extra unknown class and a novel loss,
096            Socrates loss, integrating classification and calibration into a unified optimization goal.
097
098          • A Python implementation to train Socrates, SAT and CCS-CL, and evaluate selective clas-
099            sification and confidence calibration. In addition, the code to reproduce the results of this
100
               paper is also provided.
101
             • A theoretical analysis that proves Socrates loss a) forms a regularize upper bound in the
102            Kullback-Leibler divergence, avoiding overconfident predictions and improving calibra-
103            tion. b) acts as a regularizer (of the network weights) when the model is sufficiently confi-
104            dent, avoiding miscalibration and overfitting.
105
106          • A comparative empirical analysis of Socrates, SAT, CCL-CS, in terms of calibration and
107            selective classification performance, on 6 benchmark datasets across two network architec-
               tures.

      In 2017, Guo et al. (2017) revealed that modern Neural Networks (NNs) are no longer well-
131
      calibrated and exhibit overconfidence. The degree of confidence calibration in NNs can be illustrated
132
      through visual representations and quantifiers (Section 3.2). To address the issue of miscalibration,
133   the research community has focused on developing new post-hoc and ad-hoc methods. Post-hoc
134   methods are applied to a trained model in a post-training process, such as Platt Scaling (Platt, 2000)
135   and Temperature Scaling (Guo et al., 2017). Ad-hoc methods enhance both accuracy and calibra-
136   tion during training, creating end-to-end compact models by incorporating explicitly or implicitly
137   a secondary optimization objective related to the predictive uncertainty of the model in the training
138   objective (Liu et al., 2023). Although they remain underexplored, one way to achieve calibration
139   is through the use of a specific loss function, such as Focal loss (Lin et al., 2020; Mukhoti et al.,
140   2020). According to Zhang et al. (2023), despite the effectiveness of post-hoc methods, future
141
      models should integrate calibration into the training process. To that end, we focus our method on
      calibrating through a loss function.
142
143   Alternatively, one can train reliable models by considering the option to reject a prediction when
144   the model is uncertain. Selective Classification (Geifman & El-Yaniv, 2017) can be addressed by
145   post-hoc and ad-hoc methods. Post-hoc methods perform selective classification after training, such
146   as LeCun et al. (1989) and Geifman & El-Yaniv (2017). Ad-hoc methods change the NNs training
147
      process and add extra heads or logits. Feng et al. (2023) divide these methods into learn to select,
      such as SelectiveNet (Geifman & El-Yaniv, 2019), and learn to abstain, such as Self-Adaptive Train-
148
      ing (SAT) (Huang et al., 2020), methods. According to Feng et al. (2023), adding an extra head/logit
149
      is unnecessary for the ad-hoc Selective Classification problem, and the Softmax Response is the only
150   selection mechanism required. We argue the opposite, that in the search for reliable models, the abil-
151   ity to handle unknown classes is valuable (Subsection 6.2).
152
153
      Whereas some authors have emphasized the necessity of having calibrated selective classi-
      fiers (Zhang et al., 2023), the majority of published methods (Fisch et al., 2022; Galil et al., 2023;
154
      Moon et al., 2020) have focused on post-hoc integration, which presents several drawbacks: often
155
      requiring additional data, increasing the risk of bias, diffusing the optimization goal, and sometimes
156   failing to fit when the calibration error is too complex. According to Zhang et al. (2023), a well-
157   calibrated model could not be a good discriminator and vice versa. Although SAT was designed as a
158   loss function to prevent overfitting, it has not been proven to be a promising ad-hoc calibration loss
159   function across all epochs. Wu et al. (2024) presented CCL-SC, which is currently the state-of-the-
160   art for ad-hoc calibrated selective classifiers. In contrast, Fisch et al. (2022) proposed a selective
161   classifier that rejects instances based on calibration rather than potential misclassification, which
      represents a different goal from ours.

168   3.1   P ROBLEM S ETTING : S ELECTIVE C LASSIFICATION
169
170
      Selective Classification trades classifier coverage against accuracy. It is the ability of a model to
      reject instances when there is uncertainty. The rejected instances are potential out-of-distribution or
171
      lie in the tail of the data distribution; making predictions only on samples with confidence.
172
173   Let X be the feature space, Y be the label space, and P (X , Y) be the data distribution over X ×Y. A
174   selective model is a pair (f, g), where the prediction function is f : X →
                                                                               − Y, in our case a classifier,
175   and the selection function is g : X →− {0, 1}. Then, f (x) makes a prediction when g(x) = 1, and
176
      abstains from making a prediction when g(x) = 0.
177   The performance of a selective classifier can be evaluated in terms of cost-sensitive learning (Cortes
178   et al., 2016) where the rejection cost needs to be specified, or from a Risk-Coverage perspective
179   (El-Yaniv & Wiener, 2010). Since specifying costs can be challenging (Geifman & El-Yaniv, 2017),
180   we evaluate our method using the Risk-Coverage perspective. Coverage is defined as the probability
181   mass of the nonrejected region of X , ϕ(g) = E[g(X)]. In practice, a soft selection function g̃ : X →
                                                                                                          −
182
      R is often used, constraining the coverage with a threshold τ ∈ R. Then g is defined as g(x) :=
      1{g̃(x) ≥ τ }. Given a loss function, the selective risk, which corresponds to the selective error
183
      when the loss is 0/1, with respect to P can be defined as R(f, g) = E[L(f (X), Y ) | g(X) = 1] =
184
      E[L(f (X), Y ) | g(X)]
185                            . This shows a dependency between risk and coverage; rejecting samples
                 ϕ(g)
186

187   minimization problem given a target coverage is: min R(f, g) s.t. ϕ ≥ ctarget .
188
189   We follow the Selective Classification problem for ad-hoc methods to train end-to-end selective
190
      classifiers proposed by SAT (Huang et al., 2020) and DeepGamblers (Liu et al., 2019), where the
      selection function g(·) is replaced by f (·)c where c is the number of classes. In our proposed method,
191
      similar to SAT and DeepGamblers, an additional unknown class (c + 1) represents abstention.

197

223   with or without an unknown class; well-calibrated in all cases. Inspired by the calibration principles
224   of Focal loss and influenced by the selective classification power of SAT, we introduce a method
225
      to train calibrated selective classifiers by integrating an additional unknown class, referred to as
      idk, and using an easy-to-implement novel loss called Socrates loss, which maintains a unified
226
      optimization objective of classification and calibration.

266   ularization parameters are γ, αdynamic , and △reg ; where △reg = (1 − ty )[γ p̂y log p̂idk − log p̂idk ].

277
278

279
      value even after perfect high confidence has been achieved. Lin et al. (2020) demonstrated that for
280
      misclassified samples using c.e. loss the network progressively grows more confident in its incorrect
281   predictions. Socrates loss acts as a regularizer with an increased penalty highly associated with the
282   unknown class when the model begins to overfit. Furthermore, the norms of the weights are higher
283   at the beginning of the training compared to those trained with c.e. It is when the model starts being
284   miscalibrated that there is a change in the ordering of the weight norms, due to a big increase in the
285   weight norm of the models with c.e. This behaviour shows that Socrates loss acts as a regularizer
286   when the model is sufficiently confident, avoiding miscalibration and overfitting.

      6.1   I S S ELF -A DAPTIVE T RAINING LOSS A CALIBRATION LOSS ?
327
328   To our understanding, the SAT method achieves the highest performance in the Selective Classifi-
329   cation problem with an unknown class. The first question to address is: Is Self-Adaptive Training a
330   calibrated loss? Since SAT adds an extra unknown class and modifies the loss function to alleviate
331   overfitting, it is reasonable to consider SAT loss as a potential calibration loss similar to Focal loss.
332   However, the role of SAT as a calibrator has not been explored in the literature. For this initial em-
333   pirical analysis, we set aside the Selective Classification problem and focus solely on the confidence
334
      calibration problem.
335   A detailed analysis with graphs is presented in Appendix F. To sum up, first, we observed that the
336   accuracy and loss across epochs curves for Focal Loss exhibited similar trends, with minor overfit-
337   ting noted in the Food-101 dataset during the initial epochs. In contrast, the SAT loss demonstrated
338   different trends and did not consistently prevent overfitting. Regarding calibration metrics, the ECE
339   across epochs showed a consistent downward trend for Focal Loss, except for VGG-16 when applied
340
      to the CIFAR-100 and Food-101 datasets, where an increase was observed in the initial epochs but
      remained within an acceptable ECE range. For SAT loss and VGG-16, a rise was observed in ECE
341
      after the 150 epochs in the first epochs decreasing after convergence, while the rise for ResNet-34
342
      was less discernible. In the SAT end-to-end case, ECE values were notably high during the initial
343   epochs for both architectures. The MCE across epochs displayed similar trends for Focal Loss, but
344   distinct trends were observed for SAT. Importantly, SAT does not appear to be an effective calibra-
345   tion loss and may be detrimental when the goal is to train for a small number of epochs (≤ 100), as
346   it outputs calibrated confidences only after a considerable amount of training epochs. Additionally,
347   we noted that the average confidence values of the idk class seem to be directly related to calibration,
348   reflecting similar trends as the ECE across epochs. This raised the question Might the additional idk
349   class method be beneficial or detrimental in terms of calibration? This observed behavior was the
350   main source of inspiration for incorporating predictions associated with the idk class into the novel
351
      Socrates loss to calibrate during training.
352   Based on the empirical analysis the following claim can be made: Unlike Focal loss, which produces
353   very well-calibrated models and follows similar trends across all the datasets and architectures, SAT
354   loss exhibits certain tendencies that ultimately lead to the conclusion that it is not a loss that allows
355   learning calibrated models in all the epochs and scenarios, especially when aiming to train for a
356   small number of epochs or when dealing with complex datasets such as Food-101. Additionally,
357
      when the loss is used end-to-end, the miscalibration in the first epochs is excessively large, and in
      some cases (CIFAR-100 and Food-101 with VGG-16) it remains significantly large until the end of
358
      training. When the loss is applied with first-epochs case, miscalibration begins to emerge. Therefore,

513   7     C ONCLUSIONS AND LIMITATIONS
514
515
516
      In this paper, we first empirically investigated the calibration capacity of SAT loss as a calibration
517   mechanism, finding that it does not produce well-calibrated models. This deficiency is particularly
518   detrimental for models that require only a small number of epochs or when working with hard-to-
519   classify datasets. Additionally, we found that SAT does not consistently mitigate overfitting across
520   all cases. Through this empirical study, we identified a relationship between the extra unknown class
521   and calibration, which inspired the development of our proposed loss function. To address the need
522   for ad-hoc easy-to-implement calibrated selective classifiers with an unknown class, we proposed a
523   new method that incorporates an extra unknown class and introduces a novel loss, Socrates, with a
524   unified optimization goal (classification and calibration). We theoretically and empirically analyzed
525
      this loss, demonstrating that it is an optimal calibration method without the previously enumerated
      drawbacks of SAT and CCL-SC. This new loss not only ensures strong calibration throughout all
526
      training epochs (making it suitable for models trained with fewer epochs), but also produces selective
527
      classifiers that achieve similar Selective Classification error rates to SAT and CCL-SC, while notably
528   outperforming SAT on hard-to-classify datasets such as CIFAR-100 and Food-101 and CCL-SC on
529   datasets such as SVHN for VGG-16.
530
531
      We encourage the research community to further evaluate the Socrates method across a broader
      spectrum of architectures and datasets. It is notable that this method has not been compared to post-
532
      hoc methods. We argue that the strength of this end-to-end method comes from producing compact
533
      models that do not require post-processing and additional data. Leveraging all available data during
534   training can be particularly advantageous when data is limited. Future research should incorporate
535   and evaluate additional reliability aspects to develop a more comprehensive reliability framework
536   (e.g., distribution shifts, noise, out-of-distribution, etc.). The lack of metrics that integrate reliability
537   concepts is a pressing need. For example, a model may often be well-calibrated but exhibit low
538   accuracy. Additionally, there is a need for metrics that summarize calibration performance across
539   epochs. For example, a new ECE-epochs metric could indicate whether calibration has improved or
      deteriorated at any given point.

            A NALYSIS
1137
1138   Overfitting: As stated by Mukhoti et al. (2020) and Guo et al. (2017), overfitting appears to be
1139   linked to miscalibration. Therefore, the first step towards evaluating SAT as a calibrator is to examine
1140   the accuracy and loss curves to reconfirm the alleviation of the overfitting issue (one of the claims of
1141   the SAT and Focal methods). These curves are presented in Figure 6 and 7. Focal loss consistently
1142
       maintains the same trend and does not induce overfitting with any dataset or architecture, except
       for the Food-101 dataset, where minor overfitting occurs in the initial epochs. In contrast, SAT loss
1143
       behaves differently and does not consistently prevent overfitting. SAT shows overfitting with the
1144
       SVHN and Food-101 datasets with the VGG-16 architecture during the first-epochs case, and for
1145   the end-to-end case with the challenging CIFAR-100 and Food-101 datasets across both architec-
1146   tures. Furthermore, with the CIFAR-100 and Food-101 datasets with the VGG-16 architecture, the
1147   accuracy achieved with SAT loss is significantly lower than with Focal loss. For the end-to-end case
1148   with Food-101, and ResNet-34, SAT could not train. Although these observations suggest that SAT
1149   may not be an effective calibration loss, they do not provide definitive evidence of miscalibration,
1150   necessitating further analysis.
