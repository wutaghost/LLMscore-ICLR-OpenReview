                                                   A BSTRACT
034
035           The development of large language models (LLMs) has significantly enhanced the
036           capabilities of multimodal LLMs (MLLMs) as general assistants. However, lack
037           of user-specific knowledge still restricts their application in human’s daily life. In
038           this paper, we introduce the Retrieval Augmented Personalization (RAP) frame-
039           work for MLLMs’ personalization. Starting from a general MLLM, we turn it
040           into a personalized assistant in three steps. (a) Remember: We design a key-value
041           database to store user-related information, e.g., user’s name, avatar and other at-
042
              tributes. (b) Retrieve: When the user initiates a conversation, RAP will retrieve
              relevant information from the database using a multimodal retriever. (c) Generate:
043
              The input query and retrieved concepts’ information are fed into MLLMs to gen-
044
              erate personalized, knowledge-augmented responses. Unlike previous methods,
045           RAP allows real-time concept editing via updating the external database. To fur-
046           ther improve generation quality and alignment with user-specific information, we
047           design a pipeline for data collection and create a specialized dataset for person-
048           alized training of MLLMs. Based on the dataset, we train a series of MLLMs as
049           personalized multimodal assistants. By pretraining on large-scale dataset, RAP-
050           MLLMs can generalize to infinite visual concepts without additional finetuning.
051           Our models demonstrate outstanding flexibility and generation quality across a
052           variety of tasks, such as personalized image captioning, question answering and
053
              visual recognition. The code, data and models will be available.

      1     I NTRODUCTION
055
056
057
      Recently, the development of large language models (LLMs) has significantly enhanced their lan-
      guage processing and generating capabilities (Zhao et al., 2023b). Building on this foundation, the
058
      integration of visual and textual ability through vision-language alignment brings powerful multi-
059
      modal LLMs (MLLMs) (Yin et al., 2023; OpenAI, 2023; Gemini-Team, 2024; Liu et al., 2023b;
060   Zhang et al., 2024; Han et al., 2024). MLLMs have shown significant improvement in various tasks,
061   such as image description and question answering, highlighting their potential as human’s assistants.
062   However, their lack of user-specific knowledge continues to limit their effectiveness as personalized
063   assistants in daily life.
064
      A qualified personalized assistant first needs to be able to recognize and remember user-related con-
065
      cepts, such as the dog named 〈Lala〉 adopted by the user. Although existing MLLMs have been
066   trained on large-scale datasets and possess strong recognition and classification capabilities, directly
067   transferring this knowledge to a user’s personal concepts remains challenging. For instance, cur-
068   rent leading MLLMs cannot remember your dog’s name, even if you have mentioned it before, and
069   they lack awareness of your identity and preferences. Furthermore, the assistant should generate re-
070   sponses tailored to the user’s preferences and requirements. However, collecting extensive personal
071   information to train a unique assistant for each user is impractical.
072   To address this issue, the personalization of MLLMs has become a topic of growing interest, with
073   several approaches already being proposed. MyVLM (Alaluf et al., 2024) utilizes external classifica-
074   tion heads to recognize specific concepts, and learns an embedding for each concept to personalize
075   the outputs of vision language models (VLMs). Another concurrent work, Yo’LLaVA (Nguyen
076   et al., 2024), learns a few special tokens to represent each concept. However, both approaches ne-
077   cessitate continuous learning and updating of the model as new concepts emerge. This presents a
078   challenge in dynamic, ever-changing real-world scenarios, where the computing power of users’
079   personal devices is often limited, and all data must be stored locally for privacy concerns.
080   To address these challenges, we propose the Retrieval Augmented Personalization (RAP), designed
081   to allow MLLMs to update their supported concepts without additional training. Specifically, our
082   RAP works in three key steps. (a) Remember: RAP includes a designed database to help remember
083   each concept via storing its image and basic information, e.g., name, avatar and other attributes.
084   (b) Retrieve: When a user initiates a conversation, RAP will retrieve relevant information from the
085   database using a multimodal retriever. (c) Generate: The input query and retrieved concepts infor-
086
      mation are incorporated into the MLLM’s input for personalized, knowledge-augmented generation.
      RAP requires only one image per concept with its basic information for personalization. It allows
087
      users to make real-time adjustments to the model’s outputs by modifying their personal databases,
088
      eliminating the need for retraining. A more detailed comparison is presented in Table 1.
089
090   Another significant challenge is the lack of large-scale datasets for training MLLMs’ personalized
091   generation capabilities. To address this, we design a pipeline to collect extensive training data and
092
      create a comprehensive dataset, which enables to train MLLMs to effectively understand and utilize
      user-related information for generation. Based on this dataset, we train LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023b)
093
      and Phi3-V (Rasheed et al., 2024) as novel personalized assistants and evaluate their performance
094
      across various tasks, including personalized image captioning, question answering, and visual recog-
095   nition. Experimental results demonstrate that our RAP-MLLMs excel in wide range of personalized
096   generation tasks, showcasing excellent generation quality and flexibility.
097
098
      Our contributions are summarized as follows:
099
          • We propose the RAP framework for MLLMs’ personalization, allowing models to be trained
100
            just once and adapt to diverse users and infinite new concepts without further training.
101
102       • We develop a pipeline for collecting large-scale data and create a dataset specifically designed
103         for the personalized training and evaluation of MLLMs. This dataset enables us to train a series
104         of MLLMs to function as personalized assistants.
105       • Our models demonstrate exceptional performance across various personalized multimodal gen-
106         eration tasks, including personalized image captioning and question answering. Additionally,
107         they exhibit a strong capability to recognize personal concepts within images.

122   Multimodal Large Language Models. Recently, numerous advanced large language models
123   (LLMs) (Touvron et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023b; Chiang et al., 2023; Taori et al., 2023) have
124   been proposed, showing remarkable performance in addressing a wide range of tasks. The rapid de-
125   velopment of these LLMs has led to the emergence of multimodal LLMs (MLLMs) (OpenAI, 2023;
126   Gemini-Team, 2024; Liu et al., 2023b; Zhang et al., 2024; Han et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2023), which
127   excel in general visual understanding and complex reasoning tasks. For instance, LLaVA (Liu et al.,
128   2023b;a) and MiniGPT-4 (Zhu et al., 2023) align visual and language modalities through visual in-
129   struction tuning, showcasing impressive capabilities in multimodal conversations. GPT4RoI (Zhang
130
      et al., 2023c) and RegionGPT (Guo et al., 2024) enhance fine-grained understanding and reasoning
      for specific regions by training on region-level instruction datasets. Despite these advancements in
131
      tasks such as image captioning and question answering, the lack of user-specific knowledge restricts
132
      the generation of personalized content, which hinders the practical application of MLLMs in daily
133   life. In this work, we focus on the personalization of MLLMs, enabling them to remember and
134   understand user-specific concepts, and generate personalized content tailored to user’s preferences.
135
136
      Personalization of MLLMs. In the realm of artificial intelligence, personalization typically refers
      to the process of tailoring a system, application, or model to meet the individual needs and prefer-
137
      ences (Yeh et al., 2023; Woźniak et al., 2024; Shi et al., 2024). Substantial efforts have been made
138
      to generate images of user’s personal objects or in certain context (Ruiz et al., 2023; Kumari et al.,
139   2023; Ham et al., 2024; Gal et al., 2022; Ye et al., 2023). For example, Dreambooth (Ruiz et al.,
140   2023) employs transfer learning in text-to-image diffusion models via fine-tuning all parameters for
141   new concepts. In this paper, we mainly aim at enabling MLLMs to remember and understand user-
142   specific concepts, and generate personalized language outputs. There are several works focusing on
143   the personalization of MLLMs, among which the most relevant works are MyVLM (Alaluf et al.,
144   2024) and Yo’LLaVA (Nguyen et al., 2024). MyVLM introduces the task of personalizing VLMs.
145   It utilizes external classification heads to recognize specific concepts, and learns an embedding for
146   each concept to personalize the outputs of VLMs. Yo’LLaVA personalizes LLaVA by extending its
147
      vocabulary and learning specific tokens for each concept. However, both approaches require con-
      tinuous model updates as new concepts emerge, which presents challenges in dynamic real-world
148
      applications. In this work, we propose RAP framework for the personalization of MLLMs, enabling
149
      models to be trained once while continuously updating supported concepts without further training.
150
151   Retrieval Augmented Generation. Retrieval-based methods for incorporating external knowledge
152   have proven effective in enhancing generation across a variety of knowledge-intensive tasks (Gao
153
      et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023a; Asai et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023; Yoran et al., 2023; Lin et al.,
      2023b). DPR (Karpukhin et al., 2020) introduces Dense Passage Retrieval, marking a shift from
154
      sparse to dense retrieval techniques. Later, MuRAG (Chen et al., 2022) proposes to use multimodal
155
      knowledge to augment language generation. Self-Rag (Asai et al., 2023) introduces special tokens to
156   make retrieval adaptive and controllable. ERAGent (Shi et al., 2024) presents a comprehensive sys-
157   tem for retrieval-augmented language models. With the advancements in MLLMs, RAG has been
158   widely applied to multimodal generative tasks. For instance, FLMR (Lin et al., 2023a) employs
159   multi-dimensional embeddings to capture finer-grained relevance between queries and documents,
160   achieving significant improvement on the RA-VQA setting. While existing methods primarily en-
161   hance models’ performance by retrieving from external knowledge bases, few of them consider the
      personalization task. Although RAG has been applied to image generation (Blattmann et al., 2022;

187   3      R ETRIEVAL AUGMENTED P ERSONALIZATION
188
189   Existing MLLMs typically align other modalities with language. For instance, LLaVA (Liu et al.,
190   2023b) projects visual tokens into text space, and then generates subsequent tokens using an LLM.
191   While these MLLMs perform well in various tasks, the lack of memory and comprehension of
192   personal concepts hinders effective user-specific responses. In this work, we mainly focus on per-
193   sonalizing MLLMs to generate tailored language responses, such as creating personalized captions
194
      for user’s images and answering questions about personal concepts. In this section, we detail the
      implementation steps of our proposed Retrieval Augmented Personalization (RAP). Unlike previ-
195
      ous approaches that usually necessitate additional data collection and further training to learn new
196
      concepts, our RAP does not require additional training as the user’s database expands. By pretrain-
197   ing on our dataset, our RAP-MLLMs can adapt to diverse users and infinite new concepts without
198   further training. In section 3.1, we present the RAP framework that is applicable to various types of
199   MLLMs, and then in section 3.2, we provide details of the proposed dataset.
200
201
      3.1     RAP F RAMEWORK
202
203
      Our RAP works in three main steps: Remember, Retrieve and Generate, as shown in Figure 2.
204
205   Remember. The premise of personalization is that the model can remember personal concepts and
206   relevant information, such as the dog named 〈Lala〉 adopted by 〈A〉. To facilitate this, we construct
207
      a database M to store these personal concepts, which comprises an avatar, a name, and a brief
      description for each concept. The key for each concept in the database is its visual feature, obtained
208
      by feeding its image into a pre-trained image encoder E(·). Examples of our database are presented
209
      in Figure 2. When a user initiates a conversation, the input can be represented as Q = (I, T ),
210   which may include both image I and some textual instructions T . The first step involves identifying
211   possible concepts within the input image that have been previously stored in the database. Previous
212   methods (Alaluf et al., 2024) typically need to learn an external classifier to determine whether a
213   concept appears in the input image, which requires a substantial amount of training data and can
214   only apply to specific concept. To enhance the generalizability of the recognition process, we do not
215   construct specific modules for each concept. Instead, we employ a universal detection model, such
      as YOLO (Redmon et al., 2016) and YOLO-World Cheng et al. (2024), as recognition model R(·).

260   3.2   P ERSONALIZATION DATASET
261
262   Most existing MLLMs struggle to generate personalized outputs even if additional concept informa-
263   tion is provided, and there is currently no large-scale dataset for personalized training of MLLMs.
264   To this end, we design a pipeline for data creation and curate a novel dataset specifically for the per-
265   sonalized training and evaluation of MLLMs. We use Gemini-1.5 (Gemini-Team, 2024) to generate
266
      annotations for our dataset. An overview of our pipeline and dataset is presented in Figure 3.
267   The first component of our dataset is dedicated to visual grounding. In this task, a MLLM is trained
268   to determine whether a specific concept is in an image, particularly identifying if the person or object
269   in a reference image appears in the given image. When a positive match is detected, we also require
      the model to provide the bounding box for the identified concept. For single-concept grounding, we


                                                               5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      primarily use the RefCOCO dataset (Kazemzadeh et al., 2014). Based on RefCOCO’s annotations,
271   we crop target concepts from the images and assign names to them, which serve as references for
272   specific concepts. We then query Gemini to generate concise descriptions about properties of the
273   concepts in these cropped regions, by which we construct a large-scale database including numer-
274   ous different concepts. The training data pairs images and these descriptions as queries and the
275   corresponding bounding boxes as outputs. However, data generated in this way is insufficient to
276   simulate the complexity of real-world recognition, especially when the target concept in the ref-
277   erence and input image is captured from different perspectives. To address this, we incorporate
278   the ILSVRC2015-VID video object detection dataset (Russakovsky et al., 2015), TAO (Dave et al.,
279
      2020) and CustomConcept101 (Kumari et al., 2023) to enrich our dataset. For multi-object ground-
      ing, we use the Object365 dataset (Shao et al., 2019) to construct our training data.
280
281   The second component of our dataset is designed for instruction following. This section includes
282   training data for tasks such as image captioning, image description and question answering. For the
283   image captioning and description data, we provide cropped images of target concepts, accompanied
284   by their names and related information from the large-scale database, then query Gemini to generate
285
      a caption or description that reflects the concepts depicted in the entire image. For question answer-
      ing, we first design a set of seed questions to serve as examples. These examples are used to prompt
286
      the annotator, Gemini, to generate new questions and corresponding answers. This iterative process
287
      facilitates the creation of a rich and diverse collection of conversations that MLLMs can learn from.
288   We construct such data using RefCOCO (Kazemzadeh et al., 2014), Object365 (Shao et al., 2019),
289   TAO (Dave et al., 2020) and CustomConcept101 (Kumari et al., 2023) dataset.
290
291
      To enhance alignment with real-world scenarios, it is essential to collect data featuring the same
      identity in various environments. Thus, we also include multiple images about the same individual
292
      from the CelebA dataset (Liu et al., 2015) and produce question answering data about the individual.
293
      To further diversify the dataset, we apply image editing techniques for data augmentation. This
294   includes performing random rotations and flips on the cropped images, as well as generating novel
295   views of the concepts by diffusion models. Specifically, we use Inpaint-Anything (Yu et al., 2023) to
296   separate the foreground from the background, and use Wonder3D (Long et al., 2024) and Sith (Ho
297   et al., 2024) to synthesize novel views of foreground object or person respectively. Finally, we
298   combine these elements to generate images of the target concept from different perspectives.
299
      In the generation step, the MLLM needs to prioritize accurate and contextually relevant information.
300   Considering that retrieval results can be inaccurate, potentially leading to unreasonable answers, we
301   construct negative samples by incorporating noise elements into the additional input while preserv-
302   ing the original output. This approach trains the model’s discrimination capability. By exposing
303   the MLLM to both relevant and irrelevant information during training, it learns to discern and fil-
304   ter out noise, enhancing its robustness at inference time. Additionally, we include a subset of the
305   LLaVA-Instruct-665k visual instruction dataset (Liu et al., 2023a) to retain general knowledge from
306   the original MLLM. Further details about our dataset can be found in Appendix D.

310
311   Implementation Details. We conduct experiments on LLaVA-1.5-13B (Liu et al., 2023b) and Phi3-
312   V-3.8B (Rasheed et al., 2024), resulting in two personalized MLLMs, RAP-LLaVA and RAP-Phi3-
313   V. We select YOLO-Worldv2 (Cheng et al., 2024) as the detector and construct a multimodal re-
314
      triever using Facebook AI Similarity Search (FAISS) (Johnson et al., 2021), employing a pre-trained
      CLIP ViT-L/14-336 (Radford et al., 2021) as the visual encoder. Due to the context length limitation
315
      of the backbone language model, for RAP-LLaVA and RAP-Phi3-V, we retrieve the 2 and 3 different
316
      concepts with the highest similarity, respectively. More details can be found in Appendix C.
317
318   Training. In the training phase, we skip the recognition and retrieval procedures, instead perform
319   instruction tuning to train the MLLMs. We adhere to most settings from the original experiment
320
      of LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023b), except for using a maximum learning rate of 1e-4 and training for 1
      epoch. We employ low-rank adapters (Hu et al., 2022) to reduce the number of trainable parameters,
321
      and train our models on 8 A100 GPUs with a valid batch size of 64.
322
323   Evaluation. We primarily focus on tasks that require both visual and language understanding.
      Specifically, we address image captioning and question answering in Section 4.1 and 4.2, and com-

      4.1    P ERSONALIZED I MAGE C APTIONING
359
      In this section, we evaluate our models on generating personalized image captions with user’s spe-
360   cific concepts. We extend the dataset introduced by MyVLM (Alaluf et al., 2024) via adding 16 new
361   concepts, which include both objects and humans, forming 8 concept pairs that appear together in
362   images. For each pair, there are 8-13 images used for testing. This multiple concepts setting presents
363   additional challenges for personalization.
364
      Settings. We compare our models with MyVLM and finetuning based method LLaVA-LoRA (Hu
365

480   RAP requires only 1 image with its related information provided by the user, achieving outstanding

      and descriptions in the database, RAP enables real-time editing of personalized generation settings.

      5     C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this paper, we introduce the RAP framework for personalizing MLLMs. This framework enables
533
      MLLMs to understand an infinite number of user-specific concepts, generate personalized captions
534   and respond to user-related queries. To enhance the quality of the generated content and better
535   align outputs with user’s configuration, we curate a large-scale dataset for personalized training of
536   MLLMs. Using this dataset, we train a series of MLLMs to function as personalized assistants.
537   Experimental results show that RAP-MLLMs achieve exceptional performance in various personal-
538   ized generation tasks while preserving the general knowledge of the original MLLMs. Moreover,
539   our RAP framework allows real-time adjustments to generation settings. It eliminates the need for
      retraining on new concepts and provides significant flexibility in personalized generation.

786   C     M ORE E XPERIMENTAL D ETAILS
787
788
789
      Implementation details. We utilize YOLO-Worldv2-X (Cheng et al., 2024) as the detection model,
      setting detection classes to include all categories stored in the database to reduce the interventions
790
      from unrelated objects. We construct a multimodal retriever using Facebook AI Similarity Search
791
      (FAISS) (Johnson et al., 2021), employing a pre-trained CLIP ViT-L/14-336 (Radford et al., 2021)
792   as the visual encoder. Each key in the database is generated by inputting the image of a concept
793   into the CLIP visual encoder, resulting in a 768-dimensional vector. Considering the restriction of
794   context length of the backbone language model, we retrieve the 2 most similar images from the
795   database for each region of interest. And then, we select 2 and 3 different concepts with the highest
796   similarity among all as supplementary inputs for RAP-LLaVA and RAP-Phi3-V, respectively.
797
      External knowledge base. For MMMU (Yue et al., 2024), we use 30K images paired with corre-
798   sponding captions from Wikipedia as the external knowledge base. During testing, we retrieve the
799   three most similar images based on the question’s image and incorporate only the textual knowledge
800   to the input. For InfoSeek (Chen et al., 2023), we randomly sample 5K questions from the validation
801   set and construct a knowledge base containing 50K entities from Wikipedia database provided by
802   the authors, which includes all relevant entities associated with the questions. For each question, we
803   retrieve the most similar entity and add only the textual knowledge to the input.
804   Baselines. For MyVLM, we find that when the training data is very limited, it is quite hard for
805   the classification head to work effectively. Therefore, we use data augmentation to help improve
806   its performance. Specifically, we crop the single image into several pieces containing the target
807   concept to improve the accuracy of classification heads. To distinguish between multiple possible
808   different concepts that may appear in the image, we use 〈sks1〉, 〈sks2〉. . . as concept identifiers. For
809   YoLLaVA, as there is no open-source code or model available, we present its experimental results
      as reported in the original paper (Nguyen et al., 2024).

913   F     L IMITATION
914
915   Our proposed RAP framework is a retrieval-based method. The limitations of RAP mainly concern
916   the additional computational cost of generation and the precision of the retriever. While incorpo-
917   rating external information effectively generates more specific answers, it inevitably increases the
      context length for MLLMs, leading to additional computational overhead during the generation pro-


                                                        17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      cess. We will further explore ways to mitigate this computational burden. Another limitation is the
919   personalization performance of our RAP-MLLMs depends on the retriever’s capability This pro-
920   poses need for a robust multi-modal retriever that can discern intricate features to enhance retrieval
921   precision. Despite these limitations, RAP offers a timely solution for MLLM personalization. By
922   retrieving from a user’s specific database, RAP facilitates reliable and flexible personalized genera-
923   tion, which is valuable in practical applications.
