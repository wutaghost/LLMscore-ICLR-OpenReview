011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Large language models (LLMs) have exhibited remarkable reasoning and planning
014            capabilities. Most prior work in this area has used LLMs to reason through steps
015            from an initial to a goal state or criterion, thereby effectively reasoning in a forward
016            direction. Nonetheless, many planning problems exhibit an inherent asymmetry
017            such that planning backward from the goal is significantly easier — for example, if
018            there are bottlenecks close to the goal. We take inspiration from this observation and
019            demonstrate that this bias holds for LLM planning as well: planning performance
020
               in one direction correlates with the planning complexity of the problem in that
               direction. However, our experiments also reveal systematic biases which lead
021
               to poor planning in the backward direction. With this knowledge, we propose a
022
               backward planning algorithm for LLMs that first flips the problem and then plans
023            forward in the flipped problem. This helps avoid the backward bias, generate
024            more diverse candidate plans, and exploit asymmetries between the forward and
025            backward directions in planning problems — we find that combining planning in
026            both directions with self-verification improves the overall planning success rates
027            by 4-24% in three planning domains. Code: anonymous.repo.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Large Language Models (LLMs) are increasingly capable in reasoning tasks — they can perform
034   commonsense reasoning in a broad set of contexts (Kojima et al., 2022), reason about abstract patterns
035   in data (Mirchandani et al., 2023), and learn to reason from human feedback (Liang et al., 2024). Such
036   capabilities also open up the possibility of LLMs performing long-horizon planning (Valmeekam
037   et al., 2024), where the model needs to reason about how the initial state and final goal of the problem
038   can be connected through intermediate steps. Most existing work has explored such problems by
039
      asking the model to reason in the forward direction, i.e., generating intermediate steps from the
      initial state to the final goal. However, in many planning problems, there is an inherent asymmetry:
040
      generating the correct last steps leading to the goal can be much easier than generating the correct
041
      steps from the beginning. This leads to the question: can LLMs plan better if they also reason in the
042   backward direction?
043
044   As an example, consider a robot navigating in a room ( Fig. 1): if the final goal is the bedroom at
045
      the end of a long and narrow hallway, it is natural to connect the bedroom with the beginning of the
      hallway first in the plan, and then search for the path that connects the hallway to the initial state. In
046
      this example, there is a “bottleneck" that causes the asymmetry: the number of possibilities when
047
      planning backward from the goal is constrained by the bottleneck (hallway), while the possibilities
048   fan out quickly when planning from the start. Such bottlenecks are ubiquitous in planning problems;
049   for example, in proving mathematical theorems (Loveland, 2016), there may be many possible
050   steps to start a proof of a theorem, but the final steps can be much more closely related to the
051   theorem statement and thus easier to choose. In our experiments, we quantify this bottleneck effect
052   by comparing the exact number of search steps used by common forward-search algorithm (e.g.,
053   Breadth-First Search) when applied in the forward and backward directions on the underlying graph
      of the planning problem ( Fig. 1).

                                     The goals of this paper are twofold. First, we perform extensive experiments on three classical
                                     planning problems (Section 2) and examine whether LLM-based planning is significantly impacted
                           076
                                     by the asymmetry described above, i.e., if LLMs achieve higher planning success rates in the direction
                           077
                                     (forward/backward) where fewer computations are needed. Second, we study whether LLMs can
                           078       exploit bottleneck effects by effectively planning backward (Section 3). Our results provide strong
                           079       evidence for the first hypothesis. However, unfortunately, we find that LLMs exhibit a systematic bias
                           080       of performing worse when planning backward; this may be attributed to the forward autoregressive
                           081       nature of LLM output generation, as well as biases from the training dataset.
                           082
                                     Addressing the backward reasoning bias, we propose a simple solution (Section 4): many problems
                           083
                                     can be transformed such that the original goal becomes the initial state and the original initial state
                           084       becomes the goal — we ask LLMs to first “flip” the problem, and then plan in the forward direction
                           085       (corresponding to the original backward direction). Given a planning problem, we ask LLMs to
                           086       sample possible plans in the forward direction of both the original problem and the flipped one, and
                           087       then self-verify (Stechly et al., 2024) all the plans before choosing the final one. We find that this
                           088       simple setup allows LLMs to generate many more diverse candidate plans and exploit the bottleneck
                           089       structure of the problems, improving the overall success rate in multiple planning domains by 4-24%
                           090       (Section 5). Perhaps surprisingly, we also present evidence that in certain settings LLMs can also
                           091       reason whether to flip the problem or not by examining the problem structure.

                           096       We define a (text-based) planning problem as P = (S, AS , s0 , g, Tmax , f ), where S is the space of
                           097       possible states, AS = {As }s∈S includes the space of possible actions (planned steps) at each state
                           098       s ∈ S, s0 ∈ S is the initial state, g ∈ S is the goal, Tmax is the maximum allowable steps, and f is a
                           099       ground-truth solution verifier that determines if a plan solves P , i.e., whether a plan connects s0 to g,
                           100
                                     and possibly, is of optimal length. The generated plan A = (a0 , ..., aT )T ≤(Tmax −1) is verified by f
                                                                                                                                  at
                           101       based on these rules. Each action at converts state st to st+1 , and we denote this as st −→    st+1 .
                           102       We denote pre-trained LLMs with parameters θ as pθ . Instead of the usual token-level gen-
                           103       eration, for convenience we consider LLMs generating the next step in a plan by sampling
                           104       at ∼ pθ (at |s0 , g, {a0 , ..., at−1 }, q), where q denotes the rest of the text prompt (e.g., few-shot
                           105       exemplars and instructions). For convenience, from now on we also assume that q includes text de-
                           106       scriptions of s0 , g, and (a0 , ..., at−1 ). During planning, LLMs may also generate other intermediaries
                           107       such as the estimated states along the plan (ŝ1 , ..., ŝt ), which might help the LLM reason about the
                                     next step given the current estimated state.

                                              Next we introduce the three different planning domains (Fig. 1) considered in our work. Appendix A
                              109             provides more details on the design of these domains.
                              110
                              111
                                              Graph Planning. In G RAPH P LANNING, each problem consists of a graph with N nodes (labeled
                              112             with a number 1, ..., N ). Each pair of nodes is connected with an edge with probability ρ; we consider
                              113             both undirected and directed graphs. The initial state s0 and goal g are two different nodes. In order
                              114             to vary difficulty, randomly generated graphs are filtered such that the shortest path from s0 to g
                              115             has length K. In this domain, we consider a plan correct if it is optimal, i.e., shortest among paths
                              116             between s0 and g. We use a text-based incident representation for graphs (e.g., “Node 1 is connected
                              117             to 2, 3”), which has been shown to be effective in the context of LLMs solving graph problems
                              118             (Fatemi et al., 2023).
                              119
                              120             Array Transformation. In A RRAY T RANSFORMATION, the LLM is asked to convert an initial
                              121             array of integers of length N to a goal array, e.g., from [0, 1, 3] to [1, 0, 3, 1, 0, 3], using a set of array
                              122             functions. We consider functions including: repeat to repeat the array once, cut to cut the array in
                              123             half if the first half matches the second half, shift_left and shift_right to shift the array to
                              124
                                              the left by one (e.g., [0, 1, 3] → [1, 3, 0]) or to the right, reverse to reverse the array, and swap to
                                              swap the first and last elements. Notice that each function has an inverse. We generate the problems
                              125
                                              by randomly sampling an initial array and a set of K functions which are applied to obtain the final
                              126
                                              array. We consider a plan correct if it is valid, i.e., if planned functions convert the initial array to the
                              127             goal. This domain can also be seen as a form of probabilistic context-free grammar (PCFG) (Sipser,
                              128             1996).
                              129
                              130             Blocksworld. B LOCKSWORLD is part of the PlanBench benchmark introduced by Valmeekam
                              131             et al. (2024) for examining the overall planning capabilities of LLMs. In each problem, there are
                              132             four blocks colored red, yellow, blue, or orange. The initial and goal states involve different possi-
                              133             ble stacks of the blocks, and the possible actions include unstack <color> block from
                              134             <color> block, pick up <color> block (from table), put <color> block
                              135             on <table>/<color> block. We consider a plan correct if it is valid, i.e., if the planned
                              136             steps move the initial stack to the goal.

                                              As described in Section 1, many planning problems have an asymmetry that makes a given direction

                              143             performance in a given planning direction can be predicted by the number of search steps required

                              145             planning by planing backward when the backward direction is favorable. Below we first introduce
                              146             the backward planning algorithm we use for LLMs before showing the experimental results.

      3.2   A LGORITHM : S AMPLE FORWARD ( BACKWARD ) THEN SELF - VERIFY
163
164
      While the basic version of LLM planning involves asking it to generate a single plan, we consider
165
      a more robust version where the LLM first samples multiple candidate solutions in one direction,
166
      and then self-verifies them to choose the final solution; then the verifier f checks the final plan. In
167   both stages, the LLM is shown few-shot exemplars to familiarize itself with the problem and to learn
168   to verify the candidate plans step by step. The overall algorithm is illustrated in Fig. 2. Given the
169   maximum number of attempts M , the sampling temperature of the LLM is set to be 0 for the first
170   attempt, and nonzero for the later ones. Self-verification works differently depending on the domain:
171   in G RAPH P LANNING, since the solution needs to be optimal, we save all the unique candidate plans
172   {Aj } after all M attempts, and then present all of them to the LLM and ask it to verify them as
173   well as to choose the optimal one; in A RRAY T RANSFORMATION and B LOCKSWORLD, since the
174   solution does not need to be optimal, the LLM verifies each candidate plan as soon as it is sampled,
175
      and either stops when it deems one plan correct or after all attempts. For all the experiments, we use
      the GPT-4o model from OpenAI unless noted otherwise.

182   undirected graphs in G RAPH P LANNING, and ask the LLM to plan either forward or backward. We

184
      success rates achieved by forward and backward planning at different levels of forward/backward

                                 If the LLM cannot plan backward as well as forward, how can it effectively exploit the backward
                         219     direction in planning? We propose a simple solution: flip the problem such that the original goal
                         220     becomes the new initial state and vice versa. The LLM can then plan in the forward direction for the
                         221     flipped problem, which corresponds to the backward direction of the original problem. This avoids
                         222     the bias of weak LLM planning in the backward direction. However, there are a few subtleties that
                         223     must be taken care of when flipping the planning problem.
                         224
                         225     Change of the state-dependent action space. In some cases, AS needs to be adjusted for the
                         226     flipped problem. For example, with a directed graph, an edge from Node 1 to Node 3 in the original
                         227     problem corresponds to an edge from Node 3 to Node 1 in the flipped problem — Node 3 might
                         228     not be reachable from Node 1 in the flipped problem. In contrast, there is no change needed for
                         229     undirected graphs, A RRAY T RANSFORMATION, and B LOCKSWORLD. With directed graphs, we
                         230
                                 prompt the LLM to first generate the new text representation of the graph (Fig. 4 right), and then
                                 generate a plan for the flipped problem.

                         232

                                   6       R ELATED W ORK
                         474
                                   Bidirectional planning. It is well known in classical planning (LaValle, 2006) that searching and
                         475
                                   planning from the backward direction can often reduce the computations needed. Bi-directional
                         476
                                   search has been incorporated into popular sampling-based planners (e.g., rapidly-exploring random
                         477       trees) (Jordan & Perez, 2013) and heuristics-based techniques (e.g., A*) (Kuroiwa & Fukunaga, 2020)
                         478       to improve efficiency.
                         479
                         480       Planning with LLMs. LLMs have been widely applied in different planning problems (Kambham-
                         481       pati et al.) such as text games (Yao et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023), robot planning (Huang et al.,
                         482       2022; Ahn et al., 2022; Ren et al., 2023), scientific experimentation (Wang et al., 2022a), and web
                         483       navigation (Deng et al., 2024) — their strong planning capabilities originate from pre-training with
                         484       enormous amounts of text data, which elicits reasoning capabilities (Wei et al., 2022). Beyond simple
                         485       zero- or few-shot learning, LLMs have also been combined with classical planners (Liu et al., 2023;
                                   Silver et al., 2024) and external tools (Zeng et al., 2022) to further boost performance. However, no


                                                                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      previous work has systematically studied asymmetries between forward and backward planning for
487   LLMs, or leveraged backward reasoning to improve LLM planning.
488
489   Backward reasoning in LLMs. Despite not being applied in planning problems, backward reason-
490   ing has been used with LLMs for self-verifying forward-sampled solutions to math problems (Jiang
491   et al., 2023). Another line of work (Kazemi et al., 2022; Lee & Hwang, 2024) applies backward
492   chaining, which recursively breaks the target into sub-targets based on defined rules, mostly for veri-
493   fying a statement or proof. Our work instead proposes a general solution to planning problems using
494   both forward and backward reasoning, and is motivated by a systematic examination of asymmetries
495   in forward and backward planning for LLMs.
496
497   7   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
498
499   In this work, we investigate how to enable LLMs to effectively plan in the backward direction
500   from the desired goal to improve the overall planning success rate. First, our experiments reveal
501   consistently worse performance of LLMs when planning backward. To address this, we propose
502   instructing the LLM to flip the problem first and then plan forward in the flipped problem. Combined
503   with self-verification, we find that generating candidate solutions from both directions improves
504   planning success rates by 4-24% over forward-only planning in three different domains.
505   One immediate future direction is to better teach LLMs to reason and plan backward, e.g., by
506   fine-tuning with correct forward and backward reasoning traces (Zelikman et al., 2022). We also
507   believe that our framework of combining forward and backward reasoning can be extended to general
508   reasoning problems, e.g., allowing LLMs to generate more diverse reasoning traces from the backward
509   direction (Yao et al., 2024), or, enforcing reasoning self-consistency from both forward and backward
510   directions (Wang et al., 2022b).
