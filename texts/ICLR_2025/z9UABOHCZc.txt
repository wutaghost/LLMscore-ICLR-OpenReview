009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Timestamp prediction aims to accurately determine the date and hour at which
012            an image was captured using only visual cues, with applications ranging from
013            image retrieval and metadata correction to digital forensics. In outdoor scenes,
014            this can be inferred from variables such as overall brightness, hue, and shadow
015            positions for hourly estimations, as well as weather patterns or seasonal changes
016
               for determining the date. However, these factors vary greatly depending on ge-
               ographical location, making the challenges of time-of-capture prediction closely
017
               related to geo-localization. To address this problem, we introduce GeoTimeCLIP, a
018
               novel method capable of simultaneously estimating both the capture time (i.e., hour
019            and month) and geo-location (i.e., GPS coordinates) of an image using a retrieval
020            approach. Our model employs an image encoder, a time encoder, and a location
021            encoder, aligning the time and GPS embeddings with the image embeddings in
022            a continuous high-dimensional feature space. Considering the cyclical nature of
023            days and years, we propose an effective way to represent time using Random
024            Fourier Features. To learn image-time embedding alignment, rather than applying
025            a standard contrastive loss with hard positives and negatives, we propose a more
026            effective metric learning-based objective, which provides soft targets by consider-
027
               ing the time difference between samples over a toroidal manifold. We introduce
               new benchmarks for time prediction, where we show that our jointly optimized
028
               time-location-based method outperforms baselines optimized solely for time. We
029
               also evaluate our method on existing geo-localization protocols, demonstrating that
030            our approach performs competitively with expert geo-localization methods. Our
031            shared embedding space enables various downstream tasks, such as compositional
032            retrieval and text-based retrieval.

035   1    I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Estimating the capture time and geo-location of images is crucial for applications ranging from digital
038   forensics to ecological studies and social media management. In digital forensics, accurate timestamps
039
      verify image authenticity and help detect manipulation, particularly when camera calibrations are
      suspect. This capability is essential for reconstructing events from timestamped images during
040
      accidents or natural disasters, providing critical information to first responders. Ecological studies
041
      benefit from time-ordered images to monitor changes in landscapes and wildlife, while precise
042   timestamps in social media enhance content management and chronological sorting. Despite its
043   importance, predicting time from images presents several challenges due to the intricate relationship
044   between temporal cues and location-specific factors. Time of day and year manifest differently in
045   images due to variables like scene brightness, shadows, weather, and seasonal changes, making
046   it difficult to establish consistent patterns. The complexity of the task is further compounded as
047   the visual appearance of specific hours vary substantially across different months and locations,
048   influenced by the amount and relative exposure to sunlight. Additionally, the representation of months
049   fluctuates across various latitudes, with regions near the equator experiencing relatively stable climate
050
      conditions year-round compared to regions at higher latitudes.
051   Most existing methods (Zhai et al., 2019; Salem et al., 2022; Padilha et al., 2022) rely heavily on
052   GPS data for accurate time estimation, making the absence of such metadata a significant challenge.
053   Conversely, state-of-the-art geo-localization models, such as PIGEON (Haas et al., 2024) and
      GeoCLIP (Vivanco Cepeda et al., 2024), can effectively predict locations at the country or continent

054
      level. However, accurately predicting both time and location without relying on additional inputs
055   remains an unaddressed challenge.
056
057   In this paper, we introduce GeoTimeCLIP, a retrieval-based approach for joint geo-localization and
058
      time prediction. We conceptualize time prediction as a retrieval problem, representing time as a
      month-hour pair. A schematic diagram of our framework is depicted in Figure 1. Building upon
059
      the CLIP-initialized visual model, our goal is to learn a shared embedding space where we can
060
      align visual (image), time, and location modalities. For time prediction, we first propose a time
061   representation that considers the cyclical nature days and years. We then project these representation
062   into a multi-scale, high-dimensional time embedding using random Fourier features (RFFs) (Tancik
063   et al., 2020). Next, to learn the alignment between the time and image embeddings, we explore
064   several possibilities. Existing contrastive learning methods, including CLIP (Radford et al., 2021)
065   and SimCLR (Chen et al., 2020), use other batch instances as negative samples. While suitable for
066   the image-location contrastive losses in CSP (Mai et al., 2023), GeoCLIP (Vivanco Cepeda et al.,
067   2024) and SatCLIP (Klemmer et al., 2023), due to the significant variation of visual appearance with
068   respect to geographical location, this approach does not work well for the image-time modality. As
069
      visual features vary smoothly over time, and adjacent hours or months often appear nearly identical,
      treating them as negatives severely impedes alignment. Instead of defining positive-negative pairs as
070
      in contrastive learning, we propose a novel Temporal Metric Learning approach, which encourages
071
      similarity between two instances based on their time difference. To build the target metric for our
072   proposed loss, we use the toroidal distance between the times of each instance pair to consider the
073   cyclic nature of time.
074
075
      This approach enhances performance without the need for explicit assignment of positive and negative
      samples, providing a more effective and efficient solution for time prediction. By mapping the image,
076
      location, and time modalities into a unified feature space, our model gains the ability to perform
077
      compositional retrieval tasks. For instance, given a specific time and location, it can efficiently
078   retrieve all corresponding images from a gallery that closely match the specified criteria.
079
080   Our main contributions are the following:
081
             • A framework for joint time-of-capture prediction and geo-localization by aligning the
082
               image, time and location embeddings in a shared multimodal feature space using contrastive
083            learning.
084
             • First retrieval-based method for time-of-capture prediction where we propose the novel time
085
               representation in month-hour pairs, considering its cyclic nature.
086
087
             • Novel Temporal Metric Learning based loss function for image-time alignment with soft
               targets, eliminating the need to assign positive and negative samples to the anchor. Since
088
               both hours and months are cyclic, we employ a cyclic toroidal distance instead of a regular
089
               L2 distance which results in improved performance.
090
091
             • We propose new standard benchmarks for time prediction, demonstrating that our jointly
               optimized time-location method surpasses time-only optimized baselines and competes
092
               well with expert geo-localization methods. Our shared embedding space further facilitates
093
               downstream tasks like compositional and text-based retrieval.

098   Time-of-capture prediction: Time-of-capture prediction is a relatively new problem that has only
099
      been directly addressed by a handful of prior works. Tsai et al. (2016) proposed a physically inspired
      method to infer the time of day by estimating the sun’s position and camera orientation, but their
100
      approach requires sky visibility and additional metadata, such as GPS coordinates and access to an
101
      external image database. In a different line of research, Zhai et al. (2019) introduced a data-centric
102   approach to learn geo-temporal image features. Their model uses an image, location, and time
103   encoders to generate mid-level features, which are subsequently passed to a set of classifiers for
104   predicting time and location as discrete classes. However, their evaluation shows that providing the
105   location as an input is crucial for predicting the time of day with reasonable accuracy. Similarly,
106   Salem et al. (2022) proposed a hierarchical model to predict the month, hour, and week of capture,
107   but this method also assumes known geo-location, limiting its real-world applicability. In contrast,
      our model relies solely on images to generate accurate time predictions using a retrieval approach in


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      a continuous shared feature space, with resolution determined by a gallery of arbitrary size rather
109   than discrete classes.
110
111   Other works have also explored the time-of-capture task indirectly. Li et al. (2017) presented an
112
      algorithm to verify image capture time and location by comparing the sun position, computed from the
      claimed time and location, with the actual sun position derived from shadow length and orientation.
113
      However, their approach assumes that latitude, time-of-day, and time-of-year are given, with only one
114
      potentially corrupted. Padilha et al. (2022) proposed a model for time-of-capture verification using a
115   data-centric approach, involving four encoders for ground-level images, timestamps, geo-locations,
116   and satellite images, fed into a binary classifier to predict time consistency. Similarly, Salem et al.
117   (2020) proposed a model to generate a global-scale dynamic map of visual appearance by matching
118   visual attributes across images annotated with timestamps and GPS coordinates. Both Padilha et al.
119   (2022) and Salem et al. (2020) show qualitative results on time prediction, but are limited by their
120   dependency on geo-location. In contrast, our method accurately estimates both GPS coordinates and
121   timestamps using only images.
122   Several additional methods explore problems adjacent to time prediction. Jacobs et al. (2007)
123   proposed an algorithm for geo-locating static cameras by comparing temporal principal components
124   of yearly image sequences with those from a gallery of known locations. For shadow detection,
125   Lalonde et al. (2012) used a multi-stage method to extract pixel-wise ground-shadow features and
126   find edges using CRF optimization, while Wehrwein et al. (2015) used illumination ratios to label
127   shadow points in a 3D reconstruction and compute dense shadow labels in pixel space. Another series
128   of works estimate the sun position for various downstream applications, such as computing camera
129
      parameters from timelapses (Lalonde et al., 2010) and determining outdoor illumination conditions
      (Lalonde et al., 2012; Hold-Geoffroy et al., 2017) from single images. Adapting these methods for
130
      time prediction would require additional metadata, which might not be available during inference.
131
      For example, even with correct sun position prediction, day of the year, geo-location, and compass
132   orientation are needed to accurately predict the hour.
133
134   Although the above works contribute to time prediction, they either require additional input metadata
135
      like GPS coordinates, or are not reliable in the absence of specific temporal cues. In contrast, our
      proposed GeoTimeCLIP aims to predict both time-of-day and month from a single image without
136
      relying on any additional metadata, making it more broadly applicable for real-time prediction tasks.
137
138   Global geo-localization: Geo-localization, the task of estimating the geographic coordinates of an
139   image, has gained substantial popularity in recent years. Traditionally, geo-localization methods have
140   adopted either a classification approach (Weyand et al., 2016; Seo et al., 2018; Vo et al., 2017; Muller-
141
      Budack et al., 2018; Pramanick et al., 2022) or an image retrieval approach (Regmi & Shah, 2019;
      Shi et al., 2019; 2020; Toker et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2021; 2022). The classification approach divides
142
      the Earth into a fixed number of geo-cells, assigning the center coordinate of the selected class as the
143
      GPS prediction. However, this method can result in significant errors depending on the size of the
144   geo-cells, even when the correct class is selected. In contrast, the image retrieval approach compares a
145   query image to a gallery and retrieves the one with the highest similarity. GeoCLIP (Vivanco Cepeda
146   et al., 2024) addresses the limitations of traditional approaches by framing global geo-localization as
147   a GPS retrieval problem. It leverages the pretrained CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) ViT and employs
148   contrastive learning to align image and location embeddings in a shared feature space. Other methods,
149   such as PIGEON (Haas et al., 2024), use a hybrid strategy: first employing image classification to
150   identify the top-k geo-cells with the highest probability, followed by a secondary retrieval stage for
151   refinement within and across geo-cells. However, PIGEON’s dependence on additional metadata
152
      for training—such as administrative boundaries, climate, and traffic—poses a significant limitation.
      Finally, recent methods such as Img2Loc (Zhou et al., 2024) have begun leveraging multimodal
153
      large language models (MLLMs) and retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) to achieve competitive
154
      geo-localization performance without the need for dedicated training. However, this method’s
155   effectiveness is highly dependent on the underlying MLLM and results in substantial inference
156   overhead.

287   Proposed Temporal Metric Learning: For location, the visual features change significantly due
288   to differences in landmarks, resulting in the visual embeddings not maintaining a smooth transition
289   with the distance in location embeddings. For instance, two neighborhoods in the same city might
290   appear markedly different. Therefore, formulating standard contrastive objectives with positives
291   and negatives is appropriate for location-image alignment (Eq 2). However, visual cues vary more
292
      gradually over time. Consequently, treating two samples that are temporally close as negatives can
      significantly hinder learning. This makes defining clear positives and negatives, in regular contrastive
293
      loss, challenging for time-image alignment. Motivated by these considerations, we propose a metric-
294
      learning based objective called Temporal Metric Learning, which accounts for the smooth and cyclic
295   nature of time by matching the similarity of instances according to their temporal distance.
296

      Datasets and evaluation details: For training, we use two existing datasets: MediaEval Placing
327   Tasks 2016 (MP-16) (Larson et al., 2017) and Cross-View Time (CVT) (Salem et al., 2020). MP-16
328   consists of 4.72 Million images from Flickr annotated only with GPS coordinates. CVT originally
329   consists of 206k geo-tagged smartphone pictures from the Yahoo Flickr Creative Commons 100
330   Million Dataset (Thomee et al., 2016) and 98k images from static outdoor webcams of the SkyFinder
331   Dataset (Mihail et al., 2016).
332
      Geo-localization performance is evaluated by measuring the geodesic distance between the real and
333   predicted GPS coordinates, and then computing the ratio of images that are correctly predicted given
334   five different distance thresholds (1 km, 25 km, 200 km, 750 km, and 2500 km). Time prediction
335   performance is evaluated by measuring the mean absolute month (Em ) and hour (EH ) errors between
336   the real and predicted times. We also report an overall Time Prediction Score (T P S) to select a

      Experiments on the robustness of GeoTimeCLIP: Existing publicly available datasets with time-
433   of-capture metadata either have a small number of samples or noisy timestamps. Furthermore, even
434   fewer datasets have fully open sources licenses, restricting their use to research purposes. Taking into
435   account these issues, we explore the robustness of our model on settings with limited data availability
436   as well as injected timestamp noise. For data availability, we progressively remove training samples
437   from the geo-localization and time prediction datasets going from 100% to 5% in four steps. Similarly,
438   for the noisy setting, we inject Gaussian noise into the time labels with increasing standard deviations,
439   going from 0 to 3 months and hours. In Tables 3 and 4, we show that our model is surprisingly robust
440   against both types of perturbations. Even with only 10% of the data (12.5k samples), the model is
441
      still able to learn meaningful time representations, with increased time errors of 0.3 months 0.22
      hours. In the noisy labels setting, GeoTimeCLIP is able to handle noise levels up until σ = 2 hours
442
      and months without a major drop in performance. For setting with σ = 3, the error of the month
443
      prediction task increases significantly, but the time error stays constant.

531   5    C ONCLUSION
532
      We introduce GeoTimeCLIP, a novel framework for jointly predicting the time and location of an
533
      image using a retrieval approach. GeoTimeCLIP not only shows competitive performance compared
534   to state-of-the-art geo-localization models but also introduces the capability of precise time-of-
535   capture predictions. A key innovation of our approach is the novel temporal metric loss, which
536   significantly outperforms traditional contrastive losses in time prediction tasks. Furthermore, our
537   results demonstrate that GeoTimeCLIP extends beyond standard prediction and geo-localization tasks.
538   It supports additional functionalities like compositional image retrieval, as well as text-to-location
539   and text-to-image retrieval, indicating a profound understanding of the interplay between images,
      locations, and time.

      C    I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
790
791
      Following GeoCLIP, the backbone of the image encoder is a pretrained ViT-L/14 from CLIP and the
      MLP consists of two fully connected layers with ReLU activation function and dimensions 768 and
792
      512 respectively. We use the same architecture for the time and location encoders as GeoCLIP. Both
793
      employ three RFF positional encoding layers, mapping the 2-dimensional GPS to a vector with 512
794   dimensions. The standard deviation values used to sample the RFF are σi ∈ {20 , 24 , 28 }. The MLPs
795   from the time and location encoder have three hidden layers with 1024 dimensions and a projection
796   layer to map the final embeddings into a feature space of 512 dimensions. In the location encoder,
797   we use a dynamic queue that stores the last 4096 seen locations, but we don’t use it for time. The
798   GPS coordinates and times are augmented by adding Gaussian noise with standard deviation of 150
799   meters for the in-batch GPS, 1500 meters for the GPS queue, 0.15 months and 0.15 hours for time.
800   We perform two augmentations for each image in the training set using random resized crops of size
801   224, random horizontal flipping and image normalization.

803   D    T RAINING PROTOCOL
804
805   GeoTimeCLIP is trained for 20 epochs using a cosine decay scheduler, with learning rate values
806   ranging from αmax = 3 × 10−5 to αmin = 3 × 10−7 . We use Adam optimizer with coefficients
807   β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.999 and ℓ2 penalty of 1 × 10−6 . For the contrastive losses, we use two learnable
808   temperature parameters that are optimized during training. The batch size B is set to 512 for all
809   experiments, and the models are trained on a machine with 12 CPU cores and a NVIDIA RTX A6000
      GPU.

      E     DATASET DETAILS
811
812   We apply two filters to remove samples from the CVT that don’t provide meaningful temporal
813   information. In particular, we remove all night-time and indoor images, since they often have
814   inconsistent temporal cues. To remove night images, we estimate the sunrise and sunset times from
815   the date, latitude and longitude using the General Solar Position algorithm, and remove all samples
816   before sunrise or after sunset. Then, for indoor images, we leverage a CNN model pretrained on
817   the Places365 Dataset Zhou et al. (2017). In general, night images often have inconsistent artificial
818   lighting, more noise or specialized cameras such as night vision. Indoor images also have artificial
819   lighting and controlled temperature, making it difficult to estimate the time or date.
820   Regarding the levels of noise in the dataset, the SkyFinder subset consists of images with accurate
821   time estimates, since they were collected from calibrated outdoor webcams. However, we observed
822   that CVT has a moderate amount of noisy labels. Thus in order to train a model that can accurately
823   predict the time, we need both datasets in the training set.
824   For evaluating the models, we employ a subset of unseen SkyFinder cameras, as well as two geo-
825   localization datasets used by other state-of-the-art methods for evaluation: Im2GPS3k and GWS15k.
826   Similar to GeoCLIP, we create a 100k GPS gallery to evaluate the model on Im2GPS3k, a 500k GPS
827   gallery for GWS15k, and a 100k time gallery for the SkyFinder test set. The GPS galleries are created
828   by sampling GPS coordinates from the MP-16 dataset, while the time gallery is created by sampling
829   times from the combined CVT and SkyFinder training sets.

831   F     L IMITATIONS AND REPRODUCIBILITY CHALLENGES OF EXISTING TIME
832
            PREDICTION METHODS
833
834
      Most previous time prediction methods suffer from a lack of standardization in their training and
835   evaluation protocols. For instance, Zhai et al. (2019) use subsets of the AMOS (Jacobs et al., 2007;
836   2009) and YFCC100M (Thomee et al., 2016) datasets, without providing the source code or exact
837   dataset splits necessary to replicate their experiments. Additionally, their time prediction evaluation
838   relies on cumulative error plots, but they do not provide a single numerical value summarizing the
839   performance of their model. Similarly, while Salem et al. (2020) and Padilha et al. (2022) offer
840   datasets and code, they do not include the cross-camera split for zero-shot time prediction—a more
841   challenging and informative evaluation protocol that we adopt. Moreover, their results are presented
842   only qualitatively, though they can be adapted for obtaining quantitative results. Salem et al. (2022)
843
      also omit critical details such as dataset splits for the SkyFinder dataset, do not clarify whether their
      results corresponds to same- or cross-camera evaluation, and fail to provide the source code for
844
      replication. Their use of top-k accuracy as an evaluation metric further complicates direct comparisons.
845
      Notably, none of these methods, with the exception of Salem et al. (2020) and Padilha et al. (2022),
846   compare their time prediction performances against each other, and even these comparisons are only
847   qualitative. Other time prediction approaches, including those by Tsai et al. (2016), Li et al. (2017),
848   Lalonde et al. (2012), and Hold-Geoffroy et al. (2017), also face similar challenges, such as a lack
849   of available source code, missing datasets, or datasets that are no longer hosted online. This lack of
850   standardization prevents consistent benchmarking across different methods.

878   G.2      T IME REPRESENTATION
879
880
      Motivated by the cyclical nature of time, Mac Aodha et al. 2019 used circular decomposition to
      wrap the temporal input to their geographical prior encoder, resulting in similar embeddings for dates
881
      that are close to the start and end of the year, such as December 31st and January 1st . To achieve
882
      this, for each dimension l of the temporal input x, they perform the mapping [sin(πxl ), cos(πxl )],
883   resulting in two numbers for each dimension.
884
885   Time2Vec (Kazemi et al., 2019) is a method for encoding time that captures both periodic and non-
886
      periodic patterns. It transforms scalar time values into a vector of size k + 1. The first element models
      linear, non-periodic trends, while the remaining elements are defined by a periodic activation function
887
      (e.g., sine), capturing repeating temporal behaviors like daily or weekly cycles. The representation is
888
      defined as:
889

                                                    ωi τ + ϕi       if i = 0,
890                                  t2v(τ )[i] =
                                                    F (ωi τ + ϕi ) if 1 ≤ i ≤ k,
891
892
      where F is a periodic activation function, typically sin, and ωi and ϕi are learnable parameters
      representing the frequency and phase shift, respectively.

       L    A NALYSIS OF THE LEARNED EMBEDDING SPACE
1095
1096
       One of the key motivations for GeoTimeCLIP is to align images, time, and location in a shared multi-
1097   modal embedding space. This approach is inspired by prior works like GeoCLIP (Vivanco Cepeda
1098   et al., 2024), SatCLIP (Klemmer et al., 2023), and CSP (Mai et al., 2023), which embed images
1099   and GPS coordinates in shared spaces, as well as methods like ImageBind Girdhar et al. (2023),
1100   LanguageBind (Zhu et al., 2023), and Everything At Once (Shvetsova et al., 2022b), which align
1101   multiple modalities such as images, text, videos, and audio. Our work extends this idea to include
1102   temporal information, showing in Table 1 that aligning these three modalities leads to improved time
1103   prediction performance compared to using only images and time.
1104   To explore the relationships between these modalities in the learned embedding space, we performed
1105   Principal Component Analysis (PCA) on the embeddings. While PCA has limitations in fully captur-
1106   ing the underlying structure of high-dimensional spaces, the results provide interesting qualitative
1107   insights. Figure 12(a) presents the distributions of image, time, and location embeddings, appearing
1108   in different subspaces. Figures 12(b) and 12(c) show more details about the relationship between
1109   image and time embeddings. The image embeddings are clustered in the center, surrounded by time
1110   embeddings. Notably, the directions of hours and months are well-defined: months are radially
1111
       distributed, while hours are linearly distributed in a perpendicular direction. For location embeddings
       (Figures 12(d-e)), even though the patterns are less pronounced, the embeddings at different latitudes
1112
       and longitudes still form distinct clusters in the feature space.
