009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            De novo 3D molecule generation is a pivotal task in drug discovery. However, many
012            recent geometric generative models struggle to produce high-quality 3D structures,
013            even if they maintain 2D validity and topological stability. To tackle this issue
014            and enhance the learning of effective molecular generation dynamics, we present
015            Megalodon–a family of simple and scalable transformer models. These models
016
               are enhanced with basic equivariant layers and trained using a joint continuous
               and discrete denoising co-design objective. We assess Megalodon’s performance
017
               on established molecule generation benchmarks and introduce new 3D structure
018
               benchmarks that evaluate a model’s capability to generate realistic molecular
019            structures, particularly focusing on energetics. We show that Megalodon achieves
020            state-of-the-art results in 3D molecule generation, conditional structure generation,
021            and structure energy benchmarks using diffusion and flow matching. Furthermore,
022            we demonstrate that scaling Megalodon produces up to 49x more valid molecules
023            at large sizes and 2-10x lower energy compared to the prior best generative models.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Molecular Generative models have been heavily explored due to the allure of enabling efficient
029   virtual screening and targeted drug design (Gómez-Bombarelli et al., 2018). Similar to the rise
030
      in their application to computer vision (CV) (Peebles & Xie, 2022; Esser et al., 2024; Ma et al.,
      2024), Diffusion and Flow Matching models have been applied for tasks including molecule design,
031
      molecular docking, and protein folding (Schneuing et al., 2022; Corso et al., 2023a; Abramson
032
      et al., 2024). Across CV and chemical design, the scaling of model architectures and training data
033   have seen significant accuracy improvements but questions surrounding how to scale effectively still
034   persist (Corso et al., 2024; Durairaj et al., 2024).
035
036
      Specifically for 3D molecule generation (3DMG), where the task is to unconditionally generate
      valid and diverse 3D molecules, diffusion models have shown great promise in enabling accurate
037
      generation starting from pure noise (Hoogeboom et al., 2022). The iterative nature of diffusion
038
      models allows them to explore a diverse range of molecular configurations, ideally providing valuable
039   insights into potential drug candidates and facilitating the discovery of novel compounds. However,
040   unlike in CV, which has seen systematic evaluations of training data and scaling, with tangible
041   benchmark results (Esser et al., 2024), measuring success in de novo molecule generation is quite
042   difficult. As a result, there is a nonlinear path to determining what truly is making an impact if,
043   in each model, the data, architecture, training objective, and benchmarks differ. Furthermore, the
044   commonly shared 3DMG benchmarks that do exist only evaluate 2D quantities, ignoring 3D structure,
045   conformational energy, and model generalization to large molecule sizes–all quantities that are
046   imperative for real-world use. In this work, we explore the above in the context of 3DMG and its
047
      interpretable benchmarks to directly target larger molecules and model scaling.
048   Our main contributions are as follows:
049
050          • We present Megalodon, a simple and scalable transformer-based architecture for multi-modal
051            molecule diffusion and flow matching. This is the first 3DMG model to be tested with both
052            objectives, with both obtaining state-of-the-art results. We show that our diffusion model
053            excels at structure and energy benchmarks, whereas our flow matching model yields better
               2D stability and the ability to use 25x fewer inference steps than its diffusion counterpart.

      2     BACKGROUND
086   2.1   3D M OLECULE G ENERATION
087
088   In de novo 3D molecule generation (3DMG), a molecule’s 3D structure and 2D topology are
089   simultaneously generated. We define a molecule M = (X, H, E, C) with N atoms where X ∈
090   RN ×3 , H ∈ {0, 1}N ×A , E ∈ {0, 1}N ×N ×B , and C ∈ {0, 1}N ×K represents the atom coordinates,
091
      element types, bond types adjacency matrix, and formal charges respectively. X is modeled as a
      continuous variable whereas H, E, and C are discrete one-hot variables.
092

094
      2.2   I MPORTANT Q UALITIES OF 3D M OLECULES
095
      The GEOM dataset (Axelrod & Gómez-Bombarelli, 2022) is widely used for 3D molecular structure
096   (conformer) generation tasks, containing 3D conformations from both the QM9 and drug-like
097   molecule (DRUGS) databases, with the latter presenting more complex and realistic molecular
098   challenges. Conformers in the dataset were generated using CREST (Pracht et al., 2024), which
099   performs extensive conformational sampling based on the semi-empirical extended tight-binding
100   method (GFN2-xTB) (Bannwarth et al., 2019). This ensures that each conformation represents a
101   local minimum in the GFN2-xTB energy landscape.
102   Energy, in the context of molecular conformations, refers to the potential energy of a molecule’s
103   structure, which is a key determinant of its stability. Lower-energy conformations are typically more
104   stable and are found at the minima on the potential energy surface (PES). For a generative model to
105   succeed, it must not only generate molecules that are chemically valid but also ones that correspond
106   to low-energy conformations, reflecting local minima on the PES. Thus, energy serves as the ultimate
107   measure of success in molecular modeling, as it directly correlates with the physical realism and
      stability of the generated structures.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      A key requirement for generative models is their ability to implicitly learn this energy landscape and
109   produce molecules that are local minima of the potential energy surface. However, since GFN2-xTB
110   is itself a model rather than a universal energy function, comparing energies across different potentials
111   (e.g., using GFN2-xTB optimized structures but computing energies with MMFF (Halgren, 1996))
112   can introduce systematic errors. Differences in potential models, such as optimal bond lengths, may
113   lead to unreliable results. Overall, the goal of 3DMG is to generate valid and low-energy molecules
114   mimicking the energy landscape of the GEOM dataset.

116   2.3   R ELATED W ORK
117
118   Hoogeboom et al. (2022) first introduced continuous diffusion modeling for coordinates and atom
119   types using a standard EGNN architecture (Satorras et al., 2021). Following this, many models have
120   been produced that make slight changes to the architecture and interpolant schedule to generate atom
121   coordinates and types (Song et al., 2023). While initially effective, they rely on the molecule-building
122   software OpenBabel (O’Boyle et al., 2011) to infer and update the bond locations and types, which is
123
      a critical aspect of the stability calculations. The issues and biases of OpenBabel have been heavily
      explored, and as a result, methods began to generate the bond locations and types in the generative
124
      process (Walters, 2024). Vignac et al. (2023) was the first to use continuous diffusion for coordinates
125
      and discrete diffusion for the atom and bond types, removing the OpenBabel requirement. Le et al.
126   (2024) used the same training objective but introduced a more effective equivariant architecture.
127   Recently Irwin et al. (2024) uses continuous and discrete flow matching with a latent equivariant
128   graph message passing architecture to show improved performance. For further discussion please
129   see §B

      Diffusion models inherently rely on simulating Gaussian stochastic processes. In the forward process,
233
      data points are progressively noised, converging towards a Gaussian distribution. This process,
234   derived from score-based generative models, aims to learn the score function (the gradient of the
235   data distribution’s log density) to reverse the diffusion process. The generative model effectively
236   solves a Stochastic Differential Equation (SDE) that describes how data diffuses towards noise and
237   how it can be denoised in reverse. The reverse process requires SDE simulation at every step, which
238   involves sampling from a learned probabilistic model that estimates how to remove noise. This
239   involves simulating random variables at each time step, making diffusion models highly dependent
240   on repeated stochastic simulation.
241   Flow Matching, on the other hand, learns a continuous vector field that deterministically ”flows” one
242   distribution to another. The model learns this flow by matching the velocity field that pushes samples
243   from a source distribution to a target distribution. Once the vector field is learned, generating samples
244   involves solving an ODE that defines a continuous and deterministic trajectory from the source to the
245   target distribution. Unlike diffusion models, which require simulating a series of stochastic transitions
246   (noising and denoising) over many steps, flow matching learns a single, continuous flow. Sampling
247   involves solving an ODE (or, in some cases, a deterministic SDE with noise) to move from the base
248
      distribution to the target in a smooth, deterministic fashion.
249   For DDPM, the equations only hold for the Gaussian path with access to a well-formed score function.
250   This is why techniques like mini-batch Optimal Transport (OT) can be applied to FM but not Diffusion
251   to align pdata and pref (Tong et al., 2023). In FM, the vector field is learned, which, in the absence of
252   OT, can be derived as a function of the score function, but having access to the score function is not a
253   requirement to sample in a simulation-free or deterministic way.

      Megalodon Architecture Since 3DMG allows for the simultaneous generation of a discrete
257
      2D molecular graph and its 3D structure, we intentionally designed our architecture with a core
258   transformer trunk to better model discrete data (Vaswani, 2017; Brown et al., 2020). Fig. 1 illustrates
259   the model architecture, which is comprised of N augmented transformer blocks followed by linear
260   layers for discrete data projection.
261
      First, the input structure, atom types, and bond types are fused and aggregated to create a single
262
      molecule feature. This is passed into the standard multi-head attention module with adaptive
263
      layernorm. The scaled output is then passed into separate adaptive layernorm feedforward blocks for
264   the atom types and bond types. We augment the DiT block defined in Peebles & Xie (2022) to take
265   in structure, atom types, and bond types to produce updated atom and bond types, which are then
266   passed into a simple structure layer. The structure layer only updates the predicted structure via a
267   standard distance-based EGNN update with a cross-product term (Satorras et al., 2021; Schneuing
268   et al., 2022). We emphasize that this cross-product term is critical for model performance. At a high
269   level, the transformer block updates our discrete data, and our equivariant layer updates our structure.
      Megalodon uses standardized scaling tricks such as query key prenorm and equivariant norm in

270
      our structure layer (Hayes et al., 2024). We also note Megalodon, at 4x more parameters, is more
271   memory efficient than EQGAT-diff (Le et al., 2024), enabling 2x larger batch size while still having
272   the quadratic dependency of fully connected edge features. For more details, please see Sec. C.

      Training Objective We explore Megalodon in the context of diffusion and flow matching. For our
      diffusion flavored model, following Le et al. (2024); Vignac et al. (2023) we use the same weighted
280
      cosine noise schedules, DDPM, and discrete D3PM objective. When using conditional flow matching,
281
      we apply the same training objective and hyperparameters as Irwin et al. (2024), including equivariant
282   optimal transport. In this way, for diffusion and flow matching, we train and evaluate our model in an
283   identical way including hyperparameters to prior models of the respective types.
284
285
      In our experiments with EQGAT-diff, we found that the diffusion objective with data-like priors
      possesses an interesting but potentially harmful behavior. Although the noise sample from the
286
      data-prior and the true data sample have bonds, the model consistently generates no bonds for all time
287
      ≤ 0.5, which corresponds to an interpolation with ≤ 70% of the data as seen in Fig. 2(b). Therefore
288   there is no useful information for the edge features in half the training and inference samples. As a
289   result, only when the structure error is low, as the model starts with 70% data in the interpolation,
290   does the bond prediction accuracy jump to near-perfect accuracy. Thus, only when the structure is
291   accurate was the 2D graph accurate, which is counterintuitive to the independent and simultaneous
292   objective. In other words, the 2D graph does not inform the 3D structure as one would expect to
293   happen, and we would want equal importance on the 2D topology and 3D structure.
294   To address this inefficiency, as the structure, atom type, and bond type prediction inform each other
295   to improve molecule generation, we introduce a subtle change to the training procedure similar to
296   Campbell et al. (2024). Keeping each data type having its own independent noise schedule, we
297   enable a concrete connection between the discrete and continuous data that it is modeling. Explicitly,
298   rather than sampling a single time variable, we introduce a second noise variable to create tcontinuous
299   and tdiscrete , both sampled from the same time distribution. Now discrete and continuous data
300   are interpolated with their respective time variable and maintain the independent weighted noise
301   schedules. We note that the MiDi weighted cosine schedules were already adding different levels of
302
      noise for the same time value. Now, we take that one step further and allow the model to fill in the
      structure given the 2D graph and learn to handle more diverse data interpolations.

359   results. With the FM version obtaining better topological accuracy and the diffusion version seeing

361   transformer is useful for the discrete and continuous data requirements of 3DMG, regardless of
362   the interpolant and sampling methodology. We also see that the transformer part is crucial for

452
      central question being, is the model learning how to generate molecules over the spatial and discrete

456   Analysis We see in Table 2 that EQGAT-diff is unable to generate any remotely valid structures.
457   Even though all modalities are being denoised independently at different rates, the model cannot
458   generate the structure given ground truth 2D molecule graphs. This is also seen during the sampling
459
      process, where diffusion models trained with similar denoising objectives as EQGAT-diff generate no
      bonds until the structure has seemingly converged. Therefore during most of the sampling process,
460
      the edge features which make up a large portion of the computational cost hold no value.
461
462   In comparison, Megalodon generates structures with competitive precision and recall by building a
463   relationship between the discrete and continuous data directly in the training process described in §3.
464   Half the time all data types are independently noised as normal with their respective time variables
465
      and schedules, the other half we only add noise to the structure. Therefore, our model learns to build
      a relationship between true 2D graphs and their 3D structure, as well as any interpolation between
466
      the three data tracks that are interpolated independently with different schedulers.
467
468   Megalodon demonstrates that its unconditional discrete diffusion objective is crucial for its condi-
469   tional performance. In other words, the discrete diffusion training objective improves the conditional
470   continuous generative performance. This is evident in the comparison between GeoDiff and Mega-
471
      lodon. GeoDiff is trained on the same conditional Euclidean structure objective as Megalodon (with
      similar EGNN-based architecture) with 10x more diffusion steps, with both models taking in identical
472
      inputs. We see that since Megalodon is able to generate molecules from pure noise, it better learns
473
      structure and as a result can be prompted to generate accurate structures.
474
475   Interestingly, compared to Torsional Diffusion, which initializes the 3D structure with an expensive
476   RDKit approximation to establish all bond lengths and angles and then only modifies the dihedral
477
      angles, we see quite competitive performance. Before, it was understood that by restricting the
      degrees of freedom with good RDKit structures, the performance jump from GeoDiff to Torsional
478
      Diffusion was observed. Now we see that with the same euclidean diffusion process, similar accuracy
479
      improvements can be gained by learning how to generate accurate discrete molecule topology
480   via discrete diffusion. We want to note that there have been recent advances on top of Torsional
481   Diffusion (Corso et al., 2023b) and other conformer-focused models that are not public (Wang et al.,
482   2023). We use this benchmark more to analyze the underlying multi-modal diffusion objective and
483   focus on the underlying model comparisons. Megalodon is not a conformer generation model but a
484   molecule generation model capable of de novo and conditional design. Overall, Megalodon shows
485   how independent time interpolation and discrete diffusion create the ability for the model to be
      prompted or guided with a desired 2D topology to generate accurate 3D structures.

499   4.3   U NCONDITIONAL S TRUCTURE - BASED E NERGY B ENCHMARKS
500
      Problem setup Each ground truth structure in GEOM dataset represents a low-energy conformer
501
      within its ensemble, highlighting two key aspects. First, these molecules are local minima on the
502
      GFN2-xTB potential energy surface. Second, their energies are lower compared to other conforma-
503   tions sampled in the ensemble. Previously, these quantities have not been thoroughly evaluated for
504   generated molecules. To address this gap, we directly measure how closely a generated molecule
505   approximates its nearest local minimum (i.e., its relaxed structure). We measure the energy difference
506   between the initial generated structure and its relaxed counterpart, as well as structural changes
507   in bond lengths, bond angles, and dihedral (torsion) angles. This approach allows us to evaluate
508   the ability of generative models to produce molecules that are true local minima, facilitating faster
509   ranking of generated structures without additional minimization steps.
510
      Analysis We see that for both diffusion and flow matching, Megalodon is better than its prior
511
      counterparts. Overall, Megalodon trained with diffusion performs best with roughly 2-10x lower
512   median energy when compared to prior generative models. Notably, our model’s median relaxation
513   energy difference ∆Erelax is around 3 kcal/mol, which approaches the thermally relevant interval
514   of 2.5 kcal/mol (Axelrod & Gómez-Bombarelli, 2022). Megalodon is the first method to achieve
515   such proximity to this thermodynamic threshold, marking a significant milestone in 3D molecular
516   generation. For more details and justification, please see Appendix §E.
517
      We note that while the loss function between FM and diffusion is identical in this instance, we
518   see both flow models have an order of magnitude larger bond angle error, which translates to a
519   similar energy performance gap. The xTB energy function is highly sensitive to bond lengths; small
520   deviations in bond lengths can lead to significant increases in energy due to the steepness of the
521   potential energy surface in these dimensions. A precise representation of bond lengths is crucial
522   because inaccuracies directly impact the calculated energy, making bond length errors a primary
523   contributor to higher relaxation energies in flow models. We hypothesize that since the Flow models
524   scale the input structures to have a variance of 1 to make matching the Gaussian prior easier, we
525   lower the local spatial precision necessary for bond length and angle generation (Irwin et al., 2024).
526
      5     C ONCLUSIONS
527
      Megalodon enables the accurate generation of de novo 3D molecules with both diffusion and flow
528
      matching. We show with a simple and scalable augmented transformer architecture that significant
529
      improvements are gained, especially when generating outside the region of support for the training
530   distribution as it pertains to molecule sizes. Megalodon also demonstrates the ability to achieve great
531   accuracy in conditional structure generation due to being trained to generate complete molecules
532   from scratch. We also introduce more interpretable quantum mechanical energy benchmarks that
533   are grounded in the original creation of the GEOM Drugs dataset. Overall, Megalodon explores
534   improvements in molecule design via its architecture and overall generative modeling paradigm. We
535   also explore the similarities and differences between flow matching and diffusion in this specific
536   case of multi-modal molecule design. Although we explore a portion of scaling Megalodon, further
537   improvements in creating meaningful benchmarks are needed going forward as standard validity and
538
      molecule stability metrics are already surpassing 96% and 99%, respectively. Such benchmarks are
      crucial for understanding how to gauge the value of scaling, (Esser et al., 2024).

      Here we discuss other approaches for unconditional molecule generation we find relevant in the
921
      context our our study that were not already discussed in Sec. 2. Xu et al. (2023) introduces GeoLDM
922   a geometric latent diffusion model for 3DMG. GeoLDM applies its diffusion process over a learned
923   latent representation. So rather than updating the atom position and types in euclidean space
924   everything is done inside the model. Similar to EDM, GeoLDM uses OpenBabel for bond prediction.
925   Pinheiro et al. (2024) takes a different approach than majority of prior work in representing molecules
926   as 3D voxels rather than graphs. This is akin to 3D image processing rather than point cloud
927   processing. This however requires a recovery process as the voxels are not a natural molecule
928   representation. Voxels however provide a better link to the applications of vision models which
929   majority of the diffusion framework was created for. Lastly, Song et al. (2024) introduces GeoBFN
930
      a Geometric Bayesian Flow Network, that unlike diffusion models operate in the parameter space
      rather then product space. While the integration of 3D voxels would not work for Megalodon , latent
931
      diffusion and BFN extensions are something relevant to future work.

       E.4.1    L IMITATIONS OF MMFF FOR E VALUATING GFN2- X TB S TRUCTURES
1161
1162   Previous studies have used the MMFF force field to assess the quality of generated molecular
1163   structures (Xu et al., 2022; Irwin et al., 2024). However, the choice of force field is critical when
1164   evaluating molecular geometries, as different force fields can yield significantly different energy
1165   landscapes. For ground truth conformers optimized using GFN2-xTB, the mean relaxation energy
1166
       difference ∆Erelax calculated with GFN2-xTB is nearly zero, as expected. However, when these same
       structures are evaluated using the MMFF force field, the mean ∆Erelax is approximately 16 kcal/mol,
1167
       which aligns with literature values reporting MMFF errors in the range of 15–20 kcal/mol when
1168
       compared to higher-level methods like GFN2-xTB (Foloppe & Chen, 2019). In other words, xTB is
1169   significantly more accurate than MMFF, especially for data generated with xTB.
1170
1171   In contrast, our generated molecules exhibit a mean ∆Erelax of around 5 kcal/mol when relaxed with
1172
       GFN2-xTB, significantly smaller than the error observed when using MMFF. This demonstrates that
       our model produces structures that are much closer to the GFN2-xTB energy minima compared to
1173
       what MMFF evaluations suggest. These substantial energy discrepancies—stemming from systematic
1174
       differences like optimal bond lengths and angles—highlight that MMFF is inappropriate for evaluating
1175   structures optimized or generated within the GFN2-xTB framework. Relying on MMFF can, therefore,
1176   lead to misleading assessments of structural quality.

1179
1180   In statistical thermodynamics, conformers exist in dynamic equilibrium, and their population dis-
1181   tribution is determined by their relative free energies. The equilibrium constant K between two
1182   conformers is given by:
1183

1185

       G    L IMITATIONS
1243
1244
       While we show that Megalodon performs well across a variety of 3D de novo molecules tasks there
1245   are still some limitations that are worthy of discussion.
1246
1247         • Megalodon like Le et al. (2024) and the prior edge prediction generative models before it
1248           relies on maintaining N 2 edge features, which is quite expensive. Recently (Irwin et al.,
1249           2024) was able to avoid this issue for a majority of the model architecture by fusing the
1250           edge and atom features, but this creates a trade-off between model speed and accuracy. Our
1251           ablations show that the larger edge features are critical for strong energy performance, so it
1252           is still an open question for how to best deal with discrete edge types as each atom can have
1253
               a maximum of 6 bonds at a time, so is needing to model all N potential pairings at all times
               really necessary? We leave future work to explore this in greater depth.
1254
1255         • As discussed herein, the existing 3D molecule generation benchmarks are quite limited.
1256           A common theme that has been discussed in prior work (Le et al., 2024; Irwin et al.,
1257
               2024). While we make strides in expanding the field of view of de novo design and energy-
               based benchmarks. More work needs to be done to measure important qualities, as even
1258
               for common conditional design benchmarks, metrics such as QED are not meaningful in
1259
               practice, and even more complex properties like protein-ligand binding affinity can be
1260           directly optimized for with non-3D structure-based methods (Reidenbach, 2024). For these
1261           reasons, we looked to explore conditional structure generation, but across the board, small
1262           molecule benchmarking is a current field-wide limitation when compared to the current drug
1263           discovery practices.
1264         • A general limitation for all prior 3DMG models including Megalodon is that they cannot
1265           generalize to unseen atom types due to the one hot representation during training. As a result,
1266           these models can only be used for GEOM-Drug-like molecules. While fairly extensive, it is
1267           a limitation worth noting as the flexibility is limited when compared to 2D or SMILES-based
1268           LLMs.
