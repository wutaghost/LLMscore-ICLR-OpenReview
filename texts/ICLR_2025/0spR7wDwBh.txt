009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            We present a novel experiential learning agent with causally-informed intrinsic
012            reward that is capable of learning sequential and causal dependencies in a robust
013            and data-efficient way within grid world environments. After reflecting on state-
014            of-the-art Deep Reinforcement Learning algorithms, we provide a relevant dis-
015            cussion of common techniques as well as our own systematic comparison within
016
               multiple grid world environments. Additionally, we investigate the conditions and
               mechanisms leading to data-efficient learning and analyze relevant inductive bi-
017
               ases that our agent utilizes to effectively learn causal knowledge and to plan for
018
               rewarding future states of greatest expected return.
019

021   1    I NTRODUCTION
022
023   Grid world environments come in many forms and have been studied extensively in the history of
024   Artificial Intelligence with some notable examples such as Wumpus World (Bryce, 2011), Mini-
025   grid (Chevalier-Boisvert et al., 2024), and Tileworld (Pollack & Ringuette, 1990). However, the
026   creation of intelligent grid world agents capable of learning effectively and in a data-efficient way
027
      has posed significant challenges. Current Reinforcement Learning (RL) agents struggle in some
      instances due to sequential dependencies, partial observability (Wang et al., 2023a), continual learn-
028
      ing (primacy bias Kim et al. (2024), stability-plasticity dilemma Anand & Precup (2024)), relatively
029
      high-dimensional state spaces, compared to more traditional RL tasks – even when a single value
030   per cell is provided rather than per pixel – and sometimes non-deterministic effects of actions.
031
032   Sequential dependencies usually raise the training data demand exponentially depending on the
033
      combinatorics, and, ultimately, the number of the arising options, unless the agent is capable of
      learning causal representations that transfer well because chaining them is favorable for reaching an
034
      intended outcome. Partial observability, on the other hand, may require the model to have access
035
      to information from prior states, which typically corresponds to the previously observed values in
036   a grid world outside the agent’s current field of view. In terms of input dimensionality, there is
037   a trade-off between the observation window being too small for learning an effective policy and
038   the agent’s observation window being more high-dimensional, thereby demanding more training
039   data, even though this might be the least problematic. Additionally, if the agent is able to represent
040   values outside of its observation window, a learned policy needs to consider not only the observation
041   window itself but also how it spatially relates to the remembered information beyond it.
042   With these considerations in mind, we introduce Non-Axiomatic Causal Explorer (NACE), a novel
043   experiential learning agent, which leverages causal reasoning and intrinsic reward signals to enable
044   more efficient learning as well as possesses learning mechanisms with the involved inductive biases.
045   NACE is designed to induce causal rules from temporal and spatial local changes in the grid, which
046   are often (but not always) caused by the agent. It utilizes these rules to plan for and reach future states
047   of maximum uncertainty in order to effectively learn more (causal rules) about the environment,
048   thereby improving predictability-based intrinsic reward formulations.
049   To illustrate the effectiveness of our approach, we provide a comprehensive discussion of state-of-
050   the-art Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) techniques as well as our own systematic comparison
051   within multiple grid world environments demonstrating our agent’s remarkable improvement in data
052   efficiency, achieving similar performance with about 1000 samples where DRL algorithms typically
053   require 1 million samples, representing a 1000-fold reduction in data requirements. We also thor-
      oughly investigate the conditions and mechanisms under which learning in grid world environments

      can become more data-efficient and attempt to answer the question about which inductive biases
055   can lead to close-to-optimal learning speeds in the case where the agent is not pre-equipped with

058   and their generality to raise the data efficiency of learning in such domains.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
062
063   Current RL techniques such as value-based (e.g., Deep Q-Learning (Mnih et al., 2013)) and pol-
064
      icy gradient-based (e.g., Proximal Policy Optimization (Schulman et al., 2017)) typically require
      millions of training iterations to solve grid-world environments (Zhang et al., 2020b), struggling to
065
      capture causal dependencies necessary for efficient planning and transferability.
066
067   Exploration improvements, such as intrinsic rewards based on information gain (Zhao et al., 2023),
068   prediction errors (Burda et al., 2018), or visitation counts (Zheng et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023b),
069   enhance sample efficiency but often lack structured reasoning for generalization. In contrast, Tsi-
070
      vidis et al. (2021) propose Theory-Based RL (TBRL), exemplified by EMPA (Exploring, Modeling,
      and Planning Agent), which integrates Bayesian causal modeling, structured exploration, and heuris-
071
      tic planning to generalize efficiently across tasks with minimal training. Similarly, GALOIS (Cao
072
      et al., 2022) addresses generalization by synthesizing interpretable, hierarchical programs with strict
073   cause-effect logic, though its reliance on predefined program sketches limits flexibility in loosely
074   structured environments. As a model-based RL alternative, DreamerV3 (Hafner et al., 2023) learns
075   latent state dynamics and improves behavior through imagination, enabling generalization across
076   diverse tasks with minimal domain-specific adjustments. However, its reliance on learning both la-
077   tent representations and their dynamics reduces sample efficiency compared to methods that assume
078   predefined representations.
079   Symbolic approaches like STRIPS and Behavior Trees (BTs) (Guo et al., 2023; Colledanchise &
080   Ögren, 2018) handle human-defined causal knowledge but lack adaptive learning capabilities. Sim-
081   ilarly, POMDPs (Spaan, 2012) focus on probability updates rather than causal discovery, while
082   causal networks (Pearl, 1995) and structure-learning methods (Zheng et al., 2018) face challenges
083   with ambiguity and scalability.
084
      While TBRL, GALOIS, and DreamerV3 provide solutions for structured or model-based learning,
085   their reliance on predefined logic, priors, or latent space learning introduces additional complexity.
086   Our approach proposes lightweight “empirical” causal relations learned from recurring cause-effect
087   patterns, supporting efficient real-time learning in grid worlds without predefined program sketches,
088   Bayesian modeling, or latent space learning. This ensures adaptability while retaining interpretabil-
089   ity and transferability.

091   2.1   S ELECTED RL TECHNIQUES IN GRID WORLDS
092
093   DRL often struggles with sample efficiency, requiring substantial interactions with environments.
094   This paper examines foundational algorithms, scalable architectures, and exploration-focused meth-
095   ods that address these challenges.
096   Foundational methods include Deep Q-Network (DQN) Mnih et al. (2015), which combines deep
097   neural networks with Q-learning to handle large state spaces but struggles with sparse rewards;
098   Advantage Actor-Critic (A2C) Mnih et al. (2016), which reduces variance in updates through syn-
099   chronized parallel actors but is limited by its on-policy nature; Trust Region Policy Optimization
100   (TRPO) Schulman et al. (2015), which ensures stable policy updates with trust region constraints
101   but is computationally intensive; and Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) Schulman et al. (2017),
102   which refines TRPO with clipped objectives for improved data utilization and computational effi-
103   ciency.
104   Scalable architectures such as Importance Weighted Actor-Learner Architectures (IMPALA) Es-
105   peholt et al. (2018) address multi-task learning by leveraging distributed architectures with off-
106   policy corrections, offering scalability but facing synchronization challenges, whereby exploration-
107   focused methods aim to address sparse rewards and complex state spaces. Count-Based Exploration
      (COUNT) Bellemare et al. (2016) uses pseudo-counts for better exploration but is computationally


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      demanding in large state spaces. Random Network Distillation (RND) Burda et al. (2018) incen-
109   tivizes novelty through prediction errors, however, it depends on high-quality state representations.
110   Curiosity-Driven Exploration (CURIOSITY) Pathak et al. (2017) rewards prediction errors of ac-
111   tion outcomes, fostering intrinsic motivation, while Rewarding Impact-Driven Exploration (RIDE)
112   Raileanu & Rocktäschel (2020) focuses on impactful actions but may struggle with ambiguous state
113   changes. Adversarially Motivated Intrinsic Goals (AMIGO) Campero et al. (2021) generates adver-
114   sarial goals to guide exploration, requiring robust goal-generation mechanisms for effectiveness.
115   Furthermore, model-based RL techniques can learn a model of the environment and improve their
116   behavior through imagined future scenarios Sekar et al. (2020). This framework is particularly ad-
117   vantageous in high-dimensional and variable state spaces. DreamerV3 Hafner et al. (2023) builds
118   on this principle by introducing a general algorithm designed to address a diverse range of tasks
119   with minimal domain-specific adjustments. By learning latent state dynamics and planning through
120   imagination, DreamerV3 offers broad applicability while reducing the need for extensive tuning or
121   specialized configurations. However, the additional complexity of learning latent space representa-
122   tions together with their dynamics leads to less sample efficiency compared to when representations
123
      are already present and only the dynamics need to be learned.

      3.2   I NDUCTIVE B IASES
168
169   It is well-known that favorable inductive biases can enhance sample efficiency. Below are inductive
170   biases that are incorporated in NACE and relevant for many grid world environments:
171
172         1. Temporal Locality: NACE constructs rules based solely on the current and previous state,
173            modeling relevant dependencies locally in time.
174
            2. Causal Representation: NACE’s knowledge representation is centered around the afore-
175            mentioned causal rules which can be chained and are independent of the objective.
176
177         3. Spatial Equivariance: Ability to model causal dependencies between grid cells indepen-
178
               dently of the specific location of the cells considered in the dependency. This means learned
               rules in NACE can be applied at any location.
179
180         4. State Tracking: Ability to effectively track state outside of the field of view of the agent
181            based on the recorded or estimated locations. NACE explicitly keeps track of a bird’s-
182            eye view map by recording observations into it, updating the values that are within its
183            observability window.
184         5. Attentional Bias: Relevant dependencies tend to involve values that have either observ-
185            ably changed or a different value than predicted. Only rules that show a change from the
186            previous to the current timestep, or differ from the predicted value, are considered for rule
187            formation, evidence updating, and prediction.
188
189   Additional discussions on inductive biases as well as ablation studies can be found in Appendix B.
190

527   5     C ONCLUSION
528
529   We introduced NACE, an experiential learning agent designed to enhance data efficiency in grid
530   world environments by leveraging causally-informed intrinsic rewards and inductive biases. We
531
      compared NACE with state-of-the-art DRL techniques, demonstrating that while these techniques
      are able to eventually achieve near-optimal policies, they often require significantly more data, es-
532
      pecially as task complexity increases due to factors such as sequential dependencies. NACE, by
533
      contrast, extends the RL framework to empirically support causal relations, enabling effective learn-
534   ing and decision-making even in low data settings without relying on pre-defined causal models. Our
535   causality-informed curiosity model, combined with the outlined inductive biases, facilitates system-
536   atic exploration and learning requiring significantly fewer timesteps. We hope that future work in
537   the field will strike new compromises regarding the inclusion of inductive biases, leading to highly
538   sample-efficient DRL that retains the ability to converge to optimal policies. Moving forward, we
539   plan to generalize NACE to handle three-dimensional and continuous spaces, as well as explore
      neural implementations of NACE, further advancing the capabilities of learning agents.

671   A PPENDIX A        A DDITIONAL S TUDIES AND D ISCUSSIONS
672
673
      The performance results indicate that NACE often exhibits sub-optimal outcomes. To analyze this,
674   we present contributing factors from a conceptual perspective, examine the impact of hyperparame-
675   ter choices, and assess robustness to non-determinism arising from random action consequences.
676
677         • Representational Limitations: NACE’s rule-based framework captures only spatially rel-
678           ative dependencies from one timestep to the next. It does not exploit the inherent struc-
679           tural statistics of environment generation, which are leveraged by various DRL techniques.
680           While these structural dependencies are most apparent in static environments where loca-
681           tions remain constant, they are also present in dynamic environments. For example, the
682           goal location consistently appears in the bottom-right corner not only in MiniGrid-Empty
683           levels but also in MiniGrid-SimpleCrossingS11N5-v0. NACE’s inability to utilize these
684
              broader environmental patterns limits its performance compared to methods that can.
685           Additionally, NACE’s rules are tied to an action, meaning agent-external changes that are
686           not caused by NACE need to be learned for each action separately, considerably lowering
687
              its sample efficiency by a factor of the amount of actions. The mechanism could be ex-
              tended to incorporate learning of rules without an action as a precondition, leaving it to
688
              evidence collection whether the action is considered dependent on the truth expectation of
689
              the alternative rules.
690
691         • Study of Reduced Planning Horizon: NACE’s estimation of expected returns relies heav-
692           ily on its planning horizon. Short planning horizons can significantly reduce performance,
693
              especially in tasks requiring long-term planning. To quantify this effect, we examine two
              cases: MiniGrid-DoorKey-8x8-v0, which demands longer-horizon planning, and MiniGrid-
694
              DoorKey-6x6-v0, which is less demanding in this regard. As shown in Table 8, running
695
              NACE with a planning horizon of only 8 steps in MiniGrid-DoorKey-8x8-v0 results in con-
696           vergence to an average return of 0.48, whereas extending the horizon to 100 steps improves
697           the average reward to 0.92. In contrast, in MiniGrid-DoorKey-6x6-v0, NACE maintains
698           an average reward of 0.93 regardless of the planning horizon. A similar pattern is ob-
699           served in MiniGrid-Empty-16x16-v0, where the average reward drops from 0.91 to 0.41
700           when the planning horizon is reduced from 100 to 8 steps. These results highlight NACE’s
701           dependence on adequate planning horizons for effective rule chaining and the significant
              performance degradation that occurs when the planning horizon is too short.

                          B IASES IN NACE, AND I NDUCTIVE BIASES IN DRL
726
      B.1   C AUSAL RULE R EPRESENTATION
727
728   The causal rule representation is foundational to NACE’s operation and cannot be omitted. However,
729   we analyze the effects of reducing the planning horizon, which limits the depth of chaining, in
730   Appendix A.
731
732   B.2   T EMPORAL L OCALITY AND ATTENTIONAL B IAS
733
734   Omitting these biases with larger environment sizes is infeasible due to the combinatorial explosion
735   of potential rules as we will now analyze.
736
737
             • For an environment of size w × h, the number of possible rule preconditions for a single
               timestep is 2w·h , as each particular cell can either be considered or not be considered in the
738
               precondition of a rule.
739
740          • For a time window of duration d, this expands to 2w·h·d , leading to
741
               18446744073709551616 possible rule preconditions for an 8 × 8 grid within a sin-
               gle timestep.
742
743          • NACE is tied to Markov Assumption particularly within the observational window, as all
744            rule construction and updating considers only the previous and current state. However,
745            its bird view map representation also contains values from observations of previous time
746
               steps which are currently out of view of the agent, which brings us to the next point, state
               tracking.
747
748
749
      B.3   S TATE T RACKING
750   Without state tracking, NACE lacks memory of prior observations and memory of observations
751   which are outside of its field-of-view. This results in oscillatory behavior caused by the exploration
752   strategy of the agent, as it can only utilize information the visible information.
753
754          • In our experiments across 10 runs in MiniGrid-Empty-8x8-v0, this led the agent to turn
755            indefinitely due to the curiosity model assigning low match values to previously visited
               areas (due to the lack of state tracking they are always considered to be of unknown value)


                                                        14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
               which are now outside of the field-of-view of the agent. The closest such cell is immediately
757            behind the agent with the default partial observation model in Minigrid, which explains the
758            behavior.
759
             • State Tracking plays a critical role in ensuring purposeful exploration and decision-making,
760
               for the agent to know which places have been visited and what it has been observing at the
761            particular locations, as well as which locations have yet to be observed.
762
             • Sequential dependencies often depend on state tracking. An example of this is when a door
763
               has to be opened with a key, where the key and the door is too far apart to be observed
764
               concurrently, demanding some form of spatial memory. Another form of state tracking lies
765            in the observable inventory array, which when absent would need the modeling of long-
766            range temporal dependencies (e.g. did the agent already pick up the key?) which would
767            demand a suitable model structure to be learnable by the agent.
768
769   B.4   S PATIAL E QUIVARIANCE
770
771   The absence of spatial equivariance significantly impacts sample efficiency.
772
773          • Each rule must be learned independently for every location, meaning in an 8x8 grid, the
774
               agent has to learn 64 times the same set of rules. However, since particular arrangements of
               cell values will not re-appear through the environment generation, it can take significantly
775
               longer to learn the relevant knowledge without this bias.
776
777          • Hence for the general case with an environment of size w × h, this increases the required
778            sample count at least by a factor of w · h, harming significantly the sample efficiency of the
779
               technique.
780          • Conceptually, we also would like to point out that the rule learning mechanisms do not
781            allow to learn spatial equivariance retrospectively either, while some DRL techniques, de-
782            pendent on the model structure, could potentially acquire it.
783
      These results highlight the necessity of each inductive bias in ensuring the scalability, efficiency, and
784
      functionality of NACE.
785
786
      B.5   W HICH INDUCTIVE BIASES ARE PRESENT IN THE DRL TECHNIQUES
787
788   In the main paper we outlined the inductive biases of NACE. However we would like to point out that
789   some of them are also inherent in the DRL techniques, complementing our discussion on inductive
790   biases in DRL and NACE:
791
792          • Temporal Locality: The DRL methods perform best when the Markov Assumption is met,
793            despite LSTM allowing to cope with partial observability, the need to capture long-range
794            temporal dependencies makes sample efficient learning more difficult.
795          • Causal representation: While not explicitly stated as a set of cause-effect relations,
796            DreamerV3’s learned dynamics model can predict the consequence states of actions, which
797            is not the case for the model-free DRL methods. Such modeling is to some extent inde-
798            pendent from the objective (what is rewarding), and allows an agent to train itself from
799
               simulated experience by predicting novel states, and to reach novel goals.
800          • Spatial Equivariance: Clearly the DRL techniques do not have an explicit rule represen-
801            tation, however the Convolution layers in the DRL policies allow for learned features to be
802            identified at different locations, improving generalization.
803          • State Tracking: Is not explicitly handled by the DRL techniques as a separate point, in-
804            stead it is handled in the same way as non-local temporal dependencies in the LSTM-
805            including policies, while NACE builds a bird view map explicitly, which can be considered
806            to be a form of spatial memory.
807          • Attentional Bias: While NACE has a strong prior to which cells to consider based on
808            observably changed values and prediction mismatches, the DRL policies with Convolution
809            layers are more flexible and allow an agent to learn which values are relevant in relation to
               each other.
