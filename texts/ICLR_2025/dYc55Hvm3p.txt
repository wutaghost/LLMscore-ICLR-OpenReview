011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Video-text retrieval aims to precisely search for videos most relevant to text
014            queries within a video corpus. However, existing methods are largely limited
015
               to single-text (single-event) queries and are not effective at handling multi-text
               (multi-event) queries. Furthermore, these methods typically focus solely on re-
016
               trieval and do not attempt to locate multiple events within the retrieved videos.
017
               To address these limitations, our paper proposes a novel method named Disentan-
018            gling Inter- and Intra-Video Relations, which jointly addresses multi-event video-
019            text retrieval and grounding. This method leverages both inter-video and intra-
020            video event relationships to enhance retrieval and grounding performance. At the
021            retrieval level, we devise a Relational Event-Centric Video-Text Retrieval mod-
022            ule based on the principle that comprehensive textual information leads to pre-
023            cise correspondence between text and video. It incorporates event relationship
024            features at different hierarchical levels and exploits the hierarchical structure of
025            video relationships to achieve multi-level contrastive learning between events and
026
               videos. This approach enhances the richness, accuracy, and comprehensiveness
               of event descriptions, improving alignment precision between text and video and
027
               enabling effective differentiation among videos. For event grounding, we propose
028
               Event Contrast-Driven Video Grounding, which accounts for positional differ-
029            ences among events on the 2D-temporal Score Map and achieves precise ground-
030            ing of multiple events through divergence learning for their locations. Our solu-
031            tion not only provides efficient text-to-video retrieval but also accurately grounds
032            events within the retrieved videos, addressing the shortcomings of existing meth-
033            ods. Extensive experimental results on the ActivityNet Captions and Charades-
034            STA benchmark datasets demonstrate the superior performance of our method,
035            validating its effectiveness. The innovation of this research lies in introducing
036            a new joint framework for video-text retrieval and multi-event grounding while
037
               offering new ideas for further research and applications in related fields.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
040
041
      The video-to-text retrieval task (Zhao et al. (2022); Gorti et al. (2022); Liu et al. (2022); Zhang et al.
042   (2023)) aims to accurately identify the video that best matches a text query from a video database.
043   Due to its broad application prospects in video search, recommendation systems, film entertainment,
044   intelligent surveillance, and other fields, it has become a focal point for researchers. Early research
045   primarily focused on single-text (single-event) queries, where a single textual description was used
046   to retrieve the most relevant video. However, given the limited temporal information provided by
047   a single text description, extending this task to multi-text-to-video retrieval, also known as Multi-
048   event Video-Text Retrieval (MVT-R), is essential to address the ambiguity issues associated with
049   single-text descriptions. Compared to single-text queries, multi-text queries offer richer and more
050
      detailed event descriptions, enabling the system to better understand the structure and flow of video
      content, thereby significantly enhancing retrieval performance.
051
052   Currently, in the realm of MVT-R, only MeVTR (Zhang et al. (2023)) method has explored match-
053   ing videos based on multi-text queries. However, the MeVTR method relies on event time labels
      corresponding to each textual query, significantly increasing annotation costs. Furthermore, MeVTR

112   2   R ELATED W ORK
113
      Video-Text Retrieval. Video-Text Retrieval can be categorized into SVT-R (Single-event Video-
114
      Text Retrieval) and MVT-R, based on the number of text queries used. SVT-R focuses on retrieving
115
      the video most relevant to a single text query, with the primary challenge being the effective align-
116   ment of video and text features. Early works (Torabi et al. (2016); Yu et al. (2018)) in SVT-R
117   concentrated on designing feature alignment mechanisms. With the introduction of the large-scale
118   image-text pre-trained model CLIP (Radford et al. (2021)), many researchers extended (Luo et al.
119   (2022); Zhao et al. (2022)) its application from static images to video. However, the existing meth-
120   ods primarily focused on single-query video retrieval. In contrast, MVT-R requires multiple text
121   queries for video retrieval, a task first introduced in MeVTR. This approach used the CLIP model
122   to extract visual features from videos and text query features from the input queries. It then applied
123   the K-Medoids clustering algorithm to identify key events in the video and calculated the cosine
124
      similarity between the CLIP-extracted text query features and the visual features of each key event.
      Finally, the proposed MeVTR loss function ensured that each text query matched its corresponding
125
      event, thereby improving retrieval accuracy. Overall, both SVT-R and MVT-R methods are limited
126
      to retrieval and have yet to address the temporal grounding of events within the retrieved videos.
127
128   Video Grounding. Video Grounding aims to locate specific temporal intervals of events in
129   untrimmed videos based on textual queries. Compared to Video-Text Retrieval, the main chal-
130
      lenge in Video Grounding is the precise alignment of visual content with the semantic information
      in the query. Existing Video Grounding approaches can generally be categorized into supervised
131
      and weakly supervised methods. Supervised methods require annotations of event start and end
132
      times corresponding to each textual query during training. These methods can be further divided
133   into proposal-based (Zhang et al. (2020a; 2021b); Wang et al. (2021)) and proposal-free (Mun et al.
134   (2020); Zhang et al. (2020b)) approaches, depending on whether a proposal generation module is
135   used. Proposal-based approaches adopt a two-stage model design: first, generating candidate pro-
136   posals, then jointly modeling these proposals’ corresponding video segments and textual queries,
137   and finally selecting the best proposal for the query. A representative work of this type is 2D-TAN
138   (Zhang et al. (2020a)).
139   Proposal-free approaches, on the other hand, employ a single-stage design, directly predicting event
140   start and end times from the fused video and text features (Zhang et al. (2020b)). Both types have
141   shown impressive performance in Video Grounding. However, these methods assume that the cor-
142   respondence between videos and textual queries is known during both training and testing. In real-
143   world applications, this correspondence is often unknown, requiring relevant videos to be retrieved
144   from a video corpus before performing the Video Grounding. This adds an extra layer of complex-
145   ity, as the system must first retrieve the relevant video from a large corpus, then localize the specific
146   event.
147   To reduce the manual cost of constructing time labels in supervised methods, several studies have
148   focused on Video Grounding under weak supervision. In this setting, only the correspondence be-
149   tween videos and textual queries is provided for model training, without requiring event time labels.
150   Existing weakly supervised Video Grounding(WSVG) methods can be roughly divided into Multiple
151   Instance Learning (MIL)-based (Mithun et al. (2019); Gao et al. (2019)) and reconstruction-based
152   models (Lin et al. (2020); Zheng et al. (2022a); Yang et al. (2021); Zheng et al. (2022b)), depending
153
      on their grounding mechanism. Among them, TGA (Mithun et al. (2019)) was the first method to
      address the weak supervision problem within the MIL framework. It treats videos relevant to the
154
      query as positive samples and irrelevant videos as negative samples, achieving fine-grained visual-
155
      text alignment by maximizing the matching score of positive samples while minimizing the score
156   for negative ones. Reconstruction-based methods, on the other hand, take videos and textual queries
157   as input and generate proposals that match the queries through inter-modal interactions. The vi-
158   sual features corresponding to these proposals are then used to reconstruct masked textual queries,
159   thereby localizing events. A representative method is SCN (Lin et al. (2020)). Building upon this,
160   LCNet (Yang et al. (2021)) proposed a hierarchical representation of video and text features and
161   introduced self-supervised reconstruction loss to accurately model the local correspondences be-
      tween video and text, thereby improving WSVGg performance. Meanwhile, CNM (Zheng et al.

191   3.1   P ROBLEM S TATEMENT AND A PPROACH OVERVIEW
192
      Let’s first provide a concrete definition for the MVT-RG task. Suppose we have a video corpus
193   consisting of N unedited long videos, denoted as V = {Vn }N                                 L
                                                                       n=1 , where Vn = {xl,n }l=1 and xl,n
194   represents the l-th frame of the video, with L being the total number of frames. A multi-text query
195   is represented as Q = {Qm }M  m=1 , where Qm denotes the m-th textual sub-query, and M is the total
196   number of textual sub-queries for a video. Our goals are twofold: (1) to retrieve the corresponding
197   video V ∗ from the video corpus V based on the multi-text query Q; (2) to locate the time intervals
                                                                                                ∗
198   T = {(ts,m , te,m )}Mm=1 for each textual sub-query Qm within the retrieved video V , where ts,m
199   and te,m represent the start and end times of the event, respectively. It is worth noting that this study
200   adopts a weakly supervised setting. Specifically, during training, we only know the correspondence
201
      between each multi-text query and the video, but the specific event time intervals corresponding
      to each textual sub-query are unknown. During the testing, we have no knowledge of either the
202
      correspondence between the multi-text queries and the videos or the specific time intervals of the
203
      events described in the text within the videos. The main task of our designed model is to not only
204   retrieve videos that match the textual descriptions from the video corpus but also to accurately locate
205   the time intervals of the events described in the text within the videos.
206
207
      As illustrated in Figure 2, the method proposed in this paper consists of three components: feature
      extraction, Relational Event-Centric Video-Text Retrieval (RE-CVTR), and Event Contrast-Driven
208
      Video Grounding (EC-DVG). In the feature extraction stage, we meticulously extract features from
209
      both videos and texts to ensure an adequate representation of cross-modal information. RE-CVTR
210   innovatively incorporates multi-level relational associations between different events into the mod-
211   eling framework, employing an event-centric multi-level interaction mechanism to overcome cross-
212   modal challenges in video-text retrieval. For event grounding, EC-DVG introduces an event con-
213   trast mechanism and proposes a multi-event grounding strategy based on event differences, enabling
214   more precise event-level cross-modal alignment. Through these innovative designs, the method
215   presented in this paper demonstrates significant performance improvements in simultaneously ad-
      dressing cross-modal video-text retrieval and multi-event grounding tasks.

232   Text Feature Extraction. Given a multi-text query Q containing M sentences, where each sentence
233   describes an event. Let the m-th sentence be denoted as Qm = {qm,j }Jj=1 , where qm,j represents
234   the j-th word in the m-th sentence, and J is the total number of words in that sentence. We first
235
      use Word2Vec (Mikolov et al. (2013)) to tokenize and embed each sentence, converting it into word
      vectors. The word vectors of the entire sentence are then input into an LSTM, with the final output
236
      of the network, fm,t ∈ R1×d , serving as the feature representation of sentence Qm . The feature
237
      representations of all sentences in Q can be expressed as {f1,t , f2,t , . . . , fM,t } ∈ RM ×d . We con-
238
      catenate the text representations {f1,t , f2,t , . . . , fM,t } with a class token fcls,t ∈ R1×d to form
239
      a new feature representation {fcls,t ; f1,t , f2,t , . . . , fM,t } ∈ R(M +1)×d . This new feature repre-
240
      sentation is fed into the text transformer encoder Et , and the output text features are denoted as
241
      {f˜cls,t ; f˜1,t , f˜2,t , . . . , f˜M,t } ∈ R(M +1)×d .
242
243   Video Feature Extraction:. For video feature extraction, given an untrimmed video V containing
244   T frames, we first segment the video into K non-overlapping video clips. Then, we use a pre-trained
245   C3D network (Tran et al. (2015)) to extract the visual features of each video clip. The features of
246   the entire video can be represented as {f1,v , f2,v , . . . , fK,v } ∈ RK×d . We concatenate these fea-
247   tures with a video class token fcls,v ∈ R1×d to form {fcls,v ; f1,v , f2,v , . . . , fK,v } ∈ R(K+1)×d .
248
      This feature set is fed into the video transformer encoder Ev , and the output features are repre-
249   sented as {f˜cls,v ; f˜1,v , f˜2,v , . . . , f˜K,v } ∈ R(K+1)×d . Next, we adopt the method from 2D-TAN to
250
      construct candidate moments for subsequent event grounding. Specifically, given a candidate mo-

254   gregated features into a temporal feature map F ∈ RK×K×d .

272   In the Video-Text Retrieval task, a single text query often corresponds to events that appear in multi-
273   ple videos. This issue arises from the intrinsic ambiguity of text queries and the repetition of events
274   across various videos, making it more difficult to retrieve the relevant video. To address this, some
275   researchers propose generating multi-text queries for the target video to reduce ambiguity in event
276   descriptions. However, effectively disentangling the relationship between multi-text (event) queries
277   and videos remains challenging. In response, we propose the Relational Event-Centric Video-Text
278
      Retrieval (RE-CVTR) module, based on the understanding that more detailed text descriptions fa-
      cilitate clearer associations between text and video. To fully exploit the hierarchical nature of fea-
279
      tures that embed event relationships at various levels and their corresponding videos, we propose
280

281   contrastive learning between events and videos.

      a series of events that are interrelated yet temporally distinct. These temporal differences lead to
347
      different spatial positions for the events on the 2D-temporal Score Map, as illustrated in Figure 3.
348   Manually annotating the timestamps of corresponding events for each query during training would
349   be highly labor-intensive. To address this, we propose the Event Contrast-Driven Video Grounding
350   (EC-DVG) method. This method leverages the positional differences of events on the 2D-temporal
351   Score Map to design a feature learning strategy driven by event position divergence. By utilizing
352   temporal distinctions between events, this strategy effectively differentiates events while preserving
353   their correlations, enabling efficient cross-modal Multi-event Video-Text Grounding without requir-
354   ing temporal labels. The detailed implementation of EC-DVG is shown in Figure 2(c).
355
      Suppose the feature of the m-th sentence query in the multi-text query is denoted as f˜m,t , and the
356
      temporal feature map corresponding to the video for this text query is Fv ∈ RK×K×d . We obtain
357
      the fused temporal feature map F̃m,v through Equation (8):
358

359                           F̃m,v (i, j, :) = Ws × f˜m,t ⊙ (Wv × Fv (i, j, :))                       (9)
360
361   where Ws ∈ Rd×d and Wv ∈ Rd×d are two learnable parameters, and ⊙ represents the Hadamard
362   product. We then feed F̃m,v into a convolutional layer to capture the contextual relationships among
363   adjacent candidate temporal features, denoting the output as F̄m,v .
364
365   To obtain the response scores of different candidate moments for the m-th sentence, we feed F̄m,v
366   into a prediction layer consisting of a fully connected layer and a Sigmoid activation function, re-
367
      sulting in a 2D-temporal Score Map Pm = (pm,ij )K×K . Here, pm,ij represents the relevance
      probability score between the candidate moment starting at i and ending at j, relative to the m-th
368
      sentence. By this means, we can generate corresponding 2D-temporal Score Maps for M sentence
369
      queries, which we denote as P ∈ RK×K×M , where P = {Pm }M          m=1 . To effectively leverage the
370   differences between events and disentangle the correspondences among different textual sub-queries
371   within the video, we propose a Contrastive Loss driven by the divergence of event positions on the
372   2D-temporal Score Map:

531
      In this paper, we present a novel approach for the MVT-RG task. This method addresses signifi-
      cant limitations in existing techniques that focus on single-text queries and lack the capability to
532
      ground multiple events within retrieved videos. By leveraging both inter-video and intra-video
533
      event relationships, we enhance retrieval and grounding performance. At the retrieval level, our
534   RE-CVTR module facilitates precise alignment between text and video by utilizing comprehensive
535   textual information and hierarchical event relationships. This multi-level contrastive learning not
536   only enriches event descriptions but also improves alignment precision, allowing effective differ-
537   entiation among videos. Moreover, the EC-DVG method accounts for positional variations on the
538   2D temporal Score Map among events, achieving accurate grounding through divergence learning.
539   The experimental results highlight the superior performance of our method compared to existing
      approaches, validating its effectiveness in addressing the MVT-RG task.
