011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Current semantic segmentation models are very data-hungry and require massive
014            costly pixel-wise human annotations. Generative data augmentation, which scales
015
               the train set using generative models, provides a potential remedy. However, ex-
               isting text-centric methods struggle to generate complex in-distribution data due
016
               to the limitations of text descriptions. In this paper, we propose MatchMask, a
017
               novel mask-centric generative data augmentation approach tailored for label-scarce
018            semantic segmentation. It leverages a few labeled semantic masks to generate
019            diverse, realistic, and well-aligned image-mask training pairs for semantic segmen-
020            tation models. Specifically, to adapt existing text-to-image models for semantic
021            image synthesis, we first propose a Gradient Probe Method to investigate the role
022            of each layer in the diffusion model. On this basis, we introduce a Layer-Timestep
023            Adaptive Adapter (LT-Adapter) comprising layer-adaptive cross-attention fusion
024            and time-adaptive LoRA scaling to enable efficient adaption for the critical layers.
025            Meantime, we design a robust relative filtering principle to suppress incorrectly syn-
026
               thesized regions. Moreover, the proposed approach is extended to MatchMask++
               in the semi-supervised setting to take advantage of additional unlabeled data. Ex-
027
               perimental results on VOC, COCO and ADE20K demonstrate that MatchMask
028
               remarkably enhances the performance of segmentation models, surpassing prior
029            data augmentation techniques in various benchmarks, e.g, 67.5%→74.3% mIoU
030            on VOC. Our code will be made publicly available.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Semantic segmentation aims to assign pixel-level dense semantic labels for an image. It has been
036   extensively investigated and inspired many downstream applications like autonomous driving and
037   medical imaging. Despite the rapid progress of deep neural networks, training a semantic segmenta-
038   tion model usually requires a large number of images with pixel-level annotations. The pixel-wise
039
      manual labeling is costly, laborious, and even infeasible, precluding its deployment in some scenes.
      To avert the labor-intensive procedure, data augmentation Shorten & Khoshgoftaar (2019); Shorten
040
      et al. (2021) is an effective manner to expand the diversity of existing data. However, standard
041
      image-centric transforms (e.g, flip, crop) He et al. (2016); Yun et al. (2019); DeVries (2017) offer
042   limited variation, yielding no substantive novel content.
043
044   With the advent of generative models Rombach et al. (2022); Goodfellow et al. (2014), Generative
045
      Data Augmentation (GDA) has emerged as a promising alternative. It scales the train set by producing
      synthetic samples with labels based on advanced generative models. Compared to traditional image-
046
      centric data augmentation, GDA offers a more extensive variety of data. Recent advancements in
047
      text-to-image diffusion models present phenomenal power in generating highly realistic images
048   from textual descriptions. On this basis, DiffuMask Wu et al. (2023b) uses text-guided cross-
049   attention information to localize class-specific regions and finally obtain a pixel-wise mask. Dataset
050   Diffusion Nguyen et al. (2024) carefully designs the text prompts, cross-attention, and self-attention
051   of SD to produce images paired with their corresponding segmentation masks. DatasetDM Wu
052   et al. (2023a) extends text-guided image synthesis to perception data generation by leveraging the
053   rich latent code of the diffusion model and a unified perception decoder. One key characteristic
      of these state-of-the-art methods is text-centric, which uses text as the sole input condition and

075   such methods often produce misaligned image-mask pairs (e.g, table class in Fig. 1 and more in
076   Fig. 11), resulting in incorrect supervision for model training. Unlike classification tasks typically
077   involving one class per image, semantic segmentation handles more complex scenes with multiple
078   classes and spatial relationships, demanding higher standards for generated data that text-centric
079   approaches struggle to meet.
080   To this end, we present a novel paradigm: mask-centric generative data augmentation for semantic
081   segmentation via only a few densely labeled samples. We can utilize existing masks as conditions to
082   generate numerous images with diverse characteristics. This mask-centric perspective can produce
083   informative and complex images that better align with the distribution of the target data, making it a
084   more effective data augmentation technique than text-centric methods.
085   Despite the potential of the mask-centric augmentation pipeline, another underestimated issue is the
086   training of mask-to-image synthesis models with only a small number of labeled samples. Existing
087   state-of-the-art semantic image synthesis models rely on abundant densely annotated image-mask data
088   to align semantic layout and generated image, which contradicts the original goal of generative data
089   augmentation. For example, FreestyleNet Xue et al. (2023) fine-tunes the entire U-Net parameters
090   in Diffusion models, which results in heavy overfitting in the few-shot setting (seeing Fig. 2). One
091   potential solution is to use PEFT (Parameter-Efficient Fine-tuning) techniques such as LoRA Hu et al.
092   (2021). However, despite its popularity, the optimal placement of LoRA layers remains undetermined.
093
      Most works apply LoRA empirically to specific layers (e.g, encoder blocks Zhang & Agrawala
      (2023)), which is heuristics and overlooks task-specific differences, leading to sub-optimal results
094
      for specific tasks. To customize an effective fine-tuning approach for the semantic image synthesis
095
      task, we investigate the contribution of each model layer by proposing the Gradient Probing Method,
096   which analyzes layer-specific gradients during training to evaluate their impact. Our study provides
097   a couple of interesting observations: 1) Only a minority of parameters are highly correlated with
098   spatial control. These critical parameters are dataset-agnostic and consistent across different datasets
099   (shown in Fig. 3). 2) The same attention layer in different blocks exhibits varying effects. In general,
100   the initial and final few blocks in U-Net play a more prominent role (seeing Fig. 5). It makes sense
101   because of the high feature resolution of these blocks, which is advantageous for spatial control.
102   Based on these observations, we propose a simple yet effective Layer-Timestep Adaptive Adap-
103   tater (LT-Adapter) for efficient semantic image synthesis adaption using only a tiny densely labeled
104   data set. We design a LoRA-style adapter with 0.7M parameters for the founded critical layers while
105   freezing the original models. In addition, to capture the varied impact of different layers and timesteps
106   in diffusion models, we introduce layer-adaptive cross-attention fusion and timestep-adaptive LoRA
107   scaling. We also present a relative filtering startegy to remove inaccurate regions in generated images,
      preventing negative effects on segmentation model training.

      Our study mainly focuses on two label-scarce cases: (1) the data-limited scenario: with only few
142   labeled image-mask pairs, addressed by MatchMask; (2) the semi-supervised scenario: consist-
143   ing of a limited amount of labeled data in conjunction with additional unlabeled data, solved by

145   segmentation benchmarks. It significantly boosts the performance of segmentation models in label-
146   scarce scenarios, achieving over a 4.6% mIoU improvement in the data-limited setting and 6.8% in the
147   semi-supervised setting, surpassing image-centric transform and text-centric methods. Furthermore,
148   MatchMask can be flexibly integrated with existing semi-supervised semantic segmentation methods
149   to enhance their performance (e.g, improve Unimatch from 78.3% to 79.6% mIoU on VOC).
150   Our contributions are summarized as follows:
151
152          • New Roadmap: We propose MathcMask, a novel mask-centric generative data augmenta-
153            tion for label-scarce semantic segmentation, which can produce more superior training data
154            compared to prior image-centric and text-centric techniques.
155          • New Insight: We observe that only a few fixed layers are critical for the semantic image
156            synthesis task and the varying effect in different blocks by the proposed Gradient Probe
157            Method, which provides valuable insights for future PEFT research.
158          • New Method: We customize an effective fine-tuning approach named LT-Adapter for the
159            semantic image synthesis task, enabling it to work in few-shot settings.
160          • Stronger practicality and performance: Experimental results on various benchmarks
161            demonstrate the effectiveness and superiority of our data augmentation approach.

      2.1   G ENERATIVE DATA AUGMENTATION
165
166   Data augmentation is a universal method to improve the generalization ability of deep neural networks
167   in the case of insufficient training data. Classical data augmentation techniques are performed at the
168   image level, such as geometric transformations He et al. (2016), color space transformations Jurio
169   et al. (2010), and mixing images Hendrycks et al. (2019); Ghiasi et al. (2021). Despite its simplicity,
170   the augmented data lacks diversity due to the reliance on limited source images. With the advance
171   of deep generative models Ho et al. (2020); Rombach et al. (2022), Generative Data Augmentation
172
      (GDA) has emerged as a promising alternative. It can produce diverse synthetic samples with labels
      based on the powerful generative models, which have been widely explored in the classification
173
      task Azizi et al. (2023); Kingma et al. (2014); Trabucco et al. (2023); You et al. (2024); He et al.
174
      (2022). In the semantic segmentation field, pioneering GAN-based works like DatasetGAN Li et al.
175   (2022a) and BigDatasetGAN Li et al. (2022a) utilize the feature space of pre-trained GANs and
176   design a shallow decoder for generating pixel-level labels for segmentation tasks. However, the
177   quality of the synthesized data is often dissatisfactory due to the limited representation ability of
178   early GAN models. Recent works Nguyen et al. (2024); Wu et al. (2023b) leverage text-to-image
179   models to generate more diverse images paired with masks. However, these works mainly follow the
180   text-centric paradigm, making them challenging to capture complex scenes in semantic segmentation.
181   Additionally, the synthetic data tend to be out-domain and poorly aligned. FreeMask Yang et al.
182   (2024) enhances segmentation via generating mask-based data but is designed for the fully-supervised
183
      setting. In this paper, we shed new light on a novel mask-centric GDA paradigm to generate more
      favorable data for semantic segmentation with only a few densely labeled samples.

186   2.2   L ABEL -S CARCE S EMANTIC S EGMENTATION
187
      Semantic segmentation requires very costly pixel-wise human annotations and how to achieve it using
188
      scarce annotated samples emerges as a popular topic. Semi-supervised semantic segmentation (SSSS)
189
      has been proposed to alleviate the burden of time-consuming manual labeling leveraging limited
190   labeled data along with larger amounts of unlabeled data, including self-training Zou et al. (2020);
191   Yang et al. (2022) and consistency regularization Yang et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2024). Self-training
192   generates pseudo-labels for unlabeled images and leverage them for iterative re-training. Consistency
193   regularization makes predictions invariant to perturbations under different data augmentations. In this
194   paper, we attempt to expand the limited train set by GDA. Apart from SSSS setting, we also consider
195   the more challenging data-limited scene, where no unlabeled data are available.
196
197   2.3   S EMANTIC I MAGE S YNTHESIS
198
199   Semantic image synthesis aims to generate realistic images from given semantic layouts or segmenta-
200   tion maps, including GANs-based Isola et al. (2017); Wang et al. (2018); Park et al. (2019); Liu et al.
201
      (2019); Sushko et al. (2020) methods and diffusion-based methods Wang et al. (2022b;a); Nichol et al.
      (2021); Zhang & Agrawala (2023); Mou et al. (2023); Xue et al. (2023). GAN-based methods tackled
202
      this problem under a conditional GAN framework by exploring different conditioning mechanisms in
203
      GANs to do stochastic generations that correspond to the input label map. These methods often face
204   challenges in generating diverse and fully controlled images, primarily due to the limitations in the
205   training data and the inherent instability in GAN training processes. Diffusion-based methods mainly
206   rectified specific blocks in the U-Net Ronneberger et al. (2015) architecture to enhance semantic
207   consistency. However, most existing works require a large amount of image-mask pairs for training,
208   while the applicability in scenarios with few training samples are barely explored.
209
      Several works have investigated the utilization of pre-trained models to achieve layout-controllable
210   text-to-image synthesis. Some training-free approaches Parmar et al. (2023); Chefer et al. (2023);
211   Feng et al. (2022); Hertz et al. (2022); Couairon et al. (2023) modify cross-attention maps to control
212   the condition the denoising process but shows inferior performance. Other training-based methods
213   adopt transfer learning to text-to-image diffusion models via either fine-tuning all the parameters Xue
214   et al. (2023); Lv et al. (2024) or introducing and optimizing partial parameters Mou et al. (2023) but
215   heavily rely on abundant annotated data. In this paper, we explore the importance of each layer in
      pre-trained models and propose a more efficient manner for adaption.

      Despite the impressive results of previous semantic image synthesis models, they heavily rely on
264   massive annotated data and struggle to work with limited data due to expensive fine-tuning, e.g,

266   et al. (2018); Luo et al. (2017) proves that not all parameters are necessary for the downstream task.

269   following FreestyleNet in the early training stage as pre-trained models align layout information

      3.4   R ELATIVE F ILTERING S TRATEGY
353   Despite impressive results, synthetic images still display artifacts in some cases (e.g, inferior appear-
354   ance and unexpected objects in the background in Fig. 8) due to the inherent drawback of pre-trained
355   models, which will degrade segmentation performance during training. A common solution is to
356   filter uncertain pixels by taking the final softmax output as confidence Sohn et al. (2020). However,
357   this approach heavily depends on a trained segmentation model, which is unreliable and accumulates
358   incorrect supervision in data-limited scenarios due to the confirmation bias Arazo et al. (2020).
359   We present a more robust relative filtering strategy, which identifies the outliers by comparing relative
360   differences across several synthetic images generated by the same mask. The underlying rationale is

362   segmentation models. More formally, given a semantic mask SM , we produce K images based on
363   it. Then we predict the pseudo mask P Mk for each image with a segmentation model (trained in

398   3.5   M ATCH M ASK AND M ATCH M ASK ++
399
      By leveraging the proposed LT-Adapter and Relative Filtering Strategy, we can generate diverse
400
      and high-quality training data using only scarce labeled samples. This data augmentation pipeline
401   in the data-limited scenario is called MatchMask. For the relevant semi-supervised setting, which
402   has access to additional unlabeled data, we further introduce MatchMask++, as shown in Fig. 4.
403   Specifically, based on a model trained on labeled data (enhanced with MatchMask augmentation),
404   pseudo-labeling is employed to extract more realistic masks from the unlabeled data. These pseudo
405   masks are then used for data augmentation with our adapted mask-to-image model. Notably, 1) there
406   is no need to retrain the LT-Adapter; 2) despite possible noise in the pseudo masks, the augmented
407   image-mask pairs retain excellent quality, as the mask-to-image model aligns its output with the noisy
408   masks (seeing Fig. 9), ensuring effective supervision for the segmentation model.

413
414   Datasets and Evaluation Metric. We conduct experiments on both object-centric PASCAL VOC Ev-
415   eringham et al. (2010), MS COCO Lin et al. (2014) and scene-centric ADE20K Zhou et al. (2017)
416   benchmarks. PASCAL VOC 2012 contains 21 categories (one background category), with 10,582
417
      training images and 1449 validation images. COCO 2017 includes 118,000 training images and
      5,000 validation images, covering 80 object categories and a background category. ADE20K includes
418
      150 semantic categories, with 20,210 training images and 2,000 validation images, which are highly
419
      challenging due to the complex taxonomy. In few shot settings, we adopt the number of few labeled
420   samples following semi-supervised segmentationYang et al. (2023) for VOC (92/183/366/732) and
421   COCO (232/463/925/1849), and randomly select 200 samples (about 1%) for ADE20K. The mean
422   Intersection over Union (mIoU) is adopted as the evaluation metric in our experiments.
423
      Implementation Details. For mask-to-image synthesis, we adopt the Stable Diffusion V1-4 Rombach
424
      et al. (2022) as the pre-trained model, and only fine-tune the proposed LT-Adapter with a batch size of
425
      4. We train for 100k iterations with a base learning rate of 4e-5. Image generation uses 50 PLMS Liu
426   et al. (2022) sampling steps with a classifier-free guidance Ho & Salimans (2022) scale of 2. These
427   experiments can be completed on a single NVIDIA A100 GPU with 80GB memory. For the semantic
428   segmentation task, all our experiments were conducted within the mmsegmentation Contributors
429   (2020) framework to ensure consistency and fairness in comparison. We adopt DeepLabv3+ Chen
430   et al. (2018) model with ResNe101 He et al. (2016) for VOC and COCO, and Mask2former Cheng
431   et al. (2022) with Swin-B Liu et al. (2021) for ADE20K dataset. The synthetic image number for
      each mask is set to 5 (K=5) as default. More details are presented in the Appendix.

534   5     C ONCLUSION
535
536   In this paper, we present MatchMask, a novel mask-centric generative data augmentation approach
537   for label-scarce semantic segmentation. By leveraging the proposed Gradient Probe Method, LT-
538   Adapter and Relative Filtering Strategy, we can scale the train set with diverse and high-quality data.
539   It significantly enhances the segmentation performance in both data-limited and semi-supervised
      scenarios. We hope it would offer novel insights for the community.

      B.2   C OMPARISON WITH OTHER FINE - TUNING STRATEGIES
791
792   Baseline. Since our LT-Adapter is the extension of FreestyleNet in the few-shot setting, we treat it as
793   the baseline, which fine-tunes the entire U-Net. In addition, we incorporate three common fine-tuning
794   methods: (1) fine-tuning residual blocks in U-Net; (2) fine-tuning transformer blocks in U-Net; (3)
795   fine-tuning critical layers selected by our Gradient Probe Method. To be consistent with FreestyleNet,
796   we conduct experiments on ADE20K, randomly selecting 200 training samples (about 1%) as labeled
797   data and evaluating on the validation set to verify whether the model is overfitting.
798   Evaluation Metrics. To ensure comparability with FreestyleNet, we adopt Fréchet Inception Distance
799   (FID) Heusel et al. (2017) to assess the visual quality of generated images. To measure layout
800   consistency, unlike previous methods using a pre-trained segmentation model (e.g, UperNet101 Xiao
801   et al. (2018)) on the specific dataset to calculate mIoU between GT and predictions, which suffers
802   from model’s low performance and limited generalization, we propose a more effective evaluation
803   metric termed DINO-SIM. It is based on DINOv2 Oquab et al. (2023), which is a large pre-trained
804   self-supervised model and has powerful representation capabilities. The pixel-level features learned
805   by DINOv2 are well-suited for dense prediction tasks such as semantic segmentation. We use
806
      DINOv2 to extract dense features from the generated/original images (resized to high resolution
      840 × 840) and compute the cosine similarity between these features to measure layout consistency.
807
      This method provides improved accuracy and generalization compared to traditional metrics.

      Discussions: In this work, we propose a novel mask-centric generative data augmentation paradigm
833   for label-scarce semantic segmentation. We have validated its superior performance on various
834   benchmarks. The key design is the LT-Adapter to achieve mask-to-image synthesis via only few
835   densely labeled samples, which is based on the pre-trained Stable Diffusion model. We believe the
836   semantic image synthesis process can be further enhanced by adopting more powerful generative
837   models in the future. In addition, we directly produce K images for each mask in our experiments
838   while not focusing on the value of each sample for the model training. A potential improvement
839   direction is to select more hard samples for segmentation models (e.g, the classes showcase inferior
840   performance).

      Limitations: While MatchMask has shown impressive performance for data augmentation, it still has
865   some limitations. First, the inference speed during image synthesis is unsatisfactory (e.g, 4s/image
866   on VOC or 6s/image on ADE) using PLMS sampling for 50 steps. This stems from the inherent
867   drawbacks of the diffusion model, and we believe future advances in generative paradigms or samplers
868   could address this. Additionally, although MatchMask could produce images based on the pseudo
869   mask, the synthetic image would become messy and unrecognizable if the mask is too noisy. The
870   potential solutions include combining with semi-supervised works to obtain more accurate pseudo
871   masks or designing some principles to neglect these unreliable samples.

      match given mask manner, leading to more reliable training data.
