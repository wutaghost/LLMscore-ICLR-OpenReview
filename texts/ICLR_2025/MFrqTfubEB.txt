011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            World models are receiving increasing attention in autonomous driving for their
014            ability to predict potential future scenarios. In this paper, we present BEVWorld, a
015            novel approach that tokenizes multimodal sensor inputs into a unified and compact
016
               Bird’s Eye View (BEV) latent space for environment modeling. The world model
               consists of two parts: the multi-modal tokenizer and the latent BEV sequence
017
               diffusion model. The multi-modal tokenizer first encodes multi-modality informa-
018
               tion and the decoder is able to reconstruct the latent BEV tokens into LiDAR and
019            image observations by ray-casting rendering in a self-supervised manner. Then
020            the latent BEV sequence diffusion model predicts future scenarios given action
021            tokens as conditions. Experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of BEVWorld in
022            autonomous driving tasks, showcasing its capability in generating future scenes
023            and benefiting downstream tasks such as perception and motion prediction. Code
024            will be available soon.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Autonomous driving has made significant progress in recent years, but it still faces several challenges.
030   First, training a reliable autonomous driving system requires a large amount of precisely annotated
031   data, which is resource-intensive and time-consuming. Thus, exploring how to utilize unlabeled
032
      multimodal sensor data within a self-supervised learning paradigm is crucial. Moreover, a reliable
      autonomous driving system requires not only the ability to perceive the environment but also a
033
      comprehensive understanding of environmental information for decision-making.
034
035   We claim that the key to addressing these challenges is to construct a multimodal world model for
036   autonomous driving. By modeling the environment, the world model predicts future states and
037   behaviors, empowering the autonomous agent to make more sophisticated decisions. Recently, some
038
      world models have demonstrated their practical significance in autonomous driving Hu et al. (2023);
      Zhang et al. (2024); Yang et al. (2024b). However, most methods are based on a single modality,
039
      which cannot adapt to current multisensor, multimodal autonomous driving systems. Due to the
040
      heterogeneous nature of multimodal data, integrating them into a unified generative model and
041   seamlessly adapting to downstream tasks remains an unresolved issue.
042
043   In this paper, we introduce BEVWorld, a multimodal world model that transforms diverse multimodal
044
      data into a unified bird’s-eye-view (BEV) representation and performs action-conditioned future
      prediction within this unified space. Our BEVWorld consists of two parts: a multimodal tokenizer
045
      network and a latent BEV sequence diffusion network.
046
047   The core capability of the multimodal tokenizer lies in compressing original multimodal sensor
048   data into a unified BEV latent space. This is achieved by transforming visual information into 3D
049   space and aligning visual semantic information with Lidar geometric information in a self-supervised
050
      manner using an auto-encoder structure. To reverse this process and reconstruct the multimodal data,
      a 3D volume representation is constructed from the BEV latent to predict high-resolution images and
051
      point clouds using a ray-based rendering technique Yang et al. (2023).
052
053   The Latent BEV Sequence Diffusion network is designed to predict future frames of images and point
      clouds. With the help of a multimodal tokenizer, this task is made easier, allowing for accurate future

054
      BEV predictions. Specifically, we use a diffusion-based method with a spatial-temporal transformer,
055   which converts sequential noisy BEV latents into clean future BEV predictions based on the action
056   condition.
057
058
      To summarize, the main contributions of this paper are:
059
060
             • We introduced a novel multimodal tokenizer that integrates visual semantics and 3D geome-
               try into a unified BEV representation. The quality of the BEV representation is ensured by
061
               innovatively applying a rendering-based method to restore multi-sensor data from BEV. The
062
               effectiveness of the BEV representation is validated through ablation studies, visualizations,
063            and downstream task experiments.
064
065          • We designed a latent diffusion-based world model that enables the synchronous generation
066            of future multi-view images and point clouds. Extensive experiments on the nuScenes and
067
               Carla datasets showcase the leading future prediction performance of multimodal data.

      2.1   W ORLD M ODEL
072
073   This part mainly reviews the application of world models in the autonomous driving area, focusing
074   on scenario generation as well as the planning and control mechanism. If categorized by the key
075   applications, we divide the sprung-up world model works into two categories. (1) Driving Scene
076   Generation. The data collection and annotation for autonomous driving are high-cost and sometimes
077   risky. In contrast, world models find another way to enrich unlimited, varied driving data due to their
078   intrinsic self-supervised learning paradigms. GAIA-1 Hu et al. (2023) adopts multi-modality inputs
079   collected in the real world to generate diverse driving scenarios based on different prompts (e.g.,
080   changing weather, scenes, traffic participants, vehicle actions) in an autoregressive prediction manner,
081
      which shows its ability of world understanding. ADriver-I Jia et al. (2023) combines the multimodal
      large language model and a video latent diffusion model to predict future scenes and control signals,
082
      which significantly improves the interpretability of decision-making, indicating the feasibility of the
083
      world model as a fundamental model. MUVO Bogdoll et al. (2023) integrates LiDAR point clouds
084   beyond videos to predict future driving scenes in the representation of images, point clouds, and
085   3D occupancy. Further, Copilot4D Zhang et al. (2024) leverages a discrete diffusion model that
086   operates on BEV tokens to perform 3D point cloud forecasting and OccWorld Zheng et al. (2023)
087   adopts a GPT-like generative architecture for 3D semantic occupancy forecast and motion planning.
088   DriveWorld Min et al. (2024) and UniWorld Min et al. (2023) approach the world model as 4D scene
089   understanding task for pre-training for downstream tasks. (2) Planning and Control. MILE Hu
090   et al. (2022) is the pioneering work that adopts a model-based imitation learning approach for joint
091   dynamics future environment and driving policy learning in autonomous driving. DriveDreamer
092
      Wang et al. (2023a) offers a comprehensive framework to utilize 3D structural information such as
      HDMap and 3D box to predict future driving videos and driving actions. Beyond the single front
093
      view generation, DriveDreamer-2 Zhao et al. (2024) further produces multi-view driving videos
094
      based on user descriptions. TrafficBots Zhang et al. (2023) develops a world model for multimodal
095   motion prediction and end-to-end driving, by facilitating action prediction from a BEV perspective.
096   Drive-WM Wang et al. (2023b) generates controllable multiview videos and applies the world model
097   to safe driving planning to determine the optimal trajectory according to the image-based rewards.

      2.2   V IDEO D IFFUSION M ODEL
100
101
      World model can be regarded as a sequence-data generation task, which belongs to the realm of video
102   prediction. Many early methods Hu et al. (2022; 2023) adopt VAE Kingma & Welling (2013) and
103   auto-regression Chen et al. (2024) to generate future predictions. However, the VAE suffers from
104   unsatisfactory generation quality, and the auto-regressive method has the problem of cumulative error.
105   Thus, many researchers switch to study on diffusion-based future prediction methods Zhao et al.
106   (2024); Li et al. (2023), which achieves success in the realm of video generation recently and has
107   ability to predict multiple future frames simultaneously. This part mainly reviews the related methods
      of video diffusion model.

134   The standard video diffusion model Ho et al. (2022) takes temporal noise as input, and adopts the
135   UNet Ronneberger et al. (2015) with temporal attention to obtain denoised videos. However, this
136   method requires high training costs and the generation quality needs further improvement. Subsequent
137   methods are mainly improved along these two directions. In view of the high training cost problem,

141   videos, stable video diffusion Blattmann et al. (2023) proposes a multi-stage training strategy, which
142
      adopts image and low-resolution video pretraining to accelerate the model convergence and improve
      generation quality. GenAD Yang et al. (2024a) introduces the causal mask module into UNet to
143
      predict plausible futures following the temporal causality. VDT Lu et al. (2023a) and Sora Brooks
144
      et al. (2024) replace the traditional UNet with a spatial-temporal transformer structure. The powerful
145   scale-up capability of the transformer enables the model to fit the data better and generates more
146   reasonable videos.

181   3.1   M ULTI -M ODAL T OKENIZER
182
      Our designed multi-modal tokenizer contains three parts: a BEV encoder network, a BEV Decoder
183
      network and a multi-modal rendering network. The structure of BEV encoder network is illustrated
184   in the Figure 2. To make the multi-modal network as homogeneous as possible, we adopt the
185   Swin-Transformer Liu et al. (2021) network as the image backbone to extract multi-image features.
186   For Lidar feature extraction, we first split point cloud into pillars Lang et al. (2019) on the BEV
187   space. Then we use the Swin-Transformer network as the Lidar backbone to extract Lidar BEV
188   features. We fuse the Lidar BEV features and the multi-view images features with a deformable-based
189   transformer Zhu et al. (2020). Specifically, we sample K(K = 4) points in the height dimension
190   of pillars and project these points onto the image to sample corresponding image features. The
191   sampled image features are treated as values and the Lidar BEV features is served as queries in the
192
      deformable attention calculation. Considering the future prediction task requires low-dimension
      inputs, we further compress the fused BEV feature into a low-dimensional(C ′ = 4) BEV feature.
193
194   For BEV decoder, there is an ambiguity problem when directly using a decoder to restore the images
195   and Lidar since the fused BEV feature lacks height information. To address this problem, we first
196   convert BEV tokens into 3D voxel features through stacked layers of upsampling and swin-blocks.
197   And then we use voxelized NeRF-based ray rendering to restore the multi-view images and Lidar
198
      point cloud.

359   4.2.2    B ENEFIT FOR D OWNSTREAM TASKS
360
      3D Detection. To verify our proposed method is effective for downstream tasks when used in the
361   pre-train stage, we conduct experiments on the nuScenes 3D detection benchmark. For the model
362   structure, in order to maximize the reuse of the structure of our multi-modal tokenizer, the encoder
363   in the downstream 3D detection task is kept the same with the encoder of the tokenizer described
364   in 3. We use a BEV encoder attached to the tokenizer encoder for further extracting BEV features.
365   We design a UNet-style network with the Swin-transformer Liu et al. (2021) layers as the BEV
366   encoder. As for the detection head, we adopt query-based head Li et al. (2022), which contains
367   500 object queries that searching the whole BEV feature space and uses hungarian algorithm to
368   match the prediction boxes and the ground truth boxes. We report both single frame and two frames
369
      results. We warp history 0.5s BEV future to current frame in two frames setting for better velocity
      estimation. Note that we do not perform fine-tuning specifically for the detection task all in the
370
      interest of preserving the simplicity and clarity of our setup. For example, the regular detection range
371
      is [-60.0m, -60.0m, -5.0m, 60.0m, 60.0m, 3.0m] in the nuScenes dataset while we follow the BEV
372   range of [-80.0m, -80.0m, -4.5m, 80.0m, 80.0m, 4.5m] in the multi-modal reconstruction task, which
373   would result in coarser BEV grids and lower accuracy. Meanwhile, our experimental design eschew
374   the use of data augmentation techniques and the layering of point cloud frames. We train 30 epoches
375   on 8 A100 GPUs with a starting learning rate of 5e−4 that decayed with cosine annealing policy. We
376   mainly focus on the relative performance gap between training from scratch and use our proposed self-
377   supervised tokenizer as pre-training model. As demonstrated in Table 3, it is evident that employing
      our multi-modal tokenizer as a pre-training model yields significantly enhanced performance across

379   an impressive 8.4% improvement in the NDS metric and a substantial 13.4% improvement in the

429   4.3.1       T RAINING D ETAILS .
430
431   NuScenes. We adopt a three stage training for future BEV prediction. 1) Next BEV pretraining. The
      model predicts the next frame with the {xt−1 , xt } condition. In practice, we adopt sweep data of


                                                                       8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      nuScenes to reduce the difficulty of temporal feature learning. The model is trained 20000 iters with
433   a batch size 128. 2) Short Sequence training. The model predicts the N (N = 5) future frames of
434   sweep data. At this stage, the network can learn how to perform short-term (0.5s) feature reasoning.
435   The model is trained 20000 iters with a batch size 128. 3) Long Sequence Fine-tuning. The model
436   predicts the N (N = 6) future frames (3s) of key-frame data with the {xt−2 , xt−1 , xt } condition.
437   The model is trained 30000 iters with a batch size 128. The learning rate of three stages is 5e-4
438   and the optimizer is AdamW Loshchilov & Hutter (2017). Note that our method does not introduce
439   classifier-free gudiance (CFG) strategy in the training process for better integration with downstream
440   tasks, as CFG requires an additional network inference, which doubles the computational cost.
441   Carla. The model is fine-tuned 30000 iterations with a nuScenes-pretrained model with a batch size
442   32. The initial learning rate is 5e-4 and the optimizer is AdamW Loshchilov & Hutter (2017). CFG
443   strategy is not introduced in the training process, following the same setting of nuScenes.

      5   C ONCLUSION
531
532
      We present BEVWorld, an innovative autonomous driving framework that leverages a unified Bird’s
533
      Eye View latent space to construct a multi-modal world model. BEVWorld’s self-supervised learning
534   paradigm allows it to efficiently process extensive unlabeled multimodal sensor data, leading to a
535   holistic comprehension of the driving environment. We validate the effectiveness of BEVWorld in
536   the downstream autonomous driving tasks. Furthermore, BEVWorld achieves satisfactory results
537   in multi-modal future predictions with latent diffusion network, showcasing its capabilities through
538   experiments on both real-world(nuScenes) and simulated(carla) datasets. We hope that the work
539   presented in this paper will stimulate and foster future developments in the domain of world models
      for autonomous driving.

896   The concept of a world model holds significant relevance and diverse applications within the realm of
897   autonomous driving. It serves as a versatile tool, functioning as a simulator, a generator of long-tail
898   data, and a pre-trained model for subsequent tasks. Our proposed method introduces a multi-modal
899   BEV world model framework, designed to align seamlessly with the multi-sensor configurations
900   inherent in existing autonomous driving models. Consequently, integrating our approach into current
901   autonomous driving methodologies stands to yield substantial benefits.

903   D    L IMITATIONS
904
905   It is widely acknowledged that inferring diffusion models typically demands around 50 steps to attain
906   denoising results, a process characterized by its sluggishness and computational expense. Regrettably,
907   we encounter similar challenges. As pioneers in the exploration of constructing a multi-modal world
908   model, our primary emphasis lies on the generation quality within driving scenes, prioritizing it over
909   computational overhead. Recognizing the significance of efficiency, we identify the adoption of one-
910
      step diffusion as a crucial direction for future improvement in the proposed method. Regarding the
      quality of the generated imagery, we have noticed that dynamic objects within the images sometimes
911
      suffer from blurriness. To address this and further improve their clarity and consistency, a dedicated
912
      module specifically tailored for dynamic objects may be necessary in the future.
