009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            In recent years there have been remarkable breakthroughs in image-to-video gener-
012            ation. However, the 3D consistency and camera controllability of generated frames
013            have remained unsolved. Recent studies have attempted to incorporate camera
014            control into the generation process, but their results are often limited to simple
015            trajectories or lack the ability to generate consistent videos from multiple distinct
016            camera paths for the same scene. To address these limitations, we introduce Cavia,
017            a novel framework for camera-controllable, multi-view video generation, capable
018
               of converting an input image into multiple spatiotemporally consistent videos. Our
               framework extends the spatial and temporal attention modules into view-integrated
019
               attention modules, improving both viewpoint and temporal consistency. This flex-
020
               ible design allows for joint training with diverse curated data sources, including
021            scene-level static videos, object-level synthetic multi-view dynamic videos, and
022            real-world monocular dynamic videos. To our best knowledge, Cavia is the first of
023            its kind that allows the user to precisely specify camera motion while obtaining
024            object motion. To the best of our knowledge, Cavia is the first framework that
025            enables users to generate multiple videos of the same scene with precise control
026            over camera motion, while simultaneously preserving object motion. Extensive
027            experiments demonstrate that Cavia surpasses state-of-the-art methods in terms of
028            geometric consistency and perceptual quality.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      The rapid development of diffusion models has enabled significant advancements in video generative
      models. Early efforts have explored various approaches, either training a video model from scratch or
034
      by fine-tuning pre-trained image generation models with additional temporal layers (Stability, 2023;
035
      Wang et al., 2023a; Ho et al., 2022b; Singer et al., 2022; Ho et al., 2022a; Nan et al., 2024). The
036   training data of these video models typically consist of a curated mixture of image (Schuhmann et al.,
037   2022) and video datasets (Bain et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023b;a; Nan et al., 2024). While substantial
038   progress has been made in improving model architectures and refining training data, relatively little
039   research has been conducted on the 3D consistency and camera controllability of generated videos.
040
      To tackle this issue, several recent works (Wang et al., 2023c; He et al., 2024; Bahmani et al., 2024; Xu
041
      et al., 2024; Hou et al., 2024) have attempted to introduce camera controllability in video generation,
042   aiming to ensure that generated frames adhere to viewpoint instructions, thereby improving 3D
043   consistency. These works either enhance viewpoint control through better conditioning signals (Wang
044   et al., 2023c; He et al., 2024; Bahmani et al., 2024) or by utilizing geometric priors, such as epipolar
045   constraints (Xu et al., 2024) or explicit 3D representations (Hou et al., 2024). However, despite
046   these efforts, the generated videos often lack precise 3D consistency or are restricted to static scenes
047   with little to no object motion. Moreover, it remains challenging for monocular video generators
048   to produce multi-view consistent videos of the same scene from different camera trajectories.
049   Since independently sampling multiple sequences often results in significantly inconsistent scenes,
050   generating multiple video sequences simultaneously is desirable. However, this remains extremely
051   challenging due to the scarcity of multi-view video data in the wild, leading to multi-view generations
052   limited to inconsistent near-static scenes or synthetic objects. A concurrent work, CVD (Kuang et al.,
053   2024), builds on multi-view static videos (Zhou et al., 2018) and warping-augmented monocular
      videos (Bain et al., 2021), but it can only generate videos with limited baselines, yielding inconsistent


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      results when object motion is present. Another concurrent work, Vivid-ZOO (Li et al., 2024a),
055   leverages dynamic objects from Objaverse (Deitke et al., 2023b) dataset and renders multi-view
056   videos to train a video generator. However, due to limited data sources, their results are primarily
057   object-centric frames from fixed viewpoints, lacking realistic backgrounds.
058
      To address these challenges, we propose Cavia, a novel framework that extends a monocular video
059
      generator (Stability, 2023) to generate multi-view consistent videos with precise camera control. We
060
      enhance the spatial and temporal attention modules to cross-view and cross-frame 3D attentions
061   respectively, improving consistency across both viewpoints and frames. Our model architecture
062   enables a novel joint training strategy that fully utilizes static, monocular, and multi-view dynamic
063   videos. Static videos (Zhou et al., 2018; Yu et al., 2023; Xia et al., 2024; Reizenstein et al., 2021;
064   Deitke et al., 2023b;a) are converted to multi-view formats to ensure the geometric consistency in
065   the generated frames. We then incorporate rendered synthetic multi-view videos of dynamic 3D
066   objects (Liang et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024c) to teach the model to generate reasonable
067   object motion. To prevent overfitting on synthetic data, we finetune the model on pose-annotated
068   monocular videos (Wang et al., 2023b; Nan et al., 2024) to enhance performance on complex scenes.
069
      Our framework synthesizes cross-view and cross-frame consistent videos, and extensive evaluations
      on real and text-to-image generated images show its applicability across challenging indoor, outdoor,
070
      object-centric, and large-scene cases. We systematically measure the quality of the generated videos
071
      in terms of per-video and cross-view geometric consistency and perceptual quality. Our experiments
072   demonstrate our superiority compared to previous works both qualitatively and quantitatively. Our
073   experiments demonstrate superior performance compared to previous methods, both qualitatively and
074   quantitatively. Additionally, we show that our method can extrapolate to generate four views during
075   inference and enable 3D reconstruction of the generated frames.
076
      Our main contributions can be summarized as follows,
077
078          • We propose a novel framework, Cavia, for generating multi-view videos with camera
079            controllability. We introduce view-integrated attentions, namely cross-view and cross-frame
080            3D attentions, to enhance consistency across viewpoints and frames.
081          • We introduce an effective joint training strategy that leverages a curated mixture of static,
082            monocular dynamic, and multi-view dynamic videos, ensuring geometric consistency, high-
083            quality object motion, and background preservation in the generated results.
084          • Our experiments demonstrate superior geometric and perceptual quality in both monocular
085            video generation and cross-video consistency compared to baseline methods. Additionally,
086            our flexible framework can operate on four views at inference, offering improved view
087            consistency and enabling 3D reconstruction of the generated frames.

      2.1   C AMERA C ONTROLLABLE V IDEO D IFFUSION M ODELS
092
093   Recent advancements in video diffusion models have significantly benefited from scaling model
094   architectures and leveraging extensive datasets (Bain et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023b;a), leading to
095   impressive capabilities in generating high-quality videos (Stability, 2023; Ho et al., 2022b; Singer
096   et al., 2022; Ho et al., 2022a; OpenAI). While large foundational video diffusion models exist, our
097   work focuses on enhancing camera control over video diffusion processes, a rapidly growing area of
098   research. AnimateDiff (Guo et al., 2023) and Stable Video Diffusion (SVD) (Stability, 2023) employ
099
      individual camera LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) models for specific camera motions. MotionCtrl (Wang
      et al., 2023c) improves flexibility by introducing camera matrices, while CameraCtrl (He et al., 2024),
100
      CamCo (Xu et al., 2024), and VD3D (Bahmani et al., 2024) enhance the camera control accuracy
101
      by introducing Plücker coordinates to the video models via controlnet (Zhang & Agrawala, 2023). To
102   further improve the geometric consistency, CamCo (Xu et al., 2024) applies epipolar constraints and
103   CamTrol (Hou et al., 2024) incorporates 3D Gaussians (Kerbl et al., 2023). However, these methods
104   focus on monocular video generation, limiting their ability to sample multiple consistent video
105   sequences of the same scene from distinct camera paths. CVD (Kuang et al., 2024) extends CameraC-
106   trl (He et al., 2024) for multi-view video generation, but their results are constrained to simple camera
107   and object motion. ViVid-Zoo (Li et al., 2024a) extends MVDream (Shi et al., 2023b) for multi-view
      purposes but is limited to object-centric results with fixed viewpoints. In contrast, our work explores

      view-integrated attentions for more precise camera control over arbitrary viewpoints and introduces a
109   joint training strategy leveraging data mixtures to improve novel-view performance in complex scenes.

      2.2   M ULTI - VIEW I MAGE G ENERATION
112
113   Early approaches such as MVDiffusion (Tang et al., 2023) focused on generating multiview images
114   in parallel by employing correspondence-aware attention mechanisms, enabling effective cross-view
115   information interaction, particularly for textured scene meshes. Recent approaches like Zero123++
116   (Shi et al., 2023a), Direct2.5 (Lu et al., 2024), Instant3D (Li et al., 2023), MVDream (Shi et al.,
117   2023b), MVDiffusion++ (Tang et al., 2024), CAT3D (Gao et al., 2024), and Wonder3D (Long
118   et al., 2024) have introduced single-pass frameworks for multiview generation, utilizing multiview
119
      self-attention to improve viewpoint consistency. Other works, such as SyncDreamer (Liu et al.,
      2023b), One-2-3-45 (Liu et al., 2024), Cascade-Zero123 (Chen et al., 2023) and ConsistNet (Yang
120
      et al., 2024a), incorporate multiview features into 3D volumes to facilitate 3D-aware diffusion models
121
      (Liu et al., 2023a; Watson et al., 2022). Meanwhile, techniques such as Pose-Guided Diffusion
122   (Tseng et al., 2023), Era3D (Li et al., 2024b), Epidiff (Huang et al., 2024), and SPAD (Kant et al.,
123   2024) have integrated epipolar-based features to facilitate enhanced viewpoint fusion within diffusion
124   models. Finally, approaches like V3D (Chen et al., 2024b), IM-3D (Melas-Kyriazi et al., 2024),
125   SV3D (Voleti et al., 2024) and Vivid-1-to-3 (Kwak et al., 2024) leverage priors from video diffusion
126   models to achieve multiview generation with improved consistency. However, these methods focus on
127   generating static 3D objects or scenes, while our work introduces vivid object motion into multiview
128   dynamic video generation in complex scenes.

130   2.3   4D G ENERATION
131
132
      Recent efforts in 4D generation have explored various methods (Singer et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023;
      Bahmani et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2023; Ling et al., 2023a) that use score distillation from video
133
      diffusion models to optimize dynamic NeRFs or 3D Gaussians for text- or image-conditioned scenes.
134
      Follow-up works (Jiang et al., 2023; Ren et al., 2023; Yin et al., 2023; Ren et al., 2024; Zeng et al.,
135   2024; Pan et al., 2024) investigate video-to-4D generation, enabling controllable 4D scene generation
136   from monocular videos. More recent methods (Liang et al., 2024; Xie et al., 2024; Zhang et al.,
137   2024) utilize video diffusion models to address the spatial-temporal consistency required for efficient
138   4D generation. However, these approaches primarily focus on object-centric generation and face
139   challenges in producing realistic results with complex backgrounds. In contrast, our work emphasizes
140   generating multi-view, 3D-consistent videos for complex scenes.

144   3.1   OVERVIEW
145
146   Image-to-video generation takes a single image I0 as input and outputs a video sequence O1 , · · · , On .
147   By introducing camera control, the model additionally takes in a sequence of camera information
148   C1 , · · · , Cn , which dictates the desired viewpoint changes for the output sequence. In the multi-view
149   scenario, we extend each batch of the camera control signal and output video sequence to V sequences.
150   In the following paragraphs, we present our proposed Cavia framework in detail. First, we outline
151
      the preliminaries of image-to-video diffusion and describe how camera controllability is introduced
      in monocular video generation. Then, we elaborate on the model design for multi-view consistent
152
      video generation. An overview of our framework is provided in Fig. 1.

211   Cross-frame Attention for Temporal Consistency Vanilla 1D temporal attention in the SVD
212   backbone is insufficient for modeling large pixel displacements when the viewpoint changes (Shi
213   et al., 2023b; Yang et al., 2024b). In vanilla 1D temporal attention, attention matrices are calculated
214   over the frame number dimension, and latent features only interact with features from the same spatial
215   location across frames. This limits information flow between different spatial-temporal locations.
      While this might not be a big issue for video generation with limited motion, viewpoint changes

216
      typically cause significant pixel displacements, which calls for better architecture for more efficient
217   information propagation.
218
219   To overcome this issue, we inflate the original 1D temporal attention modules in the SVD network
220
      into 3D cross-frame temporal attention modules, allowing for joint modeling of spatial-temporal
      feature coherence. The inflation operation can be achieved by rearranging the latent features before
221
      the attention matrix calculations. Consider the latent features of shape (B V F C H W) where B,
222
      V, F, C refer to the batch size, the number of views, the length of frames, and the feature dimension,
223   respectively, instead of employing 1D attention mechanism on rearranged shape of ((B V H W)
224   F C), our inflated attention operates on the rearranged shape ((B V) (F H W) C), integrating
225   spatial features into the attention matrices. A visualization is provided in Fig. 1(c).
226
      Since our rearrange operation only alters the sequence length of the attention inputs without modifying
227
      the feature dimensions, we can seamlessly inherit the pre-trained weights from the SVD backbone
228
      for our purpose. Thanks to this rearrange operation, our inflated temporal attention now calculates
229   the similarity of spatial-temporal features simultaneously, accommodating larger pixel displacements
230   while maintaining temporal consistency.

      3.3   C ONSISTENT M ULTI - VIEW V IDEO D IFFUSION M ODEL
233
234   Adding Plücker coordinates for camera control and introducing improved temporal attention allows
235   the video diffusion model to generate reasonably consistent monocular videos. However, for multi-
236   view generation, a monocular video diffusion model that generates samples independently cannot
237   ensure view consistency across multiple sequences. To address this, we introduce novel design
238   mechanisms and training strategies to extend the monocular video diffusion model to the multi-view
239   generation task.
240
241   Cross-view Attention for Multi-view consistency To improve cross-view consistency in multi-
242
      view videos, we aim to encourage information exchange during the generation process. Since our
      temporal cross-frame attention modules already handle intra-view feature connections within each
243
      video sequence, we focus on exchanging inter-view signals through the spatial cross-view modules.
244
      Inspired by MVDream (Shi et al., 2023b), we introduce 3D cross-view attention modules, inflated
245   from the spatial attention blocks of SVD (Stability, 2023). Specifically, we rearrange the V views
246   such that frames at each corresponding timesteps are concatenated before being sent into the attention
247   modules. In detail, we rearrange the latent features from shape (B V F C H W) to (((B F) (V
248   H W) C) instead of (((B V F) (H W) C). A visualization is provided in Fig. 1(b).
249
      Since only the second-to-last dimension, representing token length, is extended while other di-
250
      mensions remain unchanged, our inflated spatial attention can inherit the model weights from the
251   monocular setting. This flexibility allows our model to leverage training data with varying numbers of
252   views and facilitates extrapolation to additional views at inference. To handle multi-view generation,
253   we introduce an additional view dimension to the input data. To maintain workflow simplicity, we
254   absorb the view dimension into the batch dimension during processing of other blocks, ensuring
255   flexibility in handling different numbers of views.

355   In this section, we present experimental results and analysis. Video comparisons are included in the

361
362   3D Consistency of Frames We evaluate the 3D consistency of the generated videos using
363   COLMAP (Schönberger & Frahm, 2016; Schönberger et al., 2016). COLMAP is widely adopted
364   for 3D reconstruction methods where camera pose estimation is required for in-the-wild images.
365   We configure the COLMAP following previous methods (Deng et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2024) for
366   best few-view performance. A higher COLMAP error rate indicates poorer 3D consistency in the
367   input images. Motivated by this, we report COLMAP errors as a measure of the 3D consistency
368   of the frames. Each video is retried up to five times to reduce randomness. We randomly sample
369
      1,000 video sequences from RealEstate10K (Zhou et al., 2018) test set for evaluation. Since we have
      ground truth 3D scenes, we use the ground truth camera pose sequences as the viewpoint instruction
370
      of the video model and compare the generated frames against the ground truth images. Similar to
371
      prior works (He et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024), we extract the estimated camera poses and calculate
372   the relative translation and rotation differences. Specifically, given two camera pose sequences,
373   we convert them to relative poses and align the first frames to world origin. We then measure the
374   angular errors in translation and rotation. Unlike previous works (He et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024)
375   that calculate the Euclidean distance of translation vectors, we use angular error measurements to
376   ensure the camera pose scales are normalized, addressing scale ambiguity. As shown in Tab. 1, we
377   calculate the area under the cumulative error curve (AUC) of frames whose rotation and translations
      are below certain thresholds (5◦ , 10◦ , 20◦ ). Our method significantly outperforms existing baselines.

511
512   We provide qualitative comparisons on RealEstate10k (Zhou et al., 2018) scenes in Fig. 4 and text-
513
      to-image generated images in Fig. 5. As shown in Fig. 4, our method produces videos with precise
      camera control, whereas MotionCtrl tends to generate overly smooth trajectories that simplify the
514
      viewpoint instructions, and CameraCtrl suffers from severe distortions at novel viewpoints. For
515
      example, in the first case, the camera instruction involves multiple panning operations, first panning
516   left and then panning right. Still, MotionCtrl only pans left, ignoring the rest of the instructions.
517   CameraCtrl’s outputs, particularly in the first two cases, exhibit noticeable distortion, with the walls
518   bending in the later frames. Additionally, in the third and fourth cases, where the camera trajectories
519   cover a long distance, both MotionCtrl and CameraCtrl produce unrealistic hallucinations, introducing
520   artifacts such as merging indoor and outdoor pixels or distorting input pixels to compensate for a
521   lack of generation ability. In Fig. 5, we observe that MotionCtrl and CameraCtrl tend to generate
522   static scenes without any object motion. Although their methods produce realistic novel views,
523   the synthesized objects remain static. In contrast, our method generates vivid object motion while
524
      maintaining accurate camera control. We highlight the object motion in Fig. 5 using auxiliary red
      lines. We encourage readers to view the supplementary videos for optimal visual comparisons.

535   In this paper, we propose Cavia, a novel framework for consistent multi-view camera-controllable
536   video generation. Our framework incorporates cross-frame and cross-view attentions for effective
537   camera controllability and view consistency. Our model benefits from joint training using static 3D
538   scenes and objects, animatable objects, and in-the-wild monocular videos. Extensive experiments
539   demonstrate the superiority of our method over previous works in terms of geometric consistency
      and perceptual quality.

      A    A DDITIONAL I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
811
812
      Our training is divided into static stage and dynamic stage. Our static stage is trained for around
813   500k iterations and our dynamic stage is trained for roughly 300k iterations. The effective batch
814   size is 128 and the learning rate is 1e-4. Our video length is 14 frames for each view with the first
815   frame shared across views. Our model is fine-tuned at 256 × 256 spatial resolution from the SVD
816   1.0 checkpoint. The training data are prepared by first center-cropping the original videos and then
817   resizing each frame to the shape of 256 × 256. In the dynamic stage, 30% of iterations are used to
818   train on monocular videos. During static training, the strides of frames are randomly sampled in
819   the range of [1, 8]. For monocular videos, the strides are sampled in the range of [1, 2]. For
820   dynamic multi-view object renderings, the strides are fixed to 1 to use all rendered frames since we
821
      already introduced randomness in the frame rate during rendering. At inference time, the decoding
      chunk is set to 14 so all frames are decoded altogether. We sample 25 steps to obtain all our results.

824   B    A DDITIONAL DATA C URATION D ETAILS
825
826   In this section, we provide additional details on our data processing and curation pipelines.
827
828   Static 3D Objects Our static objects data comprises multi-view images rendered from the Obja-
829   verse (Deitke et al., 2023b) and Objaverse-XL(Deitke et al., 2023a) dataset. Similar to InstantMesh,
830   we use a filtered high-quality subset of the original dataset to train our model. The filtering goal is to
831   remove objects that satisfy any of the following criteria: (i) objects without texture maps, (ii) objects
832   with rendered images occupying less than 10% of the view from any angle, (iii) including multiple
833
      separate objects, (iv) objects with no caption information provided by the Cap3D dataset, and (v)
      low-quality objects. The classification of “low-quality” objects is determined based on the presence
834
      of tags such as “lowpoly” and its variants (e.g., “low poly”) in the metadata. By applying our filtering
835
      criteria, we curated approximately 400k high-quality instances from the initial pool of 800k objects
836   in the Objaverse dataset.
837
838   For each 3D object, we use Blender’s EEVEE renderer to render an 84-frame RGBA orbit at 512×512
839
      resolution. we adaptively position the camera to a distance sufficient to ensure that the rendered
      object content makes good and consistent use of the image extents without being clipped in any
840
      view. For each frame, the azimuths can be irregularly spaced, and the elevation can vary per view.
841
      Specifically, the sequence of camera elevations for each orbit is obtained from a random weighted
842   combination of sinusoids with different frequencies. The azimuth angles are sampled regularly, and
843   then a small amount of noise is added to make them irregular. The elevation values are smoothed
844   using a simple convolution kernel and then clamped to a maximum elevation of 89 degrees.
845
846   Static 3D Scenes Our static scenes data are sourced from RealEstate10k (Zhou et al., 2018),
847   WildRGBD (Xia et al., 2024), MVImgNet (Yu et al., 2023), CO3Dv2 (Reizenstein et al., 2021),
848   and DL3DV-10K (Ling et al., 2023b). For RealEstate10k, we use the train/test split released by
849   PixelSplat (Charatan et al., 2023). During training, we sample every 8 original frames to construct
850   the training sequences. For DL3DV-10K, we construct training sequences from the publicly available
851   7k subset. Since each video is very long for the DL3DV-10k dataset, we offline randomly sample
852
      multiple sequences from a single ground truth video to obtain multiple training data items. For
      CO3Dv2, we remove the video sequences that contain whole-black images to avoid temporally
853
      inconsistent frames. For WildRGBD and MVImgNet we use all classes available and removed
854
      sequences whose lengths are not enough for two-view training (shorter than 27 frames).
855
856
      Dynamic 3D Objects Our dynamic 3D objects are similarly rendered as the static 3D objects. The
857
      filtering pipelines remain mostly the same as the static objects, except that we introduce additional
858   workflows to consider object motion. Inspired by previous works (Liang et al., 2024; Jiang et al.,
859   2024; Li et al., 2024a) that employ animatable objects from Objaverse. We render multiple fixed-view
860   videos to examine the motion quality of the objects. We utilize lpips (Zhang et al., 2018) to measure
861   the similarity of nearby frames and consider an object to be static if lpips similarity is above a certain
862   threshold. Additionally, we render the alpha masks of the object and use this as an indicator of
863   whether the object has moved out of the visible regions. Consequently, we remove objects with too
      large or sudden movements as well as objects with little-to-no motion. These filterings result in

      Monocular Videos Our monocular video filtering pipeline involves filtering according to Particle-
891   SfM output, OCR, aesthetic score, and camera motion. As mentioned in Sec. 4.2, we first attempt
892   to annotate the camera poses for the video frames using Particle-SfM (Zhao et al., 2022). Take
893   InternVid (Wang et al., 2023b) as an example, roughly 10 million video clips are processed and
894   around 3 million samples are successfully processed by Particle-SfM. For each video, we start from
895   the first frame and randomly select a frame stride of 1 or 2. The total number of images sent to
896   Particle-SfM is 32 images. Our point count filtering is empirically implemented as a cut-off at
897   1,000 points and 40,000 points. Point clouds with too few points are removed due to the concern
898   that the frames are poorly registered. Point clouds with too many points are avoided because their
899
      limited object motion. This aggressive filtering results in around 2 million samples for further
      processing. We then evaluate all the video clips using OCR detection algorithms and remove the
900
      samples whose detected text regions are larger then 10−4 of the image resolution (i.e. 6 pixels). This
901
      process results in 604,000 samples. The next step is filtering with aesthetic scores and videos with
902   aesthetic score annotations smaller than 4 are removed. 467,000 videos are left after these filtering
903   process. Finally, we employ a camera motion classifier extended from the Open-Sora pipeline1 . The
904   main motivation is that optical-flow on consecutive frames can be summarized to a global motion
905   vector, assuming the most parts of the scene is moving in a uniform direction. Optical flow is first
906   obtained using cv2.calcOpticalFlowFarneback for each consecutive frame pairs. Then, the
907   magnitudes and directions are calculated via cv2.cartToPolar. These magnitudes and directions
908   are classified into 8 categories: static, zoom out, zoom in, pan left, tilt up, pan right, tilt down, and
909   unknown. The results of the frame pairs are summarized to obtain the final result of each video clip.
910
      When a certain type appears more than 50%, the type for the whole video clip is determined directly.
      We aggressively classify a video clip as static if any of its frame pairs is categorized into static or
911
      unknown. Finally, we obtain 355,000 clips that satisfy our needs. The process is similarly applied to
912
      OpenVid (Nan et al., 2024)’s Panda-70M subset Chen et al. (2024a) and we obtained 38,000 clips. In
913   summary, our monocular video dataset consists of 393,000 clips.

1029   In this section, we conduct extensive evaluations for ablation studies. We provide video comparisons
1030   in the supplementary. We provide thorough quantitative comparisons in Tab. 3 to illustrate the

1032
       variants used for ablation studies. The models are evaluated using RealEstate10K camera trajectories.

1036   We first examine the importance of Plücker coordinates conditioning and the cross-frame attention
1037   modules. As shown in Fig. 6, model variants without cross-frame attention contains severe distortion
1038
       artifacts, such as the bent walls. The model variant without Plücker coordinates results in simplified
       camera motion that ignores the complex camera viewpoint instructions.
1039
1040   We then evaluate the model variant without cross-view attention. As shown in Fig. 7, we observe that
1041   removing the cross-view attention module results in multiple individual video samples that contain
1042   different object motions. For example, the penguin moves differently in the first case, and the wood
1043   sticks in the fire appear differently in the second case. This behavior is not desirable because our goal
1044
       is to obtain multiple videos from different camera paths of the same scene.
1045   Finally, we examine the importance of our monocular video joint training strategy. As shown in Fig. 8,
1046   we observe that when overfitting on dynamic objects from Objaverse, the generated results tend to
1047   contain frames with simplified backgrounds. This is mainly because, during the training, all data
1048   samples from Objaverse are implemented with single random color backgrounds. Our model benefits
1049   from joint training on monocular videos and preserves the ability to generate complex backgrounds
1050
       when object motion is present.

1116
1117   Our framework has limited ability to generate large object motion, mainly due to the limitation of
1118   the base video generator SVD (Stability, 2023). We will explore better base models in future works.
1119
       Moreover, our data curation pipelines assume a simple camera model using shared camera intrinsic

1120
       complex camera intrinsic changes at inference time, which is widely adopted in cinematography.
1121
       Additionally, for simplicity, our framework is trained with normalized scales of scenes, which can be
1122   further improved if potentially calibrated with metric scale. We will explore calibration techniques
1123   for better quality if a well-generalizable metric depth estimator becomes publicly available.
