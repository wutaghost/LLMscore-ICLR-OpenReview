011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Large language models have consistently struggled with complex reasoning tasks,
014            such as mathematical problem-solving. Investigating the internal reasoning mech-
015
               anisms of these models can help us design better model architectures and training
               strategies, ultimately enhancing their reasoning capability. In this study, we con-
016
               structed a symbolic dataset to investigate the mechanisms by which Transformer
017
               models employ vertical thinking strategy based on their inherent structure and
018            horizontal thinking strategy based on Chain of Thought to achieve multi-step rea-
019            soning. We introduced the concept of buffer mechanism: the model stores various
020            information in distinct buffers and selectively extracts them through the query-key
021            matrix. We proposed a random matrix-based algorithm to enhance the model’s
022            reasoning ability, resulting in a 75% reduction in the training time required for
023            the GPT-2 model to achieve generalization capability on the PrOntoQA dataset.
024            These findings provide new insights into understanding the mechanisms of large
025            language models.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      In recent years, LLMs have emerged and demonstrated remarkable capability across various tasks
030   (Vaswani et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2018; Devlin et al., 2018; Radford et al., 2019; Touvron et al., 2023;
031   OpenAI, 2023). These models have shown impressive in-context learning abilities (Brown et al.,
032   2020; Dong et al., 2022; Garg et al., 2022) and have been applied to logical reasoning problems,
033   such as matching top human contestants at the International Mathematical Olympiad (IMO) level
034   (Trinh et al., 2024) and solving math problems (Davies et al., 2021).
035
      However, even the most advanced models still struggle with complex reasoning tasks, which in-
036   dicates the ability of LLMs to handle multi-step logical reasoning remains constrained. To truly
037   enhance the reasoning capability of LLMs, it is crucial to investigate their intrinsic mechanisms.
038
039
      Multi-step reasoning tasks encompass a broad concept, typically referring to the ability of a
      model to synthesize numerous complex conditions to answer questions. Here, we consider a
040
      representative class of syntactic structures within multi-step reasoning tasks: a sentence that in-
041
      cludes both the question and sufficient known information to answer it. For example, “Given:
042   [A]→[B]...[B]→[C]..., Question: 2-step reasoning starting from [A]”, where “...” represents
043   other textual content unrelated to logical reasoning. The answer to this question is “[C]”. When
044   a sentence contains only one logical reasoning step, it is often handled by the so-called induction
045   head in Transformer (Brown et al., 2020; Olsson et al., 2022). However, multi-step reasoning is not
046   merely a linear accumulation of multiple induction heads but involves more complex mechanisms.
047
      Large models employ two primary strategies for logical reasoning. The first, known as the Vertical
048   Thinking Strategy (VTS), outputs reasoning results in a single forward based on their inherent struc-
049   ture. This approach is efficient but demands a high level of intelligence from the model itself. As
050   shown in Fig. 1, current large models exhibit significant limitations in their vertical thinking capa-
051   bility. Another relatively less efficient approach is the Horizontal Thinking Strategy (HTS), such as
052   Chain of Thought (CoT) (Wei et al., 2022; Kojima et al., 2022) and Tree of Thought (ToT) (Yao
053   et al., 2024), Diagram of Thought (DoT) (Zhang et al., 2024a). This strategy substantially enhances
      the model’s reasoning performance. All models can produce the correct answers for tasks depicted

092

093   has also been mentioned in other works (Reddy, 2023; Bietti et al., 2024; Elhage et al., 2021). Our
094   work provides a detailed description of the concept of buffer and applies this mechanism to enhance
095   model performance.
096
      The main contributions of this work are as follows:
097
098   • We propose a buffer mechanism and found evidence that supports such mechanism being em-
099     ployed by language models during the reasoning process in symbolic multi-step reasoning tasks
100     and provide a detailed analysis of the model’s internal thinking process for vertical and horizontal
101     thinking from the perspective of the buffer.
102   • We propose a method to enhance the model’s reasoning capability, significantly improving data
103     utilization efficiency in logical reasoning datasets.
104
105   Our research deepens the understanding of the reasoning mechanisms in Transformer models and
106   provides new perspectives for further enhancing their reasoning capability. The insights gained
107   from this study can contribute to the design of more efficient reasoning models and the exploration
      of reasoning mechanisms in general artificial intelligence systems.

180   3     B UFFER M ECHANISM AND V ERTICAL T HINKING S TRATEGY
181
182   In this section, we examine the mechanism by which the Transformer model employs a vertical
183   thinking strategy for multi-step reasoning. Through conducting causal intervention(Feng & Stein-
184   hardt, 2023; Meng et al., 2022; Vig et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2024a) (Appendix J), we discovered that
185   when the number of layers exceeds the number of reasoning steps, the Transformer’s logic circuit of
186   vertical thinking can be depicted in Fig. 3. The first layer facilitates the fusion of token information
187   at odd and even positions. In each subsequent layer, which we refer to as the reasoning layer, the
188   token at the last position attends to the token carrying the next reasoning result based on the existing
189
      reasoning result, thereby accomplishing one-step reasoning.
190
191   3.1   B UFFER M ECHANISM
192
      It is important to note the distinction between information transmission triggered by attention and
193
      that induced by residual connections. In the attention mechanism, information [a] with dimension
194
      dm is first mapped to a lower embedding dimension dv through the matrix W v , and then it is
195   projected back to dm dimensions via the matrix W o . Consequently, the information [a] after
196   passing through the attention layer becomes [a]W vo . In contrast, information transmitted through
197   residual connections does not require additional processing; thus, after the first layer, the information
198   at even indices transforms to [x2i-1 ]W vo(0) + [x2i ].

      The Chain of Thought (CoT) approach, as a representative of horizontal thinking strategy, has be-
372   come a prevalent mode of reasoning adopted by current large models, with numerous studies in-
373   dicating that CoT can significantly enhance logical reasoning capability (Wei et al., 2022; Kojima
374   et al., 2022). In this section, we will demonstrate that a Transformer model utilizing CoT can achieve
375   arbitrary multi-step reasoning with only two layers. We trained a 2-layer Transformer with the 13-
376   length single-step reasoning data. During the testing phase, we fed the model’s output back into the
377   model. Through this CoT process, the model can perform 2-step, 3-step, or even higher-step reason-
      ing and it can also generalize to sentence lengths beyond the 13th position. The specific information

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      flow can be found in Appendix G. Fig. 6 presents a schematic diagram illustrating how the model
379   implements this process. In the previously mentioned vertical thinking scenario, the model needed
380   to allocate new buffer W vo(1) , W vo(2) for storing the intermediate result [b] and [c], respectively.
381   In contrast, with CoT, the model generates a new token to separately store the new intermediate
382   information, effectively replacing the information [a] in the original buffer W vo(1) , W vo(0) and
383   I (identity matrix, which can also be treated as a buffer). Thus, CoT performs multi-step rea-
384   soning tasks through buffer reuse. Unlike the vertical thinking strategy, which requires alignment
385   across multiple weight matrices, the horizontal thinking strategy only requires a few layers to satisfy
386   Ker(l) ≈ I. This significantly reduces the difficulty of model training. This may also explain why

      5   M ETHOD FOR I MPROVING T RANSFORMER ’ S DATA -E FFICIENCY
415
416
      In this section, we discuss how to improve data efficiency when employing the vertical think-
417
      ing strategy, specifically, by enabling the model to learn the essence of logical reasoning
418   with less data or training epoch. As mentioned in Section 3, this can be achieved by setting
419   W vo(l−1),T W qk(l) W vo(0),T ≈ I.
420
421   A more intuitive approach is to replace W qk(l) and W vo(l) with W qk(l) +α(l) I and W vo(l) +β (l) I,
422   where {α(l) }L           (l) L
                    l=1 and {β }l=1 are learnable parameters. This Identity Matrix-Based Algorithm
423
      (denoted as IMBA) was first proposed in Boix-Adsera et al. (2023) and has been validated from
      both theoretical and empirical perspectives for its role in facilitating the model’s learning of one-step
424
      reasoning data. However, in multi-step reasoning tasks, IMBA may lead to information interference.
425
      For instance, if W vo(l) is replaced with W vo(l) + β (l) I, the storage representation of the two
426
      pieces of information transitions from [a]W vo +[b] to [a]W vo +(β (l) [a]+[b]), which introduces
427
      interference among the different pieces of information. Therefore, this approach may not be effective
428   in enhancing the model’s ability to perform multi-step reasoning.
429
430   Based on the understanding of buffer mechanism, we propose a Random Matrix-Based Algorithm
431   (RMBA), specifically by substituting W qk(l) and W vo(l) with W qk(l) + α(l) Z (l−1) and W vo(l) +
      β (l) Z (l) , where {α(l) }L         (l) L                                (l) L
                                 l=1 and {β }l=1 are learnable parameters and {Z }l=1 is a set of fixed

433   from [a]W vo +[b] to [a]W vo +[a]Z+[b], effectively creating a new buffer.

516   6   D ISCUSSION
517
518
      Conclusion. In this study, we investigated the buffer mechanism employed by Transformer models
519   when performing symbolic multi-step reasoning using the vertical thinking strategy and horizontal
520   thinking strategy. When utilizing the vertical thinking strategy, the model stores different interme-
521   diate results in separate buffers and transfers the reasoning results with same-token matching. In
522   contrast, when applying the horizontal thinking strategy, the model reuses the existing buffers to
523   store intermediate results, enabling reasoning over an arbitrary number of steps. We validated that
524   the buffer mechanism is a key factor in enabling the model’s ID and OOD generalization capabil-
525   ities. Based on the buffer mechanism, we proposed a tailored approach, RMBA, to enhance the
526   model’s multi-step reasoning ability, significantly improving data efficiency when training GPT-2
527
      on PrOntoQA datasets.
528   Limitations and Future Work. Our current work lacks in-depth theoretical analysis. In future
529   work, we aim to conduct deeper theoretical modeling for multi-step reasoning problems and extend
530   the buffer mechanism to other types of multi-step reasoning tasks.

      A    R ELATED W ORK
811
812
      Language model reasoning There have been numerous experimental and theoretical studies on
813   language model reasoning. Abbe et al. (2022) examines the reasoning capabilities of neural net-
814   works using the Pointer Value Retrieval (PVR) benchmark, which was originally introduced in
815   Zhang et al. (2021). Sharma et al. (2023) demonstrates that applying low-rank approximation of
816   the certain weight layers to themselves can enhance reasoning performance across various tasks,
817   and Chen et al. (2024a) explains this phenomenon in a two-layer Transformers model. Wang et al.
818   (2024a) investigate both ID and OOD reasoning abilities on two synthetic tasks involving compo-
819   sition and comparison. Jiang et al. (2024) reveals that the reasoning process in Large Language
820   Models (LLMs) is influenced by token bias and that these models continue to face challenges when
821
      dealing with more complex logical tasks. Abbe et al. (2024) introduces the distribution locality and
      shows Transformers require low locality. Zhang et al. (2024b); Luo et al. (2021); Zhou et al. (2022)
822
      shows that small initialization can facilitate model reasoning. Brinkmann et al. (2024) investigates
823
      the mechanism by which language models output intermediate reasoning paths in multi-step rea-
824   soning tasks. Aubry et al. (2024) uncover a transformer block coupling phenomenon in a variety of
825   LLMs by tracing the trajectories of individual tokens as they pass through transformer blocks.
826
827
      Recently, the multi-hop reasoning abilities of models have garnered attention in the LLM field. Kil
      et al. (2024); Li et al. (2024a) promote the use of Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning by models to
828
      perform multi-step thinking through appropriate prompt engineering. Zhang et al. (2023) propose
829
      a new retrieval framework for multiple reasoning paths. Dar et al. (2022); Li et al. (2024b); Yang
830   et al. (2024); Shalev et al. (2024) pinpoint where models execute multi-step reasoning by causal
831   intervention and observing neuron activation. Biran et al. (2024) introduce a method to enhance
832   models’ reasoning abilities by repeatedly invoking intermediate layers. These works, especially the
833   causal intervention experiments, have inspired our research. However, existing studies primarily
834   conduct macro-level statistical analyses on actual complex large language models. While causal
835   intervention methods can help us identify critical paths, our work builds upon this foundation by
836   further exploring how models leverage their own weights to generate these key paths. Experiments
837   on our symbolic datasets also facilitate more in-depth experimental and theoretical investigations.
838   Understanding the mechanism of neural model Our work builds upon previous studies on the
839   attention mechanism (Voita et al., 2019; Vig, 2019; Kovaleva et al., 2019; Kobayashi et al., 2020).
840   Numerous researchers have proposed various approaches to identify the roles of different heads in
841   Transformers (Vig et al., 2020; Jeoung & Diesner, 2022; Wang et al., 2022; Conmy et al., 2023;
842   Merullo et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2023; Wang & Weinan, 2024; Amsel et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024c;
843   Wang et al., 2024c). These methods predominantly employ the concept of perturbation. Similar to
844
      the observations made by Wang et al. (2023) and Dutta et al. (2024), who noted that large language
      models typically perform information aggregation in shallow layers and information induction in
845
      deeper layers, we have also observed comparable phenomena in our study. The idea of symbolic
846
      datasets is inspired by Poli et al. (2024); Zhang et al. (2024c). There have also been some insightful
847   theoretical works on feedforward neural networks. A series of studies have explored neural network
848   preferences and generalization from the perspectives of regularization and frequency, etc. (Xu et al.,
849   2019; Wang et al., 2024b; Jacot et al., 2018; 2020; Arora et al., 2019a; 2018). And Wu & Su (2023);
850   Wang & Wu; Arora et al. (2022); Li et al. (2021); Wu et al. (2018); Zhu et al. (2018); Arora et al.
851   (2019b) investigates the dynamical behavior of neural networks, while Ren et al. (2024) examines
852   the factors influencing neural network generalization.
853   In-context learning and induction head Our work primarily investigates the model’s ability to
854   perform in-context learning. The concept of in-context learning (ICL) was first introduced by Brown
855   et al. (2020). Since then, a series of studies have utilized induction heads to investigate ICL, yielding
856   remarkable research outcomes (Olsson et al., 2022; Garg et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022; Müller et al.,
857   2021; Goldowsky-Dill et al., 2023; Bietti et al., 2024; Nichani et al., 2024; Edelman et al., 2024;
858   Chen et al., 2024b; Todd et al., 2023; Chen & Zou, 2024). It is worth noting that induction heads
859   can be considered as a special case of multi-step reasoning tasks with reasoning step equals to 1.
860   However, multi-step reasoning is not a simple linear combination of single-step reasoning. In our
861
      work, we study the mechanism that enables multi-step reasoning, which has not been explored in
      previous studies.

1135
1136
       In this section, we investigate a natural question arising from Section 3: Can the model still perform
1137
       reasoning through the vertical thinking strategy when the number of reasoning steps required by the
1138   task exceeds the number of reasoning layers? Our experimental studies reveal that, in such cases,
1139   the model exhibits a parallel reasoning strategy. Based on these observations, we hypothesize that
1140   the upper bound of the model’s reasoning capability will grow exponentially with the depth of the
1141   model. We have conducted preliminary verification of this hypothesis using a simple program.
1142
       Possible Mechanism of Parallel Reasoning. Fig. 13(a) illustrates the information flow of a 4-layer
1143
       model completing 4-step reasoning. We observe that when the reasoning steps exceed or equal the
1144   number of model layers, the model performs reasoning parallelly in one layer. In the 2nd layer,
1145   different information undergoes two reasoning operations, enabling the subsequent three layers to
1146   achieve 4-step reasoning. Moreover, starting from the 2nd layer, the model’s reasoning approach
1147   transits from matching tokens’ values to matching tokens’ positions. Token value matching is only
1148   utilized in the 1st layer.

       We regard n tokens of a sequence as n nodes, each storing position and value information. The
1189   Transformer model follows the Information Propagation (Info-Prop) Rules (denoting the node
1190   transmitting information as i and the node receiving information as j, considering the existence of
1191   the attention mask, we require i < j):
1192
1193   • Rule 1: Odd positions can pass information to subsequent even positions, i.e., node i stores an
1194     odd number a in its position information, and node j stores a + 1 in its position information.
1195   • Rule 2: The position information stored in node i and node j has common elements.
1196
       • Rule 3: The value information stored in node i and node j has common elements.
1197
1198   If any of the above three rules are satisfied, we consider that the information from node i can be
1199   transmitted to node j. When information transmission occurs, the position and value information
1200   stored in node j will take the union of the corresponding information from node i and node j.
1201
       Pseudocode 1 provides a detailed process of the Info-Prop Rules. Each Node[i] is a dictionary
1202
       containing two attributes: pos (position) and val (value). Node[i][pos] and Node[i][val] are two
1203   sets. Initially, each set contains only its own information and will be updated according to the Info-
1204   Prop Rules. Fig. 13(b) shows the number of value information stored in the last token with respect
1205   to the number of iterations under our set of rules. It can be observed that the stored information
1206   exhibits an approximately exponential growth pattern with the number of iterations.

1569   buffer mechanism when performing symbolic multi-step reasoning tasks. We assume readers are

1571   3-layer Transformer model trained as described in Section 3.2.

       ing specific attention or residual paths. Fig. 23(left) illustrates the logical circuit of the 3-layer

       Transformer performing the symbolic 2-step reasoning task. Subsequently, we conducted causal

1596   tive information(Feng & Steinhardt, 2023; Meng et al., 2022; Vig et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2024a),
1597
       we refined the perturbation scope to target a specific buffer within a token. Specifically, we indi-

1598

1604   The results reveal that, in layer l, the information stored in buffer W vo(l−1) of the final token is
1605   crucial. Modifying information in other buffers does not affect the model’s output. Combined with
1606   the observation in Appendix D that the cosine similarities between W vo(0) , W vo(1) , W vo(2) , I are
1607   nearly zero, we can confidently assert that the model performs reasoning by leveraging different
