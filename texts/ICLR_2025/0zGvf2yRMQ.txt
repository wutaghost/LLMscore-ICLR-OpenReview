011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            In this paper, we present MeshGen, an advanced image-to-3D pipeline designed to
014            generate high-quality 3D objects with physically based rendering (PBR) textures.
015
               Existing methods struggle with issues such as poor auto-encoder performance,
               limited training datasets, misalignment between input images and 3D shapes, and
016
               inconsistent image-based PBR texturing. MeshGen addresses these limitations
017
               through several key innovations. First, we introduce a render-enhanced point-
018            to-shape auto-encoder that compresses 3D shapes into a compact latent space,
019            guided by perceptual loss. A 3D-native diffusion model is then established to di-
020            rectly learn the distribution of 3D shapes within this latent space. To mitigate
021            data scarcity and image-shape misalignment, we propose geometric alignment
022            augmentation and generative rendering augmentation, enhancing the diffusion
023            model’s controllability and generalization ability. Following shape generation,
024            MeshGen applies a reference attention-based multi-view ControlNet for image-
025            consistent appearance synthesis, complemented by a PBR decomposer to separate
026
               PBR channels. Extensive experiments demonstrate that MeshGen significantly
               enhances both shape and texture generation compared to previous methods.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   With the rapid advancement of diffusion-based image generation models, there has been signifi-
032   cant progress in automatic 3D generation. In particular, methods utilizing score distillation sam-
033   pling (Poole et al., 2023) have demonstrated breakthroughs by leveraging priors from text-to-image
034   diffusion models. However, these optimization-based methods are relatively slow and face chal-
035   lenges such as mode collapse (Wang et al., 2023a;c) and the Janus problem (Armandpour et al.,
036   2023; Seo et al., 2023) due to the lack of inherent 3D information. Subsequent strategies address
037   these challenges by focusing on multi-view generation (Liu et al., 2023c; Long et al., 2023; Chen
038   et al., 2024c; Voleti et al., 2024) and large reconstruction models (Zou et al., 2023; Tang et al.,
039
      2024a; Hong et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023a; Xu et al., 2024b; Liu et al., 2024; Wei et al., 2024). The
      former generates multi-view images for 3D reconstruction. The latter maps sparse view images to
040
      compact 3D representations using neural networks, such as triplane NeRF (Chan et al., 2021) or
041
      grid 3D Gaussians (Zou et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2024a). While these methods have improved the
042   quality and speed of 3D generation, they typically use volumetric representations such as NeRF or
043   Gaussian instead of 3D meshes, resulting in further loss of quality during conversion (Hong et al.,
044   2023; Chen et al., 2024b; Tang et al., 2023). Moreover, these methods, which rely solely on render
045   loss for supervision, are highly susceptible to inconsistencies across multiple synthesized views and
046   often struggle to reconstruct objects with complex geometric structures (Sun et al., 2024).
047
      Recently, 3D native diffusion methods have garnered significant attention as a promising paradigm
048   towards mesh-oriented generation (Gupta et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023b; Zhang et al., 2024a; Li
049   et al., 2024b; Wu et al., 2024b; Hong et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024a). By mapping 3D meshes
050   into a compact latent space using 3D auto-encoders, these methods directly learn the distribution
051   of 3D shapes instead of reconstructing from generated multi-views. Despite considerable progress
052   has been made, several challenges remain unresolved. Firstly, the inherent limitations of current
053   3D auto-encoders preclude the integration of perceptual loss during training, leading to less detailed
      reconstructed meshes and consequently constrained expressiveness of the latent space. Moreover,


                                                        1

054
      existing 3D native diffusion methods typically generate simple and symmetric shapes, making it
055   challenging to match the input images, and the scarcity and poor quality of public datasets further
056   limit the generalization ability of current open-source models. In addition, existing image-guided
057   texture generation methods struggle to produce appearances consistent with the original images and
058   can only generate materials with light baked in, rather than the physically based rendering (PBR)
059   materials required in practical applications.
060
      In response to these challenges, we introduce MeshGen, a novel image-to-3D pipeline specially
061   designed to generate PBR textured meshes that closely resemble the provided image in both geom-
062   etry and appearance. Specifically, to enhance the expressiveness of the point-to-shape auto-encoder,
063   we propose a triplane-based auto-encoder that incorporates perceptual render loss during training,
064   thereby fully exploiting the memory efficiency of triplane compared to latent vector set represen-
065   tation. Next, based on the geometrical covariant property of the point-to-shape auto-encoder and
066   the appearance-invariant nature of image-to-shape diffusion, we establish an image-to-shape dif-
067   fusion model with geometric alignment and generative rendering augmentation to enhance image-
068   shape consistency and generalization ability. For texture generation, we propose using a geometry-
069
      conditioned ControlNet with reference attention fine-tuning to generate multi-view images consis-
      tent with the input image in both appearance and lightning. We then employ a PBR decomposer to
070
      estimate the PBR components in the shaded image and a texture inpainter to fill in the invisible parts.
071
      As a result of these advancements, MeshGen can generate PBR textured 3D assets with consistent
072   geometry and exceptional fidelity within 30 seconds.
073
074   To summarize, our contributions are:
075
             • We propose the MeshGen auto-encoder, which substantially improves the expressiveness
076            of the point-to-shape auto-encoder by incorporating both geometric and appearance super-
077            vision. It utilizes a coarse-to-fine optimization strategy guided by render-based perceptual
078            loss, ensuring more accurate shape representation.
079
             • We introduce a novel image-to-shape pipeline with our proposed geometric alignment
080            augmentation and generative rendering augmentation, which largely enhance image-shape
081            alignment and generalization capabilities.
082
             • We design a reference attention-based image-conditioned mesh texturing pipeline. Cou-
083
               pled with our proposed PBR decomposer, our method is capable of generating relightable
084            textures that closely align with the appearance of the input image.

093   in various 3D generation tasks (Wang et al., 2023c; Chen et al., 2023b; Lin et al., 2023; Tang et al.,
094   2023; Chen et al., 2024b; Shi et al., 2023b; Li et al., 2023c; Wang & Shi, 2023; Sun et al., 2023; Chen
095   et al., 2023c). Despite great success has been achieved, optimization-based methods still suffer from
096   slow generation speed and low success rates. To overcome these challenges, researchers have ex-
097   plored multi-view generation (Liu et al., 2023b; Tang et al., 2024b; Lu et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023c;
098   Long et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024a; Li et al., 2024a; Chen et al., 2024c; Voleti et al., 2024) and large

100

139   2.2       T EXTURE G ENERATION
140
141   Initial efforts in mesh texturing have focused on only utilizing image diffusion models through itera-
142   tive inpainting and optimization (Chen et al., 2023a; Jiang et al., 2024). TEXTure (Richardson et al.,
143   2023) presents an iterative texturing method that employs a pre-trained depth-to-image diffusion
144
      model to progressively refine a 3D model’s texture map from various views. TexFusion (Cao et al.,
      2023) enhances coherence by integrating texture information from multiple perspectives during the
145
      denoising stage. SyncMVD (Liu et al., 2023d) improves multi-view consistency by denoising in UV
146
      space and exploiting a self-attention reuse technique. FlashTex (Deng et al., 2024) proposes a light
147   ControlNet for text-to-PBR generation. In addition to methods that use only image diffusion, vari-
148   ous learning-based strategies initiate the training of generative texturing models using 3D textured
149   mesh data (Nichol et al., 2022; Luo et al., 2023; Jun & Nichol, 2023; Li et al., 2022; Collins et al.,
150   2022; Deitke et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2021b; Cheng et al., 2023). Texturify (Siddiqui
151   et al., 2022) proposes a GAN-based pipeline with face convolution to colorize meshes without di-
152   rect supervision. Point-UV (Yu et al., 2023) proposes a point diffusion to offer low-resolution global
153   information and a UV diffusion for enhancing finer details. Paint3D (Zeng et al., 2023) proposes a
154   coarse-to-fine strategy that firstly colorizes sparse views with depth-based inpainting and then im-
155
      proves texture quality within the UV space. Meta 3D TextureGen (Bensadoun et al., 2024) exploits
      a geometry-conditioned multi-view generator for text-to-texture generation.

166   3.1   R ENDER - ENHANCED AUTO - ENCODER
167
168   Transformer-based point-to-shape auto-encoder. To compress the discrete 3D meshes into a
169   continuous latent space, we adopted the same encoder as used in prior native 3D generation ap-
170   proaches (Zhang et al., 2023a; 2024a; Li et al., 2024b), namely the point-to-shape encoder. For a
171   given 3D object, we first uniformly sample NP points from its surface. Following previous methods,
172   we encode the sampled point cloud using Fourier positional encoding (Rahaman et al., 2019). Sub-
173
      sequently, a set of learnable queries is introduced to extract information from the point cloud through
      cross-attention, followed by a series of self-attentions to enhance the obtained representation. The
174
      complete encoding process can be formulated as
175
176                         z = SelfAttnn (CrossAttn(Q, FourierPE(P ))),                                 (1)
177   where n refers to the number of self-attention layers, SelfAttn, CrossAttn and FourierPE
178   represents self-, cross-attention, and Fourier positional encoding. Here Q ∈ RNz ×dz and P ∈
179   RNP ×3 represent the learnable query set and the sampled point cloud respectively, Nz and dz refer to
180   the number of learnable queries and the dimension of the latent space. To incorporate render-based
181   perceptual loss during auto-encoder training, we choose triplane as the latent representation (Wu
182   et al., 2024b) instead of the latent vector set used in 3DShape2Vecset (Zhang et al., 2023a). This
183   choice is motivated by the fact that when querying the occupancy, the latent vector set requires
184
      cross-attention with all latents, whereas the triplane only needs to pass through an MLP decoder,
      thus supporting surface extraction at a higher resolution. To obtain the occupancy of a specific
185
      point, a convolutional decoder is applied to upsample the encoded latent to a higher resolution to
186
      represent finer details. As analyzed in (Wang et al., 2023b; Wu et al., 2024b), we concatenate the
187   three planes in the height dimension instead of the channel dimension to avoid artifacts caused by
188   spatial misalignment. The occupancy of point x can be formulated as
189
                                 Occupancy(x) = MLP(UpSample(ztile ), x),                                (2)
190
191   where Upsample denotes the convolution-based upsampling network, MLP refers to the occupancy
192   decoder, ztile represents the height-concatenated triplane. As suggested in Odena et al. (2016), we
193   use interpolation with convolution instead of deconvolution.
194   Perceptual loss with ray-based regularization. Previous point-to-shape auto-encoder relies solely
195   on occupancy loss, the absence of perceptual loss leads to poor performance when reconstructing
196   high-frequency details. In response, we propose supervising the auto-encoder using the rendered
197   normal map. During training, we query the occupancy of a 2563 grid and extract iso-surface dif-
198   ferentiably (Wei et al., 2023). To compute the render loss, we exploit nvdiffrast (Laine et al., 2020)
199   to differentiably rasterize the normal map. However, we found in our early experiment that simply
200   applying render loss alone will cause severe floaters in the final output mesh (see Fig. 8), which
201
      can also be observed in previous research (Wei et al., 2024). To address this issue, we propose a
      ray-based occupancy regularization that forces the occupancy in empty spaces to approach zero. As
202
      shown in the left part of Fig. 2, for each camera ray, we uniformly sample Ns points between the
203
      ray-bounding box intersection and the surface point, enforcing their occupancy to be near zero by
204   minimizing the sum of their occupancy. To save GPU VRAM and accelerate training, we interpo-
205   late the occupancy of the samples from the values used for previous surface extraction, rather than
206   querying the triplane.
207
      Coarse-to-fine optimization. Due to the locality of differentiable marching cubes, the gradients
208
      of the render loss can only propagate to points near surface vertices. Therefore, a coarse-to-fine
209
      training process is required to ensure the effectiveness of the render loss. Specifically, during the
210   coarse stage, we apply the standard binary cross-entropy (BCE) loss for the point-to-shape auto-
211   encoder, along with a KL loss to regularize the latent space and a total variation loss (Yu et al.,
212   2021a) for reducing the floaters, i.e.

236   3.2    I MAGE - TO - SHAPE DIFFUSION MODEL WITH GENERATIVE DATA AUGMENTATION
237
238
      As shown in Fig. 4 and Fig. 5, compared to large reconstruction models, existing native 3D gener-
      ation models almost always tend to generate symmetrical shapes that lack detail, leading to image-
239
      shape misalignment. We believe this phenomenon arises because the training of existing models
240
      predominantly relies on the Objaverse dataset (Deitke et al., 2022), and a considerable portion of the
241   objects in Objaverse have symmetrical geometry and lack realistic textures. Therefore, the diffusion
242   models trained on it tend to replicate simple geometries and struggle to generalize to images with
243   complex textures or lighting. To optimize a diffusion model with strong generalization capabilities
244   on limited data, we identify two key differences between the proposed pipeline and the previous
245   NeRF-based native 3D generation pipeline (typical methods include Rodin (Wang et al., 2023b),
246   3DTopia (Hong et al., 2024), etc): (1) Geometrical covariant auto-encoder. Previous NeRF-based
247   native 3D generation methods utilize a per-object optimized neural radiance field as the latent rep-
248   resentation. This approach requires pre-computing and storing numerous latent vectors and lacks
249
      geometric covariance, as it necessitates re-optimizing the radiance field to obtain triplane latent
      variables after applying a transformation to the object. In contrast, our point-to-shape auto-encoder
250
      takes point clouds as input and does not require per-object optimization, it naturally achieves ge-
251
      ometric covariance for transformations such as rotations by directly manipulating the point cloud.
252   (2) Appearance invariant image-to-shape modeling. Previous methods learn to generate textured
253   meshes from images, resulting in the entanglement of input images and the textures of the output
254   meshes. In contrast, our diffusion model is specifically designed to map images to shapes, ensuring
255   that the same shapes produce consistent renderings, regardless of variations in textures or lighting
256   conditions. Based on both insights, we propose two data augmentations that are critical for training
257   the image-to-shape model.
258   Geometric alignment augmentation. To enhance image-shape correspondence during training, we
259   propose utilizing the geometric covariance property of our point-to-shape auto-encoder to ensure
260   that different views of the same object correspond to different latents. Specifically, for each object
261   in the dataset, we select one view from multi-view images as the condition and rotate the point
262   cloud’s azimuth to align the object’s orientation with the selected image as the target (see the middle
263   part of Fig. 2 for a simple demonstration). The aligned image-shape pairs are then used as training
264   data for the diffusion model. Our experiments reveal that geometric alignment not only expands the
265   training dataset but also significantly improves the alignment between generated shapes and images.
266
      We present the corresponding ablation study in Fig. 7.
267   Generative rendering augmentation. To enhance the generalization ability of the image-to-shape
268   diffusion, we propose leveraging the appearance-invariant property by utilizing generative rendering
269   to synthesize images with realistic textures and rich lighting based on the geometry of the object.
      Concretely, for each rendered image in the dataset, we utilize the corresponding normal map and


                                                            5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      depth map as control signals to synthesize realistic renderings with ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023b).
271   To ensure that the augmented images do not deviate significantly from the originals, we inject the
272   original image using an IP-adapter (Ye et al., 2023). We then use IC-light (Zhang et al., 2024b) to
273   generate renderings under various lighting conditions and directions. Experiments show that gener-
274   ative rendering augmentation is highly beneficial for helping diffusion models understand lighting
275   effects and generalize to real-world images (see Fig. 7 for the corresponding ablation study).
276
      Image-to-3D diffusion UNet. We adopt a UNet similar to Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022)
277   as the image-to-shape diffusion network. Following Rodin (Wang et al., 2023b), we concatenate
278   triplanes along the height dimension as input to the UNet to avoid spatial mismatches. Interactions
279   between different planes are handled via self-attention layers. To incorporate image information
280   during diffusion, we encode the input image using DINOv2 (Oquab et al., 2024) and inject the
281   extracted features into the denoising process through cross-attention. Following SD3 (Esser et al.,
282   2024), we adopt rectified flow Liu et al. (2022) with lognorm timestep sampling as the training
283   schedule. For more details on the training and inference of our diffusion UNet, please refer to
284   appendix A.

529   5                                           C ONCLUSION
530
531   In this paper, we propose MeshGen, a novel pipeline for generating delicate PBR textured mesh
532   given a single image. MeshGen encodes 3D meshes to compact latent space with a render-enhanced
533   auto-encoder. Based on our in-depth analysis of point-to-shape auto-encoder and image-to-shape
534   diffusion, we propose to train the diffusion model with geometric alignment and generative rendering
535   augmentation to address the issues of image-shape misalignment and poor generalization ability.
536   Besides, to generate PBR texture consistent with the image, we establish a reference attention-based
537
      multi-view generator followed by a PBR decomposer to obtain PBR components and a UV-space
      inpainter to fill the invisible part. Extensive experiments have demonstrated the effectiveness of our
538
      method. We hope our work will aid in a deeper understanding of native 3D diffusion and provide
539
      support for future related research.

      A.2     I MAGE - TO - SHAPE DIFFUSION MODEL
944
945   A.2.1    DATA AUGMENTATION
946
947   In generative rendering data augmentation, to enhance the similarity between the generated images
948   and the original image, in addition to using the normal depth ControlNet and IP-adapter, we set the
949   initial noise to the latent of the original image with maximum noise added. For relighting diffusion,
950
      we used IC-light (Zhang et al., 2024b). Specifically, during data augmentation, we randomly select
      one lighting direction from the pre-defined light initial latent in IC-light (i.e., uniformly select from
951
      left, right, top, and bottom), and choose one lighting condition from a set of predefined light prompts.
952
953
      A.2.2    I MAGE - TO - SHAPE DIFFUSION MODEL
954
955   Our diffusion UNet takes in the noised triplane latent and exploits 8 ResNet blocks with spatial
956   self-attention as the encoder and a symmetric architecture as the decoder. We exploit DINOv2-
957   G (Oquab et al., 2024) to encode the input image and inject the extracted feature to the diffusion
958   UNet using cross-attention. For the diffusion schedule, we follow SD3 to use the simple yet effective
959   rectified flow Liu et al. (2022) with timesteps sampled from a standard logit-normal distribution. We
960   train the image-to-shape diffusion model with our proposed augmentations on a filtered subset of
961
      GObjaverse (Qiu et al., 2024), which consists of about 120k high-quality multiview-mesh pairs.
      To handle input images with different elevations, since the meshes in Objaverse are aligned in the
962
      gravity axis, we force the diffusion to generate meshes with absolute elevation equal to zero. We
963
      experimentally found that this conditioning method works better than generating meshes with a
964   rotation in elevation, as suggested in Chen et al. (2024c). We train the diffusion UNet of 16 NVIDIA
965   A800 GPUs using bf16 precision with an effective batch size equal to 1536. The whole training lasts
966   for about 18 days.

985    Geometry-conditioned ControlNet training. To ensure the model perceives precise depth and
986    positional information, we did not transform depth to normalized disparity as done in the original
987    depth ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023b); instead, we performed a unified multi-view normalization
988
       based on camera distance and object bounding box. Specifically, the depth map is processed as
       Dnormalized = D−bias
                       scale , where bias equals to camera distance minus the length of the diagonal of
989
       the bounding box (i.e. the minimal possible depth value) and the scale equals to the length of the
990
       diagonal of the bounding box.
991
992    Multi-view target back-projection. As detailed in the main text, the obtained multi-view PBR
993    components are merged in UV space using back-projection with softmax. We apply a softmax
994
       operation with a temperature of 0.1 to ensure consistent textures. However, images generated by
       ControlNet sometimes extend beyond object boundaries, causing some pixels to be back-projected
995
       onto surfaces behind them, leading to artifacts. To address this, we propose a simple depth filtering
996
       technique. For each view, we identify locations in the depth map where sudden changes occur and
997    exclude these pixels during back-projection. Our experiments demonstrate that this approach effec-
998    tively reduces artifacts, and the color values of the corresponding surface points can be supplemented
999    by other views, as shown in the middle of Fig. 8.
1000
       UV space inpainting. Our UV space inpainter is a multi-channel ControlNet trained on top of the
1001
       LoRA fine-tuned diffusion model. The input to our inpainting model is a 9-channel image: the
1002
       first three channels represent the normal map in UV space, the middle three channels represent the
1003   position map, and the last three channels contain the masked texture map, with pixel values set to -1
1004   in regions that requires inpainting. During inference, we follow ControlNet inpainting (Zhang et al.,
1005   2023b), applying masking in the latent space to maintain consistency in areas that do not require
1006   inpainting.
