                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               Self-play has emerged as a promising approach for generating alignment data to
012            reduce the annotation costs during the alignment process. By introducing specific
013            game rules and utilizes the model’s own language capabilities to generate data
014            samples, self-play has achieved promising results. However, traditional self-play
015            methods face two major challenges: insufficient data diversity during self-iterative
016            training and difficulties in reward signal design. To solve these problems, this pa-
017            per introduces G AME I NSTRUCT, a complex multi-player adversarial environment
018            that increases the complexity of self-play generated data during self-iterative train-
019            ing. Specifically, we employ the “Chameleon Game”, where interactions between
020
               multiple players raise the diversity of the generated data, improving the model’s
               reasoning abilities, Additionally, we further propose a dynamic reward algorithm
021
               to capture signals within player conversations during the whole game. Experimen-
022
               tal results show that compared to existing self-play methods, G AME I NSTRUCT
023            achieves significant improvements on the HuggingFace Open-LLM-Leaderboard
024            reasoning benchmark while demonstrating continuous improvement and increas-
025            ing data diversity during self-iterative training.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Alignment aims to ensure the model responds according to the user’s goals and needs, which is
031   primarily achieved through learning from human-annotated instruction data and preference data(Ji
032   et al., 2024). However, the high cost of human annotated data hinders the way to develop stronger
033
      LLMs efficiently. Additionally, with the continuous development of strong LLMs such as GPT-4
      (OpenAI, 2023) and Gemini (Gemini, 2024), human face challenges when supervising responses of
034
      LLMs to keep in consistency with their preferences (Burns et al., 2023).
035
036   To solve above problems, self-play (Cheng et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024) emerges as a particu-
037   larly promising approach to generate alignment data. Self-play usually utilizes the intrinsic lan-
038   guage capability of LLMs by introducing specific game rules and generate relevant samples to im-
039
      prove LLMs. Numerous self-play methods have been proposed these days, among which generator-
      discriminator type (Cao et al., 2024) has been widely studied. SPIN (Chen et al., 2024) introduces
040
      game from the idea of GAN (Goodfellow et al., 2014), where discriminators distinguish LLMs’
041
      responses from the golden while generators generate responses as close to the golden as possible.
042   Another line of works is attacker-defender type, where attacker and defender share inverse objective.
043   SPAG (Cheng et al., 2024) utilizes Adversarial Taboo (Yao et al., 2020), where attackers trick the
044   defenders to answer specific taboo word while defenders guess the taboo word. Both of these works
045   have achieved satisfactory outcomes.
046
      However, traditional self-play methods face two major challenges: poor diversity during self-
047
      iterative training and the difficulty of reward signal design. The first challenge arises when models
048   overly rely on self-generated data, which often lacks diversity and complexity. This lack of diversity
049   and complexity limits the model’s ability to explore broader data patterns. As noted by Shumailov
050   et al. (2024b), this reliance can lead LLMs to drift from the original data distribution, eventually
051   resulting in model collapse. The second challenge lies in the design of effective reward signals.
052   Traditional methods typically focus on game outcomes rather than leveraging the rich informa-
053   tion embedded in conversations to guide the training process. This is especially important in large
      models, where focusing solely on final outcomes can cause LLMs to overlook critical intermediate

      To address this issue, this paper introduces G AME I NSTURCT, a complex multi-player adversarial
080
      environment that increases the complexity of self-play generated data during training. The core idea
081
      of G AME I NSTURCT is to use multi-player interactions and a diversified data generation process to
082   prevent the model from getting stuck in repetitive self-play, reducing the risk of model collapse
083   (Shumailov et al., 2024b). Specifically, we utilize the “Chameleon Game” (Xu et al., 2023), as de-
084   scribed in Fig 1, where civilians are given the same word and attempt to identify the chameleon,
085   while the chameleon avoids being identified. This game requires strong skills of expression, de-
086   ception, and reasoning, and the ease of collecting numerous assigned words enables a wide range of
087   topics, ensuring the diversity of the generated data. Besides, G AME I NSTRUCT introduces a dynamic
088   reward learning algorithm to capture signals within player conversations, incorporating interactions
089   between players into reinforcement learning. This allows the model to receive continuous and tar-
090
      geted feedback throughout the entire game, optimizing performance progressively and avoiding the
      limitations of relying solely on final outcomes.
091
092   To verify the effectiveness of G AME I NSTURCT, we conduct experiments on multiple open-source
093   LLMs across different training iterations and sampling temperatures. The experimental results
094   demonstrate a significant improvement over state-of-the-art self-play methods on the reasoning
095   benchmark, HuggingFace Open-LLM-Leaderboard (Beeching et al., 2023). Furthermore, our exper-
096
      iments indicate that G AME I NSTRUCT reduces the risk of model collapse and maintains continuous
      improvement, with increased data diversity during the self-iterative training.
097
098   Generally, our contribution can be summarized as follows:
099
100   • We introduce a training method called G AME I NSTURCT. By introducing a complex multi-player
101
        environment, G AME I NSTURCT enhances LLM interactions and generates more diverse data, re-
        sulting in significant improvement in their reasoning capabilities.
102
103   • We propose a reward assigning method named dynamic reward. By assigning reward based on the
104     descriptions of each players, dynamic reward better captures signals within player conversations,
105
        further improving the reasoning abilities of LLMs.
106   • We verify the effectiveness and robustness of G AME I NSTURCT, showing improvement in rea-
107     soning and a continuous improvement along during self-iterative learning in comparison with the
        state-of-the-art self-play methods.

      3.1   C HAMELEON G AME I NTRODUCTION
151
152   The Chameleon game applied in LLMs is first introduced by Xu et al. (2023), in which civilians and
153   chameleons involve an multi-turn interaction. The game consists of multiple players, divided into
154   civilians and chameleons, where civilians share a target word, and the chameleons are assigned a
155   different word. Each round players are asked to describe the assigned word. After two rounds of
156   description, all players are required to vote. If the chameleons receive more votes, the civilians win,
157   otherwise the chameleon wins. The example of Chameleon game is in Figure 1.
158   The goal of chameleons is to avoid being voted out, where they should pay attention to civilians’ de-
159   scription related to the word and deduce their assigned word or any topics related to it and describe.
160   In contrast, civilians are required to identify their own teammate and vote out the chameleon. Thus,
161   civilians need to introduce their assigned words as exact as possible to find other civilian but bring
      less information to the actual chameleon as possible.

      3.2      C HAMELEON G AME M ODELING
163
164   We identify the Chameleon Game as a multi-player zero-sum Markov game, which can be described
165   by (S, A, F, R) 1 :
166
167   • State space: S = {st , s′t , s′′t | 1 ≤ t ≤ T } indicates the history conversation of
168
        Chameleon Game, which contains three types of states. st = (w, u1 , v1 , u′1 , u2 , . . . , ut ),
        s′t = (w, u1 , v1 , u′1 , u2 , . . . , ut , vt ) and s′′t = (w, u1 , v1 , u′1 , u2 , . . . , ut , vt , u′t ) where ut , u′t
169
        and vt are the utterances of civilians and chameleon, respectively2 . States st , s′t and s′′t end with
170
        utterances ut , vt and u′t from civilians and chameleons, respectively.
171
      • Action: A marks the possible action space for LLMs to choose, which is equivalent to the possible
172
        token sequence space of specific LLMs, indicating the generation process of LLMs.
173
174
      • Transition Function F : S ∗ A = S deterministically appends the utterance ui , u′i or vi at the end
        of the dialogue, and converts s′t = F (st , vt ), forming a new game history.
175
176   • Reward r : S ×A → R evaluates actions based on their corresponding states with rewards r(s, v),
177     r(s′ , u′ ) and r(s′′ , u) , respectively. Specifically, R marks the reward of each generation process
178
        of LLMs based on the game history.
179   In this game, we denote u, u′ and v as the civilian’s policy and the chameleon’s policy. Each game
180   episode can be regarded with the following probability:

246   3.4       DYNAMIC R EWARD A SSIGNING
247
248   To better capture the information between conversations of players, we propose to use dynamic
249
      reward during reinforcement learning by applying the policy model as judge to assign reward to
      the conversation between players. By assigning each description with a certain reward, information
250
      contained in the conversation is better captured compared with splitting the global reward to each
251
      sentence with regard to the conversation turns (Cheng et al., 2024).
252
253   The commonly employed method of global reward allocation, which relies on the outcome of each
254   episodic, fails to effectively capture reward signals across dialogues (Cheng et al., 2024), since such
255
      an approach is grounded in a strong assumption that rewards between each dialogue follow a zero-
      sum pattern. However, the zero-sum nature of the overall game does not necessarily imply such
256
      a conclusion of each conversation turns. Adopting this method directly may result in the loss of
257
      important reward signals during intermediate stages.
258
259   For each state, the chameleon reward and civilian reward in the total game should be summed to
260   zero, so that the game is zero-sum:
