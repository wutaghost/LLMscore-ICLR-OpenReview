                                                   A BSTRACT
013
014            Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) have demonstrated promising perfor-
015            mance in chest X-ray (CXR) analysis. To enhance human-computer interac-
016            tion, several studies have incorporated radiologists’ eye gaze, typically through
017            heatmaps or textual prompts. However, these methods often overlook the sequen-
018            tial order of eye movements, which could provide valuable insights by highlight-
019            ing both the areas of interest and the order in which they are examined. In this
020            work, we propose a novel approach called RadEyeVideo that integrates radiol-
021            ogists’ eye-fixation data as a video sequence, capturing both the temporal and
022
               spatial dynamics of their gaze. The video, featuring a red gaze point overlaid
               on CXR images, emphasizes regions of focused attention during interpretation.
023
               We evaluate this method in CXR report generation and disease diagnosis using
024
               three general-domain, open-source LVLMs with a video input capabilities. When
025            prompted with eye-gaze videos, model performance improves by up to 25.4% on
026            Impression generation task and on average 7.9% for all tasks using scaled evalu-
027            ation metrics. Our approach enhanced open-domain LVLM models, when com-
028            bined with exemplar reports for in-context learning, outperform medical models
029            as well as those specifically trained for CXR report generation on the benchmark
030            dataset. This work highlights that domain expert’s knowledge (eye-gaze infor-
031            mation in this case), when effectively integrated with LVLMs, can significantly
032            enhance general-domain models’ capabilities in clinical tasks, pointing out a new
033
               effective approach of utilising LVLMs in healthcare and beyond.
034
035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) have emerged as promising solutions for automating tasks
038   in chest X-ray (CXR) analysis, including the generation of radiology reports, visual question answer-
039   ing, and error detection within medical reports (Bannur et al., 2024; Saab et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024;
040   Wu et al., 2023; 2024a). These models offer the potential to streamline clinical workflows, provid-
041   ing radiologists with fast, automated insights that enhance decision-making and overall diagnostic
042   efficiency.
043   However, despite these successes, the reliability of LVLMs in real-world clinical environments re-
044   mains a challenge. A key limitation is the variability and accuracy of the outputs generated by these
045   models (Xiao et al., 2024; AlSaad et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024a; Wu et al., 2024b). A promis-
046   ing solution is to incorporate human expertise through human-computer interaction. Several studies
047   have shown that integrating human input with AI models can significantly improve both accuracy
048   and reliability, often exceeding the performance of radiologists and AI models when they work
049   independently (Calisto et al., 2022; Patel et al., 2019).
050   One effective way to incorporate human expertise is through the use of radiologists’ eye-tracking
051   data collected while reviewing images. Studies have demonstrated that integrating eye-gaze infor-
052   mation into AI models enhances diagnostic accuracy by providing insights into the areas radiologists
053   focus on during image interpretation (Wang et al., 2022; Ma et al., 2023; Ji et al., 2023; Zhao et al.,
      2024b; Wang et al., 2024).

090   To address this gap, we propose a novel prompting approach, RadEyeVideo, that integrates eye-
091   tracking data as a video, capturing both the temporal and spatial order of eye movements, also known
092   as scan paths. This richer representation preserves the dynamic process of how radiologists navigate
093   and prioritize different regions in an image. By incorporating this sequential flow, our approach
094   offers deeper insights into the decision-making process, highlighting both the areas of interest and
095   the order in which they are examined. Since radiologists often follow a structured approach during
096   interpretation, capturing this sequence provides critical context for the model’s understanding.
097   Figure 1 shows how our approach distinguishes itself from previous prompting methods for LVLMs
098   in CXR analysis. The heatmap serves as a simplified snapshot of gaze movements, which fails
099   to convey any information about their sequential order (Kim et al., 2024a). Although the textual
100   prompt can contain some sequential temporal information regarding fixations, existing methods do
101   not fully leverage this capability; instead, gaze data is typically organized by duration, limiting its
102   representation of sequential order (Kim et al., 2024b). Although the textual format has the potential
103   to convey a correct sequence, when ordered, it ultimately represents all spatial and temporal infor-
104
      mation in plain text. The X and Y coordinates, represented in relative width and height, offer only
      an indirect representation. In contrast, RadEyeVideo captures the dynamic nature of gaze patterns,
105
      providing a more comprehensive understanding of radiologist behavior during image interpretation.

134   2     R AD E YE V IDEO - DYNAMIC E YE G AZE V IDEO P ROMPTING
135
136   We introduce RadEyeVideo, a dynamic eye-gaze video prompting technique, designed to incorpo-
137   rate radiologists’ eye-gaze patterns into LVLMs for chest X-ray report generation and diagnosis. The
138   key motivation behind this method is to enhance the interpretative capabilities of general-purpose
139   LVLMs by leveraging human perceptual cues, such as eye-tracking data. The rationale for incorpo-
140   rating both spatial and temporal aspects of radiologists’ gaze patterns is based on the understanding
141   that expert radiologists do not analyze medical images in a static manner; rather, they focus on
142   diagnostically relevant regions over time. Capturing these dynamics allows the model to gain in-
143   sights from the way experts interact with medical images, thus guiding it toward better diagnostic
144
      decisions.

      2.1   W HY E YE -G AZE DATA ?
147
      Radiologists demonstrate high proficiency in interpreting CXRs due to their ability to efficiently
148   scan the image and focus on areas that are clinically significant. Eye-tracking data captures these
149   visual search patterns and provides a rich source of information, reflecting the expert’s decision-
150   making process. RadEyeVideo translates these patterns into video-based prompts, which combine
151   spatial attention (where radiologists look) with temporal sequencing (how long and when they look
152   at certain regions), creating a multi-modal input that better aligns with clinical reasoning.

      3.3     E VALUATION
325
326
      In addition to using eye-tracking data as video sequences, we explore other methods of integrating
327   eye-gaze information into LVLMs for CXR report analysis, including heatmaps and textual prompts.
328   We systematically evaluate these methods to determine which most effectively enhances report gen-
329   eration, focusing on accuracy, completeness, and clinical relevance.
330
      In implementing heatmaps, we overlay red dots on the CXR images to visualize spatial patterns of
331
      eye gaze information. These dots indicate the areas of focus, with darker dots representing longer
332
      gaze durations, thereby incorporating a temporal dimension to the data. For our textual prompt ap-
333   proach, we maintain the original sequence of gaze information rather than organizing it by duration.
334   This approach still has a limitation, indirect representation of the spatial representation. The relative
335   position of the gaze in the text has to be used to infer the gaze point on the image, and LVLMs
336   capability of doing this remains unexplored. Similar to the video prompting method, we filter the
337   gaze data to retain only those fixations that exceed the average duration, ensuring that our analyses
338   focus on significant eye-gaze interactions.
339

      3.3.1    E VALUATION H YPERPARAMETERS
342
      For evaluation, we implemented a zero-shot approach for both sections, a batch size of 1, and a
343
      temperature parameter of 0. A temperature of 0 was chosen to minimize the randomness in the
344
      generated text produced by the model. The maximum length of the model’s responses for each task
345   was determined based on the mean token length of the ground truth response: 96 for the findings
346   section and 32 for the impression section. For the diagnosis, we wanted the model to generate a list
347   of possible diagnosis, so the maximum length was set as 192. This setup ensured consistent and
348   efficient experimentation across both tasks.

409   The evaluation results in Table 3 offer a comprehensive overview of performance across two sec-
410   tions: Findings and Impression, evaluated for both the alpha and beta splits. Overall, general domain
411
      models when eye gaze information is provided regardless of the method consistently outperform the
      NoEye prompting method, achieving higher average scores except for the LLaVA-OneVision model
412
      with the fixation text prompting.
413
414   RadEyeVideo Prompting as the Optimal Method
415   Our proposed method, RadEyeVideo, emerges as the most robust and effective method. Across all
416   three general domain models—LongVA, VideoLLaMA2, and LLaVA-OneVision—video prompt-
417
      ing consistently outperforms the NoEye method, demonstrating reliable performance improvements.
      The only exception is VideoLLaMA2 for the diagnosis task where RadEyeVideo only showed a de-
418
      crease in performance. While heat map increased 1.6% and fixation text increased 1.8% on average,
419
      our RadEyeVideo increased 7.9% on average. Specifically, LongVA, VideoLLaMA2, and LLaVA-
420   OneVision improve their average performance by 11.8%, 0.6%, and 11.2%, respectively. Remark-
421   ably, the LLaVA-OneVision model with RadEyeVideo surpasses both CheXagent and CXR-LLaVA
422   models, which were specifically trained on the MIMIC-CXR dataset for report generation and di-
423   agnosis. This finding underscores the potential of video-based eye-gaze prompting to bridge the
424   performance gap between general-purpose models and task-specific models in the medical imaging
425   domain.
426   Limitations of Heatmap and Fixation Text Prompts
427   While RadEyeVideo shows strong results, both heatmap prompting and fixation text prompt
428   methods do not always improve performance. While fixation text prompts show mixed results-
429   VideoLLaMA2 always gaining performance, heatmap-based prompting universally underperforms
430   when compared to the baseline across all models for the generation of the Findings section. The
431   LLaVA-OneVision model suffers from a performance drop in both splits when using heatmaps.
      Also, the LongVA model shows a consistent drop in performance when utilizing heatmaps for the


                                                         8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      beta split and the VideoLLaMA2 model suffers a similar degradation. These two models also show
433   a decrease in diagnosis performance for the alpha split.
434
435   The poor performance of heatmaps is likely due to their failure to capture the sequential and temporal
436
      dynamics of eye-gaze, which are essential for understanding medical analysis in chest X-rays. This
      supports our hypothesis that the temporal order of a radiologist’s gaze is critical for enhancing
437
      contextual awareness and clinical relevance.
438
439   Fixation text prompts, although they preserve the temporal sequence of eye-gaze, suffer from other
440   limitations. By attempting to encode both spatial and temporal information in a text format, these
441   prompts often become overly lengthy and complex, particularly when the model is asked to generate
442
      a longer response, the Findings and Diagnosis. As a result, LongVA and LLaVA-OneVision both
      performed worse in the Findings section, and LLaVA-OneVision performed worse in Diagnosis.

      5   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
516
517   In this work, we introduced RadEyeVideo, a novel video prompting approach, into general domain
518   LVLMs, enhancing their performance in chest X-ray report generation and diagnosis. Our approach
519   effectively captures the spatial and temporal dynamics of eye gaze, outperforming existing eye-gaze
520   prompting techniques and even the task specific medical LVLMs. This method demonstrates sig-
521   nificant potential for bridging the gap between general-purpose and task-specific models in medical
522   imaging. Future work will explore extending video-based prompting to tasks like visual question
523   answering and anatomical structure detection. Additionally, applying this method to other medi-
524   cal imaging areas, such as CT and MRI scans, holds promise for improving accuracy and clinical
525
      relevance across various domains in healthcare.
526
      L IMITATION
527
528   This study’s evaluation was limited by the small dataset size, due to the difficulty of obtaining
529   radiologists’ eye-tracking data. Furthermore, the MIMIC-CXR dataset may not fully capture the
530   diversity of real-world medical imaging. Future work should focus on larger and more diverse
531   datasets to better assess the method’s generalizability across different imaging modalities.

734   entities and relation types.
735
736   RaTEScore Zhao et al. (2024a) RaTEScore (Radiological Report Text Evaluation) is the latest
737
      metric employed in our study, specifically designed to assess the quality of generated medical re-
      ports. It evaluates the correct identification of key medical entities, including diagnostic outcomes
738
      and anatomical references, using a NER model. RaTEScore is robust at handling complex medical
739
      synonyms and is sensitive to negation, making it more aligned with human judgments compared to
740   other metrics.
