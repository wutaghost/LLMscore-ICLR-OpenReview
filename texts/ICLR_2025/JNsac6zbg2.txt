011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Hierarchical reinforcement learning (HRL) aims to solve complex tasks by making
014            decisions across multiple levels of temporal abstraction. However, off-policy
015
               training of hierarchical policies faces non-stationarity issues because the low-
               level policy is constantly changing, which makes it difficult for the high-level
016
               policy that generates subgoals to adapt. In this paper, we propose a conditional
017            diffusion model-based approach for subgoal generation to mitigate these non-
018            stationarity challenges. Specifically, we employ a Gaussian Process (GP) prior on
019            subgoal generation as a surrogate distribution to regularize the diffusion policy
020            and inform the diffusion process about uncertain areas in the action space. We
021            introduce adaptive inducing states to facilitate sparse GP-based subgoal generation,
022            enhancing sample efficiency and promoting better exploration in critical regions of
               the state space. Building on this framework, we develop an exploration strategy
023
               that identifies promising subgoals based on the learned predictive distribution of the
024
               diffusional subgoals. Experimental results demonstrate significant improvements
025            in both sample efficiency and performance on challenging continuous control
026            benchmarks compared to prior HRL methods.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   In the domain of Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning (HRL), the strategy to simplify complex
      decision-making processes involves structuring tasks into various levels of temporal and behavioral
032
      abstractions. This strategy excels particularly in environments where the challenges include long-term
033
      credit assignment and sparse rewards, making it an ideal solution for long-horizon decision-making
034   problems. Among the prevailing HRL paradigms, goal-conditioned HRL has been extensively
035   explored in various studies (Dayan & Hinton, 1992; Schmidhuber & Wahnsiedler, 1993; Kulkarni
036   et al., 2016; Vezhnevets et al., 2017; Nachum et al., 2018; Levy et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2020; Li
037   et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2021; Li et al., 2023). Within this framework, a high-level policy decomposes
038   the primary goal into a series of subgoals, effectively directing the lower-level policy to achieve
039
      these subgoals. The efficacy of this approach hinges on the ability to generate subgoals that are
      semantically coherent and achievable, providing a strong learning signal for the lower-level policies.
040
      The hierarchical structure not only enhances the learning process’s efficiency but also considerably
041   improves the policy’s overall performance in solving complex tasks.
042
043
      Nonetheless, the hierarchical structure in HRL has induced non-stationary state transitions, which
      remains a key challenge in off-policy training of a hierarchy of policies. The crux of the issue
044
      lies in the fact that the same high-level action taken under the same state in the past may result in
045   significantly different low-level state transitions. This is caused by the constantly changing low-level
046   policy, which renders the previous experience invalid for training. When all policies within the
047   hierarchy are trained simultaneously, the high-level transition will constantly change as long as the
048   low-level policy continues to be updated. Parallel learning of hierarchical policies remains feasible,
049   but only if the high-level policy can efficiently adapt itself to the updated low-level policy.
050   Previous works like HIRO (Nachum et al., 2018) and HAC (Levy et al., 2019) have attempted to
051   address this non-stationarity problem through a relabeling strategy that utilizes hindsight experience
052   replay (HER) (Andrychowicz et al., 2017a). This involves relabeling past experiences with high-level
053   actions, i.e., subgoals, that maximize the probability of the past lower-level actions. Essentially, the
      subgoal that induced a low-level behavior in the past experience is relabeled so that it potentially

054
      induce the similar low-level behavior with the current low-level policy. However, the relabeling
055   approach does not facilitate efficient training of the high-level policy to comply promptly with updates
056   to the low-level policy. It consistently generates incompatible subgoals, further exacerbating the
057   non-stationarity issue. Consequently, such unfit state transitions in off-policy training lead to improper
058   learning of the high-level value function, negatively affecting high-level policy exploration.
059   In this work, we propose a diffusion model (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Song et al., 2021a; Ho
060   et al., 2020) based approach for subgoal generation, aiming to address the non-stationarity issue in
061   HRL. Our approach involves modeling the state-conditional distribution of subgoals at the high level,
062   harnessing a Gaussian Process (GP) prior as an explicit surrogate distribution for subgoals. The GP
063   prior potentially reduces the amount of training data needed for the diffusion model to converge, and
064   more importantly it could help inform the diffusion policy about state regions that are more uncertain
065
      when learning the conditional distribution by providing an uncertainty quantification. To improve
      the sample efficiency and exploration of the high-level policy, we introduce inducing states, i.e.,
066
      learnable pseudo states, which adaptively summarize the states that are informative to explore. These
067   learnable pseudo states facilitate finer modeling in critical regions of the state space which pushes for
068   better exploration. Once learned, the inducing states provides implicit subgoals, which are used to
069   predict the distribution of subgoals generated by the high-level policy. Building on our GP-based
070   probabilistic formulation, we propose an exploration strategy that explicitly identifies promising
071   subgoals based on the learned surrogate distribution of diffusional subgoals. Our key insight is that
072
      novel subgoals should be both reachable and capable of guiding the agent toward unexplored areas,
      consistent with the probabilistic nature of the predictive distribution of subgoals. This approach is
073
      more efficient and stable than relying on visit counts for subgoals such as Li et al. (2022). Instead of
074   focusing on visit coverage, our method proposes reasonable subgoals based on the non-parametric
075   distribution, which can be learned more data-efficiently during training.
076
      Our main contributions are summarized as follows:
077
078
             • We introduce conditional diffusion modeling approach for subgoal generation to address the
079            non-stationarity issue of off-policy HRL.
080
             • We employ a Gaussian Process (GP) prior to model the state-to-subgoal mapping, providing
081
               a probabilistic framework that quantifies uncertainties across the state space, regularizes the
082            diffusional subgoal generation, and implicitly informs exploration in uncertain areas.
083
084
             • We propose an inducing states informed exploration strategy that seeks promising subgoals
               based on the learned predictive distribution of diffusional policy.

      3.2   U NCERTAINTY M ODELING
217
218   Whilst diffusion models possess sufficient expressivity to model the conditional distribution of
219   subgoals, they face two significant challenges in the context of subgoal generation in HRL. Firstly,
220   these models typically require a substantial amount of training data, i.e., relabeled subgoals, to achieve
221   accurate and stable performance, which can be highly sample inefficient. Secondly, standard diffusion
222
      models lack an inherent mechanism for quantifying uncertainty in their predictions, a crucial aspect
      for effective exploration and robust decision-making in HRL. To address these issues, we propose to
223
      model the state-conditional distribution of subgoals at the high level, harnessing a Gaussian Process
224   (GP) prior as an explicit surrogate distribution for subgoals.

      HRL has been a significant field of study for addressing challenges such as long-term credit assign-
350   ment and sparse rewards. It typically involves a high-level policy that breaks down the overarching
351   task into manageable subtasks, which are then addressed by a more specialized low-level policy
352   (Dayan & Hinton, 1992; Schmidhuber & Wahnsiedler, 1993; Kulkarni et al., 2016; Vezhnevets et al.,
353   2017; Nachum et al., 2018; Levy et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2020; Li et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2021; Li
354   et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023). The mechanism of this decomposition varies, with some approaches
355   utilizing discrete values for option or skill selection (Bacon et al., 2017; Fox et al., 2017; Gregor et al.,
      2017; Konidaris & Barto, 2009; Eysenbach et al., 2019; Sharma et al., 2020; Bagaria & Konidaris,
356
      2019), and others adopting learned subgoal space (Vezhnevets et al., 2017; Li et al., 2021; 2023).
357
      Despite these variations, a common challenge is the difficulty of leveraging advancements in the field
358   of off-policy, model-free RL.
359
360
      Recent efforts to enhance HRL’s learning efficiency through off-policy training have highlighted
      issues such as instability and the inherent non-stationarity problem of HRL. For instance, Nachum
361
      et al. (2018) introduces an off-policy method that relabels past experiences to mitigate non-stationary
362   effects on training. Techniques from hindsight experience replay have been utilized to train multi-
363   level policies concurrently, penalizing high-level policies for unattainable subgoals (Andrychowicz
364   et al., 2017a; Levy et al., 2019). To address the issue of large subgoal spaces, Zhang et al. (2020)
365   proposed constraining the high-level action space with an adjacency requirement. Wang et al. (2020)
366   improves stationarity by conditioning high-level decisions on both the low-level policy representation
367
      and environmental states. Li et al. (2021) develops a slowness objective for learning a subgoal
      representation function. Kim et al. (2021) introduces a framework for training a high-level policy
368
      with a reduced action space guided by landmarks, i.e., promising states to explore. Adopting the
369   deterministic subgoal representation of Li et al. (2021), Li et al. (2022) proposes an exploration
370   strategy to enhance the high-level exploration via designing measures of novelty and potential for
371   subgoals, albeit the strategy relies on on visit counts for subgoals in the constantly changing subgoal
372   representation space.
373
      The broader topic of goal generation in RL has also been explored (Florensa et al., 2018; Nair et al.,
374   2018; Ren et al., 2019; Campero et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023). GoalGAN (Florensa et al., 2018)
375   employs a GAN to generate appropriately challenging tasks for policy training, however it does
376   not condition on observations and the sequential training of its GAN and policy. Nair et al. (2018)
377   combines unsupervised representation learning with goal-conditioned policy training. Ren et al.
      (2019) proposes a method for generating immediately achievable hindsight goals. Campero et al.


                                                                                                        7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      (2021) introduces a framework wherein a teacher network proposes progressively challenging goals,
379   rewarding the network based on the student’s performance. SAGA Wang et al. (2023) introduces an
380   adversarially guided framework for generating subgoals in goal-conditioned HRL. However, akin to
381   the common challenges associated with GANs, SAGA may encounter issues such as stability and
382   mode collapse due to its implicit modeling of subgoal distributions. In contrast, HIDI explicitly
383   constructs subgoal distributions by progressively transforming noise into sample data, effectively
384   capturing multimodal distributions and ensuring more stable training dynamics.
385   Diffusion models have been introduced to offline RL domain recently (Janner et al., 2022; Wang
386   et al.; Kang et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024). Janner et al. (2022) train an unconditional
387   diffusion model to generate trajectories consisting of states and actions for offline RL. Approaches (Li
388   et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024) also extend diffusion model to offline HRL and generate trajectories
389
      at different levels. As a more related line of work, Diffsuion-QL (Wang et al.) introduces diffusion
      models into offline RL and demonstrated that diffusion models are superior at modeling complex
390
      action distributions. Kang et al. (2024) improve Diffsuion-QL to be compatible with maximum
391   likelihood-based RL algorithms. The success of offline RL methods leveraging diffusion policies
392   (Wang et al.; Kang et al., 2024) motivates us to investigate the impact of using conditional diffusion
393   model for the challenging subgoal generation in off-policy HRL.
394
      Gaussian processes can encode flexible priors over functions, which are a probabilistic machine
395   learning paradigm (Williams & Rasmussen, 2006). GPs have been adopted in various latent variable
396   modeling tasks in RL. In Engel et al. (2003), the use of GPs for solving the RL problem of value
397   estimation is introduced. Then Kuss & Rasmussen (2003) uses GPs to model the the value function
398   and system dynamics. Deisenroth et al. (2013) develops a GP-based transition model of a model-
399   based learning system, which explicitly incorporates model uncertainty into long-term planning and
400   controller learning to reduce the effects of model errors. Levine et al. (2011) proposes an algorithm
      for inverse reinforcement learning that represents nonlinear reward functions with GPs, allowing the
401
      recovery of both a reward function and the hyperparameters of a kernel function that describes the
402
      structure of the reward. Wang et al. (2024) proposes a GP based method for learning probabilistic
403   subgoal representations in HRL.

406
407   Our experimental evaluation encompasses a diverse set of long-horizon continuous control tasks
408   facilitated by the MuJoCo simulator (Todorov et al., 2012), which are widely adopted in the HRL
409   community. The environments selected for testing our framework, depicted in Figure 2, include:
410
       • Reacher: This task entails utilizing a robotic arm to reach a specified target position with its
411
        end-effector.
412
413    • Pusher: A robotic arm must push a puck-shaped object on a plane to a designated goal position.
414    • Point Maze: A simulation ball starts in the bottom left corner of a “ ⊃”-shaped maze, aiming to
415     reach the top left corner.
416    • Ant Maze (U-shape): A simulated ant starts in the bottom left of a “ ⊃”-shaped maze, targeting
417     the top left corner.
418    • Ant Maze (W-shape): A simulated ant starts at a random position within a “∃”-shaped maze,
419     aiming for the middle left corner.
420
       • Stochastic Ant Maze (U-shape): Gaussian noise (standard deviation σ = 0.05) is added to the
421     ant’s (x, y) position at each step.
422
423   We evaluate HIDI and all the baselines under two reward shaping paradigms: dense and sparse. In
424   the dense setting, rewards are computed as the negative L2 distance from the current state to the
425   target position within the goal space, whilst the sparse rewards are set to 0 for distances to the target
426   below a certain threshold, otherwise -1. Maze tasks adopt a 2-dimensional goal space for the agent’s
427
      (x, y) position, adhering to existing works (Zhang et al., 2020; Kim et al., 2021). For Reacher, a
      3-dimensional goal space is utilized to represent the end-effector’s (x, y, z) position, while Pusher
428
      employs a 6-dimensional space, including the 3D position of the object. Relative subgoal scheme is
429   applied in Maze tasks, with absolute scheme used for Reacher and Pusher1 .

452   4.2                   A NALYSIS
453
      We conduct experiments comparing with the following state-of-the-art baseline methods: (1) HLPS
454
      (Wang et al., 2024): an HRL algorithm which proposes a GP-based subgoal latent space to address the
455   non-stationarity issue; (2) HESS (Li et al., 2022): an HRL algorithm which proposes an exploration
456   strategy to avoid introducing additional non-stationarity; (3) SAGA (Wang et al., 2023): an HRL
457   algorithm that introduces an adversarially guided framework for generating subgoals; (4) HIGL
458   (Kim et al., 2021): an HRL algorithm that trains a high-level policy with a reduced subgoal space
459   guided by landmarks; (5) HRAC (Zhang et al., 2020): an HRL algorithm which introduces an
460   adjacency network to restrict the high-level action space to a k-step adjacent region of the current
461
      state; (6) HIRO (Nachum et al., 2018): an HRL algorithm that relabels the high-level actions based
      on hindsight experience (Andrychowicz et al., 2017b). Additionally, we present a theoretical analysis
462
      for HIDI in the Appendix A.1.
463
464
      Can HIDI surpass state-of-the-art HRL methods in learning stability, sample efficiency, and
465   asymptotic performance? Figure 2 illustrates the learning curves of HIDI in comparison with
466   baseline methods across various tasks. Details on Maze tasks with dense external rewards are included
467   in the Appendix. HIDI consistently surpasses all baselines in terms of learning stability, sample
468   efficiency, and asymptotic performance. The advantage of the hierarchical diffusion policy approach
469   is more pronounced in complex scenarios such as the Reacher and Pusher robotic arm tasks, and
470
      the Stochastic Ant Maze task, where the environmental stochasticity poses additional challenges in
      generating reasonable subgoals. These results highlight the benefits of employing generative models
471
      for subgoal generation, as demonstrated by HIDI and SAGA. However, SAGA exhibits signs of
472   instability in the more demanding robotic arm task Pusher, as well as considerably lower sample
473   efficiency compared to HIDI in most tasks.
474
475   Is HIDI capable of generating reachable subgoals to address the non-stationarity issue com-
476   monly encountered in off-policy training within HRL? Figure 6 in the Appendix illustrates
477   generated subgoals and reached subgoals of HIDI and compared baselines in Ant Maze (W-shape,
478   sparse) with the same starting location. This allows for an intuitive comparison of subgoals generated
479   by HIDI, SAGA, HIGL, HRAC, and HIRO. Notably, HIDI generates reasonable subgoals for the
      lower level to achieve, as demonstrated by the small divergence between the generated and reached
480
      subgoals, which provides a stable learning signal for the low-level policy. In contrast, subgoals
481
      generated by HIRO are often unachievable and fail to guide the agent to reach the final target; subgoals
482   generated by HRAC frequently get stuck in local minima due to its local adjacency constraint; HIGL
483   and SAGA show improvement in both constraining the subgoals locally while jumping out of the
484   local optimum of subgoals, yet the high-level policy is not adequately compatible with the low-level
485   skills, i.e., the increasing gap between the generated subgoal and reached subgoal leads to inferior
      performance compared with HIDI. This is further confirmed by the measure of distance between the


                                                                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      generated subgoal and reached subgoal in Table 2. Subgoals generated by HLPS and HESS lie in
487   a learned subgoal latent space and may not be quantitatively or qualitatively compared with other
488   methods.
489
490   How do various design choices within HIDI impact its empirical performance and effectiveness?
491   To understand the benefits of using a diffusion policy for generating subgoals, we constructed several
492   baselines. HIDI-A denotes a baseline without performing the proposed exploration strategy, HIDI-B
493   is a baseline without adopting the GP regularization and exploration, while Baseline is HIDI without
      difusion model, GP regularization and exploration strategy. Fig. 3 (Left) illustrates the comparisons
494
      of various baselines and HIDI:
495
496    • Diffusional Subgoals: The performance improvement from Baseline to HIDI-B shows the benefit
497     of adopting conditional diffusional model for subgoal generation, with a performance gain of
498     ∼15%.
499    • Uncertainty Regularization: The sampling efficiency and performance improvement ∼4% from
500     HIDI-B to HIDI-A indicates the advantage of employing the GP prior on subgoal generation as a
501
        surrogate distribution which potentially informs the diffusion process about uncertain areas.
502    • Exploration Strategy: The comparison between HIDI and HIDI-A shows the benefit ∼5% of the
503     proposed exploration strategy that identifies promising reachable subgoals.
504    • Diffusion Steps N : Fig. 3 (Middle) shows the learning curves of baselines using varying number
505     of diffusion steps N used in HIDI. It empirically demonstrates that as N increases from 3 to 7,
        the high-level policy becomes more expressive and capable of learning the more complex data
506
        distribution of subgoals. Since N also serves as a trade-off between the expressiveness of subgoal
507
        modeling and computational complexity, we found that N = 5 is an efficient and effective setting
508     for all the tasks during the experiment.
509
       • Exploration Probability ϵ: ϵ controls the chance to perform explorations. As shown in Fig. 3
510     (Right), when ϵ is large, e.g., 0.25, the trade-off between the expressiveness of subgoal modeling
511     and uncertainty information might be affected, i.e., excessive subgoals sampled in uncertain regions
512     may contribute to performance instability. When ϵ is small, e.g., 0.05, the gain from explorations
513     would be decreased, and we set ϵ = 0.1 for all other results.
514    • Scaling Factor η: We investigate the impact of η in Eq. (5), which balances the diffusion objective
515     and RL objective. As shown in Fig. 4 (Left), increasing η improves performance at early training
516     steps (0 ∼ 106 ), while all three settings achieve similar performance at larger training steps
517     (4.2 × 106 ∼ 5 × 106 ). We report all other results based on η = 5 without loss of generality.
518    • Scaling Factor ψ: ψ adjusts the influence of GP prior in learning the distribution of diffusional
519     subgoals. As shown in Fig. 4 (Middle) in the appendix, increasing ψ gives stronger GP prior
520
        and may slightly affect the flexibility of diffusion model learning, while decreasing ψ renders the
        model to approximate baseline HIDI-B. We set ψ = 10−3 for all other results.
521
522
      Is the proposed exploration strategy advantageous compared to the visit count-based explo-
523   ration in HRL? To investigate the advantage of the proposed exploration strategy, which adopts the
524   predictive distribution of diffusional subgoals underpinned by learned inducing states, we compare
525   HIDI with HESS, which also actively performs exploration at the high level based on the novelty, i.e.,
526   the visit count, and potential, i.e., negative distance between a sampled subgoal and the ending state
527   of a low-level trajectory. As shown in Fig. 4, HIDI significantly outperforms HESS in the robotic arm
528
      tasks Reacher and Pusher, demonstrating higher sample efficiency. Our insight is that the exploration
      of HIDI, which is based on a non-parametric distribution, might be more data-efficient and stable
529
      than relying on visit counts for subgoals as in HESS.

532   5   C ONCLUSION
533
      We presented a hierarchical reinforcement learning framework that employs conditional diffusion
534
      models for subgoal generation to address non-stationarity issues in off-policy HRL training. By incor-
535
      porating a Gaussian Process prior into the diffusion policy, we provided a probabilistic approach that
536   quantifies uncertainty in the subgoal space, enhancing sample efficiency and exploration. Introducing
537   adaptive inducing states allowed the model to focus on critical regions of the state space, improving
538   learning performance. Our exploration strategy, based on the predictive distribution of diffusional
539   subgoals, demonstrated significant advantages over existing methods, yielding superior results on
      challenging continuous control benchmarks.

      Decision Process (MDP).

      A.1.2    K EY C OMPONENTS AND L EMMAS
741
742   Diffusion Model Policy The high-level policy πθh (g|s) is modeled using a conditional diffusion
743   model (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020):
744                                                                              N
                                                                                 Y
745                          πθh (g|s) = pθh (g0:N |s) = p(gN )                        pθh (gi−1 |gi , s).   (26)
746                                                                              i=1
747
      Here, p(gN ) = N (gN ; 0, I) is the prior distribution.
748
      Lemma 1 (Convergence of the Diffusion Model Policy). Under Assumptions (1, 3, 5), and given
749
      sufficient training data and model capacity, the diffusion model policy πθh (g|s) converges to an
750   approximation of the true conditional subgoal distribution, balancing optimality and diversity.
751
752   Proof. This follows from the consistency of maximum likelihood estimation in deep generative
753   models (Song et al., 2021b). Given sufficient data and model capacity, and assuming that the model is
754   trained to convergence using stochastic gradient descent with appropriate learning rates, the learned
755   distribution πθh (g|s) approximates the true data distribution conditioned on s (Kingma & Welling,
      2019).


                                                                     14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      TD3 Regularization The diffusion model is regularized using a Twin Delayed Deep Deterministic
757   Policy Gradient (TD3) objective (Fujimoto et al., 2018).
758
      Lemma 2 (Approximate Convergence of Twin Critics). Under Assumptions (1-5), and assuming that
759
      the function approximators are trained using stochastic gradient descent with diminishing learning
760   rates (Assumption (4)), the twin critics Qϕ1 and Qϕ2 converge to functions that approximate the fixed
761   point Q∗ satisfying the Bellman equation within a bounded error.
762
763
      Proof. Building upon the convergence analysis of temporal-difference learning with function approxi-
764   mation (Tsitsiklis & Roy, 1997), and considering the modifications in TD3 to mitigate overestimation
765   bias (Fujimoto et al., 2018), we argue that under appropriate conditions, the critics minimize the mean
766   squared Bellman error. The diminishing learning rates ensure that the error does not accumulate,
767   leading to convergence to functions close to Q∗ .
768
769   GP-based Uncertainty Modeling A Gaussian Process (GP) prior with learned inducing points is
770   used to model the uncertainty in the subgoal distribution (Snelson & Ghahramani, 2006).
771
      Lemma 3 (Convergence of the GP Model). Under Assumptions (1) and (5), and given sufficient
772   data, the sparse GP model converges to an accurate approximation of the true subgoal uncertainty
773   distribution as the number of training samples and inducing points increases.
774
775
      Proof. Based on the properties of sparse GP approximations (Titsias, 2009), as the number of inducing
776   points and data samples increases, and assuming the inducing points are optimized effectively, the
777   sparse GP model’s predictive distribution converges to that of a full GP, accurately capturing the
778   uncertainty.
779
780
      Exploration Strategy The exploration strategy combines ε-greedy selection from the GP predictive
781   mean and sampling from the diffusion model to ensure sufficient exploration.
782
      Lemma 4 (Sufficient Exploration). Assuming that the exploration policy injects sufficient stochasticity
783
      into the action selection process, for any δ > 0, every δ-ball in S × G is visited infinitely often with
784   probability one.
785
786
      Proof. By ensuring a non-zero probability of selecting any subgoal in G from any state s due to
787
      the stochastic component of the exploration strategy, the induced Markov chain is irreducible and
788   aperiodic. According to the ergodicity property of Markov chains (Meyn & Tweedie, 1993), every
789   state-subgoal pair is visited infinitely often.
790
791
      A.1.3    M AIN C ONVERGENCE T HEOREM
792
793   Theorem 5 (Convergence of HIDI). Under Assumptions (1-5), and the conditions specified in
794   Lemmas 1–4, the HIDI algorithm converges with high probability to a policy πθ∗h that balances
795   optimality and diversity in subgoal generation, achieving performance within a bounded error of the
796   optimal expected cumulative reward in the continuous hierarchical MDP.
797
798   Proof. We establish convergence through the following steps:
799
      1) Sufficient Exploration: Lemma 4 ensures that the algorithm explores the state-subgoal space
800   thoroughly, preventing premature convergence to suboptimal policies due to limited exploration.
801
802   2) Value Estimation Error Bounds: From Lemma 2, the critics converge to approximations of the
      true value function within bounds determined by the function approximation capacity and learning
803
      dynamics.
804
805   3) Policy Improvement with Approximation Errors: Due to the approximation capabilities of the
806   diffusion model and GP (Lemmas 1 and 3), the policy improvement step optimizes the expected
807
      return considering both the estimated values and uncertainties, within certain error margins.
808   4) Error Propagation Control: By carefully controlling the learning rates (Assumption (4)) and
809   utilizing the stability provided by Lipschitz continuity (Assumption (3)), we ensure that errors
      introduced at each step do not accumulate excessively.


                                                        15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      5) Overall Convergence: Combining the above, HIDI converges to a policy whose performance is
811   close to the optimal policy, with the discrepancy bounded by the cumulative approximation errors
812   from its components.
813                             P∞
      Formally, let J(π) = E [ t=0 γ t R(st , gt )] denote the expected return under policy π. Then, the
814
      policy πθ∗h learned by HIDI satisfies:
815
816                                            |J(πθ∗h ) − J(π † )| ≤ ϵ,                                      (27)
817
818   where π † is the optimal policy, and ϵ is a bound that depends on the approximation errors from the
819   diffusion model, critics, and GP model.
820
821   A.1.4    D ISCUSSION
822   This convergence result demonstrates that HIDI effectively combines the strengths of diffusion models,
823   TD3, and GP-based uncertainty modeling to learn a high-level policy that generates diverse yet reward-
824   maximizing subgoals in a hierarchical reinforcement learning setting. The key is balancing exploration
825   and exploitation while managing approximation errors through careful design and adherence to the
826   stated assumptions.
