009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            We show that differentially private full fine-tuning (DP-FFT) can distort pre-
012            trained backbone features based on both theoretical and empirical results. We
013            identify the cause of the distortion as the misalignment between the pre-trained
014            backbone and the randomly initialized linear head. We prove that a sequential
015            fine-tuning strategy can mitigate the feature distortion: first-linear-probing-then-
016            fine-tuning (DP-LP-FFT). A new approximation scheme allows us to derive ap-
017            proximate upper and lower bounds on the training loss of DP-LP and DP-FFT, in
018            a simple but canonical setting of 2-layer neural networks with ReLU activation.
019
               Experiments on real-world datasets and architectures are consistent with our the-
               oretical insights. We also derive new upper bounds for 2-layer linear networks
020
               without the approximation. Moreover, our theory suggests a trade-off of privacy
021
               budget allocation in multi-phase fine-tuning methods like DP-LP-FFT.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026
      Today, many differentially-private (DP) machine learning pipelines proceed in two phases: (1) A
027
      model is pre-trained (non-privately) on a public dataset. (2) The model is then fine-tuned on private
028
      data, using DP optimization techniques such as DP stochastic gradient descent (DP-SGD) and its
029   variants (Hoory et al., 2021; De et al., 2022; Tang et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024b). Pre-training a
030   backbone model on public data enables differentially private fine-tuning to achieve improved per-
031   formance across various downstream tasks (Yu et al., 2022) and is proven to be necessary in some
032   cases (Ganesh et al., 2023a).
033
      Despite these advances, the effect of DP on fine-tuning training dynamics remains poorly under-
034
      stood. Several key questions are yet to be answered: (1) how does randomness (both of initialization
035   and DP optimization) impact the pre-trained representations? (2) What are the convergence rates of
036   common fine-tuning methods, such as DP full fine-tuning (DP-FFT) and DP linear probing (DP-LP,
037   where feature representations are frozen, and only the linear head is fine-tuned)? (3) Prior work
038   suggests that combining an early stage of DP-LP with a later stage of DP-FFT yields better privacy-
039   utility tradeoffs (Tang et al., 2023), yet there is no theoretical understanding of this phenomenon,
040   nor is it clear how to optimally combine these fine-tuning methods.
041   Answering these questions theoretically requires an analysis that can capture the fine-grained opti-
042   mization dynamics of DP fine-tuning. We seek a model of DP finetuning that satisfies 2 properties.
043
044
      1. Architecture-sensitivity: The convergence dynamics must differentiate between representa-
         tion learning in the backbone and learning in the linear head. The analyses of Bassily et al.
045
         (2014),Wang et al. (2022),Fang et al. (2023),Ganesh et al. (2023b) focus only on the network’s
046
         dimension, failing to capture this distinction.
047
048   2. Ability to model nonlinearities: The model should account for the nonlinearities introduced by
049      multi neural layers, unlike existing methods that simplify analysis by linearizing neural networks
050
         (Ye et al., 2023a; Wang et al., 2024).
051   We propose a novel approximation of DP-SGD training dynamics based on linearizing Langevin
052   diffusion around the noise term. This approach offers new insights into DP fine-tuning and signif-
053   icantly simplifies analysis by converting stochastic differential equations into ordinary differential
      equations (ODEs). We validate our theoretical predictions with real experiments.

070   Main contributions. In summary, our key contributions are:
071                1. New approximation technique: In Section 2, we derive a first-order ODE via an asymptotic
072                   expansion of the stochastic noise in Langevin diffusion. Unlike previous methods, which lin-
073                   earize neural network parameters, our technique preserves the multi-layer structure of deep
074                   learning models while simplifying the analysis. This approach, commonly used in physics and
075                   control theory (Skorokhod et al., 2002), is novel in the context of private machine learning and
076                   bridges the gap between non-private neural network theory and the private regime.
077                2. Understanding of feature distortion: In Section 3, we provide a theoretical understanding
078                   of how DP fine-tuning affects feature representations. Using our approximation, we prove
079                   that, in 2-layer ReLU networks, randomly initialized linear heads distort pre-trained backbone
080                   features in the early stages of DP-FFT. Empirically Figure 1 demonstrates that feature quality
081
                      evaluated on private data initially degrades during DP-FFT but later improves and surpasses
                      pre-fine-tuning quality. Our theory also predicts that running a single epoch of DP-LP before
082
                      transitioning to DP-FFT can mitigate this initial feature distortion, as shown empirically in
083
                      the DP-LP-FFT curve of Figure 1 (left). This insight extends the findings of Kumar et al.
084                   (2022), who showed that LP-FFT reduces feature distortion in non-private, OOD scenarios, to
085                   in-distribution settings for both DP and non-DP cases.
086
                   3. Theoretical convergence bounds: In Section 4, we present new upper and lower bounds on
087
                      the training loss of DP-LP and DP-FFT for 2-layer ReLU networks using our approximation
088                   technique. We also prove upper bounds for 2-layer linear networks without the approximation.
089                   To the best of our knowledge, this is the first convergence analysis of DP-SGD on non-linear
090                   neural network architectures.
091
                   4. Mitigating feature distortion by combining fine-tuning methods: Prior work by Tang et al.
092
                      (2023) empirically showed that combining DP-LP and DP-FFT (DP-LP-FFT) can achieve bet-
093                   ter test accuracy than either method alone. In Figure 1b, we demonstrate that allocating ap-
094                   proximately 20% of the privacy budget to DP-LP yields optimal test accuracy. In Section 5, we
095                   provide a partial theoretical explanation for this phenomenon. Specifically, our bounds suggest
096                   that DP-FFT may underperform relative to DP-LP at lower privacy budgets, while DP-LP-FFT
097                   can outperform both methods under moderate privacy budgets. These predictions are empiri-
098                   cally verified across various architectures and benchmarks in Section 5.3.

       1.1                   R ELATED W ORK
101
102   Similar empirical phenomena have been explored in non-private, out-of-distribution (OOD) contexts
103   by Aghajanyan et al. (2021), Kumar et al. (2022), Trivedi et al. (2023), and Chen et al. (2024).
104   Kumar et al. (2022) demonstrated that non-DP fine-tuning distorts pre-trained features, leading to
105   degraded OOD performance. But their theory relies on the assumption that OOD test data exists in
106   an orthogonal subspace to the fine-tuning training data, leaving their results unable to explain why,
107   in many transfer learning tasks, linear-probe fine-tuning (LP-FFT) still outperforms both LP and full
      fine-tuning (FFT) in in-distribution (ID) settings. Our work seeks to fill this research gap.

169   In this section, we introduce the concept of representation alignment, present our theoretical find-
170   ings, and validate them with experiments. Representation alignment refers to the process by which
171
      the classification head aligns itself with the pre-trained backbone features. During the DP-FFT pro-
      cess, this alignment creates a characteristic trend in feature quality: initially, the randomly initialized
172
      linear head distorts the pre-trained features, but as it better aligns with the backbone, the distortion
173
      diminishes, and the overall quality of the backbone features improves over time.

517   6     C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
518
519   We characterize the training dynamics of DP fine-tuning under a simplified theoretic setup (2-layer
520   neural networks, separable datasets with -1/1 labels) using a Langevin diffusion-based approxima-
521   tion of DP-SGD, with an asymptotic expansion of random perturbations in dynamical systems as
522
      an approximation for Langevin diffusion. Our theory identifies and explains the phenomenon of
      representation distortion and alignment during DP fine-tuning, which we confirm empirically. Our
523
      work takes a step towards understanding how different private fine-tuning strategies can be mixed
524
      to improve performance, which could be useful for designing or mixing other strategies, such as
525   memory-efficient zeroth-order optimization with differential privacy (Zhang et al., 2024a).
526
527
      Limitations and open questions There are several open questions we cannot cover in this work,
528
      such as generalizing our results to multi-layer neural networks with our approximation technique,
529   the effect of other loss functions on the fine-tuning dynamics, and loss lower bounds for DP-LP/FFT
530   without the zeroth-order approximation. Moreover, it is unclear how to apply our theory to other
531   fine-tuning methods like LoRA (Hu et al., 2022b), as well as generative models for which neural
532   collapse does not happen. Understanding whether the zeroth-order approximation can facilitate
533   analysis in these settings is an interesting and important question for future work.
