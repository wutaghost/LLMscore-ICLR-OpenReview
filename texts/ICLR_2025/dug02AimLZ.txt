                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011            Zero-sum games arise in a wide variety of problems, including robust optimiza-
012            tion and adversarial learning. However, algorithms deployed for finding a local
013            Nash equilibrium in these games often converge to non-Nash stationary points.
014            This highlights a key challenge: for any algorithm, the stability properties of its
015            underlying dynamical system can cause non-Nash points to be potential attractors.
016            To overcome this challenge, algorithms must account for subtleties involving the
017            curvatures of players‚Äô costs. To this end, we leverage dynamical system theory
018            and develop a second-order algorithm for finding a local Nash equilibrium in the
019
               smooth, possibly nonconvex-nonconcave, zero-sum game setting. First, we prove
               that this novel method guarantees convergence to only local Nash equilibria with
020
               a local linear convergence rate. We then interpret a version of this method as
021
               a modified Gauss-Newton algorithm with local superlinear convergence to the
022            neighborhood of a point that satisfies first-order local Nash equilibrium conditions.
023            In comparison, current related state-of-the-art methods do not offer convergence
024            rate guarantees. Furthermore, we show that this approach naturally generalizes
025            to settings with convex and potentially coupled constraints while retaining earlier
026            guarantees of convergence to only local (generalized) Nash equilibria.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   We consider the setting of smooth, deterministic two-player zero-sum games of the form
032            Player 1 : min f (x, y)           Player 2 : max f (x, y)       (x, y) ‚ąą G,          (Game 1)
                            x                                 y
033
034   where f can be nonconvex-nonconcave with respect to x ‚ąą Rn and y ‚ąą Rm , respectively. In the
035   unconstrained setting, i.e., when G is (Rn , Rm ), we seek to find a local Nash equilibrium. For the
036   constrained setting, we will assume that G is convex and seek a local generalized Nash equilibrium.
037   Mathematical games are commonly studied in decision-making scenarios involving multiple agents
038   in control theory (Isaacs, 1999), economics (Roth, 2002; Rubinstein, 1982), and computer science
039   (Roughgarden, 2010). In particular, several problems of interest have a natural zero-sum game
040   formulation, such as training generative adversarial networks (Goodfellow et al., 2014), pursuit-
041   evasion scenarios (Isaacs, 1999), and robust optimization (Ben-Tal et al., 2009).
042   Several recent efforts (Fiez et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2020; Chinchilla et al., 2023; Daskalakis et al.,
043   2023) consider a closely related minimax variant of Game 1, minx maxy f (x, y); however, (local)
044   minimax solutions can differ from (local) Nash equilibria in general nonconvex-nonconcave settings.
045   This difference arises from the order of agent interactions. At a Nash solution of Game 1, players
046   controlling x and y act simultaneously. In contrast, minimax points correspond to Stackelberg
047   equilibria and assert a sequential order of play: x acts first, then y follows. We highlight this fact to
048   point out that under the assumptions of Game 1, the set of all local Nash points is a subset of the set
049   of all local Stackelberg points (Mazumdar et al., 2020; Ratliff et al., 2016). In particular, local Nash
050
      and Stackelberg points have the same first-order conditions but different second-order conditions.
051   The success of first-order gradient methods for single-agent learning problems made gradient descent
052   ascent (GDA), the multi-agent analog of gradient descent, a natural starting point for solving Game
053   1. The GDA algorithm tries to find a critical point of f , i.e., where ‚ąáf = 0. GDA is known to get
      trapped in limit cycles even in the most straightforward convex-concave setting, and several works


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      have tried to modify the gradient dynamics by including second-order information to avoid this
055   entrapment and direct the solution towards a stationary point of the dynamics (BenańĪm and Hirsch,
056   1999; Daskalakis et al., 2017; Hommes and Ochea, 2012; Mertikopoulos et al., 2018; Mescheder
057   et al., 2017; Gidel et al., 2019). However, outside of the convex-concave setting, these methods
058   can converge to critical points that are not Nash equilibria. This behavior is due to the particular
059   structure of second-order derivatives of f with respect to x and y, and while they do not arise in the
060   single-agent settings, they are widely documented in the multi-agent zero-sum game setting (Balduzzi
061   et al., 2018; Mazumdar et al., 2020; Ratliff et al., 2016).
062   To guarantee that an algorithm only converges to local Nash equilibria, the algorithm‚Äôs gradient
063   dynamics must not have any non-Nash stable equilibrium points. To the best of our knowledge,
064   only two previous works, local symplectic surgery (LSS) (Mazumdar et al., 2019) and curvature
065   exploitation for the saddle point problem (CESP) (Adolphs et al., 2019), have such guarantees for
066   the unconstrained nonconvex-nonconcave version of Game 1. However, neither of these methods
067   provides any convergence rate analysis. Further, these works do not discuss the constrained setting
068   of Game 1. A variety of Bregman proximal algorithms do find local min-max points in constrained,
069
      nonconvex-nonconcave settings with at best linear rates of convergence; however, they operate under
      the restrictive, blanket assumption that every critical point of f is a local Nash equilibrium (Azizian
070
      et al., 2024), which is not generally true in nonconvex-nonconcave settings.
071
072   In this paper, we introduce second-order algorithms to solve Game 1. We highlight our specific
073   contributions below:
074
            1. We introduce Discrete-time Nash Dynamics (DND), a discrete-time dynamical system that
075
               provably converges to only local Nash equilibria of the unconstrained version of Game
076
               1 with a linear local convergence rate, while previous related work does not provide any
077            convergence rates.
078
            2. We modify this dynamical system and construct an algorithm, Second Order Nash Dynamics
079
               (SecOND), which can converge superlinearly to the neighborhood of a point that satisfies
080
               first-order local Nash conditions.
081
082
            3. We discuss the constrained setting of Game 1, where G is a convex set. In this case, we use
               Euclidean projections to modify DND and develop an algorithm, Second-order Constrained
083
               Nash Dynamics (SeCoND), which finds a local generalized Nash Equilibrium point. In
084
               contrast, previous work either does not consider this constrained setting and/or is restricted
085            to the convex-concave case.

      5    C ONCLUSION , L IMITATIONS , AND F UTURE W ORK
494
495
      We have provided algorithms that provably converge to only local Nash equilibria in smooth, possibly
496   nonconvex-nonconcave, two-player zero-sum games in the unconstrained (DND, SecOND) and
497   convex-constrained (SeCoND) settings. We have shown that DND has a linear local convergence
498   rate and that SecOND approaches a neighborhood around a fixed point superlinearly. In contrast,
499   the most closely related existing approaches for this setting have no established convergence rates.
500   Empirical results demonstrate that DND and SecOND outperformed previous related works in two
501   test problems.
502
503   Limitations and Future Work. There are three key limitations of the proposed method. (i) All
504   approaches in this paper require second-order information, which can be prohibitively expensive to
505   obtain or compute in high-dimensional scenarios. Unfortunately, the fundamental links this problem
506
      shares with dynamical system theory necessitate second-order information to provide convergence
      guarantees. (ii) Like other approaches (Mazumdar et al., 2019), we require Assumption 3 in order to
507
      ensure that the critical points of the dynamics we introduce coincide with first-order local Nash points.
508
      Finally, (iii) as in other work on zero-sum Nash games (Adolphs et al., 2019; Mazumdar et al., 2019;
509   2020), we can only provide local convergence analysis, and cannot ensure that the dynamics globally
510   converge (even if local Nash points do exist). Addressing this limitation is a key direction of future
511   work. Future work should also aim to relax Assumption 3, and use algorithms introduced in this
512   paper as building blocks for solving dynamic zero-sum games with simultaneous decision-making
513   occurring across multiple time stages.

      We include this note to give the reader intuition about our Assumptions‚Äô validity.
875
876          ‚ÄĘ Assumptions 1, 2, and 4 are standard in the literature (for example, in Adolphs et al. (2019);
877            Mazumdar et al. (2019); Azizian et al. (2024)). Because we propose second-order methods,
878            Assumption 1 ensures that the objective offers meaningful first and second-order derivatives.
879            Assumption 2 ensures that the Jacobians of any dynamical system introduced in the paper
880            can be analyzed at a critical/fixed point.
881          ‚ÄĘ In theory, Assumption 3 is required to ensure that all fixed points of the introduced algorithms
882            are critical points of the GDA dynamics (6), and vice versa. Other previous methods have
883            also had to make similar assumptions for this very purpose (Mazumdar et al., 2019), and
884            the assumption we make is easier to check in comparison. The toy and GAN examples in
885            Section 4 do not satisfy Assumption 3, yet we still observe good empirical performance by
886            our proposed approaches.
887          ‚ÄĘ Intuition for Assumption 3: Consider some smooth function g(x) and the corresponding
888            problem minx‚ąąRn g(x). Any optimization algorithm will produce iterates of the form
889            xk+1 ‚Üź xk ‚ąí őĪk pk (or xŐá = ‚ąípk in continuous-time). In particular, for any Newton-
890            like algorithm, pk = Hk ‚ąág(xk ) (for example, Hk can be the regularized hessian inverse
891
               (‚ąá2xx g(xk ) + őĽI)‚ąí1 , for some őĽ ‚Č• 0). In order to ensure convergence to a minima,
               one of the conditions developed in nonlinear optimization thoery is that pk must not be
892
