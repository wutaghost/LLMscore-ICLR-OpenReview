011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Large Language Models (LLMs) are cutting-edge generative AI models built on
014            transformer architecture, which tend to be highly memory-intensive when per-
015
               forming real-time inference. Various strategies have been developed to enhance
               the end-to-end inference speed for LLMs, one of which is speculative decoding.
016
               This technique involves running a smaller LLM (draft model) for inference over
017
               a defined window size, denoted as γ, while simultaneously being validated by the
018            larger LLM (target model). Choosing the optimal γ value and the draft model is
019            essential for unlocking the potential of speculative decoding. But it is difficult to
020            do due to the complicated influence from various factors, including the nature of
021            the task, the hardware in use, and the combination of the large and small models.
022            This paper introduces on-the-fly adaption of speculative decoding, a solution that
023            dynamically adapts the choices to maximize the efficiency of speculative decoding
024            for LLM inferences. As a drop-in solution, it needs no offline benchmarking or
025            training. Experiments show that the solution can lead to 3.55-16.48% speed im-
026
               provement over the standard speculative decoding, and 1.2-3.4× over the default
               LLMs.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Large Language Models (LLMs) are state-of-the-art generative AI models built on transformer-
032   based blocks (Brown et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022). LLMs have an enormous number of pa-
033   rameters, and recent research not only focuses on training them efficiently but also explores how to
034   optimize inference performance. In fact, there is evidence indicating that even small improvement
035   in LLM inference speeds can result in significant cost savings. For instance, Google’s infrastruc-
036   ture optimizations have demonstrated that improving inference efficiency can lead to substantial
037   reductions in operational expenses. In large-scale deployments, a 1% increase in speed can indeed
038   translate into millions of dollars saved (AI, 2023; Cloud, 2023).
039   Due to the autoregressive and memory-intensive nature of LLMs, it is challenging to optimize its
040   inference throughput. Sampling for a new token depends on the previously generated tokens. Re-
041   searchers are exploring mainly two approaches to circumvent this sequential dependence for more
042   efficient parallel executions. One is to change the model architecture thus sampling granularity to
043   parallelize the decoding process. Medusa (Cai et al., 2024), for example, introduces multiple decod-
044   ing heads to generate tokens in parallel; Lookahead Decoding (Jacobi Decoding) (Fu et al., 2024)
045
      generates multiple tokens in parallel using nonlinear systems. This approach changes the neural
      architecture and hence requires new training, the high costs of which makes them difficult to adopt
046
      in practice. The other approach is speculative decoding (Leviathan et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023).
047
      This approach first runs inference with a smaller LLM Mq , called the draft model, to generate the
048   next γ tokens (γ is called speculation window size). After generating one window of tokens (called
049   a speculation step), a verification step uses the Large LLM Mp , called the target model, to validate
050   those tokens in parallel. Upon finding the first incorrect token, the execution throws away the rest
051   of the tokens speculated by the draft model in that window and corrects the first rejected token (or
052   appends a new token when all of the tokens are accepted). From there, it continues the speculation-
053   validation process. This approach allows direct use of the pretrained LLMs, making it easier for
      adoption.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      What is crucial for unlocking the potential of speculative decoding is to choose the best speculation
055   window length, γ, and the best draft model to use. The best choices depend on the nature of the in-
056   ference task, target model, software stack, hardware, and resource availability or workload changes
057   (if running in a cloud). Suboptimal choices may not only substantially throttle the benefits but some-
058   times cause slowdowns to the inference (see Section 6). The standard approach (Leviathan et al.,
059   2023; Chen et al., 2023) relies on offline trial-and-error-based search, which not only takes long
060   time, but more importantly, cannot adapt to the changes in the tasks, target models, software stacks,
061   hardware or other runtime changes. A recent study, SpecDec++ (Huang et al., 2024), attempts to
062   improve it through a machine learning model. It trains a ResNet with many samples in offline data
063
      collection, and uses it to predict, at each generated token in actual inferences, whether the execution
      should stop speculation, so as to adapt the speculation window. Although the work shows some
064
      improvement in experiments, it requires hundreds or thousands of GPU-hours (Section 6.2) to train
065
      the model for one target-draft pair on one kind of task and one software and hardware configuration.
066   Modern LLM servers often host many LLMs and their variants (e.g., different quantizations, with
067   Lora or other fine-tuning models) and have various software and hardware configurations and task
068   types, making the solution difficult to adopt in production systems.
069
      This paper describes the first-known exploration of on-the-fly adaptive speculation, a drop-in so-
070
      lution that adapts speculative decoding at runtime without ahead-of-time training. Our explo-
071
      ration covers both speculation window size γ and the choice of draft models. It experiments with
072   several agile online methods for the adaptation, including a state machine-based mechanism, a
073   cache-enabled state machine-based method, a reinforcement learning-based approach, and a token
074   accuracy-based online window size optimization method. It analyzes these methods and evaluates
075   them on four LLMs across three GPU models and four types of inference tasks. The results show that
076   on-the-fly adaptive speculation, especially the online window size optimization, can deliver similar
077   or even better improvements than the prior method that uses extensive ahead-of-time trainings, lead-
078   ing to 3.55-16.48% speed improvement over the standard speculative decoding, and 1.2-3.4× over
079   the default LLMs. As a drop-in solution, this new approach needs no model changes, ahead-of-time
080
      preparation, lengthy training, or extensive benchmarking. It automatically adapts the optimal win-
      dow size and directs the requests to the appropriate draft models for speculation, especially suitable
081
      for large LLM service providers.
082
083   It is worth mentioning that besides adapting the speculation process, there are some other methods
084   explored in recent studies to improve speculative decoding (Li et al., 2024; Yan et al., 2024; Spector
085   & Re, 2023; Hooper et al., 2023). Online Speculative Decoding (Liu et al., 2023), for instance, uses
086
      knowledge distillation to continuously train the smaller draft model during inference, enhancing
      performance. SpecInfer (Miao et al., 2023) introduces a tree-based decoding algorithm that uses
087
      the draft model to speculate multiple possible token sequences in parallel and then validates each
088
      of these sequences by the target model to keep the longest validated one. The on-the-fly adaptive
089   speculation proposed in this current paper is from a different angle. It is hence complementary to
090   those studies in the sense that it can be integrated into the speculation process in those solutions to
091   further improve their effectiveness.

099   2.1   L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS
100
101   Large language models (LLMs) are natural language processing (NLP) models built on transformer
102   architecture. Due to their large capacity, LLMs can handle a wide range of tasks, including trans-
103   lation, code generation, and question answering. Prompt engineering involves providing the LLM
104
      with tailored instructions and guidance to improve the quality of task-specific outputs.
105   For an LLM workflow, training is one-time, and it can take several months to complete; the model
106   is then deployed for inference, which typically requires continuous serving, and often, on a large
107   scale (e.g., over 100 million users weekly). When serving LLM inference in real-time, it is mostly
      memory-bound. This means that it is bounded by the memory bandwidth of the hardware it is

      running on. More details about the memory-bound nature of LLM inference can be found in Ap-
109   pendix A. The cost of inference exceeds the cost of training over time.

      2.2    G UESS - AND -V ERIFY IN LLM S
112
113   In LLM inference, the tokens generated later are dependent on the tokens generated earlier. This
114   sequential dependency of autoregressive decoding in LLMs has led to the development of new tech-
115   niques aimed at parallelizing the decoding process. Given that text is tokenized, some tokens can
116   be easier or harder to predict by a lower-parameter LLM. This has sparked a new area of research
117   known as “guess-and-verify” optimization (Li et al., 2024; Yan et al., 2024; Spector & Re, 2023;
118   Hooper et al., 2023). In this approach, smaller draft models efficiently guess a number of tokens,
119   which are then verified in parallel by a larger target model. It is a lossless optimization, maintaining
120
      the accuracy of the results.
121   Speculative decoding is one typical “guess-and-verify” approach in LLM optimization. In this tech-
122   nique, when an LLM samples logits, it essentially predicts the probabilities of the next token. Spec-
123   ulative decoding takes advantage of this by allowing a smaller model to guess the easier tokens
124   based on its own sampling of the distribution. These tokens are then verified against a larger, more
125   accurate model.
126   In speculative decoding, the process involves guessing a set of tokens from the smaller model Mq
127   within a fixed window size, γ, and then verifying these γ tokens against the larger model Mp by
128   sampling γ + 1 tokens in parallel. If all tokens are accepted, the γ + 1 tokens are appended to the
129   generated sequence, and the process continues. If one token (say (i + 1)th) is rejected, the algorithm
130   accepts the i correct tokens, resample the (i + 1)th from an adjusted distribution in the validation,
131   and continues. The speculation and verification process is detailed in Algorithm 1.

      4.2   OTHER D ROP - IN S PECULATION M ETHODS
252
253   Besides the online optimization method, we have explored three other methods for on-the-fly γ
254   adaption.
255
      Finite State Machine (FSM)-Based Speculation. A finite state machine-based predictor (Hennessy
256
      & Patterson, 2017) is similar to an n-bit saturating counter used in branch prediction. The mecha-
257
      nism works by decreasing γ by 1 if a token from the draft model is rejected, and increasing γ by 1
258   if all tokens are accepted. During benchmarking, We still select a value for γ, but it is considered
259   an upper limit, γmax . If the draft and target models’ distributions significantly differ, γ will remain
260   low, potentially even at 0. Conversely, if the models align closely, γ should increase, approaching
261   γmax . We consider this approach particularly effective for natural language processing because cer-
262   tain parts of a sentence—like common phrases or syntactically predictable structures—are easier
263   for a smaller draft model to predict. In contrast, more unique or complex sub-sequences might be
264   harder to guess. By adaptively changing γ based on the previous token validations, we create a
265   reward system that exploits patterns and predictable structures in autoregressive generation.
266   Cache-Enabled FSM-Based Speculation. We adjust γ based on the context provided by the prompt
267   and the history of generated tokens. In settings like question-answering, an LLM often reiterates or
268   directly responds based on the context given by the user. Therefore, the user’s input can inform
269   predictions about the type of response the LLM will generate. Specifically, this approach includes a
      token cache that updates after every sampling step. Initially, the cache is populated with tokens in the


                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      prompt, set up before the prefill stage. As new tokens are sampled and validated during speculation,
271   the cache is updated with any previously unseen tokens. γ is then adjusted dynamically: It increases
272   by one if a validated token is already in the cache, and by an additional one when all speculated
273   tokens are accepted. Conversely, if none of the accepted tokens are in the cache, it decreases by one.
274   We see that this approach is particularly effective for structured tasks like QA chatbot interactions or
275   code completion, where context and history play a significant role. However, it may be less effective
276   for short prompts expecting broad and diverse content, such as tasks that require informative or
277   creative responses. In such cases, the lack of initial context or history means the cache is less
278   informative, making γ adjustments less effective, potentially leading to performance similar to the
279
      more simplistic state-based adaptation.
280   Reinforcement Learning-Based Speculation. We in addition explored a reinforcement learning
281   (RL) based approach. We use a Q-learning agent to choose a γ. The modification to the algorithm
282   is detailed in Algorithm 2 in Appendix B.4. The agent takes the previous states of γ as inputs and
283   applies an action after each validation step.

      Comprehensive Chat Dataset. We include evaluations for a comprehensive chat dataset

524
      based methods (Cai et al., 2024; Li et al.). The on-the-fly adaptation of speculative decoding is
525
      complementary to the tree-based decoding. By adaptively changing the draft tree depth, our drop-in
526   method can adjust the draft token sequence length on the fly and thus improving decoding per-
527   formance. We apply our method to the state-of-the-art EAGLE-2 (Li et al., 2024) and report the
528   results in Appendix C.4. On the MT-Bench (Zheng et al., 2023), we achieve up to 3.56× speedups
529   compared to original autoregressive decoding, and an additional 4.2% improvement over SOTA.

      7     C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this paper, we propose on-the-fly adaptation for speculative decoding to accelerate LLM infer-
534   ences. As a pure software approach, it introduces a two-level adaptation for draft model adaptation
535   and online window size adaptation with no ahead-of-time profiling or training, providing a drop-in
536   optimization for existing LLMs. We experimentally demonstrate the effectiveness of this method
537   and show 3.55% to 16.48% speedups compared to the speculative decoding, and 1.2× to 3.4× over
538   the default LLMs without speculative decoding. Among the several online adaptive methods, we
539   found that the token accuracy-based online window size optimization method works the best, con-
      sistently outperforming other methods in terms of the overall LLM throughput.
