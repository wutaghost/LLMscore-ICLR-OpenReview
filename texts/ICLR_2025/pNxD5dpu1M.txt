009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Large agent networks are abundant in applications and nature and pose difficult
012            challenges in the field of multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) due to their
013            computational and theoretical complexity. While graphon mean field games and
014            their extensions provide efficient learning algorithms for dense and moderately
015            sparse agent networks, the case of realistic sparser graphs remains largely unsolved.
016
               Thus, we propose a novel cooperative mean field game (MFG) model based on the
               large class of Chung-Lu graphs including power law networks with coefficients
017
               above two. Besides a theoretical analysis, we design scalable learning algorithms
018
               which especially apply to the challenging class of graph sequences with finite first
019            moment and infinite second moment. We compare our model and algorithms for
020            various examples on synthetic and real world networks with MFG algorithms based
021            on Lp graphons and graphexes. As it turns out, our approach outperforms existing
022            methods in many examples and on various networks due to the special design
023            aiming at an important, but so far hard to solve class of MARL problems.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
      Despite the rapid developments in the field of multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) over the
028
      last years, systems with many agents remain hard to solve in general (Canese et al., 2021; Gronauer
029
      & Diepold, 2022). Mean field games (MFGs) (Caines et al., 2006; Lasry & Lions, 2007) are a
030   promising way to model large agent problems in a computationally tractable way while providing a
031   solid theoretical framework at the same time. The idea of MFGs is to abstract large, homogeneous
032   crowds of small agents into a single probability distribution, the mean field. While MFGs have been
033   used in various areas ranging from pedestrian flows (Achdou & LauriГЁre, 2020) to oil production
034   (Bauso et al., 2016), the assumption of indistinguishable agents is not fulfilled in many applications.
035
      A particularly important class of MARL problems are those with many connected agents. Initially,
036   these agent networks were modeled by combining the graph theoretical concept of graphons (LovГЎsz,
037   2012) with MFGs, resulting in graphon MFGs (GMFGs) (Caines & Huang, 2019; 2021; Cui &
038   Koeppl, 2022; Zhang et al., 2024). Since GMFGs only model often unrealistic dense graphs,
039   subsequently MFG models based on Lp graphons (Borgs et al., 2018b; 2019) and graphexes (Veitch
040   & Roy, 2015; Caron & Fox, 2017; Borgs et al., 2018a) were developed, called LPGMFGs and
041   GXMFGs, respectively (Fabian et al., 2023; 2024). While these models facilitate learning algorithms
042   in moderately sparse networks, they exclude sparser topologies. Formally, (LP)GMFGs and GXMFGs
043   are designed exclusively for graphs with expected average degree going to infinity.
044   The learning literature contains various approaches to finding optimal behavior in MFGs, see LauriГЁre
045   et al. (2022a) for an overview. For example, Subramanian et al. (2022) develop a decentralized
046   learning algorithm where agents are able to independently learn policies, while Guo et al. (2019; 2023)
047   focus on Q-learning methods for general MFGs. For the case of cooperative MFGs without network
048   interactions, also referred to as mean field control, various learning approaches exist (Ruthotto et al.,
049   2020; Carmona et al., 2023; Gu et al., 2023). However, we are aware of only one work by Hu et al.
050
      (2023b) which learns policies for cooperative MFGs on dense networks, but not on sparse ones.
051   To learn policies for even sparser networks, we require a suitable graph theoretical framework, the
052   well-known Chung-Lu (CL) random graph model (Aiello et al., 2000; 2001; Chung & Lu, 2002;
053   2006). The CL model aligns with our aim to model sparse, large agent networks because: (i) it can
      generate sparse networks, e.g. power laws, in a scalable way; (ii) it has a solid theoretical foundation

054
      with convergence results; (iii) it possesses properties which are beneficial for the design of efficient
055   approximate learning algorithms; (iv) it is conceptually simple despite its flexibility and rich structure.
056   These points are explained and discussed in more detail in the next sections.

058
      Leveraging CL graphs, we formulate the new class of Chung-Lu cooperative MFGs (CLCMFGs).
      CLCMFGs provide a theoretically well-motivated framework for learning agent behavior in chal-
059
      lenging large networks where the average expected degree is finite, but the degree variance may
060
      diverge to infinity. On the algorithmic side, we provide a two systems approximation of CLCMFGs
061   and corresponding learning algorithms to approximately learn optimal behaviour in these complex
062   agent networks. Finally, we evaluate our novel CLCMFG learning approach for multiple problems
063   on synthetic and real-world networks and compare it to different existing methods mentioned above.
064   Overall, our contributions can be summarized as:
065
066          вҖў We introduce CLCMFGs to model large cooperative agent populations on very sparse graphs
067            with finite expected average degree:
068          вҖў We give a rigorous theoretical analysis and motivation for CLCMFGs;
069          вҖў We provide a two systems approximation and scalable learning algorithms for CLCMFGs;
070
             вҖў We show the capabilities of our CLCMFG learning approach on synthetic and real world
071
               networks for different examplary problems.

      Advantages. CL graphs are a flexible and efficient framework for generating large, sparse graph
      sequences. They have crucial advantages over other well-established graph generation approaches
148
      like the configuration model (CM) (Bender & Canfield, 1978; Wormald, 1980; BollobГЎs, 1998).
149
      Most notably, the CM generates multigraphs instead of simple graphs and the number of multiedges
150   increases drastically as the vertex degrees increase. Consequently, the CM is suboptimal for generating
151   graphs with a significant fraction of high degree nodes such as power law networks. Compared to the
152   classical BarabГЎsi-Albert model (BarabГЎsi & Albert, 1999) which only generates power law networks
153   with coefficient three (BollobГЎs et al., 2001), CL graphs are more flexible and can generate power law
154   graphs with any coefficient larger than two as well as other network types. Besides its rich class of
155   generable graphs, the CL model is theoretically well-founded and efficient implementations for large
156   graph generation exist (Fasino et al., 2021). For a detailed discussion on the mentioned differences,
157   see for example Chung & Lu (2002, Section 7).
158   Furthermore, as discussed in the introduction, the CL model can generate graphs with finite average
159   expected degree which is a crucial advantage over both graphexes and (Lp) graphons. Finally,
160   the neighbor degree distribution in CL graphs is described reasonably well by Heuristic 1 which
161   provides the foundation for our two systems approximation. Both Heuristic 1 and the two systems
      approximation are defined and explained in detail in the following sections.

351   6    E XAMPLES
352
353   We consider four problems briefly described here. Problem details can be found in Appendix C.
354
355   Susceptible-Infected-Susceptible/Recovered (SIS/SIR). The classical SIS model (Kermack &
356   McKendrick, 1927; Brauer, 2005) is a benchmark in the MFG learning literature (LauriГЁre et al.,
357   2022b; Zhou et al., 2024). Agents are infected or susceptible, resulting in the state space X := {S, I},
358   and decide to protect themselves or not. The infection probability increases without protection, and
359   with the number of infected neighbors. The SIR model (Hethcote, 2000; Doncel et al., 2022) is an
360   extension of SIS where agents can also be in an immune, recovered state R such that X := {S, I, R}.
361
362   Graph coloring (Color). Inspired by graph coloring (Jensen & Toft, 2011; Barenboim & Elkin,
363   2022), the states are finitely many colors on a circle and a target color distribution is given. Agents
364   stay at their color or costly move to a neighboring color. The objective decreases for deviations from
365
      the target color distribution and if neighbors of an agent have neighboring colors to the agentвҖҷs color.
366
367   Rumor. In the rumor model (Maki & Thompson, 1973; Gani, 2000; Cui et al., 2022), agents are
368
      either aware of a rumor (aware state A) or they have not heard the rumor (ignorant state I). Aware
      agents decide whether they spread the rumor to their neighbors or not. They are awarded for spreading
369
      the rumor to unaware agents but loose reputation for telling the rumor to already aware agents.

      8                         C ONCLUSION
467
      We have introduced the novel CLCMFGs which can depict agent networks with finite expected
468
      degree and diverging variance. After a theoretical analysis, we provided a practical two systems
469   approximation which was then leveraged to design scalable learning algorithms. Finally, we evaluated
470   the performance of our model and learning algorithms for different problems on synthetic and
471   real-world datasets and compared them to existing methods. For future work, one could extend the
472   CLCMFG model to various types of MFGs, e.g. to partial observability or agents under bounded
473   rationality. We hope that CLCMFGs and the corresponding learning approach prove to be a versatile
474   and useful tool for researchers across various applied research areas.
