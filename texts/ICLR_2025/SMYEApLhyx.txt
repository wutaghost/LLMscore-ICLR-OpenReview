009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               One of the main organizational principles of artificial and biological intelligence
012
               systems is their reliance on signed inputs: positive and negative weights in artifi-
013            cial networks, and excitatory and inhibitory synapses in the brain. However, little
014            is known about the role of inhibitory activity in high-level visual cortex such as
015            inferotemporal cortex, or how artificial neural networks (ANNs) trained for ob-
016            ject recognition segregate their learned representations into positive and negative
017            weights. Here, we dissected high-level visual mechanisms in ANNs trained with
018            ImageNet. We investigated how learned representations of ANN classification
019            units depended on their positive or negative inputs using ablation experiments and
020            feature visualization. We found that unit representations changed more when ab-
021
               lating positive- vs. negative inputs. Object-related features were abolished when
               ablating positive inputs, while still preserving background textures. This effect
022
               was more pronounced in adversarially trained robust networks. This segregation
023
               persisted in networks trained with unsupervised learning, but was not present in
024            a ResNet18 trained with Tanh instead of ReLU. We found a consistent functional
025            segregation when we trained models to replicate the activity of neurons in monkey
026            visual cortex, across the ventral stream (V1, V4, and IT). Feature visualization of
027            the neuron models produced images containing local features preferred by actual
028            neurons. Analogous to units trained for classification, the learned representations
029            of units trained to simulate neurons changed more upon ablating positive than
030            negative inputs. We conclude that ANNs for classification segregate object or
031            foreground information into the positive weights, with background or contextual
032
               information into the negative weights, in their last layer before softmax. These re-
               sults hint at the relevance of signal rectification and inhibition into shaping feature
033
               selectivity in the primate ventral stream, a hypothesis we are testing in vivo.

037   1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039   Artificial and biological intelligence systems rely on units that influence each other via signed mech-
040   anisms: hidden units interact via positive and negative weights in artificial networks, and neurons
041   interact via excitatory and inhibitory synapses in the brain. Cortical neurons divide into types defined
042   by their genetic, anatomic and functional properties (Zeng, 2022). Excitatory neurons are thought
043   to compute the main features, and inhibitory neurons are thought to provide contextual information
044   to excitatory neurons, gate and route the information in cortical circuits. Object classification in
045
      humans relies on the occipitotemporal visual system, the ”ventral stream”. Neurons in the ventral
      stream are selective to more complex visual features along the hierarchy, similar to how features
046
      increase in complexity along the depth of convolutional neural networks (CNNs) trained for object
047
      classification. In the early visual system, including the retina, lateral geniculate nucleus, and primary
048   visual cortex (V1), inhibitory neurons provide lateral inhibition, which defines the center-surround
049   receptive field organization. Lateral inhibition spatially sharpens receptive fields and enhances fea-
050   ture selectivity by suppressing redundant information in the surround of the excitatory receptive
051   field center. This is clear when the excitatory receptive field features are spots or sine-wave gratings.
052   However, the role of inhibition in the highest levels of the hierarchy in the primate ventral stream
053   remains unkown, particularly in V4, posterior, central, and anterior inferotemporal cortex (pIT, cIT,
      aIT).

054
      ANNs compute with positive and negative weights, analogous to excitation and inhibition in the
055   brain. However, it is not known how visual information is parsed across positive and negative
056   weights. Particularly, for object classification networks, it is not clear how the selectivity to different
057   object categories emerges in their output layers, where each unit corresponds to one object category.
058   One hypothesis is that information is largely segregated across absolute weight strengths (Li et al.,
059   2023). Because classification CNNs are good models of the ventral stream, we hypothesize that
060   CNN units might also segregate different kinds of visual information into their positive and negative
061   input weights.
062   To test the hypothesis of functional segregation across positive and negative weights in classification
063   networks, we performed ablation experiments and feature visualizations of output units of different
064   ImageNet-trained CNNs. By performing ablations of varying magnitude, we studied how visual
065   information was organized across and within excitatory and inhibitory weights, and if and how dif-
066   ferent ranges of weight strengths corresponded to different parts of objects and backgrounds. We
067   found that both positive and negative ablations resulted in activity changes, but only the ablation
068   of positive weights significantly changed the preferred images obtained from feature visualizations.
069
      The ablation of negative inputs resulted in images with contextual variations, i.e., changes in colors
      of the object in the foreground or in the background. For example, negative input ablation resulted in
070
      images with white backgrounds for a robust ResNet50. Gradual removal of positive inputs produced
071
      gradual deformations of the object, but not in a parts-based fashion. Surprisingly, such representa-
072   tional changes upon input ablations were more pronounced in robust networks, which are trained to
073   resist noise perturbations to images (Szegedy et al., 2014; Salman et al., 2020; Elsayed et al., 2018).
074   Total positive input ablation led to the removal of objects while preserving background features,
075   this was also quantified by an object detection network, YOLOv7 (Wang et al., 2022). To test if
076   this functional segregation generalized to other objective functions such as prediction, we used the
077   same networks as models that predicted the images responses of neurons in the ventral stream. The
078   models were simply a re-weighting of the same inputs to the final fully-connected layers of Ima-
079   geNet CNNs obtained via linear regression between the neuron responses and the neural network
080
      penultimate layer activations. Thus, if neurons had the same object bias as CNNs, they will show
      a similar functional segregation to CNNs. And if neurons did not have such object bias, but still
081
      showed a functional segregation, it would reveal another form of functional segregation. To identify
082
      their preferred features of the biological neurons that were modeled, we used a (model-free) closed-
083   loop image synthesis approach that bypassed the CNN-fitting stage (Ponce et al., 2019). Feature
084   visualization of the neuron-fitted CNN models (neuron-model units) produced images containing
085   features that were also exciting to the the neuron and qualitatively similar to the biological model-
086   free features. Neurons responded more to the visualized preferred images of the model than to the
087   natural images used for model training. Consistent with the classification units, neuron-model units
088   were also more robust to negative than positive input ablation. Yet, unlike the classification units,
089   the preferred images of neuron model units did not seem to contain objects. In sum, our work re-
090   veals that for object classification and neuron model units, respectively, the foregrounds or preferred
091
      visual features are represented in the positive input weights, while the backgrounds or contextual
      features are represented in the negative input weights.

094   2    R ELATED WORK
095
096
      Mechanistic interpretability of computer and biological vision There has been progress in
097   mechanistic interpretability in ANNs from work using perspectives from circuit dissection, like
098   those in neuroscience (Olah et al., 2020). This area of explainable artificial intelligence explains
099   model behavior by leveraging smaller network subgraphs to identify relevant features, how they
100   arise from input weights, and how they can be used to build new features hierarchically. It has
101   revealed motifs of positive and negative connections between related features that resemble the or-
102   ganization of the early visual system. Related work has focused on characterizing the object-shape-
103   and texture biases in feature visualizations by choosing sparse sets of weights to reconstruct indi-
104   vidual images (Li et al., 2023). Here we focus on the division between positive and negative inputs
105
      across the whole range of weight strengths, which was not covered in that study.
106
107   Feature visualization by closed-loop optimization To understand the information learned by
      neural networks, it serves to analyze their learned representations. For vision networks, both bio-


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      logical and artificial, the longest-standing approach is to generate images that strongly activate in-
109   dividual units, either by hand (Hubel & Wiesel, 1959) or more recently, using discriminative and/or
110   generative networks. In silico, where gradients are available, features can be obtained by performing
111   gradient-ascent from the target unit to the image pixels (Erhan et al., 2009; Nguyen et al., 2016a;b;
112   Olah et al., 2017). Because gradients are not available in vivo, gradient-free algorithms have been
113   developed to optimize images preferred by biological neurons in real-time (Ponce et al., 2019; Xiao
114   & Kreiman, 2020; Wang & Ponce, 2022). These gradient-free algorithms rely on black-box opti-
115   mization of an input to a generative adversarial network (GAN). This constraints the image search
116   space to the natural image priors learned by the GAN, avoiding high-frequency noise which can also
117
      be highly activating to the target unit but does not seem to relate well to natural images (Nguyen
      et al., 2016a). Other approaches rely on first training a neural network to predict neuronal responses
118
      to images, and then performing gradient-ascent on the network (Bashivan et al., 2019; Walker et al.,
119
      2019). Those methods have been largely used with gray-scale images. Here, we deal with color
120   images using the gradient-free approach in both our investigations in CNNs and our experimental
121   recordings of non-human primates.
122
123
      Robustness Neural networks are susceptible to adversarial attacks, where noise that is nearly im-
124
      perceptible by humans can be added to natural images, changing output classification (Szegedy et al.,
125
      2014; Salman et al., 2020; Elsayed et al., 2018). A proposed solution to the adversarial attacks is
126   robust training, which introduces noise (or another attacks) into the training phase of the network
127   with the aim to build resistance against that particular attack. In theory, robust networks should
128   function more like the primate brain, which shows limited vulnerability to such attacks. Here, we
129   study how trained robustness relates to image representations after weight ablations.

      Networks We performed our ablation studies in CNNs pretrained on the ImageNet dataset:
137   AlexNet (Krizhevsky et al., 2012), VGG16 (Simonyan & Zisserman, 2015), ResNet50 (He et al.,

150   Feature visualization For each ablation condition, we performed feature visualization by optimiz-
151   ing a GAN latent code to create an activity-maximizing image. We used this closed-loop, zeroth-
152   order-search approach to allow comparison with our neuronal experiments, where gradient ascent
153
      would not be possible. To increase the span of the stimulus space, we used two GANs: AlexNet
      fc6 DeePSiM (Dosovitskiy & Brox, 2016) which can render textures and objects, and BigGAN
154
      (Brock et al., 2019) that can render photo-realistic images with objects. For optimization, we used
155
      a variant of covariance matrix adaptation evolutionary strategy or CMAES (Wang & Ponce, 2022;
156   Loshchilov, 2015). We optimized ten images per GAN, resulting in 20 feature visualizations per
157   output unit and ablation condition. Diverse visualizations better capture the multifaceted high-level
158   representations in CNNs (Nguyen et al., 2016b). For our examples, we show the best of the 20 vi-
159   sualizations, but used all for quantitative analyses. For visualizations of neural networks predicting
160   biological neuron responses, due to experimental time restrictions, we used five visualizations per
161   ablation condition, via DeePSim only. Our experiments are performed in a PC with Nvidia 4090
      GPU, and each visualization takes about 3 mins.

      Feature analysis We computed image similarity using an ensemble of CNNs, including AlexNet,
163   ResNet50, and ResNet50 with robustness in L∞ ∈ {0.5, 1, 2, 4, 8}, inspired by (Feather et al., 2023)
164   And confirmed the results with LPIPS (Zhang et al., 2018) in the appendix. We computed their
165   activations and defined similarity as the average pairwise cosine similarity between control activity
166   vs input-ablated activity. We averaged the results over all networks. We computed objectness as the
167   maximum bounding box score provided by YOLOv7 (Wang et al., 2022).
168
169   Visual cortex electrophysiology We collected data from two animals (monkey C and monkey
170   D), each implanted chronically with multielectrode arrays of 32 or 16 channels (monkey C, N =
171   96 electrodes, monkey D, 64), in areas V1, V4 and posterior inferotemporal cortex (PIT). Some
172   electrodes captured the activity of single units, but most showed multi-unit activity (reflecting the
173   pooled activity of microclusters of neurons). The animals performed a simple fixation task, which
174
      required them to keep their eyes on a 0.25-diameter spot at the center of the screen, within a square
      fixation window measuring 0.7–1◦ per side. Images were presented for 100 milliseconds ON, 150-
175
      ms off, 4-5 images per trial, after which the animal received water or juice.
176
177
      Image dataset We collected a reference image dataset to activate neurons in the monkey along
178
      the hierarchy of V1, V4, and PIT. Because neurons vary in their preferred features, we constructed
179
      a dataset spanning the image space as represented by the neural embedding of ImageNet-trained
180   AlexNet. The embedding is the output of the last layer before softmax of AlexNet, a vector space
181   of 1000-dimensions. The images from this dataset also spanned uniformly the 1000-dimensional
182   output space of a semi-supervised trained network, trained on a billion images, ResNet50SS (Yalniz
183   et al., 2019). To define this embedding space, we performed PCA on the output activations from
184   AlexNet to the 50k ImageNet validation images, we kept the top 300 components (accounting for
185   about 95% of total explained variance). Then we partitioned the space into a defined number of
186   clusters k, according to the desired dataset size, using batched k-means to reduce computational
187   burden. After finding the k cluster centers, we could feed arbitrary images to the network, map
188
      them to the PCA space, and then pick the nearest neighbors to the cluster centers from the desired
      image space. In addition to the ImageNet validation set, we added other common neuroscience
189
      datasets (Brady et al., 2008; Kar et al., 2019; Allen et al., 2022; Hung et al., 2005) to form our image
190
      space. We selected k = 160 images, as a set that was diverse but small enough to be used in every
191   experimental session. We called this image dataset diverseSet .
192
193
      Models fit on neuronal activity We recorded responses of many neurons in the ventral stream to
194   diverseSet. We performed partial least-squares linear regression (80/20 train/test split) between the
195   neuron responses to images and the activations of the penultimate layer of AlexNet. We selected
196   one neuron or microcluster per experimental session, and performed the ablation and feature visu-
197   alizations in silico. Whenever possible, we also performed the feature visualization of the modeled
198   neuron in vivo using a gradient-free approach (Ponce et al., 2019), within the same experimental
199   session. To test whether features learned by the model were relevant to the biological neuron, we
200   recorded the neuronal responses to the preferred images of the model.

      in CNNs pretrained in ImageNet.

      classification.

342   4.2   S EGREGATION DEPENDS ON R E LU BUT NOT ON UNSUPERVISED PRETRAINING
343
344   To test whether training the networks under supervision for classification is required for the segrega-
345
      tion of object information to the positive weights, we performed ablation experiments in a network
      trained without supervision. We used the publicly available siamese network ResNet50SimSiam
346
      (Chen & He, 2020), consisting of a ResNet50 backbone trained without supervision, then its weights
347
      frozen, it was coupled to a fully-connected layer and fine-tuned to solve ImageNet1000. This net-
348   work also segregated the main features to the positive weights, but the features vanished under
349   smaller ablation strengths than the CNNs trained on classification (Fig. 20, 19). The representation
350   of this network also had small changes upon negative weight ablations. Thus, inputs from unsu-
351   pervised representations also organize themselves such that positive weights convey most of the
352   relevant features. We hypothesized that ReLU’s rectification, which yields non-negative activations,
353   causes a split into positive and negative weights. In ReLU networks, the weights define the sign
354   contributions to the next layer. Maximizing a unit’s activation involves enhancing positive inputs
355   and reducing negative ones, allowing relevant features to activate positive weights and suppress
356
      negative ones. Conversely, a network using a non-rectified activation function like Tanh can encode
      relevant features as positive inputs with positive activations and weights, or negative activations and
357
      weights. To investigate whether functional segregation is influenced by the shape of the activation
358
      function, we trained a ResNet18 model using the Tanh activation function instead of ReLU. Unlike
359   ReLU, the Tanh function is not rectified; it is anti-symmetric about zero and sigmoid-like, with out-
360   put values ranging from -1 to 1, centered at an input value of zero. Both ResNet18 networks were
361   trained using the FFCV library (Leclerc et al., 2023) for 16 epochs on the ImageNet 1000 dataset.
362   The top-5 classification accuracy was 0.797 for the network with Tanh activations and 0.870 for
363   the network with ReLU activations. The ResNet18-ReLU network behaved consistently to other
364   networks, being more susceptible to changes upon ablation of positive weights (Fig. 21, 22). In
365   contrast, the ResNet18-Tanh network exhibited similar changes in activity and representational sim-
366   ilarity for both positive and negative ablations, maintaining relevant features despite the elimination
367
      of either input polarity. Thus, rectification in activation functions is a critical factor in segregating
      features into positive and negative weights.

446   other tasks, we used the same inputs to classification units in AlexNet to predict responses of bio-
447   logical neurons in the ventral stream. We recorded responses from V1, V4, and IT cortex neurons to
448   a set of 160 diverse images, diverseSet, uniformly covering the embedding space of AlexNet. Then,
449
      we used partial least squares to do a linear regression between each neuron responses and the acti-
      vations from the penultimate layer of AlexNet (4096 units). While intermediate layers may provide
450
      better fits, we wanted to test if a simple reconfiguration of the same inputs that achieve ImageNet
451
      classification would still segregate information into positive and negative weights. We performed
452   the ablation experiments on the best neuron models (defined by the test r2 score, overall mean 0.274
453   with 0.096 std). Model weights had both positive and negative values with a ratio of 1.17 total posi-
454   tive to total negative weights (Fig. 16). The number of neurons modeled per area (V1, V4, pIT) is (7,
455   5, 23) for Monkey C and (1, 5, 18) for Monkey D. Thus, our results are largely representative of the
456   pIT cortex neurons. We presented the images from the feature visualizations of the neuron models
457   under ablations to the monkey in the same session. Because the image optimization of the model
458   predicted neuronal responses that were larger than the responses in the training data, the models ef-
459   fectively performed extrapolation — we found that the optimized images of the models activated the
460
      neurons more than the training set by over one standard deviation (Fig. 8, left). When possible, we
      also performed feature visualization in vivo for the modeled neuron. In these cases, we found that
461
      models based on just 160 images were able to capture the preferred visual features of the neuron
462
      (Fig. 8, right). While the neuron features obtained in vivo were spatially localized (procedure in
463   A.1), the model features obtained in silico were not not necessarily restricted to one location, and
464   appeared in several locations, also in mirrored or rotated versions. This reflects invariances in the
465   networks that may not exist in the neurons. Unlike the images from recognition units, images from
466   the neuron models did not resemble objects (Fig. 8, 9). The images from the neuron models under
467   positive input ablations elicited a consistent decrease of activity in the biological neurons providing
468   further support to the models despite the limited training dataset (Fig. 10, left panels). Interestingly,
469   the images from ablation experiments of single neuron models were also able to elicit changes in the
470   average response of the neuronal population. Thus, the feature changes elicited by weight ablation
471
      translate to meaningful changes in the images perceived beyond the modeled neurons to across the
      ventral stream (Fig. 10, rightmost panel).
472
473   Alike the representation changes of the recognition networks, representations of the neuron models
474   changed most with positive than negative ablations (Fig. 10). Therefore, the segregation of ablation
475   effects are not restricted to a classification objective, but also to a regression objective, as the neuron
476   models are just the same input activations that feed into the classification output of ImageNet CNNs
477
      reweighted via linear regression. While our dataset precluded fitting models following Dale’s law,
      we found models using only positive weights had lower training and test performance compared to
478
      unconstrained models (Fig. 17). Thus, receiving negative inputs from the artificial network features
479
      improved the response predictions of our biological neurons. We found features that were consis-
480   tently assigned positive/negative weights in most neurons models (more than 90%). Visualizing
481   these intermediate layer features from AlexNet fc ReLU layer (4096 units) showed positive features
482   had smaller scale edges, curvatures and spots, while negative features had more textures and larger
483   patches (Fig. 18). These features are a hypothesis for excitatory and inhibitory neurons in IT cortex,
484   which require genetic tools in the primate to be adressed experimentally. This suggests a functional
485   segregation of contextual information to inhibitory inputs in high-level visual cortex, a hypothesis
      testable with new genetic tools in non-human primates.

      6   D ISCUSSION AND CONCLUSION
519
520   Our study combined ablations with feature visualization guided by naturalistic image priors to reveal
521   the functional segregation of class-level features in the output layer of ImageNet trained CNNs:
522   positive weights contribute object information, while negative weights contribute background or
523   contextual information. This effect was enhanced in robust networks, it was present in networks
524   with unsupervised pretraining, but was absent in network trained with Tanh instead of ReLU. Our
525
      results explain how the background contribution to classification observed in (Xiao et al., 2020)
      emerges, backgrounds are primarily encoded by the negative inputs.
526
527   Importantly for neuroscience, the observed functional segregation in neuron model units in CNNs
528   hints at a functional segregation in the brain beyond the center-surround classically studied in V1.
529   And we crafted a diverse dataset for visual neuroscience recordings that is scalable. Neuron re-
530   sponses to a smaller but diverse set of naturalistic, colored images, with complex foregrounds and
531
      backgrounds, led to models capturing relevant features obtained experimentally from the neuron.
      Thus, using both model-based and model-free approaches revealed richer neuronal representations.
532
      Preferred images from neuron models with positive input ablations elicited smaller average pop-
533
      ulation responses of cortical neurons. This suggests that ablation in networks modeling neurons
534   holds potential as a method to control the population activity in the brain. To relate ablation-induced
535   changes in the images to the population responses is a future direction. This ablation based on the
536   natural division of positive and negative weights can be easily extended into arbitrary layers, e.g.,
537   using gradients to define positive and negative contributions to any arbitrary unit. And our ablation
538   approach proposes baselines for the functional differences between excitatory and inhibitory neu-
539   rons in higher cortical visual areas. Understanding the circuit mechanism of biological vision could
      aid further understanding and development of computer vision models.

765   Feature localization in vivo We conducted a perturbation-based localization to identify relevant
766   image regions from a feature visualization performed in vivo, where gradient information from the
767   animal brain is unavailable. We perturbed a circular region with a 50-pixel diameter within the
768   256-pixel image by randomly shuffling the pixels inside this circle, effectively disrupting the local
769   image structure while maintaining local contrast. We selected 30 such regions for perturbation at
770   random, excluding those that extended beyond the image boundaries. The modified images were
771   then presented to the monkey. We hypothesized that perturbing regions crucial for driving the neu-
772   ron response would lead to a decreased firing rate. To assess local image importance, we calculated
773
      the normalized response change: the difference between the firing rate response to the intact image
      and the firing rate response to the perturbed image, divided by the firing rate response to the intact
774
      image. A normalized response change of 0.5 indicates the neuron response decreased by half due to
775
      perturbation. To generate the localized response mask, we averaged the circular masks correspond-
776   ing to each perturbed region, weighted by their response change. This response mask was further
777   smoothed using a Gaussian kernel with a 30-pixel standard deviation. We defined relevant regions
778   as those causing a normalized response change of 0.5 or greater. Finally, we applied this mask to
779   the original feature visualization image to highlight the local features.

782

783

784   AlexNet. The embedding is the output of the last layer before softmax of AlexNet, a vector space
785   of 1000-dimensions. The images from this dataset also spanned uniformly the 1000-dimensional
786   output space of a semi-supervised trained network, trained on a billion images, ResNet50SS (Yalniz
787   et al., 2019). To define this embedding space, we performed PCA on the output activations from
788   AlexNet to the 50k ImageNet validation images, we kept the top 300 components (accounting for
789   about 95% of total explained variance). Then we partitioned the space into a defined number of
790   clusters k, according to the desired dataset size, using batched k-means to reduce computational
791   burden. After finding the k cluster centers, we could feed arbitrary images to the network, map
792
      them to the PCA space, and then pick the nearest neighbors to the cluster centers from the desired
      image space. In addition to the ImageNet validation set, we added other common neuroscience
793
      datasets (Brady et al., 2008; Kar et al., 2019; Allen et al., 2022; Hung et al., 2005) to form our image
794
      space. We selected k = 160 images, as a set that was diverse but small enough to be used in every
795   experimental session. We called this image dataset diverseSet .
