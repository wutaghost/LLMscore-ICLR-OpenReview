                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
010            Continual learning seeks to enable machine learning systems to solve an increasing
011
               corpus of tasks sequentially. A critical challenge for continual learning is forgetting,
               where the performance on previously learned tasks decreases as new tasks are
012
               introduced. One of the commonly used techniques to mitigate forgetting, sample
013
               replay, has been shown empirically to reduce forgetting by retaining some examples
014            from old tasks and including them in new training episodes. In this work, we
015            provide a theoretical analysis of sample replay in an over-parameterized continual
016            linear regression setting, where given enough replay samples, one would be able
017            to eliminate forgetting. Our analysis focuses on replaying a few examples and
018            highlights the role of the replay samples and task subspaces. Surprisingly, we find
019            that forgetting can be non-monotonic with respect to the number of replay samples.
020            We construct tasks where replay of a single example can increase forgetting and
021            even distributions where replay of a randomly selected sample increases forgetting
022
               on average. We provide empirical evidence that this is a property of the tasks rather
               than the model used to train on them, by showing a similar behavior for a neural
023
               net equipped with SGD. Through experiments on a commonly used benchmark,
024
               we provide additional evidence that performance of the replay heavily depends on
025            the choice of replay samples and the relationship between tasks.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Humans and other animals can seemingly learn new skills and accumulate knowledge throughout
032   their lifetimes. Continual learning algorithms aim to achieve this same capability: to produce systems
033
      that can learn from a sequence of tasks. One of the main challenges is a phenomenon typically called
      catastrophic forgetting (McCloskey & Cohen, 1989), where the learner’s performance on a previously
034
      visited task degrades once it learns new tasks. A dominant theme in continual learning has been the
035
      development of methods to address catastrophic forgetting (Li & Hoiem, 2017; Zenke et al., 2017;
036   Rebuffi et al., 2017; Kirkpatrick et al., 2017; Prabhu et al., 2020b). However, there has been limited
037   theoretical treatment of their efficacy.
038
039
      In this work, we focus on a continual learning problem consisting of a sequence of linear regression
      tasks. The tasks are designed such that a single linear model is sufficient to solve the full sequence.
040
      The aim of focusing on this problem is that it is simple enough to permit analysis while preserving
041
      some key challenges of the continual learning problem. A particularly noteworthy discovery of prior
042   work is that even in this setting, catastrophic forgetting can occur (Evron et al., 2022). However, an
043   open question remains: Can methods designed to combat forgetting succeed in this setting? As a first
044   step towards answering this question, we study one such method, experience replay.
045
      There are many variations of experience replay. For example, van de Ven et al. (2020) introduce a
046
      brain-inspired generative replay and show that it has strong performance on complex benchmarks. In
047
      this paper, we focus on sample replay, an intuitive technique where samples observed during continual
048   learning are stored and repeated back to the learner during later tasks, to help retain solutions to
049   prior tasks. This simple method has been shown to be effective at ameliorating forgetting (Rebuffi
050   et al., 2017; Rolnick et al., 2019; Aljundi et al., 2019b; Wu et al., 2019; Chaudhry et al., 2019; Tiwari
051   et al., 2022), and is often used in dynamic learning settings like reinforcement learning (Lin, 1992).
052   Replay has also been a focus of study in neuroscience, as strong experimental evidence supports
053   the hypothesis that replay plays an important role in memory consolidation (Rasch & Born, 2007;
      Oudiette & Paller, 2013).

      Some existing theoretical works in continual learning show that when tasks are revisited in a cyclical
055   order, forgetting would vanish. Evron et al. (2022) shows this in the continual linear regression setting,
056   while Chen et al. (2022) show a similar result in a more general PAC-like continual learning setting.
057   While these results show that forgetting decreases when the entire task sequence is replayed, they
058   do not consider what happens when replay occurs between tasks, and involves a subset of samples.
059   We analyze sample replay for continual linear regression and find that, when the number of replay
060   samples is small, the outcome of sample replay can vary significantly. A learner faced with a possibly
061   infinite sequence of tasks and finite memory cannot afford to hold on to a fixed number of samples
062   per task, so it is important to understand this low sample storage setting. Even when storage capacity
063
      is not a concern, studying this low sample regime could lead to insights that make learning more
      efficient.
064
065   We first prove that somewhat counter-intuitively there are worst-case scenarios where replay actually
066   causes more forgetting. We then prove a surprising stronger result, that this can still hold in a certain
067   average case sense: even when examples are sampled randomly from specific task subspaces, and
068   replay samples are chosen randomly, replay can increase forgetting on average. These findings
069
      suggest that when the memory allocated to storing replay samples is very limited, not only does the
      choice of replay samples matters, but also the relationship between tasks could dictate the effect of
070
      replay on forgetting. In addition to our theoretical contributions, we provide an empirical investigation
071
      of forgetting with sample replay to support our theoretical findings. We verify our theoretical results
072   and further show that the same surprising behavior exists in continual linear regression learning with
073   neural networks. Through experiments on MNIST continual learning benchmarks, we show that
074   there is significant variation in the effectiveness of replaying a few samples to mitigate forgetting,
075   and a task sequence where replay can increase forgetting.

080   2.1   BACKGROUND
081
      Evron et al. (2022) initiated the study of catastrophic forgetting in overparameterized linear regression.
082                                                       T
083
      They consider a sequence of linear tasks (Xt , yt )t=1 where Xt ∈ Rnt ×d , yt ∈ Rnt and nt , d are the
      number of samples per task and input dimension respectively. They assume that the sequence of linear
084
      tasks share a solution that could be obtained by jointly training on all tasks, and for each task nt < d,
085
      so any single task would not necessarily contain all the information needed to learn the common
086   solution. Despite the existence of a common solution, they show that there are sequences of tasks such
087   that learning them in a sequential manner with gradient descent will result in a significant amount of
088   forgetting, which is defined to be the average error on all previously seen tasks (Definition 2.3). In
089   this setting, d many samples would be sufficient to recover the solution to each task.
090
      Another group of techniques used to mitigate forgetting is through regularization. For example,
091
      forgetting can be eliminated using a Fisher information based weighting matrix (Kirkpatrick et al.,
092   2017; Evron et al., 2023). We note that storing such matrices would take order d2 bits of memory,
093   which is of the same order as storing d samples. Peng et al. (2023) introduce a general notion of an
094   “ideal continual learner” and instantiate it for continual linear regression. Their algorithm maintains
095   the shared null space of the previous tasks. Again, storing a null space could take order d2 bits of
096   memory. Sample replay, on the other hand, allows the learner to store much less than d many samples,
097   reducing memory requirements significantly. However, the effectiveness of replaying a few samples
098   in this linear setting is an open question and the focus of this paper.

            R EGRESSION
165
      In this section we show that replay can increase forgetting in two different settings. Each setting
166
      demonstrates a different scenario where replay can increase forgetting. The worst case setting
167   highlights how the relationships between individual samples could lead to catastrophic forgetting
168   with replay, while the average case result goes beyond interactions between individual samples
169   and highlights the role of task subspaces and the angle(s) between them. For both of these results,
170   interference within samples of each task plays an important role. Since samples within a task can
171   be revisited many times during training, this intra-task sample interference does not matter without
172   replay and we only see forgetting due to interference across tasks. With replay, however, intra-task
173   sample interference could also contribute to forgetting.
174

176           S ELECTION

178
      dramatic, or catastrophic. In addition to Assumptions 2.1 and 2.2, we require that samples have unit

185   samples for each tasks, ((Xt , yt ))t=1,...T and the replay sample(s) X mem , y mem , with restriction that

188   3.1.1    W ORST CASE RESULTS
189
      In this worst case setting, we choose the tasks and a (possibly empty) subset of samples from each
190   task to be replayed. In this setup, we show that forgetting could increase from vanishing, O T1 , to

191   Θ(1), which we have labeled catastrophic.
192
193   At the core of the tasks constructed in the worst case setting are three samples x1 , x2 and x3 , where
194
      x1 and x3 are orthogonal to one another, and x2 is linearly independent but not orthogonal to x1 or
      x3 . Consider a sequence of two tasks where the samples for the first task consists of x1 , x2 , and the
195
      second task contains only x3 . After training on the first task, there is no error on x1 and x2 . When
196
      we train on the second task, since x3 is not orthogonal to x2 , training on x3 introduces some error on
197   x2 but doesn’t introduce error on x1 , since it is othogonal to x3 . Now suppose that x2 is replayed, so
198   for the second task, we train on both x2 and x3 . Since x1 is not orthogonal to x2 , this will introduce
199   error on x1 . Thus in this three sample, two task setup, replay causes an exchange of the error on x2
200   with error on x1 . See Figure 7 in Appendix B.1 for a geometric illustration of this phenomena.
201   Theorem 3.2 (Worst case replay). Under assumptions 2.1 and 2.2, for any T ≥ 2, d ≥ 3, there is
      a sequence of T tasks and a sample (x̃, ỹ) such that without replay, forgetting is F (wT ) = O T1 ,

202
203   while with replay of (x̃, ỹ), forgetting is catastrophic, i.e., F (wT ) = Θ(1) .
204
205   In the proof, which is given in Appendix C.1 , we construct a scenario where this type of error
206
      exchange is detrimental. We construct a sequence of tasks t = 1, . . . , T , where the sample x1 occurs
      in all but the last task, while the sample x2 occurs in just one of these tasks. Then we can see that if x2
207
      is replayed in the last task (t = T ), causing error on x1 , the forgetting will be much larger, growing
208
      with the number of tasks T , since x1 occurs in almost all the tasks. Note that in this construction,
209   replaying x1 would not change the final iterate wT , since without replay, there is no error on x1 .
210   Additionally, the construction is such that without replay, there is no forgetting on any sample other
211   than x2 .

349   4.1                     E MPIRICAL E VALUATION AND E XTENSION OF THE T HEORETICAL R ESULTS
350
351   So far, we have shown that there are tasks that are realizable by linear models where sample replay
352   increases forgetting when a linear model is trained sequentially. In this set of experiments, we verify
353   these findings empirically and show that this behavior is not restricted to training with linear models.
354   The experiments here show that there are (linear) tasks where replay can increase forgetting even
355   when training nonlinear models on these tasks. We investigate the effect of replay on forgetting using
356   a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) with one hidden layer and ReLU activations on a sequence of two
357   tasks that are based on our worst case construction.
358   We consider two models: a linear model, and a MLP with one hidden layer. We consider two task
359   sequence constructions. The first construction is in R3 and is based on the construction given in
360   Theorem 3.6. The second task sequence is an extension of that construction into a higher dimensional
361   space (d = 50). Each sequence of tasks consists of two tasks. The model is trained on the first
362   task and then on the second task. Forgetting is then measured as the mean squared error of the
363   final model on the first task’s samples. When training with replay, m samples from the first task are
364   randomly selected without replacement and are combined with each batch during training on the
365
      second task. See Appendix D for further details on the experimental setup and how the construction
      in Theorem 3.6 was extended to a higher dimensional input.
366
367   Another experiment included in Appendix D.1.1, provides empirical evidence that forgetting for the
368   nonlinear models in this setting is affected by the angle between task null spaces through a mechanism
369   similar to linear models. This provides some insight into the behavior of the nonlinear models on the
370   continual linear regression problem, as seen in Figure 2.

412   to be more beneficial in the 45 degrees rotation case than the 90 degrees.
413   We take a closer look at replaying the digit 5 in the 45 degree rotation case, where replay seems to
414   have minimal effect on forgetting. Figure 4 shows the distribution of differences in forgetting, that is,
415   forgetting without replay minus forgetting with replay of a randomly selected digit 5 sample. We can
416   see that even when on average this difference is not statistically significant, in many cases forgetting
417   with replay exceeds without replay. These results suggest that there could be significant differences
418   in the efficacy of replay depending on which examples are replayed.

420   4.2.2                                                        S PLIT MNIST
421
422   In this experiment, we study whether the relationship between tasks affects forgetting with replay in
423   a class incremental setting. In one task sequence the first task involves discriminating 0’s from 1’s,
424   and the second 6’s from 7’s; in the other sequence the first task is 0 vs. 6, the second 1 vs. 7. Figure 5
425   shows that in the first task sequence replay consistently helps, but in the second forgetting initially
426   increases, and only begins helping with 4 or more samples. We hypothesize that the visual similarity
427   of the digits in the first task in the 0, 6 - 1, 7 sequence makes the forgetting worse with a small number
428
      of replay samples, as the challenging discrimination task is sensitive to the selection of samples.
429   This empirical result bears important similarities to our theoretical results, in the average case setting.
430   The relationship between the tasks in a continual learning sequence determines the effect of replay on
431   forgetting. And even in this more complicated, classification problem, there exists sequences where
      forgetting can increase with replay.

      5    R ELATED W ORK
473
474
475   For general background on continual or lifelong learning, see the surveys by De Lange et al. (2022);
476   Parisi et al. (2019); Wang et al. (2024). Theoretical studies of continual learning, and especially
477
      replay have been relatively more scarce and recent. Nevertheless, there are a few studies of continual
      learning in a linear setting. Doan et al. (2021) initiate the study of catastrophic forgetting of neural
478
      nets in the NTK regime, which also applies to linear models. Goldfarb & Hand (2023) study the
479
      effect of over-parameterization on forgetting in a linear regression setting for two tasks whose task
480   subspaces are effectively low rank and picked randomly. Lin et al. (2023) study generalization and
481   a slightly different notion of forgetting in continual linear regression. They allow the tasks to be
482   realized by different linear functions, while the task subspaces are essentially random. Li et al. (2023)
483   study the trade-off between stability and plasticity in a linear regression setting similar to (Evron
484   et al., 2022), with the distinction that they use ℓ2 - regularization while training on the second task.
485   Shan et al. (2024) study continual learning in deep learning from a statistical-mechanics point of
      view.


                                                                                9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Peng et al. (2023) define a general continual learner that has no memory constraints and incurs zero
487   forgetting. They propose an instantiation of it for continual linear regression that could require up to
488   order d2 bits of memory. Additionally, through their framework, they derive uniform convergence
489   type bounds and justify a form of sample replay where the learner keeps a task-balanced set of samples
490   from previous tasks in memory and for each new task trains on the stored and new task’s samples
491   from scratch. Note that without knowing the exact constants, these bounds do not provide much
492   information on replay of a few samples. Prabhu et al. (2020a) make a similar argument empirically
493   and show that training from scratch on the samples stored in memory plus the most recent task’s
494   samples outperforms many methods that were specifically designed to address catastrophic forgetting.
495   There is a large body of empirical work on continual learning and methods used to mitigate forgetting,
496   many of which use sample replay as a main strategy to address forgetting (Rebuffi et al., 2017;
497   Chaudhry et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2019; Aljundi et al., 2019a; Caccia et al., 2021). Although there
498   has been some concern in the literature that replaying a small number of samples might lead to
499   overfitting (Lopez-Paz & Ranzato, 2017), Chaudhry et al. (2019) find that replay of even one sample
500   per class improves performance and does not hurt generalization even when the model memorizes
501
      the replay samples. Verwimp et al. (2021) give a more complex picture of this in terms of the loss
      landscape. They find that while replay could keep the solution within a low loss region for previous
502
      tasks it could also pull the solution towards an unstable region. Motivated by forgetting in continual
503
      learning, Toneva et al. (2018) study forgetting of the examples across batches while training on a
504   task. They find that there are complex examples that are prone to be forgotten across different model
505   architectures. Prabhu et al. (2024); Zajac et al. (2023) show that their proposed replay-free methods
506   achieve better performance than replay-based ones. Our results differ from these empirical results
507   in that we focus on the existence of task sequences that would provably increase forgetting, and the
508   variability of forgetting effects when the number of replay samples is small.
509
510   6   D ISCUSSION
511
512   This work aims to initiate the theoretical study of sample replay in continual learning, complementing
513   existing empirical work. We have shown that replaying a small number of examples can increase
514   forgetting in continual linear regression. Our experimental results show that an increase in forgetting
515   is not unique to linear models and can be present when sequentially training with simple MLPs and
516   even on more challenging continual learning problems.
517   This work raises a number of important questions for future study. First, the scenarios where replay
518   provably increases forgetting are based on a few sequential tasks in a linear setting; to what extent
519   can replay increase forgetting for a richer and more natural sequence of tasks? Secondly, can we
520   provide a characterization of when replay increases forgetting? Moreover, can we give a necessary
521   and/or sufficient conditions in terms of the quality/quantity of replayed samples that would mitigate
522   forgetting? We show that the choice of the replayed samples matters, but these results do not provide
523   a formal selection procedure, especially when the learner has no prior knowledge of future tasks.

1301
1302   In all the experiments, a fully connected network with two hidden layers of size 256 was used. In all
1303   cases, training on each task was for 3 epochs, with batch size of 32, and using Adam (Kingma & Ba,
1304   2014) with learning rate of 0.001.
1305
1306   Statistical tests. When we compared the means, we used Welch’s t-test, which is similar to a
1307   student t-test while allowing the populations to have different variances.
1308
1309   Rotated MNIST. Rotated MNIST experiments are in a task incremental setting and use the training
1310
       data for all the 10 digits. The training data on the second task is the same as the training data on
       the first task, except that it is rotated. Forgetting is measure on test samples. The replay is done the
1311
       same as the regression experiments. That is, for each class, a random sample is combined with the
1312
       samples in the each batch and the replay samples are up-weighted such that the replay sample has
1313   equal weight to the rest of the samples. The no replay baseline is what the literature might call the
1314   fine tuning baseline. The network is sequentially trained on the two tasks. The optimizer is reset after
1315   training on the first task.
1316
1317   Split MNIST. These experiments are in a class incremental setting, so the network had 4 output
1318   heads. During evaluation on the first task, only the logits corresponding to the classes in the first task
1319   were taken into account. This is the case with or without replay. Again, the replay implementation
1320   here is similar to the regression experiments.
