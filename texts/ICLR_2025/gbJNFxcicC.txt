009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            This study investigates the automatic detection and segmentation of malaria para-
012            sites across various Plasmodium species using Mask R-CNN, an advanced deep-
013            learning architecture. Expanding on earlier studies in digital malaria diagnosis,
014            we apply pixel-level segmentation to overcome the drawbacks of previous ap-
015            proaches. 971 microscopic pictures of four Plasmodium species—P. falciparum, P.
016            malariae, P. ovale, and P. vivax—taken from Rwanda’s healthcare facilities make
017            up our dataset. This dataset was used to train the Mask R-CNN model, which
018
               produced excellent mean average precision (mAP) scores for all species, with P.
               vivax and P. malariae showing the most excellent performance with mAP 0.9575
019
               and mAP 0.9459, respectively. Compared to earlier techniques, this method shows
020
               notable advances in parasite localization and delineation, suggesting the possibil-
021            ity of more precise and effective malaria diagnosis in clinical settings.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Malaria, a mosquito-borne infection caused by Plasmodium parasites, poses a significant threat to
027   global health, particularly in developing nations. This disease, transmitted through the bites of in-
028   fected Anopheles mosquitoes, wreaks havoc on individual lives and communities. Four Plasmodium
029   parasites, P.falciparum, P. malariae, P. ovale and P. vivax, are known to cause malaria, with P. falci-
030   parum and P. vivax responsible for most cases and demonstrating the highest levels of virulence. In
031   2021 alone, the World Health Organization (WHO) reported an estimated 241 million cases, with
032   a staggering 95% concentrated in Africa. Furthermore, 627,000 lives were tragically claimed by
033   this disease globally, with 96% of these deaths occurring in 29 countries (WHO, 2021). Recently,
034
      various studies have been on automating the malaria diagnosis process, focusing on the microscopy
      approach. Microscopic blood smears remain the gold standard for malaria diagnosis but are incon-
035
      venient. Traditional malaria diagnosis involves preparing thin or thick blood smears and visually
036
      examining them under a microscope. Thick smears help detect the presence of parasites, while
037   thin smears help identify the parasite species and stage (Makhija et al., 2015). However, this process
038   takes time and relies heavily on operator skills, making it impractical in areas with limited resources.
039   Rapid diagnostic tests offer a more accessible and efficient alternative (Kigozi et al., 2021). This
040   study builds upon existing research on digital systems for malaria monitoring (Mukamakuza et al.,
041   2022). The more extensive study aims to streamline data collection, analysis, treatment, and public
042   information services, potentially improving malaria forecasting, management, and treatment. In line
043   with this goal, Mary et al. (2023) developed a data management model for Rwanda. Other research
044   for this paper has investigated a few computer vision deep learning models for segmentation and de-
045
      tection of parasites in 2D photos (Akpo et al., 2024; Karasira et al., 2024; Bogale et al., 2024). This
      paper focuses on enhancing malaria prediction and automating the diagnosis process to improve
046
      malaria control efforts further using the Mask R-CNN.

052   malaria diagnosis. Their ability to automatically extract robust features from images leads to high
053   performance, making it a frontrunner compared to other feature extraction techniques (Litjens et al.,
      2017; LeCun et al., 2015). A growing body of research, detailed below, showcases the effectiveness

      role of these modern information technologies in effectively combating the disease. The proposed

      diction of malaria, a critical step in managing the disease. While much of the research has focused
066
      on Plasmodium Falciparum due to its prevalence and lethality in sub-Saharan Africa, as acknowl-

069   overall malaria diagnosis and control.

      In another study, the two-stage malaria parasite detection model proposed by Yang et al. (2020)
071
      demonstrates limitations. The initial stage, tasked with selecting potential parasite candidates using
072
      grayscale image intensity values, proves inadequate due to the inherent shortcomings of traditional
073   image processing techniques. These techniques struggle to adapt to varying environmental condi-
074   tions, resulting in inaccurate parasite identification. Consequently, the performance of the subse-
075   quent CNN classifier suffers, ultimately hindering the overall effectiveness of the model.
076
      For this work, (Bogale et al., 2024) used Faster R-CNN with various backbone architectures and
077
      discovered that ResNet-50 produced the best results. This study highlighted the potential for CNN-
078
      based models to improve diagnostic speed and accuracy. Another study for this same project looked
079   at YOLOv5 for parasite identification (Karasira et al., 2024). While this method effectively detected
080   most parasites, it struggled with P. falciparum due to dataset restrictions. On the same project,
081   (Akpo et al., 2024) investigated U-Net segmentation for malaria parasites and obtained impressive
082   accuracy rates. However, the F1-score suggested that there was still space for improvement in recog-
083   nizing positive cases, highlighting the crucial need for larger datasets, particularly for Plasmodium
084   falciparum. As a result, the dataset section of this study discusses an enhanced dataset collection.
085   Building upon this foundation of promising research, this study explores the application of Mask
086   R-CNN, a robust deep-learning architecture, for automated malaria parasite detection and segmen-
087   tation. Mask R-CNN stands out for its ability to identify parasites and generate pixel-level masks,
088   providing precise localization and delineation of individual parasites within the image. This capa-
089   bility presents significant advantages for accurate diagnosis and parasite quantification, potentially
090   leading to improved patient management and treatment outcomes(He et al., 2017).

107   Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), known for their prowess in computer vision, are making
      waves in the medical field. Their ability to analyze massive amounts of medical images opens


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      doors to unprecedented advancements in image analysis, particularly in cell biology experiments.
109   This translates to increased efficiency and accuracy, paving the way for improved medical research
110   and diagnosis (Bote-Curiel et al., 2019; Zemouri et al., 2019). One of the critical strengths of
111   Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) is their modularity and flexibility. This allows existing,
112   pre-trained networks to be readily adapted and applied to new tasks by leveraging relevant labelled
113   images. This process, known as Transfer Learning, allows for rapid development of new applications
114   with minimal effort. By seamlessly integrating prior knowledge with new data, transfer Learning
115   enhances the capabilities and adaptability of CNNs, making them even more valuable in diverse
116   domains (Yao et al., 2019).
117   CNN has progressed so much that researchers have used faster R-CNN to localize and classify
118   malaria-infected cells. Faster R-CNN is a type of Region-based CNN (R-CNN). This versatile
119   object detection model utilizes a two-stage approach. The first stage, the Region Proposal Network
120   (RPN), identifies potential object locations within the image. Drawing inspiration from Faster R-
121   CNN, the second stage extracts features from each proposed region using a RoIPool technique.
122   This information is then employed for both object classification and bounding box refinement. The
123
      network utilizes shared features for both stages, leading to faster inference (Ren et al., 2015). While
      Faster R-CNN demonstrates impressive capabilities in object detection, it suffers from the limitation
124
      of generating coarse bounding boxes. This can lead to overlapping boxes, where multiple boxes
125
      enclose the same object, potentially hindering the accuracy and clarity of object detection. This
126   limitation becomes particularly significant when dealing with complex images containing numerous
127   objects or objects with intricate shapes (Hung et al., 2017).
128
129
      Recognizing the limitations of coarse bounding boxes in Faster R-CNN, Mask R-CNN emerges
      as a powerful solution. This advanced architecture builds upon Faster R-CNN’s foundation while
130
      introducing a crucial innovation: a third branch dedicated to object mask prediction. This branch
131
      operates concurrently with the branches for object detection and bounding box regression, enabling
132   Mask R-CNN to perform instance segmentation alongside object detection (Litjens et al., 2017)
133
134   Unlike Faster R-CNN’s coarse boxes, Mask R-CNN generates high-quality masks that precisely
135
      delineate the shape and boundaries of individual objects in the image. Furthermore, Mask R-CNN’s
      architecture facilitates efficient and straightforward training, and the model demonstrates remarkable
136
      adaptability to diverse applications and tasks.

      4.1   DATASETS , A NNOTATION AND P REPROCESSING
142
143
      The dataset encompasses an extensive assortment of microscopic images that depict four distinct
144   types of malaria parasites: Plasmodium Falciparum, Plasmodium Malariae, Plasmodium Ovale, and
145   Plasmodium Vivax. These images were meticulously acquired at the Rwanda Biomedical Centre
146   (RBC) Mbituyumuremyi (2024) through a specialized microscope arrangement. This setup involved
147   giemsa-stained slides observed under a microscope, equipped with a camera attached to the eyepiece
148   and linked to a laptop, as illustrated in Figure 2 below.
149
      During the adjustment of the microscope, digital images (fields) of the slides were captured and
150   stored for subsequent analysis. The images encompassed thick and thin film smears, offering a
151   diverse array of samples for examination. Each image underwent meticulous annotation using the
152   VGG Image Annotator 2.0.12 to ensure precision in identifying infected regions. This tool facilitated
153   accurate delineation of infected areas through its polygon feature. An image mostly consists of
154   parasites, white blood cells or other artefacts. In each image, the white blood cells and parasites
155   were all labelled.
156   The dataset comprises images of four Plasmodium species responsible for malaria infection in hu-
157   mans. Specifically, it contains 278 images of Plasmodium falciparum (P. falciparum), 258 images
158   of Plasmodium malariae (P. malariae), 260 images of Plasmodium ovale (P. ovale), and 175 images
159   of Plasmodium vivax (P. vivax). This distribution ensures a relatively balanced representation of
160   the major malaria-causing parasites, with a slightly lower number of P. vivax images compared to
161   the other species. To optimize the dataset for training while balancing computational resources, all
      photos were preprocessed. This technique included auto-orientation and uniform scaling to size of

200   4.2   T RAINING
201
202   For this study, several experiments were conducted on four different parasites and on all of them
203   combined to test for mixed infections. The Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) was utilized for each
204   parasite dataset with a learning rate of 0.01. The training used a batch size of 8, as we classified im-
205   ages into three or more classes. The classes for each experiment included the background, parasites,
206
      and white blood cells. With the preprocessing described before, augmentation was not included in
      this experiment as it reduced the quality of the results.
207
208   The implementation was carried out using PyTorch, with the model parameters and optimizer con-
209   figured accordingly. The hyperparameters included a learning rate of 0.01, momentum of 0.9, and
210   weight decay of 0.0005. The training process was conducted over 100 epochs using a CUDA-
211   enabled GPU. A learning rate scheduler (StepLR) was implemented with a step size of 5 and gamma
212
      of 0.1 to adjust the learning rate during training. The training loop involved iterating over the train-
      ing dataset for the specified number of epochs, with periodic validation on a separate validation
213
      set.
214

      tection and knowledge retrieval tasks. This statistic uses a confusion matrix to compare accurate

240   5.2   D ISCUSSION
241
242   The results of our study demonstrate the effectiveness of Mask R-CNN in automated malaria par-
243   asite detection and segmentation. By leveraging this advanced deep learning architecture, we have
244   addressed several limitations of previous methods and achieved promising results across different
245   Plasmodium species.
246   The capacity of Mask R-CNN to precisely segment and detect malaria parasites marks a break-
247   through in automated diagnosis. Mask R-CNN creates pixel-level masks that precisely define para-
248   site borders, unlike earlier models like Faster R-CNN, which produce coarse bounding boxes. This
249   level of precision is essential in microscopy-based diagnosis, where parasites are frequently mis-
250   taken for other blood components or abnormalities. Our findings demonstrate that all trials had high
251   mAP values, with P. vivax (0.9575) and P. malariae (0.9459) performing very well on test data.
252   These results imply that Mask R-CNN can handle morphological differences between Plasmodium
253   species well, which is crucial for correct diagnosis.
254   The two-stage parasite detection methodology described by previous researchers Poostchi et al.
255   (2018), suffered from environmental heterogeneity, resulting in erroneous parasite identification.
256   Our Mask R-CNN solution overcomes this restriction by learning robust features directly from image
257   data rather than depending on traditional image processing approaches for initial candidate selection.
258   This end-to-end learning process enables the model to adapt to changing image situations, resulting
259   in more accurate parasite detection across various samples.
260   Furthermore, Mask R-CNN’s segmentation capacity outperforms approaches that depend purely on
261   classification or bounding box detection. Our technique improves individual parasite localization
262   and delineation in the image by providing exact masks for each detected parasite.
263
      The training options outlined earlier were critical in balancing performance and computational re-
264   strictions, mainly using a pretrained Mask R-CNN model and image scaling to 256x256 pixels.
265   While the small batch size of 8 and the lack of data augmentation may limit sample diversity and
266   exposure to different picture circumstances, these decisions prioritized training stability and retain-
267   ing real microscopy features. Despite these limits, our model displayed significant generalizability
268   among Plasmodium species. This accomplishment implies that our technique efficiently used trans-
269   fer learning and negotiated the obstacles given by restricted computational resources, resulting in a
      robust model for malaria parasite detection.

      6   F UTURE W ORK AND R ECOMMENDATIONS
325
326
      Our work, while intriguing, has limitations that indicate topics for future investigation. To improve
327
      the model’s performance and applicability, we make many recommendations.
328
329   First and foremost, extending the dataset is critical, particularly for underrepresented species and
330   parasite stages. A larger dataset will boost model performance, especially for uncommon species
331
      or unconventional appearances. Second, increased computational resources allow for bigger batch
      sizes and more extensive hyperparameter adjustment, potentially improving the model’s learning
332
      capacity and generalizability.
333
334   Furthermore, carefully planned data augmentation approaches can strengthen the model’s resilience
335   to fluctuations in microscope pictures. Furthermore, expanding the model to include information
336   regarding parasite morphology and developmental stages could be a helpful addition, building on
337
      the accurate detection and segmentation accomplished in this study.
338   Additionally, real-world clinical validation is required to assess the model’s practical applicability
339   and identify areas for development in various healthcare settings. Creating techniques for interpret-
340   ing and explaining the model’s decisions helps boost trust and adoption among healthcare practi-
341   tioners.
342   Finally, longitudinal studies can evaluate the model’s long-term effectiveness and ability to adjust
343   to parasite shape or prevalence alterations. Finally, incorporating the model into current healthcare
344   systems can help it gain traction and significantly influence malaria detection and treatment.
345
      By resolving these issues, we may build on the foundation established by this study and progress in
346
      the automated malaria parasite detection and diagnosis field.

349   7   C ONCLUSION
350
351   Our research illustrates the effectiveness of Mask R-CNN for automated malaria parasite detection
352   and segmentation across several Plasmodium species. The model’s capacity to achieve high accu-
353   racy and precise localization even in the presence of morphological differences and mixed illnesses
354
      marks a significant step forward in microscopy-based diagnosis. Despite the promising results, this
      study has limitations. Future research should focus on increasing the dataset, particularly for un-
355
      derrepresented species and stages, and performing real-world clinical validations to evaluate the
356
      model’s effectiveness in various scenarios. In conclusion, our Mask R-CNN technique is promising
357   for boosting malaria diagnosis and control efforts. By solving the challenges of microscopy-based
358   parasite identification, this technique has the potential to influence global healthcare systems signif-
359   icantly.
