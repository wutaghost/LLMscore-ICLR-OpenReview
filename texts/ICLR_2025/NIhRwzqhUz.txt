009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Learning to solve the Travelling Salesman Problem (TSP) using Deep Reinforce-
012
               ment Learning (Deep RL) and Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) has shown promis-
013            ing results for small instances of the problem. We demonstrate that these meth-
014            ods can be extended to solve instances of a partially dynamic variant of the TSP.
015            Solving this partially dynamic variant more effectively exploits the strengths of
016            reinforcement learning and also presents challenges for more established methods
017            of solving the TSP. We show the policies trained using Deep RL outperform mod-
018            ified versions of TSP solvers and heuristics for different distributions of dynamic
019            vertices, including on larger instances than the policies were trained on. This
020            shows the promise of Deep RL for solving this type of dynamic routing problem
021
               which is predicted to become of great importance as logistical services become
               more flexible and responsive to customer demand. Furthermore, our method is
022
               a general purpose approach to Deep RL where the problem consists of selecting
023
               items from a dynamically-evolving and arbitrarily-sized set.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      In the Travelling Salesman Problem (TSP), we are given a list of vertices (representing locations)
030   and distances between them. A tour is the name given to the route which visits every vertex exactly
031   once and returns to the starting vertex. When solving an instance of TSP, the objective is to find the
032   shortest possible tour. It is an NP-Hard combinatorial optimization problem, which means that when
033   solving it there is typically a trade-off between the speed and the exactness of the solution. The most
034   well-known TSP solver, Concorde (Applegate et al., 2006), uses a cutting-plane method Applegate
035   et al. (2001) to solve the problem exactly and there are many heuristic methods (Rosenkrantz et al.,
036   1974; Lin & Kernighan, 1973; Christofides, 2022) which compute approximate solutions in a much
037   shorter time.
038   There are several papers which have applied Reinforcement Learning methodology to TSP. In Bello
039   et al. (2016), a pointer network (Vinyals et al., 2015) policy is trained using policy gradient methods
040   (Williams, 1992) with a critic baseline (Degris et al., 2012). By sampling the policy network multiple
041   times and selecting the best performing solution, their method produces solutions that are close
042   to optimal for examples of the TSP in 2D Euclidean space with up to 100 vertices. Kool et al.
043   (2019) present an improvement to this method by using an attention-based model and utilising a
044
      novel baseline in actor-critic which involves a greedy rollout of the best performing policy. Another
      improvement is made by Joshi et al. (2022) who use a GNN to encode TSP instances.
045
046   All three of these papers make a compelling demonstration of the effectiveness of solving TSP in-
047   stances with reinforcement learning for small numbers of vertices. They also highlight the immense
048   challenge of trying to improve upon the established methods for solving this problem.
049   In this paper, we extend the TSP by having a subset of the vertices in the final tour appear dynami-
050   cally as the tour is in progress. As we are looking to extend models used for reinforcement learning
051   on the static TSP, we devise a partially dynamic extension to the TSP (PDTSP) which keeps the
052   problem as pure as possible. In this formulation, the only additional constraint added is that dy-
053   namic vertices cannot appear in the tour before their assigned arrival time. The objective remains
      the same as the TSP, finding the shortest possible tour.

054
      The challenge associated with the partially dynamic TSP is that routing decisions need to be made
055   while the route is currently in progress. This makes it important that the solution method is capable
056   of producing updated solutions quickly when new vertices appear in the problem. An optimal so-
057   lution to this problem, i.e. the shortest tour visiting all of the static and dynamic vertices, is highly
058   unlikely to ever be achieved by any solution method because it would require not only routing opti-
059   mally through the vertices which are currently known but also routing so that dynamic vertices can
060   be incorporated optimally into the route.
061   The key benefit of RL in this situation is that it can learn a solution that is adapted to the underlying
062   distribution of dynamic vertices in the problem it is being applied to. This is something that exist-
063   ing TSP solvers cannot be expected to achieve. They have been specifically designed so that they
064   perform well on TSP problems in which the vertices are uniformly distributed in the space.
065
      The RL method works by training a neural network policy using gradient descent on simulated
066
      examples of the PDTSP to optimise tour length. These simulated examples enable the RL method to
067   learn tours which outperform modified versions of existing solution methods because the RL method
068   not only learns how to create efficient tours in the TSP but also how to best anticipate the arrival of
069   new vertices into the problem.
070
      From a practical point of view, considering a dynamic variant of the TSP is useful because as delivery
071
      and logistic operations continue to develop, they are being expected to operate on shorter timescales
072
      (Hildebrandt et al., 2023). Some concrete examples are same-day delivery services, online food-
073   delivery, ride-hailing apps, emergency repair services and emergency medical services. Being able
074   to make alterations to routes quickly in response to new locations appearing in a schedule is clearly
075   desirable in these applications.
076
      In the literature, there are existing partially dynamic extensions to the travelling salesman problem.
077
      For example, the Partially Dynamic Travelling Repairman Problem (Larsen et al., 2002), the dial-a-
078
      ride problem (Cordeau & Laporte, 2007) and the Dynamic Vehicle Routing Problem with Stochastic
079   Requests (DVRPSR) (Zhang et al., 2023). Each of these problems introduce additional constraints
080   such as time windows, time horizons, vehicle capacities, multiple vehicles and different types of
081   vertices (i.e. pick-up vertices and drop-off vertices). The objectives of these extensions typically
082   differ from the pure TSP objective of finding the shortest tour, instead optimising the waiting times
083   of customers or the total number of customers served. For this initial extension of the TSP, we choose
084   to demonstrate efficacy on two simple cases of the PDTSP, one with a unimodal spatial distribution
085   and one with a bimodal spatial distribution where the probability shifts from one mode to another as
086   the tour is built.
087   Our contributions are (a) a new partially dynamic travelling salesman environment, (b) a demonstra-
088   tion that the reinforcement learning methods and models developed for the static TSP outperform
089   static solvers and heuristics which we modify to work in the PDTSP, and (c) a demonstration of ef-
090   fective performance on larger instances than those in the training sets. This is particularly important
091   because it means that policies trained on small instances due to compute concerns can be utilised
092   effectively on larger real-world instances.

094   2    PARTIALLY DYNAMIC TSP E NVIRONMENT
095
096   The problem we consider is a dynamic variant of the Travelling Salesperson Problem (TSP) in which
097   a subset of the vertices in the problem are static and their locations are known prior to commencing
098   the tour. The remaining vertices are dynamic and their locations are revealed as the tour progresses.
099   We call this variation a Partially Dynamic Travelling Salesperson Problem (PDTSP).
100   Just like in the static TSP, the objective is to minimize the total length of this tour, Π, given by:

      For a particular instance of PDTSP, there are a set number of static and dynamic vertices. The static
109   vertex co-ordinates are sampled uniformly in the unit square. The dynamic vertex co-ordinates are
110   sampled according to a seperate spatial distribution over the unit square. When simulating data
111   for this paper, we consider unimodal and bimodal distributions. The arrival times, ta ∈ Ta of
112   the dynamic vertices are sampled without replacement from a categorical distribution with support
113   [2, N − 1].
114
      Time in the PDTSP is discrete and each time step corresponds to visiting a new vertex on to the tour.
115   At time t = 1, the salesperson will be located at the starting vertex of its tour. The agent will observe
116   the state of the problem and select the next vertex in its tour using the policy network. At time t = 2,
117   the salesperson will be located at the vertex selected at t = 1 and so on until the salesperson arrives
118   back at the starting vertex and the tour is complete. If the current time corresponds to an arrival time
119   of one of the dynamic vertices, ∃ta ∈ Ta : ta = t, then that vertex will arrive in the problem at time
120   t and can be selected by the agent at time t or any subsequent time step. Crucially, the agent cannot
121   alter steps of the tour which have been completed before the current time.
122   In the static TSP problem, the choice of starting vertex is arbitrary as the final tour remains the same
123   regardless of which vertex it begins from. In the PDTSP, the order in which vertices are visited in the
124   final tour matters because the dynamic vertices only appear in the problem after a certain number of
125   vertices have been visited. For this reason, when generating instances of PDTSP, the starting vertex
126   of the tour is fixed.

131   3.1     R EPRESENTING THE P ROBLEM AS A DYNAMIC G RAPH
132
133
      We represent the PDTSP as a dynamic graph G = {Gta : ta ∈ Ta ∪ 1}. Each graph Gta =
      (Vta , Eta ) ∈ G is a complete graph and is referred to as a snapshot of the dynamic graph. The
134
      vertices vi ∈ Vta have co-ordinates xi and the edges eij have weights corresponding to the Euclidean
135
      distance between the two connected vertices, ||xi − xj ||2 .
136
137   The first snapshot, G1 = (V1 , E1 ), is the initial graph consisting of the static vertices which are
138   present at the start of the problem. The next snapshot, Gta1 = (Vta1 , Eta1 ) occurs when the first
139   dynamic vertex arrives in the problem, Vta1 = V1 ∪ {vn+1 }, the edge set is extended to include
140
      edges from all other vertices to the new dynamic vertex. The snapshots follow this pattern until all
      dynamic vertices have been added to the graph.
141
142   Before being input to the neural network model, a graph sparsification heuristic is applied to each
143   graph snapshot Gta . The heuristic works by finding the set of k-nearest neighbours of each vertex
144   in the graph and removing edges to all the other vertices in the graph which do not belong to this
145   set. Joshi et al. (2022) show that this improves the performance of the neural network model.
146

150   2017) as depicted in Fig. 1. The encoder component takes as input the current state of the PDTSP
151   problem represented as a graph and outputs a d-dimensional embedding of each vertex in the graph.
152   The decoder component takes this embedding as input and outputs a probability distribution over
153
      actions which correspond to unvisited vertices in the PDTSP.

267   Each environment in a training batch shares the same number of static and dynamic vertices in
268   its PDTSP. When each batch is generated, the number of static and dynamic vertices for every
269   environment in that batch is randomly sampled from a range which is set prior to training. This
      method of generating training batches means that each individual batch is easier to work with but

270
      the policy still experiences a range of problem instances across batches in terms of the total number
271   of vertices and the proportion of vertices which are dynamic.
272
273   Each environment in a batch starts at time step t = 1. The state corresponding to the current feature
274
      graph of each environment is input to the encoder-decoder network and a probability distribution
      over vertices is output for each environment. A vertex is sampled for each environment and then
275
      the state of each environment is updated to add this vertex to the tour and the time step of that
276
      environment is incremented by 1. The set of arrival times, Ta , for each environment is then checked
277   to see if ∃ta ∈ Ta : ta = t. The environments which have an arrival are paused. The state of a
278   paused environment is fixed until an arrival has occurred for each environment in the batch. At that
279   point, the graphs for the batch are updated with their new vertex arrival and the batch is input to
280   the encoder and a new graph embedding is produced for each environment. The environments are
281   unpaused and then new vertices continue to be selected until the next arrival happens. The reason
282   for organizing simulations this way is to take advantage of GPU hardware which is typically much
283   faster when calculating a batch of inputs rather than inputs one at a time. 1
284   The model is trained by a policy gradient algorithm in which the loss is given by:

304   is a unimodal bivariate Gaussian distribution. From now on, the policy trained on this data will be
305   referred to as the Unimodal policy. In the second the spatial distribution of dynamic vertices is a

308

309

310   application in which demand could shift from a commercial location to a residential location as time
311   progresses.
312
313

316   (0.2, 0.2) is double that of the other and vice versa for the second half of the tour. The probability
317   density is illustrated in Fig. 2b.

402   where c is the cost and d is the distance between the two vertices in the subscript.
403
404
      The other two methods allow for more adaptation of the tour in response to an arrival. These methods
      we call rerun insertion and rerun Concorde. These methods involve using either a nearest insertion
405
      heuristic or Concorde solver to re-plan the tour through the vertices which have not yet been visited
406
      each time a new arrival happens. These two methods give more flexibility as the whole remaining
407   tour can be altered in response to an arrival and not just the position of the new dynamic vertex.

412   For the unimodal policy, Figure. 3a shows the performance of the policy on the test set with different
413   proportions of dynamic vertices. The policy is outperformed by the competing methods for the
414   instance with 0 dynamic vertices but this is expected as this is the static variant of the TSP. When
415   the proportion of dynamic vertices is increased to 0.2 then the policy starts to slightly outperform
416
      the competing methods with the gap increasing as the proportion of dynamic vertices increases.
417   Figure. 3b shows the performance of the policy on the test set of problem instances with different
418   total numbers of vertices. The policy outperforms each of the competing methods for the different
419   numbers of vertices tested. Encouragingly for the applicability of these models, the trend continues
420   for instances with more vertices than was seen in the training set.
421   There is a similar story when testing the bimodal model, the corresponding plots are shown in
422   Figures. 3c and 3d. The only difference being that the tours tend to be slightly longer on average
423   and the bimodal model is outperformed by the method which reruns Concorde after each new arrival
424   for problem instances with a 0.2 proportion of dynamic vertices. For larger proportions, the model
425   comes out on top with the gap in tour length increasing with the proportion of dynamic vertices.
426   The performance of the policies evaluated on a mismatched training set (Policy (Bimodal data) in
427   Figures. 3b and 3a and Policy (Unimodal data) in Figure. 3d and 3c) outperforms the comparison
428   methods in both cases. This suggests that the policy is not just learning where dynamic vertices are
429   likely to arrive but also how to build tours which are better at anticipating new arrivals wherever
430   they occur. There is still a small performance gap favouring the policy with matching training data
431   demonstrating that having some prior knowledge of the true distribution of dynamic vertices does
      benefit the policy.

472   7   D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
473
474   The results show that for the PDTSP, the trained policy is able to outperform comparison methods
475   including one which re-solves for an optimal tour each time a new vertex is added. This suggests
476   that the trained policy is not only learning what features make a good tour in TSP but is also learning
477   to create tours which are anticipatory in the face of dynamic vertices. The evaluation time of the
478   RL model is very fast on specialised hardware suggesting that it would be able to make live routing
479   decisions.
480   The experiments do not look at the performance of the unimodal policy on the bimodal test sets or
481   vice versa. This is because generalizing between spatial distributions that have changed in such a
482   dramatic fashion is not something that is expected in practice. The bimodal scenario already shows
483   that the model is equipped to deal with more realistic changes in the spatial distribution reflecting
484   what might happen in a city when people move from work to home for example. In light of this,
485   we believe that the best direction of future research would be to consider the robustness of a trained
      policy to small fluctuations in the spatial or temporal distributions of dynamic vertices. Also of


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      interest would be the possibility of training a baseline policy which is capable of adapting quickly
487   to new spatial distributions corresponding to different cities for example.
488
489   Another encouraging aspect of these results is that the trained policies perform well on instances with
490
      more vertices than the largest instances in the training sets. This is important because it suggests
      that the trained policies can be utilised on large instances of the dynamic TSP without requiring
491
      prohibitively expensive training on larger instances.
