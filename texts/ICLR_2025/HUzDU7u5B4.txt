009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Hallucination occurs when large language models (LLMs) exhibit behavior that
012            deviates from the boundaries of their knowledge during the response generation
013            process. Previous learning-based methods focus on detecting knowledge bound-
014            aries and finetuning models with instance-level feedback, but they suffer from
015            inaccurate signals due to off-policy data sampling and coarse-grained feedback.
016            In this paper, we introduce Reinforcement Learning for Hallucination (RLFH), a
017            fine-grained feedback-based¯ online reinforcement
                                                           ¯         ¯ ¯ method for hallucina-
                                                                    learning
018
               tion mitigation. Unlike previous learning-based methods, RLFH enables LLMs
               to explore the boundaries of their internal knowledge and provide on-policy, fine-
019
               grained feedback on these explorations. To construct fine-grained feedback for
020
               learning reliable generation behavior, RLFH decomposes the outcomes of large
021            models into atomic facts, provides statement-level evaluation signals, and traces
022            back the signals to the tokens of the original responses. Finally, RLFH adopts
023            the online reinforcement algorithm with these token-level rewards to adjust model
024            behavior for hallucination mitigation. For effective on-policy optimization, RLFH
025            also introduces an LLM-based fact assessment framework to verify the truthful-
026            ness and helpfulness of atomic facts without human intervention. Experiments
027            on HotpotQA, SQuADv2, and Biography benchmarks demonstrate that RLFH
028            can balance their usage of internal knowledge during the generation process to
029
               eliminate the hallucination behavior of LLMs.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Large Language Models have demonstrated remarkable capabilities in generating fluent and plausible
034
      responses. However, these models occasionally incorporate fabricated facts in truthful content,
035
      referred to as hallucination. For instance, as shown in Figure 1, the response of LLMs about "Turing"
036   contains erroneous factual information, such as stating he was born in 1911 and was American.
037
038   The crux of hallucination is the misalignment of the models’ generation and their internal knowledge
039
      (Xu et al., 2024). This occurs when large language models produce behavior that does not align
      with the boundaries of their knowledge during the response generation process. Such misalignment
040
      leads to various hallucinatory behaviors in the response, including misleading responses, reckless
041
      attempts, and evasive ignorance. Specifically, misleading responses refers to instances where the
042   model inaccurately answers a question within its knowledge boundary. Reckless attempts, on
043   the other hand, occur when the model attempts to respond to a query beyond its knowledge scope.
044   Evasive ignorance is when the model, despite possessing the necessary knowledge, refrains from
045   providing an answer. Unfortunately, due to the opaque nature of model knowledge, we can only
046   observe erroneous responses generated by large models or their refusal to respond, without accurately
047   determining whether they have experienced hallucinations and the specific types of hallucinations
048   they have experienced. Different types of hallucinations may even appear simultaneously within a
049   single response. This immeasurable characteristic of hallucinations presents a significant challenge
050
      for mitigating them in LLMs.
051   To address the hallucination problem in large language models, previous work can be categorized
052   into two directions: learning-based and editing-based. Learning-based methods involve detecting the
053   model’s knowledge boundaries and then fine-tuning it with feedback data to eliminate hallucinations.
      However, these methods suffer from off-policy data sampling (Zhang et al., 2024; Wan et al., 2024;

096   behind RLFH is to enable LLMs to explore the boundaries of their internal knowledge and provide
097   on-policy, fine-grained feedback on these explorations. This allows LLMs to effectively learn how
098   to balance their usage of internal knowledge during the generation process, thereby eliminating the
099
      hallucination behavior of LLMs. To this end, RLFH first requires LLMs to explore potential outcomes
      and decompose the response into atomic facts. These atomic facts then undergo an assessment to
100
      verify their truthfulness and helpfulness by comparing the generated facts with ground truth from
101
      external knowledge sources. Then starting from these fine-grained, statement-level assessments,
102   RLFH will trace back the judgment on atomic facts to the original outcomes, and construct token-level
103   dense reward signals which can be directly leveraged to optimize the policy model. Finally, we adopt
104   the online reinforcement algorithm with these token-level reward signals to adjust model behavior
105   for hallucination mitigation. Furthermore, to address the need for timely and low-cost construction
106   of reward signals in the on-policy optimization process, RLFH also introduces an LLM-based fact
107   assessment framework to verify the truthfulness and helpfulness of atomic facts without human
      intervention. Specifically, we utilize LLMs to automatically determine whether an atomic fact aligns

      with the facts described in the ground-truth document and assess the informativeness of the fact. This
109   automated fact assessment ensures that reward signals can be obtained in real-time and accurately,

      We conducted experiments on HotpotQA, SQuADv2, and Biography benchmarks to evaluate the

113
      improve the truthfulness and informativeness of the model response. Compared with the initial
114
      model, the model after RLFH has achieved a significant improvement (+17.9% FactScore on average).
115   Furthermore, compared to previous learning-based hallucination mitigation methods, RLFH can
116   better mitigate hallucination behavior through on-policy and fine-grained signals, thus achieving an
117   improvement (+2.0% FactScore on average).
118
      To sum up, the primary contributions of this paper are threefold:
119
120
      1) We propose Reinforcement Learning for Hallucination, an online reinforcement learning frame-
121
         work designed for mitigating hallucinations in large language models.
122
123   2) We propose to construct fine-grained, token-level knowledge feedback signals based on atomic fact
124      judgment. By decomposing the outcomes of large models into atomic facts, providing statement-
125      level evaluation signals, and tracing back the signals to the tokens of the original outcomes, we
126
         effectively achieve fine-grained feedback and learning for hallucination behaviors of LLMs.
127   3) We propose an LLM-driven method for evaluating the truthfulness and helpfulness of facts.
128      By automatically comparing with ground truth documents, this method effectively constructs
129      on-policy feedback signals automatically without human intervention.

134   2.1   H ALLUCINATION M ITIGATION
135
      Prior research (Zhang et al., 2023c; Ye et al., 2023; Tonmoy et al., 2024) has been dedicated to
136
      addressing the hallucination of Language Language Models. Some studies focus on reducing errors
137
      (Wang, 2019; Parikh et al., 2020) and supplementing missing knowledge (Ji et al., 2023) during data
138   curation. Other works mitigate hallucination in either pre- or post-generation by retrieving external
139   knowledge (Peng et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023b; Gou et al., 2023) or by exploiting self-consistency
140   (Manakul et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2023). Recent studies put efforts into investigating
141   the essence of the hallucination (Yu et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2024) and resort to improving the model’s
142   factuality during the alignment stage. These works focus on resolving the inconsistency between
143   the model’s generation pattern and its internalized knowledge (Xu et al., 2024) through knowledge
144   detection and coarse-grained feedback. Typically, these works attempt to delineate the boundary of
145   model knowledge through explicit prompting (Zhang et al., 2023a; Yang et al., 2023; Cheng et al.,
146
      2024; Wan et al., 2024), self eliciting (Chen et al., 2024a; Lin et al., 2024), self-evaluation (Zhang
      et al., 2024) or by probing the model’s internal states (Liang et al., 2024). Based on the detection
147
      of model knowledge, the data is meticulously crafted to align with the model’s knowledge scope.
148
      Subsequently, the model is fine-tuned with coarse-grained feedback, which inspects the truthfulness
149   of the response as a whole (Sun et al., 2022; Tian et al., 2023; Kang et al., 2024).
150
151
152   2.2   R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING FROM H UMAN F EEDBACK
153
      Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (Stiennon et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022) has
154
      emerged as a noteworthy approach reaching significant success in the domain of the Language
155
      Language Model. Given the instability of reinforcement learning, some research (Lu et al., 2022;
156   Rafailov et al., 2023; Dong et al., 2023) has attempted to learn human preferences directly from
157   labeled data. In addition to sparse rewards, some works have resorted to designing more instructive
158   rewards. One line of works (Wu et al., 2023; Lightman et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024b; Cao et al.,
159   2024) is dedicated to the acquisition of dense rewards. Another line of work (Ramé et al., 2023;
160   Eisenstein et al., 2023; Coste et al., 2024; Ramé et al., 2024) concentrates on ensemble multiple
161   reward models. Despite the majority of research prioritizing helpfulness and harmlessness, few
      studies (Wu et al., 2023; Tian et al., 2023; Liang et al., 2024) have explicitly considered truthfulness.

179
      Here’s a detailed breakdown of each step: 1) Response Generation: Given the prompt xi , the policy
180
      model π generates a corresponding response yi . This step involves the model using its current policy
181   to produce an output based on the input prompt. 2) Fine-grained Feedback from Faithful Reward
182   Model (§3.1): The faithful reward model M evaluates the generated response yi by comparing it
183   with the reference document set D. After the evaluation, the faithful reward model M provides
184   fine-grained feedback E at the statement level. 3) On-Policy Optimization with Token-level Reward
185   (§3.2): The detailed feedback E is translated into token-level rewards r. These rewards are then used
186   to update the policy model π using Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO), ensuring that the model
187   learns to reduce hallucinations effectively.

190

247
248   Given the fine-grained, statement-level feedback from the reward model, RLFH will trace back
249   the judgment on atomic facts to the original response, and construct token-level dense reward
250   signals, which can be directly leveraged to optimize the policy model. Finally, we adopt the
251   online reinforcement algorithm with these token-level reward signals to adjust model behavior for

254
255   We represent the helpfulness and truthfulness of the response through the dense reward conversion
256   presented in Figure 3. Due to the mutually exclusive nature (Xu et al., 2024) of these two objectives,
257   the model learns to balance the pursuit toward two dimensions, thereby acquiring the appropriate
258   strategy for the utilization of their internalized knowledge.
259   Truthfulness For each extracted statement, we will assign a truthfulness reward computed as follows:
260

262   where f and g represent manually designed functions that transform the labels k into scalar values. In
263   principle, f gives a positive reward to the truthful statement and a negative reward to the unverifiable
264   or false statement. Due to the phenomenon of hallucination snowball (Zhang et al., 2023b), i.e. some
265   critical errors lead to the magnification of the hallucinations, g is included to diversify the importance
266
      of different statements. The sign of the output function f is maintained by passing the outcome of the

309
      FactScore (Min et al., 2023) for evaluation out-of-distribution of different forms.

      (Tian et al., 2023); 2) advanced aligned models with the same model size of our model (7B), including

315
      and Vicuna 1.5 (Zheng et al., 2023).

      and relevant facts (# Cor.), the number of inaccurate facts (# Inc.), the ratio of responded questions

      In this work, we introduce Reinforcement Learning for Hallucination (RLFH), a fine-grained
478                                 ¯
      feedback-based online reinforcement           ¯ method ¯for hallucination
                                             learning              ¯            mitigation. RLFH enables
479   LLMs to explore their knowledge scope and adjust their behavior based on fine-grained on-policy
480   feedback. Specifically, our approach provides fine-grained knowledge feedback based on atomic fact
481   judgment and constructs token-level dense rewards for online reinforcement learning. Experiment
482   results on three factual benchmarks show that RLFH can significantly improve the truthfulness and
483   informativeness of LLMs under both in-distribution and out-of-distribution settings. For future work,
484   we plan to extend our method to mitigate hallucinations in multi-modal large language models.

678   The rollout stage in reinforcement learning requires online sampling, thus the annotation procedure
679   must concurrently consider efficiency and effectiveness. Given the time-intensive nature of this
680   process, we have implemented a pipeline prompting strategy. The entire procedure is divided into
681
      extraction, verification, and assessment, responding to the descriptions in Section 3.1, using prompts
      in Table 5, 6, and 7, respectively. As discussed in Section 4.5, most open-source models with certain
682
      capabilities in instruction following and reading comprehension are able to accomplish these tasks.
683
684   Due to the high cost and low throughput of the GPT4 API call, we opted for another single-pass
685   prompting pipeline to complete the annotation, as illustrated in Table 4. The primary distinction
686   between the single-pass and pipeline strategies lies in the granularity of the extracted statements. The
687
      pipeline strategy tends to fragment sentences in a more detailed manner resulting in more statements.
      We find the majority of open-source models failed to complete single-pass prompting satisfactorily.
