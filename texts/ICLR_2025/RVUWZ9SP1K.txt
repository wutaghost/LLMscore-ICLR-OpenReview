009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Many existing methods for operating system (OS) agents focus on predicting the
012            next action based on the current state, which constructs a predefined task exe-
013            cution pipeline. While these methods demonstrate promising performance, re-
014            liance on state cognition modules like detector or recognizer could impede execu-
015            tion efficiency, particularly in long-horizon tasks with intricate action trajectories.
016
               Recognizing the remarkable accuracy of large language models (LLMs) in pro-
               cessing short instructions, this paper proposes the ActionFiller framework. The
017
               goal is to integrate easily executable short tasks into longer, cohesive tasks using
018
               fill-in-the-blank prompts, thereby minimizing redundant operations and enhanc-
019            ing efficiency. ActionFiller employs two types of action-oriented fill-in-the-blank
020            prompts: one designed for subtasks and another for specific actions. To gener-
021            ate subtask prompts, we introduce a Foresight Optimization Agent (FOA) that
022            constructs an initial prompt by referencing past short tasks. It then fills in the un-
023            referenced parts with detailed prompts generated by a planning agent, effectively
024            retaining valuable past experiences. Next, an Action Template Agent (ATA) gen-
025            erates action prompts for each subtask. This process yields three distinct types
026            of action prompts: 1) executable action sequences, 2) non-executable action se-
027
               quences with prompt parameters, and 3) pure text descriptions. To execute the ac-
               tion prompts effectively, we propose the CohesiveFlow method, which optimizes
028
               the second and third types of prompts by leveraging the cognitive state of the en-
029
               vironment. Inspired by masked language modeling, the CohesiveFlow agent inte-
030            grates the current environmental state with previously executed action sequences
031            to update parameters and text descriptions, ensuring both feasibility and effec-
032            tiveness in execution. To validate the efficacy of our approach for long-horizon
033            instructions, we introduce a new benchmark called EnduroSeq and conduct ex-
034            periments using the WinBench short instruction dataset. The results demonstrate
035            that ActionFiller significantly enhances task completion rates and execution effi-
036            ciency, offering a novel solution for the application of intelligent agents in com-
037            plex environments.

      The development of language models (LM) has led to the emergence of AI-based agentsWang et al.

046   (2023); Wang et al. (2024a) primarily rely on two methodologies: constructing execution pipelines
047   for predefined tasksWang et al. (2024a) or using trained models to predict actions based on the
048   current stateZhang et al. (2024); Hong et al. (2024).
049   In an OS, agents analyze the current state to predict subsequent actions through a decision-making
050   mechanism that evaluates this state and selects the most optimal action. However, before making
051   decisions, state cognition modules—such as icon and text detectors—are employed to assess the run-
052   ning environmentHong et al. (2024). This approach can reduce execution efficiency and prolong ex-
053   ecution times, especially with complex long instructions. Moreover, this decision-making paradigm
      differs significantly from human cognitive processes. Humans tend to optimize their choices based

077   ment improves the responsiveness and adaptability of OS agents.
078   The ActionFiller framework consists of two types of action-oriented prompts: one for subtasks and
079   another for action sequences. Subtask prompt: The objective of the subtask prompt is to construct a

081   tional flexibility. To achieve this, we introduce a Foresight Optimization Module (FOM). Initially,

088

089   Executable action sequences can be directly executed by the OS agent. 2) Unexecutable Action
090   Sequences: Characterized by variable parameters, these sequences cannot be executed without addi-
091   tional context or information. Once the parameters are updated based on the current environmental
092   state, they can become executable. 3) Pure Textual Descriptions: This type emphasizes conveying
093   actions through natural language, providing a narrative or illustrative format. However, these de-
094   scriptions often exceed the LM’s immediate capabilities, necessitating further elaboration or context
095   for effective execution.
096   To address the limitations of the latter two types of prompts that cannot be executed directly, we pro-
097   pose the CohesiveFlow method. Inspired by masked language modeling, the CohesiveFlow agent
098   integrates current environmental data and executed action sequences to refine parameters and de-
099   compose textual descriptions into actionable sequences, ensuring both feasibility and effectiveness.
100

102   using the WinBench short instruction dataset. Experimental results indicate that ActionFiller signif-
103   icantly enhances task completion rates and execution efficiency, offering a transformative solution
104   for the deployment of intelligent agents in complex environments. In summary, our contributions

179   3.1   P IPELINE D EFINITION
180
181   In this paper, we consider an agent that employs a large language model, denoted as L, in conjunction
182
      with a text-based memory, M, using the Windows operating system as an example. To address the
      instructions provided by a human user, represented as q, the agent operates within an environment
183
      defined by an execution function E. At each step ti , the agent utilizes an observer, such as a text or
184
      a icon detector, to obtain the observation oi from the current environment state si . Subsequently, it
185   runs L(q, M, oi ) to predict the current action ai . As the action ai is executed, the environment state
186   transitions from si to si+1 according to E(si , ai ).
187
188
      This observe-act loop continues until the model predicts the stop action ai = STOP or reaches a
      predetermined termination condition, such as a maximum number of steps. The pipeline is illustrated
189
      in Figure 2(a). From this figure, it is evident that each action execution requires one perception of
190
      the environment from the observer. However, frequent reliance on the observer during long-horizon
191   instructions could be time-consuming.
192
193   To address this challenge, our objective is to minimize the number of observations as much as
194
      possible in the execution step. The general pipeline can refer to the Figure 2(b). When observation
      cannot be bypassed, we also provide detailed prompts to facilitate action prediction. Leveraging
195
      the LLM’s high accuracy with short instructions, we consider using short instructions to effectively
196
      resolve a long-horizon instruction. To achieve this, we first introduce a structural memory SM that
197   encompasses various basic functions for each application on the PC, where each function consists
198   of a sequence of instructions and actions, along with explanations for each action. Each basic
199   function contains only 3-6 action steps. We then retrieve SM to select the appropriate basic function
200   F for the instruction. We treat F as a foundational element and employ it to generate reusable
201   subtask prompts and subsequent action prompts, thereby invoking the observer only when absolutely
202   necessary. Next, we introduce two types of action-oriented fill-in-the-blank, subtask prompt and
203   action prompt.

      4.2    DATASET & BASELINES & M ETRICS
401
402   Dataset We utilize the WindowsBench and ENDUROSEQ datasets to evaluate the performance
403   of our ActionFiller framework and baseline methods. WindowsBench, originally derived from the
404   UFO agent, consists of 30 samples spanning 9 applications, along with a cross-application dataset
405   containing a rich variety of operational samples. ENDUROSEQ is a custom dataset designed to
406   include extensive subtasks and actions. It is segmented into static and dynamic categories, with the
407   former targeting single-path solutions and the latter accommodating multi-path solutions.
408   Baselines In our paper, we use GPT-4o and GPT-o1 as our baseline.
409
      Metrics We use two metrics to evaluate the performance of mobile device operation agents across
410
      different dimensions:
411
412          • Success Rate (SR): This metric quantifies the agent’s ability to successfully accomplish as-
413            signed tasks. A score of 1 is attributed when a task is fully completed, signifying successful
414            execution.
415          • Completion Rate (CR): This metric evaluates the agent’s intermediate performance during
416            task execution, specifically assessing the effectiveness of its actions. In scenarios requiring
417            complex planning, even if the task is not fully completed, incremental progress or partially
418            effective actions contribute positively to this score.

531
      In conclusion, this paper presents the ActionFiller framework as a novel approach to enhance the
      efficiency of operating system agents in executing long-horizon tasks. By leveraging the strengths
532
      of large language models and integrating short, executable tasks through innovative fill-in-the-blank
533
      prompts, ActionFiller minimizes redundancy and optimizes performance. The introduction of the
534   Foresight Optimization Agent and Action Template Agent allows for the effective generation of ac-
535   tion prompts, while the CohesiveFlow method ensures seamless execution by incorporating the cur-
536   rent environmental state. Our experiments, validated by the EnduroSeq benchmark and conducted
537   on the WinBench dataset, reveal significant improvements in task completion rates and execution ef-
538   ficiency. Overall, ActionFiller paves the way for more effective applications of intelligent agents in
539   complex environments, demonstrating its potential to redefine task execution strategies in operating
      systems.
