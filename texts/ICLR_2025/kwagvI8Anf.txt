009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have shown great success in leveraging complex
012            relationships in data but face significant computational challenges when dealing
013            with large-scale graphs. To tackle this issue, graph condensation methods aim to
014            compress large graphs into smaller, synthetic ones that can be efficiently used for
015            GNN training. Recent approaches, particularly those based on trajectory match-
016            ing, have achieved state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance in graph condensation
017            tasks. Trajectory-based techniques match the training behavior on a condensed
018            graph closely with that on the original graph, typically by guiding the trajectory
019            of model parameters during training. However, these methods require repetitive
020
               re-training of GNNs during the condensation process, making them impractical
               for large graphs due to their high computational cost, e.g., taking up to 22 days
021
               to condense million-node graphs. In this paper, we propose a novel Precompute-
022
               then-Adapt graph condensation framework that overcomes this limitation by sep-
023            arating the condensation process into a one-time precomputation stage and a one-
024            time adaptation learning stage. Remarkably, even with only the precomputation
025            stage, which typically takes seconds, our method surpasses or matches SOTA re-
026            sults on 3 out of 7 benchmark datasets. Extensive experiments demonstrate that
027            our approach achieves better or comparable accuracy while being 96× to 2,455×
028            faster in condensation time compared to SOTA methods, significantly enhancing
029            the practicality of GNNs for large-scale graph applications. Our code and data are
030            available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/GCPA-F6F9/.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Graph learning through Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) (Kipf & Welling, 2016; Hamilton et al.,
036   2017) has significantly advanced graph data analysis, providing insights into complex structures
037   in social networks (Fan et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2022), molecular structures (Guo et al., 2021;
038   Gasteiger et al., 2021), and beyond (Dong et al., 2023; Li & Zhu, 2021; Liu et al., 2020).
039
      Graph Condensation. Large-scale graphs in real-world applications, often with millions of nodes
040
      and edges, pose significant computational challenges for training GNNs (Huang et al., 2021; Gao
041
      et al., 2024). Graph Condensation (GC) (Jin et al., 2021) generates a condensed graph (synthetic
042   graph) from a large original graph, enabling models trained on the condensed graph to be directly
043   applied to the original graph, achieving comparable performance on both graphs. GC enhances
044   training efficiency by reducing computational costs associated with large-scale graphs. Recent stud-
045   ies demonstrate that GC facilitates efficient GNN training with minor performance loss (Gao et al.,
046   2024; Jin et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2022; Zheng et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024).
047
      Structure-Free (SF) Condensation. Structure-free methods have recently achieved state-of-the-art
048   (SOTA) performance in node classification tasks (Zheng et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024). These
049   methods condense the original graph into a new graph with only node features and labels, but with
050   no edges. As illustrated in Figure 1a, the condensed graphs are structure-free, with nodes only con-
051   nected to themselves. While it may be surprising that structure-free condensation can provide an
052   effective summary of the original graph, these methods obtain SOTA performance and also simplify
053   the optimization objectives compared to alternatives. Hence, we focus on structure-free condensa-
      tion in our work.

083   Trajectory-based Methods. Trajectory matching has emerged as a key technique in recent advance-
084   ments of graph condensation, as presented in methods SFGC (Zheng et al., 2024) and GEOM (Zhang
085   et al., 2024). This approach assumes that the training trajectories, i.e., the sequence of model pa-
086   rameters obtained by model training steps, should closely match for both the original and condensed
087
      graphs. The process starts by collecting training trajectories on the original graph. Then, the col-
      lected trajectories are used to set up the model on the condensed graph, aiming to keep the subse-
088
      quent training steps consistent across both versions of the graph. This matching process ensures that
089
      the training is effective even on the condensed graph.
090
091   Efficiency Issue of Trajectory-based Methods. Trajectory-based methods require a substantial
092   number of trajectories to achieve advanced performance, where each collected trajectory requires
093
      a complete training process that restarts from random parameter initialization. As depicted in Fig-
      ure 1a, trajectory-based condensation requires repeating the model training process multiple times
094
      (e.g., 200 times), each time re-initializing the parameters and training the model for multiple epochs,
095
      taking a remarkably long time (e.g., 452 hours) on million-node graphs. As presented in Figure 1b,
096   this stage takes up the majority of the total condensation time. This repetitive model re-training is a
097   key limitation as it is highly time-consuming, resulting in extended running time, as shown in Fig-
098   ure 2. This inefficiency poses a significant barrier to applying these methods in practice, which can
099   lead to missed opportunities in critical applications like social network analysis and fraud detection.
100
      Our Precompute-then-Adapt Method. To address the inefficiencies of trajectory-based meth-
101
      ods, we propose a novel Graph Condensation framework via a Precompute-then-Adapt approach
102   (GCPA). Our method employs a one-time precomputation stage and one-time adaptation learn-
103   ing stage, eliminating the need for repetitive re-training with different random initializations. The
104   precomputation stage involves extracting structural and semantic information from the original
105   graph, achieving competitive performance within a short time. The adaptation stage further refines
106   the precomputed features (representations) to improve performance with minor additional costs. As
107   a result, we achieve competitive accuracy (-0.1% to +2.1%) on node classification tasks with sub-
      stantially faster training time (96× to 2,455×) compared to SOTA trajectory-based methods.

      Our two-stage precompute-then-adapt framework differs fundamentally from existing methods.
109   With the help of precomputation of node features and adaptation of synthetic features, we achieve
110   a level of computational efficiency that was previously unattainable. This framework significantly
111   reduces training time while maintaining competitive performance, thereby opening opportunities for
112   deploying graph condensation on resource-constrained devices.
113
      We summarize the key contributions of our work as follows:
114
115
      • We propose a new framework, GCPA, for graph condensation. It is efficient, consisting only of
116     a one-time precomputation stage and a one-time adaptation learning stage. Compared to SOTA
117     methods, our framework avoids costly repetitive re-training of models, achieving significant effi-
118     ciency improvements.
119
120   • Our framework is also effective. With just the one-time precomputation stage, which extracts
121
        structural and semantic information from the original graph, our method can already surpass or
        match the performance of best baselines on 3 out of 7 benchmark datasets. With the one-time
122
        adaptation learning stage, we can further enhance performance via class-wise feature alignment,
123
        achieving SOTA results across all datasets.
124
125   • Through extensive experiments on benchmark datasets, we demonstrate that our method achieves
126     better or comparable accuracy with up to 2,455× faster training time than existing methods, mak-
127     ing it more suitable for practical applications.

132   In this section, we introduce the components and the implementation details of our framework.
133
134   2.1   P RELIMINARIES
135
136   Let G = {X, A, Y} denotes a graph, where X ∈ RN ×d denotes the node features matrix with N
137   nodes with d-dimensional features, A ∈ {0, 1}N ×N represents the adjacency matrix encoding the
138   graph structure, Y ∈ RN ×C denotes ground truth one-hot node labels on C classes, while y ∈ RN
139   is the label in vector form. Graph condensation aims at generating a synthetic graph corresponding
140   to an existing graph such that a model trained on the synthetic graph is effective when applied to the
141   original graph. Given an original graph T = {X, A, Y} with N nodes, the objective is to generate
142   a smaller synthetic graph S = {X′ , A′ , Y′ } with N ′ nodes such that a GNN trained on S achieves
143
      similar performance on T as another GNN trained directly on T for specific tasks. In particular,
      structure-free graph condensation emerges as a storage-efficient graph condensation approach where
144
      the adjacency matrix is set to an identity matrix, A′ = I, so the synthetic graph does not contain
145
      structural information.
146
147   Node classification is a prevalent task simplified by graph condensation. This task involves assigning
148   labels to nodes based on their features and structures. Formally, given a graph G = {X, A}, and
149
      a subset of nodes NL ⊆ N with known labels YL ∈ RNL ×C , the transductive semi-supervised
      node classification task involves predicting labels YU ∈ RNU ×C for an unlabeled subset of nodes
150
      NU ⊆ N . The corresponding optimization goal can be formulated as a bi-level problem,
151
152                                       min L(GNNθS (X, A), Y)
153                                         S
                                                                                                         (1)
154                               s.t.   θS = arg min L(GNNθ (X′ , A′ ), Y′ ),
                                                  θ
155
156   where θ denotes the learnable parameters of a GNN model, θS represents the optimal GNN param-
157   eters learned on the synthetic graph, L is a loss function evaluating the node classification perfor-
158   mance. Existing graph condensation approaches optimize this bi-level problem to learn an optimal
159   synthetic graph S such that a trained GNN with parameters θS yields optimal performance on T .
160   However, the bi-level optimization problem is computationally intensive as it involves nested opti-
161   mization loops. To mitigate this issue, we introduce our framework that directly optimizes synthetic
      node features for improved efficiency.

173   2.2       OVERVIEW OF GCPA F RAMEWORK
174
      The overall pipeline of the proposed precompute-then-adapt graph condensation framework is pro-
175
      vided in Figure 3. We introduce two stages, i.e., a precomputation stage and a representation
176
      adaptation learning stage to produce structure-free synthetic data. The precomputation stage in-
177   volves structure-based neighbor aggregation and semantic-based merging on the original graph,
178   which achieves competitive performance within a relatively short time. The representation adap-
179   tation learning stage further refines the precomputed features using a class-wise feature alignment
180   objective to improve performance with minor additional costs.
181
182   2.3       S TRUCTURE - BASED P RECOMPUTATION
183
184   In the context of graph-based learning models, neighbor information aggregation refers to the pro-
185   cess by which node features are enriched with the structural information from neighboring nodes.
186   This process allows a node’s feature vector to incorporate not just its own information but also that
187
      of its surrounding neighborhood. This aggregation is critical for capturing relationships and depen-
      dencies in graph-structured data.
188
189   Drawing inspiration from the graph diffusion process (Gasteiger et al., 2019), we leverage neighbor
190   structural information to pre-process the original node features. The goal of graph diffusion is to
191   smooth node features based on the underlying graph’s topology, facilitating the effective propagation
192   of information across nodes. The structure-based precomputed features H with K-hop neighbor
193
      aggregation can be recursively computed as:

198   H = H(K) is the output of the last layer, coefficient α controls the contribution of raw features to

      2.5   R EPRESENTATION A DAPTATION L EARNING
223
224   Given the limited number of condensed nodes, it is crucial that these nodes ideally depict the overall
225   representations of their respective classes (depicted by the background color in Figure 3). Although
226   the precomputation stage focuses on capturing the structural and semantic information of the original
227   graph, its non-learning process could lead to sub-optimal representations to depict the class-wise
228   overall representations, as illustrated in Figure 3.
229   To address this limitation, we introduce a representation adaptation learning stage to further refine
230   the precomputed representations. We notice that representation contrastive loss can be considered
231   to enhance node embeddings for improved classification utility (Joshi et al., 2022). In the context
232   of graph condensation, we propose to align the condensed features with the original precomputed
233   features using a class-wise representation adaptation objective.
234                                                                 ′           ′
      Specifically, we introduce an adaptation module fadapt : RN ×d → RN ×d , implemented as a Multi-
235   Layer Perceptron (MLP), to adapt the synthetic features to better depict the overall representations:
236
237                                      Z′ = β X̂′ + (1 − β)fadapt (X̂′ )
                                                                                                           (4)
238                                         = β X̂′ + (1 − β)MLP(X̂′ ),
239
      where β is a hyperparameter controlling the contribution of precomputed representations, Z′ repre-
240
      sents the adapted synthetic representations. We adopt X′ = Z′ after the learning process.
241
242   We further construct the contrastive samples by first sampling a sufficient number of nodes as an-
243   chors from the precomputed representations, from which the adaptation module learns to refine the
244
      synthetic representations. For each anchor node on the original graph, we sample a synthetic node
      belonging to the same class as a positive sample and a set of arbitrary synthetic nodes as nega-
245
      tive samples. With the sampled contrastive pairs, we optimize the cross entropy loss to distinguish
246
      between the adapted positive and negative samples:

468   4   R ELATED W ORK
469
470
471
      Graph Condensation. Graph condensation (Jin et al., 2021; 2022; Yang et al., 2023; Liu et al.,
      2022; Xu et al., 2024), derived from dataset distillation, is a technique aimed at producing a much
472
      smaller version of a graph while retaining as much information as possible from the original. Its op-
473
      timization goal is for GNNs trained on the condensed graph to perform similarly to those trained on
474   the original. Graph condensation methods are typically categorized into two types: structured graph
475   condensation, which generates both node features and graph structure, and structure-free methods,
476   which only focus on synthesizing node features without explicitly constructing graph structures.
477
      Structured Graph Condensation. These methods synthesize graph structures using a neural net-
478
      work that generates links between nodes based on their features. GCond (Jin et al., 2021) is the
479
      first such method, using a gradient matching loss between the original and condensed graphs, but
480   its nested optimization loop limits efficiency. DosCond (Jin et al., 2022) introduced a more efficient
481   one-step gradient match and a Bernoulli distribution for structure sampling. GCDM (Liu et al.,
482   2022) generates smaller graphs with a distribution similar to the original graph, using a distribution
483   matching loss measured by maximum mean discrepancy. SGDC reduces a graph set into a smaller
484   set with fewer graphs via self-supervised representation matching. SGDD (Yang et al., 2024) incor-
485   porates the original graph structure through optimal transport. GDEM (Liu et al., 2023) aligns the
      eigenbasis of the condensed and original graphs to facilitate structure learning.

      Structure-Free Graph Condensation. These methods assume that structural information can be
      embedded directly into the synthetic node features, bypassing the need to generate graph structures.
512
      GCondX (Jin et al., 2021), a variant of GCond, focuses solely on feature learning via gradient
513
      matching without the inner loop. SFGC (Zheng et al., 2024) matches training trajectories with
514   expert guidance, and GEOM (Zhang et al., 2024) adjusts the matching range for different node
515   difficulties. To improve efficiency, GC-SNTK (Wang et al., 2024) replaces the inner loop using a
516   kernel-based approach to synthesize a smaller graph efficiently.
517
      Graph Coarsening. Graph coarsening methods (Cai et al., 2021; Loukas & Vandergheynst, 2018;
518
      Huang et al., 2021; Deng et al., 2019) reduce the graph’s size by clustering nodes into super-nodes.
519
520   Coreset Selection. Coreset selection methods (Sener & Savarese, 2017; Welling, 2009; Wolf, 2011)
521   condense the graph by selecting a subset of the original nodes and retaining the edges between
522   them. K-Center (Sener & Savarese, 2017) trains a Graph Convolutional Network (GCN) (Kipf &
523
      Welling, 2016) on the original graph to generate embeddings, from which the k-nearest nodes are
      then sampled to form a subgraph.
524
525   Different from existing methods, our GCPA framework introduces a novel approach for graph con-
526   densation, simplifying the training process while enhancing performance. Our framework employs
527   a streamlined, one-time precomputation and adaptation process that extracts and aligns features ef-
528   ficiently, avoiding the computationally expensive re-training phases seen in SOTA methods.

      5   C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this paper, we propose a new framework, GCPA, for graph condensation. It is efficient, consist-
533
      ing only of a one-time precomputation stage and a one-time adaptation learning stage. Compared to
534   SOTA methods, our framework avoids costly repetitive re-training of models, achieving up to 1,890x
535   faster training time than existing methods. Our framework is also effective, surpassing or match-
536   ing the performance of the best baselines on 3 out of 7 benchmark datasets with just the one-time
537   precomputation stage, and achieving SOTA results across all datasets with a further one-time adap-
538   tation learning stage. Our framework demonstrates that precomputation is a promising solution for
539   efficient graph condensation, which is also flexible as it can be further enhanced through adaptation
      learning. In the future, we plan to explore more precomputation techniques for graph condensation.

      A.3   A BLATION S TUDY ON P RECOMPUTATION S TAGE
782
783   We conduct an ablation study on the precomputation stage and present the results in Table 8. Specif-
784   ically, we use randomly initialized synthetic features instead of sampled precomputed features. We
785   detail the steps on aligning the labels of the condensed graph with the actual classes when features
786   are initialized randomly. (1) Initialization: We map the randomly initialized features to the actual
787   classes by assigning labels based on the original class distribution. The synthetic nodes are divided
788   among the classes proportionally to their distribution on the original graph. This ensures that nodes
789   associated with the same class are identified from the beginning. (2) Adaptation: In the adaptation
790   stage, the synthetic nodes are optimized using a class-wise alignment loss to refine their features.
791
      This ensures that the synthetic features represent their respective classes more distinctly. The re-
      sults illustrate the importance of the precomputation stage, which provides precomputed features
792
      that achieve better performance than randomly initialized features during the adaptation stage.
