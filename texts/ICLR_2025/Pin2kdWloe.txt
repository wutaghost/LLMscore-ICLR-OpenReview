009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Continual Learning solutions often treat multitask learning as an upper-bound of
012            what the learning process can achieve. This is a natural assumption, given that
013            this objective directly addresses the catastrophic forgetting problem, which has
014            been a central focus in early works. However, depending on the nature of the
015            distributional shift in the data, the multi-task solution is not always optimal for
016
               the broader continual learning problem. In this work, we draw on principles from
               online learning to formalize the limitations of multitask objectives, especially when
017
               viewed through the lens of cumulative loss, which also serves as an indicator of
018
               forward transfer. We provide empirical evidence on when multi-task solutions are
019            suboptimal, and argue that continual learning solutions should not and do not have
020            to adhere to this assumption. Moreover, we argue for the utility of estimating the
021            distributional drift as the data is being received and show preliminary results of
022            how this could be exploited by a simple replay based method to move beyond the
023            multitask solution.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Continual learning (CL) (e.g. Ring, 1994; Thrun & Mitchell, 1995; Silver et al., 2013; Parisi et al.,
      2019; Hadsell et al., 2020; Lesort et al., 2020), sometimes referred to as lifelong learning, directly
029
      aims to address the problem of how to construct a model that continuously adapts. Typically, the
030
      problem definition — or rather the solution definition — comes down to a list of desiderata that is
031   expected from the system (e.g Schwarz et al., 2018; Hadsell et al., 2020; Mundt et al., 2023). The
032   debate on the ultimate goal of continual learning and the problem definition is still ongoing. In this
033   work we take the view of Mundt et al. (2023): the model needs to be able to remember previous
034   knowledge, hence to deal with catastrophic forgetting (McCloskey & Cohen, 1989; French, 1999),
035   and reuse this knowledge to learn quickly new tasks (forward transfer), under the assumption that
036   the model capacity is finite and fixed, and the amount of compute it can do per time step is finite
037   and fixed. Traditionally, fixing catastrophic forgetting has been seen as the first step towards solving
038   continual learning, as retaining some information is needed in order to exhibit transfer, and most
039
      research focused on resolving this specific aspect. In this work we question this goal, formally asking
      whether minimizing catastrophic forgetting is a good objective to achieve continuous adaptation. Our
040
      question is inspired by Kumar et al. (2023), who show theoretically that an agent with limited capacity
041
      must dynamically compromise between retaining old information and acquiring new information in
042   order to maximise its lifelong performance (formalised in Section 3). In other words, minimizing
043   forgetting alone might not achieve the other desiderata of continual learning (e.g. Wołczyk et al.,
044   2021; Wu et al., 2023; Mundt et al., 2023). To understand this trade-off we start by arguing that most
045   methods aimed at solving catastrophic forgetting rely, implicitly or explicitly, on the assumption that
046   a multi-task objective is optimal and effectively employ objectives which approximate the multi-task
047   objective. However, depending on the non-stationarity of the data, there can be interference during
048   learning that can make a multi-task objective considerably sub-optimal (e.g. He et al., 2019; Du et al.,
049   2018). Figure 1 depicts this intuition.
050   Drawing inspiration from the online learning literature, in this work we quantify optimality using the
051   average lifelong error, which aligns closely with the concept of dynamic regret, as further elaborated
052   below. In order to study the optimality of the multi-task objective we design two agents: single-task
053   (ST) and multi-task (MT). The ST agent forgets everything after each task, while the MT agent
      minimizes the multi-task objective, i.e., the average loss over all previous tasks, and represents a

091   2       BACKGROUND : F ROM M ULTI -TASK TO O NLINE L EARNING
092
093   Multitask learning (Caruana, 1997) refers to a learning process that averages the losses incurred
094   on multiple tasks. The original goal was to promote sharing of features and therefore speed up
095
      learning and resulting in solutions that generalize better. Within the Continual Learning literature
      the multitask objective comes about when analyzing the ability of systems to prevent catastrophic
096
      forgetting (McCloskey & Cohen, 1989; French, 1999) and it is consequently incorporated into several
097
      existing algorithms, either explicitly or implicitly.
098
099   Traditionally — see e.g. Parisi et al. (2019) — continual learning methods tend to be grouped into
100   three categories, according to how they approach the catastrophic forgetting problem, though this
101
      categorization is not without fault (see e.g. Titsias et al., 2019). The first category encompasses
      regularization based methods, such as Elastic Weight Consolidation (EWC) (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017).
102
      EWC explicitly assumes the multitask solution as optimal1 , and builds the method as an approximation
103
      of this objective when one does not have access to other tasks. This multitask approximation is
104   prevalent, even if sometimes implicitly, in many other regularization methods (e.g. Zenke et al.,
105   2017a; Maltoni & Lomonaco, 2018; Swaroop et al., 2019; Li & Hoiem, 2017, etc.) as recently argued
106   by Yin et al. (2020) and Lanzillotta et al. (2024). The second category of methods consist of replay
107
          1
              See equation (2) of their derivation.

108
      methods (e.g. Robins, 1995; Shin et al., 2017), where the replay is effectively emulating the multi-task
109   objective by representing the task not currently available.2 The third category, dynamic architecture
110   methods (e.g. Zhou et al., 2012; Rusu et al., 2016; Mallya & Lazebnik, 2018) avoid catastrophic
111   forgetting by increasing the capacity of the model. While these methods do not seem to directly
112   mimic the multitask objective, they effectively partition the model capacity between the tasks, which
113   is akin to maximizing the average performance under a fixed capacity constraint. In Appendix B we
114   review some of the most famous algorithms in greater detail, providing evidence for our claims. In
115   general, most continual learning algorithms do not employ a multitask objective; however they can be
116   interpreted as biased estimates thereof. In this work we choose to look at the multitask objective as
117
      an abstraction of any specific continual learning algorithm, in order to provide a high level intuition
      and formalism which can be useful more broadly for the CL community.
118
119   Online Learning (OL) on the other hand, (Cesa-Bianchi & Lugosi, 2006; Hoi et al., 2018; Orabona,
120   2019) offers a fundamentally different perspective on lifelong learning. OL prioritizes rapid adapt-
121   ability to new data over maintaining strong performance on previously seen data. In this paradigm,
122   algorithms are commonly evaluated using regret, a measure that captures the model’s ability to
123
      adapt efficiently to the evolving data stream throughout its lifetime. This emphasis on adaptability
      highlights OL’s unique approach to addressing the challenges of dynamic environments. In this
124
      work we study a common metric in OL known as the Dynamic Regret (Herbster & Warmuth, 1998;
125
      Zinkevich, 2003) which compares, at each step of the learning, the current expected cost (or reward)
126   of the agent with the minimal achievable cost (or maximal reward). This metric is particularly relevant
127   in slowly-drifting or piecewise stationary settings such as those typically arising in CL (e.g. Hadsell
128   et al., 2020). More precisely, we ignore the comparator and study instead the average lifelong error
129   without loss of generality3 .
130
      In continual learning, the adoption of OL metrics is not a new concept. In the context of Online
131
      Continual Learning (OCL) (Cai et al., 2021; Lopez-Paz & Ranzato, 2017; Aljundi et al., 2019;
132   Buzzega et al., 2020), continual learning algorithms are often evaluated using an online metric. For
133   instance, the average online accuracy metric ao (Cai et al., 2021) is directly related to the average
134   lifelong error v, with ao = 100 × (1 − v). However, the OCL setting typically assumes both training
135   and evaluation occur in an online manner. this differs from the perspective we adopt in this work.
136   We decouple the training and evaluation protocols, allowing for potentially offline objectives and
137   optimization procedures (i.e., revisiting the same data multiple times), while maintaining an online
138   evaluation of the model’s performance. This approach enables us to explore a fundamental question:
139   is minimizing forgetting the right objective for achieving lifelong adaptability?

      To do so, we define ∆T = vTST − vTM T , as the difference in average lifelong error of the two agents.
169   This quantity is central to our study.
170
171   Informally, ∆T measures the difference in the rate at which the risk on the current task decreases
172
      during training. An agent that achieves low risk early in training will have a lower average lifelong
      error compared to one that achieves a better final performance but at a slower pace. In this context,
173
      the ST agent benefits when there is significant “variation” in the task sequence, as the average MT
174
      objective may inadequately prioritize the current task. Conversely, when the number of updates per
175   task is severely limited, the MT agent’s bias toward averaging across tasks can lead to a lower overall
176   error, provided the tasks are reasonably similar. In other words, ∆T captures the trade-off between
177   stability and plasticity — or bias and variance — in a data-dependent fashion.

180

181   step of each task, while the MT agent is not reset, although its objective is updated. Crucially, we
182   do not assume that gradient descent is run to convergence. Instead, the number of update steps per
183   task, h, plays a pivotal role in our analysis. As we will demonstrate, h can determine which agent
184   performs best.

313   5      E MPIRICAL ANALYSIS
314
315   Our empirical analysis is structured into three main parts. First, we validate our theoretical framework
316   on complex continual learning benchmarks, encompassing both supervised learning and reinforcement
317   learning tasks. Next, we turn to a toy benchmark, Permuted-CIFAR10, where we can control the
318   task sequence’s instability by adjusting the permutation strength. This setup enables us to test our
319   theoretical predictions regarding the relationship between instability and task duration. Finally, we
320   showcase the practical applicability of our framework in continual learning by implementing a simple
321   variant of experience replay, where the objective is tailored to the instability of the data stream.

      Benchmarks We present results for supervised learning and reinforcement learning benchmarks.
325   For supervised learning we take two different benchmarks: the first is based on the CLEAR dataset
326   (Lin et al., 2021), a collection of images of 10 different classes spanning the years 2004-2014. We
327   split the collection into 10 tasks, one for each year. The second benchmark is a sequence of 5 different
328   open source classification datasets, with no semantic overlap between them. In particular, the tasks
329   consists in classification of automobile models (Krause et al., 2013), aircraft models (Maji et al.,
330   2013), textures (Cimpoi et al., 2014), dishes (Bossard et al., 2014) and pets (Parkhi et al.). Each
331   dataset has originally a different number of classes, samples and a different input size. To avoid
332   introducing biases in the models, we standardize all tasks to have only 30 classes, and we use the
333
      same batch size and amount of update steps in each task, regardless of the original dataset size.
      Hereafter we refer to this as the “MULTIDATASET” (MD5) benchmark. We have chosen these
334
      two benchmarks because they represent different types of distribution shifts. While the transitions
335
      from one task to the next in CLEAR are arguably smooth (the tasks differ in input resolution but
336   semantically are equivalent), in MD5 they are sharp, changing the semantics of the task altogether.
337   For reinforcement learning we rely on the Meta-World (MW) benchmark (Yu et al., 2020), which is
338   a collection of 50 distinct simulated robotic manipulation environments. We train our agents on a
339   sub-collection of 10 environments called ML10 and we evaluate their average lifelong reward on the
340   same environments in an online fashion. We chose this environment due to it being previously used
341   to highlight interference in continual learning (Wołczyk et al., 2021). More details in Appendix D.
342   Notes on the empirical setup. In line with out theoretical analysis, we use the same task duration
343   h for each task -which is also in line with typical practice in continual learning. More precisely, h
344   is the number of parameter updates performed, which may correspond to multiple passes through
345   the dataset. After each update the performance of the agent is evaluated on a separate test set, in the
346   case of supervised learning, or on new interactions with the environment. The evaluation is always
347   performed on the current task. Additionally, at the end of training on all the tasks we measure the
348   agent’s multitask (offline) accuracy ACCagent or multitask (offline) reward Ragent , which consists
349   in the average performance across all tasks, and is a typical CL metric (Lopez-Paz & Ranzato, 2017;
350
      Powers et al., 2022). To aid interpretability and comparison with the offline performance we report
      the average lifelong accuracy ao = (1 − v) × 100 which is more common in the literature (Cai et al.,
351

478   5.3   D EMO : A DATA - DEPENDENT OBJECTIVE FOR REPLAY
479
480   One of the main takeaway messages of this work is that the optimization objective in continual
481   learning should be treated as a data-dependent quantity. Broadly speaking, the objective should reflect
482
      the instability of the sequence, enabling forgetting when it is high and avoiding it when it is low.
483   We design a simple variant of the experience replay (ER) algorithm (Lin, 1992; Zhang & Sutton,
484   2020), which we call Selective Replay (SR) that does not replay from previous tasks when there is
485   high instability in the sequence. Generally, one could rely on any heuristical measure of ∆IT and adapt
      to the current stream, trading forgetting for forward transfer. In practice, in this simple experiment we


                                                                                                           9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      create a new controlled benchmark from CIFAR10, which we call ‘C10 mixed’, where we increase
487   the permutation size from 16 to 32 after 5 tasks. We know from Figure 3 that forgetting is beneficial
488   when the permutation size is 32, since the instability is very high (we choose h = 6000 such that
489   ∆T < 0). Intuitively, in this benchmark memory is useful only on the first half of the sequence,
490   where there is positive transfer between the tasks. Thus, both the ER and ST agent are suboptimal, as
491   the former is forced to remember irrelevant information -which affects its capacity to fit the new data-
492   and the latter fails to remember any useful information. SR is designed to remember the relevant
493   information and discard irrelevant one. We take advantage of the knowledge of the sequence, and
494   simply change the objective from ER to the ST objective when the instability is increased.
495   In Figure 4 we plot the test error over the training trajectory of the three agents: ST, ER and SR.
496   As expected, the SR agent has the lowest average lifelong error, and the ER agent has the lowest
497   multitask error - meaning that it has the lowest forgetting. Observe the switch in behaviour midway
498   through the sequence of task: in the first half of the sequence the ER agent outperforms the ST agent,
499   while the opposite is true in the second half. Because of its dynamic objective, the SR agent is able to
500   always adhere to the best performing behaviour.

      6    D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
522
523   In this work we explore the optimality of the multitask objective in continual learning. Multitask
524   objectives arise as a natural target to address catastrophic forgetting. However, as was highlighted in
525
      previous works as well, the multitask objective is suboptimal when considering the overall continual
      learning problem, which ultimately is about lifelong adaptability. Borrowing from the rich literature
526
      on online learning, we formalize sufficient conditions for suboptimality in the restricted scenario of
527
      convex objective and linear models. We show empirically that our theoretical results can be predictive
528   of the behaviour of the nonlinear system. We discuss the limitations of our approach in Appendix C.
529
530   Crucially we believe our work highlights at least three different observations. Firstly, while the
531
      suboptimality of the multitask objective was observed early on in continual learning, most methods
      are still heavily relying on it. We argue that this is not necessary. Indeed, we showed that one can
532
      easily modify a replay based method to take into account task similarity and be able to outperform
533
      the multitask agent. We argue that more continual learning methods should remove the reliance
534   on multitask objective or at least reason explicitly about the assumptions being made. Secondly,
535   we argue that without making assumptions on data stream, one cannot behave optimally. Thus, it
536   should be common for continual methods to exploit the structure of the data stream, either estimating
537   online or assuming it as initial condition. Third, in order to do the above, further formalization of
538   the continual learning problem and theoretical tools to describe data non-stationarity are needed.
539   In particular, connecting the field with related topics, such as online learning, but also others like
      invariances, causality, can provide a rich source to borrow from and adapt mathematical constructs.

1376   is the negative log-likelihood, and we obtained the ground truth probabilistic model, then the Fisher
1377   information matrix is equivalent to the Hessian matrix, and R̂i (θ) coincides with the second order
1378   Taylor expansion when the gradient at θi⋆ is null.

       C     L IMITATIONS
1513
1514
       Our work is a small step towards understanding and formalizing the existing assumptions in continual
1515
       learning. The theoretical framework is limited to the convex case with linear models. Nevertheless
1516   we argue that theory is useful as long as it is predictive of behavior, even if it does not describe the
1517   actual setup.
1518
1519   Additionally, the proposed formalism is not descriptive enough to address complex shifts in
1520   the data distribution, as it relies on the assumption that there are contiguous time intervals (called
1521   tasks) where the data distribution is locally i.i.d..
1522
1523   Another limitation of the work is the choice of the MT agent, which is an abstract and
1524
       unattainable rendition of continual learning algorithms. For example, experience replay may be
       biased to the current task, or simply fail to represent the past data distributions due to the limited
1525
       buffer. In order to evaluate the exact degree of optimality of any specific algorithm the multitask
1526
       objective should be modified in accordance with the algorithm.
1527
1528   Finally, the selective replay algorithm only provides a proof-of-concept idea of how the
1529   structure of the non-stationarity can be exploited by continual learning algorithms. In practice, one
1530   would need to estimate the sequence instability in order to run it. We believe that the online estimate
1531   of a sequence instability for the design of adaptive objectives is a promising avenue of future work.

1648
