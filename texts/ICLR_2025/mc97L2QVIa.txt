                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011
               Offline multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) faces significant challenges
012            due to distribution shift issues, exacerbated by the high dimensionality of joint
013            actions and complex joint behavior policy distributions. While existing methods of-
014            ten focus on independent learning or offline value decomposition with conservative
015            value estimation, they may still lead to out-of-distribution (OOD) joint actions and
016            reduced performance. This is primarily due to the lack of exploration opportunity
017            and implicit policy dependencies in offline settings. To address these challenges,
018            we propose an offline policy decomposition method incorporating joint policy
019            regularization constraints. Our approach utilizes a diffusion generative model to
020
               capture the joint behavior policy, followed by a decomposition of the extracted
               score function. This decomposition is then used to regularize individual policies in
021
               a decentralized manner. Experimental results demonstrate that our method achieves
022
               SOTA on continuous control tasks in standard offline MARL benchmarks.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   In recent years, Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning (MARL) has demonstrated remarkable progress
028   in addressing complex decision-making problems that necessitate high-quality coordination among
029   multiple entities. Significant achievements have been realized in challenging domains such as DOTA,
030   soccer simulations, StarCraft II, and AI-driven economic models (Zhang et al., 2021a; Guo et al.,
031   2023; Chen et al., 2021; Mannion et al., 2016; Zheng et al., 2020; Berner et al., 2019; Ma et al.,
032   2024). However, the online learning paradigm often impedes the application of MARL algorithms to
033
      broader real-world scenarios, particularly when simulation environments fail to accurately capture
      real-world complexities or when real-world exploration entails inherent risks and substantial costs.
034
      For example, creating a simulation environment that comprehensively replicates market economics
035
      or allowing MARL algorithms to explore government incentive policies and gather market feedback
036   for policy updates presents formidable challenges (Zheng et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024; Gao
037   et al., 2024). Consequently, offline MARL has emerged as a promising paradigm (Formanek et al.,
038   2023; 2024). By leveraging existing datasets to develop effective strategies without necessitating
039   direct environmental interaction during training, offline MARL potentially bridges the gap between
040   simulated and real-world applications.

      MARL primarily fall into two categories: independent learning methods (Yang et al., 2021; Jiang
055   & Lu, 2021; Ma & Wu, 2023; Shao et al., 2023) and offline value decomposition paradigms (Wang
056   et al., 2023a;c). These approaches typically adopt the conservatism principle from single-agent offline
057   reinforcement learning (Levine et al., 2020; Prudencio et al., 2023), employing pessimistic estimation
058   of each agent’s value function. However, these methods have inherent limitations: independent
059   learning lacks effective coordination mechanisms, while offline value decomposition methods are
060   susceptible to selecting out-of-distribution joint actions during decentralized execution.
061   Our visualization analysis of standard offline MARL datasets MAMujoco (Peng et al., 2021) reveals a
062   critical factor contributing to the performance gap between existing methods: the presence of multiple
063   optimal policy combinations, analogous to the classic multiple Nash Equilibria (NE) or equilibrium
064   selection problem in game theory (Tian et al., 2023; Franzmeyer et al., 2024). As illustrated in
065   Fig. 1, this phenomenon results in extremely complex joint policy distributions, posing unique
066   challenges for offline MARL algorithms. For instance, in SMAC cooperative tasks, teams can achieve
067   victory through various, equally efficient strategies, leading to a multi-modal joint policy space. This
068   complexity manifests even in simple XOR coordination games, where the presence of multiple global
069
      optima can render many existing algorithms ineffective. Individual-Global Maximization (IGM)
      based methods may erroneously select OOD joint actions, while independent training approaches
070
      often struggle to maintain coordination (Matsunaga et al., 2023). Alarmingly, even behavioral cloning
071
      on expert trajectory datasets can lead to suboptimal policy combinations, failing to capture the
072   intricate interdependencies among agents.
073
074   To elucidate the complexity of these challenges, consider a simple coordination game where two
075
      agents must synchronize to achieve an optimal joint action. Even in such a rudimentary structure, the
      presence of multiple global optima can render many existing algorithms ineffective. For instance,
076
      IGM-based methods may erroneously select out-of-distribution (OOD) joint actions, while decen-
077
      tralized training approaches, operating under the assumption of fixed policies for other agents, may
078   encounter similar issues. More disconcertingly, even behavioral cloning on expert trajectory datasets
079   can lead to the selection of OOD joint actions. Moreover, it is fundamentally impossible to recover a
080   joint policy distribution with multiple equilibria through any pairwise individual policies.
081
      In this work, we propose a novel offline MARL algorithm, named Offline MARL with Sequential
082
      Score Decomposition (OMSD), that leverages advanced Diffusion models to get accurate score
083
      functions of the joint behavior policies, and decompose the joint score functions into coordinated
084   individual score functions for each agent’s policy regularization.
085
086   Our method encompasses the following key contributions: (1) Introduction of Diffusion Models to
087
      accurately capture complex joint policy distributions, offering superior expressiveness compared
      to traditional Variational Auto-Encoder (VAE) or mixture of Gaussian distributions in representing
088
      the intricate (Wang et al., 2023b), multimodal distributions inherent in MARL environments. (2)
089
      Development of a novel score function decomposition method, enabling the extraction of individual
090   policies from joint policies while maintaining overall coordination. This approach overcomes the
091   limitations of conventional policy decomposition methods and effectively handles symmetric and
092   near-symmetric policy distributions. (3) Theoretically, we demonstrate the efficacy of our method in
093   handling complex MARL environments, particularly highlighting its advantages in addressing sym-
094   metric and near-symmetric policy distributions. Our analysis indicates that the approach effectively
095   mitigates OOD behaviors while preserving the underlying task’s shared reward structure. Empirical
096   results corroborate these findings, with our method significantly outperforming existing value-based
097   and policy-based approaches across multiple tasks in standard offline MARL benchmarks provided
098
      in OMAR (Pan et al., 2022).

133   Diffusion probabilistic models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020) are a likelihood-based
134   generative framework designed to learn data distributions q(x) from offline datasets D := xi , where
135   i indexes individual samples (Song, 2021). A key feature of these models is the representation of the
136   (Stein) score function (Liu et al., 2016), which does not require a tractable partition function.
137   The model’s discrete-time√generation procedure involves a forward noising process, defined as
138   q(xk+1 |xk ) := N (xk+1 ; α̃k xk , (1− α̃k )I), at diffusion timestep k. This is paired with a learnable
139   reverse denoising process, pθ (xk−1 |xk ) := N (xk−1 |µθ (xk , k), Σk ), where N (µ, Σ) represents a
140   Gaussian distribution with mean µ and variance Σ. The variance schedule is defined by αk ∈ R. In
141   this framework, x0 := x corresponds to a sample in D, and x1 , x2 , . . . , xK−1 are latent variables,
142   with xK ∼ N (0, I) for appropriately chosen α̃k values and a sufficiently large K.
143   Starting with Gaussian noise, samples are iteratively generated through a series of denoising steps.
144   The training of the denoising operator is guided by an optimizable and tractable variational lower
145   bound, with a simplified surrogate loss proposed in (Ho et al., 2020):

      Policy-based Offline MARL with CTDE Learning. In the CTDE framework, the centralized
217   training process typically leverages other agents’ actions and global states information to learn
218   optimal joint strategies. The joint policy is then appropriately decomposed to obtain executable
219   individual policies. For value-based methods, the IGM (Individual-Global-Max) principle is often
220   relied upon to decompose the centralized critic Qtot (s, a1 , a2 , . . . , an ) into local value estimation
221   networks Q̂i (s, ai ) suitable for individual policy execution. In policy-based MARL methods, such
222   as FOP (Zhang et al., 2021b) and AlberDICE (Matsunaga et al., 2023), the IGO (Individual-Global-
223   Optimal) decomposable assumption is typically used to directly extract individual policies.
224

      Equations (7) and (10) reveal that the gradients in offline policy-based MARL consist of Q-value
      gradients and behavior policy regularization terms. Unfortunately, this structure introduces significant
249
      challenges for joint policy update. An obvious problem arises in the coordination of Q-value gradients
250
      part. In offline MARL, the absence of online data collection severely limits the ability to adjust
251   policies through newcoming experiences. Consequently, the direction of Q-value gradients heavily
252   depend on the coverage of the offline datasets, potentially missing optimal gradient directions. This
253   issue is further exacerbated by the non-convex nature of the value function. Even when individual
254   agents’ policies satisfy local improvement, the gradients of the joint policy may still lead to suboptimal
255   directions due to misalignment of individual gradients (Kuba et al., 2022; Pan et al., 2022).
256
      Admittedly, the CTDE frameworks can slightly alleviate the Q-value gradients problem by directly
257   providing local gradients of the joint Q-function to each agent. However, the multi-modal property of
258   the joint behavior policy greatly chanllenge the regularization process. In online learning, due to the
259   coordinated updates between policies, the joint policy typically exhibits a unimodal nature, which
260   can be decomposed as the product of all agents’ individual policies. This property no longer holds
261   in offline datasets that contain multiple joint policies of equal quality. Applying this assumption in
262   such cases often leads to distribution shift and suboptimal policy regularization. We demonstate this
263   phenomenon in the following proposition. The proof is provided in Appendix B.3.

284   3.2   S EQUENTIAL S CORE D ECOMPOSITION
285
      To address these challenges, we need to focus on modeling joint behavior policy with powerful
286   generative models and developing effective decomposition methods. Under the BRPO framework,
287   when we have trained a perfect generative model for behavior policy, we can naturally distill the
288   score functions as policy regularization. The decomposition of joint policies thus translates to the
289   decomposition of score functions.
290
      The key challenge is to keep joint actions within the support sets of joint behavior policies while
291
      obtaining individual regularization terms for each agent. Naive factorization in the KL divergence of
292   the joint policy only constrains update directions towards individual behavior policy distributions,
293   failing to guarantee joint update directions stay close to the joint behavior policy distribution. With
294   independent learning or IGO-based CTDE frameworks, score terms become either biased and
295   uncoordinated or intractable.
296
      To address this, inspired by coordination descent and Multi-agent Transformer (MAT, Wen et al.
297
      (2022)), we adopt sequential policy decomposition as µ(a|s) = Πni µ(ai |s, ai− ), where ai− rep-
298   resents the joint actions of prefix agents of agent i. The KL divergence of the joint policy be-
299   comes DKL (Πni πi (ai |s)||Πni µ(ai |s, ai− )) and the corresponding regularization for each agent is
300   ϵ̂i = −σt ∇ai log µ(ai |s, ai− ).
301

473   6     R ELATED W ORKS
474
475   Offline MARL. Early research in offline MARL mainly made efforts to extend the pessimistic
476   principles from offline single-agent RL. For example, MAICQ (Yang et al., 2021) and MABCQ
477
      (Jiang & Lu, 2021) extended the pessimistic value estimation such as CQL to multi-agent and
      discuss the extrapolation error under exponential increasing dimension of joint actions space problem.
478
      Furthermore, OMAR (Pan et al., 2022) dealed with the local optima in independent learning paradim
479
      with zero-th order optimization. Motivated by this, CFCQL (Shao et al., 2023) further improved
480   OMAR with counterfactual value estimation to avoid over-pessimistic value estimation. Recently,
481   MACCA (Wang et al., 2023c) and OMIGA (Wang et al., 2023a) has incorporated causal credit
482   assignment technique and the IGM principle into the offline value decomposition process to enhance
483   the credit assignment. In SIT (Tian et al., 2023), authors recognized the data-imbalance problem and
484   handle it with reliable credit assignment techinique. On the other hand, AlberDICE (Matsunaga et al.,
485   2023) and MOMA-PPO (Barde et al., 2023) recognized and addressed OOD joint action coordination
      problems with alternative best response and world model based planning. Our method aligns in this


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      direction and try to model complex behavior policies with diffusion models. There are also some
487   works following the trajectory generation route, such as MAT (Wen et al., 2022), MADT (Meng et al.,
488   2021), and MADTKD (Tseng et al., 2022). These methods are beyond our discussion scoup.
489
490
      Diffusion Models in RL. Recently, motived by the great advantage of diffusion models, RL re-
      searchers turn to seek the possibilities of introducing diffusion models into RL area. Previous works
491
      can be typically divided into three topics: serving as planner, serving as policy, and serving for
492
      data augmentation. Our method mainly fall in the second topic. Single RL suffers multimodal and
493   MLE fails due to mode cover. Diff-QL (Wang et al., 2023b) and SfBC (Chen et al., 2022) used
494   diffusion model to represent the behavior policy and generate a batch of candidate actions with
495   diffusion models, then use resampling to choose the executive actions. These methods suffer the
496   inherent drawback of slow inference process of diffusion models. For this reason, some works tried
497   to accelerate the sampling process of diffusion actor. EDP (Kang et al., 2024) and consistency-AC
498   (Ding & Jin, 2023) leveraged the advanced diffusion models to accerate the action sampling in RL
499   tasks. In offline MARL, there are few works such as MADiff (Zhu et al., 2023) and DOM2 (Li et al.,
500   2023), which take diffusion models as a centralized planner or independent actors.
501
502   7    C ONCLUSION
503
504   This paper studies the key challenge of the unbiased decomposition of the joint action behavior
505   distribution in the offline MARL. We start from developing CTDE algorithms based on behavior-
506   regularized policy optimization without value decomposition and revealed an important factor which
507   greatly limited the policy constraint methods in offline MARL: the infactorization joint policy property
508   in the offline datasets. Based on this, we proposed two unbiased policy decomposition methods and
509   transfer them into the gradients of agents as the score regularization distilled from the pretrained
510
      diffusion models. The experiment demonstrates the superior of our methods and the effectiveness
      of policy improvement with coordinate joint action selection. One future work is to develop more
511
      precise and optimal policy decomposition methods to enhance the ability of offline MARL.

722   A    D ETAILED A NALYSIS OF O FFLINE MARL DATASET C HARACTERISTICS
723
724   In this appendix, we provide a more comprehensive analysis of the offline MARL datasets, expanding
725   on the observations briefly mentioned in the introduction. Our analysis focuses on the MAMujoco
726   datasets from OMAR (Pan et al., 2022), a widely used benchmark in offline MARL research.
727   Similar to single-agent offline RL, MARL datasets exhibit multi-phase distributions that vary with
728   dataset quality. As the quality of datasets decreases, the distributions become increasingly compound
729   and challenging to model accurately. This phenomenon is illustrated in Figure 4, which shows the
730   joint policy distributions for four different quality levels of MAMujoco datasets. This complexity in
731   distribution is a primary reason for the weak performance of policy-based MARL methods in offline
732   settings. Accurately representing these intricate policy distributions requires advanced generative
733   models, which are often beyond the capabilities of current offline MARL algorithms.
734   An important characteristic of offline MARL datasets is the variability in policy distributions, even
735   among datasets with similar accumulated rewards. This variability stems from the randomness
736   in source policy training and data collection processes. To illustrate this point, we conducted an
737   experiment comparing policy distributions from different random seeds on the same task and reward
738   level. Figure B.2 demonstrates how different seeds can lead to distinct policy distributions despite
739   achieving similar overall performance. This variability underscores the need for offline MARL
740
      algorithms to be robust to different policy distribution patterns.
741   A striking feature of many offline MARL datasets is the presence of symmetry in policy distributions.
742   This symmetry often arises from two main sources. First, multiple Nash Equilibria (NE) in multi-
743   agent tasks lead to multiple, equally optimal solutions. For example, in a coordination game, strategies
744   like "both agents choose left" or "both agents choose right" may be equally effective. This leads
745   to symmetric distributions in the collected data, as illustrated in Figure B.3(a). Second, agent role
746
      symmetry occurs in environments where agents have interchangeable roles (e.g., two identical units
      in SMAC). In these cases, the actions of Agent 1 and Agent 2 may be equally valid when swapped.
747
      This role symmetry manifests as symmetric patterns in the joint policy distribution.
748
749   It’s important to note that in real-world offline MARL datasets, distinguishing between these two
750   types of symmetry can be challenging. As pointed out by ?, the source of symmetry (whether from
751   multiple NE solutions or from interchangeable agent roles) is often indiscernible in the collected data.
752   The complex characteristics of offline MARL datasets, including multi-phase distributions, variability
753   across seeds, and inherent symmetries, pose significant challenges for existing algorithms. The
754   inadequate understanding of these dataset properties leads to poor performance in value decomposition
755   methods. Even with a well-designed value decomposition, the complexity of policy distributions
      can significantly hinder performance. The inherent multi-modality in these datasets, stemming
