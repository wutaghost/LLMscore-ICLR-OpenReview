011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013               Alignment tuning is crucial for ensuring large language models (LLMs) behave
014            ethically and helpfully. Current alignment approaches, including supervised fine-
015
               tuning (SFT) and preference optimization (PO), require high-quality annotations
               and significant training resources. This paper proposes a low-cost, tuning-free
016
               method using in-context learning (ICL) to enhance LLM alignment.
017
                  By comparing token generation probabilities of unaligned models with those of
018
               their aligned counterparts, we identified polarity tokens—a type of special token
019
               that guides the response trajectory and affects the model’s alignment performance.
020            Based on this, we designed heuristic rules to select ICL demonstration examples
021            that effectively influence polarity token distributions. To further improve the qual-
022            ity of the selected ICL demonstrations, we model content, style, and alignment as
023            a causal structure, and employ Average Treatment Effect (ATE) to quantitatively
024            study the impact of the style factor. We found that after restyling the ICL demon-
025            strations, they were more effective at eliciting the LLM’s alignment capabilities.
026               We packaged the restyled examples as prompts to trigger few-shot learning,
027            improving LLM alignment. Our experiments show that rewritten examples boost
028            alignment, safety, and reasoning across various tasks. Compared to the best base-
029            line approach, with an average score of 5.00 as the maximum, our method achieves
030
               a maximum 0.10 increase on the Alpaca-eval task (from 4.50 → 4.60), a 0.19
               enhancement on the just-eval-instruct benchmark (from 4.44 → 4.63), and
031
               a maximum improvement of 0.32 (from 3.53 → 3.85) on the MT-Bench dataset.
032
               These findings underscore the need for deeper analysis and theoretical understand-
033            ing of alignment for advancing future LLM research.

036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Alignment tuning helps bridge the gap between raw model capabilities and the nuanced requirements
039   of different tasks, such as delivering accurate information, maintaining user safety, and handling
040   sensitive topics with care (Shneiderman, 2020; Shen et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Qi et al., 2024b).
041   Currently, the instruction-following paradigm (Ouyang et al., 2022; Sun et al., 2023; Dai et al., 2024;
042   Rafailov et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024), which combines supervised fine-tuning
043   (SFT) and preference optimization, is widely used in alignment tuning. However, this paradigm
044
      requires high-quality annotated data and consumes significant computing resources. If there were a
      method to improve LLM alignment without modifying model parameters, it could effectively reduce
045
      training costs and increase the versatility of the alignment process (Brown, 2020). To this end, we
046
      propose an in-context learning (ICL) method, which instructs LLMs to handle downstream tasks
047   using few-shot learning from ICL demonstrations.
048
049   Many ICL demonstration selection methods have been proposed to choose demonstrations that ef-
050
      fectively elicit the desired model capabilities (Liu et al., 2022; Min et al., 2022; Ye et al., 2023; Luo
      et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2024; Choi & Li, 2024; Wang et al., 2024), however, these methods are not
051
      suitable for alignment tuning due to its unique nature.
052
053   Alignment tuning imposes two conflicting demands on LLMs: on one hand, LLMs need to provide
      more in-depth, informative, and helpful content (factuality) (Shen et al., 2023); on the other hand, for


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      safety reasons, LLMs must refuse to answer inappropriate queries (safety) (Ji et al., 2024). Thus, we
055   need to achieve a delicate trade-off between these two abilities (Anwar et al., 2024), as improving
056   the harmlessness of an LLM assistant may result in it being less helpful (Bai et al., 2022). This
057   trade-off presents a challenge when selecting demonstrations – the demonstrations must balance
058   both factuality and safety. To address this, we investigate those special tokens that influence the
059   generation trajectory and denote them as polarity tokens (Section 2). Within polarity tokens, benign
060   tokens steer content generation towards helpful and constructive outputs, while malicious tokens can
061   lead the trajectory towards harmful or undesirable outputs. Additionally, we find that the polarity
062   tokens unique to factuality and safety are entirely different, which further validates that alignment
063
      imposes two distinct requirements on LLMs that need to be balanced.
064   Identifying the polarity tokens related to alignment tuning results in a powerful tool. By observ-
065   ing changes in the generation probability of polarity tokens, we can approximate whether an ICL
066   demonstration effectively elicits LLM alignment capabilities (Section 3.1). However, we also need
067   to consider how we can further enhance the alignment capability brought by an effective ICL demon-
068   stration that alters polarity token generation probabilities.
069   Content (referring to the contextual information contained in a demonstration, including the system
070   instruction and one-shot learning example) and style (referring to the format in which the content is
071   organized and written with a specific style and structure) can impact in-context alignment (Huang
072   et al., 2024b). We hypothesize that the content and style of an ICL example have a causal relation-
073   ship with the alignment effectiveness. We consider restyling an ICL demonstration example as an
074   intervention and use Average Treatment Effect (ATE) to determine the effect of different restyling
075
      methods on alignment. Using ATE, we design a method to learn how to restyle an ICL demon-
      stration example to enhance its effectiveness (Section 3.2). We then combine these restyled ICL
076
      demonstrations to achieve a trade-off between factuality and safety (Section 3.3).
077
078   Evaluating our approach across multiple datasets and LLMs (Section 4) reveals that different bench-
079   marks have distinct preferences regarding LLM alignment capabilities, and that the combinations of
080   restyled ICL demonstrations can meet these diverse preferences. In summary, our contributions are
081
      three-fold:
082
      • We propose polarity tokens, a class of tokens that guides the content generation trajectory of
083
        LLMs and influences their alignment performance. Specifically, under different alignment re-
084     quirements, factuality and safety capabilities correspond to distinct polarity tokens. We use
085     changes in the generation probability of polarity tokens as an indicator to retrieve high-quality
086     ICL demonstration examples that can elicit LLM alignment capabilities.
087
      • We model the content, style, and alignment capabilities of ICL examples as a causal structure and
088
        employ ATE method to quantitatively explore the effect of style on alignment, thereby identifying
089
        a way to further enhance the quality of ICL demonstrations. We combine the restyled demonstra-
090     tions to balance factuality and safety, improving the LLMs’ overall alignment performance.
091
092   • We conduct a series of experiments across different datasets and LLM models, demonstrating the
093
        effectiveness and superiority of our proposed method. The experimental results show that, across
        the three benchmarks, our method achieves improvements of 2.22%, 4.28%, and 9.07% compared
094
        to the SOTA methods, respectively.

098
099   In this paper, the terms “unaligned LLMs” and “base LLMs” are interchangeably used to refer to
100   LLMs that have not undergone preference optimization. In contrast, we refer to LLMs that have been
101   fine-tuned with instructional data to promote ethical and beneficial behavior as “aligned LLMs”.
102   More details can be found in Appendix A.2.

104   2.1   T HE CHANGE IN TOKEN GENERATION PROBABILITIES DUE TO ALIGNMENT TUNING
105
106   Based on the characteristics of autoregressive models, we adopt a unique perspective—examining
107   the probability distribution of token generation—to study how alignment tuning affects and inter-
      venes in the content generated by the model.


                                                       2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Compared to base LLMs, aligned LLMs, after alignment tuning, increase the generation probability
109   of specific tokens when faced with the same queries—these tokens often guide the LLMs toward
110   producing ethical and regulated responses, where we demoted as benign tokens. Conversely, the
111   generation probabilities of other tokens, which are more likely to cause the LLMs to deviate toward
112   content lacking alignment safeguards, are reduced. We call these tokens as malicious tokens.
113
      To understand how the “alignment tuning” process affects token generation and to identify which
114
      tokens can be classified as benign or malicious, we propose analyzing the discrepancy between the
115   token generation probabilities of base LLMs and aligned LLMs. Specifically, for a given user query
116   q = {q1 , q2 , · · · }, we input it into an reference model r(x) to obtain its output o = {o1 , o2 , · · · }
117   via greedy decoding. For each position t, we define a ‘context’ at this position to be xt = q +
118   {o1 , · · · , ot−1 }. We denote the reference model’s probability distribution for predicting the next
119   token of this position as Ptrefer , where ot has the highest probability. Our analysis is driven by the
120   following questions: If we take the base model f as the reference model and compare the output
121   of the aligned model g to it, what kind of differences in probability distribution would we observe?
122   Conversely, if we treat the aligned model g as the reference model and compare the unaligned model
123
      f to g , what distinctions can we see?
124   We first view the unaligned model f as the reference model, and pass the context xt into the base
125   model f , we generate the reference output ot and the corresponding output probability distribution
126   Ptbase for sampling the next token at this position. Let ot represent the context for the t-th position,
127   thus we compute the probability distribution Ptalign of the aligned model g at the this position. If
128   alignment tuning enables the LLM to learn how to adjust the probability distribution over the output
129   vocabulary to prevent the model from favoring tokens that may lead to malicious content, then by
130   comparing P base and P align , we should observe a noticeable decrease in the generation probability
131
      of certain tokens.
132   Similarly, if we treat the aligned model g as the reference model and use its output as the reference
133   o, we can force the unaligned model f to generate o, and during this process, observe the shifts in
134   probability distributions between P align and P base .
135   Polarity tokens. When we consider the unaligned model f as the reference model, its generated
136   content tends to lack alignment safeguards and may be more harmful. As a result, in the earlier po-
137   sitions of such output (Qi et al., 2024a), malicious tokens dominate, steering the generation towards
138   harmful paths. In contrast, the aligned model g, after alignment tuning, should lower the generation
139   probabilities of these malicious tokens, pulling the output back onto the trajectory that is more in
140   line with the human values.
141   Therefore, when using the output ot from the unaligned model f as the reference, we can calculate
142   the probability distribution differences across the output vocabulary at position t. This is expressed
143   as: ∆Ptmalicious = Ptbase − Ptalign . For a validation dataset containing multiple q − o pairs, we count
144   all the tokens present in the reference outputs and calculate the average ∆Ptmalicious for each unique
145   output token ot . The output tokens with the highest average ∆Ptmalicious value are considered as
146   malicious tokens.
147
      Likewise, we view the aligned model g as the reference, and its output o as the ground-truth output.
148   We thus compute ∆P benign = P align − P base , and consider this difference as the adjustment to the
149   generation probabilities made through alignment tuning. After tuning, the aligned model g tends
150   to favor tokens that guide the generated content toward helpful and harmless outputs, whereas the
151   probability of these tokens is relatively low in the output of the unaligned model f . We also viewed
152   the tokens with the highest average difference ∆P benign as benign tokens.

      2.2   F INDINGS OF P OLARITY TOKENS
155
156   The alignment process for LLMs ensures that AI models are not only technically proficient but also
157   socially responsible, making them more suitable for real-world applications where trust and relia-
158   bility are critical. Therefore, LLMs that have undergone alignment tuning must generate factually
159   accurate information across various tasks (referred to here as “factuality”) while also refusing to
160   respond to malicious queries (referred to here as “safety”) (Shen et al., 2023). However, these two
161   capabilities present a “conflict” in terms of LLMs’ token generation preferences (Tuan et al., 2024).
      Specifically, when providing useful information, LLMs tend to generate tokens that convey a pos-

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025

      itive attitude in the initial stages of autoregressive generation (e.g., ‘Let’s’, ‘Here’s’). Conversely,
163   when refusing to respond, the probability distribution of generated tokens shifts toward polite and
164   apologetic expressions (e.g., ‘sorry’, ‘condone’, ‘cannot’). Analyzing these two capabilities together
165   reduces the distinctiveness of the distributions, thereby increasing the difficulty of analysis.
166
      To address this “conflict”, in this work, we designed two sub-tasks to facilitate a clearer analysis of
167
      polarity tokens associated with LLMs’ different alignment capabilities. The first sub-task focuses
168
      on analyzing the polarity tokens that enable LLMs to generate factually accurate information. The
169   second sub-task involves analyzing the polarity tokens that enhance the LLM’s safety capabilities.

194   3     ICL D EMONSTRATIONS FOR LLM A LIGNMENT
195
196   The analysis in Sec. 2.1 inspired us to make the following hypothesis: if an In-context Learning
197   (ICL) example positively influences the probability distribution of polarity tokens, then it should be
198   considered a high-quality ICL demonstration example. The following three questions then arise:
199   how can we use an objective, automated metric to identify a single high-quality demonstration ex-
200   ample (Section 3.1)? If we can further enhance the quality of this example, will it make it even more
201   beneficial for ICL (Section 3.2)? Multiple ICL demonstrations are often more effective than a single
202
      ICL example; how can we identify such an approximate optimal demonstration set (Section 3.3)?
203
204   3.1   S ELECTION OF S INGLE ICL D EMONSTRATIONS
205
206
      We propose the following hypothesis: a “good” ICL demonstration example should enable the model
      to significantly adjust the generation probabilities of polarity tokens, increasing the probability of
207
      generating benign tokens while reducing the probability of generating malicious tokens.
208
209   For a given user query q = {q1 , q2 , · · · }, to calculate the impact of an ICL example c on benign
210   tokens, we use the aligned model g to generate a reference output o. For all benign tokens {ob } in
211   o, we compute the enhancement in the generation probability of benign tokens by an ICL example
212
      using the following formula: ∆Pb (q, c) = Σot ∈{ob } αt [P base (ot |xt , c) − P base (ot |xt )].
213   Here, αt is used to control the importance weight of the probability difference. We believe that the
214   initial few tokens generated by the model have a greater impact on the trajectory of the generation.
215   Therefore, we assign higher weights αt to the tokens ot at the early positions, and gradually decrease
      the weight αt as the generation progresses.

236
      In this section, we hypothesize that restyling can further enhance the quality of ICL demonstrations,
237   and we validate this hypothesis through experiments.
238
239   We first provide the following definitions. content refers to the task-related information provided
240
      in an ICL example, including the system instruction and the demonstration, style represents the
      writing style of task-related information and the organizational structure of the content, and align-
241
      ment refers to the alignment effect exhibited by the model after using a particular example as a ICL
242
      demonstration.
243
244   We consider style and content to be the two most critical factors in applying ICL techniques for
245   alignment tuning. We model S (style), C (content), and A (alignment) as a causal structure (Pearl,
246
      2009), as illustrated in Figure 1. The variable C is the co-founder, which influences both S and A.
      Both C and S jointly influence alignment.

250   nipulated. On the one hand, using LLMs to modify the content of an

      lable. On the other hand, altering the content changes the nature of the
252
      demonstration, thus losing the significance of the research. Therefore,
253
      our primary interest lies in the impact of the intervenable factor S on
254   A, and we thus disregard the influence of C on A, focusing instead on
255   evaluating the effect of the controllable intervention S.

321   (approximate) optimal approximation solution (Liu et al., 2024).

      Previous research has shown that subtle interactions between the demonstrations in an ICL example
325   set can significantly influence the performance of LLMs in few-shot online learning. On the one
326   hand, maintaining a consistent response style across ICL demonstration examples can effectively
327   enhance LLM performance on downstream tasks (Lin et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024). On the other
328   hand, the multiple ICL demonstrations needs to be sufficiently diverse and complementary to fully
329   elicit LLMs’ task-oriented capabilities (Min et al., 2022). Notably, when dealing with safety tasks,
330   having refusal demonstration in the set becomes particularly crucial.
331   In Section 3.1, we already formed candidate sets {Scand f } and {Scand s }. Therefore, for the factuality
332   candidates {Scand f }, we restyled them using the “combined” style, while for the safety candidates
333   {Scand s }, we restyled them using both the “combined” and “refusal” styles. To achieve the optimal
334   trade-off between factuality and safety, we merged the restyled factuality and safety candidates into
335   a set {Scand } and employed a hierarchical traversal approach with early pruning (Hua et al., 2024) to
336   select three ICL examples4 from {Scand } to construct different demonstration sets. We evaluated the
337   performance of different combinations on the just-eval-instruct validation dataset, like what
338   has been done in Section 3.2.
339   Ultimately, we identified the three best combinations of the ICL examples. The first combination
340   consists of three factuality ICL examples restyled with the “combined” style. The second combina-
341   tion includes two factuality ICL examples and one safety example, all restyled using the “combined”
342   style. The third combination consists of two factuality ICL examples restyled with the “combined”
343   style and one safety example restyled with the “refusal” style. We refer to these combinations as
344   Restyled In-context-learning Demonstration Examples (RIDE), with the first combination denoted
345
      as RIDEf , the second as RIDEfs uni , and the third as RIDEfs hyb . We use these notations in the
      following sections. The prompts of RIDE series can be found in Appendix A.6, A.7, and A.8.

      4.1   DATASET, LLM S , AND BASELINE METHODS
351
352   Dataset. We use Alpaca-eval (a benchmark designed to assess the performance of language
353   models on natural language understanding, generation, and reasoning tasks) (Li et al., 2023),
354   just-eval-instruct (a dataset designed to assess the ethical reasoning capabilities of LLMs) (Lin
355   et al., 2024), and MT-Bench (a multi-turn dialogue dataset to evaluate various capabilities of LLMs,
356   such as reasoning, coding, and human-like interaction) (Zheng et al., 2023) as benchmarks.
357
358   LLMs. We use three models as the base models: Llama-2-7b-hf (Touvron et al., 2023), Mistral-
359   7b-v0.1 (Jiang et al., 2023), and OLMo-7B (Groeneveld et al., 2024). It is important to note that
360   these models have not undergone alignment tuning, resulting in sub-optimal alignment capabilities.

363   our work is U RIAL (Lin et al., 2024), which manually designed three ICL examples and combined

520   4.5   TAKEAWAYS OF THE E MPIRICAL S TUDY
521
      From the findings in Section 4.2, 4.2 and 4.4, in conclusion, we find that different benchmarks have
522
      different focal points when evaluating the capabilities of LLMs. In this context, both the content
523
      (whether the ICL example set is oriented toward safety or factuality) and the style (whether the
524   restyling is more structured or focused on refusal) interact with each other to jointly influence the
525   performance of ICL. Thus, through our experiments, we validate the causal relationship among con-
526   tent, style, and alignment. Furthermore, we observe that the safety and factuality capabilities of
527   LLMs are inherently conflicting, requiring us to find a trade-off to achieve the best overall perfor-
528   mance. This finding is also consistent with our analysis of polarity tokens. Therefore, the experi-
529   mental results validate the two key concepts proposed in this paper: the existence of polarity tokens
530   with different orientations toward safety and factuality, and the causal structure within alignment.
531
532   5     C ONCLUSION
533
534   In this paper, we take the first step by comparing the token generation probability differences be-
535   tween aligned and unaligned models, identifying different polarity tokens oriented towards factuality
536   and safety. By observing changes in the probability of polarity tokens, we identified a high-quality
537   single ICL demonstration. We then modeled the causal structure of style, content, and alignment,
538   using ATE as a tool to discover the restyling styles for factuality and safety. We used the restyled
539   examples as demonstrations in the ICL approach, eliciting the LLM’s capabilities for different tasks,
      and experimentally validated the effectiveness of the proposed ICL demonstrations.

759   A.1   R ELATED WORK
760
761   Impact of Alignment Tuning on Token Generation. As autoregressive generative models, LLMs
762   generate tokens by treating the input query and previously generated tokens as the context for pre-
763
      dicting the next token. They use this context to calculate the probability distribution over the output
      vocabulary. Following the principle that tokens with higher probabilities are more likely to be se-
764
      lected, the next token is randomly chosen from the output vocabulary based on these probabilities,
765
      introducing a degree of randomness to the generated content.
766
767   Many researchers believe that the process of alignment tuning modifies the token generation prob-
768   abilities of base LLMs. Lin et al. (2024) found that alignment tuning intervenes in the generation
769
      probabilities of specific tokens, thereby guiding the decoder to produce safe and reliable content. Qi
      et al. (2024a) argued that alignment tuning only achieves shallow safety alignment—it affects only
770
      the shallow positions (the first few tokens) in the generation process and fails to prevent jailbreak
771
      attempts beyond these initial positions. Similarly, Yuan et al. (2024) identified a refusal position bias
772   within the safety-tuning data, which influences the alignment performance of LLMs.
773
774   Most relevant to our work, SafeAligner (Huang et al., 2024a) proposed the concept of beneficial
775
      and harmful tokens that affect model alignment performance. However, these tokens were iden-
      tified based on the generation probability gap between the sentinel model and the intruder model,
776
      without explicitly defining which tokens are beneficial and which are harmful. Therefore, this paper
777
      introduces the concept of polarity tokens—a specific set of tokens that can influence the generation
778   of aligned content—and analyzes the relationship between different polarity tokens and alignment
779   performance.
780
781
782   ICL demonstration selection methods. Numerous methods for selecting the most representative
783   and highest-quality ICL demonstration examples have been proposed by researchers. Liu et al.
784   (2022) selects examples that are closest to the input test sample in the embedding space as a good
785   choice for ICL. Hua et al. (2024) ranks ICL demonstrations based on the average reward of the task
786
      directed by each example, using heuristic rules, and employs an early pruning hierarchical traversal
      approach to combine demonstrations. PICLe (Choi & Li, 2024) formulates the process of selecting
787
      ICL demonstrations as a Bayesian inference problem to identify high-quality demonstrations.
788
789   TopK + ConE (Peng et al., 2024) proposes a training-free method that selects examples that mini-
790   mize the inference model’s uncertainty as ICL demonstrations. Similarly, Wu et al. (2023) searches
791   for demonstrations capable of losslessly compressing test labels, while Iter et al. (2023) identify ICL
792
      demonstrations based on the cross-entropy difference of test labels.
793   However, none of these methods incorporate the essence of alignment tuning—changes in the to-
794   ken generation probability distribution—into the selection of ICL examples. In contrast, our work
795   integrates alignment tuning with ICL selection, effectively addressing this gap.
796

      A.2   BACKGROUND SETTINGS IN OUR WORK
799
800   In this paper, we use the terms “unaligned LLMs” and “base LLMs” interchangeably to refer to
801
      LLMs that have not undergone alignment processes, though they are not inherently malicious. In
      contrast, we refer to LLMs that have been fine-tuned with instructional data to promote ethical
802
      and beneficial behavior as “aligned LLMs”. We define an unaligned LLM as f (x; θ), where x is
803
      the input query and θ represents the model’s parameters responsible for generating output tokens.
804   The process of “alignment tuning” involves adjusting the parameters θ of a base LLM to produce
805   more controlled and regulated responses. Consequently, we represent the aligned LLM as g(x; β),
806   which is better aligned with human values and preferences. This process generally involves two
807   steps: supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on instructional data and reinforcement learning from human
808   feedback (RLHF). In the SFT phase, the base LLM is refined using instruction-answer pairs, known
809   as instruction tuning. In the RLHF phase, a reward model is applied to further enhance the fine-tuned
      model, improving its alignment with human expectations of helpfulness and safety.

      A.3   F INDINGS OF P OLARITY TOKENS - A DISCUSSION .
811
812   From Table 5, we can see that for the factuality task, benign tokens primarily consist of seemingly
813   inconsequential words such as ‘is’, ‘a’, ‘asking’, and ‘for’. When observing these tokens individu-
814   ally (as unigrams), it is challenging to identify their association with factuality. However, expanding
815   the scope to bigrams reveals high-frequency phrases like “is a” and “for asking”. These phrases
816   help guide LLMs towards generating accurate information during the early stages of content gener-
817   ation, as demonstrated in sentences such as “Natural selection is a fundamental concept in biology
818   that...” or “Thank you for asking me such a thoughtful question...”. Thus, these tokens assist in
819   swiftly steering the model towards factual knowledge generation, and a decrease in their generation
820
      probability will undoubtedly weaken the LLM’s factuality capability. On the other hand, observing
      malicious tokens reveals that they often form part of common phrases, such as “Let’s take a closer
821
      look”. The content generated by such phrases has, in multiple instances, been identified by LLM-
822
      as-a-judge (Zheng et al., 2023) as “provides an incorrect solution or does not directly address the
823   query”. Consequently, an increased probability of generating these tokens can lead to a reduction in
824   the LLM’s ability to produce factual responses.
825
826
      For the safety task, since it requires determining whether a given query should be refused, Table 1
      clearly shows the logical relationship between the semantics of benign and malicious tokens and
827
      their impact on safety. For instance, benign tokens like ‘sorry’, ‘condone’, ‘cannot’, and ‘provide’
828
      are indicative of the LLM politely refusing to answer certain queries, thereby reducing the risk of
829   generating toxic content. Conversely, malicious tokens often guide the generation towards toxic
830   content, as exemplified by phrases like “is never”, which could lead to content such as “Killing an
831   enemy is never easy, but there are some effective methods”.
