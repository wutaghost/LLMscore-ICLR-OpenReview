008                                                A BSTRACT
009
010            We consider the problem of model compression for Large Language Models
011            (LLMs) at post-training time, where the task is to compress a well-trained model
012            using only a small set of calibration input data. In this work, we introduce a new
013            low-rank approach to correct for quantization errors of activations in LLMs: we
014            propose to add low-rank weight matrices in full precision that act on the unquan-
015            tized activations. We then solve a joint optimization problem over the quantized
016
               representation of the weights and additional low-rank weight matrices to quantize
               both weights and activations. We focus on the case of 4-bit weight-and-activation
017
               quantization (W4A4). Using ranks equivalent to 10% of the original weight ma-
018
               trix size, our approach reduces the accuracy gap with the original model by more
019            than 50%. Using ranks equivalent to 30% of the original weight matrix, the accu-
020            racy gap is closed completely. We demonstrate our results on four recent LLMs,
021            namely Llama-2, Llama-3, Phi-3 and Mixtral models.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Large Language Models (LLMs) (Abdin et al., 2024; Dubey et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2024) have
028   demonstrated exceptional performances across a wide range of applications. However, due to their
029   massive size, these models require considerable computational and memory resources at inference.
030
      Post-training quantization (PTQ) is among the most important techniques to solve both memory and
031   compute issues during LLM inference. The majority of quantization schemes focus on compressing
032   LLMs by using weight-only quantization (Frantar et al., 2022; Shao et al., 2023; Dettmers et al.,
033   2023b). One major limitation of PTQ is the presence of magnitude outliers in the model layer
034   weights, which can severely affects the quantization process (Wei et al., 2022; Xiao et al., 2023)
035   and deteriorate the performances of quantized models. To handle them, several offline approaches
036   has been proposed in the literature, such as mixed-precision strategies (Dettmers et al., 2023b),
037   adapted rescaling Lin et al. (2024), and incoherence processing Chee et al. (2024); Tseng et al.
038   (2024). Recently, several works have introduced the use of supplementary low-rank weight matrices
039
      in full precision to mitigate quantization errors in weights Kang et al. (2024); Ou et al. (2024).
      This approach is analogous to the utilization of additional low-rank weight matrices for fine-tuning
040
      (Dettmers et al., 2023a; Hu et al., 2021).
041
042   Weight only quantization methods enable to store LLMs on smaller devices, and accelerate the
043   General Matrix-Vector Multiply (GEMV) operators in the decoding stage Lin et al. (2024); Frantar
044   et al. (2022), however, these approaches still require to keep activations in full precision (usually
045
      FP16). To improve on this, several works (Ashkboos et al., 2023; Xiao et al., 2023; Dettmers et al.,
      2022; Zhao et al., 2024) aim at quantizing both the weights and activations (and sometime KV cache)
046
      to compute the forward pass in low bit precision. Unlike weight quantization, the quantization of
047
      activations requires online strategies to compute their low bit representations on the fly (Jacob et al.,
048   2018).To deal with outliers in activations, (Xiao et al., 2023) propose to scale the weights, thus
049   reducing the magnitude range of activations. (Ashkboos et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024) propose to
050   process weights and activations using Hadamard transform. More recently Zhang et al. (2024)
051   propose to add low-rank correction terms in full precision in order to correct for quantization errors.
052   Although these approaches has been shown to be effective at W4A8, they still struggle to deal with
053   the case where activations are quantized in lower bit precision such as W4A4, leaving an opportunity
      to improve on current methods in these harder settings.

      Contributions. In this work, we improve on current SoTA approaches for PTQ, and introduce
055   LRC, a new method that leverages low-rank weight matrices in full precision to correct for activation
056   quantization errors. By jointly optimizing for quantized representations of the original weights
057   and full precision low-rank weights matrices correcting the errors of activation quantization, our
058   method allows for quantizing weights, and activations (and KV caches) to 4 bits with minimal loss
059   in accuracy. Our main contributions are summarized below.
060
061          • We introduce a general framework that aims at jointly optimizing for quantized represen-
062            tations of the original weights acting on the quantized activations, as well as full precision
063
               low-rank weights matrices operating on the unquantized activations.
064          • We derive an alternative scheme to solve the joint optimization problem and obtain a simple
065            algorithm easily compatible with recent quantization techniques, such as QuaRot (Ashk-
066            boos et al., 2024) and GPTQ (Frantar et al., 2022).
067          • Using our quantization scheme, LRC manages to reduce the accuracy gap with original
068            models by more than 50% using only low-rank matrices with 10% of the original size, and
069            outperform all current approaches at W4A4.

071   1.1   R ELATED W ORK
072
073   Dealing with Outliers. One major limitation of quantization techniques is the presence of outliers
074   in both weights and activations that can deteriorate the quality of the approximations, and lead to a
075   considerable drop in performance. A recent line of works (Ashkboos et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024)
076   proposed to apply specific rotations (and their inverses), on both weights and activations in order to
077   remove outliers while preserving exactly the output of the original model. Such a pre-processing
078   step, in combination with the GPTQ Algorithm (Frantar et al., 2022) enabling efficient quantization
079
      of the weights, and lead to SoTA performances in quantization at W4A8. In this work, we leverage
      this pre-processing step, and consider these methods as baselines for our approach where low-rank
080
      weight matrices in full precisions are added to the forward pass in order to correct for quantization
081
      errors on activations, enabling to quantize LLMs at W4A4 with a 50% gain.
082
083
      Low-Rank Correction. Recent works proposed to leverage low-rank matrices to reduce quanti-
084
      zation errors in LLMs (Zhang et al., 2024; Ou et al., 2024; Saha et al., 2024). In (Zhang et al.,
085   2024), the authors consider the case where both weights and activations are quantized, and propose
086   to add well-chosen low-rank matrices in full precision in the forward pass to reduce the quanti-
087   zation errors. To deal with outliers, they first compute some rescaling matrices (Lin et al., 2024;
088   Xiao et al., 2023) using a calibration dataset, and then propose to add low-rank approximation of
089   the rescaled residual errors between the original and quantized weights using SVD. Although the
090   rescaling process leverages statistical properties of the activations to remove outliers, Zhang et al.
091   (2024) do not exploit them for computing their low-rank correction terms. In (Ou et al., 2024), the
092   authors improve on the low-rank approach introduced in Zhang et al. (2024) and propose to apply
093
      a PCA on the output activation errors using a calibration dataset. In (Saha et al., 2024), the authors
      consider a joint formulation of the quantization problem to optimize for both the quantized weights
094
      and the low-rank terms. However, in these two works, the authors only focus on the quantization of
095
      the weights, leaving aside the quantization of activations. In this work, we also consider a joint for-
096   mulation, however our main focus is on improving the quantization of activations. We improve on
097   prior research by incorporating both the empirical distribution of activations and the errors induced
098   by activation quantization into our analysis to optimize the low-rank weight matrices.

117   Activation Quantization. While weight quantization enables to store large models at a lower

123

124
      tivations (Ashkboos et al., 2024; Xiao et al., 2023). Additionally, as quantizing activations require

126   Due to this constraint, previous work consider simple, yet efficient techniques such as round-to-
127   nearest (RTN) (Ashkboos et al., 2024) to quantize activations on the fly at inference time.
128

129
      max(abs(x)) and rounding to the nearest integer. We perform a simple hyper-parameter search
130
      for c. Our main focus is deriving a low-rank correction to the weight matrix that accounts for some
131   of the error incurred in activation quantization.

      In this work, we propose to add full precision low-rank weight matrices in the forward pass that
      act on the unquantized activations to correct for quantization errors of activations. In the following,
137
      we start by presenting the proposed framework to quantize a single weight matrix while correcting
138
      for quantization errors using additional low-rank weight matrices. Then we introduce our proposed
139   algorithm that effectively computes the quantized representations of the original weights in low-bit
140   precision and the low-rank weight matrices in full precision. We conclude this section by providing
141   an overview of the approach when applied on a standard LLM.

291   Application of LRC on LLMs. LRC consists of two stages: (1) the model is first pre-processed
292   according to the QuaRot procedure (Ashkboos et al., 2024), where Hadamard rotation matrices are
293
      fused with the weights to reduce the incoherence of both weights and activations, while maintaining
      the outputs of the original model. (2) Then, the model is quantized using the LRC algorithm 1, where
294
      both weights and activations are quantized, and optimized low-rank matrices in FP16 precision are
295
      added to the forward pass of each weight matrix.
296
297   To compute the Hessians (Σxy , Σx , Σy ) we follow Frantar et al. (2022) in using calibration dataset
298   taken from Wikitext-2: 128 randomly selected sequences of length 2048. We found that computa-
299
      tion of these matrices required 64-bit precision for numerical accuracy. By default, our subroutine
      for quantization follows GPTQ (Frantar et al., 2022), (we study other subroutines in the following
300
      section). LRC works sequentially through the weight matrices of the model, computing activations
301
      for each weight matrix, obtaining the covariance and cross-covariances matrices needed to apply
302   Algorithms 1 and solving the optimization problem 2 for each before moving to the next layer.

305   4     E XPERIMENTS
306
307   This section aims to achieve two primary objectives: (i) to demonstrate that LRC reduces the ac-
308   curacy gap with the original models by more than 50% while utilizing low-rank matrices with only
309   10% of the original size, and surpasses all existing methods under W4A4 settings; and (ii) to show
310   that when ranks corresponding to 30% of the original weight matrix are used in LRC, the accuracy
311   gap is fully eliminated. Furthermore, we establish that when activations remain unquantized and
312   only weights are quantized, the inclusion of low-rank correction terms becomes unnecessary.
313   In all our experiments, we build on top of the QuaRot (Ashkboos et al., 2024) codebase, extending
314   the method to include our approach. All our experiments focus on 4-bit quantization. We experiment
315   with quantization of Phi-3 (mini-4k-instruct), LLama-2 (7 and 13 B), Llama-3 (8B) and Mixtral
316   (8x7B). All results in the table are simulated using Pytorch.
317

322   matrix size. To show the effect of the LRC algorithm relative to previous approaches (Zhang et al.,
323   2024; Ou et al., 2024) we also consider a baseline of the QuaRot approach with SVD applied to the
      weight-matrix error (we denote this approach as SVD in tables 1, 2 and 3).

      To improve the accuracy of quantization, multiple approaches have considered use of groupsizing,
337   where weight and activation matrices are divided into groups of size e.g. 128, and each group is
338   scaled separately. This adds to the overall bitwidth, but improves accuracy. LRC can also be applied
339   with groupsizing: we repeated the experiment in Table 1 in Table 2, this time applying a groups size
340   of 128 (for activations only) to each method. Again, we see that LRC achieves multiple percentage-
341   point improvements relative to QuaRot that are not possible with simple SVD.
342
343   Weights only. To examine the effects of loss due to activation quantization, we re-ran the ex-
344   periements presented above without quantizing the activations. We used the same set up as above,
345   but we do no apply any quantizer operator in the forward pass (i.e. Qa is set to be the identity map),
346
      such that no activation quantization is performed. Table 3 shows the performances. We see that all
      methods are able to recover (almost) perfectly the accuracy of the original models in the W4 regime.
347
      This experiment indicates that when only the weights are quantized, there is minimal error to correct
348
      for SoTA approaches, and as a result, low-rank terms do not provide any additional improvement.

      5   C ONCLUSION & L IMITATIONS
400
      We have studied low-rank corrections for LLM quantization. Our main innovation is to connect the
401
      low-rank matrix to the original, pre-quantized activations, whilst processing the quantized activa-
402
      tions with a quantized weight matrix. Our method, LRC, solves jointly for the quantization of the
403   original weights and low rank structure to correct for errors induced by the quantization of activa-
404   tions. We have shown that a straight-forward approach to constructing the ranks, using SVD is not
405   effective. Our method has the added complexity of computing activation statistics Σ, but this al-
406   lows us to significantly close the accuracy gap at W4A4 by incorporating low-rank weight matrices
407   with ranks set to 10% of the original matrix sizes. To close the gap completely, we showed that
408   we needed ranks equal to 30% of the model size. Finally, we showed that LRC is composable with
409   other quantization techniques, including groupsizing.
410
411   Limitations. In this work we have not studied the computational costs of adding low-rank com-
412   putations to the forward pass. Some works have speculated that that the low-rank computation may
413
      be computable in parallel with the low-bitwidth computation: we leave such an implementation and
      a thorough study to future work.
414
415   We found that running our LRC procedure for multiple iterations did not comprehensively improve
416   the performance. We found that a single iteration was often sufficient, and anecdotally we found that
417   convergence was dependent on the damping factors used in Cholesky computations. We speculate
418   that larger calibration set may improve the condition of the Hessians.
419   Finally, our work highlights that for W4A4, there is significant information lost in quantizing activa-
420   tions. We have followed previous works in using a scale-then-round scheme, with hyper-parameter
421   search for the best scale. The need to perform activation quantization on-the-fly means that fast
422   (simple!) schemes are needed. this appears to be a productive direction for future improvements.

653
      GPTQ Algorithm. The GPTQ algorithm, introduced by Frantar et al. (2022), is a post-training
      quantization technique designed to efficiently reduce the precision of weights in large language
654
      models (LLMs) while maintaining their performance. To achieve this, the authors propose to ap-
655
      proximate a solution of the layer-wise quadratic approximation problem defined as:
656
657                                    min            Lq (W             c X∥2 ,
                                                          c ) := ∥W X − W
                                                                            2
                                 c ∈C(b)∩Rdout ×din
                                 W
658
659   where W is the original weight matrix, and C(b) is the constraint set of matrices admitting a certain
660   bit per weight b > 0 precision. The main difficulty of solving exactly this optimization problem re-
661   sides in the constraint set C(b), making the problem non-convex. To approximate a solution, (Frantar
662   et al., 2022) propose to improve the computational scheme of the greedy approach originally pro-
663   posed by LeCun et al. (1989) for pruning, and then adapted for quantization in (Frantar & Alistarh,
664   2022), by removing the ordering in the greedy quantization process, and applying the algorithm in
665   parallel over multiple columns.
666
667   Cholesky Factorization. Cholesky factorization is a numerical method used to decompose a sym-
668
      metric positive-definite matrix (PD) into the product of a lower triangular matrix with positive diag-
      onal coefficients and its transpose. This technique is particularly useful in solving systems of linear
669
      equations, performing matrix inversion, and computing the determinant of a matrix. More formally
670
      given Σ a symmetric PD matrix, there exists a unique lower triangular matrix L such that
