009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Although overparameterization theory suggests that neural networks can fit any
012            dataset with up to as many samples as they have parameters, practical limitations
013            often prevent them from reaching this capacity. In this study, we empirically
014            investigate the practical flexibility of neural networks and uncover several surprising
015            findings. Firstly, we observe that standard optimizers, such as stochastic gradient
016            descent (SGD), often converge to solutions that fit significantly fewer samples
017            than the model’s parameter count, highlighting a gap between theoretical and
018
               practical capacity. Secondly, we find that convolutional neural networks (CNNs)
               are substantially more parameter-efficient than multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs)
019
               and Vision Transformers (ViTs), even when trained on randomly labeled data,
020
               emphasizing the role of architectural inductive biases. Thirdly, we demonstrate that
021            the difference in a network’s ability to fit correctly labeled data versus incorrectly
022            labeled data is a strong predictor of generalization performance, offering a novel
023            metric for predicting generalization. Lastly, we show that stochastic training
024            methods like SGD enable networks to fit more data than full-batch gradient descent,
025            suggesting that stochasticity enhances flexibility beyond regularization effects.
026            These findings highlight the importance of understanding practical capacity limits
027            and their implications for model generalization, providing new insights into neural
028            network training and architectural design.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
      Understanding the capacity and flexibility of neural networks is fundamental to advancing deep
033
      learning research and applications. It is widely believed that neural networks can fit any training
034
      set containing at most as many samples as they have parameters. This belief stems from theoretical
035   results in universal approximation (Hornik et al., 1989) and empirical observations where large neural
036   networks can perfectly fit their training data, even with random labels (Zhang et al., 2016).
037
038
      However, in practice, the solutions found by neural networks are heavily influenced by training
      procedures, including the choice of optimizer, regularization techniques, and architectural design.
039
      These factors shape the loss landscape and determine which minima are accessible during training.
040
      Consequently, neural networks may not fully utilize their theoretical capacity to fit data, and their
041   practical flexibility can be significantly limited.
042
043   Moreover, the specific parameterization inherent in a neural network’s architecture can affect its
044
      ability to fit data. For example, convolutional neural networks (CNNs) exhibit inductive biases such
      as locality and translation invariance, which impact their performance on image data. Understanding
045
      how these architectural choices influence the practical capacity of neural networks is crucial for
046
      designing efficient and effective models.
047
048   To systematically study the practical flexibility of neural networks, we adopt the Effective Model
049   Complexity (EMC) metric (Nakkiran et al., 2021), which estimates the largest sample size that a
050
      model can perfectly fit using realistic training procedures. Unlike theoretical capacity measures, EMC
      accounts for the effects of optimization algorithms, regularization techniques, and data properties on
051
      a model’s ability to fit data.
052
053   In this paper, we conduct extensive experiments to analyze how data properties, model architectures,
      and training procedures influence EMC. Our key findings are:

055            neural networks can only fit datasets containing significantly fewer samples than the number
056            of model parameters.

               training data compared to full-batch gradient descent, suggesting that stochasticity may

067          • Activation Functions: ReLU activation functions improve a model’s ability to fit data

070   Our findings provide new insights into the practical limitations and capabilities of neural networks,
071
      highlighting the importance of training dynamics and architectural choices in determining a model’s
      capacity to fit data and generalize effectively.

074   2    R ELATED W ORK
075
076   Approximation Theory. Early deep learning theory focused on the function approximation ca-
077   pabilities of neural networks. The universal approximation theorem established that feedforward
078   networks with a single hidden layer can approximate any continuous function on compact subsets of
079   Rn , given sufficient width (Hornik et al., 1989). Subsequent studies provided upper bounds on the
080
      number of parameters required for specific function classes (Barron, 1993; Mhaskar & Poggio, 2016).
      These approximation theories typically address arbitrary compact sets or data on well-behaved mani-
081
      folds (Shaham et al., 2018) and often apply to shallow networks, limiting their practical applicability.
082
      In contrast, our work empirically measures neural network flexibility on real datasets, considering
083   various architectures and training procedures to capture factors that directly impact practical capacity.
084
085
      Overparameterized Neural Networks and Generalization. Traditional generalization theories
086   based on VC-dimension and Rademacher complexity suggest that models with low capacity should
087   generalize well (Vapnik, 1991; Bartlett & Mendelson, 2002). However, these theories do not explain
088   why highly overparameterized neural networks can generalize effectively even when they can fit
089   random labels (Zhang et al., 2016). Recent advancements in PAC-Bayes generalization theory have
090   provided frameworks to understand how overparameterized models can still generalize well by
091   assigning disproportionate prior mass to parameter vectors that fit the training data (Dziugaite & Roy,
092   2017; Lotfi et al., 2022; 2023). Empirical studies have further demonstrated that inductive biases and
093   overparameterization can enhance generalization (Huang et al., 2019; Chiang et al., 2022; Maddox
094
      et al., 2020). Specifically, Nakkiran et al. (2021) explored the data-fitting capacity of neural networks
      to understand the double-descent phenomenon, showing that overparameterization interacts with data
095
      properties and training dynamics to influence generalization.
096
097   In contrast, our study investigates the factors that influence the capacity of neural networks to fit data
098   by adopting the metric Effective Model Complexity (EMC) (Nakkiran et al., 2021). We empirically
099   measure the largest sample size that a model can perfectly fit under realistic training conditions and
100
      investigate how EMC predicts generalization performance. This approach bridges theoretical insights
      with empirical observations, providing a comprehensive understanding of neural network flexibility
101
      and its implications for generalization.
102
103
104   3    M ODEL C APACITY AND E MPIRICAL C OMPLEXITY
105
106   Quantifying Capacity. Determining how many samples a model can perfectly fit is straightforward
107   in the case of linear models, where capacity directly corresponds to the number of parameters.
      However, neural networks introduce complexity that goes beyond parameter counting. Our goal is to


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      define a practical metric that captures a neural network’s capacity to fit data under real-world training
109   conditions. This metric must meet three key criteria: (1) it should reflect the capacity to fit real-world
110   data, accounting for the influence of optimizers, regularization, and data augmentation; (2) it must be
111   sensitive to the characteristics of the training data, including the nature of the inputs and labels; and
112   (3) it should be computationally feasible to apply across diverse architectures and datasets.
113
      To address these criteria, we employ the Effective Model Complexity (EMC) metric (Nakkiran
114
      et al., 2021), which quantifies the largest sample size a model can perfectly fit using realistic
115   training routines. Unlike theoretical capacity measures that are architecture-specific or focus on
116   idealized conditions, EMC captures the impact of practical training dynamics, including optimization
117   procedures, regularization strategies, and data properties.
118
119   Computing EMC. Calculating EMC involves an iterative approach for each network size. Initially,
120   we train the model on a small number of samples. If it achieves 100% training accuracy, we re-
121   initialize and train on a larger set of randomly chosen samples. We iteratively perform this process,
122   incrementally increasing the sample size each time until the model no longer fits all training samples
123   perfectly. The largest sample size where the model still achieves perfect fitting is taken as the
124   network’s EMC. Importantly, we ensure that the initialization and data subsets are independent at
125   each iteration to maintain an unbiased capacity evaluation. Furthermore, we also tried performing all
126
      analyses instead with a relaxed requirement that the network fit 98% of its training data, which did
      not significantly affect results.
127
128   While it is possible to artificially prevent models from fitting their training set by under-training,
129   confounding any study of capacity, we ensure that all training runs reach a minimum of the loss
130   function by imposing three conditions: (1) the norm of the gradients across all samples must fall
131   below a predefined threshold; (2) the training loss should stabilize; (3) we check for the absence of
132
      negative eigenvalues in the loss Hessian to confirm that the model has reached a minimum rather than
      a saddle point.
133
134
135
      The differences between capacity, flexibility, expressiveness, and complexity. These terms
      are used in numerous ways, sometimes interchangeably and sometimes distinctly. For example,
136
      Rademacher complexity and VC-dimension are notions of complexity typically associated with
137
      flexibility, whereas the PAC-Bayes notion of complexity is information-theoretic and instead measures
138   compression. Expressiveness can describe the breadth of an entire hypothesis class, that is, all the
139   functions a model can express across all possible parameter settings. Approximation theories measure
140   the expressiveness of a hypothesis class by the existence of elements of this class, which are well-
141   approximate functions of a specified type. We will abstain from using the terms “expressiveness” and
142   “complexity” when describing EMC to avoid confusion, and we will use “capacity” and “flexibility”
143   when referring to a model’s ability to fit data in practice.
144
145   Factors Influencing EMC. Unlike VC-dimension or expressiveness concepts in approximation
146   theories, EMC depends not only on the hypothesis class but on every aspect of neural network training,
147   from optimizers and regularizers to the specific parameterization induced by the model’s architecture.
148   Choices in architectural design and training algorithms influence the loss surface geometry, thereby
149
      affecting the accessibility of certain solutions.

167   4.2   M ODELS
168
169   We evaluate the flexibility of diverse architectures, including MLPs, CNNs such as ResNet (He et al.,

171   adjust the width and depth of these architectures. For MLPs, we either increase the width by adding

185
      about our hyperparameter tuning are provided in Appendix B. By default, we use SGD.

194
      modalities. We scale a 2-layer MLP by modifying the width of the hidden layers and a CNN by

202   data-fitting capability.

204   EMC, the following sections will explore the underlying causes of these variations. Our goal is
205   to identify the distinct factors in the data and architectures contributing to these observed network

239   5.2.1        T HE B OUNDARY B ETWEEN OVERPARAMETERIZATION AND U NDERPARAMETERIZATION
240
241
      Linear regression models can fit at least as many samples as they have parameters, regardless of
      whether the labels are naturally occurring or random. The boundary between where a model has too
242
      few parameters to fit its data and where it has extra degrees of freedom is clear for linear regression.
243
      Naturally occurring labels present a more complicated scenario; for instance, if the data’s labels are a
244   linear function of the inputs, the model can fit infinitely many samples. In Figure 2, assigning random
245   labels instead of real ones allows us to explore an analogous notion of the boundary between over-
246   and under-parameterization, but in the context of neural networks. We see here that the networks fit
247   significantly fewer samples when assigned random labels compared to the original labels, indicating
248   that neural networks are less parameter efficient than linear models in this setting. Similar to linear
249   models, the amount of data they can fit scales linearly with parameter count.

      5.2.2        T HE E FFECT OF H IGH -D IMENSIONAL DATA
252
253   Linear models exhibit increased capacity when adding more features, primarily because their parame-
254   ter count directly scales with the feature count. However, the dynamics shift when examining CNNs.
255   In our setup, we avoid adding parameters as the data dimensionality increases by employing average
256   pooling prior to the classification head, a standard technique for CNNs. We investigated the EMC
257   using ImageNet-20MS, systematically resizing the input images to vary their spatial dimensions from
258   16 × 16 to 256 × 256.
259   In contrast to linear models, we find (Figure 17 in the Appendix) that CNNs, which do not benefit
260   from additional parameters as the input dimensionality increases, can fit more semantically labeled
261   data in lower spatial dimensions. This trend underscores a broader narrative in neural networks:
262   CNNs, despite their intricate architectures and capacity for complex pattern recognition, tend to align
263   better with data of lower intrinsic dimension. This observation resonates with the findings of Pope et
264   al. (Pope et al., 2020), who found that CNNs generally show enhanced generalization capabilities
265   with data of lower intrinsic dimensionality.
266   To further investigate the impact of input dimensions on EMC, we conducted an additional experiment
267    by scaling the CIFAR-10 and MNIST datasets to the ImageNet input size of 256×256 pixels (Figure 1
268   - left). Remarkably, these scaled datasets maintained higher EMC values compared to ImageNet itself,
269    despite having identical input dimensions. This finding suggests that intrinsic dataset complexity
       plays a significant role in influencing fitting capacity, beyond merely the size of the input.

      7     T HE E FFECT OF M ODEL A RCHITECTURE ON EMC
379
380
381   Having analyzed the influence of data on flexibility, we now focus on the impact of architecture.
382   We examine how different architectural properties—such as MLPs, CNNs, transformers, activation
383
      functions, and scaling strategies—contribute to flexibility.
384
385
      7.1   A RCHITECTURAL S TYLE AND PARAMETER E FFICIENCY
386
387
      To address the debate on the efficiency and generalization of CNNs versus Vision Transformers
388
      (ViTs) (d’Ascoli et al., 2021; Patro & Agneeswaran, 2023; Maurício et al., 2023; Goldblum et al.,
389   2023), we evaluated three architectures: MLPs, CNNs, and ViTs.
390
391   Our findings (Figure 4b) reveal that CNNs, characterized by hard-coded inductive biases like locality
392
      and translation equivariance, consistently outperform ViTs and MLPs in EMC (see Figure 18 in the
      Appendix for detailed scaling laws). This superiority holds across all model sizes on semantically
393
      labeled data. As analyzed in the previous section, this trend could be misconstrued as a result of
394
      better generalization over ViTs and MLPs.
395
396   To test, we examine the networks’ flexibility on randomized data (Figure 18 in the Appendix). CNNs,
397   which rely on spatial structure, fit fewer samples when spatial structure is destroyed via permutation.
398
      MLPs, lacking this preference, show unchanged flexibility. Replacing inputs with Gaussian noise
      increases both architectures’ capacity, possibly because high-dimensional noisy data is easier to
399
      separate. Notably, CNNs fit far more samples with semantic labels than with random inputs, while
400
      MLPs show the opposite trend, underscoring CNNs’ superior generalization in image classification.
401
402   Despite random data affecting architectures differently, the hierarchy of parameter efficiency remains
403   the same. This consistency suggests that CNNs’ superior parameter efficiency is inherent to their
404
      architectural design, not merely a result of better generalization. This aligns with approximation
      theory, which posits CNNs as more parameter-efficient than MLPs (Bao et al., 2014).
405
406
407
      7.2   S TRATEGIES FOR S CALING N ETWORK S IZE
408
409
      The debate on how to best scale width and depth in neural networks is ongoing. We now focus
410   on how different scaling strategies affect a network’s ability to fit data (Figure 4a and Figure 5 in
411   the Appendix). For ResNets, we consider increasing width (number of filters), increasing depth, or
412   scaling both using EfficientNet (Tan & Le, 2019) and ResNet-RS (Bello et al., 2021) scaling laws.
413   EEfficientNet balances the scaling of depth, width, and resolution; ResNet-RS adapts the scaling
414   based on model size, training time, and dataset size. For ViTs, we use the SViT (Zhai et al., 2022),
415   SoViT (Alabdulmohsin et al., 2023), and also scale depth and width separately.
416   Our analysis shows that these scaling laws, although not originally aimed at optimizing capacity,
417   perform well in improving EMC. Consistent with theoretical analyses (Eldan & Shamir, 2016), we
418   find that scaling depth is more parameter-efficient than scaling width. These findings hold even on
419   randomly labeled data, suggesting the results are not due to generalization effects.
420
421
422   7.3   ACTIVATION FUNCTIONS
423
424   Nonlinear activation functions are essential for neural network capacity; without them, networks
425   reduce to linear models. We investigate how different activation functions affect EMC, contrasting
426   nonlinear activations with linear models.
427   Our findings (Figure 16 in the Appendix) indicate that ReLU activations significantly enhance
428   capacity. Originally introduced to mitigate vanishing and exploding gradients, ReLU also improves
429   the network’s ability to fit data, likely by enhancing generalization. In contrast, tanh activation,
430   although nonlinear, does not yield similar capacity improvements, even though we can still find
431   minima with it. This suggests that ReLU’s contribution to increased capacity is not solely due to
      easier optimization.

452   8              T HE ROLE OF O PTIMIZATION IN F ITTING DATA
453
454
      Optimization techniques and regularization strategies are crucial in neural network training, influenc-
455   ing convergence rates and the nature of solutions obtained. We explore how different optimization
456   algorithms and regularization methods affect a network’s capacity to fit data, as measured by EMC.
457
458
459   8.1             C OMPARING O PTIMIZERS
460
461   We evaluated various optimizers, including SGD, full-batch GD, Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2015),
462   AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2018), and Shampoo (Gupta et al., 2018), to assess their impact.
463
      While previous work suggests that SGD has a strong flatness-seeking regularization effect leading to
464   better generalization (Geiping et al., 2021), our findings reveal a more nuanced picture. As shown in
465   Figure 3b, SGD enables models to fit more data than full-batch GD, achieving EMC levels comparable
466   to the sophisticated Shampoo optimizer. This suggests that the stochasticity inherent in SGD helps
467   the model discover minima with a higher capacity to fit data.
468
      However, with randomly labeled data, the higher EMC of SGD and Shampoo diminished, indicating
469
      that their enhanced capacity is connected to their superior generalization on the original tasks.
470
471
472   8.2             E FFECT OF R EGULARIZATION T ECHNIQUES
473
474   Classical machine learning often employs regularizers designed to reduce model capacity and prevent
475   overfitting. For example, ridge regression applies a penalty on the parameter norm to improve the
476   performance of overparameterized linear models (Hoerl & Kennard, 1970). We investigated whether
477   these regularizers impact a model’s capacity to fit data.
478
      We evaluated the EMC of a CNN trained on ImageNet-20MS using different regularization methods,
479
      including Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM) (Foret et al., 2020), weight decay, and label
480   smoothing (Müller et al., 2019) (Figure 15 in the Appendix). We found that weight decay and label
481   smoothing reduced the EMC, indicating a decreased capacity to fit data. In contrast, SAM improved
482   generalization without diminishing EMC, even on randomly labeled data. This suggests that SAM
483   facilitates the discovery of minima that generalize better without sacrificing the ability to fit large
484   amounts of data. Notably, label smoothing modifies the loss function itself, potentially hindering
485   the model from finding minima of the original objective, while SAM preserves the original loss
      landscape.

      8.3   SGD S OLUTIONS ’ F LATNESS AND EMC
487
488   We further investigated the relationship between the flatness of solutions found by SGD and their
489   EMC. Our findings, illustrated in Figure 21 in the Appendix, show that SGD consistently finds flatter
490   solutions with higher EMC than full-batch GD. This indicates that achieving a higher capacity to
491   fit data does not come at the expense of flatness. Our results challenge the conventional belief that
492   flatter minima are associated with lower capacity, suggesting that flatness and high EMC can coexist.

      9     R EPARAMETERIZATION FOR I NCREASED PARAMETER E FFICIENCY
495
496
497
      We observed that neural networks often require more parameters than the number of samples
      they can fit, indicating inefficiency in parameter utilization. To address this, we explored two
498
      reparameterization strategies aimed at increasing parameter efficiency.
499
500   First, we employed subspace training (Lotfi et al., 2022), projecting the high-dimensional parameter
501   vector of a CNN onto a randomly chosen lower-dimensional subspace and training within this reduced
502   space. This effectively reduces the number of trainable parameters while preserving capacity.
503   Second, we conducted a quantization experiment, training CNN using 8-bit precision instead of the
504   standard 32-bit precision. To maintain the same total number of bits for parameter specification,
505   we increased the number of parameters by a factor of four, resulting in a model with 4 × n 8-bit
506   parameters, equivalent in size (in bits) to a model with n 32-bit parameters.
507   Our empirical results (Figure 19 in the Appendix), demonstrate the effectiveness of these reparam-
508   eterization strategies. Subspace training significantly increased parameter efficiency, enabling the
509   network to fit more samples relative to the number of parameters for both semantic and random
510   labels. Similarly, the quantized model maintained comparable flexibility despite reduced precision.
511   Specifically, the 8-bit quantized model could fit approximately a quarter of the number of randomly
512   labeled samples as it has parameters, matching the parameter efficiency of the 32-bit model on a
513   per-bit basis. These findings indicate that careful consideration of parameter representation and
514   training space can lead to more efficient neural networks without sacrificing flexibility.
515
516   10     D ISCUSSION
517
518
      Our study reveals that parameter counting alone is insufficient for understanding a neural network’s
519
      capacity to fit data or for defining the boundary between underparameterization and overparameteriza-
520   tion. Instead, EMC is influenced by multiple factors, including architecture, optimization algorithms,
521   regularization techniques, and the nature of the data.
522
523
      These findings prompt a re-examination of conventional wisdom in neural network training. We
      observed that components such as ReLU activation functions contribute to increased capacity beyond
524
      mitigating vanishing gradients. Furthermore, stochastic optimization methods such as SGD were
525
      found to locate minima that enable the model to fit more training samples, challenging the view of
526   stochasticity solely as a source of implicit regularization.
527
528   Our results also indicate that standard neural network architectures may be inefficient in parameter
529
      utilization. By employing alternative parameterizations like subspace training and quantization,
      we enhanced parameter efficiency without sacrificing flexibility. This underscores the potential for
530
      developing more efficient neural network designs that make better use of available parameters.

       C    E MPIRICAL M ODEL C OMPLEXITY
1087
1088
       To compute the Empirical Model Complexity (EMC), we adopt an iterative approach for each network
1089
       size. Initially, we start with a small number of samples and train the model. Post-training, we verify
1090
       if the model has perfectly fit all the samples by achieving 100% training accuracy. If this criterion is
1091   met, we re-initialize the model with a random initialization and train it again on a larger number of
1092   samples, randomly drawn from the full dataset. This process is iteratively performed, increasing the
1093   number of samples in each iteration, until the model fails to perfectly fit all the training samples. The
1094   largest sample size where the model achieves a perfect fit is taken as the Empirical Model Complexity
1095   for that particular network size. It is important to note that data is sampled independently on each
1096   iteration.
1097   While it is possible to artificially prevent models from fitting their training set by under-training, thus
1098   confounding any study of capacity to fit data, we ensure that all training runs reach a minimum of the
1099   loss function by imposing three conditions:
1100
       First, the norm of the gradients across all samples must fall below a pre-defined threshold. We
1101
       observed that there is a high variance in the norms of the gradients between different networks;
1102
       therefore, we set this threshold manually after checking the norms for each network type when
1103   training with a small number of samples, where it’s clear that the networks fit perfectly and converge
1104   to a minimum.
1105
1106
       Second, the training loss should stabilize. To ensure this, we stipulate that the average loss should not
       decrease for 10 consecutive epochs.
1107
1108   Third, we check for the absence of negative eigenvalues in the loss Hessian to confirm that the model
1109   has indeed reached a minimum rather than a saddle point. To do this, we calculate the eigenvalues
1110   using the PyHessian Python package (Yao et al., 2020) and validate that after training converges, there
1111   are no eigenvalues smaller than −1e − 2. This threshold was chosen after examining the eigenvalue
1112
       distributions of different networks that fit perfectly.

       F    L IMITATIONS
1140
       Our study has several limitations that should be considered when interpreting the results.
1141
1142   First, while we conducted experiments on multiple datasets, including CIFAR-10, MNIST, and
1143   ImageNet-20MS, these datasets primarily cover image classification tasks. We did not extensively
1144   test datasets from other domains such as text, audio, or time-series data. This limited scope may
1145   affect the generalizability of our findings, particularly regarding the correlation between the EMC
1146
       gap (the difference in EMC when fitting correctly labeled data versus randomly labeled data) and
       generalization performance. The relationship we observed might not hold for datasets with different
1147
       characteristics, such as varying sizes, complexities, noise levels, or in different modalities.
1148
1149   Second, our experiments are constrained by available computational resources. We utilized NVIDIA
1150   Tesla V100 GPUs with 32 GB memory, and the total compute time was approximately 3,000 GPU
1151   hours. These limitations restricted the scale and number of experiments we could perform, potentially
1152
       affecting the robustness of our conclusions. For instance, we could not extensively explore a wide
       range of hyperparameter settings, larger models, or more diverse architectures due to computational
1153
       constraints.
1154
1155   Third, our analysis primarily focuses on certain types of neural network architectures—specifically,
1156   Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLPs), and Vision Transformers
1157   (ViTs). While these are common and widely used architectures, we did not explore others such
1158
       as recurrent neural networks, graph neural networks, or specialized domain-specific models. The
       impact of different training procedures, regularization techniques, and hyperparameter choices on the
1159
       EMC might vary with other architectures, and the correlation between EMC and generalization could
1160
       exhibit different patterns.
1161
1162   Additionally, we decided to test a wide range of factors affecting neural network flexibility but
1163   explored only a limited number of settings for each factor, rather than delving deeply into any
1164
       single factor. This breadth-over-depth approach might have missed deeper insights that a more
       focused study could reveal. For example, we did not extensively investigate how varying levels of
1165
       regularization, data augmentation techniques, or optimizer hyperparameters might affect the EMC
1166
       and its relationship with generalization.
1167
1168   Furthermore, our method of measuring EMC, while rigorous, relies on specific criteria for determining
1169   when a model has perfectly fit its training data. These criteria include achieving near-perfect training
1170
       accuracy (e.g., 99
1171   Finally, we acknowledge that we did not extensively investigate the potential failure modes of EMC
1172   as a generalization predictor. There may be conditions under which the correlation between the EMC
1173   gap and generalization performance breaks down, such as in the presence of extreme regularization,
1174   highly noisy or corrupted data, or with non-standard training procedures. Understanding these
1175   limitations is crucial for clarifying the applicability of EMC in different contexts.
1176   Despite these limitations, we believe our study provides valuable insights into the factors influencing
1177   neural network flexibility and highlights areas for further research. Future work could address
1178   these limitations by exploring a broader range of datasets from diverse domains, experimenting
1179   with additional architectures and training settings, and investigating the conditions under which
1180   the EMC-generalization correlation may not hold. Such efforts would enhance the understanding
1181   of EMC’s applicability and contribute to the development of more generalizable and robust neural
1182
       network models.
