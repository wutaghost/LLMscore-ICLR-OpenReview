011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            As speech generation technology continues to evolve, the risk of misuse through
014            deepfake audio has become a pressing concern, which underscores the critical
015            need for robust detection methods. However, many existing speech deepfake
016
               datasets fall short in terms of size, diversity, and linguistic coverage, limiting the
               ability of models to generalize effectively to unseen deepfakes. To address these
017
               limitations, we present SpeechFake, a large-scale dataset specifically designed
018
               for speech deepfake detection. With over 3 million deepfakes totaling more than
019            3,000 hours of audio, SpeechFake was generated using 40 different speech gener-
020            ation tools, including cutting-edge techniques, and spans 46 languages. This paper
021            provides a detailed overview of the dataset’s composition and statistics, emphasiz-
022            ing its scale and diversity. Additionally, we establish baseline results for Speech-
023            Fake and explore how factors such as generation methods, language diversity, and
024            speaker variation influence detection performance. We believe SpeechFake will
025            be a valuable resource for advancing speech deepfake detection research, offering
026            opportunities to explore new detection strategies and improve model robustness
027
               across diverse and evolving generation techniques. The dataset will be publicly
               available soon.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      In recent years, speech generation technology has rapidly advanced, with models in text-to-speech
      (TTS) and voice conversion (VC) systems producing highly natural and high-quality voices (Tan
034
      et al., 2021; Triantafyllopoulos et al., 2023; Ju et al., 2024). These systems are increasingly used in
035
      virtual assistants, content creation, and language learning, making speech synthesis more accessible
036   and widely adopted. However, as the realism of synthetic voices improves, so does the risk of
037   misuse, especially through speech deepfakes, where synthetic voices are used to impersonate real
038   individuals. Such deepfakes have been employed in fraud (Stupp, 2019), identity theft (Korshunov
039   & Marcel, 2018), and misinformation (Chesney & Citron, 2019), highlighting the significant harm
040   they can cause. Therefore, the growing quality and availability of speech generation systems make
041   the need for robust detection methods more urgent than ever.
042   A key challenge in developing effective deepfake detection methods is the issue of generalization.
043   Detection models often suffer from substantial performance degradation when confronted with un-
044   seen deepfakes (Yamagishi et al., 2021; Müller et al., 2022), which underscores the importance of
045   creating comprehensive datasets to support the development of robust detection systems. However,
046   current datasets for this task come with several limitations. Many publicly available datasets are
047   relatively small, and the generation techniques they include are often outdated or limited, making
048   it challenging for models to detect more advanced deepfake technologies. Moreover, most datasets
049   primarily focus on English or Chinese, offering limited representation of other languages. This lack
050
      of linguistic diversity makes it difficult for models to generalize effectively to deepfakes in languages
      beyond English and Chinese.
051
052   To address these limitations, we propose SpeechFake, a large-scale dataset designed to significantly
053   improve both the scale and diversity of data available for speech deepfake detection. The dataset
      contains over 3 million speech deepfakes, amounting to more than 3,000 hours. These deepfakes

054
      are generated using 30 publicly available speech generation tools and 10 commercial APIs, incor-
055   porating cutting-edge techniques capable of producing highly realistic synthetic speech. To support
056   multilingual detection and balance language distribution, SpeechFake is divided into two parts: the
057   Bilingual Dataset (BD), focused on English and Chinese, and the Multilingual Dataset (MD), which
058   spans 46 languages, broadening research opportunities in multilingual environments. Furthermore,
059   unlike most existing datasets that offer only binary labels (real/fake), SpeechFake provides rich
060   metadata, including generation methods, speaker identity, language, and text transcriptions, which
061   facilitates deeper research into the factors that influence deepfake detection and enables other poten-
062   tial use cases.
063   In addition, we conduct a comprehensive set of experiments to establish a baseline for SpeechFake
064   and examine key factors influencing deepfake detection performance. First, we evaluate overall
065   performance across multiple datasets to assess model generalization to both seen and unseen data
066   (Section 4.2). Next, we analyze cross-generator performance to examine how different speech gen-
067   eration methods affect detection accuracy (Section 4.3). We also investigate cross-lingual perfor-
068   mance, exploring how models trained on specific languages perform when exposed to deepfakes in
069
      other languages (Section 4.4). Finally, we assess cross-speaker performance to determine the impact
      of speaker variability on detection robustness (Section 4.5). These experiments establish a strong
070
      baseline for SpeechFake and provide valuable insights into the strengths and weaknesses of current
071
      detection systems, highlighting areas for future improvement.
072
073   We summarize our contributions as follows:
074
075   • We introduce SpeechFake, a large-scale and diverse dataset for speech deepfake detection that
076     encompasses a wide range of speech generation methods, integrates cutting-edge techniques, and
077     supports multiple languages.
078   • We conduct extensive experiments, establishing a baseline for the dataset and analyzing the im-
079     pact of factors such as generation methods, language diversity, and speaker variation on detection
080     performance.
081
      • SpeechFake provides a valuable resource for developing robust deepfake detection models,
082
        demonstrating superior performance on existing datasets. It also supports future research in im-
083     proving model generalization and advancing detection strategies for emerging techniques.

      Speech Generation Speech generation, or speech synthesis, can be broadly divided into two
111
      main tasks: Text-to-Speech (TTS) and Voice Conversion (VC). TTS systems synthesize human-like,
112   natural-sounding voices from written text. Neural network-based TTS has evolved from CNN/RNN-
113   based models (Oord, 2016; Wang et al., 2017; Shen et al., 2018) to Transformer-based architec-
114   tures (Li et al., 2019; Ren et al., 2021a), progressing from autoregressive generative models to more
115   advanced frameworks like VAE, GAN, flow, and diffusion models (Prenger et al., 2019; Kong et al.,
116   2020; Kim et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022). Additionally, TTS has transitioned from using cascaded
117   acoustic models and vocoders (Oord, 2016; Shen et al., 2018; Kong et al., 2020) to fully end-to-
118   end architectures (Ren et al., 2021a; Kim et al., 2021). With the success of large language models
119   (LLMs), recent TTS systems also incorporate LLMs for text-to-token generation (Betker, 2023; Du
120
      et al., 2024b; Guo et al., 2024).
121   While traditional TTS systems were limited to generating speech in a specific voice, newer ap-
122   proaches have enabled multi-speaker generation by incorporating speaker embeddings (Kim et al.,
123   2021; Betker, 2023). Additionally, research has advanced to few-shot and zero-shot TTS, often re-
124   ferred to as voice clone, which allows the generation of speech from text using a voice not seen
125   during training, based on just a small sample of the target voice (Arik et al., 2018; Casanova et al.,
126
      2022; Wang et al., 2023; Qin et al., 2023).
127   In contrast, voice conversion modifies an existing speech sample to match a target speaker’s voice
128   while preserving the original content. Unlike TTS, which generates speech from text, VC al-
129   ters speaker characteristics in audio. Both, however, often share similar neural architectures, like
130   sequence-to-sequence models with attention. VC has evolved from traditional parallel data and sta-
131   tistical methods (Godoy et al., 2011) to more flexible non-parallel approaches, improving conversion
132
      quality and adaptability (Kaneko & Kameoka, 2018; Li et al., 2021).
133   Meanwhile, neural vocoders play a critical role in many speech generation systems, directly gener-
134   ating speech from acoustic features like mel-spectrograms (Kong et al., 2020; gil Lee et al., 2023).
135   Recent research shows that vocoded speech plays a significant role in detecting deepfakes (Frank &
136   Schönherr, 2021; Wang & Yamagishi, 2023; 2024). Therefore, we also include vocoded speech as a
137   part of our dataset.
138   As shown in Figure 1, we classify speech generation methods into three categories based on the
139   input modality at the inference stage: TTS, VC (Voice Clone or Voice Conversion), and NV (Neural
140   Vocoder). In this classification, TTS refers to systems that generate speech from text (with optional
141   speaker IDs to specify voices). VC focuses on generating speech with a desired speaker identity,
142   whether the content comes from text or another speech sample. Finally, NV generates speech from
143   acoustic features without altering the original speaker’s identity.

200   3     DATASET C OLLECTION AND S TATISTICS
201
202   3.1   DATA C OLLECTION
203
204
      The data collection consists of two parts: real speech, sourced from existing datasets, and fake
      speech, generated using open-source speech generation methods or commercial APIs. Since most
205
      speech generation methods primarily support English or Chinese, we split our dataset into two parts
206
      to balance the samples for each language: the Bilingual Dataset, which includes English and Chi-
207   nese, and the Multilingual Dataset, which covers data from 46 languages. The basic composition of
208   our dataset is illustrated in Figure 2.
209
210   Bilingual Dataset (BD) The bilingual dataset include English and Chinese speech data. The real
211   data is sampled from four datasets, LibriTTS (Zen et al., 2019) and VCTK (Veaux et al., 2013)
212   for English, AISHELL1 (Bu et al., 2017) and AISHELL3 (Shi et al., 2020) for Chinese. The fake
213   speech data is generated using 30 open-source speech generation methods and 10 commercial APIs,
214   as illustrated in Figure 2 and detailed in Table 7 in the Appendix. The open-source models span
215   a variety of architectures, including GAN-based models (Kumar et al., 2019; Kong et al., 2020),
      Diffusion models (Liu et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2022b), Sequence-to-Sequence models (Oord,


                                                        4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      2016; Ren et al., 2021a), and Flow or VAE models (Prenger et al., 2019; Kim et al., 2021). Besides,
217   we include a collection of latest speech generation methods (highlighted in blue in Figure 2), all of
218   which were released in the past year and represent the cutting-edge in speech synthesis technology.
219
220   Multilingual Dataset (MD) The Multilingual Dataset (MD) spans 46 languages in total, including
221   9 primary languages: English (en), Chinese (zh), Spanish (es), French (fr), Hindi (hi), Japanese
222   (ja), Korean (ko), Persian (fa), and Italian (it), along with 37 additional languages. To create a
223   multilingual setting, we sample real speech data from the CommonVoice dataset (Ardila et al., 2019).
224   The fake data is generated using 6 multilingual speech generation tools, as shown in Figure 2, with
225   EdgeTTS1 supporting the widest range of languages, while the other methods cover a subset of
226   them.
227   Before the generation process, we prepare the corresponding text or audio input required by each
228   generator. This input is sampled from the real dataset we collected, including text transcriptions for
229   TTS systems and audio samples for VC and NV systems. The input data is preprocessed as follows:
230
231   • Text Preprocessing: Text inputs for TTS systems are cleaned by removing special characters,
232     punctuation, and extra spaces. For each language, we ensure that the text maintains an appropriate
233     word or character count (e.g., 5–30 words for English) and is selected to cover a diverse range
234     of phonemes comprehensively. The text is then tokenized and formatted according to the specific
235
        requirements of each TTS model, with adjustments for sentence length or phonetic transcription
        where needed.
236
237   • Audio Preprocessing: Audio samples for VC and NV systems are resampled to match the gener-
238
        ator’s required sampling rate and converted to the appropriate formats, such as mel-spectrograms
        for neural vocoders or raw waveforms for voice conversion models. Silence at the beginning and
239
        end of audio clips is trimmed to avoid introducing artifacts during generation.
240
241
      During the generation process,
242
243   • For TTS systems: The prepared text is used to generate speech for each method. If the method
244     supports multiple voices, the text is evenly split among the available voices. An exception is
245     TTS Tortoise, for which additional data is generated to support the cross-speaker experiment.
246   • For VC systems: Reference voices are sampled from the real datasets, while the content comes
247     from the selected text or the corresponding speech recordings. The text is generally split equally
248     among the reference voices. For methods supporting style transfer (e.g., CosyVoice, OpenVoice),
249     we include additional data to reflect the transformed styles.
250   • For NV systems: The generated speech is based on the original input audio selected from the real
251     datasets.
252
253   Once the speech is generated, it undergoes the following post-processing steps:
254
255
      • Quality Filtering: We apply voice activity detection (VAD) to filter out speech segments with
        less than 0.5 seconds of active speech. Additionally, generated speech with noticeable distortions,
256
        excessive noise, or unnatural artifacts is discarded.
257
258
      • Format Standardization: The remaining audio clips are standardized to a 16kHz sampling rate,
        converted to mono, and saved in WAV format to ensure consistency across all samples in the
259
        dataset.

347
      a frontend feature extractor with AASIST serving as the backend classifier. The training details for
348   each model are provided in Table 9 in the Appendix. For evaluation, we use the Equal Error Rate

      5   L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE WORK
487
488
      While SpeechFake provides a large and diverse dataset for speech deepfake detection, several limi-
489
      tations persist. Although the dataset includes 40 different speech generation tools, it does not cover
490   all current or emerging techniques, as the field of speech generation evolves rapidly. Moreover, the
491   variety of generation methods in the multilingual dataset is limited due to the scarcity of multilingual
492   speech generation systems. Additionally, while SpeechFake offers a broad range of speaker voices
493   and audio samples, the diversity in speaker identities and recording environments may still fall short
494   of fully capturing real-world scenarios. For future work, we hope to potentially expand the dataset
495   with more generation methods as they emerge, further enhancing its relevance and applicability for
496   deepfake detection research.
497
498   6   C ONCLUSION
499
500
      In conclusion, SpeechFake addresses critical gaps in existing datasets for speech deepfake detec-
501   tion by providing a large-scale, diverse collection of over 3 million deepfakes generated using 40
502   different speech generation tools, spanning 46 languages. Through extensive experiments, we es-
503   tablished baseline results and explored key factors such as generation methods, language diversity,
504   and speaker variation, which significantly impact detection performance. Our findings emphasize
505   the challenges of generalizing across unseen deepfakes while demonstrating the potential of Speech-
506   Fake to drive future advancements in model robustness and generalization. We believe SpeechFake
507   will be a valuable resource for the development of more sophisticated detection systems, helping to
508   mitigate the risks associated with the growing threat of deepfake misuse.

818   • Deepfake Detection with Privacy Preservation: SpeechFake can support research into detecting
819     deepfakes while maintaining privacy protections for both speaker identities and sensitive con-
820     tent. Techniques such as federated learning, differential privacy, or privacy-preserving machine
821     learning can be explored to allow models to detect deepfakes without exposing or compromising
822     personal data. This opens up possibilities for safer deployment of detection models in real-world
823
        applications where privacy is a concern.
824   • Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR): The synthetic speech in SpeechFake provides a valuable
825
        opportunity to assess how ASR systems handle artificially generated audio. Researchers can ex-
        plore whether incorporating deepfake speech can enhance ASR performance by training models
826
        to be more robust against synthetic variations, thereby improving accuracy and adaptability in
827
        handling diverse, challenging speech inputs.
828
      • Security and Adversarial Audio Attacks: Synthetic speech from SpeechFake can be utilized
829
        in adversarial attacks aimed at ASR systems or voice-activated applications. The dataset offers a
830     resource for developing and testing countermeasures against adversarial audio manipulations, en-
831     abling researchers to fortify voice-driven systems against security threats, ensuring their reliability
832     and resilience in real-world scenarios.
