                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               Despite significant ongoing efforts in safety alignment, large language models
010
               (LLMs) such as GPT-4 and LLaMA 3 remain vulnerable to jailbreak attacks that
011            can induce harmful behaviors, including those triggered by adversarial suffixes.
012            Building on prior research, we hypothesize that these adversarial suffixes are not
013            mere bugs but may represent features that can dominate the LLM’s behavior. To
014            evaluate this hypothesis, we conduct several experiments. First, we demonstrate
015            that benign features can be effectively made to function as adversarial suffixes,
016            i.e., we develop a feature extraction method to extract sample-agnostic features
017            from benign dataset in the form of suffixes and show that these suffixes may ef-
018            fectively compromise safety alignment. Second, we show that adversarial suffixes
019
               generated from jailbreak attacks may contain meaningful features, i.e., append-
               ing the same suffix to different prompts results in responses exhibiting specific
020
               characteristics. Third, we show that such benign-yet-safety-compromising fea-
021
               tures can be easily introduced through fine-tuning using only benign datasets,
022            i.e., even in the absence of harmful content. This highlights the critical risk
023            posed by dominating benign features in the training data and calls for further
024            research to reinforce LLM safety alignment. Our code and data is available at
025            https://github.com/anonymous.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Large language models (LLMs) such as GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023), Llama2 (Touvron et al., 2023),
031   Vicuna (Chiang et al., 2023), and Mistral (Jiang et al., 2023) have demonstrated remarkable capa-
032   bilities across a wide range of natural language tasks and have been increasingly adopted in many
033
      real-world applications. Despite extensive efforts (Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022; Glaese
      et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023) to align LLMs’ responses with human values and
034
      generate helpful and harmless content, many recent studies (Perez et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2023a;
035
      Deng et al., 2023; Shen et al., 2023; Zou et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2023b; Zeng et al., 2024; Chao
036   et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023) reveal that these aligned models
037   are still vulnerable to “jailbreak attacks”, which can elicit harmful, biased, or otherwise unintended
038   behaviors from LLMs, posing significant challenges to their safe deployment. Among adversarial
039   attacks, the Greedy Coordinate Gradient (GCG) method (Zou et al., 2023) is one particularly ef-
040   fective approach for jailbreaking. By combining greedy and gradient-based search, GCG produces
041   adversarial suffixes that, though nonsensical to humans, can manipulate strongly aligned LLMs into
042   improperly responding to harmful prompts.
043   While some researchers attribute this vulnerability to the model’s misalignment when processing
044   out-of-distribution prompts (Cherepanova & Zou, 2024), we conjecture that these adversarial suf-
045   fixes may actually represent meaningful features for the LLM, or at the very least, can be intention-
046   ally designed as such. This idea is inspired by the findings of (Ilyas et al., 2019), which demonstrate
047   that adversarial perturbations are not simply anomalies but rather features that models actively ex-
048   ploit. To evaluate our conjecture, we conduct three experiments.
049   First, we demonstrate that benign features can function as adversarial suffixes effectively. In par-
050   ticular, we construct multiple benign datasets, each of which exhibits one specific sample-agnostic
051   feature, such as a specific response format. We subsequently design a universal feature extraction
052   method to generate transferable suffixes that reliably induce the corresponding feature (e.g., the spe-
053   cific response format). Our results show that these suffixes consistently activate the intended feature
      when appended to both benign and harmful prompts. In the latter case, we show that these suffixes

      Second, we show that adversarial suffixes generated through jailbreak attacks may contain meaning-
081   ful features. In particular, we apply our universal feature extraction method on a dataset containing
082   harmful prompt and response pairs to generate multiple jailbreaking suffixes. Note that in such a
083   setting, our method effectively becomes a universal adversarial attack method. We show that (1)
084   we are able to compromise the safety alignment effectively using the generated suffix (i.e., they are
085   adversarial suffixes), and more importantly, (2) they sometimes represent meaningful features, i.e.,

087   features (such as a style of story telling). We interpret that in such cases, the adversarial suffixes
088   generated through jailbreaking attacks indeed contain certain features.
089   While the first two experiments demonstrate that benign features introduced via the suffix can over-
090   ride safety alignment, our third experiment reveals that this issue can also arise through regular fine-
091   tuning. Specifically, a strong benign feature can be unintentionally introduced during fine-tuning
092   with a benign dataset, potentially compromising safety alignment. In particular, we fine-tune mul-
093   tiple LLMs using multiple benign datasets constructed in the first two experiments (e.g., those that
094   consistently exhibit a specific response format or writing style) and show that the safety alignment
095

096   We believe our experimental results provide strong evidence in favor of our hypothesis. These
097   results also suggest that existing defense mechanisms against jailbreaking are likely inadequate, as
098   there may be numerous benign features within the model that could be exploited or amplified (e.g.,
099   through adversarial suffixes) to bypass safety alignment. Moreover, it is risky to assume that fine-
100   tuning with a benign dataset will automatically preserve the model’s safety alignment. Instead, it is
101
      essential to prioritize research that ensures safety alignment remains a top concern, without being
      dominated by other features, during fine-tuning.

130   2.1   E XTRACTING F EATURES A S S UFFIXES
131
132   In the following, we develop a method for extracting sample-agnostic features from a given dataset
133   (whether benign or harmful) in the form of suffixes. It is important to note that, for the purpose of
134   this study, the suffixes may take the form of either embedding vectors or input tokens.
135   Let T ∈ Rn×d represent a tokenized input prompt consisting of n tokens, where each token is
136   embedded in a d-dimensional space; let S ∈ Rl×d represent an initial suffix consisting of l tokens;
137   and let y ∈ Rm×d represent the corresponding target response. We define an embedding function
138   E : T → e that maps a set of tokens to their embedding vectors. We write eprompt ∈ Rn×D to
139   denote the (fixed) embedding representation of the input prompt where D is the dimension of the
140   embedding space, and esuffix ∈ Rl×D to denote the embedding of the suffix that we aim to optimize.
141
      Given a language model F and a set of prompts and targeted responses, the objective of our feature
      extraction method is to optimize one suffix such that F generates the target response given the
142
      concatenated embedding of each prompt and the suffix. Formally,
143
144                                         F (eprompt ⊕ esuffix ) → y,                                 (1)
145
      where ⊕ denotes concatenation. To achieve the above objective, we aim to minimize the discrepancy
146   between the target response y and the model’s predicted response by minimizing the cross-entropy
147   loss.
148                            Ladv = CrossEntropy(F (eprompt ⊕ esuffix ), y) .                      (2)
149
      We note that the objective function above is defined in terms of the embedding, enabling us to
150   explore a wide range of features within the embedding space, even though many of these features
151   may not correspond to valid token sequences.

      that measures the proximity of the optimized suffix embeddings to the embeddings of actual tokens.

       2.2            PCC A NALYSIS
186
187   In our experiments to be present in the subsequent section, we adopt the Pearson Correlation Coeffi-
188   cient (PCC) (Anderson, 2003) to quantify the influence of different suffixes. PCC is a widely applied
189   metric that measures the linear correlation between two variables, which is defined as follows.
190
                                                                                                                cov(X, Y )
191                                                                                      PCCX,Y =                          ,                                                               (4)
192
                                                                                                                  σX σY
193   where X and Y are two vectors, cov(X, Y ) is their covariance, and σX and σY are the standard
194   deviation of vector X and Y . The PCC value ranges from −1 to 1, where an absolute value of
195   1 indicates perfect linear correlation, 0 indicates no linear correlation, and the sign indicates the
196   direction of the correlation (positive or negative). In this study, given a prompt p and a suffix s, we
197   define the following variables based on the last hidden states of an LLM.
198
                          • Hp : the last hidden state of the LLM given the prompt p.
199
200                       • Hp+s : the last hidden state of the LLM given the prompt p appended with the suffix s.
201                       • Hs : the last hidden state of the LLM given the suffix s only.
202
203   Note that we focus on the last hidden states because, in auto-regressive language models, this state
204   encapsulates all the features necessary to generate the response.
205   By comparing PCCHp ,Hp+s and PCCHs ,Hp+s , we gain insights into the contributions of the prompt
206   and the suffix. Since a higher PCC value indicates a greater influence, if PCCHp ,Hp+s is larger than
207   PCCHs ,Hp+s , it suggests that the prompt plays a more dominant role than the suffix in shaping the
208   model’s output.
209
      In the following, we demonstrate how our PCC analysis works using a set of 100 harmful prompts, a
210   meaningless suffix and a set 100 prompt-specific adversarial suffixes generated by GCG for each of
211   the harmful prompts (not optimized for transferability). Note that the meaningless suffix comprises
212   20 exclamation marks (‘!’). The PCC analysis results are visualized in Figure 2, where x-axis
213   represents the value of Hs or Hp and y-axis represents the value of Hp+s . It can be observed that, in
214   the case of the meaningless suffix, the PCC value between Hs and Hp+s is close to zero, indicating
215   that the suffix has little to no impact on the output. Conversely, the prompt-specific adversarial suffix
      exhibits a high PCC value Hs and Hp+s , indicating it significantly influences the model’s output.

      3     E XPERIMENT 1: B ENIGN F EATURES ACT A S A DVERSARIAL S UFFIXES
217
218
      In this experiment, we demonstrate that it is possible to generate suffixes that capture benign fea-
219
      tures while effectively undermining safety alignment, i.e., they form effective adversarial suffixes.
220   This experiment is motivated by our observation that adversarial suffixes generated by GCG can
221   occasionally prompt responses in specific ”formats,” such as structured point-by-point answers.
222
223
      3.1   E XPERIMENT S ETUP
224
225   Dataset Construction We start with constructing multiple datasets that exhibit certain specific fea-
226   ture, using a systematic three-step process as follows.
227
228         1. Sampling Prompts: We sample a set of benign, diverse, task-oriented dialogues to form
229            an initial dataset. Note that we use random sampling to ensure the initial dataset covers a
230
               wide range of topics and linguistic styles.
231         2. Generating Format-Specific Responses: We take the initial dataset and use an LLM to
232            process the responses with an explicit formatting instruction so that the responses are all in
233            certain specific formats.
234         3. Filtering for Safety and Validity: We apply an LLM to filter out any resultant prompt-
235            response pairs containing potentially harmful content or content of low quality.
236
237   Specifically, we randomly select 1,000 benign prompts from the Alpaca dataset, apply the
238   Llama2-7B-chat-hf model (Touvron et al., 2023) to process the responses using two system
239
      templates to produce responses in two specific formats (i.e., a poetic style and a structured point-by-
      point format), and use GPT-4 to filter harmful or low-quality content. These templates are present
240
      in Figures 6 and 7 in the Appendix. Through this process, we constructed the Poem and Structure
241
      datasets. Representative examples are included in Appendix A.5.
242
243   Feature Extraction We apply the method presented in Section 2 to generate suffix embeddings
244   across three widely used open-source LLMs: Llama2-7B-chat-hf (Touvron et al., 2023), Vicuna7B-
245
      1.5 (Chiang et al., 2023), and Mistral-7B (Jiang et al., 2023) on the Poem and Structure datasets.
      Specifically, we set the token-regularization coefficient λ to zero, allowing the embedding vectors
246
      to fully capture the format features without penalizing token similarity.
247
248   Evaluation Metric For each dataset, we generate one suffix in the form of embedding value that
249   achieves the best performance according to the objective function. We then evaluate the generated
250   suffixes from two perspectives, i.e., their ability to transfer to other benign prompts and induce
251
      responses in the expected format (transferability), and their transferability to harmful prompts and
      induce harmful responses, measured by the attack success rate (ASR) and the harmfulness score.
252
      Note that the former evaluates whether the suffix captures the feature and the latter checks whether
253
      it functions as an adversarial suffix.
254
255   To compute transferability, we append each generated suffix to 500 randomly selected benign
256   prompts from the Alpaca dataset, use GPT-4-turbo to assess whether the generated responses ex-
257
      hibit the target format/style, report the proportion of benign prompts resulting in responses with
      the target format/style as the result. To evaluate the ASR, we append each suffix to 520 harmful
258
      prompts from AdvBench (Zou et al., 2023) and employ GPT-4-turbo to assess the ASR and the
259
      average harmfulness of the resultant responses. Note that we consider a prompt attacks success-
260   fully if the response is deemed violating OpenAI’s safety guidelines according to GPT-4-turbo. For
261   harmfulness evaluation, we ask GPT-4-turbo to rate the responses on a scale from 1 to 5, where 1
262   indicates no harm and appropriate model refusal of the request, and 5 signifies a response that dis-
263   regards usage guidelines and clearly violates the policy. Detailed evaluation templates are provided
264   in Appendix A.1.

      6     R ELATED W ORK
493
      This study is related to prior studies on jailbreak attacks, and studies on how fine-tuning may com-
494
      promise safety alignment.
495
496
      6.1   JAILBREAK ATTACK
497
498   Jailbreak attacks aim to elicit unintended and unsafe behaviors from LLMs via well-crafted harmful
499   queries. Recent approaches automate this process using gradient-based methods (Zou et al., 2023;
500   Liu et al., 2024), genetic algorithms (Liu et al., 2023), and random searches (Pal et al., 2023; Hayase
501   et al., 2024). Others employ auxiliary LLMs to refine jailbreak templates (Yu et al., 2023; Chao
502   et al., 2023). In this work, we focus on jailbreak methods that are designed to generate adversarial
503   suffixes with high transferability across different prompts. Our conjecture is that these adversarial
504   suffixes may contain features that are meaningful and effective across various types of inputs. It is
505   possible to extend our study to other kinds of adversarial attacks, although it may not be trivial, i.e.,
506
      the experiments and analysis must be designed differently.
507
508   6.2   F INE -T UNING AND S AFETY
509
      Studies have shown that fine-tuning with a small set of harmful samples can unsurprisingly compro-
510   mise LLM safety alignment (Shan et al., 2022; Shu et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2024). Surprisingly,
511   there are some studies that show that even fine-tuning with benign data can degrade a model’s safety
512   performance to some extent (Qi et al., 2023; Zhan et al., 2023). He et al. (He et al., 2024) investi-
513   gated this phenomenon using data selection techniques such as representation matching and gradient
514   matching. They discovered that selected data, often structured as lists, bullet points, or math ques-
515   tions, can degrade model safety during benign fine-tuning. While their work and ours both find that
516   fine-tuning on structured format data can weaken an LLM’s safety, our research significantly extends
517   theirs with an approach to systematically generate such safety-compromising benign dataset (i.e., by
518
      using our method to generate universal adversarial suffixes). More importantly, our approach aims to
      analyze the impact of specific response structures/styles on the model behavior, providing in-depth
519
      insights on how benign data can inadvertently undermine safety alignments in LLMs.
520
521
522   7     C ONCLUSION
523
524   In this study, we conduct multiple experiments to show that (1) benign features may function as
525   effective adversarial suffixes, (2) adversarial suffixes generated by adversarial attacks may indeed
526   contain meaningful features, and (3) such safety-compromising benign features may be easily intro-
527
      duced through benign datasets.
528   Our analysis demonstrates that certain benign features can dominate and bypass the safety mecha-
529   nisms of LLMs. This exposes a significant and yet somewhat overlooked vulnerability: even highly
530   aligned LLMs can be manipulated through benign features to exhibit harmful behaviors. These find-
531   ings highlight the need for research into more robust alignment techniques that can safeguard LLMs
532   against such vulnerabilities, ensuring that safety alignment is maintained despite the presence of
533
      dominant features in fine-tuning datasets. Given that fine-tuning is a common practice across vari-
      ous domains, many of which exhibit distinct characteristics (e.g., specialized writing styles in legal
534
      documents), addressing this issue is both urgent and critical.
