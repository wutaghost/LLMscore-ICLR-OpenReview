011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            The quest for Continual Learning (CL) seeks to empower neural networks with
014            the ability to learn and adapt incrementally. Central to this pursuit is addressing
015            the stability-plasticity dilemma, which involves striking a balance between two
016
               conflicting objectives: preserving previously learned knowledge and acquiring new
               knowledge. Existing studies have proposed numerous CL methods to achieve this
017
               trade-off. However, these methods often overlook the impact of basic architecture
018
               on stability and plasticity, thus the trade-off is limited to the parameter level. In this
019            paper, we delve into the conflict between stability and plasticity at the architectural
020            level. We reveal that under an equal parameter constraint, deeper networks exhibit
021            better plasticity, while wider networks are characterized by superior stability. To
022            address this architectural-level dilemma, we introduce a novel framework denoted
023            Dual-Architecture (Dual-Arch), which serves as a plug-in component for CL. This
024            framework leverages the complementary strengths of two distinct and independent
025            networks: one dedicated to plasticity and the other to stability. Each network is
026            designed with a specialized and lightweight architecture, tailored to its respective
027
               objective. Extensive experiments across datasets and CL methods demonstrate that
               Dual-Arch can enhance the performance of existing CL methods while being up to
028
               87% more compact in terms of parameters than the baselines.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Continual Learning (CL) seeks to enable neural networks to continuously acquire and update knowl-
034
      edge. The primary challenge in CL is catastrophic forgetting McCloskey & Cohen (1989); Goodfellow
035
      et al. (2013), i.e., directly updating neural networks to learn new data causes rapid forgetting of previ-
036   ously acquired knowledge. To learn continually without forgetting, a neural network must balance
037   plasticity, to learn new concepts, and stability, to retain acquired knowledge. However, emphasizing
038   stability can limit the neural network’s ability to acquire new knowledge, while excessive plasticity
039   can lead to severe forgetting, a challenge known as the stability-plasticity dilemma Grossberg (2013).
040
      To enhance CL, most of the research efforts Li & Hoiem (2017); Henning et al. (2021); Feng et al.
041
      (2022) are centered on developing novel learning methods that achieve a better trade-off between
042   stability and plasticity. These methods involve adding loss terms that prevent the model from changing,
043   replaying past data, or explicitly using distinct parts of the network for different tasks, etc Wang et al.
044   (2023b). In particular, architecture-based methods have achieved great success across various CL
045   scenarios Rusu et al. (2016); Rosenfeld & Tsotsos (2018); Wang et al. (2023a). Characteristically, this
046   type of method introduces an extra part of the network that is solely trained on the current data, which
047   is then integrated with other parts that have been continuously trained on the previous data Yan et al.
048   (2021); Zhou et al. (2023b). Since a new independent parameter space is used to learn the current data,
049   these methods avoid rewriting the original parameters, thus preserving the old knowledge. In this
050
      way, the conflict between stability and plasticity at the parameter level can be significantly mitigated.
051   While studies that focus on expanding and allocating architecture have achieved notable success,
052   research on the basic architectures for CL is still in its infancy. This gap is crucial because, despite the
053   ability of advanced learning methods to optimize parameters effectively, the overall CL performance
      remains constrained by suboptimal architectures Lu et al. (2024). In this regard, certain pioneer

077   To investigate this, we conducted a comparison between ResNet-18 He et al. (2016) and its wider
078   yet shallower variant, evaluating their average forgetting and accuracy on the new task. As shown in
079   Fig. 1, ResNet-18 achieves higher accuracy on the new task, indicative of better plasticity, whereas the
080
      wider yet shallower variant exhibits lower average forgetting, indicative of greater stability. However,

081
      dilemma at the architectural level as well. Given that existing works Zhou et al. (2023b); Lu et al.
082
      (2024) typically employ a uniform architecture for both stability and plasticity, this inherent dilemma
083   may limit CL performance, even when the architecture and parameters are finely optimized.
084
085   How to balance the stability and plasticity at the architectural level? An intuitive and straightfor-
086
      ward solution is to combine two independent models with distinct architectures: one dedicated to
      plasticity and the other to stability. Previous studies on CL have demonstrated that incorporating an
087
      auxiliary model, specifically trained on the current data, can enhance the plasticity of the primary
088
      model Kim et al. (2023); Bonato et al. (2024). Building on these insights, we extend from an
089   architectural perspective, proposing a novel framework that employs a plastic architecture to acquire
090   new knowledge, which is then transferred to the main model with a stable architecture. Specifically,
091   knowledge distillation Hinton et al. (2015); Romero et al. (2014) is utilized for this transfer due to
092   its proven efficacy in transferring knowledge between networks with different architectures Gou
093   et al. (2021). Consequently, our proposed framework, Dual-Architecture (Dual-Arch), leverages the
094   complementary strengths of two distinct architectures, effectively balancing stability and plasticity
095   at the architectural level. Extensive experiments show that Dual-Arch markedly enhances CL per-
096   formance with significantly fewer parameters when compared to the baselines. Code is available at
097
      https://github.com/anonymous-dual-arch/d-arch.
098   The contributions of this study are outlined as follows:
099
100                                • Through meticulous empirical studies, we demonstrate that existing architectural designs
101                                  typically exhibit good plasticity but poor stability, while their wider and shallower variants
102
                                     exhibit the opposite traits. Based on these findings, we propose a novel insight for exploring
                                     the stability-plasticity trade-off from an architectural perspective.
103
104                                • We introduce a novel CL framework, Dual-Arch, which employs dual architectures dedicated
105                                  to stability and plasticity and thus combines both advantages. Furthermore, Dual-Arch can
106
                                     be naturally incorporated with various CL methods as a plug-and-play component.
107                                • Extensive experiments demonstrate that Dual-Arch is parameter-efficient, i.e., attaining bet-
                                     ter performance with a remarkably reduced parameter count than using a single architecture.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      CL involves letting models sequentially learn a series of tasks without or with limited access to
111
      previous tasks. Typically, these tasks are framed as visual classification problems Masana et al.
112   (2022). Based on whether the task identity is provided or must be inferred, CL can be categorized
113   into three typical scenarios: Task/Class/Domain Incremental Learning (IL) Van de Ven et al. (2022).
114   Moreover, there are some works that focus on more challenging scenarios where the task boundaries
115   are blurry Arani et al. (2022); Sarfraz et al. (2022). In the Task IL scenario, the task identity of test
116   samples is accessible at inference time, so the networks only need to learn and remember how to
117   classify within each task. More generally, the Class IL scenario requires networks to predict both
118   the task identity and the sub-class label. Moreover, the Domain IL scenario does not introduce new
119   classes during the learning process, instead, it characterizes task changes by introducing a shift in the
120
      input distribution. This study mainly focuses on the Class IL scenario, which is regarded as the most
      general and realistic among three typical scenarios Van de Ven et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2023b).
121
122
123   2.1   L EARNING M ETHODS FOR CL
124
      To address catastrophic forgetting, the CL community has developed numerous CL methods aimed at
125
      striking a balance between stability and plasticity. These methods encompass a range of techniques,
126
      including memory replay, weight or function regularization, and dynamic architecture.
127
128   Replay-based methods keep a subset of the previous data information in a memory buffer and
129   thus exploit it to recover old data distributions. A straightforward implementation involves simply
130
      replaying the stored data in conjunction with the current data (i.e., joint training), thereby simulating
      the training process on a dataset that is independently and identically distributed Robins (1995);
131
      Chaudhry et al. (2018). It should be noted that such a strategy is particularly widely used in Class
132
      IL and often combined with other categories of techniques Wang et al. (2023b). However, these
133   methods require access to raw past data, which might be discouraged in some environments due to
134   privacy concerns. Instead, recently some works Lin et al. (2022); Sun et al. (2023); Lin et al. (2023)
135   elaborately construct a special parameter space of old tasks as the memory.
136
      Regularization-based methods incorporate a regularization loss term to balance old and new tasks,
137
      which can be divided into two sub-directions based on the regularization target Wang et al. (2023b).
138
      The first is weight regularization, which selectively constrains the variation of the network parameters
139   based on the importance of each parameter in performing the old tasks, e.g., EWC Kirkpatrick
140   et al. (2017) and SI Zenke et al. (2017). The second is function regularization, which targets the
141   intermediate or final output of the prediction function. This strategy typically involves transferring
142   knowledge from previous CL models to the current model through knowledge distillation to mit-
143   igate forgetting Madaan et al. (2023). For instance, LwF Li & Hoiem (2017) proposes to let the
144   model concurrently learn the soft target generated by the previous model alongside the new data.
145   Furthermore, several methods integrate knowledge distillation with memory replay, proposing more
146   advanced solutions for Class IL, such as iCaRL Rebuffi et al. (2017) and WA Zhao et al. (2020).
147   Architecture-based methods mitigate inter-task interference and thus balance stability and plasticity
148   by allocating an expanding incremental parameter space of the network for each new task Yoon et al.
149   (2017). DER Yan et al. (2021) and MEMO Zhou et al. (2023b) are two typical representatives of this
150   type of method, both of which incorporate memory replay. While these methods have demonstrated
151   impressive CL performance, the rapid growth of parameters presents a challenge Li et al. (2019);
152   Zhou et al. (2023b). This may limit their application, especially in memory-restricted scenarios.
153   Discussion. In principle, the performance of neural networks is jointly influenced by their parameters
154   and architectures. While the learning methods mentioned above mainly enhance CL by optimizing
155   the parameters or extending parameter space, the suboptimal basic architectures might still limit CL
156   performance. Our study aims to address this by proposing a plug-and-play framework that leverages
157   the complementary strengths of two distinct architectures.
158
159   2.2   N EURAL A RCHITECTURES FOR CL
160
161   Besides learning methods, there is a body of research Mirzadeh et al. (2022a;b); Pham et al. (2022)
      that concentrates on exploring optimal neural architectures for CL. In particular, ArchCraft Lu et al.

162
      (2024) delves into the influence of various network components and scaling on CL performance,
163   demonstrating that certain architectural designs are more CL-friendly than existing ones. Furthermore,
164   it is shown that a well-designed architecture can achieve superior CL performance with a smaller
165   parameter count, which is particularly beneficial for memory-constrained environments Lu et al.
166   (2024). These studies emphasize that the impact of architectural designs on CL performance is at least
167   as significant as that of the learning methods. However, it should be noted that existing studies focus
168   exclusively on the impact of architectures on the overall performance of CL. Our work extends this
169   line of inquiry by highlighting the inherent conflict between stability and plasticity at the architectural
170   level and subsequently proposing a novel solution to address it.
171
172   2.3   M ULTI M ODELS FOR CL
173
      Various existing studies have proposed employing additional models to enhance CL Li & Hoiem
174
      (2017); Kim et al. (2023); Bonato et al. (2024). In particular, certain works Pham et al. (2021); Arani
175
      et al. (2022) based on complementary learning systems McClelland et al. (1995); Kumaran et al.
176   (2016) utilize two learners (known as slow and fast learners) with different functions to achieve CL.
177   Our proposed solution shares a similar conceptual framework, crafting two independent learners that
178   assume roles of plasticity and stability respectively during the CL process. However, unlike these
179   prior efforts that employ a uniform architecture for all models, our study emphasizes the importance
180   of specific architectural designs tailored to each learner. By doing so, our study provides novel
181   insights into more effectively leveraging multiple models for CL.

      overview of the Dual-Arch framework and detail its learning algorithm.

      4.1   T HE F ORMULATION OF D UAL -A RCH
271
272   The overall framework of Dual-Arch is illustrated in Fig. 2. Unlike the existing CL paradigm which
273   relies on a single learner, the Dual-Arch framework distributes the roles of plasticity and stability
274   across two distinct models: the plastic learner and the stable learner. Inspired by existing research Kim
275   et al. (2023) that employs auxiliary models to enhance plasticity, our framework designates the stable
276   learner as the main model, with the plasticity learner serving as an auxiliary model. Throughout the
277   learning process, the plastic learner is dedicated to the extraction of new knowledge, allowing for the
278
      potential forgetting of previous knowledge. Conversely, the stable learner is responsible for retaining
      existing knowledge while integrating new knowledge with the assistance of the plastic learner.
279
280   Dual-Arch allows the combination of the strengths of both stable and plastic architectures by employ-
281   ing corresponding architectures for the two learners. Specifically, these architectures are designed
282   through targeted modifications to the original one, with the objective of enhancing plasticity or
283   stability. Additionally, to overcome the increased memory consumption associated with incorporating
284
      an additional model, we concurrently reduce the parameter counts for both learners.
285   It is also worth highlighting that the Dual-Arch framework is designed to facilitate integration with a
286   variety of CL methods, serving as a plug-and-play component. This integration can be easily achieved
287   by applying these CL methods when training the stable learner, mirroring the training process of the
288   single learner within the traditional CL paradigm. Furthermore, for replay-based methods, the replay
289   buffer is concatenated with the training data for both the stable and plastic learners.
290
291   4.2   A RCHITECTURES FOR THE S TABLE AND P LASTIC L EARNERS
292
      This subsection presents the specific architectural designs tailored to the stable and plastic learners,
293
      with the objective of achieving superior CL performance while minimizing parameter counts. Building
294   upon the insights from Sec. 3, we employ a wide and shallow architecture for the stable learner,
295   denoted as Sta-Net, and a deep and thin architecture for the plastic learner, denoted as Pla-Net.
296   Following standard practices Masana et al. (2022); Goswami et al. (2024), we have chosen ResNet-18
297   as the foundation for crafting both architectures. Specifically, Sta-Net retains the same width as
298   ResNet-18 but incorporates only half as many residual blocks. Furthermore, we modify the GAP
299   layer of Sta-Net to produce an output feature map of size 2 × 2 instead of the original 1 × 1, thereby
300   increasing the width of the classifier. To design Pla-Net, we maintain the depth of ResNet-18 while
301   reducing its width from 64 to 42 to align with the parameter count of Sta-Net.

322   The learning process of the Dual-Arch framework involves training the plastic and stable learners in
323   sequence. In the initial stage of the learning process, our framework trains the plastic learner as a new
      task emerges. At this stage, the primary objective is to facilitate the acquisition of new knowledge,

325    training objective of the plastic learner is simplified to minimize the classification loss on the current
326    training data, i.e., Lplastic = LCE . Subsequently, the stable learner is trained to integrate the
327    existing knowledge with that acquired by the plastic learner. This process entails the transfer of
328    recently acquired knowledge from the plastic learner to the stable learner via knowledge distillation.
329    Specifically, a distillation loss term is incorporated into the training objective of the stable learner, to
330    align the logit outputs between the stable and plastic learners. Following convention Hinton et al.
331    (2015), a hard label loss (i.e., cross-entropy loss) is also employed to minimize the discrepancy
332    between the predictions of the stable learner and the actual labels of the training data. Moreover,

      Baselines. To assess the efficacy of our proposed method, we integrate it into five distinct CL
379   approaches spanning the three major categories: replay-based, regularization-based, and architecture-
380   based methods. These methods include iCaRL Rebuffi et al. (2017), WA Zhao et al. (2020), DER Yan
381   et al. (2021), Foster Wang et al. (2022), and MEMO Zhou et al. (2023b). Specifically, we compare the
382   performance of Dual-Arch with that of the original ResNet-18 to evaluate the enhancements provided
383   by our method. We also select ArchCraft Lu et al. (2024) as a baseline, which employs a single
384   CL-friendly architecture to improve CL performance, to show the benefits of dual architectures.
385   Implementation Setup. For all experiments, we train all models by 200 epochs in the first task
386   and 100 epochs in the subsequent tasks. The learning rate starts from 0.1 and gradually decays with
387   a cosine annealing scheduler. A fixed memory size of 2,000 exemplars is utilized for all replay-
388   based methods during the learning process. Given the significant impact of hyper-parameters on
389   CL Cha & Cho (2024); Mirzadeh et al. (2020), the hyperparameters for all methods adhere to the
390   settings in the open-source library PyCIL Zhou et al. (2023a) to ensure a fair comparison. Following
391   convention Mirzadeh et al. (2022b); Zhou et al. (2023a), the first convolution layer and following max
392   pooling layer of networks are replaced by a 3 × 3 convolution layer with a stride of 1 for CIFAR100.
393   Evaluation Metrics. The overall performance of CL is measured by two metrics: the Last Accuracy
394   (LA) and the Average Incremental Accuracy (AIA). The LA is the total classification accuracy after
395   the last task, which reflects the overall accuracy among all classes. Further, the AIA denotes the
396   average classification accuracy over all tasks, which reflects the performance across all incremental
397   steps. The higher LA and AIA, the better overall CL performance. Let K be the number of tasks,

535   In this paper, we point out the stability-plasticity dilemma at the architectural level and further
536   introduce Dual-Arch, a novel CL framework, to address it. Dual-Arch is built on a parallel level with
537   most of the existing CL methods (architecture vs. parameter), thereby serving as a plug-in component

539   the baselines while significantly reducing the parameter counts. We hope this work inspires further
      study on exploring a better trade-off between stability and plasticity from an architectural perspective.
