                                                    Abstract
010
011
                 Personalized federated learning is widely used for heterogeneous data distribu-
012              tions across clients. However, existing methods are difficult to measure and utilize
013              these heterogeneities accurately. To address this issue, in this paper, we propose a
014              novel and efficient method named FedDFQ which uses a customized Data Identity
015              Extraction Module (DIEM) to dynamically generate metric proxies that quantify
016              data heterogeneity across different local clients in a privacy-friendly manner. The
017              metric proxies are used to assess the contributions of global parameter aggrega-
018              tion and personalized gradient backpropagation for each local client. In addition,
019              we design a plug-and-play Automatic Gradient Accumulation Module (AGAM)
020
                 that regularizes personalized classification layers with re-balanced gradients. We
                 provide theoretical explanations and experimental results that validate the effec-
021
                 tiveness of the proposed FedDFQ. With comprehensive comparisons to existing
022
                 state-of-the-art approaches, FedDFQ outperforms them on two benchmark datasets
023              in different heterogeneous scenarios. The code can be accessed at :[URL].

026   1     Introduction
027
028   Federated Learning (FL) is a distributed machine learning paradigm that allows multiple participants
029   to cooperatively train models under the guidance of a central coordinator without sharing local data.
030   This approach is widely used in healthcare Hao et al. (2020); Zhang et al. (2021) and the financial
031   field Zheng et al. (2021); Liu et al. (2021). The traditional federated learning method such as
032   FedAvg McMahan et al. (2017) obtains a global model by averaging the parameters uploaded from
033
      all clients, which is prone to be influenced by data heterogeneity and leads to significant performance
      degradation. Despite new frameworks have been introduced that ensure consistency between local
034
      and global optimization goals to obtain a more stable global model Li et al. (2019); Oh et al. (2021).
035
      However, these attempts fail to escape the paradigm of aggregating local parameters and average
036   them to obtain global parameters, struggling with data heterogeneity, and the difference in data
037   distribution between different clients may lead to suboptimal model aggregation with performance
038   degradation (Yang et al., 2019).
039
      Correspondingly, several personalized federated learning approaches have been put forward, which
040
      aim to improve the adaptability of local clients for their corresponding preserved tasks Arivazhagan
041
      et al. (2019); Fallah et al. (2020). For instance, FedRep Collins et al. (2021) separates the local
042   clients model into shared structures and personalized structures so that the shared part can represent
043   the global knowledge and the personal part is adapted to local data characteristics. However, existing
044   approaches to personalized federated learning suffer from several shortcomings: most only focus on
045   isolating the bias introduced by the heterogeneous distribution of other clients, rather than exploiting
046   complementary information in heterogeneous data. Others attempt to exploit complementary infor-
047   mation in heterogeneous data based on features from backbones with learnable parameters, which
048   suffer from biases at the initial and training stages. It impairs the generalization to the local client on
049   out-of-distribution samples.
050   To address these shortcomings, we propose a novel method, named FedDFQ, which quantifies the
051   degree of data divergence among each client and uses it to measure the contribution of individual
052   clients in this round. Specifically, we design a data representation extraction module to map local data
053   into high-dimension semantic space for security and privacy. Then metric proxies are constructed
      through semantic relationships between mapped data to quantify heterogeneity across local clients.

      2     Related work
078
079   2.1   Federal learning with heterogeneous data
080
081   Federated learning aims to solve the problems of data privacy protection and data security, allowing
082   multiple participants to jointly train machine learning models without sharing raw data. Traditional
083   federated learning methods represented by FedAvg, construct global models by training the model
084   locally at the client and sending the updated model parameters to a central server for average aggre-
085   gation. However, these methods assume that the data distributions of all clients are independently
086
      identically distributed (IID), leading to poor performance of the model on certain clients in the
      presence of data heterogeneityYe et al. (2023). Therefore, Subsequent studies aim to optimize the
087
      aggregation process of model parameters by quantifying the feature similarity of client datasets.
088
      For example, FedProto proposed a prototype learning approach that allows clients to make person-
089   alized adjustments while maintaining global consistency Tan et al. (2022b). FedBABU enhances
090   the model’s adaptability under non-independently identically distributed (non-IID) data through an
091   adaptive weight assignment mechanism Oh et al. (2021). Besides, FedProx Li et al. (2020) introduces
092   the proximal term to restrict the updates of the local model that ensure similarity with the global
093   model. FedDyn Acar et al. (2021) regularizes the local client model to reduce parameter drift with
094   the global model and bring the local optima closer to the global optima. These methods not only
095   improve the model’s ability to adapt to local data features but also enhance the model’s generalization
096   performance in heterogeneous environments. However, the best performance for each local client
097
      may not be an optimal solution for the global model.
098
099   2.2   Personalized federated learning
100
      To overcome this limitation, the concept of personalized federated learning has been proposed,
101
      aiming to improve the local adaptation and overall performance of the model by adapting to the
102   data distribution specific to each clientTan et al. (2022a). Ditto Li et al. (2021a) achieves better
103   personalized performance by introducing a regularization term, which enables the client model to
104   be locally fine-tuned while maintaining consistency with the global model. FedProto Tan et al.
105   (2022b) introduces prototypical regularization to constrain the local updates, mitigating the effect
106   of bias due to data distribution heterogeneity. FedPAC Xu et al. (2023b) leverages global semantic
107   knowledge to align local-global features for better representations and quantifies the benefit of
      classifier combination for each client as a function of the combining weights, deriving an optimization


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      problem for estimating optimal weights. FedRep Collins et al. (2021) separates the local clients model
109   into shared structures and personalized structures so that the shared part can represent the global
110   knowledge and the personal part is adapted to local data characteristics. Recently, FedHKD Chen
111   et al. (2023) applies prototype learning and knowledge distillation to train local clients for averaged
112   representations and hyper-knowledge, instead of uploading model parameters. LG-Mix Jiang et al.
113   (2024) utilizes the coverage rate of Neural Tangent Kernel to quantify the importance of local and
114   global updates and mix these importance indices to update parameters. However, these methods
115   neglect the utilization of interactions among clients with similar distributions to collaboratively train
116   personalized classifiers to reduce the variance of client models.

118   3     Method architecture
119
120   We propose an efficient method to improve federated learning in heterogeneous data distribution
121   scenarios. The overview of FedDFQ is shown in Figure 1. Specifically, we follow the design of one
122   server to multi-clients. The input image first passes the feature extraction layer and data identification
123   extraction in local clients, which aims to obtain abstract representations for classification and high-
124   dimension distribution vectors. Then we pack up the global parameters, personalized gradients,
125
      and distribution vectors uploaded to the server. The distribution vectors generate metric proxies
      that assess contributions for each client in this aggregation. Next, the server distributes the global
126
      parameters re-balanced based on contributions in metric proxies. Finally, to further improve the
127
      generalization of clients to out-of-distribution samples, we design an AGAM that regularizes the
128   classification layers with semantic-aware gradients for each client.

152   3.1   Data identity extraction module
153
154   To evaluate the data heterogeneity of different local clients and follow the objectives of federated
155   learning to protect the privacy and security of data, we propose the DIEM. Different from existing
156   methods that use abstract representations from local feature extractors to denote the data distribution,
157   these extractors with learnable parameters inevitably introduce undesirable data biases during the
158
      model initialization and training stage, leading to performance degradation with out-of-distribution
      samples.
159
160   The proposed method applies a no-parameter algorithm consisting of two data aggregation operations
161   at local clients. Specifically, we first aggregate and mean input data along the channel dimension to
      obtain the 2D feature identifiers. Next, the second aggregation averages these 2D feature identifiers

304        4.1    Experimental setup
305
           Datasets. We use two widely used bechmark datasets CIFAR-10 Krizhevsky & Hinton (2009) and
306
           Fashion MNIST Xiao et al. (2017). Specifically, CIFAR-10 contains 60,000 color images of 32x32
307
           size with 10 categories divided into 50,000 for training and 10,000 for testing. FashionMNIST
308        consists of 60,000 training images and 10,000 testing images of 28x28 resolution with 10 categories.
309        We utilize the Dirichlet distribution to model a non-independent identically distributed (non-IID) data
310        partitioning strategy Yurochkin et al. (2019). Sample indices in the dataset are iteratively assigned to
311        each client to ensure that each client has a minimum amount of data. By modeling the distribution of
312        each client’s data using the Dirichlet distribution, we can concentrate the data in specific categories,
313        resulting in a non-uniform data distribution.

      MNIST datasets. After integrating the FELPA and AGAM, FedDFQ outperforms other designs by
366   a large margin, because the AGAM is prone to fall into the local optimal solution and FELPA causes
367   semantic gaps that significantly hinder classification layers from adapting aggregated parameters
368   from other heterogeneous clients. The feature extraction layer can aggregate the parameters of other
369   clients to provide momentum support for model parameter updating and help the AGAM jump out
370   of the local optimal solution. Then the DIEM is integrated into FedAvg (DIEM) and outperforms
371   vanilla FL (w/o DIEM) by 3.13 % Acc and 0.71% Acc on CIFAR-10 and FashionMNIST datasets.

376   local and global levels and lead to a more stable training process. In more detail, the green line in

      approaches, particularly in handling more complex data distributions.

474   5   Conclusion and future work
475
476   In this paper, we propose a novel personalized federated learning method named FedDFQ including
477   IDEM, FELPA, and AGAM modules. Specifically, the IDEM measures and quantifies data hetero-
478   geneity for each client to generate metric proxies, which re-scale the contributions of local clients in
479   the current rounds. FELPA aggregates weighted parameters of feature extractors according to metric
480   proxies from each client. To further utilize the homogeneity in heterogeneous clients, AGAM is
481   designed to regularize personalized classification layers with weight gradients, improving the gener-
482
      alization of local models. Besides, we conduct extensive experiments and provide theoretical proof
      to validate the effectiveness of our FedDFQ, and our method achieves state-of-the-art performance
483
      on two benchmark datasets.
484
485   Future work includes developing more refined methods for extracting distributed features and con-
      ducting experimental analyses on more datasets.
