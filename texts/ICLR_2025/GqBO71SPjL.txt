009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We address the problem of extending the capabilities of vision foundation models
012            such as DINO, SAM, and CLIP, to 3D tasks. Specifically, we introduce a novel
013            method to uplift 2D image features into 3D Gaussian Splatting scenes. Unlike
014            traditional approaches that rely on minimizing a reconstruction loss, our method
015            employs a simpler and more efficient feature aggregation technique, augmented
016            by a graph diffusion mechanism. Graph diffusion enriches features from a given
017            model, such as CLIP, by leveraging pairwise similarities that encode 3D geom-
018
               etry or similarities induced by another embedding like DINOv2. Our approach
               achieves performance comparable to the state of the art on multiple downstream
019
               tasks while delivering significant speed-ups. Notably, we obtain competitive seg-
020
               mentation results using generic DINOv2 features, despite DINOv2 not being
021            trained on millions of annotated segmentation masks like SAM. When applied to
022            CLIP features, our method demonstrates strong performance in open-vocabulary,
023            language-based object detection tasks, highlighting the versatility of our approach.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   The field of image understanding has recently seen remarkable progress, driven by large pretrained
029   models such as CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), DINO (Caron et al., 2021; Oquab et al., 2024), or
030   SAM (Kirillov et al., 2023). A key factor behind their exceptional generalization capabilities lies in
031   the vast size of their training datasets, often composed of millions or even billions of samples.
032   3D scene representation has also advanced with machine learning approaches like NeRF (Mildenhall
033   et al., 2021) and model fitting techniques such as Gaussian Splatting (Kerbl et al., 2023). These
034   methods typically rely on a few dozen views of the scene captured from different angles. While the
035   resulting reconstructions effectively capture both appearance and geometrical information, they are
036   not directly applicable to semantic tasks, which has led to further developments.
037   The complementarity of these two families of approaches has indeed recently been exploited by
038   numerous methods that integrate geometry and semantics by uplifting image-level features extracted
039   by large pretrained models into 3D NeRF or Gaussian Splatting representations. This has led to a
040   surge in methods for tasks such as language-guided object retrieval (Kerr et al., 2023; Liu et al.,
041   2023; Zuo et al., 2024), scene editing, (Kobayashi et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2024; Fan et al., 2023),
042   or semantic segmentation (Cen et al., 2023c; Ye et al., 2024a; Ying et al., 2024).
043   The main limitation of most previous approaches lies in their reliance on optimization, which re-
044   quires an iterative process to learn a scene-specific 3D representation by minimizing reprojection
045   error across all training views. While this loss function is intuitive, a faster and more straightfor-
046   ward method for transferring 2D generic visual features to already trained Gaussian splatting 3D
047   models would be preferable, which is the purpose of this work.
048   In this paper, we demonstrate that a simple, learning-free process is highly effective for uplifting 2D
049   features or semantic masks into 3D Gaussian Splatting scenes. This process, which can be viewed
050   as an ‘inverse rendering’ operation, is both computationally efficient and adaptable to any feature
051   type. We showcase its efficiency by uplifting visual features from DINOv2 (Oquab et al., 2024;
052   Darcet et al., 2024), semantic masks from SAM (Kirillov et al., 2023) and SAM2 (Ravi et al., 2024),
053   and language features from CLIP (Ilharco et al., 2021). Then, we show that a graph diffusion
      mechanism (Kondor & Lafferty, 2002; Smola & Kondor, 2003) is helpful for feature uplifting in

054
      3D scenes. This mechanism is rooted in spectral graph theory and used in spectral clustering tech-
055   niques (Belkin & Niyogi, 2001; Shi & Malik, 2000; Meila & Shi, 2000). In the context of our
056   work, it serves multiple purposes: first, it enriches 3D features obtained from a given model such as
057   CLIP with 3D geometry, and it may leverage rich features embeddings such as DINOv2. Second,
058   graph diffusion transforms coarse segmentation inputs, such as scribbles, into accurate 3D segmen-
059   tation masks without relying on segmentation models like SAM. When evaluated on segmentation
060   and open-vocabulary object localization, our method achieves results comparable to state-of-the-art
061   techniques while being significantly faster than previous approaches relying on optimization.
062   To summarize, our contributions are threefold: (i) we introduce a simple, learning-free uplifting
063   approach that can be directly integrated into the rendering process, achieving state-of-the-art re-
064   sults when applied to SAM-generated semantic masks. (ii) we demonstrate that when using graph
065   diffusion, uplifting DINOv2 features, yields competitive segmentation results (Section 4), despite
066   DINOv2 not being trained for segmentation like SAM. (iii) We show that graph diffusion can also
067   be used to enrich 3D CLIP representations, leveraging similarities computed from DINOv2 features,
068   thereby achieving competitive performance on open-vocabulary object localization tasks.

070   2   R ELATED WORK
071
072   Learning 3D semantic scene representations with NeRF. NeRF (Mildenhall et al., 2021) uses
073   a multilayer perceptron to predict the volume density and radiance for any given 3D position and
074   viewing direction. Such representation can naturally be extended to semantic features. The early
075   works N3F (Tschernezki et al., 2022) and DFF (Kobayashi et al., 2022) distill DINO 2D (i.e., image-
076   level) features (Caron et al., 2021) in scene-specific NeRF representations. Kobayashi et al. (2022)
077
      also distill LSeg (Li et al., 2022) a CLIP-inspired language-driven model for semantic segmentation.
      Shortly after, LERF (Kerr et al., 2023) and 3D-OVS (Liu et al., 2023) learned 3D CLIP (Radford
078
      et al., 2021) and DINO (Caron et al., 2021) features jointly for open-vocabulary segmentation. These
079
      works were extended to other pretrained models such as latent diffusion models (Ye et al., 2023) or
080   SAM (Kirillov et al., 2023) for semantic segmentation (Cen et al., 2023c; Ying et al., 2024).
081
082   Learning 3D semantic scene representations with Gaussian splatting. Subsequent work have
083   relied on the more recent Gaussian splatting method (Kerbl et al., 2023), achieving high-quality
084
      novel-view synthesis while being orders of magnitude faster that NeRF-based models. Several tasks
      have been addressed such as semantic segmentation using SAM (Cen et al., 2023b; Ye et al., 2024a;
085
      Kim et al., 2024), language-driven retrieval or editing using CLIP combined with DINO (Zuo et al.,
086
      2024) or SAM (Ye et al., 2023), or scene editing using diffusion models (Chen et al., 2024; Wang
087   et al., 2024). These works learn 3D semantic representations by minimizing a reprojection loss. As
088   a single scene can be represented by over a million Gaussians, such optimization-based techniques
089   have strong memory and computational limitations. To handle these, FMGS (Zuo et al., 2024) em-
090   ploys a multi-resolution hash embedding (MHE) of the scene for uplifting DINO and CLIP repre-
091   sentations, Feature 3DGS (Zhou et al., 2024) learns a 1 × 1 convolutional upsampler of Gaussians’
092   features distilled from LSeg and SAM’s encoder and LangSplat (Qin et al., 2024) learns an au-
093   toencoder to reduce CLIP feature dimension from 512 to 3. In contrast, our approach requires no
094   learning, which significantly speeds up the uplifting process and reduces the memory requirements.
095   Leveraging 3D information to better segment in 2D. Most prior works focusing on semantic
096   segmentation leverage 2D models specialized for this task. The early work of Yen-Chen et al. (2022)
097   uplifts learned 2D image inpainters by optimizing view consistency over depth and appearance.
098   Later, subsequent works have mostly relied on uplifting either features from SAM’s encoder (Zhou
099   et al., 2024), binary SAM masks (Cen et al., 2023c;b), or SAM masks automatically generated for
100   all objects in the image (Ye et al., 2024a; Ying et al., 2024; Kim et al., 2024). The latter approach
101   is computationally expensive, as it requires querying SAM on a grid of points over the image. It
102
      also requires matching inconsistent mask predictions across views, with e.g. a temporal propagation
      model (Ye et al., 2024a) or a hierarchical learning approach (Kim et al., 2024), which introduces
103
      additional computational overhead. In this work, we focus on single instance segmentation and
104
      show that our uplifted features are on par with state-of-the-art approaches (Cen et al., 2023c;b;
105   Ying et al., 2024). Standing out from prior work uplifting DINO features (Tschernezki et al., 2022;
106   Kobayashi et al., 2022; Kerr et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2023; Zuo et al., 2024), we
107   quantitatively show that DINOv2 features can be used on their own for semantic segmentation and
      rival SAM-based models through a simple graph diffusion process that leverages 3D geometry.

      Learning 3D CLIP features for open-vocabulary object localization. For learning 3D CLIP
109   features, prior works leverage vision models such as DINO or SAM. DINO is used to regularize and

      3.2   U PLIFTING OF 2D FEATURE MAPS INTO 3D
152
      Given a set of m 2D training frames and the corresponding 3D scene obtained by Gaussian Splatting,
153   our goal is to compute generic features fi in Rc for each Gaussian i, which would be effective for
154   solving future downstream tasks, e.g., high-resolution semantic segmentation for new frames of the
155   scene, or robot navigation. In other words, fi can be seen as an extension of the color function ci ,
156   even though, for simplicity, we do not consider view-dependent features in this work.
157
      A natural approach is to consider a pre-trained vision model that provides 2D feature maps for each
158
      of the m frames used in Gaussian splatting, and then devise a technique to uplift these 2D feature
159   maps into 3D. This uplifting principle can also be directly applied to semantic masks instead of
160   generic features, as demonstrated in Section 5. Interestingly, once the features fi are computed
161   for each Gaussian i, it is possible to render two-dimensional feature maps for any new view, at a
      resolution that can be much higher than the feature maps computed for the m training frames.

      3.3          E NRICHING FEATURES BY DIFFUSION ON GRAPHS
238
239   DINOv2 features have shown remarkable performance on semantic segmentation tasks with simple
240   linear probing (Oquab et al., 2024), making them a good candidate to enrich features that lack such
241   a property like CLIP (Wysoczańska et al., 2024; Zuo et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023). Inspired by
242   spectral clustering techniques (Shi & Malik, 2000; Kondor & Lafferty, 2002; Belkin & Niyogi,
243   2001), and aligning with the goals of recent work on 2D segmentation that improve CLIP features
244   with DINO (Wysoczańska et al., 2024), we then propose to diffuse features that have been uplifted to
245   3D by leveraging pairwise similarities induced by DINOv2 while taking into account 3D geometry.
246   Graph construction From uplifted DINOv2 features f in Rn we construct a graph whose nodes
247   are given by the 3D Gaussians and whose edges, represented by a matrix A of size n×n, encode both
248   the 3D Euclidean geometry between the nodes and the similarity between their DINOv2 features.
249
      More precisely, each node i is connected to its k nearest neighbors N (i) as measured by the Eu-
250
      clidean distance between the centers of the 3D Gaussians. The edge weight between neighboring
251   nodes i and j is given by a local feature similarity Sf (fi , fj ) between their DINOv2 features, typ-
252   ically a cosine similarity or an RBF kernel. For segmentation tasks, we prevent diffusion into the
253   background by adding a node-wise unary regularization term P (fi ), a similarity between node fea-
254   ture fi and some reference features f¯. For details on Sf and P please refer to Appendix A.3.

      4.1   M ULTIPLE - VIEW S EGMENTATION
275
      We assume that a foreground mask of the object to be segmented is provided on the reference
276
      frame I1 . The foreground masks are either scribbles or a whole reference mask of the object, both
277   of which define a set of foreground pixels P. In the following, we present the proposed approaches
278   for segmentation using SAM and DINOv2 features, based on both types of foreground masks.
279
280   Multiple-view Segmentation with SAM. SAM (Kirillov et al., 2023; Ravi et al., 2024) is a pow-
281   erful model that can generate object segmentation masks from point prompts, on a single 2D image.
282   Aggregating SAM 2D segmentation masks in 3D allows for cross-view consistency and improves
283   single-view segmentation results. We proceed by generating 2D feature maps based on SAM seg-
284
      mentation masks of each training frame while only relying on the foreground mask for the reference
      frame I1 . The 2D feature maps are generated by constructing several sets of point prompts on each
285
      training frame which are then provided to SAM to obtain several segmentation masks. The point
286
      prompts are obtained using the foreground mask provided on the reference frame as described in
287   Appendix A.1. Averaging the resulting segementation masks for each frame results in the final 2D
288   SAM feature maps. These are then uplifted using the aggregation scheme in Sec. 3.2. Our final
289   prediction is obtained by rendering the uplifted feature maps into the target frame.
290
291   Multiple-view segmentation with DINOv2. We construct 2D feature maps at the patch level
292   using DINOv2 with registers (Darcet et al., 2024) and uplift them into a high resolution and fine-
293   grained 3D semantic representation which is then used for segmentation. The 2D feature maps are
294   constructed using a combination of a sliding windows mechanism and dimensionality reduction of
295
      the original DINOv2 features as described in Appendix A.2 and illustrated in Fig. 4 therein. This
      approach enhances the granularity of spatial representations by aggregating patch-level representa-
296
      tions to form pixel-level features. The 2D feature maps from the m training views are uplifted using
297
      Eq. (3) and the resulting 3D features are then re-projected into any viewing direction d using Eq. (4)
298
      to compute rendered 2D features (F̂d,p ). To obtain segmentation masks, we construct a predictor
299
      score P (F̂d,p ) for a 2D pixel p to belong to the foreground, based on its corresponding rendered fea-
300
301
      ture. The score P is obtained by comparing the rendered features (F̂d,p ) with foreground features
302   Fref := (F̂d1 ,p )p∈P corresponding to the foreground mask computed on the reference frame I1 , see
303
      Appendix A.2. The final segmentation mask is then obtained by thresholding.
304
      Enhancing segmentation with DINOv2 using 3D graph diffusion. DINOv2 provides generic
305   visual features that do not explicitly include segmentation information, unlike models such as SAM
306   that were specifically trained for such a task. Consequently, 2D projections of uplifted DINOv2
307   features might fail to separate distinct objects that have similar features. This challenge can be
308   mitigated by incorporating 3D spatial information, which may help separate them.
309
      To this end, we propose to leverage the graph diffusion process introduced in Section 3.3. We set the
310
      initial vector of weights g0 in Rn of the graph diffusion algorithm to be a coarse estimation of the
311   contribution of each Gaussian to the final segmentation mask. This initial weight vector is computed
312   by uplifting the 2D foreground mask (either scribbles or a reference mask) from the reference frame
313   into 3D using Eq. (3), normalizing and thresholding them (see appendix Sec. A.3). The nodes for
314   which g0 has a positive value define a set of anchor nodes M that are more likely to contribute to
315   the foreground. We retain the last weight vector gT and render it into 2D for segmentation (Eq. (4)).
316   The regularization term P appearing in Eq. (7) is obtained by comparing the uplifted features with
317   anchor features obtained using the foreground mask as described in the appendix.

319   4.2   O PEN - VOCABULARY OBJECT DETECTION
320
321   As in (Kerr et al., 2023; Qin et al., 2024; Zuo et al., 2024) we propose to uplift CLIP features
322   (Radford et al., 2021) which are excellent for aligning images and text, and evaluate the uplifted
323   features task of open-vocabulary detection (Kerr et al., 2023). As CLIP outputs only one feature
      vector per input image, a couple of extra steps are needed to distill CLIP into the 3D Gaussians.

324
      Construction of CLIP feature maps We follow the common practice (Kerr et al., 2023; Zuo
325   et al., 2024) of constructing multi-resolution CLIP 2D feature maps by querying CLIP on a grid of
326   overlapping patches at different scales and aggregating the resulting representations. As in Zuo et al.
327   (2024), rather than keeping the different representations separate, we choose to aggregate them with
328   a simple average pooling. These multi-resolution CLIP features are uplifted into 3D using Eq. (3).
329
330
      Refinement with DINOv2 graph diffusion We further refine those features with the diffusion
      procedure described in Sec. 3.3. To this end, DINOv2 features are also uplifted, and the similar-
331
      ity matrix is built as in Eq. (7), with no unary term P . The diffusion process locally aggregates
332
      CLIP features from Gaussians whose DINOv2 features are similar. This enhances the granularity
333   of CLIP visual representations while remaining in the CLIP feature space, allowing for downstream
334   applications with text queries. Wysoczańska et al. (2024) perform a similar procedure for 2D image
335   segmentation, with only one step in the diffusion. This process can also be related to the pixel-
336   alignment loss in (Zuo et al., 2024), as a diffusion step corresponds to a gradient step for that loss.

351   SPIn-NeRF, which contains a subsets of scenes from NeRF-related datasets (Knapitsch et al., 2017;
352   Mildenhall et al., 2019; 2021; Yen-Chen et al., 2022; Fridovich-Keil et al., 2022). The NVOS dataset
353

      another one is labeled with scribbles to be used as reference. SPIn-NeRF contains both forward-
354
      facing and 360-degree scenes, in which all frames are labeled with segmentation masks, and the
355
      standard evaluation protocol uses the segmentation mask from the first frame as reference to label
356   the subsequent frames.
357
358   Open-vocabulary object detection We evaluate on the LERF Localization dataset (Kerr et al.,
359   2023) consisting of complex in-the-wild scenes. We report our results on the extended evaluated task
360
      introduced by LangSplat (Qin et al., 2024) containing additional challenging localization samples.
361   Evaluation and hyperparameter tuning. Our segmentation results are averaged over 3 in-
362   dependent runs. Segmentation with 3D SAM masks requires setting a threshold for fore-
363   ground/background pixel assignment, and optionally choosing one of the three masks proposed by
364   SAM (representing different possible segmentations of the object of interest). Segmentation with DI-
365   NOv2 uses graph diffusion with RBF kernels as the similarities P and Sf and therefore needs three
366   hyperparameters: the 2 bandwidths of the RBF kernels, and the threshold for foreground/background
367   pixel assignment.
368   For SPIn-NeRF, all hyperparameters are chosen based on the IoU for the available reference mask.
369   For NVOS, only reference scribbles are provided, hence i) for SAM/SAM2, only one mask is gen-
370   erated, and the threshold for segmentation is fixed for all scenes for SAM and automatically chosen
371   using Li iterative Minimum Cross Entropy method (Li & Lee, 1993) for SAM 2, ii) for DINOv2 we
372   predict a SAM mask based on the scribbles of the reference frame, and choose the hyperparameters
373   maximizing the IoU with this SAM mask. This is consistent with a scenario where the user, here
374
      SAM, would choose hyperparameters based on visual inspection on one of the frames.
375   For the LERF Localization task, graph diffusion is run with P = 1 and an RBF kernel for Sf ,
376   with a set of different bandwidths. The resulting feature maps are automatically selected based on
377   the relevancy score with the text prompt. This aligns with the semantic level selection process of
      LERF (Kerr et al., 2023) and LangSplat (Kerbl et al., 2023).

      6     C ONCLUDING REMARKS AND LIMITATIONS
521
522   Learning-free uplifting. In this work, we introduce a simple yet effective aggregation mechanism
523   for transferring 2D visual representations into 3D, bypassing the traditional optimization-based ap-
524
      proach. The aggregation builds upon already trained Gaussian Splatting representations and is im-
      plemented within the CUDA rendering process, making 2D-to-3D uplifting as fast as 3D-to-2D
525
      rendering. Note however that the quality of learned 3D features is bound by that of the 3D scene
526
      reconstruction. Reconstruction by Gaussian Splatting is notoriously challenging when dealing with,
527   e.g., highly specular scenes (Jiang et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024), blurred images Zhao et al. (2024);
528   Lee et al. (2024) or high-frequency regions (Ye et al., 2024b; Zhang et al., 2024). In such scenarios,
529   learning 3D features along with 3D Gaussian Splatting reconstruction may lead to improved scene
530   geometry, opening promising perspectives for future work.
531
532
      Graph diffusion. Our proposed graph diffusion process allows injecting the rich DINOv2 repre-
533
      sentations to refine arbitrary features such as segmentation masks or CLIP embeddings. Our CLIP
534   feature refinement builds upon prior works using DINO features as a regularization (Kerr et al., 2023;
535   Zuo et al., 2024), while alleviating the computational overhead associated with joint gradient-based
536   optimization of CLIP and DINO features. However, it does rely on the adequate choice of band-
537   width hyperparameter(s) when defining the similarity graph. In this work, these hyperparameters
538   are chosen based on IoU with a SAM-predicted mask for segmentation, and based on the relevancy
539   score with the text prompt for object localization. While automatic, this decision process requires
      multiple evaluations of a success criterion with different candidate bandwidth values.

705   A     U SING LUDVIG FOR DOWNSTREAM TASKS
706
707   In this section, we describe our approach for uplifting DINOv2, SAM and CLIP models and evalu-
708   ating the 3D features on two downstream tasks: segmentation and open-vocabulary object detection.
709   As in Sec. 3, we are given a set of 2D frames I1 , . . . , Im , with camera poses d1 , . . . , dm and
710   corresponding 3D scene obtained by the Gaussian Splatting method, which can be used to uplift 2D
711   features from the m frames to 3D.
712
713   Multiple-view segmentation. For this task, we assume that a foreground mask of the object to be
714   segmented is provided on a reference frame taken to be the first frame I1 . We consider two types
715   of foreground masks: either scribbles or a whole reference mask of the object, both of which define
716   a set of foreground pixels P. In the following subsections, we present the proposed approaches for
717   segmentation using SAM and DINOv2 features, based on both types of foreground masks.
718
719   A.1   M ULTIPLE - VIEW S EGMENTATION WITH SAM
720
721   SAM (Kirillov et al., 2023; Ravi et al., 2024) is a powerful image segmentation model, that can
722   generate object segmentation masks from point prompts on a single 2D image. Aggregating SAM
723   2D segmentation masks in 3D allows for cross-view consistency and improves single-view segmen-
724
      tation results. In order to leverage SAM, we propose a simple mechanism for generating SAM 2D
      features for each frame from a foreground mask in the reference frame.
725
726   Construction of 2D feature maps. The key idea is to generate point prompts on each training
727   frame from the foreground mask provided on the reference frame. To this end, we perform an
728   uplifting of the foreground mask (Eq. (3)) and re-project it on all frames (Eq. (4)). This results in 2D
729   scalar maps that we further normalize by their average value. A higher values indicates the presence
730   of the target object. For each frame with camera pose d, we retain a subset of pixels Pd with values
731   higher than a threshold fixed for all scenes and select point prompts for SAM from this subset.
732   Finally, we compute 2D segmentation masks for each frame using SAM by randomly selecting 3
733
      points prompts from Pd , repeating the operation 10 times and averaging the resulting masks for each
      view to obtain the final 2D SAM feature maps.
734
735   Segmentation with uplifted SAM masks. The 2D segmentation masks generated by SAM are
736   uplifted using the aggregation scheme described in Sec. 3.2. Our final prediction is obtained by
737   rendering the uplifted feature maps into the target frame.
738
739   A.2   M ULTIPLE - VIEW SEGMENTATION WITH DINOV 2
740
741   DINOv2 (Oquab et al., 2024) is a self-supervised vision model recognized for its generalization
742   capabilities. In this work, we aggregate the patch-level representations produced by DINOv2 with
743   registers (Darcet et al., 2024) into a high resolution and fine-grained 3D semantic representation.
744
      Construction of 2D feature maps. We construct the 2D feature maps using a combination of a
745
      sliding windows mechanism and dimensionality reduction of the original DINOv2 features. Specif-
746   ically, we i) extract DINOv2 patch-level representations across multiple overlapping crops of the
747   training images, ii) apply dimensionality reduction over the set of all patch embeddings, ii) upsam-
748   ple and aggregate the dimensionality-reduced patch embeddings to obtain pixel-level features for
749   each image. The process is illustrated in Figure 4. This approach enhances the granularity of spatial
750   representations by aggregating patch-level representations to form pixel-level features.
751
      Segmentation with uplifted DINOv2 features. The 2D feature maps from the m training views
752
      are uplifted using Eq. (3) and the resulting 3D features are then re-projected into any viewing di-
753
      rection d using Eq. (4) to compute rendered 2D features (F̂d,p ). To obtain segmentation masks, we
754
755
      construct a score P (F̂d,p ) for a 2D pixel p to belong to the foreground, based on its corresponding
      rendered feature. More precisely, P relies on the rendered foreground features Fref := (F̂d1 ,p )p∈P

      Experimentally, the second approach is extremely efficient when the set of foreground pixels P
      covers the whole object to segment, so that P captures all relevant features. This is the case when

      a whole reference mask of the object is provided. When the foreground pixels P does not cover the
779
      whole object, as with scribbles, P can be discriminative to parts of the object that are not covered
780   by P. Therefore, we rely on the second approach for tasks where a reference mask is provided, and
781   use the simpler first approach when only scribbles serve as reference.

783   A.3   E NHANCING SEGMENTATION WITH DINOV 2 USING 3D GRAPH DIFFUSION
784
785   DINOv2 provides generic visual features that do not explicitly include information for segmentation,
786   unlike models such as SAM that were specifically trained for such a task. Consequently, using the
787   2D projections of uplifted DINOv2 features, as proposed in Sec. A.2, might fail to separate different
788   objects that happen to have similar features while still being distinct entities. This challenge can be
789   mitigated by incorporating 3D spatial information in which the objects are more likely to be well-
790
      separated. To this end, we propose to leverage the graph diffusion process introduced in Section 3.3.
791   For this task, the initial vector of weights g0 ∈ Rn representing a coarse estimation of the contribu-
792   tion of each Gaussian to the segmentation mask. We retain the last weight vector gT and render it
793   into 2D for segmentation (Eq. (4)). Below, we describe the initialization of the weight vector g0 and
794   the construction of the adjacency matrix A.
795   Initialization of the weight vector. The initial weight vector g0 is computed by uplifting the
796   2D foreground mask (either scribbles or a reference mask) from the reference frame into 3D using
797   Eq. (3), normalizing the 3D mask by its mean value over all nodes and setting to zero all values
798   below a fixed threshold. The nodes for which g0 has a positive value define a set of anchor nodes
799   M that are more likely to contribute to the foreground. The resulting weight vector is a coarse
800   estimation of how much each Gaussian contributes to a rendered 2D segmentation mask.
801
      Construction of the graph edges. We define Sf based on the cosine similarity between features
802   and choose a global unary regularization term P (fi ) on each node i to encourages similarity be-
803   tween the uplifted node feature fi and those belonging to the foreground. More precisely, P is
804   defined using a similar approach as in Sec. A.2: either as a similarity P (fi ) = Sf (fi , f¯) with the
805   averaged feature f¯ over the anchor nodes M (in the case when scribbles are provided), or as a lo-
806   gistic regression model trained on the uplifted features, so that anchor nodes’ features are assigned
807   a positive label (in the case when a full foreground mask is available). The local term Sf , typically
808   a cosine similarity, allows diffusion to neighbors that have similar features while the unary term
809   prevents leakage to background nodes during diffusion by encouraging closeness to the foreground
      features and allows using an arbitrary number of diffusion steps.

      steps are detailed below. Experiments for LUDVIG are run on a GPU A6000 ADA.

916   number of gradient steps, typically ranging from 3,000 to 30,000 for 3D feature distillation (Kerr
