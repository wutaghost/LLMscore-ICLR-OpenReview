011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Electroencephalography (EEG) is a key non-invasive technique used to investigate
014            brain activity, particularly in motor imagery (MI) research. Traditional methods
015            for classifying EEG signals often rely on handcrafted features and heuristic param-
016            eters, which can limit generalization across tasks and subjects. Recent advances in
017            deep learning, particularly few-shot learning (FSL), offer promising alternatives
018            to improve classification accuracy in scenarios with limited training data. This
019
               study explores the effectiveness of FSL algorithms, including Relation Networks,
               to enhance MI classification. It also examines how transfer learning and data aug-
020
               mentation techniques contribute to improving classification performance.
021
022            We propose a novel framework with three core modules—feature embedding,
023            attention, and relation—that facilitates the classification of unseen subject cate-
024
               gories using only a few labeled samples. The attention mechanism identifies key
               features related to the query data, while the relation module predicts query la-
025
               bels by modeling relationships between support and query data across subjects.
026
               Our experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach on two
027            benchmark datasets, BCI 2a and BCI 2b, as well as our experimental dataset.
028            The proposed FSL framework significantly outperforms traditional methods, of-
029            fering promising applications in real-time Brain-Computer Interface (BCI) sys-
030            tems across various EEG setups. This research advances the understanding of
031            machine learning in EEG applications and highlights the potential of FSL tech-
032            niques in overcoming the challenges of limited training data in MI classification.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037
038   Electroencephalography (EEG) is a non-invasive, cost-effective, and portable technique for measur-
039
      ing the brain’s electrical activity Lashgari et al. (2020). With its high temporal resolution, EEG can
      capture rapid changes in brain activity, making it a valuable tool for studying dynamic brain pro-
040
      cesses such as motor imagery (MI). MI, defined as the mental simulation of motor actions, generates
041
      distinct EEG patterns and is central to Brain-Computer Interface (BCI) research. Motor imagery-
042   based BCIs have been applied in various domains, including rehabilitation, communication, and
043   assistive device control. These systems typically capture EEG signals during MI tasks, extract rele-
044   vant features, and classify them to interpret the user’s intended actions Lashgari et al. (2020; 2021).
045
      Traditionally, feature extraction and classification of EEG signals for MI tasks have been performed
046
      in the time, frequency, and spatial domains. Many of these methods rely on handcrafted features,
047
      which are based on human expertise Altaheri et al. (2023); Lashgari et al. (2020). For example,
048   time-frequency analysis techniques such as short-time Fourier transform or wavelet transforms are
049   commonly used, while filter-bank common spatial patterns (FBCSP) have shown efficacy in the
050   spatial domain Blanco-Dı́az et al. (2024). However, FBCSP uses fixed temporal windows, which
051   may neglect individual variability and fail to leverage the full potential of time-domain information.
052   Furthermore, handcrafted features often require heuristic parameter settings, such as predefined fre-
053   quency bands, which can limit generalizability and reduce classification accuracy across different
      tasks and subjects.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      To overcome these limitations, recent research has focused on using deep learning for automatic
055   feature extraction and classification in EEG MI tasks Lashgari et al. (2021); Kumari et al. (2024).
056   Neural networks can derive relevant features directly from raw EEG data, eliminating the need for
057   manual feature engineering and improving classification performance. However, acquiring large,
058   high-quality training datasets for MI classification remains a challenge. This is due to the sophisti-
059   cated EEG equipment required, the need for controlled, noise-free environments, and the demands
060   placed on participants, which often lead to fatigue and attrition. Moreover, deep learning models
061   typically require large amounts of data to achieve optimal performance, making MI classification
062   with limited data especially challenging.
063   To address these issues, Lashgari et al. (2020) demonstrated that data augmentation (DA) techniques
064   can enhance the classification accuracy of EEG signals. Their study applied various DA methods,
065   including random rotation, translation, scaling, and flipping, to improve model robustness and gener-
066   alization. Building on this, Lashgari et al. (2021) introduced a convolutional neural network (CNN)
067   with an attention mechanism and DA strategies, achieving superior classification accuracy on bench-
068   mark MI datasets (BCI Competition IV 2a and 2b). Their end-to-end framework provided automatic
069
      feature extraction from raw EEG data and interpretable outcomes, surpassing existing methods.
070   While data augmentation can improve performance, it may not fully resolve the challenge of limited
071   data in MI classification. In this context, few-shot learning (FSL) has emerged as a promising
072   alternative, allowing models to generalize from minimal training data An et al. (2020); Ahuja &
073   Sethia (2024). FSL focuses on learning from few labeled examples, typically involving training on a
074   broader set of tasks, with each task having a limited number of labeled instances. During testing, the
075
      model must adapt to new tasks using only a handful of examples. Several studies have highlighted
      the potential of FSL in motor imagery classification, emphasizing the need for further exploration
076
      across various datasets and conditions.
077
078   This paper investigates the efficacy of few-shot learning in the context of motor imagery classifi-
079   cation, comparing it with data augmentation techniques. We introduce a novel framework, termed
080   FSL-MIC (Few-Shot Learning for Motor Imagery Classification), which minimizes the need for ex-
081
      tensive recalibration and allows users to retrain the model with minimal exercises. The proposed
      FSL-MIC architecture leverages domain knowledge to require significantly less training data than
082
      traditional methods while effectively decoding users’ hand movements.
083
084   Our goal is to enable accurate classification of unseen subject categories using limited labeled sam-
085   ples, with paired support and query data from other subjects. The proposed method consists of three
086   interconnected modules: feature embedding, attention, and relation. The feature embedding module
087
      extracts relevant features from the support and query data, the attention module focuses on key fea-
      tures in the support data relevant to the query, and the relation module predicts the label of the query
088
      sample based on the embedding space. By modeling relationships between paired samples across
089
      subjects, our approach aims to generalize well to classify query signals from unseen subjects, even
090   with limited labeled examples.
091
092   The contributions of this paper are as follows:
093
             • Introducing a class of architectures for EEG meta-learning that enables rapid adaptation
094
               based on minimal training observations.
095
096          • Presenting the FSL-MIC framework, which combines temporal convolutions and attention
097            mechanisms to reduce training time and address variability in EEG characteristics.
098
      The paper is organized as follows: Section II reviews relevant literature. Section III describes the
099
      dataset and preprocessing steps used in developing the FSL-MIC framework. Section IV details the
100
      proposed architecture. Section V presents experimental results and evaluation scenarios. Finally,
101   Section VI concludes the paper.

104   2   RELATED WORKS
105
106   Recent advancements in deep learning (DL) have significantly enhanced automatic feature extraction
107   and classification in various domains, including EEG signal analysis Lashgari et al. (2020); Sharma
      & Meena (2024). Consequently, research efforts have focused on neural network (NN) architectures,


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      training procedures, regularization techniques, optimization methods, and hyperparameter tuning,
109   often resulting in substantial improvements in decoding accuracy Sharma & Meena (2024).
110
111   Despite their potential, training deep neural network architectures from scratch poses challenges,
112
      primarily due to the substantial amount of training data required to achieve high classification accu-
      racy. This challenge is particularly pertinent for motor imagery (MI) classification, where acquiring
113
      a large volume of high-quality training samples is difficult Lashgari et al. (2020). Gathering such
114
      data necessitates expert training, sophisticated EEG equipment, and a controlled, noise-free environ-
115   ment. Additionally, MI tasks can be time-consuming and mentally taxing for participants, who must
116   minimize movements and maintain intense concentration. As a result, data collection often requires
117   multiple sessions, which can lead to participant attrition over time. Traditional methods relying on
118   large datasets thus face difficulties in generalizing across subjects and tasks.
119
      To mitigate these limitations, researchers have turned to advanced architectures that leverage data
120
      augmentation (DA) techniques. In 2021, Lashgari et al. proposed an end-to-end convolutional
121   neural network (CNN) incorporating an attention mechanism and various DA techniques Lashgari
122   et al. (2021). Their model was tested on two benchmark MI datasets, the Brain-Computer Interface
123   (BCI) Competition IV 2a and 2b. Additionally, they developed a high-density EEG dataset that
124   included both MI and motor execution (ME) tasks, significantly contributing to the field.
125
      However, while DA improves model robustness, it may not fully address the challenge of limited
126
      training samples per subject. In this context, few-shot learning (FSL) has emerged as a promising
127   solution Fei-Fei et al. (2006). This approach allows models to generalize from only a few labeled
128   examples by learning patterns from other subjects An et al. (2020). For EEG-based MI classi-
129   fication, this is particularly useful as it reduces the dependency on extensive datasets while also
130   addressing the variability between participants. In 2023 An et al. evaluated their proposed method
131   with three different embedding modules on cross-subject and crossdataset classification tasks using
132   brain–computer interface (BCI) competition IV 2a, 2b, and GIST datasets An et al. (2023).
133   Although few-shot learning methods for EEG-based MI classification remain sparse in the literature,
134   some studies have begun to explore its potential. For instance, one study utilized one-shot learning
135   with intracranial electroencephalography (iEEG) signals, employing local binary patterns to extract
136   features and learning prototype vectors in a hyper-dimensional space Burrello et al. (2019). Classifi-
137   cation was performed by measuring distances between these prototype vectors and those from query
138   signals. While this method showed encouraging results, it did not provide an end-to-end learning
139   framework.
140   In another study, researchers proposed a deep neural network with a triplet loss function for clas-
141   sifying various time series data, including ECG recordings Narwariya et al. (2020). This approach
142   highlighted the viability of few-shot learning for signal analysis, although it was limited to learn-
143   ing feature embedding networks that categorized embedded features using a fixed nearest neighbor
144   classifier. A more recent contribution introduced a relation-based few-shot learning method that
145   learns non-linear comparators by capturing end-to-end relationships between support data distribu-
146   tions and query signals, without relying on distance-based calculations between embedding vectors.
147
      This network architecture bore similarities to those used in image classification Sung et al. (2018),
      but it integrated a modified hierarchical spatial convolutional neural network (HS-CNN) suitable for
148
      1D signal analysis and incorporated an attention module to emphasize critical signals in the support
149
      set.
150
151   In this paper, we propose a novel relation-based few-shot learning method that learns end-to-end
152   relationships between support data and query signals, eliminating traditional distance-based calcu-
153
      lations. Our network architecture builds upon recent developments in MI classification Lashgari
      et al. (2021). We evaluate this framework on two benchmark datasets (BCI Competition IV 2a and
154
      2b) as well as a newly collected dataset, aiming to further validate the efficacy of few-shot learning
155
      for EEG-based MI tasks.
156
157   In summary, while few-shot learning for EEG-based MI classification is still in its early stages, the
158   existing literature indicates that this approach holds significant promise for advancing research in
159
      this domain. It offers a powerful alternative to traditional methods, particularly in scenarios with
      limited data and high inter-subject variability.

      The proposed 2-way K-shot learning framework is illustrated in Fig. 1. This framework consists
165   of three key modules: an embedding module, an attention module, and a relation module. In the
166   following sections, we will explain each of these modules in detail.

      Figure 1: Overview of the proposed few-shot relation network. The framework includes two key
      modules: embedding via attention and a relation module. Given support and query data, we obtain
187
      feature vectors concatenated at several stages in our pipeline through an embedding module. These
188
      features are fed to an attention module that focuses on key features related to the query data based
189   on attention scores. The attended features are further processed by a relation module that predicts
190   relation scores from representative vectors and query features, ultimately assigning a label to the
191   given query sample. Based on these predictions, we update the weights of the entire framework in a
192   single training episode.

      3.1   E MBEDDING M ODULE
195
196   The embedding module extracts semantic features from the input EEG signals to facilitate classifi-
197   cation. Convolutional models have been successful in many signal processing applications, as they
198   allow temporally related inputs to be processed together via a sliding-window approach. This results
199   in shared weights, where the same weight kernel is applied across the temporal domain (for a 1D
200   convolutional model over time).
201
      In our architecture (Figure 1), the convolutional layers reduce the number of parameters needed,
202   while enabling the signal to maintain its spatial relations—both across time within each electrode
203   and across electrodes on the head. The signal from each electrode channel is processed through
204   the same convolutional base, producing an output matrix of dimension C × E, where C is the
205   number of electrodes (or channels) and E is the size of the embedding dimension. Consequently,
206   the convolutional layers effectively reduce the input dimension to E, which is 100 times smaller
207   than the original samples.
208
209   3.2   S ELF -ATTENTION M ECHANISM
210
211   In the self-attention part of the network, we first initialize the weights for the Query (Q), Key (K),
212
      and Value (V) matrices Vaswani (2017); Lashgari et al. (2020). The magnitudes of Q, K, and V
      are derived from the input (I) and the corresponding weights. The attention score S is calculated as
213
      follows:

217   function to S to obtain the attention weights W :

      Each input’s value for M is then transformed using the hyperbolic tangent function to produce the

      This attention layer is integrated after the convolutional base (see Figure 1), allowing each electrode
231
      channel to interact with every other channel, resulting in a matrix of scalar values. Summing across
232   rows and normalizing these scalars produces a vector of attention scores. These scores are subse-
233   quently utilized to create a linear combination of all the electrode channel vectors, which is passed
234   to the fully connected layers for classification.
235
      A key feature of our model is its interpretability, which is enhanced by the attention mechanism. The
236
      attention scores for each EEG electrode channel can be visualized, allowing us to identify which
237
      electrodes are most influential in the model’s predictions at different time points. While the full
238   results, including data from multiple subjects, will be presented in our next paper, we have included
239   a representative example from a single subject in this work to illustrate the model’s interpretability.
240
241
      Specifically, in the Supplementary Material, we provide a heatmap of attention scores across various
      electrode channels, where color intensity represents the importance of each electrode for the model’s
242
      decision-making process. Brighter colors correspond to higher attention, highlighting the electrodes
243
      that contribute most to the classification task at each time step. This visualization offers valuable
244   insights into the spatial distribution of attention across electrodes and demonstrates how the model
245   prioritizes task-relevant features, such as specific brainwave patterns, over time.
246
247
      By focusing on a single subject for this demonstration, we aim to provide an initial insight into
      the attention mechanism’s interpretability, while recognizing that broader results involving multiple
248
      subjects will be presented in subsequent work.
249
250   While techniques like Grad-CAM (Gradient-weighted Class Activation Mapping) can visualize the
251   model’s focus on specific input regions, we prioritize the attention mechanism to improve prediction
252   accuracy rather than focusing solely on interpretability Selvaraju et al. (2017); Li et al. (2020). This
253
      allows the CNN, in conjunction with the attention mechanism, to better classify EEG signals by em-
      phasizing critical, task-relevant features across different time steps via sliding windows. Ultimately,
254
      the attention mechanism both enhances the model’s prediction performance and provides a clear,
255
      interpretable view of which features are being prioritized in the decision-making process.
256
257   The embedding module takes pairs of k support and query data as input, extracting semantic fea-
258   tures for classification using convolutional layers. The self-attention mechanism further allows the
259
      model to weigh the importance of different input elements, which is particularly beneficial when
      the relationships between inputs, such as features from different electrodes, are crucial for model
260
      performance.
261

      3.3   R ELATION M ODULE
263
264   In few-shot learning, the relation module is essential for comparing support samples with query
265   samples to generate predictions. Rather than relying on traditional distance metrics, our approach
266   utilizes a combination of convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and fully connected (FC) layers to
267   assess the relationships between the embeddings of support and query samples. The CNN extracts
268   rich feature representations, which are then processed through FC layers to learn non-linear relation-
269   ships. This enables our model to effectively identify relevant support samples for each query without
      fixed distance calculations. By emphasizing learned relationships, our relation module enhances the


                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      model’s ability to generalize from limited examples, thereby improving classification performance
271   in scenarios with scarce labeled data.
272
273   The relation module computes relation scores based on the class-representative vectors and query
274
      features. The class corresponding to the highest relation score is assigned as the predicted label for
      the query. During training, the network is optimized in an end-to-end manner using pairs of support
275
      sets and query signals from subjects in the training data. For testing, the label of a query signal is
276
      predicted using k labeled support signals from an unseen subject. Although the illustration depicts
277   a 2-way K-shot learning scenario, our proposed method can be easily extended to N-way K-shot
278   learning configurations.
279
280
      To estimate the label from the concatenated features of each support and query feature in the channel
      direction, we employ two convolutional layers with kernel sizes of 30 × 1 and 15 × 1, followed by
281
      a global average pooling layer and two fully connected layers with dimensions 256 and 100 (Figure
282
      1). For training, we utilize a focal loss function defined as follows:
283
284
                                                   X
285                                     Loss = −        α(1 − pi )γ yi log(pi )
286                                                 i
287
288   where yi is 1 if the class of the supporting data matches that of the query data, and 0 otherwise. The
289   parameters α and γ control the balance between classes and the focusing effect, respectively.
290
291
      We optimize the model parameters using the Adam optimizer, with a batch size set to 164 and an
      initial learning rate of 10−4 . The learning rate decays exponentially at a rate of 0.033% with each
292
      iteration, and the model is saved when the validation loss reaches its minimum.

      We used three datasets in this study: (1) A dataset that we collected ourselves, (2) the BCI 2a dataset,

303
      4.1.1    O UR DATASET
304
      Seven healthy volunteers (3 male and 4 female), all right-handed and aged between 23 and 30 years
305
      (mean age: 28), participated in the study, providing written informed consent prior to the experiment.
306   Participants were seated in a chair positioned 80 cm from an LCD screen, with both hands resting
307   on a table while holding a tennis ball in each hand. They were instructed to remain relaxed and
308   minimize movement and eye blinks throughout the sessions. When required to respond, participants
309   were to squeeze the tennis ball while avoiding any tension in their arms or shoulders.
310
      Each experimental session included both motor execution (ME) and motor imagery (MI) tasks (see
311
      Figure 2) and was repeated twice, resulting in a total of four sessions; however, this study focuses
312   exclusively on the MI sessions. Each session lasted between 30 to 40 minutes, with breaks of 10 to
313   15 minutes in between to help reduce fatigue. The entire experiment, including setup, was designed
314   to be completed in under 3 hours. EEG data were recorded at a sampling rate of 250 Hz using 64
315   active electrodes (BrainVision actiCHamp) arranged according to the 10/20 montage. Additionally,
316   bipolar electromyography (EMG) electrodes were placed on the brachioradialis of both arms to
317   monitor any movement during the MI tasks.
318   During the MI sessions, participants were instructed to imagine the repetitive hand movements out-
319   lined in session 1 while fixating on a cross displayed on the screen. A total of 100 trials for both left-
320   and right-hand imagery were collected in each session, with all other task aspects mirroring those
321   of session 1. This setup allowed for the assessment of motor-related EEG oscillations and ensured
322   at least minimal voluntary control over these oscillations. In total, we collected 200 trials of ME
323   and 200 trials of MI for each participant. The underlying data for this study have been uploaded to
      Figshare and are available at the following link: [xxx].

      4.2     E XPERIMENTAL S ETTING
379
380
      The performance of various CNN-based attention models was evaluated across three distinct
381   datasets: BCI 2a, BCI 2b, and an experimental dataset. Each dataset comprises different chan-
382   nel configurations—22 channels for BCI 2a, 3 channels for BCI 2b with feedback, and 64 channels
383   for the experimental dataset. The evaluation metrics include standard accuracy and DA accuracy,
384   reported with means and standard deviations.
385
      For our experiments, we employed 9-fold and 7-fold cross-validation, training the model with data
386
      samples from 8 or 7 subjects and testing on the remaining subjects for each validation episode. Each
387   training set included all experiments from a subject except the last one, which served as the vali-
388   dation set. The test set comprised the complete dataset of the remaining subjects. During training,
389   support and query data samples were randomly selected from both the training and validation sets
390   at each iteration. For testing, we divided the samples from each class into two groups: 20 samples
391   designated as support and the rest as query data.
392
      To evaluate the performance of our proposed model (RelationNet-attention), we assessed the classi-
393   fication accuracy for K-shot classification models with K = {1, 5, 10, 20}. For K = {1, 5, 10, 20}
394   experiments, K support samples were randomly chosen from the 20 support samples.
395
396
      Additionally, we compared our method against two supervised learning models: (1) CNN-attention-
      Few, trained with only 40 samples (20 for left and 20 for right) in the support set of the testing
397
      subjects, and (2) CNN-attention-All, trained with all training samples from all subjects except one.
398
      Each experiment was repeated 10 times, and the average accuracy was calculated for all cases,
399   accounting for potential variations in support sets.

      results suggest that increasing the shot count enhances the model’s learning capacity, resulting in

476   In this study, we propose a two-way few-shot classification network designed to effectively classify
477
      EEG-based motor imagery (MI) data from unseen subjects. Our results indicate that, although BCI
      2b is utilized in the referenced study An et al. (2020), our model outperforms it, achieving superior
478
      accuracy across all three datasets. Specifically, the CNN-attention-All model consistently demon-
479
      strated enhanced performance in terms of both overall accuracy and DA accuracy. The presence of
480   neurofeedback in BCI 2b simplified the classification process, allowing our model to achieve high
481   accuracy with only three channels, despite the limited number of input channels. Conversely, the
482   absence of neurofeedback in BCI 2a increased classification difficulty, even with 22 channels avail-
483   able, highlighting the additional challenge of learning from raw signals without real-time guidance.
484   Our approach differs from that of An et al. (2023) in two key ways: we utilize dataset-specific
485   data augmentation (DA) techniques based on the findings of Lashgari et al., rather than focusing on
      fine-tuning (FT), and we employ a tailored dataset split strategy that better aligns with the character-
