009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In this study, we address the challenge of computationally expensive simulations
012            of complex geometries, which are crucial for modern engineering design pro-
013            cesses. While neural network-based flow field predictions have been suggested,
014            prior studies generally exclude complex geometries. Our objective is to enhance
015            flow predictions around complex geometries, which may often be deconstructed
016            into multiple single, simple bodies, by leveraging existing data on these simple ge-
017            ometry flow fields. Using a case study of tandem-airfoils, we introduce a method
018
               employing the directional integrated distance representation for multiple objects, a
               residual pre-training scheme based on the freestream condition as a physical prior,
019
               and a residual training scheme utilising smooth combinations of single airfoil flow
020
               fields, also capitalising on the freestream condition. To optimise memory usage
021            during training in large domains and improve prediction performance, we decom-
022            pose simulation domains into smaller sub-domains, each processed by a different
023            network. Extensive experiments on four new tandem-airfoil datasets, comprising
024            over 2000 fluid simulations, demonstrate that our proposed method and techniques
025            effectively enhance tandem-airfoil prediction accuracy by up to 96%.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   In engineering design processes, simulating flow around geometries via computational fluid dynam-
031   ics (CFD) is a crucial step in modelling components. The study of partial differential equations
032   (PDE) involving intricate and multiple geometries holds significant importance in various scientific
033
      and engineering applications, such as high-lift aircraft wings and wind farm wake interactions (Rum-
      sey & Ying, 2002; Deskos et al., 2020). Analytical solutions are elusive due to the complexity of
034
      these problems. Constructing complex geometries by combining simpler shapes, though feasible,
035
      escalates simulation domains and resolution (Spalart & Venkatakrishnan, 2016), posing a challenge
036   in terms of time and computational costs.
037
038   Previous efforts to replace costly simulations with neural networks have often treated each com-
039
      ponent independently and focused on simpler geometries scenarios with more abundant data (Pfaff
      et al., 2021; Lino et al., 2021; Fortunato et al., 2022; Cao et al., 2023). However, industries like
040
      aerospace and marine already simulate simple geometries like airfoils and hydrofoils extensively,
041
      offering an opportunity to exploit this data to efficiently predict flow fields for complex geometries.
042   This paper introduces a novel method of leveraging extensive datasets from simple-geometry cases
043   for more time-efficient predictions of flow fields in complex-geometry scenarios. The study focuses
044   on tandem-airfoil, or a sequential arrangement of airfoils, chosen for its widespread application in
045   various engineering domains including compressor blades in turbomachinery (McGlumphy et al.,
046   2007), unmanned aerial vehicles (Yin et al., 2021; Okulski & Ławryńczuk, 2022), hydrofoil systems
047   for maritime vessels (Maram et al., 2021), and race car engineering (Azmi et al., 2017).
048   The proposed method leverages the freestream condition as a physics prior (Ferm, 1990) to facilitate
049   residual pre-training and smooth-combining of single airfoil predictions. Residual training, based
050   on smoothly combined single airfoil flow fields, is then employed to improve tandem-airfoil predic-
051   tions. To enhance performance and reduce GPU memory requirements, the simulation domain was
052   decomposed, and different networks are trained for each sub-domain, allowing focus on specific flow
053   regions. The directional integrated distance (DID), initially designed for single objects, is adapted to
      handle multiple objects effectively, addressing the numerical complexity that arises with increasing

      object count. This work explores a novel problem setting—the use of simple geometry flow fields to
055   improve predictions for complex geometries—through four new tandem-airfoil datasets with over
056   2000 CFD simulations1 . Our contributions include:
057
058               • First study to use flow fields of simple geometries to improve predictions of complex ge-

061               • Novel use of freestream conditions as a physics prior for residual pre-training and smooth-

105   accelerate/decelerate the flow, diverting the latter to move around the bodies. Further away from
106   the bodies, the flow will converge towards the so-called farfield boundary condition, essentially an

      2.2   R ELATED W ORK
121
122   Previous neural network (NN)-based CFD strategies in fluid simulation have adapted in various
123   ways. Graph neural networks (GNNs) have emerged as a primary method, demonstrating superior
124   performance to MLPs and CNNs (Ogoke et al., 2020; Bonnet et al., 2022; Pfaff et al., 2021). Chen
125   et al. (2021) introduced utilising both node and edge features, which are more suitable for CFD sim-
126   ulations and exhibited improved performance over basic GU-Net with vanilla graph convolutions,
127   such as that used by Bonnet et al. (2022). Architectures employing encoder-processor-decoder, with
128
      similar graph convolutions, were adopted by Sanchez-Gonzalez et al. (2020) and Pfaff et al. (2021).
      Further enhancements through multi-scalability were introduced by Fortunato et al. (2022), Lino
129
      et al. (2021), Cao et al. (2023), and Gladstone et al. (2024), with the latter three achieving faster
130
      information propagation with a GU-Net style processor. These have set the trend for NN architec-
131   ture and convolution design in CFD prediction, but they placed little focus on developing training
132   techniques that leverage existing data or physical priors.
133
134
      On the other hand, physical priors have been utilised through varying means, but still typically
      focused on flows with simpler geometries. Raissi et al. (2019) integrated physical equations into
135
      the training loss of NNs, later applied to Graph convolutional networks (GCNs) by Würth et al.
136
      (2024). This method reduces reliance on training data, but were shown on scenarios that involved no
137   internal geometries. Some incorporate the use of physics-based solvers directly into the model, such
138   as Obiols-Sales et al. (2020) to bring the network output to convergence, or de Avila Belbute-Peres
139   et al. (2020) to physically simulate flow on a coarser mesh. Additionally, the work of Pfaff et al.
140   (2021) was coupled with physics-inspired features and loss terms by Libao et al. (2023). However,
141   none of these have considered cases involving the interaction of flows from two objects in tandem.
142   Finally, Kochkov et al. (2021) further enhanced the concept of using the coarse solution in a method
143   they referred to as “learned-correction”, which capitalises on cost-effective data as the basis for
144   estimation. They used a CNN to correct the errors in very low-resolution simulation results. More
145
      recently, Loh et al. (2024) exploited residual learning, an approach which had been extensively
      applied to image super-resolution (Zhang et al., 2018; Yang et al., 2019), in GCNs to enhance the
146
      training process and guide the model to concentrate on more intricate aspects of the prediction.
147
      However, it still required coarse physical simulations. A more recent work by Mao et al. (2024)
148   has addressed domain decomposition for grid-based simulations using fixed-size subdomains and
149   CNN-based architectures, but these approaches do not directly address mesh-based CFD scenarios
150   involving complex geometries.
151
      Hence, previous methods focused on balancing cost-accuracy trade-offs, but with a dearth of meth-
152
      ods for complex geometries. Available public datasets, such as those provided by Bartoldson et al.
153
      (2023), are relatively few and are not appropriate for studying flows around multiple shapes in tan-
154   dem by leveraging simpler geometry data. Reducing data dependence for complex geometries by
155   such a method remains an area unexplored.

478
      This paper introduces a novel smooth-combining technique for DID representation in multi-object
479
      fields, a pre-training and residual training procedure based on freestream and combined fields, and
480   a multi-NN inference scheme. We also provide four tandem-airfoil datasets for future research on
481   complex geometries. Our methods were rigorously evaluated on two SOTA benchmarks, demon-
482   strating their effectiveness. Overall, our research offers a pioneering methodology that enhances pre-
483   dictive modelling in complex flow fields, providing a more robust and efficient approach with broad
484   implications for CFD and engineering design. This study centres on tandem-airfoil as a complex
485   geometry. Future work should further explore the method’s generalisation capabilities in scenarios
      like multi-airfoil cases in turbomachinery stages, bluff body configurations, or 3D cases.

      A    DATABASE G ENERATION
649
650
      Mesh Generation Various meshing methods were employed to cater to different simulation re-
651
      quirements. For simulations involving single airfoil geometries, the blockMesh utility tool provided
652   by OpenFOAM-v2112 (Jasak et al., 2007) was utilised to create a C-grid type of hexahedral mesh,
653   encompassing a domain size of 20 chord lengths. In contrast, overset meshing (Benek et al., 1986)
654   was adopted for the simulations involving double airfoil configurations, offering flexibility in ma-
655   nipulating the orientation of the airfoils. The overset mesh consists of a background mesh and one or
656   more component meshes. In our work, the background mesh, which consists of the structure grids,
657   was generated using blockMesh with a domain size of 20×24 chord-length units. On the other hand,
658   the component meshes, which represent the near-field airfoil meshes, were created using Pointwise
659   software (Karman & Wyman, 2019). To ensure accurate resolution of the boundary layer without
660
      relying on wall functions, the thickness of the first cell adjacent to the airfoil wall was set to 1 mm,
      ensuring y + << 1. Consequently, the single airfoil meshes consist of approximately 1.2 × 105
661
      cells, while the double airfoil overset meshes comprise a total of approximately 2.7 × 105 cells, with
662
      2.4 × 105 cells in the background mesh and roughly 1.5 × 104 cells in each of the airfoil meshes.
663   These refined meshes enable comprehensive and accurate simulations of the flow behaviour around
664   single and double airfoil configurations, facilitating detailed analysis of their interactions.
665
666   Dataset Generation This study considers an extra low Reynolds number (Re) scenario, wherein
667   the interaction between two airfoils results in unsteadiness in the flow fields. The ability to predict
668   such unsteadiness in the flow holds significant potential for reducing computational requirements
669   while achieving reliable field initialisation. To this end, two angles of attack (AOA), namely 0◦ and
670   5◦ , are considered. For each AOA, the datasets consist of 1014 single airfoil configurations and
671   corresponding 784 tandem airfoil configurations each with and without ground effect, comprising a
672   diverse combination of different airfoil shapes, ranging from symmetric to asymmetric and thin to
673   thick airfoils. While the inflow conditions remain fixed, variations in geometry shapes are introduced
674
      to examine flow interactions between different geometries.
675   The simulations are conducted at a Reynolds number of Re = 500 using steady-state RANS
676   solvers. For single airfoil simulations, the simpleFoam solver is employed, while the overSim-
677   pleFoam solver is utilised for tandem airfoil configurations. Both solvers employ a semi-implicit
678   method for pressure-linked equations (SIMPLE) algorithm (Caretto et al., 1973), coupled with two
679   transport equations from the k-ω SST turbulence model (Menter et al., 2003). Convergence is at-
680
      tained by iteratively solving for U , p, k and ω fields until the prescribed convergence criteria are
      met. The computational resources for this investigation involve the utilisation of 64 CPU cores of
681
      the AMD EPYCTM 7713 system, accessible through the National Supercomputing Centre, Singapore
682
      (https://www.nscc.sg).
683
684   The dataset generation was inspired by other cases with tandem airfoils from Fanjoy et al. (1997);
685   Yin et al. (2021); Hosseini et al. (2022), and cases with ground effect from Mokhtar (2005); Qu et al.
686
      (2015); Grabis & Agarwal (2019). With a total of 4380 cases, including 2352 tandem airfoil cases,
      this is, to the best knowledge of the authors, the largest public dataset containing the full flow fields
687
      around tandem shapes for machine learning purposes. For experiments, the training set, validation
688
      set, and test set were distinct, uniformly sampled sets of 80%, 10% and 10% of the whole. However,
689   for extrapolation experiments, the data reflecting the top and bottom 5% of either the AOA or Re
690   range was used as the test set. The training and validation sets were uniformly sampled sets of a 8:1
691   ratio of the remaining middle 90% range of data.

830   In this section, we will discuss the challenges of calculating the DID as done in the original work and
831   present the justification for using the smooth-combine method to estimate it instead. As mentioned
832   previously, calculating the DID in a multiple-object scenario using Alg. 1 posed certain challenges
833
      that were highlighted in red. These challenges are illustrated in Figs. 7 and 8.
834   The first challenge is in determining whether the point on the object boundary k is obstructed from
835   the point of reference i. As shown in Fig. 7(a), in a single object scenario, it suffices to ascertain
836   that either boundary face adjacent to k is on the side of the object that faces i. However, as seen in
837   Fig. 7(b), there is the possibility that k is obstructed from i by the boundary faces of another object.
838   Determining obstruction is a process that increases in complexity with the addition of every object.

      G    F EASIBILITY OF I NDIVIDUAL DID OF E ACH O BJECT
947
948   Computing a single DID for both objects simultaneously is primarily a practical decision aimed at
949
      improving efficiency and scalability. While Alg. 1 can technically compute a single DID for multiple
      objects simultaneously, its numerical complexity increases significantly with each additional object,
950
      resulting in slower training and inference speeds. For instance, Alg. 1 required 5222 seconds to
951
      compute the DID for tandem airfoils, whereas Alg. 2, which computes separate DIDs for individual
952   airfoils and then combines them, completed the task in only 3.5 seconds.
953
954   Additionally, calculating separate DIDs for each object would increase the input size proportionally
955
      to the number of objects. If the dimension of a single object’s DID is N and there are M objects,
      the total input size would scale as N × M , leading to higher memory requirements and computa-
956
      tional load on GPUs. By combining the DIDs into a single representation, our approach maintains
957
      scalability and significantly reduces computational overhead. Algorithm 2 strikes an effective bal-
958   ance, allowing for efficient handling of multi-object scenarios without sacrificing performance, as
959   discussed in Appendix E.
960
