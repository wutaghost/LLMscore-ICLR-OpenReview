009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Spurious correlations in training data significantly hinder the generalization capa-
012            bility of machine learning models when faced with distribution shifts in real-world
013            scenarios. To tackle the problem, numerous debiasing approaches have been pro-
014            posed and benchmarked on datasets intentionally designed with severe biases.
015            However, it remains to be asked: 1. Do existing benchmarks really capture biases
016
               in the real world? 2. Can existing debiasing methods handle biases in the real
               world? To answer the questions, we revisit biased distributions in existing bench-
017
               marks and real-world datasets, and propose a fine-grained framework for analyzing
018
               dataset bias by disentangling it into the magnitude and prevalence of bias. We
019            observe and theoretically demonstrate that existing benchmarks poorly represent
020            real-world biases. We further introduce two novel biased distributions to bridge
021            this gap, forming a systematic evaluation framework for real-world debiasing. With
022            the evaluation framework, we focus on the practical setting of debiasing w/o bias
023            supervision and find existing methods incapable of handling real-world biases.
024            Through in-depth analysis, we propose a simple yet effective approach that can be
025            easily applied to existing debiasing methods, named Debias in Destruction (DiD).
026            Empirical results on real-world datasets in both image and language modalities
027
               demonstrate the superiority of DiD, improving the performance of existing methods
               on all types of biases within the proposed evaluation framework.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   With the rapid development of machine learning, machine learning systems are increasingly deployed
033   in high-stakes applications such as autonomous driving (Janai et al., 2021) and medical diagnosis
034   (Ibrahim & Abdulazeez, 2021), where incorrect decisions may cause severe consequences. As a
035   result, the robustness to distribution shift is crucial in building trustworthy machine learning systems.
036   One of the major reasons why machine learning models fail to generalize to shifted distributions in
037   the real world (Koh et al., 2021; Chu et al., 2023; Xue et al., 2023) is because the existence of spurious
038   correlation in training data (Wiles et al., 2022). Spurious correlation refers to the phenomenon that
039
      two distinct concepts are statistically correlated in the training distribution, yet uncorrelated in the
      test distribution for there is no causal relationship between them (Yao et al., 2021; Chu & Li, 2023).
040
      For example, rock wall background may be correlated with the sport climbing in the training data,
041
      but they are not causally related and climbing can be indoors or on ice as well (Lee et al., 2021; Chu
042   et al., 2021; 2020). Furthermore, such spurious correlations within the data tend to be captured during
043   training (Nam et al., 2020), resulting in a biased model that fails to generalize to shifted distributions.
044   In this work, we refer to spurious correlation and bias in datasets interchangeably.
045
      To tackle the problem, various debiasing methods (Bahng et al., 2020; Nam et al., 2020; Kim et al.,
046
      2021; Lee et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2021; Lim et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023) have
047
      been proposed in recent years. And the effectiveness of the methods is benchmarked with synthetic
048   (Reddy et al., 2021; Nam et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2021) and semi-synthetic (Lee et al., 2021; Nam
049   et al., 2020; Lim et al., 2023) (referred as ”real-world dataset” in previous works) datasets designed
050   to be severely biased. However, while these benchmarks are indeed biased, they are rough and lack
051   thorough consideration of how data is truly biased in the real world. This raises two questions:
052
053            1. Do existing benchmarks really capture biases in the real world?
               2. Can existing debiasing methods handle biases in the real world?

054
      To answer the first question, we revisit the biased distribution in existing benchmarks and real-world
055   datasets, and propose a fine-grained framework for dataset bias analysis. Inspired by the framework
056   proposed by Wiles et al. (2022), which assumes the data is composed of some set of attributes, we
057   further claim that analysis of dataset bias should be conducted on the more fine-grained feature
058   (or value) level rather than attribute level, according to our observation on real-world biases. From
059   the claim, we further propose our fine-grained framework that disentangles dataset bias into the
060   magnitude of bias and the prevalence of bias, where the magnitude of bias generally measures how
061   predictive (or biased) features are on the target task and the prevalence of bias generally measures
062   how many samples in the data contain any biased feature. With our framework, we observe that the
063
      magnitude and prevalence of real-world biases are both low, in contrast with high magnitude and
      high prevalence biases in existing benchmarks. In section 3, we theoretically show that two strong
064
      assumptions are implicitly held by existing high bias prevalence benchmarks, which further validates
065
      our observation that real-world biases are low in bias prevalence.
066
067   As for the second question, due to the complexity of real-world biases, debiasing methods should
068   be capable of handling various types of biased distributions rather than solely on the narrowed
069
      distribution in existing benchmarks. Thus, we introduce two new biased distributions inspired by
      real-world applications as a complement to the biased distribution in existing benchmarks, forming
070
      a systematic evaluation framework for real-world debiasing. We focus on debiasing methods w/o
071
      bias supervision(Nam et al., 2020; Lee et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2022; Lim et al.,
072   2023; Zhao et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2023), which is more practical as bias feature is expensive to
073   annotate and sometimes even hard to notice (Li & Xu, 2021). Those methods generally involves a
074   biased auxiliary model to capture the bias, along with techniques to learn a debiased model with the
075   captured bias. (See the related work section in Appendix F for details) We refer to such paradigm as
076   debiasing with biased auxiliary model (DBAM). Our empirical results show that existing methods
077   fail to handle real-world biases under various biased distributions.
078   We further conducted an in-depth analysis of the DBAM paradigm and found that the effectiveness
079   of existing methods is reliant on the high bias prevalence of existing benchmarks and thus fails to
080   handle real-world datasets with low bias prevalence. Finally, based on our analysis, we introduce a
081   simple yet effective enhancement to the DBAM paradigm. Experiments on real-world datasets in
082   both image and language modalities show that our approach significantly boosts the capability of
083   existing methods in handling real-world biases, improving their performances on both high and low
084   bias prevalence datasets. To sum up, this work makes the following contributions:
085
086
             • Fine-grained analysis and evaluation framework. We propose a fine-grained framework
               for analyzing bias in datasets. Based on the framework we further introduce a systematic
087
               evaluation framework inspired by real-world applications for real-world debiasing.
088
089          • Theoretical insight. We derived the hidden strong assumptions held by existing benchmarks,
090
               and testify our observation that real-world biases are low in bias prevalence.
091          • Principled approach. A principled approach is proposed based on our analysis, which can
092            be easily applied to not only the DBAM paradigm but bias detection methods as well.
093          • Empirical validation. Extensive empirical results on multiple real-world datasets, distribu-
094            tions, and modalities not only validate our analysis but also demonstrate the superiority of
095            the proposed approach.

      2.2   P REVIOUS MEASURES OF SPURIOUS CORRELATION
148
      We first revisit measures of spurious correlation in previous works, then point out their insufficiency.
149
150
      Background. We assume a joint distribution of attributes y 1 , y 2 , ..., y K with y k ∈ Ak where Ak is
151   a finite set. One of these K attributes is the target of learning, denoted as y t , and a spurious attribute
152   y s with t ̸= s. The definition of spurious correlation or the measure of bias magnitude is rather vague
153   or flawed in previous works. We summarize the measures in previous works into three categories.
154
155   Target attribute conditioned probability. Previous works (Wang & Russakovsky, 2023; Reddy
156   et al., 2021) measure spurious correlation according to the probability of a biased feature as within the
157   correlated class at : Corrtcp = P (y s = as |y t = at ). A higher value indicates a strong correlation.
158
159   Spurious attribute conditioned probability. Some (Tang et al., 2021; Lee et al., 2021; Yenamandra
160   et al., 2023; Hermann et al., 2024) measure spurious correlation according to the probability of the
161   correlated class at within samples with biased feature as : Corrscp = P (y t = at |y s = as ). A higher
      value of the measure indicates a strong correlation.


                                                           3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Spurious attribute conditioned entropy. Nam et al. (2020) defined an entropy-based measure
163   of bias. They use conditional entropy to measure how skewed the conditioned distribution is:
164   Corrsce = H(y t |y s ), where H is entropy. Values close to 0 indicate a strong correlation. This is an
165   attribute-level measure, yet it is based on information theory.
166
      We then point out the following requirements a proper measure of spurious correlation should satisfy.
167
168   Spurious correlation should be measured at the feature level. As shown in Figure 1(a) and 1(c), the
169   predictivity of every value in the spurious attribute is similar in existing benchmarks. However, this
170   is not the case for real-world datasets, where it is clear that the predictivity of values in the spurious
171   attribute varies greatly, as shown in Figure 1(b) and 1(d). Therefore, to deal with real-world biases,
172
      analysis of bias should be conducted on a more fine-grained value level, i.e. feature level, rather
      than attribute level in previous works (Nam et al., 2020). Note that though Corrtcp and Corrscp are
173
      defined at the feature level, it is assumed by previous works (Lee et al., 2021; Reddy et al., 2021;
174
      Hermann et al., 2024) that it is consistent cross features in spurious attribute during benchmark
175   construction, i.e. viewed as an attribute level measure.
176
177   The spurious attribute rather than the target attribute should be given as a condition. It is well
178
      recognized that the spurious attribute should be easier than the target attribute for the model to learn
      (Nam et al., 2020; Hermann et al., 2024). Thus the spurious attribute should be more available to
179
      the model when learning its decision rules (Hermann et al., 2024) and given as a condition when we
180
      define spurious correlation.
181
182   The marginal distribution of the target attribute should be accounted for. In Corrtcp and Corrscp
183   measure of spurious correlation, the marginal distribution of the target attribute is not taken into
184
      account. This is inaccurate for even if the spurious and the target attribute are statistically independent,
      the value of Corrtcp and Corrscp could be high if the marginal distribution of spurious and target
185
      attribute is highly skewed, e.g. long-tail distributed (Zhang et al., 2021; 2023b).
186
187   Diverge rather than predictivity should be used. While Corrsce satisfies the above requirements, it
188   measures the entropy difference between the conditional and marginal distribution of the target at-
189   tribute, i.e. the predictivity difference. This is still inaccurate for when the entropy of the distributions
190
      is the same, the conditional distribution could still be highly diverged from the marginal distribution,
      thus highly correlated with the spurious attribute. However, using divergence of the distributions
191
      accurately measures how the given condition affects the distribution shift of the target attribute.

      2.5   S YSTEMATIC EVALUATION FRAMEWORK FOR REAL - WORLD DEBIASING
244
245   Based on our analysis, we further introduce two novel bias distributions inspired from real-world
246   applications. Together with high magnitude high prevalence (HMHP) distribution in existing bench-
247   marks (Nam et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2021; Lee et al., 2021; Lim et al., 2023), we form a systematic
248   evaluation framework for real-world debiasing.
249
250   Low Magnitude Low Prevalence (LMLP) Bias. Inspired by the distribution of the COMPAS
251   (Mattu) dataset shown in Figure 2(a), bias in the real world might be low in both magnitude and
252   prevalence. To take it even further, we should not even assume the dataset is biased at all when
253   applying debiasing methods, because we usually lack such information in practice. Thus, unbiased
254
      data distribution can be considered as a special case of the distribution.
255
256
      High Magnitude Low Prevalence (HMLP) Bias. As shown in Figure 2, the COCO (Lin et al.,
      2015) dataset may contain features with relatively high bias magnitude, yet low bias prevalence in
257
      the dataset due to the sparsity of the biased feature, i.e. low feature prevalence.
258
259   Note that datasets with low magnitude high prevalence (LMHP) bias do not exist due to the fact that
260   high bias magnitude is the premise of high bias prevalence. Also, the bias distributions proposed are
261   applicable to existing synthetic and semi-synthetic datasets mentioned in section 2. More details on
262
      how biased distributions are defined and how to synthesize datasets with given distributions can be
      found in Appendix B. In section 5, We further examine existing methods and our proposed method
263
      under real-world biases with the evaluation framework.

349   4.2   R ELIANCE OF DBAM METHODS ON HIGH BIAS PREVALENCE
350
351   We claim that the bias capture module of the DBAM paradigm relies on the high bias magnitude
352   of existing benchmarks, which causes failure in real-world debiasing. It is assumed by the DBAM
353   paradigm that the biased model Mb predicts according to the bias within the training data, giving high
354   loss to BC samples and low loss to BA samples (Nam et al., 2020; Lee et al., 2021; Kim et al., 2022;
355
      Lim et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2023). Existing works attribute this loss difference
      to the fact that spurious attributes are easier (Nam et al., 2020; Lim et al., 2023), i.e. learned more
356
      preferentially by models, making BA the easy sample. While such a claim is true, we claim that the
357
      dominance of BA samples in the HP datasets is another vital causing factor of the loss difference, for
358   dominant/major samples are learned more frequently than others, as shown in Figure 3(a).
359
360   However, on LP datasets, while BA samples are still easier to learn due to the biased feature, the
361
      dominant/major samples in the training data are no longer BA samples, but rather BN samples. This
      not only results in the loss difference between BA and BC samples decreasing but also causes low
362
      loss on BN samples, as shown in Figure 3(a). According to sample weighing scheme 4, such low loss
363
      on BN samples further leads to low weights for BN samples when training the debiased model, which
364   is unintended because BN samples carry an abundant amount of knowledge concerning the target
365   attribute without the interference of the spurious features. The overlooking of BN samples results in
366   server utility degradation when DBAM methods are applied to LP datasets. We further empirically
367   testify our claim in section 5.
368
369   4.3   B IAS CAPTURE WITH FEATURE DESTRUCTION
370
371   Based on our analysis in section 4.2, we introduce a minor yet effective enhancement to the bias
372   capture module in the existing DBAM framework. We name the refined framework as Debias in
373   Destruction (DiD). As shown in Figure 3(b), the problem with the existing bias capture method comes
374
      from the side branch learning on BN samples of the biased auxiliary model, which not only captures
      the bias but also learns the target feature. This is undesired for this further causes the overlooking of
375
      BN samples when training the debiased model, as discussed in section 4.2.
376
377   To prune the side branch learning of the target features, it is intuitive to destroy the target feature
      and make them unlearnable when training the biased model, as shown in Figure 3(b). Such action is


                                                                      7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      practical because the target features we intend to learn are usually clear, and no information on the
379   biased feature is required. Specifically, we can achieve this by applying target feature destructive data
380   transformation when training the biased model:
381
382                                      Lossb = GCE(Mb (Tf d (x)), y)
383   where Tf d (·) is the feature destruction transformation. As an example, in visual recognition tasks,
384   the shape of objects is a basic element of human visual perception (Geirhos et al., 2019). Therefore,
385   the patch-shuffle destruction of shape (Lee et al., 2024) when capturing bias from visual recognition
386   datasets is a feasible approach. As for NLP tasks, we adopt a word shuffling approach which we will
387   elaborate on in Appendix D.5.

398   5.1   E XPERIMENTAL SETTINGS
399
400   Metrics. Following previous works (Nam et al., 2020; Lee et al., 2021; 2023), we adopt the
401   accuracy of BC samples (BC), the average accuracy on the balanced test set (Avg), and the worst
402   group accuracy (Worst Acc.) as the evaluation metrics. We note that DiD is not a self-contained
403   method but rather a plug-in module for existing debiasing methods to improve from their original
404
      performances. Thus, the effectiveness of DiD should be measured as the performance gain from the
      base method for debiasing.
405
406
      Datasets. We adopt 8 datasets in various modalities for evaluation. Specifically, we adopt the basic
407
      setting of Colored MNIST (Reddy et al., 2021) and Corrupted CIFAR10 (Nam et al., 2020) to
408
      implement the distributions within the proposed systematic evaluation framework. We also evaluated
409   our method on more existing synthetic benchmarks who is more complex in terms of the target and
410   spurious feature: BAR (Nam et al., 2020), NICO (Kim et al., 2022), and WaterBirds (Sagawa*
411   et al., 2020). We also adopt 2 real-world NLP datasets MultiNLI (Williams et al., 2018) and
412   CivilComments-WILDS (Koh et al., 2021), and 1 real-world image dataset CelebA (Liu et al.,
413   2015).
414
415   Baselines. We adopt 7 baselines, covering classic and recently proposed DBAM methods. ERM
416   directly applies standard training on the biased datasets. LfF (Nam et al., 2020) is the first work in
417   the DBAM paradigm. DisEnt (Lee et al., 2021) disentangles bias and intrinsic features and applies
418   feature augmentation when training the debiased model. BE LfF and BE DisEnt were recently
419   proposed by Lee et al. (2023), and is based on LfF and DisEnt, respectively. JTT (Liu et al., 2021) is
420
      a classic DBAM method adapted to both the image and NLP domain. Group DRO (Sagawa* et al.,
      2020) is a classic debiasing method with bias supervision used as an upper bound. Detailed settings
421
      can be found in Appendix D.

534
535      In this work, we emphasize the importance of debias within the real world. To tackle real-world
536      biases, we first proposed a fine-grained analysis framework to analyze dataset biases, based on
537      which we further proposed a systematic evaluation framework for benchmarking debiasing methods
538      under real-world biases. According to our result, we identified the insufficiency of existing methods

         approach. In Appendix G, we further discuss the limitations and future directions of this work.

       details.

1107   classes per object class and they are long-tailed distributed (e.g., dog images are more frequently

1129   D.3   BASELINES
1130
1131   LfF (Nam et al., 2020). Learning from Failure (LfF) is a debiasing technique that addresses
1132   the issue of models learning from spurious correlations present in biased datasets. The method
1133   involves training two neural networks: one biased network that amplifies the bias by focusing on
       easily learnable spurious correlations, and one debiased network that emphasizes samples the biased


                                                         21
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1134
       network misclassifies. This dual-training scheme enables the debiased network to focus on more
1135   meaningful features that generalize better across various datasets.
1136
1137
       DisEnt (Lee et al., 2021) . The DisEnt method enhances debiasing by using disentangled feature
1138
       augmentation. It identifies intrinsic and spurious attributes within data and generates new samples by
1139
       swapping these attributes among the training data. This approach significantly diversifies the training
1140   set with bias-conflicting samples, which are crucial for effective debiasing. By training models with
1141   these augmented samples, DisEnt achieves better generalization and robustness against biases in
1142   various datasets.
1143
1144
       BE (Lee et al., 2023). BiasEnsemble (BE) is a recent advancement in debiasing techniques that
1145   emphasizes the importance of amplifying biases to improve the training of debiased models. BE
1146   involves pretraining multiple biased models with different initializations to capture diverse visual
1147   attributes associated with biases. By filtering out bias-conflicting samples using these pre-trained
1148   models, BE constructs a refined bias-amplified dataset for training the biased network. This method
1149   ensures the biased model is highly focused on bias attributes, thereby enhancing the overall debiasing
1150   performance of the subsequent debiased model.
1151
1152   JTT (Liu et al., 2021). JTT is a classic DBAM method that has been applied to both the image
1153   and NLP domains. JTT identifies challenging examples by training an initial model using standard
1154   empirical risk minimization (ERM) and collecting misclassified examples into an error set. The
1155   second stage involves re-training the model while upweighting the error set to prioritize examples that
1156   the first-stage model struggled with. This approach aims to address performance disparities caused
1157   by spurious correlations, leading to better generalization across groups with minimal additional
1158
       annotation costs.
1159
1160   Group DRO (Sagawa* et al., 2020). Group DRO is a supervised debiasing method aiming to
1161   improve the worst group accuracy. It is commonly used as an upper bound in the worst group accuracy
1162   for unsupervised methods.

       D.5    D ESIGN OF FEATURE DESTRUCTING METHODS
1189
1190   For in visual recognition tasks, the shape of objects is a basic element of human visual perception
1191   (Geirhos et al., 2019). Therefore, the patch-shuffle destruction of shape (Lee et al., 2024) when
1192   capturing bias from visual recognition datasets is a feasible approach. We adopt the patch-shuffle
1193   approach for all the visual dataset within the paper except for CelebA. We apply a gray-scale
1194   transformation for CelebA as its recognition task is hair color. Anyhow, the feature destruction
1195   method could be highly flexible for different tasks.
1196   For NLP tasks, we first introduce the common biases within the NLP domain followed by a simple
1197   design of feature destruction method in the NLP domain. The commonly used NLP datasets for
1198   debiasing are MultiNLI and CivilComments-WILDS dataset. Specifically, the bias within the
1199   MultiNLI dataset is the correlation between the negation words and the entailment task and the bias
1200   within the CivilComments-WILDS dataset is the correlation between words implying demographic
1201   identities and the toxicity task. The target features of both datasets are semantic information of the
1202
       sentences where the position of words matters, and the spurious features are the individual words
       which is insensitive to positions. Furthermore, such position sensitivity difference between target and
1203
       spurious features within NLP biases is not limited to these two datasets but rather quite common. For
1204
       example, CLIP has also been found with the ”bag of words” phenomenon (Yuksekgonul et al., 2023),
1205   which ignores the semantic meaning of the inputs and relies on words individually for prediction.
1206   As a result, a straightforward approach for feature destruction is to shuffle the words within the
1207   sentences.

1209   D.6    A PPLYING D I D TO DBAM METHODS
1210
1211   As aforementioned in the main paper, when applying our method to the existing DBAM methods
1212   Nam et al. (2020); Lee et al. (2021; 2023), we do not modify the training procedure of the debiased
1213   model Md . For both methods, we train the biased model Mb with target feature destroyed data.
1214
       This is done by simply adding a feature destructive data transformation during data processing, with
       minimal computational overhead.
1215
1216   Note, for BE Lee et al. (2023), such feature destructive data transformation is not applied when
1217   training the bias-conflicting detectors.

       the picture rather than the targeted foreground object during prediction. One subtopic of model bias

1475   Debiasing without bias supervision. In this work, we focus only on debiasing methods that do

1477
       (2023); Zhao et al. (2023); Lee et al. (2023); Park et al. (2024) in the area generally involve a biased
1478
       auxiliary model to capture biases within the training data, according to which the debiased is trained
1479   with various techniques. We call such paradigm debiasing with biased auxiliary model (DBAM).
1480   Specifically, Nam et al. (2020) is the first work that follows the DBAM paradigm, proposing to use
1481   GCE for bias capture, and the loss-based sample re-weighing scheme to train the debiased model.
1482   Lee et al. (2021) further proposed a feature augmentation technique to further utilize the captured

1486   BC samples aiming to increase the diversity of BC samples. Lee et al. (2023) proposed to first
1487

1489

1492   model. A recent pair-wise debiasing method χ2 model Zhang et al. (2023a) based on biased auxiliary
1493   models encourages the debiased model to retain intra-class compactness using samples generated via
1494   feature-level interpolation between BC and BA samples.
