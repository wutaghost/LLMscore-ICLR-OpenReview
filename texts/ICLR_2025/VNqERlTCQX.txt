011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Generative models play a pivotal role in molecular design by effectively gener-
014            ating target molecules. Among these, generative models with latent space stand
015
               out due to their robust latent space representation capabilities, powerful dimen-
               sionality reduction ability and controllability of generation. In molecular design
016
               applications, generative models with latent space convert input molecules into
017
               latent variables, capturing essential molecular features including both structural
018            and property-related characteristics. Ideally, similar molecules should map to
019            proximate latent variables. However, previous studies have shown an inconsis-
020            tency between molecular similarity in the chemical space and that in the latent
021            space. This inconsistency will impede the accurate representation and compli-
022            cate subsequent design process,such as leading to higher optimization budget. To
023            address this, we propose Molecular Similarity-Aware Consistency Regularization
024            (MSCR), a straightforward regularization approach aimed at preserving the con-
025            sistency of molecule similarity. Our method proposes a brief but effective regular-
026
               ization technique to align chemical space and latent space,clearly reflect similarity
               relationships in latent space. We leverage Matched Molecules Pairs (MMPs) to
027
               introduce more robust similarity information than other conventional augmenta-
028
               tion methods. Extensive experiments demonstrate that MSCR not only maintains
029            molecules pairs similarity but also enhance optimization performance in molec-
030            ular latent space tasks, without additional costs. Furthermore, our visualizations
031            highlight molecular inconsistencies, thus underscoring the significance of our ap-
032            proach and improving the interpretability and relevance of our work.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Molecular design is a complex process about how to design molecules with specific properties and
038   functions in order to solve practical problems. Vast and complicated chemical space makes it chal-
039
      lenging to directly identify suitable molecules. Generative model with latent space is a novel and
      principled paradigm. Molecules are mapped to continuous latent space and represented by latent
040
      variables. While a molecule is transformed to a variable, the whole design space is transformed
041
      from complicated discrete space to relatively simple continuous space. From this point of view, this
042   transformation is simplification for hard molecular design problems. Compared to discrete prob-
043   lems, continuous problems usually can be solved easier because they usually have smooth solutions
044   spaces and the objective functions and constrains in continuous problems are often easier to model
045   and analyze. In this case, lots of remarkable approaches can be used (i.e Bayesian Optimization and
046   gradient descent) directly. What’s more, molecular design process will have more interpretability
047   and controllability if the whole process base on latent space (Vahdat et al. (2021); Yi et al. (2020);
048   Nie et al. (2021)). In such space, improvement of molecular properties can be observed clearly
049   (Prykhodko et al. (2019); Winter et al. (2019)), molecular edit can be reflected regularly (Blaschke
050
      et al. (2018)) and so on.
051   Generative model can be used to generate diverse and high-quality instances. Briefly, the distri-
052   bution of desired data in latent space will match a specific and simple distribution (e.g. Gaussian
053   distribution) through training. In this case, it is easy to get adequate desired data by sampling from
      the distribution. Typically, A generative model with latent space consists of an embedding process

      and a restoring process. For example, variational autoencoders(VAEs) utilize an encoder to embed-
055   ding origin data to latent variables and recover the data with a decoder vice versa. Generator and
056   discriminator in generative adversarial networks (GANs) and adding noise and denoising process in
057   diffusion models (DMs) play the similar role. Once trained, the embedding module of generative
058   model with latent space can be used to obtain low-dimension representation of data by latent vari-
059   ables, and the data can be reconstructed based on its corresponding latent variable by the restoring
060   module. There are various generative model with latent space used in latent space molecular design
061   areas. These work has achieved promising results for generating desirable molecules and editing
062   molecules flexibly. Usually, existing work base on a paradigm, initial molecules are given to em-
063
      bedding module and their latent variable can be gain. And then using gradient descent, Bayesian
      optimization or other wonderful methods to get desired latent variables of molecules. Finally, the
064
      desired molecules can be obtained with restoring module based on chosen latent variables. For such
065
      an paradigm, an important premise is the need for high-quality latent spaces. A high-quality latent
066   space means that latent variables in it can represent molecules exactly, the space should include
067   molecules et cetera. If the latent variable can’t represent the molecule exactly, the follow-up process
068   is meaningless because these design operations work on another molecule. If latent space is small
069   and neglects a lot potential molecules, it is hard to discover appropriate molecules and make detailed
070   edit. Thus,the quality of latent variable is essential for the performance of these model.
071
      Many researches have paid attention to improve quality of latent space in generative model with la-
072   tent space specially in VAEs, often handling the so-called latent variable collapse problem-where the
073   approximate posterior distribution induced by the encoder collapses to the prior over the latent vari-
074   ables (He et al. (2019); Lucas et al. (2019)). However, these settings can not improve the quality of
075   molecular latent space effectively, because these approaches fail to consider the relative relations be-
076   tween different molecules. When mapping chemical space to latent space, both individual molecular
077   representation and the relationship between different molecules should be taken into consideration.
078   Based on Quantitative Structure-Activity Relationship (QSAR) (Tropsha (2010); Cherkasov et al.
079   (2014)), properties of a molecule depend on its structure strongly. Thus, starting from an original
080
      molecule to find structurally similar molecules with improved properties is indeed an ideal paradigm
      in molecular design, as it allows for leveraging existing knowledge and data to enhance the success
081
      rate and efficiency of research and development. Consequently, it is necessary to maintain similarity
082
      information. In other words, assuming a high-quality molecular generative model with latent space,
083   a pair of similar molecules should have similar latent variable in latent space. It means molecular
084   latent space should have consistency with chemical space in similarity. We define this consistency
085   as molecular similarity consistency (MSC). MSC is important for molecular design, especially for
086   molecular optimization. A generative model with latent space that integrates MSC can easily find
087   molecules with good properties and high similarity.
088
      Figure1 (a) illustrates the phenomenon existing in generative models with latent space. We present
089   three molecules in chemical space and latent space, where two of them are similar and the other is
090   dissimilar. Notably, the two similar molecules do not appear very similar in latent space, with one
091   being more similar to the dissimilar molecule in chemical space. Such a phenomenon suggests that
092   the current generative models with latent space do not capture the intrinsic similarity relationship
093   between molecules very well. Figure1 (b) shows that such inconsistencies are common, and we
094   have counted the chemical space similarity and latent space similarity of several methods and found
095   that there is a huge gap between these two similarities, which suggests that many of the similarity
096   relationships are broken during the mapping process of the generative model with latent space.Based
097
      on the above simple experiments, we can demonstrate that the current generative model with latent
      space has many shortcomings in MSC, which presents challenges for downstream molecular tasks.
098
099   In this paper, we propose a powerful similarity-relevant regularization technique to retain the MSC.
100   This approach addresses the goals from both distribution and metric perspectives. From the dis-
101   tribution perspectives, we ensure that similar molecules have comparable distributions in the latent
102   space. From the metric perspectives, we align the similarity relationships both in chemical space
103
      and latent space. This regularization technique is plug-and-play and can be applied to any molecular
      generative model with latent space to maintain MSC and improve the quality of the latent space and
104
      the performance of molecular design performance. Because of the comprehensive understanding of
105
      similarity between molecules, we call our work Molecular Similarity-Aware Consistency Regular-
106   ization (MSCR). There are three main contributions in our work:

311
312   With the proposed formulation and practical parameterization, we now present the training schemes
313   for MSCR. While objectives for training the model are already defined in Eq.5, it is still unclear
314   whether the two component should be trained one by one, or optimized simultaneously. Previous
315   work about generative model with latent space (Sinha et al. (2021)) shows that the two-stage train-
316   ing strategy usually leads to the better performance, and we notice the similar phenomena in our
317   experiments. This means we first train MSCR with vanilla loss, and then train the generative model
318   with latent space on the latent restoring modules. A formal description of the training process is
319   provides in Alg.3.3.
320   Some objectives may be intractable, but we can effectively approximate them using Monte Carlo
321   methods. Monte Carlo approximation is powerful because it leverages the Law of Large Numbers,
322   which states that as the number of random samples increases, the average of those samples converges
323   to the expected value of the distribution. Specifically, we approximate the regularization term by
      sampling from the dataset.

      4.2   C ONSISTENCY OF SIMILARITY TASKS
379
380   Evaluation Metrics and Setup. To comprehensively evaluate the performance of our approach in
381   maintaining the consistency of similarity, We design two metrics to evaluate the performance of our
382   model:
383
      1) Consistency of Similarity (SC): To measure whether our method actually captures the similarity
384   relationship between molecules, we directly compare the similarity relationship between the same
385   set of molecules in chemical space and in latent space, and if these two similarities are consistent,
386   then it can be shown that the latent space learns the potential similarity metric of chemical space.
387   In our work, we use Tanimoto Similarity to measure similarity in chemical space and Cosine Sim-
388   ilarity to measure similarity in latent space. Considering that Cosine Similarity takes values in the
389   range [-1,1], we normalize Cosine Similarity to [0,1] considering that the two similarity measures
390   should be aligned. Both similarity measures are as large as possible, i.e. 0 means least similar and
391   1 means most similar. To represent the consistency of these two similarity measures, we introduce
392
      Consistency of Similarity, Consistency of Similarity = Tanimoto Similarity / Cosine Similarity. The
      closer the Consistency of Similarity is to 1 then it can indicate that the Consistency of Similarity is
393
      better, which means that our model better maintains the similarity information. To calculate Con-
394
      sistency of Similarity, we randomly sampled 500 pairs of molecules and calculated their tanimoto
395   similarity. The 500 pairs of molecules are then used as input to get the latent variable of these pairs
396   of molecules and to compute their Cosine similarity.
397
398
      2) Reconstruction and Validity: For a molecular generative model with latent space, reconstruction
      accuracy and validity are key evaluation metrics. Reconstruction accuracy measures how well the
399
      model can embed a molecule into the latent space and then decode it back to its original form, in-
400
      dicating the quality of the learned latent representation. To calculate reconstruction rate, we sample
401   500 molecules as input. We report the portion of restoring molecules that are identical to the input
402   molecules. Validity, on the other hand, assesses whether the generated molecules are chemically
403   valid, adhering to basic chemical rules. High reconstruction accuracy ensures the model captures
404   the essential features of molecules, while high validity ensures the generated molecules are suitable
405   for practical use in areas like drug discovery. To compute validity, we sample 500 latent vectors
406   from the prior distribution and restore these latent variables to molecules.
407   3) Uniqueness: A robust molecular generative model should generate unique compounds, which
408   means its latent space includes adequate molecules and it can help to discover new compounds. To
409   calculate uniqueness, we randomly sample 500 latent variables and report the percentages of the
410   unique molecules among all generated molecules.

      4.3   M OLECULAR O PTIMIZATION TASKS
433
434   Evaluation Metrics and Setup. To demonstrate the improvement in downstream tasks of molec-
435   ular design through maintaining consistency of similarity, we utilize our model to produce novel
436   molecules with desired properties based on given molecules (i.e. lead optimization). We consider
437   the following two properties: GSK3β and JNK3, which measure the inhibition the scores against
438   glycogen synthase kinase-3β and c-Jun N-terminal kinase-3 target proteins,respectively. We apply
439   the following three paradigms of lead optimization :
440
441
             • Bayesian Optimization (BO): Following Jin et al. (2018), we first train a generative model
               that associate molecule with a latent variables. Then train a Gaussian Process to predict
442
               properties values given its latent variables. Then we perform 5 iterations of BO with upper
443
               confidence bound (UCB) as acquisition function.
444
445          • Gradient descent (GD): Following Hoffman et al. (2022), we train a generative model with
446            latent space. Then we use approximate gradient descent for optimization. to Every itera-
447
               tions, we randomly sample 50 neighbor latent variables. And then these latent variables are
               decoded to corresponding molecules, which can be used to calculate approximate gradients.
448
               We perform 5 iterations of such approximate gradients descent.
449
450          • Random Sampling (RS): Given an initial molecule and trained generative model with latent
451            space, we map the given molecule to the latent space and randomly sample 100 other latent
452            variables around these, decoding the sampled latent variables to obtain desired molecules.
453
454
      Results and Analysis. Following Jin et al. (2020), our evaluation effort measures various aspects
      of lead optimization. We generate 500 molecules and compute the Success,novelty and diversity.
455
      Our results are summarized in Table 2,3,4. As a whole, both for GSK3β and JNK3, our model
456
      achieves some improvement in the performance of the original method. It is worth noting that the
457   introduction of MSCR for the JNK task do not enhance Novelty when the entire model is trained on
458   the CHEMBL dataset. We believe that the main reason for this is the lack of good initial molecules
459   for JNK3 (740 positives). The number of known molecules with better JNK3 values is very small
460   compared to GSK3β (2665 positives). Therefore, in the face of lead optimization, it is difficult for
461   the model to jump out of the original given molecules, so it does not achieve better performance in
462   novelty. In addition, since the methods of BO, GD and RS are still relatively simple, it is difficult
463   to optimize very good molecules in the task of molecular optimization, which is itself very difficult.
464   However, this does not affect our comparison, and it is still very intuitive to see the improvement
465
      of the comprehensive ability of the model by adding MSCR. In this section, we did not choose
      GEOLDM for our experiments, mainly because there is no simple paradigm of lead optimization
466
      for 3D molecular optimization, and it is very difficult to optimize the properties of 3D molecules
467
      directly. Therefore, if we need to optimize GSK3β and JNK3 for GEOLDM, we need to introduce
468   additional guidance, which is beyond the content of this paper.

      5       F UTURE WORK AND C ONCLUSION
516
517
      In this paper, we present a plug-in-play regularization that maintains the consistency of chemical
518   space and latent space, MSCR. MSCR enables the model to capture the similarity relationship well
519   from both distribution and metric perspectives, improving the model’s capability. In addition we
520   have conducted experiments on multiple datasets and multiple metrics to prove that our method is
521   indeed simple and effective. It is worth noting that our approach aligns the two spaces directly
522   through distributions and metrics, but for the particular problem, these two perspectives can not
523   adequately portray the relationship between the two spaces, and in the follow-up our work is to dig
524   into the underlying relationship between the different spaces to portray the similarity, and propose a
525   more generalized approach.
