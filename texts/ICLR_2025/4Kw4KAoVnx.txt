009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               While fine-tuning large language models (LLMs) for specific tasks often yields im-
012            pressive results, it comes at the cost of memory inefficiency due to back-propagation
013            in gradient-based training. Memory-efficient Zeroth-order (MeZO) optimizers,
014            recently proposed to address this issue, only require forward passes during training,
015            making them more memory-friendly. However, compared with exact gradients,
016            ZO-based gradients usually exhibit an estimation error, which can significantly hurt
017            the optimization process, leading to slower convergence and suboptimal solutions.
018            In addition, we find that the estimation error will hurt more when adding to large
019            weights instead of small weights. Based on this observation, this paper introduces
020
               Sparse MeZO, a novel memory-efficient zeroth-order optimization approach that
               applies ZO only to a carefully chosen subset of parameters. We propose a simple
021
               yet effective parameter selection scheme that yields significant performance gains
022
               with Sparse-MeZO. Additionally, we develop a memory-optimized implementation
023            for sparse masking, ensuring the algorithm requires only inference-level memory
024            consumption, allowing Sparse-MeZO to fine-tune LLaMA-30b on a single A100
025            GPU. Experimental results illustrate that Sparse-MeZO consistently improves both
026            performance and convergence speed over MeZO without any overhead. For exam-
027            ple, it achieves a 9% absolute accuracy improvement and 3.5x speedup over MeZO
028            on the RTE task.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Fine-tuning large language models for specific tasks or datasets has become a prevalent practice in
034
      machine learning. However, a major obstacle in fine-tuning is the substantial memory requirements,
035
      which escalate as models increase in size and complexity, thereby limiting the scalability and
036   accessibility for those with limited computational resources.
037
038   To mitigate the memory constraints, Parameter Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) has been developed,
039
      allowing for the modification of only a subset of parameters and achieving comparable results to full
      model tuning (Hu et al., 2021; Lester et al., 2021; Li & Liang, 2021; Zaken et al., 2021; Zhang et al.,
040
      2023). However, PEFT methods still necessitate the calculation of gradients for backpropagation and
041
      caching of numerous activations during training, which introduces additional memory overhead. For
042   instance, Malladi et al. demonstrates that, even with PEFT, training still requires approximately 6
043   times more memory than the memory cost for inference. This discrepancy raises a critical question:
044   Can large language models be fine-tuned solely with the cost of inference?
045
      In response to these challenges, zeroth-order (ZO) optimization presents a promising solution (Spall,
046
      1992). ZO optimization is a gradient-free method that estimates gradients using only the forward pass
047
      of the model, eliminating the need for backpropagation and, consequently, reducing memory usage.
048   MeZO (Malladi et al., 2023) is a recently proposed zeroth-order method for fine-tuning LLMs that has
049   demonstrated impressive performance. However, compared to exact gradients, ZO-based gradients
050   usually exhibit an estimation error, which can be defined as noise. This noise can significantly hurt
051   the optimization process, leading to slower convergence and suboptimal solutions. Moreover, we
052   find that the estimated ZO gradient is difficult to generalize across batches. Specifically, while it can
053   successfully reduce the training loss on the sampled batch with a high probability, it is more likely to
      increase the loss on other batches.

059   selectively optimize small weights, which are more resilient to noise perturbation. By focusing on
060   these noise-resistant weights, we demonstrate that our method enables the use of larger learning rates,
061   leading to improved performance and faster convergence. Our contributions can be summarized as

083   2.1   PARAMETER -E FFICIENT F INE -T UNING
084
085   Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) is designed to facilitate efficient adaptation by updating only
086   a subset of the model’s parameters, rather than fine-tuning the entire model (Hu et al., 2021; Zaken
087
      et al., 2021). These PEFT approaches can be categorized in various ways. We mainly focus on the
      selective methods and additive methods.
088
089   Selective Methods. Selective Methods try to selectively fine-tune a portion of a model and these
090   methods have been explored in various studies. For example, Zaken et al.; Cai et al. focused on the
091   model’s bias terms, finding that fine-tuning these terms alone could rival the results of fine-tuning the
092   entire model. However, the effectiveness of this approach diminishes with larger datasets, as shown in
093
      further analysis by Zaken et al.. Beyond static parameter adjustments, there has been an exploration
      into dynamically modifying parts of the model (Brock et al., 2017). This concept was later applied to
094
      language models, with AutoFreeze (Liu et al., 2021b) confirming its viability. Nevertheless, these
095
      techniques still demand considerable computational resources and sometimes yield less optimal final
096   outcomes.
097
098   Additive Methods. Additive methods, as an alternative to updating existing parameters, involve
099
      incorporating new layers into models, with the fine-tuning process focusing solely on these added
      layers (Houlsby et al., 2019; Hu et al., 2021; Lin et al., 2020; Rebuffi et al., 2017). Traditional
100
      techniques in this category, such as adapters (Houlsby et al., 2019), implemented layer additions
101
      in a sequential manner, which unfortunately led to increased inference latency. LoRA (Hu et al.,
102   2021) has been proposed to mitigate this issue, which freezes the weights of the pre-trained model
103   and introduces trainable matrices based on rank decomposition into each layer. Then, it can directly
104   integrate the newly learned weights into the main model. Following this, IA3 (Liu et al., 2022)
105   introduced innovative methods for adding parameters, balancing parameter count with accuracy, while
106   LST (Sung et al., 2022) introduced a highway structure that learns only small, auxiliary channels,
107   aiming to decrease memory demands. Despite these advancements, additive methods generally
      require meticulous design, and many fail to reduce the computational load during the backward pass.

      2.2     Z EROTH -O RDER O PTIMIZATION
109
110   Unlike traditional gradient-based optimization methods that rely on derivatives to guide the search
111   for optimal solutions, Zeroth-Order (ZO) optimization techniques do not require derivatives for
112   optimization Spall (1992); Liu et al. (2018; 2019). These methods utilize only the value of the
113   objective function, denoted as f (x), at any chosen point x. To estimate the gradient in the direction
114   of vector z, the objective function is assessed at two points in close proximity, f (x + ϵz) and
115   f (x − ϵz), with ϵ being a minimal value. Following this, conventional optimization algorithms, such
116
      as gradient descent or coordinate descent, are implemented using these approximated gradient values.
      Currently, ZO methods have been widely used in various applications, such as adversarial attack and
117
      defense (Chen et al., 2017; Ilyas et al., 2018; Tu et al., 2019; Ye et al., 2018), Auto-ML (Ruan et al.,
118
      2019; Wang et al., 2022), natural language processing (Sun et al., 2022a;b), reinforcement learning
119   (Vemula et al., 2019), Signal Processing (Liu et al., 2020), and on-chip training (Gu et al., 2021).

139   2.2.2 S PARSITY
140   FOR Z EROTH - ORDER O PTIMIZATION
141
142
      The hypothesis proposed by Frankle & Carbin,
      known as the lottery ticket hypothesis, showed that within a densely connected neural network that
143
      is randomly initialized, there exists a subnetwork of sparse yet high-quality connections. Based
144
      on the hypothesis, model pruning aims to identify and preserve the crucial ’winning tickets’ -
145   sparse subnetworks within the larger neural network that can achieve comparable or even superior
146   performance (Sun et al., 2023; Frantar & Alistarh, 2023). In addition, Dynamic Sparse Training
147   (DST) has been proposed to reduce the training and inference cost in first-order optimization (Liu
148   et al., 2021a; Evci et al., 2020). Recently, several related works have tried to apply the sparsity to
149   zeroth-order optimization (Balasubramanian & Ghadimi, 2018; Cai et al., 2021; 2022; Chen et al.,
150   2023; Gu et al., 2021; Ohta et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2018). For example, DeepZero (Chen et al.,
151   2023) proposes a novel ZO training protocol with model pruning guided sparsity. However, these
152   methods mainly focus on the neural network training from scratch with random initialization, while
153
      the application of sparse zeroth-order optimization in fine-tuning tasks remains an area of ongoing
      exploration.

190   To quantify how the gradient noise δ hurts the optimization process, we evaluate its effect on the
191   generalization performance of the estimated gradients. Specifically, we measure whether the zeroth-
192   order gradient estimate computed on one batch can effectively reduce the loss on other held-out
193   batches. For a batch Bt = {Bt1 , Bt2 } with 32 data points, we use 16 samples to estimate the zeroth-
194   order gradient gz (θ; Bt1 ) on batch Bt1 , and evaluate it on the remaining 16 held-out samples Bt2 . The
195
      results are shown in Figure 2. Interestingly, we find a stark contrast in performance - while the
      estimated gradient gz (θ; Bt1 ) can reliably reduce the loss on the same batch Bt1 it was computed on
196
      (90% success rate), it only manages to decrease the loss on the new held-out batch Bt2 around 50% of
197
      the time. This suggests that the zeroth-order gradient estimates suffer from overfitting or noise that
198   makes them less generalizable to unseen data samples. The gradient noise δ, while allowing descent
199   on the current batch, appears to introduce errors that prevent reliable descent directions for unseen
200   batches. Therefore, the noise δ can be seen as hurting the optimization process by degrading the
201   generalization performance of the parameter updates.

254   Based on the observations from our motivation, we can create a sparse mask, m, determined by
255   parameter magnitudes. Specifically, we only update parameters of smaller magnitude. These targeted
256   parameters are defined as θ̂ = m ⊙ θ. It’s important to note that we still preserve the complete set of

260

261     threshold for each layer to set the mask before training begins. However, a significant downside

264     GPU memory or causing extra overhead.

266     layer before training and then generate the mask by comparing parameter values to this threshold
267     during each iteration. This method avoids the necessity of storing a large mask, m.

      3.3   M EMORY-E FFICIENT I MPLEMENTATION OF S PARSE -M E ZO
283
284
      In this paper, our primary aim is to introduce an efficient method for fine-tuning language models
285   using zeroth-order optimization, enhancing performance on downstream tasks. As outlined in
286   Algorithm 1, our approach involves perturbing the parameters θt twice to generate two distinct sets
287   of parameters, θt′ = θt + ϵz and θt′′ = θt − ϵz. We then use the estimated gradient to update the
288   original parameters θt . This step typically requires storing two separate sets of parameters, leading
289   to increased memory usage during fine-tuning.
290
      Recently proposed MeZO, conserves memory by saving random seeds s and using it to resample z
291   for calculating θt′ , θt′′ , and reconstructing θt without needing extra memory. However, applying a
292   sparse mask m for calculating sparse perturbation ẑ in MeZO poses a memory issue. We cannot
293   simply reconstruct ẑ by saving the random seed because the sparse mask, determined by parameter
294   magnitudes, changes when parameters are altered by the perturbation. To address this, we propose
295   potential solutions for the memory issue.
296
      1-bit Quantization: We can apply 1-bit quantization to store the mask m, as it consists solely of 0s
297   and 1s. However, this method still increases memory usage, which isn’t our goal. As a solution, we
298   introduce a novel, memory-saving approach for zeroth-order optimization that calculates the mask m
299   on the fly during the forward pass.
300
      Calculating the Mask During the Forward Pass: By computing the mask and perturb parameters
301
      in the forward pass, we eliminate the need to store perturbed parameters θt′ and θt′′ . This means
302
      we only have to keep the original parameters θt throughout training. For vanilla implementation,
303   we first need to calculate the perturbed parameters with mask m: θt′ = θt + ϵm ⊙ z. After that,
304   we can use perturbed parameters θt′ to calculate the loss value l+ with the forward process. For
305                                                               ′(i)
      example, the output of layer i can be defined as y (i) = θt x(i) + b(i) . Noted that we need to
306   save the vanilla parameters θt and mask m for vanilla implementation. However, for our proposed
307   efficient implementation, we only need to save vanilla parameters θt . More specially, we can
308   calculate the mask m(i) of layer i during the forward process and then obtain the output of this layer:
309              (i)
      y (i) = (θt + ϵm(θt )z (i) )x(i) + b(i) , where m(·) represents the function GetMask to calculate
310
      mask m . Then, we can release the memory of mask m(i) and calculate the output and mask of the
               (i)
311   next layer.

392   4.2                   P ERFORMANCE
393
394   To evaluate the performance of our proposed method S-MeZO, we initially tested it on the SuperGLUE
395   benchmark using the LLaMA-7b model. The fine-tuning results, presented in Table 10, reveal that
396   our S-MeZO method outperforms other zero-order (ZO) techniques like MeZO and R-MeZO. For
397   instance, S-MeZO boosts MeZO’s accuracy from 71.7% to 80.7% on RTE (↑ 9%) and from 75.9% to
398   80.9% on BoolQ (↑ 5%). Furthermore, all zeroth-order-based methods surpassed the performance of
399
      Zero-shot learning and in-context learning, demonstrating that zeroth-order optimization significantly
      enhances the pre-trained model’s effectiveness on downstream tasks. Finally, we can find that S-
400
      MeZO significantly bridges the performance gap between zero-order and first-order optimization
401
      methods.
402
403   To further verify the generality of our proposed S-MeZO, we also evaluate it on Mistral-7B-v0.1. The
404   performance is shown in Table 2. We can find that S-MeZO can consistently improve the performance
405
      of vanilla MeZO and narrow down the performance gap between zeroth-order optimization and
      first-order optimization. For example, S-MeZO can improve the accuracy of vanilla MeZO from 81.6
406
      to 85.3 on BoolQ and then achieve a comparable performance with full fine-tuning.

      5   C ONCLUSION
487
488
      In this paper, we propose a novel memory-efficient zeroth-order fine-tuning method Sparse-MeZO,
489   which can use a similar memory cost to the inference process. We evaluate the performance of
490   fine-tuning LLaMA and OPT with Sparse-MeZO on SuperGULE benchmark and the experimental
491   results illustrate that Sparse-MeZO can achieve a higher accuracy and faster convergence. Finally, we
492   can fine-tune LLaMA-30b on a single A100 GPU.
493
      Limitation: There is still a performance gap between our proposed method Sparse-MeZO and
494
      first-order fine-tuning methods. We plan to address these limitations and enhance Sparse-MeZO’s
495   capabilities in our future research.
