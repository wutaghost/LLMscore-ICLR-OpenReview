011                                                  A BSTRACT
012
013             Adaptive optimization methods are widely recognized as among the most popu-
014
                lar approaches for training Deep Neural Networks (DNNs). Techniques such as
                Adam, AdaGrad, and AdaHessian utilize a preconditioner that modifies the search
015
                direction by incorporating information about the curvature of the objective func-
016
                tion. However, despite their adaptive characteristics, these methods still require
017             manual fine-tuning of the step-size. This, in turn, impacts the time required to
018             solve a particular problem. This paper presents an optimization framework named
019             SANIA to tackle these challenges. Beyond eliminating the need for manual step-
020             size hyperparameter settings, SANIA incorporates techniques to address poorly
021             scaled or ill-conditioned problems. We also explore several preconditioning meth-
022             ods, including Hutchinson’s method, which approximates the Hessian diagonal of
023             the loss function. We conclude with an extensive empirical examination of the
024             proposed techniques across classification tasks, covering both convex and non-
025
                convex contexts.

      Preconditioning / Feature scaling. Preconditioning is a technique used to improve the convergence
098

099

102   where Bt ∈ Rd×d is an invertible positive definite matrix, and mt is a gradient or its approxima-
103   tion. The origin of such a step is Newton method by Newton (1687); Raphson (1697); Kantorovich
104   (1948a;b; 1949) which uses the exact Hessian to precondition the gradient of the objective function,
105   i.e. Bt = ∇2 f (wt ) and mt = ∇fi (wt ). Newton method can be very effective for minimizing
106   convex objectives. However, the prohibitive cost of computing and inverting the Hessian matrix, to-
107   gether with issues around negative eigenvalues, makes this approach impractical for machine learn-
      ing tasks. To address this issue, one can use methods that never define the Hessian of the objective

145   Interpolation Condition. The Interpolation Condition is an assumption often applied in optimiza-
146   tion and machine learning, particularly in the analysis of overparameterized models such as deep
147   neural networks. It assumes the existence of a set of model parameters w∗ such that the loss func-
148   tion f (w) achieves its infimum across all data points. This condition is indicative of a scenario
149   where the model has sufficient flexibility to perfectly fit the training data, leading to zero loss for
150   every data point. Such regimes are commonly encountered in overparameterized deep neural net-
151   works Ma et al. (2018b); Zhang et al. (2021) or non-parametric regression models Liang & Rakhlin
152
      (2020); Belkin et al. (2019), where the model’s capacity exceeds the complexity of the data, ensur-
      ing exact interpolation of the training set. This is one of the standard assumptions in analysis of
153
      methods with the Stochastic Polyak step-size e.g. Schaipp et al. (2023); Loizou et al. (2021); Gower
154
      et al. (2022); Li et al. (2023); Orvieto et al. (2022). Unless otherwise stated, our default assumption
155   is that Assumption 1 holds true.

166   In this section, we propose a general framework equation 6 for preconditioning stochastic Polyak
167   step-size methods. This framework generalizes some well-known first-order, second-order, and
168   Quasi-Newton methods from Polyak step-size perspective. The main feature of the framework is
169   that it highlights some insights about SPS and provides an instrument to generalize existing methods
170   as Polyak step-size methods. It makes them adaptive and parameter-free in the SPS setting. The
171
      generality of this framework makes it difficult to propose an explicit step. Therefore, we will focus
      on the most promising cases and provide their explicit formulations to introduce new methods.

      2.4     A FFINE AND SCALE INVARIANCE
427
428   The family of Stochastic Gradient Methods with Polyak step-size offers an update rule that alleviates
429   the need of fine-tuning the learning rate of an optimizer. However, existing first-order algorithms,
430   whether stochastic or deterministic, perform poorly on ill-conditioned datasets. One possible rea-
431   son for this is their strong dependence on the chosen basis. This is why, in machine learning, it is
      common practice to normalize data, as it makes the optimization space and basis more amenable. In


                                                                8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      the case of generalized linear models (GLM), the choice of basis is directly linked to the handling
433   of ill-conditioned datasets. Changing the basis leads to improvement of conditioning.
434   Affine invariance is one of the key features of the Newton method, which makes it basis-
435   independent (Nesterov & Nemirovskii, 1994; Nesterov, 2018). Let A ∈ Rd×d be a non-degenerate
436   matrix. We consider function ϕ(y) = f (Ay). By affine transformation, we denote f (w) →
437   ϕ(y) = f (Ay), w → A−1 y. Now, we discuss what is affine invariant friendly and what is not.
438   First of all, the local Hessian norm ∥h∥∇2 f (w) is affine-invariant: ∥z∥2∇2 ϕ(y) = ∇2 ϕ(y)z, z =
439     A⊤ ∇2 f (Ay)Az, z = ∇2 f (w)h, h = ∥h∥2∇2 f (w) . However, the norm ∥z∥2I is not affine invari-
440   ant. Second of all, Damped Newton method is affine invariant (Lemma 5.1.1 (Nesterov, 2018)).
441   It means that for the function f (w) Damped Newton method with affine invariant step-size γt
442   generates wt+1 = wt − γt [∇2 f (wt )]−1 ∇f (wt ). For a function ϕ(y), Damped Newton method
443   generates yt+1 = yt − γt [∇2 ϕ(yt )]−1 ∇ϕ(yk ). If y0 = A−1 w0 , then ∀t : yt = A−1 wt . Essen-
444   tially, we get a bijection between yt and wt . Also, the function values during the optimization
445   are the same ϕ(yt ) = f (wt ). It means that for GLM, we will automatically get the best ba-
446   sis. Finally, we can show that SANIA Newton and SANIA CG are affine invariant, because the
447
      step-size λt in equation 19 is affine-invariant friendly. All proofs are presented in Appendix D.2.

531   In this paper, we introduced a versatile and inclusive framework that not only encompasses classical
532   optimization techniques but also sheds valuable light on Polyak step-size methods. Our research
533   introduce the first Cubic Newton method with Polyak step-size which combines the efficiency of
534   stochastic methods and the robustness of Newton methods. We have presented innovative variants
535   of AdaGrad and Adam optimization algorithms that are scale invariant. Our proposed methods are
536
      affine or scale invariant, and this important development ensures the invariance of these methods to
      basis transformation, expanding their applicability and reliability in various scenarios. Our work is
537
      supported by comprehensive experiments including both convex and non-convex settings.

      A      RELATED WORK
919
920
921   Second-order methods have played a crucial role in contemporary optimization since their inception
922   in classical works focused on root-finding algorithms by Newton (1687), Raphson (1697), Simpson
923
      (1740), and Bennett (1916). Subsequent significant advancements in the Newton method and its
      local quadratic convergence rates were made by Kantorovich (1948b;a; 1949; 1951b;a; 1956; 1957).
924
      These methods have been extensively researched, refined, and enhanced in various works, with no-
925
      table contributions from Moré (1977),Griewank (1981), Nesterov & Polyak (2006). Today, they
926   are widely employed in both industrial and scientific computing. For a comprehensive historical
927   overview of the Newton method, Boris T. Polyak’s paper Polyak (2007) provides more in-depth in-
928   sights. Compared to first-order algorithms, second-order methods typically yield faster convergence.
929   However, it’s important to note that the per-iteration computational cost of second-order methods is
930   considerably higher. An example of the classical Newton method can be expressed as follows:
931
932                                                              −1
                                         xt+1 = xt − ∇2 f (xt )

933
934
      It exhibits quadratic local convergence, but it becomes impractical for large-scale optimization prob-
935
      lems due to the necessity of computing the complete Hessian and matrix inversion at each iteration.
936
      It also lack of global convergence properties and could diverge if far from the solution.
937
938   The Cubic Regularized Newton method by Yurii Nesterov and Boris T. Polyak (Nesterov & Polyak,
939   2006) is one of the main approaches to globalize the Newton method. This algorithm achieves global
940   convergence with the convergence rate O(ε−1/2 ) for convex functions. Nonetheless, a notable lim-
941   itation of the Cubic Regularized Newton method lies in the auxiliary problem, which typically re-
942
      quires running a separate optimization algorithm to solve it. Several research papers have proposed
      regularization techniques based on the gradient norm, aiming to derive an explicit regularized New-
943
      ton step Polyak (2009; 2017). In Mishchenko (2023); Doikov & Nesterov (2023), the convergence
944
      rate was improved up to O(ε−1/2 ) for convex functions, under higher assumptions on smoothness
945
      it accelerates up to O(ε−1/3 ) Doikov et al. (2024). Affine-Invariant Cubic Regularized Newton
946
      method with local Hessian norms has the convergence rate O(ε−1/2 ) and the same subproblem as a
947
      classical Newton step Hanzely et al. (2022).

       D.2     S CALE I NVARIANCE
1445
1446   Kempka et al. (2019); Zhuang et al. (2022) illustrate this important but overlooked property of an
1447   optimization algorithm. It is widely recognized that the convergence rate of minimizing a twice
1448   continuously differentiable function f through a first-order optimization algorithm depends heavily
1449   on the condition number. To mitigate the impact of the condition number, one effective approach is
1450   the use of preconditioners relying on Hessian of the function which yields affine invaraince. Con-
1451   sider the Hessian cannot be easily estimated, Zhuang et al. (2022) shows that scale invariance gives
1452   similar advantages to the use of an optimal diagonal preconditioner.
1453   They also showed why algorithms like SGD and Adam have such excellent performances in DNNs
1454   even though they are not scale invariant. Because they are intensively linked to the use of batch
1455   normalization which normalizes the gradients. Without BN, using SGD with momentum and weight
1456   decay, even a tiny learning rate will lead to divergence while training a deep neural network. But
1457   for the upgraded version of Adam–AdamW which enjoys scale invariance outperforms Adam when
       both are finely tuned.
