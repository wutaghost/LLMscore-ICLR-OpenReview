009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Self-supervised contrastive learning (SSCL) is a rapidly advancing approach for
012            learning data representations. However, a significant challenge in this paradigm is
013            the feature suppression effect, where useful features for downstream tasks are sup-
014            pressed due to dominant or easy-to-learn features overshadowing others crucial for
015            downstream performance, ultimately degrading the performance of SSCL models.
016            While prior research has acknowledged the feature suppression effect, solutions
017            with theoretical guarantees to mitigate this issue are still lacking. In this work,
018            we address the feature suppression problem by proposing a novel method, Fisher
019            Contrastive Learning, which unbiasedly and exhaustively estimates the central
020
               sufficient dimension reduction function class in SSCL settings. In addition, the
               embedding dimensionality is not preserved in practice. FCL empirically maintains
021
               the embedding dimensionality by maximizing the discriminative power of each
022
               linear classifier learned through Fisher Contrastive Learning. We demonstrate
023            that using our proposed method, the class-relevant features are not suppressed by
024            strong or easy-to-learn features on datasets known for strong feature suppression
025            effects. Furthermore, we show that Fisher Contrastive Learning consistently out-
026            performs existing benchmark methods on standard image benchmarks, illustrating
027            its practical advantages.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Among various approaches to self-supervised learning, self-supervised contrastive learning (SSCL)
033   Chen et al. (2020a); Robinson et al. (2020); Kalantidis et al. (2020); Grill et al. (2020); Radford et al.
034   (2021); Chen et al. (2020b;c); Caron et al. (2020); He et al. (2020); Chen & He (2021); Grill et al.
035   (2020) has emerged as a particularly promising technique. SSCL offers a new paradigm that exploits
036   data augmentations to create positive and negative pairs for learning data point representations. For
037   example, in the context of computer vision, positive and negative pairs are formed based on image
038   augmentation techniques such as cropping, color jittering, and adding noise. The augmented image
039   and the original image form a positive pair. Conversely, those images augmented from different
040
      images from distinct sources form the negative pairs. The objective of SSCL is to ensure that
      representations of positive pairs are closer in the embedding space than those of negative pairs. SSCL
041
      has achieved remarkable success in various machine learning tasks Chen et al. (2020a); He et al.
042
      (2020); Chuang et al. (2020); Radford et al. (2021).
043

      Feature Suppression Effect Feature suppression effect is a phenomenon in which dominant
      features (e.g., content) can overshadow and suppress other important features (e.g., style), causing
046
      SSCL to fail to learn the necessary features for downstream tasks Rusak et al. (2022), limiting
047
      the potential of SSCL. Feature suppression effects happen when the representations lose diversity
048   and become less informative. For example, easy-to-learn shared features of augmented pairs could
049   suppress the learning of other features Chen et al. (2021), and color features can suppress other
050   features like texture and shape Chen et al. (2020a); Robinson et al. (2021), despite the object class
051   often being determined by features other than just color. Consequently, the presence of "color
052   distribution" suppresses the competing features of "object class" Chen et al. (2021), leading to
053   insufficient dimension reduction space for downstream classification tasks. The resulting lack of
      discrimination power fails to capture the full complexity and richness of the data, degrading the

054
      performance of downstream tasks. Severe feature suppression effects in SSCL require effective
055   strategies with theoretical guarantees to mitigate this critical problem.
056
057   Previous theoretical investigations have shown that under the following scenarios, there would
058
      be feature suppression effects: (1) The distribution of the feature is uniformly distributed on the
      underlying space Robinson et al. (2021); (2) the augmentation tends to preserve class-irrelevant
059
      features Xue et al. (2023), and (3) the low embedding dimensionality, meaning the embedding space
060
      has a lower rank than its dimension Xue et al. (2023); Li et al. (2023). Based on their understanding
061   of how feature suppression effects happen, they propose several remedies to overcome the feature
062   suppression effect, Robinson et al. (2021) propose implicit feature modification to remove well-
063   represented features in the input samples to encourage the encoder to learn more semantic features.
064   However, the solution works only given the condition that the encoder before feature modification is
065   a shortcut solution. There is no guarantee that the learned features are effective for discrimination.
066   Built on the theoretical understanding, Xue et al. (2023) suggest two pathways to remedy the feature
067   suppression effect, one is to increase the embedding dimensionality, and the other is to prevent
068   imperfect augmentations, i.e. adding noise to class-relevant features. However, they do not offer
069
      a robust solution to imperfect augmentations and increase the embedding dimensionality. Besides
      increasing the embedding dimensionality, predictive contrastive learning Li et al. (2023) prevents
070
      the features from being suppressed by training a decoder to restore the input. However, the decoder
071
      module also preserves the class-irrelevant features like background information. Although the
072   aforementioned research has alleviated the feature suppression effects by minimizing the information
073   loss in embeddings, methods guaranteeing that the learned features are both effective and sufficient
074   in self-supervised learning are still lacking.

091   Fisher Discriminant Analysis and Sufficient Dimension Reduction Fisher discriminant analysis
092   (FDA) is a supervised dimension reduction method that projects data onto a lower-dimensional space
093   while maximizing the separation between different classes Hastie et al. (1994; 1995). FDA as a
094   classification method can be generalized to regression settings, e.g., sliced inverse regression Li
095
      (1991); Chen & Li (2001), a method of estimating the sufficient dimension reduction (SDR) subspace
      Chen & Li (1998); Cook (2007). Based on the theoretical framework of SDR, the discriminant
096
      functions in FDA recover the effective dimension reduction subspace for binary responses in the
097
      classification tasks Chen & Li (2001). In a general setting, sufficient dimension reduction subspace
098   can be defined as a sub σ-filed G of σ(X) such that Y ⊥⊥ X | G, where ⊥⊥ denotes statistical
099   independence. This indicates that G preserves all the information about Y contained in X. SDR has
100   played crucial roles in dimension reduction problems of regression Li (1991); Cook & Li (2002)
101   and classification tasks Chen & Li (2001); Wu (2008). By embedding the data into the sufficient
102   dimension reduction subspace, we can obtain a dimension reduction space that is both unbiased and
103   exhaustive Lee et al. (2013).

122   Fisher Contrastive Learning To be more robust to the feature suppression effect, we propose a
123   novel contrastive learning method, Fisher Contrastive Learning (FCL), which estimates the sufficient
124   dimension reduction subspace through nonlinear transformation. First, we reformulate the SSCL
125   problem as a dimension reduction task for classification, where the goal is to project data into a
126   subspace that enhances class separability induced by augmented image views. Second, we maximize
127   the discrimination power of the Fisher discriminant functions in the embedding space. By doing so,
128   we preserve the central sufficient dimension reduction functional class of the self-supervised learning
129   task as shown in panel (b) of Figure 1. Consequently, the embeddings learned by FCL can mitigate
130
      the feature suppression effects and retain informative components with theoretical guarantees. Our
      contributions are threefold: (1) We introduce FCL, a nonlinear sufficient dimension reduction method
131
      for SSCL, that offers a robust solution to the feature suppression effects by learning an exhaustive
132
      and unbiased function class; (2) FCL prevents the low embedding dimensionality by maximizing
133   the discrimination power of the Fisher discriminant functions; (3) we demonstrate the effectiveness
134   of the proposed method on various datasets that exhibit feature suppression effects and benchmark
135   image datasets compared with other self-supervised learning methods.

      The occurrence of feature suppression effects in SimCLR deteriorates the performance of downstream
165   tasks. Such issues occur when some features are too easy or strong to learn other discriminant features
166   for downstream tasks. To address the problem, we propose FCL, a novel method that projects the data
167   into the central sufficient dimension reduction function class. In addition, our proposed algorithm
168   can preserve the dimensionality of embeddings, further guaranteeing the embeddings are robust to
169   feature suppression effects.
170

171   SSCL to a classification task. The classification labels are defined such that the augmentations from
172   the j-th data point belong to the j-th class. There are n classes for each batch of size n. The primary
173   objective of the FDA is to find a hyperplane that optimally separates the classes by maximizing the
174   between-class variance relative to the within-class variance. For high dimensional data, the FDA may
175   not be effective enough for classification tasks Dorfer et al. (2015); Hastie et al. (1995). This motivates
176   us to introduce a nonlinear method, FCL. After a nonlinear mapping, we optimize the FDA objective
177   in the embedding space trained by the neural network. In FCL, the alignment loss corresponds to the
178   within-class variance, and the uniformity loss corresponds to the negative of between-class variance.

      4    C ENTRAL σ-F IELDS FOR N ONLINEAR S UFFICIENT D IMENSION R EDUCTION

      Sufficient dimension reduction (SDR) has been one of the most popular dimension reduction frame-
219   works in statistics (Li, 1991; Cook & Li, 2002; Li, 2018). In the classical setting, linear SDR equals
220   FDA, which seeks a low-dimensional linear classifier that captures all the information needed in a
221   classification task. In the self-supervised contrastive learning framework, we have a random vector
222   X of dimension p comprising augmented data and random variable, a random variable Y augmented
223   classification labels. If there is a matrix B ∈ Rp×d (p ≥ d) such that Y ⊥⊥ X | B⊤ X, then the
224   subspace spanned by the column space of B is referred to as a linear SDR subspace. The intersection
225   of all the SDR subspaces is called the central SDR subspace, which can be estimated by FDA.
226   Our proposed method FCL generalizes FDA to a nonlinear setting, which generalized SDR subspace
227   to SDR σ-field, preserving the essential features and variations in the augmented data through
228   nonlinear SDR. Functions are typically defined on a σ-field. A σ-field ensures that we can perform
229   key operations without leaving the domain of our measure. Similarly, the central SDR σ-field is
230   the intersection of SDR σ-field and can be estimated unbiasedly and exhaustively by our proposed
231   FCL. The unbiasedness ensures that the learned embeddings do not contain redundant information to
232   distinguish those data augmented from different data points, while exhuastiveness guarantees that
233
      the embeddings capture all the necessary information to distinguish between different data points,
      thereby preventing feature suppression.
234
235   Other contrastive learning methods would suppress class-relevant features when the augmentation is
236   imperfect Xue et al. (2023). However, our proposed FCL can preserve the class-relevant features
237   illustrated by Figure 1. We prove it by showing the unbiasedness and exhaustiveness of the estimated
238   function class by FCL, which indicates the learned embeddings contain sufficient information from
239
      the input images, including the class-relevant features. This suggests that our method is more robust
      against feature suppression effects compared with other contrastive learning methods.
240

822   C.1.1    R ANDOM B ITS
823
824   The generation of the Digits on Flower dataset with strong feature suppression effect is adapted from
825
      Chen et al. (2021). In this dataset, we concatenate a real image with an image of a random integer
      along the channel dimension. The random integer is sampled from the range [1, log2 (l)], where l is
826
      a controllable parameter. This integer is replicated across all pixel locations and expressed using l
827
      binary channels. To be noted, unlike RGB channels, these additional channels of random bits are
828   not altered by augmentation, ensuring they remain identical in both augmented views of the same
829   image. The added channels of random bits are easy-to-learn features that suppress the other features.
830   We vary the number of random bits, {0, 2, 4, 6, 8, 10}, in the dataset to control the amount of mutual
831   information between two augmented views. Also, we know that the mutual information between two
832   views given this construction is at least log2 (l). The dataset is divided into 80% training samples and
833   20% test samples.
834
835   C.1.2    D IGITS ON F LOWER DATASET
836
      The generation of the Digits on Flower dataset with strong feature suppression effects is adapted
837
      from the method described in Chen et al. (2021). In this process, for each flower image, we map
838
      a randomly sampled MNIST digit image onto the flower image. To explore the impact of feature
839   suppression, we vary the number of digit copies mapped onto the flower images with the following
840   configurations: {0, 1, 4, 9, 16} digits. The flower dataset consists of 3670 color images, each with a
841   size of 224x224 pixels. The MNIST digit images used for mapping are resized to 72x72 pixels. For
842   images overlaid with a single digit, the digit is placed at the center of the image. When four digits
843   are overlaid, their positions are at [0.3, 0.7] of the image dimensions. For nine digits, their positions
844   are at [0.25, 0.5, 0.75], and for sixteen digits, they are positioned at [0.2, 0.4, 0.6, 0.8]. The dataset is
845   divided into 80% training samples and 20% test samples.
846   By varying the number and positions of the overlaid digits, we can systematically study the effect of
847   feature suppression in the dataset. This setup allows us to control the amount of information from the
848   digits and observe how it impacts the classification performance on the flower dataset.
849
      The MNIST dataset LeCun (1998) is a large database of handwritten digits commonly used for
850
      training various image processing systems. It contains a total of 70,000 grayscale images, each of size
851   28x28 pixels. The dataset is divided into 60,000 training samples and 10,000 testing samples. Each
852   image is labeled with one of 10 classes, corresponding to the digits 0 through 9, with approximately
853   7,000 samples per class. The simplicity and cleanliness of the dataset make it a standard benchmark
854   for evaluating algorithms in self-supervised learning.
855
856   C.1.3    B ENCHMARK DATASETS
857
858   We use four benchmark real-world datasets in SSCL to evaluate the performance of our method.
859   STL-10: The STL-10 dataset Coates et al. (2011) is designed for developing unsupervised feature
860   learning, deep learning, and self-taught learning algorithms. It contains color images of size 96x96
861   pixels. The dataset includes 10 classes with 500 labeled training examples and 800 labeled testing
862   examples per class, totaling 5,000 labeled training samples and 8,000 testing samples. Additionally,
863   there are 100,000 unlabeled images for unsupervised learning tasks. The classes represent common
      objects such as airplanes, birds, and cars, making the dataset suitable for evaluating complex feature

      learning algorithms. Notice that in the pretraining stage, we only use the training examples without
865   labels.
866
867   CIFAR-10: The CIFAR-10 dataset Krizhevsky et al. (2009) is a widely used dataset for object
868
      recognition tasks. It consists of 60,000 color images of size 32x32 pixels, with 6,000 images per
      class distributed evenly across 10 classes. The dataset is split into 50,000 training samples and
869
      10,000 testing samples. Each class represents a common object such as airplanes, cars, and birds.
870
      The dataset’s diversity and balanced class distribution make it an excellent benchmark for testing
871   self-supervised learning methods and other machine learning algorithms.

873   CIFAR-100: The CIFAR-100 dataset Krizhevsky et al. (2009) is similar to CIFAR-10 but with a

      greater number of classes and finer granularity. It contains 60,000 color images of size 32x32 pixels,

875
      and 10,000 testing samples. Each class represents a specific object, such as a type of flower or
876
      insect, providing a more challenging task due to the increased number of classes and the fine-grained
877   nature of the categories. This makes CIFAR-100 a comprehensive benchmark for evaluating the
878   performance of self-supervised learning algorithms.
879
880
      TinyImageNet: The TinyImageNet dataset is a subset of ImageNet. It contains 100,000 images of 200

881
      20,000 testing samples. Compared with CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100 and STL-10 it has more images and
882
      more classes, which makes it a more challenging task.

      C.2     E XPERIMENTAL S ETTINGS
885
886   C.2.1    DATA AUGMENTATION
887
888   For Random Bits and Digits on Flower datasets, we only use random crop with resize Chen et al.
889   (2020a) as the data augmentation method. For the benchmark datasets, including MNIST, STL-
890
      10, CIFAR-10, and CIFAR-100, we employ a broader range of data augmentations. These include
      random crop with resize, random flip, color distortion, and Gaussian blur. All processes and parameter
891
      settings are consistent with those outlined in Chen et al. (2020a) to ensure a fair comparison and
892
      reproducibility of results.
893
894
      C.2.2    M ODEL S ETTINGS
895
896   Random Bits and Digits on Flower Datasets For Random Bits and Digit on Flower datasets, the
897   encoder includes three two-dimensional convolutional layers, followed by a Flatten layer and a Dense
898   layer. Batch normalization and ReLU activation functions are applied throughout the encoder. The
899   projection network consists of two Dense layers, with a final output dimension of 128. We use a
900   dense layer for classification. Throughout the pre-training, we utilize the Adam optimizer with a
901
      polynomial decay learning rate schedule. The initial learning rate is set to 0.001, with an end learning
      rate of 0. The decay steps are set to 5000 for the Random Bits dataset and 800 for the Digits on
902
      Flower dataset. The number of epochs is 10 for the Random Bits dataset and 35 for the Digits on
903
      Flower dataset. The batch size is 128.
904
905
      Benchmark Datasets For benchmark datasets, we follow the settings in SimCLR to design the
906   architecture. The backbone network is ResNet-50 for TinyImageNet and ResNet-18 for other datasets
907   (CIFAR10, CIFAR100 and STL10) He et al. (2016) and the projector is a two-layer MLP after
908   ResNet’s global average pooling layer (pool5 ). Both the input and output dimensions of each layer
909   in the projector are set to 128, with each layer followed by a ReLU activation function. During
910   unsupervised pre-training, we use a base learning rate of 0.03 with a cosine decay schedule for 600
911   epochs. The weight decay is set to 0.0005, and momentum is set to 0.9. We also incorporate a
912   warm-up phase for the first 10 epochs. The batch size is 128, and we utilize the SGD optimizer for
913   training.
914   After pre-training the network, we freeze its parameters and train a supervised linear classifier in
915   linear evaluation and transfer learning. The features used for training the classifier are extracted
916   from ResNet’s global average pooling layer (pool5 ). For training the linear classifier, we use a base
917   learning rate of 30 with a cosine decay schedule over 30 epochs. We also employs the SGD optimizer
      with a momentum of 0.9 and a batch size of 256.
