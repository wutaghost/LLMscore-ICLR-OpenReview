009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We study the problem of universal black-boxed reward poisoning attacks against
012            general offline reinforcement learning with deep neural networks. We consider
013            a black-box threat model where the attacker is entirely oblivious to the learning
014            algorithm, and its budget is limited by constraining the amount of corruption at
015
               each data point and the total perturbation. We require the attack to be universally
               efficient against any efficient algorithms that might be used by the agent. We
016
               propose an attack strategy called the ‘policy contrast attack.’ The idea is to find
017
               low- and high-performing policies covered by the dataset and make them appear
018            to be high- and low-performing to the agent, respectively. To the best of our
019            knowledge, we propose the first universal black-box reward poisoning attack in the
020            general offline RL setting. We provide theoretical insights on the attack design and
021            empirically show that our attack is efficient against current state-of-the-art offline
022            RL algorithms in different learning datasets.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
      Reinforcement learning (RL) in the offline setting (Levine et al., 2020; Agarwal et al., 2020) has
027
      become a promising framework for realizing reinforcement learning applications in practice. Offline
028   RL avoids the necessity of potentially expensive online data collection and can work with offline data
029   directly. Taking medical treatment problems as an example, collecting online data would require
030   testing on humans, which is dangerous and infeasible. In contrast, abundant recordings of how a
031   human doctor treats a patient are available for offline training.
032
      A number of effective algorithms have been proposed for the offline RL problem and achieve
033
      promising empirical performance on simulated environments. (Bhardwaj et al., 2024; Kumar et al.,
034   2020; Fujimoto & Gu, 2021; Kidambi et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2019; Cheng et al., 2022). However,
035   a practical threat arises in the real world that is usually overlooked in the simulations. In many
036   applications, the offline data is usually based on human feedback (Zheng et al., 2018; Kiran et al.,
037   2021), making it possible for an adversary to poison the reward signal in the training dataset. For
038   example, the learning agent may ask a third-party agent to provide offline data. If the third-party
039   agent is dishonest, it may provide human data with subtle malicious changes that are hard to detect
040   but can potentially cause unwanted results for the learning agent. The threat of poisoning attacks even
041   draws attention in recent reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) Baumgärtner et al.
042
      (2024); Wang et al. (2023); Wu et al. (2024), and people are concerned that such attacks may greatly
      influence the performance of current RLHF approaches. The state-of-the-art offline reinforcement
043
      learning algorithms mentioned above only consider ideal environments without such threats. A few
044
      works Li et al. (2024); Ye et al. (2023) investigate the settings with the poisoning attack. However,
045   they only consider the attacker adopting some random attack strategies, which may not be enough
046   to evaluate the robustness of an algorithm properly. Therefore, it is important to investigate the
047   vulnerabilities of current algorithms to the poisoning attacks with more efficient attack strategies.
048
      In Fig 1, we show the framework of the attack. Specifically, we focus on a universal black-box
049
      reward-poisoning attack with a limited budget such that under the attack, any originally efficient
050
      learning algorithm can no longer learn a high-performing policy. Such an attack setting is more
051   realistic due to the following reasons:
052
053   1. Universal and Black box: First, it is not in the interest of the learning agent to reveal its
         algorithm to the attacker. Second, offline RL algorithms are very different in learning strategies


                                                         1

054
         and frameworks. Therefore, it is hard for the attacker to assume which kind of learning algorithm
055      the agent will use. The attacker has to work without any knowledge of the algorithm used by the
056      agent and expect the agent to choose any efficient algorithm arbitrarily.
057
      2. Reward poisoning: In RL environments, the reward signals are usually provided by the envi-
058
         ronment, such as human users, while the state signals are determined by the agent. For example,
059      in reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF), the state signals are the prompts the
060      learner gives, while the reward signals are feedback from humans. Therefore, it is more feasible
061      for the attacker to poison rewards.
062
      3. Limited budget: Too much perturbation on the reward signals makes it easier for the learning
063      agent to detect. So, it is more practical for the attacker to work with limited budgets and perturb
064      the training process as little as possible.
065
066   We formally introduce our criteria for an attack to be practical in Section 3.
067
      Challenges: We find the following challenges for developing a practical reward poisoning attack.
068
069   1. Fixed training dataset: In the online learning setting, an attacker can mislead the learner to
070      collect data that only covers states and actions with low long-term rewards so that the learner never
071      observes how a high-performing policy would work, making it fail to learn a high-performing
072      policy (Xu et al., 2022). In contrast, the training dataset is fixed in the offline learning setting. This
073      results in the famous ‘pessimistic’ learning (Levine et al., 2020) in offline RL, and the learning
074      agent is inclined to stick to the behaviors covered by the dataset. The learner can always observe
075
         how an expert would behave (the action) in the dataset regardless of the attack. This contributes to
         the fundamental difficulty of misleading the learning agent.
076
077   2. Universal and Black box attack: A practical attacker should be oblivious to the learning agent
078      and universal efficiency against any algorithms it might use. First, it is challenging to list all
079
         possible efficient learning algorithms that an agent might use. Second, without knowing the details
         of an offline learning algorithm, it would be challenging to predict the exact learning result of the
080
         algorithm under the attack.
081
082   Contributions: To address the challenges above, our insight is to characterize general efficient
083   offline RL algorithms with the pessimistic learning feature. For a pessimistic algorithm, it will find
084   nearly the best policy well covered by the dataset. Based on the insight, we construct an attack
085   framework called the ‘adversarial reward engineering framework’ such that one can evaluate the
086   efficiency of an attack against universal efficient algorithms in the framework. To find an efficient
087   attack under the framework, the key idea of our attack is to find the high-performing policies covered
088   by the dataset and make them appear low-performing to the agent and vice versa. In this case, the
089   algorithm will believe that some poor-performing policies are near optimal. We build our main attack
090
      method, ‘policy contrast attack,’ based on the idea and theoretically analyze the insight behind it
      based on general assumptions. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first universal black-box
091
      reward poisoning attack in the general offline RL setting. We empirically test the efficiency of our
092
      attack on various standard datasets from the D4RL benchmark (Fu et al., 2020) learned by different
093   state-of-the-art offline RL algorithms (Kumar et al., 2020; Fujimoto & Gu, 2021; Kostrikov et al.,
094   2021). Our attack is efficient in most cases with a limited attack budget. We also show that our attack
095   is not sensitive to the choice of hyper-parameters. One can use our attack to assess the practical
096   robustness of offline RL algorithms. We hope that our work can inspire the development of more
097   robust offline RL algorithms.

186   4     P OLICY C ONTRAST ATTACK
187
188   In this section, we formally define and formulate the universal black-box offline RL attack as
189   an optimization problem. We consider the attacks that are universally effective against any data-
190   driven learning algorithms of high learning efficiency. We highlight the challenges in finding the
191   efficiency of an arbitrary attack and focus on a framework called the ‘adversarial reward engineering
192   framework’ where the behavior of any efficient algorithm becomes predictable under any instance of
193   the framework. Finally, we present our attack algorithm, ‘policy contrast attack,’ which is an efficient
194   instance under the framework.

219

225    Here, we justify that Assumption 4.1 is practical and can represent current efficient offline RL
226    algorithms. It has become common sense that pessimistic learning is the key to efficiency in offline
227    RL Levine et al. (2020). A pessimistic learning algorithm avoids the policies not supported by the
228
       dataset to minimize the chance of reward hacking, that is, outputting a policy that appears promising
       by the dataset but has poor performance. Li et al. (2024) has the same insight in explaining the
229
      ‘survival instinct’ of existing offline RL algorithms against attack: a pessimistic algorithm will stick to
230
       the policies covered by the dataset so that it won’t output any low-performing and out-of-distribution
231    policies regardless of the reward signals.

366   The penalty on the reward is a linear function depending on the distance between an action and the

372   will be a conflict for the aforementioned corruption strategy in this case. Intuitively, these cases
373   are less common as usually good and bad actions should not be similar. In addition, in these cases,
374   a change in reward has a similar influence on the performances of good and bad policies in the
375   adversarial function, suggesting that the corruption in these cases has a limited impact on the ranking
376   of good and bad policies in the adversarial reward. To ensure that the bad policy always performs

390   5.1   P ERFORMANCE OF D IFFERENT ATTACK S TRATEGY
391
392   We test the effectiveness of our attack against current state-of-the-art offline RL algorithms training
393   on the standard D4RL offline RL benchmarkFu et al. (2020). We show that with limited attack
394   budgets, our policy contrast attack is generally more effective.
395   Experiment setup: The offline datasets we choose from the D4RL benchmark include different
396   combinations of environments and types of trajectories. We consider HalfCheetah, Hopper, Walker2d,
397   and Ant environments. We consider the data collected by a single medium policy (medium), a single
398   expert policy (expert), a single random policy (random), a mixture of medium and expert policies
399   (medium-expert), and the replay buffer of a medium policy (medium-replay).
400
      For the learning algorithms, we choose IQL Kostrikov et al. (2021), CQL Kumar et al. (2020), and
401   TD3-BC Fujimoto & Gu (2021), which are frequently used as baselines in current offline RL studies.
402   These algorithms are implemented based on CORL library Tarasov et al. (2022). Recall that the
403   attacker also needs a learning algorithm to learn good and bad policies. To ensure that the attacker
404   has no information about the learning agent, the learning agent and the attacker always use different
405   learning algorithms.
406
      We choose three baseline attacks. The first one is the random invert attack, the only non-trivial attack
407   against offline RL in the current literature. The second and the third ones are the random policy invert
408   (RPI) attack and random policy promote (RPP) attack proposed in Xu et al. (2022) for the online
409   setting, as it is the only existing attack closely related to and can be adapted to work in the offline
410   setting. In the appendix, we show the result of using only one part of the attack strategy from the
411   policy contrast attack.
412
      For the attack budget, following Ye et al. (2023), we always set the budget for B and C to be the
413   amount required by the random inverted reward attack that corrupts 30% of the data. In the ablation
414   study, we tested the influence of different attack budgets on the attack’s efficiency.
415
416
      For the hyper-parameters of the attack, the corruption parameters ∆1 and ∆2 are set to fully utilize
      the budget B, and the attack radius d is set to fully use the budget C. The number of good policies,
417
      i.e., the number of iterations K, is set as 5. In the appendix, we show that the algorithm’s efficiency
418
      is not sensitive to the choice of corruption parameters and the number of good policies. We run 5
419   times for each experiment with different random seeds and show the average result.
420
421   Note that the construction of our attack does not depend on the assumption we made for the learning
422
      algorithms. Although the learning algorithms may not strictly satisfy the assumption of efficient
      learning algorithms, we show that our attack is still effective.
423
424   Main Results: The offline RL algorithms we choose iteratively update their learned policies. To
425   straightforwardly show the effectiveness of attacks, we use the performance of the policy learned
426   by the agent at the end of training to represent its learning efficiency. In the appendix, to intuitively
427   show the details of the training process under the attacks, we show the performance of the policies
428
      learned by the algorithm at each iteration during training. We will also show the full training log for
      the experiments in the ablation studies.
429
430   To cover as diverse offline datasets as possible, here we use only one learning algorithm for each
431   dataset. Next, we will show the case where we test the performance of different learning algorithms
      on the same dataset poisoned by our attack. Table 1 shows the main results of running our policy

      contrast attack in different learning scenarios. The naive baseline is the training with no attack. The
433   ideal effect of the attack is the training with fully inverted rewards, as the learning algorithm tries to
434   learn the worst-performing policy in this case. We observe that our policy contrast attack is usually
435   effective for any combination of datasets and learning algorithms. In contrast, the random inverted
436   attack has almost no effect on the learning result except for the Hopper-Medium Replay dataset,
437   where both attacks are similarly effective. In many cases, our attack is also significantly more efficient
438   than the RPI and RPP baseline attacks.
439   Here, we briefly discuss why the policy contrast attack should be more efficient than other baseline
440   attacks. 1. random inverted attack: At a high level, both the policy contrast and the inverted reward
441   attack want to inverse the agent’s ranking of good and bad policies. The inverted reward attack treats
442   all state actions equally and inverse the performance of all policies. In contrast, the policy contrast
443   attack focuses on the policy and the more important state actions. This could be the reason for making
444   the policy contrast attack more efficient in terms of budget. 2. RPI and RPP attacks: The PCA
445   attack focuses on the supported policies, while the RPI and RPP attacks focus on arbitrary policies.
446   Since the efficient learning algorithm mostly considers only supported policies, the poisoning of such
447
      policies should have more influence on the learning process.

676   B    I NVERTED R EWARD ATTACK
677
678   Here, we discuss the most popular offline reward poisoning attack widely considered in recent RL
679   studies Ye et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2022); Li et al. (2024). We start from an illustrative example
680   first to help better understanding the attack. Following the idea of reward engineering, one can
681   immediately find an attack that can make the agent learn the worst possible policy under Assumption
682   4.1.
683   Definition B.1 (Fully inverted reward attack). The fully inverted reward attack sets R     b = −R as
684   the adversarial reward function where R is the true reward function of the underlying environment
685   behind the dataset. The corresponding attack strategy of the fully inverted reward attack satisfies
686   ∆i = −2 · ri .
687
      The inverted reward attack flips the sign of all the rewards in the dataset. In Theorem B.2 we show
688
      the efficiency of the fully inverted reward attack.
689
690
      Theorem B.2. Under the fully inverted reward attack, the learning agent will learn the policy
      supported by the dataset with nearly the worst performance V ≤ JR (π     P0 ) = minπ∈Πµ JR (π) + δ.
691
      The budgets required by the attack are B = 2 ∗ maxi |ri | and C = 2 ∗ i |ri |.
692
693   Theorem B.2 can be derived from Theorem 4.4 directly. Maximizing the cumulative reward on −R
694   is equivalent to minimizing that on R. Therefore, an efficient learning algorithm will learn the policy
695   with the worst performance on the true reward function among the policies supported by the dataset.
696   Despite achieving the worst outcome for the learner, the inverted reward attack faces the problem of
697   requiring high budgets, which is impractical. Since a practical attack should only corrupt a small
698   portion of data, we consider an attack that can only randomly invert a part of the reward. We call this
699   attack the ‘random inverted reward attack’. This attack strategy has been widely used in previous
700
      works as a non-trivial attack Ye et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2022).
701   Definition B.3 (Random inverted reward attack). The random inverted reward attack has a parameter
      p ∈ (0, 1). The attack randomly samples p · N indexes from [1, . . . , N ] with replacement, where N
