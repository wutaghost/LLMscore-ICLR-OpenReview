009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            There is more and more attention on constrained multi-objective optimization
012            (CMOO) problems, however, most of them are based on gradient-free methods.
013            This paper proposes a constraint gradient-based algorithm for multi-objective op-
014            timization (MOO) problems based on multi-gradient descent algorithms. We first
015            establish a framework for the CMOO problem. Then, we provide a Moreau
016            envelope-based Lagrange Multiplier (MLM-CMOO) algorithm to solve the for-
017
               mulated CMOO problem, and the convergence analysis shows that the proposed
               algorithm convergence to Pareto stationary solutions with a rate of O( √1T ). Fi-
018
019            nally, the MLM-CMOO algorithm is tested on several CMOO problems and has
020
               shown superior results compared to some chosen state-of-the-art designs.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
023
024
      Multi-objective optimization (MOO) is widely used in many real-world application scenarios, such
025   as, in online advertising, the models need to maximize both the Click-Through Rate and the Post-
026   Click Conversion Rate. In MOO, one attempts to simultaneously optimize several, potentially con-
027   flicting functions. MOO has wide applications in all industry sectors where decision-making is
028   involved due to the natural appearance of conflicting objectives or criteria. Applications span across
029   applied engineering, operations management, finance, economics, and social sciences, agriculture,
030   green logistics, and health systems. When the individual objectives are conflicting, no single solu-
031   tion exists that optimizes all of them simultaneously. In such cases, the goal of MOO is then to find
032   Pareto optimal solutions (also known as efficient points), roughly speaking points for which no other
033
      combination of variables leads to a simultaneous improvement in all objectives. The determination
      of the set of Pareto optimal solutions helps decision makers to determine the best trade-offs among
034
      the several competing criteria.
035
036   MOO research can be divided into 2 categories, which are gradient-free and gradient-based methods.
037   For the gradient-free method, people focus on evolutionary and Bayesian MOO algorithms, which
038   are suitable for small-scale problems but less practical for high-dimensional MOO models and can
039
      not provide a convergence guarantee. On the contrary, the gradient-based method can provide a
      convergence guarantee in strongly convex, convex, and non-convex functions for MOO problems
040
      with different assumptions. The CMOO problem in the gradient-free method is well-developed.
041
      However, there is no gradient-based method for the CMOO problem.
042
043   Compared to conventional single-objective optimization, one key difference in MOO is the coupling
044   and potential conflicts between different objective functions. As a result, there may not exist a
045
      common solution that minimizes all objective functions. Rather, the goal in MOO is to find a Pareto
      stationary solution that is not improvable for all objectives without sacrificing some objectives. The
046
      gradient-based method MOO has 2 lines, single-objective transformation, and the conflict gradients
047
      alleviating method, where the latter has garnered more attention in recent years due to their better
048   performances. The single-objective transformation is the first step for the gradient-based method
049   MOO method. It first transfers a MOO problem into a single-objective optimization (SOO) problem
050   with a given fixed coefficient. With the sufficient algorithm in SOO, it is easy to solve. However, this
051   transformation can not give a stable guarantee for the convergence rate as it may give the farthest
052   Pareto front for the given coefficient. Then, the conflict gradients alleviating method is proposed to
053   resolve the conflicting gradients in MOO. However, none of them pay attention to the gradient-based
      CMOO problem.

      Although many gradient-free methods can provide solutions to CMOO problems, very few of them
055   can provide the convergence guarantee. In addition, gradient-free methods are more suitable for
056   small-scale and low-dimension MOO problems, which limits the application of gradient-free algo-
057   rithms. Thus, we provide a Moreau envelope-based Lagrange Multiplier (MLM-CMOO) algorithm
058   for the CMOO problem via the gradient method. Our contributions are summarized as follows.
059
060         • We propose MLM-CMOO, which solves the CMOO problem using the gradient-based
061
              method. MLM-CMOO first divides the CMOO problem into 2 parts, maximizes the min-
              imum decrease across the losses, and makes the decrease obey the constraints. To maxi-
062
              mize the minimum decrease across the losses, we use a similar method in MGDA (Sener
063
              & Koltun, 2018). To limit the decrease, we the Moreau envelope-based proximal gradient
064           method.
065
            • We provide convergence analyses for MLM-CMOO with convex multi-objectives and con-
066
              vex multi-constraints. The convergence rate of MLM-CMOO is O( √1T . )
067
068         • We conduct numerical experiments to verify the effectiveness of MLM-CMOO. The exper-
069           imental results demonstrate the efficiency of the MLM-CMOO.
070

073   in Section 5 and Section 6, respectively.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
076
077
      MOO. MOO algorithms can be grouped into two main categories. The first line of work is gradient-
078
      free methods (e.g., evolutionary MOO algorithms and Bayesian MOO algorithms (Lin et al., 2022;
079   Zhang & Li, 2007; Laumanns & Ocenasek, 2002; Deb et al., 2002; Belakaria et al., 2020)). These
080   methods are more suitable for small-scale problems but less practical for high-dimensional MOO
081   models (e.g., deep neural networks). (Do et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2022) provided the convergence
082   analysis for gradient-free methods to solve MOO problems. The second line of work focuses on
083   gradient-based approaches (Liu & Vicente, 2024; J. Fliege & Vicente, 2019; Momma et al., 2022;
084   Peitz & Dellnitz, 2018; Désidéri, 2012), which are more practical for high-dimensional MOO prob-
085   lems. However, while having received increasing attention from the community in recent years, the
086   Pareto-stationary convergence analysis of these gradient-based MOO methods attracts much more
087
      attention.
088   Various works explored the convergence rates under different assumptions in strongly convex, con-
089   vex, and non-convex functions for MOO problems. Using full gradient, MGD (J. Fliege & Vicente,
090   2019) could achieve tight convergence rates in strongly-convex and non-convex cases, i.e., linear
091   rate O(rT ), r ∈ (0, 1) and sub-linear rate O(1/T ). However, it requires a linear search of the
092   learning rate in the algorithm and sequence convergence ({xt } converges to x∗ ). The linear search
093
      of learning rate is a classic technique but does not fit in gradient-based algorithms in deep learn-
      ing. Moreover, the sequence convergence assumption is too strong. If using a stochastic gradient,
094
      SMGD (Liu & Vicente, 2024) methods make a further complicated case. The stochastic gradient
095
      noise would complicate the analysis. an O(1/T ) rate analysis for SMGD was provided in (Liu &
096   Vicente, 2024) based on rather strong assumptions on a first-moment bound and Lipschtiz continu-
097   ity of common descent direction. On the other hand, (Liu & Vicente, 2024) and (Zhou et al., 2022)
098   showed that the common descent direction provided by the SMGD method is likely to be a biased
099   estimation, which may cause divergence issues. Recently, by utilizing momentum, oCo (Fernando
100   et al., 2024) and CR-MOGM (Zhou et al., 2022) were proposed with corresponding convergence
101   guarantees. (Xiao et al., 2023) utilized a direction-oriented approach by a preference direction.
102   (Yang et al., 2023) proposed a federated MOO algorithm with GD and SGD matching previous
103   centralized MOO algorithms.
104   CMOO. Most existing CMOO research focuses on gradient-free methods. SaE-CMO (Song et al.,
105   2024) proposed a cooperative evolutionary algorithm with a dual-population approach to enhance
106   search progress. PAC-MOO was proposed in (Ahmadianshalchi et al., 2024) based on Bayesian
107   optimization. (Zhang et al., 2024) introduced a dynamic assistant population to search direction for
      CMOO. (Yang et al., 2024) proposed a feasibility tracking strategy to explore all feasible regions

      Constraint Handling Techniques. Penalty function methods, decoders, special operators, and sep-
113
      aration techniques are a simple taxonomy of the constraint handling methods in nature-inspired
114
      optimization algorithms. There are several types of penalty functions used with evolutionary al-
115   gorithms (EAs), the most important ones include (Kramer, 2010) Death penalty, Dynamic penalty,
116   Static penalty, Adaptive penalty, and Stochastic ranking. As an example of decoders, (Koziel &
117   Michalewicz, 1998) proposed a homomorphous mapping (HM) method between an n-dimensional
118   cube and feasible space. The feasible region can be mapped onto a sample space where a population-
119   based algorithm could run a comparative performance (Koziel & Michalewicz, 1998; Kim & Hus-
120   bands, 1998a;b; Koziel & Michalewicz, 1999). However, this method requires high computational
121   costs. A special operator is used to preserve the feasibility of a solution or move within a special
122   region (Michalewicz, 1996; Schoenauer & Michalewicz, 1996; 1997). Nevertheless, this method is
123
      hindered by the initialization of feasible solutions in the initial population, which is challenging with
      highly constrained optimization problems. Unlike the penalty function technique, another approach
124
      separates the values of objective functions and constraints in the nature-inspired algorithms (NIAs)
125
      (Powell & Skolnick, 1993), which is known as the separation of objective function and constraints.
126   The authors of (Hinterding & Michalewicz, 1998) initially proposed dividing the search space into
127   two phases. In the first phase, feasible solutions are found, and optimizing the objective function
128   is considered in the second phase. Representative methods of this type of CHT are the Constraint
129   dominance principle (CDP), Epsilon CHT, and Feasibility rules.

      3.1   M ULTI -O BJECTIVE O PTIMIZATION
138
139   Multi-objective optimization (MOO) is concerned with solving the problems of optimizing multi-
140   objective functions simultaneously, which can be formulated as
141                                                                           ⊤
142                                  min F (x) = (f1 (x), f2 (x), ..., fn (x)) ,                              (1)
                                       x
143
      where fi are real-valued functions, and N represents the set of the total n objectives (n > 2). The
144
      MOO problem is smooth if all objective functions fi are continuously differentiable. Different from
145
      single objective optimization where 2 solutions x, y can be ordered by f (x) < f (y) or f (x) ≥
146   f (y). MOO could have two parameter vectors where one performs better for task i and the other
147   performs better for task j, where i ̸= j. Therefore, Pareto optimality is defined to deal with such an
148   incomparable case.
149
150   Definition 3.1 (Pareto optimality). For any two solutions x1 , x2 ∈ X , we say that x1 dominates x2 ,
151   denoted as x1 ≺ x2 , if fi (x1 ) ≤ fi (x2 ) for all i, and there exists one i such that fi (x1 ) < fi (x2 );
152   otherwise, we say that x1 does not dominate x2 , denoted as x1 ⊀ x2 . A solution x∗ ∈ X is called
153
      Pareto optimal if it is not dominated by any other solution in X .
154
      Note that a set of Pareto optimal solutions is called a Pareto set. The goal of MOO is to find a Pareto
155   optimal solution, which must be Pareto critical (Custódio et al., 2011).
156
157   Definition 3.2 (Pareto criticality). A solution x∗ ∈ X is called Pareto critical if there is no common
158   descent direction d such that ∇fi (x∗ )⊤ d < 0, i ∈ M for all objectives.
159
160   This definition indicates that if x is not Pareto critical, such direction d will be a local descent
161   direction for F at point x. Optimizing through d in the local neighborhood of x can get a better
      solution that dominates x (J. Fliege & Vicente, 2019). Since Pareto criticality reflects the local

165   MOO algorithm (Fliege & Svaiter, 2000; H. Tanabe & Yamashita, 2023).

      5     E XPERIMENTS
387
388   This section introduces the experimental setups, including the datasets and models, multi-objective
389   optimization algorithm setup, and experimental settings. We use multi-task learning experiments to
390   verify the effectiveness of the proposed method. A typical MTL system is given a collection of input
391   points and sets of targets for various tasks per point. A common way to set up the inductive bias
392   across tasks is to design a parametrized hypothesis class that shares some parameters across tasks.
393   One effective solution for MTL is finding solutions that are not dominated by any others, which is
394   the same objective as MOO problems(Sener & Koltun, 2018).
395
396
397   5.1   E XPERIMENTS S ETUP
398
399   1. Datasets and Models. 1). MultiMNIST Datasets and Learning Tasks: We test the convergence
400   performance of MLM-CMOO using the “MultiMNIST” dataset (Sabour et al., 2017), which is a
401   multi-task learning version of the MNIST dataset (LeCun et al., 1998) from LIBSVM repository.
402   Specifically, to convert the hand-written classification problem into a multi-task problem, we ran-
403   domly chose 60000 images. Images are divided into 2 tasks, and each task has m = 30000 samples.
404
      In our experiments, a group of images is positioned in the top left corner, while another group of
      images is positioned in the bottom right. The two tasks are task “L” (to categorize the top-left digit)
405
      and task “R” (to classify the bottom-right digit). The overall problem is to classify the images of
406
      different tasks. All algorithms use the same randomly generated initial point. The learning rates are
407   chosen as η = β = α = 0.01. we directly apply existing single-task MNIST models.
408
409   2). CelebA Dataset and Learning Tasks: We utilize the CelebA dataset (Liu et al., 2015), consist-
410
      ing of 200K facial images annotated with 40 attributes. We approach each attribute as a binary
      classification task, resulting in a 40-way multi-task learning (MTL) problem. To create a shared rep-
411
      resentation function, we implement ResNet-18 (He et al., 2016) without the final layer, attaching a
412
      linear layer to each attribute for classification. In this experiment, we set η = 0.0005, α = β = 0.1.
413
414   3). River Flow Dataset and Learning Tasks: We further test our algorithms on MOO problems of
415   larger sizes. In this experiment, we use the River Flow dataset (Nie et al., 2017), which is for flow
416
      prediction flow at eight locations within the Mississippi River network. Thus, there are eight tasks
      in this problem. In this experiment, we set η = 0.001, α = β = 0.1. To better visualize 8 different
417
      tasks, we illustrate the normalized loss in radar charts.
418
419   2. Baseline. The NSGA-II (Deb et al., 2002) and PSL (Lin et al., 2022) are considered as our base-
420   lines. NSGA-II is a well-known MOO evolutionary algorithm, while PSL is a novel MOO Bayesian
421   optimization algorithm. NSGA-II and PSL do not handle constraints. For a fair comparison, we
422
      report the Pareto optimal solutions satisfying constraints generated from NSGA-II and PSL, thus
      NSGA-II and PSL can work towards the Pareto optimal solutions without considering constraints.
423
424   NSGA-II Setup. For binary chromosomes, we apply a single-point crossover with a probability
425   of 0.9 and a bit-flip mutation with a probability of 0.1. For real-valued chromosomes, we apply
426   a simulated binary crossover (SBX) [14] with a probability of 0.9 and nc = 2 and a polynomial
427   mutation with a probability of 0.1 and nm = 20, where nc and nm denote spread factor distribution
428
      indices for crossover and mutation, respectively.
429   PSL Setup. We follow literature (Lin et al., 2022) to set PSL parameters. At each iteration, we train
430   the Pareto set model hθ with 1000 update steps using Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 1e−5
431   and no weight decay. At each iteration, we generate 1000 candidate solutions using hθ and select
      the population size of solutions from the 1000 candidates.

473   6     C ONCLUSION
474
475   This paper studies the constrained multi-objective optimization problem. We first establish a frame-
476   work for the CMOO problem, which is suitable for gradient descent algorithms. For the formulated
477   CMOO problem, we use the Lagrange Multiplier method to make a decrease in the overall objec-
478   tive obey the constraints. Then, due to the non-smoothness of the coefficient function, we use a
479   Moreau envelope to make it smooth. Next, the convergence analysis shows that the proposed algo-
480   rithm (MLM-CMOO) convergence to Pareto stationary solutions with a rate of O( √1T ). Finally, we
481   conduct experiments to verify the effectiveness of the MLM-CMOO algorithm.
