009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            We consider the problem of length generalization in sequence prediction. We de-
012            fine a new metric of performance in this setting – the Asymmetric-Regret– which
013            measures regret against a benchmark predictor with longer context length than
014            available to the learner. We continue by studying this concept through the lens of
015
               the spectral filtering algorithm. We present a gradient-based learning algorithm
               that provably achieves length generalization for linear dynamical systems. We
016
               conclude with proof-of-concept experiments which are consistent with our theory.

019   1    I NTRODUCTION
020
021   Sequence prediction is fundamental to machine learning, with applications in NLP, forecasting, and
022   control systems. In this setting, a learner observes a sequence of tokens and iteratively predicts the
023   next token, suffering a loss that measures the discrepancy between the predicted and the true token.
024   Predicting sequences is vital for tasks like language modeling and autonomous control.
025   A key challenge in sequence prediction is understanding the role of context length—the number
026   of previous tokens used to make the upcoming prediction—and designing predictors that perform
027   well with limited context due to computational and memory constraints. These resource constraints
028   become particularly significant during the training phase of a predictor, where the computational
029   cost of using long sequences can be prohibitive. Thus, designing predictors that learn from shorter
030   contexts but generalize to longer ones is crucial. This leads us to the central question of our investiga-
031
      tion: Can we develop algorithms that learn effectively using short contexts but perform comparably
      to models that use longer contexts?
032
033   To address this question, we introduce a new performance metric—Asymmetric-Regret—which
034   measures the difference in total prediction loss between an online predictor with limited context
035   length and a benchmark predictor with a longer context. Unlike classical regret, which assumes both
036   the learner and the benchmark operate under the same conditions, Asymmetric-Regret accounts for
037
      the asymmetry in context lengths, providing a more realistic assessment of performance in resource-
      constrained settings. With a formal and well-defined notion of Asymmetric-Regret in hand, we
038
      begin our investigation with the following question: are there algorithms that can attain non-trivial
039
      bounds on the Asymmetric-Regret for natural sequences?
040
041   We explore this concept through the lens of spectral filtering algorithms (Hazan et al., 2017b; 2018).
042   Spectral filtering has emerged as a robust method for learning linear dynamical systems when the
043
      system is unknown and the hidden state is unobserved. Linear dynamical systems are a useful and
      rich class to study. Although they are applicable in many domains, they have been particularly
044
      useful in large language modeling applications. Since next-token generation is a sequence predic-
045
      tion problem, these methods are naturally applicable as a building block to use as layers in LLMs.
046   Methods which are designed to solve sequence prediction in linear dynamical systems have been
047   used to design state space models which have achieved SOTA performance on many LLM tasks,
048   with efficiency gains during both training and inference Gu et al. (2021b); Poli et al. (2023); Gu &
049   Dao (2023).pSpectral filtering was introduced in Hazan et al. (2017a) as a method which provably
050   achieves O( T ) regret when compared with the best LDS predictor (without any assumptions on
051   the sequence data). Beyond their theoretically sound properties, spectral filtering-based predictors
052   have proven practical in recent applications. Notably, the Spectral Transform Unit (Agarwal et al.,
053   2023), a neural architecture built using spectral filtering, has recently shown promise on sequence
      prediction over a range of modalities (Liu et al., 2024).

056   learning algorithm for spectral filtering and show that we can learn and make predictions on a smaller

060
      Beyond theoretical interest, our work is practically motivated by challenges in length generalization
      faced by large language models (LLMs). As previously mentioned, methods which emerged from
061
      studying linear dynamical systems have proven useful in LLMs, including spectral filtering and the
062
      Spectral Transform Unit. LLMs struggle to generalize to longer sequences than those seen during
063   training (Abbe et al., 2023; Anil et al., 2022; Jelassi et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023; Delétang et al.,
064   2022; Dziri et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024) and extensive empirical research has been dedicated to
065   addressing this (Kazemnejad et al., 2024; Shen et al., 2023; Dai, 2019; Chi et al., 2022; Li et al.,
066   2023; Press et al., 2021). Despite its importance and extensive empirical research, provable theo-
067   retical results on length generalization remain largely elusive. We view our work as a step toward
068   addressing this gap. The asymmetric regret bounds we establish in this paper imply that spectral
069   filtering is able to implicitly handle the difficult problem of deciding how to use and store tokens
070   much earlier in a sequence for next-token prediction. Since most empirical methods introduced to
071
      improve length generalization are task-specific, this is an exciting feature. It suggests that simply
      incorporating spectral filtering into neural architectures may have the potential to improve length
072
      generalization.

184   as online gradient descent (see e.g. Hazan et al. (2016)). Our new notion of Asymmetric-Regret
185   incorporates asymmetric information access between the online learner and the benchmark class.

188
189   In the online sequence prediction setting the predictor iteratively receives input ut and makes
190   prediction ŷt of the output, after which the true output yt is revealed. The goal is to minimize error
191   according to a given (convex Lipschitz) loss function `t (yt , ŷt ).
192
      In online learning, we usually do not make statistical assumptions about the generation of the input
193
      sequence. As such, performance is measured relative to a certain benchmark class of predictors. A
194
      prediction algorithm A is measured by regret, or difference in total loss, vs. a class of reference
195   predictors ⇧ref (such as linear predictors), i.e.

199   This formulation is valid for online sequence prediction of any signal. We are particularly interested
200   in signals that are generated by dynamical systems. A time-invariant linear dynamical system is
201   given by the dynamics equations
202                           xt+1 = Axt + But + wt , yt+1 = Cxt + Dut + ⇣t ,                               (1)

206   Many methods exist for linear dynamical systems and their performance guarantees rely heavily on
207
      the spectrum of A. The system is unstable whenever | max (A)| > 1 because the norm of the obser-

211   the system matrices require knowledge of the dimension of the hidden state (which may be very

213   zation and convex relaxation, however, the spectral filtering algorithm is able to efficiently predict
214   observations from marginally-stable systems with sublinear regret. We provide more background
215   on spectral filtering in Section 2.2, and more details on the rich theory of linear dynamical systems
      may be found in Hazan et al. (2020).

      So far, we have demonstrated length generalization of spectral filtering on linear systems: when
465
      trained with a shorter context length of T q it is able to compete with methods that have access to the
466
      full context T (even on marginally-stable systems that can have arbitrarily large effective memory
467   lengths). This length generalization property is most crucial in deep learning applications, in which
468   multi-layer models are stacked (with added nonlinearities) to solve non-LDS sequence prediction
469   tasks.
470
      As an empirical proof-of-concept to demonstrate that STU’s length generalization capability extends
471
      to this regime, we evaluate it on the induction heads synthetic sequence modeling task, which is
472
      commonplace in the language modeling literature (see Gu & Dao (2023)) and was experimentally
473   shown in Liu et al. (2024) to be efficiently solved by a two-layer STU. In the induction heads task,
474   the model is required to recall one token (sampled uniformly from a vocabulary) immediately after
475   a special flag token; the rest of the sequence consists of the same special blank token, which the
476   model should learn to ignore.
477
      The STU architecture we use is composed of an embedding layer, two “tensordot” STU layers with
478
      MLPs and ReLU nonlinearities, and an output projection layer (the same as in Liu et al. (2024))
479   with filters of length T = 256.
480
481   Following prior STU architecture implementations we use no autoregressive components, and so
482
      any length generalization observed here comes directly from the filtering mechanism itself. We train
      these models until convergence with a tuned Adam optimizer and various context lengths T q . The
483
      vocabulary size is set to 4.

      5       D ISCUSSION
514
515
      In review, we first introduced the notion of Asymmetric-Regret as a way to describe length gen-
516   eralization through the lens of online learning and regret minimization in games. We then proved
517   that the class of spectral filtering predictors naturally enjoys sublinear Asymmetric-Regret thereby
518   exhibiting length generalization without any change to the algorithm, albeit with some restrictions
519   on the underlying data (i.e. the spectrum of A). We introduced a new variant of spectral filtering
520   which uses two autoregressive components and achieves length generalization which is more robust
521   to the assumptions of the underlying data. Next, we used experiments on synthetic data generated
522   by an LDS to demonstrate the validity and sharpness of our theory and provided proof-of-concept
523   length generalization experiments on a synthetic nonlinear sequence prediction task.
524   Our theoretical results and initial empirical findings reveal that some type of length generalization
525   comes naturally with the spectral filtering algorithm. This result implies that spectral filtering is
526   powerful in its ability to learn the dynamics of a complicated underlying system with long memory
527   – it naturally handles the issue of what aspects in a sequence should be memorized for the future
528   and what aspects can be forgotten, whereas many existing methods are hand engineered depending
529   on the specific task. This adds to the already-exciting list of its useful (and provable) properties, in-
530
      cluding: robustness to systems with long memory and large hidden dimension, efficient training via
      convolutions, optimization convexity, and the existence of good parameter-efficient approximations.
531
      Given recent successful applications of spectral filtering as the building block for STU models in
532
      deep learning (Agarwal et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024), it would be valuable to research how to best
533   take advantage of their length generalization capacity at scale – we leave this for future work.
