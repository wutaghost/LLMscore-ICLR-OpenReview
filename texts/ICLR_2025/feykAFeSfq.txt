009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Keyboards are a key interface between humans and computers, with character
012            arrangements offering numerous layout possibilities. Many existing designs fol-
013            low standardized ergonomic principles and explore Pareto-optimality in multi-
014            objective functions using metaheuristics or deep learning. In this work, we pro-
015            pose a reinforcement learning-based approach to designing optimized keyboard
016
               layouts that integrate both technical and ergonomic considerations. Our results
               demonstrate that reinforcement learning optimization can produce layouts more
017
               efficiently than conventional designs, such as the ”QWERTY” keyboard. Specifi-
018
               cally, our approach achieves approximately an 12.4% improvement in ergonomic
019            parameters over traditional keyboards, underscoring the potential for a more data-
020            driven, systematic approach to keyboard layout optimization.

023   1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025   keyboard is an essential device that is used to interact with computer systems. Different types of
026   keyboards exist, such as physical keyboards we interface with computers and laptops (Lugay et al.,
027   2022) as well as soft keyboards (Han & Kim, 2015) such as touchscreen-based keyboards, virtual
028
      keyboards, etc (Fennedy et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2022). The layout, i.e., the arrangement of characters
      in these keyboards, may vary depending on different layout models and factors. Typing efficiency
029
      also differs according to different keyboard layouts (Deshwal & Deb, 2006). A poor arrangement of
030
      characters in the keyboard layout may put a high load on weaker fingers along with typing discomfort
031   resulting in fatigue for the users (Deshwal & Deb, 2006). Hence, optimized arrangement of the
032   characters in any type, language-based keyboard is necessary for comfortable as well as efficient
033   writing. In English keyboard layouts, ’QWERTY’ is the most established and familiar layout so
034   far (Khan & Deb, 2023), proposed by the Sholes brothers in 1873 (Eggers et al., 2003). However,
035   several researches have shown that ’QWERTY’ is not an optimized layout. Khan & Deb (2023)
036   and Oladeinde et al. have discussed its shortcomings in details. Oladeinde et al. shows that novice
037   typists make fewer errors in Dvorak and alphabetic keyboards compared to ’QWERTY’ as well
038   as the efficiency in these layouts can reach the level of ’QWERTY’ if their usage is continued in
039
      novice typists. However, the popularity and usage of ’QWERTY’ has sustained all other optimized
      layouts till now. Despite that optimization of keyboard layout is an active research field where the
040
      fundamental objective is usually to optimize the efficiency of typing (T.G. et al., 2018; Ghosh et al.,
041
      2011; Khan & Deb, 2023). This is necessary as the typing efficiency will decrease if the frequent
042   characters are not found within the accessible range.
043
044   A vast amount of research has been done on optimizing physical keyboard layout considering dif-
045
      ferent approaches while applying various optimization algorithms such as genetic algorithms, sim-
      ulated annealing, Ant colony Optimization (ACO), etc, (Glover, 1987; Light & Anderson, 1993;
046
      Oommen et al., 1989; Liao & Choe, 2013; Khorshid et al., 2010). Moreover, the improvement on
047
      the soft keyboard’s layout has also been investigated as suggested by the works of Lewis et al.;
048   MacKenzie & Zhang (1999); Zhai et al. (2000). Focus has been given to both normal and am-
049   biguous keyboards (Deshwal & Deb, 2006). In the case of normal keyboards, importance has been
050   given to mapping the respective language’s characters on the keys efficiently, resulting in ease of
051   use for the typists and typing efficiency. Different ergonomic designs and their optimization have
052   been explored in ambiguous keyboards (Deshwal & Deb, 2006; Lesher et al., 1998). In recent stud-
053   ies, emphasis has been given to optimizing layouts in different soft keyboards also, such as virtual
      keyboards (Ghosh et al., 2011), touchscreen keyboards as well as gesture-based typing (Dunlop &

054
      Levine, 2012; Bi et al., 2014; Smith et al., 2015; Oulasvirta et al., 2013), single-finger keyboard
055   layouts on smartphones (Herthel & Subramanian, 2020; Turner et al., 2020), etc. While optimizing
056   the keyboard layouts in previous works, many articles focused on a single objective function for
057   optimization. This approach can find an efficient keyboard but usually results in very dissimilar to
058   the ’QWERTY’ layout as indicated by the works of Eggers et al. (2003); Liao & Choe (2013); Khor-
059   shid et al. (2010). Such layouts while efficient can be difficult for users to track and learn compared
060   to conventional ’QWERTY’ layouts. To solve this issue, Khan & Deb (2023) considered a multi-
061   objective optimization approach while generating efficient layouts. They focused on two objective
062   functions: maximizing typing efficiency by considering the cumulative distance between consecu-
063
      tive keystrokes while typing large texts and another is maximizing similarity to ’QWERTY’. Khan
      & Deb (2023) applied the NSGA-II algorithm to produce a Pareto set of layouts ranging from highly
064
      optimized ones to more similar to ’QWERTY’ while being less optimized ones. Bi et al. (2010) used
065
      the metropolis energy minimization algorithm to generate a quasi-qwerty and optimized version of
066   soft keyboards. They found that characters moving one key away from their key positions compared
067   to the ’QWERTY’ achieved efficient layout as well as remained similar to ’QWERTY’. Nivasch
068   & Azaria (2021) proposed a deep learning model with a genetic algorithm to optimize for layout
069   of the keyboard. Similar works such as Bi & Zhai (2016); Zhai & Kristensson (2008) conducted
070   optimization of ’QWERTY’ while maintaining similarity to it.
071
      However, while designing and optimizing the keyboard layout, proper ergonomic criteria need to be
072   considered. These criteria should be properly mathematically modeled and validated considering the
073   user’s interactions with the keyboards, typing comfort, etc. Ergonomic criteria can be a collection
074   of heuristic rules derived from experimental studies and human-keyboard interactions such as rules
075   proposed by Marsan (1976). These sets of rules can work as multiple objectives that need to be
076   optimized to get an efficient layout. Eggers et al. (2003) was one of the first to consider such
077   sets of ergonomic criteria provided by Marc Oliver Wagner & Eggers (2003) and applied the Ant
078   colony optimization algorithm to develop an optimized and ergonomic-supported keyboard layout.
079   However, the ergonomics criteria are not straightforward, and achieving optimal layouts utilizing
080
      such criteria is still an active research field. Previous optimization algorithms have utilized different
      ergonomic criteria but further optimization needs to be explored (Eggers et al., 2003; Khan & Deb,
081
      2023; Bi et al., 2010). Conversely, these ergonomic criteria are not always validated in previous
082
      works and need to be addressed from a technical perspective. Furthermore, many works focused on
083   optimizing layout only considering a combinational problem. However, keeping similarities to the
084   ’QWERTY’ layout as well as the optimized version is one of the recent trends in keyboard layout
085   optimization research as discussed above.
086
      To address these aforementioned challenges, we propose a novel keyboard layout optimization
087
      method called, ’LayoutRL’ that uses Reinforcement Learning (RL) to achieve high optimality in
088
      the ergonomics criteria. We consider the six ergonomics criteria from Eggers et al. (2003); Marc
089   Oliver Wagner & Eggers (2003) while using an RL algorithm to design an optimal keyboard layout.
090   RL has the ability to find the optimal conditions of all the objective functions while generating the
091   desired system. Additionally, we show that LayoutRL can achieve an optimized yet similar layout
092   to the ’QWERTY. Furthermore, we explored the contribution of these six ergonomic criteria and
093   discussed their validity in constructing an optimized keyboard layout.

102   the layout. Finally, we discuss the LayoutRL optimization process and its algorithm to understand

199   2.2   D ESIGN C HOICES
200
201   While optimizing the layout using our proposed method, we set some design constraints while using
202
      RL to keep similarity with the ’QWERTY’ layout and the best layout generated from ’LayoutRL’.
      Though this constraint limits high-level optimization of the state space freely, it has certain appeals
203
      due to the general acceptance of the ’QWERTY’ to mass keyboard users. Our model maintains the
204
      following constrains in the design phase:
205
206          • Restrictions on the placement of alphabet and non-alphabet
207            characters: In the ’QWERTY’ layout, the English alphabets and non-alphabet num-
208            bers are placed in separate spaces by grouping. We enforce our algorithm to do the same
209            by putting restrictions in state space while assigning them a character. For example, the
210            non-alphabet characters can be placed only in the orange-colored keys only, as specified in
211            Figure 1. Conversely, the alphabet characters can be assigned only on the black-colored
212            keys as demonstrated in Figure 1. This way we try to ensure more similarities between our
213
               best layouts with the ’QWERTY’ layout.
214          • Static placement of Shift, Alt, and Space button: We considered
215            fixed positions of Shift, Alt, and Space buttons on the same keys ’QWERTY’ has and
               do not include in the optimization algorithm.

222   2.3   M ARKOV D ECISION P ROCESS FOR L AYOUT RL
223
224   We utilized a Markov decision process (MDP) formulation for optimizing the ’QWERTY’ layout.
225   Following are the components of the Markov Decision Process (MDP) model for LayoutRL.
226
            • State Space: We consider each possible alphabet-key pair, along with all other as-
227
              signed and unassigned keys on the keyboard constitute the states, S. There are 95 unique
228           characters, including capital and small alphabets and special characters, allocated to 96
229           keys. The possible state space can have approximately 96P95 different states. Due to this
230           large state space, it is computationally unfeasible to check every possible combination to
231           find the best solution. We consider the ’shift + key’ as separate keys on the keyboard.
232         • Actions: An action, a ∈ A in our MDP problem is defined as mapping an alphabet to
233           one of the 96 keys. While mapping, the algorithm checks whether the chosen key is already
234           occupied.
235         • Episode: Each episode, E consists of 95 actions taken by the agent, resulting in a com-
236           plete keyboard layout with the specified number of alphabet-key pairs. After each episode,
237           the generated keyboard is evaluated based on the six ergonomic criteria discussed above,
238           and a score is saved for use in subsequent episodes.
239         • Agent: The agent in this context creates a keyboard layout based on the available keys
240           and alphabets. The decision to create an alphabet-key pair is based on two factors: the
241           availability of the key and the weight associated with the key. To maximize the reward, the
242           agent selects the key with the highest value for a specific alphabet.
243         • Reward (r): For each action, a, the agent attempts to create an alphabet-key pair with the
244           highest weight. Initially, the reward for every pair is considered uniform, leading to random
245           allocations. Over several episodes, the state/action value matrix (weight matrix) is updated
246           after evaluating the return for each of the generated keyboards.
247         • Return: At the end of each episode, the performance of the newly generated keyboard
248           is evaluated by typing out a standard corpus and considering 6 ergonomic criteria. This
249           evaluation compares the typing cost of the new keyboard against a set number of previous
250           episodes. The return is determined by the rate of improvement in the new layout and can
251           be both positive and negative. The return also influences changes in the state/action value
252
              matrix for that specific layout.
253         • State transition probabilities (p(s, r|s, a)): The state transition in this
254           model is non-deterministic. The probability of state transition is determined by the
255           state/action value matrix, where the weights of key allocation for an alphabet are evalu-
256
              ated, and the key with the highest weight/probability is chosen.
257         • Discount Factor (γ): The agent takes actions by assigning a key to an alphabet and
258           moves to another alphabet for assigning. However, since we formulate the policy to update
259
              based on the total return after a complete episode, we conclude that state changes have no
              significant benefits and set the discount factor, γ to zero.
260
261         • Policy (π): The ultimate objective is to learn the optimal policy (π ∗ ) that will determine
262
              the optimal mapping of alphabets to different states. We learn the policy for our MDP
              from the total cost of the layout after each episode. Initially, a random/equiprobable policy
263
              is used for mapping alphabets to keys. After each episode, we compare the total cost of
264
              the present layout to the previous optimized layout. If the total cost reduces, we treat
265           the present layout as the optimized one and increase the corresponding state/action value.
266           Conversely, we decrease the state/action value if the total cost increases. The state/action
267           value will eventually converge, providing the optimal policy.

308

309
      in the matrix. This way, we can converge the state/action matrix to determine the optimal policy and
310   get an optimized layout then the ’QWERTY’ layout. Furthermore, we can set the base layout as the
311   newly generated optimized layout and continue repeating the episodes to find the more optimized
312   layout.

509   3.4   D ISCUSSION
510
511   In this work, we demonstrate how Reinforcement learning can be used to optimize keyboard layout.
512   Although various optimization algorithms have been used in this particular problem, we are the first
513
      to use the RL approach to solve for optimization. Our proposed method, ’LayoutRL’ can optimize
      the keyboard layout and generate various optimized layouts over many standard keyboards such
514
      as ’QWERTY’, ’Colemak’, etc. Furthermore, the research on such optimization problems has indi-
515
      cated the popularity of ’QWERTY’ and its familiarity with users. This led many researchers to focus
516   on proposing layouts having close similarities to the ’QWERTY’. We adopted this constraint in our
517   method also by dividing the state space into two sections for alphabets and non-alphabets. This
518   ’QWERTY’ like-similar properties addition to our algorithm enhances the usefulness of the best
519   layouts from ’LayoutRL’ because it enables these layouts to be deployed in real life to the users, not
520   just from a theoretical perspective. Additionally, we address the ergonomic perspective considered
521   in different keyboard optimization problems in this work. Six ergonomics criteria have been used in
522   various keyboard layout optimization problems (Eggers et al., 2003; Deshwal & Deb, 2006). While
523   LayoutRL optimizes the keyboard layouts utilizing these six criteria, we further explored these cri-
524
      teria by comparing their individual costs for different standard layouts. We believe this problem
      can be further optimized by incorporating aspects like unigrams, bigrams, sequence frequency of
525
      characters in the English language and ergonomic criteria along with their relative weights.
526
527
528   4     C ONCLUSION
529
530   We introduced Reinforcement Learning (RL) for the first time to optimize layouts for English key-
531   boards. Our approach integrates ergonomic criteria and practical design considerations to generate
532   optimized layouts. The best layouts produced by our method demonstrate significant improvements
533
      over the traditional ’QWERTY’ layout and other conventional designs. Our results indicate that lay-
      outs generated by ‘LayoutRL’ achieve approximately 12.4% lower costs than ‘QWERTY’. More-
534
      over, these layouts outperform conventional ones in terms of cost-efficiency when simulated with a
535
      standard English corpus. This RL-based optimization effectively generates highly efficient keyboard
536   layouts and can be extended to various design contexts, including touchscreen devices, smartphones,
537   virtual keyboards, and gesture typing. The broad applicability of our method highlights its robust-
538   ness. In future work, we plan to explore the use of similar RL-based optimization techniques for
539   keyboard layouts on a wide range of digital devices.
