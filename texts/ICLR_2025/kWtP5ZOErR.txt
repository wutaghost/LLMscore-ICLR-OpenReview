009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               The high computational costs of large language models (LLMs) have led to
012            a flurry of research on LLM compression, via methods such as quantization,
013            sparsification, or structured pruning. A new frontier in this area is given by
014            dynamic, non-uniform compression methods, which adjust the compression levels
015            (e.g., sparsity) per-block or even per-layer in order to minimize accuracy loss,
016            while guaranteeing a global compression threshold. Yet, current methods rely
017            on heuristics for identifying the “importance” of a given layer towards the loss,
018            based on assumptions such as error monotonicity, i.e. that the end-to-end model
019            compression error is proportional to the sum of layer-wise errors. In this paper, we
020
               revisit this area, and propose a new and general approach for dynamic compres-
               sion that is provably optimal in a given input range. We begin from the motivating
021
               observation that, in general, error monotonicity does not hold for LLMs: com-
022
               pressed models with lower sum of per-layer errors can perform worse than models
023            with higher error sums. To address this, we propose a new general evolutionary
024            framework for dynamic LLM compression called EvoPress, which has provable
025            convergence, low sample and evaluation complexity. We show that these theo-
026            retical guarantees lead to highly competitive practical performance for dynamic
027            compression of Llama, Mistral and Phi models. Via EvoPress, we set new state-of-
028            the-art results across all compression approaches: structural pruning (block/layer
029            dropping), unstructured sparsity, as well as quantization with dynamic bitwidths.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Model compression has become a standard way of reducing the deployment costs of large language
034
      models (LLMs). Current post-training compression techniques can be roughly categorized into
035
      quantization-based, which reduce the bit-width of weights or activations, e.g. (Frantar et al., 2022;
036   Lin et al., 2023; Dettmers & Zettlemoyer, 2022; Tseng et al., 2024), pruning-based, which sparsify
037   the weight matrices, e.g. (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023; Yin et al., 2024), or structured pruning / layer
038   dropping, which drop entire model components, e.g. (Kim et al., 2024; Men et al., 2024). While
039   constantly improving their performance, existing compression methods are reaching diminishing
040   returns in terms of accuracy-vs-compression (Dettmers et al., 2023; Tseng et al., 2024).
041
      In this context, a new direction is dynamic, or non-uniform, layer-wise compression, in which
042   different layers can be compressed to various levels, according to their “sensitivity” relative to the
043   model output. Dynamic compression allows to maximize model accuracy while satisfying a given
044   compression requirement, e.g. a target model size. Instance-specific solutions for this problem
045   have already been proposed for essentially every compression type: sparsity (Yin et al., 2024),
046   quantization (Frantar & Alistarh, 2022), or layer dropping (Kim et al., 2024; Men et al., 2024).
047   Broadly, these approaches work by assigning an error/sensitivity score function to each layer and
048   compression level, which measures the impact of its compression on output loss increase. Then, one
049   calculates a compression assignment which minimizes the sum of error scores, while still satisfying
050
      the global compression constraint. Thus, such approaches inherently assume error monotonicity:
      i.e., that a lower sum of error scores implies a lower compression error for the entire model.
051
052   Our work starts from the observation that error monotonicity does not hold generally for LLM
053   compression: specifically, there are instances where compressed models with lower sums of per-
      layer errors can perform worse than models with higher error. We illustrate this fact in Table 1,

067   Contribution. This refutation of error monotonicity implies that most prior approaches, which are
068   based on this assumption, can lead to sub-optimal solutions. Thus, it motivates our investigation
069   of alternatives towards optimal non-uniform compression. For this, we propose a new evolutionary
070   search approach called EvoPress, which is provably convergent, and is also sample and iteration
071   efficient. Thus, EvoPress is the first non-uniform compression method with guarantees; its two ef-
072   ficiency properties are critical for practicality in the context of LLMs, where the cost of evaluating
073   single models (“offspring”) is exceedingly high. We validate the approach across all three pop-
074   ular approaches for post-training LLM compression: layer dropping, one-shot sparsification, and
075
      quantization. We find that EvoPress consistently improves upon existing techniques, with major
      improvements at higher compression ratios.
076
077   In more detail, we assume a setting where we are given a pre-trained model, a compression constraint
078   such as the target model size, a set of compression options (e.g., 10 possible sparsity options per
079   layer), and aim to identify a per-layer assignment which satisfies the constraint, while minimizing
080   accuracy loss, measured in perplexity or in-context learning accuracy degradation. As is standard,
081
      e.g. (Frantar & Alistarh, 2022), from the compression options we build a level database, where each
      layer is compressed independently to each compression option. During the candidate search, our
082
      offspring are models stitched together from the level database, and our fitness function will be the
083
      difference (e.g., in KL-divergence) between the outputs of the offspring and the original model, on
084   a set of calibration samples.
085
086   At each step, our search algorithm starts with a single search point (candidate model), and generates
087
      a constant λ ≥ 1 additional offspring, by applying a mutation operation which preserves the com-
      pression constraint. The selection stage is composed of multiple steps, where we iteratively evaluate
088
      the offspring and parent on increasingly many randomly chosen samples. For instance, we may start
089
      to evaluate the parent and λ = 64 offspring on less than a single sample on the first sub-step, but
090   progressively multiply the number of calibration samples as we sift through candidates, reducing
091   variance as we obtain more competitive offspring. We found this trade-off between exploration and
092   evaluation variance essential for efficiency on LLMs, as it drastically reduces our total number of
093   evaluations relative to the case where all the initial offspring must be evaluated on a full batch.
094
      Our algorithm guarantees convergence: specifically, any linear fitness function1 defined on the n-
095
      dimensional hypercube will be maximized in expected O(k(n − k)/λ) generations under the con-
096   straint |x|1 = k, where λ is the number of offspring. The proof is quite non-trivial, as it needs to
097   adapt stochastic drift analysis techniques, via a novel potential function, to the case where multiple
098   offspring are examined in each sub-step. In Figure 1, we illustrate the algorithm’s fast convergence
099   and high efficiency on a practical example with correlated block dropping on Llama-3-8B, where we
100   determined the optimum via (expensive) exhaustive search: EvoPress is able to reach the optimum
101   in only 6 generations, using a total of only 56 model evaluations.
102   A key advantage of our approach is that it is agnostic of the model architecture and compression
103   type. We illustrate this in our experimental results, which are the first to span all three compres-
104   sion methods, across different LLM families. Specifically, results show that EvoPress significantly
105   improves upon all prior work on depth pruning in terms of accuracy-vs-compression, especially

      2   R ELATED W ORK
115
      To our knowledge, we are the first to present a unified approach which covers all types of
116
      post-training LLM compression (i.e., layer dropping / depth pruning and non-uniform pruning /
117
      quantization)–so far, these problems have generally been approached independently.
118
119   Depth Pruning. Recently, there has been a lot of interest in compression by removing entire
120   Transformer blocks, both for efficiency and to gain insights about the language model itself. Most
121
      methods are based on scoring the importance of each block, and then maximizing the importance
      of the resulting model by removing the blocks of lowest importance. Weight Subcloning (Samragh
122
      et al., 2023) proposed a multi-step process to find good initializations for an untrained smaller
123
      model given an already trained larger one, where the importance of each block is scored based on
124   the ratio of ℓ2 norms between the output embeddings of the block with and without the residual
125   connection. Shortened Llama (Kim et al., 2024) proposes scoring each block by measuring the
126   perplexity after removing the respective block from the full model. ShortGPT (Men et al., 2024)
127   uses the cosine similarity between the input and output embeddings of each block to assess its
128   importance. By contrast, Gromov et al. (2024) restrict themselves to removing consecutive blocks,
129   and score each of these removal configurations using cosine similarity.
130   Non-Uniform Pruning and Quantization. He et al. (2018); Ashok et al. (2018) were among the
131   first to consider automatic optimization of non-uniform compression, specifically for the case of
132   pruning, where developed Reinforcement Learning (RL)-based approaches. However, both ap-
133   proaches suffer from high tuning complexity and would be very hard to scale to large models.
134   Follow-up work (Hubara et al., 2021; Yao et al., 2021; Li et al., 2021) considered a similar prob-
135   lem specifically for quantization, but explore computationally-expensive solvers (e.g. ILPs) which
136   rely on the fact that quantization has only a small number of choices (precision levels) per layer.
137   SPDY (Frantar & Alistarh, 2022) considered a unified framework which reduces the problems to
138
      knapsack-type instances, and solves them optimally modulo discretization. However, SPDY ex-
      plicitly relies on monotonicity and linearity assumptions on the dependency between the per-layer
139
      errors and model output error, which we find not to hold on large models, especially in the high-
140
      compression regime (e.g., below 3 bits per parameter). Relative to SPDY, EvoPress provides guar-
141   antees for a much broader class of input functions, and focuses on efficiency for LLM compression.
142
143   The recent OWL method (Yin et al., 2024) focuses on non-uniform pruning of LLMs, and provides
144
      consistent improvements over uniform profiles via a layer scoring system which analyzes the acti-
      vation outlier structure, but does not have any theoretical guarantees. Experimentally, we find that
145
      OWL is effective especially for Llama-family models (Touvron et al., 2023) and at moderate sparsi-
146
      ties, but observe significant gaps in favor of EvoPress across all models and compression levels.
147
148   NAS and Structural Pruning. Random search is also popular in the context of structural pruning
149   and Neural Architecture Search (NAS) (Chen et al., 2020; Dong et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2020; Xu
150
      et al., 2021; Yin et al., 2021; Molchanov et al., 2022; Kurtić et al., 2024). However, such methods
      also rely heavily on re-training and have notoriously high costs, which limits their applicability to
151
      post-training compression of LLMs. Due to its low sample complexity, we believe that EvoPress
152
      could be extensible to lightweight NAS as well, and plan to investigate this in future work.

158   the optimal model that adheres to a specified compression method and constraint. Formally, given a
159
      base model M , we seek to maximize the performance of the compressed model while satisfying the
      compression constraint:

167
      domized search. The feasibility of such an approach heavily depends on two factors: the time
168
      required to evaluate the fitness of a candidate solution and the number of such function evaluations
169   needed until a satisfying result is achieved. This poses a particular challenge in our case, as assessing
170   the performance of an LLM involves substantial computational costs.

172
      Level Database. As a first step, we compress the model to different levels. It is crucial that the
      units we search over – specifically layers or blocks – are compressed independently; otherwise,
173
      we risk losing performance when stitching together the compressed model. Ideally, the difference
174
      between two compression levels should be consistent across layers. This uniformity simplifies the
175   optimization process, allowing for the free exchange of compression levels, as we will demonstrate
176   for unstructured sparsity. However, this restriction is not essential for the search procedure to ef-
177   fective. In the context of quantization we will demonstrate a relaxation of this requirement, where
178   compression steps are uniform only across layers of same size.

180
      terized by its compression level for each unit (per layer or per block). With n units, each available

199   Algorithm. EvoPress starts from upon the classic (1 + λ)-evolutionary framework, which maintains
200   a single search point at any given time. In each generation, λ offspring are generated by copying the
201   parent and then applying a mutation operator to each copy. The offspring are then evaluated on the
202   fitness function, and the fittest one is selected. As an elitist evolutionary algorithm, the (1 + λ)-EA
203   replaces its parent only if the best offspring has superior fitness.
204   We change this standard algorithm in two important ways. The first is by introducing level-switch
205   mutation, a simple mutation operator that ensures high locality while preserving the compression
206   constraint. The operator involves first randomly selecting one unit and increasing its compression
207   level. Next, a second unit is sampled until one with a matching level step size is found, and its com-
208   pression level is decreased. This approach ensures that 1) the compression constraint is preserved,
209   and 2) the offspring model maintains high similarity to the parent model – an important feature for
210   achieving rapid convergence.
211   The second modification is that we employ a very aggressive form of multi-step selection. In the first
212   stage, all λ offspring are evaluated using only a fraction of a full sample. From this, only a small
213   subset of the fittest offspring are selected to compete in the next stage, where they are evaluated
214   on a significantly larger sample size. This process is repeated once more, and in the final stage,
215   the few remaining offspring are evaluated against the parent using a ”full” minibatch, consisting of
      approximately 20-50 times the number of tokens used in the first stage.

300   4   E XPERIMENTS
301
302   We now validate the efficiency of EvoPress for determining the optimal layer-wise compression
303   across three approachs: (1) layer dropping, where the goal is to isolate the “optimal” set of blocks
304   to drop given a target ratio, (2) non-uniform unstructured sparsity and (3) non-uniform quan-
305   tization, where we are given a set of compression options per layer (sparsities or bit-widths), and
306   the goal is to find the “optimal” configuration that matches a certain model size. We focus on LLM
307   compression, given the major interest in reduction of their model size and inference latency, but our
308
      method is general and can be applied to any neural network architecture and application domain.
309   Experimental Setup. We consider base models from the Llama-2 and Llama-3 (Touvron et al.,
310   2023) families, Mistral-v0.3 (Jiang et al., 2023), and the instruction-tuned Phi3-Medium-instruct-
311   128k model (Abdin et al., 2024), and adopt KL-divergence as our fitness function as it provides
312   a stronger and more robust signal, reflecting the predictive distribution of the original model. We
313   present ablations to validate this choice in Appendix B.3.
314   Concretely, our algorithm works as follows: given a uniform or random initial configuration, for each
315   step, we generate new offspring by making random flips, sampled from min(randint(1,3),
316   randint(1,3)) (increase / decrease) of compression levels under the constraint of fixed overall
317   compression ratio. Initially, we produce a large number of configurations (64-128 in most experi-
318   ments) and evaluate each on a few data samples (a single sequence on the first round). We choose the
319   top-k best configurations and run the next selection round with fewer candidates and more samples.
320
      Finally, we take the best configuration (including the parent) and adopt the best found configuration
      for the next round. We run for a fixed number of iterations, chosen so that performance on held-out
321
      data no longer improves.

404

510   5    C ONCLUSION
511
512   We have presented EvoPress, a unified optimization framework for non-uniform compression. Evo-
513   Press is based on a new provably-convergent evolutionary search algorithm with low sample and it-
514
      eration complexity, that is especially well-suited to the loss landscapes arising in LLM compression.
      Specifically, we have shown that EvoPress can converge extremely fast to accurate configurations
515
      for various non-uniform LLM compression problems, and is also fast to execute in practice. We
516
      also emphasize the breadth of our study, our method was implemented and tested on three differ-
517   ent compression approaches, relative to prior work which largely focused on a single application.
518   Experimental results showed that EvoPress consistently outperforms prior dynamic compression
519   approaches, across all compression types, with large gaps at medium to high compression.
520
      Limitations. One interesting direction we did not investigate is the possibility of combining different
521
      compression approaches into the same search space. This would require changes to our switch
522
      mutation strategy, but should be feasible in most cases. Second, we did not investigate finer-grained
523   structured pruning (i.e., removing rows and columns from the weight matrices), as it usually requires
524   extensive retraining to recover accuracy. We plan to investigate this in future work, as our approach
525   is well-suited to it. Finally, we plan to extend our compression results for quantization, to show
526   end-to-end speedups in the context of an inference engine supporting multiple compressed formats,
527   such as vLLM (Kwon et al., 2023).
528
      Impact Statement. We presented work that aims to advance efficiency in machine learning. We
529   believe that model compression optimization is a step toward democratizing large-scale model in-
530   ference, and thus provides opportunities to foster both the development of new applications and the
531   research in the field. There are several important societal concerns about the rapidly growing use of
532   artificial intelligence, but we feel that none of them specifically concerns our work.

1205   B.1   M UTATATION R ATE (D EPTH P RUNING )
1206
1207   The mutation rate plays a crucial role in balancing exploration and exploitation. A higher mutation
1208
       rate allows for broader exploration of the search space; however, this space grows exponentially
       with the number of mutations. As a result, when attempting to approach the optimum in terms
1209
       of Hamming distance, the proportion of ”good” offspring decreases significantly with increasing
1210
       mutation rates. Consequently, in a smooth fitness landscape, we expect faster optimization with a
1211   lower mutation rate. To study the impact of mutation rate on our search process, we tested various
1212   distributions from which the number of mutations is sampled. Table 4 illustrates the effects of these
1213   distributions on the task of selecting the optimal 12 blocks to drop for Mistral-7B-v0.3. The results
1214   confirm our intuition: higher mutation rates generally reduce performance. However, sampling from
1215   the minimum of two uniform distributions ensures a reasonably high probability of selecting a low
1216   number of mutations. These offspring, with fewer mutations, then drive the optimization process,
1217   yielding to comparably lower performance drops. Conversely, when we eliminate this sampling
1218   and instead use a high, constant mutation rate, we lose the locality that is crucial for evolutionary
1219
       algorithms, leading to a significant drop in performance.

1233   Section 3, we expect the dynamic model compression problem to exhibit a smooth fitness landscape

1241   progress is relatively ”easy”, and increase it when progress becomes difficult, offering a greater

1743   G                      M ULTIMODAL COMPRESSION
1744
1745   In the main text we considered unimodal compression - either depth pruning, unstructured sparsity
1746   or quantization. A natural extension of our approach is to optimize multiple compression techniques
1747
       simultaneously, which we refer to as multimodal compression.
1748   Below, we consider the case of joint depth pruning and quantization. To simplify the setup and
1749   search space, we apply uniform quantization to all projections within each block. The optimization
1750   process alternates between two phases:
1751
1752                           • Block dropping. Multiple candidate configurations are generated by sampling blocks for
1753
                                 removal and revival. When reintroducing a block, its weights are quantized to match the
                                 bitwidth of the removed block. The best-performing configuration is selected.
1754
1755                           • Quantization. Quantization levels between ”alive” blocks from the previous step are
1756                             swapped, and the fittest one is retained.
1757
       Multimodal EvoPress approach yields both a set of blocks to be removed and a distribution of
1758
       quantization bitwidths across the surviving blocks.
1759
1760   We validate the proposed approach on Llama-3.1-8B for 25% sparsity and 4-bit quantization on
1761   average (with 2, 3, 4, 5 and 6 bit options following Section 4.3). One can observe from Figure 14
1762   that multimodal search manages to find a better solution than the starting point (the best of many
1763
       uniform samples) and exhibits relatively stable convergence.
