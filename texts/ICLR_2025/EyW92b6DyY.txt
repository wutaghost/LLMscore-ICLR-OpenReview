009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Data imputation is an essential problem in many data science applications. Exist-
012            ing methods often struggle to impute missing values in scenarios where there is a
013            lack of sufficient data redundancy. In this paper, leveraging large language mod-
014            els (LLMs) and data lakes, we propose a novel approach for retrieval-augmented
015            imputation called RAI, utilizing fine-grained tuple-level retrieval instead of tra-
016            ditional coarse-grained table-based retrieval. RAI addresses the challenges of re-
017            trieving relevant tuples for missing value imputation from a data lake, where tuples
018            have heterogeneous attributes, diverse values, and missing values. Rather than
019
               simply searching for similar tables, RAI employs a tuple encoder to learn mean-
               ingful representations for capturing tuple similarities and differences, enabling ef-
020
               fective identification of candidate tuples. The retrieved results are further refined
021
               by a tuple reranker. We also introduce a new benchmark, mvBench, to advance
022            further research. We conduct extensive experiments, demonstrating that RAI sig-
023            nificantly outperforms state-of-the-art table-based retrieval-augmented imputation
024            methods by 10.7%.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Data quality is crucial for effective data analysis, with missing values being a common issue (Abed-
030   jan et al., 2016). These arise from reasons including undefined values, collection errors, and errors in
031   SQL joins while merging datasets. Excessive missing values can significantly degrade the reliability
032   of downstream applications (Chai et al., 2023) and decision-making processes (Luo et al., 2020).
033   Therefore, numerous efforts have been made to address the problem of missing values (Abiteboul
034   et al., 1995; Jerez et al., 2010; Mahdavi & Abedjan, 2020). We can classify existing solutions into
035
      two categories: leveraging data redundancy in the table itself or leveraging external knowledge, as
      shown in Table 1.
036
037   Leveraging Data Redundancy. Most existing solutions fall into this category. The presence of
038   repeating or similar data within the table enables these methods to extract patterns, dependencies,
039   and relationships, facilitating missing value imputation. However, there is no one-size-fits-all so-
040   lution for imputation, as different methods are tailored to different scenarios and types of missing
041   values. For instance, this category is especially effective for continuous numerical data because the
042
      imputation heavily depends on the context within the table itself.
043   Nevertheless, these methods often fall short in scenarios that lack sufficient data redundancy. For
044   example, small or sparse datasets, such as web tables, typically do not have enough similar data
045   points to infer missing values. This challenge is further amplified in cases where each row contains
046   highly diverse or unique information, making it difficult to apply patterns from one part of the table
047   to another. Therefore, there is a growing need for methods that leverage external or domain-specific
048
      knowledge to compensate for the lack of data redundancy.
049   Leveraging External Knowledge. LLMs have emerged as a promising approach by utilizing their
050   vast internal knowledge (Deng et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023). However, LLMs can suffer from issues
051   like hallucinations and a lack of interpretability, making it difficult for users to trust and understand
052   the imputed values. A potential solution to these challenges is Retrieval-Augmented Generation
053   (RAG), which enhances LLMs by incorporating external data sources, offering more grounded and
      reliable imputation.

070   Challenges of RAG for Missing Value Imputation. Despite the potential of RAG for data imputa-
071   tion, current methods face several limitations.
072
      (C1) The precision of retrieval and imputation. Existing methods, e.g., RATA (Glass et al., 2023),
073
      often operate at a coarse granularity by indexing and retrieving entire tables rather than fine-grained
074
      tuples. However, in practice, imputing missing values for a single tuple often requires information
075   from only a few relevant tuples, which may be scattered across different tables in a data lake.
076
077   Figure 1 illustrates this, where filling in the missing “district”, “party”, and “birth date” values for
078
      the incomplete tuple requires gathering specific facts from multiple tables. Moreover, even when
      a relevant table is retrieved, precisely locating the useful tuples is still necessary. In the example,
079
      table T1 contains 400+ tuples, but only the matching “Bob Riley” tuple provides the information
080
      needed. Our experiments show that inputting excessive irrelevant information can actually degrade
081   the imputation model’s performance.
082
083   (C2) Handling heterogeneous data. Data lakes often contain heterogeneous data sources with vary-
084
      ing schemas, missing values, and textual representations. For example, in Figure 1, T1, T2, and
      T3 have schema heterogeneity, with the attributes “Representative”, “Governor”, and “Incumbent”
085
      having the same semantic meaning. The tables also contain missing values, e.g., “Rank” in T1.
086
      Furthermore, “R” in T1 and “Republican” in T3 illustrate variations in textual representations. This
087   heterogeneity challenges retrieval mechanisms in aligning and comparing tuples for imputation.
088
089   Contribution. We introduce a novel approach for RAG-based missing value imputation at the tuple
090   level within a data lake. Our main contributions can be summarized as follows:
091
               • We employ contrastive learning and synthesized training data to learn tuple embeddings
092
                 that capture similarities across heterogeneous data. This approach enables efficient simi-
093
                 larity searches among tuples with diverse schemas and values, addressing the challenge of
094              handling heterogeneous data (Challenge 1).
095
               • We introduce RAI, a tuple-level retrieval-augmented framework that retrieves the top-K
096
                 relevant tuples from the data lake for a given incomplete tuple, reranks them to select a
097
                 compact subset of top-k tuples, and employs LLMs for accurate and context-aware impu-
098              tation. By efficiently retrieving, reranking, and leveraging relevant tuples for imputation,
099              RAI addresses the challenge of precision in retrieval and imputation (Challenge 2).
100
               • We propose a large-scale benchmark, mvBench, with 15,143 incomplete tuples, 4.23 mil-
101
                 lion tuples within the data lake. For each incomplete tuple, the relevant tuples are labeled
102              manually, enabling a fine-grained evaluation of RAG-based data imputation.
103
104
               • We conduct extensive experiments on mvBench and compare RAI with thirteen baseline
                 methods to showcase its effectiveness in imputing missing values in small tables. Our
105
                 results indicate that RAI significantly outperforms state-of-the-art table-based retrieval-
106
                 augmented imputation methods by 10.7%. Our code and data are open-source1 .

      2     S OLUTION OVERVIEW

      2.1   P ROBLEM S TATEMENT
111
112   A data lake L = {D1 , D2 , . . . , Dk } is a collection of k tables, each of which may have a dis-
113   tinct schema. A relational table D consists of a schema, which is a set of attributes R(D) =
114   {A1 , A2 , . . . , An }, defining the columns of the table, a set of tuples {t1 , t2 , . . . , tm }, and a textual
115   caption. An incomplete tuple t is a tuple that contains one or more missing values. Given an incom-
116   plete tuple t and a data lake L, the problem of missing value imputation using data lakes involves
117   repairing t by retrieving relevant tuples from L. The goal is to ensure that the repaired tuple t closely
118   resembles its ground truth counterpart tg .
119
120   2.2   T HE RAI F RAMEWORK
121
122   We adopt a Retrieve-Rerank-Reason RAG framework for data imputation, namely RAI. An illus-
123
      trative example is shown in Figure 2, which demonstrates how to fill in the missing team for player
      Adrian Aucoin in the 2012-13 NHL season. The process is broken down as follows: Given an in-
124
      complete tuple t, the Retriever initially retrieves the top-K tuples from the data lake that are most
125
      relevant to t. In the example, the tuple ranked #1 lacks the team attribute, while the tuple ranked
126   #2 lists all the teams Aucoin has played for, but without specifying the time periods. Only the tuple
127   ranked #20, i.e., the relevant tuple, records Aucoin’s team change in the NHL in 2012. Subsequently,
128   the Reranker reranks these top-K tuples through a fine-grained comparison between t and each re-
129   trieved tuple. The most relevant tuple is then elevated to rank #2 in this example. Finally, the top-k
130   (k = 5 in the example) retrieved tuples are then provided to the Reasoner for imputing the missing
131   value rationally.
132   To effectively implement RAI for data imputation using data lakes, we face several key challenges:
133
134   (1) Retrieving relevant tuples from heterogeneous data for impution: Existing tuple embedding
135
      methods (Tang et al., 2021) fail to capture complex connections between an incomplete tuple and its
      relevant tuples. Entity matching techniques (Wang et al., 2023; Li et al., 2020) are also inadequate,
136
      as they are domain-specific, with each dataset consisting of two tables within the same domain. Thus
137
      they cannot handle diverse and heterogeneous data in data lakes. Moreover, identifying a relevant
138   tuple for imputation differs from finding a matched tuple in entity matching, as in Figure 1, the
139   relevant tuple in T3 does not describe the same entity as the incomplete tuple.
140
141
      (2) Precisely identifying relevant tuples: Previous retrieval-augmented imputation methods (Glass
      et al., 2023) lack precision in identifying relevant tuples, hindering the imputation process and bur-
142
      dening the reasoner.
143
144   (3) Enhancing reasoning with domain knowledge: Data imputation requires the reasoner to possess
145   domain knowledge and reasoning capabilities beyond simply aligning attributes and extracting val-
146   ues (Zhang & Balog, 2019; Glass et al., 2023). For instance, in Figure 1, understanding that the
147
      “party” is about “Siegelman” but not “Reiley” in T3 is necessary for accurate imputation. Similarly,
      in Figure 2, the reasoner must recognize that the “teams” in the second tuple describes all the teams
148
      the player has been in and cannot be used as evidence for imputation of the team the player was on
149
      during the 2012-2013 season.
150
151   In the following sections, we will discuss how each module in RAI is designed to address these
152   challenges, enabling effective data imputation using data lakes.
153
154   3     R ETRIEVAL AUGMENTED I MPUTATION

156   3.1   R ETRIEVER
157
158   One promising approach for handling heterogeneous data is to transform all tuples into embedding
159   vectors through a tuple encoder, enc(·). To achieve this, our basic idea is to train a tuple encoder,
160   enc(·), which ensures that embeddings of a tuple t with missing values and its relevant tuple s from
161   a data lake are similar, i.e., enc(t) ≈ enc(s). Conversely, embeddings of tuples irrelevant to t will
      be significantly distinct.

      To better capture pairwise tuple relationships, we employ contrastive learning within a Siamese net-
175
      work architecture. Despite the potential of it, there are still two main challenges. First, the hetero-
176   geneous nature of tuples in data lakes poses several obstacles in learning effective representations:
177   attribute heterogeneity due to varying schemas or formats, textual representation variance caused by
178   synonyms and abbreviations, and the presence of missing values complicate the learning of effective
179   representations and the comparison process. Furthermore, obtaining sufficient and diverse training
180   data is another significant challenge in learning tuple representations. Next, we will discuss how
181   to learn a tuple encoder with contrastive learning and how to synthesize diversified training data to
182   solve the above two challenges.

188   of training examples for N anchors, xi (i = [1, N ]). For anchor xi , we construct one positive pair

201   3.1.2         S YNTHESIZING T RAINING DATA .
202
203   Effectively training a tuple encoder with contrastive learning requires extensive training data. How-
204
      ever, the lack of suitable datasets and the high cost of manual labeling (e.g. MS MARCO (Nguyen
      et al., 2016)) necessitate the automatic synthesis of training data. To fill in this gap, we propose a
205
      novel approach that employs tuple augmentation operators and a three-step process for synthesizing
206
      training data. This approach also addresses key challenges tuple encoder faces, such as attribute
207   heterogeneity, diverse values, which we will demonstrate in the following.
208

210   tuple into its “equivalent” form. These systematically designed operators will be used to construct
211   training data that simulates the diverse and heterogeneous nature of data lakes.
212   Our data augmentation operators in Table 2 are designed to augment a tuple from three aspects:
213   caption, attribute, or value. These operators can help capture various forms of relevant tuples origi-
214   nating from diverse sources, enhancing flexibility and robustness in tuple retrieval and comparison.
215   For instance, value augmentation operators generate equivalent tuples with synonymous or missing
      values, mimicking the variance in textual representations and the presence of missing values. For

236

238   same subject entity with xi through entity linking, then augmenting those tuples. If the anchor tuple

240   the model’s ability to identify tuples that can successfully impute missing information. The latter

242   where relevant data sources vary widely. These strategies inject heterogeneity between positive and
243   anchor tuples, enabling the model to handle heterogeneous data for data imputation effectively.
244   Synthesizing Negative Tuples. We generate negative samples for each anchor tuple in two cate-
245   gories: easy and hard negatives. Easy negatives are randomly selected tuples from other tables, due
246   to the in-batch negative strategies, we do not need to construct them deliberately. Hard negatives, on
247   the other hand, are selected from the same table as the anchor tuple but represent different entities.
248   This distinction is essential as it forcing the encoder to learn more discriminative and meaningful
249   representations, enabling it to distinguish between similar but distinct tuples (Luo et al., 2023). All
250   negative samples are augmented for increasing diversity. By synthesizing both easy and hard neg-
251   atives, we create an informative and diversified training dataset. This dataset enables the model to
252
      effectively recognize negative (i.e., irrelevant) tuples to a given incomplete tuple.
253   Training Data Summary. In summary, for our retriever, the training set comprises 282,862 anchor
254   tuples from 41,260 tables, and the development set includes 9,460 anchor tuples from 775 tables.
255   Each anchor tuple is paired with 1 positive and 7 negative tuples. Notably, our encoder, trained
256   on this dataset, has shown strong generalization capabilities, performing well on larger downstream
257   datasets that contain an even greater volume of table data.
258
      Indexing. We employ the vector database Meta Faiss (Johnson et al., 2019) to encode all tuples
259   into 768-dimensional vectors using Faiss’s flat indexing system, which compresses the vectors into
260   fixed-size codes that are stored in an array.

      3.2   R ERANKER : A D ESIGN S PACE E XPLORATION
263
264   After obtaining the top-K tuples from the retriever, we find that relevant tuple is often retrieved
265   only when K is sufficiently large (e.g., K = 100), increasing the complexity of the subsequent
266   reasoning. To mitigate this issue, we introduce a fine-grained reranker component. However, despite
267   the proven effectiveness of reranking in text retrieval, the optimal reranker for retrieval-augmented
268   imputation remains unclear due to the lack of comprehensive exploration. To address this gap,
269   we conduct an extensive investigation into the design space of reranking methods for calculating
      the relevance of a retrieved tuple s from the retriever with respect to an incomplete tuple t. We

280   Fine-tuned methods involve fine-tuning a language model to enhance reranking capabilities, which

285   Prompting models, on the other hand, send prompts to LLMs without fine-tuning. These methods

287
      tuple t, retrieved tuple s) pair individually; listwise (Sun et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2023), which assesses
      and ranks an entire list of retrieved tuples (s1 , s2 . . . , sk ) collectively; and pairwise (Qin et al., 2023),
288
      which compares a set of pairs (incomplete tuple t, retrieved tuple si ) to ascertain which si is more
289
      relevant to t.
290
291   Previous research (Sun et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2023) has indicated that the pointwise method per-
292   forms poorly compared to listwise and pairwise methods. Therefore, we focus on the remaining
293
      methods, choosing the most widely recognized model framework for each. To our knowledge, this
      is the first study on using prompting methods for reranking in tuple retrieval. Our empirical results
294
      show that fine-tuned generative relevance reranking model performs best when trained on a dataset
295
      of no more than 100 incomplete tuples. Next, we will discuss the best-performing model, while the
296   other models and experimental results are discussed in Section 4.
297
298   Our Default Reranker. Following the generative relevance reranking approach (Nogueira et al.,
299   2020), we construct K pairs of an incomplete tuple ti and its top-K retrieved tuples from retriever.
300
      Each pair is serialized and concatenated into a sequence, which is then input into a seq2seq model,
      typically a T5-base model (Raffel et al., 2020a). The model outputs a single token, and a softmax
301
      function is applied to the logits associated with the “true” and “false” tokens to generate a relevance
302
      score, thus obtaining the likelihood of a retrieved tuple being relevant to the anchor. We then order
303   the top-K retrieved tuples based on these scores to rank their relevance and get top-k reranked tuples.
304

      3.3    R EASONER
306
307   After obtaining the top-k reranked results, the process proceeds to the crucial data imputation stage.
308   We leverage LLMs to perform the final reasoning step, using structured prompts to guide them in
309   generating the desired outputs. The template we used and example of the reasoning process are
310   provided in the Appendix C. Note that when an incomplete tuple contains multiple missing values,
311   imputation can be performed as long as relevant tuples for filling those missing values are retrieved,
312   as demonstrated in the example.

315
316   Our experiments aim to answer three key questions: (1) How does RAI compare to other methods
317   in terms of end-to-end imputation performance? (Section 4.3) (2) How effectiveness of our retriever

321   4.1    DATASET
322
323   Although existing datasets (Mei et al., 2021; Mahdavi & Abedjan, 2020; Glass et al., 2023) for data
      imputation provide missing data and their corresponding ground truth, they mostly lack: (1) large

                                                                6
324
      data lakes containing massive tables to assist in filling missing values, and (2) labeled relevant tuples
325   or tables for imputation. To address these limitations, we introduce mvBench, a large-scale bench-
326   mark with 15,143 incomplete tuples and 4.23 million tuples from the data lake for missing value
327   imputation. We also provide relevant tuples annotated by human experts for each incomplete tuple
328   to enable a fine-grained evaluation of the retrieval module. Our benchmark focuses on challenging
329   scenarios that require external sources for imputation, which is not well addressed by existing work
330   that typically rely on the inherent data redundancy within the table. By including these scenarios,
331   we demonstrate the effectiveness and adaptation of our tuple-level RAG approach. The detailed
332   construction of datasets is presented in Appendix F.
333   mvBench comprises five datasets collected from real-world scenarios, varying in scales, domains,
334   and sources. Table 3 presents detailed statistics of all datasets within mvBench. For each dataset,
335   a subset of incomplete tuples is randomly sampled to form the training set for the reranker (see

340   4.2   E XPERIMENTAL S ETTINGS
341
342   We introduce baselines and evaluation metrics for the reasoner, retriever, and reranker components
343   of RAI. Detailed hyperparameters and environment settings are provided in Appendix A.
344   Baselines for Reasoner (Data Imputation). We compare RAI with existing imputation solutions
345   that leverage external knowledge beyond the table itself, including LLM with in-context learning,
346   LLM with BM25 retriever, LM with fine-tuning TURL (Deng et al., 2022), and table-based retrieval-
347   augmented imputation RATA (Glass et al., 2023). As mentioned in Section 1, we focus on the
348   scenario where data redundancy within the table is lack, and thus do not include comparisons with
349   methods that primarily rely on it. For retrieval-based methods, we send the top-5 retrieved results
350   along with incomplete tuples to LLMs for final imputation. We run the first two baselines on our
351   benchmark and compare them with RAI, while the comparisons with TURL and RATA are dis-
352
      cussed separately due to differences in their settings.
353   Baselines for Retriever. We compare our retriever against five baselines: (1) BM25 (Robertson
354   et al., 2009), (2) Contriever (Izacard et al., 2021), and (3) DPR-scale (Lin et al., 2023), all of
355   which have zero-shot capabilities and excel in few-shot and zero-shot passage retrieval; (4) a BERT-
356   based tuple encoder with masked language modeling (MLM) where 30% of tuple cells are randomly
357   masked, to compare with previous works on tuple representation that only accept individual tuples
358   as input and adopt pre-training tasks centered around language modeling (Tang et al., 2021); and (5)
359
      Sudowoodo (Wang et al., 2023), a state-of-the-art entity matching method. For each dataset, follow-
      ing its original settings, we randomly select 10,000 tuples from incomplete tuples and the data lake
360
      as the labled data, then pretrain the model for 3 epochs and finetune it for 40 epochs. After that,
361
      Sudowoodo can encode tuples into vectors, enabling retrieval using FAISS. Although Sudowoodo
362   can impute missing values, it relies on redundant information within the table itself, which differs
363   from our scenario.
364
365   Baselines for Reranker. Our default reranker is a fine-tuned generative relevance reranking model,
366
      and we compare it with several baselines, including fine-tuned scoring-based methods: RoBERTa-
      LCE (Gao et al., 2021) and monoBERT (Nogueira & Cho, 2019), as well as prompting methods
367
      using GPT-3.5 with listwise (Sun et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2023) and pairwise reranking (Qin et al.,
368
      2023). In Appendix B, we provide the implementation details for those reranking methods.
369
370   Evaluation Metrics. We evaluate the end-to-end performance of RAI for data imputation using
371   Exact Match (EM) Accuracy(Izacard & Grave, 2020), which considers a generated value correct if
372   it matches any acceptable answer after normalization. The performance of the retriever and reranker
373
      are assessed using recall@K and success@K respectively.

417   Discussion on Other Data Imputation Methods. We compare RAI to two types of data imputation
418   methods: TURL (fine-tuned LM) and RATA (table-based RAG).

420   WikiTuples dataset, which is constructed from TURL’s test set. It is important to note that TURL’s
421   performance on this dataset can be considered an upper bound, as all cells to be filled appear in
422   TURL’s training set at least three times and relations between different entities are learned. Although
423   RAI achieves a slightly lower imputation accuracy compared to TURL (0.902 vs. 0.967), it is
424   remarkable considering that RAI’s training data size is only 1/14 of TURL’s. Moreover, TURL
425
      requires that the ground truth of the cell to be filled must be linked to an entity in its pre-constructed
      entity vocabulary, and it can only output entity id from this vocabulary. In contrast, RAI is designed
426
      to complement LLMs and can be easily adapted to different datasets without constraints. This
427
      flexibility and adaptability make RAI a more robust solution for data imputation tasks across various
428   domains and datasets.

      4.4   E VALUATION FOR R ETRIEVER
452
453
      Main Results. Our retriever, leveraging contrastive learning, outperforms baselines in both recall
454   and success rate across all datasets, demonstrating its superior effectiveness and generalization.
455   Table 5 shows that our retriever achieves the highest recall and success rates on all datasets except
456   Business. Notably, our retriever is pre-trained on 40k Wikipedia tables and directly applied to the
457   five datasets without additional training, showcasing its robustness and generalization capability.
458   The results also indicate that: (1) Retrievers designed for passage retrieval tasks cannot be directly
459   applied to our task. (2) Contrastive learning is crucial for an effective tuple encoder for retrieval.
460   RAI’s retriever and BERT with MLM task share the same base model and training corpus, but
461   the latter performs worst across all datasets, indicating its unsuitability for our scenario. (3) Our
462
      task differs from entity matching, as tuples describing the same entity are not necessarily relevant
      tuples. This is evident from the fact that our retriever significantly outperforms Sudowoodo, which
463
      uses labeled data, especially on the WikiTuples and Show Movie datasets. In contrast, Sudowoodo
464
      performs well on Business and Cricket Players datasets since they contains many cases where the
465   relevant tuple and the incomplete tuple describe the same entity (one of the cases in our scenario).
466
467   Ablation Study. To investigate the effectiveness of our synthesizing training data and identify key
468   factors in its construction, we conduct comparative experiments focusing on anchor tuples, pos-
469
      itives, and negatives. The experiments reveal that combining complete anchor tuples with those
      having missing values enhances retriever performance, as including missing values aligns with the
470
      intent of data imputation, while complete anchors help the retriever learn tuple structure and overall
471
      semantics. Additionally, including diverse positive samples with various heterogeneous attributes
472   from other tables significantly improves the retriever’s performance, aligning with real-world sce-
473   narios. Moreover, intuitively, treating anchors with the masked missing cell deleted as negatives
474   should help the model better understand that, if a tuple does not contain the content corresponding
475   to the masked missing value, it cannot serve as a relevant tuple, even if it is very similar to the in-
476   complete tuple. Surprisingly, adding these hard negatives leads to a significant decrease in retrieval
477   results, possibly due to the difficulty in accurately capturing and distinguishing cell-level semantics
478   when encoding tuples. In summary, the data synthesizing method for our retriever proves to be very
479   effective, with the combination of anchor tuples, positives, and negatives being crucial. The details
480
      are provided in Appendix E.3.

514   5     R ELATED W ORK
515
516   Data imputation without sufficient data redundancy has gained attention with the advancement of
517   LLMs. While some studies have applied LLMs directly to data imputation through in-context learn-
518   ing (Narayan et al., 2022) or training models to understand tabular structure and knowledge (Li et al.,
519   2023; Zhang et al., 2023), ensuring high accuracy and reliability remains a challenge. Retrieval-
520   Augmented Generation (RAG), introduced by (Lewis et al., 2020), involves retrieving relevant doc-
521
      uments from external sources to generate answers. RAG has been adopted for table-related tasks,
      such as TableQA (Herzig et al., 2021) and table augmentation (Glass et al., 2023), but the utilization
522
      of RAG in data imputation remains relatively unexplored.
523
524   Previous imputation works incorporating retrieval ideas have limitations in addressing the challenges
525   of imputing tables with limited redundancy. Some approaches retrieve information from the same
526   table (Li et al., 2015) or use simple matching (Zhang & Balog, 2019), still relying on the table’s
527
      inherent redundancy and failing to handle heterogeneous data. Others utilize external sources like
      master data (Fan et al., 2012; Interlandi & Tang, 2015) or knowledge bases (Hao et al., 2017; Chu
528
      et al., 2015), but require expert involvement, making them unsuitable for large-scale data lakes.
529
      RATA (Glass et al., 2023) employs a table-level retrieval framework for data imputation, but its
530   coarse-grained retrieval is insufficient for accurate imputation in tables lacking data redundancy.
531
532
533   6     C ONCLUSION
534
535   We introduce a Retrieval-Augmented Imputation Framework called RAI, specifically tailored for
536   addressing missing value imputation in data lakes. RAI integrates a pre-trained retriever capable of
537   identifying relevant tuples, a fine-tuned reranker to ascertain fine-grained relevance, and a reasoner
538   that applies in-context learning for the reliable imputation process. Our experiments demonstrate
539   that RAI features an exceptionally effective retrieval module, surpassing various established base-
      lines and markedly improving upon methods that depend solely on LLMs.

     793
     794   GPT-3.5 with Listwise Reranking. The listwise method utilizes a strategy where the LLM is
     795   prompted with a query and a document list and is asked to output the identifiers of the documents
     796   in a reranked order based on their relevance to the query (Sun et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2023). As
     797
           depicted in Figure 3 (a), in our scenario, this method is implemented by feeding LLMs with a set of
           retrieved tuples and an incomplete tuple, each retrieved tuple is paired with an identifier. The model
     798
           then generates a list of reranked identifiers according to the relevance to the query tuple. Due to
     799
           input size limitations of the LLM, a sliding window strategy is employed, with a window size of 30
     800   and a step size of 14, to manage larger lists. This method is supported by the work in (Sun et al.,
fi   801   2023; Ma et al., 2023).
     802
     803   GPT-3.5 with Pairwise Reranking. In addition to listwise reranking, Qin et al. (Qin et al., 2023)
     804
           introduces a pairwise reranking method utilizing LLMs as rerankers. Similar to the bubble sort
           algorithm, this technique requires LLMs to compare pairs of retrieved tuples and determine which
     805
           one is more relevant to the incomplete tuple, as shown in Figure 3 (b). Unlike the listwise strategy
     806
           that reranks an entire list, the pairwise method focuses on one-to-one comparisons, making it a less
     807   complex task.

       F.2     R ELEVANT T UPLE A NNOTATION
1091
1092   After collecting original datasets and constructing incomplete tuples with data lakes, we start to
1093   annotate the relevant tuples for each incomplete tuple. The process involves two steps: Candidate
1094   Tuples Construction and Expert Annotation.
1095
       Candidate Tuples Construction. For an incomplete tuple, we first create a candidate set by select-
1096
       ing tuples that could potentially help in imputing its missing values. We use explicit information,
1097   such as similar cell values or cells linking to the same entity, to establish effective filtering rules.
1098
1099   Expert Annotation. For each incomplete tuple, we present it and its candidate one by one to a
1100
       human expert to judge whether the candidate can fill at least one missing value in the incomplete
       tuple, i.e., be identified as relevant. Before starting the annotation, experts are instructed to apply
1101
       their domain knowledge carefully. For example, they are advised that certain attributes like a movie
1102
       director do not change over time, whereas others, such as a sports team manager, may change and
1103   thus the temporal alignment between the candidate tuple and the incomplete tuple should be verified.
1104   This manual process is time-consuming and requires specific domain knowledge. We hire 10 PhD
1105   students as our “human experts” to annotate candidate tuples corresponding to each incomplete
1106   tuple, ensuring the quality of our labels. To reduce the cost of annotation, we primarily focus on
1107   cases where the candidate set comprised 10 or fewer tuples. In total, over 200 human hours and
1108   approximately $1,000 were spent on curating relevant tuple labels for each incomplete tuple.
