009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Machine learning has increasingly been employed to solve NP-hard combina-
012            torial optimization problems, resulting in the emergence of neural solvers that
013            demonstrate remarkable performance, even with minimal domain-specific knowl-
014            edge. To date, the community has created numerous open-source neural solvers
015            with distinct motivations and inductive biases. While considerable efforts are de-
016            voted to designing powerful single solvers, our findings reveal that existing solvers
017            typically demonstrate complementary performance across different problem in-
018            stances. This suggests that significant improvements could be achieved through
019
               effective coordination of neural solvers at the instance level. In this work, we pro-
               pose the first general framework to coordinate the neural solvers, which involves
020
               feature extraction, selection model, and selection strategy, aiming to allocate each
021
               instance to the most suitable solvers. To instantiate, we collect several typical neu-
022            ral solvers with state-of-the-art performance as alternatives, and explore various
023            methods for each component of the framework. We evaluated our framework on
024            two extensively studied combinatorial optimization problems, Traveling Salesman
025            Problem (TSP) and Capacitated Vehicle Routing Problem (CVRP). Experimental
026            results show that the proposed framework can effectively distribute instances and
027            the resulting composite solver can achieve significantly better performance (e.g.,
028            reduce the optimality gap by 0.88% on TSPLIB and 0.71% on CVRPLIB) than
029            the best individual neural solver with little extra time cost.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Combinatorial Optimization Problems (COPs) involve finding an optimal solution over a set of
035   combinatorial alternatives, which has broad and important applications such as logistics (Konstan-
036   takopoulos et al., 2022) and manufacturing (Zhang et al., 2019). To solve COPs, traditional ap-
037   proaches usually depend on heuristics designed by experts, requiring extensive domain knowledge
038   and considerable effort. Recently, machine learning techniques have been introduced to automati-
039
      cally discover effective heuristics for COPs (Bengio et al., 2021; Cappart et al., 2023), leading to the
      burgeoning development of end-to-end neural solvers that employ deep neural networks to generate
040
      solutions for problem instances (Bello et al., 2017; Kool et al., 2019; Joshi et al., 2019). Compared
041
      to traditional approaches, these end-to-end neural solvers can not only get rid of the heavy reliance
042   on expertise, but also realize better inference efficiency (Bello et al., 2017).
043
044   To enhance the capabilities of neural solvers, a variety of methods have been proposed, with in-
045
      tensive effort on the design of frameworks, network architectures, and training procedures. For
      example, to improve the performance across different distributions, Jiang et al. (2022) proposed
046
      adaptively joint training over varied distributions, and Bi et al. (2022) leveraged knowledge distil-
047
      lation to integrate the models trained on different distributions. For generalization on large-scale
048   instances, Fu et al. (2021) implemented a divide-and-conquer strategy, Luo et al. (2023) proposed
049   a heavy-decoder structure to better capture the relationship among nodes, while Gao et al. (2024)
050   utilized the local transferability and introduced an additional local policy model. Diffusion mod-
051   els (Sun & Yang, 2023) have also been adapted to generate the distribution of optimal solutions,
052   demonstrating impressive results. More works include bisimulation quotienting (Drakulic et al.,
053   2023), latent space search (Chalumeau et al., 2023), local reconstruction (Cheng et al., 2023; Ye
      et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2024) and so on.

068   As various neural solvers are emerging in the community, the state-of-the-art records for the overall
069   performance on benchmark problems are frequently refreshed. However, the detailed comparison of
070   these neural solvers on each instance has been rarely discussed. Here, we empirically examined the

072   1(b). As expected from the no-free-lunch theorem (Wolpert & Macready, 1997), we find that
073
                                • As shown in Figure 1(a), there exists no single neural solver that can dominate all the other
074
                                  neural solvers on every instance, and different neural solvers win on different instances,
075                               demonstrating their complementary performance at instance level.

                                • As shown in Figure 1(b), the modification of instance distribution can almost reverse the
                                  domination relationship of neural solvers, which further verifies that different neural solvers
078
                                  are good at instances with specific characteristics due to their intrinsic inductive biases.
079
080   These observations suggest that it may potentially bring impressive improvements to the overall per-
081   formance, if multiple neural solvers are coordinated to solve instances together. In fact, recent works
082   have already made preliminary attempts from the perspective of ensemble learning (Jiang et al.,
083   2023) and population-based training (Grinsztajn et al., 2023). However, their individual solvers
084   share the same architecture, resulting in limited diversity. On the other hand, as all of the individual
085   solvers should run during inference, these methods can hardly achieve ideal efficiency.
086   Motivated by the observations above, we, for the first time, propose a general framework to coordi-
087   nate end-to-end neural solvers for COPs at instance level by selecting suitable individual solvers for
088   each instance, as illustrated in Figure 1(c). Specifically, our proposed framework consists of three
089   key components, which are summarized as follows:
090
091                             • Feature extraction: For each problem instance, extract features that can be effectively
092
                                  used to identify their characteristics.
093                             • Selection model: Based on the features of instances, train a selection model that can be
094                               utilized to identify suitable solvers for each instance.
095                             • Selection strategy: Due to the intricate structures of COPs, using only the most suitable
096                               individual solver predicted by the selection model may fail. Therefore, it is important to
097                               design robust selection strategies based on the confidence of the selection model.
098
099
      To verify the effectiveness of our proposed framework, we collect several prevailing open-source
      neural solvers and their released models with competitive performance in the community to con-
100
      struct the pool of individual solvers, and provide several implementations for each component of the
101
      framework. For feature extraction, we utilize the graph attention network (Veličković et al., 2018;
102   Kool et al., 2019) to encode COP instances, and further propose a refined encoder with pooling to
103   leverage the hierarchical structures of COPs. For selection model, we train it from the perspective
104   of classification and ranking, respectively. We also implement several selection strategies, including
105   top-k selection, rejection-based selection, and so on. Detailed descriptions are provided in Sec-
106   tion 3. We conduct experiments on two widely studied COPs: Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP)
107   and Capacitated Vehicle Routing Problem (CVRP). Experimental results exhibit that our framework
      can generally select suitable individual solvers for each instance to achieve significantly better per-

115   2     R ELATED WORKS
116
117   2.1   E ND - TO - END NEURAL SOLVERS FOR COP S
118
119   Traditional approaches for COPs have achieved impressive results, but they often rely on problem-
120   specific heuristics and domain knowledge by experts (Helsgaun, 2000; 2017). Instead, recent efforts
121   focus on utilizing end-to-end learning methods. A prominent fashion is autoregression, which em-
122
      ploys graph neural networks in an encoder-decoder framework and progressively extends a partial
      solution until a complete solution is constructed (Vinyals et al., 2015; Bello et al., 2017; Kool et al.,
123
      2019). However, these methods tend to exhibit poor generalization performance across distribu-
124
      tions and scales (Joshi et al., 2022). To address the generalization issue, considerable efforts have
125   been dedicated within the community. For example, Zhou et al. (2023) took various distributions
126   and scales as different learning tasks and adopted meta-learning over them to obtain a generalizable
127   model. Bi et al. (2022) leveraged knowledge distillation, where models trained on different distribu-
128   tions are utilized as teacher models for one generalizable student model. Liu et al. (2024) used the
129   idea of prompt learning to realize zero-shot adaptation of the pertained model by selecting the most
130   matched prompt for instances. More efforts in autoregressive methods include instance-conditioned
131   adaptation (Zhou et al., 2024a), adversarial training (Wang et al., 2024) and nested local views (Fang
132   et al., 2024), to name a few.
133   Another popular kind of end-to-end learning methods is non-regressive, which predicts or generates
134   the distributions of potential solutions. Typically, Joshi et al. (2019); Ye et al. (2023) employed
135   graph neural networks to predict the probability of components appearing in an optimal solution,
136   represented with the form of heatmap. Diffusion models (Sun & Yang, 2023; Sanokowski et al.,
137   2024) have also been adapted to generate the distribution of optimal solutions, demonstrating better
138   expressiveness than classical push-forward generative models (Salmona et al., 2022).
139
140   2.2   S OLVING COP S WITH MULTIPLE NEURAL SOLVERS
141
142   Recent studies have made preliminary attempts to integrate multiple neural solvers to enhance over-
143   all performance on COPs. For example, Jiang et al. (2023) adopted ensemble learning, where mul-
144   tiple neural solvers with identical architecture are trained on different instance distributions through
145   Bootstrap sampling to ensure diversity. During inference, the outputs of all the solvers are gathered
146   by average at each action step. Grinsztajn et al. (2023) proposed a population-based training method
147
      Poppy, where multiple decoders with a shared encoder are trained simultaneously as a population
      of solvers, with a reward targeting at maximizing the overall performance of the population. When
148
      solving a problem instance, each solver generates solutions independently, and the best solution is
149
      selected as the final result. However, these works suffer from heavy computation cost as multiple
150   solvers have to be run for each instance. Even they propose to share a common encoder for each
151   solver, experimental results still demonstrate undesired inference time (Grinsztajn et al., 2023). On
152   the other hand, different solvers share the same neural architecture, which may limit the diversity
153   and thus the final performance.
154
      Consider that the burgeoning community has proposed many methods from various perspectives,
155
      resulting in diverse end-to-end neural solvers with different inductive biases. Properly coordinating
156   these neural solvers can potentially bring a significant improvement on overall performance. Mo-
157   tivated by the observation in Figure 1(a) and 1(b), we propose to select suitable ones from a pool
158   of diverse individual solvers for each instance. Note that similar idea has been utilized in the area
159   of algorithm selection (Kerschke et al., 2019) and model selection (Zhang et al., 2023), but has
160   never been explored in the area of neural combinatorial optimization. By solver selection at instance
161   level, any type of (existing or newly constructed) neural solver can be utilized, and only the selected
      individual solvers need to be run in inference, thereby maintaining high efficiency.

      3     T HE PROPOSED FRAMEWORK
163
164
      This section will introduce the proposed framework of coordinating neural solvers for COPs. In
165   general, our target is learning to select the suitable solvers for each problem instance. To address
166   this, our proposed framework comprises three key components:
167
168          • Feature extraction: To select the most suitable neural solvers for each instance, it is essen-
169            tial to extract the instance features, which is challenging as the COPs are usually intricate.
170            In this work, we first utilize the graph attention encoder (Kool et al., 2019) to encode COP
171            instances, and further propose a refined graph encoder with pooling, which can leverage
172            the hierarchical structures of COPs to obtain better features.
173          • Selection model: We train a neural selection model with the graph encoder to identify the
174            most suitable solvers. Specifically, we implement two loss functions from the perspectives
175            of classification and ranking.
176          • Selection strategies: Due to the complexity of COPs, it may be risky to rely solely on the
177            selection model to consistently identify the most suitable solver. To address this, we pro-
178            pose compromise strategies that allow to allocate multiple solvers (if necessary) to a single
179            instance based on the confidence levels of the selection model, pursuing better performance
180            with limited extra cost.
181
182   In the following subsections, we will elaborate the three key components in our framework.
183
184   3.1   F EATURE EXTRACTION
185
186   For feature extraction, it depends on the COP to be solved. Here, we use the two most prevailing
187
      problems, TSP and CVPR, in the neural solver community for COPs (Kwon et al., 2020; Luo et al.,
      2023; Drakulic et al., 2023) as examples, which will also be employed in our experiments. TSP
188
      and CVRP involve finding optimal routes over a set of nodes. For TSP, the objective is to find the
189
      shortest possible route that visits each node exactly once and returns to the starting node. Each TSP
190   instance consists of nodes distributed in Euclidean space. For CVRP, the goal is to plan routes for
191   multiple vehicles to serve customer nodes with varying demands, starting and ending at a depot node,
192   while minimizing the total travel distance and satisfying vehicle capacity constraints (Dantzig &
193   Ramser, 1959). Both TSP and CVRP instances can be represented as fully connected graphs, where
194   nodes correspond to locations (cities or customers). The graph representation makes them suitable
195   for encoding using Graph Neural Networks (GNNs), which can effectively capture the structural
196   information inherent in these problems (Khalil et al., 2017; Kool et al., 2019). In this paper, we
197   design two types of GNN-based encoders tailored for TSP and CVRP instances as follows.
198
199   Graph attention encoder. We take the CVRP as an example to describe the computation of the
200
      graph encoder. The raw features x ∈ RN ×3 of a CVRP instance are a set of nodes {(xi , yi , mi )|i ∈
      [N ]}, where (xi , yi ) are the node coordinates, mi is the node demand, N is the number of nodes,
201
      and [N ] denotes the set {1, 2, . . . , N }. First, a linear layer is employed on every node for initial
202
      node embeddings, i.e., H 0 = xW , where W ∈ R3×d are the weights and d denotes the embedding
203   dimension. Given initial embeddings, multiple graph attention layers (Veličković et al., 2018; Kool
204
      et al., 2019) are applied to iteratively update the node embeddings as H l = AttentionLayerl (H l−1 ),
205   where l ∈ [L] and L is the number of layers. Since the graphs of TSP and CVRP are both fully
206   connected, the graph attention layer covers every pair of nodes and self-connections, which becomes
207   similar to the self-attention mechanism (Vaswani et al., 2017). Details of the attention layer are
208   provided in Appendix A.1. Finally, the node embeddings output by the last layer are averaged to
209   form the instance representation, as most COP encoders (Khalil et al., 2017; Kool et al., 2019) did.
210
211   Hierarchical graph encoder. Averaging the final node embeddings may result in sub-optimal
212   instance representations that are too flat to effectively capture the hierarchical structures inherent in
213   COPs (Goh et al., 2024). Inspired by (Lee et al., 2019), we design a hierarchical graph attention
214   encoder to address this limitation, which successively downsamples the graph of an instance using
215
      graph pooling, and aggregates features from each downsampling level to construct a comprehensive
      graph representation, as illustrated in Figure 2.

253   3.2   S ELECTION MODEL
254
      We employ a Multiple-Layer Perception (MLP) to predict compatibility scores of neural solvers,
255
      where a higher score indicates that it is more suitable to allocate the instance to the corresponding
256   neural solver. This MLP model takes the instance representation and the instance scale N as input
257   and outputs a score vector, where the value of each index is the score of the corresponding neural
258   solver. In summary, the graph encoder and the MLP are cascaded to compose a neural selection
259   model, which can produce the compatibility scores of individual solvers from the raw COP instance
260   in an end-to-end manner. Advanced neural solver features can be incorporated for richer informa-
261   tion, as discussed in Section 5. However, we find that even using fixed indices of neural solvers has
262   already been effective, which will be clearly shown in our experiments.
263   We train the selection model using a supervised dataset comprising thousands of synthetic COP
264   instances. The objective values obtained by the neural solvers are recorded as supervision infor-
265   mation. Intuitively, a neural solver with a lower objective value (for minimization) has a higher
266   compatibility score. To learn such desired score output, we employ two losses from the perspectives
267   of classification and ranking.
268
269   Classification. The selection problem is formulated as a classification task, where the most suit-
      able neural solver for a given instance serves as the ground truth label. By employing classification

      loss functions such as cross-entropy loss used in our experiments, we can train a selection model.
271   However, this approach focuses on identifying the optimal neural solver and ignores sub-optimal
272   solvers, which may lead to unsatisfactory performance when the selection is inaccurate.
273
274   Ranking. The neural solvers can be sorted according to the objective values they obtain, thereby
275   forming a ranking of the given solvers, denoted by ϕ : [M ] → [M ], where ϕ(i) is the index of the
276   rank-i solver and M is the number of solvers. We then train the selection model by maximizing the
277   likelihood of producing correct rankings based on the computed scores (Xia et al., 2008),

285   3.3   S ELECTION STRATEGIES
286
287   Considering that the intricate structures of COPs may pose great challenge to the selection model,
288   besides greedy selection, we propose several compromise strategies that allow multiple solvers for
289   a single instance based on the confidence level of the selection model, aiming to improve the overall
290
      performance with little extra cost.
291
      Greedy selection. The most straightforward approach is the greedy selection, which chooses the
292
      neural solver with the highest score. This method is efficient since only one solver is executed per
293
      instance. However, it may be inaccurate, potentially leading to sub-optimal performance.
294
295
      Top-k selection. The top-k selection method can be adopted for better optimality, where we select
296   and execute the neural solvers with top-k scores for each instance, thus constructing a portfolio of
297   multiple solvers. This approach increases the likelihood of including the optimal solver but incurs
298   additional computational overhead due to the execution of multiple solvers.
299
300   Rejection-based selection. To balance efficiency and effectiveness, we propose the rejection-
301   based selection strategy, which adaptively selects greedy or top-k selection. Recognizing that the
302   confidence of the greedy selection varies across instances, an advanced strategy is to employ the
303   top-k selection for low-confidence instances to enhance performance and utilize only the greedy
304   selection for high-confidence ones to minimize computational cost. To implement this strategy, we
305
      can use a confidence measure to determine whether to accept or reject the greedy selection. If the
      confidence in the greedy selection is below a threshold, we reject it and apply the top-k selection
306
      to the instance for improved optimality. In this paper, we adopt the simple yet effective softmax
307
      response (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2017) as the confidence measure, and define the threshold by re-
308   jecting a certain fraction of test instances with the lowest confidence levels.
309
310   Top-p selection. We further propose a top-p selection strategy that selects the smallest subset of
311   solvers whose normalized scores (i.e., selection probabilities) sum up to at least p. The value of p
312   is predefined or adjusted according to the time budget. Thus, this strategy adaptively determines
313   the number of selected neural solvers by covering a certain amount of probability mass, rather than
314   relying on a fixed number k.

      4.1      E XPERIMENTAL SETTINGS
325
326   Synthetic TSP and CVRP. We generate synthetic TSP and CVRP instances by sampling node
327   coordinates from Gaussian mixture distributions with randomized covariance matrices. In the case
328   of CVRP, vehicle capacities are generated using either the scale-related capacity or the triangular
329   distribution. We consider varying problem scales, where the scale N is sampled uniformly from
330   [50, 500]. More details of the data generation process are provided in Appendix A.2.
331
332   Datasets. For training, we generate 10, 000 TSP and CVRP instances and apply 8-fold instance
333   augmentation (Kwon et al., 2020). For test, we generate smaller synthetic datasets comprising 1, 000
334
      instances. Figures 1(a) and 1(b) in Section 1 are based on results from the CVRP test dataset. To
      evaluate the out-of-distribution performance, we utilize two well-known benchmarks with more
335
      complex problem distributions and larger problem scales (up to N = 1002): TSPLIB (Reinelt,
336
      1991) and CVRPLIB Set-X (Uchoa et al., 2017). For TSPLIB, we select a subset of instances with
337   N ≤ 1002, and CVRPLIB Set-X includes instances ranging from N = 100 to 1000. These problem
338   scales are larger than the scale N ∈ [50, 500] of our training datasets.
339
340   Open-source neural solvers. We choose recent open-source neural solvers with state-of-the-art
341   performance as the candidates, including Omni (Zhou et al., 2023), BQ (Drakulic et al., 2023),
342   LEHD (Luo et al., 2023), DIFUSCO (Sun & Yang, 2023), T2T (Li et al., 2023), ELG (Gao et al.,
343   2024), INViT (Fang et al., 2024) and MVMoE (Zhou et al., 2024b). Greedy decoding is used for
344   all the methods to avoid stochasticity. We set the pomo size to 100 and the augmentation number
345   to 8 for the methods based on POMO (Kwon et al., 2020). The number of denoising steps is set to
346   50 and the number of 2-opt iterations is set to 100 for diffusion-based methods. These individual
347   solvers constitute a neural solver zoo. Ideally, if we can always select the best solver from the
348
      zoo for each instance, the optimal performance is achieved, which is also the performance upper
      bound of our selection model. Considering that some neural solvers contribute little to the overall
349
      performance, we iteratively eliminate the least contributive solver from the candidates, resulting in
350
      a more compact neural solver zoo. This process reduces the zoo size to 7 solvers for TSP and 5 for
351   CVRP. Further details of the elimination procedure are provided in Appendix A.3.
352
353   Hyperparameters. (1) Hyperparameters of graph encoders. For the graph attention encoder,
354   we set the number of layers to 4. For the hierarchical graph encoder, we use 2 blocks where each
355   block has 2 attention layers. The embedding dimension is set to 128. Other hyperparameters of
356   encoders can be found in Appendix A.1. (2) Hyperparameters of training. The Adam opti-
357   mizer (Kingma & Ba, 2015) is employed for training, where we set the learning rate to 1 × 10−4
358   and the weight decay to 1 × 10−6 . The number of epochs is set to 50. The final model is chosen
359   according to the performance on a validation dataset with 1, 000 synthetic instances. We train 5
360   selection models using different random seeds and report the mean and standard deviation of their
361
      performance. (3) Hyperparameters of selection strategies. For the top-k strategy, we set k = 2.

      5   C ONCLUSION AND DISCUSSIONS
487
488
      In this paper, we propose a general framework for neural solver selection for the first time, which can
489   effectively select suitable solvers for each instance, leading to significantly better performance with
490   little additional computational time, as validated by the extensive experiments on two well-studied
491   COPs, TSP and CVRP. Besides TSP and CVRP, our proposed selection framework is adaptable to
492   other problems. For new problems, one only needs to customize the feature extraction component.
493   For instance, when adapting our framework to scheduling problems, one can adjust the graph atten-
494   tion encoder according to MatNet (Kwon et al., 2021) (i.e., add edge embeddings). We hope this
495   preliminary work can open a new line for the topic of neural combinatorial optimization. Within
496   the proposed selection framework, we preliminarily investigate several implementations of the three
497
      key components: Feature extraction, training loss functions, and selection strategies. Techniques
      such as hierarchical graph encoder, ranking loss, rejection-based selection, and top-p selection no-
498
      tably enhance overall performance. Beyond the techniques presented, we discuss several promising
499
      avenues for further research under this framework.
500
501   Feature extraction for neural solvers.        In our implementation, we only extracted features for
502
      problem instances and used fixed indices for neural solvers, which assumes a static neural solver
      zoo and can not directly utilize any newly added neural solver during deployment. To enable zero-
503
      shot generalization to unseen neural solvers, it is essential to construct a smooth feature space for
504
      solvers, where those with similar preferences and biases are positioned closely together. Here we
505   design a preliminary method for extracting features of neural solvers to facilitate generalization
506   to unseen solvers. This method is based on the insight that a neural solver’s preferences can be
507   characterized by representative instances where it significantly outperforms other solvers. For each
508   neural solver, we sort those instances where it performs the best in an ascending order according to
509   the ratio of the objective value that the solver obtains to the runner-up objective value, and select
510   top 1% as its representative instances. Each representative instance is then treated as a token, and
511   we apply a transformer to learn a summary feature from these instance tokens, which serves as
512   the feature representation of the neural solver. Detailed implementations and results are provided
513
      in Appendix A.9. The results show that the preliminary method enables generalization to unseen
      neural solvers, where adding an extra solver can improve the selection performance.
514
515   Furthermore, advanced neural solver features should provide richer and deeper information than
516   only using instance features to increase the capacity of the selection model. However, our prelim-
517   inary method is based on the representative instances and fails to provide deeper information into
518   the solvers’ internal mechanisms. For future improvements, some approaches may be worth explor-
519
      ing, such as utilizing large language models to encode the code of neural solvers (Wu et al., 2024)
      or learning neural representations from their trained parameters (Kofinas et al., 2024), which can
520
      access internal solver information, and potentially improving selection performance.
521
522   Runtime-aware selection for learn-to-seach solvers. In this paper, since the average runtime of
523
      most individual neural solvers is short (approximately 1–2 seconds), we ignored their time difference
      during the training of the selection model, and only used the objective values obtained by the neural
524
      solvers as supervision information (by classification or ranking). However, if there are some time-
525
      consuming learn-to-search solvers, such as NeuOpt (Ma et al., 2021; 2023) and local reconstruction
526   methods (Kim et al., 2021; Ye et al., 2024), in the solver pool, the runtime should be considered in
527   the performance ranking. In such cases, developing a runtime-aware selection method to balance
528   computational time and solution optimality would be necessary. To address this, we could penalize
529   objective values based on time consumption or simultaneously optimize both metrics using multi-
530   objective learning methods (Lin et al., 2022).
531   Enhance the neural solver zoo by training. As shown in Figure 6, current neural solvers can
532   exhibit complementary performance over instances without any modification, which has motivated
533   our framework of neural solver selection. Inspired by the population-based training (Grinsztajn
534   et al., 2023), we can further enhance their complementary ability through finetuning, i.e., each neural
535   solver is finetuned on those instances where it performs the best. We can also train new solvers from
536   scratch by maximizing their performance contribution to the current solver zoo and iteratively add
537   such new solvers for enhancement. Moreover, to facilitate the training and deployment of a neural
538   solver zoo, it is essential to develop a unified platform that provides interfaces for executing and
539
      training diverse neural solvers, such as an extension to the existing RL4CO (Berto et al., 2024).

813
814   The graph attention layer is composed of two sub-layers: a multi-head attention sub-layer (Vaswani
815   et al., 2017) and a feed-forward sub-layer. Each sub-layer is equipped with residual connection (He
816   et al., 2016) and ReZero normalization (Bachlechner et al., 2021) for stable convergence of training.
817   Denote the embedding of the i-th node as hi (i.e., the i-th row of H). Since the graphs of TSP and
818   CVRP are typically considered to be fully connected, the graph attention layer is calculated as
819
                                 ĥi = hl−1
                                        i   + αl MHAli (hl−1  l−1       l−1
                                                         1 , h2 , ..., hN ),
820
821                              hli = ĥi + αl FF(ĥi ),
822
      where l is the layer index, i is the node index, αl is a learnable parameter used in the ReZero
823
      normalization, MHA and FF are short for the multi-head attention and the feed-forward network,
824   respectively. For the implementations of the basic components MHA and FF, we refer to Vaswani
825   et al. (2017) for details. Specifically, following the common settings of previous works, we set the
826   dimension of h to 128, the number of heads in MHA to 8, and the hidden dimension of FF to 512.

830   Synthetic TSP and CVRP instances are generated for training, where the node coordinates, demands,
831   and vehicle capacities are all sampled from manually defined distributions. The scale of each in-

833   demands are introduced as follows.

835   Node coordinates To generate diverse training instances, we utilize Gaussian mixture distri-

      butions to sample the node coordinates for both TSP and CVRP, which is common in previous

838
      randomly select the number of Gaussian components c ∼ U (0, 15) (when c = 0, we use the
839   uniform distribution) and partition the nodes randomly into c groups, one for each component.
840   For each Gaussian component, we sample the mean coordinates µ = (xµ , yµ ) by xµ ∼ U (0, 1)
841   and yµ ∼ U (0, 1), and sample the variances varx and vary uniformly from [1, 100]. The covari-
                                               √             √

859   A.3   E LIMINATE USELESS NEURAL SOLVERS
860
861   The preserved neural solvers should have distinct strengths in certain problem instances, ensuring
862   that they can bring significant improvements in overall performance. Motivated by this, we propose
863   a simple yet effective heuristic strategy to build the neural solver zoo based on the assessment of
      their contribution to the overall performance.


                                                            16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      Given the alternative neural solvers S = {s1 , s2 , s3 , ....}, we assess the contribution of a specific
865   solver si ∈ S by the degradation of performance after removing it. That is, the assessment of si can
866   be formalized as
867                                   A(si ) = EI [PI (S) − PI (S/si )] ,
868
      where PI (·) denotes the performance of a neural solver zoo on instance I. Here we use the percent-
869   age of the optimality gap to define PI (·) and employ a validation set for the estimation of expec-
870   tations. According to this criteria, we can estimate the alternative solvers and remove the one with
871   the lowest assessed contribution from S. This process repeats iteratively until for all si ∈ S, A(si )
872   surpasses the predefined threshold δ, indicating the significance of each alternative neural solver.
873   In practice, we collect the prevailing competitive neural solvers in the community to compose the
874   original S and set δ as 0.01%.

1016   A.7   D ETAILED COMPARISONS OF SELECTION STRATEGIES
1017
1018   According to the mechanisms of the four selection strategies, they have different preferences in the
1019   trade-off of efficiency and optimality. Generally, for efficiency, Greedy > Rejection ≈ Top-p >
1020   Top-k, for optimality, Top-k > Rejection ≈ Top-p > Greedy. Meanwhile, the hyper-parameters of
1021   them can be used for balancing efficiency and optimality as well. As a result, the choice of differ-
1022
       ent selection strategies can be decided by the users according to their preference, and we suggest
       using Top-p or Rejection as the default choices since they can adaptively select solvers based on the
1023
       confidence of the selection model.
1024
1025   The rejection-based selection and top-p selection are both designed to achieve better performance
       with little additional time consumption. To evaluate them in detail, we tune their parameters (e.g.,


                                                        19
          Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
          rejection ratio, k, and p) to obtain a range of results. For the rejection-based selection, we use
1027      k ∈ {2, 3, 4} and vary the rejection ratio from 0.05 to 0.85 in increments of 0.05. For the top-
1028      p selection, we adjust the value of p from 0.40 to 0.95 in increments of 0.01. The results of the
1029      optimality gap and time consumption are provided in Figure 4. As shown in the figures, the rejection
1030      strategy with smaller k tends to achieve better optimality gaps using the same time consumption.
1031      Therefore, we recommend k = 2 or 3 when using rejection-based selection. Comparing top-p
1032      selection and rejection-based selection, we can not definitively conclude which strategy is superior,
1033      which is expected since they share a similar idea of utilizing confidence levels to decide whether
1034      to employ multiple solvers. However, the top-p selection may be preferable in practice due to its
1035
          simplicity, where only a single hyperparameter p requires tuning.
1036      Furthermore, these results highlight p and the rejection ratio as important hyperparameters that allow
1037      users to balance efficiency and optimality. Though using a fixed value has led to good performance
1038      in our experiments, we believe that adaptively adjusting p and rejection ratio for each instance could
1039      further improve performance. Therefore, it is interesting to develop an instance-specific adapter for
1040      p or rejection ratio by leveraging instance features, which we leave for future works.

1082   To enable generalization to unseen neural solvers, we design a preliminary feature extraction method
1083   for neural solvers, which utilizes representative instances to represent a neural solver. For each
1084   neural solver, we sort those instances where the neural solver performs the best in an ascending
1085   order according to the ratio of the objective value that the solver obtains to the runner-up objective
1086   value, and select the top 1% as its representative instances. Then, we use an instance encoder to
1087   obtain embeddings for each representative instance, serving as their token vectors. A transformer
1088
       with two layers is employed to learn a summary feature from these instance tokens, which serves as
       the feature representation of the neural solver. More details are described as follows.
1089
1090
       Instance tokenization. We use a hierarchical graph encoder as the tokenization encoder to gen-
1091
       erate embeddings for each representative instance. Note that the parameters θ′ of the tokenization
1092
       encoder are not updated by back-propagation. Instead, to stabilize the instance tokens during train-
1093   ing, we update θ′ using a momentum-based moving average of the parameters θ of the instance
1094   feature encoder: θ′ ← m · θ′ + (1 − m) · θ, where m ∈ [0, 1) is a momentum coefficient (We set
1095   m = 0.99 in experiments). Only the parameters θ are updated via back-propagation. This momen-
1096   tum update ensures that θ′ evolves more smoothly than θ, resulting in stable instance tokenization.
1097
1098   Transformer architecture. For each neural solver, we utilize the tokens of its representative in-
1099   stances along with a learnable summary token to compute a summary representation. We apply two
1100   attention layers for this purpose. The first layer is a self-attention mechanism applied over all to-
1101   kens (including the summary token), enabling interactions among them. The second attention layer
1102
       uses only the summary token as the query and all tokens as keys and values, effectively aggregating
       information from all tokens into the summary token. The final embedding of the summary token is
1103
       then output as the neural solver’s representation.
1104
1105
       Selection model with neural solver feature.          The selection model integrates both the instance
1106
       features and the neural solver features to output a score for each instance-solver pair. We employ an
1107   MLP to compute these scores. For each instance, the scores across all neural solvers are normalized
1108   to derive the probability distribution for solver selection.
1109
1110
       To integrate a newly added neural solver, we first identify its representative instances from the train-
       ing dataset and employ the aforementioned networks to compute its feature representation. The
1111
       selection model can then leverage this new solver by considering its feature during selection, with-
1112
       out the need for any fine-tuning.
1113
1114   To evaluate the effectiveness of this method, we remove the second-best neural solver from the
1115   current solver zoo, train the selection model using the supervision information provided by the
1116
       pruned solver zoo, and reintroduce the removed solver during testing. Figure 5 presents the top-
       k selection performance with and without the newly added (extra) solver. The results show that
1117
       the performance with the newly added solver is generally better than the performance without it,
1118
       demonstrating that the selection model can leverage the information of unseen solvers without any
1119   finetuning. In other words, the selection model can generalize to unseen solvers. However, we
1120   observe a slight decrease in top-1 performance, indicating that the preliminary method requires
1121   further improvement.
