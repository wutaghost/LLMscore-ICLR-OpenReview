009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Flow matching (FM) is a general framework for defining probability paths via Or-
012            dinary Differential Equations (ODEs) to transform between noise and data sam-
013            ples. Recent approaches attempt to straighten these flow trajectories to generate
014            high-quality samples with fewer function evaluations, typically through iterative
015            rectification methods or optimal transport solutions. In this paper, we introduce
016            Consistency Flow Matching (Consistency-FM), a novel FM method that explicitly
017            enforces self-consistency in the velocity field. Consistency-FM directly defines
018            straight flows starting from different times to the same endpoint, imposing con-
019            straints on their velocity values. Additionally, we propose a multi-segment train-
020
               ing approach for Consistency-FM to enhance expressiveness, achieving a better
               trade-off between sampling quality and speed. Extensive experiments demonstrate
021
               that our Consistency-FM significantly improves training efficiency by converging
022
               4.4x faster than consistency models and 1.7x faster than rectified flow models
023            while achieving better generation quality.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   In recent years, deep generative models have provide an attractive family of paradigms that can
030   produce high-quality samples by modeling a data distribution, achieving promising results in many
031   generative scenarios, such as image generation (Ho et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2023; 2024a;b). As a
032   general and deterministic framework, Continuous Normalizing Flows (CNFs) (Chen et al., 2018) are
033
      capable of modeling arbitrary probability paths, specifically including the probability paths repre-
      sented by diffusion processes (Song et al., 2021). To scale up the training of CNFs, many works pro-
034
      pose efficient simulation-free approaches (Lipman et al., 2022; Albergo & Vanden-Eijnden, 2022;
035
      Liu et al., 2022) by parameterizing a vector field which flows from noise samples to data samples.
036   Lipman et al. (2022) proposes Flow Matching (FM) to train CNFs based on constructing explicit
037   conditional probability paths between the noise distribution and each data sample. Taking inspira-
038   tion from denoising score matching (Song & Ermon, 2019), FM further shows that a per-example
039   training objective can provide equivalent gradients without requiring explicit knowledge of the in-
040   tractable target vector field, thus incorporating existing diffusion paths as special instances.
041
      Straightness is one particularly-desired property of the trajectory induced by FM (Liu et al., 2022;
042   2023; Kornilov et al., 2024; Tong et al., 2023), because the straight path are not only the shortest
043   path between two end points, but also can be exactly simulated without time discretization. To learn
044   straight line paths which transport distribution π0 to π1 , Liu et al. (2022) learn a rectified flow from
045   data by turning an arbitrary coupling of π0 and π1 to a new deterministic coupling, and iteratively
046   train new rectified flows with the data simulated from the previously obtained rectified flow. Some
047   works resort to optimizing with an optimal transport plan by considering non-independent couplings
048   of k-sample empirical distributions (Pooladian et al., 2023; Tong et al., 2023). For example, OT-
049   CFM (Tong et al., 2023) attempts to approximate dynamic OT, creating simpler flows that are more
050
      stable to train and lead to faster inference.
051   However, despite their impressive generation quality, they still lack an effective trade-off between
052   sampling quality and computational cost in straightening flows. To be more specifically, iterative
053   rectification would suffer from accumulation error, and approximating an optimal transport plan
      in each training batch is computationally expensive. Therefore, a question naturally arises, can one

082
      In this work, we propose a new fundamental FM method, namely Consistency Flow Matching
083
      (Consistency-FM), to straighten the flows by explicitly enforcing self-consistency property in the
084
      velocity field. More specifically, Consistency-FM directly defines straight flows that start from dif-
085   ferent times to the same endpoint, and further constrains on their velocity values. To enhance the
086   model expressiveness and enable better transporting between complex distributions, we resort to
087   training Consistency-FM in a multi-segment approach, which constructs a piece-wise linear trajec-
088   tory. Moreover, this flexible time-to-time jump allows Consistency-FM to perform distillation on
089   pre-trained FM models for better trade-off between sampling speed and quality.
090
091
092   Comparison with Consistency Models Consistency Models (CMs) (Song et al., 2023) learn a set
093   of consistency functions that directly map noise to data. While CMs can generate sample with one
094   NFE, they fail to provide a satisfying trade-off between generation quality and computational cost
095   (Kim et al., 2023). Moreover, enforcing consistency property at arbitrary points is redundant and po-
096
      tentially slows down the training process. In contrast, our Consistency-FM enforces the consistency
      property over the space of velocity field instead of sample space, which can be viewed as a high-level
097
      regularization for straightening ODE trajectory. While CMs are able to learn consistency functions
098
      in a general form, Consistency-FM parameterizes the consistency functions as straight flows, which
099   enables faster training convergence without the need for approximating the entire probability path.
100
101
102   Main Contributions We summarize our contributions as follows: (i) We propose a new funda-
103   mental class of FM models that explicitly enforces the self-consistency in the space of velocity field
104   instead of sample space. (ii) We conduct sufficient theoretical analysis for our proposed Consistency-
105   FM, and enhance its expressiveness with multi-segment optimization. (iii) Extensive experiments on
106   three classical image datasets demonstrate the superior generation quality and training efficiency of
107   our Consistency-FM (e.g., 4.4 times and 1.7 times faster than consistency model and rectified flow).
      Further text-to-image experiments sufficiently prove our effectiveness and generalization ability.

      2              R ELATED W ORK AND D ISCUSSIONS
132
133
      Flow Matching for Generative Modeling Flow Matching (FM) aims to (implicitly) learn a vec-
134
      tor field {vt }t∈[0,1] , which generates an ODE that admits to the desired probability path {pt }t∈[0,1]
135   (Lipman et al., 2022). The training of FM does not require any computational challenging simu-
136   lation, as it directly estimate the vector field using a regression objective which can be efficiently
137   estimated (Lipman et al., 2022). By the construction of FM, it allows general trajectory of ODE
138   and probability path, thus many effort have been dedicated to design better trajectory with certain
139   properties(Pooladian et al., 2023; Tong et al., 2023; Klein et al., 2023; Stark et al., 2024; Campbell
140   et al., 2024). One particularly desired property is the straightness of the trajectory, as a straight
141   trajectory can be efficiently simulated with few steps of Euler integration. Concurrent works Multi-
142   sample FM (Pooladian et al., 2023) and Minibatch OT (Tong et al., 2023) propose to generalize the
143
      independent coupling of data distribution p0 (x0 ) and prior distribution p1 (x1 ) to optimal transport
      coupling plan π(x0 , x1 ). Under the optimal transport plan, the learned trajectory of ODE will tend
144
      to be straight. However, their methods require constructing the approximated optimal transport plan
145
      in each training batch, which is computationally prohibitive.
146
147   Rectified Flow (Liu et al., 2022; 2023) can be viewed as a FM with specific trajectory. Rectified
148   Flow proposes to rewire and straighten the trajectory by iterative distillation, which requires multiple
149
      round of training and may suffer from accumulation error. A recent work, Optimal FM (Kornilov
      et al., 2024) proposes to directly learn the optimal transport map from p1 to p0 and use it to calculate
150
      the vector field and straight trajectory. However, computing the optimal transport map in high
151
      dimension is a challenging task (Makkuva et al., 2020), and Optimal FM (Kornilov et al., 2024)
152   only provides experiments on toy datasets. In this paper, we propose to straighten the trajectory in a
153   more flexible and effective approach by enforcing the self-consistency property in the velocity field.
154
155
      Learning Efficient Generative Models GANs (Arjovsky et al., 2017; Goodfellow et al., 2014),
156   VAEs (Kingma & Welling, 2013), Diffusion Models (Song et al., 2020b;a; Ho et al., 2020) and
157   Normalizing Flows (Rezende & Mohamed, 2015; Dinh et al., 2016) have been four classical deep
158   generative models. Among them, GANs and VAEs are efficient one-step models. However, GANs
159   usually suffer from the training instability and mode collapse issues, and VAEs may struggle to gen-
160   erate high-quality examples. Therefore, recent works begin to utilize diffusion models and contin-
161   uous normalizing flows (Chen et al., 2018) for better training stability and high-fidelity generation,
      which are based on a sequence of expressive transformations for generative sampling.

162
      To achieve a better trade-off between sampling quality and speed, many efforts have been made
163   to accelerate diffusion models, either by modifying the diffusion process (Song et al., 2020a; Bao
164   et al., 2021; Dockhorn et al., 2021; Xiao et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2024b; Wang et al., 2024), with
165   an efficient ODE solver (Lu et al., 2022; Dockhorn et al., 2022; Zheng et al., 2023), or perform-
166   ing distillation between pre-trained diffusion models and their more efficient versions (e.g., with
167   less sampling steps) (Salimans & Ho, 2022; Liu et al., 2022; Luo et al., 2024; Luo, 2023). How-
168   ever, most distillation methods require multiple training rounds and are susceptible to accumulation
169   errors. Recent Consistency Models (Song et al., 2023; Song & Dhariwal, 2024) distill the entire
170   sampling process of diffusion model into one-step generation, while maintaining good sample qual-
171
      ity. Consistency Trajectory Models (CTMs)(Kim et al., 2023) further mitigate the issues about the
      accumulated errors in multi-step sampling. However, these methods must learn to integrate the full
172
      ODE integral, which are difficult to learn when it jumps between modes of the target distribution. In
173
      this paper, we propose a new concept of velocity consistency with defined straight probability flows,
174   achieving most competitive results on both one- and multi-step generation.

      3.2   D EFINING S TRAIGHT F LOWS WITH C ONSISTENT V ELOCITY
205
206   Motivation Recent FM methods for learning straight flows typically necessitate the approxima-
207   tion the probability path pt and its marginal distributions p0 and p1 (Liu et al., 2022; 2023; Pooladian
208   et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2023) , which are computational intensive and introduce additional approx-
209   imation error. To address these challenges, we introduce Consistency-FM, a general method to
210   efficiently learn straight flows without the need for approximating the entire probability path.
211   A straightforward approach to learn straight flows is to identify a consistent ground truth vector field
212   and then use objective in Eq. (2) for training. The definition of consistent velocity is v(t, γx (t)) =
213   v(0, x), indicating the velocity along the solution of Eq. (1) remains constant. However, due to the
214   intractability of original data distribution, it is also intractable to find such a vector field, or to design
215   a conditional vector field such that the corresponding marginal velocity is consistent (Lipman et al.,
      2022; Pooladian et al., 2023).

      ments, we follow previous methods (Liu et al., 2023; Yan et al., 2024) to use Stable Diffusion v1.5
371
      (Rombach et al., 2022) as the backbone model. We set ∆t = 0.001 in all experiments. Multi-
372   segment consistency-FM is applied only for experiments where the number of function evaluations
373   (NFE) is greater than 1. And the NFE is equivalent to the number of segments for the multi-segment
374   Consistency-FM. We employ Consistency-FM based distillation training with a batch size of 128 for
375   200,000 iterations, consuming a total of 25 A100 GPU days. In comparison, the distillation process
376   of InstaFlow (Liu et al., 2023) requires 110 A100 GPU days. Thus our training cost is only 23% of
377   that of InstaFlow’s distillation process. More experimental settings can be found in Table 5 of the
      Appendix B, and we also provide our code in the supplementary materials.

523   5      C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
524
525   In this paper, we introduce a new fundamental class of FM models, namely Consistency Flow Match-
526   ing (Consistency-FM), to explicitly enforces self-consistency in the velocity field. Consistency-FM
527   directly defines straight flows starting from different times to the same endpoint, and is optimized by
528   a multi-segment training approach for enhancing expressiveness. This work theoretically and empir-
529
      ically presents our new fundamental flow matching model. Extensive experiments demonstrate that
      our Consistency-FM significantly improves training efficiency by converging 4.4x faster than con-
530
      sistency models and 1.7x faster than rectified flow models while achieving better generation quality.
531
      Based on our Consistency-FM, we here propose two potential future research directions:
532
533            • Compositional Text-to-Image Generation: Our Consistency-FM demonstrates superior
534              text-to-image results, especially in conveying complex textual semantics. By applying
535              Consistency-FM to existing compositional T2I models (Yang et al., 2024a), we believe
536              this would significantly improve both efficiency and quality of existing methods.
537            • Efficient Video Generation: Training video diffusion models is a time-consuming proce-
538              dure (Blattmann et al., 2023; Tian et al., 2024), and accelerating training process is a critical
539              challenge. Employing our Consistency-FM on training video diffusion models would ef-
                 fectively reduce training costs while keeping high-quality generation.
