011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Self-critic has become a crucial mechanism for enhancing the reasoning perfor-
014            mance of LLMs. However, current approaches mainly involve basic prompts for
015
               intuitive instance-level feedback, which resembles System-1 processes and lim-
               its the reasoning capabilities. Moreover, there is a lack of in-depth investigations
016
               into the relationship between LLM’s ability to criticize and its task-solving per-
017
               formance. To address these issues, we propose Critic-CoT, a novel framework
018            that pushes LLMs toward System-2-like critic capability. Through a step-wise
019            CoT reasoning paradigm and the automatic construction of weak-supervision data
020            without human annotation, Critic-CoT enables LLMs to engage in slow, analytic
021            self-critique and refinement, thereby improving their reasoning abilities. Experi-
022            ments on GSM8K and MATH demonstrate that our enhanced model significantly
023            boosts task-solving performance by filtering out invalid solutions or iterative re-
024            finement. Furthermore, we investigate the intrinsic correlation between critique
025            and task-solving abilities within LLMs, discovering that these abilities can mutu-
026
               ally reinforce each other rather than conflict.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Enhancing the reasoning abilities of large language models is essential for creating more intelli-
031   gent and reliable AI systems, which has drawn extensive attention from researchers (Chollet, 2019;
032   Bubeck et al., 2023; Morris et al., 2024). From a cognitive perspective, the procedure of human
033   reasoning involves constant reflection and revision (Hegel et al., 1991; Kierkegaard, 1989; Popper,
034   1934), which has inspired increasing focus on integrating self-critic mechanisms in the reasoning
035   process of large-scale models (Kim et al., 2023; Shinn et al., 2023; Madaan et al., 2023). This in-
036   volves iteratively allowing the model to generate feedback on its own responses and then refining
037   its reasoning based on the feedback. Compared with traditional critic methods that depend on feed-
038   back from external sources (Saunders et al., 2022; McAleese et al., 2024), self-critic relies solely
039
      on the model’s internal capabilities, thus reducing the high cost of additional human annotation, and
      serving as a promising potential solution to scalable oversight (Leike et al., 2018; Burns et al., 2023;
040
      Cao et al., 2024).
041
042   However, current studies primarily focus on utilizing LLMs’ critique abilities to enhance their per-
043   formance. Yet, relatively little attention has been given to the investigation and development of the
044   critique ability itself. Firstly, existing critique methods are often overly simplistic, typically relying
045
      on a basic prompt to directly point out the error, without stepwise Chain-of-Thought examination
      or training procedure, which leads to relatively poor self-critic accuracy (Luo et al., 2023; West
046
      et al., 2024). Specifically, proposing a valid critique is a complicated task that requires a thorough
047
      understanding of statements and precise negativity. However, current LLMs are normally not ex-
048   plicitly trained for critic capability. Therefore, these simple approaches usually tend to “criticize”
049   like System-1, which is more intuitive and likely to make mistakes, rather than more rigorous and
050   deliberate System-2 (Kahneman, 2011; Yu et al., 2024), while shifting LLMs from System-1 to-
051   ward System-2 emerges as a promising approach for improving the reasoning capability (OpenAI,
052   2024). This limitation diminishes the effectiveness of self-critic and, further, self-correct (Huang
053   et al., 2024). Secondly, the capabilities of task-solving and self-critic are both dependent on the
      model’s inherent knowledge, while there is currently a lack of in-depth exploration regarding the

074   poses a significant obstacle to the subsequent development in this direction.
075   To this end, this paper is devoted to diving into the following critical research questions:
076
077              • How can we enhance a model’s critique ability, pushing it toward System 2 reasoning?
078

      To answer the above questions, as shown in Figure 1, we propose Critic-CoT, a novel framework
081
      designed to enhance LLMs’ reasoning abilities. Through step-wise Chain-of-Thought critique for-
082
      mat and weak supervision, our method is able to strengthen System-2-like critic ability, without the
083   intensive cost of human annotation. Specifically, during training, we let LLMs criticize and refine
084   their solutions in a complete CoT way, and collect successful pairs that convert wrong solutions into
085   correct ones, or affirm the validity of original right solutions. After supervised fine-tuning on the
086   obtained step-wise critic-refine data, we enable the target LLM to analyze and criticize each step of
087   its generated reasoning procedure, so that it can filter out wrong attempts and preserve the correct
088   ones with greater precision. During inference, to leverage the model’s abilities of CoT-critique and
089   refinement, we employ two strategies: (1) majority vote filtering involves using the critic model
090   to evaluate multiple generated solutions and filter out those incorrect; and (2) iterative refinement,
091
      on the other hand, involves repeatedly critiquing and refining a solution until no further error is
      detected.
092
093   Through a series of experiments on the dataset of GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021a) and MATH
094   (Hendrycks et al., 2021), we found that our trained critic model can fairly distinguish incorrect
095   solutions from correct ones, and improve the reasoning accuracy via iterative refinement or critic
096   filtering. These results demonstrate the helpfulness and effectiveness of our proposed method. Ad-
097
      ditionally, we observed that our critic model already exhibits noticeable performance improvements
      in task-solving, even in the absence of additional critique steps during the decoding phase. Such
098
      findings reveal that strengthening the ability to critique and refinement would not compromise the
099
      task-solving performance, but improve it. This also suggests the presence of an intrinsic mechanism
100   by which critique ability and task-solving capability mutually reinforce one another.
101
102   We summarize our main contributions as follows:
103
                 • We propose Critic-CoT, which pushes the critic paradigm of LLMs from System-1-like
104
                   incentive “thinking” toward System-2-like deliberate “reasoning”.
105
106              • Through experiments, we find that Critic-CoT can effectively teach the model to criticize
107                and refine its own output step by step, thus noticeably improving the reasoning perfor-
                   mance.

115   2.1   D ISCRIMINATIVE V ERIFIER FOR M ATHEMATICS
116
      To further improve the reasoning ability of Large language models, one applicable approach is
117
      through the use of reward models, which can either be used in reinforcement learning during train-
118   ing (Ouyang et al., 2022) or rejection sampling at test time (Cobbe et al., 2021b). While outcome-
119   supervised reward models (ORMs) allow for the automatic collection of training data based on the
120   signal of the gold answer, process-supervised reward models (PRMs) would be more advantageous
121   for more precise feedback, better interpretability and stronger alignment (Lightman et al., 2024).
122
      To reduce the considerable human labeling cost and difficulty for dense annotation, a series of works
123
      based on automatic approaches have been proposed (Wang et al., 2023a; Chen et al., 2024b; Luo
124   et al., 2024; Snell et al., 2024), all under the heuristic that for an incorrect solution, the first error step
125   is where the continuation of previous step would lead to a correct answer. This may bring noise into
126   training data due to false positives and negatives (Luo et al., 2024). Moreover, annotation based on
127   the implicit solution continuation alone does not leverage LLM’s emerging ability of critic, which
128   is in a more explicit and analytic way and brings better explainability (Saunders et al., 2022; Yuan
129   et al., 2024; Luo et al., 2023; McAleese et al., 2024). Additionally, binary 0/1 discrimination alone,
130   whether outcome-based or process-based, remains more similar to System-1 reasoning rather than
131   the desirable System-2, thus may not fully leverage the computation power support by empirically
132
      successful Chain-of-Thought prompting (Feng et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024).
133
134   2.2   C RITIC M ODEL
135
      Learning from natural language feedback could be beneficial (Chen et al., 2024a). With the devel-
136
      opment of LLM, whether it can discriminate and criticize its own output in a text-generation manner
137   becomes an interesting topic (Luo et al., 2023; Zeng et al., 2023), with doubts at least on off-the-
138   shelf LLMs that are not specially trained for such task (Huang et al., 2024; West et al., 2024; Liu
139   et al., 2024). Later, several works are proposed to improve self-reflection via a carefully designed
140   prompting pipeline (Zhang et al., 2024b; Yan et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024). Current applications,
141   such as response evaluation, heavily rely on the reference (Zheng et al., 2023). Therefore, given
142   the limited critic ability of current LLMs, how to train a robust and applicable critic model is worth
143   investigating. Concurrently, Zhang et al. (2024a) trained a generative reward model on the outcome
144   level rather than the process level but did not incorporate refinement into the schema.
145   From the perspective of recursive reward modeling (Leike et al., 2018; Saunders et al., 2022) and
146   scalable oversight (Burns et al., 2023), McAleese et al. (2024) recently trained “CriticGPT” to assist
147   human labelers, which aims to improve the ability of human rather than the base model, i.e. improve
148   the overall recall of error detection, rather than precision. While in this paper, we try to explore
149   whether improving the reasoning ability of LLM without costly human annotation is applicable.

156   followed by the training process in Section 3.1, and finally, the inference strategies in 3.3.

205   3.2      AUTO T RAIN : T WO -S TAGE T RAINING
206
      To enable the model to acquire self-critiquing and refining capabilities, we first need to provide it
207   with basic critiquing abilities, followed by self-critique for further enhancement. The overall training
208   procedure is divided into two stages.
209
210   Stage 1 In the first step, we collect high-quality critique data to provide the model’s basic cri-
211   tiquing ability. Specifically, we first sample both positive and negative solutions from a representa-
212   tive instruction-following model MG on the dataset D. Then, we utilize SOTA LLMs like GPT4-
213   Turbo to serve as critic model MC . For each generated attempt Att, the critic model will retry at
214   most k times to produce a valid critique until it reaches one ofSthe weak supervision constraints.
215   This will form the critic-refine dataset D1 = {(Q, Att, Cri)} {(Q, Att, Cri−1 , Att′ )} for fine-
      tuning the initial model M0 into the critic model M1 . Note that in this process, we actually distill

217   bound of the student model is not necessarily limited by the teacher model’s performance.

219   Stage 2 In the second step, we leverage the model’s self-critique to enhance its critiquing and
220   refining capabilities further. Namely, we let the learned critic model M1 criticize and refine its

223   criticize and refine at most k times. In case the model fails to successfully critique even after k

229   3.3     I NFERENCE : S ELF -C HECK
230
      To leverage our learned abilities of critique and refinement for more precise reasoning, we employ
231
      two different inference strategies: “iterative refine” and “critic as filter”.
232
233   Iterative Refine One single-turn refinement, which consists of multiple steps, may still contain
234   errors. Therefore, we could iteratively inspect the refined solution, and re-refine once the critique
235   found a mistake, and only output the final solution if it’s convincing for the critic, or if it reached
236   the maximum retry. To avoid de-generation after too many refinements, we set the maximum refine
237   depth d = 8, and restart from the initial solution after d unsuccessful refinement at most n = 8
238   times. Figure 3 presents a single successful round of critique and refinement.
239
240   Critic As Filter Self-consistency is an effective way to reduce variance and improve accuracy.
241   With the ability to critique, we can filter out predict-to-be-wrong answers to further boost the per-
242   formance. Specifically, for the m attempts S = {(Att, P red)}, we first let our model M check
243
      each attempt and obtain the stepwise label, which is Sc = {(Att, P red, L)}. And then those which
      detect the error at some step are filtered out and reach Sc′ = {(Att, P red, L)| − 1 ∈
                                                                                           / L}. Finally, we
244
      perform the majority vote to get the answer.

530   5    C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this paper, we introduced the Critic-CoT paradigm to enhance the reasoning abilities of Large
533
      Language Models, through a more System-2-like, step-by-step Chain-of-Thought critique. Our ap-
534   proach leverages weak supervision to construct training data for critiques and refinements, thereby
535   reducing the reliance on extensive human annotation. We demonstrated the effectiveness of our
536   method through substantial improvements across the dataset of GSM8K and MATH. Additionally,
537   our results present that training on the capabilities of critique and refinement alone improves task-
538   solving performance, which indicates a mutual-reinforce mechanism within the LLMs. We hope our
539   work may inspire further investigations into the advancement of the self-critic framework and the
      transition toward System-2 reasoning.

      A.2           D ISCUSSION
879
      A.2.1             D IFFERENCES B ETWEEN C RITIC -C OT AND R EFLEXION
880
881   We adopt a similar approach to Relexion (Shinn et al., 2023), which leverages natural language
882   critique to facilitate refinement, but our method diverges in the following ways:
883
884   Step-wise CoT Critique Reflexion translates and augments the binary reward signal from the
885   environment to natural language, but on an instance level. Instead, fine-grained Chain-of-Thought
886   analysis at the step level, which is more systematic, and enables us to locate the error and start
887
      refinement from a specific step, rather than refine the whole attempt.
888
      Enhanced Critic ability While Relexion proposed an in-context learning pipeline for policy op-
889
      timization under the oracle success/fail binary feedback signal, Huang et al. (2024) showed that
890
      without external feedback, vanilla LLMs cannot self-correct effectively due to limited critique abil-
891   ity. Therefore, to teach the LLMs the ability of intrinsic self-critique, our approach tries to learn the
892   critique ability itself, through Critic-CoT training, and can apply it to test-time situations where the
893   oracle feedback signal is not available.
894
895   A.2.2             S OURCE OF I MPROVEMENT
896
897   As discussed in Sections 4.3 and 4.5, the improvements can be attributed to two key factors: training
898
      with critique and refinement, and application of critique and refinement at test time.
899
                    • Strengthening the ability to critique and refinement would not compromise the task-solving
900                   performance, but improve it. Therefore, our critic model already exhibits noticeable per-
901                   formance improvements in task-solving, even in the absence of additional critique steps
902                   during the inference phase.
903
                    • At the inference phase, we can actively leverage the model’s ability to reflect on its rea-
904                   soning and correct mistakes via Critic As Filter and Iterative Refinement, which leads to
905                   additional improvements.
906
907
      A.2.3             C OMPARISON B ETWEEN I NFERENCE M ETHODS
908
909   As the results in Table 1, 2, 3 shows, the performance of majority vote and Critic As Filter surpass
910   Iterative Refinement. We believe it’s due to the intrinsic challenges of refinement and the relatively
911   limited search space.
912
      On the one hand, for Iterative Refinement to work properly, it requires the model to 1) Detect errors
913   on an attempt; 2) Refine the mistakes; 3) Exit if no further errors are detected. This pipeline could be
914   more sensitive to error accumulation. Moreover, it only edits on a single example and has a limited
915   retry, which is sample-efficient, but may not explore the solution space more actively, as majority
916   vote does. Specifically on the dataset of GSM8K, the invocation statistics are as follows:
917
                    • Majority vote: 1319 * 96 = 126,624

923   On the other hand, majority vote is a strong baseline, as it requires massive sampling. It leverages

      more reasonable candidates, rather than equally account for all the predicted answers as the vanilla

927
      strong ability to critique.
