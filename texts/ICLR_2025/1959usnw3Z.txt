009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) are powerful models for learning representations
012            of attributed graphs. To scale GNNs to large graphs, many methods use various
013            techniques, such as sampling and decoupling, to alleviate the “neighbor explo-
014            sion” problem during mini-batch training. However, these sampling-based mini-
015            batch training methods often suffer from greater information loss than decoupling-
016            based methods or full-batch GNNs. Besides, most original segmentation methods
017            for large graphs usually lose a large number of edges, resulting in suboptimal per-
018
               formance when performing mini-batch training. Therefore, we propose a Chordal
               Graph Sampling-based mini-batch Training algorithm for GNNs on large scale
019
               graph datasets, called CGST. CGST includes a balanced chordal graph parti-
020
               tion module and a batch random aggregation module to improve performance on
021            node classification tasks while maintaining main information of the original graph
022            structure. Experiments on three large-scale graph datasets prove the effectiveness
023            of CGST.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   The Graph Neural Network(GNN) has garnered significant attention in the realm of graph-based ap-
029   plications, encompassing tasks such as semi-supervised node classification (Kipf & Welling, 2017),
030   link prediction (Zhang & Chen, 2018), and recommender systems (Ying et al., 2018a). Within
031   the framework of a given graph, GNN employs graph convolutional operations to compute node
032   embeddings across multiple layers iteratively. At each layer, a node’s embedding is derived by ag-
033
      gregating information from its neighboring nodes, subsequently undergoing one or more layers of
      linear transformations and nonlinear activations. The resultant embedding at the final layer is then
034
      utilized for various downstream tasks. For instance, in scenarios involving node classification, the
035
      final layer embedding is fed into a classifier to infer node labels, facilitating end-to-end training of
036   GNN parameters.
037
038   Despite the notable achievements of GNNs in numerous graph-related applications, the training of
039
      GNNs for extensive graphs poses a significant challenge. Unlike text or images with usually con-
      strained lengths or sizes, practical graph data can frequently present itself in immensely expansive
040
      scales or dimensions, reflecting the complexity and magnitude inherent in real-world graph struc-
041
      tures. For instance, the 2019 Facebook social network comprises 2.7 billion users (Leskovec &
042   Mcauley, 2012), exemplifying the immense scale of real-world graphs. Managing such large-scale
043   graphs through full-batch GNN training, where all nodes are processed together to update parame-
044   ters, is infeasible. Nevertheless, previous mini-batch GNN training methods often suffer from huge
045   information loss, since the connections and cross-community nodes are removed when partition-
046   ing the large-scale graphs into subgraphs. Consequently, the training of deep and expansive GNNs
047   remains a formidable task, impeding their deployment in various large-scale graph applications,
048   including social networks, recommender systems, and knowledge graphs.
049   To solve the issues, researchers have proposed sampling-based methods to train GNNs based on
050   mini-batch of nodes, which only aggregate the embeddings of a sampled subset of neighbors of
051   each node in the mini-batch. Among them, one direction is to use a node-wise neighbor-sampling
052   method. For example, GraphSAGE (Hamilton et al., 2017) calculates each node embedding by
053   leveraging only a fixed number of uniformly sampled neighbors. Although this kind of approach
      reduces the computation cost in each aggregation operation, the total cost can still be large. As

072   In this paper, we propose a novel subgraph-sampling based mini-batch training method called CGST
073   to efficiently train GNNs without losing too much information. Rather than building a GNN on
074   the full training graph and then sampling across the layers, we first sample the training graph and
075
      partition it into several chordal subgraphs with balanced size. By doing so, CGST can signifi-
      cantly reduce the CPU memory required for GNN training, making it more scalable to larger graph
076
      datasets. Besides, CGST also applies random aggregation technique among subgraphs to alleviate
077
      the the influence of partitioning the whole graph. By building a complete GNN model on the sub-
078   graphs, CGST aims to maintain the performance characteristics of the full-batch training, avoiding
079   the accuracy degradation seen in some prior sampling-based approaches. This allows us to capture
080   the inherent node dependencies within the sampled subgraphs while avoiding the need to store the
081   entire computation graph in GPU memory during back-propagation.
082
      Our main contributions can be summarized as below:
083
084   • Balanced Chordal Subgraph Partition Module. To solve the first challenge that previous graph
085     partition methods are difficult to form tight clusters, we build a graph partition module to split
086     the original graph into subgraphs with balanced size. In this module, we extract appropriately
087     connected subgraphs so that little information is lost when propagating within the subgraphs. A
088     graph partition method is applied to generate several well-partitioned chordal subgraphs, which
089     means chordal graphs with balanced sizes.
090   • Random Aggregation Module. To solve the second challenge that graph clustering algorithms
091     tend to remove edges and cross-community nodes from the original datasets, we propose a random
092     aggregation clustering approach to incorporate between-cluster links and reduce variance across
093     batches.
094   • Evaluation on three large-scale datasets. Under extensive experiments on four real-world
095     datasets, we show that CGST provides consistent boosts in the performance of node classifica-
096     tion tasks over large-scale graph datasets.

      2.1   L AYER S AMPLING
101
102   A neural network model that extends convolution operation to the graph domain is first proposed
103   by Bruna et al. (2013). Further, Kipf & Welling (2016); Defferrard et al. (2016) speed up graph
104   convolution computation with localized filters based on Chebyshev expansion. They target relatively
105   small datasets and thus the training proceeds in full batch. In order to scale GCNs to large graphs,
106   layer sampling techniques (Hamilton et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2018b; Ying et al., 2018b; Chen et al.,
107   2018a; Gao et al., 2018; Huang et al., 2018) have been proposed for efficient minibatch training. The
      layer sampling algorithm of GraphSAGE (Hamilton et al., 2017) performs uniform node sampling


                                                             2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      on the previous layer neighbors. It enforces a pre-defined budget on the sample size, so as to bound
109   the minibatch computation complexity.
110
111   S-GCN (Chen et al., 2018a) further restricts neighborhood size by requiring only two support nodes
112
      in the previous layer. The idea is to use the historical activations in the previous layer to avoid
      redundant re-evaluation. FastGCN (Chen et al., 2018b) performs sampling from another perspective.
113
      Instead of tracking down the inter-layer connections, node sampling is performed independently for
114
      each layer. It applies importance sampling to reduce variance, and results in constant sample size in
115   all layers. However, the minibatches potentially become too sparse to achieve high accuracy. Huang
116   et al. (2018) improves FastGCN by an additional sampling neural network. It ensures high accuracy,
117   since sampling is conditioned on the selected nodes in the next layer. Significant overhead may be
118   incurred due to the expensive sampling algorithm and the extra sampler parameters to be learned.
119   In addition, the work in Zeng et al. (2018a) proposes a subgraph based training algorithm that is
120   scalable with respect to GCN depth, and also highly parallelizable on multi-core machines.
121
122   2.2   S UBGRAPH S AMPLING
123
124   Instead of sampling layers, the works of Zeng et al. (2018b) and Chiang et al. (2019) build mini-
125   batches from subgraphs. Zeng et al. (2018b) proposes a specific graph sampling algorithm to ensure
126   connectivity among minibatch nodes. They further present techniques to scale such training on
127   shared-memory multi-core platforms. More recently, ClusterGCN (Chiang et al., 2019) proposes
128   graph clustering based minibatch training. During pre-processing, the training graph is partitioned
129
      into densely connected clusters. During training, clusters are randomly selected to form minibatches,
      and intra-cluster edge connections remain unchanged.
130
131
132   2.3   GNN D ECOUPLING
133
134   Another line of research focuses on improving model capacity. Applying attention on graphs, the
135
      architectures of Zeng et al. (2019) better capture neighbor features by dynamically adjusting edge
      weights.Klicpera et al. (2018) combines PageRank with GCNs to enable efficient information prop-
136
      agation from many hops away. To develop deeper models, “skip-connection” is borrowed from
137
      CNNs (He et al., 2015; Huang et al., 2017) into the GCN context. In particular, JK-net Xu et al.
138   (2018) demonstrates significant accuracy improvement on GCNs with more than two layers. Note,
139   however, that JK-net (Xu et al., 2018) follows the same sampling strategy as GraphSAGE (Hamilton
140   et al., 2017). Thus, its training cost is high due to neighbor explosion. In addition, high order graph
141   convolutional layers (Zhou, 2017; Lee et al., 2018; Abu-El-Haija et al., 2019) also help propagate
142   long-distance features. With the numerous architectural variants developed, the question of how to
143   train them efficiently via minibatches still remains to be answered.

258

259   several well-partitioned chordal subgraphs, which means chordal graphs with balanced sizes.
260
261   A chordal graph is one in which all cycles of four or more vertices have a chord, which is an edge
262
      that is not part of the cycle but connects two vertices of the cycle. Equivalently, every induced
      cycle in the graph should have exactly three vertices. The chordal graphs may also be characterized
263
      as graphs that have perfect elimination orderings, as graphs in which each minimal separator is a
264
      clique, and as the intersection graphs of subtrees of a tree. In mathematical, a chordal graph can be
265   formally defined as below:
266
267   Definition 1 Chordal graph. A graph is chordal if every cycle of length at least 4 has a chord. A
268   vertex v of G is called simplicial in G if N (v) is a clique in G. The ordering {v1 , . . . , vn } of the
269   vertices of G is a perfect elimination order of G if for all i, vi is simplicial in G[v1 , . . . , vi ]. Also, a
      graph is chordal if it has a perfect elimination order.

301   4.3   R ANDOM AGGREGATION
302
303   In this module, in order to fetch each node i’s neighbor nodes’ embeddings, we need to further
304   aggregate each neighbor node’s neighbor nodes’ embeddings as well. Although vanilla subgraph-
305   sampling based method achieves good computational and memory complexity, there are still two
306
      potential issues:
307   • After the graph is partitioned, some links are removed. Thus the performance could be affected.
308
      • Graph clustering algorithms tend to bring similar nodes together. Hence the distribution of a
309
        cluster could be different from the original data set, leading to a biased estimation of the full
310
        gradient while performing SGD updates.
311
312   In order to tackle the aforementioned challenges, we introduce a batch random aggregation mecha-
313   nism designed to integrate inter-cluster connections and reduce variability across batches. Initially,
314   we segment the graph into clusters denoted as k clusters C1 , · · · , Ck with a relatively large value
315   of k. When forming a batch B for a Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) update, instead of select-
316   ing a single cluster, we randomly pick n clusters, denoted as c1 , . . . , cn and include their nodes
317
      Vc1 ∪ · · · ∪ Vcn into the batch. Moreover, the connections between the chosen clusters,
318                                         {Aij | i, j ∈ c1 , . . . , cn }
319   are reintroduced. This method ensures the reintegration of inter-cluster links and reduces batch-to-

476
      datasets is longer. This is because chordal graph partitioning algorithm tend to bring similar nodes
477
      together. Hence the distribution of a cluster could be different from the original data set, leading to
478   a biased estimation of the full gradient while performing SGD updates. In summary, the experiment

        6             C ONCLUSION
528
529
       We propose a new algorithm namely CGST for scaling GNNs to large-scale graph datasets. CGST
530    includes two modules: a balanced chordal graph partition module and a batch random aggrega-
531    tion module. The first module extracts appropriately connected subgraphs so that little information
532    is lost when propagating within the subgraphs. A graph partition method is applied to generate
533    several well-partitioned chordal subgraphs, which means chordal graphs with balanced sizes. The
534    second module solves the challenge that graph clustering algorithms tend to remove edges and cross-
535    community nodes from the original datasets. Finally, under extensive experiments on four real-world
536    datasets, we show that CGST provides consistent boosts in the performance of node classification
537    tasks over large-scale graph datasets.
