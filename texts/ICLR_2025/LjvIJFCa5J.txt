009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Vision-and-language navigation (VLN) aims to guide autonomous agents through
012            real-world environments by integrating visual and linguistic cues. Despite no-
013            table advancements in ground-level navigation, the exploration of aerial naviga-
014            tion using these modalities remains limited. This gap primarily arises from a
015            lack of suitable resources for real-world, city-scale aerial navigation studies. To
016            remedy this gap, we introduce CityNav, a novel dataset explicitly designed for
017            language-guided aerial navigation in photorealistic 3D environments of real cities.
018
               CityNav comprises 32k natural language descriptions paired with human demon-
               stration trajectories, collected via a newly developed web-based 3D simulator.
019
               Each description identifies a navigation goal, utilizing the names and locations
020
               of landmarks within actual cities. As an initial step toward addressing this chal-
021            lenge, we provide baseline models of navigation agents that incorporate an internal
022            2D spatial map representing landmarks referenced in the descriptions. We have
023            benchmarked the latest aerial navigation methods alongside our proposed base-
024            line model on the CityNav dataset. The findings are revealing: (i) our aerial agent
025            model trained on human demonstration trajectories, outperform those trained on
026            shortest path trajectories by a large margin; (ii) incorporating 2D spatial map in-
027            formation markedly and robustly enhances navigation performance at a city scale;
028            (iii) despite the use of map information, our challenging CityNav dataset reveals a
029
               persistent performance gap between our baseline models and human performance.
               To foster further research in aerial VLN, we have made the dataset and code avail-
030
               able at https://anonymous.4open.science/w/city-nav-77E3/.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   In the rapidly evolving field of Vision-and-Language Navigation (VLN) (Anderson et al., 2018b;
037   Krantz et al., 2020), the integration of linguistic cues with visual data has opened new frontiers in
038   autonomous navigation systems. Recently, significant progress has been made in the development of
039
      VLN datasets across varied environments from indoor house scenes (Khanna et al., 2024; Ku et al.,
      2020; Liu et al., 2021; Qi et al., 2020; Wijmans et al., 2019) to outdoor urban scenes (Chen et al.,
040
      2019; Hermann et al., 2020; Mirowski et al., 2018), including robotics applications (Anderson et al.,
041
      2020; Shah et al., 2023). While existing datasets have been primarily developed for ground-level
042   navigation applications, such as home-assistance robots and autonomous vehicles, the extension
043   of VLN to aerial domains has remained relatively unexplored. Aerial navigation presents unique
044   challenges, including vast 3D spaces and a notable lack of real-world data in existing datasets. This
045   scarcity has impeded progress in aerial VLN and further advancements in unmanned aerial vehicle
046   (UAV) applications.
047
      Aerial VLN, which guides UAVs through environments using both visual observations and language
048   instructions, offers distinct advantages over traditional algorithm-based route planning, especially in
049   scenarios where the destination is uncertain. For instance, autonomous navigation utilizing simple
050   language instructions can significantly enhance search and rescue operations for missing persons
051   or objects in complex terrains, such as mountainous or urban environments (Karaca et al., 2018).
052   Furthermore, it proves invaluable in monitoring and search activities under adverse conditions, such
053   as natural disasters or in areas with unreliable Global Navigation Satellite System (GNSS) sig-
      nals (Wang et al., 2023; Khan et al., 2021).

      While a few studies have begun to explore aerial navigation, they often rely on less realistic data
083
      sources, which limit their practical application in real-world scenarios. For instance, Fan et al.
084   (2023) utilized satellite imagery for aerial navigation, which spurred advancements in aerial VLN
085   agents, yet it overlooked the 3D geometries encountered in actual UAV flights. Similarly, Liu et al.
086   (2023c) employed data from virtual urban environments created by game engines. This approach
087   facilitated diverse 3D maneuvers but compromised the complexity and realism found in high-density
088   point clouds typically scanned in the real world, thus reducing the practical utility of geographic
089   information. These studies highlight the urgent need for more realistic and comprehensive datasets
090   that accurately capture the complexities and challenges inherent in aerial VLN.
091   In this work, we introduce CityNav, a dataset specifically designed for language-guided aerial nav-
092   igation on a city scale. This dataset is aimed at developing intelligent aerial agents that are capable
093   of identifying specific geographical objects in real-world cities based on natural language descrip-
094   tions. It includes descriptions for city-scale point cloud data from SensatUrban (Hu et al., 2022),
095   along with corresponding trajectories for training aerial agents. To gather a substantial number of
096   trajectories in photorealistic 3D environments, we developed a novel web-based 3D flight simulator
097   that is synchronized with world maps and integrated with the Amazon Mechanical Turk (MTurk).
098   Figure 1 depicts our 3D flight simulator and a collected trajectory as an example. In this simulator,
099   users control the aerial agent, which possesses six degrees of freedom, through a continuous 3D
100   state space to reach a destination that corresponds to a language-goal description. Unlike previous
101   works (Fan et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023c), we utilized 3D scans of actual cities and their geographic
102   information to collect human-generated trajectories. These geo-aware trajectories allow the aerial
103   navigation model to efficiently narrow down the exploration space.
104   In total, we acquired 32K trajectories corresponding to natural language descriptions approximately
105   5.8K objects such as buildings and cars. This quantity is about four times greater than that of the
106   existing aerial VLN dataset (Liu et al., 2023c). Moreover, these instructions are high-level and
107   lack specific step-by-step guidance, creating a more challenging and realistic setting compared to
      existing aerial navigation tasks that feature fine-grained instructions. To the best of our knowledge,

124   city data and includes a substantial collection of human-collected geo-aware trajectories paired with
125   textual descriptions.
126   Alongside the detailed descriptions and human demonstrations, we provide a map-based baseline
127   method for city-scale aerial navigation that utilizes a semantic map, which interprets the text and se-
128   mantic categories of geographic landmarks. In contrast to previous methods, our approach employs
129   real-world map data to guide the agent toward the target object, augmented by the agent’s observed
130   images. We benchmarked baseline methods on the CityNav dataset and demonstrated that our map-
131   based method significantly outperforms the latest aerial VLN approaches (Liu et al., 2023c). The
132   main contributions can be summarized as follows:
133

134
        with MTurk to collect large-scale human-generated flight trajectories at city scale.
135
136   • We introduce CityNav, a novel aerial navigation dataset featuring 32,637 language-goal descrip-
137     tions paired with human demonstrations, utilizing 3D scans of real cities and their geographic
138
        information.
139   • We provide a baseline model for aerial navigation agents that includes an internal 2D spatial map

      Vision-and-Language Navigation (VLN) involves guiding an agent to a destination using both lin-
148
      guistic instructions and visual observations. The Embodied AI community has devoted significant
149
      efforts to developing various VLN datasets to benchmark this task. Table 1 presents the representa-
150   tive VLN datasets, broadly categorized into ground-level and aerial navigation.
151
152   Ground-level navigation datasets. Recent advancements in 3D scanning technologies have sig-
153
      nificantly enhanced the ability to create highly accurate and photorealistic datasets of indoor 3D
      scenes (Chang et al., 2017; Dai et al., 2017; Ramakrishnan et al., 2021; Savva et al., 2019; Yesh-
154
      wanth et al., 2023). Building on these developments, a wide range of VLN datasets has been pro-
155
      posed, encompassing applications in robotics such as vision-and-language navigation (Anderson
156   et al., 2018b; Jain et al., 2019; Krantz et al., 2020; Ku et al., 2020; Ramrakhya et al., 2022), embod-
157   ied referring expressions (Qi et al., 2020; Khanna et al., 2024), embodied question answering (Das
158   et al., 2018; Wijmans et al., 2019; Yu et al., 2019; Majumdar et al., 2024), vision-and-dialog nav-
159   igation (Nguyen & Daumé III, 2019; Thomason et al., 2020), and daily-life tasks (Shridhar et al.,
160   2020; Srivastava et al., 2022). In outdoor environments, images from Google Street View are often
161   utilized to replace 3D data, capturing a wide variety of roadside views and landscapes from around
      the world. For example, the TouchDown (Chen et al., 2019) dataset is designed for studying natural


                                                            3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      language navigation and spatial reasoning in a real-world visual urban environment, consisting of
163   9,326 examples of instructions paired with human demonstrations to reach a goal within the Google
164   Street View environment. Additionally, Talk2Nav (Vasudevan et al., 2021) contains verbal navi-
165   gation instructions for 10,714 trajectories collected in an interactive visual navigation environment
166   based on Google Street View. In contrast, ground-level navigation in both indoor and outdoor scenes
167   typically searches a predetermined route, resulting in a narrow search area compared to the aerial
168   domain.
169   Aerial navigation datasets. While the majority of Vision-and-Language Navigation (VLN) datasets
170   primarily focus on ground-level navigation tasks, our work explores the distinct challenges posed
171   by aerial navigation. This area presents unique challenges, such as indeterminate routes and a vast
172   3D search space. Initially, aerial navigation relied on GNSS and visual sensors to ensure safe and
173   efficient flight within expansive aerial spaces (Chambers et al., 2011; Huang et al., 2017; Ross et al.,
174   2013; Shen et al., 2014). These systems are effective but often struggle in areas where GNSS signals
175   are unreliable or absent. Progressing from traditional systems, vision-based approaches leverage
176   machine learning to process visual data, enabling UAVs to swiftly adapt to new weather conditions,
177
      unexpected obstacles, or altered landscapes (Dhiraj et al., 2017; Fraundorfer et al., 2012; Giusti et al.,
      2015; Kouris & Bouganis, 2018; Loquercio et al., 2018). The latest advancements integrate natural
178
      language processing with vision, allowing UAVs to understand and execute commands that incorpo-
179
      rate both visual references and linguistic instructions, thus facilitating more complex and interactive
180   tasks. However, the availability of such datasets in this field remains limited. LANI (Misra et al.,
181   2018) was the first dataset designed to evaluate UAV operations controlled by linguistic naviga-
182   tion instructions, consisting of 6,000 trajectories obtained in the virtual environment CHALET (Yan
183   et al., 2018), which lacks photorealism and offers a relatively small navigation environment. Recent
184   studies have expanded to outdoor environments, covering broader areas. For instance, the AVDN
185   dataset (Fan et al., 2023), which includes 3,064 aerial navigation trajectories with human-to-agent
186   dialogue, utilizes satellite images from the xView dataset (Lam et al., 2018) to depict both urban
187   and rural scenes. However, these satellite images, not captured by UAVs, lack clarity in geograph-
188
      ical features and fail to simulate realistic environments for aerial navigation. In a more flexible
      setting, AerialVLN (Liu et al., 2023c) carries out the aerial VLN task using a 3D simulator of 25
189
      city scenarios, supporting continuous state navigation with 8,446 trajectories collected in virtual
190
      city environments by experienced human UAV pilots. In contrast, our proposed CityNav utilizes 3D
191   point cloud data from SensatUrban (Hu et al., 2022) as UAV flight environments, representing 3D
192   scans of real-world urban areas. This study also leverages linguistic annotations and a 3D map with
193   geographical information from the CityRefer dataset (Miyanishi et al., 2023) as language-goal infor-
194   mation, and we have collected 32,637 human-generated trajectories, marking a significant increase
195   over previous aerial VLN datasets.

      3     C ITY NAV DATASET
199
200   Our aerial navigation task is designed to locate target objects based on linguistic descriptions and
201
      the agent’s first-person view images. For this purpose, we utilized the CityRefer dataset (Miyanishi
      et al., 2023), filtered to provide textual annotations for geographical objects in urban scenes from
202
      the SensatUrban dataset (Hu et al., 2022). We use two cities: Birmingham, with a total area of
203
      1.30 km2 , and Cambridge, with a total area of 3.35 km2 . Similar to a prior aerial VLN task (Liu
204   et al., 2023c), the aerial agent is spawned at a random location within an outdoor environment, either
205   previously encountered (seen) or new (unseen). The agent’s mission involves continuously exploring
206   the 3D environment until it successfully locates the target object, whose position is unknown. To
207   facilitate this, we employed imitation learning to train the navigation policy of the aerial agent. We
208   developed a web-based 3D flight simulator, integrated with MTurk, to collect a substantial amount
209   of human demonstrations, as detailed in Section 3.1. Data collection via MTurk and the dataset’s
210   quality control processes are further outlined in Section 3.2. Finally, we analyze our CityNav dataset
211   in Section 3.3.

302   In the initial collection round, we gathered trajectories corresponding to each description in the
303   CityRefer dataset (Miyanishi et al., 2023). This dataset encompasses 35,196 descriptions of 5,866
304   objects, where each object is annotated with six distinct descriptions across 34 scenes.
305
306   Quality control. To ensure the integrity of the aerial navigation data, we implemented a stringent
307
      filtering process and conducted re-collection of trajectories. During the initial data collection phase,
      we discontinued assigning tasks to participants who consistently recorded long distances to the goal
308
      or who failed to move their agents from the starting point. As a result, trajectories exceeding a
309
      30 m distance to the goal were excluded, retaining 81.6% of the collected data. We subsequently
310   re-collected trajectories for the discarded data. However, in this re-collection phase, 39.1% of the
311   trajectories that still exceeded the 30 m threshold were again removed, ensuring only feasible trajec-
312   tories were used for evaluation and training of the agent’s navigation policy. Finally, we collected
313   32,637 pairs of descriptions and trajectories for 5,850 objects via our web-based simulator, repre-
314   senting 92.8% of the descriptions in the CityRefer dataset. The distribution of target object types
315   was diverse, comprising 48.3% buildings, 40.7% cars, 7.4% ground, and 3.6% parking lots, each
316   with varied sizes, shapes, and colors.
317   Data collection was conducted using MTurk, requiring a total of 711 hours of labor at an estimated
318   hourly rate of $12.83, resulting in a total expenditure of $9,123. The study involved 171 participants.

      Shortest paths and human demonstrations. We developed a set of trajectories based on the short-

363   Evaluation. We evaluated the navigation performance using four standard metrics commonly em-
364   ployed in VLN tasks (Anderson et al., 2018b; Liu et al., 2023c; Qi et al., 2020): Navigation Error
365   (NE), Success Rate (SR), Oracle Success Rate (OSR), and Success weighted by Path Length (SPL).
366   NE represents the linear distance in meters from the goal to the agent’s stopping point at the end
367   of an episode. SR reflects the proportion of episodes in which the agent successfully stops within
368
      20 meters of the destination. OSR measures the percentage of episodes where the agent’s trajectory
      comes within 20 meters of the target location on the xy-plane at any point during the navigation.
369
      SPL calculates the success metric adjusted by the ratio of the optimal path length to the length of the
370
      path actually taken by the agent, rewarding shorter, more efficient routes (Anderson et al., 2018a).
371

      5        C ONCLUSION
518
519
      This paper introduces CityNav, a city-scale aerial Vision-and-Language Navigation (VLN) dataset
      comprising 32,637 descriptions paired with human-generated trajectories, covering 5,850 geograph-
520
      ical objects across real urban environments. We conducted a comprehensive benchmark of exist-
521
      ing aerial VLN models and our map-based goal prediction model using this dataset. Experimen-
522   tal results revealed that our proposed model, which integrates 2D spatial map representations with
523   human-generated geo-aware trajectories, significantly improves navigation performance and main-
524   tains robustness in challenging conditions. Given these findings, we assert that the CityNav dataset
525   represents a valuable resource for both benchmarking and developing advanced intelligent aerial
526   agents.
527
      Limitations and future work. The CityNav dataset currently does not encompass agent-object
528   interactions or dynamic elements such as moving vehicles and pedestrians in urban simulations,
529   which limits its realism and applicability to real-world scenarios. In this work, interactions with
530   stationary objects, particularly tall buildings, were not actively considered due to the nature of the 3D
531   city scans that we used. The role of distant object visibility, while not explicitly addressed, is another
532   factor that could influence navigation performance. To enhance realism and navigation accuracy,
533   future work could focus on integrating physical interactions, real-time data, and considerations for
534   distant object visibility, expanding its utility and applicability.
535   Broader impacts. CityNav has the potential to significantly enhance urban surveillance and emer-
536   gency response tasks by enabling aerial agents to navigate using natural language. However, the
537   adoption of these technologies also introduces ethical concerns, particularly regarding privacy and
538   data security. It is crucial to address social acceptance and regulatory challenges, engage with com-
539   munities to ensure equitable benefits are distributed equitably, and mitigate potential risks to privacy
      and safety.

915   Navigation strategy. In the aerial VLN task, the exploration space is vast, making it crucial to
916   narrow down the search area. To address this, our approach mimics the way humans leverage geo-
917   graphic information (landmarks) to reduce the exploration range. As illustrated in Figure 1, human
      demonstrations rely on the landmarks mentioned in the description (e.g., Sidney Street) to navigate


                                                                        17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      toward the landmark’s vicinity. Once near the landmark, humans focus their search on the area
919   around it to find the target object. This human strategy enables efficient navigation by focusing
920   efforts around landmarks rather than random exploration.
921
922
      To validate this concept, we analyzed the trajectory data collected in the CityNav dataset, which
      includes geographic information. For each trajectory, we extracted the landmark name from the
923
      associated description using an LLM (GPT-3.5) and computed whether the agent passed over the
924
      landmark polygon for both SP and HD trajectories. The results showed that agents passed over
925   landmarks 36.3% of the time for HD trajectories, compared to 24.6% for SP trajectories. Then, we
926   also analyzed whether agents passed within a certain radius of the landmark center. We observed
927   that HD trajectories demonstrated a significantly higher proportion compared to SP, with 35.5% of
928   HD trajectories passing within 20 meters of a landmark, compared to 24.0% for SP. Similarly, within
929   40 meters, 62.5% of HD trajectories passed near a landmark, compared to 51.9% for SP.
930
      Additionally, we calculated the number of actions performed within 50m of a landmark polygon,
931   revealing that HD trajectories averaged 95.4 actions per trajectory compared to 59.8 actions for SP
932   trajectories. These findings highlight that HD trajectories engage in more focused and thorough
933   exploration around landmarks, which likely contributes to their superior performance in Table 2.

979    While the CMA model excels in tasks that require spatial reasoning, it lacks the inherent capability

982    address this limitation, we introduce a sophisticated model known as the map-based goal predictor,
983    as illustrated in Figure 11.

       The model outputs two values: predicted goal coordinates and predicted progress. The predicted
       goal coordinates estimate the location of the target object based on prior observations, while the
1006
       predicted progress calculates the ratio of the remaining to the total distance from the starting point to
1007
       the goal. To approach the predicted coordinates, the agent updates these coordinates based on new
1008   observations and executes up to five actions consecutively at each time step. When the predicted
1009   progress exceeds a set threshold, the agent ceases iterations and proceeds directly to the predicted
1010   goal coordinates
1011
       The predicted goal coordinates are derived from a navigation map, updated by the ‘Navigation Map
1012
       Generator’ module, depicted on the right side of Figure 11. This map comprises five channels: the
1013
       first two channels monitor navigation history, and the remaining three record the locations of entities
1014   relevant to the description. The ‘view area map’ channel, the first, represents the area currently visi-
1015   ble in the RGB and depth images. The second, ‘explored area map,’ aggregates view area maps over
1016   time. The third to fifth channels—‘landmark map’, ‘target map’, and ‘surroundings map’—identify
1017   the entities mentioned in the descriptions. Landmarks, exact locations identifiable via online maps
1018   such as ‘Grand Square’, are extracted using names and contour coordinates from the CityRefer
1019   dataset (Miyanishi et al., 2023). Targets and surroundings, described in the narrative (e.g., ‘a build-
1020   ing with a grey roof’ and ‘a red van with black stripes’), are detected using Grounding DINO (Liu
1021   et al., 2023b), an open-set object detector. The segmentation masks of these entities are refined using
1022
       Mobile-SAM (Zhang et al., 2023), with coordinate transformations applied to accurately place the
       masks on the map.
1023
1024   If the predicted progress value surpasses a predefined threshold, it indicates that the agent is likely
1025   near the target’s visible location. As the final action, the agent must ascertain the exact location of
       the target from the observations and position itself accordingly. MGP utilizes Set-of-Mark prompt-

       ing (Yang et al., 2023) for visual prompting. The RGB image is initially annotated with labels from
1027   the segmentation masks provided by Semantic-SAM (Li et al., 2023). A vision-language model,
1028   LLaVA-1.6-34b (Liu et al., 2023a), is then prompted to select a label. The agent moves to the center
1029   of the bounding box corresponding to the selected label’s segmentation mask. Finally, the agent
1030   descends to a height of 5 meters above ground level at the xy coordinates of its current position.
