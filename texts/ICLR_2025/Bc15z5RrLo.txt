009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Additive models, such as Neural Additive Models (NAMs), are recognized for
012            their transparency, providing clear insights into the impact of individual features
013            on outcomes. However, they traditionally rely on point estimations and are con-
014            strained by their additive nature, limiting their ability to capture the complexity
015            and variability inherent in real-world data. This variability often presents as dif-
016            ferent influences from the same feature value in various samples, adding complex-
017            ity to prediction models. To address these limitations, we introduce MixNAM, an
018
               innovative framework that enriches NAMs by integrating a mixture of experts,
               where each expert encodes a different aspect of this variability in predictions from
019
               each feature. This integration allows MixNAM to capture the variability in feature
020
               contributions through comprehensive distribution estimations and to include fea-
021            ture interactions during expert routing, thus significantly boosting performance.
022            Our empirical evaluation demonstrates that MixNAM surpasses traditional addi-
023            tive models in performance and is comparable to complex black-box approaches.
024            Additionally, it improves the depth and comprehensiveness of feature attribution,
025            setting a new benchmark for balancing interpretability with performance in ma-
026            chine learning. Moreover, the flexibility in MixNAM configuration facilitates
027            the navigation of its trade-offs between accuracy and interpretability, enhancing
028            adaptability to various data scenarios.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Deep neural networks (DNNs) have proven exceptional at modeling complex data relationships,
      achieving remarkable results across various domains, such as computer vision and natural language
034
      processing (Young et al., 2018; Hassaballah & Awad, 2020). Despite these successes, the opaque
035
      nature of DNN outputs often makes them non-interpretable, which hinders their usage in high-stakes
036   areas like healthcare and finance where the basis of decisions is crucial (Ghnemat et al., 2023; Liao
037   & Varshney, 2021). This critical limitation has spurred research into interpretable machine-learning
038   models (Alvarez Melis & Jaakkola, 2018; Ghorbani et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2020; Yeh et al., 2020).
039   These models aim to make the reasoning behind decisions more transparent, facilitating trust and
040   wide adoption in sensitive applications.
041
      Among the interpretable approaches developed, additive models stand out for their transparency,
042   enabling direct visualization of how individual features impact predictions (Hastie, 2017). Additive
043   models independently encode each feature through a non-linear transformation and subsequently
044   combine them linearly for final predictions, simplifying the understanding of the influence from
045   each feature. Based on the framework of additive models, Neural Additive Models (NAMs; Agarwal
046   et al., 2021) attempt to model the complex patterns of each feature with DNNs, offering a flexible
047   and differentiable representation of features while maintaining the additive structure.
048   While the additive nature of feature information in NAMs allows for clear explanations of individ-
049   ual features, this structure inherently limits their ability to learn intricate mappings between input
050   features and target outputs. Thus, the performance of these models often lags behind that of their
051   complex black-box counterparts, which can learn interactions among features. Furthermore, addi-
052   tive models traditionally produce a single outcome for each feature value, an approach that fails to
053   account for the variability in outcomes that occur in complex real-world scenarios. The variability
      in model prediction refers to the extent to which the predicted outputs of a model fluctuate or differ


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      when the same input feature value is presented across different samples. For example, the same
055   health metric might have different implications depending on an individual’s age group or other
056   contextual factors. By relying on point-to-point estimations, these models do not capture the actual
057   distribution of possible outcomes associated with a given feature value. Recent attempts to enhance
058   NAMs with uncertainty estimation seek to address this issue (Bouchiat et al., 2023; Thielmann et al.,
059   2024). However, these approaches are still limited by their inevitable assumptions of prior distri-
060   butions and the additive nature of the entire models, which restrict their ability to accurately reflect
061   complex underlying distributions and interactions.
062   To address the limitations of traditional additive models while retaining their interpretative bene-
063   fits, we introduce MixNAM, a novel and general framework that extends beyond the constraints of
064   NAMs. MixNAM integrates multiple expert feature encoders operating in parallel, each of which
065   encodes a different aspect of the variability in model predictions. Moreover, a dynamic routing
066   mechanism is introduced to assess and combine the relevance of different experts for the final pre-
067   diction. Our experiments demonstrate that MixNAM significantly outperforms all existing additive
068   models, matching the performance of complex black-box methods. Different from traditional addi-
069
      tive models that offer point estimations, MixNAM provides a more nuanced understanding of feature
      impacts by visualizing the range and distribution of possible outcomes for each feature value. The
070
      results further reveal that MixNAM can be seen as a generalization of NAM, with the flexibility to
071
      adjust the balance between detailed distribution estimations and straightforward point estimations,
072   catering to specific needs for transparency and performance. The contributions of this work can be
073   summarized as follows:
074
075          • We propose MixNAM, a general framework built upon NAMs, by incorporating a mixture
076            of experts with dynamic routing algorithms.
077          • MixNAM demonstrates superior performance compared to traditional additive models and
078            is comparable to complex black-box models.
079          • MixNAM preserves the capability of explaining feature contributions through visualiza-
080            tions, enhancing the current point estimations with nuanced distribution estimations.
081
             • MixNAM enables a seamless transition from detailed distribution estimation to precise
082
               point estimation, effectively balancing model accuracy and interpretability.

091   corresponding feature encoders f1 , · · · , fn which map the input values from feature domains to the
092   prediction domain. The final predicted value of the target output y is computed as

103   2.2   N EURAL A DDITIVE M ODELS
104
105   Developed from traditional additive models, Neural Additive Models (NAMs; Agarwal et al., 2021)
106   incorporate neural networks to encode each feature, marking a significant evolution in the modeling
107   capability of GAMs. This neural integration allows NAMs to capture more complex feature relation-
      ships without sacrificing the interpretative benefits. Subsequent developments, such as Neural Basis

      Models (NBMs; Radenovic et al., 2022) and Gaussian Process Neural Additive Models (GP-NAMs;
109   Zhang et al., 2024) have further refined this approach. They focus on efficiency and scalability, re-
110   ducing the complexity and enhancing the ability of models to manage larger datasets with complex
111   underlying structures. Recent research has also explored extending NAMs to include uncertainty
112   estimation, providing a more comprehensive explanation of feature influence by considering distri-
113   bution parameters and Bayesian inference methods (Bouchiat et al., 2023; Thielmann et al., 2024).
114   However, these models generally rely on a simplistic assumption about output distributions, which
115   may limit their effectiveness in real-world scenarios where data interactions are complex.

117   2.3   M IXTURE OF E XPERTS
118
      The Mixture of Experts (MoE) technique has gained substantial attention from the research com-
119
      munity recently for its ability to enhance both the scalability and expertise of neural networks. It is
120
      designed to learn multiple specialized sub-models, known as “experts”, on different subtasks. A gat-
121   ing network is trained to dynamically assign a new input to the most relevant experts (Jacobs et al.,
122   1991). One key advantage of the MoE model is its efficiency and adaptability in handling large-scale
123   problems. Shazeer et al. (2017) explored scaling MoE for applications in massive neural network
124   architectures by incorporating a sparse gating network to select only a small subset of experts for
125   each input. They demonstrate that integrating MoE layers can significantly boost model capacity
126   with a minor loss in computational efficiency. Recent work also explores the adaptation of MoE in
127   the Transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017), encouraging their use in cutting-edge research
128   such as large language models (Artetxe et al., 2022; Jiang et al., 2024; Lepikhin et al., 2021) and
129
      vision language models (Lin et al., 2024; Shen et al., 2023).

380   In addition to demonstrating the accuracy and interpretability of MixNAM, we further explore how
381   these two characteristics can be adjusted to reach a balance. As mentioned in Section 3.3, an expert
382   variation penalty is implemented during training to ensure that MixNAM develops meaningful ex-
383   pert functions. Our qualitative and quantitative analyses demonstrate that, by adjusting the penalty
384   weight λ, MixNAM presents a general framework of “constrained interpretable models”, which
385   provides an opportunity to control the trade-offs between model accuracy and its interpretability.
386

533
534   We introduce MixNAM, a general framework that advances additive models by incorporating a
535   mixture of experts. Our experiments show that MixNAM overcomes the inherent limitations of tra-
536   ditional additive models, enhancing accuracy while preserving interpretability. MixNAM not only
537   provides detailed explanations of feature output distributions but also allows for a flexible balance
538
      between accuracy and interpretability, adapting to various application needs. This framework ex-
      tends the utility of additive models, providing advanced performance and insightful interpretability,
539
      which are crucial for real-world machine-learning applications.

       MixNAM can have a significant impact on high-stakes areas such as finance and healthcare where
1230   the explanation of predictions is as crucial as the predictions themselves. The introduction of Mix-
1231   NAM advances the capabilities of additive models, which may encourage the deployment of re-
1232   lated research in practical applications. Moreover, MixNAM generalizes existing Neural Additive
1233   Models, offering a new direction to enhance additive models by balancing the accuracy and inter-
1234   pretability under the MixNAM framework. However, MixNAM does not inherently address issues
1235   of fairness or mitigate implicit bias in the data. Instead, it provides transparent feature explanations
1236   that may highlight these biases. Therefore, careful considerations and responsible use are essential
1237   when applying MixNAM in scenarios where fairness is a critical concern. The transparency of Mix-
1238
       NAM may also serve as a tool for identifying data biases, contributing to equitable and accountable
       AI systems.
