009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Despite recent advances in AI, the development of systems capable of executing
012            complex, multi-step reasoning tasks involving multiple tools remains a significant
013            challenge. Current benchmarks fall short in capturing the real-world complex-
014            ity of tool-use reasoning, where verifying the correctness of not only the final
015            answer but also the intermediate steps is important for evaluation, development,
016            and identifying failures during inference time. To bridge this gap, we introduce
017            ToolComp, a comprehensive benchmark designed to evaluate multi-step tool-use
018
               reasoning. ToolComp is developed through a collaboration between models and
               human annotators, featuring human-edited/verified prompts, final answers, and
019
               process supervision labels, allowing for the evaluation of both final outcomes and
020
               intermediate reasoning. Evaluation across six different model families demon-
021            strates the challenging nature of our dataset, with the majority of models achiev-
022            ing less than 50% accuracy. Additionally, we generate synthetic training data
023            to compare the performance of outcome-supervised reward models (ORMs) with
024            process-supervised reward models (PRMs) to assess their ability to improve com-
025            plex tool-use reasoning as evaluated by ToolComp. Our results show that PRMs
026            generalize significantly better than ORMs, achieving a 19% and 11% improve-
027            ment in rank@1 accuracy for ranking base and fine-tuned model trajectories, re-
028            spectively. These findings highlight the critical role of process supervision in both
029
               the evaluation and training of AI models, paving the way for more robust and
               capable systems in complex, multi-step tool-use tasks. *

      1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Recent advancements in large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable progress
      in a range of natural language processing tasks. These models have achieved state-of-the-art per-
036
      formance across diverse benchmarks, including question answering, summarization, and reasoning
037
      tasks. In order to further increase the usefulness of LLMs, a growing area of research is centered
038   around the development of agentic capabilities, particularly their ability to autonomously interact
039   with external tools to solve complex, multi-step tasks as well as to interact with human systems such
040   as the web or mobile devices.
041
      However, evaluating the effectiveness of these tool-use capabilities remains a pressing challenge.
042
      While there have been notable efforts in developing benchmarks for tool-use capability, these often
043
      assess isolated instances of tool use, focusing on whether the model can invoke the correct tool
044   at the right time (Huang et al., 2024; Zhuang et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2021). Additionally, while
045   benchmarks for multi-step tool usage exist, most focus only on scoring the correctness of the final
046   answer (Mialon et al., 2023), despite that the complex nature of multi-step reasoning often requires
047   the evaluation for partial correctness or step-wise correctness of the reasoning trajectories. This can
048   be valuable for both understanding model failure modes and developing systems that can improve
049   upon these intermediate reasoning flaws.
050   To address these shortcomings, we introduce ToolComp, a benchmark comprising 485 complex,
051   human-verified prompts that require language models to chain together multiple tool calls, accom-
052   panied by human-edited step-wise and final answers. By demanding intricate tool interactions and
053
          * Code, ToolComp benchmark, and synthetic training data will be made publicly available.

      providing human verification, ToolComp offers a rigorous assessment of a model’s ability to per-
055   form complex, multi-step reasoning and tool use. We evaluate the current landscape of state-of-
056   the-art models on their ability to chain together tool calls to reach the final answer, as well as their
057   step-wise reasoning ability.
058
      Moreover, in light of recent works demonstrating how process supervision significantly improve
059
      reasoning in language models (Lightman et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024a), we explore the best
060
      methods for improving agentic tool-use reasoning by conducting an initial comparative analysis be-
061   tween process-supervised reward models (PRMs) and outcome-supervised reward models (ORMs)
062   on ToolComp. Our results demonstrate that process-supervised models outperform outcome-based
063   approaches, underscoring the importance of training models with and evaluating against process
064   supervision signals.
065
      In order to avoid early contamination, we plan to open source the entire benchmark when either 1)

      1.1   C ONTRIBUTIONS AND K EY TAKEAWAYS
069
070   Our key contributions and takeaways are summarized as follows:
071
072          • Introduction of ToolComp We introduce ToolComp, a multi-tool reasoning and process
073            supervision benchmark with 485 human-edited/verified prompts and final answers, de-
074            signed to evaluate a model’s ability to perform multi-step tool-use tasks (Section 3).
075          • Step-by-Step Process Annotations ToolComp includes 1731 detailed per-step supervision
076            labels, enabling a comprehensive assessment of a model’s intermediate reasoning when
077            performing complex, multi-step tool-use tasks (Section 3).
078          • Assessment of State-of-the-Art Models We evaluate 16 models across 6 different model
079            families on their ability to perform complex multi-step tool-use tasks as well as their in-
080            termediate reasoning ability. We find that o1-preview has the best performance, achieving
081            61.83% against the human-verified final answers and 80.18% against the process supervi-
082            sion labels (Section 4 and Section A).
083          • Process-Supervision Outperforms Outcome-Supervision Our analysis shows that
084            process-supervised reward models outperform outcome-based reward models by 19% in
085            rank@1 accuracy on base model generations and by 11% in rank@1 accuracy on fine-
086            tuned model generations (Section 5 and Section B).
087          • Release of Synthetic Training Data and Evaluation Pipeline We provide the complete
088            synthetic training dataset, which includes 11K prompts, 11K preferred and dis-preferred
089            full trajectories for ORM training, and 15K preferred and dis-preferred steps for PRM
090
               training. We also release the evaluation pipeline for ToolComp. (Section 5).

094   Benchmarks for Complex Tool Use Planning With rising interest in tool-augmented LLMs
095   (Schick et al., 2023; Patil et al., 2023; Qin et al., 2023), several benchmarks have been introduced
096   to assess their abilities. Earlier benchmarks were designed to assess a model’s ability to do proper
097   retrieval, execution, and extraction of one tool call for specific tasks such as general question an-
098   swering (Yang et al., 2018; Joshi et al., 2017), fact verification (Thorne et al., 2018), or answering
099
      temporal queries (Chen et al., 2021; Kasai et al., 2024; Zhang & Choi, 2021; Dhingra et al., 2022; Vu
      et al., 2023). However, these benchmarks fail to assess a model’s ability to plan and chain together
100
      multiple tool calls to answer more complex queries. More recent benchmarks aimed at evaluating
101
      multiple tool calls are often placed within or dependent on state-full systems (such as a code-base
102   and/or a dynamic database) (Yan et al., 2024; Jimenez et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023). Although
103   these types of benchmarks assess a language model’s ability to chain together multiple tool calls,
104   the evaluation may penalize general-purpose language models that are not familiar with the given
105   environments. Other benchmarks primarily rely on state-based evaluations, where the final state
106   of the system is assessed against the desired state (Li et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2021), or win-rates
107   against another reference state-of-the-art model (Qin et al., 2023), both of which lack the rigour of
      human-verified ground truth final answers. Closest to our work, the GAIA benchmark is a collection

      Process Reward Models Recent work has shown the power of utilizing process supervision sig-
129
      nals, which are granular signals on the step-wise correctness of a solution, as opposed to outcome
130   supervision signals, which are broad signals on the correctness of the entire solution. Utilizing these
131   signals, Lightman et al. (2023) and Wang et al. (2024a) have shown dramatic improvements in per-
132   formance in ranking solutions to mathematical reasoning tasks and using these signals to further
133   improve performance in traditional RLHF algorithms such as Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO)
134   (Schulman et al., 2017).
135
      In this work, through a hybrid human-AI annotation workflow, we generate per-step process su-
136   pervision labels, which uniquely enable us to rigorously evaluate a model’s intermediate reasoning
137   capability. Table 1 provides a comparative overview of popular tool-use benchmarks, including
138   our work, ToolComp. In addition, we investigate how to best apply process supervision signals to
139   improve multi-step tool-use reasoning, which introduces novel design challenges compared to its
140   application in mathematical reasoning. For instance, the granularity of supervision becomes a key
141   consideration, where we must decide between supervising the entire ReAct (Yao et al., 2023) pro-
142   cess or its subcomponents. These design choices alongside comparisons with outcome supervision
143   are explored in detail in Section 5.

182   3.3       P ROMPT C REATION
183
184   In developing the prompts for this dataset, there are two main criteria we desire each prompt to
185
      satisfy: 1) the solution to the prompt contains a chain of dependent tool calls to answer and 2)
      the final answer to the prompt can be programmatically verified. To achieve this, we generate
186
      a set of candidate prompts through few-shot prompting which are then refined and validated by
187
      human annotators. The overall process includes 1) manually developing in-context (IC) examples,
188   2) generating initial prompts, 3) an iterative process of filtering prompts, adding filtered prompts as
189   negative IC examples, and regenerating more prompts, and 4) human refinement. These steps are
190   described in more detail in Appendix C.1
191
192   3.4       C HAT VS . E NTERPRISE U SE C ASES
193
194   In creating the benchmark, we developed two subsets of prompts, coined ToolComp-Enterprise and
195   ToolComp-Chat. ToolComp-Enterprise allows the use of 11 tools and aims to emulate settings
196   in which LLM agents must compose a larger number of expressive APIs together correctly, such
197   as in enterprise settings. The second subset, ToolComp-Chat, is designed to test general purpose
198
      chatbots with the minimally sufficient set of tools for information retrieval and processing tasks,
      namely Google Search and Python. We chose only google search and python execution as these are
199
      standard tools found in major chat-bot providers. We only allow the respective tools for each subset
200
      during prompt generation, labeling, and evaluation. ToolComp-Enterprise contains 287 examples
201   and ToolComp-Chat contains 198 examples.

      3.5       L ABEL C REATION
204
205   To create the process supervision labels as well as the final answer for each prompt, we utilize a
206   hybrid human-AI approach, where the language model and human annotators use the same tools to
207   collaborate to get to the final answer. We start by prompting the Policy Model LLM to outline a
208   plan, called Action Plan, on which tools to call and in what order using the prompt in E.1. We have
209   human annotators validate/modify the Action Plan, which is then appended to the sequence before
210   using the LLM to formulate tool calls. We then use the LLM to call tools in the ReAct format, where
211   the specific prompt can be found in E.2.
212   We asked the annotators to rate if a step is Correct (i.e., the step is a reasonable action towards
213   achieving the final answer) or Incorrect (i.e., the step is nonsensical, incorrect, or is not a reasonable
214   action towards acheiving the final answer). All components of the ReAct Step (Thought, Action,
215   Action Input) must be marked as Correct or Incorrect by the annotator. If the annotator marks a
      step as Correct, the model is allowed to proceed further and generate the next step. If the annotator


                                                                                  4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      deems a step as Incorrect, they must modify the step to make it correct. Once corrected, the model
217   is then prompted to advance to the next step with the human-corrected step as part of its context.
218   This is repeated until the Finish Action is chosen by the LLM and marked as Correct by the anno-
219   tator or until the annotator corrects an Action step to ‘Finish’ because we have enough information
220   to answer the question. The overall flow is shown in Figure 1. An example golden trajectory is
221   available in Appendix F.1 and an example annotated trajectory is available in Appendix F.2. We
222   use FireFunction-V1 as the Policy Model LLM (at the time, this was the best open-source tool-use
223   LLM) and humans as the annotators (Fireworks, 2024).
224   With this process, we retrieve, per task, a valid step-by-step chain of tool calls that successfully
225   gets to the final answer along with step-wise correct/incorrect labels and associated rewrites. The
226   correct/incorrect labels and the associated rewrites allow us to assess intermediate reasoning through
227   LLM-as-judge evaluations (described in Section 4.3).

229   3.6   Q UALITY C ONTROL
230
231   To ensure the highest quality of ToolComp, we conduct a thorough manual inspection of all exam-
232   ples. Any data samples with ambiguous prompts, erroneous process supervision labels, or incorrect
233
      final answers are redone. After the initial creation of the benchmark, the authors collaborated with
      three trusted annotators to perform a final re-review of all samples and make any necessary correc-
234
      tions.
235
236   As a final quality control step, we evaluate the entire benchmark using GPT-4o (May 2024), GPT-4
237   Turbo, Claude 3.5 Sonnet, and Llama 3.1 405b (OpenAI et al., 2024; Dubey et al., 2024; Anthropic).
238   We identify the set of data samples where all models’ answers differed from the ground truth final
239
      answers. We then repeated the refinement process on these samples, as they represented the most
      challenging and/or potentially mislabeled data points. This iterative approach yielded the final ver-
240
      sion of ToolComp.

245   4.1   E VALUATION M ETRIC
246
      We have two metrics to evaluate the quality or the correctness of a model’s final answers: LLM
247
      Grading and Exact Match. For the final answer evaluations in this section (Table 2), we use LLM
248
      Grading since it rewards correct answers without penalizing minor formatting issues. Our Exact
249   Match evaluation methodology and the corresponding results are shown in Appendix A.1.
250
251   LLM Grading By using LLM grading against ground truth answers we opt to be charitable to
252   exact formatting and focus on assessing the tool use capabilities of the model. We intentionally
253   choose not to focus on final answer formatting given that (1) there are existing benchmarks that
254   assess formatting ability (e.g. FOFO (Xia et al., 2024)) and (2) our final answers are quite complex,
255   containing multiple elements, lists which may or may not be sorted, and dictionaries. This approach
256   prompts an LLM Judge to look at the prompt, the ground truth answer, and the model’s answer and
257   asks the model to classify it as Incorrect, Correct, or Correct with Bad Formatting. We use GPT-4
258   Turbo as the de-facto judge for all of our models (OpenAI et al., 2024). The prompt used is shown
259
      in Appendix E.5. We consider both Correct and Correct with Bad Formatting as a win (accurate)
      and Incorrect as a loss (inaccurate).

      4.3   LLM- AS -J UDGE E VALUATIONS
335
336   We further evaluate these models using our process supervision labels, aiming to assess each model’s
337   effectiveness as a pairwise judge in selecting the human-corrected step over the step generated by the
338   original policy used during annotation. To mitigate position bias, we swap the order of the human-
339   corrected and model-generated steps and conduct two separate predictions for each arrangement.
340   Additionally, models are permitted to indicate a tie. If a model designates a tie at least once, or
341   consistently predicts the same position (before and after swapping) for a given data sample, we
342
      classify the outcome as a tie. Mirroring the methodology used in RewardBench (Lambert et al.
      (2024)), we score losses as 0, ties as 0.5, and wins as 1. We show the results below in Table 3.

345   4.4   I NTERMEDIATE R EASONING VS . F INAL A NSWER
346
      Figure 2 shows the correlation between a model’s intermediate reasoning performance and final an-
347
      swer accuracy based on the multi-step tool-use tasks in ToolComp. The standard Pearson correlation
348
      coefficient is r = 0.63 with a statistical p-value of 0.0084, which makes the correlation statistically
349   significant under a significance level of 0.05 (Freedman et al., 2007). Intuitively, this suggests that
350   with stronger step-wise performance as assessed by our LLM-as-judge evaluations, we can expect
351   an increased likelihood of reaching the correct final answer. However, the moderate magnitude of
352   the correlation value could be due to additional signals captured by the step-wise reasoning evalua-
353   tions that are not captured by evaluating final answers. Work done by Havrilla et al. (2024) similarly
354   suggests that there is complementary and non-overlapping information in step-wise and final answer
355   refinement, further highlighting the importance of assessing intermediate reasoning.

402   Recent works have demonstrated the power of process supervision signals in domains such as math-
403   ematical reasoning (Lightman et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024a). Despite this, the application of
404   process-supervised signals towards tool-augmented LLMs remains under-explored. By focusing on
405   the process rather than just the outcome, process-supervised models could provide more granular
406
      feedback during multi-step tool-use, leading to faster convergence, especially in complex applica-
      tions where each step is associated with dynamic feedback from the environment. In this section,
407
      we provide a preliminary analysis to assess the value of process supervision for multi-step tool-use
408
      by training PRM and ORM models using additionally generated synthetic data.
409
410
      5.1   E XPERIMENT D ESIGN
411
412   Training Dataset For the generation of synthetic prompts and the process/outcome supervision
413   labels, we mirror the strategies outlined in Section 3.3 and Section 3.5, respectively. We use Llama-
414   3.1-8b-Instruct as the Policy Model generator and Chat GPT-4o as the Critic Model generator (Ope-
415   nAI et al., 2024; Dubey et al., 2024). A detailed accounting of the training data generation, including
416   generation parameters, dataset sizes, and methodology can be found in Appendix D.1. Furthermore,
417   the construction of the outcome-supervised, process-supervised reward modelling and supervised
418   fine-tuning datasets are detailed in Appendix D.2.
419
420   Reward Model Training Objective We equip the base model, Llama-3.1-8b-Instruct (Dubey
421   et al., 2024), with a linear layer to serve as the reward head. For ORM training, the reward model
422   places a probability of correctness of the whole preferred and dis-preferred trajectory. For PRM
423
      training, we experiment with 4 different levels of process supervision. The first axis of variation
      experiments with including/excluding the “Observation” of the ReAct step, which is the output ob-
424
      served from the tool call. The second axis of variation ablates the granularity of the process signals,
425
      i.e., rewarding the whole ReAct step or rewarding each substep of the ReAct step (recall that a ReAct
426   step contains Thought, Action, and Action Input, the correctness of which is determined/modified
427   by the Critic Model). This leads us to 4 total supervision methods: Full ReAct step with or without
428   Observation, Sub ReAct Step with or without Observation. Under each type of supervision, the
429   PRM places a probability of correctness of the preferred step and dis-preferred step. The training
430   objective is given by the average Binary Cross Entropy loss, assessing the probability of correctness
431   with the corresponding label. A more detailed explanation of the training objective is in Appendix
      D.3 and training implementation along with hyper-parameters is in Appendix D.5.

432
      Evaluation To evaluate, we use our ORM and PRM models to select the best response out of a
433   set of candidates. Specifically, we use a generator model to produce 50 completions per problem in
434   ToolComp. We experiment with two different generator model in these experiments: base Llama-
435   3.1-8b-Instruct and a supervised fine-tuned Llama-3.1-8b-Instruct model, which is trained on all of
436   the full preferred trajectories in the synthetic training data. After collecting these completions, we
437   use the trained ORM and PRM to rank these completions, returning the best answer according to
438   this ranking. We then assess the rank@1 accuracy by judging the correctness of said highest scoring
439   completion against the ground truth final answer per problem. For the ORM, we simply use the
440   reward score it places on each completion. For the PRM, in order to compress per-step scores into
441
      one single score for the entire chain, we experiment with different aggregation functions, namely:
      min, max, average, and product.
442
443   To account for variance in the rankings, we perform 500 permutations of 30 random samples from
444   the 50 total completion per problem, and calculate the average rank@1 accuracy. Moreover, we vary
445   the dataset sizes at 10, 25, 50, and 100 percent of the full dataset in order to assess how performance
446   of each method scales with increasing training data.

448   5.2   R ESULTS
449
      PRM outperforms ORM in selecting the best trajectory In Figure 3, we observe that for both
450
      the base model generations and the SFT model generations, our best PRM model (see Table 4)
451   outperforms the ORM in rank@1 accuracy. On the base model generations, the PRM achieves an
452   accuracy of 42.65% compared to the ORM accuracy of 23.89%. Moreover, towards enhancing an
453   already fine-tuned model, we see that the PRM is able to push rank@1 accuracy to 60.25%, nearly
454   matching the generator’s pass@30 performance. Overall, these results suggest that PRMs are able
455   to efficiently translate these per tool call step-level signals to provide superior performance than
456   utilizing just outcome-level signals. In Appendix B, we also find the PRM scales better than the
457   ORM on increasing prompt complexity.
458
459   Full step with observation is the best PRM supervision method Table 4 shows the performance
460   of the PRM using different supervision methods trained on the full-scale dataset. We see that pro-
461
      viding the model signals about whether an entire step, including the observation, was correct or
      incorrect led to the best performance. These findings suggest that 1) intermediate information from
462
      the environment (tool call outputs) provide valuable signal to PRMs as these dynamic signals pro-
463
      vide additional insight in determining the correctness of a trajectory, and 2) there is a balance to
464   be struck when determining the granularity of supervision during training. Intuitively, providing
465   less granular, full-step signals gives the model more freedom to learn and identify what makes one
466   step better than another, without being constrained by potentially noisy or overly detailed sub-step
467   labels. This allows the model to generalize more effectively, rather than being limited by specific,
468   finer-grained supervision.

513   6   L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE D IRECTIONS
514
515
      In this study, we focus solely on applying outcome and process supervision to the reward model.
516   Although fine-tuning the policy model with supervision from the reward model using reinforcement
517   learning (RL) is a logical next step, we leave this for future work and focus instead on the contributed
518   dataset and the value of process supervision even without RL.
519
      A notable limitation of our work is the reliance on synthetic data to scale the policy. We hypothe-
520
      size that incorporating human-generated data to expand the training set could enhance the tool-use
521
      capabilities beyond the performance of the state-of-the-art critic model, which was used to label our
522   synthetic dataset.
523
524   Additionally, the restricted set of tools used in this work, primarily focused on information retrieval
525
      and data processing, presents another limitation. In contrast, a common approach in the field in-
      volves employing specialized models for various tasks such as image generation and translation.
526
      This opens up further questions regarding how process supervision could facilitate the scaling of
527
      more nuanced capabilities when integrating with other specialized models.

1357   C.1     P ROMPT C REATION D ETAILS
1358
       Step 1: Develop In-Context Examples We crafted high-quality in-context (IC) examples with
1359
       supporting reasoning, which we call ‘processes’, to guide the prompt generation. These processes
1360
       are Chain of Thought reasonings that describe the process by which we came up with the prompt.
1361   One of the IC Prompts and a corresponding CoT is shown in Appendix C.2
1362
1363
       Step 2: Generate Initial Prompts Using the IC examples, we generated synthetic prompts, en-
1364
       suring diversity by selecting random subsets of IC examples. Each subset used distinct in-context
1365
       prompts and randomly sampled tools from its set of available tools. The seed prompt used in this
1366   step in Appendix C.3.
1367
1368
       Step 3: Filtering We manually inspected each prompt to ensure they were reasonable, interesting,
1369
       and challenging, labeling them as Good, Too Simple, or Nonsensical with justifications for each
1370   classification. These labeled examples served as IC inputs for GPT-4 Turbo (OpenAI et al., 2024) to
1371   classify additional prompts. We iteratively review the outputs, make necessary edits, and add more
1372   IC examples. Through three iterations, the filtered prompts were of high quality, exhibiting only
1373   minor mistakes.
1374
1375   Step 4: Human Refinement After filtering, annotators reviewed the finals prompts to resolve any
1376   issues related to complexity, clarity and ambiguity. We gave clear instructions on ambiguity (only
1377   one possible correct answer) and complexity (requires two or more tool calls to answer), instructing
1378   our annotators to ensure the prompt has only one correct answer that is complex, challenging and
1379   requires the use of tools.

1666
1667   Outcome Reward Model The base model is equipped with a linear layer to serve as the reward
1668   head, which outputs a scalar reward value per token. We place a special RM token at the end of the
1669   preferred and dis-preferred completion, followed by an EOS token. The training objective is then
1670   given by a Binary Cross Entropy loss on the reward value output on the special RM token. We use
1671   Llama-3.1-8b-Instruct as the base model.
1672
1673   Process Reward Model Similarly, the base model is equipped with a linear layer to serve as the
       reward head, which outputs a singular scalar value. We test 4 different levels of supervision. For the


                                                        31
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1674
       Full Step with Observation and Full Step without Observation, a singular special RM token is placed
1675   at the end of the observation step and at the end of the action input step, respectively. Moreover, for
1676   Sub Step with Observation and Sub Step without Observation, 4 special RM tokens are placed after
1677   each ReAct step (including the observation) and 3 special RM tokens are placed after each ReAct
1678   step (excluding the observation), respectively. For all variation, the training objective is given by the
1679   average Binary Cross Entropy loss across the reward value output at each of the special RM tokens
1680   and the corresponding label. We use Llama-3.1-8b-Instruct as the base model.

1682   D.4   PRM VS . ORM E VALUATION
1683
1684   Ranking Trajectories We use a generator to generate 50 completions per problem in ToolComp.
1685
       Then for each completion, we discard all completions that did not reach a final answer. We then use
       an ORM and PRM to rank the completions per problem. For the ORM, we simply take the sigmoid
1686
       of the reward score it places on the completions as that is the probability the model assigns that the
1687
       trajectory is correct. For PRM, given a trajectory, we collect a list of probability scores for each
1688   step. To combine the list of probability scores into a single number, we experiment with a couple of
1689   aggregation functions, namely: min, max, average, and product. In order to account for variance in
1690   the ranking scores, we perform 500 permutations of the completion per problem, leaving 20 out at
1691   random each permutation. We consider the best-of-30 accuracy, which takes the best trajectory as
1692   ranked by the corresponding method and evaluates the correctness of that single trajectory.
1693
1694   Training Dataset Scales In order to assess the generalizability of PRM vs. ORM with increasing
1695   scales of data, we vary the dataset sizes to be 10, 25, 50, and 100 percent of the full dataset. At each
1696   dataset scale, we train the models for 3 epochs and then take the best performing model on a held
1697   out synthetically generated validation set.
