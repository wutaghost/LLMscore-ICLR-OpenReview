011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            In Earth sciences, unobserved factors often lead to spatially nonstationary distribu-
014            tions, causing relationships between features and targets to vary across locations.
015            Traditional tabular machine learning methods struggle to effectively model this
016
               spatial heterogeneity. While approaches like Geographically Weighted Regres-
               sion (GWR) capture local variations, they often miss global patterns, overfit local
017
               noise, and lack the ability to model temporal changes in spatial heterogeneity. Our
018
               research aims to model spatiotemporal heterogeneity. To achieve this, we propose
019            an end-to-end approach that fits the entire dataset to capture global patterns, while
020            designing the model as a conditional generative framework to learn sparse spa-
021            tial heterogeneity, mitigating overfitting through localized condition sharing. Our
022            method involves four key steps: constructing a spatiotemporal graph, encoding
023            tabular features, aggregating spatial heterogeneity node embeddings via graph
024            convolutions, and decoding with spatial condition vectors for location-specific
025            predictions. We validate our approach by predicting vegetation gross primary pro-
026            ductivity (GPP) using global climate and land cover data (2001–2020). Trained on
027
               50M samples and tested on 2.8M, our model achieves an RMSE of 0.836, outper-
               forming GWR (2.149), LightGBM (1.063) and TabNet (0.944). Visual analysis of
028
               the learned node embeddings reveals clear spatial heterogeneity patterns and their
029
               temporal dynamics.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      In Earth science, tabular machine learning is widely used to model environmental and geographical
035
      relationships, such as predicting climate change impacts on vegetation (Lu et al., 2024) and under-
036   standing tropical cyclones’ effects on precipitation (Qin et al., 2024). Accurate modeling is crucial
037   for reliable environmental predictions.
038
039
      However, most tabular machine learning methods assume unordered samples, raising questions
      about their applicability to all Earth science problems. While a global mapping requires all influ-
040
      encing factors to be known, many factors in Earth science, such as soil nutrients, microbial activity,
041
      and biodiversity, are difficult to measure, leading to incomplete information. This introduces a sig-
042   nificant challenge: the spatial distribution of missing variables is often non-stationary, meaning that
043   the relationship between the remaining features and the target variable changes with spatial loca-
044   tion (Fotheringham et al., 2009). For example, the relationship between temperature and vegetation
045   carbon accumulation rates may vary across regions due to differences in species, soil quality, and
046   altitude. Current models capture common patterns but fail to address spatial heterogeneity, high-
047   lighting the need for better methods to model spatial variability.
048   One solution is to use local models like Geographically Weighted Regression (GWR) (Fotheringham
049   et al., 2009), which adjusts coefficients based on location to capture spatial variability. However,
050   GWR lacks temporal modeling, limiting its ability to capture the evolution of spatial heterogene-
051   ity. To address GWR’s temporal limitation, Geographically and Temporally Weighted Regression
052   (GTWR) (Fotheringham et al., 2015) was developed, using spatiotemporal metrics to model vari-
053   ability. However, GTWR struggles with nonlinearity, prompting the development of hybrid methods
      like Geographically and Temporally Neural Network Weighted Regression (GTNNWR) (Wu et al.,


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      2021), which uses a Spatiotemporal Proximity Neural Network (STPNN) to model nonlinear spa-
055   tiotemporal heterogeneity.
056
057   Despite these advancements, current methods still fit spatial weights locally, based on neighborhood
058
      samples, leading to several challenges: 1) Learning objective: Local models may miss common
      patterns across regions by focusing too much on local variability; 2) Model complexity: Fitting
059
      spatial weights for each location can result in a highly dense parameter space, making the model
060
      prone to overfitting local noise; 3) Computational efficiency: In Earth science, datasets are often
061   massive, and methods with computational complexity proportional to sample size may struggle with
062   large-scale data.
063
064
      Our study aims to learn spatiotemporal heterogeneity, aligning with previous methods’ objectives.
      To address the limitations of existing research, we draw inspiration from the success of end-to-end
065
      learning in computer science, particularly the DETR model (Carion et al., 2020). We propose a
066
      unified optimization process that leverages an end-to-end learning framework to capture the spa-
067   tiotemporal heterogeneity of variable relationships across the entire sample space. Additionally,
068   instead of using explicit geographically and temporally weighted models, we propose a conditional
069   generative model with local parameter sharing, reducing the risk of overfitting due to dense spa-
070   tiotemporal weights. Our approach aims to effectively model the dynamic and spatially varying
071   relationships between variables in Earth science data.
072
      Building on this idea, we have developed method, which employs graph neural networks (GNNs)
073   (Wu et al., 2020) to implicitly learn mappings with spatiotemporal heterogeneity from Earth science
074   data. Our method consists of four key components: First, in the preprocessing stage, we cluster the
075   global land grid and map the cluster centers to spherical coordinates, using the K-nearest neighbors
076   algorithm to construct a spatial adjacency graph. Each cluster category shares a spatial condition
077   vector during decoding. In the representation learning and prediction stages, we employ a tabular
078   feature encoding module and a spatial heterogeneity encoding module to encode the tabular data
079   features and spatial heterogeneity conditions, respectively. The decoding module uses this encoded
080   information to predict the target variables. The tabular feature encoding module uses linear self-
081
      attention over two dimensions to simultaneously capture the attribute features of the tabular data
      and their temporal dynamics. The spatial heterogeneity module aggregates node embeddings using
082
      a spatiotemporal GCN (Graph Convolutional Network (Kipf & Welling, 2016)), producing spatial
083
      condition vectors that describe the spatial heterogeneity at each location. Finally, the decoding
084   module uses the spatial condition vectors as target vectors and the tabular feature encodings as
085   memory vectors, applying a transformer decoder to generate predictions.
086
087
      To validate our approach, we created the Climate2GPP dataset, using the ERA5 climate dataset
      (Muñoz-Sabater et al., 2021), the MCD12C1.061 MODIS Land Cover dataset (Friedl & Sulla-
088
      Menashe, 2022), and the PML V2 0.1.7 GPP dataset (Zhang et al., 2019). Spanning from 2001
089
      to 2020 with an 8-day temporal resolution, this dataset includes approximately 50 million samples
090   for training and 2.8 million samples for testing. Our method achieved an RMSE of 0.836 on the test
091   set, significantly outperforming GWR (RMSE 1.937), classical tabular machine learning methods
092   like LightGBM Large (RMSE 1.063) and deep learning methods like TabNet (RMSE 0.944).
093
      We also analyzed the GNN’s node embeddings visually, observing spatial distribution patterns that
094
      help us understand the spatial heterogeneity of variable relationships and their temporal evolution.

      Tabular Machine Learning and Geographically Weighted Models: Tabular machine learn-
100
      ing methods have been widely applied in Earth science for tasks such as predicting environmental
101
      changes and understanding geographical phenomena. These methods, including popular algorithms
102   like LightGBM (Ke et al., 2017) and XGBoost (Chen & Guestrin, 2016), are designed under the as-
103   sumption that samples are independent and identically distributed, which limits their applicability in
104   scenarios with spatial dependencies. While geographically weighted models, such as GWR (Fother-
105   ingham et al., 2009) and GTWR (Fotheringham et al., 2015), have been introduced to address spatial
106   heterogeneity by adjusting coefficients locally, they face significant limitations. GWR models fail
107   to capture temporal evolution, and while GTWR extends this capability, both models struggle with
      nonlinearity and exhibit high computational complexity when applied to large datasets. GWR-RF

139   We address the problem of learning under spatiotemporal heterogeneity by framing it as a con-
140   ditional generation task, where predictions are made based on data attributes conditioned on spa-
141   tiotemporal contexts. Our approach focuses on four key issues: representing spatiotemporal con-
142   ditions, encoding tabular attributes, generating predictions under these conditions, and ensuring
143   end-to-end optimization. To represent the spatiotemporal conditions, we construct a graph where
144
      node embeddings are learned by aggregating local spatiotemporal information using graph convolu-
      tions. For encoding tabular attributes, we design a dual-attention transformer encoder that captures
145
      both temporal and feature-level dependencies. The prediction process utilizes a transformer decoder,
146
      where spatiotemporal conditions are treated as the target sequence and tabular data as the memory
147   sequence. The entire framework, including learnable node embeddings, feature aggregation, and
148   prediction modules, is optimized end-to-end through gradient descent.

      3.1     S PATIOTEMPORAL C ONDITIONAL G RAPH C ONSTRUCTION
151
152   To capture the spatiotemporal heterogeneity, we propose a Spatiotemporal Conditional Graph
153   (STCG). The STCG is defined as G = (V, E), where V is the set of nodes and E is the set of
154   edges. Each node vi,t ∈ V represents a spatiotemporal point (λi , ϕi , t), where λi and ϕi are the
155   longitude and latitude of node i, and t is the time. Each node has an embedding vi,t that captures the
156   spatiotemporal condition at that point. The prediction for a spatiotemporal location is influenced by
157   the embedding vi,t of the corresponding node in the STCG. The construction of the STCG involves
158
      the following steps.

160   V , we apply K-means clustering to the global land grid. This step reduces computational complex-

      condition. Specifically, the cluster centers C = {c1 , c2 , . . . , ck } are determined by minimizing the

231   Our primary goal is to develop an efficient module that extracts both temporal representations (cap-

      A.1    BACKGROUND
543
544   What is Gross Primary Productivity (GPP): Gross Primary Productivity (GPP) measures the
545   amount of Carbon Dioxide (CO2 ) that plants absorb from the atmosphere and convert into biomass
546   through photosynthesis. In simple terms, GPP represents how much energy plants capture from
547   sunlight to support growth. GPP is driven by several environmental and biological factors, each
548   of which plays a key role in plant growth: Solar radiation, Temperature, Water availability, Nutri-
549   ent availability, CO2 concentration and Vegetation type and biodiversity. These factors interact in
550   complex ways, and their influence on GPP can vary across different geographical regions.
551   Why is a Spatially-Aware Model Necessary for GPP Prediction: Although many factors influ-
552   encing GPP are measurable, we are not always able to fully observe all of them. This incomplete
553   measurement means that the unobserved variables often vary across space in non-stationary ways.
554   As a result, the relationship between the observed variables (e.g., temperature, radiation) and GPP
555   also changes with spatial location. Therefore, a spatially-aware model is essential to capture these
556   location-dependent relationships and make accurate predictions.
