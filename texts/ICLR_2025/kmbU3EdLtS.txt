011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Document layout analysis is essential for Document Intelligence, playing a pivotal
014            role in automated understanding and processing of document content. Most exist-
015
               ing approaches within this domain are predicated on computer vision techniques
               that concentrate on image modality, despite documents containing both rich visual
016
               and textual information. While recent advances in multi-modal approaches begin to
017
               incorporate word embeddings to enhance recognition capabilities, they also incur a
018            substantial computational burden. Moreover, the diversity of document structures
019            demands models with great robustness, especially during the document editing
020            process. In this paper, we introduce pluggable and efficient data pre-processing
021            strategies to boost the layout analysis performance. Firstly, we discover that element
022            categories depend on relative relationships and propose a Graphical Multi-modal
023            Data Fusion technique, which constructs a graph to establish connections between
024            disparate textual segments. Secondly, in terms of structural diversity of documents,
025            we devise a Spatial Geometric Transformation strategy to improve model robust-
026
               ness against layout alterations. Our methods operate during the pre-processing
               phase, which facilitates straightforward integration with existing models to achieve
027
               significant accuracy increase with negligible extra computations. Experimental
028
               results show that our strategies illustrate State-Of-The-Art performance across
029            multiple document layout analysis datasets.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Document Intelligence encompasses the ability to understand, analyze, and process document content
035   with deep learning methods(Cui et al., 2021). It includes sub-tasks such as Document Layout Analysis
036   (DLA)(Pfitzmann et al., 2022; Cheng et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024),
037   Document Classify(Harley et al., 2015; Larson et al., 2022; Naeem et al., 2022), Key Information
038   Extraction (KIE)(Jaume et al., 2019; Xu et al., 2022; Luo et al., 2023), Table Recognition(Li et al.,
039
      2020b; Abdallah et al., 2022; Long et al., 2024), Optical Character Recognition (OCR)(Nayef et al.,
      2017; Shi et al., 2016; Li et al., 2023) , Visual Question Answering (VQA)(Mathew et al., 2021;
040
      Tanaka et al., 2021; Ishmam et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2024), etc. Among them, DLA stands as a
041
      fundamental branch, tasked with detection and recognition of various textual, graphical, and tabular
042   elements from documents. There are multiple application scenarios of DLA, including information
043   retrieval, archive digitization, etc. Besides, one of the main ways to acquire training data for Large
044   Language Models (LLMs) is to obtain structured corpus from books, papers, newspapers, etc., which
045   are stored in PDF or Word documents(Liu et al., 2024; Weber et al., 2023). Therefore, it is crucial to
046   analyze the layout of original documents and extract texts to increase the quality of training data for
047   LLMs. Nonetheless, DLA is a challenging task in terms of the diversity of document structures. The
048   occurrence of a minor error significantly disrupts the integrity of the overall extracted architecture,
049   leading to misinterpretations of hierarchical structure and flow of information.
050   Modern DLA models can be divided into two approaches: Vision-based and Multi-modal. The first is
051   putting DLA as a computer vision task, where the primary frameworks are object detectors(Cheng
052   et al., 2023) and instance segmentation models(Lee et al., 2019). The other approach utilizes both
053   visual and textual features(Zhang et al., 2021; Da et al., 2023). Different from computer vision tasks,
      DLA benefits greatly from the textual semantic information and the integration of both modalities

069   promotes the ability to detect and recognize document components (Yu et al.). Compared with Vision-
070   based methods, Multi-modal DLA models possess higher accuracy, but require more computation, as
071   the complexity of handling multiple modal inputs for feature extraction.
072   As for DLA, we observe that the relative relationships between text elements play a decisive role. The
073   category of a sentence is influenced not just by its intrinsic features, but also by the semantics, position,
074   and visual characteristics of associated texts. For instance, relocating a "Page-header" to the bottom of
075   a document will let DLA model reclassify it as a "Page-footer". Therefore, establishing connections
076   between sentences is beneficial, as it enables models to more accurately comprehend document
077   structures. Graph neural networks(Kipf & Welling, 2016; Velickovic et al., 2017) are effective in
078   explicitly modeling node relationships(Han et al., 2022b), so it is promising to construct graphic
079
      connections in DLA task. In addition, documents often experience random positional alterations of
      sentences during editing and it is crucial to integrate geometric transformation techniques to simulate
080
      the above changes, thereby increasing generalization to layout disturbances.
081
082   In this paper, we put forward efficient and pluggable data pre-processing approaches for DLA to
083   enhance recognition performance. On one hand, we propose a Graphic Multi-modal Data Fusion
084   method that fosters sentence connectivity. We firstly employ parsing plugins, such as pdfplumber1 ,
085
      python-docx2 , OCR, etc., to extract sentences and words from original documents, and utilize a
      tokenizer for words tokenization and acquiring word embeddings. Inspired by Transformer-iN-
086
      Transformer(Han et al., 2021; 2022a), we construct a word graph for each sentence to integrate
087
      fine-grained semantic features and set sentences as nodes to construct a sentence graph to build
088   relationships. Finally, we fuse the words, sentences, and image information for object detection.
089   On the other hand, we propose the Spatial Geometric Transformation at sentence, paragraph and
090   page levels to facilitate DLA models to handle the subtle differences of document layout. To
091   increase semantic diversity in sentence dimension, we propose a remix operation to promote sentence
092   combinations. For paragraph level, we put up scale and translate operations to change document
093   structures. For the entire page level, we randomly crop them to change the global characteristics.
094   Our proposed data pre-processing methods can be directly combined with various types of object
095   detectors with negligible increases in computational cost. As Figure 1 shows, we improve the accuracy
096   of all categories compared with baseline performance, Even for elements that rely more on image
097   features such as Picture and Table. Compared with existing SOTA models VGT(Da et al., 2023)
098   on DocLayNet(Pfitzmann et al., 2022), we achieve higher accuracy with almost half computations
099   ("82.4 mAP, 643.7G FLOPS" vs. "81.2 mAP, 1248.9G FLOPS").

      Vision-based models. These methods put DLA as pure vision tasks, as shown in Figure 2(a). Li
      et al.(Li et al., 2020a) treated DLA as an object detection task and introduced a domain adaptation
125
      module to address cross-domain document detection. Cheng et al.(Cheng et al., 2023) proposed
126
      TransDLANet, which utilizes an adaptive element matching mechanism that aligns the query embed-
127   ding more closely with the ground truth. Different from object detection, Lee et al.(Lee et al., 2019)
128   tackled the DLA task with a segmentation approach and incorporated a trainable multiplicative layer
129   technique to enhance the accuracy of object boundary detection, thereby improving the performance
130   of pixel-level segmentation networks.
131
      With the development of self-supervised pretraining, document intelligence models also shift from
132
      "supervised learning" to a paradigm of "self-supervised pretraining to fine-tuning". The LayoutLM
133   series models(Xu et al., 2020b;a; Huang et al., 2022), Unidoc(Gu et al., 2021), Selfdoc(Gu et al.,
134   2024) constructed a document level pre-training method to utilize images and textual semantic
135   features as well as layout relationships for and pre-training. However, when these models are applied
136   to downstream DLA tasks, they only consider visual features without textual information, which
137   leads to significant gaps in pre-training and fine-tuning. Therefore, they are essentially Vision-based
138   methods, as shown by the dashed lines in Figure 2(a).
139   Muti-modal models. The other type of DLA models are of Muti-modal methods, as shown in
140   Figure 2(b). Wordgrid represents that this image is a semantic image composed of word embedding,
141   and its spatial size is consistent with the image. Yang et al.(Yang et al., 2017) treated document
142   semantic structure extraction as a pixel-wise segmentation task. The proposed model classifies pixels
143   based on both visual features and sentence-level grids to elevate the precision of layout analysis.
144   Zhang et al. (Zhang et al., 2021) proposed VSR, which integrates visual, semantic, and relational
145   information to significantly improve detection accuracy. Da et al.(Da et al., 2023) put forward a
146   two-stream model VGT. The two-stream framework includes a Vision Transformer (ViT) for encoding
147
      image features and a Grid Transformer (GiT) for encoding textual features based on a 2D token-level
      grid representation of the document. Besides, this method comprises two pre-training methods called
148
      Masked Grid Language Modeling (MGLM) and Segment Language Modeling (SLM). VGT achieves
149
      SOTA performance on multiple DLA benchmarks.
150
151   Although Multi-modal approaches offer superior accuracy over Vision-based methods, the higher
152   computational cost and parameters cannot be ignored. Current Multi-modal DLA models often involve
153
      an additional large-scale network to extract textual features, almost doubling the computational
      load. To address these challenges, we propose the efficient data pre-processing techniques that
154
      significantly enhance the accuracy while minimally increasing computational requirements, as shown
155
      in Figure 2(c).

297   3.2    S PATIAL G EOMETRIC T RANSFORMATION
298
299   Regular data enhancement methods usually operate on entire images, which cannot alter the overall
300   arrangement of documents, nor can they change the layout of sentences and paragraphs. For example,
301   no matter how we resize the page image, the relative position between paragraphs remains unchanged.
302
      We are also unable to modify the texts to generate a new paragraph. This highlights the limitation
      of regular approaches, which maintain the fixed layout of textual elements and do not account for
303
      dynamic adjustments within the structures. Document layout analysis primarily focuses on learning
304
      the structural characteristics of documents, especially the interrelationships between elements. As
305   shown in Figure 4, our main contribution is to construct a strategy to synthesize new image structures
306   of documents, which transitions from sentence-level, through paragraph-level, and ultimately to
307   page-level.
308
      For document spatial augmentation, arbitrary changes may result in illogical pages, thereby generating
309
      noisy data that could hurt the precision. To mitigate this, we introduce tailored constraint conditions
310
      at each level of data augmentation. We have also redefined the operations involved and the detailed
311   description is as follows.
312
313   Sentence Remix. For the first, we propose sentence remix to enhance diversity by selectively
314
      replacing original word with another one from a distinct sentence. It is crucial to ensure that the
      newly synthesized document images are plausible. For instance, replacing a larger font title word
315
      with a smaller font text word could lead to image distortion during the resizing process. Therefore,
316
      any word replacement must meet two criteria to maintain the integrity and realism of the document
317   image: (1) It belongs to the same class as the origin. (2) The box scale difference is within 5%. We
318   use (xo1 , y1o , xo2 , y2o ) as the original word position and (xt1 , y1t , xt2 , y2t ) as the target word position. For
319   document image I, this process can be described as the following:

      4.1   DATASETS AND I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
359
360   To demonstrate the effectiveness and universality of our method, we conduct experiments on four
361   DLA datasets (DocLayNet(Pfitzmann et al., 2022), D4 LA (Da et al., 2023), PubLayNet(Zhong et al.,
362   2019), Docbank(Li et al., 2020c)) with two object detection models (FasterRCNN(Ren et al., 2015)
363   based on a ResNet50(He et al., 2016) backbone, CascadeRCNN(Cai & Vasconcelos, 2018) with
364   DiT(Li et al., 2022) backbone). Please refer to Appendix A.1 for a detailed introduction of the
365   datasets.
366   The experimental equipment is 8 × NVIDIA V100 GPUs with 32G graphics memory. Our experimen-
367   tal framework is built upon Detectron2(Wu et al., 2019). For DocLayNet, the training configuration
368   includes a batch size of 32 and a total of 32,000 training steps. For the training of FasterRCNN,
369   we use the SGD optimizer with an initial learning rate of 2e-2, reducing it by a factor of 0.1 at the
370   20,000th and 26,000th steps. We also follow the common practice by initializing the model with
371   weights pre-trained on the Microsoft COCO 2017 benchmark(Lin et al., 2014). For CascadeRCNN,
372   we opt for the AdamW optimizer(Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017), setting the initial learning rate to
373   2e-4 following DiT(Li et al., 2022) specifications. Experiments on the D4 LA, PubLayNet, DocBank
374
      datasets are performed with CascadeRCNN, using a batch size of 32 and an initial learning rate of
      2e-4, with training steps totaling 12,000, 120,000 and 200,000, respectively.
375
376   For performance assessment, we rely on the mean Average Precision (mAP) at IoU intervals
377   [0.50:0.95:0.05] as our primary metric for accuracy. To evaluate computational efficiency, we
      also measure Floating Point Operations Per Second (FLOPS) under an 800×800 input image.

519   5      C ONCLUSION
520
521   In this paper, we present a novel data pre-processing strategy specifically tailored for enhancing the
522   recognition capabilities in document layout analysis. We provide a comprehensive analysis of the
523   prevailing methodologies: Vision-based and Multi-modal models, and introduce our innovative con-
524   tributions: Graphic Multi-modal Data Fusion and Spatial Geometric Transformation. Our approaches
525
      are integrated into the pre-processing phase, ensuring a minimal computational overhead above the
      original detection models. The experimental results not only substantiate the superiority with the
526
      highest mAP but also highlight its efficiency, achieving these results with a reduced count of FLOPS
527
      compared to the SOTA performance. Furthermore, we validate the versatility and adaptability of our
528   methods across FasterRCNN and CascadeRCNN, showing the potential as a pluggable solution.
529
530   In the future, we envision the expansion of our approaches to encompass a broader array of document
531
      scenarios, including large-scale document models, table recognition, and key information extraction
      tasks.

      A.4       D ETAILED C ODE FOR CONSTRUCTING EDGES IN SENTENCE GRAPH
888
      The following code shows how the edges are established when we build the sentence graph. The core
889
      is to calculate the minimum distance as the reference and find the index of the nearest 4 nodes of
890   each sentence. Since it is an undirected graph, we also consider the bidirectional structure.
891
892            import numpy as np
893
894
               def sentences_edges_construct(boxes, nearest_num=4):
               """
895            Parameters
896            ----------
897            boxes: numpy.ndarray
898            A numpy array of shape (N, 4), where each row represents a box
899
               with the format (x_min, y_min, width, height).
900            nearest_num: int
901            The number of nearest boxes to find for each box. Default is 4.
902
903            Returns
904
               -------
               final_edge_index: numpy.ndarray
905            An array of shape (2, E) containing the indices of nearest boxes.
906            The first column contains the indices of the boxes, and the second
907            column contains the indices of their nearest boxes.
908            """
909
               # Calculate the x and y coordinates of the centers of the boxes
910            centers_x = boxes[:, 0] + boxes[:, 2] / 2
911            centers_y = boxes[:, 1] + boxes[:, 3] / 2
912
913            # Calculate the absolute differences in x and y coordinates between
914
                   the centers of all pairs of boxes
               dx = np.abs(centers_x[:, np.newaxis] - centers_x)
915            dy = np.abs(centers_y[:, np.newaxis] - centers_y)
916
917            # Calculate minimum distances along X and Y axes
               min_dx = (boxes[:, 2][np.newaxis,:] + boxes[:, 2][:, np.newaxis]) / 2


                                                                                             17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
            min_dy = (boxes[:, 3][np.newaxis,:] + boxes[:, 3][:, np.newaxis]) / 2
919
920         # Calculate the minimum distances between each pair of boxes
921             considering the edge lengths
922         # Use a series of conditions to determine the minimum distance for
                each pair:
923
            # 1. If the x-distance is less than the minimum possible x-distance,
924             use the y-distance minus the minimum possible y-distance
925         # 2. If the y-distance is less than the minimum possible y-distance,
926             use the x-distance minus the minimum possible x-distance
927         # 3. If both distances are greater than or equal to their respective
                minimums, use the Euclidean distance
928
            # 4. If both distances are greater than their respective minimums,
929             use 0 (no overlap)
930         min_dist = np.where((dx < min_dx) & (dy >= min_dy),dy - min_dy,
931         np.where((dx >=min_dx) & (dy < min_dy),dx - min_dx,
932         np.where((dx >= min_dx) & (dy >= min_dy),
            np.sqrt((dx - min_dx) ** 2 + (dy - min_dy) ** 2),0)))
933
934         # Set the diagonal elements to infinity to ignore each rectangle’s
935             distance to itself
936         np.fill_diagonal(min_dist, np.inf)
937
            # Find the indices of the nearest boxes for each rectangle
938
            nearest_indices = np.argsort(min_dist, axis=1)[:, :nearest_num]
939
940         # Create an array of pairs of indices representing the connections
941             between boxes and their nearest boxes
942         edge_index =
                np.column_stack((np.repeat(np.arange(len(nearest_indices)),
943
                nearest_num), nearest_indices.ravel())).T
944
945         # Swap the order of indices to create the reverse connections and
946             concatenate them with the original pairs
947         new_edge_index = np.stack((edge_index[1], edge_index[0]), axis=1).T
            final_edge_index = np.concatenate((edge_index,new_edge_index),axis=1)
948
949         # Remove duplicate pairs and return the final array of unique
950             connections
951         final_edge_index = np.unique(final_edge_index, axis=1,
952             return_index=False)
953
            return final_edge_index
