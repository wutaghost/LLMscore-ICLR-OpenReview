027                                                  A BSTRACT
028
029
                Text-based image editing aims to generate an image that corresponds to the given
                text prompt, but with the structure of the original source image. Existing methods
030
                often rely on attention maps in diffusion models (DMs) for structure preservation,
031
                as these features are considered to play a primary role in determining the spatial
032             layout. However, we find that these methods struggle to preserve the spatial lay-
033             out when applied to few-step DMs (e.g., SDXL-Turbo), limiting their use cases
034             to the slower multi-step DMs (e.g., Stable Diffusion). In this work, we investigate
035             the limitations of these approaches in terms of intermediate feature representa-
036             tions. Our findings indicate that for few-step DMs, the attention layers have less
037             influence in determining the structure. To tackle this, we localize layers within
038             the network that better control spatial layout and inject these features during the
039             editing process. Additionally, we disentangle structural information from other
040
                features to avoid conflicts between the injected features and the text prompt. This
                ensures that the edited image faithfully follows the prompt while preserving the
041
                source structure. Our method outperforms existing text-based editing baselines.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
045
046   Recent advances in generative modeling have led to the development of text-to-image (T2I) Diffu-
047   sion Models (DMs) (Rombach et al., 2022; Saharia et al., 2022; Betker et al., 2023; Podell et al.,
048   2023; Esser et al., 2024), which generate high-quality images from text prompts. However, the gen-
049   eration process is computationally intensive and time-consuming due to the many reverse diffusion
050
      steps required. To address this, recent approaches such as the Latent Consistency Model (LCM) (Luo
      et al., 2023a) and ADD (Turbo) (Sauer et al., 2023) have been proposed, enabling image generation
051
      with significantly fewer steps (e.g. less than 4 steps).
052
053   Meanwhile, the release of off-the-shelf large-scale T2I DMs has brought increased interest in text-
      based image editing methods (Hertz et al.; Tumanyan et al., 2023; Cao et al., 2023). Here, this editing

054
      task focuses on two fundamental objectives: 1) the edited image should align with the provided text-
055   prompt, and 2) attributes not explicitly specified in the prompt should be preserved with that of
056   the original source image. In particular, preserving the structural information of the original source
057   image, such as the overall spatial layout and object poses, is crucial in this task. To accomplish this,
058   many editing methods (Hertz et al.; Tumanyan et al., 2023; Cao et al., 2023; Chung et al., 2024;
059   Hertz et al., 2024) utilize the fact that attention maps in self- and cross-attention layers significantly
060   affect the spatial layout of synthesized images, and provide them as a structural guidance during the
061   editing process. Within this paper, we refer to these methods as attention injection.
062   However, we observe that existing text-based image editing techniques validated on multi-step DMs
063   face challenges when applied to few-step DMs, such as SDXL-Turbo (Sauer et al., 2023; Podell
064   et al., 2023) and LCM-LoRA (Luo et al., 2023b). As shown in Fig. 1, prior attention-based methods
065   (e.g. P2P (Hertz et al.) and PnP (Tumanyan et al., 2023)) often fail to produce satisfactory results
066   when used with these models. They either fail to preserve the spatial layout of the source image or
067   result in degraded image quality.
068   To investigate the causes of these failures, we analyze how attention map injection affects the fea-
069   tures in few-step DMs as in Fig. 2. A notable difference against multi-step DMs is that attention
070   maps in few-step DMs do not trigger meaningful structural changes, as visualized in Fig. 2.
071
      We hypothesize that in few-step DMs, the spatial layout is mainly determined by features in the
072
      non-residual 1 path, rather than self- and cross-attention maps that exist in residual form (i.e., will
073   be added to the skip connection), as in Fig. 2. This motivates us to modify the features in the non-
074   residual path, specifically those between generator blocks without skip connections. This way, we
075   provide explicit structural guidance to the output image, that can not be ignored via skip-connection.
076   In Fig.2, we verify that this type of approach, which we refer to as feature injection, can successfully
077   generate images that share the overall structure with that of the source image, as desired.
078
      However, feature injection has its own drawback: attribute leakage. In the second-rightmost image
079   of Fig. 4-(b), the target attribute (huski) exhibits specific attributes of the source image (eagle). This
080   indicates that the appearance and structural information are entangled within the injected feature.
081   Thus, by simply substituting the entire feature as is, the appearance information of the source image
082   may leak, causing conflicts with the edit prompt that specifies contradictory attributes.
083
      Accordingly, we aim to design a feature that provides structural guidance without interfering with
084
      the edit prompt. To extract structure-relevant information from features, we apply Singular Value
085   Decomposition (SVD) to the source image feature by treating each spatial feature as a data point. We
086   observe that the components with the highest variance across the spatial dimension mainly capture
087   structural information rather than appearance, as spatial information tends to vary most across pixels
088   in an image. Thus, we leverage these high-variance directions from the source image, combining
089   them with appearance information (e.g., identity, object class) from the edited features. This way,
090   we obtain the edited feature that has the structural information of the source image, but with the
091   overall appearance information of target edited image, aligning with the objective of the text-based
092   image editing task.
093   However, simply combining features leads to undesirable artifacts due to the spatial misalignment
094   between the source-structure and edit-appearance features. To address this, we propose a spatial
095   matching technique to further improve the editing quality. In detail, we compute a correspondence
096   map between features of the source and the edited image, and warp the edited appearance features
097   accordingly. At last, we demonstrate that the proposed method outperforms baseline methods in both
098   qualitative and quantitative comparisons for text-based editing in the few-step DMs.

144
      multi-step counterparts, these methods focus on the inverted noise space while overlooking the inter-
145
      mediate feature space of the model. Differently, in terms of intermediate feature representation, we
146   propose an editing approach that can successfully provide structural guidance, while also retaining
147   the ability to reflect the text prompt.

161   few-step model are significantly lower than their role in multi-step DMs, when determining the spa-
      tial layout. Contrary to conventional multi-step DMs (e.g., Stable Diffusion), we cannot manipulate

231   3.2   D ECOMPOSING S TRUCTURAL AND A PPEARANCE FACTORS IN F EW-S TEP DM S
232
      Entangled Structure and Appearance Factors within the Feature Space. Above, we observed
233
      that features extracted from specific blocks (e.g. Block #3) encode spatial information. However,
234
      while these features effectively capture spatial layouts, they still retain some appearance attributes
235   from the source image which may potentially hinder the overall editing process. For example, exam-
236   ining the rightmost image in Fig. 4-(b) (marked as ‘∼256’), we observe that directly injecting these
237   features leads to suboptimal editing. The edited image maintains the appearance of the source image
238   (narrow eyes and short ears), which we indicate as the attribute leakage phenomenon. This occurs
239   because the tiger’s appearance information exists in the injected features (i.e. Block #3 features),
240   and interferes with the synthesis process. This observation emphasizes the necessity of a method to
241   isolate only the structural information before injecting into the editing pipeline.
242   Structural Information in Top-K Singular Components. To gain intuitions. we apply Singular
243   Value Decomposition (SVD) to features obtained from a single image, by treating each spatial fea-
244   ture as a data point. The intuition behind it is that the features already contain abundant and diverse
245   spatial information, so only the basis with the highest variance would mostly contain structure-
246   relevant information rather than appearance. Also, the most variable information (i.e. directions with
247   the highest variances) across pixels in a single image would be spatial information, as appearance
248   information such as textures of styles can be shared across most pixels. In the first row of Fig. 4-(b),
249
      we visualize the image feature projected on the top significant singular vectors (#0-3), an also those
      projected onto less significant singular basis (#15-18). The results from the projections onto the top
250
      singular vectors clearly reflects spatial layout of each image. In contrast, projections onto the lower
251
      singular vectors (#15–18) reveal almost no structural information, which aligns with our intuition.
252
253   To further validate and quantify the statements about the decomposition between structure-aware
254   information and appearance information, we conducted further analysis. We analyze the correlation
255
      between singular vectors associated with large singular values and the spatial information. In de-
      tail, we gradually project the features on basis vectors from highest to lowest singular values and
256
      accumulate them. Then, we quantify the structural information that the projected features have. To
257
      quantify the structural information, we measure the structural distance between the projected fea-
258   tures and that of DINOv2 feature extracted from the generated images. Since the similarity map
259   from DINOv2 is known to represent the structure of image (Tumanyan et al., 2022), this distance
260   can be interpreted as a measurement regarding the degree of involvement of structural information in
261   the generator feature. As observed in Fig. 4-(a), similarity maps’ differences rapidly decrease when
262   singular values are high. Differently, the error between the projected and original features is more
263   smoothly reduced, supporting the claim that components of larger singular values mostly contain
264   spatial information instead of appearance-relevant ones.
265   Analysis on Non Top-K Components. Regarding the linearity of SVD, information apart from the
266   structure can be simply obtained by projecting a feature onto the non top-K components. This infor-
267   mation captures attributes other than shape, such as object classes or style, which we will refer to as
268   appearance information. This claim is supported by the second row of Fig. 4-(b). We observe that
269   transferring components with high singular values preserves the overall structure of the source im-
      age, while low singular vectors maintain the detailed appearance. With these insights, the following

      3.3   S TRUCTURE -P RESERVING T EXT-BASED E DITING (SPE DIT )
296
297   Overview. Based on the observations above, we develop our main method with the following three
298   key ideas: 1) disentangling the structural and the appearance information in the feature represen-
299   tation by SVD and projection, 2) improving spatial alignment between the source-structure and
300   edit-appearance, and finally 3) compositing these features and performing feature injection with it.
301   This way, we provide structural guidance without harming the editing quality.
302   Structure-Appearance Decomposition Here, we elaborate on the details to isolate the structural
303   information from the source feature, and the appearance information of the edit feature. To reem-
304   phasize, this decomposition is designed in order to follow the given text condition, while preserving
305   the source image structure.
306   First, we start with the noise identical to the source image for synthesizing the edited image in the
307   reverse diffusion process. Also, for the sake of simplicity, we omit the layer index and timestep
308   notation. Let the features of the source and edited images during the diffusion reverse process be
309   denoted as Fsrc ∈ Rhw×c and Fedit ∈ Rhw×c , respectively. To decompose the spatial information
310   from Fsrc , we first apply SVD to Fsrc and obtain the basis of channel vectors V. As mentioned
311   earlier, spatial information is dominant in the components of high singular values. Thus, we project
312   the source feature Fsrc by top-K basis vectors V[0:k] to decompose the spatial information from it.
313   Then, we also project the feature of edited image Fedit by remaining basis vectors V[k:] to maintain
314   the appearance of the target image. This process is defined as follows:

528      5                            C ONCLUSION
529
530     This work addresses the limitations of attention-injection-based text-to-image (T2I) editing meth-
531
        ods, particularly their inability to preserve the source image structure when applied to few-step
        diffusion models (DMs). Our analysis reveal that attention layers in few-step DMs have less in-
532
        fluence on determining the spatial layout. To resolve this, we identify specific positions within the
533
        network that determine spatial layout and injected features at these points during the editing pro-
534     cess. We also mitigate attribute leakage by disentangling structural factors using the singular values
535     of source features. This decomposition isolates structural information, ensuring that the appearance
536     attributes from text prompt do not conflict with those of the injected source features. Further, we
537     address misalignment issue between source structure and target appearance factors with a warping-
538     based spatial matching step, leading to improved visual quality. Extensive experiments demonstrate
539     that our method effectively captures both the source structure and the text prompt, outperforming
        baseline methods in both quantitative and qualitative results.
