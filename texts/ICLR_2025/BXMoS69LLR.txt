009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Membership inference (MI) attacks try to determine if a data sample was used
012            to train a machine learning model. For foundation models trained on unknown
013            Web data, MI attacks are often used to detect copyrighted training materials, mea-
014            sure test set contamination, or audit machine unlearning. Unfortunately, we find
015            that evaluations of MI attacks for foundation models are flawed, because they
016            sample members and non-members from different distributions. For 9 published
017            MI evaluation datasets, we show that blind attacks—that distinguish the member
018            and non-member distributions without looking at any trained model—outperform
019            state-of-the-art MI attacks. Existing evaluations thus tell us nothing about mem-
020
               bership leakage of a foundation model’s training data.

023   1   I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Many foundation models (Bommasani et al., 2021) such as GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023), Gemini (Gemini
026   Team, 2023) and DALL-E (Ramesh et al., 2021) are trained on unknown data. There is great interest
027   in methods that can determine if a piece of data was used to train these models. Such methods—
028   called membership inference attacks (Shokri et al., 2017)—have been studied for foundation models
029   in many recent works (Shi et al., 2023; Duan et al., 2024; Ko et al., 2023; Dubiński et al., 2024;
030   Zhang et al., 2024a; Meeus et al., 2023). Applications include privacy attacks (Carlini et al., 2021),
031   demonstrating the use of copyrighted material (Meeus et al., 2024b), detecting test data contamina-
032
      tion (Oren et al., 2023), or auditing the efficacy of methods to “unlearn” training data (Bourtoule
      et al., 2021).
033
034   To evaluate the performance of a membership inference attack, it is common to train a model on a
035   random subset of a larger dataset, and then ask the attacker to distinguish these members from the
036   remaining non-members by interacting with the model. Emulating this setup for foundation models
037   is hard, since we often do not have access to a held out set sampled from the same distribution as
038
      the training set.
039   Existing MI evaluations thus create member and non-member datasets a posteriori, typically by
040   picking members from sources known or suspected to be in the targeted foundation model’s training
041   set, and then attempting to emulate the distribution of these sources to sample non-members.
042   Unfortunately, we show these strategies are severely flawed, and create easily distinguishable mem-
043   ber and non-member distributions. As a special case of this flaw, concurrent work of Duan et al.
044   (2024), Maini et al. (2024) and Meeus et al. (2024a) finds a temporal shift between members and
045   non-members in the Wiki-MIA dataset (Shi et al., 2023). We show this issue is persistent by identify-
046   ing significant distribution shifts (beyond temporal shifts) in 9 MI evaluation datasets for foundation
047   models, for both text and vision. Worse, we show that existing MI attacks perform “worse than
048   chance” on these datasets. Specifically, we design “blind” attacks, which completely ignore the
049   target model, and outperform all reported results from state-of-the-art MI attacks (see Table 1).
050   Our methods are naive: for some datasets with a temporal shift, we apply a threshold to specific dates
051   extracted from each sample; for other text or text-vision datasets, we build simple bag-of-words or n-
052   gram classifiers on captions. We find that such simple methods work even for MI evaluation datasets
053   that were explicitly designed to remove distribution shifts between members and non-members. Our
      work shows that removing biases in a post-hoc fashion is highly brittle.

      2   BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORK
080
081   Web-scale training datasets. Foundation models are often trained on massive datasets collected
082
      from web crawls, such as C4 (Raffel et al., 2020), the Pile (Gao et al., 2020) or LAION (Schuh-
      mann et al., 2022). However, there has been a trend towards using undisclosed training sets for
083
      models like GPT-2 to GPT-4 (Radford et al., 2019; OpenAI, 2023), Gemini (Gemini Team, 2023) or
084
      DALL-E (Ramesh et al., 2021). Even recent open models like LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023) do not
085   release information about their training dataset. Some datasets have been released for research pur-
086   poses, such as RedPajama (Together, 2023), Dolma (Soldaini et al., 2024) or LAION (Schuhmann
087   et al., 2022). Notably, these datasets lack a designated test set.
088
089
      Membership inference attacks. Membership inference attacks aim to determine whether a given
090
      data point was used to train a machine learning model (Shokri et al., 2017). Early attacks applied a
091   global decision function to all samples (e.g., by thresholding the model’s loss (Yeom et al., 2018)).
092   Current state-of-the-art attacks calibrate the attack threshold to the difficulty of each sample (Carlini
093   et al., 2022).
094
095
      Membership inference for foundation models. Membership inference attacks have been applied
096   to LLMs (Shi et al., 2023; Duan et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024a; Meeus et al., 2023), diffusion mod-
097   els (Dubiński et al., 2024), CLIP (Ko et al., 2023), and other foundation models. The motivations for
098   these attacks include using them as a component of a privacy attack (Carlini et al., 2021), for eval-
099   uating unlearning methods (Shi et al., 2023), or to detect the use of copyrighted data (Meeus et al.,
100   2024b). Due to the lack of a dedicated test set (and possibly even an unknown training set) for the
101   targeted models, many of these works design custom evaluation datasets for membership inference
102   attacks, by collecting sources of data that were likely used (respectively not used) for training.
103
104   Evaluating membership inference. Membership inference attacks were originally evaluated with
105   average-case metrics such as the ROC AUC on a balanced set of members and non-members (Shokri
106   et al., 2017). More recent work advocates for evaluating the attack’s performance in the worst-case,
107   typically by reporting the true-positive rate (TPR) at low false-positive rates (FPR) (Carlini et al.,
      2022).

108
      Membership inference evaluations are usually set up so that a baseline attack (which does not query
109   the target model) achieves 50% AUC (or equal TPR and FPR). Some authors have also considered
110   cases where the attacker has a non-uniform prior (Jayaraman et al., 2020). In either case, the goal of
111   an MI attack is to extract information from the model to beat a baseline inference that does not have
112   access to the model.
113
      Many MI evaluation datasets for foundation models introduce distribution shifts between members
114
      and non-members, which allows for baseline attacks with non-trivial success. In concurrent work,
115   Duan et al. (2024) and Maini et al. (2024) identify a temporal shift between members and non-
116   members in one dataset—WikiMIA (Shi et al., 2023)—and demonstrate that some attacks fail when
117   temporal shifts are removed. Our work shows that this issue is much broader: virtually all evaluation
118   sets proposed for membership inference on foundation models are flawed. We further show that
119   existing attacks do not just exploit distribution shifts, but they do so sub-optimally and are easily
120   beaten by blind baselines. These attacks thus perform “worse than chance”.

122   3     B LINDLY I NFERRING M EMBERSHIP
123
124   3.1   D ISTRIBUTION S HIFTS IN MI E VALUATION DATASETS
125
126   MI evaluations measure the ability to distinguish a model’s training set members from non-members.
127   A baseline attack (with no knowledge of the model) should do no better than random guessing—i.e.,
128   an AUC of 50% or equal TPR and FPR. For current MI evaluation datasets for foundation models,
129   this is not the case, due to intrinsic differences between members and non-members. We discuss the
130   most common reasons for this discrepancy below.
131
132
      Temporal shifts. MI evaluation sets based on a hard data cutoff for some evolving Web source
      (e.g., Wikipedia or arXiv) introduce a temporal shift between members and non-members. We can
133
      thus blindly distinguish members from non-members if we can answer a question of the form: “is
134
      this data from before or after 2023?”
135
136
      Biases in data replication. Even if we know how the training set was sampled, building an in-
137   distinguishable dataset of non-members is challenging (Recht et al., 2019). Slight variations in the
138   procedures used to create the two datasets (Engstrom et al., 2020) could be exploited by a blind
139   attack.
140
141   Distinguishable tails. Some works filter and process the data to maximally align the member and
142   non-member distributions (e.g., by matching linguistic characteristics). However, distributions that
143   are hard to distinguish on average may still be easy to distinguish for data outliers.
144
145   3.2   B LIND ATTACK T ECHNIQUES
146
147   In this section, we introduce simple “blind” attack techniques to distinguish members from non-
148   members in MI evaluations. The datasets we consider consist of either text, or images and text. For
149
      simplicity, we focus only on the text modality in both cases. We do not aim for our blind attacks to
      be optimal. We prioritize simple and interpretable methods to show that existing evaluation datasets
150
      suffer from large distribution shifts. Our blind attacks typically aim to achieve a high TPR at a low
151
      FPR (i.e., very confident predictions of membership for as many samples as possible).
152
153
      Date detection. Some text samples (e.g., from Wikipedia, arXiv, etc.) might contain specific dates.
154   Heuristically, it is unlikely that a text contains specific dates referencing the future. Thus, to place
155   an upper-bound on the date at which a text was written, we simply extract all dates present in the
156   text (using simple regular expressions). We then predict that a sample is a member if all referenced
157   dates fall before some threshold. Such an approach can have false positives when a text sample only
158   references dates far in the past.
159
160   Bag-of-words classification. Explicit dates are just one textual feature we can use. More gener-
161   ally, we can aim to predict membership from arbitrary words in the text. To this end, we train a
      simple bag-of-words classifier. Here we have to guard against overfitting, since we can always find

      The dataset and evaluation. In concurrent work, Meeus et al. (2024a) also note the issue of a
      temporal shift in their previous dataset we discussed above. To remedy this, they build a new MI
294
      evaluation set where the non-members are still arXiv articles from March 2023, but the members
295

302   research topics are emerging or disappearing from the mainstream. As an illustrative example, if
303   we wanted to show non-membership, some topics provide a very strong signal: we find that articles
304   from March 2023 are about 25× more likely to contain the terms “GPT-4” or “iccvfinalcopy” than
305   articles from February 2023.3

307   select individual words that have the highest TPR-to-FPR ratio on this data (top examples include
308   “pre-registered”, “speaker-dependent” or “MMoE”). We then predict that a sample from the held-
309   out set is a member if and only if it contains one of these words. This yields a blind TPR of 2.7% at
310   an FPR of 1%, slightly better than the best MI attack that has access to a trained model. While this
311   TPR is small, it does show that temporal biases are persistent even in simple word choices.

375   MI attack.

      4.2.3      P ROJECT G UTENBERG
379
380   The dataset and evaluation. This dataset (Meeus et al., 2023) consists of books from Project
381   Gutenberg. The members are books contained in the RedPajama dataset (Together, 2023), more
382   specifically the set of “PG-19” books collected by Rae et al. (2019) which were all originally pub-
383   lished before 1919. The non-members consist of books added to the Project Gutenberg repository
384   after the creation of the PG-19 corpus in February 2019. To mitigate the obvious distribution shift
385   in the publication years of members and non-members, Meeus et al. (2023) filter both the mem-
386
      bers and non-members to only contain books published between 1850 and 1910 (i.e., members are
      books published between 1850 and 1910, which were added to Project Gutenberg before Febru-
387
      ary 2019. Non-members are books that were originally published in the same period, but added to
388
      Project Gutenberg after February 2019). The authors evaluate a new MI attack (see Section 4.1.4 for
389   details) against the OpenLLaMA model (Geng & Liu, 2023) trained on RedPajama.
390
391
      Our attack. While the publication dates of the members and non-members are similarly dis-
392
      tributed, we find that the date on which a book is added to Project Gutenberg still introduces a
393
      noticeable distribution shift. Indeed, it appears that the format of the preface metadata that Project
394   Gutenberg adds to books has changed at some point between the collection of PG-19 and the non-
395   member corpus (e.g., the link to the HTML version of a book used to have a .htm file extension,
396   and this was changed to a .html extension for books added after a certain date). By exploiting
397   such small discrepancies, our greedy n-gram attack applied to the first 1,000 characters of a book
398   achieves 55.1% TPR at a 1% FPR, 3 times higher than the best MI attack evaluated on this dataset.
399
      Of course, it may be possible to preprocess this particular dataset to get rid of these formatting
400   discrepancies. Nevertheless, we believe our results illustrate how brittle the process of re-collecting
401   an identically distributed non-member set can be. Moreover, even if we ignore any formatting
402   information, and apply our attack directly to the book text, we can still distinguish members and
403   non-members far better than chance, with a TPR of 16.6% at a 1% FPR.

406   5       A PATH F ORWARD : THE P ILE , DATAC OMP AND DATAC OMP -LM
407
408   As we have seen, building MI evaluation datasets a posteriori is incredibly challenging, due to
409   the many possibilities for distribution shift. It may be possible to apply more rigorous filtering
410   techniques to minimize these shifts, so that even strong blind attacks cannot reliably distinguish
411   members and non-members. Any MI attack’s performance should then be compared against these
412
      strong blind baselines.
413   An alternative avenue is to forego MI evaluations on arbitrary foundation models, and focus on those
414   models for which identically distributed sets of members and non-members are available. A prime
415   example is the Pile (Gao et al., 2020), which has an official test set. Models trained on the Pile
416   training set—e.g., Pythia (Biderman et al., 2023) or GPT-NeoX-20B (Black et al., 2022)—are thus
417   a popular target for MI attack evaluations (see e.g., (Duan et al., 2024; Maini et al., 2024; Li et al.,
418
      2023)). Unfortunately, the Pile only contains text, and is too small to train state-of-the-art language
      models.
419
420   A more recent and broadly applicable alternative is to use models trained on the DataComp (Gadre
421   et al., 2024) and DataComp-LM (Li et al., 2024) benchmarks. These benchmarks contribute multiple
422   models (ViTs and LLMs) trained on data sampled randomly from a larger dataset. More precisely,
423   these benchmarks introduce two datasets, CommonPool and DCLM-POOL, which contain respec-
424
      tively 12.8 billion image-text pairs and 240 trillion text tokens sampled from Common Crawl. To
      enable experiments at smaller budgets, the authors produce smaller data pools sampled randomly
425
      from the full pool. They then train a variety of models on each pool (see Table 4).5,6

      6   C ONCLUSION
462
463
      We have shown that current evaluations of MI attacks for foundation models cannot be trusted, as
464   members and non-members can be reliably distinguished by simple blind attacks with no knowledge
465   of the model. State-of-the-art MI attacks may thus be ineffective at extracting any actual member-
466   ship information, and cannot be relied on for applications such as copyright detection or auditing
467   unlearning methods. The MI evaluation datasets we investigated should likely be discarded due to
468   the large distribution shifts between members and non-members, and replaced by datasets with a
469   random train-test split—e.g., the Pile or DataComp.
