009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Preference alignment is pivotal for empowering large language models (LLMs) to
012
               generate helpful and harmless responses. However, the performance of preference
013
               alignment is highly sensitive to the prevalent noise in the preference data. Recent
014            efforts for this problem either marginally alleviate the impact of noise without the
015            ability to actually reduce its presence, or rely on costly teacher LLMs prone to
016            reward misgeneralization. To address these challenges, we propose the RObust
017            Preference Optimization (ROPO) framework, a novel iterative alignment approach
018            that integrates noise-tolerance and filtering of noisy samples without the aid of
019            external models. Specifically, ROPO first formulates the training process with
020            adaptive noise reduction as an optimization problem, which can be efficiently
021            solved in an iterative paradigm. Then, to enhance this iterative solving process
022
               with noise-tolerance and noise-identification capabilities, we derive a robust loss
               that suppresses the gradients from samples with high uncertainty. We demonstrate
023
               both empirically and theoretically that the derived loss is key to the noise-tolerance
024
               and effective filtering of noisy samples. Furthermore, inspired by our derived loss,
025            we propose a robustness-guided rejection sampling technique to compensate for
026            the potential important information in discarded queries. Experiments on three
027            widely-used datasets of dialogue and post-summarization demonstrate that ROPO
028            significantly outperforms existing preference alignment methods in the practical
029            noise setting and under artificial random symmetric noise, with its advantage
030            increasing as the noise rate increases.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Recent research indicates that the significant achievements of Large Language Models (LLMs) in
037   understanding various queries and providing helpful responses [1] rely on the preference alignment,
038   which aligns LLMs’ responses with human values and expectations [63; 6; 28]. A typical preference
039
      alignment approach is Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) [7; 76], which first
      trains a reward model to fit human preferences and subsequently employs an RL algorithm [44] to
040
      guide LLMs to generate high-reward responses. However, due to the potential risks of misgeneralized
041
      reward modeling [7] and the unstable training [29; 45] of RLHF, various ranking-based methods
042   represented by Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) [42] bypass the explicit reward modeling stage
043   and eschew RL techniques via directly optimizing the implicit reward margins between preferred and
044   dis-preferred responses [69; 59; 47]. Owing to the stable and computationally lightweight supervised
045   learning paradigm, ranking-based methods have emerged as competitive alternatives to RLHF, thus
046   drawing increasing attention recently [45; 63].
047
      Despite their impressive performance on preference alignment, ranking-based methods heavily rely
048   on high-quality preference data, which is costly and limited in practice [21; 8]. First, the noise
049   (e.g., incorrect or ambiguous preferences) in the preference data is unavoidable [58]. Many recent
050   studies have observed the presence of preference noise at levels of 20%-40% across various scenarios
051   [16; 23; 74; 55; 11; 72; 36], whether the annotators are humans or LLMs. Second, the performance of
052   LLMs will significantly deteriorate when trained with noisy preferences [10; 16; 23]. For instance, a
053   10% increase in the noise rate may lead to a 30% decrease in the performance of DPO in terms of win
      rate [16]. Therefore, it is highly desirable to develop noise-robust preference alignment techniques.

054
      To address these problems, some recent studies have explored the label smoothing [10; 35] and
055   regularization [16] techniques to alleviate the impact of preference noise. However, these methods
056   can only marginally mitigate the side effects of noise, as the noisy samples are still involved in the
057   training phase. Besides, [16] also attempts to filter out noisy samples but requires another teacher
058   LLM (i.e., a reward model serving as the proxy of the Bradley-Terry model [5]) to assign confidence
059   values to samples, which introduces additional computational costs. Moreover, the teacher LLM may
060   not necessarily provide the correct preference direction on some specific domain [7], and this method
061   is shown to be ineffective at reducing random symmetric noise [16].
062   In this paper, we propose the RObust Preference Optimization (ROPO) framework, an iterative
063   alignment approach that unifies noise-tolerance and filtering of noisy samples without the aid of
064   external models. We first provide a general formulation of learning from noisy preference data
065   as a constrained optimization problem, where we dynamically assign a quality-aware weight for
066   each sample (see Section 3.1). Then, we solve the problem through a provably convergent iterative
067   paradigm, consisting of two alternating steps: noise-tolerant model training and noisy sample filtering.
068   The main contributions of our method are as follows.
069
      • We propose a robust preference alignment framework that unifies noise-tolerance and filtering of
070     noisy samples. Without the need for any external LLM, the model’s robustness and discrimination
071     ability against noisy samples gradually improve as the alternating iterative training proceeds.
072
      • We derive a robust loss function by suppressing the gradients of samples with high uncertainty.
073
        The loss contains a noise-aware term, which not only prevents the model from over-fitting to noisy
074     samples but also facilitates identifying noisy samples versus clean samples1 (see Section 3.2).
075
      • We propose a robustness-guided rejection sampling technique to compensate for the potential
076
        important information in discarded queries (see Section 3.3), which improves the data quality and
077     thus leads to further improvement in alignment performance.
078
      • We conduct extensive experiments on three widely-used datasets (i.e., UltraFeedback Binarized,
079
        Alpaca Comparison, and TL;DR) with Mistral-7B, Llama-2-7B, Llama-3-8B, Llama-2-13B, and
080     Llama-3-70B. The evaluation results on AlpacaEval, Arena-Hard, and MT-Bench show that the
081     performance of ROPO remains stable in both practical and artificial noisy scenarios.

      2.1   A LIGNMENT OF L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS
092
093   Most of the existing LLM alignment frameworks first fine-tune a pre-trained model on high-quality
094   datasets of downstream tasks (e.g., dialogue and post-summarization) via maximum likelihood, in
095   order to teach the model to respond to queries. We denote the supervised fine-tuned model πsft . Then,
096   we train the model πθ (initialized by πsft ) based on human preference data. Specifically, a preference
097   sample contains a query x, responses y1 and y2 , and a ranking label c provided by annotators. We
098   use c = 0 to indicate that y1 is preferred to y2 (denoted y1 ≻ y2 | x) and use c = 1 to indicate the
099
      opposite. We assume that the preference data (x, y1 , y2 , c) is sampled from a distribution D.
100   A popular formulation of the generation of preferences is the Bradley-Terry (BT) model [5], i.e.,
101   P ∗ (y1 ≻ y2 | x) = σ(r∗ (y1 , x) − r∗ (y2 , x)), where σ is the sigmoid function, and r∗ is a latent
102   and inaccessible reward function. The key to existing preference learning methods is to explicitly or
103   implicitly approximate r∗ or P ∗ . RLHF [37] approximates r∗ by training a parameterized reward
104   model rϕ via maximum likelihood on preference data, then uses the well-trained rϕ to provide signals
105
      for the reinforcement learning of πθ .
106      1
           In Section 3.2, we demonstrate that the cross-entropy loss (i.e., DPO loss) cannot distinguish between noisy
107   samples and clean samples in the context of preference learning, even though it is widely used for learning from
      noisy data in other scenarios such as image classification [20; 30].

      3     ROBUST P REFERENCE O PTIMIZATION
154
155
      We propose RObust Preference Optimization (ROPO), an iterative preference alignment framework.
156   ROPO alternates between noise-tolerant model training and noisy sample filtering, as shown in Figure
157   1, which is mathematically equivalent to iteratively solving a constrained optimization problem
158   (Section 3.1). In the model training step, we introduce a robust loss function by suppressing the
159   gradients of samples with high uncertainty, which prevents the model from over-fitting to the noisy
160   preference. In the sample filtering step, we filter out noisy samples based on the magnitude of their
161   training losses. The key to ROPO is that our proposed loss contains a noise-aware term, which not
      only features noise-tolerance, but also facilitates identifying noisy samples versus clean samples

      (Section 3.2). Further, we propose a robustness-guided rejection sampling technique to compensate
163   for the potential important information in discarded queries and thus improve the data quality (Section
164   3.3). For detailed proofs of the theorems in this section, please refer to Appendix E.

      samples. However, compared with ℓna , ℓdpo leads to a “softer” optimal preference probability, which
318   could potentially avoid discrimination against minorities by LLMs. Besides, the aggressive weighting
319   strategy may be useful for clean preference datasets (although they are rare). Thus, it is considered
320   necessary to incorporate a minor component of ℓdpo into the final loss. From this perspective, the
321   hyperparameter α plays an important role in trading-off between aggressive (ℓdpo ) and conservative
322   (ℓna ) gradient weighting strategy. Given that the weight of ℓna is α > 2 times greater than that of
323   ℓdpo (in our experiments and ablations, α ≥ 6), ℓna dominates the optimization process. Thus, the
      incorporation of ℓdpo does not hurt the noise-tolerance and noise filtering too much.

      3.3      ROBUSTNESS - GUIDED R EJECTION S AMPLING
325
326   The sample filtering step effectively reduces the proportion of noise but may also discard some
327   important queries. For example, a query designed to eliminate the occupational discrimination in
328   LLMs may be filtered out because the ranking label of its associated responses is incorrect. Thus,
329   inspired by the sample distinguishing ability of our proposed ℓropo , we propose a rejection sampling
330   technique to compensate for the essential but discarded information and thus improve the robustness
331   of our ROPO framework. Specifically, we sample K responses y                      eK to x for each sample
                                                                           e1 , . . . , y

      (x, y1 , y2 ) that is filtered out and generate 2K candidate samples

336   that we treat the model’s responses as dis-preferred ones compared to the original responses, which
337
      suppresses the potential unsatisfactory or even harmful information in the model’s outputs.
338   Discussion. The rejection sampling is a popular approach of data augmentation to improve the data
339   quality and performance in existing preference alignment methods [12; 31; 65; 67; 60]. Specifically,
340   [12] ranks newly-collected responses based on their rewards and selects the highest ranked one to
341   add to the dataset. To address the issue of the excessively high rejection rate and thus improve
342   the effectiveness of rejection sampling, [65] proposes a multi-step sampling technique, which also
343
      requires an external reward model. Besides, [60] and [67] consider rejection sampling for the
      multi-objective preference alignment, where [60] projects multi-objective reward vectors onto one
344
      dimension and then selects samples based on the scalar rewards, while [67] augments samples near the
345
      Pareto front of multi-dimensional rewards, leading to a strong multi-objective alignment performance.
346   Compared to the aforementioned methods, which all rely on rewards provided by external models, our
347   robustness-guided rejection sampling technique selects new samples based on loss values that reflect
348   the quality of the samples. Moreover, our technique benefits from being independent of external
349   LLMs, thus leading to computational and memory efficiency.

      5    R ELATED W ORK
505
506   Preference Alignment of LLMs. The most representative paradigm of preference alignment is
507   RLHF [76; 37], which involves training a reward model to capture human preferences and then
508   steering LLMs towards producing high-reward responses through RL algorithms [44]. However, in
509   real applications, RL-based methods are complex and prone to instability during training [42; 64; 69].
510   Therefore, many recent studies have explored more straightforward and stable alternatives for RLHF
511   [69; 42; 47; 61; 28; 27; 63; 71]. Among these studies, the most promising direction is to use a
512
      contrastive or ranking loss to calibrate the likelihood of the output sequence. Specifically, RRHF [69]
      introduces a ranking loss to encourage larger likelihoods for better responses and smaller likelihoods
513
      for worse responses. Besides, another important work is DPO [42], which implicitly optimizes the
514
      same objective as existing RLHF-based methods and enables human preference alignment directly
515   with a simple cross-entropy loss. In addition to the aforementioned methods using data in the form of
516   (x, y1 , y2 , c), where c is the preference label, some recent studies [13; 15; 8] have also used data in
517   the form of (x, y, c), where c is an annotation of the response y, for preference alignment.
518
      Learning from Noisy Data. In the era of deep learning, there is an urgent demand for large-scale
519
      training samples, and the cost of manually annotating or filtering data is prohibitively expensive in
520
      most circumstances [48]. Therefore, learning from noisy data has become increasingly important,
521   which primarily falls into three categories. The first category is sample-selection based methods
522   [53; 41; 38; 49], which identify high-quality samples before training and filter out noisy samples.
523   For example, [53] uses the training dynamics to identify valuable samples. The second category is
524   weighting-based methods, which assign greater weights for important samples and lesser weights for
525   noisy samples [43; 18; 46]. Besides, another important area of research is dedicated to the design of
526   loss functions that are robust to noise [17; 62; 70]. The findings in [17] indicate that the traditional
527   cross-entropy loss is sensitive to the label noise, while symmetric loss functions are robust to such
528   noise. Furthermore, recent advances in LLMs have also underscored the essential role of data quality
529
      in both pre-training and supervised fine-tuning (SFT) phases of LLMs [34; 75; 22].
530
531
      6    C ONCLUSION
532
      Robust preference optimization is critical for the LLM alignment, as noisy preferences are inevitable
533
      in practical scenarios. Unlike existing methods, which rely on label smoothing or external LLMs for
534   the sample selection, we propose a robust preference alignment framework that unifies noise-tolerant
535   model training and effective filtering of noisy samples. Specifically, we incorporate a noise-aware loss
536   term to prevent the model from over-fitting to noise. Besides, we demonstrate that the proposed noise-
537   aware term plays a crucial role in distinguishing noisy samples from clean ones. Furthermore, we
538   propose a robustness-guided rejection sampling technique to compensate for the potential information
539   reduction caused by the filtering stage. We provide extensive theoretical and empirical evidence to
      demonstrate the effectiveness of our proposed ROPO framework.

953   B    D ISCUSSION ON P REFERENCE N OISE
954
955   Due to the inherent differences in annotators’ preferences, the preference noise is usually unavoidable.
956   In this section, we discuss the definition and identification of preference noise.
957
958
      Before giving the definition of preference noise, we invite our readers to pay attention to the following
      two points.
959
960
      1. This paper focuses on noisy preferences rather than the more general noisy preference data. The
961      former refers specifically to the noise in preference labels, while the noise corresponding to the
962      latter may come from multiple factors such as preference labels, text quality, and the matching
963      degree between queries and responses. It is interesting and meaningful to study a wider range of
964      noisy data, but it is beyond the scope of our paper and related work [35; 10; 16].
965
      2. Like related work [35; 10; 16], this paper is based on the Bradley-Terry (BT) model. The BT
966      model assumes the existence of a “gold”, latent, and inaccessible reward model r∗ . Then, we
967      can express the BT preference probability P ∗ (y1 ≻ y2 | x) for a sample (x, y1 , y2 ) using the
968      reward model r∗ . Intuitively, the BT model assumes that there are mainstream preferences in
969      human society that reflect values such as peace, friendliness, honesty, etc. Differently, there are
970      also studies on multifaceted or multidimensional preferences [33; 24], but defining noise for them
971      is challenging because it is difficult to have a “ground truth” label. Therefore, our following
         discussion is based on the assumption of the BT model.


                                                           18
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       Definition of preference noise. For a sample (x, y1 , y2 , ĉ), if P ∗ (ĉ) > 0.5, then the sample is
973    clean; otherwise, the sample is noisy. Because the BT model usually represents preferences that are
974    consistent with mainstream values of human society, so the formation of such noise is usually caused
975    by the personal preferences or cognitive biases of annotators. Please note that the annotators can be
976    humans or LLMs.
977
       Identification and detection of preference noise. As mentioned above, the definition of preference
978
       noise is based on the inaccessible reward model, so we can never identify preference noise accurately.
979    However, we can estimate the noise rate by using advanced LLMs as the proxy for the BT model or
980    computing the inter-annotator agreement.
981
982    • Using advanced LLMs as the proxy for the BT model [16]. Given a dataset, we can prompt
983      advanced LLMs (e.g., GPT-4, Llama-3-70B-Instruct [14], and Qwen-2-72B-Instruct [66]) to
984      identify the noise. For example, we can provide them with rules and ask them to rate or rank the
