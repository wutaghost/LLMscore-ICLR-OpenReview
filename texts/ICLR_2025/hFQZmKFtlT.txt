009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Understanding whether and to what extent token sequences generated by large
012            language models (LLMs) are the result of regurgitating memorized training data
013            or are based on meaningful learning of the training data’s syntax and semantics
014            has many important implications. In order to cleanly measure and disentangle to-
015            ken recollection by rote (memorization) from generation with understanding, we
016
               create an experimental framework that is based on training LLMs over sequences
               generated using formal grammars. Our framework allows us to better understand
017
               the interplay between the two types of learning, namely, by rote vs. with under-
018
               standing. Using our framework we make several striking observations that hold
019            consistently across different open-source model families (Pythia, Llama, and Mis-
020            tral): (a) we find that the learning types are at odds with each other during training,
021            i.e., rote learning harms understanding and by developing understanding, models
022            forget previously memorized sequences, (b) we find that entropy of the training
023            datasets impacts the ease of learning, with lower entropy datasets being easier
024            to learn with understanding and higher entropy datasets being easier to learn by
025            rote, (c) we highlight the difficulty of determining the type of learning involved
026            in a model based solely on recollecting a training data sequence. Our surprising
027
               results have significant downstream implications in the study and usage of LLMs.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031               ”Every teacher knows that there is a profound difference between a student
032             learning a lesson by rote and learning it with understanding, or meaningfully.”
033                  – Herbert Simon in The Sciences of the Artificial, Third Edition, 1996
034
      The unsupervised training objective of generative models, particularly auto-regressive large lan-
035
      guage models (LLMs), raises the potential for learning training data both by rote (Bender et al.,
036   2021) and with understanding Bubeck et al. (2023). Our goal in this paper is to create a framework
037   that enables us to better understand, measure and distinguish the two types of learning that influence
038   the generation (recollection) of next token in LLMs namely, memorization, i.e., learning by rote, and
039   generalization, i.e., learning with understanding. Such distinction is challenging, but it has many
040   important implications ranging from assessing privacy risks (Biderman et al., 2023a; Carlini et al.,
041   2021) and copyright concerns (Petroni et al., 2019; Reisner, 2023) with training LLMs to developing
042   a foundational understanding of the cognitive abilities of LLMs, i.e., how efficiently they represent,
043   store, and retrieve information.
044   To illustrate the central challenges and key ideas of this work, let us conduct a thought experiment.
045   Imagine an English speaker and a German speaker commit a paragraph in German to memory. When
046   recollecting the paragraph, do the two speakers rely on rote learning to the same or different extents?
047   Intuitively, the German speaker understands the syntax and semantics of the tokens in the paragraph,
048   while the English speaker sees the paragraph as a sequence of alphabet tokens. Even before reading
049   the paragraph, given some prefix, the former is more likely to predict the next token correctly than
050
      the latter. So it stands to reason that the extent of rote learning involved in recollecting the paragraph
      is higher for the English speaker than the German speaker.
051
052   Our thought experiment above highlights the importance of disentangling the two types of learning
053   that are involved in the generation (recollection) of next tokens in the training data. But, how can
      we isolate and estimate the extents of the two types of learning? In contrast to prior works on

      LLM memorization, our simple but crucial observation is that it is not possible to directly measure
077
      a model’s memorization. However, it is possible to measure the model’s ability to generalize to
078   other test data, i.e., sequences with similar syntax and semantics as the training data. The extent to

080   its ability to recollect the training data and its ability to generate test data.
081
      To operationalize our ideas, we created a “clean” experimental framework, where we train LLMs
082
      to memorize sequences generated by formal grammars such as regular and context-free grammars.
083
      Formal grammars allow us to study the phenomena of memorization in isolation from generaliza-
084   tion. Formal grammars i) allow us to generate training data following the syntax of our choice and
085   guarantee that models have not seen such data during pre-training, ii) enable us to generate test data
086   following the same syntax as training data, which can then be used to estimate the model’s gener-
087   alization and memorization, and iii) give us precise control over all aspects of the data-generation
088   process, such as string length, alphabet size, and entropy. Achieving all of these properties with nat-
089   ural language would not be possible. However, as our findings largely reflect the learning abilities
090   of the LLMs and are not specific to the chosen grammar, we expect them to also apply to natural
091   language data.
092   The goal of our experiments is to unveil how the two types of learning evolve when LLMs are trained
093   or fine-tuned over datasets. Specifically, we hope to understand the learning dynamics, i.e. when
094   memorization begins and when generalization ends throughout repeated exposition to the training
095   data and how the model behaves. Figure 1(a) shows how an LLM’s learning evolves when trained
096   over a dataset generated by an example formal grammar. The differences in the progression of loss
097   curves for “training”, “test”, and “random” sequences show that both types of learning are involved
098
      and the interplay between them. Memorization (learning by rote) of training data begins at epoch six;
      when training and test data curves diverge. Improvement in generalization (learning by understand-
099
      ing) ends at epoch ten; when test data reaches its minimum loss. The extent to which recollection of
100
      training data is based on memorization can be estimated by subtracting the recollection that can be
101   attributed to generalization as shown in Figure 1(b).
102
103   In order to obtain a comprehensive understanding of the learning processes in LLMs, we perform
104
      an extensive set of experiments over pretrained models from different families (Pythia, Llama, and
      Mistral) with parameter counts spanning more than an order of magnitude. We use data generated
105
      from formal grammars different distributions, vocabularies and string structures.
106
107   In all cases, we observed similar learning dynamics over repeated training iterations (epochs). We
      consistently observe that models frequently begin the memorization phase before they end the gen-


                                                                        2
108
      eralization phase. But, the exact start and end of the phases as well as their relative impact in
109   generating (recollecting) tokens varies based on the models, training dataset sizes and their underly-
110   ing grammars. We further analyze the interplay between the two types of learning, motivated by the
111   following questions:
112
      Q1: Can we determine whether and to what extent a trained model has memorized a given training
113
      data sequence? Many prior studies attempted to answer this question based only on the extent
114
      to which the trained model can recollect the tokens in the given sequence. We show why it is
115   impossible to make such determinations, and how the prior works erred by failing to distinguish
116   cleanly between the two types of learning. Specifically, due to differences in their training, it is
117   possible for two models to recollect tokens in a sequence to the same extent, with one having begun
118   the memorization phase, while the other has not started memorization. Beyond quantifying token
119   recollection, quantifying the memorization of a model requires quantifying its generalization.
120
      Q2: How does learning by rote affect learning with understanding and vice versa? While we
121   observe that learning by rote and learning with understanding do co-exist, once the generalization
122   phase ends, additional training iterations improve one at the expense of the other. Specifically,
123   after the generalization phase, more memorization comes at the cost of generalization performance.
124   Equally striking, however, is that once a training dataset has been memorized, switching to and
125   training over a new dataset (with the same underlying syntax) leads to forgetting of the previously
126   memorized data.
127   Q3: Are some training datasets easier to generalize or memorize than others? To address this
128   question, we compare the generalization and memorization performance of models over datasets
129   drawn from grammars with the same syntax and alphabet, but with different entropy values, i.e.,
130   different probability distributions over generated sequences. We find that models generalize better on
131   grammars with lower entropy, i.e., they are easier to learn with understanding, but models memorize
132   better on grammars with higher entropy, i.e., they are easier to memorize by rote.
133   Our work stands in contrast to many related works that solely focused on LLM memorization from
134   the perspective of quantifying privacy risks. Some of the phenomena we unveil as we tackle the
135   above questions are surprising and unexpected. Many have significant implications for quantify-
136   ing memorization, understanding how memorization works, and estimating the risks of memoriz-
137   ing different types of training data. For many of the observed phenomena, we do not have clear
138   (mechanistic) explanations as of why they happen. But, we feel it is important to report them to
139   the community, as they rule out certain theories related to memorization and can give rise to new
140   ones. We also think that our findings can motivate further studies that will increase our foundational
141
      understanding of LLMs’ cognitive abilities.
142
143
144   Related work: The topic of memorization has received great attention in the context of LLMs that
145   are trained on large “internet-scale” data (Song & Shmatikov, 2019; Carlini et al., 2019; Huang
146   et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2021; Biderman et al., 2023a; Mattern et al., 2023; Lukas et al., 2023;
147
      McCoy et al., 2023). Most of these works propose a definition of memorization to test whether
      the model can generate a given string (present in the training data) using particular prompts or
148
      prefixes. While they subtly differ in how exactly they operationalize a measure of memorization, at a
149
      higher level, all these works are concerned with answering the “why” question around memorization,
150   e.g. why should memorization be a practical concern? To this end, these works show compelling
151   examples of cases where memorization can hurt (e.g. privacy leaks via reconstruction (Carlini et al.,
152   2021) or membership inference (Mattern et al., 2023)). Similarly, there is also a case to be made
153   for memorization being desirable in cases where the goal is to generate facts and reduce LLM
154   hallucinations. Grounding the generation by LLMs in some verified training data sources can be an
155   effective way to generate trustworthy information (Li et al., 2023; Borgeaud et al., 2022; Khandelwal
156   et al., 2019; Tay et al., 2022; AlKhamissi et al., 2022; Petroni et al., 2019; Guu et al., 2020; Haviv
157   et al., 2022).
158   We differ from existing works in a key aspect. Our goal is to build a foundational understanding
159   of how these models memorize and how memorization interplays with generalization. Thus, we do
160   not engage with the question of memorization being desirable or undesirable, and rather provide
161   observations on how memorization happens at an input-output level. Our work adds to the nascent
      literature focused on building a better scientific understanding of memorization in LLMs (e.g. (Tiru-


                                                        3
162
      mala et al., 2022; Jagielski et al., 2022; Carlini et al., 2022; Kharitonov et al., 2021; Huang et al.,
163   2024; Schwarzschild et al., 2024)).

      In order to create a “clean” experimental setup, we use strings generated by probabilistic formal
      grammars to train and test LLMs. In contrast to natural language, probabilistic grammars provide
169
      us with a clearly defined syntax and control over all aspects of the data generation process. A
170
      grammar defines a probability distribution over strings. Given an alphabet T for a grammar G, the
171   probability distribution PG over strings from the alphabet T is PG : T ∗ → [0, 1], where T ∗ is the
172   set of strings generated by T . We consider here only grammars that generate finite-length strings.
173   In our experiments, we use training and test sets Dtrain , Dtest ∼ PG sampled from PG to train and
174   evaluate models.
175
      We call ℓ = |T | the size of the alphabet over tokens T . The alphabet T of a grammar is a subset of
176
      a much larger vocabulary of all tokens V used by an LLM, T ⊂ V . Tokens in an LLM’s vocabulary
177   can range from single characters to entire words, and its size spans from tens of thousands to a few
178   hundred thousand tokens. In our experiments, we use token alphabets corresponding to lowercase
179   characters in the Latin alphabet and integer numbers. We often refer to the elements of the alphabet
180   as characters, even though they are technically tokens.
181
      In Section 5, we discuss how the entropy of a grammar affects the ability of an LLM to recollect
182
      it by rote or with understanding. The entropy H(G) of a grammar G is defined as the entropy
183   of the probability distribution over all strings that can be generated by the grammar (Cover, 1999;

      3.1                DYNAMICS OF M EMORIZATION AND G ENERALIZATION
259
260   We disentangle learning by rote vs. learning with understanding by characterizing the training dy-
261   namics of an LLM at the memorization and the generalization phases, respectively. In particular,
262   we ask two questions, that we empirically answer through training different LLMs on the random
263   and the hierarchical grammars and observing their ability to predict tokens from the training and test
264   datasets generated by the grammar used for their training.
265
      In Figure 2, we see the training and the test loss of different models exposed to different grammars,
266   as a function of the training epochs. We observe that in the initial training epochs, the training and
267   test loss of an LLM decreases similarly. This implies that, albeit never seeing the test data, the LLM
268   demonstrates equal ability to generate the next token regardless of whether the prefix is from the

      training or the test dataset. Hence, the LLM can transfer its knowledge to the unseen test set. During
271   this period, we say that the LLM is recollecting tokens with understanding.

274
      epoch where the test loss starts diverging from the training loss. At the point of this divergence,
      the LLM starts to recollect tokens by rote, and it can no longer generate test strings as accurately
275
      as training strings. In Figure 2, we highlight the start of the memorization phase with a cross mark.

279   When does generalization (learning with understanding) end? As we discussed above, memo-
280   rization starts at the point where the test loss of the LLM diverges from its training loss. However,
281   the test loss may still decrease in epochs following this point of divergence. We say that general-
282   ization ends at the epoch when the test loss reaches its minimum; after this epoch, test loss never
283   decreases. In Figure 2, we highlight the points with the lowest test loss with triangle markers.
284   Analyzing memorization and generalization phases. We study the epoch when an LLM reaches
285   (resp. ends) memorization (resp. generalization) and the corresponding test loss for different training
286   sizes across different models and grammars in Figure 2. Our observations are the following:
287
288   • Overlap between the two phases. In different models and grammars, the generalization phase
289     and memorization phase may overlap (Figure 1a and 2). During these overlapping epochs, the
290     total recollection of an LLM is partly with understanding and partly by rote. It is only before the
291     start of memorization, that the LLM learns with understanding, and after the end of generalization
292
        that the LLM learns by rote.
293   • Varying Training Size Increasing training size results in delayed memorization and longer gen-
294     eralization, and the corresponding test loss is lower – the observation holds across models and
295     grammars. For example, in Pythia-1B model on the random grammar, memorization starts at
296     epoch 9 with training size n = 8, compared to epoch 20 with n = 64, and the corresponding test
297     loss is 3.24 for n = 8 and 3.15 for n = 64. For the hierarchical grammar, memorization starts at
298
        epoch 9 for n = 8, but at epoch 25 for n = 64, and the corresponding test loss is 1.46 for n = 8
        and 0.59 for n = 64. A similar observation holds at the end of generalization.
299
300   • Different Models. The start of memorization and end of generalization may vary among models
301     for the same grammar and training size. For example, in the random grammar and n = 8, Pythia-
302     1B, Llama3-8B, and Mistral-Nemo-12B start memorization at epoch 9, 6, and 5, respectively,
303     and end generalization at epoch 20, 16, and 16, respectively. Thus, Pythia-1B model, takes more
304
        epochs to start memorization and end generalization compared to other models. The correspond-
        ing test loss at these two points of interest also vary among models. These observation leads us to
305
        carefully inspect if similar recollection of tokens by different models may occur where one model
306
        is in memorization phase while another is in generalization phase, which we discuss next.

      3.2   Q UANTIFYING MEMORIZATION IN PRACTICE
310
      Quantifying memorization and determining whether a model has memorized a string has been the
311
      focus of many prior studies (Biderman et al., 2023a; Carlini et al., 2022; Tirumala et al., 2022). All
312   these studies use the recollection accuracy of tokens in order to define their memorization measures.
313   We claim below that such measures do not provide sufficient insight into memorization and may
314   lead to wrong conclusions. Then, we open the floor to discuss some possible alternatives.
315
      Existing memorization measures. Carlini et al. (2022) claim that an LLM has memorized a string
316
      when it can successfully recollect the next 50 tokens given a prefix. This leads to an underestimation
317
      of memorization, as it takes a very long time for an LLM to predict correctly 50 consecutive tokens.
318   On the other hand, Tirumala et al. (2022) claim that a single token in a string is memorized when
319   an LLM can correctly predict it given some context. This definition is much more liberal, leading
320   to an overestimation of memorization, as models can predict many individual tokens correctly via
321   understanding, rather than by rote. Using the right number of tokens to be predicted correctly is
322   a parameter that is hard to set a priori. These examples illustrate that measuring memorization is
323   tricky, as it is easy to be either overly conservative or overly liberal, and it is not clear what the right
      parameter setting should be.

341   The impossibility of estimating memorization based on recollection. In fact, we claim that
342   merely looking into recollection of tokens without differentiating between two modes of learning:
343   learning by rote and with understanding, makes it impossible to decide memorization. To demon-
344   strate this, we show an example of two LLMs that correctly recollect the same amount of tokens,
345   while being in different phases; one is in the generalization and another is in the memorization
346
      phase. This example is illustrated in Figure 3. Here, we use two training sizes (n = 8 and n = 64),
      and thus, one LLM witnesses more training data than another. We observe that for an identical
347
      training loss, such as 0.6 for Pythia-1B, the LLM trained with n = 8 is recollecting via rote (test
348
      loss diverges to ∼ 0.8), while the LLM with n = 64 recollects with understanding (test loss remains
349   close to 0.6) at the point where the n = 64 model starts into the Memorization-Phase. The same
350   observation holds for all three models. Therefore, recollection over training strings cannot be the
351   sole indicator to accurately estimate memorization, one needs to differentiate between recollection
352   by rote and with understanding.
353
      An alternative quantification of memorization. Given the above discussion, we claim that in order
354
      to quantify memorization one needs to take into consideration both the training loss and test loss of
355   the LLM at any training epoch. Based on this intuition, one candidate measure can be:
356
357                                                                                                            Loss(M, Dtrain )
                                                 memorization(M, Dtrain , Dtest ) = 1 −                                         ,
358                                                                                                            Loss(M, Dtest )
359
      where Loss(M, Dtest ) > 0 and Loss(M, Dtest ) ≥ Loss(M, Dtrain ). Intuitively, as long as both
360
      losses are similar, the memorization is negligible; memorization increases with an increasing differ-
361   ence between the test and the training loss. Since, in practice, the denominator is always greater or
362   equal to the numerator, our measure takes values in [0, 1].
363
364
      One can think of the above measure as a suite of measures; Instead of using loss to quantify mem-
      orization, other performance metrics (such as accuracy) can also be used with the same high-level
365
      interpretation: memorization is the difference of the LLM’s performance between training and test
366
      datasets computed using appropriate performance metrics.
367
368   Such a family of measures come with their own set of shortcomings. These measures depend on the
369   model’s performance on train and test data. While we have access to an unseen test set from the same
370
      distribution in our experimental setting, it may be more challenging in practical settings as one must
      generate test and training data to compute such measures. We think that defining and computing
371
      such measures in practice will provide new insights regarding the dynamics of memorization of
372
      LLMs and leave it as a direction for future work.

472   5            A RE SOME DATASETS EASIER TO GENERALIZE / MEMORIZE THAN OTHERS ?
473
474   So far, the distributions PG induced by the random and hierarchical grammar were the same. An
475   interesting question, however, is how the distribution PG that data is sampled from affects a model’s
476   ability to learn it by understanding and by rote. One factor that is closely associated with memoriza-
477   tion is entropy (i.e. via compressibility), and here we study how it affects both memorization and
478   generalization. In a nutshell, we make the following observations: 1) the entropy of a grammar’s
479   distribution strongly affects a model’s ability to learn it by understanding and by rote, and 2) lower
480   entropy distributions are easier to generalize to, but harder to memorize to than higher entropy ones.
481   To study the impact of entropy on learning, we create distributions based on the random and hierar-
482   chical grammars that differ in their entropy. For the random grammar, we change the entropy in two
483   ways: i) we change the size of the alphabet T , using sizes ℓ ∈ 2, 7, 26, and ii) we oversample the
484   first token (“a”), such that the entropy of the ℓ = 26 distribution matches that of the ℓ = 2 (H2 ) and
485   ℓ = 7 (H7 ) distributions. Note that both reducing the alphabet size and oversampling certain tokens
      from the alphabet reduces the entropy of the distribution based on that alphabet. For the hierarchical


                                                                                    9
486
      grammar, we skew the probabilities of the production rules to values p ∈ {0.5, 0.75, 0.9}. Here,
487   higher values of p correspond to lower entropy. We train models on training sets of size 8 and use
488   test sets of size 10 for the random grammars and 100 for the hierarchical grammars.
489
490
      Results. Figure 5 shows the impact of varying the entropy of the distribution in three different ways,
      for three different models. As we decrease the entropy of the distribution PG , we consistently ob-
491
      serve that models generalize better to the distribution, i.e. the lowest test loss they achieve decreases
492
      with entropy. On the other hand, strings become easier to memorize as entropy increases. Thus, a
493   lower-entropy grammar is easier to recall by understanding in the generalization phase, but harder
494   to recall by rote in the memorization phase, and vice versa.
495
496
      Practical Implications. Our findings demonstrate that not all strings are equally memorable or
      generalizable – in fact, we show that memorability and generalizability is intrinsically related to
497
      the entropy of the distribution from which data is sampled from. Generalizing these findings to
498
      natural language strings remains an open challenge, however, as it is unclear how one would esti-
499   mate the entropy of such strings. Intuitively, however, our findings imply that less typical strings
500   (higher entropy), are easier for an LLM to memorize. Our observations also encourage potential
501   conjectures for how generalization or memorization may be occurring in LLMs – for example, bet-
502   ter generalization over lower entropy data could be related to their better compressibility, while
503   better memorization over higher entropy data could be related to length of unique prefixes needed
504   to recollect the next token.

      6   C ONCLUDING D ISCUSSION
508
509   Conclusions: In this paper, we study the phenomenon of learning by rote, i.e. memorization, and
510   its connection with learning with understanding, i.e. generalization, at a foundational level. We
511
      use strings sampled from random and hierarchical formal grammars to create controlled “labora-
      tory” conditions. This data-generation process ensures that 1) the distribution of training data is
512
      well-known and identical between training and test sets, and 2) we have full control over the data-
513
      generation process, which allows us, for instance, to change the entropy of the distribution.
514
515   We make a number of intriguing observations, including that models exhibit a Generalization-Phase
516   and a Memorization-Phase which overlap, that learning by rote destroys generalization, but also that
517
      the inverse is true, and that entropy has a significant impact on the learnability of strings from a
      distribution; lower-entropy distributions are easier to learn by understanding, but higher-entropy
518
      strings are easier to learn by rote afterwards. Based on our results, we argue, that determining the
519
      degree of memorization is not possible by measuring only the performance on training strings, since
520   models can achieve the same level of loss on a dataset using both memorization and generalization.
521   Instead, measures of memorization must contrast the recollection performance on the training set
522   with that on an unseen test set from the same distribution.
523
      Our findings have significant implications for studies focusing on quantifying memorization, under-
524
      standing how memorization works, and estimating privacy risks with memorization. Furthermore,
525
      many of our empirical findings cannot be easily explained and the quest for a comprehensive ex-
526   planatory theory of all our findings raises many open and challenging questions.
527
528   Limitations: Our insights on memorization rely on synthetic data generated with formal grammars,
529
      and it is possible that some of the observations might change for real-world data. We also focus on
      observing what happens during memorization and generalization, and leave it up to future work to
530
      study the mechanisms responsible for the observed behavior.

689   Hierarchical Grammar. We consider a hierarchical grammar, which is an example of a proba-
690   bilistic context free grammar, as shown below. In the grammar, N = {S, A7, A8, . . . , A16} and
691   T = {1, 2, 3, . . . , 9}. The grammar has four levels of hierarchy: the non-terminals from top to
692
      bottom levels are {A16}, {A13, A14, A15}, {A10, A11, A12}, and {A7, A8, A9}, followed by ter-
      minals {1, 2, 3, . . . , 9}. Each non-terminal (except the start non-terminal) has two expansion rules,
693
      consisting of non-terminals from the immediate lower level. Further, the expansion rules are prob-
694
      abilistic, where the sum of probabilities of all expansion rules from a given non-terminal is 1. A
695   grammar can be balanced or skewed by varying the probability distribution of expansion rules. In
696   our experiments, we vary the skewness as p = {0.5, 0.75, 0.9}: the probabilities of the two expan-
697   sion rules are p and 1 − p, respectively. Intuitively, p = 0.5 generates strings of almost uniform
698   probability, while higher p results in skewing the probability mass to a subset of strings.
