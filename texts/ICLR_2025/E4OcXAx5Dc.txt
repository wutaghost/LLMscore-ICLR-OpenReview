011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            We study the classic problem of prediction with expert advice under the constraint
014
               of differential privacy (DP). In contrast to earlier work in this area, we are in-
               terested in distributed settings with no trusted central curator. In this context,
015
               we first show that a classical online learning algorithm naturally satisfies DP and
016
               then design two new algorithms that extend and improve it: (1) RW-AdaBatch,
017            which provides a novel form of privacy amplification at negligible utility cost,
018            and (2) RW-Meta, which improves utility on non-adversarial data with zero pri-
019            vacy cost. Our theoretical analysis is supported by an empirical evaluation using
020            real-world data reported by hospitals during the COVID-19 pandemic. RW-Meta
021            outperforms the classical baseline at predicting which hospitals will report a high
022            density of COVID-19 cases by a factor of more than 2× at realistic privacy levels.

      1     I NTRODUCTION
025
026
      Many practical algorithmic and organizational problems, from routing data to allocating scarce re-
027   sources, involve repeated predictions about the future state of a system. Prediction with expert
028   advice (Cesa-Bianchi & Lugosi, 2006) is a flexible framework developed in the online learning lit-
029   erature that can be used to model these problems as an iterative game between a player and nature:
030   at each time step t = 1, . . . , T , the player receives suggestions from each of n ‘experts’ and must
031   decide whose advice to follow. Nature then reveals the gain of each expert at that time step, and the
032   player receives a reward based on their actual choice. The player’s goal is to maximize their total
033   reward over the course of the entire game.
034   Over the past several decades, the field of online learning has developed many sophisticated algo-
035   rithms for this problem with impressive performance guarantees (Adamskiy et al., 2012; Luo &
036   Schapire, 2015; Korotin et al., 2020). In many applications, however, predictions are either implic-
037   itly or explicitly about human behavior. Optimizing purely for predictive accuracy in this context is
038   risky because the algorithm might inadvertently leak sensitive data through its outputs, analogous to
039   the well-understood privacy risks in batch learning (Shokri et al., 2017; Carlini et al., 2023).
040   To mitigate this issue, researchers have developed several experts algorithms that provably satisfy
041   differential privacy (DP), a popular method for controlling the information that a statistical algorithm
042   leaks about its input (Dwork et al., 2006). But despite strong theoretical results, these algorithms
043   rely on several common assumptions that limit their practical application. Our goal in this work is to
044   overcome this limitation by designing algorithms that rely on more general assumptions, allowing
045   them to be used in contexts where current state-of-the-art methods cannot be easily applied.
046
047   1.1   O UR C ONTRIBUTIONS
048
      Our main contribution is the design, analysis, and evaluation of two new algorithms for prediction
049
      with expert advice under local DP: RW-AdaBatch and RW-Meta. We describe the specific advan-
050
      tages of our approach below.
051
052   More realistic assumptions. Unlike prior work, our algorithms are compatible with the local model
053   of DP, which does not assume the existence of a trusted central data curator. This makes them appli-
      cable to settings like healthcare, transportation, or energy usage where predicting aggregate behavior

      is socially useful but individual-level records are often too sensitive to share. Also unlike existing
055   work, our algorithms are anytime (Dean & Boddy, 1988), meaning that they do not require advanced
056   knowledge of the input size to optimize parameters.
057
058
      Novel form of privacy amplification. The RW-AdaBatch algorithm offers amplified privacy guar-
      antees by adaptively batching incoming data points together as they arrive. This gives each of the
059
      batched points a “crowd” to hide in, improving the final privacy analysis of the algorithm’s outputs in
060
061
      a manner analogous to the celebrated shuffle model (Erlingsson et al., 2019). Our analysis leverages
      the theory of random walks to prove that this batching carries zero utility cost with high probability.
062
063   Private learning in dynamic environments. The RW-Meta algorithm leverages meta-learning to
064   greatly improve performance in dynamic environments at no additional privacy cost. The key chal-
065   lenge is that in the experts problem, it is possible to leak information through both our choice of
066   expert and the actual advice the chosen expert gives us. Prior work addresses this issue by implic-
067
      itly assuming that expert advice is independent of any sensitive data, but this is a severe limitation.
      To overcome this, we exploit correlated noise and linearity to simultaneously compute advice for
068
      many data-aware experts and the optimal choice of expert, all at a fixed privacy cost. To the best of
069
      our knowledge, the resulting private experts algorithm is the first that is capable of achieving high
070   absolute performance on inputs where the best action varies dynamically over time.
071
072   Empirical validation. We rigorously evaluate our algorithms’ performance using a combination of
073
      synthetic data and real-world data reported by hospitals during the COVID-19 pandemic. On the
      privacy side, we show that the the privacy amplification of RW-AdaBatch is substantial and only
074
      grows over time, corresponding e.g. to a nearly 10× improvement in the worst-case value of the
075
      privacy parameter δ when t = 10, 000. On the utility side, we find that RW-Meta outperforms the
076   classical algorithm by a factor of more than 2× at predicting which hospitals will report the highest
077   density of COVID-19 patients each week. It would be essentially impossible for any algorithm based
078   on data-independent experts to match the performance we observe.

081   2   R ELATED W ORK
082
083
      The study of online learning with DP was initiated in 2010 by the papers of Dwork et al. (2010) and
084   Chan et al. (2011), which examined the problem of privately updating a single counter. Later work
085   has gone on to apply their framework to more complicated statistics (Perrier et al., 2018; Bolot et al.,
086   2013; Wang et al., 2021). Jain et al. (2012) formalized the idea of private online learning through
087   the lens of convex optimization and designed algorithms for optimizing strongly convex functions
088   while preserving privacy. Subsequently, Thakurta & Smith (2013) extended the study of private
089   online learning to include the partial information or bandit setting, where the learner only observes
090   the reward for the specific action they chose, which has also been studied in Hannun et al. (2019)
091   and Azize & Basu (2024). Recently, considerable interest in private online convex optimization has
092
      been driven by machine learning where it is used to study the properties of DP-SGD and related
      algorithms (Kairouz et al., 2021; Choquette-Choo et al., 2023; 2024).
093
094   The works studying private prediction with expert advice that are most similar to ours are Asi et al.
095   (2023) and Agarwal & Singh (2017), which both fall under the central model of DP. We also note
096   that very recent parallel work by Gao et al. (2024) explores online prediction from experts in the
097   federated setting, where m clients all attempt to solve a single experts problem simultaneously with
098
      limited communication. Their Fed-DP-OPE-Stoch algorithm is the first experts algorithm that we
      are aware to explicitly satisfy local DP, and therefore shares motivation with our work. However,
099
      the federated version of the problem and the stochastic adversaries they consider lead to substantial
100
      technical differences between our results.
101
102   Like us, Asi et al. (2023) exploit the infrequent-switching behavior of a classical online learning
103   algorithm (Shrinking Dartboard, by Geulen et al. (2010)) to develop differentially private algorithms
104
      for prediction from expert advice. Their focus is on the high-dimensional regime, where the gap
      between local and central DP is most pronounced (Edmonds et al., 2019; Duchi et al., 2013).
105
106   The algorithms in Agarwal & Singh (2017) represent the state-of-the-art in the low-dimensional
107   regime that we target. All of the algorithms developed in that work rely on the binary tree aggrega-
      tion technique (Dwork et al., 2010; Chan et al., 2011), which can be used to simultaneously estimate


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      any partial sum of a sequence with O(log1.5 T ) noise scale while satisfying DP. While highly effi-
109   cient, this technique fundamentally requires the time horizon T to be known in advance, and also
110   assumes the existence of a central trusted data curator.
111
112
      Our work overcomes these limitations at the cost of a weaker worst-case dependence on the dimen-
      sion of the problem, which is a consequence of satisfying local vs. central DP (Duchi et al., 2013).
113
      We therefore emphasize that our goal is not to directly compete with these state-of-the-art algorithms
114
      in the settings they target (i.e. central DP, static data, known time horizon). Rather, we aim to design
115   algorithms with weaker starting assumptions that can perform well in settings that the SOTA cannot
116   be (easily) applied to (i.e. local DP, shifting data distributions, unknown time horizon).

121   3.1   N OTATION
122
      Given a vector v ∈ Rn , we denote its k th element with the subscript vk , and its k th smallest element
123
      using parentheses as in v(k) . We use the term ‘leader’ to refer to the argmax over the elements of
124   a vector v, and the term ‘gap’ to refer to the quantity v(n) − v(n−1) . For two random variables
125   X and Y , we say that X is stochastically larger than Y , denoted X ≥st Y , if for all x we have
126   P[X > x] ≥ P[Y > x]. We use Φ and φ to denote the standard Gaussian CDF and PDF respectively.
127
128   3.2   P REDICTION WITH E XPERT A DVICE
129
130   Prediction with Expert Advice can be thought of as a special case of online linear optimization
131   (Abernethy et al., 2014). At each time step t ∈ [T ], we choose an action xt from the action set
132   X , which in our case is the n-dimensional probability simplex. We then observe the gain vector
133   gt ∈ [0, 1]n and receive reward ⟨xt , gt ⟩. A typical goal is to minimize static regret, which is defined
134   as the difference between the reward we actually receive and the reward of the best single action in
                               PT                PT
135   hindsight, i.e. maxx∈X t=1 ⟨x, gt ⟩ − t=1 ⟨xt , gt ⟩.
136                                                                                          √
      Many algorithms are known which can achieve optimal static regret bounds of O( T log n) (Cesa-
137   Bianchi & Lugosi, 2006). Unlike statistical learning, these guarantees do not rest on any distribu-
138   tional assumptions and still hold even when the data is adversarially generated. In the oblivious
139   adversary model, the gain vectors are fixed in advance (with full knowledge of the algorithm), while
140   in the adaptive adversary model, incoming gain vectors are allowed to depend on our earlier deci-
141   sions. These models turn out to be equivalent in the non-private setting, but recent work has shown
142   that very private algorithms can be forced to incur O(T ) regret by adaptive adversaries (Asi et al.,
143   2023). We focus exclusively on oblivious adversaries in this work.
144
145   3.3   S TATIC VS . DYNAMIC E NVIRONMENTS
146
147
       The definition of static regret makes the most sense in contexts where sequential data points are
148
      roughly independent, e.g. because they are drawn i.i.d. from some fixed distribution or because they
149
      are truly adversarial. In practice, however, it is often the case that the best action can vary systemati-
150   cally over time, in which case tight bounds on static regret do not imply strong absolute performance.
151   To address this issue, the online learning community has developed many algorithms that achieve
152   tight bounds with respect to stronger notions of regret (Zinkevich, 2003; Hazan & Seshadhri, 2009;
153   Herbster & Warmuth, 1998), but no comparable line of work yet exists in the private online learning
154   literature. We will design (private) algorithms for both settings.
155

161   gain is defined in terms of local statistics of these datasets, i.e. gt = f (g(D1,t ), . . . , g(D|C|,t )). We

240   4.2   S TATIC E NVIRONMENTS : A DAPTIVE BATCHING
241
242   The RW-FTPL algorithm was not originally designed with privacy in mind. The authors’ primary
243   goal was to create a low-regret algorithm which changes its prediction only a small number of
244   times in expectation. Variations on this goal have been explored extensively in the online learning
245   literature (Kalai & Vempala, 2005; Geulen et al., 2010; Altschuler & Talwar, 2018), and Asi et al.
246   (2023) observe that there is a conceptual connection between privacy and limited switching, as both
247
      constraints limit the ways data can be used.
248   In this section, we develop this intuition by connecting the limited switching of RF-FTPL with the
249   idea of permutation-invariance, which is a critical tool in amplifying the central DP guarantees of lo-
250   cal DP algorithms (Erlingsson et al., 2019). The high level idea is that in some cases, we can reliably
251   predict that our algorithm will not switch predictions in the near future no matter what data it sees.
252   When this happens, we can safely batch together all incoming data for some time before updating
253   the algorithm’s internal state with all of the points simultaneously. This makes the algorithm fully
254   invariant to permutations within each batch, amplifying its central DP guarantee.

351   4.3   DYNAMIC E NVIRONMENTS : M ETA - LEARNING
352
353   Recall that algorithms which minimize static regret, including RW-FTPL, often struggle in settings
354   where the best action changes systematically over time. The RW-Meta algorithm we present in this
355   section partially addresses this issue by showing how a key design pattern in many non-private,
356   adaptive online learning algorithms can be efficiently made to satisfy DP. Specifically, RW-Meta
357   uses a multilayer structure with many candidate algorithms (learners) along with a central meta-
358
      learner whose job is to select the best learner to use at each time step. The selected learner then
      determines our final action as a function of the (noisy) data seen so far. Our main technical inno-
359
360
      vation lies in the careful use of correlated noise and linearity, which allows us deploy much more
361
      powerful learners than prior work at no additional privacy cost.
362   Utility Analysis. We formalize our learners as a set of functions f1 , . . . , fm : Rn,∞ → X . At
363   time step t, each learner makes a prediction xt,i = fi (g̃1 , . . . , g̃t−1 ), and the meta-learner chooses
364   jt ∈ [m]. The meta-learner then receives the same gain as the chosen learner, i.e. ⟨xt,jt , gt ⟩. Our
365   goal is to minimize the regret of the meta-learner with respect to the best single learner in hindsight.
366   The starting observation for our method is that the value ⟨xt,i , g̃t ⟩ is an unbiased estimate for the
367   gain of expert i at time t. Specifically, let Xt ∈ Rm×n be the matrix whose ith row is xt,i . Then we

468     5.2            P ERFORMANCE OF RW-M ETA
469
470     Methodology and Dataset. While prior work on prediction with expert advice has largely relied on
471
        synthetic data alone (Erven et al., 2011; Korotin et al., 2020; Jun et al., 2017; Gao et al., 2024), we
        evaluate the performance of RW-Meta with real-world data from the COVID-19 pandemic. Specifi-
472
        cally, we use data from the United States Department of Health and Human Services (DHHS, 2024)
473
        containing weekly reports from thousands of hospitals from mid-2020 through 2023. Our goal is to
474     predict which hospitals will report the highest density of COVID patients each week.
475
476     Following Altieri et al. (2021), we consider models that first forecast the exact proportion of COVID
477
        cases in each hospital and then make a final prediction by taking the argmax of their forecast. The
        gain of a learner is the true proportion of COVID patients in the hospital they select. Our evalu-
478
        ation uses 13 learners consisting of rolling Gaussian regression algorithms with window sizes of
479
        8/16/32/64 and weak/medium/strong regularization, as well as the basic RW-FTPL algorithm. We
480     focus solely on Gaussian regression models because they are a popular choice for medical forecast-
481     ing and their theoretical assumptions are a good match for the additive Gaussian noise we use to
482     protect privacy. The specific range of hyperparameters we consider was chosen manually through
483     exploratory analysis with a disjoint slice of the dataset, guided by the goal of maximizing variety
484     while excluding non-functional or redundant learners. We evaluate our algorithm on data from three
485     states (New Mexico, Pennsylvania, and California), which were chosen to cover a diverse range of
        population sizes while being geographically large enough to decorrelate different hospitals.


                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      We remove any hospitals that did not appear to participate in the DHHS’s data sharing program,
487   defined as reporting fewer than 100 suspected cases over the 3 year period. This left us with data
488   from 24, 146, and 293 hospitals in each state respectively, covering the 148 weeks between August
489   9th, 2020, and June 4th, 2023. We define adjacency to cover both whether an individual goes to
490   the hospital and the specific hospital they choose, and compute the sensitivity of the gain vectors
491   based on the minimum non-zero number of beds reported by any hospital at the given time step
492   (minimum 4.3, maximum 14.6). We evaluate our algorithm under four different privacy levels: no
493   privacy (µ = ∞), low privacy (µ = 1), medium privacy (µ = 0.5) and high privacy (µ = 0.25).
494   Finally, we compare RW-FTPL and RW-Meta against the state-of-the-art private FTPL algorithm of
495
      Agarwal & Singh (2017). This comparison must be heavily caveated because their algorithm only
496
      offers central DP guarantees and was not designed to operate in dynamic environments at all; we
497
      include these results not to demonstrate the superiority of one algorithm over the other, but to better
498
      contextualize the costs of satisfying local vs. central DP and the potential benefits of moving beyond
499
      the paradigm of data-independent experts. For fair comparison, we consider two possible strategies
500
      for setting the parameters of their algorithm: a greedy strategy that injects the minimum amount
501
      of noise required to still satisfy (central) µ-GDP, and a cautious strategy that optimizes worst-case
502
      bounds on regret (and therefore might add more noise than required at the given privacy level).
503
504   Results. We repeat our evaluation 100 times and report average results, which are visualized in
505   Figure 2 and summarized numerically in Table 1. Averages are reported alongside 95% confidence
506   intervals based on the central limit theorem with Bonferroni correction. Across all settings, we find
507   that RW-Meta significantly outperforms both RW-FTPL and the Min Regret variant. The Min Noise
508   variant performs substantially better on this dataset despite weaker theoretical bounds, which is facil-
509   itated by the fact that, as a central DP algorithm, it can more easily control the amount of noise used.
510   Nonetheless, it lags behind RW-Meta in all settings. This is because RW-Meta is able to consistently
511   achieve negative static regret at low and moderate privacy levels, which is essentially impossible to
512   match using the data-independent experts that are assumed in prior work. Meanwhile, when privacy
513   is high, binary tree aggregation is able to offer only modest improvements in noise scale at the time
514   horizon we consider. This is why why we see all three static experts algorithms clustering together
515   when µ = 0.25.
516
517   Separately, we find that RW-Meta performs around 90% as well as the best linear model in each
518   setting on average. Which exact linear model performs best varies considerably across settings,
519   however. For instance, the learner with window size 8 and strong regularization is the best perform-
520
      ing learner in the high privacy setting for the Pennsylvania dataset, but only middle of the pack for
      New Mexico (where the best learner instead has a window size of 64). Across different privacy lev-
521
      els the variation is even larger, with the best learners in the low/no privacy settings performing the
522
      worst in the high privacy setting. This heterogeneity highlights the appeal of using meta-learning,
523
      as it eliminates the need to risk committing to a single set of parameters in advance.

526   6   C ONCLUSION
527
528   We have presented two algorithms for the fundamental problem of prediction with expert advice,
529   both of which satisfy local DP. In static environments, our RW-AdaBatch algorithm is a costless
530   upgrade over the classical algorithm we build off of, improving the privacy of its outputs with prov-
531   ably insignificant impact on utility. In dynamic environments, our RW-Meta algorithm uses a novel,
532   privacy-preserving variant of a classic online learning technique to quickly adapt to shifts in the
533   input data, and we show that the resulting performance gains can be won at no additional privacy
534
      cost. Our analysis is supported by robust empirical evaluation, demonstrating that our algorithms
      are practical and can achieve high performance in a real-world, privacy-critical prediction task.

738
      The primary limitation of our work is that satisfying local DP sometimes requires adding unreason-
739
      ably large amounts of noise, particularly when gain vectors are high-dimensional. To guarantee low
740
      regret, our approach requires gain vectors with sensitivity smaller than their literal dimension, or
741
      else a silo-like setting where several sensitive values can be averaged locally before being sent to the
742
      algorithm, but this may not always be achievable in practice.

      A.1.2    E XTENSION TO M ORE C OMPLEX L EARNING P ROBLEMS
745
746   While the experts problem is flexible enough to represent many real world tasks, it is also interesting
747   from a theoretical perspective because it is one of the most fundamental problems in online learning.
748   This naturally raises the question of whether the methods developed in this work can be extended to
749   more complicated learning problems.
750
751
      With respect to metalearning, RW-Meta fundamentally requires a linear gain function to compute
752   unbiased estimates for the gain of each learner. So, while it can be extended to online linear opti-
753   mization problems beyond prediction with expert advice, it is not clear that the same method could
754   be generalized to the larger class of online convex optimization. On the other hand, it may be possi-
755   ble to extend RW-Meta to partial information settings such as linear bandits where it is possible to
      derive unbiased estimates of (linear functions of) the expected reward at each time step.


                                                        14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      Meanwhile, RW-AdaBatch relies on the fundamental property that the maximum of a linear function
757
      over the convex hull of a small number of extreme points is always attained at one of those points.
758
      It would therefore be difficult to extend it to settings like online linear optimization over the sphere
759
      where the action set lacks this structure. On the other hand, the same property holds for maximums
760
      of convex functions over convex hulls, and so it is possible that the ideas behind RW-AdaBatch
761
      could be extended to some instances of online convex optimization.

1053
1054   All experiments are carried out on a laptop running Ubuntu 22.04 with an intel i5-1135G7 CPU and
1055   16GB of RAM. We implement our algorithms using Python 3.10, numpy 2.0.0 (Harris et al., 2020),
1056

1057   Although RW-FTPL is very straightforward, Both RW-Meta and RW-AdaBatch include non-trivial
1058   computations as subroutines which must be implemented efficiently. We briefly describe our imple-
1059   mentation choices here.
1060   In the case of RW-Meta, we use the LOBPCG algorithm as implemented in scipy to find the leading
1061   eigenvalue of Σ∗t at each iteration, with the leading eigenvector from the previous round as an initial
1062   guess. We find that at the problem sizes we consider, this step is not a significant bottleneck (taking
1063   about 2ms per iteration), and we therefore do not pursue any further optimizations.

1066   point precision when computing extreme values of the Gaussian PDF/CDF, we use the mpmath
1067   library for this task, which supports arbitrary precision arithmetic and numerical integration. We

1069   the required value of β necessary to achieve a given failure probability δ. In practice, the discrete
1070   nature of the choice of batch size means that the small differences between the true inverse function
1071   and the interpolated approximation are insignificant, while the corresponding speed-up is dramatic.
