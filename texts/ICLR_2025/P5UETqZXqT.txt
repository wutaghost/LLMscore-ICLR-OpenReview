011                                                  A BSTRACT
012
013               The success of generative models has reached a unique threshold where their out-
014               puts are indistinguishable from real data, leading to the inevitable contamination of
015               future data collection pipelines with synthetic data. While their potential to gener-
016               ate infinite samples initially offers promise for reducing data collection costs and
017               addressing challenges in data-scarce fields, the severe degradation in performance
018               has been observed when iterative loops of training and generation occur—known
019               as “model collapse.” This paper explores a practical scenario in which a pretrained
020
                  text-to-image diffusion model is finetuned using synthetic images generated from
                  a previous iteration, a process we refer to as the “Chain of Diffusion.” We first
021
                  demonstrate the significant degradation in image qualities caused by this iterative
022
                  process and identify the key factor driving this decline through rigorous empirical
023               investigations. Drawing on an analogy between the Chain of Diffusion and biologi-
024               cal evolution, we then introduce a novel theoretical analysis based on quantitative
025               trait modeling. Our theoretical analysis aligns with empirical observations of the
026               generated images in the Chain of Diffusion. Finally, we propose Reusable Diffusion
027               Finetuning (ReDiFine), a simple yet effective strategy inspired by genetic muta-
028               tions. ReDiFine mitigates model collapse without requiring any hyperparameter
029               tuning, making it a plug-and-play solution for reusable image generation.

032    1       I NTRODUCTION
033
034    Can state-of-the-art AI models learn from their own outputs and improve themselves? As generative
035    AI models (e.g., GPT, Diffusion) now churn out uncountable synthetic texts and images, this question
036    piqued curiosity from many researchers in the past couple of years. While some show positive
037    results of self-improving (Huang et al., 2022; Gerstgrasser et al., 2024), most report an undesirable
038
      “model collapse”—a phenomenon where a model’s performance degrades when it goes through
       multiple cycles of training with the self-generated data (Bertrand et al., 2023; Gillman et al., 2024;
039
       Taori & Hashimoto, 2023; Shumailov et al., 2023; Dohmatob et al., 2024a; Fu et al., 2024; Marchi
040
       et al., 2024; Martı́nez et al., 2023b). When large language models (LLMs) are trained with their
041    own outputs, it begins to produce low-quality text that has a lot of repetitions (Dohmatob et al.,
042    2024b), and its linguistic diversity declines rapidly (Guo et al., 2024; Briesch et al., 2023); image
043    models also show quality degradation (Bohacek & Farid, 2023; Martı́nez et al., 2023a) and loss of
044    diversity (Alemohammad et al., 2023; Hataya et al., 2023).
045
      The goal of this paper is to investigate model collapse in the practical scenario of fine-tuning pre-
046
      trained text-to-image diffusion models. An end user of text-to-image diffusion models often wants to
047   fine-tune the latest model to generate images with a very specific style (e.g., creating characters in the
048   style of Pokémon). In fact, hundreds of new fine-tuned diffusion models are uploaded regularly on
049   platforms like CivitAI1 , each designed to produce different styles of images. When users scrape the
050   internet to collect images of the style they want, it becomes almost inevitable that synthetic images
051   will be included in their datasets. This is because the number of real images is limited, while synthetic
052   images can be generated in massive quantities and dominate online sources. As a result, users will
053

               https://civitai.com/

      feel compelled to include more synthetic images in their datasets to keep up with the data demands of
055   these ever-growing, data-hungry models.
056
057   To this end, we conduct a thorough investigation into how various hyperparameters commonly used
058
      during diffusion fine-tuning (e.g., learning rate, diffusion steps, prompts) impact model collapse.
      From our extensive empirical analysis, we make a crucial observation: the classifier-free guidance
059
      (CFG) scale is the most significant factor that influences the rate of model collapse. Moreover, we
060
      observe a fascinating phenomenon that the direction of degradation varies with different CFG values—
061   low CFG results in low-frequency degradation in images, while high CFG leads to high-frequency
062   degradation. The critical role of CFG in determining both the rate and direction of model collapse is
063   a novel insight previously unrecognized in the literature.
064
      To gain a deeper theoretical understanding of this phenomenon, we draw upon the concept of
065
      quantitative trait modeling from statistical genetics. Unlike existing studies that attribute model
066
      collapse to limited sample sizes and conclude it leads to zero variance (Alemohammad et al.,
067   2023; Bertrand et al., 2023; Shumailov et al., 2023), we propose that the underlying cause is a
068   truncation-based selection process modulated by CFG. This theoretical model accurately describes
069   our experimental observations. Moreover, we show that other theoretical work on model collapse can
070   also be connected to statistical genetics models, such as random genetic drift and its variants (Fisher,
071   1999; Wright, 1931; Kimura, 1955; Paris et al., 2019). This fresh angle of drawing parallels with
072   statistical genetics offers a new framework to understand the mechanism of model collapse.
073   Finally, inspired by our empirical results and theoretical insights, we introduce the Reusable Diffusion
074   Finetuning (ReDiFine) method. Our experiments show that selecting an appropriate CFG can
075   significantly slow down model collapse. However, finding the optimal CFG is computationally
076   expensive, requiring numerous iterations of finetuning. To address this, we propose ReDiFine as a
077   plug-and-play solution that achieves a comparable reduction in collapse rate without the need for any
078   hyperparameter tuning. The default ReDiFine setting effectively mitigates model collapse across all
079   four datasets we tested. Moreover, ReDiFine is effective at mitigating model collapse not only in a
080   worst-case scenario of having fully-synthetic dataset, but also in a more practical case, where we have
081
      a mix of real and synthetic images the training set. Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
082   • We performed thorough and extensive empirical investigations into model collapse when we
083     finetune a diffusion model with diffusion-generated images. We tested an exhaustive list of
084     experimental parameters on four datasets (2 digital art & 2 natural images) to identify what affects
085     model collapse. Our analysis reveals that CFG scale is the most influential factor that not only
086     controls the rate of model collapse but also dictates the type of image degradation (Section 3).
087   • We provide a novel theoretical analysis of model collapse based on quantitative trait modeling that
088     can accurately predict how power spectra of generated images evolve over iterations (Section 4).
089   • We propose a simple yet effective strategy to mitigate model collapse, named ReDiFine, which
090     combines condition drop finetuning and CFG scheduling. Across all four datasets, ReDiFine
091     successfully generates more “reusable” images, which can be reused to finetune a diffusion model
092     without causing a severe model collapse (Section 5).

096   The self-consuming training loop and the associated phenomenon known as “model collapse” have
097   become significant areas of study in the past two years (Martı́nez et al., 2023a;b; Taori & Hashimoto,
098   2023; Alemohammad et al., 2023; Bohacek & Farid, 2023; Guo et al., 2024; Bertrand et al., 2023;
099   Dohmatob et al., 2024a; Briesch et al., 2023; Gillman et al., 2024; Fu et al., 2024; Marchi et al.,
100   2024). Model collapse, defined as “a degenerative process affecting generations of learned generative
101   models, where generated data end up polluting the training set of the next generation of models” in
102   Shumailov et al. (2023), has been observed in both language and image generative models.
103   Empirical studies on LLMs (Briesch et al., 2023) reveal that linguistic diversity collapses, especially
104   in high-entropy tasks (Guo et al., 2024), although this can be mitigated with data accumulation (Ger-
105   stgrasser et al., 2024). In image generation, several works (Martı́nez et al., 2023a;b; Alemohammad
106   et al., 2023; Hataya et al., 2023; Bohacek & Farid, 2023; Bertrand et al., 2023) note image degrada-
107   tion when diffusion models are recursively trained with self-generated data. We conduct extensive
      empirical experiments to reveal the most significant factor causing model collapse in text-to-image

      Theoretical studies on model collapse have largely focused on the reduction of diversity, typically
      framed as either decreasing covariance in continuous domains (Alemohammad et al., 2023; Shumailov
132
      et al., 2023; Bertrand et al., 2023) or shrinking support in discrete domain (Dohmatob et al., 2024b;
133
      Marchi et al., 2024). The finite number of generated samples per iteration has been identified as
134   a key cause of model collapse by several authors (Bertrand et al., 2023; Shumailov et al., 2023;
135   2024; Dohmatob et al., 2024b; Fu et al., 2024), while others (Alemohammad et al., 2023; Ferbach
136   et al., 2024; Marchi et al., 2024) emphasize that sampling bias reduces the generative model’s
137   effective distribution. In contrast, we present a novel theoretical framework based on quantitative
138   trait modeling, shifting the focus from variance reduction to mean drift induced by the selection
139   process. We further demonstrate that many existing theories of model collapse can be understood as
140   extensions of classical results from statistical genetics.
141   Regarding mitigation strategies for model collapse, existing works echo the importance of incorporat-
142   ing a large proportion of real data throughout the training loop (Alemohammad et al., 2023; Bertrand
143   et al., 2023; Fu et al., 2024; Ferbach et al., 2024) or accumulating additional data (Gerstgrasser et al.,
144   2024). The only mitigation strategy beyond altering the training data composition is proposed by
145   Gillman et al. (2024), who suggests a self-correcting self-consuming loop using an expert model to
146   correct synthetic outputs. While this approach is demonstrated in human motion generation with a
147   physics simulator, having an expert model may not be feasible and too costly for many real-world
148
      applications. In our work, we propose an alternative solution through reusable image generation, and
      show that it can mitigate model collapse more effectively than mixing more real data.

239   3.2     M ODEL C OLLAPSE IN THE C HAIN OF D IFFUSION
240
241   In this section, we make a series of observations regarding the model collapse behavior in the Chain
242
      of Diffusion. We conduct extensive investigations to reveal the most impactful factor in the model
      collapse and analyze how this factor contributes to the Chain of Diffusion.
243
244
      Observation 1: Model collapse is universal in the Chain of Diffusion. We observe significant
245
      image degradation in all four datasets (see Figure 1b) in the Chain of Diffusion. The quality of
246
      generated images begins to clearly deteriorate in the third iteration and it drops even more rapidly
247   once the visible degradation emerges, reaching an unrecognizable level in two or three additional
248   iterations. We investigated a variety of different scenarios (summarized in Figure 2) to see if this
249   degradation is an anomaly of specific hyperparameter settings or if it is a ubiquitous phenomenon. We
250   tested various dataset sizes for D0 and Dk , increasing the size of synthetic datasets Dk by generating
251   more than one image per prompt, mixing real images from D0 to Dk , changing the descriptiveness
252   of prompts, freezing U-Net or text encoder during finetuning, and various other hyperparameters
253   (# sampling steps, # epochs, learning rate, and CLIP skip). We also tested adding a small Gaussian
254   noise in each image in the original set D0 to see if having small random perturbations can improve
255
      reusability, and investigated if the degradation happens for larger Stable Diffusion. In all settings we
      tested, image degradation was universally present and very fast. We plot the results for Pokemon on
256
      Figure 2 where y-axis is collapse rate and x-axis is the FID1 (better when closer to the origin). While
257
      adding noise to images and mixing 90% real images to every iteration as proposed by Alemohammad
258   et al. (2023); Bertrand et al. (2023); Fu et al. (2024); Ferbach et al. (2024); Gerstgrasser et al. (2024)
259   show the lowest collapse rate, they still exhibit significant degradation as shown in quantitative values
260   (FID score has been doubled in 6 iterations). Visual inspection for images is provided in Appendix C.
261
262   Observation 2: CFG is the most significant factor that impacts the model collapse. Throughout
263   all our experiments, classifier-free guidance (CFG) had the biggest impact in the speed of model
264   collapse. CFG scale was first introduced in Ho & Salimans (2022) to modulate the strength between
265   unconditional and conditional scores at each diffusion step as follows:
266
            Total Score = Unconditional Score + CFG · (Conditional Score − Unconditional Score).            (4)
267
268   High CFG means that we emphasize the conditional score for the given prompt more, which pushes
269   the generation to align better with the prompt and often leads to higher-fidelity images. On the other
      hand, lower CFG places less weight on the conditional score and provides more diversity in generated

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      images. For those familiar with temperature sampling (Ackley et al., 1985), CFG plays a similar role
271   as temperature, which adjusts the trade-off between fidelity and diversity.
272
273   In Figure 2, we observe that as we increase the CFG scale, the image quality in the first iteration
274
      improves (smaller FID1 on x-axis), which is expected from our understanding of CFG. A more
      surprising part is that this comes at the cost of a worse collapse rate (an increase on the y-axis). Also,
275
      when the CFG scale is as high as 7.5 or 10.0, the improvement in FID1 plateaus, and increased CFG
276
      worsens both FID1 and collapse rate. Similarly, when the CFG scale is too low—below 2.0—the
277   improvement in collapse rate plateaus and both FID1 and collapse rate begin to increase. There is an
278   optimal region of CFG values (near 2.5, specific to Pokemon), where we achieve low collapse rate
279   while maintaining a good quality in the first iteration as well. Moreover, Figure 3c presents FID for
280   Pokemon to demonstrate how different CFG scales affect the performance. CFG scale 2.5 achieves
281   the most robust performance for all metrics for Pokemon. Interestingly, optimal CFG scales differ for
282   different styles: 2.5 for animated or hand-writing datasets (Pokemon and Kumapi) and 1.5 for photo
283   datasets (CelebA and Butterfly) as shown in Appendix B.
284
285   Observation 3: High CFG scales cause high-frequency degradation and low CFG scales cause
286   low-frequency degradation. CFG scale does not only affect the speed of model collapse, but also
287   the pattern of model collapse. As shown in Figure 3a, CFG 1.0 makes the images progressively
288
      more blurry in the Chain of Diffusion, eventually collapsing to images without any structure, which
      we refer to low-frequency degradation. On the other hand, for CFG 7.5 how images degrade looks
289
      completely different: some features start to be emphasized excessively, repetitive patterns begin to
290
      appear, and the overall color distribution becomes saturated. The t-SNE plot in Figure 3b clearly
291   demonstrates that the distribution shift over iterations follows distinct paths for high, low, and medium
292   CFG scales. These patterns were consistent in all four datasets and detailed results can be found
293   in Appendix B. In Section 4, we detail how different patterns of power spectra in high-frequency
294   regions from different CFG scales can be understood using the framework from genetic biology.
295
      Implications of our observations. Our extensive investigations show that a high CFG of 7.5, a
296
      common choice to generate visually appealing images, significantly increases collapse rate to achieve
297   slightly better FID1 . Sampling for maximizing the perceptual quality was coined as ‘sampling
298   bias’ in Alemohammad et al. (2023). While they reported a monotonic increase in collapse rate as
299   CFG increased from 1.0 to 2.0, we show that the holistic picture is not entirely monotonic when
300   we look at a wider range of CFG scales from 1.0 to 10.0. It shows an intriguing trade-off between
301   perceptual quality and reusability. This suggests that developers concerning reusability of images can
302   substantially improve future generations by carefully choosing CFG.

      This section introduces a novel perspective to understand the model collapse in generative models by
307
      drawing parallels between genetic biology and the Chain of Diffusion. Distinguishable iterations in
308   the Chain of Diffusion—where each iteration is clearly separable with no duplicated individual, and
309   the current iteration originates from the previous one—mirror genetic processes involving successive
310   iterations of parents and offspring. By applying quantitative trait modeling from statistical genetics,
311   we provide a framework to describe how images evolve across iterations in the Chain of Diffusion.
312
      We begin by introducing quantitative trait modeling and its underlying mathematical assumptions.
313
      Leveraging these assumptions, we derive a theorem showing that the mean trait value exhibits
314   linear drift, while the variance stabilizes over time. We then show that this model can successfully
315   capture the three key behaviors observed in Section 3: high-frequency degradation, low-frequency
316   degradation, and optimal CFG scale performance. This suggests that different CFG scales in the
317   Chain of Diffusion can be viewed as varying selection strategies, with power spectra corresponding
318   to quantitative traits. We use the term “iteration” instead of “generation” for genetic generation to
319   avoid confusion with image generation.

372   4.2   E XPLAINING THE C HAIN OF D IFFUSION WITH Q UANTITATIVE T RAIT M ODELING

393   4.3    S TATISTICAL G ENETICS AS A U NIFYING L ENS TO M ODEL C OLLAPSE
394
395   Quantitative trait modeling, a tool borrowed from statistical genetics, serves as a solid theoretical
396   framework for interpreting our experimental results. As genetic processes and self-consuming training
397
      of generative models share a lot of similarities, we find that other concepts in mathematical genetics
      also have close ties to existing theoretical work on model collapse. For instance, the traditional
398
      Wright-Fisher model (Wright, 1931) describes how traits evolve from one generation to the next in
399
      a finite population. Due to randomness in the sampling process, the composition of traits within a
400   finite population drifts over time and eventually collapses to a single phenotype. The Wright-Fisher
401   model and its continuous variants (Tataru et al., 2017) are exactly equivalent to the collapse behavior
402   modeled with simple categorical or Gaussian distributions in Alemohammad et al. (2023); Bertrand
403   et al. (2023); Shumailov et al. (2023; 2024). Given its widespread use in genetics, many extensions
404   of the Wright-Fisher model continue to be an active area of research, such as including different
405   mechanisms of selection (He et al., 2017; Kaj et al., 2024) or mutations (Charlesworth, 2020). Beyond
406   the current work and existing theoretical studies on model collapse, we believe that statistical genetics
407   offers a unified perspective to understand model collapse by reducing the complex dynamics of the
408
      self-consuming loop of generative model training to a small number of parameters, from which we
      can gain valuable insights.

411   5     R EUSABLE I MAGE G ENERATION WITH R E D I F INE
412
413   In the previous sections, we have discovered a significant role of CFG in model collapse and that
414   a good choice of CFG can mitigate the collapse effectively. However, the optimal CFG value that
415   minimizes the collapse rate is different for each dataset (e.g., CFG 1.5 for CelebA, CFG 2.5 for
416   Pokemon). There is no efficient way of finding an optimal CFG other than performing a grid search
417   over a wide range of values and iteratively fine-tuning the diffusion model to evaluate the collapse
418   rate for each configuration. In practical scenarios, it is unlikely that end users who are not machine
419
      learning experts will go through such a process during finetuning, just to prevent a potential model
      collapse that can happen in the next generation. This raises the question: How can we design a
420
      finetuning and generation strategy that is user-friendly and can slow down model collapse effectively?
421
422   To address this question, we again draw inspiration from the evolution process in nature. In biology,
423   mutations naturally counteract genetic drift and preserve diversity. Furthermore, selection in nature is
424   often a soft process rather than a hard truncation, as illustrated in Figure 4a. This soft selection allows
425
      for the inclusion of outliers, thereby maintaining the overall genetic diversity. We connect these
      biological inspirations with two strategies: condition drop finetuning to include more randomness and
426
      CFG scheduling during generation to transform hard selection to a softer one6 . We propose Reusable
427
      Diffusion Finetuning (ReDiFine) method that incorporates these two ideas and show that it is highly
428   effective at mitigating model collapse without the need for any finetuning.

459   Condition drop finetuning. We introduce condition drop finetuning, which randomly drops the text
460   condition during finetuning to update both the conditional and unconditional scores. Although condi-
461   tion drop was suggested in the original CFG paper (Ho & Salimans, 2022), it is not a common practice

464
      accumulate over multiple iterations, leading to a significant gap as the Chain of Diffusion progresses.
      On the other hand, condition drop finetuning with drop probability 0.2 preserves the norm of Diff
465
      over the iterations (see Figure 38b).
466
467   CFG Scheduling. We propose gradually reducing the CFG scale during diffusion steps to mitigate
468   the impacts of overemphasizing the conditional score in later stages, which can lead to high-frequency
469   degradation. Specifically, we exponentially decrease the CFG scale s during T diffusion steps as
470   s = s0 × e−α×t/T , where s0 is the initial CFG scale and α is the rate of exponential decay. This
471
      scheduling approach is consistent with findings by Balaji et al. (2022), which suggest that different
      diffusion steps contribute uniquely to the generation process. High CFG lets us capture high-level
472
      semantic information accurately during the initial diffusion steps while lower CFG in later steps can
473
      prevent generating unnecessary high-frequency details.

527   6    C ONCLUSION
528
529   The influx of AI-generated data into the world is inevitable and training sets that consist of synthetic
530   data will be part of the AI development pipeline. In this paper, we empirically and theoretically
531   studied the scenario of finetuning a model with its own generated data, where a gradual degradation
532   called “model collapse” happens. We (1) identify the most impactful factor through comprehensive
533   empirical investigations, (2) develop a novel theoretical perspective inspired by statistical genetics
534   to explain model collapse, and (3) propose an effective mitigation strategy that generates reusable
535   images for future training.
536   We started this paper with a question: can current AI models learn from their own output and improve
537   themselves? Our paper shows a glimpse that widely-used text-to-image models are not ready to
538   improve from their own creation quite yet. While we presented one solution focused on generating
539   reusable data, many open directions remain, such as developing algorithms that can distinguish
      between real and synthetic data and apply different learning techniques accordingly.

1480
       We conducted an ablation study to understand how the probability of dropping text embedding during
1481
       finetuning affects the image quality in the Chain of Diffusion. We examine 0.1, 0.2, and 0.4 as
1482
       Stable Diffusion is trained using 0.1 or 0.2. For both Pokemon and CelebA-1k datasets, a probability
1483   of 0.2 works the best, as shown in Figure 34 and Figure 35, respectively. Interestingly, condition
1484   drop finetuning helps to mitigate the color saturation problem, but its effect decreases with a higher
1485   probability. For both of these datasets, condition drop finetuning can mitigate image degradation to
1486   some degree, but still, there is a large quality degradation that needs to be improved.
