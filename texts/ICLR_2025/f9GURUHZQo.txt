009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Computer system workload traces, which record hardware or software events during
012            application execution, are essential for understanding the behavior of complex
013            systems and managing their processing and memory resources. However, obtaining
014            real-world traces can be challenging due to the significant collection overheads in
015
               performance and privacy concerns that arise in proprietary systems. As a result,
               synthetic trace generation is considered a promising alternative to using traces
016
               collected in real-world production deployments. This paper proposes to train a
017
               large language model (LLM) to generate synthetic workload traces, specifically
018            microservice call graphs. To capture complex and arbitrary hierarchical structures
019            and implicit constraints in such traces, we fine-tune LLMs to generate each layer
020            recursively, making call graph generation a sequence of easier steps. To further
021            enforce learning constraints in traces and generate uncommon situations, we apply
022            additional instruction tuning steps to align our model with the desired trace features.
023            Our evaluation results show that our model can generate diverse realistic traces
024            under various conditions and outperform existing methods in accuracy and validity.
025            We show that our synthetically generated traces can effectively substitute real-world
026
               data in optimizing or tuning systems management tasks. We also show that our
               model can be adapted to perform key downstream trace-related tasks, specifically,
027
               predicting key trace features and infilling missing data given partial traces.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Computer system workload traces document hardware or software events that occur as applications
      execute on computing machines, receive requests, process them, and serve responses. Such traces are
034
      vital for analyzing complex computer systems and optimizing their CPU, memory, IO and networking
035
      resource allocation and management. However, obtaining real-world traces is often hindered by
036   privacy concerns and their general unavailability. As an alternative, synthetic traces provide limitless
037   size and variety, offering significant advantages for testing and analysis, including the ability to
038   simulate challenging conditions like stress-testing environments. While recent advances in generative
039   machine learning, including LSTMs (Sherstinsky, 2020), GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2014), and
040   diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020), have improved synthetic trace generation, these methods typically
041   only generate specific fields, such as the number of requests or resource types (Bergsma et al., 2021),
042   or are confined to fixed-structure traces, like network packets (Jiang et al., 2023; Yin et al., 2022).
043   In this paper, we show how to use large language models (LLMs), transformer-based (Vaswani et al.,
044   2017) neural networks pre-trained autoregressively on large and diverse text datasets (Brown et al.,
045   2020; Touvron et al., 2023), to generate synthetic workload traces. It has been shown that LLMs can
046   be readily adapted to model a variety of domains besides natural language, such as protein sequences
047   (Shen et al., 2024), code (Roziere et al., 2023), and tabular data (Borisov et al., 2023). LLMs can
048   produce outputs that are well-aligned with user inputs in several flexible ways such as fine-tuning
049   model weights (Ouyang et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2021), and can generalize to new user inputs at
050
      inference (Chung et al., 2022; Sanh et al., 2021). Thus, LLMs have the potential to generate synthetic
      traces that accurately model the structure of real-world traces while following user prompts.
051
052   Despite their potential, using LLMs for synthetic trace generation presents significant challenges.
053   Traces are often logged in a tabular format and follow an underlying structure such as a graph, where
      the graph can be arbitrarily deep and wide. This means that it is non-trivial to represent valid traces as

074   trace generation. One of our key innovations is to generate call graphs with structural constraints
075   by recursively generating subgraphs, or layers. This approach allows the model to break down the
076   complex task of reasoning about hierarchical graph structures and complex constraints into multiple
077   easier tasks. To further enhance the model’s ability to follow structural constraints and to meet
078   user-requested attributes, we perform instruction tuning. During this phase, the model learns to
079

      performing simple arithmetic and logical checks to ensure adherence to the desired structure.

081   We demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach by fine-tuning Llama-2 7B (Touvron et al., 2023)
082   with our method on microservice trace data and performing a series of evaluations. The results show
083   that the proposed recursive generation and use of intermediate instructions significantly enhance
084   the model’s ability to generate valid outputs for complex (i.e., deep and wide) call graph structures.
085
      When compared to traces from learned generative models and a probabilistic model, synthetic traces
      produced by our model more closely match the distribution of real traces. We further show that
086
      our fine-tuned model performs well when used for downstream tasks for trace feature prediction
087
      compared to Llama-3.1 405B, one of the state-of-the-art LLMs.
088
089   We summarize our key contributions below:
090   • We introduce a novel method for creating valid and rich synthetic microservice traces using LLMs:
091     (1) We recursively generate layers of subgraphs along with prompts for subsequent layers and (2)
092     We also align the LLM to describe the generated layers with intermediate instructions.
093   • We show that recursive generation and intermediate instructions improve the validity of synthetic
094     traces. Also, synthetic traces generated by our model are more realistic regarding distribution
095     similarities than those from a baseline generative model and a handcrafted expert model.
096   • We demonstrate that synthetic traces can effectively substitute real data for training microservice
097     management tasks. Furthermore, our model generalizes to unseen user-requested attributes during
098     inference and can be further adapted for downstream tasks like infilling missing trace data.

100   2    BACKGROUND
101
102   Microservice Call Graphs. In modern software architecture, an application is typically constructed
103   as a constellation of multiple microservices (Gan et al., 2019; Luo et al., 2022; Huye et al., 2023),
104   each with specific functionalities and dependencies on one another. When users interact with
105   these applications, for instance, by sending HTTP requests to web servers, a complex sequence of
106   communications among these microservices is triggered. Thus, a user request induces a microservice
107   call graph, which maps the control/data flow and dependencies among the microservices involved in
      fulfilling the user’s request.


                                                                   2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Figure 1 is an example of a social network application deployed with several microservices (8 in total).
109   In the figure, the red arrows indicate communications between microservices involved in processing
110   the user’s request. The request is sent to a microservice (e.g., “Front end” in Figure 1) and waits for
111   the communication to terminate. If the microservice requires additional communication to handle
112   the request, then it triggers another microservice call (e.g., from “Front end” to “Authentication”
113   in Figure 1). These communications triggered by a user’s request form a microservice call graph with
114   four microservices. The vertices of the graph correspond to microservices (or the client), while the
115   edges correspond to API calls invoking the microservices. Note that some edges are not part of the
116   call graph as the corresponding microservices are not invoked in processing this particular request.
117   Each call graph can be represented as a tabular log trace with a textual description of the features
118   of each API call (i.e., edges), including the source and destination of the request, type of request
119   (e.g., HTTP and RPC), and start/finish time. As call graphs have a hierarchical structure, the tabular
120   trace should preserve the parent-child relationships by ensuring that the child’s source matches the
121   parent’s destination. Moreover, the start and end times of each call should be consistent with each
122   other: (1) the start time of a microservice must be earlier than its finish time, and (2) the parent-child
123
      relationships must be honored, i.e., the parent’s start time must precede the child’s, and parent’s finish
      time must follow the child’s. Finally, the IDs within a call graph (dot-decimal numbers provided for
124
      each call) must also be hierarchically connected to form a DAG structure.
125
126   Synthetic Trace Generation using Machine Learning. The analysis of microservice traces plays
127   a pivotal role in improving the performance and reliability of services, and guides techniques that
128   enable high-performance and efficient use of the underlying machines. Representative use cases
129
      include critical path analysis (Zhang et al., 2022), anomaly detection (Xie et al., 2023), root cause
      analysis (Ikram et al., 2022), cluster management (Qiu et al., 2020), and cluster scheduling (Singhvi
130
      et al., 2021). Unfortunately, access to traces remains challenging due to business and privacy concerns.
131
132   Given the importance and limited availability of public computer system traces, including microser-
133   vice traces, several recent studies have explored generative models for synthetic trace generation.
134   Existing works (Lin et al., 2020; Jiang et al., 2023) leverage GAN (Goodfellow et al., 2014) and
135
      diffusion (Ho et al., 2020) models to generate network packet traces, while other work (Bergsma
      et al., 2021) uses LSTMs (Sherstinsky, 2020) to generate virtual machine workload traces. Even
136
      though the generative models have shown effectiveness in each domain, the methods are used only
137
      for predicting specific fields or following training data distributions without conforming to structural
138   constraints. These methods do not apply to microservice call graphs because they cannot handle the
139   hierarchical structures of the call graphs.
140
141
      Since traces have specific structures that can be represented in tabular form, machine learning
      methods for synthetic tabular data generation could be applied to synthetic trace generation. Recent
142
      approaches, such as TVAE (Xu et al., 2019) and GReaT (Borisov et al., 2023), leverage VAE (Kingma
143
      & Welling, 2013) and language models to advance synthetic tabular data generation techniques.
144   However, these methods have limitations when applied to microservice traces, as they do not account
145   for the hierarchical structure of call graphs within tabular representations. We provide a detailed
146   comparison with tabular data generation methods in §4.

150   3    T RAINING LLM S TO G ENERATE M ICROSERVICE T RACES
151
152
153
      Our goal is to train a generative model for microservice call graph traces. We want to allow end-
      users to simulate various scenarios, such as stress-testing a novel software application or feature,
154
      by conditioning the model’s output on user-requested attributes including the application being
155
      invoked, the number of microservice communications (i.e., graph edges), and the overall latency
156   of the application. Given the limitations of existing trace generation approaches, we turn to LLMs,
157   which are transformer-based (Vaswani et al., 2017) models with billions of parameters. We initialize
158   our model from a general-purpose LLM pre-trained on a large and diverse text dataset, as these
159   models have shown effectiveness when adapted for specialized domains such as proteins (Shen
160   et al., 2024) and code (Roziere et al., 2023). In addition, LLMs can be conditioned in a variety of
161   arbitrary manners, including natural language prompting (Ouyang et al., 2022) and structured input
      sequences (Borisov et al., 2023).

      This section presents our approach for training an LLM to generate microservice call graphs. We train
180   our model in two stages. In the first stage, we pre-train the model to learn the complex interactions
181   between the vertices and edges in real call graph data. We describe how we encode call graphs, stored
182   as tabular data, into a text format that can be tokenized and processed by the LLM. Then, we detail a
183   novel approach to improve the model’s generation of large, complex graph structures. We propose to
184   decompose the graph generation task into a series of simpler, recursive subgraph generation tasks
185   that allow the model to reason about local features while respecting global structure. In the second
186   training stage, we fine-tune the model to follow user instruction and allow flexible generation of call
187   graphs with desired attributes. During instruction tuning, we propose to include a series of natural
188
      language reasoning steps that reinforce the model’s ability to adhere to constraints during inference.
189
190   3.1     P RE - TRAINING
191
192   We pre-train our model on call graphs using an autoregressive language modeling objective. This
193   stage adapts the general-purpose LLM, which was previously trained to model natural language text
194   sequences, to the more specialized domain of microservice call graphs.
195
196   3.1.1      E NCODING C ALL G RAPHS AS T EXT
197
198   LLMs expect sequences of text as input, so we must encode our dataset of call graphs into text-based
199   representations before training our model. As detailed in §2 and shown in Figure 1, microservice
200   call graphs are initially stored as tables. Rows represent edges (i.e., communications between
201
      microservices), while columns describe features for each edge. We follow the method proposed
      by GReaT (Borisov et al., 2023) and encode features in a natural language format, which requires
202
      minimal pre-processing and allows us to exploit the LLM’s pre-training on diverse, natural texts. Our
203
      lossless encoding procedure preserves all necessary information to recover the unique graph that
204   produced the tabular data. Besides edge features, we also encode global attributes of the call graph to
205   serve as conditioning information for the model.
206
207
      Tabular call graph X has m columns of features with textual names {f1 , f2 , . . . , fm } and n rows of
      edges {x1 , x2 , . . . , xn }. We denote the value of feature j ∈ {1, . . . , m} for edge i ∈ {1, . . . , n} as
208
      vij . We encode each edge xi as a text sequence ti = [ti1 , ti2 , . . . , tim ], where tij is a description
209
      of the j-th feature with the format tij = [ϕ(fj ), vij ]. Here, ϕ(f ) encodes feature name f into a
210   text template with a subject-predicate structure to provide a natural language description of feature
211   value vij . For instance, the encoding for edge 1 in Figure 1 would be [Edge ID is 0, Source
212   is Client, Destination is Front end, Type is HTTP, Communication
213   starts at 0 ms, Communication finishes at 24 ms]. We encode tabular call
214   graph X to the equivalent text-based representation t = [t1 , t2 , . . . , tn ], formed as a sequence of the
215   text-encoded edges ti . We note that the structure and constraints of the call graph only depend on the
      feature values and are invariant to the specific feature ordering imposed by the columns of the tabular

      data. Therefore, during training, we randomly shuffle the order of the features within each edge as in
217   Borisov et al. (2023) to remove any spurious associations that arise from position information.
218
219   Apart from individual edges, the overall call graph can also be described by attributes, including the
220
      maximum depth, the total number of edges, and the total communication latency. These attributes
      are useful for summarizing the complex interactions between edges and can be fed to the model
221
      as a prompt to condition call graph generation. Let call graph X have r attributes with names
222
      {a1 , a2 , . . . , ar } and corresponding values {w1 , w2 , . . . , wr }. We encode the attributes as a text
223   sequence c = [c1 , c2 , . . . , cr ], where cj is a description of the j-th attribute with the format cj =
224   [aj , “ : ”, wj ]. See the Conditions shown in red in Figure 2 for a simplified example of text-encoded
225   call graph attributes. We include the attributes at the start of each text-encoded call graph sequence
226   and predict them along with the edge tokens during pre-training. Similar to the edge features, we
227   randomly shuffle the order of graph attributes during training. We additionally drop each attribute
228   independently with probability pdrop to allow flexible prompting with arbitrary subsets of attributes.
229
230   3.1.2    R ECURSIVE G ENERATION
231
      We propose to break down the task of generating a call graph into a series of recursive layer generation
232
      tasks to handle complex structures. Starting from the initial attributes, or prompt c, the task for the
233   model at each layer is to generate the edges originating from the Start Node specified in the prompt.
234   The model also generates a new prompt for the next layer based on the previous layer prompt and the
235   edges generated in the current layer. This new prompt is then re-used to condition the model’s output
236   for the next layer. The recursive process continues until the requested attributes c are satisfied.
237
      Formally, for an encoded call graph t = [t1 , t2 , . . . , tn ], we partition the edges ti into a sequence of
238
      layers [t1 , t2 , . . . , tl ], where l ≤ n. Each layer is comprised of a sequence of edges that share the
239   same parent (i.e., source) node, and no two edges are shared by layers. For call graph conditions c
240   that describe t, we introduce layer conditions cj , j ∈ {1, 2, . . . , l + 1}. Layer condition cj encodes
241   the attributes of the remaining portion of the call graph after the sequence of layers [t1 , t2 , . . . , tj−1 ]
242   has been generated, and we define c1 := c and cl+1 := ∅. We decompose the conditional call graph
243   distribution as a chain of conditional layer distributions:

      3.2     I NSTRUCTION T UNING
253
254   We perform supervised fine-tuning after pre-training to improve the model’s ability to generate call
255   graphs following user instructions. Different from pre-training, we do not calculate loss for the
256   model on the initial call graph attributes c (equivalent to the first layer conditions c1 ), which are
257   now treated as a fixed prompt. The user can supply additional natural language instructions for the
258   model, and in §4.4, we provide results for two types of additional instruction. We further supplement
259   the instructions with additional prompts, which can be programmatically generated from a template
260   based on the user-requested attributes, to aid the model’s reasoning abilities, as detailed in §A.3.
261
      These prompts convert the attributes, including numbers, application ID strings, and other non-natural
      language inputs, into natural language instructions.

291
292   We demonstrate the effectiveness of our method in two major aspects: (1) synthetic trace quality in
293   terms of structural validity (§4.1), distribution similarity (§4.2), and usefulness to train and evaluate
294
      machine learning-driven microservice management tasks (§4.3), and (2) benefits from our use of

297   2022) on 1.36 million microservice call graph samples from the Alibaba v2022 dataset (Luo et al.,
298   2022), corresponding to 1.1B tokens. We reserve 10% of these samples for validation. Instruction
299   tuning datasets were created by randomly selecting 5% of the training graphs and reformatting them
300
      for instruction tuning. The training lasted four epochs, using a temperature of 0.8 and top-K of 50
      for trace generation, unless otherwise specified. Further details on data preprocessing and training
301
      hyperparameters are provided in Appendix A. We compare synthetic trace quality with various
302
      structured data generation methods such as GReaT (Borisov et al., 2023) and TVAE (Xu et al., 2019),

387   4.3   U SEFULNESS OF S YNTHETIC DATA AS ML T RAINING DATA
388
389   The synthetic dataset is intended to serve as a substitute for real data in the training process. Thus,
390
      we assess how well state-of-the-art microservice management tasks for critical component extraction
      in FIRM (Qiu et al., 2020) and anomaly detection in TraceVAE (Xie et al., 2023), which use machine
391
      learning (ML) models, perform when the models are trained on the synthetic datasets. Specifically,
392
      the ML models are evaluated using real test data, and their results are compared to their original
393   performance when trained on the real training dataset.
394
395   When choosing training data, we first select a subset of traces from real data and label them with
396
      corresponding conditions (e.g., critical microservices). To have similar distributions between synthetic
      and real traces, we extract instructions from the selected real traces and use them to generate synthetic
397
      traces. We do not include invalid call graphs using the same accuracy metrics in §4.1. We train each
398
      downstream task using 5K call graphs and evaluate it using 2K call graphs for testing. We consistently
399   use the same test dataset derived from real data. We use the default hyperparameters set by FIRM
400   and TraceVAE (Qiu et al., 2020; Xie et al., 2023), and set labels based on 99-percentile latencies.
401   We run each experiment 5 times, varying the random seeds or test datasets, and report the average
402   performace for the following two evaluation tasks. We use synthetic traces generated by GReaT and
403   the Alibaba probabilistic model as baselines for comparing the performance of ML models.
404
      Critical Component Extraction. For efficient resource management, FIRM (Qiu et al., 2020)
405   predicts critical components (microservices likely to violate service level objectives (SLO)) from call
406   graphs with support vector machines (SVMs) using latency-related features and determines additional
407   resource types and amounts for the critical components. For our evaluation, we train SVMs to detect
408   critical components using two popular applications (apps A and B) from our trace dataset. For each
409   application, we randomly sample call graphs and train two SVMs: one with real data and one with
410   synthetic data generated by our fine-tuned model. Figure 5 shows the accuracy of the models when
411   evaluated on real call graphs from the test set. SVMs trained on synthetic data perform similarly to
412   those trained on real data, differing by less than 1.5 percentage point. SVMs trained on synthetic
413
      traces from baselines exhibit an accuracy gap ranging from 6 to 81 percent points.
414   Anomaly Detection. For operators to efficiently diagnose system failures, anomaly detection models
415   predict whether microservice call graphs include anomalous characteristics like irregular graph
416   structure or time. We assess our synthetic data quality using TraceVAE (Xie et al., 2023), a variational
417   autoencoder (VAE) model that detects anomalous microservices in terms of time consumption. We
418   train TraceVAE models using real and synthetic trace data, similar to our previous experiment.
419
      Figure 5 reports ROC AUC for the models evaluated on real test data. Again, we see that training on
      synthetic data generated by our method consistently yields results comparable to those obtained from
420
      real data.
421
422   In conclusion, the synthetic traces from our method demonstrate similar performance to real traces
423   and have the potential to be leveraged in various use cases, as demonstrated by the above evaluation
424   tasks. We attribute our method’s comparable performance with real data to its ability to generate
425
      complex structures and capture diverse characteristics. We also find similar results with two additional
      classification tasks by fine-tuning Llama-2 7B models. We describe the tasks and results in §D.4.

465
466     We extend our evaluation beyond generating synthetic traces, demonstrating the utility of our pre-
467     trained model in performing downstream tasks related to microservice traces. The trace pre-trained
468     model is adapted to each downstream task through additional fine-tuning. We focus on scenarios
469     where partial information from distributed environment traces is available, emphasizing the challenges
470
        posed by incomplete data. This section compares our fine-tuned model with the standard Llama-2
        7B, which lacks specific training on call graph data, and with Llama-3.1 405B by providing task
471
        descriptions and up to 16 examples in prompts (i.e., in-context learning (Brown et al., 2020)), to

        highlight the importance of domain-specific training.

474     Predicting Uncommon Communication Patterns. The task is to predict uncommon communication
475     patterns (as in §4.4) based on the first 10 lines of a trace. We train the original Llama model and our
476
        adapted model for this binary classification task on 15K samples. Each sample’s prompt comprised the
        first 10 edges of a real trace, with binary labels indicating the presence of uncommon communication
477
        patterns in the subsequent trace sections.
478
479     Results, detailed in the left of Figure 7, indicate that the original Llama-2 7B model achieves only
480     60.6% accuracy, suggesting insufficient training for recognizing uncommon patterns. Additionally,
481     in-context learning with Llama-3.1 405B shows lower accuracy (45.6%), indicating larger models
482
        trained on typical internet data lack capability to solve tasks from domain-specific data. In contrast,
        our model achieves 76.8% accuracy, demonstrating its enhanced capability to interpret and predict
483
        based on partial trace data.
484
485     Infilling Missing Data. Missing data is common in large-scale trace logging, such as in Alibaba’s
        microservice call graphs, where 67% of traces contain missing values (Huye et al., 2024). This

506   5    OTHER R ELATED W ORK
507
508   Adapting LLMs for Specific Domains. Pre-trained LLMs are increasingly adapted for specialized
509   domains due to their vast, diverse training datasets, which enable broad generalization capabilities.
510   Examples include fine-tuning LLMs for programming (Roziere et al., 2023), quantitative reasoning
511   (Lewkowycz et al., 2022), and semiconductor manufacturing (Liu et al., 2023). Our work is the
512   first to apply this approach to computer system traces involving data with specific structures and
513   constraints. Our focus is on generating synthetic trace data by fine-tuning these models to handle the
514   specific requirements of this domain.
515   Making Language Models Follow Instructions. Recent advancements have focused on enhancing
516   LLMs’ ability to follow instructions through prompting (Li & Liang, 2021; Shin et al., 2020; Wei
517   et al., 2022) and instruction tuning (Ouyang et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2021; Chung et al., 2022). These
518   two sets of methods are relevant to our setting since they augment powerful pre-trained LLMs to
519   improve their performance on new tasks. Our approach seeks to refine output expressiveness within
520   set prompts, aiming for greater fidelity in synthetic data production.
521   Multi-step Reasoning with LLMs. Iterating with LLMs over multiple steps is an effective strategy
522   to solve complex problems. For instance, Tree-of-thoughts (Yao et al., 2024) solves problems by
523   decomposing into smaller thoughts and exploring diverse reasoning paths over different thoughts.
524   Multi-step reasoning is also useful to handle long-context scenarios by summarizing iteratively (Wang
525   et al., 2023) and diving into subproblems (Lee & Kim, 2023). In contrast to the above approaches,
526   our approach learns to generate traces with specific structures and instructions for subsequent layers.

      This paper presents a training method for pre-trained LLMs tailored for generating microservice
      trace graphs through a recursive call graph generation scheme complemented by instruction tuning
532
      with intermediate instructions. Our evaluation results demonstrate that our approach outperforms the
533
      baselines in generating accurate and valid call graphs and shows improved distributional similarity
534   to real-world traces. In addition, we show that synthetic traces can effectively serve as a substitute
535   for real data in training microservice management tasks, such as critical component detection and
536   anomaly detection. They further highlight the effectiveness of instruction tuning in refining the
537   generation of call graphs according to user-specified features and reveal the potential for using our
538   model in various downstream tasks, such as prediction and data infilling, by further training the
539   model. While this paper focuses primarily on microservice call graphs, our approach holds promise
      for broader applicability to other computer system traces with similar structural characteristics.

769   A.2    T RAINING DATA P REPROCESSING
770
771   From the Alibaba microservice v2022 traces (Luo et al., 2022), we use the first-hour call graph traces
772   as our training data, which consist of 6434 unique microservices collected from more than 10 clusters.
773   The traces are collections of API calls, where each API call includes communication information
774   between the two microservices. Note that the dataset anonymizes the service and microservice names.
775
      Service ID is a nine-digit number starting with the prefix "S_" instead of using a real service name
      (e.g., social network), and microservice is a five-digit number starting with the prefix "MS_". We
776
      construct call graphs using the trace ID field (i.e., API calls with the same trace ID belong to
777
      one call graph). When constructing call graphs, we remove calls with missing information (e.g.,
778   destination microservice IDs are unknown) and remove call graphs that are not connected (e.g.,
779   missing edges). To remove redundancy, we filter out call graphs that have the same structure and
780   fields (e.g., service ID, latency) for all API calls. The distributions of training data after removing
781   redundancy are shown in Figure 8.

784   A.3    T RAINING DATA E XAMPLES
785
786   From the call graph traces, we create text-based representations of call graphs as described in Sec-
787   tion 3.1.1. First of all, Figure 9 is a training data example of converting a call graph into a tabular
788   data format, which is the baseline in §4.1. At the beginning, we include high-level information about
789   the call graph including service ID, the number of edges, and depth of the call graph. Each line inside
790   the <edges> block corresponds to a call in a call graph. 6 fields exist for each call including the
791   edge ID, source/destination microservices, communication type, and communication start/finish time.
792   Figure 10 shows an example training data sample for recursive generation as described in Section 3.1.2.
793   Each sample consists of a sequence of layers, where each layer includes the edges and the conditions
794   for the next layers. At the beginning of each layer, we provide high-level information to explain
795   connections with the previous layers (e.g., start_node, caller), structure in the call graph
796   (e.g., remaining_depth, num_edges, start_edge_id), and time-related information (e.g.,
797   latency, start_communication_at). Note that the number of fields in each edge is reduced
798   from 6 to 5 since the edges share the same start node. Also, the edge ID field is an integer, not a
799   dot-decimal number. For each next layer, the condition is described in each <subgraph> block
800
      starting with the edge ID to be extended.
801   Figure 11 is an example of instruction-tuning data. The instruction starts with a system prompt
802   followed by conditions as in Figure 10. We further explain the condition in natural language formats
803   along with user-requested features as studied in §4.4. In the output section, we include Chain-of-
804   Thought scratchpads at the end of <edges> block and at the beginning of <subgraph> blocks,
805   which elaborate on the number of edges to generate and constraints of subgraph conditions. For
806
      instance, the scratchpad includes descriptions regarding the depth requirement to let LLMs understand
      better that the depth field should be decreased by 1 from the current layer’s depth.

808   As described in Section 3.1.1, we drop each call graph attribute randomly with probability pdrop . We

      B     C ONSTRAINTS IN C ALL G RAPH L AYERS
919
920
      In this section, we describe constraints to be met for each generated call graph layer to be correct.
921   First of all, the generation results are considered invalid if the output does not have the valid format
922   with <edges> and <subgraph> tags.
923
924
      Edges. For each edge, we check the following conditions. First of all, each edge should include the
      5 fields: edge ID, destination, communication type, and communication start/finish time. Secondly,
925
      we check whether the right number of edges are generated as described in the condition. Third, the
926
      communication start time should be equal to or greater than the communication start time described
927   in the condition, and should not be greater than the communication finish time of the edge. Lastly,
928   the communication finish time should be equal to or less than the latency field in the condition.
929
930
      Next Layer Conditions. For the next layer conditions, we first check whether the next layer
      conditions should be generated or not. If the remaining depth field in the instruction is 0 or the
931
      number of edges that need to be generated is 0, no <subgraph> blocks should be generated.
932
933   Then, we check the validity of each field in the next layer conditions. First of all, the edge ID inside
934   the <subgraph> block should be found in the edges generated in the current layer. For the depth,
935   the remaining depth field should be less than the remaining depth of the instruction. Additionally, at
936
      least one of the resulting subgraphs must have a depth that is reduced by one compared to the original
      graph. For the start node and caller fields, they should be copied from the destination from
937
      the parent edge and the start node from the instructions, respectively. Lastly, we check the latency
938
      and communication start time by comparing the values to those of the parent edge. The latency of a
939   child layer should not be greater than the communication finish time of the parent edge. Also, the
940   communication start time of a child layer should not be less than the communication start time of the
941   parent edge.
942
      After generating both edges and the next conditions, we check if the sum of the number of edges
943
      matches the number of edges in the instruction.

946   C     L IMITATIONS
947
948   In this section, we discuss a few limitations of our work and potential approaches to overcome the
949   limitations. The recursive method improves call graph generation accuracy compared to generating
950   the entire trace at once, but a key drawback is that previously generated edges are discarded, as
951   only the conditioning information from the prior layer is passed to the next layer generation steps.
952   Although dropping previously generated results has little impact on the output in microservice call
953
      graph generation, where direct neighbors exert the most influence (Zhang et al., 2024), we believe
      incorporating past information, such as previous layers or a time series of call graph traces, could be
954
      beneficial.
955
956   Furthermore, our method uses manually constructed instruction templates, which may lead to
957   suboptimal generation quality, as we are not using the full potential of language models pre-trained
958   with trillions of tokens (Touvron et al., 2023). Following the methods of Liu et al. (2024); Gunasekar
959
      et al. (2023); Li et al. (2024), we believe that diversifying instructions using LLM-generated output is
      a potential method to improve the ability of LLMs to follow user intentions.
960
961   Lastly, while protecting sensitive data during synthetic trace generation is an important research
962   challenge, this paper does not address privacy concerns. Our framework assumes that the data used to
963   train a model such as ours is curated at the source to remove sensitive attributes/values. As part of our
964   future work, we plan to investigate whether our model exposes sensitive information and implement
965
      privacy-preserving techniques (e.g., differential privacy) during fine-tuning LLMs (Yu et al., 2022).
