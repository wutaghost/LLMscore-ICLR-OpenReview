009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Causal learning from data has received a lot of attention in recent years. One
012            way of capturing causal relationships is by utilizing Bayesian networks. There,
013            one recovers a weighted directed acyclic graph in which random variables are
014            represented by vertices, and the weights associated with each edge represent the
015            strengths of the causal relationships between them.
016            This concept is extended to capture dynamic effects by introducing a dependency
017            on past data, which may be captured by the structural equation model. This for-
018            malism is utilized in the present contribution to propose a score-based learning
019            algorithm. A mixed-integer quadratic program is formulated and an algorithmic
020
               solution proposed, in which the pre-generation of exponentially many acyclicity
               constraints is avoided by utilizing the so-called branch-and-cut (“lazy constraint”)
021
               method.
022
               Comparing the novel approach to the state-of-the-art, we show that the proposed
023
               approach turns out to produce more accurate results when applied to small and
024
               medium-sized synthetic instances containing up to 25 time series. Lastly, two in-
025            teresting applications in bioscience and finance, to which the method is directly
026            applied, further stress the importance of developing highly accurate, globally con-
027            vergent solvers that can handle instances of modest size.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   The problem of causal learning using graphical structures has received considerable attention from a
033   wide range of communities in recent years. This attention comes from the wide range of applications
034   including, but not limited to, medicine (Rajapakse & Zhou, 2007), machine learning (Koller &
035   Friedman, 2009), econometrics (Luetkepohl, 2005; Demiralp & Hoover, 2003; Malinsky & Spirtes,
036   2018) and others (Guo et al., 2020; Assaad et al., 2022).
037   One key reason for this is that in many applications data is abundant, but modeling using first prin-
038   ciples may be difficult due to the complexity of the problem at hand (Guo et al., 2020). Some of this
039   complexity may arise due to an abundance of non-linear effects, only a partial ability to observe the
040   system, or unexpected stochastic effects influencing the system. For a detailed discussion on these,
041   please refer to Friedman et al. (2013); Kungurtsev et al. (2024).
042   Other issues that are inherent to graphical structure learning from time series data are related to the
043   sampling timescales and scaling to large instances, these have been addressed in Abavisani et al.
044   (2023); Ouyang et al. (2024), respectively. A key benefit of learning via graphical structures is the
045   full explainability of the output; the network may be either used to compute outputs for different
046   situations or the learned graph structure may be inspected and dependencies of particular interest
047   analyzed.
048   In this contribution, we revisit the score-based learning of dynamic Bayesian networks utilizing
049   a directed acyclic graph (DAG) structure augmented by additional time dependencies from data
050   (Murphy, 2002; Dean & Kanazawa, 1989; Assaad et al., 2022). This approach to learning causality
051   has been successfully applied to a variety of problems, many of which are related to applications
052   in medicine (Zandonà et al., 2019; van Gerven et al., 2008; Michoel & Zhang, 2023; Zhong et al.,
053   2023). In addition to medical applications, the dynamic Bayesian network approach representations
      are widely used in econometrics (Hoover & Demiralp, 2003b) and financial risk modeling (Ballester


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      et al., 2023). This broad scope of applications has spawned a large number of excellent solvers that,
055   under different assumptions, can discover the underlying causal structure of a system. The use of
056   various assumptions is key to ensure the tractability of a solver, since the the number of constraints
057   that is needed to impose to acyclicity of the representing graph is super-exponential in the number
058   of random variables.
059
      One of the possible assumptions is to separate observational and interventional data (Gao et al.,
060
      2022), which reduces the number of dependencies that need to be found. Another is the assump-
061   tion of continuous underlying dynamics represented by stochastic differential equations Bellot et al.
062   (2021). One can also assume a priori knowledge about time-lagged data and incorporate this knowl-
063   edge into the solver Sun et al. (2021). One can also deal with the general problem and propose
064   local methods (Pamfil et al., 2020; Gao et al., 2022), which can scale further at the cost of some
065   loss of accuracy. Note that many of the previous works also combine several of these approaches to
066   arrive at solvers that are tractable and applicable to a wide range of applications. However, it should
067   be noted that many methods may not identify DAG representations of casual dependencies under
068   certain conditions Kaiser & Sipos (2022); Reisach et al. (2021). One of the possible causes is that
069
      many of them only converge to a local stationary point for the optimization problem.
070   We utilize mixed-integer programming to learn dynamic Bayesian networks. All of the previous
071   works mentioned above focus mostly on solving the curse of dimensionality and scaling with ad-
072   equate precision. On the other hand, we focus on leveraging fundamental principles that apply to
073   quadratic mixed-integer programs to find global solutions to the score-based DAG learning prob-
074   lem, which results in a high-quality reconstruction of the DAG. Furthermore, we tackle the curse
075
      of dimensionality by avoiding the pre-generation of the acyclic constraints. It is shown that given
      sufficient data, only a small amount of these constraints are actually needed to ensure the acyclicity
076
      of the resulting graph, which leads to the runtime generation of these constraints granting a large
077
      speedup over the version of the algorithm that uses all of the constraints for the entire duration of the
078   computation. The formulation and its implementation are easily reproducible, making it accessible
079   to a wide range of potential practitioners.

            B RANCH - AND -B OUND - AND -C UT
207
      One of our main contributions is the development of a branch-and-bound-and-cut algorithm to solve
208
      the formulation mentioned above. Since the acyclic constraints 18 need to be imposed only for
209
      the edges of the graph representing the intra-slice level, all of what follows is only applied to the
210   intra-slice graph. While we leverage the traditional branch-and-bound approach as described in
211   (Achterberg, 2007, e.g.), we incorporate cycle exclusion constraints equation 18 using ”lazy” con-
212   straints. These are only enforced once an integer-feasible solution candidate is found. If a violation
213   of a lazy constraint occurs, the constraint is added across all nodes in the branch-and-bound tree.
214   At the root node, only O (|E|) constraints 14 and 15 are initially used. Cycle-exclusion constraints
215   equation 18 are added later. Note that this method is not a heuristic and does not lead to a possi-
      bly harmful reduction (or extension) of the solution space leading to omitting possible solutions or


                                                               4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      returning solutions which are not DAGs. Furthermore, it is shown that the number of constraints
217   that are actually needed in a computation is many orders of magnitude less than the number of all
218   possible constraints.
219
220
      Once a new mixed-integer feasible solution candidate is identified, detecting cycles becomes
      straightforward using a depth-first search (DFS). If a cycle is detected, the corresponding lazy
221
      constraint equation 18 is added to the problem. The DFS algorithm solves the problem of cycle
222
      detection in a worst-case quadratic runtime relative to the number of vertices in the graph, which
223   contrasts with algorithms that separate related inequalities from a continuous relaxation (Borndörfer
224   et al., 2020; Cook et al., 2011), such as the quadratic program in our case. Three variants of adding
225   lazy constraints for the problem were tested.
226
227          • Adding a lazy constraint only for the first cycle found.
228          • Adding a lazy constraint only for the shortest cycle found.
229
             • Adding multiple lazy constraints for all cycles found in the current iteration in which an
230
               integer-feasible solution candidate is available.
231
232   The third mentioned variant was found to consistently deliver the best results, despite (Achterberg,
233   2007, Chapter 8.9). Therefore, it is applied in all the numerical tests that follow.

      6   DATA G ENERATION
237
238
      We generate data in a manner similar to that described in Zheng et al. (2018) and Pamfil et al.
      (2020). The evaluation of ExDBN was performed on the synthetic data generated as follows.
239
      First, a random intra-slice DAG was created using either the Erdős-Rény (ER) model or the scale-
240
      free Barabási–Albert (SF) model. The DAG weights were sampled uniformly from the intervals
241   [−2.0, −0.5] ∪ [0.5, 2.0].
242
243   Next, inter-slice graphs were generated using the ER model. For each inter-slice graph, weights were
244
      sampled from the interval [−0.5α, −0.2α] ∪ [0.2α, 0.5α], where α = 1/η t−1 , η ≥ 1 is the decay
      parameter, and t is the time of the slice. t = 0 corresponds to the intra-slice, while t ∈ {1, . . . , p}
245
      represents the inter-slices.
246
247   The data samples are then generated using the structural equation model equation and adding Gaus-
248   sian noise with either variance 1 or different variance for each variable sampled uniformly from a
249   given interval.
250   Specifically, we have adapted the ER and SF generators from Zheng et al. (2018) for dynamic
251   networks. Notice that this results in a slightly different generator than in Pamfil et al. (2020), which
252   may explain some of the differences in the performance of DYNOTEARS, compared to the original
253   article.

      In recent years, many solvers have been developed to facilitate the graphical learning of Bayesian
258   networks that represent causality (Pamfil et al., 2020; Hyvärinen et al., 2010; Malinsky & Spirtes,
259   2018; Gao et al., 2022; Dallakyan, 2023; Lorch et al., 2021). Each of these solvers (including the
260   one presented) faces the curse of dimensionality, which somewhat restricts the applicability of each
261   solver, and thus through testing needs to be provided. It is impossible to test the proposed solution
262   w.r.t. every solver developed. There is, however, a significant branch of development that allows for
263   direct comparison, and by transitivity of results, the comparison with many previous solvers follows.
264   In 2020, Pamfil et al. (2020) have developed a locally convergent method, called DYNOTEARS, that
265   learns causality as a Bayesian network that supersedes the solution methods previously developed
266   (Hyvärinen et al., 2010; Malinsky & Spirtes, 2018; Zheng et al., 2018). Further developments based
267   on previous publications include formulating the problem in the frequency domain or defining dif-
268   ferentiable Bayesian structures (Dallakyan, 2023; Lorch et al., 2021). In the following, we provide a
269   head-to-head comparison with DYNOTEARS and thus by transitivity with the methods documented
      by Hyvärinen et al. (2010); Malinsky & Spirtes (2018).

452   As noted in (Reisach et al., 2021), the noise variances and data scale may be important for some
453
      algorithms to perform well. We tested ExDBN on normalized data and noticed a significant perfor-
      mance drop. Therefore, ExDBN is suitable for problems in which the data of the samples have a
454
      true scale.
455
456   The results of the tests can be divided into two categories by the average number of edges. Figures
457   1, 2 and 3 show higher-degree graphs (average degree 3) and Figure 4 shows the reconstruction of
458   a lower-degree graph. In the case of the lower degree graph, it is clear that both DYNOTEARS and
459
      ExDBN perform similarly, with ExDBN performing better than DYNOTEARS some of the time,
      with the converse being true equally often.
460
461   In the case of the identification of higher degree graphs, however, one can notice that the worst pos-
462   sible performance and the mean performance are much closer in the case of ExDBN, where we can
463   point out for instance the G score in the case with 1000 samples. In these instances, the difference
464   between the worst possible G score difference is between 0.3 and 0.5 in the case of DYNOTEARS
465
      but stays well below 0.1 in the case of ExDBN. The aforementioned can be interpreted as superior
      reliability of the solution as the worst possible reconstruction is consistently better.
466
467   Focusing on the 1000-sample case, while somewhat taking into account the previous ones, too, we
468   see that the performance gap between the solvers increases in favor of ExDBN as we increase the
469   number of samples. In the lower sample cases, one may also observe that ExDBN outperforms
470   DYNOTEARS for many graph sizes in the mean and consistently outperforms DYNOTEARS in the
471
      worst possible case (min/max depending on the metric).
472   Note that the global convergence of the method, which is rooted in the fundamentals of mixed-
473   integer quadratic programming, allows us to increase the computation time, which leads to im-
474   proving the metrics reported further. While some time-sensitive applications like short-term stock
475   evaluation might not be able to benefit from this, others like biomedical applications might benefit
476   as a computation lasting several days, in which the accuracy in measurably improved (by monitor-
477
      ing the duality gap) is desirable. See Figure 5 for the comparison of running time versus solution
      quality.
478
479   We also made a comparison with VarLiNGAM (Hyvärinen et al., 2010). We used the default settings
480   of the algorithm. ExDBN performed better in all scenarios tested.

483
484   In biomedical sciences, there is a keen interest in learning dynamic Bayesian networks to estimate
485   causal effects (Tennant et al., 2020) and identify confounding variables that require conditioning. A
      recent meta-analysis (Tennant et al., 2020) of 234 articles on learning DAGs in biomedical sciences

487   none of the DAGs were as sparse as the commonly considered random ensembles; median saturation
488   was 46%, that is, each of all possible arcs appeared with probability 46% and does not converge to

      As an example, we consider a recently proposed benchmark of Ryšavý et al. (2024), where the Krebs

495   estimator is found. However, it should be noted that depending on the number of samples and
496   noise, it may be that even the maximum-likelihood estimator is not sufficiently accurate. However,
497   this does not reflect poorly on the method itself, but is rather a matter of the modification of data
498   collection methods associated with the experiment. In a one-hour time limit, ExDBN can find a
499   solution with the 38% duality gap.

501   7.4   A PPLICATION IN F INANCE
502
503   In financial services, there are also several important applications. The original DYNOTEARS paper
504
      considered a model of diversification of investments in stocks based on dynamic Bayesian networks.
      Independently, Ballester et al. (2023) consider systemic credit risk, which is one of the most impor-
505
      tant concerns within the financial system, using dynamic Bayesian networks. They found that the
506
      transport and manufacturing sectors transmit risk to many other sectors, while the energy sector and
507   banking receive risk from most other sectors. To a lesser extent, there is a risk transmission present
508   between approximately 25% of the sectors pairs, and these network relationships explain between 5
509   % and 40 % single systemic risks. Notice that these instances are much denser than the commonly
510   used random ensembles.
511
      We elaborate on the example of Ballester et al. (2023), where 10 time series capture the spreads of
512
      10 European credit default swaps (CDS). Considering the strict licensing terms of Refinitiv, the data
513   from Ballester et al. (2023) are not available from the authors, but we have downloaded 16 time-
514   series capturing the spreads of 16 European CDS with RED6 codes 05ABBF, 06DABK, 0H99B7,
515   2H6677, 2H66B7, 48DGFE, 6A516F, 8A87AG, 8B69AP, 8D8575, DD359M, EFAGG9, FF667M,
516   FH49GG, GG6EBT, NN2A8G, from January 1st, 2007, to September 25th, 2024. This amounts to
517   more than 11 MB of time series data when stored as comma-delimited values in plain text. Although
518   the procedure for learning the dynamic Bayesian network in Ballester et al. (2023) is rather heuristic,
519   we can solve the mixed integer programming (MIP) instance for the 16 European CDS in 2 minutes.
520   In the heuristic of Ballester et al. (2023), they first perform unconditional independence tests on each
521
      set of two time series containing an original series and a lagged time series, to reduce the subsequent
      number of unconditional independence tests performed. There are 45 unconditional and conditional
522
      independence tests performed first, to suggest another 200 conditional independence tests. We stress
523
      that the procedure of Ballester et al. (2023) does not come with any guarantees, while our instance
524   equation 13 is solved to global optimality. The run-time to global optimum of 2 minutes (using L2
525   regularization) validates the scalability of mixed-integer programming solvers.

      8     C ONCLUSION
529
530   Dynamic Bayesian networks have wide-ranging applications, including those in biomedical sciences
531
      and computational finance, as illustrated above. Unfortunately, their use has been somewhat limited
      by the lack of well-performing methods to learn them. Our method, ExDBN, provides the best pos-
532
      sible estimate of the DBN, in the sense of minimizing empirical risk equation 13. Significantly, our
533
      method does not suffer much from the curse of dimensionality, even for real-world dense instances,
534   which are typically challenging for other solvers. This is demonstrated most clearly in the case
535   of systemic risk transmission detailed in Section 7.4, in which the global minimizer is found in 2
536   minutes. Additionally, the use of the guarantees on the distance to the global minimizer (so-called
537   MIP gap, available ahead of the convergence to the global minimizer) provides a significant tool
538   for fine-tuning the parameters of the solver in the case of real-world application, where the ground
539   truth is not available. Combined with global convergence guarantees of the maximum likelihood
      estimator, this provides a robust method with state-of-the-art statistical performance.
