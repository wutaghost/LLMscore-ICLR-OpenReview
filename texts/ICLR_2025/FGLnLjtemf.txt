011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            For adversarial attacks on infrared detectors, previous works have focused on de-
014            signing the physical patches through temperature variations, overlooking the im-
015            pact of infrared emissivity on infrared imaging. In fact, infrared emissivity sig-
016            nificantly affects infrared radiant intensity at the same temperature. In this paper,
017            a QR-like adversarial attack patch is designed by manipulating the surface emis-
018
               sivity of objects to alter the infrared radiation intensity emitted from the object’s
               surface, called Emissivity QR-like Patch (E-QR patch). In this paper, the surface
019
               emissivity of the object is manipulated through the adjustment of surface rough-
020
               ness. Various levels of surface roughness are realized by a commonly used metal
021            material, galvanized iron sheets, to produce physically adversarial patches with
022            diverse infrared radiation intensity. Considering the possible transformation dis-
023            tributions between the digital and physical domains, a physical E-QR patch, which
024            is robust to noise, angle, and position, is generated by an expectation over the
025            transformation framework. Smoothing loss is incorporated to minimize the loss in
026            physical reconstruction, thereby effectively mitigating shooting errors in the phys-
027            ical domain induced by abrupt pixel changes in the digital domain. Experimental
028            results show that the E-QR patch achieves more than 80% attack success rate for
029
               infrared pedestrian detectors in a physical environment.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Infrared object detector that trained by Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) has received extensive at-
035   tention within the field of computer vision because of their significant performance. Infrared object
036   detector is widely used in pedestrian detection (Biswas & Milanfar, 2017), autonomous driving (Dai
037   et al., 2021) due to its unique advantages, such as night imaging, temperature measurement and
038   others. (Szegedy et al., 2013)
039   However, DNNs’ lack of interpretability and robustness makes it vulnerable to attack. Szegedy et al.
040   (2013) first discovered that the DNNs-based image classifier is susceptible to malicious devised
041   noise, which make DNNs output incorrect results with high confidence. These images with added
042   adversarial perturbations do not look different from clean images to the human eye. The process
043   described above is known as adversarial attack. Adversarial attacks can be classified into white-box
044   attacks, black-box attacks and gray-box attacks (Akhtar & Mian, 2018; Kloukiniotis et al., 2022). In
045
      white-box attack, the attacker has total knowledge of the targeted network, the adversary can easily
      detect potential vulnerabilities of the targeted model and generate strong attacks fooling easily the
046
      model (Goodfellow et al., 2014a; Moosavi-Dezfooli et al., 2016a; Madry et al., 2017; Jiang et al.,
047
      2022). Whereas, in black-box attack, the structure of the targeted architecture and its parameters are
048   unknown to the adversary. the adversary can observe the model outputs to receive some substantial
049   properties and compromise the targeted model (Li et al., 2022; Su et al., 2019a; Liu et al., 2016).
050   Attacks between white box and black box attacks are called gray-box attacks. The inducibility of
051   attack can be categorized into target and non-target attacks (Akhtar & Mian, 2018). In non-targeted
052   attacks, the adversary only needs to add perturbations to make the target model produce wrong
053   results, while a targeted attack needs to make the DNNs model produce wrong results toward an
      intended target. Some works show that adversarial examples can exist not only in the digital world

091   Recently, there have been studies on infrared adversarial attacks, utilizing the infrared properties of

      and resistors (Bendelac et al., 2021) are implemented through electrical control energy-consuming

095
      ness of electronically controlled patches, (Wei et al., 2023a; Hu et al., 2023) employed the chemical
096   reaction of the warming and cooling pastes to attack the infrared detector. However, it is difficult
097   for the above chemical reaction to control the surface temperature precisely for a long period. Then
098   some works utilized the insulation materials to alter the the object’s surface temperature. The ad-
099   versarial attacking methods based on insulation materials (Zhu et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2023b) used
100   aerogel’s thermal insulation properties to change the object’s surface temperature, thus creating a
101   binary adversarial patch. Overall, the existing infrared adversarial attack methods rely on manip-
102   ulating the surface temperature of objects, resulting in a limited richness of textures in infrared
103   images.
104   To address the aforementioned challenges, we introduce another physical factor affecting infrared
105   radiation intensity: infrared emissivity. Infrared imaging is influenced by temperature and infrared
106   emissivity on infrared radiation intensity (Hou et al., 2022). By using materials with different in-
107   frared emissivity, different infrared radiation intensities can be generated at the same temperature
      without deliberately changing the surface temperature of the object. Diversified emissivity can be

      achieved not only by using a variety of material properties but also by using different roughness of
109   a material (Zhang et al., 2023). We introduced a common substance, a galvanized iron sheet, and
110   polished it into different roughness as raw materials for physical adversarial patches. As depicted
111   in Figure 1, two galvanized iron sheets exhibit completely different pixel values captured by the
112   same infrared sensor. It is obvious that adversarial patches with intricate texture structures can be
113   achieved by using galvanized iron sheets with different degrees of roughness.
114
      In this paper, we propose a physically easy-to-implement infrared attack method called “Emissivity
115   QR-like Patch” (E-QR patch). First, the galvanized iron sheets with different degrees of roughness
116   are used to control the emissivity of the object’s surface. Specially, these galvanized iron sheets
117   are reshaped as a QR-like patch absorbed on a soft magnetic sheet though magnetic force. Second,
118   the smoothing loss is incorporated to minimize the the loss in physical reconstruction. Finally, the
119   position and the degree of roughness of the galvanized iron sheets are considered as the decision
120   variables, which are determined by the black-box optimization. It is obvious that the proposed E-
121   QR patch is easy to implement. Since these galvanized iron sheets are fixed on the soft magnetic
122   sheet through magnetic force, the resulting E-QR patch is a reusable physical adversarial carrier.

      2.1   I NFRARED P HYSICAL A DVERSARIAL ATTACKS
147
148
      The current adversarial attacks against infrared images are shown in Table 1. Zhu et al. (2021) used
149
      the heat from small light bulbs to build an infrared adversarial patch on a circuit board to attack
150   the infrared pedestrian detector. But the small bulb board can only attack at a specific angle of
151   human body and not stealthy and easy to implement. In order to solve this problem, they design
152   a “wearable” attack (Zhu et al., 2022) by a piece of clothes. It uses aerogel to make a QR-like
153   adversarial texture and change the adversarial examples from 2D into 3D as a clothe to insulate
154   thermal, which can change the pixel values of the pedestrian surface. Although the above approach
155   achieves an effective attack on the physical world, the adversarial medium is attention-grabbing and
156   look unnatural to human. To realize a physically stealthy and easy to implement infrared attack,
157   a method (Wei et al., 2023a) achieved by putting the cooling and warming paste inside clothes to
158
      change temperature as a adversarial patch while another work (Wei et al., 2023b) reshapes the type
      of aerogel patch into a irregular shape.
159
160   Although the existing methods have made good progress, they are limited by temperature-controlled
161   mode, which makes it challenging to achieve the effect of low-cost and prolonged attacks. Most of
      them can only define the patch as a binary pattern, reducing the attack success rate.

      2.2   D IGITAL - PHYSICAL M ODELING
163
164   Physical adversarial attacks need to face the complexity of real-world environments, which requires
165   strong robustness of the generated adversarial examples. With the continuous exploration of attack
166   methods, Several digital-physical modeling techniques are widely recognized and used to improve
167   the performance of physical attacks. Physical adversarial attacks on visible light have found that di-
168   rect printing of adversarial perturbations can distort due to the printer. Non-Printability Score (NPS)
169   (Shapira et al., 2023) has been devised to measure the distance between the adversarial perturbation
170
      and the printer. Furthermore, the natural world also suffers from noise and deformation problems.
      Expectation Over Transformation (EOT) (Athalye et al., 2018) has been proposed to consider poten-
171
      tial transformations in the physical world. EOT discards the paradigm form to constrain the solution
172
      space. Instead, it utilizes the imposed expected distance between the adversarial inputs and the
173   original inputs. It has been shown that it is difficult for cameras to capture extreme differences in
174   neighboring pixels due to sampling noise (Sharif et al., 2016). To fit natural images’ smoothness,
175   the Total variation norm (TV) (Singh et al., 2022) is able to maintain perturbation smoothness.
176
177   2.3   M OTIVATION
178
179   As was previously indicated, several approaches have explored infrared physical adversarial attacks.
180   However, all of these approaches generate adversarial patch by changing temperature of object sur-
181   face, which is limited to deploy in physical world. In order to expand applicable scenarios, this paper
182
      builds physical infrared adversarial patch based on manipulating the infrared emissivity of object’s
      surface.
183
184   Current physical adversarial patches are disposable while attacking different DNNs. In this study, a
185   new material, galvanized iron sheets with different roughness, is used to generate infrared adversarial
186   patch, and soft magnets are used as the background. Changing the arrangement order of galvanized
187   patches with different roughness could generate a new infrared physical adversarial patch.

408   the total number of patch cells with the same area. Pixel bit depth quantitative analysis is to change
409
      the number of pixel values that can be selected in each patch cell, e.g., when the bit depth is 2, the
      pixel values that can be chosen are 0 and 0.5. When the bit depth is 3, the pixel values that can be
410
      selected are 0, 0.25, 0.5, and so on after that. Table 2 reports the results of evaluating the attack
411
      effectiveness of our method (E-QR) with random patches. According to the above results, we can
412   draw the following conclusions. The proposed E-QR patch outperforms random patches across the
413   board. The effectiveness of the attack decreases as the resolution of the patches gradually increases,
414   which we attribute to the sharp rise in the number of patches to be optimized, leading to an increase in
415   the difficulty of solving the parameters and making it difficult to optimize a higher-quality solution
416   in the same amount of time. Under the influence of the patch pixel bit depth, the attack effect at
417   the ends of the parameter interval performs better than the middle. Therefore, choosing the 5 × 5
418   resolution for the E-QR patch is reasonable. Subsequent experimental alignments are conducted

445
      The experiment result details are shown at Appendix B. Initially, we tested the attack success rate

450   and environmental factors by having a person stand 2m away from the detector while performing
451   various movements such as angular rotations, standing up, and sitting down. E-QR Patch perform
452   well in small rotations, both standing and sitting postures exhibited good robustness. However,
453   with big rotations, crucial information from the patch became obscured, leading to a sharp decline
454   in ASR. Furthermore, the effectiveness of the E-QR Patch is affected by the infrared radiation of
455
      different environments in different temperature fields.

471   5       C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
472
473   This paper introduces an adversarial attack for infrared detectors, known as the E-QR patch. This
474   innovative approach involves utilizing the different roughness of galvanized iron sheets to modify
475   the infrared emissivity of object surfaces, creating adversarial patches characterized by intricate tex-
476   tures. Consequently, these patches hide individuals by fooling the detector with infrared sensors.
477   Moreover, we have developed a reusable physical adversarial carrier by exploiting the magnetic
478
      properties of soft magnetic sheets to adhere galvanized iron sheets. This innovative carrier system
      enhances the practicality and sustainability of the adversarial attack, contributing to its real-world
479
      applicability. A comprehensive set of experiments conducted in both digital and physical domains
480
      provides compelling evidence for the effectiveness of our E-QR patch in successfully circumvent-
481   ing detection models. In the future, we hope to overcome the shortcomings of physical objects at
482   different scales, putting the adversarial patch on 3D-based modeling to improve the robustness of
483   scales. Moreover, emissivity-based adversarial attack patches can be combined with infrared stealth
484   technology to achieve cross-band adversarial attacks.

      A    P HYSICAL REALIZATION
612
613
614   Galvanized iron sheets are used as the primary material for fabricating the patches. Varying degrees
615   of sanding were applied to modify the emissivity of their surfaces, as shown in Figure 1. The patches
616
      consist of a smooth sheet of galvanized iron to achieve a low reflectivity surface and a rough sheet
      of galvanized iron to enhance its surface emissivity. These two different roughness levels were
617
      used to finalize the physical patch by affixing the iron sheet to a soft magnetic sheet with magnetic
618
      property. The galvanised iron sheet is easy to paste or remove on the soft magnetic sheet, so a soft
619   magnetic sheet can be multiplexed with a variety of physical adversarial patch patterns. The thermal
620   emissivity of the soft magnetic sheet is similar to conventional clothing so adhered to the human
621   body over an extended. Patches of identical dimensions were also crafted from all three materials to
622   validate the efficacy of our patches. The attack performance of these clothes was then tested in the
623   real world.
624
      We employed the FLIR ONE Pro camera with a thermal resolution of 160×120 for infrared imaging.
625   Throughout the capture process, the camera is connected to a Xiaomi phone, enabling real-time
626   image display. We captured images of individuals in various indoor and outdoor scenarios, with
627   the distance between the camera and the subjects ranging from 1 to 4 meters. The images depict
628   individuals in different poses, such as standing and sitting.

632
633
      We first explored the robustness of the E-QR patch over distance by testing the attack success rate
634
      with the detected person looking squarely at the infrared detector, holding the antagonistic patch,
635
      and starting at a distance of 1m and gradually moving backward by 1m. The average ASR is 81.9%.
636   As shown in Figure. 4, E-QR patch achieves an 93.8% ASR at a distance including 1-2 meters,

638   resolution of the detector, the adversarial patch gradually deforms as the distance of the detected
639   person increases, resulting in a rapid decrease in ASR.
