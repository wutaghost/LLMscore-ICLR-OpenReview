009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Diffusion models have achieved remarkable success in image synthesis, but the
012            generated high-quality images raise concerns about potential malicious use. Exist-
013            ing detectors often struggle to capture distinctive features across different training
014            models, limiting their generalization to unseen diffusion models with varying
015            schedulers and hyperparameters. To address this issue, we observe that diffusion-
016
               generated images exhibit progressively larger differences from real images across
               low- to high-frequency bands. Based on this insight, we propose a novel image
017
               representation called Natural Frequency Deviation (DEFEND). DEFEND applies a
018
               weighted filter to the Fourier spectrum, suppressing less discriminative bands while
019            enhancing more informative ones. This approach, grounded in a comprehensive
020            analysis of frequency-based differences between real and diffusion-generated im-
021            ages, enables robust detection of images from unseen diffusion models and provides
022            resilience to various perturbations. Extensive experiments on diffusion-generated
023            image datasets show that our method outperforms state-of-the-art detectors with
024            superior generalization and robustness.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021) have achieved remarkable success in
029
      image synthesis, producing high-quality and diverse results. However, the easy accessibility and
030   realistic generated images present a significant challenge, as they can be easily misused for malicious
031   purposes, such as fabricating evidence or misleading the public, raising serious social, privacy, and
032   ethical concerns (Devlin & Cheetham, 2023). Therefore, how to detect diffusion-generated images
033   has become an urgent and critical issue recently.
034
      Recent methods for detecting diffusion-generated images focus on specific model characteristics, such
035
      as using reconstruction error (Wang et al., 2023), leveraging pre-trained vision-language models (Ojha
036   et al., 2023), or identifying artifacts introduced by the upsampling layers (Tan et al., 2024). However,
037   these approaches are limited in their generalization to different diffusion models. They often rely on
038   specific models for reconstruction, require relevant generated images as reference sets, or depend on
039   architectural features like upsampling layers, making them less effective when detecting images from
040   unknown or unseen diffusion models.
041
      With all the above concerns in mind, we raise the following question: Can we develop a general
042   diffusion-generated image detector based on their inherent difference with natural real images? As
043   we do not rely on specific diffusion-generated images, the detector should be sufficiently general
044   and robust. To this end, we first analyze the difference between natural real images and diffusion-
045   generated images in the frequency domain, as the frequency domain contains more distinguishable
046   information than the pixel domain (Van der Schaaf & van Hateren, 1996), as shown in Fig. 1. We
047   can observe that there exists a clear discrepancy between natural real images and diffusion-generated
048   images in their Fourier spectrum, specifically in the mid- and high-frequency band, which could serve
049   as discriminative clues for detection.
050   To leverage this, we first conduct a comprehensive frequency analysis on diffusion-generated images
051   and natural real images to explore the intrinsic discrepancy clues, which indicates that the discrim-
052   inability increases from low- to high-frequency bands. Based on our analysis, we further propose
053   a general image representation termed Natural Frequency Deviation (DEFEND): By designing a
      frequency-selective function that serves as the weighted filter banks, it restrains the less discriminative

      2    R ELATED W ORK
094
095
      Diffusion models. Diffusion Models have achieved remarkable success in image synthesis task (Ho
096   et al., 2020; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Rombach et al., 2022). The main idea of diffusion models is
097   inspired by the non-equilibrium thermodynamics proposed in (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015). Typically,
098   diffusion models define two Markov chains of diffusion steps that first slowly add Gaussian noise
099   to clean images, until disturbing them into isotropic Gaussian noise (termed diffusion or forward
100   process); then they learn to reverse the diffusion process to generate clean samples from the noise
101   (termed denoising or reverse process). Due to substantial efforts focusing on improving model
102   architectures (Rombach et al., 2022), sampling methods (Song et al., 2020; Lu et al., 2022), and
103   optimizing processes (Ho & Salimans, 2022; Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021), recent diffusion models
104
      are capable of generating high-quality images beyond human imagination at an extremely low cost,
      which can be a double-edged sword. Thus, developing general and robust diffusion-generated image
105
      detectors has recently become a critical issue.
106
107   Generated image detection. Recent generative models, such as GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2014;
      Karras et al., 2018; 2019; Brock et al., 2018) and Diffusion models (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021;


                                                           2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Nichol et al., 2021; Rombach et al., 2022; Ramesh et al., 2022), have achieved remarkable success
109   in image generation task. Various detectors have been proposed to prevent the malicious use of
110   generated images (Chai et al., 2020; Qian et al., 2020). To develop a general GAN-generated
111   image detector,(Wang et al., 2020) introduce carefully designed pre- and post-processing with data
112   augmentation. To detect generated and manipulated images,(Chai et al., 2020) propose to use patch-
113   level artifacts. Recently, (Wang et al., 2023) found that diffusion-generated images are easier to
114   reconstruct by diffusion models than real images. They propose a representation DIRE based on
115   reconstruction error. (Ojha et al., 2023) propose to use the pre-trained vision-language models to
116   learn discriminative clues for detection. NPR (Tan et al., 2024) explore the artifacts introduced by
117
      the up-sampling layer in diffusion model architectures. These methods still, however, highly rely on
      specific patterns in training diffusion-generated images, which could lead to performance drops when
118
      detecting unseen models. Instead, we exploit the intrinsic statistic difference with natural real images
119
      in the frequency domain to discriminate from diffusion-generated images.
120
121   Frequency artifacts in generated images. Some prior works have demonstrated that generated
122   images exist artifacts in the frequency domain (Dzanic et al., 2020; Ricker et al., 2022; Corvi et al.,
123
      2023a; Yu et al., 2019; Chandrasegaran et al., 2021). To detect GAN-generated images,(Frank et al.,
      2020) analyzes artifacts by discrete cosine transform (DCT). (Dzanic et al., 2020) leverage the Fourier
124
      spectrum discrepancy in GAN- and VAE-generated images. (Qian et al., 2020) propose exploring
125
      the frequency clues to detect generated and manipulated images. There are already some works
126   that find specific patterns in diffusion-generated images in the frequency domain which could serve
127   as clues for detection, such as (Ricker et al., 2022) analyze the frequency fingerprints of different
128   diffusion models, (Corvi et al., 2023a;b) analyze the artifacts in both spatial and frequency domains,
129   (Li et al., 2024) train a mask on Fourier spectrum and use the cosine similarity with a reference
130   set for detection. These methods, however, focus mainly on the frequency distribution of specific
131   diffusion-generated images, which ignores the natural real images’ inherent distributions and may
132   lead to limited generalization. In this paper, we instead focus on the general frequency difference
133   between natural real images and diffusion-generated images and propose a new image representation
134
      by restraining the less discriminative bands and enhancing the more discriminative ones.

204   With the above observation that diffusion-generated images exist frequency discrepancy with natural

408   Robustness to unseen perturbations. The robustness to unseen perturbations is also a major concern

      for existing detectors, as there are various but common post-preprocessing perturbations in real-

      against three common but widely used perturbations on images generated from ADM (the same as

      the training set), including Gaussian Noise, Gaussian Blur, and JPEG Compression, following (Wang
412   et al., 2020; 2023). For each perturbation, we employ three different severity levels to disrupt images:
413      = 0.001, 0.005, 0.01 for Gaussian Noise, = 1, 2, 3 for Gaussian Blur, and quality = 75, 50, 25
414   for JPEG Compression. The results are shown in Fig. 5. From the results, we first observe that
415   existing detectors would suffer from common perturbations, especially for Gaussian Noise. This
416   indicates that some of the representations these detectors rely on might not be sufficiently robust to
417   real scenario disruptions. Our proposed representation suffers significantly less from the above three
418   perturbations, with only slight or even no performance drops. This indicates that, by exploring the
419   discriminative clues with natural real images across all frequency bands, our proposed representation
420
      has impressive robustness against common perturbations.

521   5     C ONCLUSION
522
523   In this paper, we focus on the intrinsic statistical difference between natural real images and diffusion-
524   generated images in the frequency domain. Specifically, we first conduct a comprehensive frequency
525   analysis that shows that the diffusion-generated images exhibit increasing differences with natural
526   real images from low- to high-frequency bands. Upon this observation, we propose a new image
527   representation DEFEND by designing a specific frequency-selective function that serves as the
528   weighted filter banks on the Fourier spectrum to restrain the less-discriminative frequency bands,
529   low-frequency and to enhance the more discriminative ones, high-frequency. Extensive experiments
530
      on various public diffusion-generated image datasets demonstrate the superiority of our proposed
      method with impressive generalization and robustness against other state-of-the-art competitors. We
531
      hope our method could provide insights for detecting generated images from the perspective of
532
      analyzing natural real images, i.e., in the frequency domain. In the future, we aim to extend our
533   idea and method to other AI-generated content (AIGC) detection tasks, such as different generative
534   models to facilitate the development of AIGC safety.
