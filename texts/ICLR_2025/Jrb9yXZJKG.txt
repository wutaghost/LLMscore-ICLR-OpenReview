                                                            A BSTRACT
023
024
                   Computer Aided Design (CAD) is indispensable across various industries. Text-
025                based CAD editing, which automatically modifies CAD models following textual
026                instructions, is important yet not extensively studied. Existing work explores de-
027                sign variation generation, which randomly alters specific parts of a CAD model,
028                offering no control over the final appearance. This work introduces CAD-Editor
029                for text-based editing. We leverage Large Language Models (LLMs) as the back-
030                bone to take the concatenation of textual instruction and original CAD sequence
031                as input and predict the edited CAD sequence, where the sequence representation
032                of a CAD model is designed for easier processing by LLMs. Moreover, we pro-
033
                   pose fine-tuning LLMs by using a synthetic dataset followed by a selective dataset.
                   The synthetic data is produced by leveraging powerful existing models, including
034
                   design variation generation models for producing paired CAD models and multi-
035
                   modal models for capturing textual differences between these pairs. The selective
036                data is created by choosing top examples from outputs of the initially fine-tuned
037                LLMs based on human feedback or metrics. In this way, a large-scale synthetic
038                dataset offers basic capability while a selective dataset that is less noisy and better
039                aligned with human intentions boosts performance further. Extensive experiments
040                demonstrate the advantage of CAD-Editor both quantitatively and qualitatively.

      1      I NTRODUCTION
044
045
      In the modern digital era, Computer Aided Design (CAD) has become indispensable across various
      industries, including automotive, aerospace, manufacturing, and architectural design. CAD is piv-
046
      otal in creating everything from automobiles to airplanes and excavators to elevators, revolutionizing
047
      how we design and build. Most modern CAD design tools employ the “Sketch-and-Extrude (SE)
048   Operations” (Shahin, 2008; Camba et al., 2016), where designers first sketch loops of 2D curves
049   to define outer and inner boundaries of profiles, then extrude these profiles into 3D shapes, and fi-
050   nally combine these shapes to construct complex models. The creation of CAD models involves
051   an iterative process, where an initial draft undergoes multiple modifications until it meets the user’s
052   needs. Throughout this procedure, natural language is widely used to convey how the CAD model
053   should be adjusted. For those without design expertise, natural language offers the most accessible
      and straightforward means of expressing their needs. For professional designers, it acts as a vital


                                                                     1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      medium for fast, detailed, and clear communication. Consequently, a system capable of automat-
055   ically editing a CAD model based on textual instructions, called text-based CAD editing, could
056   revolutionize the entire CAD design process. It would greatly speed up CAD model development
057   and enable more people, particularly those with limited design expertise, to create CAD models.
058
      Text-based CAD editing presents several distinct challenges. First, the textual instructions are highly
059
      varied, such as deletion (the 1st case in Figure 1 ), addition (the 5th case in Figure 1), local change
060
      (the 2nd and the 3rd case in Figure 1) and global change (the 4th case in Figure 1). Besides, it
061   demands on an accurate understanding of the structure of CAD’s SE operations. For example,
062   to complete the editing task in the 2nd case of Figure 1, one must identify which part of the SE
063   operations represents the ‘inward curvature’. Moreover, it also requires comprehending geometric
064   concepts, such as specific shapes as the cylinder and hexagonal holes shown in the 1st and 3rd cases,
065   and positions like corners shown in the 5th case of Figure 1. Additionally, there is an absence of
066   naturally-existing datasets, making it even more intractable.
067   While text-based CAD editing is important and challenging, it receives little research attention.
068   Existing work (Wu et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2022; 2023) primarily involves altering specific parts of a
069   CAD model randomly while leaving others unchanged, without any control over the final appearance
070   of the new CAD model, called design variation generation for brevity. For example, SkexGen (Xu
071   et al., 2022) supports variations in a CAD model’s topology, geometry, or extrusion level through
072   disentangled codebooks. Hnc-CAD (Xu et al., 2023) enables variations at loop, profile, or solid
073   levels and completes an entire CAD model from a partial one using a neural code tree representation.
074   In this work, we aim to exploit the capabilities of large-scale models and high-quality data, rather
075   than exploring specialized methods, to tackle the mentioned challenges for the first time. This is
076   motivated by the success of large language models (LLMs), where the model size and data quality
077   have been shown to be key factors in their success (OpenAI, 2023; Touvron et al., 2023). Moreover,
078   developing more specialized techniques depends on having a large enough model and sufficient
079   training data as an initial step. Specifically, our main idea is to utilize pre-trained LLMs as the
080   backbone, generate a large-scale synthetic dataset for initial fine-tuning, and enhance the fine-tuning
081
      with a small-scale selective dataset. There are three main advantages to this approach. First, LLMs
      inherently possess strong text comprehension abilities, which are beneficial in handling diverse and
082
      complex textual instructions. Second, there is evidence that LLMs have learned CAD-related codes
083
      in the pre-training (Makatura et al., 2023), potentially aiding in understanding CAD’s SE operations
084   and geometric concepts. Third, the combination of synthetic and selective data offers a practical and
085   efficient way to obtain sufficient training data when naturally existing datasets are absent.
086
087
      To achieve this, as an initial step, we formulate text-based CAD editing as a sequence-to-sequence
      (seq2seq) problem, where the input is the concatenation of the textual instruction and the sequence
088
      of the original CAD model and the output is the sequence of the edited CAD model. Here, the
089
      CAD sequence is constructed by adjusting the existing design of the CAD sequence Xu et al. (2022)
090   to enhance their readability and comprehensibility for LLMs. Specifically, we allow for variable
091   length and use textual tokens to represent both categorical and numerical variables in CAD’s SE
092   operations. Next, we propose synthesizing a high-quality training dataset by summarizing CAD
093   model variations and using it for the initial fine-tuning of LLMs. Concretely, we harness the strong
094   capability of existing models to efficiently synthesize this data. Existing design variation genera-
095   tion models are used to produce paired CAD models, and pre-trained Multi-modal LLMs (MLLMs)
096   with a multi-level captioning strategy are employed to derive the textual differences between images
097   rendered from these paired CAD models. Moreover, we construct a selective dataset with less noise
098
      and better alignment with human intentions and use it for further fine-tuning LLMs to improve per-
      formance. Specifically, this selective dataset is built on the observation that sampling the initially
099
      fine-tuned model several times will yield at least one good result. We use human feedback or a met-
100
      ric—directional CLIP score—to choose a small-scale set of good examples from multiple outputs of
101   the initially fine-tuned LLM. We refer to the entire framework as CAD-Editor for ease of reference.
102   The major contributions of this work are as follows:
103
104
             • We introduce a new task called text-based CAD editing, which allows for precise edits via
               textual instructions, aligning more closely with real-world user needs.
105
             • We propose CAD-Editor, which exploits the potential of large-scale models and high-
106
               quality data to develop the first model for text-based CAD editing. We formulate text-based
107            editing as a seq2seq problem and employ pre-trained LLMs as our backbone. Moreover,

110          • We build the first benchmark for text-based CAD editing based on DeepCAD dataset (Wu
111            et al., 2021), and demonstrate the superiority of CAD-Editor over baselines in terms of
112            sequence validity, text-CAD alignment, and generation quality.

      CAD Generation. Parametric CAD, characterized by its sketch-and-extrude operations, dominates
121
      mechanical design. The recent development of large-scale parametric CAD datasets has facilitated
122   the adoption of learning-based approaches. These methods utilize the historical sequence of CAD
123   modeling and sketch constraints to generate engineering sketches and solid models (Willis et al.,
124   2021b; Wu et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2022; 2023; Seff et al., 2020). The resulting sequences can be
125   processed by a solid modeling kernel to produce editable parametric CAD files, which include either
126   2D engineering sketches (Willis et al., 2021a; Seff et al., 2022) or 3D CAD shapes (Xu et al., 2022;
127   2023). CAD generation models have focused on tasks such as reverse engineering from point clouds
128   (Khan et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2024), unconditional generation (Wu et al., 2021), and design variations
129   (Xu et al., 2022; 2023). For instance, SkexGen (Xu et al., 2022) allows fixing topology codes
130
      to maintain shape geometry, geometry codes to maintain dimensions and positioning, or extrusion
      codes to maintain the height of extruded sketches along with their 3D combinations. Similarly, Hnc-
131
      CAD (Xu et al., 2023) leverages the inherent hierarchies within CAD models to facilitate editing
132
      at the loop, profile, and solid levels, and offers auto-completion by adding or attaching additional
133   sketches. While previous approaches can generate diverse shapes based on high-level guidance, they
134   overlook the challenge of enabling CAD editing through textual instructions, which often results in
135   arbitrary editing rather than meaningful changes. Providing user control over the generation process,
136   while preserving design intent, is key for the adoption of generative models in real-world CAD
137   software. Different from existing works, our method leverages both the sketch-and-extrude features
138   and the natural language capabilities of pre-trained LLMs to enable instruction-based editing, which
139   allows users to directly edit through explicit textual instructions.
140   Text-based Editing. Text-based editing is crucial across various domains, as textual instructions
141   are expressive, precise, and intuitive, enabling users to easily isolate and modify specific objects
142   or visual attributes. This task has been extensively explored in areas such as 3D editing (Mikaeili
143   et al., 2023), image editing (Meng et al., 2021; Brooks et al., 2023), and video editing (Chai et al.,
144   2023; Ceylan et al., 2023). For example, InstructPix2Pix (Brooks et al., 2023) utilizes synthetic data
145   generated by a language model and a text-to-image model to create a large dataset of image editing
146   examples, enabling instruction-based edits for images. StableVideo (Chai et al., 2023) introduces
147
      a text-driven video editing framework that employs a novel inter-frame propagation mechanism to
      achieve consistency-aware video editing. These advancements have inspired our approach to text-
148
      based CAD editing, and we are the first to apply text-based editing within the CAD domain.
149
150   Large Language Models. In recent years, scaling pre-trained language models (PLMs) has con-
151   sistently enhanced their capacity for downstream tasks. This trend has led to the development of
152   increasingly larger PLMs, such as ChatGPT OpenAI (2023), GPT-4 Achiam et al. (2023), and
153
      Gemini-Pro Team et al. (2023), each boasting over 100 billion parameters. These models, collec-
      tively known as large language models (LLMs), have gained widespread attention. The availability
154
      of open-source LLMs like LLaMA Touvron et al. (2023) has further accelerated research in this field.
155
      LLMs distinguish themselves from smaller models through remarkable emergent capabilities, par-
156   ticularly in-context learning Brown et al. (2020) and chain-of-thought prompting Wei et al. (2022);
157   Kojima et al. (2022). They have revolutionized generative tasks, showing significant improvements
158   over traditional neural networks in areas such as code generation Chen et al. (2021); Nijkamp et al.
159   (2022); Chen et al. (2023) and material generation Gruver et al. (2024). Additionally, the strategic
160   use of LLMs for generating synthetic data to aid training has opened new avenues for research and
161   application Xu et al. (2024); Yu et al. (2024). This synthetic data is often rich and diverse, providing
      substantial benefits for model training and performance.

      3       M ETHODS
176
      In this section, we present CAD-Editor, the first generative model for text-based CAD editing. We
177
      frame the task as a seq2seq generation problem, where the sequence representation of a CAD model
178   is adapted for easier process and comprehension by LLMs (Section 3.1). Then, we introduce a initial
179   fine-tuning stage of LLMs on a synthetic dataset, which is produced by summarizing CAD model
180   variations (Section 3.2). Moreover, we enhance this with further fine-tuning on a selective dataset,
181   which is built by choosing top examples from outputs of the initially fine-tuned LLMs (Section 3.3).
182
183   3.1       T EXT- BASED CAD E DITING AS S EQ 2S EQ G ENERATION
184
185   In previous work, a CAD model has been formulated as a sequence for other CAD generation
186   tasks (Wu et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2022; 2023). Building upon these efforts, we formulate the
187   text-based CAD generation as a seq2seq generation task and adjust the sequence representation of
188
      a CAD model (Xu et al., 2022) to be more comprehensible for LLMs. As shown in the left side of
      Figure 2, we combine the textual instruction with the original CAD sequence as input, and represent
189
      the output as the edited CAD sequence. Moreover, as illustrated in the right side of Figure 2, SE
190
      operations of a CAD model are expressed as a series of text tokens. These include 1) topology
191   tokens indicating a curve type (e.g., line, arc, or circle), 2) geometric tokens for point coordinates
192   which are discretized into integers and then represented as text, 3) extrusion tokens where both
193   Boolean operations (e.g., add, cut, or intersect) and numerical parameters are converted to text, and
194   4) end-primitive tokens (e.g., EoC, EoL, EoF, EoS or EoE for the end of a curve, loop, face, sketch
195   or extrusion). Unlike previous work, we use text tokens (i.e., natural language descriptions) instead
196   of binary representations for categorical operations such as curve types and extrusion operations
197   as well as numerical attributes such as point coordinates and extrusion parameters. Besides, we
198   support variable token lengths instead of using placeholders to standardize the command length.
199
      This approach simplifies processing and interpretation for LLMs and reduces the sequence length.
200
201   3.2       I NITIAL F INE -T UNING WITH S YNTHETIC DATASET
202
      With the seq2seq formulation introduced above, it is feasible to fine-tune pre-trained LLMs for text-
203
      based CAD editing, provided there is a training dataset comprising triplets of 〈textual instruction,
204   original CAD sequence, edited CAD sequence〉. However, such data is neither naturally available
205   nor cost-effective to manually label. Existing models, fortunately, offer potential solutions for con-
206   structing these triplets in an economical and efficient manner. Design variation generation models
207   can generate paired CAD models but lack the ability to produce corresponding text instructions (Xu
208   et al., 2022; 2023). On the other hand, multi-modal LLMs (MLLMs) cannot generate CAD models
209   but excel in understanding rendered CAD images (Achiam et al., 2023). Therefore, we propose har-
210   nessing the complementary strengths of these existing models to systematically generate the needed
211   training set. For ease of reference, we call this training set synthetic dataset, denoted by Dsynthetic .
212
      Below, we detail the two critical steps for generating this synthetic dataset Dsynthetic (see Figure 3).
213   Paired CAD Generation by Design Variation. In this step, we get CAD pairs by feeding an
214   existing CAD model into design variation generation models to obtain its variants, with certain parts
215   of the CAD model being randomly altered. The process of CAD model editing naturally involves
      modifying, adding, and removing operations. To comprehensively capture these aspects, we select

229   suitable design variation models for constructing CAD pairs. Specifically, we generate CAD pairs
230   related to modifying operations by using SkexGen (Xu et al., 2022), where a certain code in the
231
      codebook is fixed to achieve this. Besides, we generate CAD pairs related to adding operations by
      using Hnc-CAD (Xu et al., 2023), where auto-completion is utilized to achieve this. Additionally,
232
      we use the generated CAD model from Hnc-CAD’s auto-completion as the source and the original
233
      CAD model as the target to construct the CAD pairs related to removing operations.
234
235   The design variation generation model can sometimes introduce noise by altering a CAD model too
236   drastically or not at all. To ensure data quality, we implement a data filtering strategy. Specifically,
237
      we render each generated CAD pair into images, calculate the cosine similarity between them, and
      exclude pairs with similarity below a certain threshold. In our experiments, thresholds are set at 0.95
238
      for the CAD pairs generated by SkexGen and 0.9 for Hnc-CAD. Additionally, we filter out CAD
239
      pairs showing no significant changes in the CAD sequence level.
240
241   Textual Instruction Generation by Multi-level Captioning. In this step, we render the CAD pairs
242   from the last step as images and input the images into MLLMs to get the textual difference. Note that
243
      we opt to identify differences at the rendered image level instead of the CAD sequence level because
      changes are more easily perceived visually. In the preliminary study, we observe that even advanced
244
      MLLMs, e.g., GPT-4o, often make mistakes when directly describing how to edit one CAD model
245
      into another, such as incorrect positional relationships, numbers of shapes, or types of shapes. To
246   mitigate this issue, we introduce a multi-level captioning strategy, which breaks down this complex
247   task into several simpler sub-tasks, thereby improving the overall accuracy. Specifically, this method
248   involves the following three sub-tasks, with full prompts available in Appendix A. 1) Describe CAD
249   images. Initially, we generate detailed descriptions for each CAD image in the pair with MLLMs
250   (e.g., GPT-4o). This step involves a meticulous examination of each image’s geometric proper-
251   ties, including the type and number of elements, the proportions of sizes, positional relationships
252   between elements, and any additional noteworthy details. 2) Identify differences. Next, we feed
253   both the detailed descriptions and the CAD images into the MLLM to generate editing instructions,
254
      summarizing necessary modifications in a clear and direct manner. The detailed description helps
      in mitigating the complexity of identifying changes solely from the images. 3) Compress instruc-
255
      tions. Lastly, we compress the editing instruction into a single sentence, limited to a maximum of 10
256
      words. This ensures that the instructions are precise yet concise, making them easier to understand.
257
258   Similarly, we also implement data filtering to ensure data quality. We filter out CAD pairs with too
259   many changes by excluding instructions with more than three edits. We also filter out pairs with no
260
      significant changes by excluding instructions containing phrases like “no transformation is needed”.
261   Finally, by assembling the CAD pairs from the first step and the textual instruction from the sec-
262   ond step, we derive a synthetic dataset Dsynthetic with triplets of 〈textual instruction, original CAD
263   sequence, edited CAD sequence〉. We then fine-tune pre-trained LLMs using Low-Rank Adapters
264   (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2022) on this synthetic data, resulting in a basic model for text-based editing
265   referred to as an initially fine-tuned LLM.

347   distance between a generated sample and its nearest neighbor in the real set. ”Jensen-Shannon
348   Divergence” (JSD) measures the similarity between real and generated distributions based on the
349   marginal point distribution. Additionally, we use D-CLIP, as described in Section 3.3, to assess how
350   well the changes in CAD models align with the editing instructions at the image level. It is important
351   to note that the output CAD command sequence does not always produce a valid 3D shape. In some
352   cases, the output commands may result in an invalid curve or other issues, making it impossible to
353
      extract a point cloud from that CAD model. Therefore, we also report the Valid Ratio, which is the
      percentage of output CAD models successfully rendered to an image.
354
355   Implementation Details. We use Llama-3-8b-Instruct as our base LLM, fine-tuning it for 70 epochs
356   using PyTorch’s Distributed Data Parallel (DDP) on 4 NVIDIA A6000-46GB SMX GPUs. The
357   initial learning rate is set to 1e-4 with a maximum token length of 1024. We employ Low-Rank
358   Adapters (LoRA) with a rank of 32. When employing the LLM for inference, we set the temperature
359
      as 0.9 and top-p as 0.9 to generate varied results in each trial.
360   Baselines. In our experiment, we compare our results with baseline methods, including: 1) previous
361   CAD design variation generation models such as SkexGen and Hnc-CAD, and 2) foundation models
362   not specifically tailored for CAD generation tasks, including closed-source commercial LLMs like
363   GPT-4o. We design two prompting methods: the first is a basic method that explains the design
364   rules of CAD operation sequences; the second is a dynamic 3-shot method, which, in addition to the
365
      basic explanation, includes the three most similar instructions based on text cosine similarity and
      their corresponding CAD pairs from the training set as in-context learning examples. The detailed
366
      prompt can be found in Appendix B.
