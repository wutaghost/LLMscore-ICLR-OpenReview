009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Chip design relies heavily on generating Boolean circuits, such as AND-Inverter
012            Graphs (AIGs), from functional descriptions like truth tables. This generation oper-
013            ation is a key process in logic synthesis, a primary chip design stage. While recent
014            advances in deep learning have aimed to accelerate circuit design, these efforts
015            have mostly focused on tasks other than synthesis, and traditional heuristic methods
016            have plateaued. In this paper, we introduce ShortCircuit, a novel transformer-based
017            architecture that leverages the structural properties of AIGs and performs efficient
018
               space exploration. Contrary to prior approaches attempting end-to-end generation
               of logic circuits using deep networks, ShortCircuit employs a two-phase process
019
               combining supervised with reinforcement learning to enhance generalization to
020
               unseen truth tables. We also propose an AlphaZero variant to handle the double
021            exponentially large state space and the reward sparsity, enabling the discovery of
022            near-optimal designs. To evaluate the generative performance of our model , we
023            extract 500 truth tables from a set of 20 real-world circuits. ShortCircuit success-
024            fully generates AIGs for 98% of the 8-input test truth tables, and outperforms the
025            state-of-the-art logic synthesis tool, ABC, by 18.62% in terms of circuits size.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   The rapid proliferation of AI has triggered an unprecedented surge in computational demands,
031   exceeding the capabilities of existing hardware and thereby becoming a major bottleneck to AI’s
032   continued growth. Chip design plays a pivotal role in enabling the next-generation of computing
033
      systems. However, traditional methodologies struggle to keep pace with the accelerating demands,
      underscoring the need for innovative chip design approaches to accelerate the design process and
034
      discover novel architectures. At its core, a chip is the physical embodiment of a Boolean function,
035
      transforming binary inputs into desired outputs. Creating these embodiments is facilitated by logic
036   synthesis, a crucial step in chip design that converts functional descriptions into directed graphs
037   comprising logic gates. The resulting circuit must balance competing objectives, including power
038   efficiency, performance, and silicon area (PPA), presenting a formidable optimization challenge. In
039   this paper, we investigate the use of Machine Learning (ML) to generate optimized digital circuits
040   directly from Boolean logic specifications, offering a fresh perspective on the chip design process.
041
      Truth tables provide a complete and unambiguous way of representing a Boolean function by
042   exhaustively enumerating the output values for all possible binary input combinations. As such, we
043   use truth tables as the input Boolean logic description for our problem. The output of our approach are
044   directed graphs (DAGs) in the form of an AND-Inverter Graphs (AIGs), a widely used data structure
045   in Electronic Design Automation (EDA) (Mishchenko and Brayton, 2006; Wolf et al., 2013). AIGs
046   consist of 2-input AND-gates as nodes, connected with normal or inverted wires, offering a simple,
047   scalable, and universal intermediate representation for various EDA applications. Their popularity in
048   the industry stems from their ability to efficiently represent complex Boolean functions, making them
049   an ideal choice for our ML-based circuit generation approach.
050   Recent efforts attempt to accelerate chip production by leveraging the development of ML methods
051   at different steps of EDA (Huang et al., 2021; Gubbi et al., 2022) , notably for placement (Ward
052   et al., 2012), routing (Alawieh et al., 2020), and for logic synthesis (Tu et al., 2024). Rather than
053   directly tackling the graph generation problem, most ML methods for logic synthesis focus on
      the optimization of synthesis recipes, which are sequences of operators acting on a logic graph to

054
      modify its structure while preserving the associated boolean function. More recently, deep-generative
055   methods emerged aiming to generate logic graphs rather than sequences of graph operators d’Ascoli
056   et al. (2024); Li et al. (2024a); Dong et al. (2023). The generative approach offers higher potential as
057   it offers more flexibility than working with a fixed set of operators; on the other hand, this approach
058   involves exploring a much larger space, due to the double exponential growth of the search space
059   with the number of Boolean function inputs.
060
      Such a vast search space renders traditional ML methods ineffective for optimal AIG generation.
061   However, recent advances have demonstrated that, with tailored model architectures and exploration-
062   exploitation-aware training protocols, remarkable performance can be achieved even in tasks involving
063   such large spaces. Indeed, we can attribute the success of methods like AlphaGO Silver et al. (2016),
064   AlphaZero Silver et al. (2017), AlphaFold Jumper et al. (2021) and even the emergence of large
065   language models Devlin et al. (2019); Kaplan et al. (2020) to the development of custom model
066   architectures capturing structural properties of board games, proteins, or language. Moreover,
067   the training of these models either leverages naturally abundant data or employs specific data-
068   augmentation and exploration-exploitation strategies to improve their performance.
069   In this work, we propose ShortCircuit, a new transformer-based architecture structurally adapted for
070   generating AND-Inverter graphs. Our transformer takes logic nodes represented as truth tables as
071   input, and each forward pass predicts the next AND node to create in order to realize a target truth
072   table. Moreover, we utilize an AlphaZero policy variant to effectively navigate the large state space
073   and discover more compact designs.
074   We summarize our contributions as follows. i) We formally define the challenging problem of
075   generating AIGs from target truth tables, characterized by a doubly exponential state space and a
076   quadratically expanding action space. ii) We introduce ShortCircuit, a novel AIG-aware transformer-
077   based architecture, enabling effective exploration of this vast search space. iii) We propose a two-step
078   training approach, combining supervised learning and reinforcement learning, to efficiently learn
079   generalizable patterns and prune the search space, improving solution quality and scalability. Finally,
080   iv) we empirically demonstrate the effectiveness of ShortCircuit by producing circuits 18.62% smaller
081
      compared to the state-of-the-art logic synthesis, tool ABC (Mishchenko et al., 2007), and showcase
      the potential of ML methods to revitalize the field of logic synthesis with a fresh perspective.
082

085   detail our proposed approach, notably our model architecture 4 and our tailored training procedure in
086   section 5. We finally present in section 6 an empirical evaluation of our method’s performance.

088   2     BACKGROUND
089
090   A digital circuit cascades logic gates to realize a Boolean function f : {0, 1}n → {0, 1}m , mapping
091   a Boolean vector of size n to a Boolean vector of size m. An And-Inverter Graph (AIG) is a Directed
092   Acyclic Graph (DAG) that is commonly used to represent a Boolean function at the early stage of the
093   chip design process, due to its simplicity and ubiquity.
094

099

130   2.2      T RUTH TABLES
131
132   As for any other logical graphs, we can capture the behavior of an AIG by propagating Boolean
133   values from its primary inputs to its primary outputs and applying the logical operations encountered
134   on the directed paths. Considering again the exemplar AIG from Fig. 1, if I1 = 1, I2 = 1, and
135
      I3 = 0, propagating the Boolean signals we can verify that the AIG output at O is 1. By enumerating
      all possible input combinations and recording the corresponding output values for each gate, we can
136
      build the AIG’s full truth table, as shown in Table 1. Each row corresponds to the values of the AIG
137
      nodes for a specific set of entries (I1 , I2 , I3 ) = (i1 , i2 , i3 ) ∈ {0, 1}3 displayed on the left part of the
138   table. We can extract from this representation a binary vector of size 2n for each AIG node. For
139   instance, the "truth table" vector representation of node ∧4 is (0 0 0 1 0 0 0 1)⊤ , and the primary
140   output one is (1 1 1 1 0 0 0 1)⊤ . After discussing related works in more details, we will explain in
141   the next section how our method utilizes this rich vector representation to perform AIG generation.

143   2.3      R ELATED WORKS
144
145   We discuss heuristics for AIG generation and ML methods for logic operator sequence optimization
146   in Appendix C, and focus here on the deep learning approaches tailored to logic graph generation.
147
148   Learning to Generate One Circuit at a Time A first approach to generate Boolean networks
149   with deep neural networks consists of substituting the gates and wires of a logic circuit by learnable
150   nodes and connections to form a neural network (Belcak and Wattenhofer, 2022; Zimmer et al., 2023;
151
      Hillier et al., 2023). The network parameters are learnt by minimizing the error made by forward
      passes compared to the target binary output. On the one hand, this method allows to cope with larger
152
      number of primary inputs as, instead of learning an entire family of logic graphs (e.g., the 8-input
153
      AIGs), it is specialized on one particular target truth table. On the other hand, it requires to train a new
154   neural network for each target truth table, representing a significant runtime bottleneck. Therefore,
155   several works inspired by the development of foundational generative models have followed another
156   direction, consisting in learning the synthesis process itself with a deep neural network.
157
158   Learning Circuit Synthesis using Deep Learning While Roy et al. (2021) use a CNN backbone
159   to generate prefix circuits by adding or deleting nodes in a N × N grid, Li et al. (2024b) employ
160   an auto-regressive diffusion model to generate DAG for high-level synthesis stage , and Dong et al.
161   (2023) design a two-level GNN architecture to synthesize analog circuits that work with non-binary
      signals. Closer to our work are Boolformer (d’Ascoli et al., 2024) and Circuit Transformer (CT) (Li


                                                                                  3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      et al., 2024a), both tackling digital network synthesis with an auto-regressive transformer-based
163   architecture. They train their policies with a supervised training phase, predicting the next element
164   of a logic graph given a target truth table, and doing inference through beam-search or MCTS
165   simulations. Contrary to our work, Boolformer and CT use a symbolic representation of the Boolean
166   formula associated to a logic graph that is encoded via depth-first search. Therefore instead of
167   representing already built nodes by their truth tables and predicting the next node to add to the graph,
168   they tokenize the symbols and let their model generate the next symbol of the logic formula (a logical
169   operation, an input or an output), which requires more forward passes than for our method to produce
170   a similar AIG. Besides, given a target truth table T⋆ , Boolformer directly takes as input the boolean
171
      representation of T⋆ as we do, while Li et al. (2024a) pass a (non-optimal) AIG realizing T⋆ to the
      CT to generate an improved logic network.

174   3     P ROBLEM D EFINITION
175
176   Truth tables encompass the results for all possible input values, but do not provide sufficient structural
177   information to derive an AIG with the corresponding output representation. Heuristics that generate a
178   Boolean expression, or equivalently an AIG, from a truth table generally lead to exponentially large
179
      solutions that need further refinement. Consequently, solving this AIG generation problem would
      significantly impact digital circuit design.
180
181   Problem Definition: Given a target truth table T⋆ ∈ {0, 1}n , construct an AIG with the minimum
182   number of nodes, such that its output node O has a truth table TO that matches T⋆ .
183   Note that the size of a truth table associated with  an n-input AIG is 2n , thus, the set containing all
                                                     2n
184   truth tables of size 2 has a cardinality of 2 , which corresponds to a magnitude of 1019 for the
                             n
185   space of truth tables that a 6-input AIG can represent. Exploring such a large space efficiently requires
186   developing specific models and training techniques. These must exploit the structural properties of
187   the problem while managing the exploration-exploitation trade-off inherent in such scenarios.
188
189   3.1   S TATE R EPRESENTATION & N OTATIONS
190
191   We formally define an AIG with n inputs as a graph G = (V, E), where V and E represent the node
192   and edge sets. To capture the dynamic nature of AIG construction, we introduce a temporal parameter,
193
      t, which simultaneously represents the current time step and the number of AND-nodes in the graph,
      denoted as Gt = (Vt , Et ). This allows us to model the evolution of the AIG over time, with the graph
194
      growing as new AND-nodes are added. We assign a unique integer ID to each node in the graph,
195
      regardless of its type; thus, at time t the node set is Vt = {I1 , . . . , In , ∧n+1 , . . . , ∧n+t }, or with a
196   node-type agnostic notation, Vt = {v1 , . . . , vn+t }. Following this notation, the graph G0 represents
197   an AIG containing only input nodes, i.e., with V0 = {I1 , I2 , ..., In }.
198
199
      In our generative process, we encode the state corresponding to AIG Gt as a 3-tuple st = (Tt , T⋆ , At ).
      Here, Tt = {T1 , T2 , ..., Tn+t } is the set of truth tables associated with the current nodes, T⋆ is the
200
      target truth table, and At = {an+1 , an+2 , ..., an+t } is the ordered set of actions performed so far.
201
      Each action generates a new AND-node by connecting two existing nodes in one of four possible
202   configurations: (vi , vj ), (vi , ¬vj ), (¬vi , vj ), and (¬vi , ¬vj ). Our goal is to perform a series of
203   N actions, transforming G0 into a terminal AIG GN such that the truth table of the last generated
204   AND-node, Tn+N , matches or is the negation of the target truth table T⋆ . Note that our environment
205   is stateful, as its history influences future decisions. Furthermore, our environment poses an additional
206   challenge due to its action space expanding quadratically with each new node we add.
207

213   it outputs a probability distribution over the set of AND-nodes that can be built by combining any
214   pair of already existing nodes, taking edge types into account. Formally, let |Vt | denote the number
215   of nodes in the current state of the graph, then the action space is a 4 × |Vt | × |Vt | tensor, where each
      |Vt | × |Vt | slice corresponds to a connection type. Specifically, the cell (i, j) in a given slice indicates

258   Positional Encoding (PE) In natural language processing (NLP) tasks, PE allows transformers to
259
      capture a sequential relation in the inputs. In our setting, we do not need to hint the model about the
      graph structure graph, as the truth tables already convey all the necessary information. Moreover,
260
      the self-attention operation should treat every node equally, as there is no limitation regarding which
261
      two nodes can be combined to form a new node. Nevertheless, the model should be able to treat
262   differently the built nodes and the target truth table, leading us to introduce two learnable positional
263   encoders to distinguish them (“Node PE” on Fig. 2).
264
265   Policy Module Transformers are the state-of-the-art architecture to handle sequential data. These
266   models particularly shine when trained to predict the next token in a sequence by outputting a
267   fixed-size tensor representing a sampling probability over a token glossary. In our case though, the
268   set of nodes that we can build grows at each step, as more pairs of nodes can be combined to produce
269   the next node. Inspired by NLP tasks, for which attention scores among related tokens are high, we
      use attention to guide next node generation. Thus, we directly use the final self-attention map of

270
      four parallel policy modules to get the probability to connect any two nodes with specific edge types.
271   Each policy module has P transformer encoder layers and outputs a final self-attention layer. In
272   the last self-attention layer, we exclude the entry corresponding to the target node and returns the
273   self-attention scores based on the existing AIG nodes embeddings. We aggregate the scores from the
274   four policy modules and mask the ones associated to already built nodes and their negate versions.
275   We finally apply a softmax to the remaining scores to produce a single probability distribution.
276
277   Value Module The value module is a critical component to assess how favorable a state is and
278   therefore prevent expanding unpromising states. Our value module consists of S many transformer
279   encoder layers. As the quality of a state not only depends on the nodes that are present in the graph at
280   that stage, but also on the target truth table that should be realized, the value module also uses the
281
      two learnable type-based positional encoders introduced above. After performing the embedding, we
      compute the cross-attention between the graph nodes and the target truth table, which yields a new
282
      vector representation of the target. Finally, we feed this vector to a linear layer producing a single
283
      value, which should reflect the quality of the current state.

286   5     T RAINING S HORT C IRCUIT
287
288   The sparse nature of the problem makes it practically impossible to discover functionally correct
289
      graphs when exploring the double exponential state space uniformly. This challenge necessitates
      a more effective training approach for our ShortCircuit. To address this, we propose a two-stage
290
      training regimen consisting of a supervised pre-training stage to initialize the policy module, followed
291
      by an AlphaZero-style fine-tuning phase to improve the policy module and train the value module.
292   Although pre-training the policy module provides a good prior to predict the most useful next actions,
293   simply following it does not guarantee that an AIG matching T⋆ will be constructed due to the
294   inherent difficulty of the problem. This limitation highlights the importance of the fine-tuning stage,
295   which aims to refine the policy module and leverage the value module for improved performance.
296
297   5.1   P RE -T RAINING
298
299   Just as large language models in NLP are pre-trained using next-token prediction, we can pre-train our
300   transformer model on a next-node prediction task. This approach requires a corpus of single-output
301
      AIGs that our model can learn to regenerate node by node, starting from the truth tables of the
      primary inputs and a target T⋆ . Since the AIG generation problem has not yet been addressed by
302
      the ML community, there is no large, well-structured corpus of publicly available (truth table, AIG)
303
      pairs to perform this pre-training. Therefore, we first curate a dataset of single-output AIGs for the
304   pre-training of ShortCircuit by leveraging existing open-source collections of digital circuits.
305
306   Data Extraction To generate an AIG dataset with the desired input and output sizes, we utilize
307   the EPFL benchmarks (Amarú et al., 2015), which contain a collection of 20 real circuits realizing
308   arithmetic and control functions. On average, the arithmetic and control circuits have 175 inputs and
309   137 outputs with 22520 AND-nodes, as detailed on Table 4 and 5. Since these circuits have more
310   inputs than the AIGs we aim to generate, we extract subgraphs, or cuts, from them. A cut refers to
311   a connected subset of nodes in the AIG that divides the graph into two disjoint parts. The root of
312   a cut is the node to which all directed paths within the cut converge to and a leaf node is a node in
313
      the cut that have at least one fanin outside of the cut. By design, a cut forms a single-output AIG
      with a number of inputs corresponding to the number of leaves. We defer to Appendix B.2 the full
314
      description of the cut extraction method we develop to build a dataset of single-output AIGs.
315
316

      Pre-Training Flow With the prepared augmented data, we can proceed to train our policy module to
366

367
      with KL divergence and cross-entropy, both of which measure the distance between two probability
368   distributions. In practice, KL divergence loss yielded better results. Besides, the backbone of our
369   model being a transformer, we implement a custom masking strategy during training. to maintain
370   causality in the auto-regressive generation process. Since the primary inputs and the target truth table
371   are available from the start, and as there is no causality for their existence, we allow full attention for
372   their embeddings, and only apply a causal mask for the rest of the nodes.

374   5.2    F INE -T UNING
375
376   Fine-tuning aims to align the value and policy module so they operate effectively together. Unlike the
377   policy module, we cannot properly initialize the value module during pre-training as the generated
      dataset only contains successful examples, which would mislead the value module to consider that


                                                                      7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      all states are “good”. Skipping pre-training, though, would lead to a random exploration of the vast
379   search space of truth tables, which would likely result in encountering only “bad” states, preventing
380   the model from learning what a “good” state is. Therefore, the most viable option to train our value
381   module is through experience, by performing searches with a pre-trained policy module. We utilize
382   AlphaZero as the orchestration framework to refine the policy and value modules.
383
384   AlphaZero AlphaZero has demonstrated remarkable success in board games with enormous
385   state spaces, such as chess (1044 ) and Go (10170 ). Since truth tables features similar state space
386   problem, we adapt AlphaZero’s effective search and pruning capabilities to navigate AIG generation.
387   By combining a policy module to propose actions and a value module to evaluate state viability,
388   AlphaZero strikes a balance between exploitation and exploration. We adapt and modify the selection
389
      strategy, predictor upper confidence bound applied to trees (PUCT) used by AlphaZero, as follows:
390
                                                                           pP
                                                                                a N (s, a)
391                    PUCT(s, a) = Q(s, a) + b Q(s, a; θ) + c P (s, a; θ)
                                                                            N (s, a) + 1
392
393   where, Q(s, a) represents the propagated discounted discovered reward, while Q(s, a; θ) represents
394   the predicted expected Q-value, P (s, a; θ) is the policy module’s probability distribution, N (s, a)
395   tracks state visitations, and b and c are parameters balancing exploration and exploitation. Computing
396   Q(st , a; θ) for every action is too expensive, so we initialize Q(st , a) = Q(st , a; θ) = 0, perform
397
      the action that maximizes PUCT(st , a), and only compute the value of the state Q(st+1 ) once we
      visit it. The term Q(s, a) + bQ(s, a; θ) represents the exploitation in PUCT, as if during search
398
      our method discovers a "good" state or a terminal state, we exploit it and focus the search locally
399
      to
      √P discover more compact designs. The term P (s, a; θ) suggests actions to perform, but the term
400
           a N (s,a)/(N (s,a)+1) promotes exploration.
401
402   AlphaZero stores intermediate results and metadata, such as Q(s, a), Q(s, a; θ), P (s, a; θ), and
403   N (s, a), in the nodes visited during MCTS. These nodes are associated with states and form a tree,
404   where edges indicate the actions performed to reach each node-state pair. When simulation starts,
405
      we mark the initial state as the root node, compute the action distribution, and inject Dirichlet noise.
      During simulation, AlphaZero follows PUCT to choose actions and continues until meeting one of the
406
      three following stopping conditions: encountering a state s that is not expanded, reaching a maximum
407
      number of steps, or arriving at a terminal state. If the state is not expanded, we need to compute
408   Q(s; θ) and P (s, a; θ) for that state and we back-propagate Q(s; θ) to the previous MCTS nodes, and
409   increment N (s, a). Once we complete the given number of simulations, AlphaZero applies the most
410   visited action, argmaxa∈A N (s, a). In our case, we rather follow the observed discounted reward
411   argmaxa∈A Q(s, a) as we find the visitation count signal too noisy given our simulation budget.
412
413   Fine-Tuning Flow Generating trajectories for millions of truth tables is computationally challeng-
414   ing. Thus, to best exploit our resources, our fine-tuning regimen consists of data collection and model
415   training processes. These data collectors generate trajectories and add their findings to a fixed length
416   replay buffer. Under the hood, the data collectors store the metadata, including truth tables and discov-
417   ered reward Q(s), of the MCTS root node for each step in the trajectory in the replay buffer. Successful
418
      trajectories receive a reward of 1, while failed ones receive − min (hd (TN , T⋆ ), hd (TN , ¬T⋆ )), where
      hd is the Hamming distance and TN is the last generated truth table.
419
420   The trainer process randomly samples data from the replay buffer and uses the truth tables as input
421   for the model. Since the value module aims to predict the Q-value of a state, the goal is to minimize
422   the mean squared error (MSE) between the predicted value and the retrieved Q(s). The target
      distribution for the policy module is the normalized number of visitations N (s,a)/ a N (s,a). Similar to
                                                                                         P
423
424
      pre-training, we minimize the KL-divergence between the output of the policy module and the target
      probability distribution. Finally, the trainer process broadcasts the updated weights asynchronously
425
      to the data collectors after a given number of training steps.

      7     C ONCLUSION
525
526
      In this paper, we introduced ShortCircuit, a novel transformer-based architecture for generating
527
      AIGs from a target truth table. Our approach combines a structurally aware transformer model with
528   an AlphaZero-inspired policy variant, enabling efficient navigation through the doubly exponential
529   state space associated with truth tables. In our experiments, we demonstrated the effectiveness of
530   ShortCircuit in producing high-quality AIGs that are significantly smaller than those generated by
531   one of the state-of-the-art logic synthesis tools ABC and the trained Boolformer. Specifically, our
532   method achieved a relative size reduction of 18.62%, and 15.13% respectively.
533
      This work contributes to the expanding field of ML applications in chip design, showcasing the
534   potential of deep learning to revitalize the field with new perspectives. We demonstrated that it
535   is possible to generate high-quality AIGs from truth tables, paving the way for future research in
536   this area. Future work will focus on extending ShortCircuit to handle AIGs with multiple outputs,
537   integrating our approach with existing logic synthesis tools, and exploring its application in industrial
538   settings. Finally, our goal is to enable the creation of more efficient, scalable, and innovative
539   computing systems, and we believe that ShortCircuit is an important step towards realizing this
      vision.

      B.2   C UT EXTRACTION
828
829
830   To extract a cut from a given node with a target number of inputs n, we designate the given node as
831   the root and of the cut and its two parents as the initial leaf set. We then iteratively remove a random
832   node from the leaf set and add its parents to the leaf set while maintaining the leaf property. This
833   process continues until the leaf set contains n nodes. Finally, we create an AIG out of the visited
834
      nodes within the cut where we mark the leaf set as the inputs and the root node as the output. We
      provide the outline of the procedure in Algorithm 1.
835
836
837
838   Algorithm 1 Cut Extraction
839   Require: Root node: r, number of cut inputs: n
840    1: leaf_set = {left_parent(r), right_parent(r)}
841    2: node_set = {r}
842    3: while size(leaf_set) < n do
843    4:    node = leaf_set.random_pop()
844    5:    node_set.insert(node)
845    6:    leaf_set.insert(left_parent(node)) & leaf_set.insert(right_parent(node))
846    7:    Ensure leaf property in leaf_set
847
       8: Construct AIG from leaf_set and node_set
848
849
850
      We can modify this algorithm to extract additional cuts per node by repeating the process until
851
      we find a cut with n − 1 leaf nodes. For this cut, instead of randomly expanding a leaf node, we
852   create n − 1 copies of the cut and expand each leaf node individually, storing the resulting cuts.
853   In practice, we actually employ Algorithm 2 to extract AIGs from the EPFL circuits. Although
854   Algorithm 1 conveys the core idea of AIG extraction, the following algorithm is more effective from
855   an engineering standpoint, as it allows to extract more cuts from the same node.
856
      The revised algorithm takes two inputs: n, the desired number of input nodes, and the root node. It
857
      initializes the leaf set by adding the parents of the root node. It then iteratively removes a random
858   node from the leaf set and expands it using Algorithm 3. This process continues until the cut contains
859   n − 1 leaf nodes, at which point we create n − 1 copies of the current cut state and expand each leaf
860   node to generate n unique cuts. For brevity, we omitted the details of ensuring the leaf property after
861   node expansion in the main text, which is addressed in Algorithm 4. This algorithm ensures that no
862   node in the leaf set has a parent also in this set, which would violate the leaf property. If a parent is
863   already in the leaf set, we can remove the node from the set and add the other parent that is not yet in
      the leaf set.

      C.1   H EURISTICS FOR AIG G ENERATION AND O PTIMIZATION
898   As the inference of CNFs using exact SAT solvers often lead to exponentially large expressions,
899   various heuristics such as Karnaugh maps (Karnaugh, 1953), or Quine-McCluskey methods (Quine,
900   1952; 1955; McCluskey Jr., 1956)), and algorithms (Rudell and Sangiovanni-Vincentelli, 1987) have
901   been designed to obtain more compact expressions or circuits. Further efforts accompanying the
902   rise in chip demand led to the development of widely used logic synthesis libraries that implement
903   equivalence-preserving Boolean network operators. The open-source library ABC (Mishchenko et al.,
904   2007)) notably comprises dozens of logic graph operators aiming at reducing a network size or depth
905   (Mishchenko et al., 2011; Mishchenko and Brayton, 2006). Interestingly, some important operators
906
      such as resub or rewrite (Darringer et al., 1981; Mishchenko et al., 2006) acts on the subject
      graph through a series of local modifications involving small single-output AIGs. Besides, applying a
907
      single operator on a logic network is suboptimal compared to applying several operators sequentially,
908
      though finding the best sequence is also a hard problem (Riener et al., 2019).
909
910
      C.2   M ACHINE L EARNING FOR L OGIC S YNTHESIS
911
912   Many ML approaches have been explored to tackle the operator flow optimization progress. Some
913   stateless optimization methods, such as Bayesian optimization (Grosnit et al., 2022; Feng et al., 2022),
914   search for the best flow without considering the subject graph specificities. Alternatively, state-based
915   methods formulate the operator sequence optimization as a Reinforcement learning problem, and
916   train policies on selected features of the logic network. While some works use high-level statistics of
917   the subject graph (e.g., its number of nodes) (Hosny et al., 2020; Zhou and Anderson, 2023; Qian
      et al., 2024), others rely on tailored graph convolutional networks (GCN) to extract richer features at


                                                        17

918
      the cost of a longer training time (Haaswijk et al., 2018; Peruvemba et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2020;
919   Basak Chowdhury et al., 2023). Similarly, standard deep network architectures, such as CNNs (Yu
920   et al., 2018), LSTMs (Yu and Zhou, 2020), or GCNs (Wu et al., 2022) have been trained to predict
921   the quality of a logic synthesis flow in a supervised way. Contrary to these works, we target AIG
922   generation itself and not operator optimization.
