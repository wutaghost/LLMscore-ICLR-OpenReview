009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Grokking, or the delayed generalization phenomenon, describes the abrupt and
012            rapid improvement in test accuracy that occurs after a model has been overfitted
013            for a prolonged period. This phenomenon was first identified by Power in the
014            context of operations on a prime number field. Over the past two years, a range
015            of mathematical analyses has been conducted to investigate grokking, typically
016
               involving the use of the hidden progress measure which mean a function that can
               anticipate the occurrence of grokking. We believe that a comprehensive and rig-
017
               orous mathematical modeling approach can invigorate the research on this task
018
               and provide a unified perspective for understanding previous research. This paper
019            introduces a novel approach by modeling the task as a unique dynamical system.
020            Using mathematical derivation within this framework, we propose a robust hid-
021            den progress measure that effectively captures the grokking phenomenon across
022            all operations on prime number fields. This approach not only provides a more
023            complete understanding but also offers deeper insights into the underlying archi-
024            tecture of the model. Based on this understanding, we also proposed a method to
025            accelerate grokking without involving regularization or altering the model archi-
026            tecture.
027
028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   For a long time, it has been widely believed that model overfitting results from an excessive reliance
031   on biases within the dataset, causing the model to lose its ability to generalize to new data. However,
032   the observation of grokking (Power et al. (2022)) challenges this understanding. Grokking offers a
033   new perspective on the training process, suggesting that improvements in training accuracy may
034   only indicate a superficial ”mastery” of the task by the model.
035
      In recent years, numerous attempts have been made to explain grokking, typically following a pattern
036   of hypothesis formulation - theorem construction - experimental validation. From a mathematical
037   perspective, these efforts are phenomenological, lacking a deep exploration of the origins of the
038   theoretical tools employed. To fully understand the grokking phenomenon, it may be necessary
039   to undertake more structured work, specifically the development of a comprehensive mathematical
040   model. Such a model would situate the problem within an established mathematical framework,
041   thereby providing access to a variety of powerful analytical tools.
042   At first, Our work focused on placing the task where grokking happens, within the framework of
043   dynamical systems. This allows us to utilize the tools of dynamical systems to examine the mathe-
044   matical structure of the entire task. By studying the properties of the phase space of the dynamical
045   system, we aim to further analyze the reasons behind the occurrence of grokking in these tasks. In
046   Figure 1, we illustrate our understanding of the aforementioned concept, where we posit that work
047   at the structural level should be prioritized.
048   A complete mathematical framework has allowed us to re-evaluate previous approaches to analyzing
049   grokking. We acknowledge the existence of a hidden progress measure (Nanda et al. (2022)), which
050   is a function embedded within the model’s update process that can accurately represent grokking.
051   We re-modeled the task using a dynamical systems approach, which allowed us to anchor the math-
052   ematical background of multiple hidden progress measures. Ultimately, based on our analysis of
053   the specific dynamical system associated with this task, we have proposed a robust and elegant hid-
      den progress measure. This measure can precisely track the occurrence of grokking and does not

095   2.1   G ROKKING
096
097   Grokking was first proposed in Power et al. (2022), which happened on a model of a two-layers
098   transformer decoder to solve several algorithmic tasks. Initial understanding of grokking focused
099
      on the size of the dataset and some studies proposed the concept of ’critical dataset size’ like Zhu
      et al. (2024) and Huang et al. (2024). The essence of these methods is to explain mutation behavior
100
      through the linear variation of dataset size. Some studies have also recognized that grokking might
101
      be a common phenomenon in classification tasks, prompting researchers to approach the problem
102   from a structural perspective like Liu et al. (2022) and Thilak et al. (2022) . However, the number of
103   classification tasks in which the grokking phenomenon has been observed is limited, leading some to
104   speculate that grokking is a result of the Transformer architecture, as discussed Wang et al. (2024).
105   Some studies also focus on changing the architecture to investigate variations in the phenomenon like
106   Park et al. (2024), Kunin et al. (2024) and Lee et al. (2024). And there have always been researchers
107   who associate grokking with emergence, and notable work in this area includes: Mallinar et al.
      (2024), He et al. (2024), Zhao et al. (2024).

122   2.2   P ROGRESS M EASURE
123
124   The concept of a progress measure was first introduced in Barak et al. (2022), essentially as a
125   smooth function of continuous changes in activation values used to predict model behavior. One
126   of perspectives is based on frequency and Fourier coefficients, inspired by circuit signal analysis,
127
      with representative papers including Nanda et al. (2022), Zhou et al. (2024) and Furuta et al. (2024).
      The second perspective is local complexity, derived from linear region analysis, with representative
128
      papers including Humayun et al. (2024). The third perspective is based on information theory and
129
      its representative paper is Clauw et al. (2024). Recently, some methods have aimed to provide a
130   more unified perspective like Yunis et al. (2024) and Song et al. (2024).

      4.3   M AIN E MBEDDING D IFF
305
306   In this section, we introduce our designed hidden progress measure that we call main embedding
307   diff and its properties. Based on this, we provide an in-depth analysis of grokking, which will be
308   validated in Section 5.
309   First, we provide a precise definition of the hidden progress measure for deep learning models. The
310   focus is on formalizing the qualitative description of this concept presented in Barak et al. (2022).
311
      Definition 5 (Hidden progress measure). Let the complete set of parameters of a deep learning
312
      model be denoted by W, and the update step by n. Given a function f : W × Z+ → R , if there
313
      exists a mapping ϕ ◦ f : W × Z+ → {0, 1} such that ϕ ◦ f takes the value of 1 when a specific
314   phenomenon occurs and 0 otherwise, then we call f a hidden progress measure of this specific
315   phenomenon of the model.
316
317   We now introduce the methodology by which we derived our progress measure. We define an input
                 −1
318   region as Fc,i,j,p ({1}) and interpret the task as a mapping from an input region to Vi . The input
319   region must satisfy the requirements outlined in Theorem 3.
320   Theorem 4 (Main result). We divide the aforementioned task into two parts: the first part involves
321   encoding using an embedding matrix, while the second part employs a transformer to decode and
322   perform classification. The purpose of the latter part is to establish the region mapping described
323   in Theorem 3, whereas the former part ensures that the input meets the requirements of the input
      region for the latter part.

      In this paper, we propose moving beyond a phenomenon-driven approach to theorem-based thinking
532
      and instead advocate for the use of structured mathematical tools. Based on this belief, we re-
533

534   that the cause of the grokking phenomenon is that, when the optimization task has a simple phase

      C.2   L OCAL COMPLEXITY
919
920   Unlike Fourier decomposition, which aligns directly with the classical approach to studying dynam-
921   ical systems, the concept of local complexity corresponds more closely to the method known as
922   Poincaré sections in dynamical system analysis.
923
      The Poincaré section method is a fundamental technique in the study of dynamical systems, offering
924
      a way to reduce the dimensional complexity of continuous systems by examining their intersections
925   with a lower-dimensional subspace. This method involves selecting a hyperplane, referred to as the
926   Poincaré section, which the system’s trajectories intersect transversely. By analyzing the sequence
927   of intersection points, one can study discrete dynamics and capture essential features of the system’s
928   behavior, such as periodic orbits, quasi-periodic motions, and chaotic trajectories. The Poincaré
929   section thus serves as a powerful tool for visualizing and understanding the qualitative nature of
930   dynamical systems, providing insights into stability, bifurcations, and the global structure of phase
931   space. In the context of learning algorithms and complex systems, applying Poincaré sections helps
932   identify invariant sets and analyze long-term behavior, thereby enhancing our capacity to predict
933
      and control system dynamics.
934   Definition 12. Let Φt : M → M denote a smooth dynamical system defined by the flow Φt (x),
935   where x ∈ M and M is an n-dimensional differentiable manifold (phase space). A Poincaré section
936   Σ ⊂ M is a codimension-one submanifold (i.e., of dimension n − 1) such that:
937
938         1. Σ is transverse to the flow Φt (x), meaning that at every point x ∈ Σ, the flow vector

944   defined by the intersections of the trajectories of the original flow with the Poincaré section Σ. For
945
      a point x ∈ Σ, the Poincaré map P (x) is the point of the next intersection of the trajectory through
      x with Σ. Formally,

952   Theorem 9. Let x∗ ∈ Σ be a fixed point of the Poincaré map P : Σ → Σ, i.e., P (x∗ ) = x∗ .
953   Then, x∗ corresponds to a periodic orbit of the original flow Φt . The stability of the periodic orbit
954   is determined by the eigenvalues of the derivative DP (x∗ ) of the Poincaré map at x∗ .
955   If all the eigenvalues of DP (x∗ ) have magnitudes less than one, the periodic orbit is stable; if any
956   eigenvalue has a magnitude greater than one, the periodic orbit is unstable.
957
958   In light of the local complexity defined in Humayun et al. (2024), this concept treats the hierarchy of
959   deep networks as a nested chain of affine transformations. Let us consider an affine transformation
960   from one layer to the next, given by y = W x + b. Using wi to represent the i-th row vector of W ,
961   we can define a family of hyperplanes {wi x + bi = 0}. The local complexity is then expressed by
962   the number of hyperplanes intersecting the convex hull formed by the samples.
963   The Poincaré section indicates that an increase in local complexity corresponds to an increase in
964   the density of trajectories on the hyperplane. When the trajectory density approaches a stable value,
965   it often signifies that the dynamical system has converged to a stable solution, representing the
966   completion of generalization.
