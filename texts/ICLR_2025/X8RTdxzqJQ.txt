009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Learning effective data representations is crucial in answering if two samples X
012
               and Y are from the same distribution (a.k.a. the non-parametric two-sample testing
013
               problem), which can be categorized into: i) learning discriminative representations
014            (DRs) that distinguish between two samples in a supervised-learning paradigm, and
015            ii) learning inherent representations (IRs) focusing on data’s inherent features in an
016            unsupervised-learning paradigm. However, both paradigms have issues: learning
017            DRs reduces the data points available for the two-sample testing phase, and learning
018            purely IRs misses discriminative cues. To mitigate both issues, we propose a novel
019            perspective to consider non-parametric two-sample testing as a semi-supervised
020            learning (SSL) problem, introducing the SSL-based Classifier Two-Sample Test
021            (SSL-C2ST) framework. While a straightforward implementation of SSL-C2ST
022
               might directly use existing state-of-the-art (SOTA) SSL methods to train a classifier
               with labeled data (with sample indexes X or Y) and unlabeled data (the remaining
023
               ones in the two samples), conventional two-sample testing data often exhibits
024
               substantial overlap between samples and violates SSL methods’ assumptions,
025            resulting in low test power. Therefore, we propose a two-step approach: first, learn
026            IRs using all data, then fine-tune IRs with only labelled data to learn DRs, which
027            can both utilize information from whole dataset and adapt the discriminative power
028            to the given data. Extensive experiments and theoretical analysis demonstrate that
029            SSL-C2ST outperforms traditional C2ST by effectively leveraging unlabeled data.
030            We also offer a stronger empirically designed test achieving the SOTA performance
031            in many two-sample testing datasets.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036
037   Two-sample tests aim to solve the problem of “Whether two samples are drawn from the same
038   distribution?”. Classical two-sample tests, including t-tests which test the empirical mean differences
039
      between two samples, often need to assume that samples are drawn from specific distributions (e.g.,
      Gaussian distributions with the same variance). To alleviate the strict assumptions, non-parametric
040
      two-sample tests are proposed to solve the problem only based on observed data (Gretton et al.,
041
      2012a;b; Heller & Heller, 2016; Székely & Rizzo, 2013; Jitkrittum et al., 2016; Chen & Friedman,
042   2017; Ghoshdastidar et al., 2017; Lopez-Paz & Oquab, 2018b; Ramdas et al., 2017; Sutherland et al.,
043   2017; Gao et al., 2018; Ghoshdastidar & von Luxburg, 2018; Lerasle et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2020;
044   Kirchler et al., 2020; Kübler et al., 2020; Cheng & Xie, 2021; Kübler et al., 2022; Kübler et al., 2022;
045   Liu et al., 2021; Deka & Sutherland, 2023; Bonnier et al., 2023).
046
      For example, the Kolmogorov-Smirnov (K-S) test is designed to compare the cumulative distribution
047
      functions derived from two samples, but it can only be effective in extremely low-dimensional data
048   (Kolmogorov, 1933; Smirnov, 1948). The maximum mean discrepancy (MMD) test adopts the kernel
049   mean embedding of distribution and uses it to measure the discrepancy between two distributions
050   Gretton et al. (2012a). The statistics used in these non-parametric two-sample tests are also widely
051   adopted in many other fields, such as domain adaptation, causal discovery, generative modeling,
052   adversarial learning, and more (Gong et al., 2016; Bińkowski et al., 2018; Stojanov et al., 2019; Cano
053   & Krawczyk, 2020; Oneto et al., 2020; Gao et al., 2021; Fang et al., 2021b; Zhong et al., 2021; Fang
      et al., 2021a; Song et al., 2021a; Tahmasbi et al., 2021; Taskesen et al., 2021; Bergamin et al., 2022).

      To improve the test power of non-parametric two-sample tests in practical applications, recent studies
055   have shown that learning good data representations is crucial before performing two-sample testing
056   (Kirchler et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2020; 2021; Gao et al., 2021; Bergamin et al., 2022). For example,
057   Kirchler et al. (2020) directly use a pre-trained feature extractor to extract features of two samples
058   and find it is useful to increase the test power during the testing. Meanwhile, Liu et al. (2020) propose
059   a unified learning paradigm to learn deep-net representations of data via maximizing the test power of
060   MMD and show that the learned representations can help capture the difference between two samples.
061   Recently, Biggs et al. (2023) point out that, after discarding the sample information (namely, we do
062   not know which sample the data belongs to), learning representations from whole samples will not
063
      influence the type I error of permutation-based testing methods, which further justifies the correctness
      of learning good representations for testing.

085   Although the supervised paradigm has obtained success in many fields (Gao et al., 2021; Bergamin
086
      et al., 2022), we have to use part of samples to train a good classifier (Lopez-Paz & Oquab, 2018b)
      or a kernel function (Liu et al., 2020), which will cause fewer samples are used in the final testing
087
      procedure. Namely, the procedure of splitting samples into training and testing sets will naturally
088
      lower the test power. There has to be a trade-off between the extra power provided by the learned
089   functions/kernels and the sacrificed power due to the sample-splitting procedure. For example, Biggs
090   et al. (2023) recently reveal that combining several pre-defined kernels on the whole samples can
091   provide higher test power compared to deep-kernel MMD test (Liu et al., 2020) on some datasets,
092   indicating that, in some cases, the sacrificed power might be higher than the extra power provided by
093   learned functions or kernels.
094
      In the unsupervised paradigm, researchers try to develop testing methods that do not need the data-
095   splitting procedure. To avoid sacrificing power from the data-splitting procedure, Kübler et al. (2020)
096   propose a new testing method based on the linear-time estimator of MMD and the selective inference
097   framework. Because Kübler et al. (2020) use a linear-time estimator of MMD, there is a test-power
098   reduction compared to the U-statistic or V-statistic of MMD. Then, Schrab et al. (2023) and Biggs
099   et al. (2023) propose new ways to combine several kernels in a given candidate set and perform the
100   two-sample testing directly on the whole samples. Empirical experiments support that their newly
101   proposed statistic, MMD-FUSE, enjoys even higher test power than the most effective method in the
102   first paradigm given a good candidate set. However, there is still an open question in this paradigm:
103
      can we always expect a relatively good kernel in the candidate set for any given two samples?
104   Motivation. Based on the development of the two-sample testing methods reviewed above, it can
105   be seen that both the supervised paradigm and unsupervised paradigm have their own issues. For
106   the supervised paradigm, we have to use a relatively large amount of data to ensure that we can
107   learn a good function or kernel, resulting in a possibly higher sacrificed power. For the unsupervised
      paradigm, a good candidate set is key but we do not have supervision to find such a candidate set.

124    Thus, to obtain a better two-sample testing method, we might aim to reduce the requirement of a
125    large training set in the supervised paradigm or to provide supervision to find a good kernel in the
126    unsupervised paradigm. The first aim is quite similar to the advantage that semi-supervised learning
127    (SSL) can bring in classification: Given many unlabeled data, SSL methods can help us obtain a good
128
       classifier even the training set is small (Balcan & Blum, 2010).
129    Our contributions. In this paper, we revisit the non-parametric two-sample testing as an SSL
130    problem and propose an SSL-based classifier two-sample test (SSL-C2ST). SSL-C2ST extends the
131    state-of-the-art (SOTA) two-sample testing method C2ST by incorporating SSL methods.
132    In our experiments, we firstly implemented several SOTA SSL methods, depending on different SSL
133    frameworks, such as consistency regularization (CR) (Xie et al., 2020), pseudo labeling (PL) (Lee
134    et al., 2013), generative models (GM) (Kingma & Welling, 2013), and hybrid methods (HB) (Sohn
135    et al., 2020), within the C2ST framework. However, the result was not satisfactory (see Table 1),
136    because two-sample testing fundamentally differs from typical classification tasks; it is a problem
137    of distinguishing between two distributions (or saying two samples) rather than an instance-level
138    classification. Furthermore, the high degree of overlap between the two samples in testing dataset
139    challenges the basic assumptions of these SSL methods, such as HDGM in Figure 2b and Figure 2c,
140
       where two distributions are largely overlapped, violating the assumptions of many SSL methods.
141    This violation of assumptions leads SSL methods to have low test power in two-sample testing. Our
142    method, SSL-C2ST, is implemented in two phases. At first, we learn the IRs from whole dataset in
143    an unsupervised autoencoder-based representation learning (Tschannen et al., 2018a). Following this,
144    we apply the C2ST framework, not by training an randomly initialized classifier, but by fine-tuning
145    the pre-trained encoder with an added classification layer in order to learn the DRs.
146    We provide the first theoretical analysis to show that, with a high probability, involving a larger testing
147    set (without sample information) in the training process will lead to a higher lower bound of the test
148    power of SSL-C2ST, verifying the effectiveness of SSL-C2ST in theory. Besides, the empirical test
149    power on three benchmark datasets also shows that SSL-C2ST clearly outperforms C2ST.
150    On the empirical side, we are also interested in the data representations extracted by the trained
151    classifier in SSL-C2ST. We perform MMD tests (with a linear kernel) on the different-level data rep-
152    resentations of the testing set, called SSL-C2ST-M. These tests clearly outperforms the corresponding
153    baselines empirically. Notably, SSL-C2ST-M outperforms the MMD with the deep kernel (MMD-D
154    (Liu et al., 2020)) and MMD-FUSE (Biggs et al., 2023) on the MNIST and ImageNet dataset.
155
       Impact of our study in the field. The success of SSL-C2ST(-M) might provide evidence that
156    SSL-based testing methods have the potential to overcome the key issues of both paradigms. For the
157    supervised paradigm, SSL-based testing methods can leverage the useful information in the testing set
158    (without sample information), thus we can expect either to use a smaller training set (sacrificing less
159    power) or to learn a better function/kernel (more extra power) with the help of the useful information
160    in the testing set. For the unsupervised paradigm, SSL-based testing methods might provide some
161    supervision to guide the learning process of unsupervised learning or to form a better candidate set
       that contains a function/kernel that can help distinguish between two samples better.

179   Two-sample Test. Two-sample test is one of the statistical hypothesis tests that aims to assess
180   whether two independent identically distributed i.i.d. samples, denoted by SP = {xi }ni=1 ∼ Pn

260   In this section, we will discuss the key assumptions of traditional SSL methods. Then, we will
261   analyze whether we can directly apply those methods in our two-sample testing scenarios.
262   Assumptions of SSL methods. In principle, incorporating unsupervised information from unlabeled
263   data has the potential to enhance the predictions made by purely supervised learning models. However,
264   the efficacy of SSL is often relied on some assumptions below (Chapelle et al., 2006).
265
          • Smoothness assumption: If points x1 and x2 are close, then so should be their labels y1 , y2 .
266
          • Cluster assumption: If points are in the same cluster, they are likely to be of the same class.
267       • Manifold assumption: The (high-dimensional) data lie (roughly) on a low-dimensional manifold.

275
      two-sample testing problem settings, there are normally overlapping between two samples. As we

279   point in two samples, but allocated with different labels, this will notably influence the SSL methods

      Empirical result for validity of SOTA SSL methods on two-sample testing. The empirical
283   results, presented in Table 13 , show that the application of SOTA SSL methods on C2ST not only
284   underperforms our proposed method but also often yields poorer results compared to the original
285   C2ST on HDGM-Medium and HDGM-Hard datasets, which are the common overlapping distribution
286   data in the context of two-sample testing. This underperformance can be attributed to the fundamental
287   nature of the two-sample testing problem, which is distinct from typical classification tasks. In
288   two-sample testing, the two input samples should not inherently possess class labels. During training,
289   we manually assign labels to facilitate distinction by the classifier, whereas in testing, we consider
290   the two samples holistically rather than focusing on individual instance accuracy. Furthermore,
291
      standard SSL methods, which primarily enhance classification through data augmentation based on
      smoothness assumptions or infer pseudo labels based on clustering assumptions, aim to generate
292
      high-confidence training data. However, in two-sample testing, these approaches are flawed; data
293
      augmentation may alter the samples’ distributions, and pseudo label inference often proves inaccurate.
294   These discrepancies lead to the ineffectiveness of these SSL methods in two-sample testing contexts.
295   Therefore, we propose a two-sample test through a two-phase SSL approach, shown below.

303   5.1   O UR P ROPOSAL : SSL-C2ST
304
305   As the two-sample testing problem violates the native assumptions of SOTA SSL methods, we
306   propose a pipeline that follows the definition of SSL, which utilizes the unlabelled samples and
307   labelled samples in two phases. The first phase is an unsupervised auto-encoder-based (AE-based)
308
      representation learning, which learns a feature extractor that captures the inherent features for both
      samples. The next phase is the same as the C2ST pipeline, where the feature encoder in the model is
309
      not randomly initialized, but extracted from the representation learning in the previous phase. The
310
      ablated part of this approach compared to the C2ST is the AE-based representation learning, so its
311   effectiveness will be aligned with AE-based representation learning, which relies on the manifold
312   assumption, and that is particularly well-suited for two-sample testing scenarios The paradigm of
313   SSL-C2ST is shown in Figure 3, consisting of three steps: 1) learning IRs; 2) learning DRs; and 3)
314   performing two-sample testing.

450   In this section, we will analyze the experiment result of SSL-C2ST on two commonly used benchmark
451   datasets and one advanced ImageNet dataset. Also, we will discuss the empirical extensions based on
452   SSL-C2ST (i.e., SSL-C2ST-M), and present the comparative analysis of experimental results across
453   three benchmarks. The overview information of three datasets and experimental implementation
454   details of proposed method and other two-sample testing methods can be found in Appendix C.
455   Datasets. We conducted experiments on five different datasets to thoroughly evaluate our methods.
456   To assess the performance of current SSL methods applied to two-sample testing, we utilized three
457   synthetic datasets: HDGM-Easy, HDGM-Medium, and HDGM-Hard. Moreover, we conduct the
458   experiments of our proposed methods against other SOTA two-sample testing methods to evaluate
459   the effectiveness of semi-supervised paradigm on three datasets: MNIST, ImageNet, and HDGM.
460   Detailed descriptions of these datasets are provided in Appendix C.1.
461   Baselines. We evaluate the performance of our proposed methods SSL-C2ST and SSL-C2ST-M
462   against several SOTA baseline methods in two-sample testing, specifically C2ST, C2ST-M, MMD-D,
463   and MMD-FUSE. These baselines serve as competitive references to highlight the improvements
464   achieved by our approach. For comprehensive details on each baseline method, including their
465   implementations and parameter settings, please refer to Appendix C.2 and C.3.
466   Ablation Study: Verification of SSL-C2ST over C2ST. We first verify the effectiveness of SSL-
467   C2ST via comparing SSL-C2ST and C2ST, which provides empirical evidence for Theorem 5.3. The
468   implementation details of SSL-C2ST and C2ST can be found in Appendix C.2.
469
      The visualized result of how our SSL-C2ST outperforms C2ST is displayed in Figure 4. In both
470
      dataset MNIST and HDGM-Hard, we can see that the test power of SSL-C2ST is higher than that of
471
      C2ST no matter how many numbers of two samples are drawn from the distribution. Although the
472   differences between two methods are little when N is small, the test power of SSL-C2ST has a huge
473   gap over C2ST when N is large enough and converges to 1 with a relative smaller N compare to C2ST.
474   This also verifies our theoretical analysis that our model is more likely to have large improvement of
475   test power if the number of unlabelled samples in the semi-supervised learning is sufficiently large.
476
      Compared to C2ST, SSL-C2ST learns a compact and potentially more informative representation

      of the whole data, which makes efficient use of the unlabelled test data. This can not only discover

529
530   Non-parametric two-sample testing is an important problem in both statistics and machine learning
531   fields. This paper presents a new paradigm, semi-supervised learning-based classifier two-sample test
532   (SSL-C2ST), to learn better data representations for addressing this problem and gives a theoretical
533   analysis of why the proposed paradigm can have a higher test power compared to two representative
534   paradigms in the field. In the end, an advanced empirical testing method, SSL-C2ST with MMD (SSL-
535   C2ST-M), is presented in the experiments and shows superior performance compared to previous
536
      testing methods. Both theoretical analysis and empirical evidence show that the proposed new
      paradigm might be a cure for key issues of the existing two paradigms in the two-sample testing field,
537
      paving a new road to revisit and address the two-sample testing problem.

735   B    OVERVIEW OF M AJOR C ATEGORIES OF SOTA SSL METHODS
736
737
      Building on the SSL assumptions, we will recap how contemporary SOTA SSL methods incorporate
738   these principles and assumptions, setting the stage for an analysis of their applicability to the specific
739   challenges presented by our problem setting.
740
741
      Transductive vs Inductive learning. Classification tasks within machine learning can typically
      be categorized within two distinct problem settings: transductive and inductive learning (Chapelle
742
      et al., 2006). Transductive learning is concerned with predicting the labels of the specific unlabeled
743
      data that was present during the training process, emphasizing a tailored fit to this data. Inductive
744   learning, on the other hand, focuses on the generalization of the learned classifier to new, unseen
745   data. In learnable two-sample testing, the goal is to test whether the given two samples are drawn
746   from same distributions. To make it, we firstly split samples into labelled set and unlabelled set,
747   then find out that whether it is possible to learn a classifier that can distinguish two samples from
748   the mixed unlabelled set. It becomes apparent that applying SSL methodologies to the two-sample
749   testing problem inherently requires a transductive learning approach. This conceptual groundwork
750   necessitates a detailed examination of current SSL methods to identify their foundational assumptions
751   and evaluate their performance in two-sample test scenarios.
752   Major categories. Currently, we identify that there are five main categories of SOTA SSL methods:
753   consistency regularisation, pseudo-labelling, graph-based, generative models and hybrid (often
754   a combination of consistency regularisation and pseudo-labelling) (Yang et al., 2023). We will
755   succinctly explicate how they work, and how they are applied for our downstream two-sample testing
      tasks in the experiments of various levels of HDGM.


                                                         14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
             • Consistency Regularisation: Based on the manifold assumption or the smoothness assump-
757            tion, the consistency regularisation methods apply consistency constraints to the final loss
758            function, where the intuition is that if the data follows the smoothness assumption or man-
759            ifold assumption, even though we construct some perturbations in the inputs, it will not
760            influence the output of classification (Xie et al., 2020).
761
             • Pseudo-Labelling: Pseudo-labelling uses its own predictions to generate labels for unlabeled
762            data, which are then used to further train the model. It relies on the assumptions that
763            model’s high-confidence predictions are accurate. This assumption is based on the cluster
764            assumption for the validity and efficacy of propagating labels to unlabelled data based on
765            model predictions (Lee et al., 2013).
766
             • Graph-Based: Graph-based methods will construct a similarity graph based on the raw
767            dataset, where each node represents a data instance, and weighted-edge represents the
768            similarity between two data instances. Based on the smoothness assumption, the label
769            information can be propagated from labelled nodes to unlabelled nodes, if two nodes are
770            closely connected in the constructed graph (Song et al., 2021b).
771
             • Generative Models: Generative methods learn to model the underlying distribution of both
772            labelled data and unlabelled data, using this learned representation to generate new data
773            points and infer missing labels. Based on the manifold assumption, the generative models
774            aim to learn the underlying low-dimensional manifold and generate data points that adhere
775            to the same manifold, used for further model training (Kingma & Welling, 2013).
776
             • Hybrid: Hybrid methods are just combination of multiple methods, such as consistency
777            regularisation, pseudo-labelling, and sometimes generative approaches. These models
778            typically rely on the smoothness assumption and cluster assumption, in order to infer the
779            labels of unlabelled data (Sohn et al., 2020).

      C.6   A DISCUSSION ABOUT SUPERVISED SEQUENTIAL TWO - SAMPLE TESTING AND
919         APPLICABILITY OF SSL-C2ST
920
921   Supervised sequential two-sample testing represents another approach to utilizing testing data (Pan-
922   deva et al., 2022). In this framework, a classifier is trained to determine whether two samples from
923   a single batch originate from the same distribution. Initially, batches are split and fed sequentially
924   into the classifier as testing data. Batches that do not reject the null hypothesis are concatenated with
925   previous batches and used as training data for the classifier, continuing until all batches are exhausted
926
      or a single batch rejects the null hypothesis. The sequential nature of the test emerges from the use of
      e-values, which are updated as more data becomes available, allowing for a dynamic assessment of
927
      the testing hypothesis. However, this method should not be directly compared to our method due to
928
      different problem settings and designs. Firstly, in sequential two-sample testing, data are split into
929   several batches and tests are conducted on single, small batches. Conversely, in other supervised
930   two-sample testing approaches, data are only split into two halves, creating a trade-off between the
931   number of training and testing samples.
932
      Furthermore, the design of our SSL-C2ST method is compatible with any other supervised two-
933
      sample testing framework, including sequential two-sample testing. As long as a proportion of data is
934
      used for testing, we can remove the labels from this testing data and concatenate it into the training
935   data. This allows us to learn IRs through representation learning, followed by the original supervised
936   two-sample testing framework.
