009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Speculative decoding is a prominent technique to accelerate large language model
012            inference by leveraging predictions from an auxiliary draft model. While effective,
013            in application-specific settings, it often involves fine-tuning both draft and target
014            models to achieve high acceptance rates. As the number of downstream tasks
015            grows, draft models add significant complexity to inference systems. Recently
016            several single model architectures viz. Medusa have been proposed to speculate
017            tokens in non-autoregressive manner, however, their effectiveness is limited due
018
               to lack of dependency between speculated tokens. We introduce a novel specu-
               lative decoding method that integrates drafting within the target model by using
019
               Multi-stream attention and incorporates future token planning into supervised fine-
020
               tuning objective. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first parameter-efficient
021            approach that scales well with an increasing number of downstream tasks while
022            enhancing downstream metrics and achieving high acceptance rates, attributable to
023            the interdependence among the speculated tokens. Speculative Streaming speeds
024            up decoding by 1.9 - 3X in a diverse set of tasks, such as Summarization, Structured
025            Queries, and Meaning Representation, while improving generation quality and us-
026            ing ∼10000X fewer extra parameters than alternative architectures, making it ideal
027            for resource-constrained devices. Our approach can also be effectively deployed in
028            lossless mode for generic chatbot applications where speculative instruction tuning
029
               is performed while keeping base model frozen. In such setups, we achieve 2.9 -
               3.2X speedup while maintaining the integrity of the base model’s output.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Large transformers are today’s preeminent tool for language modeling. The quality of these models
035
      improves as they scale (Kaplan et al., 2020), leading to the introduction of the state-of-the-art multi-
036   billion parameter models (Brown et al., 2020; Thoppilan et al., 2022; Chowdhery et al., 2023; Touvron
037   et al., 2023a). While these models are effective for token generation, they incur a high inference cost
038   as the model and its transient states need to be loaded into computing memory for each subsequently
039   generated token. This poses a challenge to the deployment of large autoregressive transformers,
040   particularly for user-facing applications with stringent latency requirements.
041
      Given the memory-bound nature of large language model (LLM) inference, recent work (Leviathan
042   et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023) proposed Speculative Decoding as an effective technique to accelerate
043   decoding based on concepts borrowed from speculative computation (Burton, 1985) to exploit the
044   available extra compute. The core of speculative decoding is the idea of speculating multiple candidate
045   future tokens first, and then verifying them all in parallel. To achieve this, as shown in Figure 1a.(i), a
046   two-model paradigm approach is used: a small auxiliary “draft” model for candidate speculation and
047   a large “target” model for verification (Leviathan et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023). Although effective
048   in accelerating LLMs, speculative decoding complicates deployment. Training also becomes more
049   demanding and complicated, as a separate draft model needs to be trained and aligned with the target
050
      model for each application. It is also not resource-efficient, requiring to host two models in memory
      during token prediction.
051
052   In this paper, we propose Speculative Streaming, a single-model speculative decoding approach
053   that unifies speculation and verification, obviating the need for a separate draft model as shown in
      Figure 1a.(ii). This is accomplished by incorporating multi-stream attention into the target model to

094   2         R ELATED W ORKS
095
096   The original speculative decoding approach (Chen et al., 2023; Leviathan et al., 2023) utilizes a
097   smaller draft model to generate a candidate sequence of tokens to be verified by the target model.
098   Recent SD variants propose parallel computation along the batch axis (Sun et al., 2023b), and tree-
099   structured batches (Miao et al., 2023; Spector & Re, 2023) to improve the acceptance rates of the
100   guessed tokens by the target model and to further boost the performance. However, these methods
101   encounter a common limitation: the necessity of developing an accurate and independent draft model
102   for each downstream application. First, training such a draft model aligned with the main model is
103   not trivial (Zhou et al., 2023). Second, hosting two different models increases the system complexity,
104
      and is more computationally and operationally expensive to maintain as number of applications grow.
105   Recently, single-model speculation has also been considered. In particular, inspired by (Qi et al.,
106   2020; Stern et al., 2018), Medusa (Cai et al., 2023) extends the main model to predict future tokens in
107   parallel by training multiple output heads. While it does not require a draft model, each Medusa head
      of size (hidden_size × vocab_size) requires significant nonnegotiable additional parameters which

      introduce deployment challenges on resource-constrained devices. Furthermore dependency between
109   speculated tokens is not guranteed (Ankner et al., 2024) limiting speedups. (Ankner et al., 2024)
110   improves speculation procedure of (Cai et al., 2023) by using autoregressive draft head to introduce
111   dependency between speculated tokens, however small size draft head tends to be sub-optimal and
112   increasing draft head size leads to similar issues as those with (Leviathan et al., 2023; Zhou et al.,

116   method without learning new parameters. While this approach is parameter efficient, speedups are
117
      limited as speculation procedure is not learnable. We discuss further approaches related to inference
      efficiency in Appendix J.

122   3.1     M OTIVATION
123
124
      Existing speculative decoding techniques often enforce a strict decoupling of the training objectives
      between draft and target models (Leviathan et al., 2023), or between draft models and auxiliary
125
      heads (Cai et al., 2023). While this separation has been effective, we propose that these objectives are
126
      not inherently orthogonal. Instead, they can be aligned during training. Specifically, we hypothesize
127   that, similar to the main residual stream, the model can process “speculative” residual streams which
128   can be optimized to approximate the residual streams of future tokens, extending beyond immediate
129   next-token prediction. By conditioning immediate next token prediction on speculative streams
130   as well as previous context, the model gains the ability to predict upcoming tokens with a richer
131   contextual scope. As a result, this approach mitigates the risks of overly greedy decoding, providing
132   a more informed and contextually aware generative process.
133   Our goal is to develop an end-to-end trainable, single-model framework that integrates future token
134   planning, enhances generation quality, and scales efficiently across multiple downstream applications.
135   We propose following modifications to achieve these objectives. (a) Speculative stream design and
136   initialization as described in Section 3.1.1 (b) Parallel speculation and verification as described
137   in Section 3.1.2 (c) Parallel tree draft pruning, described in Section 3.1.3 and finally (d) Training
138   objective as described in Section 3.1.4.
139
140   3.1.1    S TREAMS D ESIGN AND I NITIALIZATION
141
      Parameter efficient supervised fine-tuning (Hu et al., 2022) of decoder-only pre-trained language
142
      models involves training low-rank adapters to predict next target token yt given context tokens
143   (x1 ....xm ) and previous target tokens (y1 ..y<t ) on downstream applications. Although effective,
144   this objective generates each token greedily and lacks a sense of future token planning (Qi et al.,
145   2020) which may lead to sub-optimal generation quality (see Section 4.1.2). To inherently embed a
146   notion of future token planning, we modify the training objective of the target model from next token
147   prediction to n-gram prediction using multi-stream attention. This objective facilitates proactive
148   token planning and mitigates over-fitting to local correlations (Yang et al., 2019; Qi et al., 2020).
149   Furthermore, we extend this framework by sharing the key/value cache across all streams, allowing
150   each of the γ streams to generate speculative tokens with negligible latency overhead when the model
151
      is memory-bound. Specifically, each added stream predicts p(yt+j |y<t , x), where 1 <= j <= γ,
      while main stream predicts p(yt |y<t , x).
152
153   In lossless mode, attention mechanism of main stream remains same as the standard multi-head
154   attention mechanism (Vaswani et al., 2017) while in shared mode, we enable the main stream to
155   attend to speculative streams, allowing it to plan its residual transformations based on anticipated
156   future residual states by modifying the attention mechanism as

      3.1.2   PARALLEL S PECULATION AND V ERIFICATION
217
218   In standard draft-target speculative decoding (Leviathan et al., 2023), speculation and verification
219   processes happen sequentially. Speculative Streaming makes this process efficient by parallelizing
220   speculation and verification. In each forward pass, the draft generated in the previous step is verified
221   and a new draft is generated as shown in Figure 2. For instance, in step s, if draft tokens (y˜1 ..y˜δ )
222
      are accepted where 0 < δ ≤ γ, main stream Mδ is used to issue a correction token and logits from
      speculative streams Sδ(1...γ) are used to generate draft for step s + 1.
223
224   Instead of using a linear sequence of speculated tokens for verification, we sample a tree of tokens
225   from main and speculative streams, such that each path in the tree is one possible verification
226   candidate. Tree drafting enables accepting the longest matching candidate sequence and more tokens
227   can be advanced during each forward pass. To create a tree draft, instead of sampling 1 token from
228
      logits of speculative streams, (z1 ...zγ ), we sample top k tokens and form a tree of sampled tokens as
      shown in Figure 2, such that tokens sampled from stream n are predecessors of tokens sampled from
229
      stream n + 1. We process a tree draft of speculative tokens in one forward pass by creating an additive
230
      attention mask (Vaswani et al., 2017) such that each node in the tree attends to its predecessor.

      3.1.3   PARALLEL T REE P RUNING
239
240   One of the issues with the naive creation of a speculative tree draft is that every permutation between
241   k tokens sampled from each stream needs to be considered as a viable speculative candidate for the
242   next verification
               Pγ       pass. For instance, sampling k tokens from each of γ streams results in tree draft of
243   size 1 + g=1 k g . Furthermore, each of the draft tokens is batched with γ speculative streams in
244   MSA layers to ensure that the generation
                                         Pγ      of the next draft happens in the same forward pass, resulting
245   in a batch size of (1 + γ) ∗ (1 + g=1 k g ). As batch size increases, target model inference becomes
246   compute-bound, obviating the latency benefit of sampling more tokens. We mitigate this problem by
247
      introducing a parallel tree draft pruning layer, which prunes less probable tokens from the input tree
      draft based on transition probability between parent and immediate child tokens. To obtain transition
248
      probabilities without using proxy models, we use an early-exiting-based technique. Specifically,
249
      hidden states of the main stream at layer l, M l are passed through a low-rank linear transformation
250   oθ , where the rank θ is typically set to a small value like 8 to keep parameter overhead minimal.
251   We use original language modeling head, H to obtain early exit logits, z̃ = H(oθ (M l ). z̃pc is used
252   to approximate transition probability between parent token p and child token c. The pruning layer
253   can be inserted at any point in the network, guided by the trade-off between forward pass latency
254   and pruning accuracy. Early insertion reduces latency but risks pruning potentially valuable tokens.
255   Conversely, late insertion retains more "good" tokens but comes at the cost of increased forward
256   pass latency. In all experiments described in Section 4.1, we insert the pruning layer just before
257   speculative stream insertion. More details can be found in Appendix Figure 11.

274   3.1.5      S HARED VS . L OSSLESS M ODES
275
276   We investigate two primary deployment scenarios for mainstream LLMs. In generic chatbot-like
277   use cases, pre-trained LLMs are instruction-tuned, and it is crucial to maintain the base model’s
278
      output distribution while achieving speedup. For such cases, our approach operates in a lossless
      mode, where no trainable parameter sharing occurs between the main and speculative stream residual
279
      transformations (α0 = 0 in Equation (5)), and the main stream’s attention mechanism remains
280
      unchanged, as outlined in Section 3.1.1. Conversely, in application-specific scenarios, adapter
281   parameters are shared between the main and speculative streams, and the main stream’s attention
282   mechanism is modified ( Equation (1)) to enhance task-specific response quality.

      4.1.2        W HY D OES I T W ORK ?
427
428   Generation Metrics: To investigate the improvements in generation quality achieved by our approach,
429   we designed an experiment where the model predicts the next token while attending to a set of future
430   γ ground truth tokens beyond the next token. Our hypothesis was that by granting the model access
431   to these future tokens, the attention mechanism would enhance its ability to anticipate and plan for
      the next token, thus improving generation quality. Specifically, we postulated that:


                                                                         8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
433                              p(yt = gt |y<t , yt+1..t+γ , x) > p(yt = gt |y<t , x)                     (7)
434
435   Here, gt represents the ideal ground truth token that maximizes the generation quality metrics. To
436   validate this hypothesis, we modified the attention mask, allowing the model’s residual states to
437   "peek" into future residuals. As shown in Figure 8, this modification led to significant improvements
438   in generation metrics.
439   While such access to future tokens is not feasible during inference, where future states are unavailable,
440   our approach enables the model to approximate future residual states using speculative streams. As
441   demonstrated in Figure 7, these speculative streams, Stj , progressively align with the true residual
442   states of the next tokens as they propagate through the model layers. Crucially, our method allows the
443   primary stream, Mt , to attend not only to the current context up to token yt but also to the speculative
444   streams Stj . This multi-stream attention mechanism refines the transformations within Mt , aligning
445   them more closely with the context of the upcoming γ tokens. As a result, the model effectively
446
      "plans" for future tokens, leading to measurable improvements in generation quality.
447   Speedup: Medusa attempts to generate the hidden states of speculative tokens y(t+1...t+γ) by
448   applying a simple context independent transformation to the last hidden state of the current token
449   yt . However, this method has significant limitations. The absence of attention mechanisms results
450   in lower similarity metrics between the speculative hidden states generated by Medusa and the true
451   hidden states, which are obtained by feeding the actual next token into the model (see Figure 7). In
452
      contrast, our proposed technique leverages multi-stream attention, wherein speculative streams are
      allowed to attend to each other as well as to the main stream. As these streams propagate through the
453
      model layers, they more closely approximate the true hidden states of the actual next tokens, resulting
454
      in higher similarity, thereby increasing the acceptance rate of the speculated tokens.

477   5     C ONCLUSION
478
479   In this paper, we proposed Speculative Streaming, a method to accelerate decoding of large language
480
      models. Compared to the standard speculative decoding approaches, Speculative Streaming removes
      the need for an auxiliary “draft” model. Instead, it unifies speculation and verification by efficiently
481
      fusing multiple speculative streams into a single “target” model. Speculative Streaming simplifies
482
      the fine-tuning process and achieves better generation quality and speedup compared to previous
483   approaches. It is also parameter efficient and removes the need for loading two models into the
484   memory, making it a suitable approach for resource-constrained scenarios.

666   In this section, we go into more detail of tree draft sampling, flattening, and pruning. As shown in
667   the main paper, when processing prompt (x1 ...xt ), we insert speculative streams along with the last
668   token to generate logits, zt corresponding to main stream and (zt1 ...ztγ ) corresponding to speculative
669   streams. Tree draft is sampled following the procedure described in Section 3.1.2. The sampled
670   draft is then flattened along the sequence length dimension and the attention mask is composed such
671   that child nodes attend to their predecessors starting with root as shown in Figure 11 and Figure 12.
672   The root token of the tree draft is the correction issued by main stream. Each iteration after prompt
673   processing involves verifying the previous tree draft and sampling a new one. After passing the tree
674
      draft through N − Ns layers, we use contextual features learned by middle layers to approximate
      transition probability between parent and child tokens. As shown in Figure 11, since the transition
675
      probability between token “parameter′′ and “compare′′ is less than a set threshold, we prune the
676
      sub-tree starting from “compare” in the feature domain , and m2 , m5 , m6 are pruned. Please note
677   that the key value cache of layers 0..(N − Ns − 1) before the pruning layer is not trimmed at this
678   point to keep pruning latency overhead minimal. Key value cache backtracking is done lazily after
679   each generation step. Speculative streams are inserted alongside each node in the pruned draft.
680   Layers (N − Ns ..N ) use Multi-stream attention as described in Equation (1) and Equation (2). The
681   verification procedure finds the longest matching path in the pruned tree that main stream can accept.

843   D    S EGMENT ATTENTION
844
845   Naive training with speculative streaming increases the batch dimension along the sequence length
846   axis by a factor of γ, resulting in attention computation reaching peak memory usage with larger
847   batches. To address this issue, we propose a segment-based attention method that significantly reduces
848   peak memory consumption while enhancing training throughput. We divide each training sample into
849   a prompt and multiple segments of completion. Since each stream corresponding to each token must
850   attend to the previous streams of the same token as well as to the prompt tokens, we can eliminate the
851   need for prompt streams in our design. Furthermore, by segmenting the completion, we retain only
852   the streams associated with the required segments in memory, as illustrated in Figure 14. This design
853
      significantly reduces peak memory consumption and ensures the scalability of our approach when
      training with larger batch sizes, ultimately yielding improved throughput.

       I   PARAMETER OVERHEAD
981
982
       In terms of parameters, each Medusa head adds about h2 + hv parameters, where h is the hidden size
983
       and v is the vocabulary size. The number of Medusa heads also scales linearly w.r.t. γ, the length of
984    the speculative window, which in turn increases parameter overhead linearly with γ. On the other
985    hand, Speculative Streaming uses speculative adapters which do not scale with γ. Although, Stream
986    identifier embeddings scale with γ, the parameter overhead associated with each embedding is linear
987    to h. Furthermore, in fine-tuning settings “speculative adapter" parameters are shared with base
988    model adapters, therefore, parameter overhead associated with our approach is just γh.

       J   A DDITIONAL R ELATED WORKS
992
       The inference speed of large language models (LLMs) is often constrained by the sequential nature
993
       of auto-regressive decoding, which requires a complete forward pass of the network for each token
994
       generated. To mitigate the high inference latency, various strategies have been proposed to reduce the
995    memory footprint of LLMs. Techniques such as model quantization (Frantar et al., 2022; Yao et al.,
996    2022; Dettmers et al., 2023), knowledge distillation to smaller models (Gu et al., 2023; Agarwal
997    et al., 2023), and pruning (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023; Sun et al., 2023a) have emerged as effective
998    solutions. More recently, Confident Adaptive Language Modeling (CALM) (Schuster et al., 2022) has
999    introduced a method to dynamically adjust computational resources per token through early exiting
1000   in decoder layers. While CALM shows promise, it is hindered by issues related to key-value (KV)
1001   mismatch (Corro et al., 2023). To address the KV mismatch problem, skip decoding (Corro et al.,
1002   2023) allows for the bypassing of an increasing number of layers based on the position in the decoded
1003
       sequence. While this approach eliminates KV mismatch, the predefined restrictions on the number of
       layers bypassed can lead to suboptimal generation quality. In contrast, speculative decoding methods
1004
       provide a significant advantage over dynamic computing approaches, as they maintain generation
1005
       quality while enhancing inference efficiency.
