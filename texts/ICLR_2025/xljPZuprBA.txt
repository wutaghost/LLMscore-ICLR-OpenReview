011                                                       A BSTRACT
012             Desirable random graph models (RGMs) should (i) reproduce common patterns
013             in real-world graphs (e.g., high clustering), (ii) generate variable (i.e., not overly
014             similar) graphs, and (iii) remain tractable to compute and control graph statistics.
015             A common class of RGMs (e.g., Erdős-Rényi and stochastic Kronecker) outputs
016             edge probabilities, and we need to realize (i.e., sample from) the edge probabil-
017             ities to generate graphs. Typically, each edge’s existence is assumed to be de-
018             termined independently for simplicity and tractability. However, with edge inde-
019
                pendency, RGMs theoretically cannot produce high subgraph densities and high
                output variability simultaneously. In this work, we explore realization beyond
020
                edge independence that can better reproduce common patterns while maintain-
021
                ing high tractability and variability. Theoretically, we propose an edge-dependent
022             realization framework called binding that provably preserves output variability,
023             and derive closed-form tractability results on subgraph (e.g., triangle) densities in
024             generated graphs. Practically, we propose algorithms for graph generation with
025             binding and parameter fitting of binding. Our empirical results demonstrate that
026             binding exhibits high tractability and well reproduce patterns such as high cluster-
027             ing, significantly improving upon existing RGMs assuming edge independency.

029   1       I NTRODUCTION
030
      Random graph models (RGMs) help us understand, analyze, and predict real-world systems (Droby-
031   shevskiy & Turdakov, 2019), with various practical applications, e.g., graph algorithm testing (Mur-
032   phy et al., 2010), statistical testing (Ghoshdastidar et al., 2017), and graph anonymization (Back-
033   strom et al., 2007). Desirable RGMs should generate graphs with common patterns in real-world
034   graphs, such as high clustering,1 power-law degrees, and small diameters (Chakrabarti & Faloutsos,
035   2006). At the same time, the generated graphs should be variable, i.e., not highly-similar or even
036   near-identical, and the RGMs should be tractable, i.e., we can compute and control graph statistics.2
037   Many RGMs output individual edge probabilities and generate graphs accordingly, e.g., the Erdős-
038   Rényi model (Erdős & Rényi, 1959), the Chung-Lu model (Chung & Lu, 2002), the stochastic block
039
      model (Holland et al., 1983), and the stochastic Kronecker model (Leskovec et al., 2010). To gen-
      erate graphs from edge probabilities, we need realization (i.e., sampling), where edge independency
040
      (i.e., the edge existences are determined mutually independently) is widely assumed for the sake of
041
      simplicity and tractability. Although edge-independent RGMs have high tractability and they may
042   reproduce some common patterns (e.g., power-law degrees and small diameters), they empirically
043   fail to preserve some other patterns, especially high clustering (Moreno et al., 2018; Seshadhri et al.,
044   2013). Moreover, edge-independent RGMs theoretically cannot generate graphs with high triangle
045   density and high output variability at the same time (Chanpuriya et al., 2021).
046   Naturally, we ask: Can we apply realization without assuming edge independency so that we can
047   improve upon such RGMs to generate graphs with common patterns and high variability, while still
048   ensuring high tractability? To address this question, we propose and explore the concept of edge
049   probability graph models (EPGMs), i.e., RGMs that are still based on edge probabilities but do
050
      not assume edge independency, from theoretical and practical perspectives. Our key message is a
      positive answer to the question. Specifically, our novel contributions are four-fold:
051
          1
052        High clustering means high subgraph densities, as used by, e.g., Newman (2003) and Pfeiffer et al. (2012).
          2
053        In this work, tractability refers to the feasibility of deriving graph statistics, rather than the ability to handle
      large-scale graphs (which we refer to as scalability).

      3.2 E DGE DEPENDENCY IN RGM S
117
118
      Despite the popularity of EIGMs, edge dependency also widely exists in various RGMs, e.g., prefer-
      ential attachment models (Barabási & Albert, 1999), small-world graphs (Watts & Strogatz, 1998),
119
      copying network models (Kleinberg et al., 1999), random geometric graphs (Penrose, 2003), and ex-
120
      ponential random graph models (Lusher et al., 2013). Some other models use additional mechanisms
121   on top of existing models to introduce edge dependency by, e.g., directly forming triangles (Pfeiffer
122   et al., 2012; Wegner & Olhede, 2021). Exchangeable network models (ENMs) (Lovász & Szegedy,
123   2006; Diaconis & Janson, 2007) also involve edge dependency, where isomorphic graphs are gener-
124   ated with the same probability (i.e., all nodes are treated probabilistically in symmetry). However,
125   ENMs cannot generate graphs with sparsity and power-law degrees, which are common patterns
126   in real-world graphs (Crane & Dempsey, 2016). Recent efforts have introduced asymmetry among
127   nodes to enhance expressiveness (Crane & Dempsey, 2018; Wu et al., 2025). In the same spirit but
128   from a different perspective, we aim to improve expressiveness by introducing dependence among
129
      edges upon EIGMs. Since we build RGMs based on edge-probability models, the nodes are asym-
      metric (i.e., non-exchangeable), except for the Erdős-Rényi model with uniform edge probabilities.
130
      Notably, the closed-form tractability results on subgraph densities derived by us (Theorems 5.8
131
      and B.5) are usually unavailable for existing RGMs with edge dependency (Drobyshevskiy & Tur-
132   dakov, 2019). Usually, only asymptotic results, as the number of nodes approaches infinity, are
133   available for such models (Ostroumova Prokhorenkova, 2017; Gu et al., 2013; Bhat et al., 2016).
134   In this work, we propose novel edge-dependent RGMs with the following desirable properties:
135
      • Reproducing common patterns observed in real-world graphs across different domains, e.g., high
136      clustering, power-law degrees, and small diameters (Chakrabarti & Faloutsos, 2006).
137
      • Having high variability, generating graphs with low overlap (see Definition 3.1).
138
      • Having high tractability, with the feasibility to obtain closed-form results of graph statistics.

      7    C ONCLUSION AND DISCUSSIONS
523
524   In this work, we show that realization beyond edge independence can better reproduce common
525
      patterns while ensuring high tractability and variability. We formally define EPGMs and show their
      basic properties (Section 4). Notably, even with edge dependency, EPGMs maintain the same vari-
526
      ability (Property 4.7). We propose a pattern-reproducing, tractable, and flexible realization frame-
527
      work called binding (Algorithm 1) with two practical variants: local binding (Algorithm 2) and
528   parallel binding (Algorithm 3). We derive tractability results (Theorems 5.8 and B.5) on the closed-
529   form subgraph densities, and propose efficient parameter fitting (Section 5.4; Lemmas 5.12-5.15).
530   We conduct extensive experiments to show the empirical power of EPGMs with binding (Section 6).
531   Limitations
      Limitations andand future
                          future directions.
                                 directions. EPGMs with binding generate more isolated nodes than EIGMs
532   due to higher variance. Fortunately, we can address the limitation by fitting and controlling the num-
533   ber of isolated nodes with the tractability results, as mentioned in Remark 5.11. The performance
534   of EPGMs depends on both the underlying edge probabilities and the way to realize (i.e., sample
535   from) them. In this work, we focus on the latter, while finding valuable edge probabilities is an
536
      independent problem. Notably, as shown in Section 6.5, it is possible to jointly optimize both edge
      probabilities and their realization. As discussed in Remark 5.4, binding only covers a subset of
537
      EPGMs, and we will explore the other types of EPGMs (e.g., EPGMs with lower subgraph densi-
538
      ties) in the future. Combining binding with other mechanisms in existing edge-dependent RGMs to
539   create even stronger RGMs is another interesting future direction.

       Between the two, FDGMs means any distribution of graphs, i.e., any RGM, is allowed.
1791   They only discussed general overlap-related triangle-density upper bounds in those categories of
1792   RGMs, without detailed tractability results for practical graph generations. Specifically, their graph
1793
       generation algorithm is based on maximal clique enumeration (MCE). However, given a graph,
       MCE itself can take exponential time (Eblen et al., 2012).
1794
1795   Also, what we focus on in this work, i.e., the category of binding-based EPGMs, is a subset of
1796
       EPGMs and are not “fully general” as FDGMs. On the other hand, NIGMs are associated with
       node embeddings, where we have a node embedding space (i.e., a distribution) E and a symmetric
1797
       function e : E × E → [0, 1], and each node i has a node embedding xi sampled from E i.i.d., and
1798
       each edge (i, j) exists with probability e(xi , xj ) independently. Our binding-based EPGMs do not
1799   fall in this category either.

       D.3    S UBSET SAMPLING
1802
1803   As mentioned in Footnote 7 in Section 5.2, we use independent node sampling (yet still with edge
1804   dependency) which is simple, tractable, and works well. Specifically, independent node sampling
1805   allows us to easily compute the marginal probability of each node binding sampled in each round,
1806
       which is involved in the derivation of our tractability results. Also, as shown in our experiments,
       with binding schemes using independent node sampling, we still achieve significant empirical im-
1807
       provement over EIGMs. In the most general case, considering the sampling probabilities of all 2|V |
1808
       subsets would be intractable. Recently, a line of works has been proposed for tractable and differ-
1809
       entiable subset sampling (Xie & Ermon, 2019; Pervez et al., 2023; Ahmed et al., 2023; Sutter et al.,
1810   2023), and exploring more flexible node sampling schemes is an interesting future direction to be
1811   explored.

1818
