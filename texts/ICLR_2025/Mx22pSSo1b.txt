009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Conventional self-supervised learning (SSL) methods, such as SimCLR and Sim-
012            Siam, have demonstrated significant effectiveness. However, their feature repre-
013            sentation is not robust to image rotations, as rotational augmentation may nega-
014            tively impact the framework. In this paper, we address this limitation by apply-
015            ing SSL to group-equivariant CNNs, specifically rotation-equivariant CNNs, to
016            develop robust features. To learn expressive, rotation-invariant features, we in-
017            troduce our training method, Guiding Invariance with Equivariance (GIE), which
018            simultaneously trains both invariant features and the equivariance score for im-
019
               ages. The equivariance score guides the rotation-equivariant features through
               an attention-weighted sum mechanism, enabling the development of rotation-
020
               invariant features. Through experiments, we demonstrate that our GIE method
021
               not only extracts high-performing features under four discrete rotations but also
022            achieves robustness to random-degree rotations through rotation augmentation
023            training. These results highlight the effectiveness of our method in achieving ro-
024            bust rotation-invariance.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   How do humans recognize rotated images? Although it may seem simple and straightforward for
030   humans to recognize rotated images, Figure 1 shows that this is not always the case. When at-
031   tempting to read rotated text, we don’t read it directly but rather follow a sequential process. In this
032   process, we first try to understand how the image is rotated, then mentally ”rotate” it back to its
033
      original position before reading. Similarly, when identifying objects in a rotated image, our brain
      doesn’t immediately recognize the object. Instead, it analyzes the image, determining the angle at
034
      which the recognizable “object” emerges, and then mentally rotates it back to its correct orientation
035
      before accurately identifying the object. In some cases, such as with the number 9, it can be difficult
036   to determine how many degrees the image has been rotated, making it challenging to accurately rec-
037   ognize the number. The difficulty in analyzing such samples is a natural phenomenon and supports
038   the claim that humans perform a sequential process when analyzing rotated images.
039
      However, in deep learning, analyzing and rotating an image to its original position before extracting
040
      features requires using the model twice, which is resource-intensive. With this motivation, we pro-
041
      pose the Guiding Invariance with Equivariance (GIE) method, which applies the sequential process
042   at the feature level rather than the image level (see Figure 1). We used a rotation-equivariant model
043   as the feature extractor, ensuring that the output features behave equivariantly with respect to the
044   input image’s rotation. From these features, we obtained an equivariance score that indicates the
045   degree of image rotation, allowing us to apply the sequential process at the feature level, similar
046   to how humans analyze images. Through this process, we can naturally extract rotation-invariant
047   features guided by the equivariance scores.
048   We conducted experiments using a self-supervised learning (SSL) approach to train the rotation-
049   invariant features extracted by the GIE method and evaluated the model across various experimental
050   datasets. We tested two SSL methods, SimCLR (Chen et al., 2020) and SimSiam (Chen & He, 2021),
051   using CIFAR10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009), STL10 (Coates et al., 2011), and ImageNet100 (Tian et al.,
052   2020) as datasets. The experimental results with the two SSL methods and three datasets showed
053   that, in all cases, the rotation-invariant features extracted by the GIE method achieved higher linear
      classification accuracy for 0, 90, 180, and 270-degree rotations compared to other baseline feature

146   an E(2)-CNN backbone network following the ResNet architecture.

      3.1   OVERVIEW
152
153
      In Figure 2a, we have illustrated our GIE method. To obtain rotation-invariant features, we employed
154
      a rotation-equivariant backbone network F to extract the rotation-equivariant feature F (X). Unlike
155
      previous SSL approaches, we designed an equivariance predictor module using a 1 × 1 group convo-
156   lution layer from the e2cnn library (Cesa et al., 2021) to maintain equivariance of the equivariance
157   score, where the score refers to the output of the equivariance predictor. We assigned an orientation
158   alignment loss to facilitate the learning of the equivariance score. Using the learned equivariance
159   score and the rotation-equivariant features extracted from the backbone, we conducted a guiding
160   invariance process to create rotation-invariant features. For the training loss functions, we combine
161   the conventional SSL loss with the orientation alignment loss. Also, the background information
      related to group-equivariant CNNs mentioned here is presented in Appendix A.1

      3.2    G ROUP - EQUIVARIANT CNN S
163
164   Group-equivariant CNNs (GCNNs) maintain equivariance with a predefined image transformation
165   group G, making them effective for extracting equivariant features. We used a cyclic group of order
166   4 (i.e., p4-group), corresponding to 90-degree rotations, as our group G. Our backbone model F
167   is based on the ResNet architecture, with the layers replaced by equivariant layers provided by the
168   e2cnn library.
169   The output feature vector exhibits equivariance, where the rotation of the input image corresponds
170   to a permutation in the group dimension of the feature vector. Formally, let X represent the input
171   image, and F (X) ∈ R|G|×K be the equivariant feature passed through the backbone F . We define
172   G is p4-group, hence |G| = 4. Then, F (X) can be expressed as follows:
173                                    F (X) := [f0 , f1 , f2 , f3 ],   fi ∈ RK                     (1)
174                                                               2   3
      Since F (X) exhibits rotation-equivariance, for rX, r X, r X, which represent the input X rotated
175
      by 90◦ , 180◦ , 270◦ , respectively, we have the following relationships:
176
177
                                        F (rX) = F (r−3 X) = [f3 , f0 , f1 , f2 ]                   (2)
178                                     F (r2 X) = F (r−2 X) = [f2 , f3 , f0 , f1 ]                                  (3)
179                                          3             −1
                                     F (r X) = F (r X) = [f1 , f2 , f3 , f0 ]                        (4)

      Due to the characteristics of the equivariant backbone model, we can replace image rotation with a
181
      feature-level permutation.
182
183   Edixhoven et al. (2023) analyze the exact equivariance of GCNNs. As mentioned, since typical GC-
184   NNs do not achieve exact equivariance, we adjusted the input image size following the mathematical
185
      conditions from Edixhoven et al. (2023) to ensure exact equivariance in the final output feature vec-
      tor. By setting this, we ensured that the final output feature vector maintained exact equivariance.
186
      The details are as follows.

      Exact equivariance in GCNN on p4-group (Edixhoven et al., 2023) A GCNN is exactly equiv-
189   ariant to rotations of multiples of 90-degree if the following equation holds for all layers in the
190   network:
191                                          (i − k) mod s = 0.                                          (5)
192   where i is the rectangular image size, k is the kernel size and s is the stride. Based on the previous
193   equation, we reshaped the images in each dataset to fit the model architecture in the experiments.

195   3.3    E QUIVARIANCE PREDICTOR
196
197   After the feature vector is extracted, it is fed into an equivariance predictor (see Figure 2b). The pre-
198   dictor consists of a 1 × 1 group-equivariant convolution, batch normalization, and a ReLU layer, all
199   designed to preserve group-equivariance using the e2cnn library. This design enables the equiv-
200
      ariance predictor to analyze the input feature vector effectively while maintaining the rotation-
      equivariance of the output. We set the final output dimension to a 1-regular representation (4 di-
201
      mensions), which enabled the generation of an equivariant 4-dimensional score, referred to as the
202
      equivariance score.
203
204   The distinctive characteristic of the equivariance score is its rotation-equivariance; if the input im-
205   age is rotated by 90 degrees, the original equivariance score shifts laterally by one position. Conse-
206
      quently, once the equivariance score S(X) for an input image X is determined, the scores for the
      rotated images S(rX), S(r2 X), and S(r3 X) are automatically defined due to their rotational equiv-
207
      ariance. In other words, if we define S(X) := [a0 , a1 , a2 , a3 ], where ai ∈ R are scalar values, then
208
      S(rX), S(r2 X), and S(r3 X) are determined as [a3 , a0 , a1 , a2 ], [a2 , a3 , a0 , a1 ], and [a1 , a2 , a3 , a0 ],
209   respectively. Thus, a 4-dimensional vector effectively captures the properties of these four rotated
210   image states (see Figure 3).

      3.5   L OSS FUNCTION
251
252   To simultaneously train the rotation-invariant feature and the equivariance score, we combined the
253   loss used in traditional self-supervised learning with another loss designed for equivariance score
254   training. We introduce the orientation alignment loss that we used for training.
255
256   Orientation alignment loss The orientation alignment loss, as used in Lee et al. (2023), ensures
257   that the equivariance scores of different image views match. We have simplified the orientation
258   alignment loss since we do not need to align the orientations of the images.
259   Let X be the input image, and define X1 , X2 as the outputs of different transformations T1 , T2
260   applied to X (i.e., Xi = Ti (X), i = 1, 2. see Figure 2a). We define our orientation alignment loss
261   as follows:
262                                                   X4

263                             LOri (X1 , X2 ) = −      S(X1 )i log(S(X2 )i )                        (9)
264                                                        i=1

265   This is essentially the cross-entropy loss between S(X1 ) and S(X2 ). Unlike the method used in
266   the original Lee et al. (2023), since the dominant orientation in our training dataset is aligned to 0
267   degrees, we used the cross-entropy loss without any additional shift operations.
268
269   Total loss We integrate the conventional self-supervised learning loss (SSL loss) with our equiv-
      ariance loss to form the loss function. For SimCLR, the SSL loss is infoNCE (Oord et al., 2018),

      Analysis of equivariance score We examined the distribution of the equivariance score for the
462   pretrained model using the GIE method on the ImageNet100 dataset (see Table 3 and Figure 5).
463   Although we did not use any loss function that amplifies a particular dimension (e.g., rotation pre-
464   diction loss), the equivariance score was dominated by specific dimensions, with a majority of the
465   samples showing dominant values in these dimensions. We refer to these scores as dominant scores.
466   This phenomenon aligns well with our motivation and intent for the equivariance score to reflect the
467   relative orientation in which an image is most easily recognized.
468   Additionally, by analyzing the equivariance score, we gained insights into the overall characteris-
469   tics of different datasets. As shown in Table 3, the STL10 and the ImageNet100 exhibited dominant
470   score proportions of 91.34% and 89.01%, respectively, indicating a strong bias toward specific orien-
471   tations. In contrast, the Oxford 102 Flowers (Nilsback & Zisserman, 2008) dataset, which contains
472   more rotation-invariant images, showed a dominant score proportion of only 28.40%. These re-
473   sults demonstrate that the equivariance score effectively captures both the rotation-invariance and
474   rotation-equivariance of images.
475   In Figure 6, we analyzed several image samples by rotating them and examining the patterns of
476   their equivariance scores. For objects that are equivariant to rotation, such as cars and birds, the
477   equivariance scores exhibited periodic and regular patterns in response to rotation. In contrast,
478   images that are rotation-invariant, like flowers, generated noisy equivariance scores, highlighting
479   the distinction between the two types.
480   To verify whether performance drops on rotation-invariant datasets, we measured the linear classifi-
481   cation accuracy of the pretrained backbone on other datasets across four discrete 90-degree orienta-
482   tions. As shown in Table 4 in Appendix B.2, the GIE model outperformed the baseline backbones,
483   such as the E(2)-CNN and the E(2)-CNN with group pooling, even on rotation-invariant datasets
484   like Oxford 102 Flowers and MTARSI (Wu et al., 2020). This result indicates that the equivariance
485   score not only represents recognizable orientations but also functions as a complex attention weight,
      supporting the robustness of GIE.

675   A.1   P RELIMINARIES ON GCNN
676
677   Group convolution Group convolution is a generalization of traditional convolution in neural
678   networks, where the transformation group (such as rotations, reflections, or translations) is applied to
679   the feature maps. Instead of performing convolution only over spatial translations, group convolution
680   processes data using symmetries from a specific group. This allows the model to capture patterns
681   and symmetries beyond simple shifts, making it more robust to transformations like rotations.
682
683   Equivariance and invariance Equivariance and invariance are two important concepts in repre-
684   sentation learning. Equivariance refers to a property where a transformation applied to the input
685   results in a corresponding transformation in the output. For example, a neural network is equivariant
686   if rotating an image leads to a rotated feature map in the output. Formally, for a function f and a
687   transformation T , the function is equivariant if f (T (x)) = T (f (x)). On the other hand, invariance
688   means that the output remains unchanged when a transformation is applied to the input. A function
689   is invariant to a transformation T if f (T (x)) = f (x). Invariance is useful for tasks where the output
690
      should be insensitive to specific transformations, like object recognition regardless of orientation,
      while equivariance is crucial for capturing structured changes in the input data.
691
692
693   Exact equivariance Exact equivariance refers to a strict form of equivariance, where the model’s
694   output perfectly follows the transformation applied to the input. In an exactly equivariant system, the
695   transformation of the input always leads to a predictable and mathematically precise transformation
696
      of the output, without any loss of information. This differs from approximate equivariance, where
      the correspondence between transformed inputs and outputs may not be perfect but is close enough
697
      for practical purposes.
698

731        CIFAR10 CIFAR10 is an image recognition dataset consisting of 60,000 32x32 color images
732

733

734

735        test images. For the exact equivariance of feature, we set the training image size 33x33 in the
736        experiments.
737
738
           We used an E(2)-CNN backbone following the ResNet18 architecture. The initial number of chan-
           nels consisted of 20 regular representation units (80 dimensions), and the final output feature in-
739
           creased by a factor of 8 to become 160-regular representation units (640 dimensions). The equiv-
740
           ariance predictor uses two 1 × 1 group-equivariant convolution layers, employs 512-regular rep-
741        resentation units (2048 dimensions) for the intermediate node type, and, as previously mentioned,
742        uses 1-regular representation unit (4 dimensions) for the output type. Both SimCLR and SimSiam
743        utilized the SGD (Ruder, 2016) optimizer, with a learning rate of 0.06 and a batch size of 512.
744        We conducted SSL training for 800 epochs, after which the trained backbone was frozen, and a
745        linear classifier was attached for 100 epochs to measure linear classification accuracy. To prevent
746        overfitting, the base learning rate was set to 30 and decreased using a cosine learning rate scheduler.
747
748        STL10 STL10 is an image recognition dataset specifically designed for unsupervised and semi-
749        supervised learning. It includes 10 classes and features 100,000 unlabeled images, 5,000 training
750        images, and 8,000 validation images. We conducted pretraining on a combined set of 105,000
