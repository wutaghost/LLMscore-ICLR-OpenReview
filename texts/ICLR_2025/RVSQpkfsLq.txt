                                                        A BSTRACT
008
009
                   Game roles can be reflections of personas from a parallel world. In this paper, we
010
                   propose a new style of game-play to bridge self-expression and role-playing: open
011                role-playing games (ORPGs), where players are given the autonomy to craft and
012                embody their unique characters in the game world. Our vision is that, in the real
013                world, we are individually similar when we are born, but we grow into unique
014                ones as a result of the strongly different choices we make afterward. Therefore,
015                in an ORPG, we empower players with freedom to decide their own growing
016                curves through natural language inputs, ultimately becoming unique characters.
017                To technically do this, we propose a special engine called Delta-Engine. This
018                engine is not a traditional game engine used for game development, but serves
019
                   as an in-game module to provide new game-play experiences. A delta-engine
                   consists of two components, a base engine and a neural proxy. The base engine
020
                   programs the prototype of the character as well as the foundational settings of
021
                   the game; the neural proxy is an LLM, which realizes the character growth by
022                generating new code snippets on the base engine incrementally. In this paper,
023                we self-develop a specific ORPG based on delta-engines. It is adapted from the
024                popular animated series “Pokémon”. We present our efforts in generating out-of-
025                domain and interesting role data in the development process as well as accessing
026                the performance of a delta-engine. While the empirical results in this work are
027                specific, we aim for them to provide general insights for future games. 1

      1       I NTRODUCTION
031
      The virtual world, often more idealistic than reality, presenting a utopian escape and an alternative
032
      life, has captivated human imagination for decades. Films like “Free Guy” and “Ready Player One”
033
      have presented this vision for us. Role-playing games (RPGs) offer players the opportunity to step
034   into a well-designed character and enjoy its growth in a virtual game world, e.g. an Egyptian guard
035   in “Assassin’s Creed”, an American West bounty hunter in “Red Dead Redemption”. However,
036   conventional RPGs come with inherent limitations, where players are bound to some pre-scripted
037   characters. As time passes, they may find themselves merely acting out someone else, without any
038   personal connection. Rather, players desire to become another version of themselves in a parallel
039   world. To fulfill such deepest desire for autonomy and self-expression, this paper introduces the
040   concept of open role-playing games (ORPGs), which allow players to craft their own identities,
041   attributes, and powers, etc.
042   How ORPGs are played to make each player’s character distinguished In reality, while we are
043   individually similar at birth, we make different choices (whether actively or passively) as we grow up
044   afterward, which shape us into totally different people. This vision underpins the primary feature of
045   ORPGs: players are given the autonomy to manipulate the growth of their characters and become truly
046   unique ones. We notice that current RPGs also offer some extent of autonomy by providing players
047   with different options when their characters grow. However, it is very hard to exhaust every possible
048
      option and fulfill the desire of every player. In this paper, we consider a generalized situation, where
      players are able to direct the growth with free-form natural language descriptions, e.g. “Let me learn
049
      a talent to burn the enemy”. Such broad semantic space of natural language unlocks an unprecedented
050
      openness over traditional RPGs. However, the openness in ORPGs do not mean the players can
051   become anybody or anything without constraint. All of the attempts should be contextualized within
052   the specific game world, e.g. adhering to the physical laws and established worldview.

      How ORPGs are technically implemented As aforementioned, the character growth in ORPGs is
055   driven by natural language, which is difficult to interpret for existing game systems. We thus propose
056   Delta-Engine. It is a neural engine (Wu et al., 2024a) incorporating a large language model (LLM)
057   (Brown et al., 2020; OpenAI, 2023; Touvron et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023; Team et al., 2024). LLMs
058   can serve as a powerful non-linear function to transfer natural language instructions into some kinds
059   of outputs, such as the engine’s code. Specifically, a delta-engine is composed of two components: a
060   base engine and a neural proxy. The base engine is the initial coding of the character. The neural
061   proxy is an LLM, enabling the character growth by generating new code snippets that expand the
062   base engine. A delta-engine is different from traditional game engines (e.g. Unity, UE), which serve
063
      as a platform for game development, but serves as an embedded module within the game system.
      It would bring an entirely novel game-play experience, where the code of a player’s character is
064
      personalized and dynamically generated.
065
066   To materialize our concept, we have developed a tangible ORPG Free Pokémon based on delta-
067   engines, which will be open-sourced. The characters in the game are inspired from the popular
068   Pokémon animated series2 . In the game, players are born with a common pokemon template. From
069
      there, they have the freedom to grow up, learning their desired talents, described through natural
      language, being a unique pokemon to their tastes. An ORPG is data-driven. Therefore, data acts
070
      as a significant element in the development process, for aligning the neural proxy to enhance its
071
      adaptability. However, the data needed can be very domain-specific. Given the high cost of manually
072   annotated data, this paper explores a collaborative design approach that leverages both human
073   expertise and LLMs to generate high-quality data.

      AI-driven RPGs belong to a profound field of study that spans a series of sub-topics, e.g. role-playing
      (Shanahan et al., 2023; Värtinen et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024), narrative (Wu et al., 2024c; Zhao
079
      et al., 2024a;b), world building (Bruce et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023b), data creation (Wang et al.,
080
      2023c; Rezwana & Maher, 2023). This paper focuses on the form of the virtual world, i.e. to evolve,
081   and proposes a special engine to enable such evolution. The basic logic of our world engine is the
082   instruction-driven game engine (Wu et al., 2024b), a neural engine incorporating LLMs as integral
083   components. Our engine can be triggered by a high-level evolving instruction to generate new code
084   snippets. A relevant recent work is GameNGen (Valevski et al., 2024), a real-time game engine based
085   on diffusion models. As opposed to GameNGen, which renders the content directly from prompts,
086   our delta-engine generates the executable code and embed it into the base engine. The eventual
087   rendering and operation is still done by the backbone engine, which is made by code.
088   The chosen playground in our work belongs to role-play games (RPGs), a genre that has seen
089   significant advancements in recent years due to the integration of LLMs (Wu et al., 2024c; OpenAI,
090   2023; Touvron et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023). As opposed to these efforts, our
091   work enhances the player experiences by offering biodiversity of virtual roles. Any role can become
092   a truly unique one through exclusive evolution.
093   We are not the first to choose “Pokémon” as the topic in the research. A most recent work is
094   Pokéllmon (Hu et al., 2024), but they do an orthogonal job from us. Pokéllmon is an LLM-based
095   framework for powerful battle strategies based on existing pokemon characters. Free Pokémon is a
096   novel game genre, allowing players to generate their own pokemon characters (Butler et al., 2017).
097   In addition, our work does not focus on generating visual assets of new pokemon characters (Liapis,
098   2018; Geissler et al., 2020). We notice that it is also an interesting line for further developing ORPGs.
099   Procedural content generation (PCG) (Shaker et al., 2016; Smith et al., 2011; Summerville et al.,
100   2018) can be another relevant line of work to ORPGs. Practically, the player’s behavior won’t directly
101   affect the delta-engine, but rather go through a procedure, which eventually decides the prompt to the
102   neural proxy. In this paper, we do not focus on the design of the evolving procedure but only study
103   the naked delta-engine.
104
      There is a large body of work studying AI players across various game domains, e.g. Atari (Mnih
105   et al., 2013), Minecraft (Fan et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023a), StarCraft, (Vinyals et al., 2019),
106   Werewolf (Xu et al., 2023), SIMA (Team et al., 2024), CRADLE (Tan et al., 2024).

      3    D ELTA -E NGINE
109
110
      Base Engine A base engine is the initial state of the delta-engine. It depicts the prototype of the
111   virtual world, typically a mass of objects with associated methods and basic utilities.
112
113   As any object (e.g. environment, individual) grows, it acquires new properties. As a result, its
114
      associated engine is given new code. Considering an individual born with a blank template, its initial
      engine may be several lines of code supporting its only walking ability. As it grows stronger and
115
      learns to run and even fly, its codebase will be updated and expanded to reflect these new properties.
116
117
      Neural Proxy The neural proxy is a neural wrapper around the base engine, which scales the base
118   engine by producing new code. In our paper, it is a large language model (LLM), particularly one of
119   those that are additionally pre-trained on code, e.g. CodeLLaMA (Rozière et al., 2023), CodeGemma
120   (Mesnard et al., 2024).
121
      We denote all the objects and methods of the engine at some moment as a state. The LLM proxy
122
      seeks to predict the new state moment-by-moment. To make this process efficient, we ensure that the
123
      proxy always generates the incremental code on top of the current engine state, either adding new
124   features or overloading existing ones.
125
126   Incremental Prediction Given an input and the current engine state, a delta-engine seeks to predict
127   the incremental value. This idea can be formalized as:
128
129                                             ∆yt = F(yt−1 , xt )                                          (1)
130   where F is the neural proxy, xt is the input, yt−1 is the current engine state, and ∆yt is the incremental
131   value of yt−1 and yt . The initial state y0 is the base engine.
132
      yt can be obtained by merging ∆yt and yt−1 :
133
134                                             yt = m(∆yt , yt−1 )                                          (2)
135
      where m is the merge function.
136
137
      Retrieval y0 can be super large for a complicated virtual world and yt−1 will also become more
138   and more as it evolves, negatively impacting the engine’s scalability. We notice that evolution, both in
139   nature and in virtual worlds, tends to occur gradually, which means each evolution step of an object is
140   relevant to only a small fraction of the current engine. Therefore, for each prediction, we retrieve the
141   relevant parts of the engine dynamically, denoted as yet−1 , to replace the entire yt−1 as the reference:
142
                                                ∆yt = F(e
                                                        yt−1 , xt ).                                         (3)
143
144   yet−1 is a sparse version of yt−1 , which is the key to the delta-engine’s scalability to very long turns.
145
      We have the neural proxy determine the entries to retrieve itself (Yu et al., 2023). Concretely, it takes a
146   pre-step, predicting which parts of the engine are essential for scale. To do this, we index all methods
147   within the engine using their names and prompt the neural proxy with the skeleton overview of the
148   engine, which only keeps the structure and method names and skips the detailed implementation.
149   Then, we extract the implementation of the methods from the engine according to the method names.
150
      We will illustrate a concrete case of incremental prediction in the next section.

210   data engineering. Developers are tasked to craft a sufficient amount of data to align the neural proxy.
211   This process is labor-intensive. Even experienced professional designers can’t always come up with
212   fresh and innovative ideas. A recent philosophy is to synthesize pseudo data using powerful LLMs
213   as generators (Wang et al., 2023c; Wu et al., 2024c). However, the synthetic data is somewhat low-
214   quality and there can be significant and unknown biases within it (Shumailov et al., 2023). Instead, we
215   adopt a human and AI co-design process, where LLMs are harnessed as assistant designers, working
      collaboratively with human designers. We first discuss two major demands of generated data.

237   Being Novel Players are highly creative. For example, they won’t be satisfied with similar content
238   for long; they keep discovering novel and imaginative elements in the virtual world. Therefore, it is
239   crucial for the delta-engine to scale to a broad range of novelty. However, we find that LLMs are
240   not good at creating novel content based on given instances. Rather, they lean to combine existing
241   content, such as merging two talents into a new one; or make superficial modifications, such as
242
      transforming a regular dog to a bigger dog. Such secondary data no longer enhances scalability, the
      emergence of which necessitates a leap into out-of-domain content.
243
244
245   Being Interesting Interestingness further aligns the delta-engine to the player base. Our demand is
246   to refine the data design process by picking out the interesting portion from large amount of data.
247   As opposed to novelty, which can be straightforwardly measured using similarity scores, however,
248   quantifying interestingness has always been a challenging task (Nelson & Mateas, 2007; Todd et al.,
249   2024). It is highly subjective and lacks a precise definition, making it difficult to devise instructions
250
      for LLMs to assess.

252   5.1     P ROTOTYPES E NHANCED I MAGINATION
253
254   We conjecture that LLMs lack or even do not have imagination; their creative outputs are still guided
255   by the prompts they receive. However, the naive prompts e.g. “please use your imagination” fail
256   to offer useful clues to inspire the LLM’s imagination. To address this, we propose to leverage an
257   explicit prototype, a descriptive paragraph of an entity or scene, as the imaginative foundation. It
258   facilitates the generation of novel content by providing a concrete reference point.
259   Figure 3 illustrates the idea. For example, we seek to use Tyrannosaurus as the prototype to design
260   a pokemon role. We retrieve the corresponding description from Wikipedia and prompt the LLM
261   generator. The result is a novel pokemon characterized by stronger bite power, aligning with the
262   notable feature of Tyrannosaurus. In addition to real-world entities, prototypes can also come from
263   fictional sources. In our project, we retrieve the animals from Wikipedia, e.g. Tyrannosaurus,
264   Smilodon, Sperm Whale; also retrieve the virtual creatures from “Monster Hunter”3 , a popular action
265   video game. The distinction between the two sources is that the super-natural creatures in Monster
266
      Hunter typically lead to higher novelty of the pokemon roles designed upon them. However, these
      roles may go too far, increasing the likelihood of grammatical errors within the generated code, which
267
      we will discuss below.

297   5.2   TAGS OF I NTEREST
298
299   We hypothesize that interestingness is an accumulation of potential factors that may pique the users’
300
      interest (Althöfer, 2010). This implies that the more potential factors there are, the more likely the
      users will find the content interesting. Therefore, we introduce an interestingness evaluator based on
301
      these factors, which we call Tags of Interest (ToI). We then need a tagger to label them out given the
302
      instance.
303
304   Firstly, we establish a set of ToI. Since they vary significantly across different scenarios, this is a
305   heuristic process. We can construct a one-dimensional “interestingness vector”, where each tag
306
      is represented as one bit. For example, if a pokemon role has the ability to boost its power, the
      corresponding bit of this tag will be set to 1; otherwise, set to 0. We use a rule-based tagger to mine
307
      the potential tags of a role from its role code. For example, if a pokemon role can boost its power,
308
      it will inevitably overload the method get power. Based on the interestingness vector, we set a
309   threshold; if the magnitude of the vector does not reach the threshold, the sample will be filtered out.

      5.3   H UMAN AND AI C O -D ESIGN
312
313   Figure 3 illustrates our co-design process with the dual participation of human and AI (LLM)
314   designers to generate the training data we need. In this process, we seek to generate two parts of data,
315   role script and role code. The former is a natural language json script that details the pokemon role.
316   We use a script-code pair to identify a role. Eventually, we split each role into several states as the
317   training samples for doing incremental prediction.
318   Concretely, we initialize the sampling pool with 20 manually-crafted seed instances of script-code
319   pairs. The human designer first determines the prototype and prompts the LLM designer to generate
320   a novel role script based on the prototype. Then, the LLM designer is prompted to program the role
321   script into the role code. More specifically, we sample 5 instances from the sampling pool to augment
322   the LLM’s coding, while only sampling 1 instance to augment its designing. A key observation is that
323   the in-context instances of other role scripts may bias the effect of the provided prototype, incurring
      low creativity of the response. On the other hand, in-context instances act as useful references for the


                                                                                 6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      LLM to generate high-quality role code since the programming step does not rely on creativity but
325   accuracy. The LLM designer we use is either of GPT4 or Claude3. The newly designed script-code
326   pair will be sent to the evaluator, which is a joint process with both rule-based and manual strategies.
327   First, code that fails to compile or introduces new methods yet without calling them will be filtered
328   out. Second, code that fails to pass the interestingness threshold will also be discarded. After the
329   rule-based filtering, the human designer makes the final check on the script and code. Eventually, we
330   place the new instance into the sampling pool ready for the next cycle of design.
331   An important trick is that, we replace the third-party LLM designers (GPT4/Claude3) with one of the
332   trained neural proxy in the middle of the design process. We find them, yet powerful, still struggle
333   with the nuanced requirements of the programming problem in our project, providing low-accuracy
334   responses, while the trained model can tackle much better.
335
      The incorporation of AI greatly accelerates the creative process of human designers (Rezwana &
336
      Maher, 2023), creating high-quality data. In Figure 3, we observe that human designers mainly act as
337   a prototype designer and a joint evaluator to refine the eventual instances.

      6.1      BASIC S ETTING
347
348   To access the quality of the co-designed data, we prepare another set of data of the same size purely
349   synthesized by Claude3 . Specifically, the synthetic data is generated using a similar pipeline as in
350   Figure 3. The difference is that we automatically sample the official pokemon roles as the prototypes,
351   while canceling the manual evaluation step, since these two steps necessitate human participation.
352   We show the data statistics in the upper half of Table 1.
353
      On the other hand, we prepare two sets of test data, corresponding to easy and hard. The easy-level
354   data comprises 19 existing pokemon roles, all of which have appeared in official pokemon games. We
355   sample them from the internet. This set of data is easier because the majority of roles in the training
356   data, including purely synthesized and co-designed, inevitably share similarity with the existing ones.
357   Their distributions are closer as a result. To deeply access the scalability of the engine, in addition,
358   we invite 10 volunteers to manually craft the hard-level data. All of them are not only experienced in
359   playing pokemon games, but also have a wealth of experience with a wide range of games, greatly
360   allowing them to design novel pokemon roles. Eventually, we obtain 16 original role scripts. We
361   manually program them and obtain the ground truth role code. Beyond originality, volunteers are
362
      asked to craft more moves and abilities for one role, which helps us to better evaluate the scalability.
      From Table 1, we observe that the number of evolution steps and sentence length of hard-level data is
363
      much more than those of easy-level data.
364
365   We fine-tune the CodeGemma-7b model (Mesnard et al., 2024)4 . CodeGemma is a code LLM that is
366   additionally pre-trained on a large number of code corpora. We train each model using LoRA (Hu
367   et al., 2022) with r = 8, α = 32, learning rate 1.5e-4, and batch size 4 for 5 epochs.
368   We report two scores.
369
      Exe%: We calculate the success rate of executing the role code on top of the engine. Specifically, we
370
      randomly synthesize 100 roles as imaginary opponents and have the role under test to battle with
371
      them, choosing a random action each time. One success will be counted if all actions are executed
372   successfully against all opponents.
373
374   Acc%: We further verify the accuracy/correctness of the role code. This step is done by GPT4, which
375
      is prompted to compare two code snippets. Note that we calculate the correctness only among the
      successfully executed code.

      6.2   M AIN R ESULTS
395
396   From the lower half of Table 1, we find that the two models trained on synthetic data and co-designed
397   data (Sy. & Co.) perform comparatively on the easy test set. This is due to the closer gap between
398   training and test data in this scenario. More specifically, the co-designed data by both humans and AI
399   performs slightly better. The resultant model and its retrieval-augmented version achieves a full Exe
400   rate, and the latter also achieves a full Acc rate.
401   On the other hand, the hard test data delivers a large distribution gap from the training data, leading to
402   noticeable performance drop across all three model counterparts. More importantly, the gap between
403   Exe and Acc becomes more pronounced. The elevated Exe rate indicates that the trained model is
404   inclined to respond with executable code even if the input role is unfamiliar. However, the accuracy
405   of code is much harder to fulfill. We find that the model trained only on synthetic data is notably
406   weaker. This is due to the fact that the synthetic data is too limited and doesn’t provide useful signals
407   for out-of-domain generalization. In contrast, the co-design process produces high-quality data with
408   out-of-domain signals, which significantly generalizes the model, improving the Acc rate from 58.6
409
      to 83.9. Furthermore, we find that the retrieval technique also shows its positive impact, further
      improving Acc to 89.7.
410
411   In addition to out-of-domain generalizability, the delta-engine’s scalability includes its scaling
412   performance through long evolution steps. To do this, we conduct another experiment where we
413   continuously scale the delta-engine. Specifically, we randomly sample abilities and moves from the
414   existing database and repeatedly prompt the neural proxy to scale, until it gives a non-executable
415
      response. The result will be a “super patchwork” pokemon role. We repeat this process 100 times.
      Figure 4 shows two histograms, where we demonstrate the scaling performance of the engine through
416
      the evolution steps and the engine size. The engine size refers to the number of tokens of its current
417
      code. Intuitively, we observe that as the evolution increments, the performance exhibits a pronounced
418   degradation. More specifically, as the evolution accumulates to 20 steps, only half of the cases give
419   an executable response. A similar trend can be seen as the engine size accumulates to 5000. Note
420   that, the length limit of the CodeGemma model is 8192. However, we find that the introduction of
421   the retrieval technique brings a nice scalability in the face length increasing. The resultant model
422   maintains a nice performance up to 30 evolution steps. This is because the retrieved engine state is
423   much smaller compared to the entire engine. We illustrate a case on the right side of Figure 4. The
424   model only retrieves the type change method from the engine as the context for the following
425   incremental prediction.
426   So far, we haven’t explore larger-sized LLMs, though they can be promisingly stronger.
