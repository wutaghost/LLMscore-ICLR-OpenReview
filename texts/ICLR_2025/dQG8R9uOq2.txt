011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            At the intersection of computer vision and computational biology, large-scale Pro-
014            tein Language Models (PLMs), particularly the ESM series, have made significant
015
               advances in understanding protein structures and functions. However, these mod-
               els are mainly pre-trained on pure residue sequence, often lack explicit incorpo-
016
               ration of structural information, highlighting an opportunity for enhancement. In
017
               this paper, we design a parameter-efficient fine-tuning method, SI-Tuning, that
018            injects structural information into PLMs while preserving the original model pa-
019            rameters frozen and optimizing a minimal task-specific vector for input embed-
020            ding and attention map. This vector, extracted from structural features like di-
021            hedral angles and distance maps, introduces a structural bias that enhances the
022            model’s performance in downstream tasks. Extensive experiments show that our
023            parameter-efficient fine-tuned ESM-2 650M model outperforms SaProt, a large-
024            scale model pre-trained with protein structural data, in various downstream tasks
025            with a reduction of 40.3% GPU memory and 39.8% time consumption.

030

031   tual merger with Natural Language Processing (NLP) techniques has catalyzed the emergence of
032   Protein Language Models (PLMs), as elucidated by Rao et al. (2019). These innovative models
033   adeptly navigate the vast landscapes of protein sequences through self-supervised learning, exhibit-
034   ing a remarkable capability to unravel the intricate patterns of residue interactions reflective of co-
035   evolutionary processes, a domain extensively explored by Anishchenko et al. (2017) and Rao et al.
036   (2020). In this cutting-edge arena, models such as UniRep (Alley et al., 2019), ProtTrans (Elnag-
037   gar et al., 2021), ESM (Rives et al., 2021; Meier et al., 2021; Rao et al., 2021; Lin et al., 2022),
038   and Evoformer (Hu et al., 2022; Jumper et al., 2021) stand out, having been rigorously pre-trained
039
      on comprehensive datasets. Their exceptional generalization skills have been pivotal in advancing
      research across a spectrum of downstream tasks central to deciphering protein structures and func-
040
      tions. These encompass structure prediction, functional annotation, and protein-protein interactions,
041
      alongside the innovative field of protein design, as evidenced by notable works (Jumper et al., 2021;
042   Gligorijević et al., 2021; Corso et al., 2022; Hsu et al., 2022). Leveraging Parameter-Efficient Fine-
043   Tuning (PEFT) methods could significantly enhance downstream tasks on these PLMs performance
044   with only a minimal increase in trainable parameters.

      2.1.1    S EQUENCE -BASED R EPRESENTATION
123
124
      Sequence-based protein representation learning conceptualizes protein sequences within the frame-
125
      work of natural language processing. Recent endeavors have drawn inspiration from large pre-
126   trained language models, embarking on a self-supervised learning journey across billions of protein
127   sequences to decipher evolutionary information. These sequence-based protein large models (PLMs)
128   conform to the paradigm established by BERT (Devlin et al., 2018) (Bidirectional Encoder Repre-
129   sentations from Transformers), regarding individual protein sequences as sentences and amino acid
130   residues as words. By randomly masking a proportion of residues and deploying multiple layers
131   of self-attention and feedforward networks, these models engage in masked language modeling to
132   predict obscured residues, thereby capturing the interdependencies between residues.
133   Proteins represented by sequence-based PLMs exhibit commendable performance across a variety
134   of downstream tasks. Harnessing a wealth of unlabeled data, the ESM family—comprising contri-
135   butions from Rives et al. (2021); Lin et al. (2023) —has been adept at capturing the evolutionary
136   relationships among proteins, identifying conserved patterns across different species that are essen-
137   tial for biological functionality, and applying these insights to tasks such as protein classification,
138   structure prediction, and interaction prediction. Our paper utilizes the advanced ESM-2 (Lin et al.,
139   2023) model as the backbone. Furthermore, the ESM-1v, developed by (Meier et al., 2021), is a
140
      variant within the Evolutionary Scale Modeling (ESM) family of protein language models. It’s de-
      signed to predict the impacts of mutations on protein functions. Research by Rao et al. (2020) has
141
      demonstrated that Transformer attention maps are capable of learning contacts from unsupervised
142
      language modeling tasks. Elnaggar et al. (2021) trained various Transformer-based models on ex-
143   tensive protein sequence data from the UniRef and BFD databases, encompassing up to 393 billion
144   amino acids. Rao et al. (2021); Biswas et al. (2021); Meier et al. (2021) enhanced the capabilities of
145   Protein Language Models (PLMs) by leveraging training on Multiple Sequence Alignment (MSA)
146   data, which enriches models with evolutionary context and functional insights from a diverse array
147   of protein sequences.
148
149
150   2.1.2    S TRUCTURE -BASED R EPRESENTATION
151
152   The functional attributes of proteins are intrinsically determined by their structures, making
153
      structure-based approaches fundamentally superior for learning rich protein representations com-
      pared to sequence-based methods. The advent of AlphaFold2 (Jumper et al., 2021) has revolution-
154
      ized protein structure prediction, enabling access to extensive structural data. Proteins are often
155
      modeled as graphs with amino acids as nodes and chemical bonds as edges, employing graph neural
156   networks for representation learning through techniques such as contrastive learning and denoising
157   score matching (Hermosilla & Ropinski, 2022; Chen et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2022). GearNet (Zhang
158   et al., 2022) introduces a novel Step-wise Dual Learning approach for addressing the weakly super-
159   vised domain adaptation (WSDA) problem, refining the edge representation of proteins. Another
160   noteworthy development is SaProt (Su et al., 2023), which has shown superior capability in captur-
161   ing the geometric configurations of proteins by integrating a structure-aware vocabulary from 3D
      protein structures, albeit at the cost of significant computational resources and time.

      2.2   PARAMETER -E FFICIENT F INE -T UNING
163
164
      Large-scale pre-trained models have achieved significant success in both natural language process-
165
      ing (Devlin et al., 2018; Qiu et al., 2020) and computer vision fields (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020),
166
      reaching state-of-the-art performance across various tasks. However, the conventional approach of
167   fine-tuning all parameters of pre-trained models becomes less feasible with the increase in model
168   size and task quantity, as retraining all model parameters for complete fine-tuning is impractical.
169   To mitigate this issue, various parameter-efficient transfer learning methods have emerged, with the
170   PEFT(Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning) method fine-tuning only a small number of parameters to
171   achieve robust performance. Several popular lightweight PEFT schemes are now available.
172
      Adapter tuning (Houlsby et al., 2019) introduced small neural modules called adapters between lay-
173   ers of the pre-trained model. Each adapter consists of a down-projection to a lower-dimensional
174   space, followed by a non-linear activation function and an up-projection back to the original di-
175   mension, with only the parameters within these adapter modules being updated during fine-tuning.
176   Prompt tuning, introduced by Lester et al. (2021), is a parameter-efficient method that fine-tunes
177   pre-trained models by attaching trainable vectors (referred to as prompts) to the input embeddings.
178   Unlike methods that modify the model architecture or require training a large number of parameters,
179   prompt tuning adjusts only these additional vectors while keeping the original model parameters
180   frozen. Inspired by the success of prompt-based learning, Li & Liang (2021) proposed prefix tun-
181
      ing, which involves adding a set of tunable prefix vectors to the keys and values of the multi-head
      attention layer in each transformer block. These prefix vectors are optimized during fine-tuning,
182
      while the original pre-trained parameters remain unchanged. Lora, proposed by Hu et al. (2021),
183
      modifies the attention and feed-forward layers of the transformer by learning a low-rank matrix ap-
184   proximating the updates to the original weight matrices. The original weights are kept frozen, and
185   only the low-rank matrices are updated during fine-tuning.
186
187
      Currently, the application of PEFT techniques to large protein models is relatively scarce. PEFT-
      SP (Zeng et al., 2023) utilizes methods like LoRA and Adapter Tuning in the ESM-2 model to better
188
      leverage evolutionary knowledge from protein sequences, leading to more accurate predictions of
189
      signal peptides in proteins. Another approach (Sledzieski et al.; 2023) employs LoRA for training
190   models to predict protein-protein interactions and homooligomer symmetry, demonstrating perfor-
191   mance that either matches or exceeds traditional fine-tuning methods while significantly reducing
192   memory and parameter usage. Current PEFT applications in protein large models are tailored to
193   specific tasks. Our research aims to develop a more generalized PEFT paradigm, incorporating pro-
194   tein structural information into large protein language models to enhance performance across a wide
195   range of downstream tasks.

201   As shown in 2, our structural information injecting tuning (SI-tuning) provides a paradigm of
202   Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) for large protein language models.
203
      Based on pre-trained PLM, we first obtain the sequence information, individual level structural in-
204   formation (angle series) and pairwise structural information (distance map) of the input protein.
205   Then, the angular embedding generated by angle series is injected into the original sequence em-
206   bedding. This results in a protein embedding enriched with geometric information. This enhanced
207   embedding is then fed into the PLM encoder comprising N blocks. The pairwise relationship of
208   amino acid (protein contact map), processed through radial basis functions (RBF), is injected into
209   each attention block, interacting with the attention matrix to enhance the model’s structural aware-
210   ness. Finally, the feature vectors produced by the encoder are passed through an output head tailored
211   for various downstream tasks.
212   For a fair comparison, we employ the ESM classification head which consists of a dense layer,
213   followed by dropout for regularization, a nonlinear activation function (tanh), and a final linear
214   projection layer that maps the processed features to the desired number of output labels. During
215   training, we employ LoRA as the base parameter-efficient fine-tuning method to better integrating
      structural information into pre-trained large protein language models.

      3.1         S EQUENTIAL S TRUCTURAL I NFORMATION E NHANCED E MBEDDING
241   In stereo-chemistry, the sequence of dihedral angles and bond lengths is commonly used to rep-
242   resent the polymers backbones, especially proteins, in internal coordinates. This individual level
243   structural information contains sequential features (angle series) for each amino acid residues in
244   protein structure.
245
246   3.1.1          S EQUENTIAL S TRUCTURAL I NFORMATION F ORMATION
247
248   The sequential structural information of proteins can be characterized by several critical angles that
249
      define their conformation:
250
                  1. Psi (ψ) and Phi (ϕ) Angles: These are the dihedral angles along the protein backbone,
251
                     which dictate the backbone’s configuration and influence whether the structure forms alpha
252                  helices or beta sheets.
253
                  2. Chi (χ) Angles: These angles represent the dihedral angles of protein side chains, with
254
                     each residue having up to four χ angles. These angles are crucial as they determine the
255
                     spatial arrangement of protein side chains, impacting the protein’s function and structure.
256
257
                  3. Theta (θ) and Tau (τ ) Angles: These bond angles in the protein backbone describe the
                     geometric shape of the backbone.
258
259               4. pLDDT scores(p): This is a confidence score that estimates the reliability of each predicted
260                  residue in a protein structure.
261
262
      We leverage the angular structural information above as the first kind of structural information for
      each amino acid apart from residue type, α = {ψ, ϕ, χ1 , χ2 , χ3 , χ4 , θ, τ } ∈ RN ×8 , where N is
263
      the sequence length. Considering the cyclic nature of these angles, we transform these angles with
264
      trigonometric function to form the raw angular structural information xangle ∈ RN ×16 :
265
266                                                  xangle = [sin(ψ), cos(ψ), ..., sin(τ ), cos(τ )].
267
268   This transformation could ensure the periodic property of angle which is suitable for the subsequent
269   embedding processing. For scenarios where predicted structures are utilized, we further integrate
      the confidence score pLDDT scores p into the sequential structural information. The pLDDT scores


                                                                                             5

270
      indicate the confidence levels for each residue’s predicted structure, enabling us to appropriately
271   weight the angular information. This integration allows the pLDDT scores to complement the angu-
272   lar structural information, thereby enhancing the model’s capacity to incorporate confidence levels
273   into the embedding process:
274
275                         xangle = [sin(ψ), cos(ψ), ..., sin(τ ), cos(τ ), p] ∈ RN ×17 .

277   3.1.2    A NGULAR E MBEDDING G ENERATION AND E NHANCEMENT
278
279   To effectively incorporate structural information into the embeddings of protein sequences, we em-
280   ploy a self-gating mechanism, which is a straightforward yet powerful method for feature projection
281   and filtering. This mechanism processes the raw angular structural information, Xangle , by applying
282   a series of linear projections followed by nonlinear activations. This process can be formulated as
283
      follow:
284
                                      Fangle = f (Xangle ) ⊙ σ(g(Xangle )),
285
286   where f and g represent linear transformations, while σ denotes the activation function. Here, we
287   utilize the Gaussian Error Linear Unit (GELU), which can be seen of as a smoother variant of ReLU
288   and is commonly used in the most advanced Transformers.
289
      Finally, the resultant angle embedding Fangle is fused with the original sequence embedding Fseq
290   via weighted sum resulting in an enhanced input embedding:
291
292                                  Finput = Fseq ∗ (1 − γ) + Fangle ∗ γ,
293
294   where γ is a learnable weight that balance the influence of Fangle with the original sequence em-
295
      bedding Fseq .

297   3.2     E NHANCED G EOMETRIC E NCODING WITH R ADIAL BASIS F UNCTIONS
298
299   In the study of molecular representations, topological information such as chemical bonds and in-
300   teratomic interactions is crucial for understanding molecular structures and properties. Therefore,
301   beyond sequence representations, e.g., amino acid residues and dihedral angles, geometric informa-
302
      tion is also a vital aspect for representation. In this paper we leverage the distance matrix as the
      geometric information, the distance map represents the distance between all possible amino acid
303
      residue pairs, which is much more concise than 3D atomic coordinates and is invariant to rotations
304
      and translations. Inspired by Geoformer (Wang et al., 2024), we design the Geo-Inject Attention to
305   inject the geometric information into pre-trained PLMs, which enables PLMs to perceive geometric
306   information.

      3.3   L OW-R ANK ADAPTATION FOR PLM
347
348   To better integrating structural embeddings into pre-trained PLM, we leverage a commonly used
349   PEFT method Low-Rank adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021) to efficiently adapting PLM to down-
350   stream tasks with our structural embeddings (Fangle and Fcorrd ). It is worth noting that our struc-
351   tural embeddings can be incorporate with various PEFT methods for foundation model. Here we
352   choose LoRA since it do not introduce additional latency during inference for computational effi-
353
      ciency.
354   To be specific, each projection matrix W∗ for the query (Q), key (K) and value (V ) in the attention
355   block is augmented with a corresponding low-rank update matrix ∆W = BA. Here, B ∈ Rdh ×r
356   and A ∈ Rr×dk , where dh is the dimension of input X and r is the rank which is much less than dh
357   and dk . The transformation can be formulated as follow:
358                                    Q       Wq          α B q Aq
                                         !         !                  !
359                                    K = Wk X +              Bk Ak X,
360                                    V       W            r B A
                                                   v                 v    v
361
      where α is a scaling constant that adjusts the contribution of the low-rank updates relative to the
362
      original weight matrices. Following Hu et al. (2021), each A is initialized with a random Gaussian
363   distribution and B is set to zero. This setting ensures that ∆W = BA starts from zero, preserving
364   the original learned parameters of the ESM-2 model at the beginning of training. During training,
365   the pre-trained weights W∗ are frozen.

529   5   C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this paper, we propose Structural information injection tuning (SI-Tuning), a parameter-efficient
532
      fine-tuning framework, for PLM fine-tuning. Considering the lack of structural comprehension
533
      of current PLMs, we introduce two injection strategies to integrate different kinds of structural
534   information into pre-trained PLM. To be specific, for individual level structural information, i.e.
535   angle series, we fuse the angular embeddings into the original sequence embeddings. While for
536   pairwise relationship of amino acid residues, i.e. distance maps, we first transform these maps with
537   radial basis functions and inject this correlation into the attention matrix of each attention blocks.
538   Combining these injection strategies, we efficiently fine-tune the PLM with LoRA modules for
539   downstream tasks. Extensive experiments on five downstream datasets confirmed the effectiveness
      of our approach.
