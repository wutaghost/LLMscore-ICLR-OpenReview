009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Backpropagation (BP) remains the dominant and most successful method for
012            training parameters of deep neural network models. However, BP relies on two
013            computationally distinct phases, does not provide a satisfactory explanation of
014            biological learning, and can be challenging to apply for training of networks with
015            discontinuities or noisy node dynamics. By comparison, node perturbation (NP)
016
               proposes learning by the injection of noise into network activations, and subsequent
               measurement of the induced loss change. NP relies on two forward (inference)
017
               passes, does not make use of network derivatives, and has been proposed as a model
018
               for learning in biological systems. However, standard NP is highly data inefficient
019            and unstable due to its unguided noise-based search process. In this work, we
020            investigate different formulations of NP and relate it to the concept of directional
021            derivatives as well as combining it with a decorrelating mechanism for layer-wise
022            inputs. We find that a closer alignment with directional derivatives together with
023            input decorrelation at every layer strongly enhances performance of NP learning
024            with large improvements in parameter convergence and much higher performance
025            on the test data, approaching that of BP. Furthermore, our novel formulation allows
026            for application to noisy systems in which the noise process itself is inaccessible.
027
028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Backpropagation (BP) is the workhorse of modern artificial intelligence. It provides an efficient
031   way of performing multi-layer credit assignment, given a differentiable neural network architecture
032   and loss function (Linnainmaa, 1970). Despite BP’s successes, it requires an auto-differentiation
033   framework for the backward assignment of credit, introducing a distinction between a forward,
034   or inference phase, and a backward, or learning phase, increasing algorithmic complexity and
035   impeding implementation in (neuromorphic) hardware (Kaspar et al., 2021; Zenke & Neftci, 2021).
036   Furthermore, BP has long been criticized for its lack of biological detail and plausibility (Grossberg,
037   1987; Crick, 1989; Lillicrap et al., 2016), with significant concerns again being the two separate
038
      forward and backward phases, but also its reliance on gradient propagation and the non-locality of
      the information required for credit assignment.
039
040   Alternative algorithms have been put forth over the years, though their inability to scale and difficulty
041   in achieving levels of performance comparable to BP have held back their use. Many focus either
042   on greedy local learning rules or on random feedback matrices and projections, which are deemed
043   more plausible than symmetric feedback weights (Dellaferrera & Kreiman, 2022; Frenkel et al., 2021;
044
      Kohan et al., 2018; Hinton, 2022)
045   The category of algorithms we focus on in this work are perturbation algorithms, such as node
046   perturbation (NP) (Dembo & Kailath, 1990; Cauwenberghs, 1992), which require no feedback
047   connectivity at all. In NP, node activations are perturbed by a small amount of random noise. Weights
048   are then updated to produce the perturbed activations in proportion to the degree by which the
049   perturbation improved performance. This method requires a measure of the loss function being
050
      optimized on a network both before and after the inclusion of noise. Such an approach is appealing
      because it leverages the same forward pass twice, rather than relying on two computationally distinct
051
      phases. It also does not require non-local information other than the global performance signal.
052
053   Despite these benefits, Hiratani et al. (2022) demonstrate that NP is extremely inefficient compared
      to BP, requiring two to three orders of magnitude more training cycles, depending on network depth


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      and width. In addition, they found training with NP to be unstable, in many cases due to exploding
055   weights. Another phenomenon uncovered in their work is that the covariance of NP’s updates is
056   significantly higher than that of BP, which can mathematically be described as an effect mediated by
057   correlations in the input data.
058
      Another proposed approach, referred to as weight perturbation (WP), is to inject noise directly into
059
      the weights of a network, rather than the activations (Werfel et al., 2003; Fiete & Seung, 2006). This
060
      method shows similarities to evolutionary algorithms and can outperform NP in special cases (Züge
061   et al., 2021). A downside to WP is that there are many more weights than nodes in neural networks,
062   making the exploration space larger thus slowing down learning. In fact, both NP and WP lag far
063   behind BP in terms of efficiency. This is to be expected, however, as noise perturbations conduct a
064   random search through parameter space, rather than being gradient directed.
065
      In this work, we put forward three contributions. First, we reframe the process of node perturbation
066
      together with the subsequent measurement of the output loss change in terms of directional derivatives.
067   This provides a more solid theoretical foundation for node perturbation and, consequently, a different
068   update rule, which we refer to as iterative node perturbation. Directional derivatives have been related
069   to NP before in the context of forward gradient learning (Baydin et al., 2022; Ren et al., 2022).
070   This approach is different from ours in that the perturbations are used to estimate gradients, but
071   node-perturbed forward passes are not performed network-wide, making these approaches unsuitable
072   for use in true noisy systems.
073   Second, we introduce an approximation to iterative node perturbation, referred to as activity-based
074   node perturbation, which is more efficient and has the additional advantage that it can be imple-
075   mented in noisy systems such as imprecise hardware implementations (Gokmen, 2021) or biological
076   systems (Faisal et al., 2008), where the noise itself is not directly measurable.
077
      Third, we propose to use a decorrelation method, first described by Ahmad et al. (2023), to debias
078
      layer-wise activations and thereby achieve faster learning. Because NP-style methods directly
079   correlate perturbations of unit activities with changes in a reward signal, decorrelation of these unit
080   activities helps to eliminate confounding effects, making credit assignment more straightforward. In
081   addition, as demonstrated by Hiratani et al. (2022), correlations in the input data lead to more bias
082   in NP’s updates by increasing their covariance. By combining decorrelation with the different NP
083   methods, we find that it is possible to achieve orders of magnitude increase in model convergence
084   speed, with performance levels rivaling networks trained by BP in certain contexts.

      2.1.3    ACTIVITY- BASED NODE PERTURBATION
176
177   Though theoretically grounded, INP incurs significant overhead compared to the direct noise-based
178   update method. Specifically, in order to precisely compute directional derivatives, noise must be
179   applied in a purely layer-wise fashion, requiring a high degree of control over the noise injection. This
180   is less biologically plausible and functionally more challenging implementation with less potential
181
      for scaling. Furthermore, the number of forward passes required to compute such an update for a
      whole network for INP is then the number of layers plus one, L + 1, potentially many times larger
182
      than the two passes required for NP.
183
184   To balance learning speed and computational cost we instead approximate the directional derivative
185   across the whole network simultaneously. This involves assuming that all node activations in our
186   network are independent and treating the entire network as if it were a single layer. This can be
187
      achieved by, instead of measuring and tracking the injected noise alone, measuring the state difference
      between the clean and noisy forward passes of the whole network. Note that this is a significant
188
      departure from traditional node perturbation in which access to the underlying noise source is required.
189
      Concretely, taking the definition of the forward pass given by NP in Eq. 2, we define
190
                                                              δal
191                                       ∆WlANP = N δL             x⊤                                       (6)
192
                                                            ||δa||2 l−1
                   PL
193   where N = l=0 Nl is the number of units in the network, δal = ãl − al is the activity difference
194   between a noisy and a clean pass in layer l and δa = (δa1 , . . . , δaL ) is the concatenation of activity
195   differences. The resulting update rule is referred to as activity-based node perturbation (ANP).
196   Appendix C provides a derivation of this rule.
197   Here, we have now updated multiple aspects of the NP rule. First, rather than using the measure of
198   noise injected at each layer, we instead measure the total change in activation between the clean and
199   noisy networks. However, relative to BP our derived ANP rule has a biased estimation in gradient
200   measurement. Appendix D describes this bias and shows that, when considering finite noise injection,
201   this bias is measurable in closed form and is interpretable.
202   Second, this direct use of the activity difference also requires a recomputation of the scale of the
203   perturbation vector. Here we carry the rescaling out by a normalization based upon the activity-
204   difference length and then upscale the signal of this perturbation based upon the network size.
205
      Note that the ANP rule is now a hybridization of the directional derivative and traditional node
206
      perturbation. In the limit where the nodes are not interdependent (i.e. when we have a single layer),
207
      it is equivalent to INP and upon averaging across many samples can approximate the true gradient
208   with a distinct bias based upon correlations in propagated noise. When used in a layered network
209   structure, it ‘assumes’ that all nodes are mutually independent even though they are not. This is the
210   only remaining approximation for this approach and it is desirable to allow whole network learning
211   without direct access to the noise vectors.

      4    D ISCUSSION
487
488
489   In this work, we explored several formulations of NP learning and also introduced a layer-wise
490
      decorrelation method which strongly outperforms baseline implementations. In our results we show
      robust speedups in training compared to regular NP, suggesting that our alternative formulations of
491
      NP could prove to be competitive with traditional BP in certain contexts. We attribute this increased
492
      efficacy to a more efficient credit assignment by virtue of decorrelated input features at every layer,
493   as well as an attenuation of the bias in NP updates caused by their covariance.
494
495   The performance of all of our proposed methods is substantially improved by including decorrelation.
496
      When comparing NP, INP and ANP, a number of observations are in order. First, INP yields the best
      results stemming from its robust relationship to the directional derivative and underlying gradient.
497
      The INP method does, however, require running as many forward passes as there are layers in the
498
      network (plus one ‘clean’ pass). This is a drawback for this method and should be considered when
499   analyzing its performance, though it should also be considered that it could remain efficient on
500   hardware in which all forward passes are parallelizable. Second, decorrelated variants of NP and
501   ANP both perform well. An additional advantage of the latter though is that one does not need
502   explicit access to the noise vectors, which has important implications for the development of physical
503   learning machines, as described below.
504
      Noise-based learning approaches might also be ideally suited for implementation on neuromorphic
505   hardware (Kaspar et al., 2021). First, as demonstrated in Figure 5, our proposed DANP algorithm
506   scales well even when there is no access to a ‘clean’ model pass. Furthermore, DANP does not
507   require access to the noise signal itself, in contrast to NP. This means that such an approach could
508   be ideally suited for implementation in noisy physical devices (Gokmen, 2021) even in case the
509   noise cannot be measured. Even on traditional hardware architectures, forward passes are often
510   easier to optimize than backward passes and are often significantly faster to compute. This can be
511   especially true for neuromorphic computing approaches, where backward passes require automatic
512   differentiation implementations and a separate computational pipeline for backward passes (Zenke &
513
      Neftci, 2021). In these cases, a noise-based approach to learning could prove highly efficient.
514   Exploring more efficient forms of noise-based learning is interesting beyond credit-assignment alone.
515   This form of learning is more biologically plausible as it does not require weight transport nor any
516   specific feedback processing (Grossberg, 1987; Crick, 1989; Lillicrap et al., 2016). There is ample
517   evidence for noise in biological neural networks (Faisal et al., 2008) and we suggest here that this
518   could be effectively used for learning. Furthermore, the positive impact of decorrelation on learning
519
      warrants further investigation of how this mechanism might be involved in neural plasticity. It is
      interesting to note that various mechanisms act to reduce correlation or induce whitening, especially
520
      in early visual cortical areas (King et al., 2013). Additionally, lateral inhibition, which is known
521
      to occur in the brain, can be interpreted as a way to reduce redundancy in input signals akin to
522   decorrelation, making outputs of neurons less similar to each other (Békésy, 1967). As described
523   in (Ahmad et al., 2023), decorrelation updates can rely exclusively on information that is locally
524   available to the neuron, making it amenable to implementation in biological or physical systems.
525
      Though the presented methods for effective noise-based learning show a great deal of promise, there
526
      are a number of additional research steps to be taken. The architectures considered are still relatively
527
      shallow, and thus an investigation into how well this approach scales for very deep networks would
528   be beneficial. Testing the scalability of these approaches to tasks of greater complexity is also crucial,
529   as are their application to other network architectures such as residual networks, recurrent networks
530   and attention-based models. Our scaling results do demonstrate that by resampling the noise we
531   achieve better and better alignment with the gradient direction, by virtue of the formal correspondence
532   between directional derivatives and our formulations of node perturbation. In this sense, our approach
533   can be viewed as an alternative to forward accumulation, where we exchange memory complexity
534   (maintaining all partial derivatives) for time complexity (repeated forward passes with resampled
535   noise vectors).
536   In general, our work opens up exciting opportunities since it has the potential to bring gradient-free
537   training of deep neural networks within reach. That is, in addition to not requiring a backward pass,
538   efficient noise-based learning may also lend itself to networks not easily trained by backpropagation,
539   such as those consisting of activation functions with jumps, binary networks or networks in which
      the computational graph is broken, as in reinforcement learning.

      A    W EIGHTS PROPAGATE NOISE CORRELATIONS
649
650
      Traditional NP updates have a number of sources of error. One reason is that use of the injected noise
651
      ϵl to compute the learning signal ignores how the output activity of layer l is not only impacted by
652   the noise injected directly into it, but also by the cumulative effect of perturbations added to previous
653   layers. If the noise is randomly sampled from a Gaussian distribution with mean zero, one might
654   be tempted to simply assume that the perturbations from previous layers cancel out in expectation,
655   but this assumption ignores the correlations introduced into these perturbations by the network’s
656   weight matrices at all preceding layers. Multiplying a random vector by a non-orthogonal matrix will
657   introduce a non-random covariance structure into it. To see why, consider a transformation Wx = y,
658   where W is a randomly initialized weight matrix and x is an uncorrelated input vector for which it
659   holds that ⟨xx⊤ ⟩ = I with I the identity matrix. The covariance matrix of y can be described as
660
                         covy = ⟨yy⊤ ⟩ = ⟨(Wx)(Wx)T ⟩ = ⟨(Wx)(x⊤ W⊤ )⟩
661
662                           = ⟨Wxx⊤ W⊤ ⟩ = W⟨xx⊤ ⟩W⊤ = WIW⊤ = WW⊤
663
      Therefore, any matrix for which WW⊤ ̸= I will add some covariance structure into output vector y,
664
      even when input vector x is uncorrelated.

713

714

       Figure 9 shows train and test loss for (D)NP, (D)ANP and (D)BP for the SARCOS dataset (Vijayaku-
975    mar & Schaal, 2000). The task is to solve inverse dynamics problem for a seven degrees-of-freedom
976    SARCOS anthropomorphic robot arm by mapping from a 21-dimensional input space (7 joint posi-
977    tions, 7 joint velocities, 7 joint accelerations) to the corresponding 7 joint torques. The network is a
978    fully connected architecture with two 32-unit hidden layers and was trained with MSE loss and the
979    leaky ReLU activation function. The network was trained for 500 epochs and results were averaged
980    over three random seeds. A learning rate search for each algorithm was started at 10−7 , doubling the
981    learning rate until the algorithm became unstable. A decorrelation learning rate of 10−4 was used for
982    DNP and DANP and 10−3 for DBP.
983    The results indicate that while NP and ANP lag BP in performance, DNP and DANP perform almost
984    as well as BP. Like in other experiments reported in this work, DBP performs best overall. Final test
985    losses were 11.18 (NP), 10.51 (DNP), 15.86 (ANP), 9.79 (DANP), 9.48 (BP) and 7.02 (DBP).
