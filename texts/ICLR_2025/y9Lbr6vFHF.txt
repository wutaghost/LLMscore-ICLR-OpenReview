009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Backdoor attacks have seriously threatened deep neural networks (DNNs) by em-
012            bedding concealed vulnerabilities through data poisoning. To counteract these
013            attacks, training benign models from poisoned data garnered considerable interest
014            from researchers. High-performing defenses often rely on additional clean subsets,
015            which is untenable due to increasing privacy concerns and data scarcity. In the ab-
016            sence of clean subsets, defenders resort to complex feature extraction and analysis,
017            resulting in excessive overhead and compromised performance. In the face of these
018            challenges, we identify the key lies in sufficient utilization of the easier-to-obtain
019
               target labels and excavation of clean hard samples. In this work, we propose a
               Bi-perspective Splitting Defense (BSD). BSD splits the dataset using both semantic
020
               and loss statistics characteristics through open set recognition-based splitting (OSS)
021
               and altruistic model-based data splitting (ALS) respectively, achieving good clean
022            pool initialization. BSD further introduces class completion and selective dropping
023            strategies in the subsequent pool updates to avoid potential class underfitting and
024            backdoor overfitting caused by loss-guided split. Through extensive experiments on
025            benchmark datasets and against representative attacks, we empirically demonstrate
026            that our BSD is robust across various attack settings. Specifically, BSD has an aver-
027            age improvement in Defense Effectiveness Rating (DER) by 16.29% compared to 5
028            state-of-the-art defenses, achieving clean-data-free backdoor security with minimal
029            compromise in both Clean Accuracy (CA) and Attack Success Rate (ASR).
030

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Recent studies exposed the vulnerabilities of deep neural networks (DNNs) to various attacks (Carlini
035   & Wagner, 2017; Moosavi-Dezfooli et al., 2016; Kurakin et al., 2018; Zeng et al., 2019; Ilyas
036   et al., 2018), among which backdoor attacks (Li et al., 2022; Wenger et al., 2021; Zhang et al.,
037   2021; Wang et al., 2020) have emerged as a significant threat due to their ease of execution and
038   profound impact. Owing to their non-model-manipulation property and congruence with actual
039
      model training scenarios, data-poisoning-based backdoor attacks (Goldblum et al., 2022; Shafahi
      et al., 2018) stand out as prevalent and impactful threats, highlighting the importance of backdoor
040
      defense research. Taking facial recognition as an example (Figure 1), poisoned data may induce the
041
      DNNs to erroneously learn a strong correlation between the adversary-defined trigger pattern (e.g.,
042   sunglasses) and the target label (e.g., a high-authority individual). While behaving normally without
043   the trigger, the backdoored model predicts any individuals wearing sunglasses as the pre-determined
044   high-authority person. Following the current mainstream research on backdoor attacks, we focus on
045   image classification tasks as the entry point for studying backdoor defenses.
046
      Recently, a branch of in-training defenses has focused on training benign models directly from
047
      poisoned data, which is particularly significant when developing our own models using untrustworthy
048   datasets. They primarily adhere to a data-splitting paradigm that differentiates between benign
049   and poisoned samples, and disrupts the association between trigger patterns and target labels to
050   mitigate backdoor behaviors. To name a few, Anti-backdoor learning (ABL) (Li et al., 2021a)
051   isolates poisoned samples through local gradient ascent and unlearns the underlying malicious pattern.
052   Decoupling-based defense (DBD) (Huang et al., 2022) utilizes self-supervised learning for a secure
053   feature extractor to identify unconfident samples as malicious. Adaptive splitting-based defense
      (ASD) (Gao et al., 2023) further introduces meta-split to identify clean hard samples.

064   Intuitively, clean subsets play a vital role in various backdoor defenses (Zhu et al., 2024; Liu et al.,
065   2018; Wu & Wang, 2021; Zeng et al., 2021; Li et al., 2023a), as they could provide insight into
066   the decision-making features of clean samples (e.g., as long as we know the facial characteristics
067   of people, we can accurately recognize them without being misled by accessories). In-training
068   defenses rely on clean seed samples for better performance (Gao et al., 2023). However, recollecting
069   a clean subset can be extremely expensive when the training set has a large number of classes
070   (e.g., collecting new benign facial records for millions of people within the facial database), and
071   manually checking a large training set to select a clean subset is time-consuming and risky for privacy
072
      leaks. In addition, the potential presence of stealthy malicious ’clean sets’ can further undermine the
      effectiveness of these defenses, as triggers like sunglasses and image warping are hard to identify.
073
      While certain in-training defenses seek to work without clean subsets, they may involve complicated
074
      feature extraction and analysis, suffering from significant training costs (Huang et al., 2022) and
075   compromised performance (Li et al., 2021a; Chen et al., 2022a; Tran et al., 2018; Weber et al., 2023;
076   Liu et al., 2023).
077
078
      In this work, we focus on improving the state-of-the-art in-training defense under the challenging
      non-clean-seed-involved scenario. We identify the insufficient utilization of the easier-to-obtain
079
      target labels and clean hard samples of existing methods, and propose a Bi-perspective Splitting
080
      Defense (BSD) that splits the dataset using both semantic and loss statistics characteristics of poisoned
081   samples.
082
083   Specifically, BSD first initialize the clean and poison pools through open set recognition-based
084
      splitting (OSS) and altruistic model-based data splitting (ALS). OSS reframes the identification of
      poisoned samples within the target class as an open-set recognition problem. Non-target classes are
085
      designated as known-known classes (KKCs) to warm up the main model, thus true clean samples
086
      within the target class are distinguished as an unknown-unknown class (UUC) because their semantic
087   information is unseen to the model. ALS utilizes an altruistic model to reveal reliable clean hard
088   samples with high loss difference to the main model. Since the above two mechanisms complement
089   each other by employing different judgment perspectives, the intersection of their results provides a
090   robust initialization.
091
      Subsequently, to prevent potential underfitting of certain classes (i.e., clean pools do not encompass
092
      all the classes) and to capture evasive poisoned samples (i.e., clean pools include some poisoned
093   samples), BSD adopts class completion and selective dropping strategies during subsequent pool
094   updates, ameliorating the loss-perspective-only splitting result.
095
096
      In summary, our main contributions are:
097
             • We investigate the realistic and challenging task of training time backdoor defense without
098            clean seed samples, and identify two main breakthrough points of the problem.
099
100
             • We propose two novel pool initialization mechanisms in BSD, namely ALS and OSS. They
               leverage the loss statistics of clean hard samples based on altruistic models and reframe the
101
               splitting as an open set recognition task for better initialization respectively, accomplishing
102
               effective backdoor defense free from the clean seed samples.
103
104          • We introduce two new pool update strategies based on the altruistic model to address the
105
               potential collapse. Class completion and selective dropping deal with the missing classes
               and evaded poison samples respectively.
106
107          • Extensive experiments demonstrate that BSD has an average improvement in Defense
               Effectiveness Rating (DER) by 16.29% compared to 5 state-of-the-art defenses.

      2     R ELATED WORKS
109
110
      Currently, the countermeasures for backdoor attacks fall into two main categories:
111
112   Post-training backdoor defenses focus on repairing a backdoored model with a set of locally
113   prepared clean training sets. Trigger inversion (Sur et al., 2023) is a popular method to reconstruct
114
      the trigger pattern and then unlearn it to renovate the model. In addition to trigger-synthesis defenses,
      pruning, distillation, finetuning, and model connectivity analysis (Liu et al., 2018; Wu & Wang, 2021;
115
      Li et al., 2023a) are widely applied in the realm of backdoor defense as well. Despite the promising
116
      results, most post-training methods assume using an extra clean set for defense, which introduces
117   potential limitations.
118
119   In-training backdoor defenses aim at training a benign model from the polluted dataset, which holds
120
      considerable practical significance (Chen et al., 2022a; Tran et al., 2018; Weber et al., 2023; Liu et al.,
      2023). Following an intuitive idea of splitting the dataset into clean and poison pools and treating them
121
      separately, several representative training-time defenses, namely Anti-backdoor learning (ABL) (Li
122
      et al., 2021a), Decoupled-based defense (DBD) (Huang et al., 2022), and Adaptive splitting-based
123   defense (ASD) (Gao et al., 2023), have garnered attention. Anti-backdoor learning (ABL) (Li et al.,
124   2021a) isolates a small ratio of poisoned samples through local gradient ascent and unlearns these
125   samples to neutralize the effect of remaining poisoned samples in the clean pool. Decoupling-based
126   defense (DBD) (Huang et al., 2022) utilizes self-supervised learning to acquire a benign feature
127   extractor and uses a clean subset to initialize the classifier head. Then, it separates the suspicious
128   according to the loss magnitude and breaks the link between the trigger and the target label through
129   semi-supervised learning. Adaptive splitting-based defense (ASD) (Gao et al., 2023) further improves
130   the initialization based on clean seed samples and introduces meta-split to identify clean hard samples,
131
      achieving higher clean accuracy (CA). Besides these defenses, adopting differential-privacy SGD (Du
      et al., 2019) and strong data augmentation (Borgnia et al., 2021) can also defend against backdoor
132
      attacks to some degree. Our BSD belongs to the data-splitting in-training defenses and makes further
133
      adaptions.

      4    P ROPOSED METHOD
200
      Our BSD has three main components as illustrated in Figure 2. As we assume no extra clean subset
201
      access, pool initialization is vital to the defense. To ensure a secure initialization, open set recognition-
202
      based splitting (OSS) and altruistic model-based splitting (ALS) focus on the perspectives of image
203   semantic information and loss statistics respectively. Based on the altruistic model introduced in
204   ALS, we further improve the pool update with class completion and selective dropping strategy.
205
206
      (1) OSS is motivated by the similarity between the open-set recognition task and poison sample
      detection in backdoor defense. As the main model is warmed up using Dnt , poison samples are
207
      unknown-known-classes (UKCs) whose semantic information is included in Dnt , thus having smaller
208
      minimum distances to feature clusters of known-known-classes (KKCs). Clean target samples fall
209   into a new cluster and have larger minimum distances. Detailed description in Section 4.1.1.
210
211   (2) ALS highlights the clean hard samples with high loss values in the altruistic model, which could
212
      filter out the overfitted poison samples. A detailed description of ALS is provided in Section 4.1.2.
213   (3) Subsequent training of BSD follow a loss-guided split, which uses the loss difference of a sample
214   between the main and altruistic model to distinguish samples. BSD compensates the less selected
215   categories and drops the evaded poison samples using class completion and selective dropping
      strategies respectively. A detailed description of subsequent training is provided in Section 4.2.

226   4.1.1    O PENSET RECOGNITION BASED SPLITING
227
      Openset recognition (OSR) is a task that aims to identify whether a test sample belongs to one
228
      of the semantic classes in a classifier’s training set. In the context of OSR, identifying unknown-
229   known classes (UKCs) and unknown-unknown classes (UUCs) are two major tasks. Here UKCs
230   refer to classes for which no samples are available in training, but their side information (such as
231   semantic/attribute information, etc.) can be obtained during training. UUCs refer to classes that do
232   not have any relevant information during the training process: not only have they not been seen, but
233   there is also no side information during the training process.
234
      We notice that distinguishing the clean target samples and poison samples is related to the UKCs
235   and UUCs identification in OSR. The poison samples are sort of UKCs because the triggers do not
236   corrupt their semantic information. Hence, we set out to cast the clean target samples to UUCs, which
237   can reframe the splitting within the target class into an OSR problem.
238
      To make the poison samples and clean target samples belong to the UKCs and UUCs respectively,
239
      the known-known classes (KKCs, i.e. the training set) should contain the semantic classes of UKCs
240
      (Dp ), while information of the UUCs (Dct ) is not included. Therefore, we construct the KKCs with
241   the non-target classes (Dnt ) which satisfies both requirements above. Thus, we can train the main
242   model fθ on Dnt for its warm-up, i.e., θ = argminLsemi (Dnt ; fθ ).

      Defense Baselines. We compare our proposed BSD with five existing backdoor defenses, namely Fine-
336
      pruning (FP) (Liu et al., 2018), Neural Attention Distillation (NAD) (Li et al., 2021b), Anti-Backdoor-
337   Learning (ABL) (Li et al., 2021a), Decoupling-based Backdoor Defense (DBD) (Huang et al., 2022),
338   and Adaptive Splitting-based backdoor Defense (ASD) (Gao et al., 2023). The detailed settings for
339   all defense baselines are as suggested in ASD. For our BSD, we adopt the MixMatch (Berthelot
340   et al., 2019b) semi-supervised training framework for the main model, following Decoupling-based
341   Defense (DBD) and Adaptive Splitting-based Defense (ASD). The altruistic model undergoes a
342   warm-up phase with 25 epochs, utilizing the Adam optimizer, cross entropy loss, with a learning rate
343   of 0.001. The default warm-up epochs for the main model in OSS are set to 20 (T1 = 20), with a
344   default fixed β of 0.2. Class completion training spans 60 epochs (T2 = 90), and selective dropping
345
      training spans 30 epochs (T3 = 120). The clean pool ratio α follows a sinusoidal growth curve
      during class rebalance training, starts at 0.2, and reaches an upper limit of 0.6 at the end of the class
346
      completion stage, after which it remains fixed. Additional details are available in Appendix C.4.
347
348   Evaluation metrics. We assess the effectiveness of backdoor defenses using two widely used metrics:
349   Clean Accuracy (CA) and the attack success rate (ASR). To be specific, the CA is the accuracy of
350   clean data, the ASR is defined as the proportion of poisoned samples that are misclassified as the
351
      target class by the model. In the context of backdoor defense, superior performance is characterized
      by higher CA and lower ASR. To comprehensively evaluate the performance of defense methods, we
352
      include another metric named Defense Effectiveness Rating (DER) (Zhu et al., 2023a), higher DER
353
      indicate better defense performance. The detailed definition of DER is provided in Appendix C.5.

364   them with lower ASRs when OSS is used as the alternative initialization. Additionally, the CAs of
365
      BSD surpass those of FP and NAD. Concerning recently proposed training-time defenses, the BSD
      has best result in general. ABL, which assumes no presence of clean subsets, has relatively close
366
      performance under CIFAR-10 & BadNets, GTSRB & BadNets, and GTSRB & Blend. Nevertheless,
367
      the CA under CIFAR-10 & WaNet indicates a class underfitting collapse (CAs on certain classes are
368   close to 0%) and its performance is inferior to that of BSD in general. For another representative
369   training-time defense DBD, although it has a slight edge in ASRs on CIFAR-10, its average ASRs
370   and CAs fall behind our BSD. ASD, which assumes an extra small clean seed set is characterized by
371   consistent high CAs and stable ASRs. However, BSD still surpasses it in general. In summary, our
372   BSD performance remains competitive and, in some cases, surpasses that of state-of-the-art methods.

408   5.3   R ESISTANCE TO MORE ATTACKS
409
410   In addition to the representative attacks presented in the main results, we investigated four more
411   attacks that may be threatening to existing defenses. They consist of one invisible attack, ReFool (Liu
412   et al., 2020), and three clean-label attacks, LC (Turner et al., 2019), SIG (Barni et al., 2019), and
413   Narcissus (Zeng et al., 2023b). ReFool uses a physical yet stealthy reflection trigger, which makes
414
      the backdoor hard to detect. LC, SIG, and Narcissus belong to the clean-label attack, which is a type
      of tricky backdoor attack that does not change the label of samples, making most of the defenses
415
      ineffective (where DBD has the most significant performance degradation). For our BSD, clean-label
416
      attacks are less threatening. While the OSS mechanism can be evaded as the semantic information is
417   Dt is consistent. Fortunately, ALS still functions effectively with its loss-perspective splitting in this
418   scenario, compensating for the limitations of OSS. As shown in Table 2, BSD is not evaded by any
419   of the attacks and achieves the best average DER. Additional details of attack implementation are
420   available in Appendix C.3.

      5.6    T RAINING COST
479
      Our BSD incorporates an altruistic model to assist with pool initialization and updates, which may
480   raise concerns about increased training costs. However, as shown in Table 4, the training cost of BSD
481   is comparable to, or even lower than, that of ASD (Gao et al., 2023). This is due to three key factors:
482   1) The altruistic model is updated through standard training rather than MixMatch, significantly
483   reducing time. 2) The altruistic model is only updated before stage 3, and its training primarily runs
484   in parallel with the main model. 3) An imbalanced pool size, as seen in the early stages of ASD,
485   often triggers frequent dataloader updates in MixMatch, whereas the clean pool size in BSD is more
      balanced and suitable during training.

758
      A.1     E XTENDED RELATED WORKS
759
760
      With the advance of clean subset extraction and backdoor detection, many works tries to split clean
761   subset from poison training sets. Zeng et al. (2023a) proposed detecting poisoned data by identifying
762   shifts in data distributions, which results in high prediction loss when training on the clean portion
763   of a poisoned dataset and testing on the corrupted portion. They solve a relaxed of the splitting
764   optimization problem with the help of a weight-assigning network. Although promising empirical
765   results were presented, the proposed META-SIFT only guarantees a relatively small subset ((Zeng
766   et al., 2023a), page 10, Figure 5). As a result, META-SIFT still relies on effective downstream
767   defenses, such as NAD and ASD, included in our baselines, while also increasing the hyperparameter
768   search space. Pan et al. (2023) are motivated by the same distributional shift phenomenon and
769
      proposed an effective splitting algorithm, ASSET. However, they assume that the defender has an
      extra set of clean samples (named ”base set” in (Pan et al., 2023)), which doesn’t suit the background
770
      of our paper, where no extra clean set is available. Plus, ASSET is faced with the same problem that
771
      requires effective downstream defenses to conduct the defense.
772
773   In general, these works indeed provide valuable insights into the poisoned data splitting problem
774   and could inspire our future research. However, they are faced with two major problems. 1) Cannot
775
      guarantee a 100% correct split that can be directly used for training; 2) Rely on an extra clean set
      which violates the constraints of our scenario.

882   A.5           ROBUSTNESS AGAINST ALL 2 ALL ATTACKS
883
884   All-to-all (all2all) attacks may pose challenges to certain components of our defense, particularly
885   OSS and selective drop. However, all2all attacks are not typically considered essential scenarios in
886   backdoor defense research currently (Li et al., 2021a; Huang et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2023b; Guan
887   et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023), for the following reasons: 1) The increased number of trigger-target
888
      pairs in all2all attacks requires significantly more training epochs for success. And all2all attacks
      reduce clean accuracy and exhibit slower convergence, making them easier to detect. (Huang et al.
889
      (2024), Page 2: “As the number of classes increases, the accuracy and the attack success rate will
890
      decrease.”) 2) Research on all2all attacks remains limited (Li et al. (2022), Page 10: “However, there
891   were only a few studies on all-to-all attacks. How to better design the all-to-all attack and the analysis
892   of its properties remain blank.”). 3) In practical applications, all2all attacks do not allow attackers to
893   arbitrarily control predictions to specific targets, limiting their real-world threat.
894
      Nevertheless, we still conducted supplementary experiments on BadNets with an all2all setting.
895
896   Attack setting: Following BadNets, with yt = (y + 1)%nc , where nc is the number of classes.
897
      Defense setting: To handle multiple target labels, BSD incurs additional computational costs by
898   iterating through all classes as pseudo-targets during OSS. Clean indices from each pseudo-target are
899   intersected to form the final OSS result. Additionally, we early stop at Stage 2 to avoid meaningless
900   cost in Stage 3.
901
      Since all-to-all attacks do not fundamentally change the nature of poison-label attacks, OSS remains
902
      effective for each individual classes. We visualized OSS spliting results in Figure 5, which reveals
903
      effective separation of clean samples of OSS. The CA, ASR, and DER performance are presented in
904   Table 11, demonstrating a significant DER improvement compared to baseline methods. Notably,
905   while BSD’s ASR increases under all-to-all attacks, it effectively limits the attack success rate to the
906   level of random prediction (1/nc = 10%).
907
      In conclusion, our BSD method remains effective against all-to-all attacks. Furthermore, the OSS
908
      module can serve as a highly effective component for identifying clean samples in other backdoor
909
      defense methods.

1202   D.1    S UPPLEMENTARY OVERVIEW OF BACKDOOR ATTACK R ESEARCH
1203
1204   The common implementation of backdoor attacks is realized by injecting a few poisoned samples into
1205   the training dataset, i.e., data-poisoning-based backdoor attacks, inducing the model to build a link
1206   between the trigger (i.e., a visual particular pattern) and target class (Gu et al., 2017). Thus the model
1207   consistently outputs the target label once the trigger is attached to the inputs in the inference stage.
1208   Poison-label backdoor attacks are currently the most common attack paradigm, where the trigger
1209   pattern in the poisoned samples is directly connected to the target class by relabeling, inducing the
1210   model to treat the trigger as a decision-making feature of the target class. Recent research (Hu et al.,
1211   2022; Li et al., 2020; Qi et al., 2021) focuses on more invisible trigger designs through generative
1212   models and feature space optimizations, as well as exploring backdoor attacks in wider tasks like
1213   natural language processing.
1214
1215   D.2    I LLUSTRATION OF THE MODEL COLLAPSE
1216
       As presented in Figure 8, the splitting-based defenses (loss-guided ones specifically) encounter two
1217
       kinds of model collapse. In backdoor overfitting collapse, poison samples take effect and have low
1218
       loss values, which consistently corrupt the clean pool and lead to a backdoored model. Likewise,
1219   in class underfitting collapse, the rareness of certain classes will lead to higher loss values, making
1220   them less chosen to be clean samples, which forms a vicious cycle. Note that these two collapses
1221   are common in other categories of defenses as well, while it’s more explainable in splitting-based
1222   defense.

1401

1402
1403   Threat model for the attackers. Following existing work (Gao et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2017; Gu
       et al., 2017; Turner et al., 2018), we assume that the attackers can access the entire dataset and know

       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1404

1405   poisoned samples are injected into the training dataset.
1406
1407   Method for adaptive attack. Our method initializes the clean pool using bi-perspective splitting
1408
       through OSS and ALS, which separate poisoned samples based on semantic and loss statistics,
       respectively. In general, there is a contradiction between increasing the loss values of poisoned
1409
       samples (to bypass ALS) and achieving backdoor objectives. Furthermore, maintaining high semantic
1410
       similarity to the target class (to bypass OSS) adds to the complexity. To craft such a trigger pattern
1411   that satisfies the above objectives, we use a PGD optimization to search for an average noise (among
1412   non-target classes) that is semantically close (judged by a proxy model) to the target class (to bypass
1413   OSS). Meanwhile, we control the ℓ∞ ball bound as 8/255 and the poisoning rate as 0.01 to prevent it
1414   from being an obvious trigger that will be easily fitted (to bypass ALS).
1415

1416
       15; step size, 1.5/255; perturbation magnitude, 8/255; trigger size, 32×32; and poisoning rate, 0.01.
1417   Although the attacker is assumed to have no knowledge of the model structure, we adopt a more
1418   challenging setting where the adversary uses the same model structure as the proxy model.
