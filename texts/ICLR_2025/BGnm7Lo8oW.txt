009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Prompting a Large Language Model (LLM) to output Chain-of-Thought (CoT)
012            reasoning improves performance on complex problem-solving tasks. Moreover,
013            several popular approaches exist to “self-improve” the CoT reasoning abilities of
014            LLMs on tasks where supervised (question, answer) datasets are already avail-
015            able. An emerging line of work explores whether self-improvement is possible
016            without these supervised datasets, instead utilizing the same large, unstructured
017            text corpora as used during pre-training. This would overcome the data availabil-
018
               ity bottleneck present in current self-improvement methods, and open the door to-
               wards compute-only scaling of language model reasoning ability. We investigate
019
               a fundamental question in this line of work: What constitutes a suitable reward
020
               function for learning to reason during general language model pretraining? We
021            outline the desirable qualities of such a reward function and empirically demon-
022            strate how different functions affect what reasoning is learnt and where reasoning
023            is rewarded. Using these insights, we introduce a novel reward function called
024            Reasoning Advantage (RA) that facilitates self-improving CoT reasoning on free-
025            form question-answering (QA) data, where answers are unstructured and difficult
026            to verify. We also perform an exploratory experiment optimizing RA on general
027            unstructured text using offline RL, and our analysis indicates that future work
028            should investigate methods for generating a more diverse set of CoTs.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Large Language Models (LLMs) have become increasingly effective at solving complex reasoning
      tasks (Huang & Chang, 2022; Kojima et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2023; Havrilla et al., 2024b). A
034
      key driver of this success has been the discovery of Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning (Wei et al.,
035
      2023), whereby a model outputs a step-by-step “thought process” before arriving at a final answer.
036
037   While some CoT reasoning ability emerges naturally from pretraining on unstructured web-text data
038   (Fu et al., 2023), it is through further supervised finetuning (SFT) on curated question-answering
039
      (QA) datasets (Saparov & He, 2023), as well as Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback
      (RLHF) (Ouyang et al., 2022), that CoT becomes such a powerful tool. Considerable effort is being
040
      placed in curating large-scale (question, CoT, answer) datasets (Cobbe et al., 2021; Saparov & He,
041
      2023; Liu et al., 2023), with models increasingly being used “in the loop” to help generate initial
042   reasoning traces or refine existing ones (Zelikman et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2024). In certain do-
043   mains like mathematics, it is also possible to further automate dataset generation by sampling many
044   CoTs and selecting those which lead to ground-truth answers (Zelikman et al., 2022). However,
045   despite these recent advancements, there are significant limitations to relying on curated datasets for
046   improving CoT abilities. It is becoming increasingly difficult and prohibitively expensive to curate
047   sufficiently challenging, large-scale (question, CoT, answer) datasets across the diverse set domains
048   that today’s general models can tackle. For instance, a popular benchmark of just 500 graduate-level
049   biology, physics, and chemistry questions with CoT reasoning and answers cost over $120,000 to
050
      produce and required thousands of human expert hours (Rein et al., 2023).
051   To address these limitations, an emerging line of work explores self-improving CoT reasoning ability
052   in a self-supervised setting—leveraging the large, unstructured datasets used for pretraining (Zelik-
053   man et al., 2024) instead of relying on curated QA or RLHF datasets. In this new setting, the LLM
      learns to produce CoT reasoning for the task of next-token prediction: given n tokens from the pre-

060

067   To our knowledge, our work is the first to provide this type of analysis on reward functions towards
068   self-improving CoT reasoning on unstructured text. Our investigations reveal critical shortcomings
069   in commonly used reward functions, including an inability to differentiate between meaningful CoT
070   reasoning and random word sequences (poor what: failing to reward effective reasoning), as well
071   as a tendency to incentivize reasoning at locations where predicting following tokens is trivial (poor
072   where: inability to pick out useful locations for reasoning). Drawing on these insights, we introduce
073   a novel reward function called Reasoning Advantage (RA), an augmentation of standard language
074   modeling loss, and show that it addresses many of these limitations.
075   To facilitate more efficient study of self-improving CoT reasoning, we also introduce an open-

077   (Hendrycks et al., 2020) to be closer to the unstructured text setting. Specifically, by removing its

082
      reasoning on unstructured text.

084   MMLU-F REE -F ORM does not allow for using exact-match accuracy as a reward metric (similar
085   to unstructured pretraining text), and yet offers a higher density of clear opportunities for CoT rea-
086   soning compared to typical pre-training corpora. This makes it an ideal stepping-stone towards the
087
      ultimate goal of self-improving CoT reasoning on unstructured text. In Section 5.2, we demonstrate

088
      F REE -F ORM, improving zero-shot transfer accuracy on GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021) by nearly 7%,
089

      2   BACKGROUND
125   CoT Reasoning Given n prefix tokens p, performing CoT reasoning refers to an LLM M gener-
126   ating a sequence of reasoning tokens r before the m answer suffix tokens s. The goal of generating
127   CoT reasoning tokens before the final answer is to maximize PM (s|p, r), the probability of the
128   answer suffix tokens s conditioned on both the prefix p and the CoT reasoning tokens r. The prefix-
129   suffix pair can be any token sequence, ranging from question-answer pairs in mathematical datasets
130   to arbitrarily split sentences from an unstructured text corpus.
131   Traditionally, CoT reasoning has been elicited by pretending few-shot examples of (question, CoT,
132   answer) to the prefix. This approach relies the pattern-completion tendencies of LLMs to continue
133   this structure for subsequent outputs. Alternatively, it has also become popular to elicit CoT rea-
134   soning by appending prompts like “Let’s think step by step.” to the prefix (e.g., to the end of input
135   questions), especially for instruction-tuned models.
136
137   Self-Improving CoT Reasoning as Reinforcement Learning Self-improvement refers to any
138   process where an LLM is finetuned on self-generated data, leading to performance gains without
139   human intervention or assistance from larger models. This process can be framed as a Reinforcement
140   Learning (RL) problem. In RL, an agent interacts with an environment by taking actions a ∈ A in
141   states s ∈ S to maximize cumulative rewards. The agent receives a reward Rt = R(st , at ) after
142
      each action atP
                    and aims to learn a policy π(a|s) that maximizes the expected cumulative discounted
                      ∞
      reward Gt = k=0 γ k Rt+k , where γ ∈ [0, 1] is the discount factor.
143
144   In the context of CoT generation, each token can be viewed as an action at , with the current string of
145   generated tokens representing the state st so far. We focus on a sparse reward setting where rewards
146   are 0 until CoT generation is complete, and with a discount factor γ = 1. The reward function maps
147   the prefix p, CoT reasoning tokens r, and answer suffix s to a real number R(p, r, s) ∈ R, with
148
      higher rewards for CoTs that better predict the suffix. As long as this reward function doesn’t require
      external intervention from humans or more powerful models, optimizing it through RL methods
149
      constitutes self-improving CoT reasoning.

      Self-Improving CoT Reasoning on General-Purpose, Unstructured Data This setting explores
163   the possibility of self-improving CoT reasoning given only an unstructured corpus of text, without
164   access to a curated dataset of (question, CoT, answer) or (question, answer) pairs. In this setting, the
165   model generates and inserts intermediate CoT reasoning at various points in a sequence of tokens
166   (for example, at various points in a web-document that shows how to apply the quadratic formula).
167
      A key challenge in this setting is evaluating the performance of CoT reasoning tokens inserted
168
      into general-purpose text. The accuracy-based reward Racc is ineffective here, as it would almost
169   always be 0, providing minimal learning signal. Instead, language modelling performance—the log-
170   likelihood of the suffix conditioned on the prefix and CoT—serves as a more natural starting point
171   for a reward function:
172
173                                       Rloss (p, r, s) = log PM (s|p, r)                                   (2)

175   We aim to help advance the field towards this setting, enabling self-improving CoT reasoning on un-
176
      structured text at the pretraining. In this paper, we specifically focus on identifying key shortcomings
      of commonly used reward functions and introducing a new function to address these limitations.

179
180   LLM Reasoning Various works have looked at improving the reasoning capabilities of LLMs.
181   Rajani et al. (2019) improve the commonsense reasoning ability of language models by training on
182
      human explanations for commonsense problems. Nye et al. (2021) generate tokens in a “scratchpad”
      for intermediate computations when solving multi-step reasoning problems. On difficult algorith-
183
      mic tasks, Pfau et al. (2024) show that LLMs can even be trained to leverage meaningless filler
184
      tokens under dense supervision, in place of legible CoTs. Further, theoretical analyses by Merrill &
185   Sabharwal (2023) and Feng et al. (2024) show that CoT improves the expressivity of Transformers
186   (Vaswani et al., 2017).
187
188   LLM Self-Improvement Using Supervised Datasets Iterated learning approaches involve LLMs
189   generating new outputs and using “successful” ones to improve generation quality (Anthony et al.,
190   2017; Vani et al., 2021; Polu et al., 2022). Such methods have been applied to LLMs (Zelikman
191   et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2024). However, much of the research on LLM
192
      self-improvement has been limited to question-answer domains where accuracy is an appropri-
      ate success measure, such as multiple-choice questions or simple numeric answers. This limi-
193
      tation is evident inPthe policy gradient objective approximated by STaR (Zelikman et al., 2022):

195   dicator function with respect to ground truth labels. Clearly, this breaks down in settings where
196   ground truth labels are not available, such as open-ended or “free-form” QA setting as well as

225   4   R EWARD F UNCTIONS FOR S ELF - IMPROVING C OT R EASONING
226   In Section 2, we framed self-improving Chain-of-Thought (CoT) reasoning as a Reinforcement
227   Learning (RL) problem. Given n tokens from a pre-training corpus (the prefix p), the model gener-
228   ates a CoT r and receives a reward based on how well the CoT helps predict the following m tokens
229   (the suffix s). Previous works have primarily explored two reward functions for self-improving CoT
230   reasoning: loss and accuracy. Here, we explore other potential reward functions and their character-
231   istics from the perspective of facilitating self-improving CoT reasoning on unstructured web-text at
232
      pretraining scale.
233   There are several key criteria to consider when designing such a reward function. Primarily, it should
234   reward high-quality reasoning over CoTs containing logical errors or simply random characters. As
235   shown in Section 5.1, this is not always the case. Moreover, for the purposes of self-improving CoT
236   reasoning, the reward function must not depend on an stronger source of intelligence (i.e., using a
237   more powerful LLM to verify the correctness of its CoT). Further, for reasonable use on pretraining
238
      scale datasets, evaluating the function should be fast and ideally parallelizable—requiring a minimal
      number of model forward passes.
239
240   In this work, we do not consider using an LLM-as-judge to evaluate or verify CoTs since: (1) it
241   may rely on a stronger model, which is not self-improvement, and (2) while one could use the same
242   model for both generation and verification, this approach incurs too much computational overhead
243   to apply to pretraining scale data as it requires the decoding of an answer to be verified against the
244
      ground truth, and the verifier itself needs to generate CoT tokens. We also do not consider accuracy-
      based metrics, since free-form answers are often impossible to verify using exact-match, and using
245
      an LLM-as-judge to compute accuracy faces the issues mentioned above. Thus, we choose to focus
246
      on the family of “loss-based” reward functions. These functions compute the token-by-token log-
247   likelihood of the suffix tokens s0,...,m−1 , given the CoT r and prefix p:
248
249
                                      log P (s|p, r) = log P (s0 |p, r)
250                                                  + log P (s1 |p, r, s0 )
                                                                                                         (3)
251                                                  + log P (s2 |p, r, s0,1 )
252                                                  + ...
253
      The most basic reward function in this family is R(p, r, s) = log P (s|p, r). This family of reward
254   functions offers several key advantages. They are computationally efficient, since they can be eval-
255   uated by an autoregressive model in a single forward pass and can be parallelized across a batch
256   of CoTs. Also, they do not require access to any external form of intelligence, a requirement for
257   self-improvement. Most importantly, this family of functions does not rely on an using exact-match
258   accuracy to compare with the answer suffix, enabling multiple valid answers and accommodating
259   ambiguity in formatting (a key property of unstructured text).

261   log P (s|p, r), we focus our analysis on two key modifications: clipping the log probabilities and

343   which suggests that the main advantage of clipping lies in distinguishing truly random reasoning.
344
345   Where reasoning is rewarded Next, we investigate how different function reward reasoning at
346   different locations in a document. Using 1,000 problems each from GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021),
347   CSQA (Talmor et al., 2018), and MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2020), we first format each problem’s
348   question, multiple choice options, and answer as a single text string. We then create four (prefix,
349   suffix) pairs per problem by splitting at different points: 1) mid-question, 2) after the question but
350   before the multiple choice options, 3) after the multiple choice options (the ideal location for CoT
351
      reasoning), and 4) mid-answer. This setup aims to mimic a key fact about unstructured pretrain-
      ing text: not all locations are suitable for CoT reasoning. That is, reasoning may be unhelpful if
352
      produced too early (insufficient context) or too late in a document.
353
354   To evaluate each reward function, we frame this as a binary classification task: identifying the ideal
355   location (after the multiple choice answers but before the solution) versus the three suboptimal lo-
356   cations. Using reward as a classifier and computing the AUC for this classification task. We find
357
      that RA performs best, followed by delta loss and standard loss (see Table 2). Notice that functions
      which use a baseline consistently outperform those without, with clipping providing additional im-
358
      provement. Particularly, subtracting the “Empty CoT” baseline helps distinguish between locations
359
      that have low loss due to effective CoT reasoning versus locations that have low loss because the
360   suffix is trivial to predict without any reasoning (i.e., with an empty CoT). This partially explains
361   why standard loss performs so poorly: it favors locations halfway through the answer where suffix
362   prediction becomes trivial. Table 4 shows results for the complete list of evaluated reward functions.
363
364   Summary Across both experiments, the Reasoning Advantage (RA) reward function outper-
365   formed standard loss and delta loss. As for the two main augmentations, clipping and baseline, we
366   can summarize their effects. Clipping is often beneficial, almost never harmful, and requires min-
367   imal extra computation. We explore the impact of different clipping values in Appendix B.1 (Fig-
368   ure 5). And incorporating a baseline value provides a substantial boost in performance—especially
369
      the “Empty CoT” baseline. Moreover, a key advantage of the “Empty CoT” baseline is that it doesn’t
      require generating any additional CoTs per (prefix, suffix) pair. In contrast, the “Average CoT” base-
370
      line requires taking the average loss over multiple CoTs for a single location. Appendix B.1 contains
371
      tables which show results for all combinations of augmentations. Notice that two combinations and
372   the non-normalized version of RA performed slightly better on the what experiments, but they per-
373   formed much worse on the where experiments. RA is the only function with strong performance on
374   both tasks.

461   6     C HALLENGES AND F UTURE D IRECTIONS
462
      As it becomes increasingly challenging and expensive to curate large-scale (question, CoT, answer)
463   datasets (Rein et al., 2023), the reasoning community has begun focusing on the challenging task of
464   self-improving CoT reasoning on unstructured, pretraining-scale text. Our work frames this chal-
465   lenging task as an RL problem and demonstrates the effectiveness of RA at identifying useful reason-
466   ing, determining useful locations for reasoning, and facilitating self-improvement in the simplified
467   MMLU-F REE -F ORM setting. There is still more work to be done in order to solve the full, unstruc-
468   tured pretraining setting. In this section, we present an exploratory experiment that provides key
469   insights into the barriers that must be overcome to achieve self-improvement at pretraining scale.
470   Specifically, we attempt to use our novel Reasoning Advantage (RA) reward function with the of-
471   fline RL procedure from Section 5.2 to self-improve CoT reasoning on OpenWebMath (Paster et al.,
472   2023), a pretraining corpus of unstructured web-text data. The two main steps of this procedure
473   are: (1) generate a large batch of CoTs and self-insert them into OpenWebMath, and (2) finetune on
474   the CoTs with the highest reward scores. Our analysis indicates that this method is not sufficiently
475   powerful to escape the local optimum of extremely conservative CoT reasoning that just summarizes
476   previous information instead of attempting to actually solve problems (see Appendix C for exam-
477
      ples). In Section 6.1, we provide key insights into why this method is not sufficient. In Section 6.2,
      we provide a more detailed experimental setup and additional results.
478
479   6.1   K EY I NSIGHTS (NEW SUBSECTION)
480   To understand why this procedure fails on OpenWebMath, we isolate the problem into two key
481   components: generating diverse CoTs and identifying useful CoTs. In our offline RL approach, the
482   role of the reward function is purely to identify useful CoTs to use for training, and Section 5.1
483   demonstrates that RA excels at this task. This suggests that the remaining challenge lies in genera-
484   tion. Indeed, our analysis shows that only 0.01% of the generated CoTs achieve a reward above 0.2,
485   which is our filtering threshold for RA (a decent threshold for “good reasoning” in our experience).
      Moreover, many of the CoTs that passed the filtering threshold exhibited the conservative strategy


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      described previously: they simply summarize past information from the context. This explains why
487   the model learned to be overly conservative. However, these overly conservative CoTs which made
488   it past the RA threshold were still superior to those that did not pass the threshold (those ones mainly
489   contained incorrect reasoning that predicted the subsequent tokens incorrectly). This indicates that
490   RA actually succeeded at its job of identifying the best reasoning from the generated batch of CoTs,
491   and that the main issue indeed lies with the lack of diversity in the generated CoTs.
492
      Thus, to facilitate self-improvement using RA, we must crucially generate a diverse-enough set of
493   CoTs so that there are enough useful samples for RA to identify. This remains a critical barrier
494   for future work to investigate. To increase the diversity of explored CoTs, future work might use
495   Quality-Diversity (Mouret & Clune, 2015) or other evolutionary techniques (Fernando et al., 2023;
496   Samvelyan et al., 2024), which could generate more diverse CoTs. It would also be worth exploring
497   different prompting strategies (we used a single system prompt to generate these CoTs, and did not
498   spend much time prompt engineering). Better exploration may also be facilitated by using online
499   RL, but the only existing method in this direction generates a CoT at every token in a document (Ze-
500   likman et al., 2024), which is highly inefficient. Thus, we believe that our computationally feasible
501
      offline RL approach of generating CoTs in large, offline batches and performing supervised fine-
      tuning is key to enabling the self-improvement of CoT reasoning at the pretraining scale. However,
502
      future work should investigate ways of generating a more diverse batch of CoTs in order for this
503
      method to work. One possible idea is to use a combination of RHO-1 (Lin et al., 2024) and RA.
504
505   To encourage future research in this direction, we will open-source our offline RL code, which runs
506   on an academic compute budget. We will also open-source MMLU-F REE -F ORM, which we believe
507
      acts as a useful intermediate benchmark between curated QA data and general language pretraining.
508   6.2   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP AND A DDITIONAL R ESULTS (NEW SUB SECTION)
509
      We first finetune Mistral-7B-Instruct (Jiang et al., 2023) on a small set of CoTs to learn the
510   “[THOUGHT]...[/THOUGHT]” syntax. Then, we randomly sample 50,000 (prefix, suffix) pairs
511   from OpenWebMath and generate CoTs for each location using Mistral-7B-Instruct with a temper-
512   ature value of 0.5. From this pool of generated CoTs, we create three variants of an augmented
513   OpenWebMath dataset by selecting 3,200 CoTs using different filtering methods: (1) random selec-
514   tion, (2) best loss scores, and (3) best RA scores.
515
      Throughout training, we evaluate each model’s CoT reasoning ability on a holdout set of OpenWeb-
516   Math documents. At each checkpoint, we identify locations where “[THOUGHT]” is the predicted
517   next token, generate CoTs at these points, and measure three metrics on the holdout documents (ex-
518   cluding CoT tokens but using them as context): standard loss, delta loss, and RA. Figure 8 show
519   three plots—each measuring one of these metrics at various checkpoints throughout training. Notice
520   that each line represents an entirely different model trained on differently filtered CoTs.

      7 C ONCLUSION (UPDATED, BETTER CLARITY OF CONTRIBUTIONS )
522
      As it becomes increasingly challenging and prohibitively expensive to curate large-scale (question,
523
      CoT, answer) datasets, the LLM reasoning community has began to focus on the challenging task
524
      of self-improving CoT reasoning on unstructured text at the pretraining scale. We frame this as a
525   reinforcement learning problem and investigate a fundamental question: What constitutes a suitable
526   reward function for learning to reason during general language model pretraining? We outline the
527   desirable qualities of such a reward, point out critical shortcomings in many commonly used reward
528   functions, and introduce Reasoning Advantage (RA), a novel reward function which addresses these
529   limitations. Further, we provide a comprehensive analysis on how different functions affect: (1) the
530   ability to identify effective CoT reasoning (what reasoning is rewarded), and (2) the ability to pick
531   out useful locations to produce CoT reasoning (where reasoning is rewarded). To our knowledge,
532   our work is the first to provide this type of analysis on reward functions for self-improving CoT
533
      reasoning on unstructured text.
534   We introduce MMLU-F REE -F ORM, a small step towards the full unstructured pretraining setting,
535   and demonstrate that only RA is able to facilitate generalization when self-improving CoT reasoning
536   on MMLU-F REE -F ORM. There is still more work to be done in order to solve the full, unstructured
537   pretraining setting, and we present an exploratory experiment that provides key insights into the
538   barriers that must be overcome to achieve self-improvement at pretraining scale. Most importantly,
539
      future work should investigate methods for generating a more diverse set of CoTs. We will open
      source all of our code, which runs on academic compute, to facilitate future work in this direction.
