011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            We propose a novel framework called FDA for generating Fair synthetic data
014            through Data Augmentation, offering the first method with provable trade-off
015
               guarantee between fairness and faithfulness. Unlike other existing methods, our
               approach utilizes a novel joint model that consists of two sub-models: one focused
016
               on enforcing strict fairness constraints while the other dedicated to preserving fi-
017
               delity to the original data, coupled with a tuning mechanism that provides explicit
018            control over the trade-off between fairness and faithfulness. Specifically, our FDA
019            framework enables explicit quantification of the extent to which the generated fair
020            synthetic data preserve faithfulness to the original data, while achieving an inter-
021            mediate level of fairness determined by a user specified parameter α ∈ [0, 1]. The-
022            oretically, we show that the resulting fair synthetic data converge to the original
023            data in probability when α tends to 1, thereby implying convergence in distribu-
024            tion. Our framework can be also combined with some GAN-based fair models,
025            such as DECAF, to further improve the utility of the resulting synthetic data in
026
               downstream analysis, while carefully balancing fairness. Furthermore, we obtain
               an upper bound of the unfairness measurement for downstream models trained
027
               on the generated fair synthetic data, which can help users to choose appropriate
028
               α. Finally, we perform numerical experiments on benchmark data to validate our
029            theoretical contributions and to compare our FDA with other methods.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Artificial intelligence (AI) and machine learning (ML) algorithms have increasingly been used to
035   improve decision-making in almost all aspects of our lives (Zhao et al., 2018; Bogen & Rieke,
036   2018; Cohen et al., 2020; Mukerjee et al., 2002; Angwin et al., 2016; Berk et al., 2021). However,
037   there is mounting evidence showing that the developed algorithms may inherit biases and injustices
038   from historical data, leading to unfair decisions that discriminate against certain populations (Dastin,
039
      2018; Datta et al., 2018; Lu et al., 2020; de Vassimon Manela et al., 2021).If not properly addressed,
      biased or unfair decision-making may lead to violations of equality and anti-discrimination laws
040
      (Krishnamurthy, 2021; Wachter et al., 2021). The emerging field of algorithmic fairness seeks to
041
      address this urgent issue by mitigating the bias and discrimination in the AL and ML systems.
042
043   Broadly speaking, the bias mitigation methods can be categorized into three types: pre-processing,
044   in-processing, and post-processing. For a comprehensive overview of these methods, we encour-
045
      age readers to refer to recent review papers, such as (Pessach & Shmueli, 2022; Hort et al., 2022;
      Mehrabi et al., 2021; Caton & Haas, 2024), and the extensive references cited within these works.
046
      Pre-processing methods modify the biased training data, with the goal that any downstream model
047
      trained on debiased data would achieve desired fairness requirements. In-processing methods mod-
048   ify the algorithms by enforcing fairness constraints during training, with the goal that the trained
049   algorithms achieve the desired fairness requirements on all real-life data. Post-processing methods
050   modify the predictions based on a trained unfair model, with the goal that the final predictions sat-
051   isfy certain fairness requirements. In recent years, as a pre-processing method, fair synthetic data
052   generation has gained significant momentum (Feldman et al., 2015a; Zhang et al., 2017b; Calmon
053   et al., 2017; Xu et al., 2018; Zemel et al., 2013; Xu et al., 2019; van Breugel et al., 2021; Rajabi &
      Garibay, 2022). For example, Xu et al. (2019) proposed FairGAN, a GAN-based method to create

073
074   Generally, the task of generating a fair synthetic data that preserves the properties of the original data
075   creates a tension. On one hand, achieving fairness requires modification of the unfair data, which
076   may inadvertently impact the faithfulness of the synthetic data to the original data. On the other
077
      hand, the synthetic data should faithfully represent the statistical properties of the original data, in
      order to preserve its utility for downstream analysis and modeling. Therefore, achieving fairness
078
      requires sacrificing some level of faithfulness, and vice versa. Due to the inherent competing nature
079
      between these two goals, a trade-off between fairness and faithfulness is necessary characterized
080   when generating fair synthetic data. In practice, striking the right balance involves careful consider-
081   ation of the goals, stakeholders’ priorities, and ethical implications in a given application.
082
083
      Contribution. In this paper, we propose FDA, a Fair synthetic data generation framework through
      Data Augmentation. FDA is built upon a joint modeling framework consisting of a fair model Mfair
084
      and a faithful model Mfaithful , coupled with a tuning mechanism to achieve a provable trade-off be-
085
      tween fairness and faithfulness in the generated synthetic data. This allows an explicit quantification
086   of the extent to which the generated fair synthetic data preserve faithfulness to the original data,
087   while meeting specific fairness requirement controlled by α ∈ [0, 1], a user specified bias reduction
088   parameter that quantifies the amount of biases removed from the original unfair data. Theoretically,
089   setting α = 0, the resulting synthetic data satisfy absolute fairness, with maximum reduction of the
090   faithfulness to the original data. Conversely, setting α = 1, the resulting synthetic data achieves per-
091   fect similarity to the original data and is proved to converge to the original data in probability, further
092   implying convergence in distribution. When setting α ∈ (0, 1), users can achieve an intermediate
093   level of fairness while maintaining a certain level of faithfulness to the original data, both quantified
094
      by α. Our framework can be combined with GAN-based fair models such as DECAF, to further
      improve the data utility of the resulting fair synthetic data in downstream analysis, while achiev-
095
      ing an intermediate level of fairness. In contrast to black-box methods that require time-consuming
096
      training, our FDA framework generates synthetic data directly from the predictive distributions de-
097   fined by our chosen joint model, which follows simple Gaussian distributions. Furthermore, to guide
098   users to choose appropriate unfairness reduction parameter α, we provide theoretical analysis on the
099   fairness of downstream models trained on the generated fair synthetic data. As far as we know, our
100   FDA is the first method to provide all these desired features, with a comparison of our FDA with
101   other methods summarized in Table 1.

178   3    T HE PROPOSED FDA METHOD
179
180   Overview. The main idea is to simulate synthetic data from the predictive distribution defined under
181   a joint model, denoted as MFDA , using both observed D and additionally augmented data. A similar
182   approach was previously considered in Jiang et al. (2022) to generate privacy preserving synthetic
183   data. In this work, we build upon their method to address the challenge of generating fair synthetic
184   data, providing provable theoretical guarantees on the trade-off between fairness and utility, as well
185   as the convergence of the synthetic data to the original data in both probability and distribution.
186   Specifically, MFDA consists of two sub-models: (i) a fair model (see equation 2), denoted as Mfair ,
187   which specifies certain relationship between Yi and the feature vector Xi and the sensitive attribute
188   Si , such that it imposes exact fairness constraint; and (ii) a faithful model, Mfaithful , which generates
189   Zi (see equation 3 for details) given Yi , for each i ∈ [n], such that Zi are noisy copies of Yi with
190   accuracy level controlled by tuning parameters. The fair synthetic data are then generated as samples
191   from the corresponding predictive distributions that are defined by the model MFDA .
192   By design, these Zi contain information about Yi so that Mfaithful plays the role of enforcing the
193   resulting synthetic data to be close (and thus faithful) to the original data. In contrast, Mfair plays
194   the role of imposing the desired fairness requirement, e.g., the DP constraint. Under such a frame-
195   work, both models Mfaithful and Mfair influence the resulting synthetic data, with their respective
196   contributions determined by the values of the tuning parameters introduced in Mfaithful (can be seen
197   in equation 3 and discussed thereafter). As a result, the tuning parameters control the relative in-
198
      fluence from Mfaithful and Mfair , and thus balance between the two competing goals, fairness and
      faithfulness to the original data. Given the proposed framework is general, the data considered can
199
      be any type and need not to be limited to be binary or discrete as considered by many others. Next,
200
      we present details of Mfaithful and Mfair when Yi ’s are continuous; the discussion on how to use the
201   proposed framework when Yi ’s are categorical is given in Remark 3.4.
202
203   Fair model. The fair model Mfair can be written in the following general form:
204                                                                                      iid

207   model parameter. Notably, the simplest choice of Mfair is a constant mean model (CMM) with
208   f = β0 being a constant function. Alternatively, one could also choose f to be a GAN-based fair
209   predictor, e.g., DECAF (discussed in Remark 3.3).

211   omitted in Mfair to fulfill the fairness requirement, loss of information and utility is inevitable.
212   In other words, generating synthetic data solely from Mfair could result in considerable loss of
213   faithfulness to the original data. As pointed out by many others (Feldman et al., 2015a; Xu et al.,
214   2018; Chzhen & Schreuder, 2022; Zhao & Gordon, 2022; Tran et al., 2022), a sustainable solution
215   would be to accept some degree of compromise on fairness in order to preserve the utility of the
      original data in the downstream analysis. We achieve this goal by introducing a faithful model as a

302   Quantifying the fairness and faithfulness of synthetic data relative to the original data has been
303   a longstanding challenge in previous fairness studies. While some prior works provide analytic
304   bounds to characterize these properties, our work is the first to offer a closed-form solution, enabling
305   an exact quantification of the trade-off between fairness and faithfulness. In this section, we proide
306   a detailed discussion on how the synthetic data D̂ generated by our FDA framework achieves an
307   α-reduction in unfairness, where α is the bias reduction factor introduced in Remark 3.1, and how
308   its faithfulness to the original data can be explicitly expressed as a function of α.

449
450   In this section, we conduct experiments on the Adult dataset to demonstrate how our FDA framework
451   when combined with DECAF, can enhance the faithfulness of synthetic data to the original data

453   achieving an intermediate level of fairness.
454   Specifically, in our FDA framework, we let Mfair be the GAN-based model DECAF (van Breugel
455   et al., 2021) (we term the corresponding model FDA-DECAF). To emphasize that, in our origi-
456   nal FDA framework, Mfair is a constant mean model, namely CMM, we term the corresponding
457   model FDA-CMM. We generate debiased synthetic data using DECAF, FDA-DECAF and FDA-

459   model trained on the resulting debiased synthetic datasets using these methods. Again, to ensure

462
      of the model using accuracy, precision, recall, and AUROC; (2) fairness: we assess the fairness of
463   the downstream model trained on the synthetic data using the total variation distance based measure
464   |P(Ŷ |S = 1) − P(Ŷ |S = 0)|.
465
466   Figure 3 shows the results6 of our experiments, repeated 10 times. As expected, FDA-DECAF leads
467
      to consistently better utility in the downstream prediction for any α ∈ (0, 1) when compared with
      DECAF. This finding is supported by our theoretical result given in Theorem 3.7. By introducing
468
      a joint modeling approach (including Mfair and Mfaithful ), coupled with the tuning mechanism to
469
      allow for an intermediate level of fairness, our FDA framework enables the resulting synthetic data
470   to maintain a certain level of faithfulness to the original data, thereby enhancing the downstream
471   prediction performance. When α → 1, our FDA framework allows the utility of the downstream
472   model trained on the resulting synthetic data to fully recover the utility using the original data. In
473   contrast, DECAF enforces exact fairness in the synthetic data, which can lead to significant loss of
474   utility in the downstream model as shown in Figure 3.

478   When achieving similar utility-fairness trade-off, our FDA-CMM method offers significant advan-
479   tages in terms of computational efficiency, ease of implementation, stability and interpretability.

739   when P(S = smajority ) ≫ P(S = s) for all s ∈ [K] \ {smajority }.
740   In general, for any s ∈ [K], the larger the ωs , the closer the µY |X is to the optimal ν (the minimizer
741   in equation 1). This leads to some natural choices of (ω1 , · · · , ωK ), including ωs = P(S = s)
742   and ωs ∝ 1/P(S = s). When using the former, the optimal ν will be closer to the conditional
743   distribution of Y for the majority group; when using the latter, the optimal ν will be closer to the
744   conditional distribution of Y for the minority group. Alternatively, one could use equal weights by
                    1
745   letting ωs = K   , when all groups are similar in size.
