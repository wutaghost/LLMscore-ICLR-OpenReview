009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Recent advances in the fields of natural language processing and computer vi-
012            sion have shown great potential in understanding the underlying dynamics of the
013            world from large-scale internet data. However, translating this knowledge into
014            robotic systems remains an open challenge, given the scarcity of human-robot in-
015            teractions and the lack of large-scale datasets of real-world robotic data. Previous
016            robot learning approaches such as behavior cloning and reinforcement learning
017            have shown great capabilities in learning robotic skills from human demonstra-
018            tions or from scratch in specific environments. However, these approaches of-
019            ten require task-specific demonstrations or designing complex simulation envi-
020
               ronments, which limits the development of generalizable and robust policies for
               new settings. Aiming to address these limitations, we propose an agent-based
021
               framework for grounding robot policies to the current context, considering the
022
               constraints of a current robot and its environment using visuomotor-grounded
023            language guidance. The proposed framework is composed of a set of conver-
024            sational agents designed for specific roles—namely, high-level advisor, visual
025            grounding, monitoring, and robotic agents. Given a base policy, the agents col-
026            lectively generate guidance at run time to shift the action distribution of the base
027            policy towards more desirable future states. We demonstrate that our approach
028            can effectively guide manipulation policies to achieve significantly higher suc-
029            cess rates both in simulation and in real-world experiments without the need
030            for additional human demonstrations or extensive exploration. Project videos at
031
               https://sites.google.com/view/motorcortex/home.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   In recent years, the advent of foundation models, such as large-scale pre-trained language models
037   (LLMs) and visual language models (VLMs), has shown great capabilities in understanding context,
038   scenes, and the underlying dynamics of the world. Furthermore, emergent capabilities such as in-
039   context learning have shown great potential in the transfer of knowledge between domains, e.g.,
040   via few-shot demonstrations or zero-shot inference. However, the application of these models to
041   robotics is still limited, given the intrinsic complexity and scarcity of human-robot interactions and
042   the lack of large-scale datasets of human-annotated data or demonstrations.
043   Most approaches that target using LLMs and VLMs in robotics often fall in one of two directions.
044   Fine-tuning the models (Brohan et al., 2023; Ahn et al., 2022), which have shown great real-world
045   capabilities, at the expense of large platform-dependent datasets. Even though several efforts try to
046   overcome the data availability problem (Collaboration et al., 2023), the scarcity of robots and the
047   broad range of robotics skills, make this process extremely taxing. A second line of work relies on
048   using code as an interface between the language models and the robotic systems (Liang et al., 2023;
049   Vemprala et al., 2023), which although leverages the skilled coding capabilities of this type of model,
050
      is highly dependent on handcrafted functions to interface the platform actions and perception.
051   Unsupervised approaches in robotics have shown great progress in exploring large state spaces and
052   learning common sets of behaviors in complex environments. However, most of these approaches
053   aim to learn the underlying dynamics of the environments from scratch, taking a large amount of
      iterations to learn seemingly basic skills.

068   In this context, we propose a cognitive approach motivated by how humans learn to improve per-
069   formance. Exploration, abstraction, reasoning, and self-improvement are key components of human
070   learning (Francis et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2023). In this paper, we aim to achieve self improvement
071   by generating visuomotor guidances. We design an agent-based framework where a team of con-
072   versational agents with specific roles works together to refine the robot’s base policy via grounded
073   visuomotor guidance as shown in Figure 1. For instance, as illustrated in Figure 2, upon a request
074   from the advisor agent, the grounding agent can look for objects by not only detection and tracking
075
      but also higher level reasoning to broaden the search, e.g., search inside a cupboard to find a mug;
      the robotic agent can reason about its own capabilities and constraints to provide feedback on the
076
      guidance generated by the advisor agent. Motivated by recurrent models, the progress of task per-
077
      formance is updated in a hidden state and passed down during the iterative guidance generation. The
078   guidance is represented in the action distribution such that it can be easily combined with the base
079   policy.
080
081
      In our experiments on a set of benchmark tasks, we quantitatively show that our approach signifi-
      cantly improves the task success rate of two state-of-the-art robot policies. Our preliminary exper-
082
      iments on zero-shot learning show promising results where our approach enables the robot to learn
083
      to perform certain tasks from scratch without any demonstrations or other training material. We
084   qualitatively demonstrate our approach on a real robot learning to perform composite manipulation
085   tasks. Our contributions are as follows:
086
087
      • We propose g-MotorCortex, an agentic robot policy grounding framework that can self-
        improve by generating the action-scoring guidance functions to update the action distribution of a
088
        base policy in an online manner. Our experiments in both simulated and real-world robot settings
089
        show significant performance improvements on both adapting existing policies and learning new
090     skills from scratch.
091   • In order to enable robustness against cluttered and unseen environments, we propose a grounding
092     agent that performs multi-granular object search, which enables flexible visuomotor grounding.
093   • Our approach shows a promising potential for learning skills from scratch after deployment and
094     being generalizable across various base policy classes, tasks, and environments. To promote fur-
095     ther research in this direction, we open source the code, models, and system prompts.

099
      Vision-Language Models for Robot Learning: Several works explore the notion of leveraging
      pre-trained or fine-tuned Large Language Models (LLMs) and/or Vision-Language Models (VLMs)
100
      for high-level reasoning and planning in robotics tasks (Hu et al., 2023; Ahn et al., 2022; Liang
101
      et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2022; Singh et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023a; Ha et al., 2023; Ding et al.,
102   2023; Michał et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024; Mu et al., 2024)—typically decomposing
103   high-level task specification into a series of smaller steps or action primitives, using system prompts
104   or in-context examples to enable powerful chain-of-thought reasoning techniques. This strategy
105   of encouraging models to reason in a stepwise manner before outputting a final answer has led
106   to significant performance improvements across several tasks and benchmarks (Hu et al., 2023).
107   Despite these promising achievements, these approaches rely on handcrafted primitives (Ahn et al.,
      2022; Huang et al., 2022; Liang et al., 2023; Michał et al., 2024), struggle with low-level control, or


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      require large datasets for retraining. furthermore, various approaches that leverage VLMs for robot
109   learning suffer from a granularity problem when using off-the-shelf models in a single-step/zero-
110   shot manner (Huang et al., 2023b) or are unable to perform failure correction without costly human
111   intervention (Huang et al., 2022; Michał et al., 2024; Liang et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2023b).
112   In contrast, our framework bridges high-level reasoning with low-level control, by leveraging an
113   agentic framework for online modification of a base policy’s action distribution at test-time, without
114   requiring human feedback or datasets for fine-tuning. Moreover, we mitigate the granularity problem
115   by proposing a flexible and recurrent way of using VLMs to query open-vocabulary perception
116   models.
117   Agent-based VLM frameworks in Robotics: Rather than using single VLMs in an end-to-end
118   fashion, which might incur issues in generalization and robustness, various works have sought to
119   orchestrate multiple VLM-based agents to work together in an interconnection multi-agent frame-
120   work. Here, multiple agents can converse and collaborate to perform tasks, yielding improvements
121   for the overall framework in online adaptability, cross-task generalization, and self-supervision (Xu
122   et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024; Parakh et al., 2023). These agentic frameworks have provided possi-
123
      bilities for enabling the identification of issues in task execution, providing feedback about possible
      improvements. Challenges remain, however, in that this feedback is often not sufficiently grounded
124
      on the spatial, visual, and dynamical properties of embodied interaction to be useful for policy adap-
125
      tation; instead, the generated feedback is often too high-level or provides merely binary signals of
126   success or failure.
127
128   Self-guided Robot Failure Recovery: Guan et al. (2024) offer an analysis of frameworks for lever-
129
      aging VLMs as behavior critics. Some approaches have explored integrating such pre-trained mod-
      els to improve the performance of reinforcement learning (RL) algorithms. For instance, Ma et al.
130
      (2023) use LLMs in a zero-shot fashion to design and improve reward functions, however this ap-
131
      proach relies on human feedback to generate progressively human-aligned reward functions and
132   further requires simulated retraining via RL, with high sample-complexity. On the other hand, Ro-
133   camonde et al. (2023) avoid the need for explicit human feedback by directly using a VLM (CLIP)
134   to compute the rewards to measure the proximity of a state (image) to a goal (text description),
135   enabling gains in sample-efficiency for guiding a humanoid robot to perform various maneuvers in
136   the MuJoCo simulator. A limitation of this approach, however, lies in the difficulty of generating
137   rewards for long-horizon or multi-step tasks, which are characteristic of tasks involving complex
138   agent-object interactions. Liu et al. (2023b) present a framework for detecting and analyzing failed
139   executions automatically. However, their system focuses on explaining failure causes and proposing
140
      suggestions for remediation, as opposed to also performing policy correction. In this paper, we pro-
      pose a framework that directly adapts a base policy’s action distribution, during deployment, without
141
      requiring additional human feedback.

144   3    G ROUNDING ROBOT P OLICIES WITH G UIDANCE
145   3.1 P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
146   We consider a pre-trained stochastic policy π : O × S → A that maps observations ot and robotic
147   states st to action distributions aπ,t , at each time step t. Our objective is to generate a guidance distri-
148   bution gt that, when combined with this base policy, enhances overall performance during inference
149
      without requiring additional human demonstrations or extensive exploration procedures. Specifi-
      cally, we aim to develop a modified policy πguided : O × S → A that achieves better performance on
150
      tasks where the original policy π struggles. We define this new policy as follows:
151
152                           πguided (ag,t |ot , st ) = π(aπ,t |ot , st ) ∗ G(aπ,t |ot , s′t+1 ),             (1)
153   where G : A × O × S → [0, 1] is a guidance function that maps observation ot , action at , possible
154   future state s′t+1 into a guidance score gt . The ‘∗’ operator here denotes the operation of combining
155   both distributions conceptually, which we explore in detail in Section 3.4. For the scope of this
156   project, we assume that a dynamics model D : S × A → S is available, which can forecast possible
157   future states of the robot s′t+1 = D(st , aπ,t ) given the current state st and action aπ,t .
158   Focusing on leveraging the world knowledge of Vision Language Models, while avoiding adding
159   latency to the action loop, we choose to express these guidance functions as Python code. By
160   integrating these code snippets into action loop of the base policies, we eliminate the need of time-
161   consuming queries to large reasoning models. Samples of the format and content of the guidance
      functions generated by the framework are presented in the Appendix A.2.

      3.2 A M ULTI - AGENT G UIDANCE FRAMEWORK FOR S ELF I MPROVEMENT
181
182   In order to generate the guidance function G, we leverage a group of conversational agents empow-
183   ered with visual grounding capabilities and tool usage. Illustrated in Figure 2, the framework is
184   composed of four main agents: an Advisor Agent, the Grounding Agent, the Monitor Agent, and
185   the Robotic Agent. We provide system prompt samples in Appendix A.1.
186
      Advisor Agent. A Vision Language Model is responsible for breaking down the task and communi-
187   cating with the other agents to generate a sound guidance function for a given task.
188
189   Grounding Agent. A Vision Language Model that iteratively queries the free-form text segmentation
190
      models to locate (Cheng et al., 2023a; Liu et al., 2023a), track (Cheng et al., 2023b), and describe
      elements relevant to the task execution.
191
192   Monitor Agent. Responsible for identifying the causes of the failures in the unsuccessful rollouts,
193   the Monitor Agent consists of a Vision Language model equipped with a key frame extractor.
194   Robotic Agent. Language Model equipped with descriptions of the robot platform, a robot’s dy-
195   namics model and wrapper functions for integration with the base policy. It criticizes the provided
196   guidance functions to reinforce its relevance to the task and alignment with the robot’s capabilities.
197
      3.3   G UIDANCE P ROCEDURE
198
199   The conversational agents interact with each other through natural language and query their under-
200   lying tools to iteratively produce a guidance code tailored to the task in hand, the environment, and
201   the robot’s capabilities. The information flow between these agents is depicted by Figure 2.
202
      For a given task expressed in natural language and an image of the initial state of the environment,
203
      the Advisor Agent uses Chain-of-Tought (Wei et al., 2023) strategy to generate a high-level plan of
204   the steps necessary to accomplish the task. Being able to query a Grounding Agent and the Robotic
205   agent, the Advisor is able to collect relevant information about trackable objects and elements in the
206   environment, as well as the capabilities and limitations of the robotic platform.
207
      For a given plan and list of relevant objects required for the task completion, the Grounding Agent
208
      uses grounding Dino (Liu et al., 2023a) and the Segment Anything Model (SAM) (Cheng et al.,
209
      2023a) to locate the elements across multiple granularities and levels of abstraction. For instance,
210   if an object is not immediately found, the agent will actively look for semantically similar objects
211   or will look for higher-level elements that could encompass the missing object. For example, if the
212   object “cup” is to be located, and it could not be immediately found, the agent could search for
213   similar object like a “mug”. If it still struggles to locate it, the agent could search for a “shelf” and
214   then try to find the “cup” or “mug” in the cropped image of the “shelf”. If an object is found, it is
215   added to a tracking system (Yang & Yang, 2022). This process enhances the Segment and Track
      Anything (Cheng et al., 2023a) approach with flexible multi-granular search. The object statuses are

          reported back to the Advisor Agent, which iterates on the action plan or proceeds with the generation
217       of a guidance function grounded on the trackable objects located.
218
219       The Robotic agent acts as a critic to improve the guidance function generated. Equipped with a
220
          Python interpreter and details of the base policy’s action space, the agent can evaluate the guidance
          function in terms of feasibility and relevance to the robot’s capabilities. Once a function suffices the
221
          system’s requirements, it is saved to be used in the action loop, in combination with the dynamics
222
          model, to provide a guidance score for possible actions sampled from the base policy.
223
224       After the execution of a rollout and the identification of failure in the task completion, the Monitor
225       Agent is triggered to analyze the causes of the failure. By extracting key frames from the rollout
226
          video using PCA (Maćkiewicz & Ratajczak, 1993) and K-means clustering, the agent can feed a
          relevant and diverse set of images to the Visual Language Model prompted to access the failure
227
          causes. In the iterative applications of our framework, the Monitor Agent provides this feedback to
228
          the Advisor Agent, which can use this information to refine the guidance functions generated in the
229       previous iterations.

          3.4 G UIDANCE AND POLICY INTEGRATION
251
252       Aiming to guide a wide range of policies, our framework is designed to work both with continuous
253
          and discrete action spaces. In this section, we discuss the operation of combining the guidance
          function with the base policy’s action distributions. Furthermore, we discuss how deterministic
254
          regression models can be adapted to work with our framework.
255
256       Action-space Adaptation. We assume the availability of a dynamics model D that can forecast pos-
257       sible future states of the robot given a possible action a′π,t . In the manipulation domain, a dynamics
258       model is often available in the form of a forward kinematics model, a learned dynamics model, or
259
          a simulator. Oftentimes, the action space A of policies them-self is the same as the robot’s state
          S either being or joint angles of the robot or the gripper’s end-effector pose. For the last cases,
260
          where both the action and state space are expressed in SE(3) integrating the guidance function with
261
          a base policy would only require a multiplication of the guidance scores with the action probabili-
262       ties of the base policy. In other scenarios, adapting the robot’s action and state space to match the
263       representation of the visual cues (position, orientation, and size) would be required.
264
265
          Considering the visual grounding, the action space and the state space share the same representation
          (SE(3)), the operation to combine the guidance function with the base policy can be expressed as
266
          an element-wise weighted average:
267
268
                                               πguided = (1 − α)π · αG,                                    (3)
269       where α ∈ [0, 1] represents the percentage of guidance applied with respect to the base-policies
          distribution and is here denoted as guidance factor.

292   Adaptation of Regression Policies. To properly leverage the high-level guidance expressed in the
293   guidance functions and the low-level capabilities of the base policy, it is desired that the policy’s
294   action space be expressed as a distribution. In the case of regression policies that do not provide
295   uncertainty estimates, several strategies can be employed to infer the action distribution. One com-
296   mon approach is to assume a Gaussian distribution centered at the predicted value and compute the
297
      variance using ensembles of models trained with different initialization, different data samples, or
      different dropout seeds or different checkpoint stages (Abdar et al., 2021). Other strategies to infer
298
      the distributions of the model include using bootstrapping, Bayesian neural networks, or using a
299
      mixture of Gaussians (Mena et al., 2021).

      3.5   L EARNING N EW ROBOT S KILLS FROM S CRATCH
302
303   We note that this framework can enable robots to acquire new skills from scratch through zero-shot
304   learning or self-improvement via iterative guidance updates. By leveraging the system’s ability to
305   ground guidance in the visual features of the environment, the system can perform tasks without
306   prior training. Applying 100% guidance over untrained policies, the robot explores the environment
307   with purpose, learning basic skills and refining them iteratively to improve success. In section 4, we
308   provide an analysis of the system’s performance in such challenging scenarios.

313   Task Definitions: We demonstrate the efficacy of g-MotorCortex, in simulation on the RL-

321     a single RGBD camera input, as described in the GNFactor setup (Ze et al., 2024).
322   • (Sim): RL-Bench, learning from scratch: Aiming to explore the capabilities of
323     g-MotorCortex on learning new skills from scratch, we selected 4 challenging tasks from
        the RL-Bench benchmark: turn tap, push buttons, slide block to color target, reach and drag.

      • (Real): Sequenced Multi-button Press: Here, the agent must use its end-effector to press multi-

337
      • Act3D samples waypoints in the Cartesian space (R3 ) and predicts the orientation and gripper
338
        state for the best scoring sampled waypoint, combining a classification and regression strategies

346   guidance framework. Furthermore, the common representation of the action and state spaces of

349

350
      of simplicity, we assume a Gaussian distribution over the action space, with the mean centered
351   on the predicted values and the standard deviation fixed on a constant value. The outputs of the
352   classification component of Act3D (waypoint positions) were directly considered as samples of a
353   distribution over the Cartesian space (R3 ).

359
      improve the performance of pre-trained base policies on specific robotics tasks and environ-
360   ments without additional human demonstrations? (2) Does the proposed multi-granular percep-
361   tion capabilities effectively guide the policy in challenging cluttered environments? (3) Does
362   g-MotorCortex enable policies to learn new skills from scratch? (4) What is the effect of guid-
363   ance on expert versus untrained policies?

365

      g-MotorCortex can learn tasks from scratch using a random base policy with 100% of guidance

      (α). Given that we are not affecting the policy, the product of the iterative learning from scratch

481   relationships, and task completion criteria, among others. We can qualitatively see the effect of
482   the learning process by comparing the guidance scripts for different iterations of the same task.
483   Appendix A.2 includes two samples of guidance code for the same task but on different iterations.
484   We can see that the code corresponding to the second iteration is very simple and only accounts for
485   Euclidean distance and button order; while by the fifth iteration considerations like orientation come
      into play.

      5   C ONCLUSION
494
495   Summary: In this work, we proposed g-MotorCortex, a novel framework for the self-
496   improvement of embodied policies. Our self-guidance approach leverages the world knowledge
497   of a group of conversational agents and grounding models to guide policies during deployment. We
498   demonstrated the effectiveness of our approach in autonomously improving manipulation policies
499   and learning new skills from scratch, in simulated RL-bench benchmark tasks and in two challenging
500
      real-world tasks. Our results show that the proposed framework is especially effective in improving
      the following high-level task structures and key steps to solve the task. This capability can be well
501
      suited for improving pre-trained policies that struggle with long-horizon tasks or for learning new
502
      simple skills from scratch.
503
504   Limitations: From an analysis of the guided rollouts, a few of the tasks variations proved chal-
505   lenging for the perception models used by the grounding agent, leading to false positives detections
506
      or failure to locate specific objects. This limitation was mainly observed in simulation task, were
      the graphics object representations, even though simplified, do not always match the representations
507
      used to train the object detection models. This limitation could be addressed by integrating more
508
      robust object detection models or verification procedures to ensure the correct detection of objects in
509   the scene. Moreover, occasional inaccuracies on scene understanding by the Visual Language Model
510   (VLM) have been observed, leading to the generation of inaccurate guidance codes and unexpected
511   behaviors. Even though recent advances in large vision-language models have shown great potential
512   in understanding the underlying dynamics of the world from large-scale internet data, translating
513   this knowledge into out-of-distribution domain, such as robotics, while preventing hallucinations
514   remains an open challenge.
515   Future Works: Regarding future works, we think that combining the proposed framework with fine-
516   grained exploration techniques would allow the policy to explore in a targeted manner the low-level
517   details of the task, while leveraging the high-level guidance provided by our framework. This may
518   enrich the guidance codes with the necessary low-level details required to perform more complex
519   tasks successfully.
520
      Furthermore, the guidance function generation could be further improved by composing and adapt-
521   ing from a repository of successful guidance functions from previous experiences. This could be
522   achieved by incorporating Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG) (Lewis et al., 2021) into our
523   multi agent framework. This modification could allow the guidance system to learn new simple
524   skills from scratch by interacting with the environment and leveraging this collected knowledge to
525   guide the policy more effectively.
526
      Aiming to incorporate the knowledge captured by the guidance functions into the base policy, an
527   experience replay and finetuning mechanisms could be incorporated into our current system. This
528   modification could allow the framework to use past guided experiences to improve the base policy
529   in a sample efficient manner. This could be achieved in a targeted matter by leveraging Low Rank
530   Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021).
