028                                                                            A BSTRACT
029
030                 Diffusion models have recently achieved remarkable results for video genera-
031
                    tion. Despite the encouraging performances, the generated videos are typically
                    constrained to a small number of frames, resulting in clips lasting merely a few
032
                    seconds. The primary challenges in producing longer videos include the substantial
033
                    memory requirements and the extended processing time required on a single GPU.
034                 A straightforward solution would be to split the workload across multiple GPUs,
035                 which, however, leads to two issues: (1) ensuring all GPUs communicate effec-
036                 tively to share timing and context information, and (2) modifying existing video
037                 diffusion models, which are usually trained on short sequences, to create longer
038                 videos without additional training. To tackle these, in this paper, we introduce
039                 Video-Infinity, a distributed inference pipeline that enables parallel processing
040                 across multiple GPUs for long-form video generation. Specifically, we propose two
041                 coherent mechanisms: Clip parallelism and Dual-scope attention. Clip parallelism
042
                    optimizes the gathering and sharing of context information across GPUs which
                    minimizes communication overhead, while Dual-scope attention modulates the
043
                    temporal self-attention to balance local and global contexts efficiently across the
044
                    devices. Together, the two mechanisms join forces to distribute the workload and
045                 enable the fast generation of long videos. Under an 8 × Nvidia 6000 Ada GPU
046                 (48G) setup, our method generates videos up to 2,300 frames in 5 minutes.

049   1      I NTRODUCTION
050
051   A long-standing pursuit of human beings is to replicate the dynamic world we live in, in the digital
052   system. Traditionally dominated by physics and graphics, this effort has recently been enhanced by
053   the emergence of data-driven generative models (Rombach et al., 2022; Ho et al., 2022b; Harvey
      et al., 2022; Ho et al., 2022a), which can create highly realistic images and videos indistinguishable


                                                                                          1
054
      from reality. However, these models typically produce very short video segments, with most limited
055   to 16-24 frames (Guo et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023a; 2024). Some models extend to 60 or 120
056   frames (Zhaoyang et al., 2024; hpcaitech, 2024), but compromise heavily on resolution and visual
057   quality.
058
      Generating long videos poses substantial challenges, primarily due to the extensive resource demands
059
      for model training and inference. Current models, constrained by available resources, are often trained
060
      on brief clips, making it difficult to sustain quality over longer sequences. Moreover, generating a
061   minute-long video in one go can overwhelm GPU memory, making the task seem elusive.
062
063   Existing solutions, including autoregressive, hierarchical, and short-to-long methods, offer partial
064
      remedies but have significant limitations. Autoregressive methods (Henschel et al., 2024; Yin et al.,
      2023) produce frames sequentially, dependent on preceding ones. Hierarchical methods (Chen et al.,
065
      2023b; Yin et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024) create keyframes first, then fill in transitional frames.
066
      Furthermore, some approaches treat a long video as multiple overlapping short video clips (Qiu et al.,
067   2023; Wang et al., 2023a). These methods are not end-to-end; they often miss global continuity,
068   require extensive computation, especially in regions of overlap, and struggle with consistency across
069   different segments.
070
      To bridge these gaps, we introduce a novel framework for distributed long video generation, termed
071
      Video-Infinity. On the high level, it works in a divide-and-conquer principle. It breaks down the task
072
      of long video generation into smaller, manageable segments. These segments are distributed across
073   multiple GPUs, allowing for parallel processing. All clients should work collaboratively to ensure the
074   final video is coherent in semantics.
075
076
      This setup, while straightforward, faces two principal challenges: ensuring effective communication
      among all GPUs to share contextual information, and adapting existing models—typically trained on
077
      shorter sequences—to generate longer videos without requiring additional training.
078
079   To overcome these challenges, we introduce two synergistic mechanisms: Clip parallelism and
080   Dual-scope attention. Clip parallelism enables efficient collaboration among multiple GPUs by
081   splitting contextual information into three parts. It uses an interleaved communication strategy to
082
      complete the sharing in three steps. Building on the capabilities of Clip parallelism, Dual-scope
      attention meticulously adjusts the temporal self-attention mechanisms to achieve an optimal balance
083
      between local and global contexts across devices. This balance allows a model trained on short clips
084
      to be extended to long video generation with overall coherence.
085
086   Even more exciting, by leveraging both strategies, Video-Infinity reduces memory overhead from a
087   quadratic to a linear scale. With the power of multiple device parallelism and sufficient VRAM, our
088
      system can generate videos of any, potentially even infinite length.
089   As a result, our method significantly extends the maximum length of videos that can be generated
090   and accelerates the speed of long video generation. Specifficly, on an 8 × Nvidia 6000 Ada (48G)
091   setup, our method manages to generate videos up to 2300 frames in just 5 minutes. Our contributions
092   are summarized as follows: (1) We are the first to address long video generation using distributed
093   parallel computation, enhancing scalability and reducing generation times. (2) We introduce two
094
      interconnected mechanisms: Clip parallelism, which optimizes context information sharing across
      GPUs, and Dual-scope attention, which adjusts temporal self-attention to ensure video coherence
095
      across devices. (3) Our experiments show that our approach is over 100 times faster than the
096
      recent work Streaming T2V (Henschel et al., 2024) and 10 times faster than the concurrent work
097   FIFO-Diffusion (Kim et al., 2024) when generating ultra-long videos.

100   2     R ELATED WORKS
101
102   2.1   D IFFUSION MODELS
103
104   Diffusion models have gained significant attention in recent years due to their impressive ability
105   to generate high-quality media. Originally introduced for image synthesis, models like Denoising
106   Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPM) (Ho et al., 2020) and Latent Diffusion Models (LDM) (Rom-
107   bach et al., 2022) have demonstrated state-of-the-art performance in image generation. These models
      progressively denoise a Gaussian noise distribution by learning a sequence of reverse transformations.


                                                        2
108
      Beyond images (Ho et al., 2020; Rombach et al., 2022), diffusion models have also shown promise
109   in audio (Kong et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023) and 3D generation (Luo & Hu,
110   2021; Poole et al., 2022). Adaptations of diffusion models for video generation incorporate temporal
111   modules to capture the sequential nature of video frames. For instance, Video Diffusion Models
112   (VDM) (Ho et al., 2022b) and Flexible Diffusion Model (FDM) (Harvey et al., 2022) effectively
113   extend diffusion frameworks to video data, overcoming challenges like temporal consistency and
114   quality degradation. More recent models such as AnimateDiff (Guo et al., 2023), ModelScope (Wang
115   et al., 2023b), and VideoCrafter (Chen et al., 2023a; 2024) can now produce video clips with better
116   dynamics and improved visual quality.
117
118   2.2   T ECHNIQUES FOR LONG VIDEO GENERATION
119
120   Streaming T2V (Henschel et al., 2024) introduces a method that relies on a conditional attention
121
      module to ensure smooth transitions between video segments and a scene-preserving mechanism for
      content consistency. However, this method requires training and is not end-to-end, posing limitations
122
      on its practicality. FreeNoise (Qiu et al., 2023) utilizes rescheduled noise sequences and window-
123
      based temporal attention to improve video continuity. Despite these innovations, the rescheduled
124   noise contributes to limited dynamics in the generated videos, and the overlapping attention windows
125   introduce additional computational overhead. The concurrent work, FIFO-Diffusion (Kim et al.,
126   2024) employs a sliding pipeline to achieve longer video generation, also leveraging multiple GPUs,
127   but falls short in efficiency (see Table 1). Another concurrent work, FreeLong (Lu et al., 2024),
128   blends global low-frequency video features with local high-frequency details to maintain consistency
129   and fidelity in long video generation. However, it is still limited to single-GPU generation.
130
131   2.3   D ISTRIBUTED DIFFUSION
132
133   Recently, various distributed parallel methods have been applied to diffusion models to reduce
134   the latency of each denoising step in diffusion models. ParaDiGMS (Shih et al., 2024) utilizes
135   step-based parallelism, where each denoising step is executed on a different GPU device in parallel.
136
      However, this approach tends to waste much computation. Another method, DistriFusion (Li et al.,
      2024), divides images into patches, allowing different patches to be denoised on separate GPUs.
137
      This approach ensures synchronization among patches and achieves minimal computational waste.
138
      However, it is designed specifically for image diffusion and requires significant communication
139   overhead and specialized hardware support to achieve low latency.

144   Diffusion Models in Video Generation
145   The process of generating videos using diffusion models involves progressively denoising the latent
146   representation, denoted as xt , where t ranges from 0 to T . The initial noisy video latent is represented
147   by a random noise tensor xT . With each denoising step, xt is updated to a clearer latent xt−1 . This
148   iterative process continues until xT is denoised to x0 , which is then fed into a decoder to generate the
149   final video. The key aspect of updating xt to xt−1 is computing the noisy prediction ϵt , given by:
150   ϵt = Eθ (xt ). where Eθ represents the diffusion model.
151   The diffusion model Eθ can be implemented using various architectures, such as U-Net (Ronneberger
152   et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2022b; Harvey et al., 2022; Guo et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023a) or DiT (Peebles
153   & Xie, 2023; hpcaitech, 2024; Zhaoyang et al., 2024). These diffusion models are generally composed
154   of several similar layers. More specifically, the initial random noise tensor is written as xT ∈
155   RF ×H×W ×C , where F represents the number of frames, H and W denote the height and width of
156   each frame, respectively, and C is the number of channels.

191   4     D ISTRIBUTED L ONG V IDEO G ENERATION
192
193
      At the core of our pipeline, Video-Infinity segments the video latent into chunks, which are then
194
      distributed across multiple devices. An overview of our method is shown in Figure 3, where we
195
      divide the video latent along the temporal dimension. Such partitioning allows for parallel denoising
196   on different devices, each handling non-overlapping frames. To facilitate this, we propose Clip
197   parallelism, detailed in Section 4.1, a mechanism that efficiently synchronizes temporal information
198   across devices. Additionally, we incorporate Dual-scope attention in Section 4.2, which modulates
199   temporal attention to ensure training-free long video coherence and reduces the cost of context
200   synchronization.
201
      Formally, Video-Infinity splits the noisy latent xT ∈ RF ×H×W ×C into N sub-latent clips xiT ∈
202
      RFclip ×H×W ×C , where i ∈ [1, N ], Fclip = F/N represents the number of frames in each clip, and N
203   represents the total number of clips. This structured segmentation facilitates an even load distribution
204   across N devices. Additionally, the spatial modules of video diffusion models operate independently
205   across frames, which eliminates the need for inter-device communication and maintains consistency
206   in the outputs across different devices.

300   4.2     D UAL - SCOPE ATTENTION
301
      Applying attention in parallel inference incurs new challenges. The original attention module requires
302
      simultaneous access to all input tokens (Shaw et al., 2018). Adopting it under Clip parallelism, neces-
303   sitates aggregating tokens across devices, resulting in tremendous communication costs. Additionally,
304   as observed in Figure 4(a), attention mechanisms trained on shorter video clips often degrade in
305   quality when extended to longer sequences.
306
      To address these issues, we introduce the Dual-scope attention module. It revises the computation of
307
      K-V pairs to incorporate both local and global contexts into the attention. For each query token from
308
      frame a, its corresponding keys and values are computed from tokens in the frame set Aa = N a ∪ G:
309
310            • Local Context (N a ). This includes the |N a | neighboring frames of a, from which the keys
311              and values are derived to capture the local context. This local setup is typically achieved
312              through window attention, focusing on the nearby frames to enhance the temporal coherence.
313
               • Global Context (G). In contrast, the global context consists of frames uniformly sampled
314
                 from videos across all devices. This context provides keys and values from a broader range,
315
                 giving the model access to long-range information.
316
317   In practice, the keys K and values V are constructed by concatenating the tokens from both contexts
318   K = Concat(Klocal , Kglobal ) and V = Concat(Vlocal , Vglobal ), where Klocal and Qlocal is derived from
319   N a and Kglobal and Qglobal from G. We find that this modified key-value computation can be easily
320   incorporated into existing temporal attention without additional training, as shown in Figure 4(a),
321   enhancing the coherence of long videos.

      Base model. In the experiments, the text-to-video model VideoCrafter2 (Chen et al., 2024) (320 x
338
      512) is selected as the base model of our method. VideoCrafter2, which was trained on 16-frame
339   videos, excels at generating video clips that are both consistent and of high quality. It is also
340   the highest-scoring open-source video generation model under the VBench (Huang et al., 2023)
341   evaluation, achieving the top total score.
342
      Metrics evaluation. VBench (Huang et al., 2023) is utilized as a comprehensive video evaluation
343
      tool, featuring a broad array of metrics across various video dimensions. For each method, videos are
344
      generated using the prompts provided by VBench for evaluation. The metrics measured encompass all
345   the indicators under the Video Quality category in VBench, including subject consistency, background
346   consistency, temporal flickering, motion smoothness, dynamic degree, aesthetic quality, and imaging
347   quality. Given that VBench’s evaluation is typically performed on video clips of 16 frames, we
348   have modified the evaluation method for videos longer than 16 frames: we randomly sample five
349   16-frame clips from each video to evaluate separately, and then calculate the average score of these
350   assessments.
351   Baslines. Our approach is benchmarked against several other methods:
352
353    • FreeNoise (Qiu et al., 2023): We chose FreeNoise as a baseline because it is also a training-free
354      method that can base the VideoCrafter2 (Chen et al., 2024) model, which also serves as our base
355      model, to generate long videos. It employs a rescheduling technique for the initialization noise
356      and incorporates Window-based Attention Fusion to generate longer videos.
357
       • Streaming T2V (Henschel et al., 2024): To assess our method’s effectiveness in generating longer
358
         videos, StreamingT2V was chosen as our baseline. Streaming T2V involves training a new model
359
         that uses an auto-regressive approach to produce long-form videos. Like our approach, it also has
360      the capability to generate videos exceeding 1000 frames.
361
            OpenSora V1.1 (hpcaitech, 2024), a video diffusion model based on DiT (Peebles & Xie, 2023),
362
            supports up to 120 frames, can generate videos at various resolutions, and has been specifically
363
            trained on longer video sequences to enhance its extended video generation capabilities.
364
365
      Dual-scope attention setting. In the implementation of the Dual-scope attention, the number of
366   neighboring frames N i is set to 24, with 12 frames coming from the preceding clip and 12 frames
367   from the subsequent clip. The number of global frames, G, is set to 8. To balance consistency and
368   dynamics during the denoising process, the weights of frames in G and N i are dynamically adjusted.
369   Specifically, the weight of G increases by 10 for timesteps t greater than 800, whereas the weight of
370   N i increases by 10 for timesteps t less than or equal to 800.
371
      Implementation details. By default, all parameters of the diffusion are kept consistent with the
372   original inference settings of VideoCrafter2 (Chen et al., 2024), with the number of denoising steps
373   set to 30. Our experiments are conducted on 8 × Nvidia 6000 Ada (with 48G memory) . To
374   implement the temporal module in Clip parallelism, we utilized the torch.distributed tool
375   package, employing Nvidia’s NCCL as the backend to facilitate efficient inter-GPU communication.
376   Additionally, all fps conditions are set to 24, and the resolution is set to 512 × 320. Note that the
377   resolution for Streaming T2V cannot be modified; thus, videos are generated at its default resolution

494   remains consistent, the lack of shared context in the transition areas leads to anomalies. For instance,
495   the content of snow in frame 22 abruptly transitions to a dog, highlighted in red. These examples
496   highlight the importance of global and local context synchronization for video generation.

      6    C ONCLUSION
499
500
      We presented Video-Infinity, a distributed inference pipeline that leverages multiple GPUs for long-
501
      form video generation. We present two mechanisms, Clip parallelism and Dual-scope attention,
502   to address key challenges associated with distributed video generation. Clip parallelism reduces
503   communication overhead by optimizing the exchange of context information, while Dual-scope
504   attention modified self-attention to ensure coherence across devices. Together, these innovations
505   enable the rapid generation of videos up to 2,300 frames long, vastly improving generation speeds
506   compared to existing methods. This approach not only extends the practical utility of diffusion models
507   for video production but also sets a new benchmark for efficiency in long-form video generation.

511
      To fully harness the potential of our method, it relies on the availability of multiple GPUs. Additionally,

513   our current method generates longer videos using only a single prompt or simple multi-prompt setups,
514   which could result in less diverse content in the final video.
