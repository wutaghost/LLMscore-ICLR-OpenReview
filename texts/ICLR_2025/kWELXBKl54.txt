011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Real-time analysis and prediction of meteorological anomalies protect human lives
014            and infrastructure. Traditional methods rely on numerical threshold setting and
015            manual interpretation of weather heatmaps with Geographic Information Systems
016
               (GIS), which can be slow and error-prone. Our research redefines Meteorological
               Anomalies Analysis(MAA) by framing it as a Visual Question Answering (VQA)
017
               problem, thereby introducing a more precise and automated solution. Leveraging
018
               Vision-Language Models (VLM) to simultaneously process visual and textual data,
019            we offer an effective aid to enhance the analysis process of weather heatmaps. Our
020            initial assessment of general-purpose VLMs (e.g., GPT-4-Vision) on MAA revealed
021            poor performance, characterized by low accuracy and frequent hallucinations due
022            to inadequate color differentiation and insufficient meteorological knowledge. To
023            address these challenges, we introduce ClimateIQA, the first meteorological
024            VQA dataset, which includes 8,760 wind gust heatmaps and 254,040 question-
025            answer pairs covering four question types, both generated from the latest climate
026            reanalysis data. We also propose Sparse Position and Outline Tracking (SPOT),
027
               an innovative technique that leverages OpenCV and K-Means clustering to capture
               and depict color contours in heatmaps, providing ClimateIQA with more accurate
028
               color spatial location information. Finally, we present Climate-Zoo, the first
029
               meteorological VLM collection, which adapts VLMs to meteorological applications
030            using the ClimateIQA dataset. Experiment results demonstrate that models from
031            Climate-Zoo substantially outperform state-of-the-art general VLMs, achieving an
032            accuracy increase from 0% to over 90% in MAA verification.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Meteorology is essential for disaster preparedness, agricultural planning, and climate resilience,
038   as it encompasses the study of weather patterns, climate change, and severe weather events. One
039   important task in meteorology is Meteorological Anomalies Analysis (MAA), which includes both
040   anomalies detection and interpretation. MAA has become increasingly urgent due to the rise in
041   these events driven by global warming. Traditionally, MAA relies on numerical threshold setting
042   and manual analysis of weather anomaly heatmaps, which visualize data such as temperature, wind
043   speed, and precipitation (Wunsch et al., 2022). Nonetheless, traditional methods using Geographic
044
      Information Systems (GIS) to analyze these heatmaps often require manual work, making the process
      time-consuming and error-prone.
045
046   In recent years, AI has advanced in processing large datasets and accelerating meteorological tasks.
047   Machine learning models, such as Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), have been used for
048   weather prediction and anomalies detection. Yet they mostly use numeric data and often yield
049   low accuracy (Fang et al., 2021). Large Language Models (LLMs), such as ChatClimate (Vaghefi
050
      et al., 2023), ClimateGPT (Thulke et al., 2024), and ClimSight (Koldunov & Jung, 2024), excel at
      generating textual weather reports but struggle with interpreting visual data, which are an essential
051
      part in MAA.

      Qwen-VL (Bai et al., 2023), and LLaVA 1.6 (Liu et al., 2024a) have broad abilities in visual

      2.2         AI FOR METEOROLOGY
134
135   The integration of AI in meteorology has seen many applications, such as employing AI for long-
136   term weather prediction (Lam et al., 2022), typhoon trajectory forecasting (Bi et al., 2022), and
137   weather classification (Dalal et al., 2023). Models like Pangu-weather Bi et al. (2023), Fengwu Chen
138   et al. (2023), and NeuralGCM Kochkov et al. (2024) are outstanding. The advent of LLMs like
139   ClimSight (Koldunov & Jung, 2024), ChatClimate (Vaghefi et al., 2023), Arabic Mini-ClimateGPT
140   (Mullappilly et al., 2023), and ClimateGPT (Thulke et al., 2024) has broadened the scope of textual
141
      data processing in meteorology. These models have been instrumental in assimilating general
      meteorological knowledge related to climates, answering common queries, and offering insights.
142
      However, these models predominantly rely on textual data. This becomes particularly limiting
143
      when addressing complex challenges such as the analysis of anomalies distributions in heatmap,
144   where textual data alone proves inadequate and prone to inaccuracies, often leading to serious
145   hallucinations (Bulian et al., 2023). Meteorologists often need to interpret data from satellite images
146   (Liu et al., 2024b), radar (Guastavino et al., 2022), heatmaps (Lee et al., 2024), and isobaric maps
147   (Xu et al., 2024) to make accurate assessments. Nonetheless, there remains a lack of VLMs capable
148   of interpreting such visual meteorological data.

      4.1   DATA COLLECTION AND PRE - PROCESSING
217
218   Our meteorological data were derived from the ERA5 hourly dataset on single levels. ERA5, produced
219   by the European Centre for Medium-Range Weather Forecasts (ECMWF) (Hersbach et al., 2020),
220   provides comprehensive global climate and weather records since 1940. This dataset is created using
221   data assimilation techniques that integrate model outputs with observational data, enhancing accuracy
222   by reconciling forecasts with new observations every 12 hours. For this study, we selected hourly
223   wind gust data for 2023. Due to the complexity of wind gust heatmaps, which contains two times
224   colors than precipitation or temperature heatmaps, wind gust data is especially representative for
225   identifying anomalies. The method developed with wind gust data can be conveniently extended in
226
      the future to analyze less complex heatmaps, such as heatmaps for heatwaves, droughts, and heavy
      precipitation.
227
228   To classify wind speeds, we employed the Beaufort Scale (Monmonier, 2005), a widely recognized
229   system that quantifies wind speed by observing its impact at sea or on land. The Beaufort Scale
230   categorizes wind speeds from 0 to 12, with each level associated with a specific wind speed range
231   and descriptive physical conditions. In our analysis, each wind force level is represented by a
232
      unique color gradient, starting from white for the lowest wind speeds and progressing through light
      cyan, aquamarine, light green, light lime green, light lemon yellow, light yellow, peach, light coral,
233
      salmon, deep pink, and dark magenta, culminating at dark purple for the highest wind speeds. In
234
      meteorological research, Beaufort scale level 8 (20.8 m/s) is commonly used to demarcate extreme
235
      weather conditions Radinović & Ćurić (2014); Weaver et al. (2021). Therefore, we marked colors
236   after "peach" as anomalies in the heatmaps. To facilitate the geographical localization of anomalies,
237   we superimposed a world map onto each heatmap.
238
239
      To extract and compile color information from meteorological heatmap images, we developed a
      method called "SPOT" (Sparse Position and Outline Tracking). Here are the process:
240
241   1. Color Segmentation: Initially, our SPOT method extracts contours from heatmaps based on
242   four primary colors: red, yellow, white, and green, using OpenCV as the color filter (Culjak et al.,
243   2012). We obtain the contour coordinates of each color region to address the issue of irregular shapes
244   often encountered in heatmaps. This process is iterated twice to ensure accuracy, selecting the best
245
      segmentation result to mitigate errors.
246   2. Representative Point Selection: Given the large volume of contour coordinate data, we represent
247   each color region’s geographical location and distribution shape using a minimal set of points. We
248   start by determining the number of points needed based on the area of each color region within the
249   image. To tackle the challenge of representing irregularly shaped color regions with a few coordinates,
250   we applied the K-Means clustering algorithm to compute the centroid coordinates for each region.
251
      We set the random state to 0 to ensure reproducibility and eliminate randomness. The number of
      clusters (k) is determined by the area of the color regions: Less than 1% of the total area: 1 point.
252
      1%-5% of the total area: 3 points. 5%-10% of the total area: 5 points. More than 10% of the total
253
      area: 10 points.
254
255   3. Filtering Outliers: We implemented a rule-based function to ensure all points fall within their
256   respective color regions. Any points found outside these regions are automatically excluded and
257
      replaced with new points from the nearest valid contour. In a processed heatmap containing 5,448
      points, approximately 122 points may fall outside the contour, resulting in an efficiency rate of about
258
      97.7%. Our method reroutes these outlier points to maintain the robustness and accuracy of the model.
259
      As shown in Figure 3 of our paper, each purple dot precisely represents the spatial location and shape
260   of the corresponding color region.
261
262   As illustrated in Figure 3, each purple dot represents the position and shape of its corresponding color
263
      region. With the help of the SPOT method, we can correctly identify the spatial location of different
      color regions with 100% accuracy. The pseudo-code of SPOT is in Appendix 1.

307

308

309

350
351
      This section details our methods to enhance the performance of VLMs on MAA through prompt-
352
      tuning and supervised fine-tuning with ClimateIQA.
353
354   Base models Based on model performance on VLM benchmarks (Goyal et al., 2017; Lu et al.,
355   2022), we selected three state-of-the-art VLMs as our base models for improvement: Llava-v1.6-
356
      mistral-7b (Liu et al., 2024a), Qwen-VL-Chat (Bai et al., 2023), and Yi-VL-6B (Young et al., 2024).
      Llava-v1.6 excels in multimodal understanding, Qwen-VL-Chat in visual dialog tasks, and Yi-VL-6B
357
      in visual reasoning.
358
359   Supervised fine-tuning
360   Supervised fine-tuning involves instructing a pre-trained model to improve its performance on a
361   specific task by providing task-specific information. As depicted in Figure 5, we used 70% of the
362   data for training, 10% for validation, and 20% for testing. Our strategy included freezing the weights
363   of the visual encoder and employing a unified encoder layer to reduce computational costs and
364   mitigate overfitting risks. This approach is supported by (Khattak et al., 2023), who demonstrated
365   that pre-trained visual encoders are proficient at extracting meaningful features. Additionally, the size
366   of the training dataset significantly impacts fine-tuning effectiveness. We conducted experiments with
367   different dataset sizes (10k, 50k, 100k, and 203k) to evaluate the effects on fine-tuning performance
368
      and ultimately selected the best-performing model.
369   Training details We conducted full-parameter training on three prominent large-scale VLMs: Llava-
370   v1.6 (7B parameters), Qwen-VL-Chat (7B parameters), and Yi-VL-6B (6B parameters), utilizing the
371   Swift toolkit for its efficiency and flexibility. In addition to full-parameter tuning, we also fine-tune
372   these models with Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) layers to further improve their adaptability. LoRA
373   introduces a low-rank decomposition of the model’s weight matrices, allowing efficient adaptation to
374
      new tasks with minimal additional parameters. By setting the LoRA rank to 8, with an alpha value of
      32 and a dropout probability of 0.05, we balance adaptation capacity and computational efficiency.
375
376   To expedite the training process, we employed 8 H100 80G GPUs and utilized Distributed Data
377   Parallel along with DeepSpeed. The batch size was set to 1, and the learning rate was 0.0001 (1e-4).
      The entire experiment was conducted for a single epoch, spanning a total duration of 22 days.

393   We designed a comprehensive framework to assess the performance of the models across four
394   categories of questions. Our goal was to understand the model’s adaptability and accuracy across

396   F1 Score For Verification Questions, we evaluate the model’s ability to judge the correctness of
397   statements using recall, precision, and F1 score (the harmonic mean of precision and recall).
398
      Element Match Score For Enumeration Questions, we compute a match score (MS) between
399   the ground truth (x) and model-generated answer (y). This involves comparing the sets (x) and (y)
400   formatted as ["New York", "High Seas of the North Atlantic Ocean", "Canary Islands"], representing
401   ground truth and model output, respectively. The match score calculation involves determining
402   correct matches via the set intersection size (common elements in both sets (x) and (y)) and incorrect
403   matches via the symmetric differences (elements present in one set but not in the other). In cases
404   where both sets (x) and (y) are empty (union size of zero), the match score is defined to handle
405   division by zero and set to zero. Otherwise, the match score ranges between -1 and 1, where a score
406   closer to 1 indicates more accurate and complete answers with fewer hallucinations (incorrect items),
407
      and a score closer to -1 indicates poor performance with many hallucinations. The score is determined

      7    L IMITATIONS
487
488
      Although our VLMs demonstrate potential in detecting anomalies, their accuracy, currently at 91%,
489   can be further enhanced. A notable limitation is the model’s difficulty in accurately identifying colors
490   in heatmaps, largely due to training data comprising only fully intact heatmaps. Inspired by the
491   chain-of-thought prompting technique, we propose dividing the original heatmap into nine sub-images
492   to generate individual question-answer pairs for fine-tuning. This approach aims to improve the
493   model’s color localization skills, ultimately enhancing accuracy. Additionally, integrating VLMs
494   with traditional threshold-based methods could create a robust hybrid system, combining interactive
495   strengths with precise anomaly detection.
496   Meanwhile, our initial dataset, focused solely on wind gust data due to its complexity (13 colors),
497   poses another limitation. Future research should incorporate additional weather factors like tempera-
498   ture and precipitation to build a more comprehensive dataset. This would enable more generalized,
499   robust VLM training, improving performance across various anomalies detection tasks.

503
      This study presents pioneering work in integrating VLMs for MAA, providing a robust framework
504
      for anomalies detection and interpretation. Through a series of carefully designed experiments, we
505
      identified key issues of current general-purpose VLMs and proposed a framework of solutions to
506   improve their performance. We introduced the SPOT method for precise color localization, developed
507   ClimateIQA, the first large-scale dataset in this field, and established Climate-Zoo, a collection
508   of state-of-the-art VLMs adapted for meteorological applications. Models based on Qwen-VL-

510   geo-indexing, and description tasks of MAA. This research pushes the boundaries of AI usage in
511   meteorology and contributes a benchmark for heatmap-based MAA, guiding future research to dive
512   further into this field.
