009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            We deal with the problem of gradient estimation for stochastic differentiable relax-
012            ations of algorithms, operators, simulators, and other non-differentiable functions.
013            Stochastic smoothing conventionally perturbs the input of a non-differentiable
014            function with a differentiable density distribution with full support, smoothing it
015            and enabling gradient estimation. Our theory starts at first principles to derive
016
               stochastic smoothing with reduced assumptions, without requiring a differentiable
               density nor full support, and presenting a general framework for relaxation and
017
               gradient estimation of non-differentiable black-box functions f : Rn → Rm . We
018
               develop variance reduction for gradient estimation from 3 orthogonal perspectives.
019            Empirically, we benchmark 6 distributions and up to 24 variance reduction strate-
020            gies for differentiable sorting and ranking, differentiable shortest-paths on graphs,
021            differentiable rendering for pose estimation, as well as diff. cryo-ET simulations.

024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
      The differentiation of algorithms, operators, and other non-differentiable functions has been a topic
026   of rapidly increasing interest in the machine learning community [1]–[7]. In particular, whenever we
027   want to integrate a non-differentiable operation (such as ranking) into a machine learning pipeline,
028   we need to relax it into a differentiable form in order to allow for backpropagation. To give a concrete
029   example, a body of recent work considered continuously relaxing the sorting and ranking operators
030   for tasks like learning-to-rank [5], [7]–[15]. These works can be categorized into either casting sorting
031   and ranking as a related problem (e.g., optimal transport [16]) and differentiably relaxing it (e.g., via
032   entropy-regularized OT [7]) or by considering a sorting algorithm and continuously relaxing it on the
033   level of individual operations or program statements [5], [11], [14], [15]. To give another example, in
034
      the space of differentiable graph algorithms and clustering, popular directions either relax algorithms
      on a statement level [5] or cast the algorithm as a convex optimization problem and differentiate the
035
      solution of the optimization problem under perturbed parameterization [2], [3], [17].
036
037   Complementary to these directions of research, in this work, we consider algorithms, operators,
038   simulators, and other non-differentiable functions directly as black-box functions and differentiably
039   relax them via stochastic smoothing [18], i.e., via stochastic perturbations of the inputs and via
040
      multiple function evaluation. This is challenging as, so far, gradient estimators came with large
      variance and supported only a restrictive set of smoothing distributions. More concretely, for a
041
      black-box function f : Rn → Rm , we consider the problem of estimating the derivative (or gradient)

      Before delving into individual choices for distributions, we provide a clarification for multivariate
187   densities µ : Rn → R≥0 : We consider the n-dimensional multivariate form of a distribution as the
188   concatenation of n independent univariate distributions.
                                                          Qn Thus, for µ1 as the univariate formulation
189   of the density, we have the proportionality µ(ϵ) ≃ i=1 µ1 (ϵi ). We remark that the distribution by
190   which we smooth (Lϵ) is not an isotropic (per-dimension independent) distribution. Instead, through
191   transformation by the scale matrix L, e.g., in the case of the Gaussian distribution, Lϵ covers the
192   entire space of multivariate Gaussian distributions with arbitrary covariance matrices.
193   Beyond the Gaussian distribution, the logistic distribution offers heavier tails, and the Gumbel
194   distribution provides max-stability, which can be important for specific tasks. The Cauchy distribu-
195   tion [30], with its undefined mean and infinite variance, also has important implications in smoothing:
196   e.g., the Cauchy distribution is shown to provide monotonicity in differentiable sorting networks [12].
197   While prior art [22] heuristically utilized the Cauchy distribution for stochastic smoothing of argmax,
198   this had been, thus far, without a general formal justification.
199   In this work, for the first time, we consider Laplace and triangular distributions. First, the Laplace
200   distribution, as the symmetric extension of the exponential distribution, does not lie in the space of
201   exponential family distributions, and is not differentiable at 0. Via Lemma 3, we show that stochastic
202   smoothing can still be applied and is exactly correct despite non-differentiablity of the distribution.
203   Second, with the triangular distribution, we illustrate, for the first time, that stochastic smoothing
204   can be performed even with a non-differentiable distribution with compact support ([−1, 1]). This
205   is crucial if the domain of f has to be limited to a compact set rather than the real domain, or in
206   applications where smoothing beyond a limited distance to the original point is not meaningful.
207
      A hypothetical application for this could be differentiating a physical motor controlled robot in
      reinforcement learning where we may not want to support an infinite range for safety considerations.

267   2.3   S MOOTHING OF THE A LGORITHM VS . THE O BJECTIVE
268
269   In algorithmic supervision settings, we can write our training objective as ℓ(h(y)) where y is the
      output of a neural network model, h is the algorithm, and the scalar function ℓ is the training objective


                                                          5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      (loss function) applied to the output of the algorithms. In such cases, we can distinguish between
271   smoothing the algorithms (f = h) and smoothing the loss (f = ℓ ◦ h).
272
273   When smoothing the algorithm, we compute the value and derivative of ℓ Eϵ [h(y +ϵ)] . This requires
274
      our loss function ℓ to be differentiable and capable of receiving relaxed inputs. (For example, if the
      output of h is binary, then ℓ has to be able to operate on real-valued inputs from (0, 1).) In this case,
275
      the derivative of Eϵ [h(y + ϵ)] is a Jacobian matrix (see Corollary 2).
276
277   When smoothing the objective / loss function, we compute the value and derivative of Eϵ [ℓ(h(y + ϵ))].
278   Here, the objective / loss ℓ does not need to be differentiable and can be limited to operate on discrete
279   outputs of the algorithm h. Here, the derivative of Eϵ [ℓ(h(y + ϵ))] is a gradient.
280   The optimal choice between smoothing the algorithm and smoothing the objective depends on
281   different factors including the problem setting and algorithm, the availability of a real-variate and
282   real-valued ℓ, and the number of samples that can be afforded. In practice, we observe that, whenever
283   we can afford large numbers of samples, smoothing of the algorithm performs better.

285   3     R ELATED W ORK
286
      In the theoretical literature of gradient-free optimization, stochastic smoothing has been extensively
287   studied [18], [19], [39], [40]. Our work extends existing results, generalizing the set of allowed
288   distributions, considering vector-valued functions, anisotropic scale matrices, enabling k-sample
289   median differentiation, and a characterization of finite definedness of expectations and their gradients
290   based on the relationship between characteristics of the density and smoothed functions.
291
      From a more applied perspective, stochastic smoothing has been applied for relaxing convex opti-
292
      mization problems [2], [3], [22]. In particular, convex optimization formulations of argmax [2], [22],
293   the shortest-path problem [2], and the clustering problem [3] have been considered. We remark that
294   the perspective of smoothing any function or algorithm f , as in this work, differs from the perspective
295   of perturbed optimizers. In particular, optimizers are a special case of the functions we consider.
296
      While we consider smoothing functions with real-valued inputs, there is also a rich literature of
297
      differentiating stochastic discrete programs [41]–[43]. These works typically use the inherent
298
      stochasticity from discrete random variables in programs and explicitly model the internals of the
299   programs. We consider real-variate black-box functions and smooth them with added input noise.
300
301   In the literature of reinforcement learning, a special case or analogous idea to stochastic smoothing
302
      can be found in the REINFORCE formulation where the (scalar) score function is smoothed via a
      policy [18], [20], [44], [45]. Compared to the literature, we enable new distributions and respective
303
      characterizations of requirements for the score functions. We hope our results will pave their way
304
      into future RL research directions as they are also applicable to RL without major modification.

306   4     E XPERIMENTS
307
308   For the experiments, we consider 4 experimental domains: sorting & ranking, graph algorithms, 3D
309   mesh rendering, and cryo-electron tomography (cryoET) simulations. The primary objective of the
310
      empirical evaluations is to compare different distributions as well as different variance reduction
      techniques. We begin our evaluations by measuring the variance of the gradient estimators, and
311
      then continue with optimizations and using the differentiable relaxations in deep learning tasks. We
312
      remark that, in each of the 4 experiments, f does not have any non-zero gradients, and thus using
313   first-order or path-wise gradients or gradient estimators is not possible.

505       S UMMARY OF E XPERIMENTAL R ESULTS
506
          Generally, we observe that QMC and RQMC perform best, whereas antithetic sampling performs
507       rather poorly. In low-dimensional problems, it is advisable to use RQMC (cartesian), and in higher
508       dimensional problems (R)QMC (latin), still works well. As for the covariate, LOO typically performs
509       best; however, the choice of sampling strategy (QMC/RQMC) is more important than choosing the
510       covariate. In sorting and ranking, the Cauchy distribution performs best for large numbers of samples
511       and for smaller numbers of samples, the Laplace distribution performs best. In the shortest-path
512       case, the logistic distribution performs best, and Gaussian closely follows. Here, we also observe
513       that with larger numbers of samples, the optimal standard deviation decreases. For differentiable
514       rendering, the logistic distribution performs best.
515
516       L IMITATIONS
517
         A limitation of our work is that zeroth-order gradient estimators are generally only competitive if the
518
         first-order gradients do not exist (see [55] for discussions on exceptions). In this vein, in order to
519      be competitive with custom designed continuous relaxations like a differentiable renderer, we may
520      need a very large number of samples, which could become prohibitive for expensive functions f .
521      The optimal choice of distribution depends on the function to be smoothed, which means there is
522      no singular distribution that is optimal for all f ; however, if one wants to limit the distribution to a
523      single choice, we recommend the logistic or Laplace distribution, as, with their simple exponential
524      convergence, they give a good middle ground between heavy-tailed and light-tailed distributions.
525      Finally, the variance reduction techniques like QMC/RQMC are not immediately applicable in single
526      sample settings, and the variance reduction techniques in this paper build on evaluating f many times.
527
528       5             C ONCLUSION
529
530      In this work, we derived stochastic smoothing with reduced assumptions and outline a general
531
         framework for relaxation and gradient estimation of non-differentiable black-box functions. This
         enables an increased set of distributions for stochastic smoothing, e.g., enabling smoothing with
532
         the triangular distribution while maintaining full differentiablility of fϵ . We investigated variance
533
         reduction for stochastic smoothing–based gradient estimation from 3 orthogonal perspectives, finding
534      that RQMC and LOO are generally the best methods, whereas the popular antithetic sampling method
535      performs rather poorly. Moreover, enabled by supporting vector-valued functions, we disentangled
536      the algorithm and objective, thus smoothing f while analytically backpropagating through the loss ℓ,
537      improving gradient estimation. We applied stochastic smoothing to differentiable sorting and ranking,
538      diff. shortest-paths on graphs, diff. rendering for pose estimation and diff. cryo-ET simulations.
539      We hope that our work inspires the community to develop their own stochastic relaxations for
         differentiating non-differentiable algorithms, operators, and simulators.

      When we have a function f that is not defined with a compact range with f : Rn → R, and have a
      density µ with unbounded support (e.g., Gaussian or Cauchy), we may experience fϵ or even ∇fϵ
886
      to not be finitely defined. For example, virtually any distribution with full support on R leads to the
887
      smoothing fϵ of the degenerate function f : x 7→ exp(exp(exp(exp(x2 )))) to not be finitely defined.
888
889   We say a function, as described via an expectation, is finitely defined iff it is defined (i.e., the
890   expectation has a value) and its value is finite (i.e., not infinity). For example, the first moment of
891
      the Cauchy distribution is undefined, and the second moment is infinite; thus, both moments are not
      finitely defined.
892
893   We remark that the considerations in this appendix also apply to prior works that enable the real
894   plane as the output space of f . We further remark that writing an expression for smoothing and the
895   gradient of a arbitrary function with non-compact range is not necessarily false; however, e.g., any
896   claim that smoothness is guaranteed if the gradient jumps from −∞ to ∞ (e.g., the power tower in
897
      the first paragraph) is not formally correct. We remark that characterizing valid f s via a Lipschitz or
      other continuity requirement is not applicable because this would defeat the goal of differentiating
898
      non-differentiable and discontinuous f .

       MNIST Sorting Benchmark Experiments We train for 100 000 steps at a learning rate of 0.001

1142   the optimal γ ∈ {1, 1/3, 0.1}.

1144   Warcraft Shortest-Path Benchmark Experiments Following the established protocol [17], we
1145   train for 50 epochs with the Adam optimizer at a batch size of 70 and an initial learning rate of 0.001.

       The learning rate decays by a factor of 10 after 30 and 40 epochs each. The model is the first block of
       ResNet18. The hyperparameter γ = 1/β as specified in Figures 13 and 14.

       Utah Teapot Camera Pose Optimization Experiments We initialize the pose to be perturbed by
1149
       angles uniformly sampled from [15◦ , 75◦ ]. The ground truth orientation is randomly sampled from
1150
       the sphere of possible orientations. The ground truth camera angle is 20◦ , and the ground truth camera
1151   distance is uniformly sampled from [2.5, 4]. The initial camera distance is sampled as being uniformly
1152   offset by [−0.5, 6], thus the feasible set of initial camera distance guesses lies in [2, 10]. The initial
1153   camera angle is uniformly sampled from [10◦ , 30◦ ]. We optimize for 1 000 steps with the Adam
1154   optimizer [(β1 , β2 ) = (0.5, 0.99)] and the CosineAnnealingLR scheduler with an initial learning
1155   rate of 0.3. We schedule the diagonal of L to decay exponentially from [0.1, 5◦ , 5◦ , 0.25◦ ] · 100.75
1156   to [0.1, 5◦ , 5◦ , 0.25◦ ] · 10−1.75 (the dimensions are camera distance, 2 pose angles, and the camera
1157   angle). As discussed, the success criterion is finding the angle within 5◦ of the ground truth angle.
1158   There is typically no local minimum within 5◦ and it is a reliable indicator for successful alignment.
1159
1160   Differentiable Cryo-Electron Tomography Experiments The ground truth values of the parame-
1161
       ters are set to 300 kV for acceleration voltage, 3 mm for the focal length, and the ground truth sample
       specimen is centered as (x, y) = (0, 0) nm units. For reporting errors, the acceleration voltages are
1162
       normalized by a factor of 100 to ensure that all parameters vary over commensurate ranges. For the
1163
       2-parameter optimization, the feasible set of acceleration voltage varied over a range of [0, 1000] kV
1164   and the feasible set of the specimen’s x-position varied over the range [−5, 5]. For the 4-parameter
1165   optimization, the feasible set of acceleration voltage varied over a range of [0, 600] kV, the focal
1166   length ranges over [0, 6] mm, the x- and y-positions range over [−3, 3]. We use the Adam optimizer
1167   for both experiments, with [(β1 , β2 ) = (0.5, 0.9)]. For the MC Search baseline, we generate sets of
1168   n uniform random points in the feasible region of the parameters, generate micrographs for these
1169   random parameter tuples using the TEM simulator [53], and identify the parameter tuple in the set
1170   having the lowest mean squared error with respect to the ground truth image. The RMSE between
1171   this parameter tuple and the ground truth parameters is the metric for the specific set of n randomly
1172
       generated values. This is repeated 20 times to obtain the mean and standard deviation of the RMSE
       metric at that n.
