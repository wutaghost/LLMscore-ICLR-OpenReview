009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011                Machine unlearning is the study of methods to efficiently remove the influence
012                of some subset of the training data from the parameters of a previously-trained
013                model. Existing methods typically require direct access to the “forget set” – the
014                subset of training data to be forgotten by the model. This limitation impedes pri-
015                vacy, as organizations need to retain user data for the sake of unlearning when a
016                request for deletion is made, rather than being able to delete it immediately. We
017
                   first introduce the setting of blind unlearning – unlearning without explicit access
                   to the forget set. Then, we propose a method for approximate unlearning called
018
                   R ELOAD, that leverages ideas from gradient-based unlearning and neural network
019
                   sparsity to achieve blind unlearning. The method serially applies an ascent step
020                with targeted parameter re-initialization and fine-tuning, and on empirical unlearn-
021                ing tasks, R ELOAD often approximates the behaviour of a from-scratch retrained
022                model better than approaches that leverage the forget set. Finally, we extend the
023                blind unlearning setting to blind remedial learning, the task of efficiently updating
024                a previously-trained model to an amended dataset1 .

027   1       I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Machine unlearning poses the problem of removing the influence of certain instances in the train-
030   ing data on a given statistical model (Bourtoule et al., 2019). Motivated by “right to be forgotten”
031
      provisions, like those in the European Union’s General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) (Euro-
      pean Parliament & Council of the European Union), methods in machine unlearning aim to provide
032
      efficient means to “forget” specific data points from a trained model without requiring that it be
033
      retrained from scratch. As larger models become more prevalent (Achiam et al., 2023), the need to
034   unlearn specific data instances without retraining from scratch is increasingly important.
035
036   Contemporary unlearning methods generally require explicit access to the so-called “forget set” – the
037
      subset of training data to be forgotten by the model. For example, one approach entails performing
      steps of gradient ascent on the loss landscape characterized by the forget set in order to remove
038
      its influence on the model weights (Thudi et al., 2022). However, the reliance of these methods
039
      on the forget set introduces a tension in the context of preserving user privacy: in order to enable
040   unlearning, organizations must retain the complete original set of user data on which the model was
041   trained. The retention of this data, even for the purpose of unlearning, can expose organizations and
042   individuals to risks associated with data breaches or unauthorized access. To bridge this gap, there is
043   a clear need for unlearning methods that operate without requiring access to the forget set. Existing
044   work aims to reduce the reliance on the forget set, but is limited to the constrained task of forgetting
045   classes of data, and requires knowing which classes are being forgotten (Tarun et al., 2023).
046
      This work presents an algorithm for machine unlearning in the absence of an explicitly defined forget
047
      set; a setting we establish as “blind unlearning.” Our method, R ELOAD, assumes that the modeller
048
      only has access to (a) a model trained on a dataset D, (b) the “retain set,” Dnew ≜ D\Df orget ,
049
      and (c) cached gradients from the last iteration of training on D. Notable in its absence from these
050   requirements is the forget set – this means that R ELOAD allows for deletion of instances in Df orget
051   at the conclusion of training without inhibiting downstream unlearning.

117   Machine Unlearning. In the machine unlearning setting, the transformation of D into Dnew
118   consists of first identifying a subset of the data whose influence to remove, Df orget , and taking
119   Dnew ≜ D\Df orget . These remaining instances represent the portion of the training data that is
120   retained – the full training set, less those instances to be forgotten. The goal of approximate unlearn-
121   ing methods – of which R ELOAD is one (see Section 2.2) – is to efficiently learn an approximation
              ∼                                                                                            ∗
122   of M(θ ) . The classical setting assumes that the modeller has access to the trained model M(θ ) ,
123   the training dataset D, the remaining data Dnew , and the forget set Df orget (Cao & Yang, 2015).
124   Remedial Learning. The unlearning setting is subject to the restriction that Dnew ≜ D\Df orget ;
125   however, one may also consider the broader setting wherein Dnew is the result of some arbitrary
126   item-wise transformation to D. Formally, let f : X × Y → X × Y denote a transformation,
127   and write (Xi′ , Yi′ ) = f (Xi , Yi ). Then, Dnew represents the result of applying f item-wise to K
128   elements of D, and applying the identity transform to the remaining N − K elements, as
129
                              Dnew = {(Xi′ , Yi′ )}i=1,...,K ∪ {(Xi , Yi )}i=K+1,...,N .                     (3)
130
131   This represents a generalization of the unlearning problem, as we wish to “unlearn” the influence of
132   {(Xi , Yi )}i=1,...,K on our original model, and “relearn” the influence of {(Xi′ , Yi′ )}i=1,...,K . This
133   setting encompasses the following data transformations, among others:
134
      1. Covariate Correction: Dnew = {(Xi′ , Yi )}i=1,...,K ∪ {(Xi , Yi )}i=K+1,...,N , where Xi′ repre-
135
         sents a corrected version of the features Xi , and indices K + 1, ..., N correspond to those with
136
         erroneous covariates (e.g., data was corrupted during collection/pre-processing).
137
138
      2. Label Correction: Dnew = {(Xi , Yi′ )}i=1,...,K ∪ {(Xi , Yi )}i=K+1,...,N , where Yi′ represents a
         corrected version of the label Yi , and indices K +1, ..., N correspond to those that were originally
139
         mis-labelled during annotation.
140
141   3. Backdoor Removal: Dnew = {(Xi′ , Yi )}i=1,...,K ∪ {(Xi , Yi )}i=K+1,...,N , where Xi′ represents
142
         a version of the features Xi lacking the injected backdoor pattern, and indices K + 1, ..., N
         correspond to those that were originally transformed with a backdoor during processing. Models
143
         trained with backdoors in the training set learn shortcuts (Geirhos et al., 2020), which can be
144
         exploited to induce misclassification.
145
146   This work studies how the R ELOAD algorithm accomplishes tasks both in the unlearning setting,
147   and in the setting of remedial learning.
148
      Blind Unlearning / Blind Remedial Learning. In contrast to the classical unlearning (and remedial
149
      learning) settings, in which the modeller has access to the forget set, our setting assumes no such
150   access. We call this setting blind unlearning (or blind remedial learning). The blind unlearning /
151   remedial learning setting has access to the trained model MD , the new dataset Dnew , and (poten-
152   tially) some limited information about the original data, ID , from which which neither Df orget (in
153   the unlearning setting) or D (in the remedial learning setting) can be fully reconstructed.

155   2.2   R ELATED W ORK
156
157   Exact and Approximate Unlearning. Exact unlearning refers to the subclass of algorithms that
158   provide formal guarantees of the extent to which information about Df orget was removed from
159
      the weights of a model. The trivial method for exact unlearning consists of naively retraining the
      model from scratch (this is considered the gold-standard for machine unlearning; see Cao & Yang
160
      (2015); Thudi et al. (2022); Shaik et al. (2024)). Other exact unlearning methods include SISA
161
      (Bourtoule et al., 2019), which partitions the data to accelerate retraining, Certified Data Removal


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      (Guo et al., 2019), which performs a Newton update in the opposite direction of the gradient with
163   respect to Df orget , and Certified Graph Unlearning (Chien et al., 2022), which builds on Certified
164   Data Removal using the graph topology to enforce guaranteed unlearning. Unlike exact unlearning
165   methods, approximate unlearning algorithms (like R ELOAD) aim to recover the behaviour of a model
166   naively retrained on the new set without providing any formal theoretical guarantees. These methods
167   can be sub-classified into either gradient-based or weight-saliency based algorithms.
168
      Gradient-Based Approximate Unlearning. Many existing approximate unlearning algorithms per-
169                                              ∗
      form an optimization procedure on M(θ ) using the forget set Df orget and the retain set Dnew . One
170   simple standard approach applies gradient ascent on the loss with respect to Df orget , in order to
171   undo the parameter updates induced by those instances during training (Graves et al., 2021; Thudi
172   et al., 2022). Another gradient-based approach leverages a teacher-student method: “Bad Teacher”
173   performs knowledge distillation based on one trained model on Dnew (the “good teacher”) and one
174   a randomly initialised model on Df orget (the “bad teacher”) (Chundawat et al., 2022); SCalable
175   Remembering and Unlearning unBound (SCRUB) similarly distills a student model from a teacher
176   trained on D, but the student learns to selectively disobey the teacher by directly maximizing the loss
177   on Df orget (Kurmanji et al., 2023). A third family directly manipulates the structure of the learned
178
      representation space using gradients: Distance-based Unlearning via Centroid Kinematics (DUCK)
      (Cotogni et al., 2023) drives representations of elements in Df orget towards the nearest incorrect
179
      centroid in the feature space, while Boundary Unlearning (Chen et al., 2023) implements class-level
180
      unlearning by shifting the decision boundary corresponding to the class(es) defining Df orget .
181
182   Weight Saliency-Based Approximate Unlearning. Another class of approximate unlearning meth-
183   ods derives from the hypothesis that identifiable substructures in neural networks often correspond to
184
      different subsets of the training data (Pfeiffer et al., 2023). These methods leverage ideas from neu-
      ral sparsity (Frankle & Carbin, 2018; Chen et al., 2024) to perform targeted unlearning on specific
185
      parameters. Saliency unlearning (SalUn) uses a threshold on ∇θ L(Df orget ) to identify parameters
186
      containing the most signal about Df orget and focuses model updates on these parameters (Fan et al.,
187   2023). Selective Synaptic Dampening (SSD) (Foster et al., 2023) extends this idea to avoid gradient
188   steps by scaling parameters based on their Fisher Information Matrix importance scores.
189
190
      Blind Unlearning. This setting involves unlearning without access to Df orget at the instanced of
      unlearning. It then reduces to taking a model fit on one dataset D and adapting it to fitting a new
191
      dataset Dnew . This connects to domain adaptation, in which differences in datasets may not be
192
      explicitly defined. In blind unlearning it is not available. An unlearning baseline, Finetuning (FT)
193   (Warnecke et al., 2023) on the retain-set Dnew fulfills the blind criteria. Catastrophically forgetting
194   the last k layers (CF-k) and Exact-unlearning the last k layers (EU-k) (Goel et al., 2022) are also
195   blind. Fisher Forgetting (Fisher) (Golatkar et al., 2020) is also a blind unlearning algorithm, but is
196   theoretically bound by class unlearning. Both FT and CF-k provide no strong theoretical indication
197   of unlearning. EU-k does by re-initialising the last k layers of the model. Our method, R ELOAD,
198   provides a stronger indication by selectively re-initialises parameters which know the most about
199   the knowledge we wish to remove.

      5   D ISCUSSION
487
488
      This work introduces the setting of blind unlearning, machine unlearning without direct access to the
489   “forget set”. This setting allows for improved privacy procedures in practical settings, by enabling
490   the immediate deletion of data when an unlearning request is received rather than retaining the data
491   for the purpose of downstream unlearning. Our method, R ELOAD, combines insights from gradient-
492   based unlearning (to remove top-level information from all parameters) with selective parameter
493   re-initialization. The blind setting ensures that as long as practitioners store the last step gradients
494   of their model on the training set, they have the capacity to unlearn data when it is removed from
495   their system. We recommend that future work study the performance of R ELOAD at larger scales,
496   such as those presented by modern large language models (Achiam et al., 2023), and investigate the
497
      utility of other choices for ID beyond the cached gradients used in R ELOAD.
498   Despite operating in the blind setting, R ELOAD outperforms benchmark machine unlearning algo-
499   rithms that enjoy direct access to Df orget , suggesting that it is an empirically effective unlearning
500   algorithm. However, R ELOAD admits a modest tradeoff between computational efficiency and per-
501   formance in this regime. We finally observe that machine unlearning represents a special case of
502   remedial learning, a setting that is especially important for efficiently correcting errors in the train-
503
      ing data used to train models. R ELOAD remains an efficient, performant method in this regime,
      suggesting that our work may contain generalizable insights about about learning to fit arbitrary
504
      downstream transformations of data.
