009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Graph State Space Models (SSMs) have recently been introduced to enhance
012            Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) in modeling long-range interactions. Despite
013            their success, existing methods either compromise on permutation equivariance
014            or limit their focus to pairwise interactions rather than sequences. Building on
015            the connection between Autoregressive Moving Average (ARMA) and SSM, in
016
               this paper, we introduce GRAMA, a Graph Adaptive method based on a learn-
               able Autoregressive Moving Average (ARMA) framework that addresses these
017
               limitations. By transforming from static to sequential graph data, GRAMA lever-
018
               ages the strengths of the ARMA framework, while preserving permutation equiv-
019            ariance. Moreover, GRAMA incorporates a selective attention mechanism for
020            dynamic learning of ARMA coefficients, enabling efficient and flexible long-
021            range information propagation. We also establish theoretical connections be-
022            tween GRAMA and Selective SSMs, providing insights into its ability to capture
023            long-range dependencies. Extensive experiments on 14 synthetic and real-world
024            datasets demonstrate that GRAMA consistently outperforms backbone models
025            and performs competitively with state-of-the-art methods.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Neural graph learning has become crucial in handling graph-structured data across various domains,
030
      such as social networks (Kipf & Welling, 2016; Hamilton et al., 2017), molecular interactions (Xu
031   et al., 2019; Bouritsas et al., 2022), and more (Khemani et al., 2024). The most popular framework
032   of neural graph learning is that of Message Passing Neural Networks (MPNNs). Some prominent
033   examples are GCN (Kipf & Welling, 2016), GAT (Veličković et al., 2018), GIN (Xu et al., 2019),
034   and GraphConv (Morris et al., 2019). However, many MPNNs suffer from a critical shortcom-
035   ing of oversquashing (Alon & Yahav, 2021; Di Giovanni et al., 2023), that hinders their ability
036   to model long-range interactions. To address this limitation, several proposals were made, from
037   graph rewiring (Topping et al., 2022; Di Giovanni et al., 2023; Karhadkar et al., 2023), to multi-
038   hop MPNNs (Gutteridge et al., 2023), weight space regularization (Gravina et al., 2023; 2024a),
039
      as well as Graph Transformers (GTs) (Yun et al., 2019; Dwivedi & Bresson, 2022; Kreuzer et al.,
      2021b). Specifically, GTs became popular because of their theoretical and often practical ability
040
      to capture long-range node interactions through the attention mechanism. However, the quadratic
041
      computational cost of full attention limits their scalability, and in some cases, they were found to un-
042   derperform on long-range benchmarks when compared to standard MPNNs (Tönshoff et al., 2023).
043
044   At the same time, State Space Models (SSMs), such as S4 (Gu et al., 2021c) and Mamba (Gu et al.,
045
      2023), have emerged as promising, linear-complexity alternatives to Transformers. SSMs leverage
      a recurrent and convolutional structure to efficiently capture long-range dependencies while main-
046
      taining linear time complexity (Nguyen et al., 2023). Contemporary models like Mamba develop
047
      selective filters that prioritize context through input-dependent selection, offering compelling advan-
048   tages in processing long sequences with reduced computational demands compared to transformers
049   (Gu et al., 2023). Despite these benefits, adapting SSMs to the non-sequential structure of graphs
050   remains a significant challenge. Perhaps the biggest challenge lies in the fundamental question of
051   “how to transform a graph into a sequence?”. To this end, several approaches were proposed, from
052   a graph-to-sequence heuristic in Wang et al. (2024a), to studying the relationship between SSMs and
053   spectral GNNs by pairwise interactions (Huang et al., 2024), as well as sample-based random walk
      sequencing of the graph (Behrouz & Hashemi, 2024). However, as we discuss later, some of them

      lose the permutation-equivariance property desired in GNNs, while others do not take advantage
055   of the sequence processing ability of SSMs. These limitations hinder their ability to fully lever-
056   age sequence-processing capabilities, especially for addressing oversquashing in GNNs. To resolve
057   these issues, we propose, instead, a complementary approach – transforming a static input graph into
058   a sequence of graphs, combined with an adaptive neural autoregressive moving-average (ARMA)
059   mechanism, called GRAMA. We show that GRAMA is theoretically equivalent to an SSM on
060   graphs. Our GRAMA allows us to enjoy the benefits of sequential processing mechanisms like
061   SSMs, coupled with any GNN backbone, from MPNNs to graph transformers, while maintaining
062   backbone properties, such as permutation-equivariance.
063   Main Contributions. Our Adaptive Graph Autoregressive Moving Average (GRAMA) model
064   offers several advancements in the conjoining of dynamical systems theory into GNNs:
065
066   • Principled Integration of SSMs in GNNs. We enable the use of sequence-based models (like
067     ARMA) coupled with virtually any GNN backbone, by transforming graph inputs into temporal
068     sequences without sacrificing permutation invariance.
069   • Theoretical Understanding of the coupling of SSMs and GNNs. We demonstrate that augment-
070     ing GNNs with ARMA via our GRAMA has an equivalent SSM model.
071   • Mitigation of the oversquashing problem. We provide the theoretical foundation that our
072     GRAMA effectively addresses the oversquashing phenomenon in GNNs and improves the long-
073     range interaction modeling capabilities.
074   • Strong Practical Performance. We demonstrate our GRAMA on three popular backbones (GCN
075     (Kipf & Welling, 2016), GatedGCN (Bresson & Laurent, 2018), and GPS (Rampášek et al., 2022))
076
        and show the compelling performance by GRAMA on 14 synthetic and real-world datasets.

080   We now provide an overview and discussion of related topics and works to our GRAMA. In Ap-
081   pendix A, we discuss additional related works.
082
      Long-Range Interactions on Graphs. GNNs rely on message-passing mechanisms to aggregate in-
083
      formation from neighboring nodes, which limits their ability to capture long-range dependencies, as
084
      highlighted by Alon & Yahav (2021); Di Giovanni et al. (2023). Models like GCN (Kipf & Welling,
085   2016), GraphSAGE (Hamilton et al., 2017), and GIN (Xu et al., 2019) face challenges such as over-
086   smoothing (Nt & Maehara, 2019; Oono & Suzuki, 2020; Cai & Wang, 2020; Rusch et al., 2023)
087   and over-squashing (Alon & Yahav, 2021; Topping et al., 2022; Di Giovanni et al., 2023), which
088   hinder long-range information propagation—critical in applications like bioinformatics (Baek et al.,
089   2021; Dwivedi et al., 2022b) and heterophilic settings (Luan et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024b). To
090   address these limitations, various methods have emerged, including graph rewiring (Topping et al.,
091   2022; Karhadkar et al., 2023), weight space regularization (Gravina et al., 2023; 2024a), and Graph
092   Transformers (GTs). GTs, which capture both local and global interactions, have been particu-
093
      larly promising, as demonstrated by models like SAN (Kreuzer et al., 2021c), Graphormer (Ying
      & Leskovec, 2021), and GPS (Rampášek et al., 2022). These models often incorporate positional
094
      encodings, such as Laplacian eigenvectors (Dwivedi et al., 2021) or random-walk structural encod-
095
      ings (RWSE) (Dwivedi et al., 2022a), to encode graph structure. However, the quadratic complexity
096   of full attention in GTs presents scalability challenges. Recent innovations like sparse attention
097   mechanisms (Zaheer et al., 2020; Choromanski et al., 2020), Exphormer (Shirzad et al., 2023), and
098   linear graph transformers (Wu et al., 2023; Deng et al., 2024) address these bottlenecks, improving
099   efficiency and scalability for long-range propagation.
100
      State Space Models (SSMs). SSMs, traditionally used for time series analysis (Hamilton, 1994b;
101
      Aoki, 2013), process sequences through latent states. However, classic SSMs struggle with long-
102   range dependencies and lack parallelism, limiting their computational efficiency. Recent advances,
103   such as the Structured State Space Sequence model (S4) (Gu et al., 2021c; Fu et al., 2023), mitigate
104   these issues by employing linear recurrence as a structured convolutional kernel, enabling paral-
105   lelization on GPUs. Despite this, simple SSMs still underperform compared to attention models
106   in natural language tasks. Mamba (Gu et al., 2023) improves the ability of SSMs to capture long-
107   range dependencies by selectively controlling which sequence parts influence model states. Mamba
      has shown promising results, outperforming Transformers in several benchmarks (Gu et al., 2023;


                                                       2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Liu et al., 2024) while being more computationally efficient. The combination of SSMs with graph
109   models presents challenges, particularly in transforming the articulated connectivity of graphs into
110   sequences. For instance, Graph-Mamba (Wang et al., 2024a) orders nodes by degree, but this heuris-
111   tic approach sacrifices permutation-equivariance, a desirable property in GNNs. Similarly, Behrouz
112   & Hashemi (2024) propose generating sequences via random walks, which improves performance
113   but also sacrifices permutation-equivariance while adding non-determinism to the model. Also, turn-
114   ing a graph into a sequence based on a policy, such as sorting nodes by degree, limits direct use of
115   the input graph, as multiple graphs can share the same node degrees and thus be indistinguishable.
116   Huang et al. (2024) explored links between spectral GNNs and graph SSMs, focusing on pairwise
117
      interactions; however, this design choice may not fully exploit the sequence-handling capacity of
      SSMs and may reach the state of oversquahsing earlier because of the use of powers of the adja-
118
      cency matrix (Di Giovanni et al., 2023). In this work, we harness the potential of SSMs by adopting
119
      a structure inspired by the connection between SSMs and ARMA models. By transforming static
120   graphs into sequences, GRAMA maintains permutation-equivariance, a desired property in GNNs
121   (Bronstein et al., 2021), also useful for long-propagation (Pan & Kondor, 2022; Schatzki et al.,
122   2024), while enabling effective learnable and selective long-range propagation.
123
      Autoregressive Moving Average Models (ARMA). ARMA models, introduced by Whittle (1951),
124
      combine an autoregressive (AR) component, modeling dependencies on previous time steps, with a
125
      moving average (MA) component, considering residuals. Widely applied in stationary time series
126   analysis (Box et al., 1970), ARMA models are equivalent to state space models (SSMs) (Hamilton,
127   1994a). An ARMA(p, q) model considers previous p states and q residuals δ(·), and is governed by
128   the following equation:

133
      Although ARMA models are traditionally used for processing sequences, they have also been stud-
134
      ied for classical graph filtering (Isufi et al., 2016) and more recently formulated as an MPNN in
135
      Bianchi et al. (2019). The Graph ARMA model (Bianchi et al., 2019) introduced a learnable ARMA
136   version for GCNs, using recursive 1-hop filters to create a structure resembling ARMA methods. In
137   this paper, we introduce GRAMA, a method that leverages neural ARMA models by transform-
138   ing a static graph input into a graph sequence. Different than Bianchi et al. (2019), which uses
139   the static input graph and formulates a recursive ARMA model through a spectral convolution per-
140   spective, our GRAMA incorporates a selective and graph adaptive mechanism that learns ARMA
141   coefficients along the graph sequence. This dynamic adjustment of coefficients directly addresses
142   oversquashing by preserving long-range dependencies and enabling adaptive control over feature
143   propagation. Additionally, Bianchi et al. (2019) uses an ARMA(1, 1) model with non-linearities
144
      between steps, hindering its direct conversion into an SSM, while we show that our GRAMA has
      an equivalent SSM, providing deeper theoretical understandings.

147   3   GRAMA
148
149   Although a graph is a static structure, the process of message passing introduces a dynamic element.
150   In message passing, information is propagated through the graph, allowing nodes to update their
151   states based on the states of their neighbors. This dynamic behavior can be viewed through the
152   lens of dynamical systems, where the state of each node evolves according to certain aggregating
153
      rules, as discussed in Section 2. This perspective is instrumental in Recurrent Neural Networks
      (RNNs), which are designed to handle sequential data and capture temporal dependencies. By
154
      treating the message-passing process as a dynamical system, we can leverage the strengths of RNNs
155
      to model the evolution of node states over time. The model we propose, GRAMA, takes inspiration
156   from the architectural structure of the latest generation of sequential models, like S4 (Gu et al.,
157   2021a), Mamba (Gu et al., 2023), LRU (Orvieto et al., 2023b), and xLSTM (Beck et al., 2024).
158   To import these powerful sequential models to graph learning, we first translate static input graphs
159   into sequences of graphs. Then, the GRAMA block transforms such graph sequence into another
160   graph sequence, while considering the structure of the graph. Each GRAMA block is linear, and
161   non-linear activations are applied between GRAMA blocks to increase the flexibility of the overall
      model. Below, we discuss in detail the different aspects of our GRAMA – from its initialization

185   Initialization. Processing information with ARMA or SSM frameworks, by design, requires a se-
186   quence. As discussed in Section 2, previous studies on graph SSMs have chosen to transform the
187   graph into a sequence by means of heuristic node ordering, random walk sampling, or by consider-
188   ing pairwise interactions (edges) as sequences of length 2. While these choices are valid, and show
189
      strong performance in practice, they also introduce challenges compared to common graph learning
      approaches, or may not fully utilize the underlying sequence processing framework. Specifically,
190
      the first two approaches (node ordering and walk sampling) do not maintain the permutation equiv-
191
      ariance desired in GNNs, and the third (pairwise interactions) considers only very short sequences,
192   while one key benefit of the ARMA and SSM frameworks is their ability to capture long-range
193   dependencies in long sequences (Gu et al., 2021b). To address these challenges, we propose to
194   transform a static graph into a sequence of graphs, such that each node is equipped with a sequence

265   3.2     L EARNING A DAPTIVE G RAPH ARMA C OEFFICIENTS
266
      We now introduce our graph adaptive approach for learning the ARMA coefficients, which is a key
267
      component in our approach to allow a flexible and selective GRAMA.
268
269   Naive ARMA Learning. The most straightforward way to learn the AR and MA coefficients,
      {ϕi }pi=1 and {θj }qj=1 , is to consider them as parameters of the neural network and learn them via


                                                                 5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      gradient descent. However, this yields coefficients that are identical for all inputs, thereby not adap-
271   tive. This approach is directly linked to non-selective weights in SSM models (Gu et al., 2021c),
272   which were shown to be less effective compared to selective coefficients (Gu et al., 2023).
273   Selective ARMA Learning. To allow selective ARMA coefficient learning similarly to Mamba (Gu
274   et al., 2023), we use an attention mechanism (Vaswani et al., 2017) applied over the state and residual
275   sequences F(s) , ∆(s) at each GRAMA block s = 1, . . . , S. The rationale behind this construction
276   is that an attention layer assigns scores between elements within the sequence. Formally, we obtain
277   two scores matrices AF(s) , A∆(s) ∈ [0, 1]L×L . The last row in each matrix represents the predicted
278   coefficients for our GRAMA, {ϕi }pi=1 and {θj }qj=1 , respectively. However, the SoftMax normal-
279   ization in standard attention layers yields non-negative pairwise values, which is not consistent with
280
      the usual choice of ARMA coefficients. Therefore, we follow the self-attention implementation
      (Vaswani et al., 2017) up to the SoftMax step, and we normalize the scores to be in [−1, 1] while
281
      complying with a sum-to-one constraint. We note that, this procedure facilitates learning stability,
282
      such that ARMA coefficients do not explode or vanish, and its design is guided by the insights from
283   Theorems 4.3 and 4.4. We also note that this overall construction yields two-fold adaptivity in the
284   predicted ARMA coefficients: First, the attention mechanism allows to be selective with respect to
285   its input, which are the sequences F(s) , ∆(s) . Second, because these sequences are coupled with
286   a GNN backbone, as shown in Equation (6), it implies that the input node features and the graph
287   structure influence the coefficients. We provide further implementation details in Appendix C, and
288   a comparison between naive and selective ARMA learning in Appendix F.1.

296   Connection to SSM. As discussed in Section 3, each GRAMA block is fundamentally an ARMA
297   model. In Theorem 4.1, we formalize the equivalence between ARMA models and linear SSMs.
298   This allows us to interpret our GRAMA model as a stack of graph-informed SSMs through the
299   backbone GNN encoded in Equation (6).
300   Theorem 4.1 (Equivalence between ARMA models and State Space Models). For every ARMA
301   model, there exists an equivalent State Space Model (SSM) representation, and conversely, for every
302   linear SSM, there exists an equivalent ARMA model representation.

      Long-Range Interactions. A key distinction between standard MPNNs and our GRAMA lies in its
325   neural selective sequential mechanism, which uses learned ARMA coefficients to operate across two
326   domains: the spatial graph domain via a GNN backbone, and the sequence domain via the ARMA
327   mechanism, enabling selective state updates. Remarkably, the state matrices of each GRAMA
328   block play a significant
                                  role in the  propagation of the information from the first sequence of node
329   features, F(0) = f (0) , . . . , f (L−1) , to the last sequence of node features after S GRAMA blocks,

330   F(S) = f (LS) , . . . , f (L(S+1)−1) , especially for large L and S. In fact, if the entries of the k-th
331   power of the state matrix of a GRAMA block vanish, then for a stable GRAMA it is impossible
332   to model long-range dependencies of k hops, in the sequence, as we show in Lemma B.1. This
333   fact relates to a broadly acknowledged problem in the RNN literature, the vanishing gradient issue
334   (Hochreiter et al., 2001; Bengio et al., 1994; Orvieto et al., 2023a): the entries of the powers of
335   a matrix with a spectral radius less than 1 can quickly vanish, making it challenging for gradient-
336   based algorithms to effectively long-range patterns. Therefore, to bias the long-term propagation of
337
      the information of a GRAMA block, we can initialize the state matrix to have its eigenvalues close
      enough to the unitary circle , following the footsteps of recent RNN methodologies (Orvieto et al.,
338
      2023b; Arjovsky et al., 2016; De et al., 2024). In fact, the closer the eigenvalues are to the unitary
339
      circle, the slower the powers of the state matrix vanish (Horn & Johnson, 2012). The following
340   Theorem 4.4 provides a criterion to control the long-range interaction of GRAMA.
341
      Theorem 4.4 (GRAMA allows long-range interactions). Let us be given a GRAMA              Pp block with
342
      autoregressive coefficients {ϕi }pi=1 . Assume the roots of the polynomial P (λ) = λp − j=1 ϕj λp−j
343
      are all inside the unit circle. Then, the closer the roots P (λ) are to the unit circle, the longer the
344
      range propagation of the linear SSM corresponding to such a GRAMA block.
345
346   The results derived in this section provide the theoretical foundation and motivation for the employ-
347   ment of GRAMA as a method to address the oversquashing phenomenon in GNNs, and to enhance
348   long-range interaction modeling capabilities, as we show in our experiments in Section 5.

524   6    C ONCLUSION
525
526   We introduced GRAMA, a novel sequence-based framework that enhances the long-range inter-
527   action modeling ability and feature update selectivity of Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) through
528   the integration of adaptive neural Autoregressive Moving Average (ARMA) models with potentially
529   any GNN backbone. We draw a theoretical link between SSM models and GRAMA, to build solid
530
      groundwork and understanding of the qualitative behavior of GRAMA. Compared with several
      existing Graph SSMs, our GRAMA allows to benefit from long-range interaction modeling abil-
531
      ities, while maintaining permutation equivariance. Through a series of extensive experiments on
532
      14 synthetic and real-world datasets, we demonstrated that GRAMA consistently offers competi-
533   tive performance with well-established baseline models, from classical MPNNs to more complex
534   approaches such as Graph Transformers and Graph SSMs. Overall, GRAMA offers a theoretically
535   grounded and powerful, flexible solution that bridges the gap between contemporary sequential mod-
536   els and existing graph learning methods, stepping forward towards a new family of graph machine
537   learning models.

       GNNs based on Differential Equations. Building on the interpretation of convolutional neural
1029   networks (CNNs) as discretizations of ODEs and PDEs (Ruthotto & Haber, 2020; Chen et al., 2018;
1030   Zhang et al., 2019), several works, including GCDE (Poli et al., 2019), GODE (Zhuang et al.,
1031   2020), and GRAND (Chamberlain et al., 2021), among others, view GNN layers as discretized
1032   steps of the heat equation. This framework helps manage diffusion (smoothing) and sheds light on
1033   the oversmoothing problem in GNNs (Nt & Maehara, 2019; Oono & Suzuki, 2020; Cai & Wang,
1034   2020). In contrast, Choromanski et al. (2022) introduced an attention mechanism based on the heat
1035   diffusion kernel. Other models, such as PDE-GCNM (Eliasof et al., 2021) and GraphCON (Rusch
1036   et al., 2022), combine diffusion with oscillatory processes to maintain feature energy. Recent work
1037
       has explored anti-symmetry (Gravina et al., 2023), reaction-diffusion dynamics (Wang et al., 2023;
       Choi et al., 2023), convection-diffusion (Zhao et al., 2023), and fractional Laplacian ODEs (Maskey
1038
       et al., 2023). While most focus on spatial aggregation in DE-GNNs, temporal aspects are also
1039
       addressed in (Eliasof et al., 2024b; Gravina et al., 2024b; Kang et al., 2024). Overall, we refer
1040   to this family of models as DE-GNNs. These models are related to SSM models, which are also
1041   based on ODEs. Also, some of the DE-GNNs were shown to be effective against oversquashing as
1042   architectures, and therefore we include them in our experimental comparisons.
1043
       Multi-hop GNNs. Multi-hop GNN architectures were extensively studied in previous years, lead-
1044
       ing to several popular architectures such as JK-Net (Xu et al., 2018), MixHop (Abu-El-Haija et al.,
1045
       2019), and more recently DRew (Gutteridge et al., 2023). These works take inspiration from earlier
1046   works like DenseNets (Huang et al., 2017), where the main idea is to consider a combination of fea-
1047   ture maps from multiple layers, instead of only considering the last layer feature map as in ResNets
1048   (He et al., 2016). We now distinguish our GRAMA from JK-Net , MixHop, and DRew. First, these
1049   methods do not stem from a dynamical system perspective that allows the construction of models
1050   like ARMA or SSM. Second, methods like JK-Net can become computationally expensive if many
1051   layers are used within a network, as it considers all previous layers, and it is only used within the
1052   final layer in a GNN, rather than an architecture that considers multiple past values at each layer of
1053   the network. Third, in GRAMA we propose a selective attention mechanism to ARMA coefficients,
1054
       as described in Section 3.2.

1251
1252   Our GRAMA is illustrated in Figure 1, and it is comprised of three main components: (i) the
1253   initial embedding, which is described in Equation (2). The role of this part is to transform a static

1256

1257

1260   in the case of a graph level task, we first pool the node features using mean pooling as in Xu et al.
1261   (2019), to obtain a prediction vector gout (P OOL(f (L·S−1) )) ∈ Ro . In the case of node-level tasks,
1262

1450   E     C OMPLEXITY AND RUNTIMES
1451
1452   We discuss the theoretical complexity of our method, followed by a comparison of runtimes with
1453   other methods.
1454   Time Complexity. We analyze the case where linear in graph size (nodes and edges) complexity
1455   MPNN (such as GCN) is used within GRAMA. Our GRAMA is comprised of L initial MLPs, S
1456   GRAMA blocks, each with L recurrent steps, and a final readout layer. Note that L is the sequence
1457   length, which is a hyperparameter and does not exceed the value of 50 in our experiments. The
       initial MLPs operate on the input f ∈ Rn×c and embed them to a hidden dimension d. Therefore,


                                                                         27
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1458
       their time complexity is O(L · n · c · d). Each GRAMA block is comprised of two attention layers –
1459   one for the pooled states sequence and the other for the residual pooled sequence, and a GNN layer
1460   for predicting the current step residual, which operates on graph node features. The attention layer
1461   time complexity is O(L2 d2 ) because the pooled (across the graph nodes) sequence is of shape L×d,
1462   and the GNN layer complexity is O(n + m), where n is the number of nodes and m is the number
1463   of edges in the graph. Note that usually m ≫ n, so the GNN complexity can be rewritten as O(m);
1464   however, in the following, we keep the more general case. In total, we have S GRAMA blocks,
1465   where S is a hyperparameter, and is typically small, up to 4. The final readout layer is a standard
1466   MLP and, therefore, has the time complexity of O(n · d · o) for node-wise tasks, and O(d · o) for
1467
       graph-level tasks. Therefore, the overall time complexity (including initial MLPs and readout) of
       our GRAMA is O L · n · c · d + SL · (n + m + L2 · d2 ) + n · d · o .
1468
1469   Space Complexity. We analyze the case where linear in graph size (nodes and edges) complexity
1470   MPNN (such as GCN) is used within GRAMA. The space complexity of the initial MLPs is O(L·c·
1471   d). The space complexity for each GRAMA block is O(d2 ) for the GNN layer, and similarly O(d2 )
1472   for the two attention layers. Overall, we have S such blocks. The readout layer space complexity is
1473
       O(d · o). Thus, the overall space complexity (including initial MLPs and readout) of GRAMA is
       O(L · c · d + S · d2 + d · o).
1474
1475   Runtimes. We provide runtimes for GRAMA alongside other methods, such as Graph GPS and
1476   GCN, in Table 7. In all cases, we use a model with 256 hidden dimensions and a varying depth
1477   (changing the sequence length L from 2 to 16 in our GRAMA with S = 2 GRAMA blocks, re-
1478   call that GRAMA depth is SL, and the number of layers is the backbone for other methods) and
1479
       report the training and inference times, as well as the performance on the Roman-Empire dataset,
       for reference. As can be seen from the results in the Table, our GRAMA is positioned as a middle
1480
       ground solution in terms of computational efficiency, between linear complexity MPNNs like GCN
1481
       and quadratic complexity methods like GPS. Notably, our GRAMA achieves better performance
1482   than GCN and GPS, and maintains its performance as depth increases, different than GCN. Still,
1483   in some cases, lower computational cost might be a strong requirement, for example, on edge de-
1484   vices. To this end, we can use the naive learning approach of ARMA coefficients, as discussed in
1485   Section 3.2, which still utilizes our GRAMA but avoids the use of an attention mechanism for a
1486   selective ARMA coefficient learning. In this case, as we show in Table 7, it is still possible to obtain
1487   significant improvement compared with the baseline performance with lower computational time,
1488   although with less flexibility that is offered by our selective ARMA coefficient learning. In addi-
1489   tion, for a broader comparison, we consider the best-performing variant of GPS (GPSGAT+Performer
1490
