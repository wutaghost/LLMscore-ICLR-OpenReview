009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Fair representation learning is a promising way to mitigate discrimination in
012            downstream tasks. Many existing fair representation learning methods require
013            access to sensitive information, but the collection of sensitive information is often
014            difficult and even involves privacy issues. Additionally, a model trained to be fair
015            with respect to one sensitive attribute may not ensure fairness for other sensitive
016
               groups. Thus, how to flexibly address fairness issues when we have limited ac-
               cess to sensitive information is a challenging problem. In this work, we answer
017
               this question: “given limited sensitive information, can we learn a represen-
018
               tation to be fair w.r.t. varying sensitive groups?” To achieve this, we propose
019            a novel two-step framework. We first learn a disentangled representation by em-
020            ploying Non-linear Independent Component Analysis (Nonlinear ICA). Second,
021            we remove sensitive information in the latent space to obtain fair representation.
022            The learned representation can be easily adapted to be fair w.r.t different sensitive
023            groups and to be used for different downstream tasks without re-training. Among
024            the entire process, only a small portion of sensitive information is required in
025            the second step to learn a fair representation. We compare with methods that re-
026            quire different amounts of sensitive information on real-world image and tabular
027
               datasets. We empirically demonstrate the utility and flexibility of our approach,
               and our method is capable of achieving improved fairness results in various tasks.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Fairness in machine learning is a vital issue because more and more machine learning algorithms
033   are applied to high-risk tasks, such as loan granting, medical applications, and university admission
034   (Corbett-Davies & Goel, 2018). In many cases, machine learning models inevitably encode human
035   discrimination due to the distribution of data.
036   Currently, fair representation learning has been proven to be a good way to address fairness concerns
037   (Zemel et al., 2013). Specifically, a fair representation encodes the input features in the latent space
038   and reduces the impact from sensitive groups (Liu et al., 2022; Quadrianto et al., 2019; Kehrenberg
039   et al., 2020; Park et al., 2021). Various approaches have been explored, including adversarial training
040   (Edwards & Storkey, 2015; Madras et al., 2018), regularization (Tran et al., 2021; Sarhan et al., 2020;
041   Quadrianto et al., 2019), reweighting (Krasanakis et al., 2018; Yan et al., 2022; Li & Liu, 2022), and
042   disentanglement (Park et al., 2021; Locatello et al., 2019a; Sarhan et al., 2020), and distribution
043   mapping (Balunovic et al., 2021; Cerrato et al., 2022)
044   One problem with many existing methods is that they require all sensitive information when learning
045   a fair representation. Collecting sensitive information is expensive in the real world and even raises
046   privacy concerns. This limits the application of many of the existing methods in practice.
047
      Two strategies have been proposed to tackle this challenge: methods that require no sensitive infor-
048   mation and methods that require a part of sensitive information. Distributionally Robust Optimiza-
049   tion (DRO) (Hashimoto et al., 2018) and Adversarial Representation Learning (ARL) (Lahoti et al.,
050   2020), are designed to function without accessing any sensitive information. These approaches fo-
051   cus on optimizing the worst-performing sample subsets to improve group fairness. However, as the
052   authors point out, such an approach may get stuck in optimizing noisy data, leading to a significant
053   drop in performance (Hashimoto et al., 2018). Moreover, it introduces a strong constraint on the
      objective function by only optimizing the worst-performing samples. Chai et al. (2022) introduced a

      novel methodology employing a knowledge distillation framework to mitigate fairness issues with-
055   out relying on sensitive information. However, this strategy requires a larger teacher network to
056   overfit the training set, resulting in parameter inefficiency. Grari et al. (2021) proposed a method
057   utilizing a Bayesian variational autoencoder to infer sensitive information. It demands substantial
058   expert knowledge of the data. Moreover, the effectiveness of this method significantly depends upon
059   the accuracy of the sensitive attribute proxies.
060
      The alternative approach leverages partial sensitive information, it can be categorized into two
061   groups: optimization with partial dataset and pseudo-labeling-based methods. Fair models with
062   Bilevel Optimization (BiFair) (Ozdayi et al., 2021), and Just Train Twice (JTT) (Liu et al., 2021)
063   are two exemplar methods of the first category. BiFair assigns a weighted training set to achieve
064   utility and fairness, where the weight of each training sample is learned through a partially sensitive
065   labeled dataset. JTT optimizes the worst performance samples by retraining on up-weighted train-
066   ing samples. They held out a validation set to tune the up-weight factor. However, BiFair relies
067   on bilevel optimization, which is inherently challenging to resolve effectively (Beck et al., 2023).
068   JTT needs training twice, which can become prohibitively expensive for large-scale training, and
069
      the performance of JTT is sensitive to hyperparameters (Liu et al., 2021). Another type of approach
      is through pseudo-labeling (Nam et al., 2022; Jung et al., 2022). These methods first train a network
070
      with a limited amount of sensitive information and subsequently assign pseudo-sensitive labels to
071
      the dataset. Then they use robust training techniques to ensure equitable performance across dif-
072   ferent sensitive groups. However, the accuracy of pseudo-sensitive labeling significantly influences
073   overall performance. It poses considerable risks in high-stakes tasks, where errors in labeling could
074   lead to substantial consequences.
075
      Additionally, another challenge is that representations lack the flexibility to adapt fairness across dif-
076
      ferent attributes. For example, while current methods enable the learning of representations that are
077
      fair for gender, these cannot be easily modified to achieve fairness with respect to race. Retraining
078   such representations is often expensive.
079
080   Creager et al. (2019) first proposed the concept of flexible representation learning. Subsequent
081
      studies (Usama & Chang, 2022; Kehrenberg et al., 2020) extended this idea by advocating for the
      learning of representations that can accommodate various sensitive attributes. They enumerate all
082
      potential sensitive attributes as candidates and subsequently learn two vectors to separately encode
083
      non-sensitive and sensitive information. The non-sensitive vector is then employed to train a fair
084   classifier. Although this method has improved flexibility, it is necessary first to clarify all possible
085   sensitive groups. Retraining the representation may still be required if the target sensitive informa-
086   tion is not considered from the outset.
087
      In summary, existing methodologies that address limited sensitive information encounter optimiza-
088
      tion challenges or risks for predicting sensitive attributes. Moreover, they often lack the necessary
089
      flexibility. While there are methods designed to enhance flexibility, they need all sensitive attributes
090   during training. This requirement not only imposes a significant burden on collecting sensitive in-
091   formation but also necessitates re-training whenever a new sensitive attribute emerges.
092
093
      In this work, we propose a new solution to address the above-mentioned challenges in terms of
      flexibility and ease of use in fairness representation learning. We utilize a generative disentangled
094
      representation learning method to map the input into each independent latent space. The learn-
095
      ing process is guided by Nonlinear Independent Component Analysis (Nonlinear ICA), which is
096   theoretically identifiable—capable of uncovering the true generative variables. We postpone the dis-
097   cussion of how Nonlinear ICA differs from other disentanglement learning approaches to Section
098   3.1. After learning the disentangled representation, we identify dimensions correlated with sensitive
099   information by calculating differences in conditional distributions. This disentangled representation
100   can then adapt to meet fairness requirements across various sensitive groups. Notably, the procedure
101   for learning fair representations requires only a small portion of sensitive information. Furthermore,
102   the generative nature of the latent vectors allows for straightforward manipulation, facilitating image
103   modification and generation with altered labels.
104   We summarize our contributions as follows:
105
106          • Our method learns a flexible and fair representation, without presetting sensitive informa-
107            tion. Thus, there is no need to retrain the representation and it can be easily adapted to be
               fair with varying sensitive groups.

110          • Our method is versatile, enabling a range of downstream tasks including fair classification

115   2   R ELATED W ORK
116   Fairness with limited demographics. Several approaches have been proposed to mitigate bias
117   when sensitive information is limited. BiFair (Ozdayi et al., 2021) dynamically adjusts weights for
118   data points to achieve both good utility and low bias for the model. Just Train Twice (JTT (Liu et al.,
119   2021)) first trains an ERM model, then trains with unweighted error data points to boost performance
120   in the worst-performing group. Spread Spurious Attribute (SSA (Nam et al., 2022)) utilizes partially
121   labeled sensitive information to infer sensitive labels in the training set, subsequently employing this
122   pseudo-sensitive information to train a robust model. Similarly, Confidence-based Group Label as-
123   signment (CGL (Jung et al., 2022)) uses pseudo-labeling but differs in its approach. A sensitive
124
      label classifier is trained to assign pseudo-sensitive labels based on confidence levels. CGL also in-
      corporates a robust training method to mitigate bias in the model. FairLisa (Liu et al., 2024) utilizes
125
      an adversarial debiasing approach, sequentially training a debiasing filter and discriminator. How-
126
      ever, a recent study (Han et al., 2023) shows that “adversarial debiasing methods exhibit instability.”
127   In contrast to existing methods, our approach avoids adversarial training, thereby enhancing stabil-
128   ity. Moreover, our learned representations can be adapted to promote fairness for various sensitive
129   attributes without necessitating re-training.
130
      Flexible fair representation learning. Flexible fair representation learning is a methodology de-
131
      signed to ensure fairness across various sensitive groups at test time, as first introduced by (Creager
132
      et al., 2019). They utilized a variational autoencoder to segregate non-sensitive and sensitive in-
133   formation into distinct latent vectors. The sensitive latent vector is specifically designed to encode
134   diverse sensitive attributes, enhancing the model’s flexibility. These vectors are trained to ensure
135   minimal mutual information. Subsequently, the non-sensitive latent vector is employed to train fair
136   classifiers. Aligning with this concept, Usama & Chang (2022) employed Maximum Mean Dis-
137   crepancy (MMD) in VAE to ascertain the independence of the non-sensitive latent vector from the
138   sensitive attribute. Similarly, Kehrenberg et al. (2020) proposed a flow-based model connected with
139   an adversarial mechanism to reduce sensitive information leakage during encoding. Our work differs
140   from these methodologies in two critical aspects: (1) Existing methods necessitate the enumeration
141
      of all sensitive attributes during training. In contrast, our approach eliminates this requirement, of-
      fering a significant advantage in adaptability. (2) Creager et al. (2019) and Usama & Chang (2022)
142
      require the availability of all sensitive attributes during training, and Kehrenberg et al. (2020) re-
143
      quires a training set where the mutual information between the target label and sensitive information
144   is minimized. Our method efficiently works with a limited subset of sensitive labels, enhancing
145   flexibility and significantly strengthening privacy protections.

165   The objective of our work is to learn a fair and disentangled representation. Non-linear Independent
166   Component Analysis (nonlinear ICA) is widely used in the field of representation learning due
167   to its theoretical guarantees of identifiability, which ensures the uniqueness of the representation
168   (Hyvärinen et al., 2023). We leverage the principles of nonlinear ICA and expand them within
169   the realm of fair representation learning. Practically, the flow-based model, as an invertible neural
170
      network, aligns perfectly with the requirements of nonlinear ICA. In this section, we first introduce
      the preliminaries of our work, followed by presenting our approach.

174   3.1   P RELIMINARIES
175
176
      Nonlinear ICA in disentanglement The objective of nonlinear ICA is to uncover the hidden
177
      generative processes that influence the observed data and to separate mixed signals into their inde-
178
      pendent sources. The nonlinear ICA decomposes an observed variable into a mixing function and
179   independent components. Specifically, we assume that an observed data x is generated by a gener-
180   ating latent vector zgen through a mixing function f , where zgen is unobservable. Nonlinear ICA
181   estimates zgen by learning an invertible mixing function f from x, such that the learned represen-
182   tation z = f −1 (x) is close to zgen . The mixing function f maps the latent representation z to the
183   input observed data x: f : z 7→ x, and since f is invertible, we also have: f −1 : x 7→ z.
184
      Hyvarinen et al. (2019) and Khemakhem et al. (2020) give a theoretical analysis that by providing
185
      some auxiliary variables, the nonlinear ICA is able to find a latent variable up to a transformation
186
      of the generating latent variable. Auxiliary variables can be time structures, class labels, or other
187   variables that latent variables are conditioned on. Sorrenson et al. (2020) further demonstrates that
188   the representation estimated by a flow-based model can be a translation and scaling of the generating
189   latent vector.
190
      Comparing other Disentanglement method. Extensive research has been conducted on learning
191
      disentangled representations, utilizing methods such as probabilistic component analysis (Tipping
192
      & Bishop, 1999) and variational autoencoders (VAEs) (Kingma, 2013). The majority of these stud-
193   ies concentrate on VAEs and employ regularized objectives to direct the learning process towards
194   disentangled latent representations (Khemakhem, 2022). Unfortunately, these methods lack iden-
195   tifiability guarantees. An identifiable method can uniquely estimate and reveal the true latent vari-
196   ables. The absence of identifiability results in arbitrary representation, where the representation may
197   alias the true latent variable (Hyvärinen et al., 2023). Consequently, the learned latent representa-
198   tion might couple sensitive information with target information, posing significant challenges for
199   debiased learning. Additionally, Locatello et al. (2019b) demonstrate that without guaranteed iden-
200   tifiability, the disentanglement of latent representations is highly sensitive to random seeds, making
201
      it less suitable for downstream tasks. Fortunately, nonlinear ICA provides a theoretical foundation
      that ensures identifiability (Mathieu et al., 2019; Locatello et al., 2019b; Rolinek et al., 2019). For-
202
      mally, Theorem 1 in (Hyvarinen et al., 2019) states that under the assumptions of invertibility for
203
      the mixing function, existing of auxiliary variable, and smoothness for the density function of z, the
204   function estimated by nonlinear ICA yields a consistent estimator of the demixing function. This
205   theorem suggests the applicability of the identifiability property of nonlinear ICA to recover the
206   generative latent variables zgen .
207
      Flow-based model implements nonlinear ICA. A flow-based model is a type of generative model,
208
      which uses an invertible neural network (INN) to transform the input probability distribution into
209
      an easy-to-sample probability distribution. Concretely, the input data x is generated from the latent
210   representation z through the function f . The function f is a composite function consisting of several
211   simple and invertible functions, f = f1 ◦ ... ◦ fk , where k is the number of composite functions. By
212   dedicated design, the inverse of f and the log-determinant of the Jacobian matrix can be easily com-
213   puted (Dinh et al., 2014; 2016; Kingma & Dhariwal, 2018). For a flow-based model, the relationship
214   z = f −1 (x) aligns with the setting of nonlinear ICA. Moreover, due to its inherent nonlinearity and
215   invertibility, the flow-based model can be a natural choice for constructing the mixing function in
      nonlinear ICA.

358   4.1   E XPERIMENT SETUP
359
360   We validate our approach on three datasets: UCI Adult (Becker & Kohavi, 1996) CelebA (Liu et al.,
361   2015), and UTKFace (Zhang & Qi, 2017). Each dataset is divided into Group-labeled and Group-
362
      unlabeled sets, in line with the limited demographics fairness research (Nam et al., 2022; Jung et al.,
      2022; Zhang et al., 2022). We provide an introduction to datasets in the Appendix B.
363
364   The comparison methods in our study are categorized based on their usage of sensitive information.
365   The first category, which does not utilize sensitive information, includes Empirical risk minimization
366   (ERM), DRO (Hashimoto et al., 2018), ARL (Lahoti et al., 2020); The second category, employing
367   limited sensitive information as Ours, comprises BiFair (Ozdayi et al., 2021), JTT (Liu et al.,
368
      2021), SSA (Nam et al., 2022), CGL (Jung et al., 2022), NIFR (Kehrenberg et al., 2020), FairLISA
      (Liu et al., 2024). Please refer to Appendix C and E for detailed implementation.
369
370   Metrics. We report results through two metrics: Accuracy and Fairness. For the fairness notion, we
371   apply demographic parity (DP) (Dwork et al., 2012), equal opportunity (EOp) (Hardt et al., 2016),
372   and equalized odds (EOd) (Hardt et al., 2016) to measure the degree of fairness.
373   Implementation.: For image datasets, we use the GLOW model as an encoder to learn the latent
374   representation. The GLOW model consists of 4 blocks for both CelebA and UTKFace datasets. Each
375   block contains 32 iterations of flow steps. We follow the origin paper of GLOW and implement one
376   flow step consisting of Actnorm, 1 × 1 convolution, and affine coupling. We resize the image to
377   64 × 64. The parameter of GLOW is optimized by Adam optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2014), with a
      learning rate 10−4 . We show the training details in section E of the Appendix.

470   Discussion The objective of the proposed method is to learn a debiased representation that encodes
471
      minimal sensitive information, rather than optimizing the representations with respect to a specific
      fairness metric. From Table 1-3, it is evident that the proposed method achieves optimal results in
472
      terms of DP and EOd. However, EOp may not always reach optimal levels. We have conducted
473
      experiments evaluating the MMD scores under different fairness notions to further investigate this
474   aspect. For DP, we compute the MMD score using p(z|a = 0) and p(z|a = 1). For EOp, we
475   compute the MMD score using p(z|y = 1, a = 0) and p(z|y = 1, a = 1). For EOd, we compute
476   MMD in the two scenarios, one is p(z|y = 1, a = 0) and p(z|y = 1, a = 1), the other is p(z|y =
477   0, a = 0) and p(z|y = 0, a = 1), then we take an average. The number of samples is the same when
478   computing the MMD score under different fairness considerations. We plot the top 10 MMD scores
479   under different notions in CelebA and UTK datasets, as shown in Figure 5.
480   Although the MMD score is computed under different fairness considerations, we observe that the
481   dimensions identified share similarities across different fairness notions. This suggests that by re-
482   moving the shared dimensions containing sensitive information, we can simultaneously improve all
483   three fairness metrics. However, EOp shows less overlap with the other two notions, as indicated by
484   the right three orange bars in Figure 5a that do not align with the other two bars. This discrepancy
485   leads to less comparable results in EOp. Nonetheless, it is important to note that modifying a single
      fairness metric is not the primary objective of this work.

500   4.2.3 D ISENTANGLED I MAGE GENERATION
501   In addition to fair classification tasks, Our approach can also be employed in the field of image
502   generation. We visualize the generated images by manipulating the latent dimension in z with
503   the highest correlation to a certain attribute. Concretely, we screen the dimension in the latent
504   space with the highest MMD score. We randomly feed samples into the INN model to extract the
505   latent representation. We alter the value of the identified most sensitive-related row and feed the
506   altered latent representation back to the inverse of the INN model to generate an image, as shown in
507   Figure 6. Our results indicate that a change in a single row of the latent space is sufficient to alter
508
      certain attributes of the synthesized image. The difference between our method and the GLOW for
      manipulating images with an attribute lies in the update direction. GLOW computes the direction
509
      using zpos − zneg , where zpos = E[z|att = 1] and zneg = E[z|att = 0], att is the attribute
510
      targeted to change. This approach potentially involves all dimensions in z. In contrast, our method,
511   which is theoretically identifiable, manipulates only one dimension in the latent space to demonstrate
512   semantically meaningful changes.

532   5   C ONCLUSION
533
      In this paper, we utilize the nonlinear ICA to find a theoretically guaranteed disentangled repre-
534   sentation and build a fair classifier based on the representation. The proposed method is sensitive
535   information free for learning disentangled latent representation, and requires a small portion of the
536   sensitive information to capture the sensitive related dimension in the latent space. Even if the sen-
537   sitive information changes, we can adapt the representation without retraining. We evaluate our
538   method on three real-world datasets and compare it with the state-of-the-art methods. We empiri-
539   cally show that our approach promotes group fairness in different tasks. Future research could be
      the generalization of the method, like exploring its application in fair clustering tasks.

821   For a fair comparison, we employ ResNet-101 (He et al., 2016), whose complexity is compara-
822   ble to that of GLOW, as the backbone model for both the CelebA and UTKFace datasets. We do
823   not incorporate pre-trained weights in our implementation. We resize each image to 64 × 64, and
824   augmentation techniques are not employed. For the baseline implementation, we turn all hyperpa-
825   rameters based on the optimal accuracy and fairness trade-off as introduced in section 4.1 on the
826   group-labeled dataset.
827   ERM: For both CelebA and UTKFace datasets, the ERM model is trained by utilizing a cross-
828   entropy loss function. We use Adam optimizer to update the parameters of the network, with a
829   learning rate of 1 × 10−4 . We train the ERM model for 30 epochs with batch size 128.
830
      DRO: In the implementation of Distributionally Robust Optimization (DRO), we employ the iden-
831
      tical backbone model as utilized in the ERM model. We turn the hyperparameter η ∈ {0.1, 0.2, 0.3}
832   to control the threshold of the loss for optimization. We select η = 0.2 as the optimal setting for
833   both the CelebA and UTKFace datasets.
834
835
      ARL: For the Adversarial Representation Learning (ARL) approach, an auxiliary network is neces-
      sary. Following the original author’s suggestion, we implement this using a smaller-scale network,
836
      specifically selecting ResNet-18 as the adversarial network without loading pre-trained weights. The
837
      auxiliary network is optimized using Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 10−5 .
838
839   JTT For the Just train twice (JTT) method, the backbone model is the same as the ERM model.
840   For the CelebA dataset, we follow the original paper’s implementation, setting the hyperparameter
841
      λup = 50 and T = 1. For the UTKFace dataset, we adjust these hyperparameters to λup = 20 and
842
      T = 10. The initial training phase utilizes the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 10−4 . For the
      second training phase, we use the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 10−4 and a weight decay
843
      of 10−4 as suggested in the paper (Liu et al., 2021).
844
845   BiFair: In implementing the BiFair method, we maintain the same model architecture as utilized
846   in the baseline models. For the CelebA and UTKFace datasets, we set the hyperparameters with
847   Tin = 80, Tout = 20, and λ = 0.8. We use the Adam optimizer unchanged as the official code
848
      (Ozdayi et al., 2021). The inner optimizer is configured with a learning rate of 10−3 , and the learning
      rate for the outer optimizer is set at 10−4 .
849
850   SSA: In the implementation of the SSA, we utilize the ResNet-101 architecture for both the pseudo-
851   sensitive label assignment and the robust optimization phases. During the pseudo-sensitive labeling
852   phase, the Adam optimizer is employed with a learning rate of 10−3 , and we train 50 epochs. We set
853   τgmin = 0.95 as the same as the official implementation (Nam et al., 2022); For the robust training
854
      phase, we adopt the Group-DRO method for robust optimization, following the authors’ recommen-
      dation. We use Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 10−4 , l2 regularization 10−4 , and training
855
      for 50 epoch.
856
857   CGL: The CGL method encompasses two training phases. we employ ResNet 101 for both stages
858   as recommended in the original paper (Jung et al., 2022). We utilize the officially released code
859   to implement the CelebA and UTKFace datasets. We use the Adam optimizer with a learning rate
860
      of 10−4 . In the second phase, for consistency in comparison, we adopt Group-DRO as the robust
      training technique, aligning with the approach used in the SSA.
861
862   NIFR: The NIFR, a flow-based model like ours, is implemented using the GLOW (Kingma &
863   Dhariwal, 2018) model as recommended in the paper (Kehrenberg et al., 2020). For latent encoding,
      we adhere to the official code, selecting 12 dimensions in z to represent sensitive information and

906
      tation for downstream tasks. We expect a single representation should be adaptable to meet the
      requirements of various tasks while protecting different sensitive attributes. Therefore, the selection
907
      of u is critical. As discussed in Section 3.2, we extend our justification to the task of protecting the
908
      attribute ‘Young’. The Pearson correlation coefficients and balance ratio for this are shown in Figure
909   10. We shadowed the attribute that served as the auxiliary variable. It is clearly observable that the
910   selected attribute still fulfills the criteria. Therefore, this guarantees that the learned representation
911   is capable of disentangling different sensitive information across the latent dimensions. The disen-
912   tanglement effect is demonstrated in Section 4.2.3, where two distinct dimensions capture gender
913   and age information.

940   Image Dataset For the CelebA and UTKFace datasets, we utilize GLOW to implement the in-
941   vertible neural network that learns the representations introduced in Section 3.2. The foundational
942
      architecture of the GLOW model consists of a sequence of flow layers. Each flow layer consists of
      an Actnorm layer, a 1 × 1 convolution layer, and an affine coupling. For the CelebA and UTKFace
943
      datasets, we select the hyperparameters for the depth of the flow, K = 32, and the number of levels,
944
      L = 4.
945
946   For the CelebA dataset, we select the auxiliary variable u = {High Cheekbones, Mouth Slightly
947   Open, Straight Hair, Oval Face}. Each sample is associated with a corresponding auxiliary label,
948
      yielding a total of 24 = 16 possible combinations of auxiliary variables. It should be noted that
      a large number of auxiliary variables is generally necessary to achieve identifiability, according to
949
      Khemakhem et al. (2020). However, Sorrenson et al. (2020) observed that the identifiability property
950
      can still be maintained with a smaller number of auxiliary variables, indicating that a large number is
951   not a necessary condition. For the hyperparameter λ, we consider the set λ ∈ [0.25, 0.5, 1, 1.25, 1.5].
952   The optimal value of λ is selected to maximize the objective function defined as Acc × (1 − EOd)
953   within the group-labeled dataset. Specifically, for the CelebA dataset, the optimal λ is determined
954   to be 1.5.
955
      For UTKFace dataset, the auxiliary variable u is determined based on age. Specifically, we catego-
956
      rize the age labels into four groups: the first group contains ages from [0, 20), the second group from
957   [20, 40), the third from [40, 60), and the fourth includes ages [60, 116]. Within each batch, samples
958   are grouped based on u, and we compute statistics of each group in the latent space, minimizing the
959   objective function as defined in Eq 2. The network parameters are updated using the Adam opti-
960   mizer with a learning rate of 1 × 10−4 , and the model is trained for 200 epochs. The hyperparameter
961   λ = 1 in the UTKFace dataset.

      For the image dataset in the fair classification task, we employ a single-layer perceptron, which
963   proves sufficient for performing the classification. This classifier is optimized using an SGD op-
964   timizer with a learning rate of 0.09. In task transitions, such as within the CelebA dataset where
965   the objective shifts to predicting ‘blonde hair’ while protecting gender information, we apply the
966   method outlined in Section 3.2. This requires only retraining the single-layer perceptron.

       propose a flow-based model leveraging non-linear ICA for fair representation, mitigating biases
980

981
       generative model, our methods need a relatively large-scale dataset to learn a high-quality latent
