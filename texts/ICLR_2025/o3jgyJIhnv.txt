009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            In this paper, we propose PADriver, a novel closed-loop framework for personal-
012            ized autonomous driving (PAD). Built upon Multi-modal Large Language Model
013            (MLLM), PADriver takes streaming frames and personalized textual prompts as
014            inputs. It autoaggressively performs scene understanding, danger level estimation
015            and action decision. The predicted danger level reflects the risk of the potential
016            action and provides an explicit reference for the final action, which corresponds
017            to the preset personalized prompt. Moreover, we construct a closed-loop bench-
018
               mark named PAD-Highway based on Highway-Env simulator to comprehensively
               evaluate the decision performance under traffic rules. The dataset contains 250
019
               hours videos with high-quality annotation to facilitate the development of PAD
020
               behavior analysis. Experimental results on the constructed benchmark show that
021            PADriver outperforms state-of-the-art approaches on different evaluation metrics,
022            and enables various driving modes.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   In real-world driving, users may prefer to choose different autonomous driving styles that are
028   determined based on user preferences (Jin et al., 2023a; Cui et al., 2024; Sha et al., 2023; Chen et al.,
029   2023). For example, passengers in a hurry may opt for a higher speed to reach their destination,
030   while others may prefer a slower yet more comfortable journey. Therefore, personalized autonomous
031   driving (PAD) is crucial, which aims to adjust the autonomous driving mode based on the drivers’
032   preferences. PAD can be assessed based on two key elements: speed and comfort. Speed is mainly
033
      related to the driver’s character, and is also limited by the traffic rules. Frequent lane changes,
      acceleration, and deceleration affect the driving experience of different drivers - comfort. Previous
034
      works for personalized driving (Ling et al., 2021; Schrum et al., 2024) are oriented towards individual
035
      users. They face challenges in generalizing the needs of different human groups.
036
037   Most existing end-to-end driving models (Chitta et al., 2021; Prakash et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2022;
038   Hu et al., 2023) are generally trained within a single driving mode by mimicking a single expert.
039
      However, PAD needs to be satisfied with different personalized modes, which makes it difficult
      for these methods to learn multiple distributions in the same model. Recently, knowledge-driven
040
      large language models (LLMs) (OpenAI, 2023b;a) show a set of capabilities for task reasoning, state
041
      prediction, and action planning based on textual detection results (Fu et al., 2023; Wen et al., 2023; Jin
042   et al., 2023a; Cui et al., 2024; Sha et al., 2023; Jin et al., 2023b; Chen et al., 2023), which shows the
043   potential for personalized planning with different modes. However, sparse textual representation may
044   make it difficult to fully provide enough scene information for decision and planning tasks. Therefore,
045   the Multi-modal Large Language Models (MLLMs) using the text and visual information (Radford
046   et al., 2021; Li et al., 2023; Alayrac et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023b;a; OpenAI, 2023c), are necessary
047   to enable comprehensive scene understanding. However, those MLLM-based studies (Wang et al.,
048   2023a; Shao et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023b) only make some short-term action adjustments according
049   to human notifications, without involving the driving modes switching.
050   To provide users with personalized driving experiences, we introduce PADriver, a personalized closed-
051   loop autonomous driving (AD) framework based on MLLMs. The PADriver is designed with three
052   modes: slow, normal, and fast. Users can directly change the mode by setting different personalized
053   prompts. To make an appropriate decision based on the surrounding environment, we introduce the
      concept of danger level that assigns a danger score to each potential action. PADriver takes the proper

054
      action associated with the output scene description, personalized prompt, and estimated danger level.
055   To ensure smooth planning, we also introduce the ego states queue that stores the ego states over the
056   past frames, serving as part of textual prompts. Moreover, we establish a new benchmark based on
057   the Highway-Env simulator (Leurent, 2018) to comprehensively evaluate the performance of different
058   closed-loop methods, which can avoid the expensive cost of real-world data collection and safety
059   evaluation. The benchmark PAD-Highway contains 250 hours data with high-quality annotation to
060   encourage the development of personalized driving.
061   Our PADriver has the following advantages compared to existing approaches: (1) Through the
062   personalized prompt, PADriver can perform different driving modes at any time, given the preference
063   of users. (2) PADriver serves as the first work to explicitly model the danger level of the corresponding
064   action among all existing MLLM-based methods. The estimated danger level provides an important
065   reference for the final decision, associated with driving mode.
066
      In summary, our contributions are listed as follows:
067
068           • We present a novel closed-loop driving system for personalized driving. The danger level is
069             further introduced to model the risk of potential actions.
070
              • A new benchmark PAD-Highway containing dataset and metrics is introduced to compre-
071
                hensively evaluate the performance of different closed-loop methods.
072
073           • Our approach with slow mode achieves state-of-the-art performance, while other modes
074             provide some potential choices for users.

078   1.1.1    E ND - TO - END AUTONOMOUS D RIVING M ODELS
079
      The evolution of autonomous driving technology is characterized by the development of data-driven
080
      approaches, especially for the end-to-end models (Chitta et al., 2021; Prakash et al., 2021; Wu
081
      et al., 2022), which simplify the overall process by directly translating sensory inputs into control
082   commands.
083
084   Some recent advances further propel the end-to-end driving by enhancing perception, improving
085
      occlusion detection, and integrating multi-modal sensor information. ST-P3 (Hu et al., 2022) aligns
      temporal BEV features to construct a dense cost map, utilizing hand-crafted rules to derive the
086
      optimal planning trajectory. UniAD (Hu et al., 2023) integrates multiple scene prediction modules by
087
      employing task queries as the interfaces, which facilitate the connection of each prediction module
088   with a transformer decoder structure, leading to the final planning stage. VAD (Jiang et al., 2023)
089   employs a strategy to vectorize and regularize the planning trajectory alongside the driving scene,
090   thereby effectively incorporating vectorized scene information to enhance the planning process.
091   ThinkTwice (Jia et al., 2023b) uses a scalable decoder, incorporating the dense and spatial-temporal
092   priors to extract information from critical regions. This approach leverages the identified features to
093   improve the precision of coarse action predictions through meticulous refinement.
094
095   1.1.2    M ULTI - MODAL L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS
096
097   The advent of Large Language Models (LLMs), including Palm (Chowdhery et al., 2023), Vi-
098   cuna (Chiang et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2023), LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023a;b), and GPTs (Radford
099
      et al., 2018; 2019; OpenAI, 2023a;b), marks a pivotal shift in artificial intelligence research. Simul-
      taneously, the emergence of Multi-modal Large Language Models (MLLMs) like CLIP (Radford
100
      et al., 2021), BLIP-2 (Li et al., 2023), Flamingo (Alayrac et al., 2022), the LLaVA series (Liu et al.,
101
      2023b;a), and GPT-4V (OpenAI, 2023c) represents a substantial advancement in the field. These
102   models have extended the boundaries of traditional LLMs by incorporating sophisticated reasoning
103   abilities with the capability to process and interpret images, point clouds, and other modalities. Fur-
104   thermore, the LLaVA series (Liu et al., 2023b;a) introduce a visual instruction tuning method, which
105   has demonstrated superior performance in tasks requiring perception and spatial reasoning. These
106   models, trained on extensive and diverse datasets derived from the webs, have successfully injected
107   the common-sense knowledge applicable to a wide range of domains, such as robotics (Brohan et al.,
      2023) and autonomous driving.

      1.1.3    MLLM FOR I NTELLIGENT V EHICLES
109
110   Recent researches (Jin et al., 2023a; Xu et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023; Jin et al., 2023b; Cui et al., 2024)
111   have integrated intelligent agent with LLMs to enhance real-world performance, leveraging spatial
112   reasoning and perception capabilities. For instance, Talk2BEV (Dewangan et al., 2023) introduces
113   an innovative Bird’s-Eye View (BEV) representation, merging visual and semantic information to
114   facilitate Visual Question Answering (Visual QA), which encompasses spatial and visual reasoning
115
      tasks. Similarly, LiDAR-LLM (Yang et al., 2023) employs LLMs to process point clouds, addressing
      3D captioning, grounding, and QA tasks.
116
117   MLLMs are further extended to explore the planning and decision tasks (Liu et al., 2023c; Mao et al.,
118   2023; Sha et al., 2023). Drive-like-Human (Fu et al., 2023) reconsiders the constraints of existing
119   autonomous driving frameworks and proposes a paradigm shift through the integration of diverse LLM
120   APIs. Dilu (Wen et al., 2023) further combines reasoning and reflection modules to make informed
121
      decisions based on common-sense knowledge and the accumulated experience of GPTs. However,
      these methods utilize LLMs and rule-based priors to translate scenes into text, demonstrating the
122
      scene understanding and logical reasoning capability of LLMs. LMDrive (Shao et al., 2023) aims
123
      to integrate MLLMs with multi-modal sensor data, enabling the system to make decisions based
124   on a comprehensive understanding of both environment and natural language guidance. It also
125   integrates the alerts from human with navigation system. DriveMLM (Wang et al., 2023a) introduces
126   a closed-loop MLLM-based system for behavior planning in Carla simulators (Dosovitskiy et al.,
127   2017). It merges the driving regulations, user commands, and sensor inputs to guide the driving
128   decisions and provide explanations.
129
      Some previous approaches (Cui et al., 2024; 2023) based on LLMs are related with the concept of
130   PAD. Driven by the human commands, they frequently adjust the temporary actions. Though their
131   efforts show the potential of MLLMs or LLMs for personalized driving, our work has significant
132   differences with them. These methods primarily focus on addressing the preferences of specific users,
133   posing challenges to widespread application. In contrast, our PADriver integrates multiple driving
134   modes within a single MLLM-based framework to accommodate personalized and diverse driving
135   preferences for general scenes.

      2     M ETHODOLOGY
138
139
      In this section, we introduce PADriver, a novel framework designed for personalized autonomous
140
      driving, enabling closed-loop control of autonomous vehicles. First, we show the overall architecture
141   of our framework. Next, the textual prompts, which are input to the PADriver system, are presented
142   in detail. Lastly, we describe how the final action is made based on the danger level estimation.
143
144
      2.1     OVERALL A RCHITECTURE
145
146   As illustrated in Figure 1, our PADriver, is built upon the multi-modal large language models
147   (MLLMs) and evaluated on the closed-loop simulator Highway-Env. Given time step t, the current
148   Bird’s Eye View (BEV) frame It and textual prompts are input to the PADriver system. The BEV
149   frame is tokenized into vision tokens by the vision encoder (e.g., CLIP (Radford et al., 2021)).
150
      The vision tokens are then projected into the word embedding space for alignment through a linear
151
      layer. Simultaneously, the textual prompts, including the system prompt <SYSTEM>, personalized
152   prompt <PERSONALIZE>, and ego state prompt <EGO_STATE>, are tokenized by the large lan-
153   guage models(LLMs) tokenizer (e.g., LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023a;b)), which converts natural
154   language prompts into textual tokens. Vision tokens and textual tokens are concatenated together and
155   further input to the LLM. The LLM autoregressively generates scene descriptions <DESCRIPTION>,
156   danger level assessments <DANGER_LEVEL> for each possible action, and the final action deci-
157   sion <ACTION>. The final action is implicitly associated with both danger level estimation and
158   scene description. The overall process is illustrated in Figure 2.

      System Prompts:
192   The system prompts <SYSTEM> mainly include three components: 1) the basic environment descrip-
193   tions of the ego car and other cars, 2) the traffic rules to inform the model of actionable constraints,
194   which indicate when to take or avoid the corresponding action, and 3) other appropriate notices and
195   instructions that may help the system.
196   Personalized Prompts: The personalized prompts <PERSONALIZE>, designed to reflect the agent’s
197   driving preferences, are categorized into three distinct modes: slow, normal, and fast. The fast mode is
198   designed to prioritize speed while ensuring safety. Conversely, the slow mode represents the standard
199   setting, focusing on minimizing operation frequency. The normal mode is the most comfortable one,
200   achieving a good trade-off between the speed and safety.
201   Ego State Prompts: The ego state prompts <EGO_STATE> constitute a critical element of the
202   PADriver. Unlike prior works (Jia et al., 2023a) that rely on image-action pair logs, our strategy
203   utilizes a memory queue of ego states. The queue includes n historical states from preceding
204   frames, encompassing the velocity v and the coordinates of the ego vehicle (x, y). The ego state
205   queue can be represented as {(vt , xt , yt ), (vt−1 , xt−1 , yt−1 ), . . . , (vt−n , xt−n , yt−n )}. The ego state
206   queue is updated at each time-step and is readily accessible through the Inertial Measurement Unit
207   (IMU) (Faisal et al., 2019) of the ego vehicle.

      2.4   M ODEL T RAINING
276
277   Like the training schedule of previous MLLMs, our PADriver follows two-stage training process:
278   Pretraining and Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT). The capability of MLLMs usually benefits from the
279   pretraining on a large amount of data. Such large-scale pretraining enables MLLMs to acquire a
280   general understanding of the ego vehicle’s surroundings as well as the risk estimation of different
281   actions. Additionally, the SFT process is designed to refine the MLLM’s ability to comprehend the
282   wide array of human decision-making knowledge in driving scenarios. MLLMs can be trained to
283   imitate the personalized behaviors using a small amount of SFT data. Therefore, we first pretrain
284
      our framework with large-scale data and then apply SFT with a small amount of data. Details are
      provided in Section 4.1.

287   3     PAD-H IGHWAY B ENCHMARK
288
289   We introduce a comprehensive benchmark to conduct fair comparison. The benchmark is built based
290   on the Highway-Env simulator (Leurent, 2018) for closed-loop evaluation. First, we define the
291   detailed settings for the closed-loop evaluation, ensuring reproducibility. Subsequently, we introduce
292   a giant driving decision dataset PAD-Highway to encourage the development of PAD analysis. Finally,
293   we present a set of evaluation metrics designed to thoroughly assess performance from multiple
294   perspectives, including efficiency, safety, and comfort.

296   3.1   BASIC S ETTING
297
298   The simulated scene is generated with a duration of 30 seconds and a frequency of 10 Hz. The
299   experiment is deployed within a simulated environment featuring a four-lane motorway. Each scene
300   totally has 30 vehicles with a vehicle density of 2.0, and the maximum speed of each vehicle is 30 m/s
301   for regulatory issues of traffic rules. Except for the aforementioned specifications, all experimental
302
      conditions follow the default configurations provided by the Highway-Env simulator.
303   The Highway-Env simulator can theoretically generate an infinite number of scenes given the seeds.
304   For evaluation, we select the foremost 30 continuous seed numbers, ranging from 0 to 30, differing
305   from Dilu (Wen et al., 2023) that randomly selects 10 seeds from a manually predetermined set. The
306   remaining seeds are used to generate the scenes for training data collection.
307
308   3.2   PAD-H IGHWAY DATASET
309
310   We introduce a comprehensive data generation pipeline to construct PAD-Highway dataset. We
311   utilize the Highway-Env simulator to collect Bird’s Eye View (BEV) frames, as well as the states,
312   which include the action, speed, and coordinates of both the ego and other vehicles for each frame.
313   Our methodology is divided into two principal collection modes: the rule-based and human-based
314
      collection modes.
315   Rule-based Part: This collection accelerates the process of accumulating large amounts of data
316   through either random or rule-based actions. The rule is set based on the current states (coordinates
317   and speed) of all cars. For example, if the ego car is too close to the front car, the first priority is
318   to change the lane to avoid a crash when the side lane is safe. The second priority is to slow down.
319   Moreover, the past actions of ego car are also considered, to avoid frequent lane and speed changes.
320   Human-based Part: The human-based collection mode focuses on acquiring data reflective of
321   human driving behaviors to enhance the LLM’s comprehension of diverse human decision-making
322   strategies in driving. We develop an annotation system for human-based collection and arrange nearly
323   20 people to drive the ego car using the Highway-Env simulator, to collect the decisions of human
      driving behaviors. During the collection stage, each person first finishes the 30s driving and then

526   5    C ONCLUSION
527
528
529   In this paper, we introduce PADriver, a closed-loop framework for personalized autonomous driving
530   that leverages a Multi-modal Large Language Models (MLLMs). By processing streaming frames,
531   ego states, and personalized textual prompts, PADriver effectively carries out scene understanding,
532   danger level estimation, and action decision-making in an autoregressive manner. Furthermore, a
533   closed-loop benchmark PAD-Highway is established, which uses the Highway-Env simulator to
534   thoroughly assess the planning performance of our approach. Our experimental results on this
535   benchmark demonstrate that PADriver effectively achieves personalized driving with three modes:
536
      fast, normal, and slow, while achieving great driving performance across various evaluation metrics.
537   Limitation and Future Work: PADriver is currently based on Highway-Env scenes. We plan to
538   extend it to other simulators, such as WayMax (Gulino et al., 2023) and CARLA (Dosovitskiy et al.,
539   2017), or practical autonomous driving scenarios. Additionally, PADriver is based on the observation
      of Bird’s Eye View (BEV) scenes, which can be extended to surround-view images or point clouds.
