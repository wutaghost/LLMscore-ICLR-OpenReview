009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Federated learning (FL) is crucial for collaborative model training, yet it faces
012            significant challenges from data heterogeneity, particularly label skews across
013            clients, where some classes may be underrepresented or absent entirely. In one-shot
014            FL, where clients only communicate with the server once, this problem becomes
015            even more challenging. Recent solutions propose incorporating open-set learning
016
               (OSL) to tackle this issue by detecting unknown samples during inference, but
               current methods like FedOV lack adaptability to varying client data distributions. In
017
               this paper, we provide a theoretical analysis proving that improving OSL algorithms
018
               can effectively address label skews in one-shot FL, since one-shot FL is learnable
019            through good OSL algorithms regardless of label skews. We also empirically
020            evaluate state-of-the-art OSL algorithms and identify their limitations. Based on
021            these insights, we propose FedAdav, an adaptive algorithm that combines OSL
022            signals to significantly improve ensemble accuracy in one-shot FL under label
023            skews. Through extensive experiments, we demonstrate that exploring better OSL
024            is key to overcoming label skew challenges in federated learning.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Federated learning (FL) (McMahan et al., 2016) enables collaborative model training across dis-
029
      tributed clients without the need to share raw data. While traditional FL requires multiple commu-
030   nication rounds to achieve strong model performance, one-shot FL (Guha et al., 2019) reduces this
031   overhead by limiting communication to a single round. This efficiency makes one-shot FL particularly
032   useful for applications such as model hubs (Vartak, 2016), where pre-trained models are shared and
033   aggregated to form powerful ensembles across multiple data sources (Wang et al., 2023).
034
      A major challenge in one-shot FL is label skew (Li et al., 2021b), where clients have highly imbalanced
035
      or non-overlapping class distributions. In practice, this occurs when some clients have abundant
036   data for certain classes, while other clients have limited or no data for those classes. For example,
037   in healthcare, disease prevalence varies significantly across regions, causing models trained on
038   localized data to be biased towards certain classes. Label skew can severely affect the performance
039   of one-shot FL, as the lack of iterative communication rounds prevents the model from adjusting to
040   these imbalances. Without addressing this issue, the global model may fail to generalize well across
041   the combined data from clients.
042   To address the problem of label skew, recent research has applied open-set learning (OSL) to one-shot
043   FL. OSL allows models to classify known classes while detecting and rejecting out-of-distribution
044   (OOD) samples that the model hasn’t encountered during training (Salehi et al., 2021; Ye et al., 2022).
045   This capability is especially valuable in applications like autonomous driving and healthcare, where
046   unknown situations can arise. For instance, FedOV (Diao et al., 2023) introduces an "unknown"
047   class by generating outliers from corrupted training data to handle unseen classes. While effective
048   in addressing label skew, FedOV only relies on a single OSL signal from corrupted data samples,
049   limiting its flexibility.
050   In this paper, we tackle the challenges posed by label skews in one-shot FL by exploring both
051   theoretical and empirical perspectives. Our theoretical analysis proves the learnability of one-shot
052   FL with OSL, emphasizing the importance of improved OSL algorithms for handling label skews.
053   Based on these insights, we propose FedAdav, a novel adaptive algorithm that combines multiple
      OSL signals to enhance one-shot federated ensemble accuracy. Through comprehensive experiments,

054
      we demonstrate that FedAdav outperforms state-of-the-art (SOTA) OSL algorithms under severe
055   label skew conditions.

             • Theoretical Contribution: We prove the learnability of one-shot FL ensembles with OSL.

      2.1   O NE -S HOT FL AND L ABEL S KEWS
076
      Federated learning (FL) enables training machine learning models across multiple clients without
077   requiring raw data to be shared (McMahan et al., 2016; Kairouz et al., 2019; Yang et al., 2019; Li
078   et al., 2019). In one-shot FL, clients communicate with the server only once, significantly reducing
079   communication costs compared to traditional multi-round FL (Guha et al., 2019). Applications
080   such as model hubs benefit from one-shot FL by allowing pre-trained models to be aggregated from
081   multiple data sources, creating a powerful ensemble (Vartak, 2016; Wang et al., 2023).
082
      However, label skews, where clients have very imbalanced or non-overlapping class distributions,
083   pose a major challenge in one-shot FL. As each client trains on locally skewed data, the global
084   model is biased towards the dominant classes in each client, leading to poor generalization. This is
085   particularly problematic for one-shot FL, since the lack of multiple communication rounds prevents
086   the model from correcting imbalances or filling gaps in class distributions (Li et al., 2021b).
087
      To address the label skews in one-shot FL, FedOV (Diao et al., 2023) proposes to create an “unknown”
088
      class by generating corrupted versions of in-distribution data during local training, which are then
089   treated as outliers. Clean data and corrupted data are put into a batch for training. The model learns
090   to distinguish known classes from outliers, enhancing its ability to handle label skews in one-shot FL.
091   The loss function for FedOV is:

      2.2   O PEN -S ET L EARNING (OSL)
102
103   Traditional closed-set learning focuses on classifying known classes, but open-set learning (OSL)
104   extends this by enabling models to detect and reject out-of-distribution (OOD) samples (Salehi et al.,
105   2021; Ye et al., 2022). OSL is essential in safety-critical applications, such as autonomous driving,
106   healthcare, and cybersecurity, where unknown or unseen data can emerge during deployment. For
107   instance, a self-driving car may encounter objects or scenarios that are not part of the training set,
      and failing to correctly identify them as unknown can lead to dangerous consequences.


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Among popular OSL methods, maximum softmax probability (MSP) (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2017)
109   and maximum logit score (MLS) (Hendrycks et al., 2022a) are often used for scoring outliers.
110   However, these approaches primarily focus on OOD detection based on a single score function,
111   without leveraging additional signals or tasks.
112
      RotPred (Hendrycks et al., 2019b) is a self-supervised OSL method that adds a pretext task to help
113
      the model learn better feature representations. Specifically, RotPred requires the model to predict
114
      the rotation of input images (0°, 90°, 180°, or 270°). This task forces the model to extract more
115   meaningful features to improve its ability to detect outliers, especially in cases where the training
116   data is limited or imbalanced. The loss for RotPred is given by:

121
      Despite its effectiveness, RotPred can struggle on datasets where the rotations cannot be differentiated
122   from the original data, such as in the case of digits 0 and 1.
123
124   Existing OSL benchmarks primarily evaluate methods on static tabular and image datasets. For
125
      example, KNN-based outlier detection is tested on tabular data (Campos et al., 2016), while both
      shallow and deep anomaly detection methods are benchmarked in works like (Ruff et al., 2021).
126
      Recent efforts like ADBench (Han et al., 2022) and OpenOOD (Yang et al., 2022) test OSL and OOD
127
      detection on datasets such as MNIST, CIFAR-10, and ImageNet.
128
129   However, these benchmarks focus on score functions and metrics like AUROC and FPR@95, which
130   are effective in traditional settings but may not suit one-shot FL with label skews. In one-shot FL,
131
      where client data distributions are imbalanced, these standard metrics can struggle. Setting thresholds
      for OOD detection becomes critical, yet existing methods such as extreme value theory (EVT) (Coles
132
      et al., 2001) and percentile-based thresholds (Liang et al., 2017) are under-explored in federated
133
      settings. Thus, prior OSL benchmarks, while useful, may not fully capture the complexities of OSL
134   in one-shot FL with label skews.

      2.3   R ELATED T HEORETICAL S TUDIES
137
138   Prior FL theoretical works usually prove the convergence of specific algorithm (Wang et al., 2021;
139   Mohri et al., 2019), personalized FL (Deng et al., 2020; Hanzely et al., 2020) or incentive mechanisms
140   (Kang et al., 2019). One-shot FL theory is under-explored. We only find one recent theoretical work
141   (Jhunjhunwala et al., 2023) on one-shot FL. However, its focus is on Fisher Information and the
142   convergence proof is restricted to sufficiently wide two-layer ReLU network. Our proof focuses on
143   one-shot FL open-set ensemble and does not depend on network structure. Another related work
144
      (Zhu et al., 2023) studies multi-round FL on OSL tasks of medical applications, while ours focuses
      on applying OSL to address label skews in one-shot FL.
145
146   Mostly related to our work, the theoretical studies on class-incremental learning (CIL) (Kim et al.,
147   2022; 2023) show that OSL and CIL can improve each other, and CIL can be learned by OSL. CIL
148   is similar to one-shot FL in that the training set is divided into several tasks with different classes,
149   and the raw data of past tasks (except a few exemplars) are not accessible. However, there are three
150
      main differences when applying them to one-shot FL. (1) CIL assumes that the classes of each task
      do not overlap, while FL clients can have overlapping classes. (2) CIL learns from a sequence of
151
      tasks and can exploit information from stored exemplars or previous models, while in one-shot FL,
152
      the client cannot access any data or model of other clients. (3) The form of one-shot FL ensemble is
153   very different from the final model in CIL.

309   4     M ETHODS : A DAPTIVE OSL TO A DDRESS L ABEL S KEWS IN O NE -S HOT FL
310
311   Our theoretical findings reveal that improving open-set learning (OSL) is key to enhancing perfor-
312   mance in one-shot FL, particularly when dealing with label skews. To address this, we propose
313   FedAdav, a novel adaptive algorithm that integrates multiple OSL methods to overcome the limitations
314   of existing approaches.
315
      In this section, we first discuss two state-of-the-art OSL methods, FedOV and RotPred, which serve as
316   the foundation for FedAdav. Then, we describe how FedAdav adaptively combines these approaches
317   to effectively handle the challenges posed by label skews in one-shot FL.
318
319   4.1   F ED OV AND ROT P RED : BASELINE OSL M ETHODS
320
321   FedOV (Diao et al., 2023) is SOTA algorithm to tackle label skews in one-shot FL by incorporating
322   OSL. It works by generating outliers from corrupted in-distribution samples, which are then assigned
323   to an "unknown" class. This allows the model to distinguish between known and unknown classes,
      providing a way to handle the missing classes in the client datasets due to label skews. However,


                                                                    6
           Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
           FedOV relies heavily on these generated outliers as the sole signal for OOD detection, limiting its
325        adaptability in diverse real-world scenarios where outliers may exhibit varied characteristics.
326
327        RotPred (Hendrycks et al., 2019b) is another popular OSL method that improves outlier detection
328
           by adding a self-supervised task—predicting the rotation of input images. By forcing the model to
           predict one of four rotation angles (0°, 90°, 180°, or 270°), RotPred enhances the feature learning
329
           process, which aids in OOD detection. While RotPred has proven effective in certain contexts, it can
330
           struggle with datasets where the notion of rotation is ambiguous or uninformative.
331
332        Both methods have demonstrated strong results individually, but their limitation motivate the need for
333        a more flexible and adaptive approach.
334
335        4.2     F EDA DAV: F EDERATED A DAPTIVE VOTING
336
337        FedAdav builds on the strengths of both FedOV and RotPred by adaptively combining their respective
338        signals. The goal is to enhance outlier detection and classification accuracy in one-shot FL ensembles
339        by dynamically adjusting the use of these signals during training and inference.
340
           Training Procedure. Each client in FedAdav initially trains a local model using the sum of FedOV
341
           and RotPred loss functions. The combined loss is L = LFedOV + λLRotPred . LFedOV is the outlier
342
           detection loss from FedOV, which classifies in-distribution samples and corrupted outliers. LRotPred is
343        the self-supervised loss from RotPred, which predicts the rotation of input images. λ is the trade-off
344        parameter between two losses.
345
346
           The total loss is adaptively adjusted during training. After a given number of rounds Tcheck , if the
           rotation prediction loss LRotPred still exceeds a predefined threshold τ , FedAdav disables the RotPred
347
           component and continues training solely with FedOV. In such case, we assume that the rotation
348
           prediction loss is not a good indicator for the train dataset. Since rotation prediction is a 4-way
349        classification task, we set τ = 0.5. According to empirical results, this threshold can filter out
350        classes that cannot be predicted rotations, e.g., digit 0, 1, or classes containing different angles. This
351        adaptive training mechanism ensures that the model only uses the most relevant signals, avoiding the
352        computational overhead of training on tasks that do not contribute to improved performance.
353
           Inference Procedure. During inference, FedAdav uses a combination of FedOV and RotPred signals
354
           to make predictions. If the rotation prediction loss is high (indicating high uncertainty in predicting
355        the rotation), the sample is rejected as an outlier. Otherwise, the sample is processed by FedOV, and

382   Datasets. We experiment with three image classification datasets with increasing difficulty level:

386   #C = k assigns k unique labels for each client, while pk ∼ Dir(β) divides each class samples
387   using Dirichlet distribution.
388   Models. We experiment with two neural network architectures. In accordance with OpenOOD
389   (Yang et al., 2022), we use the LeNet for MNIST and ResNet-18 for CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100.
390
      Baselines. Besides the SOTA one-shot FL algorithm FedOV (Diao et al., 2023) and naive closed-set
391
      ensemble baseline, we select a total of 9 SOTA OSL algorithms from OpenOOD (Yang et al., 2022)
392
      to apply to the one-shot FL setting. Specifically, we select the top 10 OSL algorithms from CIFAR-10
393   and CIFAR-100 leaderboards, and remove those requiring external outlier examples or local data
394   embedding information to suit the FL settings.
395
396
      Hyper-parameters. By default, we use Adam optimizer with learning rate 0.001, and train 200 local
      epochs with batch size 64. For FedAdav, we set λ = 1, Tcheck = 50 and τ = 0.5 by default.

      CE+MLS (Hendrycks et al., 2022a), and 11) CE+ReAct (Sun et al., 2021). CE means normal training
      with cross entropy loss. MSP (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2017) is a naive baseline where the maximum

424   From extensive experiments, we make the following findings.
425
      In terms of overall accuracy. (1) To address label skews, it will be good to combine generated
426   outliers and self-supervised learning. FedAdav achieves the best accuracy in 5 out of the 6 settings,
427   and achieves the top 2 accuracy in all settings. FedAdav significantly improves the federated ensemble
428   accuracy compared to the other SOTA OSL algorithms. (2) Pure data augmentation is not suitable
429   to addressing label skews, although they can improve generalization in closed-set learning. PixMix
430   and RegMixup can be viewed as data augmentation methods. Unlike FedOV which regards the
431   destructed data as class “unknown”, PixMix and RegMixup view the augmented data as the seen
      class, which do not directly address the OSL task. (3) Pure post-processing methods (e.g., GEN, MLS


                                                                           8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      and ReAct) and LogitNorm are not suitable to addressing label skew settings in FL. Under scenarios
433   where the number of classes is limited, the model tends to be over-confident. Especially when there
434   is only one seen class, the closed-set model consistently outputs that class without capturing any
435   useful features, according to information bottleneck theory (Tishby & Zaslavsky, 2015). In such
436   cases, the performance of these methods can be bad, since the closed-set model itself forgets much
437   feature-related information.
438
      In terms of partition. In extreme label skew partitions, except FedAdav, FedOV and RotPred,
439   other explored OSL algorithms cannot perform well under such settings. In fact, many prior OSL
440   algorithms (Neal et al., 2018; Yoshihashi et al., 2019b; Perera et al., 2020b; Zhou et al., 2021) are not
441   designed and tested on datasets with one or two seen classes, thus the settings are under explored.
442
      In summary, some prior OSL algorithms cannot be well adapted to one-shot FL settings, especially
443
      when the number of classes is limited. PixMix provides marginal improvement (< 1%) over RotPred,
444
      LogitNorm and pure closed-set training. Algorithms trained with cross-entropy (CE) loss provide
445   marginal improvement over closed-set ensemble baseline. Thus, our proposed FedAdav combines
446   the advantages of prior algorithms, and finally achieves the SOTA federated ensemble accuracy.

475   6                   C ONCLUSION
476
477
      This paper explores the integration of open-set learning (OSL) into one-shot federated learning (FL)
478
      ensembles, with a particular focus on addressing the challenge of label skews. We provide both
479
      theoretical insights and empirical validation. Our theory proves that one-shot FL can be effectively
480   learned through well-designed OSL algorithms. Through extensive experiments on 11 state-of-the-art
481   OSL algorithms, we identify two key strategies for improving one-shot FL: generating outliers
482   and utilizing self-supervised tasks. Building on these findings, we introduce FedAdav, an adaptive
483   algorithm that combines these OSL techniques to substantially enhance ensemble accuracy under
484   label skew conditions. Our results demonstrate that combining multiple OSL signals can significantly
485   improve federated learning performance, providing a strong foundation for future research aimed at
      addressing label skews in FL.

      A    M ORE R ELATED W ORKS ON OSL
703
704

707
      We follow a recent survey (Salehi et al., 2021) to introduce the OSL works.
708   In prior studies, OSL has three slightly different settings: anomaly detection (AD), open-set recogni-
709   tion (OSR), and out-of-distribution (OOD) detection (Salehi et al., 2021). Their goals are similar
710   while the experimental settings are slightly different. AD regards one single class as known. OSR
711   generally trains on a few classes and evaluates both the test accuracy of seen classes and the unknown
712   detection capability between seen and unseen classes. OOD detection usually trains on a whole
713
      dataset and tests on other semantically different datasets. Under classification tasks, OSR and OOD
      detection become the same (Salehi et al., 2021). In summary, they share the same goal of distinguish-
714
      ing unknown samples from known samples. OSR and OOD detection have label supervision with an
715
      extra task of classifying seen classes. AD can be viewed as a special case of OSR and OOD detection
716   where only one seen class exists.
717
718   For AD, (Zhou & Paffenroth, 2017) utilizes the reconstruction loss of auto-encoder trained on normal
719
      samples as the outlier score. ALOCC (Sabokrou et al., 2018) combines reconstruction loss with
      GAN loss to utilize the semantic-level similarity instead of pixel-level distance to distinguish outliers.
720
      Mem-AE (Gong et al., 2019) selects sparse latent features to store unique features for reconstructing
721
      normal samples. Old-is-Gold (Zaheer et al., 2020) utilizes the low-epoch generator to generate low-
722   quality examples as outliers. DeepSVDD (Ruff et al., 2018) forces the latent features of auto-encoder
723   to form a hyper-sphere and computes the outlier score by the distance to the center. GT (Golan
724   & El-Yaniv, 2018) requires the model to predict which transformation is applied and regards the
725   prediction error as outlier score. CSI (Tack et al., 2020) applies contrastive learning to distinguish
726   shifted distribution from normal samples. Cutpaste (Li et al., 2021a) proposes cutting or scarring part
727   of the image as outlier samples.
728   For OSR and OOD, OpenMax (Bendale & Boult, 2016) utilizes the logits before softmax to estimate
729   the outlier score. G-OpenMax (Ge et al., 2017) trains GAN and interpolates embeddings between
730   known classes to generate outlier samples. OSRCI (Neal et al., 2018) generates outliers on the
731   embedding space with low probability of known classes. C2AE (Oza & Patel, 2019) uses auto-
732   encoder reconstruction error of each class to identify outliers and applies extreme value theory (EVT)
733   to set the threshold. CROSR (Yoshihashi et al., 2019a) proposes reconstructing the latent vector of
734   each layer to preserve the information to distinguish known and unknown samples. GDFR (Perera
735   et al., 2020a) integrates self-supervised learning with classification and inputs both the original and
736
      reconstructed image to extract richer features. PROSER (Zhou et al., 2021) regards the interpolation
      between known classes as outliers. OpenGAN (Kong & Ramanan, 2021) generates fake images
737
      between the embeddings of true samples and outlier examples. ODIN (Liang et al., 2017) discounts
738
      the logits during training and applies adversarial learning to pre-process the image to better distinguish
739   outliers. In (Lee et al., 2018), softmax is replaced by the distance to the center of each class. Another
740   work (Lee et al., 2017) trains GAN while maximizing the confidence entropy to generate outlier
741   samples in the low confidence region. OE (Hendrycks et al., 2019a) trains with auxiliary outlier
742   samples from other datasets to improve the capability of detecting outliers. (Hendrycks et al., 2019b)
743   finds out that self-supervised learning tasks can improve outlier detection. (Yu & Aizawa, 2019)
744   trains an ensemble of models and encourages diversity of outputs when training on outlier samples.
745   G-ODIN (Hsu et al., 2020) trains separate headers for the outlier detection and closed-set classifier.
746   (Serrà et al., 2019) observes that simpler OOD samples can lead to higher confidence and adds
747
      complexity measurement to the outlier score. Energy-based OOD (Liu et al., 2020) uses the energy
      function as the outlier score, which cares more about the maximum logit value. LogitNorm (Wei et al.,
748
      2022) normalizes the logits to address the over-confidence problem of deep neural networks. MOS
749
      (Huang & Li, 2021) divides the datasets into various semantic groups and trains an OSL model for
750   each group. GradNorm (Huang et al., 2021) utilizes the gradient with respect to uniform probability
751   distribution as the outlier score function. ReAct (Sun et al., 2021) limits the logits with an upper
752   threshold to alleviate the overconfident predictions. BATS (Zhu et al., 2022) proposes truncated
753   batch normalization to restrain the negative effects of extreme features. (Mirzaei et al., 2022) applies
754   diffusion model with early stopping to generate high-quality outlier examples. A recent work (Tao
755   et al., 2023) proposes to synthesize virtual outliers in latent space by sampling around the boundary
      of true samples.
