009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Although Neural Scene Flow Prior (NSFP) and its variants have shown remark-
012            able performance in large out-of-distribution autonomous driving, the underlying
013            explanation for their generalization capabilities remains unclear. To this end, we
014            analyze the generalization capabilities of NSFP via uniform stability and find that
015            it exhibits a generalization bound, which is inversely proportional to the number of
016
               point clouds. These findings provide solid theoretical evidence to explain the ef-
               fectiveness of NSFP in large-scale point cloud scene flow estimation tasks for the
017
               first time. To enhance practical scene understanding, we extend NSFP and pro-
018
               pose a multi-frame neural scene flow (MNSF) scheme, which extracts temporal
019            information across multiple frames. In this way, MNSF has better temporal con-
020            sistency than NSFP. Moreover, we theoretically analyze its generalization abilities
021            and demonstrate that it achieves a tight generalization bound with a convergence
022            rate similar to NSFP. Extensive experimental results on large-scale autonomous
023            driving Waymo Open and Argoverse datasets demonstrate that MNSF achieves
024            state-of-the-art performance. The code is attached to the submission.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Scene flow estimation stands out as a key endeavor for perception and understanding the 3D world in
029
      autonomous driving and robotics, aiming to determine motion fields within dynamic environments
030   based on RGB images or point clouds (Teed & Deng, 2020; Liu et al., 2019b; Wang et al., 2022b).
031   The existing point cloud scene flow consists of learning-based and optimization-based methods.
032   Most learning-based methods (Liu et al., 2019b; Zhang et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2023) demonstrate
033   superior performance on small-scale synthetic datasets (Menze & Geiger, 2015; Mayer et al., 2016),
034   but struggle to generalize effectively to large-scale real-world scenarios (Chodosh et al., 2024; Li
035   et al., 2023). In contrast, optimization-based methods (Li et al., 2021; 2023) show superior general-
036   ization performance in real-world autonomous driving scenarios, e.g., Waymo and Argoverse (Sun
037   et al., 2020; Chang et al., 2019).
038   As a classical optimization-based method, NSFP (Li et al., 2021) has demonstrated its strong ca-
039   pability to handle dense point clouds (about 150k+ points), showcasing remarkable generalization
040   capabilities in open-world perception scenarios (Najibi et al., 2022; Chodosh et al., 2024). In addi-
041   tion, FNSF (Li et al., 2023) employs a distance transform strategy (Rosenfeld & Pfaltz, 1966; Breu
042   et al., 1995) to significantly accelerate the optimization speed of NSFP without sacrificing its perfor-
043   mance on out-of-distribution (OOD) autonomous driving scenes. Thus, NSFP and FNSF emerge as
044
      potentially powerful and dependable methods. However, the exceptional performance of NSFP and
      FNSF in processing large-scale point clouds needs theoretical analysis and still remains an intuition
045
      or empirical finding. The lack of a deeper understanding of NSFP hinders further progress in the
046
      field of neural scene flow estimation.
047
048   To address this issue, we conduct a theoretical investigation into the generalization error of NSFP
049   through the framework of uniform stability (Bartlett & Mendelson, 2002; Bousquet & Elisseeff,
050
      2002). Our findings reveal that the upper bound of NSFP’s generalization error inversely correlates
      with the number of input point clouds. This analysis provides a foundational understanding of why
051
      NSFP excels in managing large-scale scene flow optimization tasks.
052
053   However, we can not further improve NSFP and FNSF by directly increasing the number of points,
      because the full point cloud in a frame (about 100k points) has already been used as the input. There-

054
      fore, we seek to exploit the valuable information from previous frames from a temporal perspective,
055   which improves the temporal consistency and overcomes the upper bound of point numbers in each
056   frame. In this way, we aim to improve the scene flow estimation (t → t+1) by using previous frames
057   (t-1 → t). Surprisingly, there appears to be a notable gap in research focused on utilizing such valu-
058   able temporal information for improving the two-frame point cloud scene flow estimations. Such
059   a gap is particularly unexpected, because the extensive body of research in optical flow estimation
060   (Wulff et al., 2017; Janai et al., 2018; Maurer & Bruhn, 2018; Liu et al., 2019a; Stone et al., 2021;
061   Hur & Roth, 2021; Mehl et al., 2023) have shown the importance of temporal information from
062   previous frames, even amidst rapid motion changes in optical flow.
063   In this paper, we propose a straightforward and efficient approach, namely MNSF, for estimating
064   scene flow by using multiple frames. Specifically, we employ two FSNF models to calculate the
065   forward (t → t+1) and backward (t → t-1) flows, respectively. These flows, naturally opposing in
066   direction, are then reconciled through a motion model that inverts the backward flow. In this way,
067   the inverted backward flow and forward flow are aligned in the same temporal direction. Further-
068   more, we introduce a temporal fusion module to encode these flows and predict the final flow. More
069
      crucially, we theoretically derive that the generalization error of MNSF is bounded, which guaran-
      tees the convergence of optimization. Experimental results on Waymo Open and Argoverse datasets
070
      show that MNSF outperforms FNSF by a large margin. We expect this study to provide analytical
071
      insights and encourage investigation into exploiting temporal information in scene flow estimation.

074   2    R ELATED WORK
075
076   Scene flow estimation. Scene flow estimation from 2D images has been extensively explored in
077   recent years (Teed & Deng, 2020; Menze & Geiger, 2015; Ma et al., 2019; Schuster et al., 2021;
078   Maurer & Bruhn, 2018; Hur & Roth, 2021; Jiang et al., 2019). On the other hand, researchers
079   estimate scene flow directly from 3D point clouds via full/self-supervised training schemes (Liu
080   et al., 2019b;c; Gu et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2020; Puy et al., 2020; Kittenplon et al., 2021; Wang
081
      et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2020; Vedder et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2022b; Li et al., 2022; Zhang et al.,
      2023; Peng et al., 2023; Lang et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2024). Specifically, these methods mainly
082
      extract point-based features and compute correspondences between two point clouds. Based on
083
      accurate correspondences, these methods achieve superior performance on synthetic KITTI Scene
084   Flow (Menze & Geiger, 2015) and FlyingThings3D (Mayer et al., 2016) datasets. However, they fail
085   to generalize to more realistic and larger autonomous driving scenarios (Pontes et al., 2020; Li et al.,
086   2021; Najibi et al., 2022; Dong et al., 2022; Jin et al., 2022; Chodosh et al., 2023), e.g., Waymo
087   Open (Sun et al., 2020) and Argoverse (Chang et al., 2019) datasets. In comparison, NSFP (Li et al.,
088   2021) uses a Multi-Layer Perception (MLP) to estimate the scene flow and demonstrates powerful
089   generalization ability in large-scale autonomous driving scenarios. More recently, FNSF (Li et al.,
090   2023) speeds up NSFP by using Distance Transform without sacrificing the performance.
091   Multi-frame optical flow. Extensive studies focus on using multi-frames to estimate optical flow
092   (Golyanik et al., 2017; Maurer & Bruhn, 2018; Ren et al., 2019; Schuster et al., 2021; Hur & Roth,
093   2021; Mehl et al., 2023). Ren et al. (2019) discovers that performance improvements are relatively
094   smaller when the frame number is more than three. In this way, these studies obtain more accurate
095   results by considering three consecutive frames, which achieves a compromise between temporal
096   information and efficiency (Wulff et al., 2017; Janai et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2019a; Stone et al.,
097   2021). Specifically, these methods aim to learn a motion model across different frames, because
098
      optical flow fields are temporally smooth and distributed around a low-dimensional linear subspace
      (Irani, 1999; Janai et al., 2018). The motion model can exploit valuable information and predict the
099
      motion field of the current frame based on previous frames. Then, a fusion module combines the
100
      previous and current predictions to estimate an accurate result in the current frame.

323   error between the estimated scene flow and the pseudo ground truth scene flow; (6) Inference time
      t(ms) measures the computation time for the scene flow estimation.

390   LiDAR scan often contains 100K - 1000K points in real-world autonomous driving scenarios (Jund
391   et al., 2021). Therefore, we evaluate scene flow methods with the full point cloud as the input.
392   NSFP achieves 78.21/75.15% strict accuracy, but the computation time costs 15310/15214 ms. To
393   accelerate the optimization process, NSFP (linear) replaces the MLP with a linear model and po-
394   sitional encoding. In this way, NSFP (linear) speedups the optimization process almost two times
395   and achieves worse performance compared to NSFP, i.e., accuracy decreases by about 15%. FNSF
396   achieves almost 30× speedup and improves the strict accuracy to 85.34/87.04%. Meanwhile, FNSF
397
      (linear) slightly accelerates FNSF, suffering from a relatively large drop in performance.
398   All the above methods only use two frames (t and t+1) and neglect to utilize previous frames.
399   FNSF (joint) estimates the previous flow (t-1 → t) and the current flow (t → t+1) at the same time.
400   However, such an intuitive scheme obtains worse strict accuracy (82.61/84.77%) than FNSF. The
401   interpretation of this phenomenon is that a single MLP fails to encode different motion fields si-
402   multaneously, because points in the frame t-1 and the frame t may have the same position (x, y, z)
403
      with different motion fields. These inconsistent samples are difficult to be learned by DNNs (Liu
      et al., 2023). In contrast, the proposed method exploits valuable temporal information from previous
404
      frames and outperforms FNSF and FNSF (joint).

406   OOD generalizability. To conduct a fair comparison with learning-based methods (Puy et al., 2020;
407   Wang et al., 2022b; Zhang et al., 2023; Lang et al., 2023), we further extend the proposed method
408   to process a reduced number of points, i.e., 8,192 points. Current learning-based methods could
409
      only process a fixed and small number of points due to their cumbersome networks (Peng et al.,

414   2021; Chodosh et al., 2023).

521   5     C ONCLUSION
522
523   In this paper, we theoretically analyze NSFP’s generalization ability and explain its effectiveness
524   for large-scale point cloud scene flow estimation. Inspired by the theoretical findings, we propose
525   an MNSF dedicated to large-scale point clouds. Furthermore, we conduct a theoretical analysis and
526   demonstrate that MNSF’s generalization error is bounded. Comprehensive case studies across five
527   metrics confirm that MNSF significantly improves performance. Additionally, MNSF’s robustness
528   in processing fast motion cases and the high flatness of the minima in loss landscape underscore its
529   effectiveness in large-scale OOD autonomous driving scenarios.
530

532   6     L IMITATION
533
534   MNSF needs to create a DT map using rasterization following (Li et al., 2023), which may bring
535   discretization errors. In our case studies, we build a DT map with relatively fine-resolution grids,
536   balancing the computation and the accuracy. In this way, the resolution of the DT map needs to be
537   chosen for different scenarios. Moreover, from a theoretical standpoint, we present for the first time
538   a generalization evaluation based on uniform stability for both NSFP and MNSF. We acknowledge
539   that we do not verify the applicability of theoretical results for all methods, whether within or outside
      the NSFP families. We leave them in the future work.

       Cycle consistency constraint. We conduct experiments to figure out whether the proposed method
1064   can be further improved by the cycle/temporal consistency loss, because it is common practice to

1066   et al., 2022a) for multi-frame point clouds, by constraining the distance between point clouds from
1067   different frames. To this end, a temporal consistency loss or a cycle consistency loss is usually used
