009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Parameter Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) methods have gained popularity and de-
012            mocratized the usage of Large Language Models (LLMs). Recent studies have
013            shown that a small subset of weights significantly impacts performance. Based
014            on this observation, we introduce a novel PEFT method, called Gaussian noise
015            Injected Fine Tuning of Salient Weights (GIFT-SW). Our method updates only
016            salient columns, while injecting Gaussian noise into non-salient ones. To identify
017            these columns, we developed a generalized sensitivity metric that extends and uni-
018            fies metrics from previous studies. Experiments with LLaMA models demonstrate
019
               that GIFT-SW outperforms full fine-tuning and modern PEFT methods under the
               same computational budget. Moreover, GIFT-SW offers practical advantages to
020
               recover performance of models subjected to mixed-precision quantization with
021
               keeping salient weights in full precision.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026
      Modern LLMs demonstrate remarkable generalization capabilities on unseen tasks. However, fine-
027
      tuning remains crucial to enhance these models performance or to restore the performance after
028
      compression techniques like quantization (Dettmers et al., 2024; Moskvoretskii et al., 2024), prun-
029   ing (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023; Kim et al., 2023), or tensor decomposition have been applied. Given
030   the large scale of modern LLMs, fine-tuning all parameters can be computationally and memory-
031   intensive. To overcome this challenge, Parameter Efficient Fine-Tuning schemes have been devel-
032   oped, aimed to improve model performance while using limited computational and memory re-
033   sources.
034
      To date, PEFT methods have not matched the accuracy of full fine-tuning (Nikdan et al., 2024),
035   highlighting the need for new approaches that can close this gap while still minimizing resource use.
036   Additionally, most PEFT methods involve adding extra parameters, which increases computational
037   demands.
038
      To address those issues and enhance the performance of efficiently trained LLMs, we introduce a
039
      novel PEFT method, GIFT-SW. This approach focuses on updating a small subset of salient weights
040
      while injecting noise into the non-salient weights. The development of this method is grounded in
041   observations from previous studies and the related questions they raise, which we aim to answer:
042
043   Previous research has shown that there is a small subset of salient weights which can significantly
044
      affect the effectiveness of post-training quantization (PTQ) (Dettmers et al., 2022; 2023; Kim et al.,
      2023) and pruning techniques (Yin et al., 2023; Frantar & Alistarh, 2023; Sun et al., 2023). More-
045
      over, Gurnee et al. (2024) identified a group of ”universal neurons” that are critical to a model’s
046
      functionality, emphasizing the importance of selecting and updating these salient weights. Question
047   1: Does updating a small subset of salient weights is sufficient to adjust the model?
048
049   Recent studies have demonstrated that Perturbed Gradient Descent (PGD), with noise injections
050
      applied both before and after the gradient step, can stabilize convergence and help prevent overfit-
      ting (Poole et al., 2014; Zhu et al., 2018; Jin et al., 2021). Question 2: Does Injecting Noise helps
051
      convergence?
052
053   PGD is commonly employed to enhance model robustness by approximating the quantization pro-
      cess (Shvetsov et al., 2022; Shin et al., 2023; Défossez et al., 2021). This increased robustness


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      can aid in maintaining the quality of the quantized model. Question 3: Does injecting noise helps
055   robustness?
056
057   Selecting salient weights is a significant challenge, particularly in quantization and pruning, and it
058
      is central to our method. In our paper, we derive a general formulation for all previously established
      saliency metrics and present experiments to compare their effectiveness.
059
060   The main contributions of our work can be summarized as follows:
061
062              • We introduce a novel PEFT method for pre-trained and quantized LLMs, called GIFT-SW.
063                It is designed to fine-tune weights in salient columns while injecting Gaussian noise into
064
                   non-salient weights, which are kept frozen during training.
065              • We generalize sensitivity metrics for identifying salient columns in pre-trained LLMs. We
066                compare various novel and existing instances of the proposed general form and identify a
067                new metric, which on average outperform previously studied in the literature metrics (Xiao
068
                   et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2024).
069              • Experiments demonstrate that GIFT-SW outperforms modern PEFT methods and full fine-
070                tuning baselines across most zero-shot tasks. GIFT-SW for LLaMA models achieve com-
071                parable accuracy to the corresponding state-of-the-art TÜLU2 models, despite fine-tuning
072                only 3% of the parameters and utilizing ten times less computational resources.
073
      We provide the code with GIFT-SW integrated into the popular PEFT library Mangrulkar et al.
074
      (2022), making it easy to use 1 .

079   2.1       PARAMETER EFFICIENT FINE - TUNING OF LLM
080
081   One of the most popular method with high efficiency is LoRA (Hu et al., 2021), which trains the
082
      low-rank adapters. Recent modifications to the method aim to improve the initialization of the
      adapters (Liu et al., 2024) and enhance the low-rank representation of pre-trained weights by adding
083
      sparse adapters (Nikdan et al., 2024). Another improvement of the learning capacity of LoRA is
084
      given by DoRA (Liu et al., 2024), which fine-tunes magnitude and direction components of the pre-
085   trained weights. This method achieves considerable performance across various fine-tuning tasks.

      2.2       S ALIENT W EIGHTS IN LLM S
088
089   The identification of salient weights2 is one of the main problems in weight pruning. Recently, sev-
090   eral approaches have been proposed to identify such weights in LLMs, including SparseGPT (Fran-
091   tar & Alistarh, 2023), Wanda (Sun et al., 2023), and OWL (Yin et al., 2023).
092
      Dettmers et al. (2022) demonstrated that a small subset of outliers in input activations has a substan-
093
      tial impact on LLM performance, highlighting the relationship between the activation outliers and
094   the salient weights. Many subsequent Post-Training Quantization (PTQ) methods used similar or
095   identical pruning metrics to identify these salient weights (Dettmers et al., 2023; Xiao et al., 2023;
096   Lee et al., 2024).
097
      In our work, we generalize the identification metrics for salient weights by considering metrics from
098
      both the literature on pruning and quantization.

142   2.4      N OISE I NJECTIONS
143
144   In this section, we briefly describe Gaussian Noise Injections (GNI) and its benefits. Then we discuss
145   studies which show close similarity between quantization noise and Gaussian Noise. Therefore, to
146
      examine our third question, we sample noise relative to quantization levels, leaving other sampling
      options for future work.
147
148   Gaussian Noise Injections (GNI). Perturbed Gradient Descent (PGD) is a family of methods that
149   involve adding or multiplying weights with samples from some random distribution, during an op-
150   timization procedure. Gaussian noise injection (GNI) after the gradient step helps to escape saddle
151   points efficiently in non-convex optimization (Jin et al., 2021). However, Gaussian noise injections
152
      before the gradient step helps to escape from the spurious local optimum (Zhu et al., 2018).
153   In our work, we use GNI before evaluating the gradient. To prevent variance explosion, Orvieto et al.
154   (2023) recommend adding noise to only one layer per training iteration, demonstrating that GNI acts
155   as a regularization method. Liu et al. (2023) investigate fine-tuning pre-trained language models with
156   GNI, suggesting an initial learning of layer-wise variance parameters for noise distributions before
157   adding noise to all weights. Their results indicate this approach outperforms independent layer-wise
158
      noise injections.
159   Quantization Noise Injections (QNI). Quantization aware training (QAT) is applied to mitigate
160   accuracy degradation after quantization. However, uniform quantization 3 Q is a non-differentiable

187   2.5      S TRAIGHT T HROUGH E STIMATOR
188
189   The most popular QAT technique incorporating quantization operation into the traning process is
190
      Straight Through Estimation (STE)4 (Bengio et al., 2013; Shang et al., 2023), which basically re-

191
      tages compared with QNI5 , as STE is biased and may cause weight oscillation between quantization
192
      steps. Shin et al. (2023) demonstrated that pretraining models for the following quantization with
193   QNI instead of STE results in better performance. More technical details are provided in Section C.

196   3       M ETHOD
197
198
      GIFT-SW consists of the following steps:
199           (1) Identify a fixed number of salient columns using a chosen sensitive metric, based on a small
200               calibration set. This number remains consistent across all layers.
201           (2) Split columns of the matrices into subsets of salient columns and regular ones.
202
203           (3) During training, add noise to the weights in non-salient columns and update weights only
204
                  in the salient columns.
205   Thus, the method depends on two main design choices: 1) how to choose salient columns and 2)
206   the parameters of noise injections. We cover the choice of metrics in Section 3.1. Noise injection
207   details are provided in Section 3.2.

470   6.1   C OMPARISON SENSITIVITY METRICS
471
472   We study sensitivity metrics with respect to different noise levels (various perturbation magnitudes),
473   which translate into varying quantization precision. In this experiment, the non-salient weights of
474   LLaMA2 and TÜLU2 with 7B and 13B parameters. Models are quantized with QUIK, the salient
475   weights are not updated. We select 128 columns of salient weights.
476   Mean results for zero-shot tasks in Table 4 show that for most precisions, the best performance is
477   achieved with salient columns identified by Equation 1 with γ = 1, ρ = ∞, τ = ∞ (second col-
478   umn). Columns identified by the squared l2 norm of the input feature (the OWQ metric) show better
479   performance only for TÜLU2 quantized to 3 and 2 bits. Choosing salient columns solely by the input
480   features (the QUIK metric) leads to underperformance, especially for 2 bit. Therefore, identifying
481   salient columns sensitive to quantization noise requires considering both the weight quantization
482
      error and the maximum values of input activation.
483   Based on the results, we chose the best-performing sensitivity metric with γ = 1, ρ = ∞, τ =
484   ∞. However, the results do not reveal a clear rule for selecting the optimal sensitivity metric, as
485   performance varies across different bit-widths and models with no discernible pattern. This remains
      an area for future research.

      6.3   Q UANTIZATION B EFORE AND A FTER T RAINING
496
497   From studies related to QAT, it is known that pre-training a model with noise injection enables to
498   improve its predictive capabilities after quantization (Défossez et al., 2021; Shvetsov et al., 2022).
499   Based on those observations, in this section we examine the performance of the quantized LLaMA2-
500   7b after fine-tuning full precision salient columns in several settings:
501
502          • Pre-GIFT-SW. Applying GIFT-SW prior to the quantization.
503          • Post-GIFT-SW. Applying GIFT-SW after the quantization.
504
505
             • Salient FT. Fine-tuning salient columns after quantization with no noise injected
506
      In the case of the pre-training, the bit-width for the model quantization corresponds to the noise level
507
      injected during the training. For the post-training, the noise injection is always performed at 4 bit.
508
509   Table 5 presents the average scores achieved by the models across evaluation benchmark. In the
510   case of 4 bit quantization the Pre-GIFT-SW model considerable outperforms other models. But in
511
      the case of 3 and 2 bits, fine-tuning salient columns after quantization enables to achieve quantized
      models better generative capabilities.
512
513   It can be explained by significant deviation of the quantized weights from their original values that
514   is induced by the extremely low-bit quantization. As a result, the interrelations between the salient
515   weights and the quantized weights are disrupted, and the positive effect of pre-training disappears.
516   However, post-training of the salient weight enables to form them new relations with other weights,
517
      so the model partially recovers its generative capabilities.
518   Also it can be observed that application of Post-GIFT-SW and Salient FT to model quantized in 3
519   bit gives the similar scores. But in the case of 2 bit quantization, the noise injection improves the
520   fine-tuning of the quantized model.

      7     C ONCLUSION
523
524
      In this paper, we introduce GIFT-SW, a parameter-efficient fine-tuning method that trains only
525
      weights in a small subset of salient columns while injecting quantization noise into the frozen
526
      weights. GIFT-SW proves to be superior to previous fine-tuning strategies in both full precision and
527   quantized settings, requiring less compute budget. In data scaling experiments, GIFT-SW demon-
528   strates greater stability than previous PEFT methods and outperforms both PEFT and full fine-tuning
529   across nearly all data budgets. Our ablation studies show that QNI is beneficial but only with salient
530   weights. Although GIFT-SW outperforms previous methods, further research is needed to determine
531   how to maximize its performance in quantized settings.
532
      We generalize the criterion for selecting salient columns from previous studies and empirically com-
533   pare various parameters. Our experiments show that while some criteria perform better than others,
534   none emerge as a clear dominant choice. This significant finding underscores the need for further
535   research to refine these criteria.

538   8     L IMITATIONS
539
      We find the main limitations of our work as follows:


                                                        10
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


540
           1. We report results of GIFT-SW exclusively for LLaMA models. Currently, numerous open-
541           source pre-trained LLMs with high generative capabilities are available. However, LLaMA
542           models are the most commonly chosen for studying the efficiency of modern PEFT and
543           quantization methods. Despite the architectural similarities among most LLMs, future ex-
544           periments with different models are necessary.
545        2. For quantizing models, we use only the GPTQ method, which is widely used for mixed-
546           precision quantization of LLMs. This method improves the performance of quantized mod-
547           els by aggregating quantization error into columns stored in full precision. However, GIFT-
548           SW can be easily integrated with other methods, such as conventional RTN or QuantEase.
549        3. Experiments with GIFT-SW report results for salient columns selected using the sensitivity
550           metric (1) with γ = 1. Our proposed metric, based on our analysis, shows high sensitivity
551           of the salient columns to quantization in most LLaMA2 cases. However, other sensitivity
552           metrics may yield better performance for GIFT-SW and mixed-precision quantization in
553           different LLMs.
554        4. Noise parameters for fine-tuning the salient weights are determined using the QNI ap-
555           proach. However, other noise distributions may also enhance the fine-tuning process. Iden-
556           tifying the optimal noise distribution is beyond the scope of this paper.
557        5. In this study, we focus on developing the GIFT-SW algorithm for effective fine-tuning of
558           LLMs, but we do not provide computationally efficient implementations of CUDA kernels
559           for the algorithm. In the future, CUDA kernels for GIFT-SW can be developed based on
560           the code from QUIK Ashkboos et al. (2023) and OWQ Lee et al. (2024).
561        6. We train GIFT-SW with only a few fine-tuning instruction sets, selected for their size and
562           high benchmark results in previous studies. However, expanding the number of fine-tuning
563           sets could make the experiments more comprehensive.
564        7. We evaluate our method using six distinct benchmarks inherited from various previous
565           studies. In future research, it would be beneficial to include more benchmarks to gain
566           additional insights.

796   were as specified in the original papers, and the rank was set to 64 to match the number of trainable

798   250M for LLaMA2-13b, 167M for LLaMA3-8b.
799   For our method, the learning rate was set to 1 × 10−4 for salient columns of LLaMA2 models and
800   to 1 × 10−5 of the LLaMA3 model. We fixed the number of salient columns at 128, such that the
801   number of trainable parameters is 174M for LLaMA2-7b, 272M for LLaMA2-13b, and 176M for
802   LLaMA3-8b.
