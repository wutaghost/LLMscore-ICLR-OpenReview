011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent research on integrating Large Language Models (LLMs) with Graph Neu-
014            ral Networks (GNNs) typically follows two approaches: LLM-centered models,
015            which convert graph data into tokens for LLM processing, and GNN-centered
016
               models, which use LLMs to encode text features into node and edge represen-
               tations for GNN input. LLM-centered models often struggle to capture graph
017
               structures effectively, while GNN-centered models compress variable-length tex-
018
               tual data into fixed-size vectors, limiting their ability to understand complex se-
019            mantics. Additionally, GNN-centered approaches require converting tasks into
020            a uniform, manually-designed format, restricting them to classification tasks and
021            preventing language output. To address these limitations, we introduce a new ar-
022            chitecture that deeply integrates GNN with LLM, featuring three key innovations:
023            (1) Structure-Aware Transformers, which incorporate GNN’s message-passing ca-
024            pabilities directly into LLM’s transformer layers, allowing simultaneous process-
025            ing of textual and structural information and generating outputs from both GNN
026            and LLM; (2) Graph-Text Cross-Attention, which processes full, uncompressed
027
               text from graph nodes and edges, ensuring complete semantic integration; and
               (3) GNN-LLM Twin Predictor, enabling LLM’s flexible autoregressive genera-
028
               tion alongside GNN’s scalable one-pass prediction. GL-Fusion achieves outstand
029
               performance on various tasks. Notably, it achieves state-of-the-art performance
030            on OGBN-Arxiv and OGBG-Code2,

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Research in Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) has long focused on learning from graph with pre-
036   processed vector features, often overlooking the rich textual information contained in raw data.
037   Recently, many studies have recognized that better utilization of these text features can enhance
038   performance. This has led to a focus on text-attributed graphs (TAGs), graphs with node, edge, and
039   graph-level text attributes. In addition, some tasks may contain a task description, questions, and
040   candidate answers in natural language. The combination of GNNs and pretrained Large Language
041   Models (LLMs), which can efficiently encode these text attributes and information, has garnered
042   significant interest.
043   Two main approaches have emerged for combining GNNs and LLMs: GNN-centered methods and
044   LLM-centered methods. GNN-centered methods focus on typical graph tasks like node classifica-
045   tion and link prediction. These methods convert text into representation vectors for graph nodes
046   or edges using LLMs, which are then fed into GNNs to produce final predictions. GNN-centered
047   approaches excel at capturing structural information. However, compressing rich textual features
048   into fixed-length vectors results in information loss. Moreover, The importance of different text
049   features varies depending on the task. For example, in wiki knowledge graph (KG) datasets, en-
050
      tity descriptions may include a person’s name, nationality, or occupation. A name might be crucial
      for identifying relatives but irrelevant for finding their workplace. Additionally, GNN architectures
051
      cannot generate natural language, making it difficult to perform tasks like question answering.
052
053   LLM-centered methods, on the other hand, use a multi-modal approach by projecting graph repre-
      sentations into the language space, allowing LLMs to generate text answers to graph-text questions.

076   However, this approach faces similar challenges. Graph tasks vary widely in domains, and current
077   LLM-centered models lack dynamic encoding by GNNs that adapt to tasks, so these models strug-
078   gles to generalize to diverse datasets or tasks. Furthermore, language supervision is often indirect
079   and less efficient compared to direct graph supervision, making training more difficult. For example,
080   LLMs, with their autoregressive nature, cannot simultaneously predict all nodes in a graph, unlike
081   GNNs, which can process nodes in parallel. Moreover, GNNs focus on classifying nodes into a finite
082
      set of classes, while LLMs operate in the much larger and noisier token space of natural language,
      complicating the training process.
083
084   To handle tasks involving both graphs and text, we introduce Graph-Language Fusion (GL-Fusion),
085   an architecture that combines the strengths of GNN-centered and LLM-centered models. As shown
086   in Figure 1(a), graphs are paired with node and edge text features to solve tasks described in natural
087   language. In Figure 1(b), the task description is included as part of the input text sequence, and
088
      a special token <node> is added for each graph node. Each node’s feature is generated by a text
      encoder based on its text attribute, which is then incorporated into the sequence. To process this
089
      combination of graph and text tokens, our architecture includes:
090
091   • Structure-Aware Transformer Layers: We modify the original attention layers for language
092     input to graph structure-aware attention with both graph and text inputs, which maintains causality
093     for language generation and further enabling transformer to encode graph structure. Different
094     from graph transformer, which encodes graph only and cannot process text, our transformer can
095
        naturally encodes graph tokens conditioning on the text tokens before it in sequence, and encodes
        text tokens after the graph based on the graph. This two-way information exchange between
096
        graph and text overcomes the limitations of older models, where either graph or text embedding
097
        is irrelevant from the other, and achieves better expressivity.
098
      • Graph-Text Cross-Attention: Previous models often compress either graph or text information
099
        into a single vector, leading to information loss. In our model, graph structure is preserved across
100     each transformer layer without compression. To avoid compressing textual features, we introduce
101     Graph-Text Cross-Attention layers, where attention is applied between node text and the main
102     sequence (which includes the <node> tokens). These layers retain the complete text information
103     of each node, allowing the model to more precisely extract task-relevant information by focusing
104     on the most important tokens.
105   • GNN & LLM Twin-Predictor: Different tasks require different prediction mechanisms. For
106     traditional GNN tasks, such as node classification, having the LLM generate predictions for each
107     node would slow down inference and be constrained by graph size. In such cases, direct readout
        from the GNN is more efficient. Conversely, tasks that require natural language outputs benefit


                                                                                                       2
108
          from the autoregressive capabilities of LLMs. Unlike previous models, which rely on either a
109       GNN or LLM predictor, GL-Fusion retains both as predictors. During training, supervision can
110       be applied to both models, and during inference, both outputs are generated simultaneously. Users
111       can choose the most appropriate output for the task or consider both outputs together.
112
      With these modifications, we propose GL-Fusion, a unified architecture that achieves competitive
113
      performance by integrating structural and textual information. To demonstrate this, we conducted
114   experiments on various tasks, including basic graph property prediction, node classification, knowl-
115   edge graph completion, commonsense question answering, and code graph-to-text generation. Our
116   GL-Fusion achieves outstanding performance across all these datasets, outperforming various com-
117   binations of GNNs and LLMs. Notably, our model even achieves state-of-the-art performance on
118   two Open Graph Benchmark (Hu et al., 2020) datasets: ogbn-arxiv and ogbg-code2. These results
119   demonstrate the powerful capability of our GL-Fusion architecture.

126   Text-Attributed Graph (TAG) A TAG is represented as G = (V, E, X), where V =
127   1, 2, 3, . . . , n is the set of n nodes, E ⊆ V × V × Z Le is the set of edges. Each edge (i, j, Eij ) ∈ E

129   denoted as X ∈ Zn×Ln , where Xi represents the Ln -token text feature of node i. Here, Ln and
130   Le represent the maximum lengths of node and edge text, respectively. Text of varying lengths is
131
      padded to the same length.
132
      Problem Settings We consider a TAG G and a task description T ∈ Z Lt . The task can be node
133
      classification, link prediction, graph classification, or text generation. As illustrated in Figure 1,
134
      our model processes a TAG along with a task description. The graph is integrated into the task
135   description as part of the input sequence to our model, where the graph forms a subsequence starting
136   with a special <graph start> token and ending with a <graph end> token. Each node is
137   represented by a <node> token. For each specific task, we use a task-specific prediction head,
138   and the model also leverages the LLM itself to generate the answer in text form. We focus on
139   the supervised learning setting, where the dataset is split into training, validation, and test sets.
140   Models are trained on the training set, hyperparameters are tuned on the validation set, and test set
141   performance is reported.

143   3     GL-F USION : A H IGHLY I NTEGRATED GNN-LLM M ODEL
144
145   This section presents the architecture of our GL-Fusion model, which integrates structure-aware
146   transformer layers, graph-text cross-attention blocks, and GNN & LLM twin predictors. Given input
147   sequence T ∈ ZLt and input graph G = (V, E, X), we first convert T to sequence representation
148   t ∈ RLt ×d with token embedding layer. Then we use an existing text encoder (BehnamGhader et al.,
149   2024) to convert node text sequences X to uncompressed text embeddings x ∈ Rn×Ln ×d , and we
150   also convert edge text Eij ∈ ZLe to compressed text embedding eij ∈ Rd . Our GL-Fusion model
151
      will update input sequence representations t in each transformer layer and use t to produce the final
      prediction.

277   3.3   GNN & LLM T WIN P REDICTOR
278
279   Since our model uses a transformer architecture and maintains causality, it can naturally generate
280   text outputs, which we refer to as the LLM predictor. Additionally, since graph nodes are treated
281   as tokens in the input sequence, the transformer’s output representations for these tokens can be
282
      considered node-level GNN representations, which can be fed into pooling layers and linear layers
      to produce predictions—referred to as the GNN predictor. Both predictors have their advantages
283
      and disadvantages, as outlined below:
284
285
      Rationale for GNN as a Predictor. While LLMs can perform various language tasks, they face
286
      limitations in autoregressive generation, including:
287
288   • Natural Language Output: LLMs can natively produce text predictions, which is challenging for
289     the GNN predictor.
290   • Numerical Output: GNN predictors naturally output numerical values, making them well-suited
291     for regression or ranking tasks. In contrast, LLMs produce token sequences, making it difficult to
292
        generate numerical outputs as text.
293   • Scalability: LLMs predict outputs one by one in an autoregressive manner, which can be ineffi-
294
        cient for tasks requiring multiple predictions for all nodes or edges. In contrast, GNNs generate
        all predictions for all nodes or edges in parallel.
295
296
      • Training Efficiency: Training models end-to-end through LLM-generated sequences is more in-
        direct and harder to control, as the LLM’s loss function is constrained by “perplexity” (i.e., the
297
        cross-entropy loss on token space), which often includes task-irrelevant tokens instead of focus-
298
        ing solely on the task-relevant labeling space. With GNNs, we can provide direct and dense
299     supervision on the target classes, improving training efficiency.
300
301
      To leverage both approaches, we employ a twin-predictor architecture, allowing for simultaneous
      predictions from both the GNN and LLM. A graph readout component, tailored to specific tasks, is
302
      added after the final layer for GNN predictions. This component is trained using cross-entropy loss
303
      for classification tasks or mean squared error for regression tasks. During inference, it can generate
304   predictions for graph nodes as needed.

309   LLM-Centered Approaches LLM-centered methods leverage pretrained language models to
310   handle graph data by translating graph structures into text formats, like adjacency lists (Liu & Wu,
311   2023; Guo et al., 2023b; Chen et al., 2024). While these methods have shown some promise, they
312
      often suffer from issues related to node and edge ordering and can struggle with large graphs due
      to the limited context windows of LLMs. A more effective solution has been to use GNNs to gen-
313
      erate node representation sequences that are fed into LLMs. For example, Chai et al. (2023) used
314
      embeddings from Message Passing Neural Networks (MPNNs) to represent target nodes, enabling
315   the LLM to answer basic structural questions. Similarly, Tang et al. (2024) aligned both structural
316   and textual inputs using GNNs and Transformers, achieving better accuracy by processing all node
317   embeddings through a frozen pretrained LLM. However, these approaches can still face limitations,
318   such as task-agnostic encoders, as seen in Qin et al. (2024), which limit their ability to transfer
319   across different domains.
320
321   GNN-Centered Approaches In GNN-centered methods, LLMs are primarily used to encode tex-
322   tual data that is then fed into GNNs for tasks like node classification and link prediction. Early
323   graph datasets such as Cora (McCallum et al., 2000) and CiteSeer (Giles et al., 1998) used basic text
      embeddings, but these often failed to capture complex textual information. More recent datasets,

340   GNN-LLM Fusion Recent efforts aim for a deeper integration of GNNs and LLMs. One exam-

      approach improves text encoding for graph nodes, it overlooks the inclusion of task descriptions or

344
      nisms restricts the model’s ability to dynamically extract task-specific information. Building on this,
345
      Jin et al. (2023) adapted the GraphFormers architecture by refining its pretraining strategy, aiming
346   to better capture text relationships between neighboring nodes. While these methods have made
347   strides, there is still a need for more tightly integrated approaches that can fully exploit the potential
348   of GNNs and LLMs working together.

352
353
      To evaluate the potential of GL-Fusion as a new architecture combining GNN and LLM, we con-
      ducted experiments on various tasks. These include synthetic tasks to validate its capacity to capture
354
      basic graph properties, traditional GNN tasks such as node classification and link prediction to as-
355
      sess its ability to solve graph-related tasks, commonsense question-answering tasks to test its ability
356   to leverage knowledge graphs for language generation, and code graph tasks to evaluate its capacity
357   to generate text based on graph structures. Through these experiments, we demonstrate GL-Fusion’s
358   strong potential to effectively combine GNN and LLM architectures. Further details on the experi-
359   ments can be found in Appendix A.

510   7   L IMITATIONS & B ROADER I MPACTS
511
512   We recognize several limitations that can guide future research. First, while our architecture unifies
513   GNNs and LLMs and shows effectiveness across various tasks, we have not tested it across a com-
514   prehensive range of task types due to the diversity of domains and data properties. Future work will
515   involve refining our model on a broader spectrum of tasks to address these gaps.
516   Additionally, although our architecture provides a solid foundation for graph-text integration, our
517   experiments have relied on training separate models individually. We have not yet established a
518   unified set of pretrained parameters for all tasks due to resource and data limitations. Large-scale
519   multi-task pretraining remains an objective.
520
      Regarding societal impacts, while our model aids in processing graph data, it also risks generating
521   incorrect or misleading information. Research focused on enhancing the reliability of LLM outputs
522   is crucial, and our work can support these efforts.

      A     E XPERIMENTAL DETAILS
649
650
      Architecture All our models are based on Llama-3-8b, which utilizes a 32-layer Transformer,
651
      with message passing at layers 0, 4, 8, 12, 16, 20, 24, and 28, and node cross-attention at layers
652
      3, 7, 11, 15, 19, 23, 27, and 31. For the original LLaMA-3 layers, we employ LoRA with rank
653   r and weight α = 2r, while newly added layers are trained with full parameters. The LLM used
654   for sentence encoding is LLM2Vec (BehnamGhader et al., 2024) model based on Llama-3-8b, is
655   fine-tuned with Lora on its last layer. For ogbl-arxiv dataset, we use r = 64. For CSTAG datasets,
656   we use r = 4. For all other datasets, we use r = 32. GNN in our model in implemented with torch
657   geometric (Fey & Lenssen, 2019). Some parameters are loaded from pretrained LLM.
658
659          • For structure-aware Transformer Layers, we leverage the pretrained Llama-3-8B model as
660            the backbone. As detailed in Section 3.1, we introduce new parameters through modifica-
661            tions to positional encoding and attention masks. Additionally, for the <graph node>
662
               token, we simply add an embedding to the existing layer. The newly added MPNN com-
               ponent is initialized randomly. We fine-tune the transformer parameters using the Low-
663
               Rank Adaptation (LoRA) method from the peft library. This approach freezes the original
664
               transformer layers and optimizes only the newly introduced low-rank layers. The MPNN
665            parameters, on the other hand, are trained entirely from scratch.
666
667          • For the Graph-Text Cross-Attention layers in our paper, all parameters are initialized ran-
668            domly and trained from scratch.
669
             • For the GNN prediction modules, which are simple linear layers for generating outputs,
670            parameters are initialized randomly and trained from scratch.
671
672          • Overall, most parameters are initialized from pretrained models and frozen. Only about
673            10% of the parameters in the entire model are optimized.
674
675   Training Process All experiments are trained on A800 GPUs with one epoch. The optimizer is
676   AdamW with learning rate=3e-5, weight decay=0.1. For node classification and csqa datasets, we
677   ensemble the prediction of GNN and LLM. For link datasets, we use GNN output only. For graph
678   level tasks and synthetic tasks, we use text output only. The entire model is trained end-to-end, with
679   joint training of all components.
680
681   Baselines Baselines and experiments setting used in different works on combining GNN and LLM
682   varies a lot. Therefore, we directly use the results reported by baseline works as shown in the
683   maintext.
