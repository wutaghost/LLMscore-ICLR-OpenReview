                                                 A BSTRACT
012
               Modeling human preferences is crucial for aligning foundation models with human
013
               values. Traditional reward modeling methods, such as the Bradley-Terry (BT)
014
               reward model, fall short in expressiveness, particularly in addressing intransitive
015            preferences. Although supervised pair preference models and pair reward models
016            can express general preferences, their implementation is highly ad-hoc and cannot
017            guarantee a consistent preference probability of compared pairs. Additionally,
018            they impose high computational costs due to their quadratic query complexity
019            when comparing multiple responses. In this paper, we introduce preference rep-
020            resentation learning, an approach that embeds responses into a latent space to
021            capture intricate preference structures efficiently, achieving linear query complex-
022            ity (matching the efficiency of the BT reward model). Additionally, we propose
023
               preference score-based General Preference Optimization (GPO), which generalizes
               reward-based reinforcement learning from human feedback. Experimental results
024
               show that our General Preference representation model (GPM) outperforms the
025
               BT reward model on the RewardBench benchmark with a margin of up to 9.1%
026            and effectively models cyclic preferences where any BT reward model behaves
027            like a random guess. Furthermore, evaluations on downstream tasks such as Al-
028            pacaEval2.0, following the language model post-training with GPO and our general
029            preference model, reveal substantial performance improvements with margins up
030            to 8.3%. These findings indicate that our method may enhance the alignment of
031            foundation models with nuanced human values.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Modeling human preferences is a cornerstone in developing foundation models that interact seamlessly
036   with users. In natural language modeling and reinforcement learning, aligning models with human
037
      intent and values has led to significant advancements, including improved text generation and
      enhanced decision-making policies (Ouyang et al., 2022; Christiano et al., 2017). Traditional
038
      approaches often rely on reward modeling, wherein a reward function is learned to guide the
039
      optimization of policies. While effective in certain contexts, these methods face expressiveness and
040   computational efficiency challenges, particularly when addressing complex or intransitive human
041   preferences (Tversky, 1969; Munos et al., 2023).
042
      Preference learning algorithms typically employ pairwise comparisons to
043
      capture human judgments (Ibarz et al., 2018; Ziegler et al., 2019). The Bradley-
044   Terry (BT) model (Bradley & Terry, 1952) is popular for modeling such
045   pairwise preferences due to its simplicity and computational efficiency: given
046   K responses, a BT reward model cost O(K) inference-time compute to output
047   the reward dictating the preferences. The efficiency of the BT model comes
048   from the implicit assumption that each option can be conveniently represented
049   by a scalar reward, which inevitably limits the model’s capacity to capture
050   the richness of human judgments that may be context-dependent or exhibit
051   intransitivity (Gardner, 1970).                                                  Figure           1:

087   Based on the above observations, it is thus natural to raise the following question:
088
089                          Is there a principled way to model general preference?
090
      In this paper, we answer this question affirmatively by proposing preference representation learning,
091   which bridges the gap between expressiveness and efficiency in general preference modeling. Our
092   method embeds responses into a multi-dimensional latent space that captures the complex preference
093   structure beyond transitive relations while allowing for efficient querying of preferences. Notably, our
094   approach achieves a computational complexity of O(K), matching the efficiency of the BT model
095   but with enhanced expressiveness.
096   The main contributions of our work are summarized as follows:
097
      • We introduce preference representation learning for general preference modeling, enabling both
098
        efficient and expressive representation of human preferences. Our approach generalizes the Bradley-
099
        Terry (BT) reward model by embedding responses into a latent space, capturing complex structures,
100     including intransitive preferences. Notably, our General Preference representation model (GPM)
101     achieves a query complexity of O(K) for evaluating preferences among K responses which match
102     the complexity of the Bradley-Terry reward model, an improvement over the O(K 2 ) complexity of
103     traditional supervised preference models that rely on pairwise inputs (see Section 4).
104   • We demonstrate GPM’s effectiveness across various tasks, including CyclicPreference (ours) and
105     the renowned RewardBench (Lambert et al., 2024). Specifically, GPM models intransitive (e.g.,
106     cyclic) preferences with 100% accuracy, whereas the BT reward model performs like random
107     guessing (see Section 6.1). Additionally, GPM outperforms the BT reward model on RewardBench
        with performance margins of up to 9.1% (see Section 6.2).

114   2     R ELATED W ORK
115
      Reward-Based Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF). The earlier approaches
116   to modeling human preference for language model alignment usually learn a reward model from a pref-
117   erence dataset. The human preference is assumed to follow the Bradley-Terry (BT) model (Bradley &
118   Terry, 1952) or the Thurstone model (Thurstone, 2017). LLM policies then are fine-tuned to maximize
119   these scalar reward signals for better alignment (Christiano et al., 2017; Ziegler et al., 2019; Ouyang
120   et al., 2022). Later, the direct preference optimization (DPO) methods are proposed by Rafailov et al.
121   (2024) to only implicitly learn a reward model represented by an LLM. The human preference is
122   still assumed to follow the Bradley-Terry model. However, the reliance on scalar rewards imposes a
123   total ordering on preferences, which may not reflect the intransitive or stochastic nature of human
124
      judgments (Tversky, 1969; Agranov & Ortoleva, 2017).
125   Preference-Based Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback. Recently, there emerged a
126   line of works that directly estimates the preference probability without imposing a reward-based
127   preference model or any transitivity assumptions (Lou et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2023; Wang et al.,
128
      2023) either for preference-based RL or in the context of RLHF. Efforts have been made to optimize
      policies directly from pair-wise preference comparisons, thereby mitigating the limitations of scalar
129
      reward functions (Munos et al., 2023; Swamy et al., 2024; Rosset et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024b).

142   3.1   R EWARD -BASED R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING FROM H UMAN F EEDBACK
143
144   The most prevalent approach to aligning language models with human preferences is to consider
145   a scalar reward function r(y; x) that assigns a numerical score to each response. The preference
146
      between two responses is then determined solely by the reward scores for the two responses. For
      example, the Bradley-Terry (BT) model (Bradley & Terry, 1952) is a widely used method for
147
      modeling pairwise preferences in this context. However, the BT model can not capture intransitive
148
      (e.g. cyclic) preferences effectively (Bertrand et al., 2023). Under BT model, the probability that
149   response y is preferred over y′ is given by:
150
                                    P(y ≻ y′ | x) = σ r(y; x) − r(y′ ; x) ,

151
152   where σ(z) = 1/(1 + e−z ) is the logistic (sigmoid) function.
153   In practice, the reward function r(y; x) is learned by maximizing the likelihood of the observed
154   preference data. Once the reward function is established, policy optimization techniques, such as
155   Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) (Schulman et al., 2017), can be applied to adjust the language
156   model to generate responses that maximize expected rewards. The optimization problem can be
157   formulated as:
158                 max Ex∼X , y∼πθ (·|x) [r(y; x)] − βEx∼X [KL (πθ (· | x) ∥ πref (· | x))] ,            (1)
159                   θ

160   where θ are the parameters of the policy πθ , πref is a reference policy (often the pre-trained or
161   supervised-fine-tuned language model), β is a scaling parameter that controls the strength of regular-
      ization, and KL denotes the Kullback-Leibler divergence.

179   Existing approaches often involve concatenating the prompt and responses with a template and
180   training an LLM-based sequential classifier in a supervised learning manner. For example, Jiang
181
      et al. (2023) simply concatenate the three segments (x, y1 , y2 ) sequentially and form a single input
      sequence with special tokens as separators:
182
         ‘<s> <source> x </s> <candidate1> y1 </s> <candidate2> y2 </s>’
183
      Then a sequential classification head on the last token is trained to predict the preference. Another
184   example is Munos et al. (2023), which uses the following template for text summarization:
185
      ‘You are an expert summary rater. Given a piece of text and two of
186   its possible summaries, output 1 or 2 to indicate which summary
187   is better.
188   Text - 〈text〉, Summary 1 - 〈summary1〉, Summary 2 - 〈summary2〉.
189   Preferred Summary -’
190   Then use the last logit for an arbitrarily chosen token as s(y1 ≻ y2 |x) for training.
191   However, due to the language model’s position encoding (Press et al., 2021; Su et al., 2024) and the
192   causal attention (Radford et al., 2018; 2019) mechanism not being symmetric, the candidate’s order
193   in the concatenation will affect the final prediction results. It is mitigated by randomly shuffling the
194   two responses in the training dataset but the output is still highly asymmetric. Another limitation is
195   that how to represent the preference score can be highly ad-hoc. The two examples above already use
196
      different templates and different linear heads (sequential classification v.s. language modeling).

218   In this section, we propose a general preference representation learning framework that can model
219   human preferences efficiently and expressively. Each response is embedded as a vector in a latent
220   space, and the preferences are modeled through interactions between these representations (embed-
221   dings) using a skew-symmetric operator. We first define preference representations, which serve as
222
      the foundation for modeling the relationships between responses.
223   Definition 4.1 (Preference Representations). Given a prompt x, we assign to each response y a
224   preference representation vector vy|x ∈ R2k . These representations are designed to capture the
225
      features relevant to human preferences beyond what can be represented by scalar rewards.

386
387   To address Q1, we evaluate the ability of GPM to capture intransitive, cyclic preferences that
388   traditional transitive models (like the BT model) struggle to represent.
389   Cyclic Preference Dataset. We constructed a dataset by inducing cyclic preferences from the
390   Ultrafeedback dataset Cui et al. (2024). The dataset includes responses evaluated across four key
391   metrics: instruction following, honesty, truthfulness, and helpfulness. We created preference cycles
392   such as: instruction following ≻ honesty ≻ truthfulness ≻ helpfulness ≻
393   instruction following, ensuring the presence of intransitive cycles. We further generated
394
      four sub-datasets by omitting one metric from each cycle, resulting in 4 different datasets with 216 to
      363 instances.
395
396   Training and Evaluation. We trained GPM using the Gemma-2B-it language model as the base and
397   evaluated the models based on their ability to predict the human-provided preferences in these datasets.
398

      L = − log σ(rw − rl ) (Ouyang et al., 2022). Since cyclic preferences are inherently intransitive, we
399
      measure accuracy as the percentage of correctly predicted human preferences, where higher scores
400
      indicate better handling of non-transitive preferences. As shown in Table 1, the GP representation

404   confirming the theoretical advantages of using a preference representation-based approach over
405   traditional reward models that assume transitivity (more on implementation details are presented in

529   7     C ONCLUSION
530
531   In this work, we introduce preference representation learning, a framework for modeling human
532
      preferences that can capture complex, intransitive structures like cyclic preferences. Our General
      Preference representation model (GPM) achieves linear complexity (matching the efficiency of the
533
      Bradley-Terry model) while maintaining the ability to model intricate preference relationships. It
534
      consistently outperforms traditional models like Bradley-Terry reward models across various bench-
535   marks, including cyclic preference datasets and real-world tasks from RewardBench. Additionally,
536   incorporating preference scores from GPM into policy optimization methods, such as SPPO and
537   the newly introduced General Preference Optimization (GPO), led to performance improvements
538   in downstream tasks that require alignment with intricate human preferences, as demonstrated in
539   benchmarks like AlpacaEval 2.0.

873   Proof of the Proposition A.1.
874
875   Proof. Let R ∈ R2k×2k be a real matrix satisfying the following properties:
876   1. Skew-symmetry with respect to the inner product:
877
                                    ⟨Rv, w⟩ = −⟨Rw, v⟩,          ∀ v, w ∈ R2k .
878
879   2. Magnitude preserving:
880                                     ⟨Rv, Rv⟩ = ⟨v, v⟩,       ∀ v ∈ R2k .
881
882   Recall that the standard inner product in R2k is given by ⟨v, w⟩ = v⊤ w, which is symmetric:
883   ⟨v, w⟩ = ⟨w, v⟩.
884   From the skew-symmetry condition, we have:
885
                                   ⟨Rv, w⟩ + ⟨Rw, v⟩ = 0,          ∀ v, w ∈ R2k .
886
887   Since ⟨Rw, v⟩ = (Rw)⊤ v = w⊤ R⊤ v, the above condition becomes:
888
                                   v⊤ R⊤ w + w⊤ R⊤ v = 0,          ∀ v, w ∈ R2k .
889
890   This implies that R⊤ is skew-symmetric:
891
                                                     R⊤ = −R.
892
893   From the magnitude-preserving property, we have:
894
895                      ⟨Rv, Rv⟩ = (Rv)⊤ Rv = v⊤ R⊤ Rv = v⊤ v,                ∀ v ∈ R2k .
896   Therefore,
897                                                 R⊤ R = I2k .
898   Using R⊤ = −R, we obtain:
899

1377   Intransitivity in Game Theory. The symmetric zero-sum game and its intransitivity have also been
1378   frequently studied in the context of game theory. Balduzzi et al. (2018) was motivated by evaluation
1379   among different agents, showing that any symmetric zero-sum game can be decomposed into a
1380   “transitive” game and a “cyclic” game, and proposed Nash averaging for better agent/task evaluation.
1381   Balduzzi et al. (2019) generalized the results from matrix games to functional-form games and
1382   propose new algorithms to construct diverse populations of effective agents. Czarnecki et al. (2020)
1383   investigated the geometrical properties of real-world games (e.g., Tic-Tac-Toe, Go, StarCraft II) and
1384   proposed that real-world games have a “spinning top” geometry, with a strong transitive dimension
1385
       and gradually diminishing non-transitive cyclic dimensions. Very recently, Bertrand et al. (2023)
       examined the limitations of the Elo rating system and proposed an alternative “disc decomposition”
1386
       method that can better handle both transitive and cyclic game dynamics.
