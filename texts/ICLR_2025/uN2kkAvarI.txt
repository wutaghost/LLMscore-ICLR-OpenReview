009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Online reinforcement learning (RL) with sparse rewards poses a challenge partly
012            because of the lack of feedback on states leading to the goal. Furthermore, ex-
013            pert offline data with reward signal is rarely available to provide this feedback
014            and bootstrap online learning. How can we guide online agents to the right so-
015            lution without this on-task data? Reward shaping offers a solution by providing
016            fine-grained signal to nudge the policy towards the optimal solution. However,
017            reward shaping often requires domain knowledge to hand-engineer heuristics for
018
               a specific goal. To enable more general and inexpensive guidance, we propose
               and analyze a data-driven methodology that automatically guides RL by learning
019
               from widely available video data such as Internet recordings, off-task demonstra-
020
               tions, task failures, and undirected environment interaction. By learning a model
021            of optimal goal-conditioned value from diverse passive data, we open the floor to
022            scaling up and using a wide variety of data sources to model general goal-reaching
023            behaviors relevant to guiding online RL. Specifically, we use intent-conditioned
024            value functions to learn from diverse video and incorporate these goal-conditioned
025            values into the reward. Our experiments show that video-trained value functions
026            work well with a variety of data sources, exhibit positive transfer from human
027            video pre-training, can generalize to unseen goals, and scale with dataset size.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Many sequential decision-making tasks are naturally defined with a sparse reward, meaning the
033
      agent only receives positive signal when the goal has been achieved. Unfortunately, these sparse
      reward tasks are especially challenging in reinforcement learning (RL) (Sutton, 2018) since they
034
      provide no signal at intermediate states, effectively requiring exhaustive search. Practitioners often
035
      resort to collecting task-relevant prior data (Pomerleau, 1988) or hand-designing task-relevant dense
036   reward functions (Mataric, 1994). However, manually collecting this high-quality data or defining a
037   task-specific reward is time-intensive and not general.
038
039
      To solve this problem in RL, we should guide the search procedure online towards the desired goal.
      This dictates the usage of some general prior informing the agent what states lead to others to direct
040
      it to the goal. Humans make use of extensive prior knowledge when attempting to accomplish
041
      tasks: for example, we know that finding a mug generally requires us to try looking in cabinets and
042   that opening them requires interacting with the handle. We posit that this prior can in fact be learned
043   with task-agnostic environment data and general manipulation videos to develop a sense of “how the
044   world works.” This data is easily collected in the environment or mined from the web, respectively.
045
      To leverage both of these data types, we choose to learn from video, enabling the use of a myriad
046
      of datasets without needing embodiment-specific actions or task-specific rewards. The nature of
047
      video data availability on the web also allows for training on other environments. We hypothesize
048   that learning models from various video sources will expand the data support, enabling generaliza-
049   tion and successful goal-reaching guidance. Crucially, we elect to represent our prior as a goal-
050   conditioned state-value function V (s, g), that for any image s and desired target g, estimates the
051   temporal distance between the two states. Learning this type of model easily plugs into online RL
052   by penalizing the predicted distance from the goal. Also, using a value-learning approach allows
053   ingesting suboptimal reaching data, further relaxing our requirements for the training data, as op-
      posed to other behavioral prior methods (Escontrela et al., 2023). Lastly, goal-conditioned value

087   We observe that reformulating online sparse RL problems with Video-trained Value functions (ViVa)
088   shows a number of benefits. Firstly, we see generalization to new goals unseen in prior data in the

090
      (Singh et al., 2020). Thirdly, we see that pre-training on Ego4D can significantly improve perfor-
091
      mance but is not sufficient to solve online RL alone, necessitating some environment finetuning.
092   Lastly, we see that ViVa improves online performance as data scale increases and can enable solving
093   complex robotic tasks on Franka Kitchen (Gupta et al., 2019), another robotic simulator.

096   2   R ELATED W ORK
097
098   Solving sparse online RL problems is a difficult challenge due to the lack of reward feedback. One
099   way to make it easier is to better explore the environment to more reliably reach the goal state and
100   begin backing up rewards. They range from simple noisy behaviors (Haarnoja et al., 2018b) to
101   structured behavioral priors (Ecoffet et al., 2021; Bharadhwaj et al., 2021; Kearns & Singh, 2002;
102   Brafman & Tennenholtz, 2003). Some methods utilize intrinsic bonuses (Schmidhuber, 2010) to
103   minimize uncertainty (Kolter & Ng, 2009; Pathak et al., 2019; Houthooft et al., 2017; Still & Pre-
104
      cup, 2012) or to seek novelty (Burda et al., 2018; Pathak et al., 2017; Ostrovski et al., 2017; Tang
      et al., 2017; Bellemare et al., 2016). Unfortunately, these methods break down in complex visual
105
      environments and intricate robotic control settings due to the large state and action space.
106
107   To narrow this search, a prior is desirable to inform the agent of what states or actions to explore
      more. One way to do this is to inject domain knowledge into the reward function, guiding it to

      the goal. This family of approaches, known as reward shaping, can accelerate learning the opti-
109   mal policy (Ng et al., 1999; Mataric, 1994; Hu et al., 2020; Devlin & Kudenko, 2012; Wiewiora,
110   2003). However, hand-crafting these rewards does not generally scale to many tasks and is often
111   over-designed for one domain (Jiang et al., 2020; Mahmood et al., 2018; Haarnoja et al., 2018a;
112   Malysheva & Kudenko, 2018; Hussein et al., 2017; Brys et al., 2015). A more ideal way to have a
113   general prior is to learn it from a wide range of available data. In this work, we explore the effect of
114   various video data sources in providing robotics-relevant dynamics information to downstream RL.
115   Many methods elect to use this cheap video data to learn a rich image representation through re-
116   construction objectives (Xiao et al., 2022), constrastive learning (Nair et al., 2022), value-functions
117   (Bhateja et al., 2023), or predictive objectives (Shah & Kumar, 2021). There is also a family of
118   approaches that model videos through inferring latent actions from states, and use environment-
119   specific action-labelled data to map these latent actions to real actions (Ye et al., 2024; Edwards
120   et al., 2019; Schmidt & Jiang, 2024; Bruce et al., 2024). Bhateja et al. (2023) propose V-PTR which
121   is particularly similar to our approach but only utilizes the trained ICVF encoders as a pre-trained
122   representation for offline RL. Our method aims to use a distance-function rather than a pre-trained
123
      encoder to directly guide a goal-conditioned online RL agent. This resembles temporal distance
      learning methods (Pong et al., 2020; Mezghani et al., 2023) such as Dynamical Distance Learning
124
      (DDL) (Hartikainen et al., 2020) where policy-conditioned distance learning and online RL for dis-
125
      tance minimization is alternated. However, DDL uses distances for unsupervised skill discovery and
126   preference-learning, and importantly do not extend to internet-scale interaction data.
127
128   The most similar approach is Value-Implicit Pretraining (VIP) (Ma et al., 2023) whereby a internet-
129
      scale video-trained representation function induces a distance to shape the reward. Our method
      differs from VIP in a few different ways. First, our method explicitly uses temporal-difference
130
      learning as opposed to time-contrastive learning, as done in VIP. Second, we explicitly focus on
131
      downstream online RL rather than direct imitation or smooth trajectory optimization. Third, we
132   present a bi-level pre-training procedure to not only take advantage of task-agnostic human video,
133   but also environmental interaction data. We therefore identify with other offline-to-online methods
134   (Xie et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2021; Agarwal et al., 2022; Zheng et al., 2023; Andrychowicz et al.,
135   2018; Li et al., 2023) whereas VIP compares to other pre-trained representation distances. These
136   offline-to-online methods often assume action access though which limits the scope of usable data.
137   Our method’s access to environmental interaction data dictates comparison to RLPD (Ball et al.,
138   2023), a method which runs online RL and mixes training batches with offline data samples, as well
139   as JSRL (Uchendu et al., 2023), a method which condenses offline data into a policy to assist online
140
      exploration.

         5.2                     E XPERIMENTS
354
355      Corrupted AntMaze We first use the D4RL AntMaze (Fu et al., 2021) environment to visually
356      analyze the robustness to states seen outside of the training distribution. Environment and training
357      details are further expanded upon in Appendix A.3. We modify the D4RL diverse prior dataset
358      which includes the training transitions of a random start goal-reaching policy. Importantly, we
359      corrupt this dataset by removing all trajectories containing points near the goal-region as shown in
360      Figure 2. We train a 3-layer Multilayer Perceptron with 512 units each using Equation 4 as the
361      training objective and display the learned value function, after 45 minutes of training on a Tesla
362      V100 16GB GPU, in Figure 2. Evidently, we observe generalization to the goal region when it has
363
         not been seen during value training. The benefits of this generalization can be seen when running
         downstream online RL are shown in Figure 4. We conclude that learning a simple ICVF network
364
         on offline data is enough to develop a prior that generalizes to the unseen goal and prevents the
365
         failures that RLPD exhibits on sparse-reward tasks when the offline dataset doesn’t contain the
366      goal. Our comparison shows that JSRL exhibits similar extrapolation to new goals in the space of
367      expert actions as opposed to values. However, this similar extrapolation ability seems to fail when
368      introduced to more complex visual environments. In these settings ViVa is able to take advantage of
369      Ego4D pre-training whereas JSRL cannot.
370
         RoboVerse off-task transfer We use the RoboVerse (Singh et al., 2020) simulator (COG) to test

      6   D ISCUSSION
458
459
      In this paper, we proposed a method for transferring goal-reaching priors found in video data to
      downstream online RL problems by learning an intent-conditioned value function. This method can
460
      enable sparse-reward task solving, generalization to new goals, and positive transfer between tasks.
461
      Our analysis of ViVa illustrates the importance of using value function pre-training on video data as
462   opposed to other methods of utilizing prior data. Our scaling experiments show that this is due to
463   the wide support that this method can take advantage of, namely from the availability and generality
464   of video data as well as the lack of assumptions for value learning.
465
      Comparisons with JSRL depict the superiority of value functions as a representation of prior data as
466
      opposed to classical imitative policies. We hypothesize this is because value learning uses a method
467
      akin to shortest-path finding within data to discover an underlying temporal structure as opposed
468   to naively matching the next action. Furthermore, direct imitative policies prevent support from
469   action-free data sources such as Ego4D. However, latent-imitation methods could be explored to
470   leverage actionless datasets. Regardless, the ViVa paradigm should therefore provide insight to RL
471   practitioners looking to harness extra data and ameliorate the absence of rewarded prior data.
472
      Limitations and future work We note that a limitation of value functions is the weak zero-shot ex-
473
      trapolation ability when far out of domain. This can be seen through the poor 0% scale performance
474   shown in Figure 6 which is presumably because RoboVerse is significantly different than Ego4D.
475   But when there is fine-tuning involved (shown at scales larger than 0% in Figure 6), this Ego4D
476   pretraining helps, offering 2x performance boost in the low-data regime. These results make it clear
477   that pre-training offers a way to make fine-tuning more effective, but cannot work on its own as it’d
478   need task-relevant data. A direction of future work would be to find ways to encode more explicit
479   forms of abstraction in the value function in order to extrapolate deeply when given only off-domain
480   pre-training data (such as Ego4D). This would help to improve pure zero-shot performance when
481   given no environment data.
482   We also notice that ViVa utilizes some action-labelled robotic data for fine-tuning which is assumed
483   to be exploratory or somewhat relevant to the downstream task. An exciting future direction would
484   be to pair ViVa without fine-tuning with an exploration algorithm online to run the fine-tuning dur-
485   ing the online RL phase, thus simplifying the training pipeline by removing a separate fine-tuning
      phase. This method would also allow for resolving value errors through collecting counterfactual


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      examples since these errors can be detrimental to performance by forcing the agent into states that
487   are erroneously near the goal. This way, ViVa could even be used to form a curriculum based on
488   state-values or uncertainties in state-values to guide exploration in harder problems. Lastly, a natu-
489   ral extension includes utilizing language goals for the intent-conditioned value function, harnessing
490   multi-modal features, and extending into the real-world.

      shown in Table 1. We preprocess the video dataset by shaping to 256 × 256, center cropping the
      middle 224 × 224, then resizing it to 128 × 128. The ICVF itself is structured with an encoder
730
      which converts the state, future outcome, and goal into embeddings. For the encoder, we utilize the
731
      26-layer ResNet-v2. The training loss is displayed in Figure 8. We train with 1 v4 TPU for 1.5 days.
732
733   Once embedded, we concatenate the latents and pass them into an ensemble of 2 Multilayer Per-
734   ceptrons, each with LayerNorm and to produce the value estimate. We train the ICVF for 1 million
735
      steps. For RoboVerse and Franka Kitchen, we apply the same exact training process, but on a the
      fine-tuning dataset. Antmaze doesn’t utilize pretraining and functions on states, so it has a different
736
      setup. For our final experiments, we swept across checkpoints to identify strong value functions to
