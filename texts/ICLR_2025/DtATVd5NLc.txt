009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Climate prediction models parameterize a range of atmospheric-oceanic processes
012            like clouds, turbulence, and gravity waves. These physical parameterizations are
013            a leading source of uncertainty and strongly influence future projections of global
014            temperature rise. We present a fresh approach to developing parameterizations for
015            coarse-climate models by leveraging pre-trained AI foundation models (FMs) for
016            weather and climate. A pre-trained encoder and decoder from a 2.3 billion param-
017            eter FM (NASA and IBM’s Prithvi WxC) — which contains a latent probabilistic
018            representation of atmospheric evolution — is fine-tuned to create a data-driven
019            predictor of atmospheric gravity waves (GWs). Current climate models are not
020
               fine enough to resolve GWs. We create an ML-based parameterization that learns
               GW fluxes from high-resolution “GW resolving” climate models to represent them
021
               in “GW missing” coarse-climate models. The fluxes predicted by our fine-tuned
022
               model are comprehensively evaluated using a set of three tests. Comparison with
023            a baseline (Attention U-Net) reveals the superior predictive performance of the
024            fine-tuned model throughout the atmosphere. The model outperforms the baseline
025            even in regions excluded from the FM pre-training. This is quantified using the
026            Hellinger distance which is 0.11 for the baseline and 0.06, i.e., roughly half, for
027            the fine-tuned model. FMs are largely unexplored in climate science. Our findings
028            emphasize their versatility and reusability to accomplish a range of weather- and
029            climate-related downstream applications, especially in a low-data regime. These
030            FMs can be further leveraged to create new parameterizations for other earth-
031
               system processes.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Accurate prediction of future climate is a trillion-dollar challenge with critical consequences for the
037   world economy, food security, global health, and urban planning. State-of-the-art future climate
038   projections are highly uncertain. Obtaining reliable projections of future climate requires urgent
039   improvements in existing climate models, many of which are strongly influenced by parametric
040   uncertainty, scenario uncertainty, and structural uncertainty (Morrison & Lawrence, 2020; Lee et al.,
041   2023). This study aims at demonstrating the untapped potential of AI foundation models to improve
042   numerical climate models by addressing one of the leading sources of climate model uncertainty:
043   physical parameterizations.
044   Foundation models (FMs) can be broadly defined as flexible task-agnostic models which are pre-
045   trained using a self-supervised learning objective (Bommasani et al., 2022). Pre-trained FMs are
046   then fine-tuned to perform a broad range of sub-tasks a.k.a. downstream tasks. A good example is
047   OpenAI’s ChatGPT, which is first pre-trained on large language datasets and is subsequently fine-
048   tuned to perform several other language-related tasks.
049   FMs are largely unexplored in climate science. Only a couple of weather and geospatial FMs exist
050   to date (AtmoRep (Lessig et al., 2023), ClimaX (Nguyen et al., 2023), and Prithvi (Jakubik et al.,
051   2023). Otherwise, the use of large AI models in meteorology, like FourCastNet, PanguWeather, and
052   GraphCast (Pathak et al., 2022; Bi et al., 2023; Lam et al., 2023), is mostly restricted to weather
053   prediction. Simply put, despite substantial training costs, these models have been limited to accom-
      plishing just one task: medium-range weather forecasting. In this study, we use a state-of-the-art

      FM, Prithvi WxC (Schmude et al., 2024), for weather and climate applications and apply it for the
055   downstream task of developing data-driven physical parameterizations for climate models.

057   Background: Numerical climate models couple together multiple components of the earth system
058
      (atmosphere, ocean, land, ice, etc.) to predict climate evolution over years, decades, centuries, and
      beyond. Climate models often operate at a grid resolution of 100-300 km. This resolution is direly
059
      insufficient to even resolve the smaller-scale processes like clouds, precipitation, turbulence, gravity
060
      waves, etc. These processes are crucial for global energy balance. The traditional approach is to
061   couple the computational fluid solver a.k.a. the dynamical core with a suite of physical parame-
062   terizations to crudely capture the unresolved effects (Alexander & Dunkerton, 1999; Lott & Miller,
063   1997; Bogenschutz et al., 2012; Iacono et al., 2000, to name a few parameterizations).

084   As is discussed in Hourdin et al. (2017), most parameterizations are an inadequate representation
085   of the respective process physics. They are subjected to a series of simplifications that compromise
086   the process physics. This can be attributed partly to a limited process understanding and partly
087   to the need to be efficient emulators that adhere to a prescribed climate model design. So, errors
088   stemming from their design and parametric tuning often add up and result in inaccurate dynamics
089   and momentum imbalances, leading to uncertainties in future climate projections (Golaz et al., 2013;
090   Mauritsen et al., 2012; Zhao et al., 2018).
091   Related research: Using AI to learn from data and improve climate model parameterizations is an
092   area of active research (see Mansfield et al., 2023; Eyring et al., 2024, for a review). Recent deep
093   learning approaches include (a) learning process evolution from high-resolution models or parame-
094   terization data to represent it in coarse-resolution models (Espinosa et al., 2022; Chantry et al., 2021;
095   Yuval & O’Gorman, 2023; Gupta et al., 2024b; Lu et al., 2024), (b) using equation discovery or sim-
096   ilar techniques to learn analytical forms of sub-grid scale momentum closures (Zanna & Bolton,
097   2020; Jakhar et al., 2024), and (c) hybrid probabilistic combination of single-scenario high-fidelity
098
      data and multi-scenario low-fidelity data (Bhouri et al., 2023). Irrespective of the approach, the
      scarcity of high-resolution high-quality training data and low generalizability limits rapid progress.
099
100   Contribution: In this study, we introduce a fresh approach to promote the development of AI-driven
101   climate model parameterizations. We blend the pre-trained encoders and decoders from the new
102   Prithvi WxC foundation model with high-quality downstream task-specific data. We hence create
103   a fine-tuned AI model that can skillfully predict and represent the missing subgrid-scale physics
104
      in coarse-climate models (which do not resolve the process). We demonstrate the effectiveness of
      our approach using atmospheric gravity waves (GWs) as a test process. The approach could be
105
      generalized to other processes like clouds and precipitation.
106
107   GWs are intermittent, small-scale (spatial scale O(1)-O(1000) km) perturbations generated around
      thunderstorms, jet stream disturbances, strong flow over mountains, etc. (Fritts & Alexander, 2003).


                                                                 2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      GWs couple the different layers of the atmosphere by carrying near-surface momentum and energy
109   to stratospheric and even mesospheric heights. GWs influence clear air turbulence, surface extremes,
110   stratospheric circulation, and ocean heat transport. Thus, they are crucial to the earth’s momentum
111   budget yet are not resolved in climate models due to coarse grid resolution (Plougonven et al., 2020).
112
      Scientific importance: a coarse O(100) km climate model practically misses all GW effects be-
113
      cause it cannot resolve these small-scale waves (Achatz et al., 2023). So, we develop an ML model
114
      that learns GW effects from a high-resolution climate model/data (which resolves a substantial por-
115   tion of the GW physics). This model can then be coupled to a coarse-resolution climate model to
116   represent “missing” GW physics. This principle opens avenues to develop more physics-inclusive
117   ML schemes to represent other missing processes (like clouds and precipitation) in coarse climate
118   models.
119
      Instantaneous prediction: our fine-tuned model skillfully predicts the GW momentum fluxes given
120
      the background atmospheric state, as shown in Figure 1. The structure of the excited GWs on 18
121   July 2015 is shown in Figure 1a. The model predicts the intermittent intensification of the fluxes
122   around the Andes in South America and the Prince Charles Mountains in Antarctica. The intense
123   fluxes over the Andes extend over to the Drake Passage and parts of the Southern Ocean, indicating
124   that the finetuned model can learn and represent the lateral propagation and transient evolution of the
125   generated waves; a physical feature absent in current model parameterizations (Kruse et al., 2022).
126
      Our approach takes Prithvi WxC’s high-dimensional latent space and clubs it with limited GW data
127   from ERA5 to create a generalizable data-driven scheme for coarse-climate models:
128
129          • Faster training, better performance, heterogenous data: using pre-trained encoders and
130            decoders allows faster training of the fine-tuned model compared to task-specific baselines.
131            Despite different data sources for pre-training and finetuning, the fine-tuned model out-
132            performs the baseline in predicting the global momentum flux distribution, regional flux
133
               distribution, and intermittent flux evolution.
134          • Generalizes well to new regions: the fine-tuned model outperforms the specialized base-
135            line model even in the middle-to-upper stratosphere region where Prithvi WxC was not
136
               pre-trained.
137          • Improved physics representation: our scheme represents key aspects of GW physics
138            which traditional climate model parameterizations do not: transient evolution (vs. steady-
139
               state evolution), and full three-dimensional evolution (vs. pure vertical evolution).

143   2.1   T HE P RITHVI W X C F OUNDATION M ODEL FOR WEATHER AND CLIMATE
144
145
      Prithvi WxC, jointly developed by NASA and IBM, is a transformer-based deep learning archi-
      tecture which combines ideas from several recent transformer architectures in order to effectively
146
      process regional and global dependencies of the input data and to efficiently process longer sequence
147
      lengths of tokens. This allows the model to, for instance, run in different spatial contexts or infuse
148   additional tokens from off-grid measurements to the model during finetuning. Prithvi WxC has 2.3
149   billion trainable parameters and is trained on 160 atmospheric channels using 40 years of 3-hourly
150   MERRA-2 reanalysis data at a 0.5◦ ×0.625◦ spatial resolution.
151
      The validation of Prithvi WxC extends from zero shot evaluations for reconstruction and forecasting
152
      to other downstream tasks such as downscaling of weather and climate models, the prediction of
153
      hurricane tracks, and climate model parameterization. The architecture of the pre-training backbone
154   is shown in Figure 2. More details are provided in Schmude et al. (2024).

357   The dynamical evolution of atmospheric GWs can notably vary with height, region (latitude and
358   longitude), season, etc. The steady-state distributions conceal this. For a more stringent evaluation,
359   we divide the global domain into 5 regions and 4 altitudes. The five regions comprise the two
360   hemispheric poles, the two hemispheric midlatitudes, and the tropics. The four regions comprise the
361   lower troposphere, the upper troposphere, the lower stratosphere, and the upper stratosphere.
362   The observed and predicted monthly mean distributions over the 20 slices are shown in Figure 6.
363   The predicted and averaged fluxes agree quite well throughout the lower and upper troposphere. The
364   Hellinger distances are consistently lower than 0.02 in most cases; an exception being the northern
365   hemispheric poles in the upper troposphere. Within the troposphere, the baseline (in orange) has a
366   slightly better Hellinger distance than the finetuned model but both models largely agree well on the
367   captured distributions.
368   Compared to the troposphere, the Hellinger distances are higher throughout the stratosphere. The
369   tropics and midlatitudes in the lower stratosphere have distances within our 0.05 threshold, but
370   the polar regions have distances of up to 0.14. The baseline performance, however, gets much
371   worse in the upper stratosphere with distances reaching up to 0.62 in the northern hemisphere upper
372   stratosphere. In contrast, the distances for the fine-tuned model are constrained within 0.15. The
373   fine-tuned model, thus outperforms the baseline in the stratosphere, with conspicuous differences.
374   The baseline model has a lower variance than the fine-tuned model even as the Prithvi WxC encoder-
375   decoder model was not trained on upper stratospheric data at all (Figure 9). The performance im-
376   provement, then, can be attributed to the substantially higher volumes (20 years) of pre-training data
377   as opposed to merely four years of ERA5 data for baseline. This allows for more mature develop-
      ment of the FMs latent space, leading to more effective learning during fine-tuning.

480   4   D ISCUSSION
481
482   The application of foundation models in climate science is largely unexplored. The analysis pre-
483   sented here clearly establishes that the atmospheric evolution learned by large, transformer-based
484   architectures, can be leveraged to simplify, improve, and expedite the creation of physical parame-
485   terizations for climate models; ultimately improving climate prediction accuracy. From a machine
      learning perspective, since a foundation model (here Prithvi WxC) is typically trained on large


                                                                                   9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      amounts of data, its latent encoder-decoder space contains a rich abstract representation of the atmo-
487   spheric evolution. In the case of Prithvi WxC, the data ranges from winds, humidity, and radiation,
488   to even leaf area index and soil moisture. Rather than creating task-specific ANNs, CNNs, etc.
489   from scratch, we showed that pre-trained encoders from weather foundation models can instead be
490   leveraged to create better predictive models for atmospheric-oceanic processes. As an added benefit,
491   this approach allows blending data from multiple streams — synthetic high-resolution model data,
492   satellite trains, terrestrial remote sensing data, ground observations, etc.
493   Our fine-tuned model learns a new atmospheric process, gravity waves, from a totally different
494   dataset, and clearly outperforms the Attention U-Net baseline on all three metrics. In doing so,
495   we also devised a new metric – the tail-Hellinger distance – which allows focusing explicitly on
496   the distribution tails. More specifically, our fine-tuned model both learns the three-dimensional
497   evolution of GWs in the atmosphere and generalizes to regions unseen during training. As a result,
498   this model can arguably represent the missing gravity wave physics in coarse-climate models better
499   than traditional single-column physical parameterizations. This provides further incentive to develop
500   data-driven parameterizations for other parameterized processes like clouds and precipitaion.
501
502   4.1   L IMITATIONS AND FUTURE WORK
503
      First, skillful offline performance does not necessarily equate to skillful online performance
504
      (Brenowitz et al., 2020). Thus, efforts are under way to couple our fine-tuned scheme to a coarse-
505
      climate model and assess its online performance and speed.
506
507   Second, numerical climate models, climate reanalyses, and data assimilation systems can have sys-
508   tematic biases. Training fine-tuned models on a given foundation model thus presents the danger of
509
      the inherent biases in training data to be carried over to the fine-tuned model. Such potential dan-
      gers can be alleviated by (a) using multiple data streams, for e.g., using data from multiple climate
510
      reanalyses, (b) by combining high-resolution climate data from models with multiple underlying
511
      numerics (spectral, finite-volume, spectral element, etc.), (c) using climate model data from mul-
512   tiple climate-change scenarios, (d) by using high-quality data during fine-tuning, and (e) using a
513   probabilistic ensemble of initialization to quantify predictive uncertainty. As a straightforward test,
514   the encoders and decoders from other large AI weather forecasting models can be used to develop
515   and intercompare a series of fine-tuned climate model parameterizations (work in progress).
516
      Third, ERA5 still misses a substantial portion of atmospheric gravity waves (with wavelengths
517
      shorter than 150-200 km), so our fine-tuning data is certainly not of the highest quality. This will
518   be improved in future work where the fine-tuning will be accomplished using kilometer-scale high-
519   resolution models instead. Finally, while our fine-tuned scheme generalizes well to regions unseen
520   during pre-training, limited fine-tuning still questions its generalizability to future-climate scenarios.
521   This is yet to be tested and is left for future work.
522
523   4.2   B ROADER I MPACT
524
525   Foundation models open avenues to using multi-source observations to facilitate AI-powered climate
526   research. Due to constraints on computing power, we are still decades away from being able to
527   run climate models (such as those participating in CMIP) multiple decades and centuries into the
528
      future at kilometer or sub-kilometer resolutions. This means climate prediction will continue to
      miss crucial sub-grid physics and will continue to rely on physical parameterizations of unresolved
529
      processes. This prohibits effective climate action and decision-making and also limits a complete
530
      mechanistic understanding of our climate. We have demonstrated a strong application of an existing
531   weather and climate foundation model to climate model improvement. Ideally, foundation models
532   like Prithvi WxC can be used for a whole spectrum of other climate applications.
533
534
      Since the fine-tuned models can be less costly to train than the baselines — because only a frac-
      tion of parameters are retrained — this approach also has the potential to reduce the carbon emis-
535
      sions associated with the from-scratch training of task-specific ML emulators. Used alongside other
536
      foundation models, like Prithvi HLS (Jakubik et al., 2023), one can use this approach to even cre-
537   ate lightweight, finetuned models for key applications like wildfire prediction, predicting hurricane
538   storm surges, and regional heat wave impacts, potentially improving extreme climate prediction and
539   climate change preparedness.

765   We devised a new metric to compare the distribution tails, the tail-Hellinger distance, as follows:
766   Assume two distributions, p and q, with q being the true underlying distribution and p being the pre-
767   dicted distribution. The tail-Hellinger distribution metric most effectively captures the differences
768   in the tails regions if:
769
      (a) the distance of p is computed w.r.t. the underlying prior q. Unlike the Hellinger distance, the

775   R V captures the extremes of the distribution for the prescribed tolerance ϵ. Thus, by definition,
