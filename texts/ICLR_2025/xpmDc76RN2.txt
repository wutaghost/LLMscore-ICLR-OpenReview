009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In this paper, we analyze the optimization of operator networks for solving ellip-
012            tic PDEs with variational loss functions. While approximation and generalization
013            errors in operator networks have been extensively studied, optimization error re-
014            mains largely unexplored. We apply Restricted Strong Convexity (RSC) theory to
015            rigorously examine the optimization dynamics of operator networks trained with
016            variational loss, providing theoretical guarantees for convergence and training sta-
017            bility. We further investigate the role of the condition number of A in optimization
018
               and demonstrate that preconditioning strategies significantly improve convergence
               rates, establishing a solid theoretical basis for the empirical benefits of precondi-
019
               tioning. We also address the lower bound of a key quantity, qt , which ensures con-
020
               vergence. To prevent qt from vanishing, we propose an algorithm that adaptively
021            incorporates additional weights into the variational loss function, leveraging val-
022            ues already computed during training, thereby avoiding any extra computational
023            costs. Finally, we validate our theoretical assumptions through numerical exper-
024            iments, demonstrating their practical applicability and confirming the effective-
025            ness of preconditioning, with significant improvements in training performance
026            and convergence rates.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Scientific machine learning (SciML) has advanced through approaches like physics-informed neural
032   networks (PINNs) (Raissi et al., 2019), the Deep Ritz Method (DRM) (Yu et al., 2018), and the Deep
033
      Galerkin Method (DGM) (Sirignano & Spiliopoulos, 2018), which use neural networks to approx-
      imate solutions to complex partial differential equations (PDEs). Additionally, operator learning
034
      methods, such as the Deep Operator Network (DeepONet) (Lu et al., 2021) and Fourier Neural Op-
035
      erator (FNO) (Li et al., 2020), map input parameters (e.g., initial/boundary conditions or forcing
036   terms) directly to PDE solutions. Originally developed as supervised learning methods, DeepONet
037   and FNO have later been expanded to include the principles of PINNs, enabling them to work in
038   unsupervised learning scenarios as well. They are referred to as Physics-Informed Deep Operator
039   Networks (PI-DeepONet) (Wang et al., 2021b) and Physics-Informed Neural Operators (PINO) (Li
040   et al., 2024), respectively. As a result, these methods allow for the modeling of intricate physical
041   systems while reducing dependency on extensive labeled datasets.
042   However, many of these methods face optimization challenges, particularly in imposing accurate
043   boundary conditions and balancing the physics-informed loss with the boundary loss (Wong et al.,
044   2022; Krishnapriyan et al., 2021). Furthermore, the loss functions typically involve derivatives of the
045   network with respect to input variables, leading to a highly intricate optimization landscape. Addi-
046   tionally, even when the loss functions converge near zero, there is no guarantee that the approximate
047   solutions are close to the true PDE solutions.
048   In recent years, operator learning methods based on the variational loss form have gained attention
049   as a means to improve the accuracy and efficiency of solving PDEs. Notable examples include
050   the Finite Element Operator Network (FEONet) (Lee et al., 2023) and the Unsupervised Legendre
051   Galerkin Network (ULGNET) (Choi et al., 2023), which approximate PDE solutions by combining
052   neural networks with classical numerical methods, such as the Finite Element Method and Spectral
053   Methods. These methods effectively circumvent the aforementioned issues by incorporating basis
      functions. The use of basis functions, combined with variational losses, not only eliminates the need


                                                        1
054
      for additional penalty losses to impose boundary conditions but also simplifies the loss structure
055   by removing the need to differentiate neural networks with respect to input variables. This enables
056   them to handle singularly perturbed problems, boundary layer problems, and complex geometries
057   more efficiently (Choi et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2023).
058
      With the advancement of machine learning techniques, the need for rigorous theoretical analysis
059
      has become increasingly evident. In particular, the demand for a deeper understanding of optimiza-
060
      tion, which significantly affects both the efficiency and stability of training, has grown substantially.
061   Most traditional optimization frameworks are primarily grounded in convex settings (Boyd & Van-
062   denberghe, 2004). However, it is well known that deep learning models are challenging to analyze
063   within these frameworks (Liu et al., 2020). To address this limitation, the Neural Tangent Kernel
064   (NTK) theory (Jacot et al., 2018), along with the PŁ-condition (Garrigos & Gower, 2023), has been
065   developed to provide convergence guarantees, drawing significant attention in the deep learning
066   community (Du et al., 2019; Allen-Zhu et al., 2019; Zou et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2022; 2021a;
067   Gao et al., 2023). Nonetheless, these theories often necessitate that the model be infinitely wide or
068   confined to the near-initialization regime, which considerably diverges from practical applications.
069
      Another noteworthy framework for analyzing optimization is the Restricted Strong Convexity (RSC)
      framework. While RSC has been extensively studied in linear or convex settings (Wainwright, 2019;
070
      Negahban & Wainwright, 2012; Zhang & Cheng, 2015), its application to deep learning models has
071
      only been explored in recent years. Motivated by this, we aim to extend the RSC theory to SciML
072   techniques, which are known to be more challenging to analyze than traditional machine learning
073   methods. Specifically, our main contributions are as follows:
074
075          • We apply the RSC theory to investigate the optimization process using GD in unsupervised
076            operator learning methods with variational loss. Our analysis rigorously proves the conver-
077            gence of the optimization error, providing theoretical guarantees for training stability.
078          • Building on these theoretical insights, we examine the impact of the condition number of
079            A, which is determined by the PDE structures in operator networks using variational loss
080            forms, on the optimization process. We demonstrate how preconditioning strategies can
081            significantly improve convergence rates. This provides a solid theoretical foundation for
082
               the empirical observation that preconditioning enhances training efficiency.
083          • In addition, we propose an algorithm that adaptively improves the lower bound of qt , a key
084            quantity in optimization dynamics that ensures convergence. By adjusting the weights in
085            the variational loss function, the proposed algorithm prevents qt from vanishing during the
086
               training process, leading to improved convergence rates.
087          • Through numerical experiments, we validate the assumptions underlying our theoretical
088            analysis, demonstrating that they hold in most practical cases. These experiments further
089            confirm the effectiveness of preconditioning, showing significant improvements in both
090
               training performance and convergence rates.

      In this section, we analyze the optimization process of operator learning methods with variational
195
      loss using RSC theory. This provides an alternative convergence theory to the commonly used
196   NTK-based approaches. From this analysis, we explore the impact of the condition number on
197   convergence and also discuss the relationship between RSC theory and NTK theory.

199   3.1   RSC T HEORY IN THE O PERATOR N ETWORK WITH THE VARIATIONAL LOSS FORM
200
201   In this section, we develop a theoretical framework for analyzing the optimization process of oper-
202   ator learning methods based on the variational form using RSC theory. However, it is important to
203   note that this analysis is not restricted to our operator learning method alone. The same framework
204   can be extended to other operator learning methods or PINNs that employ variational loss functions.
205   RSC theory has been extensively studied in other settings, such as linear models and convex loss
206   functions (Wainwright, 2019; Negahban & Wainwright, 2012; Zhang & Cheng, 2015). More re-
207   cently, in the work of Banerjee et al. (2022), RSC theory has been applied to analyze the optimiza-
208   tion process of deep learning models for simple supervised learning tasks. Naturally, one might
209   hope to extend this analytical framework to the training dynamics of scientific machine learning
210   (SciML) methods. However, for many unsupervised approaches that use PDE residual loss func-
211   tions, intended to embed the physical laws described by PDEs into neural networks, the inherent
212
      complexity of these loss functions poses significant challenges for directly applying optimization
      theories, including RSC.
213
214   In contrast, as mentioned in Subsection 2.2, the methods of operator learning based on the variation
215   loss effectively circumvent these issues by leveraging the variational form of PDEs and employ-
      ing basis functions. Building on this advantage, we extend the RSC-based analysis to operator

      One well-known approach to reducing the condition number is the use of preconditioning. In this
325   method, a preconditioner matrix P is applied to transform the system of equations into an equivalent
326   system with a lower condition number. Specifically, preconditioning replaces the matrix A with
327   P A, where P is chosen to ensure that the condition number κ(P A) is significantly smaller than
328   that of A. This transformation results in a system that is easier to optimize and converges more
329   quickly.
330
      By showing that a reduced condition number directly improves the convergence rate in the optimiza-
331   tion process, our analysis explains why preconditioning leads to faster and more efficient training.
332   This alignment between theoretical and empirical results highlights the importance of precondition-

334   ULGNET. The numerical tests supporting these observations can be found in Section ??, where we

522   6   C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATIONS
523
524
525
      In this paper, we applied RSC theory to analyze the optimization process in unsupervised opera-
      tor learning methods utilizing variational loss. Our rigorous analysis demonstrated the convergence
526
      of the optimization error, establishing theoretical guarantees for training stability. We highlighted
527
      the significance of the condition number of A in influencing convergence rates and showed that
528   preconditioning strategies can substantially enhance training efficiency. Additionally, we addressed
529   the lower bound of qt , proposing an algorithm that adaptively improves this bound without incur-
530   ring extra computational costs. Our numerical experiments validated the assumptions underlying
531   our theoretical framework and confirmed the effectiveness of preconditioning, revealing significant
532   improvements in training performance and convergence rates.
533
      Despite the promising results, this study has certain limitations. First, while the theoretical analysis
534   is grounded in RSC, the applicability of these results may vary in highly complex or non-standard
535   optimization scenarios. Additionally, the proposed algorithm relies on the assumption that the con-
536   dition number of A can be effectively managed throughout the training process, which may not
537   hold true in all real-world applications. Future work should explore the robustness of the proposed
538   method in diverse contexts and assess its performance under varying conditions. Moreover, addi-
539   tional empirical studies are needed to further examine the impact of different weight matrices and
      their configurations on optimization dynamics.

      A     R ELATED WORKS
649
650   Variational loss in SciML Variational loss-based neural networks have emerged as an alterna-
651   tive to traditional PINNs, focusing on enhancing accuracy and stability through the use of varia-
652   tional principles. Prominent examples include VPINN, hp-VPINN, and Galerkin Neural Networks
653   (Kharazmi et al., 2019; ?; Ainsworth & Dong, 2021), which reformulate the residual minimization
654   problem into a variational form, employing test functions to minimize errors across the entire do-
655   main. This approach has demonstrated improved accuracy over standard PINNs, particularly when
656   dealing with weak solutions or complex boundary conditions. In the realm of operator learning,
657   methods such as ULGNET (Choi et al., 2023) and FEONET (Lee et al., 2023) also utilize variational
658
      loss forms. Similar to variational neural networks, these operator learning methods incorporate basis
      functions, enabling them to effectively manage complex domains and challenging boundary condi-
659
      tions. By leveraging these basis functions, these methods provide improved accuracy and stability
660
      when solving PDEs in intricate geometries or in the presence of singular perturbations. While this
661   paper focuses on operator networks with variational losses, our approach can also be extended to
662   other operator learning methods or PINNs that employ variational loss functions.
663
664   RSC and NTK The NTK theory (Jacot et al., 2018) has been widely utilized to analyze optimiza-
665   tion in deep learning (Du et al., 2019; Allen-Zhu et al., 2019; Zou et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2022;
666   2021a; Gao et al., 2023). While NTK-based approaches offer strong theoretical insights into the
667   convergence of gradient descent, they generally require extremely wide neural networks and depend
668   on the near-initialization regime, which limits their practical applicability. This limitation is particu-
669   larly evident in the case of PINNs, where NTK theory struggles to address the complexity introduced
670
      by PDE residuals (Bonfanti et al., 2024). In contrast, the recently developed RSC theory serves as
      an alternative tool for analyzing the optimization process of deep learning models. Although RSC
671
      has been previously applied to demonstrate geometric convergence in various settings (Wainwright,
672
      2019; Negahban & Wainwright, 2012; Zhang & Cheng, 2015), its application to deep learning was
673   introduced only recently by Banerjee et al. (2022). Building on this work, we extend RSC theory to
674   operator learning methods with variational loss forms, highlighting its potential as a new analytical
675   tool in the field of SciML.

       C.4   P RECONDITIONING
1081
1082   In our experiments, we applied two types of preconditioning techniques as follows:
1083
1084         • Type 1: For this approach, we selected the preconditioner P as the exact inverse of the
1085           matrix A. This method fully compensates for the condition number of A.
1086         • Type 2: In this case, we chose P as a diagonal matrix, where each diagonal entry is the
1087           inverse of the corresponding diagonal element of A. While less accurate than Type 1, this
1088           method reduces the computational cost associated with the inverse of the full matrix A,
1089           providing a balance between efficiency and accuracy.
