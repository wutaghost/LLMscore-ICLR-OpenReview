                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               Representation learning is widely used for estimating causal quantities (e.g., the
012            conditional average treatment effect) from observational data. While existing rep-
013            resentation learning methods have the benefit of allowing for end-to-end learning,
014            they do not have favorable theoretical properties of Neyman-orthogonal learners,
015            such as double robustness and quasi-oracle efficiency. Also, such representation
016            learning methods often employ additional constraints, like balancing, which may
017            even lead to inconsistent estimation. In this paper, we propose a novel class of
018            Neyman-orthogonal learners for causal quantities defined at the representation
019            level, which we call OR-learners. Our OR-learners have several practical advan-
020
               tages: they allow for consistent estimation of causal quantities based on any learned
               representation, while offering favorable theoretical properties including double
021
               robustness and quasi-oracle efficiency. In numerous experiments, we show that,
022
               under certain regularity conditions, our OR-learners improve existing represen-
023            tation learning methods and achieve state-of-the-art performance. To the best of
024            our knowledge, our OR-learners are the first work to provide a unified framework
025            of representation learning methods and Neyman-orthogonal learners for causal
026            quantities estimation.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030   Estimating causal quantities has many applications in medicine (Feuerriegel et al., 2024), policy-
031   making (Kuzmanovic et al., 2024), marketing (Varian, 2016), and economics (Basu et al., 2011). Here,
032   different causal quantities are of interest such as the conditional average treatment effect (CATE) and
033   the conditional average potential outcomes (CAPOs). For example, in personalized medicine, CATE
034   estimation can help in predicting the relative benefits of different treatment options, so that the one
035   with the best health outcome is selected.
036   Recently, representation learning methods have gained wide popularity in estimating causal quantities
037   from observational data (e.g., Johansson et al., 2016; Shalit et al., 2017; Hassanpour & Greiner,
038   2019a;b; Zhang et al., 2020; Assaad et al., 2021; Johansson et al., 2022). One benefit of representation
039   learning methods is that they allow for end-to-end learning. Specifically, these methods aim to learn
040   low-dimensional representations where sometimes additional constraints are enforced to tackle
041   inherently causal inductive biases. This typically helps to reduce the estimation variance, especially
042   in low-sample low-overlap settings. For example, balancing is a common constraint to reduce
043   the influence of instrumental variables among the covariates (Johansson et al., 2022), which helps
044
      to improve the finite-sample performance when the data-generating mechanism indeed has many
      instruments. Similarly, disentanglement aims to address an inductive bias that different nuisance
045
      functions might share or not share common information.
046
047   However, constraints on representations can be problematic: constrained representations can lose
048   their asymptotic validity when too strict constraints are applied and estimation becomes inconsistent.
049   This phenomenon is also known as representation-induced confounding bias (Johansson et al., 2019;
050
      Melnychuk et al., 2024). As a remedy, we later present a framework to quasi-oracle efficiently (and,
      thus, consistently) estimate causal quantities even based on asymptotically invalid representations.
051
052   A related literature stream seeks to estimate causal quantities through a model-agnostic framework of
053   Neyman-orthogonal learners. Prominent examples are the DR-learners and the R-learner (Vanstee-
      landt & Morzywołek, 2023; Morzywolek et al., 2023). They usually split estimation into two stages:

065   nuisance functions estimation and target model fitting, and, notably, any machine learning model can

067   learners offer several favorable theoretical properties. For example, under regularity conditions,
068   Neyman-orthogonal learners guarantee double robustness and quasi-oracle efficiency in asymptotic
069   regime (Chernozhukov et al., 2017; Foster & Syrgkanis, 2023). Further, by employing a separate
070   target model in the second stage, Neyman-orthogonal learners help to address another causal inductive
071   bias, namely that the ground-truth CATE function can be ”simpler” than individual CAPOs (Curth &

      van der Schaar, 2021a). Yet, the connections between Neyman-orthogonal learners and the end-to-end
      representation learning methods are still not well understood.
073
074   In this paper, we unify two streams of work, namely, representation learning methods and Neyman-
075   orthogonal learners. Specifically, we propose a novel, general framework to perform an asymptotically
076   quasi-oracle efficient (and, thus, consistent) estimation of causal quantities based on the learned
077   representations, which we call orthogonal representation learners (OR-learners). Our OR-learners
078
      are highly flexible as they target at estimating different causal quantities, like CAPOs and CATE, at
      the representation level of heterogeneity (Fig. 1). Furthermore, our OR-learners effectively solve the
079
      drawbacks of constrained representations (i.e., representation-induced confounding bias caused by too
080
      strict constraints) and bring favorable theoretical properties associated with Neyman-orthogonality,
081   namely, double robustness and quasi-oracle efficiency.
082
083   In sum, our contributions are as follows:1 (1) We introduce the OR-learners, a novel framework to
084
      unify representation learning methods and Neyman-orthogonal learners. (2) We show theoretically
      that our OR-learners address the drawbacks of existing end-to-end representation learning methods.
085
      That is, our OR-learners allow us to perform a quasi-oracle efficient estimation of causal quantities
086
      while offering other favorable properties related to Neyman-orthogonality. (3) We demonstrate that,
087   under regularity conditions, our OR-learners improve the performance in estimating causal quantities
088   for existing representation learning methods.

090   2       R ELATED W ORK
091
092   Our work aims to unify two streams of work, namely, representation learning methods and Neyman-
093   orthogonal learners. We briefly review both in the following (see the full overview in Appendix A).
094   Representation learning for estimating causal quantities. Several methods have been previously
095   introduced for end-to-end representation learning of CAPOs/CATE (see, in particular, the seminal
096   works by Johansson et al., 2016; Shalit et al., 2017; Johansson et al., 2022). A large number of
097   works later suggested different extensions to these. Existing methods fall into three main streams:
098   (1) One can fit an unconstrained shared representation to directly estimate both potential outcomes
099   surfaces (e.g., TARNet Shalit et al., 2017). (2) Some methods additionally enforce a balancing
100
      constraint based on empirical probability metrics, so that the distributions of the treated and untreated
      representations become similar (e.g., CFR and BNN Johansson et al., 2016; Shalit et al., 2017). Im-
101
      portantly, balancing based on empirical probability metrics is only guaranteed to perform a consistent
102
      estimation for invertible representations since, otherwise, balancing leads to a representation-induced
103   confounding bias (RICB) (Johansson et al., 2019; Melnychuk et al., 2024). Finally, (3) one can
104   additionally perform balancing by re-weighting the loss and the distributions of the representations
105   with learnable weights (e.g., RCFR Johansson et al., 2022). We later adopt the representation learning
106   methods from (1)–(3) as baselines.

      Neyman-orthogonal learners. Causal quantities can be estimated using model-agnostic methods,
109   so-called meta-learners (Künzel et al., 2019). Prominent examples are the R-learner (Nie & Wager,
110   2021) and DR-learner (Kennedy, 2023; Curth et al., 2020). Meta-learners have several practical
111   advantages (Morzywolek et al., 2023): (i) they oftentimes offer favorable theoretical guarantees
112   such as Neyman-orthogonality (Chernozhukov et al., 2017; Foster & Syrgkanis, 2023); (ii) they can
113   address the causal inductive bias that the CATE is “simpler” than CAPOs (Curth & van der Schaar,
114   2021a), and (iii) the target model obtains a clear interpretation as a projection of the ground-truth
115   CAPOs/CATE on the target model class. Curth & van der Schaar (2021b) provided a comparison of
116   meta-learners implemented via neural networks with different representations, yet with the target
117
      model based on the original covariates (the representations were only used as an interim tool to
      estimate nuisance functions). However, in our work, we study the learned representations as primary
118
      inputs to the target model.
119
120   Research gap. Our work is the first to unify representation learning methods and Neyman-orthogonal
121   learners. As a result, one can combine any representation learning method from above with our
122   OR-learners, which then (i) offer favorable properties of Neyman-orthogonality and (ii) address the
123
      causal inductive bias that the CATE is “simpler” than CAPOs.

160   Plug-in learners. A naı̈ve way to estimate CAPOs and CATE is to simply estimate µ̂x0 (x) and µ̂x1 (x)
161   and ‘plug them into’ the identification formulas for CAPOs and CATE. For example, an S-learner
      (S-Net) fits a single model with the treatment as an input, while a T-leaner (T-Net) builds two models


                                                           3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      for each treatment (Künzel et al., 2019). Many end-to-end representation learning methods, such as
163   TARNet (Shalit et al., 2017) and BNN without balancing (Johansson et al., 2016), can be seen as
164   variants of the plug-in learner: In the end-to-end fashion, they build a representation of the covariates
165   ϕ = Φ(x) ∈ Φ ⊆ Rdϕ and then use ϕ to estimate µ̂xa (x) = µ̂ϕa (Φ(x)) with the S-Net (BNN w/o
166   balancing) or the T-Net (TARNet).
167
      Yet, plug-in learners have several major drawbacks (Morzywolek et al., 2023; Vansteelandt &
168
      Morzywołek, 2023). (a) They do not account for the selection bias, namely, that µ̂x0 is estimated better
169   for the treated population and µ̂x1 for untreated. (b) In the case of CATE estimation, the plug-in learners
170   might additionally fail to address the causal inductive bias that the CATE is a “simpler” function than
171   both CAPOs, as it is impossible to add additional smoothing for the CATE model separately from
172   CAPOs models. (c) It is also unclear how to consistently estimate the CAPOs/CATE depending on the
173   subset of covariates V ⊆ X. For example, it is unclear how to estimate representation-level CAPOs,
174   ξaϕ (ϕ) = E(Y [a] | Φ(X) = ϕ), and CATE, τ ϕ (ϕ) = E(Y [1] − Y [0] | Φ(X) = ϕ), especially when
175   the representations are constrained.

      Apart from addressing the issues of plug-in learners (a)–(c), Neyman-orthogonal learners provide two
      favorable asymptotical theoretical properties (Foster & Syrgkanis, 2023; Kennedy, 2023): double
229
      robustness and quasi-oracle efficiency, and, thus, are (in some sense) asymptotically optimal for
230

233   nuisance functions to be nearly identical to the minimizer of the target loss with the oracle nuisance
234   functions even if the nuisance functions are estimated with slow rates (see Appendix B for the further
235   details). We refer to Remark 2 in Appendix C for a formal statement about double robustness and
236   quasi-oracle efficiency.

      Motivation. The theory on Neyman-orthogonal learners (Morzywolek et al., 2023; Vansteelandt &
      Morzywołek, 2023) does not provide a guidance on how to choose V ⊆ X. Also, to the best of our
250
      knowledge, Neyman-orthogonal learners were not studied through the lens of different representations
251
      Φ(X), chosen in place of V . For example, if the representation Φ(X) itself is learned to be predictive
252   of µxa , as in all the end-to-end representation learning methods, fitting the target model based on
253   V = Φ(X) may be beneficial compared to other choices of V . We aim to study this research gap
254   and thus introduce a novel class of Neyman-orthogonal learners called orthogonal representation
255   learners (OR-learners).
256
      Overview of our OR-learners. Our OR-learners use neural networks to fit a target model g based on
257
      the learned representations Φ(X). They proceed in three stages (see Fig. 2): (0) fitting a representation
258   network, (1) estimating nuisance functions (if necessary), and (2) fitting a target model. At the stage
259   (0), any representation learning method can be performed. Then, at the stage (1), we might need
260   to additionally fit nuisance functions (e. g., when the constrained representations were used at the
261   stage (0) so that µ̂ϕa can be inconsistent wrt. µ̂xa ). Finally, at the stage (2), we utilize different
262   DR- and R-losses, presented in Sec. 3.1, to fit the target model and, thus, yield a final estimator of
263   CAPOs/CATE.
264   Variants. In the following, we discuss different variants of our OR-learners depending on the type
265   of representations they are based: in Sec. 4.1, 4.2, and 4.3 we separately consider unconstrained,
266   constrained invertible and constrained non-invertible representations. For the latter two types of
267   representations, we consider balancing with empirical probability metrics as the main constraint.
268   For each, we present new theoretical results for our OR-learners, where we discuss the following
269   questions: (i) How can the learned representation space be interpreted? (ii) Does the representation
      ensure asymptotic validity in light of the representation-induced confounding bias (RICB)? (iii) How

      will our OR-learners help in that the target model based on the representation g(ϕ) can outperform
271   the original end-to-end representation learning predictor µ̂ϕa ? and (iv) How can the trained target

274   4.1   OR- LEARNERS FOR UNCONSTRAINED REPRESENTATIONS
275
      We consider representations Φ(X) as unconstrained if they are outputs of some fully-connected
276
      subnetwork, FCϕ , and the overall output, µ̂ϕa (Φ(X)), aims to minimize a (weighted) MSE loss wrt.
277
      to the outcome Y (see stage (0) in Fig. 2). Examples include vanilla representation networks without
278   balancing, e. g., TARNet (Shalit et al., 2017), BNN (Johansson et al., 2016), DragonNet (Shi et al.,
279   2019), CFR-ISW (Hassanpour & Greiner, 2019a), and BWCFR (Assaad et al., 2021).
280
281
      (i) Interpretation of the learned representations. Neural networks can handle increasingly more
      complicated regression tasks by simply adding more layers. This can be formalized with the notion of
282
      (Hölder) smoothness: Each layer induces a new space in which the ground-truth regression function
283
      becomes smoother and thus easier to estimate (see Remark 3 in Appendix C).
284
      Remark 3 (Smoothness of the hidden layers). Under mild conditions on the representation network,
285
      there exists a hidden layer (marked by V ) of the network with an increased smoothness.
286
287   In our setting of CAPOs/CATE estimation, we consider V = Φ(X). Thus, if learned well enough,
288
      the representation-subnetwork FCϕ and the induced representation space Φ(·) : X → Φ should
      simplify the task of CAPOs/CATE estimation.
289
290   (ii) Validity wrt. the RICB. The unconstrained representations Φ(X) can be also considered
291   asymptotically valid when dϕ ≥ 2. As an example of valid representation Φ(X) with dϕ = 2, we
292   can consider {µx0 (X), µx1 (X)} (see Proposition 4 in Appendix C).
293   Proposition 4 (Valid unconstrained representation with dϕ = 2). The representation Φ(X) =
294   {µx0 (X), µx1 (X)} is valid for CAPOs and CATE.
295   These representations can be learned arbitrarily well in the asymptotic regime, given sufficiently
296   deep representation subnetwork, FCϕ , with unconstrained representations (that follows from the
297   universal approximation theorem). Hence, in the case of dϕ ≥ 2, the unconstrained representations
298   do not induce the representation-induced confounding bias (RICB). That is, although, in general,
299   (Y [0], Y [1]) ̸⊥
                      ⊥ A | Φ(X), the representation contains all the sufficient information for estimation
300   of µxa , and, hence, the causal quantities can be consistently estimated solely with Φ(X): ξax (x) =
301   ξaϕ (Φ(x)) = µϕa (Φ(x)) and τ x (x) = τ ϕ (Φ(x)) = µϕ1 (Φ(x)) − µϕ0 (Φ(x)). Additionally, in the
302   absence of constraints and the RICB, the original representation network µ̂ϕa (Φ(x)) can be used as a
303   consistent estimator of µ̂xa (x).
304   (iii) How will our OR-learners help? OR-learners proceed by using the original representation
305   network as the estimator for µ̂xa (x) and additionally fit a covariate propensity score network π̂ax (x).

346   4.2         OR- LEARNERS FOR INVERTIBLE REPRESENTATIONS WITH BALANCING
347   Now, we turn our attention to how our OR-learners affect invertible representations, where we
348   enforce additional balancing with empirical probability metrics. Balancing aims to minimize some
349   empirical probability metric between treated and untreated distributions of the representations,
350   namely, dist(P(Φ(X) | A = 0), P(Φ(X) | A = 1)). To enforce balancing, we use empirical integral
351   probability metrics (IPMs), Wasserstein metric (WM), or maximum mean discrepancy (MMD), as
352   suggested in (Shalit et al., 2017; Johansson et al., 2022) (see definitions in Appendix B). Further,
353   we use normalizing flows (Tabak & Vanden-Eijnden, 2010; Rezende & Mohamed, 2015) for the
354   representation subnetwork FCϕ to enforce a strict invertibility. Examples of such networks are CFR
355   (Shalit et al., 2017), CFR-ISW (Hassanpour & Greiner, 2019a), and BWCFR (Assaad et al., 2021),4
356
      which we call CFRFlow, CFRFlow-ISW, and BWCFRFlow, respectively.
357   (i) Interpretation of the learned representations. As we used a normalizing flow as the repre-
358   sentation subnetwork, the transformation Φ(·) becomes a diffeomorphism. Therefore, it can only
359   non-linearly scale down or up different parts of the original space X . Then, in order to minimize
360   the original MSE loss, the representation network would scale up the parts of space to increase the
361   smoothness of µϕa (ϕ) (see Remark 3 and Proposition 5 in Appendix C). At the same time, balancing
362
      can only scale down regions of the space X with the lack of overlap (see Proposition 6 in Appendix C).
363
364   Proposition 5 (Smoothness via expanding transformations). A representation network with a repre-
365   sentation Φ(X) achieves higher Hölder smoothness of µaϕ (·) by expanding some parts of X .
366   Proposition 6 (Balancing via contracting transformations). A representation network with a rep-
367   resentation Φ(X) reduces the IPMs, namely, WM and MMD, between the distributions of the
368   representations P(Φ(X) | A = 0) and P(Φ(X) | A = 0) by contracting some parts of X .
369   Therefore, the final learned representation would combine both scaling up due to effort in smoothing
370   and scaling down due to balancing. If both scaling up and down happen in the different areas of
371   the covariate space, then balancing could be beneficial. On the other hand, if both are happening in
372   the same parts of the space, balancing renders itself useless and any amount of it can only harm the
373
      performance of the representation network. This important result allows us to formulate a crucial
      inductive bias needed for balancing to perform well: areas with the lack of overlap need to coincide
374
      with the areas with low heterogeneity of potential outcomes/treatment effect.

407   4.3     OR- LEARNERS FOR N ON - INVERTIBLE REPRESENTATIONS WITH BALANCING
408   Finally, we discuss how our OR-learners perform based on the non-invertible (general) representations
409   where balancing with empirical probability metrics is enforced. This type of representations were
410   implemented by numerous methods (see the overview in Sec. 2).
411
      (i) Interpretation of the learned representations. The learned representations have a similar
412
      interpretation as in Sec.4.2 (i). However, the representation network is now not only allowed to scale
413   down or up different parts of the original covariates space, but also to fold it, project it, etc. At the
414   same time, the results of Remark 3, Propositions 5 and 6 still hold in this case. For example, when
415   balancing is applied, non-overlapping parts of the space could be simply folded together (Keup &
416   Helias, 2022) or projected onto some subspace (i. e., transformations with the Lipschitz constant less
417   than one would be applied).
418
      (ii) Validity wrt. the RICB. When too much balancing is applied, the representations may (i) lose
419   heterogeneity and (ii) induce the RICB (Melnychuk et al., 2024). That means that (i) no asymptotically
420   consistent estimation based solely on the representations Φ(x) is possible, e. g., ξax (x) ̸= ξaϕ (Φ(x));
421   and (ii) the consistent estimation of the representation level causal quantities itself requires the access
422   to the original covariates, i. e., ξaϕ (ϕ) ̸= µϕa (ϕ).
423
      (iii) How will our OR-learners help? Asymptotically, our OR-learners will help to remove the
424
      RICB so that we can consistently estimate representation level CAPOs and CATE. Yet, they cannot
425   recover the lost heterogeneity and will only estimate causal quantities at X y level of heterogeneity,
426   where X y ⊆ X : X y ⊥  ⊥ A. Interestingly, in the extreme case of the heterogeneity loss (i. e., when
427   representations are constant, Φ(X) = 0), our OR-learners would yield (semi-parametrically) efficient
428   estimators of average potential outcomes (APOs) and average treatment effect (ATE). We refer to
429   Proposition 7 in Appendix C for further details.
430   Proposition 7 (Consistent estimation with Φ(X) = 0). For constant representations Φ(X) = 0, our
431   OR-learners yield semi-parametric efficient (augmented inverse propensity of treatment weighted
      (A-IPTW)) estimators of APOs and ATE / overlap-weighted ATE.

      On the other hand, in the low-sample setting, our OR-learners will “undo” the effect of balancing by
433   employing the covariate propensity score. Therefore, our OR-learners on the one hand can “undo”
434   the benefit brought by balancing (if there is such a setting), and, on the other, partially fix the damage
435   after applying too much balancing.
436
      (iv) Interpretation of the target model. The target model obtains similar interpretation as in Sec. 4.2
437
      (iv). However, in the case of the non-invertible representations with balancing, only X y level causal

      (e.g., regularization strength). We can even consider asymptotically-equivalent alternative variants
523
      of Neyman-orthogonal learners where constraints are enforced for the second-stage model (see
524
      Remark 8 in Appendix C). Yet, our choice of OR-learners with V = Φ(X) is based on (i) a crucial
525   inductive bias that the high-dimensional covariates lie on some low-dimensional manifold and (ii) a
526   finite-sample consideration, that the representation network has learned it well in comparison to a
527   second-stage model with an unstable loss (e. g., DR-learner with high inverse propensities).
528
      Implications. We discovered that the inductive bias for balancing is the exact opposite from the
529
      regularity conditions of Neyman-orthogonal learners. In Sec. 4.2, we showed that balancing assumes
530
      that the lack of overlap coincides with the lack of potential outcomes/treatment effect heterogeneity
531   (thus, these parts of covariate space will be ignored in the loss of the representation network). On
532   the other hand, Neyman-orthogonal learners do not make such an assumption and consider the areas
533   with the lack of overlap as uncertain. For example, the DR-learners would try to infinitely up-weight
534   any observations in those areas (due to extreme inverse propensity weights) and the R-learner would
535   ignore them (assign the weights of zero). Even if the inductive bias (that the lack of overlap implies
536   the lack of heterogeneity) can be assumed, it is still unclear how to choose an optimal amount of
537   balancing on practice (Curth & van der Schaar, 2023). We thus advise against using balancing for
538   representations.

758   Our work aims to unify two streams of work, namely, representation learning methods (Sec. A.2)
759   and Neyman-orthogonal learners (Sec. A.1). We review both in the following and then discuss the
760   implications for our work.
761
762   A.1       R EPRESENTATION LEARNING FOR ESTIMATING CAUSAL QUANTITIES
763
764   Several methods have been previously introduced for end-to-end representation learning of CA-
765   POs/CATE (see, in particular, the seminal works by Johansson et al., 2016; Shalit et al., 2017;
766   Johansson et al., 2022). Existing methods fall into three main streams: (1) One can fit an uncon-
767
      strained shared representation to directly estimate both potential outcomes surfaces (e.g., TARNet
      Shalit et al., 2017). (2) Some methods additionally enforce a balancing constraint based on empirical
768
      probability metrics, so that the distributions of the treated and untreated representations become
769
      similar (e.g., CFR and BNN Johansson et al., 2016; Shalit et al., 2017). Importantly, balancing
770   based on empirical probability metrics is only guaranteed to perform a consistent estimation for
771   invertible representations since, otherwise, balancing leads to a representation-induced confounding
772   bias (RICB)(Johansson et al., 2019; Melnychuk et al., 2024). Finally, (3) one can additionally perform
773   balancing by re-weighting the loss and the distributions of the representations with learnable weights
774   (e.g., RCFR Johansson et al., 2022).

      Validity of representations for consistent and orthogonal estimation. As mentioned previously,
823   balancing representations with empirical probability metrics without strictly enforcing invertibility
824   generally leads to inconsistent estimation based on representations. This issue was raised as a
825   representation-induced adaptation error (Johansson et al., 2019) in the context of unsupervised
826   domain adaptation and as a representation-induced confounding bias (RICB) (Melnychuk et al., 2024)
827   in the context of estimation of causal quantities. More generally, the RICB can be recognized as a
828   type of runtime confounding (Coston et al., 2020), i. e., when only a subset of covariates is available
829   for the estimation of the causal quantities. Several works offered a solution to circumvent the RICB
830   and achieve consistency, e. g., Assaad et al. (2021) employed IPTW based on original covariates,
831
      and Melnychuk et al. (2024) used a sensitivity model to perform a partial identification. However, to
      the best of our knowledge, no Neyman-orthogonal method was proposed to resolve the RICB (see
832
      Fig. 5).

834   Note on non-neural representations. Multiple works also explored the use of non-neural representa-
835   tions for the estimation of causal quantities, also known under the umbrella term of scores. Examples
836   include propensity/balancing scores (Rosenbaum & Rubin, 1983; Antonelli et al., 2018), prognostic
837
      scores (Hansen, 2008; Huang & Chan, 2017; Luo & Zhu, 2020; Antonelli et al., 2018; D’Amour &
      Franks, 2021), and deconfounding scores (D’Amour & Franks, 2021). However, we want to highlight
838
      that these works focus on different, rather simpler than ours settings:
839
840    • Propensity, balancing, and deconfounding scores (Rosenbaum & Rubin, 1983) were employed the
841      estimate average causal quantities Antonelli et al. (2018); D’Amour & Franks (2021). Examples
842      are average potential outcomes (APOs) and average treatment effect (ATE). This is because
843      they lose information about the heterogeneity of the potential outcomes/treatment effect. In our
844      work, on the other hand, we study a general class of heterogeneous causal quantities, namely,
845      representation-conditional CAPOs/CATE.
846    • Prognostic scores (Hansen, 2008) can be used for both averaged (Antonelli et al., 2018; Luo &
847      Zhu, 2020; D’Amour & Franks, 2021) and heterogeneous causal quantities (Huang & Chan, 2017).
848      In (Huang & Chan, 2017; Luo & Zhu, 2020), they are used in the context of a sufficient covariate
849      dimensionality reduction. Yet, these works either (i) make simplifying strong assumptions
850      (Antonelli et al., 2018; Luo & Zhu, 2020; D’Amour & Franks, 2021), so that the prognostic scores
851      coincide with the expected covariate-conditional outcome; or (ii) consider only linear prognostic
852
         scores (Huang & Chan, 2017; Luo & Zhu, 2020). To the best of our knowledge, the first practical
         method for non-linear, learnable representations was proposed by (Johansson et al., 2016; Shalit
853
         et al., 2017; Johansson et al., 2022).
854
855   Hence, the above-mentioned works operate in much simpler settings and are not relevant baselines
856   for our work.

863   learners must be instantiated with some machine learning model to perform (1) and (2). Meta-learners
      have several practical advantages (Morzywolek et al., 2023): (i) they oftentimes offer favorable

       In this section, we provide the formal definition of notions such as Neyman-Orthogonality and Hölder

977    B.1    A SSUMPTIONS
978
979    Identifiability. The identification of CAPOs/CATE from observational data requires further assump-
980    tions, which are standard in the literature (Rubin, 1974). The reason is that the fundamental problem
981    of causal inference: the counterfactual outcomes, Y [1 − A], are never observed, while the potential
982    outcomes are only partially observed, i. e., Y = A Y [1] + (1 − A)Y [0]. Therefore, it is standard to
983    assume (i) consistency: if A = a, then Y [a] = Y ; (ii) overlap: P(0 < πax (X) < 1) = 1; and (iii) un-
984
       confoundedness: (Y [0], Y [1]) ⊥ ⊥ A | X. Given the assumptions (i)–(iii), both CAPOs and CATE are
       identifiable from observational data as expected covariate-conditional outcomes, ξax (x) = µxa (x), or
985
       as the difference of expected covariate-conditional outcomes, τ x (x) = µx1 (x) − µx0 (x), respectively.
986
987    Smoothness. To consistently estimate CAPOs and CATE (e. g., with neural networks), we follow
988    Curth & van der Schaar (2021b); Kennedy (2023) and make regular (Hölder) smoothness assumptions
989    (see Appendix B for the definition). We assume the ground-truth response function µxa (·) to be
990
       βa -smooth, the ground-truth propensity score πax (·) to be γ-smooth, and τ x (·) to be δ-smooth (for
       βa , γ, δ > 0).
