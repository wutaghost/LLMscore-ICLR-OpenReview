009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Low-rank adaptation (LoRA) is a powerful parameter-efficient fine-tuning method
012            that utilizes low-rank projectors A and B to learn weight updates ∆W for adap-
013            tation targets W . However, while the low-rank structure of A and B enables
014            high hardware efficiency, it also restricts the overall weight update to be low-
015            rank, which limits the adaptation performance. In this paper, we propose low-rank
016            interconnected adaptation across layers (Lily). Specifically, we employ a hierar-
017            chical framework where low-dimensional projectors (LPs) retained for downward
018
               projection at a particular level, while globally-shared high-dimensional projector
               (HP) experts perform upward projection across all levels of layers. This intercon-
019
               nected asymmetric structure makes the adaptation much more dynamic and breaks
020
               the low-rank weight-update constraint of LoRA when using the same parameters
021            budget. Furthermore, Lily’s cross-layer connections facilitate the capture of in-
022            tricate information and dependencies across different layers, thereby enhancing
023            the model’s representational capabilities. Experiments across various modalities,
024            architectures, and model sizes underscore Lily’s great performance and efficiency.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   For foundation models like Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017b), fine-tuning on downstream tasks is
030   a typical usage, but full fine-tuning (FFT) of large models like large language models (LLMs) incurs
031   huge computational and storage costs and risks forgetting previously learned knowledge (Biderman
032   et al., 2024). Linear probing, which fine-tunes only the final modules like classification heads, ad-
033
      dresses these issues but leads to significant performance degradation since it doesn’t update weights
      from the backbone. To tackle these challenges, parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) has received
034
      significant attention. In PEFT, a model’s backbone weights are frozen, and lightweight trainable
035
      modules are introduced to efficiently learn task-specific knowledge. Among all PEFT methods,
036   Low-rank Adaptation (LoRA (Hu et al., 2021)) is one of the most widely applied techniques, espe-
037   cially in LLMs. LoRA introduces a pair of low-rank projection matrices for each adaptation target,
038   consisting of a downward adapter A and an upward adapter B, to approximate ∆W in FFT. Due
039   to its low-rank nature, LoRA offers significant computational and storage savings, effectively alle-
040   viating the burdens of FFT while significantly outperforming linear probing by learning the weight
041   updates for backbone weight.
042   However, LoRA and many subsequent improvements to the method (Miles et al., 2024), (Zhang
043   et al., 2023), (Zhong et al., 2024) have a limitation: the overall learned weight updates ∆W are also
044   restricted to be low-rank because of its low-rank structure, which limits the model performance dur-
045   ing adaptation. We recognize that one of the problems lies in the fact that the source of information
046   is limited for each adaptation target in LoRA, as shown in Fig. 1. It can be observed that each layer
047   in LoRA receives information only from the very layer they are situated. This prompts a question:
048   How can we enable a more dynamic and expressive adaptation with high-rank weight-updates by
049   providing more sources of information for an adaptation target?
050   In this paper, we propose Low-rank interconnected adaptation across layers (Lily), a novel frame-
051   work for more expressive and performative PEFT. Specifically, we decouple the downward low-
052   dimensional projector (LP) and its corresponding upward high-dimensional projectors (HP), making
053   them not tightly-bonded. Each LP is connected to all the HPs, and vice versa, as shown in Fig. 1.
      This results in a hierarchical structure where LPs are still retained at a particular level to perform

      Parameter Efficient Fine-Tuning Typical usage of foundation models includes pre-training on
      large datasets and fine-tuning on various downstream tasks. Parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT)
100
      thus emerges as a promising field, aiming to fine-tuning the model efficiently with minimal pa-
101
      rameters while maintaining performance and preserving previously learned knowledge, addressing
102   drawbacks posed by conventional fine-tuning techniques like full fine-tuning or linear probing. Cur-
103   rent PEFT research can be mainly categorized into two types: 1) adapter-based methods (Hu et al.,
104   2021), (Chen et al., 2022), (Pfeiffer et al., 2020b), (Jie & Deng, 2023) (Houlsby et al., 2019b) and
105   2) prompt-based methods (Tu et al., 2023b) (Tu et al., 2023a). Adapter-based methods introduce
106   lightweight adapters into the Multi-Head Self-Attention (MHSA) or the Feed-Forward Network
107   (FFN) blocks within the Transformer architecture. On the other hand, prompt-based methods ap-
      pend trainable tokens as prompts to the input sequence fed to certain parts of the model.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Among these various PEFT techniques, low-rank adaptation (LoRA (Hu et al., 2021)) stands out as
109   one of the most well-known methods. LoRA introduces a pair of projection matrices A and B per
110   adaptation target W . The low-dimension projector (LP) A projects input x to low-dimension space,
111   and the high-dimension projector (HP) B restores it to its original dimension. Multiplying these
112   projection matrices approximates the weight update ∆W in FFT. Recent work (Hao et al., 2024)
113   has shown that LoRA adapters are essentially performing random projection to the gradient using
114   a fixed matrix. This restricts the learned weight update to low-rank subspace and thus imitating
115   the model performance. Meanwhile, A and B are tightly coupled, therefore the adaptation process
116   only has information access from current layer, without an understanding of information from other
117
      layers, which could be beneficial to modeling dependencies across various layers.
118   Mixture of Experts Mixture of Experts (MoE) is an active research area that has garnered significant
119   attention, especially in the field of large language models (LLMs). Conditional computation, where
120   different parts of the network are activated on a per-example basis, has been proposed to enhance
121   model capability without increasing computation (Davis & Arel, 2013) (Bengio et al., 2013) (Eigen
122   et al., 2013) (Almahairi et al., 2016). The sparsely-gated MoE layer is introduced to implement
123
      this idea, consisting of numerous sub-networks (Shazeer et al., 2017). A trainable gating network,
      known as a ”router”, determines the combination of experts for each example. There are already
124
      PEFT methods like MoLORA (Zadouri et al., 2023) and MOLA (Gao et al., 2024a) which apply
125
      the MoE design concept to PEFT. However, these methods simply treat the adapters combined in
126   LoRA as a single expert. A concurrent research Wu et al. (2024), utilizes LP and HP sub-spaces as
127   the experts but fails to overcome the limitation discussed in previous section. Another concurrent
128   work, HydraLoRA Tian et al. (2024) also explores an asymmetric design for LoRA. A fundamental
129   difference from our work is that we consider the interaction across layers from the model and deploy
130   an model-wide asymmetric design to allow cross-layer connection.

368   We state that Lily achieves weight updates that with higher rank than LoRA. To validate our claim,

376   This empirical analysis essentially validate our claim that Lily achieves high-rank updates with the
377   same parameter budget. We credit it to the model-wide sharing mechanism and the cross-layer
      asymmetric design, which facilitates dynamic and expressive adaptation.

460
461   we analyze the impact of these choices on performance. We denote the number of LP experts and HP
462   experts as ne 1 and ne 2, respectively. For simplicity, we make them identical in the experiments,
463   denoted as ne. We refer to the number of layers each expert attends to as attention granularity. As
464
      the value of ne increases, the attention granularity becomes finer. As shown in Fig. 6, the results
      from the VTAB-1K benchmark indicate different patterns. For instance, on the DTD dataset, the
465
      best performance is achieved when ne is 4, while on sNORB-Azim, performance increases with the
466
      increase in ne. Increasing ne leads to more parameters and finer attention granularity. However,
467   finer attention granularity does not necessarily lead to better overall performance. For example, on
468   Resisc45, DTD, Cifar100, sNORB-Ele, dsPr-LoC, Flowers102, and EuroSAT, the negative impact
469   of increasingly finer attention granularity eventually outweighs the benefits of increased parameters,
470   leading to a decrease in overall performance. In other tasks, different patterns may occur because the
471   positive effect of attention granularity on performance is consistently strong, or its negative effect is
472   not enough to offset the benefits of increased parameters, resulting in a generally increasing perfor-
473   mance with ne. This phenomenon provides an important insight: for most tasks, simply increasing
474   parameters may not lead to better performance. Instead, only when attention granularity and the
475
      number of parameters reach a good tradeoff can we achieve the best performance.

477   4.5.4                  D OES L ILY E XHIBIT S ELECTIVITY ?
478
      Lily uses routers to assign varying weights to different HP experts, thereby achieving selective in-
479
      formation combination. We illustrate this selectivity in Fig. 8. We use a setup with three HP experts
480   and select three layer levels (1, 13, 22) to calculate the total weight assigned to each expert. The
481   results reveal a clear selectivity: for different layers, the router assigns significantly different weights
482   to different HP experts. For instance, on Cifar100, the middle layer is predominantly dominated by
483   HP 2, whereas the deep layer is primarily dominated by HP 1 and HP 2. In contrast, on Retinopathy,
484   both the middle and deep layers are dominated by HP 3. This selectivity ensures that, even when dif-
485   ferent layers share information, the inherent differences between layers are still taken into account,
      making the adaptation more flexible and comprehensive.

528   5   C ONCLUSION
529
530   In this paper, we propose low-rank interconnected adaptation (Lily), a novel framework for efficient
531   fine-tuning via inter-connectivity of adapters. Lily enables each layer to access information from
532   others during adaptation through a hierarchical structure. Additionally, it successfully overcome the
533   low-rank update limitation of LoRA, enabling high-rank update and therefore better adaptation capa-
534
      bility. Our approach consistently improves performance across various modalities, model sizes, and
      architectures, surpassing existing methods with enhanced efficiency. In summary, Lily’s versatility
535
      and efficiency make it a promising approach for a wide range of applications.

      A     M ORE DISCUSSION ABOUT L ILY
760
761
      A.1     M ODEL S TRUCTURE AND D ESIGN I NTUITION OF L ILY
762
      Within the overall framework of Lily, we delve into specific implementation details and model
763   design insights. First, we have established the relationship between LPs and HP: LP is confined
764   to specific levels of layers, capturing features that enable the router to selectively assign weights to
765   the HP experts. In contrast, HP is a model-wide module comprising multiple experts, each of which
766   contains information from a particular level of layers. We highlight several key aspects which are
767   not heavily discussed in the methodology section:
768
769   A.1.1    N UMBER OF LP S
770
      Since LP is limited to specific layers, the simplest approach would be to place an LP at each layer
771
      of the module to be adapted (e.g., the query transformation in MHSA). However, this setup may
772
      not be necessary: the importance of each layer varies, and many layers have significantly lower
773   importance than others (Zhang et al., 2023). To achieve greater parameter efficiency, we can set up
774   fewer LPs, with each LP focusing on a level of layers rather than a single layer. For example, an
775   LP can focus on shallow layers (e.g., layers 0, 1, 2, etc.) or deep layers. To enable a single LP to
776   handle multiple layers, we can share an LP across multiple layers. By doing so, we eliminate the
777   redundancy of having an LP at each layer, reduce the number of parameters, and increase efficiency.
778   This is exactly the strategy adopted by most of the experiments.
779
780   A.1.2    N UMBER OF HP E XPERTS
781
      Regarding HP, the number of experts can be arbitrarily set, enabling more flexible configurations.
782
      In our experiments, for the sake of simplicity, we set the number of HP experts equal to the number
783   of LPs, thereby equating the granularity of LP and HP.
784
785   A.1.3    ROUTERS S ETUP
786
787   There are also different settings that can be employed for the router. First, we can bind the router to
788   HP, resulting in only one router per model. However, since the number of parameters in the router is
789   relatively small, having only one router per model may not lead to significant selectivity. Therefore,
790   we can also bind the router to LP, configuring a separate router for each LP. Most of our experiments
791
      use the latter setup, but in the vision experiments on Vim, we use the single-router and no-lp-sharing
      setup to verify its effectiveness. From the results, we can see that this setup also performs well. As
792
      future work, we can verify the effectiveness of using the latter setup on Vim, which may potentially
793
      lead to superior performance.
794
795
      A.1.4    H YPERPARAMETERS
796
797   We detail the hyperparameters used in Lily. Specifically, we use Lily r to represent the hidden-
798   dimension of the projectors: LPs and HPs. It serves the same function as r in LoRA. We use Lily s
799   to represent the scaling factor used by Lily. It is mostly searched within the range of {0.01, 0.1, 1.0,
800   10.0, 100.0}. We use ne 1 to represent the number of LPs used in the model. Since the LPs can be
801
      shared as discussed in the previous section, ne 1 does not need to equal the number of layers in the
      model. We use ne 2 to represent the number of HP experts in the model-wide HP module. In our
802
      experiments, we set ne 1 = ne 2 to improve parameter-efficiency and simplicity.
803
804
      A.1.5    D ESIGN I NTUITION
805
806   Lily employs a hierarchical structure to enable updates with higher-ranks than LoRA. However,
807   simply equally connecting all the HPs to the LPs can not achieve the best performance. From the
808   perspective of feature and information utilization across layers, simply aggregating all HPs for an LP
809   ignores the distinctness of the features from current layers. Meanwhile, it reduced the variability of
      the combinations of gradient projection matrices (Si and Ci,j are constants now), making the rank of


                                                        15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      the weight update higher than that of LoRA (since multiple distinct random matrices are used), but
811   still not high enough for the best performance because of the lack of variability during combination.
812   Therefore, we introduce selectivity into the inter-connectivity as well as discussed below, making
813   the combination of HPs data-dependent so that each Si is unique across the time-steps, enabling
814   updates with even higher ranks. The approach has similarity to Hao et al. (2024), where random
815   matrix is constantly resampled to ensure updates with higher ranks. We further conduct an analysis
816   in Appendix G.
817
818   A.2     E FFICIENT I MPLEMENTATION FOR W EIGHTED C OMBINATION
819
820   One intuitive implementation of the weighted combination in Lily is make the inputs go through all
821
      the experts and then sum the results up, from which we could observe that it perform Ne times of
      matrix multiplication, Ne times of scalar multiplication and Ne times of matrix addition. Therefore,
822
      despite its intuitive nature, the computational burden of this approach is quite formidable.
823
824   However, Eq. 3 which is adopted in Lily only utilize Ne times of scalar multiplication, Ne times
825   of matrix addition and 1 time of matrix multiplication. This saves roughly Ne times of matrix
826   multiplication, which can be significant as the size of the model and number of adaptation targets
827
      increases. For a x′ size of RN ×d and a PH ∈ Rd×C , the floating-point operation (FLOPs) of these
      two implementation are:

842   A.3     ACTUAL IMPLEMENTATION OF L ILY
843
844   We present the actual implementation of Lily in Fig. 9. For the example here, we choose the imple-
845   mentation from visual adaptation tasks (i.e., VTAB-1K benchmark). For LLM, the implementation
846   is a bit more complicated because of modifications to the huggingface PEFT library (Mangrulkar
847   et al., 2022), but the fundamental adaptation process is the same. Specifically, given an input, we
848   first use the corresponding LP of the current layer to project it to a low-dimensional representa-
849
      tion. After that, we use the low-dimensional representation to selectively assign weights for the HP
      experts. Once we obtain all the weights for the experts, we set out to combine these HP experts
850
      accordingly, as discussed in Appendix A.2. After the weighted combination, we use the obtained
851
      combined HP to project the low-dimensional representation to high-dimension, therefore acquiring
852   the extra knowledge gained through adaptation.

1118

1119   & Dao, 2023) (Zhu et al., 2024). Specifically in SSM module, Mamba utilize parameters (∆, A,
1120   B, C) to transform an input sequence x(t) to an output sequence y(t) using a hidden state h(t).
1121   The discretization process converts A and B into Ā and B̄, respectively, using the time step size
1122   parameter ∆. Structured state space models, inspired by continuous systems, can be computed
1123   similarly to RNNs or in the form of global convolution due to their linear time invariance (LTI)
1124   property. Mamba introduces a selective property to structured state space model, tying parameters
1125   to the current input, which breaks the LTI property and hinders parallel training. To address this,
1126   Mamba employs a hardware-aware algorithm, enabling its SSM module to possess the selective
1127

1225   H    H OW TO A LLOCATE PARAMETERS ?
1226
1227   Since Lily alters the traditional LoRA’s layer-bound setup, increasing the parameters of Lily can
1228   be achieved through two approaches: 1) increasing ne, i.e., increasing the number of LP and HP
1229   experts, and 2) increasing the rank, i.e., increasing the parameter size of each individual LP or
1230   HP expert. In this section, we investigate which factor has the greatest impact on performance.
1231   We conduct experiments on the commonsense reasoning task. Specifically, we maintain the same
1232   parameter count and learning rate, and achieve the same parameter count by setting different ranks
1233
       and adjusting the corresponding ne (e.g., r=16, ne=4 versus r=8, ne=8). The results are shown in
       Fig. 11, from which we observe that more LP and HP experts with smaller rank (i.e., bigger ne
1234
       and smaller rank) generally performs worse. We argue that this is because, although increasing the
1235
       attention granularity allows for finer details, the resulting performance gain is not as significant as the
1236   gain obtained by increasing the rank, i.e., increasing the model’s capacity to learn more information.
1237   This gives us an insight that, in Lily, increasing ne to increase the parameters is less effective than
1238   directly increasing the rank in terms of potential performance gain.
