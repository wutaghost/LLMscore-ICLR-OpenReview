011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Artificial intelligence (AI) continues to transform industries and research at an
014            accelerated pace, bringing forth numerous challenges related to transparency and
015            accountability in AI-driven decision-making. Decision-makers and stakeholders
016
               require not only a clear understanding of how these systems generate predictions
               but also assurance that these processes are conducted ethically and responsibly.
017
               These challenges highlight a critical need for effective tools to evaluate and enhance
018
               the interpretability of AI models. To address this gap, we propose a new set of
019            explainer-agnostic metrics aimed at evaluating the interpretability of AI models
020            in the context of specific explainers. By focusing on global and local feature
021            importance, as well as surrogate models, our metrics capture key elements such as
022            feature stability, fluctuations in prediction behavior, and contrasts in feature rele-
023            vance across conditional subsets. By quantifying these complex dynamics as clear
024            scalar measures, we offer a structured framework for assessing model transparency,
025            fairness, and robustness. We demonstrate the practical utility of our approach
026            through case studies on a set of benchmark datasets, revealing deeper insights into
027
               model interpretability that facilitate more informed decision-making among AI
               developers and stakeholders. Ultimately, our work aims to foster AI systems that
028
               are not only technically reliable but also transparent, fair, and accountable, thereby
029
               advancing the development of ethical AI practices.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Despite the remarkable recent evolution in prediction performance by artificial intelligence (AI)
035
      models, they are often deemed as “black boxes”, i.e. models whose prediction mechanisms cannot be
036   understood simply from their parameters. An explainable or interpretable algorithm is one for which
037   the rules guiding its prediction decisions can be questioned and explained in a way that is intelligible
038   to humans. Specifically, interpretability regards the ability to extract causal knowledge about the
039   world from a model, and explainability pertains to the capability to articulate precisely how a complex
040   model arrived at specific predictions, detailing its mechanics. Understanding AI models’ behavior is
041   essential for explaining predictions to support decision-making, debugging unexpected behaviors
042   (contributing to improving model accuracy), refining modeling and data mining processes, verifying
043   that model behavior is reasonable and fair, and effectively presenting predictions to stakeholders.
044   The main goal of explainable artificial intelligence (XAI) encompasses several critical objectives Ali
045   et al. (2023). Firstly, XAI aims to empower individuals by enabling them to make informed decisions,
046   mitigating the potential harms of fully autonomous decision-making systems. Secondly, it seeks
047   to enhance decision-making by providing transparent information about the outputs of AI models,
048   facilitating well-informed choices. Thirdly, XAI identifies and addresses vulnerabilities that could
049   compromise machine learning-based systems, bolstering their resilience. Lastly, Lastly, it endeavors
050
      to boost user confidence in AI systems by promoting transparency and fostering a clear understanding
      of the decisions made by these models.
051
052   The literature offers various approaches to assessing explainability methods. Quantitative metrics
053   often evaluate whether these methods meet specific quality and reliability criteria (Bodria et al.,
      2023). Common metrics include fidelity—how well the explanation aligns with the underlying model


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      (Guidotti et al., 2018), stability—whether similar inputs yield consistent explanations (Alvarez Melis
055   & Jaakkola, 2018), faithfulness—how accurately the explanation reflects the true behavior of the
056   model (Alvarez Melis & Jaakkola, 2018), monotonicity—whether more of a certain feature leads
057   to a stronger explanation (Luss et al., 2021), and complexity—how easily the explanation can be
058   understood. Qualitative assessments are similarly varied and are categorized into functionally-
059   founded, application-grounded, and human-grounded, each offering different perspectives on the
060   utility and interpretability of the explainability method.
061   However, most existing approaches are tied to specific explainability frameworks or model architec-
062   tures, which limits their generalizability and usefulness in varied deployment scenarios. To address
063   these challenges, this study introduces a novel set of explainer-agnostic metrics that can evaluate the
064   outputs of any XAI method used for classification or regression tasks. These metrics encapsulate
065   the behavior of explanations into a singular, concise representation, making them highly applicable
066   in automated systems that monitor the trade-off between model accuracy and explainability. By
067   quantifying this balance, the proposed metrics help assess both the transparency and risk associated
068   with AI models in deployed environments.
069   Our main contributions are:
070
071           • Explainer-Agnostic Metrics: We propose a set of explainer-agnostic metrics designed to
072             evaluate the outputs of any explainer, making them applicable across various model types
073
                and settings.
074           • Consistent Feature Evaluation: Our metrics capture fundamental behaviors of the expla-
075             nations, providing a consistent framework for evaluating the feature importance of any
076
                explainer in deployment scenarios, ensuring uniformity across models and explainers.
077           • Publicly Available Tools: All metrics and benchmark datasets are made publicly available
078             via a Python library, providing the community with tools to implement and further develop
079             these methods.
080
      The paper is organized as follows: Section 2 presents our proposed methods for explainable AI
081   at local and global levels; Section 3 discusses a set of applications for these methods; and finally,
082   Section 4 outlines our main findings and potential directions for future research.

      2     P ROPOSED EXPLAINER - AGNOSTIC METRICS
086
      The proposed metrics are model-agnostic and explainer-agnostic, meaning they can be applied to
087
      any type of model without requiring access to the model’s internal structure or parameter estimates.
088   These metrics only require the predictions ŷ from a trained model f and the explanations of any
089   explainer E applied in f . Additionally, our methods extend existing concepts in explainable AI (XAI),
090   including (i) permutation feature importance, (ii) partial dependence plots, and (iii) surrogate models.
091   By summarizing explainer outputs into a single value, proposed metrics provide a concise measure
092   that captures essential behaviors of the explanations, enabling a consistent evaluation of model’s
093   explainations across different settings.
094   Figure 1 illustrates a simplified framework for explainer-agnostic metrics. Given a set of features
095
      X and a target vector Y , both the black-box model f and its surrogate fˆ serve as inputs to the
096   explainer E. The nature of E can vary, but we assume three possible types of outputs: (1) local feature
097   importance focuses on understanding the contribution of features in the predictions of each instance;
098   (2) global importance provides insights into the overall model by indicating how much each feature
099   contributes to the model’s predictions across the entire dataset. The (3) surrogate importance involves
100   approximating the complex model with a simpler, interpretable model (the surrogate model fˆ).
101
      Following, we describe the explainer-agnostic metrics proposed.

240   2.4     M ETRICS BASED ON S URROGATE M ODELS
241
242
      Surrogate models are interpretable models that approximate the behavior of complex black-box
      models, allowing for interpretability in tasks such as feature importance analysis. These models are
243
      especially valuable in Explainable AI (XAI) because they enable the decomposition of predictions
244
      into understandable components. To ensure reliability in model interpretation, it is essential to
245   evaluate the stability of the features and their importances across different samples.
246
247   Feature and importance stability assess how consistently surrogate models rely on specific features or
248
      feature rankings when subjected to data variations (e.g., bootstrapping). High stability implies that
      the surrogate model provides robust explanations that do not fluctuate significantly with changes in
249
      data, which is critical for trustworthiness in model interpretability.
250
251
      2.4.1    S URROGATE P ERFORMANCE
252
253   Surrogate performance metrics are based on a simple surrogate model, typically a decision tree
254   with a maximum depth of 3. This model is chosen for its interpretability and ease of visualization.
255   The objective of this analysis is to quantify the discrepancy between the predictions of the original
256   complex model and the surrogate model, using two key metrics: surrogate accuracy and accuracy
257   difference.
258   Definition 2.7 (Surrogate Performance). Let y represent the true target values, ypred the predicted
259   values from the original model, and ysurrogate the predicted values from the surrogate model. Let
260
      L denote an accuracy measure, such as classification accuracy or another appropriate metric. The
      surrogate accuracy (SGA ) and accuracy difference (SGD ) are defined as follows:
261
262           • Surrogate Accuracy (SGA ): The accuracy of the surrogate model compared to the original
263             model’s predictions:
264                                             SGA = L(ypred , ysurrogate ).
265             This metric reflects how well the surrogate model replicates the predictions of the original
266             model.
267
268
              • Accuracy Difference (SGD ): The difference in accuracy between the original model and
                the surrogate model, defined as:
269
                                           SGD = L(y, ypred ) − L(y, ysurrogate ).

270
                This metric measures the performance loss when using the surrogate model instead of the
271             original model.
272
273   A lower value of SGD indicates that the surrogate model closely approximates the original model,
274   whereas a higher value indicates a larger discrepancy between the two models. SGA quantifies
275   how well the surrogate captures the behavior of the original model, while SGD reveals the trade-off
276   between interpretability (using the surrogate) and accuracy (compared to the original model).
277

619   A    L ITERATURE AND T ECHNICAL BACKGROUND
620
621   The literature in explainable methods for AI models has been gaining prominence as AI models
622   proliferate in virtually all areas of society (such as predicting hypertension (Elshawi et al., 2019),
623   healthcare ElShawi et al. (2020), COVID-19 diagnosis Buckmann et al. (2022), economics and
624   finance Thimoteo et al. (2022)). In this section, we discuss some of the XAI methods and forms to
625   categorize them based on their proprieties. The discussion is far from being exhaustive, given how
626   effervescent this field is, but the goal of this section is to help the reader understand our proposed
627
      XAI metrics and how they relate to the rest of the literature.
628   In this way, we can attribute a prominent role to metrics in achieving these objectives. Unlike other
629   strategies that may lead to misleading interpretations of their outcomes, well-defined metrics tend to
630   efficiently elucidate issues and enhance risk management in sensitive processes. Moreover, the use of
631   metrics facilitates comparisons between different sets of results, making it effective, for instance, in
632   hypothesis validation. Several factors, however, complicate the definition of metrics. In the case of
633
      XAI, the absence of a ground truth for explainability adds a layer of complexity to the comparison of
      different strategies, as pointed out by Zhou et al. (2021). In this context, potential strategies involve
634
      considering aspects such as fidelity, unambiguity, and overlap, as discussed by Lakkaraju et al. (2017).
635
      The existing literature introduces various methods for evaluating explainability techniques Mothilal
636   et al. (2021). Mothilal et al. (2021) presents a framework that unifies strategies centred around feature
637   attribution and counterfactual generation. In contrast, other studies propose explainability metrics
638   grounded in algorithmic stability Fel et al. (2022); Khaire & Dhanalakshmi (2022); Nogueira et al.
639   (2018). However, it is essential to clarify that our objective is not to evaluate the explainability
640   methods themselves. Instead, our focus is on providing insights based on the importance attributed to
641   features by different models. In doing so, we aim to facilitate comparisons of explanations across
642   models.
643   We can define at least three categories of metrics with an emphasis on the explainability of results
644   generated for AI models: (i) subjective, (ii) objective, and (iii) computational. The subjective metrics
645   are employed when evaluating aspects that elicit subjective responses from users. This type of metric
646   may be based on trust, understanding, and satisfaction, as proposed in Hoffman et al. (2018). The
647   objective metrics are those related to observed aspects, for example, in users performing a particular
      task. They can be measures of time for the execution of the task or accuracy. Schmidt & Biessmann


                                                        12
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


648
      (2019) seeks to objectively measure the quality of explainability methods and shows that quick and
649   highly accurate decisions represent a good understanding of explainability. Narayanan et al. (2018)
650   evaluate explainability results based on subjective (satisfaction) and objective (response time and
651   accuracy) metrics. Furthermore, computational metrics are derived from mathematical indicators that
652   assess the quality of explanations generated by an XAI method. Since these metrics are based on
653   specific equations, user intervention is not necessary for obtaining them, making this type of metric
654   suitable for automated systems.
655
656   A.1    C OMPUTATIONAL M ETRICS FOR E VALUATING XAI O UTPUTS
657
658   With the growing use of explainability methods for machine learning models, there is also an increase
659   in studies that seek to evaluate the results based on feature importance or feature-attribution. Works
660
      investigating computational metrics may aim to construct metrics that assess methods based on their
      desired properties, such as fidelity, stability, comprehensibility, representativity, and consistency. Fel
661
      & Vigouroux (2020) focuses on constructing metrics that address representativity and consistency.
662
      The work proposed by Nguyen & Martı́nez (2020) suggests a set of metrics that also rely on certain
663   properties, and in the case of evaluating feature-attribution methods, the metrics (monotonicity and
664   non-sensitivity) seek to assess how faithful the methods are. On the other hand, other strategies
665   evaluate feature-attribution methods based on different factors. Rong et al. (2022) proposes an
666   information-theoretic strategy to evaluate feature-attribution methods.
667
668   A.2    R ELEVANT APPROACHES IN XAI METHODS
669
670   The permutation feature importance was first introduced by Fisher et al. (2019). The work proposed by
671   Fisher et al. (2019) constructs a model-agnostic method for machine learning predictions. Intuitively,
672   if altering the values of certain features results in a considerable change in the AI model error,
673   this feature is considered to be important. Alternatively, features are deemed unimportant if the
674   AI model error remains unchanged after altering its values. Permutation feature importance is a
675
      powerful tool because its interpretation is intuitive and it can be applied to any model – i.e. it is an
      easy-to-understand global XAI method. In addition, it does not require retraining the model, nor
676
      knowing its estimates and nor its modus operandi. On the other hand, there is no consensus in the
677
      literature about whether a training or test set should be used to compute the feature’s importance.
678
679   Another explainability method is the partial dependence curve. A machine learning model f is often
680   a function of a multitude of features x, which makes it infeasible to plot the estimated model in
681
      a high-dimensional space. Instead, the partial dependence curve, can be used to assess how the
      predicted outcome of a model f behaves as a function of values of a particular selected feature xs ,
682
      after averaging f over the values of all other features xA . Intuitively, the partial dependence curve
683
      can be interpreted as the expected/average model response as a function of the input feature of interest
684   Pedregosa et al. (2011). This helps with model explainability since it makes it possible to assess
685   whether the relationship between the outcome and a feature is linear, and/or monotonic, or more
686   complex. For example, if applied to a linear regression, the partial dependence plot always shows
687   a linear relationship. The estimation assumes that features are not correlated. The violation of this
688   assumption indicates that the averages calculated for the partial dependence may include data points
689   that are implausible.
690   The relationship between features and the outcome of an AI model is often too complex to be easily
691   summarized in black-box models. On the other hand, there are models where explainability is
692   straightforward, such as linear regression or relatively small decision trees. It turns out that one could
693   use the latter simpler models – also referred to as surrogate models – to approximate a complex
694   black-box model, often locally (i.e. for a subset of observations), and reap the explainability properties
695   of the approximating model. In other words, for a subset of observations, it is possible that a simple
696   and explainable model can approximate reasonably well complex predictions made by a complex
697   model, and, in turn, offer a straightforward connection between features and output in the prediction
698
      process. This surrogate model approximation can be implemented by fitting a simple explainable
      model to a dataset corresponding to the same features used to train the complex AI model, while
699
      using the outcome generated by the AI model, as opposed to using the observed outcome.
700
701   The additive feature attribution methods are also well-defined in literature, with many applications
      Li (2022); Man & Chan (2021); Feng et al. (2021); Meng et al. (2020); Parsa et al. (2020); Nohara


                                                         13
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


702
      et al. (2022); Rodrı́guez-Pérez & Bajorath (2020); Alabi et al. (2023); Gabbay et al. (2021); Magesh
703   et al. (2020). As described by Lundberg & Lee (2017), this class of methods have an explanation
704   model there is a linear function of binary variables. Several models follow this additive feature
705   attribution definition. The local interpretable model-agnostic explanations (LIME) proposed by
706   Ribeiro et al. (2016) focuses on providing explanations for any classifier (or regressor) at the local
707   level. As an additive feature attribution method, it aims to explain why a particular prediction was
708   made for a specific instance. The purpose of the Shapley additive explanations (SHAP) method
709   introduced by Lundberg & Lee (2017), uses Shapley values Shapley et al. (1953) to compute feature
710   attribution. Taking as input a set function v : 2n → R, we can define the Shapley value ϕi (v) for
711
      a specific variable i as your contribution to the payoff through the weighted average of all possible
      combinations.
