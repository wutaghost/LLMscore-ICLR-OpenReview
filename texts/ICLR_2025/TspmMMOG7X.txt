011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            GPT-4o, an omni-modal model that enables vocal conversations with diverse emo-
014            tions and tones, marks a milestone for omni-modal foundation models. However,
015            empowering Large Language Models to perceive and generate images, texts, and
016            speeches end-to-end with publicly available data remains challenging in the open-
017            source community. Existing vision-language models rely on external tools for the
018            speech processing, while speech-language models still suffer from limited or even
019
               without vision-understanding abilities. To address this gap, we propose EMOVA
               (EM-otionally Omni-present Voice Assistant), to enable Large Language Models
020
               with end-to-end speech capabilities while maintaining the leading vision-language
021
               performance. With a semantic-acoustic disentangled speech tokenizer, we notice
022            surprisingly that omni-modal alignment can further enhance vision-language and
023            speech abilities compared with the corresponding bi-modal aligned counterparts.
024            Moreover, a lightweight style module is proposed for flexible speech style controls
025            (e.g., emotions and pitches). For the first time, EMOVA achieves state-of-the-art
026            performance on both the vision-language and speech benchmarks, and meanwhile,
027            supporting omni-modal emotional spoken dialogue. Demos are available in the
028            project page: https://emova-anonymous.github.io/.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   OpenAI GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024), a new milestone for omni-modal foundation models, has rekindled
035   people’s attentions on intelligent assistants that can see (i.e., perceiving fine-grained visual inputs),
036   hear (i.e., understanding vocal instructions) and speak (i.e., generating vocal responses) simultane-
037   ously. Most existing Multi-modal Large Language Models (MLLMs) focus on two modalities only,
038   either vision-language (Bai et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024a) or speech-language (Chu et al., 2024; Xie &
039
      Wu, 2024), demonstrating severe demands for omni-modal models with visual, language and speech
      abilities. How to effectively empower Large Language Models (LLMs) to process omni-modal data
040
      in an end-to-end manner remains an open question.
041
042   Existing omni-modal LLMs (Chen et al., 2024b; Fu et al., 2024b) generally build upon Vision LLMs
043   and integrate the speech modality by adopting a speech encoder like Whisper (Radford et al., 2023),
044   which extracts continuous features from speech, similar to how images are processed, and enables
045
      speech understanding. However, these models still rely on external Text-to-Speech (TTS) tools for
      generating speech responses, limiting their ability to support real-time interactions. AnyGPT (Zhan
046
      et al., 2024), instead, opts for a fully discretization manner, which first discretizes all data modalities
047
      (i.e., images, texts, and speeches), followed by omni-modal auto-regressive modeling. This enables
048   AnyGPT to handle multiple modalities with a unified end-to-end framework, facilitating real-time
049   interactions with the help of streaming decoding. However, the discrete vision tokenizer adopted
050   by AnyGPT struggles to capture visual details, especially for high-resolution images, making it far
051   behind its continuous counterparts on vision-language benchmarks. Moreover, none of the existing
052   works explore speech style controls (e.g., emotions and pitches) with LLMs. Therefore, our question
053   arises: How to build an end-to-end omni-modal LLM enabling spoken dialogue with vivid emotions
      while maintaining state-of-the-art vision-language performance?

074
      In this paper, we propose EMOVA (EMotionally Omni-present Voice Assistant), a novel end-to-
075
      end omni-modal LLM with state-of-the-art vision-language and speech capabilities while supporting
076
      emotional spoken dialogue. Fig. 1 shows an overview of the model framework. A continuous vision
077   encoder captures the fine-grained visual details, while the discrete speech tokenizer and detokenizer
078   enable the end-to-end speech understanding and generation. Specifically, the speech-to-unit (S2U)
079   tokenizer converts the input speech waveforms into discrete speech units as LLM inputs, while the
080   unit-to-speech (U2S) detokenizer reconstructs the speech waveforms from the LLM’s output speech
081   units. To seamlessly integrate the speech modality with LLMs, we meticulously design a semantic-
082   acoustic disentangled speech tokenizer to decouple the semantic contents and acoustic styles of the
083   input speeches (Tao et al., 2024), where 1) semantic content (i.e., what it says) captures the semantic
084   meanings of input speeches, which is finally discretized and aligned with LLMs, while 2) acoustic
085
      style (i.e., how it says) captures the diverse speech styles (e.g., emotions and pitches). Utilizing the
      semantic-acoustic disentanglement of our speech tokenizer, we further introduce a lightweight style
086
      module to support spoken dialogue with vivid emotions and pitches. As in Sec. 4.1, this innovative
087
      disentanglement design better facilitates the modality alignment between texts and speeches while
088   maintaining flexibility for diverse speech style controllability and personalization.
089
090   With the end-to-end omni-modal architecture of EMOVA, we empirically demonstrate that publicly
091
      available bi-modal image-text and speech-text data are sufficient for omni-modal alignment, utilizing
      the text modality as a bridge. This eliminates the need for omni-modal data (i.e., image-text-speech),
092
      which is usually scarce. Surprisingly, we find that omni-modal alignment can further improve both
093
      vision-language and speech capabilities through joint optimization, even when compared with their
094   bi-modal aligned counterparts. Finally, only a small amount of mixed-modality samples are required
095   to teach the model to respond in the desired format. For the first time, EMOVA achieves state-of-
096   the-art performance on both vision-language and speech benchmarks (see Table 1 for comparisons).
097
      The main contributions of this work contain three parts:
098
099         1. We propose EMOVA, a novel end-to-end omni-modal LLM that can see, hear and speak.
100            We use a continuous vision encoder and a semantic-acoustic disentangled speech tokenizer
101            for seamless omni-modal alignment and diverse speech style controllability.
102
            2. We introduce an efficient text-centric omni-modal alignment which can further improve the
103            vision-language and speech capabilities, even compared with the corresponding bi-modal
104            aligned counterparts (i.e., image-text only and speech-text only alignment).
105
            3. For the first time, our EMOVA achieve state-of-the-art comparable performance on both
106            the vision-language and speech benchmarks simultaneously, while supporting flexible spo-
107            ken dialogues with vivid emotions.

123   2     R ELATED W ORK
124
125   Vision Large Language Models (VLLMs) integrate vision modality into Large Language Models
126   (LLMs) (Touvron et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023b), enabling advanced understanding and reasoning
127   over visual instructions (Liu et al., 2024b; Bai et al., 2023; Gou et al., 2023; 2024). Recent efforts in
128   VLLMs can be broadly categorized into three directions, including 1) Vision encoders (Oquab et al.,
129   2023; Chen et al., 2021b; 2023a) are enhanced and aggregated for robust representations (Lin et al.,
130   2023; Li et al., 2024b; Tong et al., 2024). 2) High-resolution methods are proposed to overcome
131   the fixed resolution of pre-trained vision encoders (e.g., 336 × 336 for CLIP (Radford et al., 2021)),
132
      empowering LLMs to perceive fine-grained visual information (Liu et al., 2024a; Dong et al., 2024;
      Huang et al., 2024; Luo et al., 2024). 3) High-quality instruction data is essential for the VLLMs to
133
      generate accurate and well-formed responses following instructions (Laurençon et al., 2024; Li et al.,
134
      2024a; Chen et al., 2024b). In this paper, besides achieving state-of-the-art vision-language perfor-
135   mance, we further introduce speech understanding and generating capabilities into our EMOVA.
136
137   Speech Large Language Models (SLLMs) empower speech interaction with LLMs. Continuous
138   SLLMs (Wu et al., 2023; Chu et al., 2024) utilize the speech encoders (Radford et al., 2023) to extract
139   continuous speech embeddings for LLM, which, however, only support speech understanding, rely-
140   ing on external TTS modules for speech generation, and therefore, hampering real-time interaction.
141   Discrete SLLMs (Zhang et al., 2023a), instead, first discretize speech signals with speech tokenizers,
142   followed by auto-regressive modeling. Recent works (Fang et al., 2024; Xie & Wu, 2024) further
143   combine the continuous speech encoders with the discrete speech tokenizers for better performance.
144
      Although effective, none of the existing works explore speech style controllability in SLLMs (e.g.,
      genders, emotions, and pitches), which is essential for real-life spoken dialogue.
145
146
      Omni-modal Large Language Models support visual, text, and speech capabilities with a unified
147
      architecture simultaneously. Similar to the continuous SLLMs, InternOmni (Chen et al., 2024b) and
148
      VITA (Fu et al., 2024b) connect a speech encoder with VLLMs, supporting speech understanding
149   only. Instead, AnyGPT (Zhan et al., 2024) proposes a unified architecture to discretize and conduct
150   auto-regressive modeling for image, text, and audio simultaneously, which, however, suffers from
151   inevitable information loss brought by discretization, especially for the high-resolution visual inputs.
152   In this work, we propose EMOVA, the very first unified Omni-modal Large Language Models with
153   state-of-the-art vision-language and speech performance at the same time.

156
157   3.1   F ORMULATION
158
159   Denote the Large Language Model (LLM) as f (·) and the text, visual and speech inputs as XT , XV
160   and XS , respectively. XT is converted to discrete tokens UT via a text tokenizer (Gage, 1994), while
161   the visual input XV is first encoded with a vision encoder v(·) as EV = v(XV ), and then projected
      into the text embedding space with a projector p(·) as HV = p(EV ). As for the speech input XS , a

164   is then trained to compute the joint probability of the output text and speech units UoT , UoS as

172   LLM. We adopt the LLaMA-3.1-8B (Dubey et al., 2024) as our base LLM f (·), due to its superior
173   performance among publicly available checkpoints, which is equipped with a tiktoken text tokenizer
174   and a vocabulary size of 128,256, supporting both multilingual textual inputs and outputs.

      Vision encoder and projector. We utilize InternViT-6B (Chen et al., 2024b) as our visual encoder

      v(·) with 448 × 448 base resolution, and C-Abstractor (Cha et al., 2024) with two ResBlocks (both
177   before and after the pooling layer) and 4× downsample rate as vision projector p(·). To process the
178   high-resolution inputs, the high-resolution image-slicing (Liu et al., 2024a) is used, where visual to-
179   kens for one image are concatenation with a low-resolution thumbnail and the origin high-resolution
180   image with separators in each line, allowing a maximum of nine tiles during training.
181
182   3.2   S PEECH T OKENIZATION
183
184   Speech-to-unit (S2U) tokenizer. Following Tao et al. (2024), we adopt the SPIRAL (Huang et al.,
185   2022) architecture for the speech encoder s(·) to capture both phonetic and tonal information, which
186
      is then discretized by the quantizer q(·) utilizing the finite scalar quantization (FSQ) (Mentzer et al.,
      2023). The size of the speech codebook is 4,096, while the sample rate is 25 tokens per second.
187
      Once discretized, the speech modality can be simply integrated into LLMs by concatenating the text
188
      vocabulary and speech codebook.
189
190   Our S2U tokenizer provides the following advantages: 1) Data efficiency: after pre-training on large-
191   scale unlabeled speech data, it requires only a small amount of speech-text pair data for fine-tuning.
192
      2) Bilingual: the speech codebook is shared among different languages (i.e., English and Chinese),
      sharing unit modeling abilities across languages. Check more training details and comparisons with
193
      other speech tokenizers (Zhang et al., 2023b) in Appendix A.1.
194
195   Semantic-acoustic disentanglement. To seamlessly align speech units with the highly semantic
196   embedding space of LLMs, we opt for decoupling the semantic contents and acoustic styles of
197   input speeches. Specifically, given input speechs XS , both semantic embedding Esemantic and
198   style embeddings Estyle are extracted separately, while only the Esemantic is quantified by q(·) to
199   generate speech units US . By changing Estyle while maintaining the same Esemantic , we can easily
200   control speech styles without disturbing the semantic contents of recovered speeches. Moreover, the
201
      disentanglement facilitates modality alignment among speeches and texts, as later shown in Sec. 4.1.
202   Unit-to-speech (U2S) detokenizer with style control. Building on VITS (Kim et al., 2021), our
203   U2S detokenizer adopts a conditional VAE architecture (see Fig. 7). To achieve vivid style controls,
204   we utilize the semantic-style disentanglement of our S2U tokenizer (as discussed above) and adopt
205   a novel style embedding to control the speech styles (e.g., speaker identities, emotions, and pitches).
206   Specifically, the LLM f (·) is trained to generate both the output speech units UoS and a style label.
207   The speech units UoS are converted to unit embeddings Eosemantic , while the style label is utilized to
208   generate a unique style prototype Eostyle . Both Eosemantic and Eostyle are taken as inputs to speech
209   decoder d(·, ·) to synthesize the output speech waveform YoS . See Appendix A.2 for more details.
210   Our U2S detokenizer is pre-trained on LibriTTS (Zen et al., 2019) and AISHELL-1 (Bu et al., 2017)
211   and subsequently fine-tuned on synthetic style-rich speech data. Specifically, due to the scarcity of
212   real-life style-rich data, we utilize TTS tools (Du et al., 2024) to synthesize speech samples diverse
213   in genders, pitches, and emotions. As for style prototypes, Emotion2Vec (Ma et al., 2023) is adopted
214   to select the most representative samples with the highest confidence in conveying the desired style.
215   Our empirical results reveal that even one representative style reference speech has been sufficient
      to control the speech styles flexibly and precisely.

      4     O MNI - MODAL A LIGNMENT AND I NSTRUCTION T UNING
233
234
      To achieve the omni-model alignment, it is ideal to utilize large-scale omni-modal image-text-speech
235   data, which, however, is either without reach due to copyright (Nagrani et al., 2022) or limited in the
236   quality (Miech et al., 2019). An alternative is to use existing image-text data with TTS-synthesized
237   speeches, which is not only computationally expensive but also hampers data diversity, as most TTS
238   tools generate speech in similar patterns. Recent works (Chen et al., 2024b; Fu et al., 2024b) choose
239   to integrate the speech modality into a well-structured VLLM via a sequential training manner with
240   bi-modal alignment datasets. However, the relationships between different modalities and how to
241   effectively leverage multiple bi-modal alignment datasets remain an open question.
242   In this work, we explore omni-modal text-centric alignment by utilizing publicly available bi-modal
243   alignment datasets, including both image-text (e.g., captioning) and speech-text (e.g., ASR and TTS)
244   datasets. With the text modality as a bridge, EMOVA ultimately becomes a unified system capable
245   of understanding and generating multiple modalities in a coherent and integrated manner. Specifi-
246   cally, in Sec. 4.1, we explore the following three questions:
247          1. Does the integration of the speech modality conflict with the vision-language capabilities?
248          2. How to represent speech modality to foster omni-modal alignment?
249
             3. Is sequential alignment of multiple modalities optimal?
250
251   Then we introduce the omni-modal instruction tuning pipeline and the overall training paradigm of
252
      our EMOVA in Sec. 4.2 and Sec. 4.3, respectively.
253
254   4.1    O MNI - MODAL T EXT-C ENTRIC A LIGNMENT
255
      Settings. To answer the questions above, we experimentally compare the following omni-modal
256
      training paradigms: 1) VL→Speech conducts image-text alignment first followed by speech-unit-
257   text alignment using the full speech data and 10% of image-text alignment data to avoid catastrophic
258   forgetting, similar to InternOmni (Chen et al., 2024b) and VITA (Fu et al., 2024b). 2) Speech→VL
259   instead performs speech-unit-text alignment first and then aligns images with texts using 10% of
260   the speech unit-text data and the full image-text data. 3) Joint aligns both modalities simulta-
261   neously. Note that unless otherwise specified, we use the S2U tokenizer introduced in Sec.3.2 to
262   extract speech units for all speech data, which effectively disentangles semantic and acoustic fea-
263   tures. Joint-entangled derives speech units using HuBERT (Hsu et al., 2021), which does
264   not achieve semantic-acoustic disentanglement effectively with only Kmeans clustering. 4) VL and
265
      Speech only align the vision and speech modalities with texts, respectively, serving as baselines
      (see Appendix B.1 for more details).
266
267   Evaluation. For speech capabilities, we directly evaluate the aligned model’s performance on
268   the ASR task of LibriSpeech (Panayotov et al., 2015), while for vision-language, we fine-tune the
269   model using a small amount of high-quality visual instruction data (i.e., the 665K SFT data from
      ShareGPT4V (Chen et al., 2023d)) and evaluate the fine-tuned model on common vision-language

311

313   forms its sequential counterparts (i.e., VL→Speech and Speech→VL) on both vision-language
314   and speech benchmarks, probably due to catastrophic forgetting when integrating a new modality.
315
      In light of these observations, we have chosen to pursue the ultimate alignment strategy that simul-
316
      taneously aligns image-text and speech-unit-text for EMOVA, which offers two important benefits,
317
      1) it fosters mutual enhancement between vision-language and speech capabilities, and 2) it avoids
318   the issue of catastrophic forgetting during sequential alignment of multiple modalities.

      Emotion-enriched instruction data synthesis. Due to the scarcity of omni-modal instruction data
353   (i.e., dialogues involving images, speeches, and texts simultaneously), we opt for synthesizing omni-
354   modal instruction data from existing text and visual instruction datasets. First, we select instruction
355   data suitable for the vocal expression by filtering out the non-vocal data (e.g., code and mathematical
356   formulas). Second, we clean the selected data to be more vocal by removing text formatting elements
357   (e.g., ∗∗ and \n\n). Then, we obtain style labels for the remaining dialog contexts, including gen-
358   ders (male, female), pitches (normal, low, high), and emotions (neutral, happy,
359   sad, angry), resulting in 24 different speech styles. The style labels are obtained by prompting
360   GPT-4o1 to make reasonable inferences given the dialogue context. Finally, we convert the tex-
361
      tual instructions and responses into speeches using the latest TTS tools (i.e., CosyVoice (Du et al.,
      2024) and Azure AI Speech), and the style labels are used to control the style of the synthesized
362
      speech data. To further improve the diversity of the data, each instruction is synthesized by ran-
363
      domly selecting one of the 39 available speakers. Ultimately, we gather 120K speech-text and 110K
364   speech-image data pairs in total. More details can be found in Appendix B.2.
365
366
      Data organization and chain of modality. The omni-modal instruction data can be represented
367
      as Domni = {(xV , uS , xoT , costyle , uoS )i }N
                                                     i=1 , where the input consists of the optional queried image
368
      xV and the speech units of the instruction uS , while the output consists of the textual response xoT ,
369   the predicted speech style labels costyle , and the output speech unit uoS . Note that we train EMOVA to
370   explicitly select styles (e.g., emotions and pitches), which are utilized to determine the corresponding
371   style embedding for the U2S detokenizer (Sec. 3.2). Moreover, since directly generating the speech
372   responses is challenging, we decompose the speech response procedure into three primary steps:
373   1) recognizing user instructions into texts; 2) generating textual responses based on the recognized
374   instructions; 3) generating the style labels and response speech units based on the textual responses.
375   For ease of parsing during deployment, the target outputs are formatted as JSON, as shown in Fig. 3.

399   4.3   OVERALL T RAINING PARADIGM
400
401
      Inspired by Chen et al. (2023d), a three-stage training paradigm is adopted for EMOVA,
402          • Stage-1: Vision-language pre-alignment. The purpose is to align visual features into the
403            embedding space of LLMs. Only the vision projector p(·) is trained.
404          • Stage-2: Omni-modal text-centric alignment. This stage jointly performs the vision-
405            language and speech-language alignment simultaneously. We train the LLM f (·), vision
406            projector p(·), and the deeper half of vision encoder v(·) layers.
407          • Stage-3: Omni-modal instruction tuning. To empower EMOVA to respond accurately
408            to omni-modal instructions, we organize different datasets with various types of instruc-
409            tions to enforce EMOVA to learn generalization across tasks, as detailed in Sec. 5.1.
410

416   strategy (Liu et al., 2024a) adopted.

478
479   In this section, we evaluate the end-to-end spoken dialogue capabilities of EMOVA. As discussed
480   in Sec. 4.2, the model takes an input image xV and user instructions in the form of speech units uS ,
481
      and outputs text responses, style labels, and corresponding speech units. To ensure comprehensive
      evaluation, we propose the following evaluation metrics (see Appendix D for more details):
482
483          1. End-to-end spoken dialogue score assesses the model’s dialogue performance based on
484             the generated speeches, with a score ranging from 0 to 10, reporting the average.
485          2. Unit-input-text-output score focuses on the quality of the text responses of LLM when
                the inputs are speech units, bypassing errors from speech synthesis.

490            tively it generates speech units from text.
491         5. Style label classification accuracy evaluates the accuracy of the model in selecting the
492            appropriate speech style labels (Sec. 3.2).
493         6. Style controllablity assesses the style controllability of U2S detokenizer with the given
494            conditional style labels via the confusion matrix between generated and recognized styles.

529   6   C ONCLUSION
530   This work presents EMOVA, an innovative end-to-end omni-modal large language model that ef-
531   fectively aligns vision, speech, and text simultaneously. We employ a continuous vision encoder to
532   capture fine-grained visual details, while a discrete, semantic-acoustic disentangled speech tokenizer
533   and detokenizer enable end-to-end speech understanding and generation. A lightweight style mod-
534   ule further supports spoken dialogue with vivid emotions. By using text as a bridge, we demonstrate
535   that omni-modal alignment is achievable without relying on scarce omni-modal image-text-speech
536   data, which not only enhances both vision-language and speech capabilities but also surpasses its
537   bi-modal counterparts through joint optimization. For the first time, EMOVA achieves state-of-the-
538
      art performance on both vision-language and speech benchmarks, setting a novel standard for the
      omni-modal models for versatile and expressive omni-modal interactions.

867   A     M ORE ON S PEECH T OKENIZER
868
869   A.1   S PEECH - TO -U NIT (S2U) T OKENIZER
870
871   Overview. To process the speech input XS , our S2U tokenizer consists of a speech encoder s(·)
872   with a quantization module q(·). First, the speech input is passed through s(·), producing a con-
873
      tinuous latent representation ES = s(XS ). Then, the quantization module q(·) converts Es into
      discrete units US = q(Es ). The final output is an ID sequence US = [u1 , u2 , · · · ], where each ui
874
      corresponds to a unique speech unit.
875
876   After this S2U extraction process, the speech is represented by quantized IDs instead of quantized
877   embeddings. For example, a speech signal is represented as an ID sequence like [782, 463, 550, · · · ],
878   which can be treated as a special form of text. As a result, to integrate speech tokens into LLM f (·),
879
      we only need to expand the LLM’s original vocabulary VT by adding a set of speech unit tokens VS ,
      similar to Zhang et al. (2023a). The expanded vocabulary is thus the union V = VT ∪ VS . In this
880
      work, the same codebook is shared across multiple languages, such as English and Chinese, enabling
881
      the unit modeling abilities to be shared across languages. This design enables simply integration of
882   the speech modality into the LLM with minimal data requirements (see experiments in Sec. 4.1).
883
884
885
      Training of S2U. The training of the S2U encoder involves three primary components: the speech
      encoder, the quantization module, and the phone decoder. First, the speech encoder is trained with
886
      a large amount of unlabeled speech with contrastive loss in a self-supervised learning manner (Tao
887
      et al., 2024). The dataset utilized is the 10000-hour English speeches from GigaSpeech (Chen et al.,
888   2021a) and the 10000-hour Chinese speeches from WenetSpeech (Zhang et al., 2022), both of which
889   encode large variations in speakers, styles, and acoustic environments. Subsequently, the speech
890   encoder, along with the quantization module and phone decoder, are optimized on a small amount
891   of speech-text pair data, which is derived from the train-clean-100 subset of LibriSpeech (Panayotov
892   et al., 2015) in English and AISHELL-1 (Bu et al., 2017) in Chinese. The phone label is obtained
893   using the phone set in Charsiu (Zhu et al., 2022). During training, the speech encoder encodes input
894   speeches into continuous latent representations that are rich in both phonetic and tonal information.
895   Then, the quantization module is used to convert the continuous outputs from the speech encoder
896
      into discrete units. Finally, the phone decoder converts the quantized embeddings into a sequence
      of non-tonal/tonal phones, ensuring that the speech units capture necessary information related to
897
      semantic contents in both non-tonal and tonal languages. After training, only the speech encoder
898
      and the quantization module are used in EMOVA.
899
900
901   Comparisons with SpeechTokenizer in AnyGPT. Our S2U tokenizer differs from the Speech-
902
      Tokenizer (Zhang et al., 2023b) used in AnyGPT (Zhan et al., 2024), in the following aspects:
903   (1) SpeechTokenizer encodes both semantic contents and acoustic details of speeches, while our
904   S2U tokenizer focuses solely on semantic contents. This design reduces the LLMs’ burden of disen-
905   tangling different aspects of speech information, facilitating the modality alignment between speech
906   and text modalities during LLM training.
907   (2) Compared with SpeechTokenizer, our S2U tokenizer offers a more concise representation and
908   helps to simplify and accelerate the generation of EMOVA. SpeechTokenizer employs tokens from
909   eight RVQ layers with a 50Hz frame rate to represent speech, thus a 10-second speech corresponds
910   to 500 × 8 = 4000 tokens. However, we reduce the frame rate from 50Hz to 25Hz and utilize only
911   one token to represent each frame, and thus, a 10-second speech can be represented by only 250
912   tokens. Moreover, AnyGPT requires a two-stage generation process, involving autoregressive (AR)
913   semantic token generation followed by the non-autoregressive (NAR) acoustic token generation.
914
      Instead, we only need to generate speech units capturing semantic contents in a fully AR manner.
915   (3) SpeechTokenizer lacks an explicit structure design to deal with tonal languages like Chi-
916   nese, therefore, the processing ability in Chinese is not demonstrated in either SpeechTokenizer
917   or AnyGPT. In contrast, our S2U tokenizer incorporates training constraints to better capture tone
      variation in phone, making it effective for both the non-tonal and tonal languages. This further en-

918
      hances EMOVA’s multilingual speech processing capabilities, enabling it to effectively handle both
919   English and Chinese.
920
921   In summary, our S2U tokenizer improves the compactness and generality of speech representation,
922
      facilitates LLM training, and enhances its multilingual speech ability. Experimental results show
      that our model significantly outperforms AnyGPT in ASR tasks, as shown in Table 6.

966   leading to 24 unique styles and 24 × K candidates. Empirically we find that genders and pitches

969   recognition (SER) tool, to measure the emotion intensity of each candidate reference, and rank them
970   in terms of the confidence of the desired emotion. We select the Top-1 candidate reference in each
971   combination style to be the prototype of this condition. Finally, the most representative 24 reference
      speeches are selected from the 24 × K candidates.

1130   F         L IMITATIONS
1131
1132   Duplex modeling. In the current version, EMOVA can only process either visual/speech/text in-
1133   puts or produce speech/text outputs at the same time. For a communication experience that mirrors
       human interaction, handling inputs and outputs simultaneously is crucial. Recent works like VITA


                                                                         21
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1134
       (Fu et al., 2024b) and LSLM (Ma et al., 2024) have begun to explore duplex modeling. VITA fo-
1135   cuses on recognizing speech in noisy environments during the generation process to facilitate timely
1136   responses. LSLM, on the other hand, attempts to halt speech production when it detects a com-
1137   mand or voice. Recently, a ground-breaking work named Moshi (Défossez et al., 2024) develops a
1138   model supporting fully duplex modeling. The adeptness at simultaneously managing the informa-
1139   tion streams from both the user and the assistant allows Moshi to converse with human beings in
1140   real-time scenarios.
1141   However, incorporating emotion into this duplex modeling presents additional challenges. Emo-
1142   tional states can fluctuate throughout a conversation, and how to generate appropriate responses
1143   given the user’s previous and current emotional cues has not been thoroughly investigated. We will
1144   dive into this topic in the future work.
1145
1146   Direct unit-to-unit generation. Although speech units have served as speech representation, they
1147   are predominantly adopted in conjunction with text-based assistance (Zhang et al., 2023a). However,
1148   the direct generation from unit to unit without text assistance is an area that has not been extensively
1149   explored. In Lee et al. (2021), speeches from the source language are directly translated into speech
1150   units of the target language for speech-to-speech translation. Similarly, Nguyen et al. (2023) builds
1151
       a language model directly on speech units, enabling spoken dialogue generation from raw audio.
       Both works develop models in speech-only data.
1152
1153   In the current version of EMOVA, the text modality is integrated into the speech generation process
1154   to transfer textual knowledge to the speech modality, thereby enhancing the correctness of speech
1155   responses. In the future, we will strengthen the model’s direct unit-to-unit generation capabilities to
1156   boost the speed of speech generation and augment the model’s comprehension of speech modality.
1157
1158   Vision configurations. Currently, we only utilize a single vision encoder pre-trained via a vision-
1159
       language manner, while recent works have shown effectiveness by combining vision encoders pre-
       trained by different manners (e.g., self-supervised pre-training (Liu et al., 2022)) and architectures
1160
       (e.g., MoE (Zhili et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024c)). We prioritize visual understanding in this work,
1161
       while the incorporation of (controllable) visual generation (Chen et al., 2023c; Gao et al., 2023;
1162   Li et al., 2023b; Wang et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023d; Gao et al., 2024) is also appealing to better
1163   empower EMOVA in real-life applications (Li et al., 2024c; 2022; Han et al., 2021).
