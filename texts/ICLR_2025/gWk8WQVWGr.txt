009                                           Abstract
010
011           By accessing only the surrogate model, attackers can craft adversarial
012           perturbations to fool black-box victim models into misclassifying a given
013           image into the target class. However, the misalignment between surrogate
014           models and victim models raises concerns about defining what constitutes a
015           successful targeted attack in a black-box setting. In our work, we empirically
016           identify that the vision-language foundation model CLIP is a natural good
017           indicator to evaluate a good transferable targeted attacks. We find that a
018
              successful transferable targeted attack not only confuse the model on the
              vision modality towards the target class, but also fool the model on the
019
              text modality between the original class and target class. Motivated by
020
              this finding, we propose a simple yet effective regularization term to boost
021           the existing transferable targeted attacks. We also revisit the feature-based
022           attacks, and propose to boost the performance by enhancing the fine-grained
023           features. Extensive experiments on the ImageNet-1k dataset demonstrate
024           the effectiveness of our proposed methods. We hope our finding can motivate
025           future research on the understanding of targeted attacks and develop more
026           powerful techniques.

029   1   Introduction
030
031   While deep neural networks have achieved remarkable progress across various applications,
032   their vulnerability to adversarial examples has raised significant concerns regarding the
033
      reliability of their practical deployment. Adversaries can craft targeted attacks to generate
      imperceptible perturbations and add them to benign samples, manipulating the decisions of
034
      these models. Moreover, the existence of adversarial transferability enables the application of
035
      adversarial examples generated on white-box surrogate models to efficiently attack black-box
036   models as well. Exploring methods to enhance the transferability of targeted adversarial
037   attacks can provide valuable insights into the nature of adversarial examples and motivate
038   the design of robust learning techniques for trustworthy AI applications.
039
      Targeted attacks are more challenging than untargeted ones because they require the crafted
040
      perturbation to not only confuse the neural networks but also to misclassify the object
041
      as a specific target class. Several studies have explored transferable targeted adversarial
042   attacks, focusing on designing advanced objectives, input transformations, and feature- and
043   model-based attack methods. These methods optimize the perturbation based on feedback
044   from the neural networks to achieve the target class. For instance, Zhao et al. (2021) and
045   Weng et al. (2023) propose maximizing the logit of the target class, while Inkawhich et al.
046   (2019b), Gao et al. (2021), and Inkawhich et al. (2019a) suggest amplifying the image features
047   associated with the target class, etc. Despite these strategies and tricks designed to enhance
048   performance, a natural question arises: What is the key factor contributing to successful
049   targeted attacks in a black-box setting?
050   To answer this question, we first need criteria to justify the success of black-box targeted
051   attacks in general. As shown in fig. 1, while previous work usually evaluates targeted
052   attack performance on a limited number of victim models, it fails to establish a reliable
053   criterion when there is no knowledge about the dataset used to train the victim models,
      e.g., the potential misalignment in the definition of the target class between the surrogate

096   2     Related work
097
098   2.1   Targeted Transferable Adversarial Attack
099
100   Szegedy et al. (2014) first identifies the existence of adversarial examples, which are crafted
101   by adding the human-imperceptible perturbation on benign samples to fool models’ decisions.
102   Targeted transferable adversarial attacks are the most threatening in real-world applications,
103   which target manipulating the black-box models’ decisions at attackers’ will. Many methods
104
      have been proposed for targeted transferable adversarial attacks, which can be generally
      categorized into four kinds, namely the input transformation-, advanced objective-, feature-
105
      and model-based methods.
106
107   Input transformation-based methods advocate exploiting the input diversity for better
      generalization ability in the optimization process, thus improving the adversarial transferabil-


                                                     2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      ity. While the methods studying the untargeted adversarial attacks can be directly used in
109   the context of targeted attacks, such as DIM, TIM, and Admix, there are some novel input
110   transformation-based methods mainly studying the targeted attacks. For example, Byun
111   et al. (2022) diverse the inputs based on 3D objects to enhance the targeted adversarial
112   attacks. Liu & Lyu (2024) propose a local mix-up strategy to randomly mix regions of
113   transformed adversarial images with each other, thus boosting the input diversity towards
114   better targeted adversarial attack performance.
115   Advanced objective-based methods design specialized loss functions to boost targeted
116   adversarial attacks. Li et al. (2020) introduce the Poincare distance as the similarity metric
117   to make the gradient more self-adaptive and suitable in the context of targeted attacks, thus
118   alleviating the curing of inherent noise in decreasing the transferability. Zhao et al. (2021)
119   propose the logit loss to directly enlarge the logit output of the targeted class to boost the
120   targeted attack performance. Weng et al. (2023) proposes to increase the logit margin to
121   deal with the saturation problem for better targeted adversarial transferability.
122   Feature-based methods focus on optimizing the latent space of adversarial images to
123   improve the targeted adversarial transferability. Wei et al. (2023) enhances the targeted
124   adversarial transferability by maximizing the similarity between the latent features of original
125   images and cropped images. Inkawhich et al. (2019b) and Gao et al. (2021) optimize the
126   feature of adversarial examples towards that of the image from target class. Inkawhich et al.
127   (2019a) attack the image by maximizing its posterior probability of the features for the target
128   class. Byun et al. (2023) fuses features of other benign samples with those of adversarial
129
      examples to boost the targeted adversarial transferability,
130   Model-based methods train better surrogate models or directly use the generative model to
131   generate adversarial perturbation for targeted adversarial attacks. Springer et al. (2021) find
132   that a surrogate model that is more robust to adversarial perturbation can be leveraged to
133   craft adversarial examples with highly targeted adversarial transferability. Yang et al. (2022a)
134   propose a hierarchical generative network to generate targeted adversarial perturbation to
135
      fool neural networks. Wang et al. (2023) design a generative adversarial training framework
      for targeted attacks, which consists of a generator used for crafting targeted adversarial
136
      examples, and feature-label dual discriminators to detect the adversarial examples from the
137
      images of the target class.
138
139
140   2.2   Adversarial Defense
141
142   Several strategies have been proposed to mitigate the risk of adversarial attacks on neural
143   networks. These include adversarial training (Madry et al., 2018; Tramèr et al., 2018; Wang
144   et al., 2021), input preprocessing (Xie et al., 2018; Naseer et al., 2020), feature denoising (Liao
145
      et al., 2018; Xie et al., 2019; Yang et al., 2022b), and certified defenses (Raghunathan et al.,
      2018; Gowal et al., 2019; Cohen et al., 2019), among others. Liao et al. (2018) developed a
146
      denoising autoencoder, referred to as the High-level Representation Guided Denoiser (HGD),
147
      which aims to cleanse adversarial perturbations. Xie et al. (2018) introduced a technique
148   that involves random resizing of the image and adding padding to reduce adversarial effects,
149   called Randomized Resizing and Padding (R&P). Xu et al. (2018) proposed the Bit Depth
150   Reduction (Bit-Red) method, which reduces the bit depth per pixel to mitigate perturbations.
151   Liu et al. (2019) defended against adversarial attacks using a JPEG-based compression
152   method on adversarial images. Cohen et al. (2019) employed randomized smoothing (RS) to
153   train a certifiably robust classifier. Naseer et al. (2020) proposed a Neural Representation
154   Purifier (NRP) designed to eliminate perturbations. We use defense methods to evaluate the
155   performance of targeted adversarial attacks.

180   3.1   Overview of Three Popular Methods and Beyond
181
      We start the discussion from studying three popular targeted attack methods, which achieves
182
      the state-of-the-art performance in recent years, and generally followed by others.
183
184   Logit (Zhao et al., 2021) directly maximizes the logit output of the target class using
185   a large number of iterations, achieving superior target transferability. While it is simple
186
      to implement and effective compared to optimizing the loss shown in Eq. (1) (Pros.), it
      overlooks the competition class and the original class, leading to performance degradation in
187
      targeted transferability (Cons.).
188
189   Logit-margin (Weng et al., 2023) builds upon the Logit attack by scaling the logits with a
190   temperature factor and an adaptive margin, which is the difference between the top-1 and
191   top-2 logits. Additionally, it reveals that minimizing the cosine similarity between the input
192
      feature of the final classification layer and the classifier weights of the target category can
      improve transferability. This method benefits from considering the competition class (Pros.),
193
      showing significant improvement over the Logit method. However, it is still limited by
194
      under-explored targeted features and exhibits poor transferability across different black-box
195   models (Cons.).
196
197   CFM (Clean Feature Mixup) (Byun et al., 2023) is a targeted adversarial attack method
198
      based on feature fusion. It pre-computes the clean features of benign samples and randomly
      mixes them with the features of adversarial examples during the attack process. The diverse
199
      features introduced encourage the attack to explore more alternative optimization directions
200
      on the landscape, thus achieving an effective and efficient targeted transferable attack (Pros.).
201   However, focusing solely on the feature space of the targeted class limits its potential for
202   performance improvement (Cons.).
203
204
      By utilizing the target class information of the surrogate model from different levels, i.e.,
      the logit at the most abstracting bottom level to the features at the top level, we observe
205
      consistent improvements in attack performance under the black-box setting (see table 2)
206
      and derive the following assumption: the key to successful transferable targeted attacks is
207   to fully utilize the generalized information of the target class to amplify robust target class
208   features while alleviating competition class features, where the competition class is typically
209   the original class..
210

356   3.4     Unraveling the Secrets Behind CFM’s Success
357
      From previous results, CFM stands out as the most effective targeted attack method,
358
      leveraging the features of benign samples to craft adversarial perturbations that approach the
359
      target class in the latent space. As discussed in the previous section, CFM employs a mixup
360   of a limited set of images, neglecting specific information of the target class. Intuitively, in the
361   context of transferable targeted attacks, one might expect that using more images belonging
362   to the target class would yield better performance compared to using purely random images.
363   But does this assumption hold true?
364
      To answer this question, we set up four pool of images used for feature mix-up during the
365
      CFM attack, including 1) Random: we use random images for mix-up, which is the original
366   implementation of CFM; 2) Original : we use the original image features for mix-up; 3)
367   Target: we collect the images for each target class, and only the target class images are used
368   for mixup during the attack; and 3) Combination: we randomly mix-up the target class
369   image features with the original image features to achieve a good diversity of image features
370   as well as involving more target features for better guidance.
371

411
412   While previous findings have shown that

424   grained features, making the model more
425   robust and mitigating the effectiveness of

      4   Conclusion
445
446   In this work, by studying three advanced targeted adversarial attack methods, we derive the
447   general insight that modeling the target class information suitably can significantly boost
448   targeted adversarial transferability. We empirically find that CLIP serves as an excellent
449   indicator for modeling target class information, enhancing attack performance. We also delve
450   into feature-based attacks to uncover underlying principles in deisgning an efficient targeted
451   attack, including the careful design of mix-up strategies and the importance of feature
452   diversity. Furthermore, we propose leveraging fine-grained features to improve targeted
453   adversarial transferability. Extensive experiments on the ImageNet-1K dataset, along with
454
      various defense models and commercial APIs, robustly demonstrate the effectiveness of our
      proposed method.
