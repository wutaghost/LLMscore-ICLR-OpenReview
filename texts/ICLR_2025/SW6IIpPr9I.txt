009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            A novel algorithm, called stochastic order learning (SOL), for reliable rank esti-
012            mation in the presence of label noise is proposed in this paper. For noise-robust
013            rank estimation, we first represent label errors as random variables. We then for-
014            mulate a desideratum that encourages reducing the dissimilarity of an instance
015            from its stochastically related centroids. Based on this desideratum, we develop
016
               two loss functions: discriminative loss and stochastic order loss. Employing these
               two losses, we train a network to construct an embedding space in which instances
017
               are arranged according to their ranks. Also, after teaching the network, we iden-
018
               tify outliers, which are likely to have extreme label errors, and relabel them for
019            data refinement. Extensive experiments on various benchmark datasets demon-
020            strate that the proposed SOL algorithm yields decent rank estimation results even
021            when labels are corrupted by noise.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Rank estimation aims to predict the rank or ‘ordered class’ of an object, which is a fundamental
027
      problem in machine learning. It has a variety of applications, such as facial age estimation (Ricanek
      & Tesafaye, 2006; Shin et al., 2022), aesthetic score regression (Kong et al., 2016), and medical
028
      assessment. Many approaches have been proposed for rank estimation (Pan et al., 2018; Li et al.,
029
      2021; Liu et al., 2018). Recently, order learning techniques (Lim et al., 2020; Shin et al., 2022; Lee
030   et al., 2022) have shown promising results.
031
032   Although these techniques provide remarkable results, their performance may be sub-optimal under
033
      the presence of label noise, as they are trained based on the assumption of accurate label annotations.
      In many real-world scenarios, however, it is quite challenging to obtain error-free annotations of
034
      ‘ordered data.’ Unlike nominal data, it is difficult to distinguish between classes of ordered data,
035
      since there is often no clear distinction between ranks. Figure 1(a) and (b) show nominal data for
036   classification and ordered data for rank estimation, respectively. Note that predicting the exact rank
037   of an instance is hard even for humans.
038
039
      Many algorithms have been developed to train machines using imperfect data with noisy labels,
      but most of them are for classification (Yao et al., 2022; Ye et al., 2023) or segmentation (Yang
040
      et al., 2020). Unlike classification, different errors have different severities in rank estimation. As
041
      classification-based approaches do not consider this ordinal property, they yield poor performances
042   when applied to noisy rank estimation. Therefore, it is crucial to develop a noise-robust algorithm
043   specialized for the task of rank estimation.
044
045
      In this paper, we propose a novel algorithm, referred to as stochastic order learning (SOL), to es-
      timate ranks reliably under the existence of label noise. Given a training dataset with noisy labels,
046
      we first model the label errors with random variables. Hence, each instance relates stochastically to
047
      multiple ranks rather than deterministically to a single rank. We then train an embedding network
048   based on a desideratum, which encourages minimizing stochastic dissimilarities of instances from
049   their corresponding centroids. To achieve this, we develop the discriminative loss and the stochastic
050   order loss. Moreover, after the training, we identify outliers that are likely to have extreme label
051   errors and relabel them to refine the noisy dataset. Extensive experiments demonstrate that the
052   proposed SOL algorithm provides reliable rank estimation results on various ordered datasets. Fur-
053   thermore, SOL even reduces the overall label noise of a given dataset based on the outlier detection
      and relabeling.

      The contributions of this paper can be summarized as follows.
067
068       • We extend the concept of order learning to cope with noisy data by designing a stochastic
069         approach; we model label errors as random variables and derive embedding space constraints
070         to sort instances according to their stochastically related ranks.
071       • Also, we propose outlier detection and relabeling schemes to identify instances with extreme
072         label errors and reduce the overall noise level of a given dataset.
073       • Experiments on various benchmark datasets for facial age estimation, aesthetic score regression,
074         and medical image assessment validate the effectiveness of the proposed SOL under label noise.

077   2     R ELATED W ORK
078
079   Learning from noisy labels: With the availability of substantial training data, deep learning has
080   shown impressive performance in numerous machine learning tasks, but the performance may de-
081
      grade severely in the presence of label noise. Thus, learning from noisy labels has been an active
      area of research; various attempts have been made to alleviate the adverse impacts of label noise.
082
      Some are based on robust loss functions (Ghosh et al., 2017; Zhang & Sabuncu, 2018; Lyu & Tsang,
083
      2019; Ma et al., 2020; Ye et al., 2023). Other methods include regularization (Tanno et al., 2019;
084   Menon et al., 2020; Xia et al., 2020), robust network architecture (Han et al., 2018a; Goldberger &
085   Ben-Reuven, 2022), and selective data sampling (Han et al., 2018b; Jiang et al., 2018; Song et al.,
086   2019). These methods, however, focus on classification or segmentation tasks.
087
      Compared to classification, only a few noise-robust regression algorithms have been developed
088
      (Garg & Manwani, 2020; Liu et al., 2020; Yao et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2024). Garg & Manwani
089
      (2020) first considered label noise in ordinal regression. They, inspired by Natarajan et al. (2013),
090   proposed an unbiased estimator approach, in which loss correction takes place to cope with class-
091   dependent label noise. Liu et al. (2020) designed a label regularization strategy to suppress possible
092   noise in ordinal datasets. Yao et al. (2022) developed a variant of Mixup (Zhang et al., 2018), which
093   improves generalization performance by training on virtual examples linearly interpolated from two
094   training samples. To make the Mixup technique more suitable for regression tasks, they sampled a
095   pair with closer ordinal labels with a higher probability. Recently, Liu et al. (2024) proposed a robust
096   algorithm for support vector ordinal regression. Even though their algorithm addresses label noise
097   explicitly, it only handles cases in which the number of ordinal labels is less than 5. In contrast, the
098
      proposed algorithm can deal with more difficult settings where datasets have more than 50 ranks.
099   Rank estimation: Different from ordinary classification, rank estimation aims to predict the ordered
100   class of an object. Many rank estimation methods estimate object ranks directly using classifiers or
101   regressors. Early methods convert a rank estimation problem into multiple binary classification
102   problems (Frank & Hall, 2001; Li & Lin, 2006). Recently, several techniques have been developed
103   to perform deep ordinal regression more effectively, such as pairwise regularization (Liu et al.,
104
      2018), soft labels (Diaz & Marathe, 2019), continuity-aware probabilistic network (Li et al., 2019),
      and uncertainty-aware regression (Li et al., 2021).
105
106   Order learning: Order learning (Lim et al., 2020) is a new approach to rank estimation based on
107   the idea that relative assessment is easier than absolute assessment. Instead of direct prediction,
      Lim et al. (2020) estimated the rank of an instance by comparing it with references of known ranks.

457   4.4      A NALYSIS
458
459   Label refinement: The proposed SOL algorithm is capable of refining noisy ranks, as well as
460   obtaining a reliable rank estimator from a noisy training set. In Table 5, we report MAEs between a
461   noisy rank rx and the true rank r̄x and the standard deviations of such noise levels before and after
462   the label refinement. In this test, we use the CLAP2015 and MORPH II datasets. Note that the MAE
463   or the standard deviation is reduced in 11 out of 12 tests, confirming the effectiveness of the label
464
      refinement scheme.
465   For further analysis, we test how the refinement changes the number of instances at each noise level
466   (i.e. label error). Figure 4 plots such statistics on the MORPH dataset at various κ’s. The red boxes
467   in Figure 4 specify the numbers of instances with high noise levels. We see that the number of
468   instances with an extreme noise level is reduced in general. Especially, at κ = 0.4, the number
469   of instances with 2 ≤ ex ≤ 4 is increased, while that with ex ≥ 7 is reduced significantly. It
470
      is desirable because severe label errors hinder the construction of a well-sorted embedding space.
      Consequently, in Tables 1∼4, the refinement generally boosts the performances of SOL.
471
472   We also provide examples of detected outliers in Figure 5. These examples are from MORPH II
473   at κ = 0.4. With the refinement scheme, instances with extreme label errors in Figure 5(a) are
474   relabelled more faithfully to the true ranks. Along with these successful cases, we also show some
475   failures cases in Figure 5(b). Although SOL succeeds in detecting these instances as outliers, it fails
476
      to correct the labels in the right directions. We see that facial images with closed eyes tend to be
      corrected wrongly. More label refinement results are provided in Appendix D.1.
477
478
      Ablation study: Table 6 compares ablated methods for the loss functions in (7). Method I employs
479
      the discriminative loss ℓdisc only, while method II does the stochastic order loss ℓorder only. Com-
480   pared with method III (SOL), both methods I and II degrade the rank estimation results, indicating
481   that both losses contribute to the performance improvement and are complementary to each other.
482   Note that method I yields poor results, since the discriminative loss alone cannot construct a mean-
483   ingful embedding space; it is trivial to reduce ℓdisc to zero by merging all instances into a single
484   point in the embedding space. However, by comparing methods II and III, we see that ℓdisc helps
485   to sort instances in the embedding space by properly attracting and repelling instances according to
      their ranks.

523   σ is the standard deviation of label errors e. The larger σ, the more strongly each instance x is
524   associated with the centroids for distant ranks from rx . Therefore, it may hinder constructing a rank
525   discriminative embedding space. On the other hand, if σ is small, the network training is vulnerable
526   to label errors because each instance x is related to nearby centroids only. Hence, at σ = 1, which
527   is the default option, SOL achieves good results in most tests.
528
529   5     C ONCLUSIONS
530
531   The SOL algorithm for rank estimation in the presence of label noise was proposed in this work.
532   First, we represented label errors as random variables. Then, we formulated a desideratum to reduce
533   the dissimilarity of an instance from the stochastically related centroids. Using the discriminative
534   loss and the stochastic order loss, we constructed an embedding space satisfying the desideratum.
535   Specifically, we trained a network to arrange the instances according to their unknown true ranks.
536   Moreover, by using the trained network, we identified outliers, likely to have extreme label errors,
537   and relabelled them for data refinement. Extensive experiments on various rank estimation tasks
538
      demonstrated that the proposed SOL algorithm yields decent rank estimation results even when
      labels are corrupted by noise.

      E     DATASETS
811
812
      MORPH II (Ricanek & Tesafaye, 2006): It is a dataset for facial age estimation, consisting of 55K
813
      facial images in the age range [16, 77]. It provides age, gender, and race labels. As in Chang et al.
814   (2011), we use 5,492 Caucasian images, which are divided into training and test sets with ratio 8:2.
815
816   CLAP2015 (Escalera et al., 2015): It is for apparent age estimation; the apparent age of each
817   image was rated by at least 10 annotators within the range [3, 85], and the mean rating was used as
818   the ground-truth. This dataset provides 4,691 facial images in total, which are split into 2,476 for
819   training, 1,136 for validation, and 1,079 for testing.
820
      AADB (Kong et al., 2016): It is a dataset for aesthetic score regression, composed of 10,000 pho-
821   tographs of various themes such as scenery and close-up. We use 8,500 images for training, 500
822   for validation, and 1,000 for testing. Each image is annotated with an aesthetic score in [0, 1]. We
823   quantize the continuous score with a step size of 0.01 to have 101 score ranks.
824
825   RSNA (Halabi et al., 2019): It is a dataset for pediatric bone age assessment, containing 14,236
826   hand radiographs. We employ the official evaluation protocol in Halabi et al. (2019): 12,611 for
827
      training, 1,425 for validation, and 200 for testing. The bone age range is [0, 216] in months.

      F.3   R ELABELING SCHEME
893
894   In our proposed relabeling scheme, the rank of detected outliers are adjusted by the same magnitude
895   using (20). Here, we assess the performance of when each detected outlier is relabeled using dif-
896   ferent magnitudes. Specifically, we adjust the rank of each outlier instance by half of the absolute
897   difference between its noisy and estimated rank. Table 16 lists the results on the CLAP2015 dataset.
898   Compared to when no relabeling method is used, method II shows performance improvement. How-
899
      ever, our proposed relabeling scheme provides better results. Using the same average values to adjust
      the ranks prevent drastic changes in rank labels, thus it results in more stable performance.

      SOL is the first attempt to extend the concept of order learning to noise-robust rank estimation.
911   Hence, not all details in real-world scenarios are covered. It is a future research issue to set noise
912   distributions adaptively according to each instance, and design an adaptive mechanism for selecting
913   optimal values of hyper-parameters.
