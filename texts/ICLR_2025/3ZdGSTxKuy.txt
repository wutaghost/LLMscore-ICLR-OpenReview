011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Humans usually show exceptional generalisation and discovery ability in the open
014            world, when being shown uncommonly new concepts. Whereas most existing
015            studies in the literature focus on common typical data from closed sets, and open
016
               world novel discovery is under-explored in videos. In this paper, we are interested
               in asking: what if atypical unusual videos are exposed in the learning process?
017
               To this end, we collect a new video dataset consisting of various types of un-
018
               usual atypical data (e.g. sci-fi, animation, etc.). To study how such atypical data
019            may benefit representation learning in open-world discovery, we feed them into
020            the model training process for representation learning. Taking out-of-distribution
021            (OOD) detection as a task to evaluate the model’s novel discovery capability, we
022            found that such a simple learning approach consistently improves performance
023            across a few different settings. Furthermore, we found that increasing the categor-
024            ical diversity of the atypical samples further boosts OOD detection performance.
025            These observations in our extensive experimental evaluations reveal the benefits
026            of atypical videos for visual representation learning in the open world, together
027
               with the newly proposed dataset, encouraging further studies in this direction.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Human cognition excels at generalising from limited information and discovering new concepts in
038   dynamic and unpredictable environments (Lieder & Griffiths, 2020; Saxe et al., 2021). This ability to
039
      adapt to unfamiliar stimuli in an open world contrasts with the limitations faced by current machine
      learning models (Heigold et al., 2023), especially in the field of video understanding. Current models
040
      operate mainly in closed hypothetical environments where all possible categories are predefined
041
      during training, which limits their ability to handle the variety of unpredictable scenarios often
042   encountered in real-world applications (Zhou et al., 2021; Kejriwal et al., 2024). The question
043   remains whether models can be enhanced to navigate the open world with the same adaptability as
044   human cognition.
045
      Previous advancements in video understanding have largely focused on closed-set environments,
046
      where the model is trained and tested on well-curated (Zhu et al., 2022), typical datasets such
047
      as UCF101 (Soomro, 2012), Kinetics400, and HMDB51 (Kuehne et al., 2011). Although these
048   models perform well within known distributions, they encounter significant difficulties when ex-
049   posed to out-of-distribution (OOD) data (Acsintoae et al., 2022; Rame et al., 2022), thereby limit-
050   ing their applicability to open-world environments where new and unknown categories frequently
051   emerge (Chen et al., 2023; Ming et al., 2022). There are also ways to use generative modelling, such
052   as GANs (Kong & Ramanan, 2021; Grcić et al., 2021) to generate virtual data or virtual features to
053   help with OOD detection (Du et al., 2022). Existing datasets, despite being useful benchmarks, do
      not encourage models to generalise beyond the constraints of the training distribution (Zhang et al.,


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      2021). As a result, the challenge of detecting and adapting to novel instances in the open world
055   remains an underdeveloped area in video representation learning.
056
057   The above-mentioned observations and limitations in current closed-set studies raise a crucial ques-
058
      tion: Would that help the models’ capability in open-world scenarios if introducing atypical and
      uncommon video data during training? By exposing models to data that lies outside the typical
059
      distribution, we argue that it may lead to a more robust capacity for OOD detection and novel dis-
060
      covery (Salehi et al., 2022). Addressing this question necessitates a reconsideration of traditional
061   video classification datasets and opens the possibility of utilising more diverse and atypical data
062   during training.
063
064
      Atypical data, characterised by its departure from common real-world categories, offers a unique
      avenue to challenge and enhance model generalisation. Unlike conventional datasets, which largely
065
      comprise trivial, everyday activities, atypical data refer to a wide range of unusual and outlier sce-
066
      narios, such as those found in science fiction, animation, and anomalous real-world situations. These
067   atypical samples present a broader spectrum of visual content, providing an opportunity for models
068   to learn from examples that deviate from the norm (Rame et al., 2022). We anticipate that incorporat-
069   ing this type of data during training will allow the model to better handle open-world environments.
070
      In order to systematically investigate the effectiveness of training with atypical data, we leverage a
071
      simple yet fundamental task – out-of-distribution (OOD) detection (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2017). It
072
      is a critical problem in deep learning, especially in open-world settings where models are frequently
073   exposed to data that diverges from the distribution they were trained on (Chen et al., 2023). The
074   primary objective of OOD detection is to identify when a sample originates from an unseen or novel
075   distribution, which is crucial for downstream tasks such as new class discovery and incremental
076   learning (Yang et al., 2024). This capability is fundamental for models operating in open-world
077   environments, where the ability to detect and adapt to novel inputs is critical for robust perfor-
078   mance (Morteza & Li, 2022). An illustration is shown in Figure 1.

097   To incorporate atypical data during training, we adopt the well-established outlier exposure (OE)
098   strategy (Hendrycks et al., 2019), which was designed to enhance models’ ability to recognise OOD
099   inputs (Papadopoulos et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2023a; Zhang et al., 2023). The core concept behind OE
100   is to leverage auxiliary outliers during training, enabling the model to learn to distinguish between
101   in-distribution (ID) and OOD samples more effectively (Ming et al., 2022). However, addressing
102   the essential distribution gap between surrogate OOD data and the unseen OOD inputs remains
103   challenging (Zhu et al., 2023b), as it is hard to know the prior knowledge of potential OOD inputs
104
      that would be encountered at the inference stage, and intentionally collect them (Zhu et al., 2023a).
      Our approach seeks to mitigate this by using a diverse and atypical dataset during the training phase,
105
      aiming to better equip models to handle a wide range of potential OOD scenarios.
106
107   Extensive experiments validate the effectiveness of incorporating auxiliary outlier samples in the
      video domain, which significantly improves model performance. Furthermore, our analysis shows

      that exposure to atypical video data (e.g. sci-fi, animation, abnormal, and unintentional) during
109   training significantly improves the model’s ability to detect OOD inputs compared to training with
110   only traditional video datasets. Notably, we observe that the diversity of atypical samples plays
111   a crucial role in this process. Models trained with more diverse atypical datasets show greater
112   robustness in identifying novel and unseen distributions. These findings highlight the potential and
113   effectiveness of the introduced atypical data in visual representation learning in the open-world
114   setting, suggesting future investigation in this direction.

118   2.1   OPEN - WORLD L EARNING AND OOD D ETECTION
119
120   Open-world learning (Kong & Ramanan, 2021; Yang et al., 2022; Vaze et al., 2021), which requires
121   models to recognise and adapt to novel inputs, has been a key challenge. OOD detection is an es-
122   sential task dedicated to handling unknown and unseen data (Yang et al., 2022). The main purpose
123
      of this task is to determine whether a sample is derived from the learned distribution Din . A sample
      in Din is called in distribution, otherwise it is called out of distribution, denoted as Dout . The OOD
124
      distribution Dout often simulates unknowns encountered during deployment, e.g. samples from un-
125
      related distributions (Zhu et al., 2023a), so that the Dout label set does not intersect with Din in the
126   OOD problem setting. Out-of-distribution (OOD) detection and open set recognition (OSR) (Vaze
127   et al., 2021; Geng et al., 2020) are closely related tasks in machine learning, both aim to deal with
128   unknown or unseen data, but OOD is a binary classification problem that focuses more on deter-
129   mining whether a sample belongs to ID or OOD, whereas OSR is an additional multiclassification
130   problem with the need to detect unknown classes (Yang et al., 2024; Salehi et al., 2022).
131
132   2.2   O UTLIER E XPOSURE FOR OOD D ETECTION
133
134   While the test time OOD distribution Dout remains inherently unknown (Zhu et al., 2023a), recent
135
      studies, notably by Hendrycks et al. (2019), have demonstrated the effectiveness of using Daux
      drawn from an auxiliary unlabelled dataset, to regularise the model during training. This approach
136
      leverages auxiliary outliers to encourage the model to reduce its confidence in anomalous inputs.
137
      By exposing the model to these auxiliary outliers during training, the model can better generalise to
138   detect unknown OOD samples at test time (Hendrycks et al., 2019; Zhu et al., 2023b).
139
140   Previous studies (Ming et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023a; Wahd, 2024) have shown
141
      that introducing auxiliary unlabelled data for OOD detection of outlier exposures in the text and
      image domains is very effective. However, in the same setting as the text and image domains,
142
      relatively less work has been done on OOD detection using anomaly exposure for the video domain,
143
      which may be related to the existence of a dedicated video anomaly detection (VAD) task (Sultani
144   et al., 2018; Acsintoae et al., 2022; Nayak et al., 2021) for the video domain. However, the biggest
145   difference between the OOD task for video action recognition and the VAD task is the difference
146   in their purpose, where VAD is more concerned with deviations and anomalies in behaviour or
147   patterns. In contrast, the goal of OOD for video category recognition is to expand the categories and
148   the identification of unknown categories (Yang et al., 2024).
149
150   2.3   V IDEO DATASETS
151
152   Video datasets have played a crucial role in advancing computer vision research, especially in recog-
153
      nising human behaviour through video analysis. The success of this field has been largely due to
      the various video datasets released to support this research (Kuehne et al., 2011; Kay et al., 2017;
154
      Soomro, 2012; Wang et al., 2014). Most contemporary datasets are designed for tasks such as hu-
155
      man movement classification and localisation, aiming to distinguish between various human activi-
156   ties (Poppe, 2010; Kong & Fu, 2022; Sun et al., 2022). Although these datasets provide benchmarks
157   for evaluating model performance, they are limited in their representation of atypical data—rare,
158   extreme, or fictional events that occur in real-world applications (Acsintoae et al., 2022).To address
159   this, in this paper, we propose to explore unusual atypical data, including videos from anomaly de-
160   tection, unintended actions, and fictional or animated media. We argue such atypical data is essential
161   to open-world learning (e.g. OOD detection) in the video domain by exposing models to a broader
      range of variability.

      The proposed atypical. To further enhance OOD detection, we introduce four atypical datasets: (i)
325   anomaly detection videos from Ped2, CUHK Avenue, and ShanghaiTech, (ii) unintentional actions
326   from the Oops dataset, (iii) science fiction scenes sourced from movie trailers, and (iv) animated

      To ensure a clear distinction between intra-distributional (ID) and extra-distributional (OOD) cate-
330
      gories, we followed the method proposed by (Hendrycks et al., 2019; Cen et al., 2023) to remove
331   the overlap between dataset categories. Specifically, we removed 6 overlapping action categories in

      This means that the categories in UCF101, HMDB51, Kinetics400, and MiT-v2 do not overlap at all
336
      in the experiment. Furthermore, the atypical dataset is significantly different from the categories in
337   these common video datasets in terms of conceptual and visual features. By implementing category
338   orthogonality, we effectively ensure that the OOD data are truly representative of the anomalous
339   samples and avoid potential information leakage between the ID data and the OOD data, thus en-
340   hancing the validity and reliability of OOD detection.

342   4.3   E VALUATION M ETRICS AND I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
343
344   Evaluation metrics. Following the methods of (Hendrycks et al., 2019; Yang et al., 2022; Zhu
345   et al., 2023a; Ming et al., 2022), We evaluate the OOD detection methods based on their ability
346   to identify OOD samples, treating OOD examples as the positive class. We use three metrics:
347
      FPR95 (False Positive Rate at 95% True Positive Rate), AUROC (Area Under the ROC Curve),
      and AUPR (Area Under the Precision-Recall Curve). AUROC and AUPR are holistic metrics that
348
      summarise performance across multiple thresholds. FPR95 measures the false positive rate when
349
      the true positive rate is fixed at 95%, reflecting how robust the detection method is in practical
350   scenarios. AUROC represents the probability that an OOD example receives a higher score than an
351   in-distribution example, where a higher AUROC is better, with 50% indicating random performance.
352   AUPR is particularly useful in imbalanced datasets with few OOD examples, as it considers the base
353   rate of anomalies.
354
355   Implementation details. All experiments are based on the ResNet3D-50 (Kataoka et al., 2020)
356   architecture as our backbone. The baseline is trained using only ID data with a cross-entropy loss
357   for multi-class classification over 100 epochs. The initial learning rate is set to 0.1 and decays
358   following a cosine learning rate schedule. For OOD sample testing, we use the MSP method. In the
359   outlier exposure setting, we fine-tune the pre-trained baseline model by introducing various outlier
360
      datasets, optimising the objective function as shown in equation 2. The fine-tuning process lasts for
      5 epochs. During fine-tuning, we again apply a cosine learning rate schedule with an initial learning

523   6    C ONCLUSION
524
525   In this paper, we propose a novel dataset, termed atypical, which contains a large collection of
526   video data that deviates from conventional, well-defined categories. This dataset was introduced
527   to better address the challenges of open-world scenarios and to explore its impact on the critical
528   task of OOD detection. We investigated how incorporating atypical video data enhances OOD de-
529   tection in open-world settings. Our experiments suggest that training with a smaller, yet diverse
530   set of atypical samples—such as those depicting science fiction, animation, unintentional actions,
531
      and abnormal events—substantially improves the model’s robustness in identifying unseen distribu-
      tions. The diversity within the atypical dataset played a crucial role in driving these improvements,
532
      underlining the importance of extending traditional datasets with more varied and unconventional
533
      content. Looking ahead, atypical data presents several promising avenues for future research. One
534   key direction is the continued enrichment of these datasets to better capture the unpredictability
535   of real-world environments. Furthermore, developing adaptive learning techniques that integrate
536   new atypical samples during inference could enable models to evolve dynamically, maintaining re-
537   silience in ever-changing conditions. The integration of multimodal data, such as audio and text,
538   with atypical video also holds the potential for enhancing models’ ability to capture the complexity
539   of open-world scenarios. Ultimately, research on atypical data opens new possibilities for advancing
      open-world learning and improving OOD detection.
