009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Research on loss surface geometry, such as Sharpness-Aware Minimization
012            (SAM), shows that flatter minima improve generalization. Recent studies further
013            reveal that flatter minima can also reduce the domain generalization (DG) gap.
014            However, existing flatness-based DG techniques predominantly operate within a
015            full-precision training process, which is impractical for deployment on resource-
016            constrained edge devices that typically rely on lower bit-width representations
017            (e.g., 4 bits, 3 bits). Consequently, low-precision quantization-aware training is
018
               critical for optimizing these techniques in real-world applications. In this paper,
               we observe a significant degradation in performance when applying state-of-the-
019
               art DG-SAM methods to quantized models, suggesting that current approaches
020
               fail to preserve generalizability during the low-precision training process. To ad-
021            dress this limitation, we propose a novel Gradient-Adaptive Quantization-Aware
022            Training (GAQAT) framework for DG. Our approach begins by identifying the
023            scale-gradient conflict problem in low-precision quantization, where the task loss
024            and smoothness loss induce conflicting gradients for the scaling factors of quan-
025            tizers, with certain layers exhibiting opposing gradient directions. This conflict
026            renders the optimization of quantized weights highly unstable. To mitigate this,
027            we further introduce a mechanism to quantify gradient inconsistencies and se-
028            lectively freeze the gradients of scaling factors, thereby stabilizing the training
029
               process and enhancing out-of-domain generalization. Extensive experiments val-
               idate the effectiveness of the proposed GAQAT framework. On PACS, both 3-bit
030
               and 4-bit exceed directly integrating DG and QAT by up to 4.5%. On DomainNet,
031
               our 4-bit results deliver nearly lossless performance compared to the full-precision
032            model, while achieving improvements of up to 1.39% and 1.06% over the SOTA
033            QAT baseline for 4-bit and 3-bit quantized models, respectively.

036   1    I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Deep learning models have demonstrated remarkable performance across various computer vision
039   tasks, such as classification (He et al., 2016; Sandler et al., 2018; Dosovitskiy, 2020), detection (Zhu
040   et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2022b), and semantic segmentation (Zhou et al., 2022b; Strudel et al.,
041   2021). However, these models typically experience significant performance degradation in real-
042   world applications due to domain shift, which manifests as poor generalization to previously unseen
043   data distributions. Domain generalization (DG) seeks to address this challenge by enabling models
044   trained on observed source domains to generalize effectively to unseen target domains. Strategies
045
      such as domain alignment (Li et al., 2018c; Muandet et al., 2013), data augmentation (Zhou et al.,
      2021; Volpi et al., 2018), and meta learning (Li et al., 2018a; Balaji et al., 2018) are commonly
046
      employed techniques. Recent studies (Gulrajani & Lopez-Paz, 2020), however, indicate that de-
047
      spite the development of these sophisticated techniques, basic empirical risk minimization (ERM)
048   still yields comparable out-of-distribution generalization when experimental conditions are carefully
049   controlled. Concurrently, growing attention has been directed towards the geometry of the loss land-
050   scape (Li & Giannakis, 2024; Foret et al., 2020; Andriushchenko & Flammarion, 2022; Wen et al.,
051   2023) in generation, particularly the Shareness-aware Minimization (SAM) that pursues flatter min-
052   ima during training. Recent works (Cha et al., 2021; Wen et al., 2023) has shown that a flatter
053   minimum could lead to a smaller DG gap. Inspired by previous studies of flat minima (Izmailov
      et al., 2018; Foret et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2022; Zhuang et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023b; Wang et al.,

      Despite the demonstrated effectiveness of flatness-aware methods in improving out-of-domain gen-
079   eralization, they are confined to full-precision training, which means the resulting models of current
080   methods are not very practical to deploy. In other words, in many real-world applications, espe-
081   cially those involving deployment on edge devices and are truly vulnerable to domain shift envi-
082   ronments, models operate under very computationally-constrained resources. Although the trained
083   low-precision computations, a.k.a. the quantization-aware training (Zhou et al., 2016; Tang et al.,
084   2022; Esser et al., 2019), have been extensively studied in I.I.D research for improving the runtime
085   efficiency, in which the models are trained with simulated quantization during the forward-backward
086   process and thus the weights can be aware of the numerical change, there still are challenging to
087
      achieve the generalized quantization-aware training for domain generalization, as (a) distinct objec-
      tives: Low precision aims to reduce model complexity, but conflicts with maintaining generalization.
088
      and (b) training instability: how to ensure the proper convergence for the low-precision weights as
089
      the simulated quantization and sharpness-aware minimization both involve specific gradient approx-
090   imation (Wen et al., 2023; Nagel et al., 2022; Tang et al., 2024). In fact, we have observed when
091   directly applying DG-SAM methods (Wen et al., 2023) to quantization-aware training (Esser et al.,
092   2019; Zhou et al., 2016), there could be an unexpected degradation of the model’s generalization
093   performance (e.g., the average out-of-domain performance drops by 28.36% when quantized to 4
094   bits in PACS).
095
      In this paper, we propose the Gradient-Adaptive Quantization-Aware Training (GAQAT) frame-
096   work for domain generalization. Specifically, we first incorporate the smoothing factor term into
097   the quantizer to ensure that both quantization and smoothness can be optimized jointly. Though
098   the optimization objective seems reasonable and is optimizable, the quantizer receives two distinct
099   gradients of the quantization and sharpness-aware minimization. By conducting a thorough analysis
100   of the behavior of the quantizer gradients, we reveal that the significant conflicts between task loss
101   (empirical loss) and smoothness loss induced by the gradient approximations cause the generaliza-
102   tion ability of the trained model to degrade, even worse-performing than models optimizing a single
103   objective. To this end, we define the gradient disorder that depicts the inconsistency of gradient
104
      directions during training to quantify the magnitudes of gradient conflicts. Based on this, we further
      design a dynamic freezing strategy, which selectively enables or disables the update of quantizers
105
      according to their gradient disorders, thus ensuring global convergence for the overall performance.
106

295   Based on these findings, we propose a selective freezing strategy to address scaling factor instability
296   and improve flatness convergence. Persistently freezing the gtask of certain layers without selectively
297   unfreezing them in specific cases may result in suboptimal convergence. Therefore, we adopt a

299

300

301

302   convergence and enhance the model’s generalization performance in DG scenarios. Full process is

308   4.1                                 E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP AND I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
309
      Quantization. We follow established practices in Quantization-Aware Training (QAT) literature
310
      by employing the LSQ-type method (Esser et al., 2019) to quantize both weights and activations.
311
      The quantization scaling factors are learned with a fixed learning rate of 1 × 10−5 . We use Mean
312   Squared Error (MSE) range estimation (Nagel et al., 2021) to determine the quantization parameters
313   for weights and activations. Due to the risk of test data information leakage of supervised pre-
314   trained weights revealed by Yu et al. (2024b), we employ MoCo-v2 (Chen et al., 2020) pretrained
315   ResNet-50 as initialization as recommended. Then we fine-tune the model using Empirical Risk
316   Minimization (ERM) to obtain a full-precision model with generalization capabilities, which serves
317   as the baseline for quantization. The weights and activations are fully quantized, except for the first
318   convolutional layer, which quantizes only the activations, and the final linear layer, which remains
319   unquantized, striking a balance between efficiency and model capacity. We evaluate the performance
320
      under extremely low bit-width conditions of 3 and 4 bits.
321   Datasets and evaluation protocol. We conduct a comprehensive evaluation on two widely used
322   DG datasets: PACS (Li et al., 2017), containing 9,991 images across 7 categories and 4 domains,
323   and DomainNet (Peng et al., 2019), consisting of 586,575 images across 345 categories and 6 do-
      mains. We basically follow the evaluation protocol of DomainBed (Gulrajani & Lopez-Paz, 2020),


                                                                                                                                                       6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      including the optimizer, data split, and model selection, where we adopt test-domain validation as
325   our model selection strategy for all algorithms in our experiments. For PACS, for each time we
326   treat one domain as the test domain and other domains as training domains, which is the leave-one-
327   domain-out protocol commonly adopted in DG. For DomainNet, following Yu et al. (2024b), we
328   divide the domains into three groups: (1) Clipart and Infograph, (2) Painting and Quickdraw, and
329   (3) Real and Sketch. Then we employ the leave-one-group-out protocol, where we treat one group
330   of two domains as test domains and other two groups as training domains each time. For the number
331   of training steps, for full-precision models we set it as 5,000 for PACS and 15,000 for DomainNet
332   following Cha et al. (2021), while for quantization training we use 20,000 for PACS and 50,000 for
333
      DomainNet. To reduce time cost, for quantization training we conduct validation and testing for
      DomainNet only after 45,000 steps.
334
335   Hyperparameter settings. Given the substantial computational resources required by the original
336   DomainBed setup, we adjust the hyperparameter search space and conduct grid search to reduce
337   computational cost following SAGM (Wang et al., 2023). The search space of learning rate is {1e-5,
338   3e-5, 5e-5}, and the dropout rate is fixed as zero. The batch size of each training domain is set as
339
      32 for PACS and 24 for DomainNet. Following SAM (Foret et al., 2020), we fix the hyperparameter
      ρ = 0.05. Following SAGM (Wang et al., 2023), we set α in Equation (1) as 0.001 for PACS and
340
      0.0005 for DomainNet, and set weight decay as 1e-4 for PACS and 1e-6 for DomainNet.
341
342   For PACS, the gradient disorder threshold r is selected from {0.28, 0.30, 0.32} for both 3-bit and 4-
343   bit quantization. The number of freeze steps is selected from {300, 350, 400} for 4-bit quantization,
344   and from {100, 150, 200} for 3-bit quantization. For DomainNet, r is selected from {0.20, 0.25}
345
      for 4-bit quantization, and from {0.02, 0.03} for 3-bit quantization. The number of freeze steps
      is chosen from {3000, 4000} for 4-bit quantization, and from {200, 300} for 3-bit quantization, as
346
      we observed that conflicts are more severe in 4-bit than in 3-bit quantization. To reduce the high
347
      computational cost, we first select the shared hyperparameters, i.e. learning rate, weight decay,
348   through grid search, which serve as the base hyperparameter configuration. Then we fix the base
349   configuration and conduct further grid search on our specific hyperparameters, i.e. freeze steps,
350   freeze threshold.

452   5.1     D OMAIN G ENERALIZATION
453
454   In practical applications, when deploying machine learning models, test data distribution may differ
455   from the training distribution, a common phenomenon known as distribution shift (Liu et al., 2021;
456   Yu et al., 2024a; Koh et al., 2021). Domain generalization (DG) aims to enhance a model’s ability
457   to generalize to unseen domains (Wang et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2022a). Common strategies include
458
      domain alignment (Muandet et al., 2013; Li et al., 2018c; Zhao et al., 2020), meta learning (Li
      et al., 2018a; Balaji et al., 2018; Dou et al., 2019), data augmentation (Zhou et al., 2021; Carlucci
459
      et al., 2019), disentangled representation learning (Zhang et al., 2022a) and utilization of causal
460
      relations (Mahajan et al., 2021; Lv et al., 2022). Inspired by previous studies of flat minima (Iz-
461   mailov et al., 2018; Foret et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2022; Zhuang et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023b),
462   flatness-aware methods start to gain attention and exhibit remarkable performance in domain gen-
463   eralization (Cha et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a), such as SAGM(Wang et al.,
464   2023), which improves generalization by optimizing the angle between weight gradients. However,
465   these methods primarily focus on full-precision models, which are impractical for deployment on
466   edge devices commonly used in high-risk scenarios and do not take into account the factors specific
467   to quantization. We specifically focus on strategies to enhance model generalization in quantized
468   training environments.

      5.2   Q UANTIZAION - AWARE T RAINING
487
488   Quantizaion-aware training (QAT) involves inserting simulated quantization nodes and retraining the
489   model, which achieves a better balance between accuracy and compression ratio (Hubara et al., 2021;
490   Nagel et al., 2020). DoReFa (Zhou et al., 2016) and PACT (Choi et al., 2018) use low-precision
491   weights and activations during the forward pass and utilize STE techniques (Bengio et al., 2013)
492   during backpropagation to estimate gradients of the piece-wise quantization functions. LSQ (Esser
493   et al., 2019) adjusts the quantization function by introducing learnable step size scaling factors. Re-
494
      cently, some works have explored the possibility of improving quantization performance by freezing
      unstable weights to further enhance results (Nagel et al., 2022; Tang et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023);
495
      however, these methods have only considered the Identically Distributed (I.I.D.) assumptions. Due
496
      to distribution shifts in unseen data—which often occur in practical applications—the quality and
497   reliability of quantized models cannot be guaranteed (Hu et al., 2022).

500   6     CONCLUSION AND FUTURE WORK
501
502   In this paper, we propose GAQAT for domain generalization. We introduce a smoothing factor into
503   the quantizer to jointly optimize quantization and smoothness. Our analysis of quantizer gradients
504   revealed significant conflicts between task loss and smoothness loss due to gradient approximations,
505
      impacting generalization. To address this, we define gradient disorder to quantify quantizer gra-
      dient conflicts and designed a dynamic freezing strategy that selectively updates quantizers based
506
      on disorder levels, ensuring global performance convergence. Extensive experiments on PACS and
507
      DomainNet, along with ablation studies, demonstrate the effectiveness of GAQAT.
508
509   Limitations and future work. Although we incorporated SAGM’s smoothing objective into quan-
510   tization, other smoothing objectives may also impact scaling factor gradients, suggesting future re-
511
      search potential. Our experiments reveal varying domain sensitivity to scaling factor gradients, but
      we only examined conflicts between task and flatness objectives. The relationship between domains
512
      and scaling factors remains unexplored.
