010                                               A BSTRACT
011
012            Recent advancements in 3D Large Language Models (3DLLMs) have highlighted
013            their potential in building general-purpose agents in the 3D real world, yet chal-
014            lenges remain due to the lack of high-quality robust instruction-following data,
015
               leading to limited discriminative power and generalization of 3DLLMs. In this pa-
               per, we introduce Robin3D, a powerful 3DLLM trained on large-scale instruction-
016
               following data generated by our novel data engine, Robust Instruction Genera-
017
               tion (RIG) engine. RIG generates two key instruction data: 1) the Adversarial
018            Instruction-following data, which features mixed negative and positive samples
019            to enhance the model’s discriminative understanding. 2) the Diverse Instruction-
020            following data, which contains various instruction styles to enhance model’s gen-
021            eralization. As a result, we construct 1 million instruction-following data, con-
022            sisting of 344K Adversarial samples, 508K Diverse samples, and 165K bench-
023            mark training set samples. To better handle these complex instructions, Robin3D
024            first incorporates Relation-Augmented Projector to enhance spatial understand-
025            ing, and then strengthens the object referring and grounding ability through ID-
026
               Feature Bonding. Robin3D consistently outperforms previous methods across
               five widely-used 3D multimodal learning benchmarks, without the need for task-
027
               specific fine-tuning. Notably, we achieve a 7.8% improvement in the grounding
028
               task (Multi3DRefer) and a 6.9% improvement in the captioning task (Scan2Cap).

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Spatial Intelligence [Li, 2024] refers to the ability of AI to understand the 3D world and reason
034
      within 3D space. Related ideas, such as Embodied AI [Duan et al., 2022] and Robotic Agent [Bous-
035
      malis et al., 2023], express a similar aim to build general-purpose assistants in the 3D real world. To
036   achieve this goal, researchers have drawn inspiration from the success of 2D Multimodal Large Lan-
037   guage Models (MLLMs) [Liu et al., 2024c; You et al., 2023] and have started exploring the potential
038   of 3D Large Language Models (3DLLMs) to create general agents [Hong et al., 2023; Chen et al.,
039   2024a; Wang et al., 2023b; Huang et al., 2023a] in the 3D domain or to attain Spatial Intelligence.
040
      Instruction-following tuning [Liu et al., 2024c;a;b] in MLLMs refers to training the LLM to execute
041
      natural language commands by integrating both textual and visual information. In contrast to the
042   versatile image-text pairs employed for training 2D MLLMs, collecting 3D instruction-following
043   data for 3DLLM remains a significant challenge. Although existing works have made progress
044   [Hong et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023b; Chen et al., 2024c] in generating more instruction data,
045   they still lack robustness in two aspects: 1) Most of the existing instruction data consist of positive
046   pairs, lacking adversarial or negative samples. Therefore, models trained on such data tend to be
047   less discriminative because they might overfit to the positive pairs and are more likely to hallucinate
048   positive responses to any input. 2) Some instruction data also lack diversity in language styles, as
049   human annotators or generative models [OpenAI; Wang et al., 2023a] are typically asked to follow
050
      fixed instructions when describing objects [Chen et al., 2020; 2021], or the data is generated using
      predefined templates [Achlioptas et al., 2020], which may limit models’ generalizability.
051
052   To address these challenges, we introduce Robin3D, a robust and powerful 3D Large Language
053   Model tuned on large scale instruction-following data generated by our novel data engine, Robust
      Instruction Generation (RIG) engine. Specifically, RIG is designed to generate two types of data:

055   samples with positive ones. This process decouples the potential memorized positive pairs in the

106   3D Vision-Language Learning Recent advancements in 3D vision-language (3D-VL) learning
107   [Chen et al., 2020; Achlioptas et al., 2020; Azuma et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2021; Kang et al.,
      2024b] have focused on bridging the gap between 3D scene understanding and natural language,

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      which is essential for developing embodied intelligence. Similar to 2D vision-language learning
109   [Kazemzadeh et al., 2014; Kang et al., 2024c; Johnson et al., 2016; You et al., 2023; Kang et al.,
110   2024a; Antol et al., 2015; Zhang et al., 2024; Kang et al., 2024d], a variety of tasks such as 3D Visual
111   Grounding [Chen et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2023; Achlioptas et al., 2020], 3D Dense Captioning
112   [Chen et al., 2021], and 3D Question Answering [Azuma et al., 2022; Ma et al., 2022] have been
113   proposed to evaluate the ability of human instruction following in relation to 3D object properties
114   and spatial relationships. Initial efforts focus on building a task-specific model for a single tasks,
115   such as EDA [Wu et al., 2023] for grounding and Vote2Cap-DETR++ [Chen et al., 2024b] for
116   captioning. Recent research has shifted towards developing unified models capable of handling
117
      multiple 3D scene-language tasks. Approaches like 3DJCG [Cai et al., 2022] and D3Net [Takahashi
      & Mitsufuji, 2020] leverage task synergies, while 3D-VisTA [Zhu et al., 2023], 3D-VLP [Jin et al.,
118
      2023] and PQ3D [Zhu et al., 2024] introduce pre-training techniques and unified representations to
119
      align 3D visual data with language. However, their dependence on task-specific heads restricts their
120   flexibility for more generalized user-assistant interactions.
121
122
      3D Large Language Model Following the success of 2D Multimodal Large Language Models
123   [You et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024c;a;b], researchers begin to explore the reasoning
124   abilities and promising generalizability of LLMs in human instruction following within the 3D do-
125   main, leading to the emergence of 3D Large Language Models (3DLLMs). Models like PointLLM
126   [Xu et al., 2023] and Imagebind-LLM [Han et al., 2023] show strong performance in object-level
127   tasks by mapping 3D data into LLMs. However, they face difficulties in handling scene-level rea-
128   soning. 3D-LLM [Hong et al., 2023] incorporates positional embeddings and location tokens, and
129   Oryx [Liu et al., 2024d] offers a solution to support multi-view arbitrary resolution image. How-
130   ever, their reliance on 2D encoders limits its ability to fully capture 3D spatial structures. Models
131
      such as LL3DA [Chen et al., 2024a], Chat-3D [Wang et al., 2023b], LEO [Huang et al., 2023b], and
      Scene-LLM [Fu et al., 2024] have made progress in improving scene-level dialogue capabilities,
132
      showing promising results in question-answering and captioning tasks. However, their insufficient
133
      visual grounding capability limits their application in Embodied AI or Robotic Agents, which re-
134   quire precise object localization and manipulation following human instruction. To further enhance
135   grounding abilities, Grounded 3D-LLM [Chen et al., 2024c] introduces referent tokens and con-
136   trastive learning to unify grounding and textual responses. Similarly, Chat-3D v2 [Huang et al.,
137   2023a] proposes the use of object identifiers (object IDs) for referring and grounding. Building
138   on Chat-3D v2[Huang et al., 2023a], Chat-Scene [Huang et al.] further incorporates DINO v2
139   [Oquab et al., 2023] to provide strong multi-view, object-centric 2D representations. Despite these
140   advancements, current 3D LLMs, which are trained solely on positive 3D visual-language pairs and
141   template-based instructions, suffer from suboptimal generalization and a potential for overfitting.

145   3.1   P RELIMINARY
146
147   To train a 3D LLM using instruction fine-tuning, we first represent the 3D scene as a sequence of
148   vision tokens, then append it with system and question prompts, expressed as sequences of language
149   tokens, to indicate the task. Taking the above tokens as input, a LLM is supervised to output the
150   answer tokens via next token prediction. Specifically, as shown in Fig. 2, given the point cloud of
151   a 3D scene, we use the pre-trained 3D segmenter Mask3D [Schult et al., 2023] to extract object
152   features along with their corresponding 3D masks. Following Huang et al., we further sample each
153
      object’s point cloud based on the 3D masks, normalize it, and employ the pre-trained Uni3D [Zhou
      et al., 2023] to extract unified object-centric 3D features. Additionally, 2D masks projected from
154
      the 3D masks are used to sample and average 2D features, which are extracted by DINO v2 from
155
      multi-view images of each object. Our Relation-Augmented Projector fuses the 3D features and
156   position embeddings from Mask3D and Uni3D into our final 3D features. In line with Huang et al.,
157   we incorporate special tokens {< OBJi >}i=1...n as object IDs into the vocabulary. These ID tokens
158   are paired with 2D & 3D object features to indicate each object, for referring to the object in the
159   input instruction or grounding the object in model’s output. Our ID-Feature Bonding combines each
160   object feature with its corresponding object ID, and appends the system and question prompts at the
161   beginning of the sequence, which are then fed into the LLM. For more details on the object IDs and
      the extraction of different types of features, please refer to Huang et al..

      3.2      R ELATION -AUGMENTED P ROJECTER (RAP)
184   As shown in Fig. 2 (bottom), to obtain relation-aware 3D features while preserving the unified
185   object-centric characteristics, our RAP considers three types of 3D features: a) the object features
186   from Mask3D, Xmask3d , which are scene-level respresentations, containing spatial relationship, as
187   they come across multiple cross-attention layers to exchange information. b) the position embed-
188   dings of the Mask3D, Xpos , which are directly projected from the coordinates of the corresponding
189   object features. c) the unified object features, Xuni3d , from Uni3D. Our RAP is formulated as:
190
           X = Concat(NormL2 (Xuni3d ), NormL2 (Xmask3d )),                                               Xrap = MLP(X) + MLP(Xpos )                                                        (1)
191
192   where NormL2 is the L2 normalization, Concat is the concatenation alongside the channel dimen-
193   sion, and MLP is a multi-layer perceptron with GELU activation [Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2016].
194   The Xrap represents our final relation-aware unified 3D features, which is augmented by the spatial
195   relationship information from Mask3D and the position embeddings.
196
197   3.3      ID-F EATURE B ONDING (IFB)
198
      We propose IFB for better referring and grounding in our instruction-following data by improving
199
      the connection between object IDs and object features. As shown in Fig. 2 (middle), we first use two
200
      identical ID tokens to wrap the object features. Adhering to the causal attention nature of the LLM,
201   this approach links ID information to the object features via the first ID token, and links object
202   information to its ID via the second ID token. Secondly, we propose a post-vision order, which
203   places the vision tokens at the end of the input sequence, closer to the answer tokens generated by
204   the model. This approach mitigates attention deviation from the answer tokens to the ID-Feature
205   pairs, a problem caused by their relative token distance and rotary position embeddings [Su et al.,
206   2024; Ma et al., 2023], while reinforcing the visual information for improved answer generation.
207   The post-vision order is structured as: [<System tokens>, <Instruction tokens>, <Vision tokens>,
208   <Answer tokens>], where <Vision tokens> comprises the ID tokens and object feature tokens.

244   4.1.1        H YBRID O BJECT P ROBING E VALUATION (HOPE) – Fig. 3(upper left)
245
246
      To construct a scene-level category-based task, we introduce HOPE, which is inspired by the POPE
      benchmark [Li et al., 2023] in 2D domain. POPE evaluates the tendency of 2D MLLMs to hal-
247
      lucinate by asking yes/no questions about the presence of one specific object at a time. Building
248
      on this, HOPE further incorporates such hallucination challenges into the training stage in the 3D
249   domain, aiming to train our model to be more discriminative. Additionally, HOPE presents a hybrid
250   scenario, introducing greater complexity to further advance the decoupling of memorized positive
251   vision and language pairs. Specifically, given a 3D scene, we ask the model to determine the pres-
252   ence of various randomly specified objects. The objects may or may not be present in the scene,
253   and each existing object might have one or more instances. The model is required to answer “No”
254   when the object is not present in the scene, and answer “Yes” with the object ID of each instance of
255   the object when it exists. As shown in Fig. 3 (upper left), the question combines multiple objects,
256   separated by semicolons (“;”), and the answer combines responses for each object, also separated
257
      by semicolons. This structure creates a challenging setting that involves hybrid recognition of both
      positive and negative object presence, combined with multi-instance object localization.

260   4.1.2        H YBRID R EFERRING O BJECT C LASSIFICATION (HROC) – Fig. 3(upper right)
261
      Referring Object Classification [You et al., 2023] evaluates a model’s ability to understand a re-
262
      ferred region in 2D domain, focusing on a classification problem by “Region-in Text-out” format.
263
      Our HROC dataset extends this task into the training data for 3D domain to create an object-level
264   category-based task, by incorporating adversarial and hybrid challenges. Specifically, in a 3D scene,
265   we randomly create hybrid positive and negative ID-Category pairs to form our questions, as il-
266   lustrated in Fig. 3 (upper right). A positive pair consists of a valid object ID and the ground truth
267   category. The bounding box of the corresponding object ID must overlap with one ground truth
268   bounding box, and the category of the overlapping object is defined as the ground truth category.
269   A negative pair includes a valid object ID and a randomly selected category that is present in the
      scene but not the ground truth category to serve as an adversarial challenge. The model is required

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      to answer “Yes” for positive pairs and “No” with the correct category for negative pairs. The pairs
271   and corresponding answers are separated by semicolons (“;”).

      4.1.3    PARTIAL FACTUAL 3D V ISUAL G ROUNDING (PF-3DVG) – Fig. 3(lower left)
275
      Our PF-3DVG introduces a scene-level expression-based task, featuring three types of data in
276   3DVG: unfactual data, partially factual data, and factual data. For unfactual data, given a 3D scene,
277   we randomly select a reference from Sr3D+ [Achlioptas et al., 2020] where the indicated object
278   does not exist in the scene. The model is required to answer “No” when prompted with the question,
279   as shown in the first example of Fig. 3 (lower left). For partial factual data, given a reference from
280   Sr3D+ and its corresponding 3D scene, we randomly switch the described spatial relationship with
281   a different one based on the predefined template of Sr3D+. For example, as shown in the second
282   example of Fig. 3 (lower left), we change the original reference “the pillow lying on the couch” to
283   “the pillow under the couch”. In this case, it is still possible for human annotators to ground the
284
      target based on this partial factual information, but this introduces an adversarial challenge since the
      information is not completely accurate. Therefore, we require the model to retify the information
285
      and answer “It is ‘lying on’” while providing the grounding result (object ID). Notably, we only use
286
      references whose target object has no distractors sharing the same category, ensuring that the partial
287   factual information is still informative enough for grounding the target and does not lead to ambi-
288   guity. For factual data, we randomly augment the spatial relationship with its synonym to improve
289   diversity. For example, the synonym of “below” can be “under”, “beneath”, or “underneath”.

      4.1.4    FAITHFUL 3D Q UESTION A NSWERING (3DFQA) – Fig. 3(lower right)
292
293   The original 3D Question Answering (QA) task [Azuma et al., 2022] includes only positive samples,
294   which can potentially lead to the model memorizing fixed combinations of 3D scenes and QA pairs.
295   To address this, we propose Faithful 3D Question Answering, a scene-level expression-based task
296   which incorporates both negative and positive examples with an additional grounding requirement.
297   To construct negative samples, we first sample a QA pair and collect the related objects that are
298   mentioned in the question or the target objects of the answer from Azuma et al. [2022]. Then,
299   we randomly select a 3D scene that lacks those related objects. A new instruction is added to the
300   question: “If you can, answer the question... and provide all the IDs...” as illustrated in Fig. 3 (lower
301
      right). In this case, the model must faithfully answer “No” based on the absence of related objects
      in the 3D scene and must not provide any object IDs, demonstrating its reliance on the scene for
302
      making decisions. For positive samples, directly taken from Azuma et al. [2022], the model must
303
      answer the question while faithfully grounding its “evidence” for the answer, i.e., providing the IDs
304   of the related objects. Therefore, the model trained on our 3DFQA dataset is forced to generalize
305   beyond memorization, learning to respond faithfully to both positive and negative samples.

309
      The Diverse data aim to enhances the model’s generalization by first incorporating multiple different
310
      types of instruction-following data and then increasing the linguistic diversity of the instructions.
311
312   We first collect large scale data from different tasks outside the benchmark dataset. Specifically,
313   given a 3D scene, we collect question-answering pairs from the following tasks: 1) Category
314   Question-Answering task from Huang et al. [2023a], where the model is asked to answer the cate-
315
      gory of a specified object. 2) Nr3D Captioning task from Huang et al. [2023a], where the model is
      asked to caption the spatial relationship of a specified object to its neighboor. The ground truth is
316
      constructed from Nr3D [Achlioptas et al., 2020] dataset. 3) Appearance Captioning task from Chen
317
      et al. [2024c], where the model is asked to captioning the physical attributes or visual characteristics
318   of a specified object. The ground truth is generated by CogVLM [Wang et al., 2023a]. 4) Region
319   Captioning task from Chen et al. [2024c], where the model is asked to caption the region encircling
320   a specified object. The ground truth is organized by ChatGPT [OpenAI]. 5) End to end 3D Visual
321   Grounding from Nr3D dataset [Achlioptas et al., 2020], where the model is not provided ground
322   truth candidates, different from the original setting in Nr3D. 6) End to end 3D Visual Grounding
323   from Sr3D+ dataset [Achlioptas et al., 2020], where the model is also not provided ground truth
      candidates, different from the original setting in Sr3D+.

406   5.1    B ENCHMARKS AND M ETRICS
407
408   We provide quantitative results on five widely-used 3D multimodal learning benchmarks: ScanRefer
409
      [Chen et al., 2020] for 3D Visual Grounding, Multi3DRefer [Zhang et al., 2023] for General 3D
      Visual Grounding including zero, single and multiple target objects, Scan2Cap [Chen et al., 2021]
410
      for 3D Dense Captioning, ScanQA [Azuma et al., 2022] for 3D Question Answering, and SQA3D
411
      [Ma et al., 2022] for 3D Situated Question Answering. The vision data are all based on the ScanNet
412   dataset [Dai et al., 2017], which contains real world 3D point clouds across 1,513 indoor scenes with
413   detailed object annotations. All these benchmarks follow the same data split as ScanNet.
414
415
      We follow the standard evaluation metrics widely adopted in the respective benchmarks. For Scan-
      Refer, we calculate accuracy at Intersection over Union (IoU) thresholds of 0.25 and 0.5 (Acc@0.25,
416
      Acc@0.5). For Multi3DRefer, we use the F1 score with IoU thresholds of 0.25 and 0.5 to measure
417
      performance. In Scan2Cap, we apply the CIDEr@0.5 and BLEU-4@0.5 (C@0.5, B-4@0.5) met-
418   rics, combining standard captioning metrics with the IoU metric. For ScanQA, the METEOR and
419   ROUGE metrics, denoted as M and R, are employed. Lastly, SQA3D is assessed with exact match
420   accuracy (EM) and its extended form, EM-R, as suggested by LEO [Huang et al., 2023b].
421
422
      5.2    I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
423
424
      We extract 150 object features from each 3D scene, along with the corresponding position embed-
425
      dings and 3D masks generated by Mask3D. The 2D Projector, as shown in Fig. 2, is a two-layer
426   MLP. We use the Vicuna-7B-v1.5 model [Chiang et al., 2023] as our LLM and fine-tune it using
427   LoRA [Hu et al., 2021] (with a rank of 16) by Cross Entropy loss. The global learning rate is for-
428   mulated as [batch size × base learning rate × number of GPUs] and is set to 0.00064, with a cosine
429   annealing schedule. For our results in Tab. 1, we first train for 2 epochs on the RIG-generated data,
430   and then train for 2 epochs on the benchmark training sets in the second stage. We train for 1 epoch
431   for each stage to efficiently conduct ablation studies of RIG-generated data. For ablation on RAP
      and IFB, we train for 1 epoch on the benchmark training sets to avoid potential compound effects.

      5.3     Q UANTITATIVE R ESULTS
433
434   We classify current methods into two categories: Task-Specific Training and Joint Training. Task-
435   Specific Training refers to models only trained for a specific task, while Joint Training means train-
436   ing on multiple tasks jointly. Our Robin3D does not conduct task-specific fine-tuning.
437
438   • Task-Specific Training: As shown in Table 1, models like EDA and M3DRef-CLIP perform well
439     on their respective tasks due to customized model design for the task. However, they lack the
440     ability to generalize to other tasks. Models like Vote2Cap-DETR++ and SQA3D encounter the
441     similar issue. Therefore, they are not suitable to serve as general-purpose 3D AI agents.
442
      • Joint Training: Benefiting from sharing the knowledge across multiple tasks, models like 3D-
        VisTA and PQ3D show decent performance across multiple tasks, but their dependence on task-
443
        specific heads restricts their generalizability. Models like LEO and Chat-Scene show promising
444
        results by leveraging LLMs, but their sole training on positive pairs and template-based instruc-
445     tions leads to suboptimal generalization.
446   • Our Robin3D: Due to the robust instruction data generated by RIG, Robin3D significantly outper-
447     forms previous models across all the benchmarks. Specifically, Robin3D brings a 6.9% improve-
448     ment on Scan2Cap CIDEr@0.5 and a 5.3% improvement on ScanRefer Acc@0.25. Notably, on
449     the evaluation of Multi3DRefer, which contains zero-target cases that are challenging for mod-
450     els to be discriminative and learn to say “No”, our Robin3D achieves a 7.8% improvement in
451     F1@0.25 and a 7.3% improvement in F1@0.5.

      6 C ONCLUSION
532
      To build a general-purpose AI agent in the 3D real world, we identify the problem of a lack of ro-
533
      bust instruction training data in current 3DLLMs. To tackle this challenge, we introduce Robin3D,
534   a powerful 3DLLM trained on large-scale instruction-following data generated by our novel data
535   engine, Robust Instruction Generation (RIG) engine. We generate and collect 1 million instruction
536   data, including benchmark data, adversarial data, and diverse data. To better handle these complex
537   instructions, Robin3D incorporates a Relation-Augmented Projector to enhance the understanding of
538   spatial relationships among objects, and ID-Feature Bonding for better object referring and ground-
539   ing. Finally, Robin3D achieves state-of-the-art performance across five widely-used 3D multimodal
      learning benchmarks, making significant progress towards Spatial Intelligence.
