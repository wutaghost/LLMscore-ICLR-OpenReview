011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Dynamic data pruning, also known as data importance sampling, has been proposed
014            to improve the efficiency of training large machine learning models. In the case
015
               of sampling with replacement, the optimal sampling distribution to minimize the
               variance is to sample proportionally to the gradient norm. However, this could
016
               result in repeated samples, which is an undesirable property. Noticing that most
017
               dynamic data pruning methods that avoid repeated samples can be viewed as
018            weighted Bernoulli sampling, in this work, we derive the optimal distribution to
019            minimize variance, thereby accelerating convergence. Furthermore, to eliminate
020            the need for an extra computation for gradient estimation, we study the use of his-
021            torical statistics. We then propose Omission (Optimal inspired historical weighted
022            Bernoulli sampling), which tracks the exponential moving average of the squared
023            gradient norm and adopts scale normalization as well as probability smoothing to
024            improve the performance. Experiments on image classification and large language
025            model instruction finetuning tasks show that our proposed method achieves higher
026
               accuracy than other dynamic pruning methods.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      The substantial growth in dataset sizes has made training large machine learning models a challenging
031   and time-consuming task. However, it is crucial to recognize that not all samples contribute equally to
032   the learning process, and more importantly, the relevance of samples can change dynamically during
033   training. Some samples might be initially important but become less informative as the model learns.
034   This observation has motivated the field of dynamic data pruning, also known as data importance
035   sampling, which aims to enhance the training efficiency of machine learning models (Alain et al.,
036   2015; Zhao & Zhang, 2015; Katharopoulos & Fleuret, 2018). This technique dynamically assigns
037   non-uniform sampling probabilities to each sample, with the goal of reducing variance in the gradient
038   estimator and thereby accelerating the minimization of the loss function. In the case of sampling
039
      with replacement, it has been shown that the optimal sampling weight distribution for minimizing
      variance is proportional to the gradient norm of each sample (Alain et al., 2015; Zhao & Zhang, 2015).
040
      However, directly computing the full per-sample gradient to obtain the norm defeats the purpose
041
      of importance sampling. Therefore, several works approximate gradient norm using alternative
042   approaches, such as focusing on the gradient of the final layer (Katharopoulos & Fleuret, 2018).
043
044   Despite the theoretical justification, a notable drawback of sampling with replacement is its potential
045
      to generate repeated samples, which can be an undesirable property. Instead, researchers have started
      to propose other dynamic data pruning methods that avoid repeated samples (Jiang et al., 2019; Raju
046
      et al., 2021; Qin et al., 2023; Thao Nguyen et al., 2024). We observe that most of these methods
047
      can be broadly categorized as weighted Bernoulli sampling. However, the sampling weights in
048   these methods are typically determined by some heuristics, not through variance minimization. This
049   limitation motivates our research to find optimal sampling weights for weighted Bernoulli sampling.
050   We prove that the sampling probability should be proportional to the gradient norm for samples with
051   smaller gradients, while being capped at one for those with larger gradients. Furthermore, for the
052   case where there are random variations caused by drop-out or data augmentation, we conclude that
053   the sampling probability should be linear to the square root of the expected gradient norm squared or
      capped at one.

054
      Furthermore, to avoid additional forward passes on unselected samples, we study the use of historical
055   statistics to approximate the gradient norm at the current step. We then propose Omission (Optimal
056   inspired historical weighted Bernoulli sampling). Inspired by the optimal sampling probability
057   distribution under random variation, we maintain an exponential moving average of the squared
058   gradient norm for each sample. To enhance performance, we also normalize the scale by tracking the
059   exponential moving average across all the samples and conduct probability smoothing to improve the
060   performance.
061   We conduct extensive experiments on several image classification and large language model instruc-
062   tion finetuning tasks. The results show that our proposed method achieves higher accuracy than other
063   dynamic pruning methods.
064
      Our contribution can be summarized as follows.
065
066          • We derive the optimal distribution to minimize variance in weighted Bernoulli sampling,
067            thereby accelerating convergence.
068          • Inspired by the optimal distribution, we propose Omission (Optimal inspired historical
069            weighted Bernoulli sampling), which utilizes historical statistics to avoid additional forward
070            passes on unselected samples
071          • We propose to track the exponential moving average of the squared gradient norm and adopt
072            scale normalization as well as probability smoothing to improve the performance.
073          • Our results show consistent improvements against state-of-the-art dynamic data pruning
074            algorithms.

      Consequently, we should design the sampling distribution to minimize the variance. In the case
109   of sampling with replacement, this leads to an optimal sampling distribution where the sampling
110   probability of each sample is proportional to its gradient norm ∥∇ℓi (θt )∥ (Alain et al., 2015; Zhao

113   which still requires a forward pass on unselected samples.
114
      Despite the theoretical justification, sampling with replacement could result in repeated samples,
115   which can be undesirable. Instead, researchers have started to propose other dynamic data pruning
116   methods that avoid repeated samples. Jiang et al. (2019) propose to use the forward pass to obtain
117   the loss of each sample and conduct Bernoulli sampling, where the sampling probability of each
118   sample is determined by the ranking of its loss. Qin et al. (2023) propose to use the previous loss
119   function value as the score to eliminate the additional forward pass. Samples with scores larger than
120   the average are selected with probability 1, while the rest are selected with a discounted probability
121   r. Raju et al. (2021) propose to maintain a moving average of the previous loss function value as
122   the score. A fixed fraction of F lowest score samples are selected with probability ϵ, while the rest
123
      are selected with probability 1. Thao Nguyen et al. (2024) propose to prune a fraction of Fi of the
      lowest-loss samples, where Fi is determined by the epoch number i.
124
125   These methods can all be roughly viewed as weighted Bernoulli sampling, as samples do not repeat
126   and are selected independently. However, the sampling weights in these methods are all determined
127   by some heuristics, not through variance minimization. This limitation motivates our research to find
128   optimal sampling weights for weighted Bernoulli sampling.

      3.3   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
276
277   For our proposed methods, we provide two different implementations. The first implementation
278   strictly follows equation 4, which obtains a larger batch B through random shuffling. We then use
279   weighted sampling to select a batch of size b. This has a variable number of samples at each step with
280   an expected value of b. The second implementation is an approximation to the first one based on the
281   observation that, unlike the method of Katharopoulos & Fleuret (2018) and Jiang et al. (2019), which
282   relies on the statistics of the current step, our score relies on historical statistics, which is determined
283   at the beginning of the epoch. Thus, we can instead sample from all of the n training samples and
284
      then randomly group them into batches of size b.
285   We observe little performance differences for the two implementations, so the second implementation
286   might be preferable in practice, as it produces a fixed batch size while being easier to implement.

288   3.4   C ONVERGENCE
289
290   It is known that all importance sampling methods converge to the stationary point of the original
291   objective (or to the optimum for a convex objective) as long as it is unbiased and the sampling
292
      probability is lower bounded. This is due to the fact that the convergence proof of most optimization
      methods like Sec 2.3 of (Défossez et al.) only requires the gradient estimator to be bounded and
293
      unbiased.
294
295   In our algorithm, we ensure the sampling probability is lower bounded by adopting probability
296   smoothing. Furthermore, the sampling of our proposed method is unbiased, which many previous
297   works on data selection have overlooked. These ensure the convergence of our proposed method.

299   4     R ELATED W ORK
300
301   In this section, we discuss previous work in static and dynamic data pruning.
302
      Static Data Pruning.
303
304   Static data pruning chooses a fixed subset (coreset) of the whole training set throughout training.
305   Static data pruning methods can be categorized as error based (Toneva et al., 2019; Paul et al., 2021),
306   geometry based (Welling, 2009; Sener & Savarese, 2018), gradient matching based (Killamsetty
307   et al., 2021a), and bilevel optimization methods (Killamsetty et al., 2021b;c).
308   Forgetting (Toneva et al., 2019) counts the number of forgetting events for each sample during
309   training, samples that are more unforgettable are the pruned. GRAND and EL2N (Paul et al., 2021)
310   statically prune samples based on the gradient norm and the L2-norm of the error. Herding (Welling,
311   2009) incrementally and greedily add samples that minimize the distance between the coreset center
312   and the center of the original training set. K-Center greedy (Sener & Savarese, 2018) is a greedy
313   approximation to the minimax problem, where the coreset is selected to minimize the largest distance
314
      of each sample to the closest coreset sample. GradMatch (Killamsetty et al., 2021a) select the coreset
      that approximates the gradient of the full training set. Lastly, Glister (Killamsetty et al., 2021b) and
315
      Retrieve (Killamsetty et al., 2021c) treat static data pruning as a bilevel optimization problem.
316
317   Dynamic Data Pruning.
318   Dynamic data pruning is also known as data importance sampling. Since the variance of an unbiased
319   gradient estimator can be connected with the expected reduction of the loss function, the optimal
320   sampling distribution to minimize the variance for sampling with replacement has been studied.
321   Alain et al. (2015) and Zhao & Zhang (2015) derive that in the case of sampling with replacement,
322   the optimal sampling probability of each sample is proportional to its gradient norm ∥∇ℓi (θt )∥.
323   Alain et al. (2015) propose a distributed setting where ∥∇ℓi (θt )∥ is computed by the worker nodes.
      Zhao & Zhang (2015) propose to use the smoothness constant of the per-sample loss to approximate

      ∥∇ℓi (θt )∥. Katharopoulos & Fleuret (2018) propose to use the gradient norm of the logits as an
325   approximation, which requires a forward pass on unselected samples.
326
327   Despite the theoretical justification, sampling with replacement could result in repeated samples,
328
      which can be undesirable. Instead, researchers have started to propose other dynamic data pruning
      methods that avoid repeated samples. Jiang et al. (2019) propose to use the forward pass to obtain

341   and LLM fine-tuning tasks. For image classification, we consider CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and
342   ImageNet-100. For LLM, we evaluate the performance of instruction finetuning LLaMA-7B using

      Since Qin et al. (2023) already show that static pruning methods in general perform worse than
      dynamic pruning methods, we only compare our method with other dynamic pruning methods.
349
      Specifically, we include the following algorithms in our comparisons:
350
351          • Weighted Replacement (Katharopoulos & Fleuret, 2018): Sampling with replacement. The
352            sampling weights are proportional to the gradient norm of the logits, which is obtained from
353            an additional forward pass.
354          • Selective Backprop (Jiang et al., 2019): Bernoulli sampling. The sampling weights are
355            decided by the ranking of the loss obtained from an additional forward pass.
356          • Extra Forward Bernoulli: Bernoulli sampling. The sampling weights follow our derived
357            sampling distribution. Uses the gradient norm of the logits, which is obtained from an
358            additional forward pass.
359
             • Infobatch (Qin et al., 2023): Samples whose previous loss is lower than the average are
360            selected by a discounted probability.
361
             • Historical Replacement: Our modified version of Weighted Replacement (Katharopoulos &

363

               utilizes historical statistics.
365
366          • Reducing Epoch: Reduces the number of epochs without any pruning.
367          • Reducing Epoch (scaled): Reduces the number of epochs without any pruning and increases

514   6       C ONCLUSIONS
515
516   Noticing that most dynamic data pruning methods that avoid repeated samples can be viewed as
517   weighted Bernoulli sampling, in this work, we derive the optimal distribution to minimize variance,
518   thereby accelerating convergence. We then propose Omission (Optimal inspired historical weighted
519   Bernoulli sampling), which tracks the exponential moving average of the squared gradient norm and
520
      adopts scale normalization as well as probability smoothing to improve the performance. Experiments
      on image classification and large language model instruction finetuning tasks show that our proposed
521
      method achieves higher accuracy than other dynamic pruning methods.
522
523   Limitations and Future Work. Even though we derive the optimal sampling distribution to minimize
524   the variance of weighted Bernoulli sampling given a fixed sampling budget, the optimal sampling
525   budget for each epoch is still unknown. While we adopt a fixed 50% sampling ratio in our experiments,
526
      methods with dynamic sampling ratios have been proposed. For example, the annealing mechanism
      proposed by Infobatch (Qin et al., 2023) disables dynamic data pruning near the end of the training.
527
      This can be seen as a way to set different sampling budgets for each epoch, which can be combined
528
      with our proposed method. We leave the topic of setting a dynamic sampling ratio for future
529   exploration.
