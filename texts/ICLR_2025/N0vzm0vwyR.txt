                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011
               Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) achieve high performance in image clas-
012
               sification tasks but are challenging to deploy on resource-limited hardware due
013            to their large model sizes. To address this issue, we leverage Mutual Informa-
014            tion, a metric that provides valuable insights into how deep learning models retain
015            and process information through measuring the shared information between input
016            features or output labels and network layers. In this study, we propose a struc-
017            tured filter-pruning approach for CNNs that identifies and selectively retains the
018            most informative features in each layer. Our approach successively evaluates each
019            layer by ranking the importance of its feature maps based on Conditional Mutual
020            Information (CMI) values, computed using a matrix-based Rényi α-order entropy
021
               numerical method. We propose several formulations of CMI to capture correla-
               tion among features across different layers. We then develop various strategies
022
               to determine the cutoff point for CMI values to prune unimportant features. This
023
               approach allows parallel pruning in both forward and backward directions and
024            significantly reduces model size while preserving accuracy. Tested on the VGG16
025            architecture with the CIFAR-10 dataset, the proposed method reduces the number
026            of filters by more than a third, with only a 0.32% drop in test accuracy.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Convolution Neural Network (CNN) has achieved remarkable success in various tasks such as image
032   classification, object detection, and segmentation (Zhang et al., 2019), (Li et al., 2021). Deeper ar-
033   chitectures such as VGG16 (Simonyan & Zisserman, 2014) and ResNet (He et al., 2016) have shown
034   superior performance in handling complex image classification tasks. However, the effectiveness of
035   these networks is often reliant on very deep and wide architectures, resulting in a very large number
036   of parameters that lead to longer training and inference time, and create challenges when deploying
037   them on resource-constrained devices (Blalock et al., 2020), (Yang et al., 2017).
038   CNNs often contain redundant weights and parameters, as certain weights learned in a network are
039   correlated (Sainath et al., 2013). To reduce network size and improve inference speed, network
040   pruning techniques target different components such as weights, filters, and channels, using a range
041   of criteria (see Related Work). A common approach is to measure the weight magnitudes to identify
042   unimportant connections (Han et al., 2015), (Molchanov et al., 2016), (Aghasi et al., 2020).
043   A less explored approach involves using mutual information between the network’s output and la-
044   tent features to detect redundant filters. Yu et al. (2020) assessed the information flow in CNNs
045   by leveraging the Rényi α-order entropy and conducted a preliminary analysis using Conditional
046   Mutual Information (CMI) to identify key filters. However, their study only uses CMI within a sin-
047   gle layer, without considering the shared information among features across layers. Furthermore,
048   the CMI-permutation method used to retain filters drastically underestimates the number of useful
049   features. We confirmed in our experiments that the retained features in Yu et al. (2020) lead to a
050
      significant drop, of more than 80%, in model accuracy.
051   In this paper, we build upon the concept of using CMI from Yu et al. (2020) to develop an effective
052   method for pruning CNNs while preserving high accuracy. Our key contributions include advancing
053   CMI computation across layers, defining optimal CMI cutoffs, and developing pruning strategies
      applicable to all CNN layers. Specifically, we introduce novel CMI formulations that capture shared

054
      information across multiple layers, improving the measure’s effectiveness in assessing feature im-
055   portance. We also propose two methods for determining the CMI cutoff point to ensure optimal
056   feature retention. Finally, we develop a robust algorithm for pruning CNN layers bidirectionally,
057   starting from the most critical layer. Evaluations on the VGG16 architecture with the CIFAR-10
058   dataset demonstrate a 26.83% reduction in parameters and a 36.15% reduction in filters, with only
059   a minimal 0.32% drop in test accuracy, underscoring the effectiveness of our approach. We provide
060   our code and data at: https://github.com/cmiprune/cmiprune.git.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
063
064
      Deep neural network pruning has seen major advancements in recent years, with various approaches
065
      on reducing model complexity while maintaining performance. These approaches can be catego-
066
      rized into pruning at initialization, dynamic pruning, unstructured pruning, and structured pruning.
067
068   Pruning at initialization involves selecting weights or neurons likely to contribute little to the overall
069   network performance and removing them without using any gradient steps. Sadasivan et al. (2022)
070
      designed OSSuM for pruning at initialization by applying a subspace minimization technique to
      determine which parameters can be pruned. Tanaka et al. (2020) proposed an approach to measure
071
      parameter importance called synaptic saliency and ensured that this metric is preserved across layers.
072
      However, Frankle et al. (2020) critically examined popular pruning methods at initialization and
073   argued that pruning during training remains more effective.
074
075   Dynamic pruning approaches adjust the pruning process during training or inference. Shneider
076
      et al. (2023) explored disentangled representations using the Beta-VAE framework, which enhances
      pruning by selectively eliminating irrelevant information in classification tasks. Chen et al. (2023)
077
      introduced OTOv3 that integrates pruning and erasing operations by leveraging automated search
078
      space generation and solving a novel sparse optimization.
079
080   Unstructured pruning removes individual weights rather than entire structures like filters, result-
081   ing in more flexibility but less hardware efficiency. Molchanov et al. (2019) proposed a Taylor
082
      expansion-based pruning method that estimates the importance of weights by their impact on the
      loss function. Aghasi et al. (2020) introduced Net-Trim, which removes individual weights by for-
083
      mulating the pruning problem as a convex optimization to minimize the sum of absolute entries of
084
      the weight matrices. Ding et al. (2019) introduced Global Sparse Momentum SGD, a weight pruning
085   technique that dynamically adjusts the gradient flow during training to achieve high compression ra-
086   tios while maintaining model accuracy. Lee et al. (2019) demonstrated the role of dynamical isome-
087   try in ensuring effective pruning across various architectures without prior training. Han et al. (2015)
088   combined weight pruning, quantization, and Huffman coding to achieve significant compression.
089
      Structured Pruning focuses on removing entire channels, filters, or layers, making it more com-
090
      patible with modern hardware. He & Xiao (2023) provided a comprehensive survey in structured
091   pruning of deep convolutional neural networks, emphasizing the distinction between structured and
092   unstructured pruning and highlighting the hardware-friendly advantages of structured approaches.
093   Crowley et al. (2018) suggested that networks pruned and retrained from scratch achieve better ac-
094   curacy and inference speed than pruned-and-tuned models. You et al. (2019) developed the Gate
095   Decorator method that employs a channel-wise scaling mechanism to selectively prune filters based
096   on their estimated impact on the loss function, measured through a Taylor expansion. Lin et al.
097   (2022) grouped consecutive output kernels for pruning. Xu et al. (2019) integrated low-rank approx-
098   imation into the training process, dynamically reducing the rank of weight matrices to compress the
099
      network. Considering Convolutional Neural Networks, various approaches have been introduced for
      filter pruning. Guo et al. (2020) pruned filters using a differentiable Markov process to optimize per-
100
      formance under computational constraints; Sehwag et al. (2020) pruned filters based on an empirical
101
      risk minimization formulation; Liu et al. (2019) utilized a meta-learning approach; Molchanov et al.
102   (2016) interleaved greedy criteria-based pruning with fine-tuning by backpropagation, using a crite-
103   rion based on Taylor expansion to minimize impact on the loss function. Li et al. (2020) developed
104   EagleEye, a pruning method that leverages adaptive batch normalization to quickly and efficiently
105   evaluate the potential of pruned sub-nets without extensive fine-tuning. He et al. (2017) proposed a
106   channel pruning method based on LASSO regression and least squares reconstruction. Zhuang et al.
107   (2018) incorporated additional discrimination-aware losses to maintain the discriminative power of
      intermediate layers. He et al. (2019) proposed filter pruning via Geometric Median targeting re-

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      dundant filters to reduce computational complexity. Yu et al. (2020) proposed applying Conditional
109   Mutual Information and Permutation-test to retain a set of important filters.
110
111   This paper shares a common objective with prior work in the structured pruning domain, particularly
112
      focusing on filter pruning for Convolutional Neural Networks. While existing methods employ
      various pruning criteria, our study explores the application of mutual information (MI), specifically
113
      leveraging the matrix-based α-order Rényi entropy computation to produce MI values which are
114
      used to guide the pruning process. This paper contributes to the area of applying MI in machine
115   learning, emphasizing the use of MI to identify and retain the most informative filters across layers.

118   3     C OMPUTING THE CMI VALUES OF C ANDIDATE F EATURE S ETS
119
120
      In this section, we analyze the use of Conditional Mutual Information (CMI) as a metric to measure
      feature importance, and discuss several approaches to ordering the features in each CNN layer and
121
      computing their CMI values. We propose new CMI computation that leverages shared information
122
      across layers and further exploit Markovity between layers to make the computation efficient.
123
124
      3.1   S ELECTED F EATURES S ET AND N ON - SELECTED F EATURES S ET
125
126   We first define the notation used for the rest of the paper. Let X and Y be the input and output
127   data of the CNN. We consider a pretrained CNN model that has N CNN layers, {Li }i=1,...,N . Each
128   layer Li contains multiple feature maps obtained by feed-forwarding the training data to this layer
129   using the layer filters. At each layer Lk , the feature map selection process involves separating the
130   set of feature maps Fk at layer Lk into two distinct sets: the selected set Fks and the non-selected set
131   Fkn , that is, Fk = {Fks , Fkn }.
132   Selected feature set Fks is a subset of the feature map set Fk at layer Lk and consists of feature
133   maps selected according to a selection criterion as discussed later in Section 4. The selection criteria
134   are designed to retain a high test accuracy on the retrained CNN model after pruning.
135
      Non-selected feature set Fkn is the rest of the feature maps at layer Lk , i.e. Fkn = Fk \ Fks , which
136   consists of feature maps that do not significantly contribute to the model’s performance, and hence
137   can be pruned to simplify the model complexity without compromising accuracy.
138
139
      Selection metric: We are interested in the information that the feature maps in each layer convey
      about the CNN output, which can be measured by the mutual information (MI) between the feature
140
      map set Fk and the output Y . Note the following MI relationship:
141
142
                             I(Y ; Fk ) = I(Y ; Fks , Fkn ) = I(Y ; Fks ) + I(Y ; Fkn |Fks )                 (1)
143   We observe that the selected feature set Fks will convey most information about the output Y if the
144   second term of the summation in Eq. (1) is sufficiently small. This second term measures the con-
145   ditional mutual information (CMI) between the non-selected feature set and the output, conditioned
146
      on the selected feature set. That is to say, given the selected feature set Fks , if the non-selected fea-
      ture set Fkn does not bring much more information about the CNN output, then it can be effectively
147
      pruned without affecting CNN accuracy performance. As such, in our algorithms, we will compute
148
      the CMI values of various candidate feature sets for pruning to determine the best set to prune.
149

201
      input layer to the output layer, passing through multiple hidden layers. In each propagation, the
202   computation flows in a single direction, with the latent features at each layer depending only on

288   4     D ETERMINING A C UTOFF P OINT FOR CMI VALUES IN E ACH L AYER
289
290
      After ordering the features of each CNN layer and computing the CMI values of candidate sets of
291   features as in Section 3, the features are arranged in descending order of CMI values. The next step
292   is to determine a cutoff point within the ordered list of CMI values such that the set of features with
293   CMI value at the cutoff point is selected and retained, and the set of features with lower CMI, which
294   contributes little to the CNN output, is pruned. In this section, we propose two methods to identify
295   such a cutoff point based on the Scree test and X-Mean clustering.
296
297
      4.1   I DENTIFYING C UTOFF P OINT USING S CREE T EST
298
299   The Scree test (Cattell, 1966) is first proposed in Principal component analysis (PCA) to deter-
300   mine the number of components to be retained using their eigenvalues plotting against their com-
301   ponent numbers in descending order. The point where the plot shifts from a steep slope to a
302   more gradual one indicates the meaningful component, distinct from random error (D’agostino Sr
303   & Russell, 2005). Furthermore, Niesing (1997) introduced the Quotient of Differences in Ad-
304   ditional values (QDA) method, which identifies the q th component that maximizes the slope
305   s(q) = (λq − λq+1 )(λq+1 − λq+2 )−1 where λq is the eigenvalue for the q th component in PCA.
306   Here we apply the QDA method (Niesing, 1997) to the list of decreasing CMI values obtained as in
307   Section 3. To explore more than one candidate cutoff point, we propose to find K CMI values that
308   correspond to the top K largest slopes as

366   5     A LGORITHMS FOR P RUNING A LL L AYERS OF A CNN BASED ON CMI
367
368   We now combine methods from the previous two sections in an overall process to systematically
369   traverse and prune every layer of a CNN. We propose two algorithms that differ in their starting
370   layer and pruning direction. One algorithm begins at the first convolutional layer and prunes forward
371   through the network. The other algorithm starts at the layer with the highest per-layer pruning
372   percentage and simultaneously prunes both forward and backward from there.
373   The pruning process consists of three phases as illustrated in Figure 3. The first phase is Data
374   Preparation which generates the feature maps of each layer. We start with a pre-trained CNN model
375   that feeds forward the data using mini-batch processing through each CNN layer Lk to produce a set
376   of feature maps Fk . The second stage is the main Pruning Algorithm in which every convolutional
377   layer of the CNN is processed and pruned in a certain order. The last stage is Retraining of the
      pruned model to fine-tune the model parameters to improve accuracy performance.

      5.1   F ORWARD M ODEL P RUNING
404
405   In Forward Pruning, the algorithm starts with the first convolutional layer and prunes all convolu-
406   tional layers sequentially from first to last. At each layer, the algorithm applies the chosen feature
407   ordering and CMI computation method (Section 3) to produce the decreasing CMI value list, then
408   applies the chosen cutoff point identification method (Section 4). In cross-layer CMI computation,
409   the CMI values of each layer are computed by conditioning on the selected feature sets of previous
410   layers. Algorithm 4 describes this forward pruning procedure.
411
412   5.2   B I - DIRECTIONAL M ODEL P RUNING
413
      We design Bi-directional Pruning to improve the previous pruning approach by first determining the
414
      most effective layer to begin the pruning process. We propose to start with the layer that has the
415
      highest per-layer pruning percentage while maintaining an acceptable post-pruning accuracy. First,
416   we perform trial-pruning of each convolutional layer of the CNN individually, using per-layer CMI
417   computation and either the Scree test or X-Means method. This initial stage lets us identify the
418   layer with the highest pruning percentage as the starting layer for the full CNN pruning process.
419   Next, we start from the identified best layer and proceed by using cross-layer CMI computation to
420   prune the original CNN in both directions, forward and backward. For compact CMI computation,
421   at each new layer, the compact CMI values are conditioned on the immediately previous layer that
422   was pruned, which can either be the preceding layer (in forward pruning) or the succeeding layer
423   (in backward pruning). For full CMI computation, we condition the CMI on all previously pruned
424
      layers from the starting layer in the corresponding direction. We note that in Bi-directional pruning,
      per-layer CMI computation in Eq. (4) is only used at the initial stage to determine the starting layer;
425
      after that, the pruning process uses cross-layer CMI computation in Eq. (8) or Eq. (6). Algorithm 5
426
      outlines the detailed procedure of Bi-directional Pruning.

      The Permutation-test (Yu et al., 2021) shows the smallest pruned model size but at a drastically
      reduced test accuracy to only 10.02% even after retraining. This shows that the Permutation test was
521
      not able to differentiate unimportant features from the important ones and hence pruned aggressively
522
      and indiscriminately. In contrast, the proposed Scree-test and X-means both achieve more than a
523   third of the features pruned while still retaining most of the accuracy of the original model. The
524   results show that Scree-test is slightly more robust than X-means by achieving both a higher pruned
525   percentage and a better retrained-accuracy. This could be because Scree-test is more effective at
526   preserving the most important feature maps compared to X-means.

      7      C ONCLUSION
529
530
      In this study, we introduced novel structured pruning algorithms for Convolutional Neural Networks
531
      (CNNs) by using Conditional Mutual Information (CMI) to rank and prune feature maps. By ap-
532   plying matrix-based Rényi α-order entropy computation, we proposed several CMI-based methods
533   for identifying and retaining the most informative features while removing redundant ones. Two
534   different strategies, Scree test and X-means clusterng, were explored to determine the optimal cutoff
535   points for pruning. We also examine both forward and backward prunings which were found to be
536   effective. Our experiments demonstrated that the proposed approach significantly reduces the num-
537   ber of parameters by more than a third with negligible loss in accuracy, achieving efficient model
538   compression. This method provides a promising framework for deploying CNN models on resource-
539   constrained hardware without compromising performance. Future work may explore extending this
      approach to other network architectures and tasks beyond image classification.
