009                                                         A BSTRACT
010
011            The performance of a model trained with noisy labels is often improved by simply retraining the
012            model with its own predicted hard labels (i.e., 1/0 labels). Yet, a detailed theoretical characterization
013            of this phenomenon is lacking. In this paper, we theoretically analyze retraining in a linearly
014            separable setting with randomly corrupted labels given to us and prove that retraining can improve
015            the population accuracy obtained by initially training with the given (noisy) labels. To the best of our
016            knowledge, this is the first such theoretical result. Retraining finds application in improving training
017
               with local label differential privacy (DP) which involves training with noisy labels. We empirically
               show that retraining selectively on the samples for which the predicted label matches the given
018
               label significantly improves label DP training at no extra privacy cost; we call this consensus-based
019
               retraining. As an example, when training ResNet-18 on CIFAR-100 with   = 3 label DP, we obtain
020            6.4% improvement in accuracy with consensus-based retraining.

023   1    I NTRODUCTION
024
025   We study the simple idea of retraining an already trained model with its own predicted hard labels (i.e., 1/0 labels and
026   not the raw probabilities) when the given labels with which the model is initially trained are noisy. This is a simple
027   yet effective way to boost a model’s performance in the presence of noisy labels. More formally, suppose we train a
028   discriminative model M (for a classification problem) on a dataset of n samples and noisy label pairs {(xj , ŷj )}nj=1 .
029   Let θ̂0 be the final learned weight/checkpoint of M and let ỹj = M(θ̂0 , xj ) be the current checkpoint’s predicted hard
030   label for sample xj . Now, we propose to retrain M with the ỹj ’s in one of the following two ways:
031
032
           (i) Full retraining: Retrain M with {(xj , ỹj )}nj=1 , i.e., retrain M with the predicted labels of all the samples.
033       (ii) Consensus-based retraining: Define Scons ∶= {j ∈ {1, . . . , n} ∣ ỹj = ŷj } to be the set of samples for which the
034            predicted label matches the given noisy label; we call this the consensus set. Retrain M with {(xj , ỹj )}j∈Scons ,
035            i.e., retrain M with the predicted labels of only the consensus set.
036
      Intuitively, retraining with predicted hard labels can be beneficial when the underlying classes are “well-separated”.
037
      In such a case, the model can potentially correctly predict the labels of many samples in the training set far away
038
      from the decision boundary which were originally incorrectly labeled and presented to it. As a result, the model’s
039   accuracy (w.r.t. the actual labels) on the training data can be significantly higher than the accuracy of the noisy labels
040   presented to it. Hence, retraining with predicted labels can potentially improve the model’s performance. This intuition
041   is illustrated in Figure 1 where we consider a separable binary classification problem with noisy labels. The exact
042   details are in Appendix A but importantly, Figures 1a and 1b correspond to versions of this problem with “large” and
043   “small” separation, respectively. Please see the figure caption for detailed discussion but in summary, Figure 1 shows us
044   that the success of retraining depends on the degree of separation between the classes.
045   The motivation for consensus-based retraining is that matching the predicted and given labels can potentially yield
046   a smaller but much more accurate subset compared to the entire set; such a filtering effect can further improve the
047   model’s performance. As we show in Section 5 (see Tables 3 and 7), this intuition bears out in practice.
048

051   perspective, we are not aware of any work proving that retraining a model with its predicted hard labels can be beneficial

083   The primary reason for our interest in retraining is that it turned out to be a simple yet effective way to improve training
084   with local1 label differential privacy (DP) whose goal is to safeguard the privacy of labels in a supervised ML problem
085
      by injecting label noise (see Section 3 for a formal definition). Label DP is used in scenarios where only the labels
      are considered sensitive, e.g., advertising, recommendation systems, etc. (Ghazi et al., 2021). Importantly, retraining
086
      can be applied on top of any label DP training algorithm at no extra privacy cost. Our main algorithmic contribution
087
      is empirically demonstrating the efficacy of consensus-based retraining in improving label DP training (Section 5).
088   Three things are worth clarifying here. First, as a meta-idea, retraining is not particularly new; however, its application
089   – especially with consensus-based filtering – as a light-weight way to improve label DP training at no extra privacy
090   cost is new to our knowledge. Second, we are not advocating consensus-based retraining as a SOTA general-purpose
091   algorithm for learning with noisy labels. Third, we do not view full retraining to be an algorithmic contribution; we
092   consider it for theoretical analysis and as a baseline for consensus-based retraining.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
117
118   Self-Training (ST). Retraining is similar in spirit to ST (Scudder, 1965; Yarowsky, 1995; Lee et al., 2013; Sohn et al.,
119
      2020) which is the process of progressively training a model with its own predicted hard labels in the semi-supervised
      setting. Our focus in this work is on the fully supervised setting. This is different from ST (in the semi-supervised
120
      setting) which typically selects samples based on the model’s confidence and hence, we call our algorithmic idea of
121
      interest retraining to distinguish it from ST. In fact, we show that our consensus-based sample selection strategy leads
122   to better performance than confidence-based sample selection in Appendix H. There is a vast body of work on ST and
123   related ideas; see Amini et al. (2022) for a survey. On the theoretical side also, there are several papers showing and
124   quantifying different kinds of benefits of ST and related ideas (Carmon et al., 2019; Raghunathan et al., 2020; Kumar
125   et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2020; Oymak & Gulcu, 2020; Wei et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2022). But none of these works
126   characterize the pros/cons of ST or any related algorithm in the presence of noisy labels. In contrast, we show that
127   retraining can provably improve accuracy in the presence of label noise in Section 4. Empirically, ST-based ideas have
128   been proposed to improve learning with noisy labels (Reed et al., 2014; Tanaka et al., 2018; Han et al., 2019; Nguyen
129   et al., 2019; Li et al., 2020; Goel et al., 2022); but these works do not have rigorous theory. In the context of theory on
130
      label noise and model’s confidence, Zheng et al. (2020) show that if the model’s predicted score for the observed label
      is small, then the observed label is likely not equal to the true label. Note that the results of Zheng et al. (2020) pertain
131
      to the correctness of the observed labels, whereas our results pertain to the correctness of the predicted labels.
132
133   Self-Distillation (SD). Retraining is also similar in principle to SD (Furlanello et al., 2018; Mobahi et al.,
134   2020); the major difference is that soft labels (i.e., predicted raw probabilities) are used in SD, whereas we use hard
135   labels in retraining. Specifically, in SD, a teacher model is first trained with provided hard labels and then its predicted
136   soft labels are used to train a student model with the same architecture as the teacher. SD is usually employed with a
137   temperature parameter (Hinton et al., 2015) to force the teacher and student models to be different; we do not have
138   any such parameter in retraining as it uses hard labels. SD is known to ameliorate learning in the presence of noisy
139   labels (Li et al., 2017) and this has been theoretically analyzed by Dong et al. (2019); Das & Sanghavi (2023). Dong
140   et al. (2019) propose their own SD algorithm that uses dynamically updated soft labels and provide some complicated
141
      conditions of when their algorithm can learn the correct labels in the presence of noisy labels. In contrast, we analyze
      retraining with fixed hard labels. Das & Sanghavi (2023) analyze the standard SD algorithm in the presence of noisy
142
      labels with fixed soft labels but their analysis in the classification setting requires some strong assumptions such as
143
      access to the population, feature maps of all points in the same class having the same inner product, etc. We do not
144   require such strong assumptions in this paper (in fact, we present sample complexity bounds). Moreover, Das &
145   Sanghavi (2023) have extra `2 -regularization in their objective function to force the teacher and student models to be
146   different. We do not apply any extra regularization for retraining.
147
148   Label Differential Privacy (DP). Label DP (described in detail in Section 3) is a relaxation of full-data DP
149   wherein the privacy of only the labels (and not the features) is safeguarded (Chaudhuri & Hsu, 2011; Beimel et al., 2013;
150   Wang & Xu, 2019; Ghazi et al., 2021; Malek Esmaeili et al., 2021; Ghazi et al., 2022; Badanidiyuru et al., 2023). In
151   this work, we are not trying to propose a SOTA label DP algorithm (with an ingenious noise-injection scheme); instead,
152
      we advocate retraining as a simple post-processing step that can be applied on top of any label DP algorithm (regardless
      of the noise-injection scheme) to improve its performance, at no extra privacy cost. Similar to our goal, Tang et al.
153
      (2022) apply techniques from unsupervised and semi-supervised learning to improve label DP training. In particular,
154
      one of their steps involves keeping the given noisy label of a sample only if it matches a pseudo-label generated by
155   unsupervised learning. This is similar in spirit to our consensus-based retraining scheme but a crucial difference is


                                                                   3
156
      that we do not perform any unsupervised learning; we show that matching the given noisy label to the model’s own
157   predicted label is itself pretty effective. Further, unlike our work, Tang et al. (2022) do not have any rigorous theory.

507   6   C ONCLUSION
508
509
      In this work, we provided the first theoretical result showing retraining with hard labels can provably increase model
      accuracy in the presence of label noise. We also showed the efficacy of consensus-based retraining (i.e., retraining on
510
      only those samples for which the predicted label matches the given noisy label) in improving label DP training at no
511
      extra privacy cost. We will conclude by discussing some future directions of work. Our theoretical results in this work
512   focus on the full retraining scheme. Given that consensus-based retraining worked very well empirically, we would
513   like to analyze it theoretically as a future work. Another potential extension of our work is to analyze retraining under
514   non-uniform label noise models. We also hope to test our ideas on larger scale models and datasets in the future.
