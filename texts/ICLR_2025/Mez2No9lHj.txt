009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models (LLMs) have become pervasive due to their knowledge
012            absorption and text-generation capabilities. Concurrently, the copyright issue for
013            pretraining datasets has been a pressing concern, particularly when generation
014            includes specific styles. Previous methods either focus on the defense of identical
015            copyrighted outputs or find interpretability by individual tokens with computational
016            burdens. However, the gap between them exists, where direct assessments of how
017            dataset contributions impact LLM outputs are missing. Once the model providers
018
               ensure copyright protection for data holders, a more mature LLM community
               can be established. To address these limitations, we introduce CopyLens, a new
019
               framework to analyze how copyrighted datasets may influence LLM responses.
020
               Specifically, a two-stage approach is employed: First, based on the uniqueness of
021            pretraining data in the embedding space, token representations are initially fused
022            for potential copyrighted texts, followed by a lightweight LSTM-based network
023            to analyze dataset contributions. With such a prior, a contrastive-learning-based
024            non-copyright OOD detector is designed. Our framework can dynamically face
025            different situations and bridge the gap between current copyright detection methods.
026            Experiments show that CopyLens improves efficiency and accuracy by 15.2% over
027            our proposed baseline, 58.7% over prompt engineering methods, and 0.21 AUC
028            over OOD detection baselines.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Following the release of GPT-3 (Brown et al. (2020)), LLM-driven applications have gained increasing
      attention in both industry and academia. As transformer architectures mature, the demand for high-
034
      quality data has become urgent. However, a major security concern that hinders the establishment of
035
      the LLM community is the challenging task of protecting dataset copyright. Specifically, the current
036   model providers lack the capability to identify which datasets contribute most to a response. This
037   inability makes data holders hesitant to expose their datasets to LLM training since copyrighted
038   datasets are of high quality but no credit is given to themselves. Conversely, if model providers could
039   pinpoint the contributions of datasets to a response, a beneficial transactional framework between the
040   data holder and LLM customer could be established. (Cui & Araujo (2024))
041
      In order to build a complete copyright dataset contribution analysis framework, two stages
042   are needed. First, the framework detects whether LLM outputs involve copyrighted contents,
043   then analyzes the sub-dataset contributions to outputs identified as copyrighted. However, most
044   works either focus only on the first stage with fixed tasks, i.e. only detecting verbatim sentences from
045   books, or only on finding explainability in tokens, which is similar to the second stage but needs huge
046   computation. (Duarte et al. (2024); Shi et al. (2023); Vyas et al. (2023)) Besides detecting identical
047   copyrighted contents, previous studies tackle copyright issues from three aspects: erasing copyrighted
048   data, protecting pretraining data, and assessing data impact. For data erasure, the need for finetuning
049   in the LLM unlearning approach is computationally heavy and unreliable. (Eldan & Russinovich
050
      (2023); Yao et al. (2023); Min et al.; Shi et al. (2023)). In pretraining data protection, watermarking
      for source traceability degrades model performance and loses trace accuracy in generated texts.(Wu
051
      et al. (2023)) Therefore, we mainly focus on assessing data impact.
052
053   Data impact assessment generally includes mathematical methods and prompt engineering ones.
      Despite works to build similarity metrics or gradient-based explanations, most mathematical methods

054
      are preliminary, focusing on conceptual proof with complex mathematical functions. (Scheffler
055   et al. (2022); Grosse et al. (2023); Deng et al. (2024)) Prompt engineering in LLM attribution
056   aims to ask ”what the response is based on”, emphasizing factual grounding in cited documents
057   rather than identifying styles or sub-datasets contributions. This approach differs in goals and may
058   amplify hallucinations. (Zuccon et al. (2023)) In summary, detecting verbatim itself cannot avoid
059   copyright infringement with particular elements, while mathematical methods take up too much
060   computation. The gap between such a fixed task and unreasonable computational cost exists in data
061   impact assessment, blocking the evaluation of which datasets contribute most to LLM outputs.
062   In this work, we focus on identifying the pretraining datasets that contribute most significantly to
063   LLM outputs, and corresponding efficient non-copyright detectors. Current works target copyright by
064   finding statistical differences in loss or final layer logits outputs to distinguish copyright verbatims.
065   Can more information be revealed by looking into LLM’s inherent architecture from the
066   model provider’s perspective? Inspired by this, our framework explores the information within
067   LLM’s hidden states. However, the development of such a framework presents several challenges.
068   Firstly, which inherent information affects effective attribution in this task is unknown. Secondly, the
069
      difference between non-copyright detection and contribution analysis of copyrighted ones makes it
      hard to combine them into a single framework. Thirdly, due to the large size of LLMs, the copyright
070
      analysis framework must be lightweight and efficient.
071
072   To protect dataset copyright in LLMs, we propose CopyLens, a framework that both extracts rep-
073   resentations from LLM dialogues to calculate the contribution of training datasets, and detects
074   non-copyright LLM outputs. We address the unclear problem of copyright analysis by first abstract-
075
      ing it into a sub-dataset source classification problem, then extending to more realistic generation
      scenes to prove effectiveness. Our framework leverages patterns in Multi-Head Attention (MHA)
076
      where LLM outputs are linked to datasets. By exploring temporal dependency in layer-wise MHA,
077
      we build a lightweight LSTM-based framework that efficiently performs dataset-level copyright
078   analysis. Based on such LSTM temporal prior, an efficient built-in non-copyright detector is proposed.
079   We validate our framework on BERT, GPT series, demonstrating comparative performance with
080   limited training samples while applicable to different generation scenes. Our contributions can be
081   summarized as follows:
082
083          • Recognizing the need for dataset-level copyright in the LLM community, we introduce
084            CopyLens, a lightweight, LLM-training free framework that can both identify the contribu-
085            tion among different training datasets to LLM outputs and detect non-copyright ones with
086            limited training samples.
087
088          • We emphasize the necessity of copyrighted data information triggered in LLM architecture
089            by looking into hidden space. Based on three effective information extraction methods, an
090
               LSTM-based copyright analysis framework that aggregates the inherent layer-wise MHA
               relationship in LLMs is proposed. CopyLens shows classification accuracy over 94.9% in
091
               less than 1.5 hours training time.
092
093          • An efficient non-copyright OOD detector is designed based on LSTM prior information
094            with 93.83% accuracy and an AUC score of 0.954, which surpasses previous OOD detection
095            methods. Considering several generative types that involve copyright issues, we design
096            prompts and employ the generated LLM outputs in different scenes to evaluate our frame-
097            work. For the qualitative study, CopyLens aligns with baselines, showing better detection in
098            three controlled scenes. Quantitatively, CopyLens shows an average MSE loss under 0.05 in
099            the text mixture task.

102   2   BACKGROUND
103
104   Driven by extensive demand for refining texts, LLM-assisted editing offers substantial potential. A
105   critical issue is copyright protection, particularly when models use multiple copyrighted sub-datasets.
106   Specifically, it is important to determine whether the generated LLM outputs is based on such datasets,
107   and if so, which ones matter? We first outline the copyright challenges inadequately addressed before,
      review existing methods, then overview the architecture basic of LLM.

      2.1   C OPYRIGHT TAKEDOWN AND T RAINING DATA A SSESSMENT ON LLM O UTPUTS
109
110   Early LLMs gathered datasets through web crawling, which limits manual review of copyrighted
111   material. Patterns of copyrighted works may be replicated, leading to resembled generation. A brief
112   overview of the most relevant research is provided here, with an extended one in the Appx. A.3.
113
      Copyright Takedown in LLMs Copyright takedown starts by detecting related content. As LLM
114   shows properties different from small models when scaling, such as finetuned loss, output logit
115   distribution, paraphrasing attack, and perplexities, such features help differentiate copyrighted texts.
116   (Li et al. (2024); Shi et al. (2023); Duarte et al. (2024); Li (2023) ) However, simply tracking LLM
117   outputs or prompting cannot deal with complex tasks. Despite recent evaluation, the general copyright
118   analysis framework is not yet defined, with token length limited to 200, and small datasets limited to
119   books and news. Therefore, large improvement space remains. (Wei et al. (2024))
120   Mathematical and Prompt Engineering Training Data Assessment How does the inclusion of
121   copyrighted material as training data in open-source models affect the resulting outputs? Mathematical
122   approaches like K-Near Access-Free (K-NAF) similarity have been developed, but these methods are
123   preliminary and not universally applicable (Vyas et al. (2023); Scheffler et al. (2022); Elkin-Koren
124   et al. (2023)). The mathematical mechanisms of such problems are not clear, making attempts limited
125   even if token and layer-level analysis is studied. (Wang et al. (2023b)) Despite current efforts in
126   making gradient-based influence functions computable, computing gradients for all training data is
127   still an unacceptable cost. (Grosse et al. (2023))
128   Prompt engineering is currently used in LLM attribution, asking ”What is the answer based on”.
129   Direct attribution refers to asking the LLMs to provide attribution while answering. (Sun et al.) Post-
130   retrieval means answering and then asking the LLMs to attribute based on it, while post-generation
131   attribution uses both questions and answers. (Reddy et al. (2023); Gao et al. (2022)) However, such
132   works mainly focus on whether the outputs are facts grounded on pretraining data. Besides, it shows
133   hallucinations and knowledge conflicts in professional fields. (Zuccon et al. (2023); Li et al. (2023))

      3.1.3    T WO -S TAGE S OLUTION F RAMEWORK FOR C OPYRIGHTED DATASET P ROTECTION
234
235   Different from previous methods that only focus on copyright dataset detection (stage 1), we
236   combine two stages by using learned prior from contribution analysis of copyrighted datasets
237   to LLM outputs (stage 2) to help build an efficient detector in stage 1. The reason is that detailed
238   copyright analysis in stage 2 allows us to learn rich semantics, which we leverage as prior knowledge
239   to enhance efficiency and reduce redundancy in stage 1. Therefore, by first training stage 2 and
240   fine-tuning embedding on stage 1, we propose an integrated two-stage solution framework.
241   The prior learning starts in stage 2. To process texts containing copyrighted elements, we first obtain
242   layer-wise MHA outputs, inspired by Fig. 2, which will be discussed in the next section. To reduce
243   computational costs while preserving key information, we propose three extraction strategies. After
244   extracting representations, we apply an LSTM-based classifier with a softmax output layer to evaluate
245   the contributions of potential datasets, as shown in Fig. 3(b). With the learned LSTM prior, in stage
246   1, we introduce a contrastive-learning-based non-copyright detector. This detector fine-tunes both the
247
      global projector and the pre-trained LSTM weights, as illustrated in Fig. 3(a).

      5   C ONCLUSION
531
532
      We highlight the need to identify which copyrighted datasets contribute most to LLM outputs.
533
      Therefore, the framework CopyLens is proposed from a model provider’s perspective, exploring
534   LLM architectures. Our two-stage framework extracts representations from MHA outputs, followed
535   by LSTM-based analysis to identify copyrighted sub-datasets contributions. We further design a
536   lightweight non-copyright detector based on LSTM priors, showing high accuracy and AUC. Validated
537   across various real-world scenarios, including text continuation, style transfer, style elimination,
538   and text mixture, CopyLens efficiently combines two stages for copyrighted data protection, detects
539   dataset contribution with minimal data, and is robust to different generation lengths, outperforming
      prompt-engineering and distance-based baselines.

741   Data Filtering Filtering out copyrighted data directly avoids copyright issues in LLMs. Previous
742   studies show possible ways such as utilizing synthetic or deduplicated data Kandpal et al. (2022); He
743   et al. (2023). Whereas, such approaches can detrimentally affect model performance due to the high
744   quality of copyrighted datasets.
745   LLM unlearning Researches show that LLMs can memorize training datasets, which causes trials in
746   letting LLMs forget training data. (Carlini et al. (2021); Eldan & Russinovich (2023)) However, the
747   unlearning method was proved unreliable using a Min-k probability attack. (Shi et al. (2023))
748
      Watermark of copyrighted data Adding a watermark to the copyrighted victim dataset makes
749
      tracing possible. Watermark based on linguistics features exploits statistical distribution, token
750   entropy, and so on. (Wu et al. (2023); Kirchenbauer et al. (2023); Lee et al. (2023)) Despite being
751   training-free, this method is vulnerable to attacks. (Panaitescu-Liess et al. (2024)) The token sampling
752   method switches the watermark process to the LLM decoding stage, showing superior robustness.
753   (Kuditipudi et al. (2023)) Similar approaches include adding a watermark during logit generation.
754   (Liu et al. (2023)) End-to-end learning-based watermark methods are also proposed, which can
755   also attribute data sources. However, the requirement of pre-training LLMs poses a significant
      computational burden. (Zhang et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2023a))

880   Tab. 2 shows that in vanilla classification scenes, interval-based sampling outperforms pivotal-token-

882   theory and take k = 3 in the smallest data scale as an example.
883   Why interval-based sampling method is effective? We formularize the overall information extraction
884   and classification process based on mutual information (MI). MI quantifies the statistical association
885   between variables, exploring both linear and non-linear dependencies, and can be used to identify
886   relationships in potential causality. A larger MI shows a closer relationship between the two variables.
887
      The process of the proposed framework can be summarized into two parts: first extracting token rep-

      resentations from MHA outputs, then feeding into the LSTM-based contribution analysis framework.
