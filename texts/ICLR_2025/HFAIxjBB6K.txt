009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Softmax is the most commonly used probabilistic activation function in classifi-
012            cation tasks, partly due to its tendency to over-penalize non-target classes with
013            high prediction scores. However, this property becomes detrimental in autore-
014            gressive generation tasks, where multiple valid predictions may exist. Unlike con-
015            ventional classification task, which seeks a single correct answer, autoregressive
016
               models are expected to assign high probabilities to various plausible outputs to
               ensure diversity in generation. However, during training, gradient bias caused
017
               by Softmax over-penalizes non-target predictions with high probabilities, limit-
018
               ing output diversity and hindering optimization convergence. To alleviate this,
019            we propose Gradient Suppressed Softmax (GS-Softmax), which reduces the gra-
020            dient contributions of high-probability non-target classes. Through experiments,
021            we demonstrate that GS-Softmax improves both the diversity of generated content
022            and optimization convergence. Code and pre-trained models will be made public.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Autoregressive models, leveraging the “predicting next token in the sequence” training stratege
      and transformer structure (Vaswani, 2017), have achieved remarkable success in language mod-
028
      eling (Radford, 2018; Brown, 2020; Touvron et al., 2023). Recently, building upon VQVAE (Van
029
      Den Oord et al., 2017), which represents images as sequences of discrete tokens, the “next token
030   prediction” paradigm has also shown its effectiveness in image generation (Tian et al., 2024; Sun
031   et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024), surpassing diffusion-based methods (Song & Ermon, 2019; Ho et al.,
032   2020; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Lu et al., 2022; Rombach et al., 2022) on several benchmarks.
033
      The next token prediction task in autoregressive generative models is a specialized form of classi-
034
      fication, where the model learns to predict the probability distribution of the next token based on
035
      the preceding tokens in a sequence. Unlike conventional classification tasks that seek a single cor-
036   rect answer, these models should assign high probabilities to multiple plausible outputs to ensure
037   both reasonable and diverse generations. For instance, given the sequence “His name is,” the model
038   should favor various possible names rather than one.
039
      However, autoregressive generative models are typically trained in a self-supervised manner (Rad-
040
      ford, 2018; Brown, 2020; Touvron et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2024; Tian et al., 2024), where the model
041
      predicts a single target token during training and is optimized to maximize the probability of this la-
042   bel. This suppresses other valid predictions, leading to overconfidence in one outcome. We find that
043   this issue is further exacerbated by the use of Softmax, which amplifies the focus on the predicted
044   target while diminishing diversity in the output.
045
      Probabilistic activation functions (PAF) are essential in machine learning for converting model out-
046
      puts (i.e., logits) into probability distributions. Among them, Softmax (Bridle, 1989) has emerged
047
      as the dominant choice, particularly in classification tasks, owing to its propensity to excessively pe-
048   nalize high probabilities for non-target classes. This makes Softmax highly effective for single-label
049   classification problems. However, in autoregressive generation tasks, where multiple valid options
050   often exist, Softmax can become problematic. In these cases, with Softmax, the optimization will
051   focus more on suppressing potentially valid but non-target predictions, as they naturally carry high
052   probabilities (Holtzman et al., 2019), rather than maintaining a balanced distribution across all plau-
053   sible options. This misalignment not only limits the diversity of the model’s generated content but
      also hinders its ability to properly learn the underlying data distribution.

      To address the misalignment, we need to solve the issue where Softmax overly penalizes high-
078
      probability non-target predictions. The Softmax operation can generally be broken down into two
079
      steps: (1) applying a positive mapping function (PMF) to transform model outputs (i.e., logits) into
080   positive values, and (2) normalizing these values so that their sum equals one. By default, Softmax
081   uses Fs (x) = ex as its PMF, whose derivative increases with the input. This results in larger
082   gradients flow through logits with higher values, leading to the over-penalization of potential valid
083   predictions. To address this gradient bias, we propose to replace Fs with a function whose derivative
084   does not continuously grow as the input increases. Based on this criteria, we design two positive
085   mapping functions F1 and F2 (see illustration in Figure 1).
086   Through experiments, we demonstrate that replacing the PMF in Softmax with F1 or F2 leads to
087   performance improvements, validating our analysis and findings. We refer to this variant as Gradient
088   Suppressed Softmax (GS-Softmax). In a series of comprehensive experiments, we further show that
089   GS-Softmaxnot not only enhances the diversity of generated content but also improves optimization
090   convergence compared to traditional Softmax.

      3     M ETHOD
128
      Most autoregressive image generation models (Yu et al., 2021; Esser et al., 2021; Lee et al., 2022;
129
      Sun et al., 2024; Tian et al., 2024) adopt Softmax as the probabilistic activation function. In this
130
      section, we first demonstrate that using Softmax inherently conflicts with the training objective of

132   activation function: Gradient Suppressed (GS) Softmax.
133
134   3.1     S OFTMAX IS NOT OPTIMAL FOR AUTOREGRESSIVE GENERATIVE MODEL
135
136   In autoregressive tasks such as text or image generation, a single input can have multiple plausible
137   continuations. For instance, the prompt “My name is” could be followed by various valid comple-
138   tions. Thus, the autoregressive generative model should be capable of assigning high probabilities to
139   various reasonable answers, as this is essential for implementing sampling strategies such as top-k
140   (Fan et al., 2018) and top-p sampling (Holtzman et al., 2019) during inference. These sampling
141
      strategies, in turn, are key to ensuring the model’s generations are diverse and valid.
142   In this section, we first reveal that the training objective of autoregressive generative models con-
143   tradicts the expectation that they should assign high probabilities to all potential correct answers.
144   Then, we demonstrate that this issue is further exacerbated when Softmax is used.
145

      4.1   S ETTINGS
242
      To validate the effectiveness of GS-Softmax, we conduct experiments following the setup of LLa-
243   maGen (Sun et al., 2024), which achieves state-of-the-art performance with a straightforward algo-
244   rithmic design. We outline the training setup below and refer the reviewer to (Sun et al., 2024) for
245   further details.
246
      Training. We train models using Softmax and GS-Softmax on the widely used 256×256 ImageNet
247
      benchmark using consistent settings. Specifically, for all experiments, the learning rate is set as 10−4
248
      with a batch size of 256, and we use the AdamW optimizer with β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.95, and a weight
249   decay of 0.05. All models are trained for 300 epochs. In addition, gradient clipping with a maximum
250   norm of 1.0 is utilized. The dropout rate is 0.1 for input token embeddings, attention layers, and
251   feedforward networks (FFN). We also apply a 0.1 dropout to the class condition embedding for
252   classifier-free guidance.
253
      Evaluation. We evaluate the effectiveness of our method using Fréchet Inception Distance (FID)
254
      (Heusel et al., 2017) and sFID (Nash et al., 2021) as the primary metrics, as they consider both
255   the diversity and quality of the generated images. Additionally, we also report Inception Score (IS)
256   (Salimans et al., 2016) and Recall (Kynkäänniemi et al., 2019) for a comprehensive comparison.
257   For fairness, we fix the random seed as 0 and use the evaluation scripts provided by Sun et al.
258   (2024). Without special specification, all experiments in Section 4 are performed on ImageNet with
259   256 × 256 resolution using GPT-B. By default, as suggested by Sun et al. (2024), we set CFG=2,
260   temperature=1, and use top-k sampling to perform the evaluation.

      5.1   S ELECTION OF POSITIVE MAPPING FUNCTION
365
      As discussed in Section 3.2, the derivative of the positive mapping function F (x) should not con-
366   tinuously increase as F (x) increases. Based on this, we introduce two alternative positive mapping
367   functions: F1 (x) (equation 7) and F2 (x) (equation 8). The key difference between them is that
368   F1′ (x) decreases as x increases for x ≥ 0, while F2′ (x) = 1 for x ≥ 0. Both of them have lower
369   derivatives when x is large compared to the mapping function Fs used in Softmax (equation 1),
370   whose derivative keeps growing as x increases.
371
      To validate the effectiveness of the criteria we suggested in Section 3.2, we trained models using
372   either Fs , F1 (x) (equation 7) or F2 (x) (equation 8) as the positive mapping functions and compared
373   their performance. As shown in Table 6, both F1 (x) and F2 (x) outperform the original Softmax
374   function. This confirms the effectiveness of our approach, demonstrating that over-penalizing po-
375   tentially correct candidates harms performance. Additionally, F1 and F2 did not demonstrate a clear
376   superiority over each other in the image evaluation metrics. However, F1 continuously achieved bet-
377   ter perplexity scores on both the training set and validation set, indicating it helps the optimization
      converge better. Therefore, we default to using F1 (x) as the positive mapping function.

401   5.2    OVER PENALIZATION AND OVER CONFIDENCE
402
403
      In standard classification tasks, several methods have been proposed to prevent the model from
      becoming overly confident in its predictions. These approaches also help to reduce the penalty
404
      on non-target high-probability categories during optimization. In this section, we explore whether
405
      methods aimed at reducing model confidence, such as label smoothing (Szegedy et al., 2016) and
406   temperature scaling (Hinton, 2015), can benefit autoregressive image generation models.
407
408   Temperature scaling does not benefit in the training stage. As shown in Table 7, incorporating
409
      temperature scaling during training did not lead to performance improvements. We attribute this
      to the fact that although temperature scaling can make the predicted probability distribution either
410
      flatten or sharper, it does not alter the relative magnitude of the gradients—i.e., a larger probability
411
      still results in a larger gradient. As a result, as long as softmax is used, optimization will continue to
412   focus more on reducing the probability of high-confidence, non-target classes, which is misaligned
413   with the objectives of autoregressive generative models, as discussed in Section 3.1.
414
415
      Label smoothing. Label smoothing (Szegedy et al., 2016) addresses model overconfidence by
      modifying the labels of non-target classes from 0 to a small constant. However, as shown in Table 8,
416
      applying label smoothing leads to a noticeable increase in both training loss and a decline in image
417
      evaluation metrics. We hypothesize that this degradation arises from the increased optimization
418   challenge introduced by label smoothing. Moreover, it can also be attributed to the fact that label
419   smoothing does not fundamentally solve the problem. When label smoothing is applied, all non-
420   target labels remain the same. In this case, when Softmax is utilized, the optimization still focuses
421   more on minimizing the probabilities of potential valid but non-target options. Considering this, it
422   may be more effective to adjust the distribution of probabilities for non-target classes based on their
423   predicted probabilities.

470   6     R ELATED W ORKS
471
472   Autoregressive Image Generation. Autoregressive models for image generation convert 2D images
473   into 1D sequences of pixels or tokens, generating each RGB pixel or token in a predefined order.
474   Early works (Van Den Oord et al., 2016; Van den Oord et al., 2016; Parmar et al., 2018; Chen
475   et al., 2020) have demonstrated the effectiveness of autoregressive models on generating RGB pixels,
476   achieving comparable performance compared to generative adversarial network (Goodfellow et al.,
477
      2014; Brock, 2018; Karras et al., 2019; Kang et al., 2023). Following works such as VQ-GAN (Esser
      et al., 2021; Lee et al., 2022) significantly improved performance by conducting autoregressive
478
      learning in the latent space of VQVAE (Van Den Oord et al., 2017; Razavi et al., 2019a), where
479
      images are represented as token sequences. VQVAE-2 (Razavi et al., 2019b) and RQ-Transformer
480   (Lee et al., 2022) employ a similar approach but use a stacked VQVAE, which represents images
481   through hierarchical codes in the latent space. Moreover, inspired by BERT (Devlin, 2018), masked
482   prediction models (Chang et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2023a;a;b) enable predicting multiple tokens at
483   each step, significantly reducing the deploying cost. More recently, autoregressive image generation
484   methods (Sun et al., 2024; Tian et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024) have shown promising performance,
485   excels methods based on diffusion (Song & Ermon, 2019; Ho et al., 2020; Dhariwal & Nichol,
      2021; Lu et al., 2022; Rombach et al., 2022) on several benchmarks.

      Over-confidence issue in classification tasks. In classification tasks, deep neural networks of-
504   ten exhibit an over-confidence problem, where the model assigns excessively high confidence to
505   a single prediction while all others are greatly suppressed, which limits the generalizability of the
506   trained model. To address this issue, label smoothing (Szegedy et al., 2016) smooths the hard la-
507   bels of training samples to prevent the model from becoming overly confident in the target class.
508   Temperature scaling (Guo et al., 2017) adjusts the temperature parameter of the softmax function
509   to better calibrate the model’s predicted probabilities, making them more reflective of actual confi-
510   dence. Moreover, Pereyra et al. (2017) proposed to use both confidence penalty and label smoothing.
511   In general, methods focusing on alleviating over-confidence problems often do not consider how to
512
      adjust confidence between non-target classes. Consequently, they are not effective in mitigating the
      over-penalization issue caused by Softmax in autoregressive generative model scenarios, as we have
513
      discussed and verified in Section 5.2.
514
515   Variants of Softmax. Various softmax variants have been proposed to enhance the performance.
516   For example, Hierarchical Softmax (Morin & Bengio, 2005) and Taylor Softmax (Banerjee et al.,
517   2020) are proposed to improve computing efficiency. Martins & Astudillo (2016) proposed Sparse-
518
      max, which produces sparse probability distributions, enabling models to focus on a subset of classes
      and improving interpretability. Moreover, Gumbel-Softmax (Jang et al., 2016) is proposed to facil-
519
      itate differentiable sampling from categorical distributions, making it suitable for applications in
520
      variational inference. In this work, to alleviate the over-penalty problem caused by Softmax, we
521   introduced a new variant: Gradient Suppressed (GS) Softmax.
522
523
524   7     C ONCLUSION
525
526   In this work, we identify a key issue in autoregressive generative models: during training, the gradi-
527
      ent bias introduced by Softmax will overly penalize potentially valid predictions, limiting the diver-
      sity of the generation and hindering the model’s ability to learn the true data distribution. To address
528
      this, we propose to mitigate the gradient bias by replacing the exponential function in Softmax with
529
      a gradient-suppressed alternative. Through comprehensive experiments, we verify the effectiveness
530   of this strategy and introduce Gradient Suppressed Softmax, which improves both generation di-
531   versity and training efficiency. We hope our work helps the following researches to design a more
532   suitable probability activation function for autoregressive generative models.
533
      Limitation. Intuitively, GS-Softmax should also be effective in autoregressive text generation tasks.
534
      However, we didn’t verify this through experiments due to the limitation of the computing resource,
535
      considering it requires training multiple large language models from scratch.
