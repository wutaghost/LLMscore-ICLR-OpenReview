009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            With expansive state-action spaces, efficient multi-agent exploration remains a
012            longstanding challenge in reinforcement learning. Although pursuing novelty,
013            diversity, or uncertainty attracts increasing attention, redundant efforts brought
014            by exploration without proper guidance choices poses a practical issue for the
015            community. This paper introduces a systematic approach, termed LEMAE, choos-
016            ing to channel informative task-relevant guidance from a knowledgeable Large
017            Language Model (LLM) for Efficient Multi-Agent Exploration. Specifically,
018
               we ground linguistic knowledge from LLM into symbolic key states, that are
               critical for task fulfillment, in a discriminative manner at low LLM inference
019
               costs. To unleash the power of key states, we design Subspace-based Hindsight
020
               Intrinsic Reward (SHIR) to guide agents toward key states by increasing reward
021            density. Additionally, we build the Key State Memory Tree (KSMT) to track tran-
022            sitions between key states in a specific task for organized exploration. Benefiting
023            from diminishing redundant explorations, LEMAE outperforms existing SOTA
024            approaches on the challenging benchmarks (e.g., SMAC and MPE) by a large
025            margin, achieving a 10x acceleration in certain scenarios. Our code is available at
026            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/LEMAE.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Exploration stands as a fundamental issue in reinforcement learning (RL) (Du et al., 2023; Liu et al.,
032   2023). Researchers have developed several exploration strategies directed by novelty, diversity, or
033
      uncertainty (Linke et al., 2020; Burda et al., 2018b; Pathak et al., 2017), mainly in single-agent
      reinforcement learning. However, these methods may induce task-irrelevant redundant exploration,
034
      especially in complex environments (Du et al., 2023). In the realm of Multi-Agent Reinforcement
035
      Learning (MARL), the need to mitigate exploration redundancy becomes even more urgent due to the
036   challenges like exponential expansion of the state-action spaces. Widespread real-world applications,
037   including MOBA games (Qu et al., 2023), social science (Jaques et al., 2019), and multi-vehicle
038   control (Xu et al., 2018), further underscore the growing need for efficient multi-agent exploration.
039
      This work identifies task-relevant guidance as an important consideration in enhancing exploration
040
      efficiency. Incorporating priors in exploration mechanism design, such as complex reward structures,
041
      typically requires expert knowledge and substantial human efforts (Liu et al., 2023; Abbeel & Ng,
042   2004). Hopefully, recent advances have witnessed the remarkable reasoning and planning capabilities
043   of Large Language Models (Touvron et al., 2023; Achiam et al., 2023), providing a plausible choice
044   to facilitate efficient exploration through LLM’s effortless prior provision. However, it is non-trivial
045   to effectively comprise linguistic LLM priors into symbolically represented RL tasks (Peng et al.,
046   2023; Carta et al., 2023), and the investigation of practical ways to avoid nuisances caused by such
047   an expression discrepancy is of critical importance.
048   In response to the above issue, we propose LEMAE, a novel framework to enable efficient multi-
049   agent exploration with LLM. The framework primarily consists of two components: (i) key states
050   localization with LLM and (ii) key state-guided exploration. The first component automatically
051   localizes key states essential for task completion, through LLM discrimination, thereby injecting
052   LLM priors into the RL process. Specifically, the discriminator function induced by LLM works to
053   discriminate key states from rollout trajectories, avoiding the overburden of LLM inference costs.
      The second component harnesses the localized key states as meaningful guidance to achieve efficient

      3    R ELATED W ORKS
109
110   LLM in Decision Making. Large Language Models have shown impressive capabilities across
111   downstream tasks (Touvron et al., 2023; Radford et al., 2019; Brown et al., 2020). Recent advances
112   indicate a growing trend of using LLM in decision-making problems (Wang et al., 2023b). A primary
113   challenge is grounding LLM’s linguistic knowledge into specific low-level control tasks typically
114
      represented in symbolic form (Peng et al., 2023; Carta et al., 2023), especially in RL. Creating
      linguistic twin tasks (Carta et al., 2023) is intuitive but require substantial manual workloads. Some
115
      works employ LLMs as high-level planners, e.g., coding with APIs (Liang et al., 2023), using human-
116
      annotated or LLM-summarized actions (Yao et al., 2022; Shinn et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023; Zhu et al.,
117   2023; Wang et al., 2023a). Despite significant progress, they rely on difficult-to-obtain low-level
118   policies or APIs, limiting real-world applicability. Recently, LLMs have been integrated with RL
119   to enhance low-level decision making (Cao et al., 2024). LLMs act as environmental information
120   processors, reducing learning complexity (Paischer et al., 2022; 2024; Kim et al., 2024; Wang et al.,
121   2024), but cannot directly facilitate efficient exploration. Some works utilize LLMs as goal selectors
122   in goal-conditioned RL (Su & Zhang, 2023; Shukla et al., 2023), teacher policy (Zhou et al., 2023), or
123   task sampler (Zhang et al., 2023b) but require predefined task pools, skills or subgoals. Other methods
124   like LLM-based reward or policy design (Klissarov et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2023; Kwon et al., 2023;
125
      Song et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024) rely on large annotated datasets, frequent LLM
      inferences, or are restricted to simple tasks with ample information. Fine-tuning (Carta et al., 2023;
126
      Shi et al., 2023) demands enormous data and resources. LLaMAC (Zhang et al., 2023a) employs
127
      multiple LLMs to balance exploration and exploitation but emphasizes step-wise decision-making
128   via frequent LLM calls. ELLM (Du et al., 2023) enhances exploration using LLM but depends on
129   predefined symbolic observation captioner and frequent LLM inferences. Its semantic similarity-
130   based rewards may also struggle to generalize across scenarios. In contrast, LEMAE integrates
131   linguistic LLM priors into symbolic states with minimal task information and LLM inference costs,
132   achieved by localizing key states in rollout trajectories using LLM-generated discriminator functions.
133   Efficient Multi-Agent Exploration. Exploration efficiency has long been a focal point in
134   RL (Thrun, 1992; Cai et al., 2020; Seo et al., 2021; Mahajan et al., 2019; Jeon et al., 2022; Ecoffet
135   et al., 2019). Typical exploration methods focus on random exploration (Mnih et al., 2013; Rashid
136   et al., 2018) or heuristic indicators, such as diversity or novelty, to facilitate exhaustive exploration,
137   particularly in single agent exploration (Linke et al., 2020; Burda et al., 2018b; Pathak et al., 2017;
138   Burda et al., 2018a; Bellemare et al., 2016). Despite their success, they may induce notable redundant
139   exploration due to a lack of task-relevant guidance (Du et al., 2023). The exponential expansion of the
140   state-action spaces renders exhaustive exploration impractical in multi-agent settings. Consequently,
141
      efficient multi-agent exploration (MAE) becomes increasingly imperative and necessary (Jeon et al.,
      2022; Liu et al., 2021). MAE is also challenging due to the complex configurations, e.g., the entangled
142
      effect of multi-agent actions and intricate reward design (Liu et al., 2023; Qu et al., 2023; Xu et al.,
143
      2023). Given our emphasis on efficient exploration, we prioritize evaluation in multi-agent settings.
144   Some MAE methods encourage influential behaviors during agent interactions (Liu et al., 2023;
145   Jaques et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2019). Nevertheless, they may lead to unintended coalitions or require
146   additional priors (Liu et al., 2023). Certain studies leverage subgoals to guide exploration (Jeon
147   et al., 2022). However, due to challenges in integrating task-related information into subgoals, they
148   either necessitate human expertise for subgoals design (Tang et al., 2018; Kulkarni et al., 2016) or
149   struggle to identify useful subgoals (Jeon et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2021). A recent influential work,
150   WToE (Dong et al., 2023), focuses on when to explore by identifying discrepancies between the
151   actor policy and an environmental-change-detection policy, which does not employ intrinsic rewards
152
      as guidance. Distinguished from the above, this work underscores the significance of task-relevant
      guidance in exploration and utilizes the key state priors extracted from LLM to enable efficient
153
      multi-agent exploration.

183   et al., 2023), potentially reducing training efficiency. This motivates us to integrate task-relevant

185   are limited in the field. This work identifies the Key States as the novel task-relevant prior, which
186   corresponds to intermediate states with explicit semantics and expressions pertaining to the task.
187   Meanwhile, Proposition 4.1 explicitly reflects the efficacy of incorporating them.

200   4.2      K EY S TATES L OCALIZATION WITH LLM
201
      To reduce manual workload, we employ LLM to localize key states. Although generating the
202
      aforementioned symbolic key states can be straightforward, LLM’s weakness in comprehending
203
      symbolic states or environment details necessitates additional information in certain tasks and can
204   lead to errors and hallucinations that are difficult to detect. Here, we stress the importance of LLM’s
205   discriminative ability to localize key states in rollout trajectories to better leverage LLM’s general
206   knowledge. The rationale is that discrimination demands only a high-level task understanding and is
207   more reliable and universal than naive generation, as discussed in detail in Appendix B.1.
208
      To discriminate key states, we prompt LLM to generate m (determined by LLM) discriminator
209
      functions {Fi }mi=1 , as depicted in Fig. 2. Each discriminator function Fi (the ‘IsKeystatei(s)’ block
210   in Fig. 2) takes in the state st at timestep t and outputs a boolean value to tell whether st is the
211   corresponding key state κi . Such an approach systematically annotates each state in trajectories as a
212   key state instance (st → sκt 2 in Fig. 2) or not. Notably, LEMAE injects task-relevant information into
213   the symbolic states without predefined components such as observation captioners (Du et al., 2023) or
214   environment codes (Xie et al., 2023), which require manual fine-tuning, may be unavailable in many
215   scenarios, or could introduce extra information. In addition, the discriminator functions’ reusability
      avoids frequent calls, and our method empirically requires fewer than three LLM inferences for a


                                                                                  4
216
      specific task. These advantages highlight the potential of LEMAE to expand the scope of application
217   scenarios with fewer constraints and reduced costs.
218
219   We design prompts to alleviate the burden of labor-intensive prompt engineering across tasks. As
220
      illustrated in Fig. 2, each task’s prompt is structured by a standardized prompt template and task
      information. The prompt template, consistent across tasks, primarily contains several role instructions
221
      to guide LLM in role understandings (including promoting labor division among agents in MARL)
222
      and output constraints, e.g., in JSON format. For a new task with symbolic state space, the prompt
223   template requires only essential details, i.e., the task description and the state form, which can be
224   easily extracted from the task document without additional processing, making it less demanding
225   than previous methods (Ma et al., 2023; Du et al., 2023). LLM then generates key states definitions
226   and discriminator functions, which can be easily extracted from the JSON response via key-value
227   matching. An extension to vision-based tasks is described in Appendix F.2.
228
      Considering that LLM sometimes generates inaccurate responses and non-executable codes, we
229   develop a Self-Check mechanism to enable LLM’s autonomous evaluation and response improvement,
230   which is inspired by recent approaches (Shinn et al., 2023; Dhuliawala et al., 2023). The mechanism
231   comprises two checking operations: LLM rethinking and code verification. The former prompts
232   LLM with a set of queries for self-assessment, ensuring compliance with specified criteria. The latter
233   verifies the executability of discriminator functions with actual state inputs, providing feedback until
234   all functions are executable. Table 1 further examines its effectiveness.
235   We use GPT-4-turbo from OpenAI API and prompt and response details are attached in Appendix D.

239   4.3.1    S UBSPACE - BASED H INDSIGHT I NTRINSIC R EWARD
240
241   With the annotated key states, trajectories can naturally be segmented into sub-trajectories. Drawing
242
      inspiration from Andrychowicz et al. (2017), we integrate hindsight intrinsic rewards by conceptualiz-
      ing the annotated key states as sub-trajectories’ subgoals, which is further discussed in Appendix E.3.
243
      Such integration guides the policy toward achieving these key states by increasing reward density,
244
      thus reducing manual reward design burdens. Moreover, the state vector index from the discriminator
245   function constitutes the reward-related subspace of the state (Liu et al., 2021). Here, we write the
246   Subspace-based Hindsight Intrinsic Reward (SHIR) function as:
247
248                       rIκm (t) = ∥Φm (st ) − Φm (κm )∥ − ∥Φm (st+1 ) − Φm (κm )∥,                     (1)
249   where ∥·∥ denotes a distance metric, e.g., Manhattan distance or cosine similarity; Φm (s) = (se )e∈υm
250   restricts the state space to elements e ∈ υm , se is the e-th element of the full-state s, and υm ⊂ N+
251   refers to the subset of entire state space from the discriminator function Fm .
252
      Given that rewards generally rely on a limited subset of the entire state space (Liu et al., 2021;
253
      Todorov et al., 2012), adopting subspace-based rewards helps avoid the potential redundancy and bias
254
      associated with the design of intrinsic rewards in the entire state space. LEMAE is also applicable to
255   scenarios where rewards depend on the global state space, as it imposes no strict constraints. Hence,
256   the final reward function is further derived as:
257
258                                      r(t) = α · rE (t) + β · rIκm (t),                                (2)
259   where rE denotes the extrinsic reward with α, β ∈ R+ non-negative scaling factors.

261   4.3.2    K EY S TATES M EMORY T REE
262
263   To organize exploration with memory, we introduce the concept of Key States Memory Tree (KSMT).
264   It tracks transitions between key states and further serves exploration and planning. Compared
265   with the naive ϵ-greedy method, gradually revealing the KSMT helps avoid redundant exploration
266
      throughout the state space, revealing its potential for real-world applications. Notably, LEMAE is
      compatible with other memory structures, such as Directed Acyclic Graphs.
267
268   Construct KSMT: Initialized at the root node, KSMT dynamically expands by iteratively incorporat-
269   ing key state chains obtained from annotated trajectories, as outlined in Algorithm 2. These steps
      repeat until either reaching the success state or fully depicting the transitions between key states.

      Explore with KSMT: To discover new KSMT branches, we adopt an exploration strategy that
283   balances high-randomness policy πθϵh for exploring under-explored nodes with low-randomness
284   policy πθϵl to minimize interference with policy learning, as shown in Algorithm 2. Upon reaching a
285   leaf node, agents execute πθϵh to deepen KSMT. While reaching a non-leaf node ξi , the agents take
286   πθϵh with probability pi to expand the breadth or πθϵl with probability 1 − pi for progression towards
287   the next key state. The probability pi is calculated as pi = di1+1 , with di the degree of the node ξi as
288   an indicator of the degree of under-exploration. The exploration phase completes upon the discovery
289   of the success state. We also prune branches that do not lead to success to circumvent task-irrelevant
290   key states. In this way, KSMT enables exploration in a more meaningful state subspace.
291
      Plan with KSMT: Since KSMT acts as a dynamic model within the key state space, we plan the
292   subgoal for the final sub-trajectory based on it. As shown in Fig. 2b, given the achieved key states
293   chain, we identify the corresponding branch (κ2 → κ1 → children) in KSMT through a lookup
294   operation. Since they have been validated by memory, the children represent the most likely next key
295   states, from which we randomly sample the final subgoal. This process mainly handles cases where
296   trajectories fail to reach a key state as the final subgoal. It enhances SHIR and improves the efficacy
297   of exploring KSMT by encouraging agents to access existing key states.

527   6     C ONCLUSION
528
529   Summary of This Work: We present LEMAE, a novel framework that benefits multi-agent explo-
530   ration with task-specific guidance from LLM. LEMAE executes the key states localization with LLM
531   and enables the key state-guided exploration to improve sample efficiency. In this way, we can (i)
532   build up connections between LLM and RL to ground linguistic knowledge into decision-making, (ii)
533   reduce the manual workload in accessing knowledge and intensive inference calls from LLM, and
534   (iii) significantly boost exploration efficiency through guided and organized exploration. Extensive
535   experiments further examine the effectiveness of LEMAE in typical benchmarks.
536   Limitations & Future Investigations: In developing LEMAE, we made efforts to compensate for
537   the pitfalls of concurrent LLMs, e.g., careful preparation for prompt engineering and task-related
538   prior provision to avoid the nuisances in LLM usages. All of these can be circumvented with the
539   progress of LLM’s capability enhancement. This work paves the way for LLM-empowered RL to
      achieve the potential in complicated decision-making scenarios.

      B     F URTHER D ISCUSSIONS
896
897   B.1   T HE I NSIGHTS B EHIND K EY S TATES D ISCRIMINATION
898
899   In our considered scenarios, we claim that discrimination is generally easier and more universal
900   than key state generation by LLM, particularly in the context of high-dimensional states and partial
901   observability. The reasons are as follows:
902
            1. Discrimination focuses on high-level task understanding and identifying key state charac-
903
               teristics, while generation requires detailed, low-level comprehension, assigning values to
904            each element. This makes generation more challenging and error-prone, particularly in
905            high-dimensional settings. Discrimination equivalently simplifies the output space to key
906            state labels, thus alleviating issues like hallucinations.
907         2. In implementations, errors in discriminator functions are easier to examine and correct
908            through testing with real states. In contrast, errors in generated key states are harder to detect
909            and are typically inferred from training performance.
910
            3. In cases of partial observability, generating key states directly is unreliable. For example, in
911            the Pass task, the positions of hidden switches are unknown and must be inferred from the
912            door’s status. LLM cannot generate key states accurately without knowledge of the specific
913            agents’ positions required to activate a switch.
914
915   B.2   L IMITATIONS
916
917   We build a bridge between LLM and RL to facilitate efficient exploration by leveraging task-related
      guidance provided by LLM. However, persistent constraints inherent to LLMs, such as their limited


                                                         17
918
      capacity to comprehend task-specific information and the inevitable hallucination, become bottlenecks
919   in our approach, which induces the following limitations:
920
921         1. We mitigate heavy prompt engineering through the use of a standardized prompt template but
922            the necessity persists for manually providing task information for LLM. Thus, we assume the
923            availability of semantic meanings for symbolic states. This assumption is feasible, as these
924            manually designed states have inherent meanings documented in task specifications (Liu
925            et al., 2021; Samvelyan et al., 2019b), and is no stronger than prior works requiring a state
926            captioner (Du et al., 2023) or environment code (Ma et al., 2023), which also involve manual
927
               fine-tuning or access to additional state information.
928         2. Constrained by the limitations of LLM’s capabilities and the inherent issue of hallucination,
929            it may face challenges in directly providing effective key state priors for more complex tasks.
930            Besides, due to our use of LLMs, this work primarily focuses on tasks with symbolic states.
931            Future research could extend its application to more complicated tasks, e.g., image-based
932            tasks, by employing advanced multi-modal LLMs. We provide an initial attempt to extend
933
               LEMAE beyond symbolic tasks in Appendix F.2.
934
      Since the efficiency of the proposed LEMAE is essentially derived from versatile LLMs, we believe
935   that the surge of foundation model exploration will flourish LLM-empowered RL.
936
937
      B.3   F UTURE W ORKS
938
939
      The success of the proposed LEMAE highlights the necessity and efficacy of empowering RL with
940   LLM. To enhance performance and extend applicability, we will explore two avenues for future
941   research aimed at addressing the identified limitations. These avenues are outlined as follows:
942
943         1. Streamlining the task information provision through multi-modal self-collection: Multi-
944            modal LLMs are garnering increasing attention for their ability to comprehend situations
945            through various modalities. Incorporating them with self-exploration and memory mecha-
946            nisms shows promise in automating the collection and understanding of task information,
947
               thereby streamlining the implementation and enhancing the adaptability of LEMAE. We
               provide an initial attempt to extend LEMAE beyond symbolic tasks in Appendix F.2.
948
949         2. Unleashing the power of better LLM with an iterative feedback mechanism: Undoubtedly,
950            given the rapid pace of LLM development, the emergence of more powerful LLMs is
951            imminent. On one hand, we intend to harness the capabilities of these advanced LLMs.
952            On the other hand, to fully unleash the potential of LLMs, we plan to devise an iterative
953
               feedback mechanism to feedback LLM in LEMAE during RL training to mitigate issues
               like hallucinations and errors in task understanding.
954
955
956   B.4   B ROADER I MPACTS
957
      Large Language Models have demonstrated considerable potential in showcasing impressive ca-
958
      pabilities across various downstream tasks. However, research on empowering RL with LLMs is
959
      still nascent. As a pioneering endeavor to empower RL with LLM, we propose a general approach
960   facilitating efficient exploration in RL with task-specific guidance from LLM.
961
962         1. For the research community, the publication of this work will inspire further exploration
963            into encouraging the integration of LLMs with RL to address the inherent challenges in RL,
964            such as efficient exploration, limited sample efficiency, and unsatisfactory generalization.
965            Additionally, our design promotes the application of discrimination and coding to ground
966            linguistic knowledge from LLMs into symbolic tasks.
967         2. LEMAE shows promise for real-world deployment in scenarios requiring efficient explo-
968            ration, such as autonomous vehicle control and robot manipulation. Moreover, as LLM is
969            growing by leaps and bounds, it is foreseeable that LEMAE can be applied to more chal-
970            lenging real-world tasks by taking advantage of more powerful LLM. Notably, to mitigate
971            potential risks, it is imperative to conduct LLM generation and RL training under human
               supervision, thereby ensuring undesirable outcomes are averted.

1312   E.2     C OMPARISON WITH LLM R EWARD D ESIGN
1313
1314   We conduct additional experiments comparing LEMAE with a baseline called Eureka-si, which can
1315   be seen as a single-iteration variant of Eureka (Ma et al., 2023), where LLM designs rewards directly.
1316   For fairness, we does not adopt evolutionary optimization in Eureka and use LLM to generate reward
1317   functions with the same role instructions as in Eureka, while maintaining designs like Self-Check as
1318
       in LEMAE. As shown in Fig. 3, Eureka-si is comparable to LEMAE in simple tasks like Push-Box
       but fails in challenging tasks with characteristics like partial observability, such as Pass, where hidden
1319
       switches make it difficult to design effective reward functions. In contrast, LEMAE consistently
1320
       demonstrates impressive performance. Notably, comparing LEMAE with Eureka directly would
1321   be unfair since Eureka’s evolutionary search requires multiple training iterations and candidates,
1322   leading to significantly more sampling and training than LEMAE. Overall, LEMAE’s advantage
1323   over RL algorithms lies in incorporating prior knowledge from the LLM, and its advantage over
1324   other LLM-based methods is due to our designs for better LLM incorporation, such as utilizing
1325   discrimination, SHIR, and KSMT.

1327   E.3     C ONNECTION AND C OMPARISON WITH HER
1328
1329   The proposed Key State-Guided Exploration is similar to Hindsight Experience Replay
1330
       (HER) (Andrychowicz et al., 2017) in form, where key states and subgoals are certain states from
       sampled trajectories. However, unlike HER, which samples goals from memory using random or
1331
       heuristic strategies and often struggles with shaped rewards, our method incorporates LLM priors for
1332
       more targeted goal selection (key states localization). Additionally, the proposed KSMT and SHIR
1333   facilitate organized exploration and enhanced reward guidance.
1334
1335   We conduct additional experiments to further confirm the advantages of our method. We evaluate
1336
       HER with IPPO as the backbone in MPE. We use the future strategy for goal selection, as proposed in
       the HER paper, and employ a reward function based on the Manhattan Distance, which we find to be
1337
       the best match. However, as depicted in Fig. 3, HER does not perform well on both MPE tasks. This
1338
       outcome suggests that the random sampling strategy for goals may not be sufficient, underscoring the
1339   importance of incorporating LLM priors for efficient exploration as we proposed.

1344
1345   The Multiple-Particle Environment serves as a widely-adopted benchmark for multi-agent scenarios.
1346
       In this work, we employ tasks specifically crafted for evaluating multi-agent exploration, proposed

1347
       (2021). In this section, we provide details of the four sparse-reward tasks we adopted.
1348
1349   • Pass: In the Pass task, depicted in Fig. 10a, two agents are positioned in a room of 30 x 30
       grid. The room is divided into two halves by a wall featuring a door. Each half-room contains an


                                                          25
1350
       invisible switch, the details of which are not contained in the state or prompt for LLM. The door
1351   permits passage only when one of the switches is occupied by an agent. Initially situated within the
1352   left half-room, both agents must cooperate to transfer to the right half-room. The external reward
1353   function is denoted as rE = I(two agents are in the right room), where I represents the indicator
1354   function.
1355
       • Secret-Room: Secret-Room is an extension task of Pass. As illustrated in Fig. 10b, the configuration
1356

1357   three doors. Within each room, there is an invisible switch; notably, the switch in the left room
1358   has the capability to control all three doors, whereas each right room’s switch exclusively controls
1359   its respective door. The grid size is 25 x 25. Two agents are initialized within the left room and
1360   are required to collaborate in order to transition to the real target room, which is the right room 2.
1361   The external reward function is denoted as rE = I(two agents are in the right room 2), where I
1362   represents the indicator function.
1363   • Push-Box: As depicted in Fig. 10(c), two agents and a box are initially positioned within a 15
1364   x 15 grid. To successfully move the box, both agents must simultaneously exert force in the same
1365   direction. The task is deemed accomplished when the box is successfully pushed to the wall. The
1366   external reward function is denoted as rE = I(the box is pushed to the wall), where I represents
1367   the indicator function.
1368

1581   F.5     D ISCUSSION ON THE KSMT
1582
1583   Using KSMT could pose a limitation due to potential memory costs in certain scenarios. However,
1584
       this has not been a significant issue in our experiments, as the key states are relatively few, primarily
       focusing on the most critical ones, with a natural sequential relationship typically existing between
1585
       them. Notably, LEMAE is also compatible with other memory structures, such as Directed Acyclic
1586
       Graphs (DAGs), which could be an interesting direction for future exploration.
1587
1588   To demonstrate the effectiveness of LEMAE with other memory structures, in scenarios where task
1589   completion follows a linear pattern (e.g., Init → A → B → Success), we employ a more efficient
1590
       strategy by using a KSMT variant with a single branch representing the sequential order of key states.
       Specifically, we systematically assign a priority value to each key state, continuously updating it
1591
       based on its occurrence order within the sequence of attained key states. The determination of the
1592
       ranking of key states within the one-branch KSMT relies on this established priority.
1593
1594   As illustrated in Fig. 14, an ablation study is conducted to compare the performance between raw
1595   KSMT and the one-branch KSMT variant across six maps in SMAC. The results demonstrate the
1596
       increased necessity of employing the one-branch KSMT variant for tasks involving a larger number of
       agents and greater complexity, such as 5m vs 6m, 3s5z vs 3s6z, and MMM2. Consequently, we have
1597
       adopted the one-branch KSMT approach for these specific SMAC tasks: 5m vs 6m, 3s5z vs 3s6z,
1598
       and MMM2.
1599
1600
       F.5.1    C ONNECTIONS WITH G O -E XPLORE
1601
1602   Go-Explore (Ecoffet et al., 2019) is an influential work tackling exploration in RL. The similarities
1603   between our KSMT and the archive in Go-Explore lie in both methods organizing exploration
1604   through memory, i.e., by selecting possible historical states to explore. The differences and partial
1605   contributions of LEMAE are as follows: (1) Our key states are semantically meaningful and task-
1606
       critical, whereas the archived states in Go-Explore are randomly explored; (2) Our KSMT samples
       key states based on actual key states transitions, enhancing its reliability; (3) We propose Explore
1607
       with KSMT to balance exploration and exploitation, thereby reducing exploration complexity by
1608
       focusing on a more meaningful state subspace.
