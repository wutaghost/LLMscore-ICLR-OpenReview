009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Active learning (AL) optimizes data labeling efficiency by selecting the most
012            informative instances for annotation. A key component in this procedure is an
013            acquisition function that guides the selection process and identifies the suitable
014            instances for labeling from the unlabeled pool. However, these acquisition methods
015            suffer from high computational costs with large unlabeled data pools, posing a
016            roadblock to their applicability on large datasets. To address this challenge and
017            bridge this gap, we introduce a novel plug-and-play unlabeled data pruning strat-
018
               egy, ActivePrune, which leverages language models to prune the unlabeled pool.
               ActivePrune implements a two-stage pruning process: an initial fast evaluation
019
               using perplexity scores from an n-gram language model, followed by a high-quality
020
               selection using metrics for data quality computed through a quantized LLM. Ad-
021            ditionally, to enhance the diversity in the unlabeled pool, we propose a novel
022            perplexity reweighting method that systematically brings forward underrepresented
023            instances for selection in subsequent labeling iterations. Experiments on trans-
024            lation, sentiment analysis, topic classification, and summarization tasks on four
025            diverse datasets and four active learning strategies demonstrate that ActivePrune
026            outperforms existing data pruning methods. Finally, we compare the selection
027            quality ↔ efficiency tradeoff of the data pruning methods and demonstrate that
028            ActivePrune is computationally more efficient than other LLM score-based prun-
029
               ing methods, and provides up to 74% reduction in the end-to-end time required for
               active learning.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Active learning (AL) optimizes data labeling efficiency by selecting the most informative instances of
035
      an unlabeled dataset for annotation, thereby enhancing model performance with minimal labeled data
036   (Tsvigun et al., 2022b). This plays a crucial role in reducing labeling efforts and the associated costs
037   by strategically choosing examples for annotation. For example, active learning can help medical
038   experts efficiently prioritize which clinical texts should be labeled first, saving up to 30% annotation
039   time while training a model for clinical named entity recognition (Wei et al., 2019).
040
      The core challenge in active learning is determining which examples from the pool of unlabeled
041
      data should be labeled. For this purpose, different acquisition functions, including uncertainty and
042   diversity heuristics, are used to guide the selection process (Zhang et al., 2023). However, as the
043   size of the unlabeled pool increases, the computational cost and latency of the acquisition functions
044   escalate significantly (Maekawa et al., 2022; Tsvigun et al., 2022b; 2023). High acquisition latency
045   leads to several issues: it significantly hinders the interactivity of the AL process by increasing the
046   waiting time of experts, leads to higher computational requirements, and thus causes the overall cost
047   of the labeling procedure to increase (Maekawa et al., 2022).
048   In this work, we thus ask the question, “Can we efficiently reduce the size of the unlabeled pool
049   without compromising selection quality in active learning?”. To this end, a few heuristics have been
050   proposed. The simplest and widely used method is random selection (Maekawa et al., 2022), where
051   random instances from the unlabeled pool are selected before applying the acquisition strategy. A
052   better subsampling method, called unlabeled pool subsampling (Tsvigun et al., 2022b), selectively
053   updates uncertainty scores for a subset of instances. In each AL iteration, a portion of the most
      uncertain instances from preceding iterations is selected, along with a limited number of instances

082

083
      increase diversity in the unlabeled pool for subsequent iterations, we propose a novel perplexity
084   reweighting process to systematically prioritize underrepresented instances for selection and then
085   present a theoretical basis for this method. To validate the effectiveness of ActivePrune, we conduct
086   experiments on translation, sentiment analysis, topic classification, and summarization tasks on four
087   diverse datasets and four active learning methods, and demonstrate that our approach outperforms
088   existing unlabeled data selection algorithms across multiple AL strategies. Finally, we compare
089   the selection quality ↔ efficiency tradeoff of the data pruning methods in AL and demonstrate that
090   ActivePrune performs better than other score-based pruning methods (Perplexity, ASK-LLM) while
091   being 97% computationally more efficient compared to such methods. Moroever, ActivePrune
092
      provides up to 74% reduction in the end-to-end AL time, thus enabling efficient active learning.
093   Implications. Our work provides a high-quality AL-strategy agnostic method of reducing the size
094   of large datasets so that they can be used in active learning. This enables more efficient utilization
095   of computational resources and increases the interactivity of the labeling process, thereby saving
096   the time of labeling experts. Finally, it paves the way for broader applicability of active learning
097   techniques to high-volume, domain-specific datasets.

099   2     R ELATED W ORK
100
101   Our work brings together two closely related but distinct (and well-studied) domains of data pruning
102   and active learning. Thus, we provide an overview of the literature on these two areas and then briefly
103   describe the existing approaches of large language models for data quality assessment and pruning.
104
105   2.1     ACTIVE L EARNING
106
107   Active learning (AL) techniques focus on selectively querying unlabeled data that, when labeled,
      are most beneficial for model training (Ren et al., 2021; Deng et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2020). This


                                                             2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      selective data querying aims to reduce annotation costs while improving model performance. Pool-
109   based active learning methods are primarily uncertainty-based (Zhao et al., 2020), diversity-based (Hu
110   & Neubig, 2021; Pendas et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2022), or hybrid (Azeemi et al., 2024; Margatina
111   et al., 2021a; Dor et al., 2020; Korakakis et al., 2024). Despite the improvements in query strategies,
112   the trade-off between high-quality acquisition and computational overhead remains a central issue
113   in AL research. Approaches like Bayesian query strategies offer sophisticated means to quantify
114   uncertainty but at a significant computational and memory cost (Siddhant & Lipton, 2018). This
115   has led to a continued search for methods that balance informative sample selection with practical
116   constraints on the computation and reduce the waiting time between iterations.
117
118   2.2   DATA P RUNING
119
120   Data Pruning focuses on the elimination of redundant or less informative samples from the training
121   dataset to reduce computational costs in training (Kothawade et al., 2021; Killamsetty et al., 2021b;
122   Kothyari et al., 2021; Azeemi et al., 2022; Schreiber et al., 2020; Lee et al., 2021; Sorscher et al.,
123   2022), improve model generalization (Paul et al., 2021; Marion et al., 2023; Azeemi et al., 2023a;
124   Sachdeva et al., 2024), or reduce bias and remove harmful content. Different metrics are used
125
      in data pruning techniques to assess the informativeness or redundancy of data samples, such as
      geometry-based (Agarwal et al., 2020; Sener & Savarese, 2018), uncertainty-based (Coleman et al.,
126
      2019), margin-based (Park et al., 2022), gradient-matching metrics (Mirzasoleiman et al., 2020;
127
      Killamsetty et al., 2021a), forgetting scores (Toneva et al., 2018; Azeemi et al., 2022), and metrics
128   based on training trajectories (Paul et al., 2021). Despite their utility, these methods often face
129   challenges regarding their generalizability across different architectures or datasets and may incur
130   non-negligible overheads (Paul et al., 2021; Bartoldson et al., 2023). This can be mitigated through
131   self-supervised pruning metrics, which do not require initial training runs (Sorscher et al., 2022;
132   Azeemi et al., 2023b), or dynamic data pruning methods, which select samples throughout training
133   based on their relevance (Raju et al., 2021; He et al., 2023; Qin et al., 2023). In contrast to the existing
134   methods, our focus in this work is to develop a novel data pruning mechanism that is specifically
135   tailored for active learning.
136
137   2.3   LLM S FOR DATA F ILTERING
138
139   Recent works have leveraged large language models (LLMs) for data quality assessment, opening up
140   new dimensions for optimizing training datasets. Techniques focusing on quality-score sampling, as
141   discussed by Maini et al. (2024) and Engstrom et al. (2024), employ LLMs to evaluate and select
142   high-quality training samples based on various criteria, including perplexity filtering (Marion et al.,
143
      2023; Muennighoff et al., 2024; Ankner et al., 2024) and similarity to high-quality data sources (Xie
      et al., 2023; Anil et al., 2023; Javaheripi et al., 2023). These methods aim to prioritize samples
144
      that are representative or have desirable characteristics, thereby enhancing the overall quality of the
145
      training dataset. Furthermore, model-based training-run simulators (Guu et al., 2023) and submodular
146   selection routines (Coleman et al., 2019) represent newer approaches to assess and select the training
147   data to increase the efficiency of downstream fine-tuning.

150   3     M ETHOD
151
152   In this section, we introduce ActivePrune, a framework for efficient pruning of the unlabeled pool
153   to enable computationally feasible active learning. ActivePrune has the following key components:
154
155         1. A fast, approximate quality evaluation of the entire unlabeled pool using perplexity scores
156            (Section 3.2).
157
            2. A high-quality assessment for a select subset of candidates through a quantized LLM
158
               (Section 3.3).
159
160         3. A reweighting algorithm that incorporates the labeled instances after each AL iteration to
161            reweight the perplexity distribution and promote the selection of examples from previously
               underrepresented subpopulations (Section 3.4).

      3.1     P RELIMINARIES
163
164   We follow the standard active learning setup used in previous works (Hu & Neubig, 2021; Tsvigun
165   et al., 2022b). Consider an unlabeled dataset U = {xi }N   i=1 and the labeled set L which is initially
166   empty. In each iteration, the acquisition strategy Q selects the most informative examples for
167   annotation which are then annotated by human experts H(·). During the experiments, we simulate
168   this labeling procedure by acquiring the actual labels from the dataset, following the protocol from
169   previous works (Tsvigun et al., 2022b; Hu & Neubig, 2021; Tsvigun et al., 2023). The labeled
170
      examples are used to train the acquisition model M, added to the labeled dataset L, and excluded
      from the unlabeled dataset U. The model is then evaluated on an unseen validation set. This process
171
      continues until the budget b is exhausted. In each iteration, we construct a filtered unlabeled pool F
172
      using ActivePrune, which is used by the acquisition strategy instead of the complete unlabeled pool
173   U.
174
175   3.2     P ERPLEXITY C ALCULATION
176
177   The first step in ActivePrune is to perform a quality evaluation for the complete unlabeled pool
178   U. For this purpose, we first compute the perplexity scores for each example in the unlabeled pool
179   P = {p1 , p2 , . . . , pn }. This process must be computationally feasible so that large datasets are
180   processed quickly. Thus, for fast and efficient perplexity calculation, we use the KenLM 5-gram
181   language model (Heafield, 2011) along with the SentencePiece tokenizer (Kudo & Richardson, 2018).
182
      KenLM enables fast and memory-efficient language model queries through the Probing and Trie
      data structures, and uses a modified version of Kneser-Ney smoothing. The Probing variant of
183
      KenLM achieves a computation speed of 1818 perplexity queries per millisecond (Heafield, 2011)
184
      on the English Gigaword corpus (Parker, Robert et al., 2009). We use the KenLM trained on the
185   Oscar dataset (Suárez et al., 2020) derived from CommonCrawl, which contains web-crawl data.
186   Using KenLM trained on web-crawl data enables the perplexity filtering procedure to generalize
187   more effectively across diverse domains, enhancing its robustness to various types of datasets, as
188   demonstrated by the results in Table 1.
189
      Once the perplexity scores are computed, we sort them in an ascending order Psorted =
190
      argsort(P, order = ascending) and select the 1...k indices that have the lowest perplexity scores:
191   Ps = {pi ∈ Psorted | i = 1, 2, . . . , k}. The examples with the lowest perplexity scores are then
192   added to the filtered pool: F = F ∪ Ps . The examples with high perplexity scores are filtered out
193   (based on a pruning percentage p) as they represent highly surprising and potentially noisy examples
194   (Muennighoff et al., 2024; Marion et al., 2023; Sachdeva et al., 2024) and these examples are thus
195   re-evaluated through data quality scores in the next step before they can be selected for labeling.
196

      Calculating data quality scores through an LLM over a sizable dataset can be computationally expen-
217   sive. Therefore, we use a quantized LLM for the computation of data quality scores. Quantization
218   (Dettmers et al., 2022) significantly reduces the cost of inference by representing weights in limited
219   bits. We use 4-bit quantized LLMs (Dettmers et al., 2024) which have been shown to match the full
220   16-bit fine-tuning performance despite using a fraction of memory.
221
      After calculating the data quality scores, we sort them in a descending order Qsorted =
222
      argsort(Q, order = descending) and select the 1...k indices having the highest data quality scores:
223   Qs = {qi ∈ Qsorted | i = 1, 2, . . . , k}. These are then added to the filtered pool as well: F = F ∪ Qs .
224   The final unlabeled pool containing examples selected through perplexity and data quality scores is
225   then passed to the acquisition function which begins the next AL iteration.

228   3.4   P ERPLEXITY R EWEIGHTING

      xi , given the set of newly labeled instances L ⊆ L, is defined as:

      The perplexity of the instances in the unlabeled pool which are farther away from the newly labeled
247
      instances is decreased after reweighting, thereby increasing their chance of being selected through
248   bottom-k perplexity sampling. β is a hyperparameter that controls the sensitivity to the adjustment
249   factor. Higher values of β result in a steeper decrease in the perplexity for instances farther away
250   from those recently labeled. Note that perplexity reweighting takes place outside the AL procedure
251   itself and instead within the data pruning step, making it independent of any specific AL strategy.
252   Thus, perplexity reweighting is different from other sample reweighting techniques in AL that are
253   typically designed to increase batch diversity for specific acquisition functions, e.g., BADGE (Ash
254   et al., 2019).
255   The following proposition outlines the mechanism by which perplexity reweighting enhances diversity
256   across multiple iterations.
257
258
      Proposition 1. Let U = {xi }N   i=1 be the unlabeled pool and Lt be the labeled set at iteration t. Let
      S ⊆ U represent an underrepresented subset, containing the instances in S that have perplexity
259
      scores significantly different from those in Lt . Under the perplexity reweighting procedure, for any
260
      xi ∈ S, the probability of selecting xi increases progressively, ensuring its selection within a finite
261   number of iterations.
262
263
      The detailed proof for this proposition is included in Appendix A, which shows that the perplexity
264   reweighting mechanism prevents the data pruning process from focusing narrowly, as underrepre-
265   sented instances are systematically brought forward for selection. This is done progressively by
266   reducing the perplexity of such instances over multiple AL iterations, which in turn guarantees that
267   these are selected with high probability within a finite number of AL iterations, thereby promoting
268   diversity in the selected data. In practical scenarios, although the total number of AL iterations may be
269   constrained by the available budget, perplexity-based reweighting still ensures that underrepresented
      instances are consistently prioritized for selection.

291   4.2   M ODELS

      For computing the LLM data quality scores and the LLM perplexity scores, we use the Gemma-2B

      4.3   ACTIVE L EARNING M ETHODS
305
306   We consider several active learning methods for evaluation.
307
308   Random Sampling draws training examples uniformly at random and is considered a competitive
309
      baseline in AL (Hu & Neubig, 2021; Tsvigun et al., 2023).
310   Least Confidence (Lewis, 1995) selects the examples on which the model is least confident.
311
      Coreset (Sener & Savarese, 2017) samples based on representations extracted from the model’s
312
      penultimate layer.
313
314   Normalized Sequence Probability (NSP) (Ueffing & Ney, 2007) is an uncertainty-based strategy
315   designed to measure the uncertainty in a model’s predictions on a specific sequence. It forms the
316
      basis for numerous other uncertainty-based active learning (AL) methods.
317   In-Domain Diversity Sampling (IDDS) (Tsvigun et al., 2023) aims to choose sentences distinct
318   from those previously annotated to enhance the diversity of the final collection. At the same time, it
319   avoids picking noisier instances that differ significantly from the documents in the in-domain dataset.

      4.5      T RAINING S ETTINGS
379
380   For the translation task on the IT domain dataset, we use the BART-base variant (with 139 million
381   parameters) trained on the WMT14 En-De out-of-domain parallel corpora, following the setup in
382   previous works (Dou et al., 2019; Hu & Neubig, 2021). SacreBLEU score is used for the evaluation
383   on the test set of the IT domain dataset. For abstractive text summarization on the AESLC dataset,
384   we follow the setup used in Tsvigun et al. (2023) and train the BART-base model. ROUGE-L score is
385   used for the evaluation on the test set of AESLC. For binary sentiment classification on the IMDB
386
      dataset and topic classification on the AG News dataset, we follow the protocol from Tsvigun et al.
      (2022b) and fine-tune the RoBERTa-base model. F1-score is used for the evaluation of the model on
387
      the test set of IMDB and AG News datasets.
388
389   In each AL iteration, the acquisition model is trained for 5 epochs, with a learning rate of 2e-5, weight
390   decay of 0.028, and a batch size of 16. Gradient clipping was set at 0.28, and a warmup steps factor
391   of 0.1 was applied to the learning rate scheduler. For the 4-bit quantization of the Gemma-2B model,
392
      we use the CUDA implementation provided by bitsandbytes 1 .
393   At the start of each iteration, the data pruning strategy selects 25% of the dataset (thus pruning away
394   75% of the dataset), which is then passed to the AL strategy. 1% of the dataset is selected by the AL
395   strategy, added to the labeled pool (i.e., emulating the labeling process), and then used for training
396   the acquisition model, mirroring the protocol presented in Tsvigun et al. (2022b). β (perplexity
397   reweighting sensitivity) is set to 0.1 after tuning through validation set. We evaluate the acquisition
398
      model on the unseen test dataset after each AL iteration.

404   We compare ActivePrune with the other four data pruning strategies in Table 1. For each combination
405   of the dataset, AL strategy, and data pruning method, we do three runs with different seeds and report
406
      the mean evaluation metric along with the standard error. Across all datasets, ActivePrune performs
      better than other strategies in the final AL iteration with statistically significant differences. On the
407
      IT domain dataset with IDDS strategy, ActivePrune shows the highest SacreBLEU consistently
408
      over 5 AL iterations, with an increase of 15.61% (25.06 to 28.97) after the final iteration compared
409   to the runner-up baseline (ASK-LLM). For the NSP strategy, ActivePrune shows an increase of
410   1.44% SacreBLEU over Perplexity (30.53 to 30.97) to 2.92% over Random (30.09 to 30.97). For
411   the AESLC dataset, the difference in ROUGE-L score between ActivePrune and other strategies
412   goes up to 6.37%. In certain configurations, the performance of ActivePrune even exceeds the no
413   pruning baseline. For instance, our method achieves a SacreBLEU score of 30.97 on the IT domain
414   dataset with NSP strategy, compared to 30.57 SacreBLEU without pruning. We hypothesize that this
415   performance increase results from the selective removal of noisy examples from the entire unlabeled
416   pool.
417   For the AG News and IMDB classification tasks, ActivePrune demonstrates the highest F1-score
418   across most AL iterations for both Coreset and Least Confidence strategies. Note that the relative
419   performance of other strategies varies between datasets, pointing towards differences in the data
420   distribution. For example, ASK-LLM does not perform better than Perplexity and UPS on IMDB
421   dataset. However, ActivePrune is able to perform consistently well, indicating the robustness of this
422   strategy to different domains and distribution shifts.

      6     C ONCLUSION
525
526
      In this work, we introduced ActivePrune, a novel plug-and-play method for enabling efficient
527
      active learning through data pruning using language models. Our approach follows a two-stage
528   pruning process: a fast evaluation using perplexity scores from an n-gram language model, and a
529   high-quality selection using quantized LLM. Additionally, we introduce a perplexity reweighting
530   method that increases the diversity of the selected examples. Our experiments with diverse datasets
531   and tasks demonstrate that ActivePrune effectively reduces the size of the unlabeled pool without
532   compromising the selection quality of examples. Moreover, ActivePrune enables more efficient
533   utilization of resources during active learning by reducing the computational cost associated with
534   large unlabeled pools. Future work includes the application of ActivePrune on larger datasets to
535   demonstrate its utility in enabling efficient active learning at scale.
536   Limitations. The implications of pruning the unlabeled pool on the privacy and fairness of the final
537   trained models were not explored in this study. Data pruning may lead to disproportionate pruning of
538   different subpopulations in the dataset, potentially leading to the insufficient representation of specific
539   groups. To address these issues, it is essential to thoroughly evaluate explainability and fairness for
      models that undergo active learning after a data pruning procedure.
