009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011            Object counting is an important task in computer vision with many real-world applica-
012            tions. In practical settings, factors such as lighting conditions and object density can vary
013            dramatically, leading to distribution shifts then causing inaccurate counting. We found
014            that existing domain adaptation (DA) methods cannot be directly applied to the count-
015            ing task, as they usually assume changes across different domains are task-irrelevant and
016            focus on utilizing domain-invariant features for prediction. However, in object counting
017
               tasks, changes in object density which could happen across domains are task-relevant and
               cannot be ignored. Therefore, applying existing DA methods to the counting task can ig-
018
               nore the information about density changes, resulting in unreliable counting. To address
019
               this limitation, we propose the Binary Alignment Network (BiAN). Unlike traditional DA
020            methods that align distributions of entire image representations, BiAN segments objects
021            of interest and aligns the distributions of the object-specific features across domains. This
022            targeted alignment allows us to disregard irrelevant features, such as lighting conditions
023            while preserving essential information about changes in object density. We theoretically
024            demonstrate that BiAN achieves superior adaptability in counting tasks by introducing
025            conditional alignment—aligning features conditioned on the presence of objects. Exten-
026            sive experiments on two distinct counting tasks and eight dataset combinations show that
027            BiAN outperforms state-of-the-art methods.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Object counting is an important task in computer vision with a wide range of real-world applications, in-
032   cluding crowd monitoring, traffic analysis, and biomedical imaging. Accurate counting of objects within
033   images or video frames is crucial for decision-making processes in various industries and research domains
034   (He et al., 2021). However, in practical settings, factors such as lighting conditions, object density, and back-
035   ground complexity can vary dramatically across different environments. These variations lead to distribution
036   shifts between training data (source domain) and deployment scenarios (target domain), posing significant
037   challenges for object counting models (Wang et al., 2019b).
038   To address distribution shifts, domain adaptation (DA) methods have been widely explored in machine
039   learning. These methods aim to improve the generalization capabilities of models by aligning the feature
040   distributions between source and target domains (Singhal et al., 2023). In tasks like image classification and
041   semantic segmentation, DA methods generally assume that domain shifts are task-irrelevant, meaning the
042   shifts do not affect the core features necessary for accurate predictions (Kong et al., 2022; Xie et al., 2023b;
043   Bousmalis et al., 2016). By focusing on learning domain-invariant features, these methods strive to maintain
044
      performance across different domains.
045   However, this assumption does not hold in the context of object counting. Changes in object density across
046   domains are inherently task-relevant, as the primary goal is to accurately estimate the number of objects

079   objects of interest and aligning the distributions of their features across domains. By isolating the features
080   within the objects, our method effectively filters out task-irrelevant variations such as lighting conditions

      alignment ensures that the model remains sensitive to variations that directly impact the counting task.
082
083   We further provide a theoretical framework demonstrating that BiAN achieves superior adaptability by in-
084   troducing conditional alignment—aligning features conditioned on the presence of objects. This approach
085   allows the model to account for density variations between domains more effectively than traditional meth-
086   ods. By conditioning on object presence, BiAN maintains sensitivity to the number and arrangement of
087
      objects, which are critical factors in accurate counting.
088   Our main contributions are summarized as follows:
089
090   • We highlight the shortcomings of existing domain adaptation techniques when applied to object counting
091     tasks. Specifically, the existing DA methods contempt the dynamic density across scenes as the task-
092     relevant factor, violating the DA assumption. The aligned density distribution causes source-consistency
093     density estimation, leading to decay in counting performance.

      2.1   O BJECT C OUNTING
112
113   Object counting is a fundamental task in computer vision, with applications in various fields, such as crowd
114   monitoring, cell counting, and traffic analysis (Loy et al., 2013). Traditional counting methods rely on
115   supervised learning, which requires a large amount of annotated data (Jiang et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2021;
116   Gao et al., 2021). Recent advances in deep learning have significantly improved the performance of counting
117   models. For instance, Kernel-based Density Map Generation (KDMG) (Wan et al., 2022) employs a kernel-
118   based density map to estimate the object count. SAU-Net (Guo et al., 2022) combines the advantages of
119   SANet and U-Net to achieve high counting accuracy. STEERER (Han et al., 2023) cumulatively selects
120
      and inherits discriminative features to resolve scale variations. Despite the remarkable performance of these
      models, they are limited by the requirement of large amounts of annotated data when encountering domain
121
      variety. Therefore, GAN-based UDA counting methods have been proposed, such as Counting Object via
122
      scale-aware adversarial Density Adaptation (CODA) (Li et al., 2019), devised to address distinct object
123   scale and density distributions. Additionally, SSIM Embedding Cycle GAN (SECycle) (Wang et al., 2019b)
124   has emerged as a potent solution for counting in natural crowd scenes by synthesizing target-like images.
125   To amplify the model’s adaptability across intricate scenarios, the novel Latent Domain Generation (LDG)
126   (Zhang et al., 2023) method has been introduced, generating the latent domain to learn the distribution from
127   domains. The advanced research adopts the latest approaches in other fields, such as SaKnD (Xie et al.,
128   2023a) which utilizing diffusion modules to enhance generalizability and CrowdGraph (Zhang et al., 2024a)
129   which proposed an algorithm via pure graph neural network. To the best of our knowledge, there remains a
130   research gap in discriminate migration for preserving task-relevant information across domains.

288   the unconditional alignment leads to the significant constraint of lowering the joint decision error, caus-

290

756   A.1     M ORE R ELATED W ORK
757
758
      In Unsupervised Domain Adaptation (UDA), there are common to adopt the component-wise alignment to
759
      align the feature distribution across domains. The most common method is to align the marginal distribution
760   of the feature space (Jiang et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2024b; Yao et al., 2023; Lopez-Rodriguez & Mikola-
761   jczyk, 2022; Zhao & Wang, 2022; Zhou et al., 2023). MGA (Zhang et al., 2024b) designs category-level
762   discriminator to align the distributions on the category-level. D-adapt (Jiang et al., 2022) deploys the bound-
763   ing box alignment for mitigating the domain shift on object-level. Our method is actually different from
764   these methods. Their goal is only to align the distribution of object-relevant features under the assumption
765   that unconditional alignment can reduce the joint decision error all the time. It also assumes that the un-
766   conditional alignment will not violate the inter-object contextual distribution. Previous methods that align
767   the entire image encourage the pseudo-label distributions of the source and target domains to converge, po-
768
      tentially overlooking significant gaps between the true label spaces due to inherent label shifts (Zhao et al.,
769
      2019). This issue is represented by the inequality:dJS (Y, Y ′ ) ≥ dJS (Y, Ŷ ′ ), where dJS denotes the Jensen-
770   Shannon divergence between the label distributions Y (source domain), Y ′ (target domain), and Ŷ ′ (pseudo
771
      labels of the target domain). Although source domain labels are accessible, the inequality holds due to the
      unchangeable nature of the true label shift. According to Theorem 1, minimizing the estimated label differ-
772
      ence encourages unsupervised domain adaptation methods to reduce dJS (D, D′ ). However, combining this
773
      with the inequality leads to a higher lower bound of ϵU , the target risk, due to the immutable label space
774   difference. This theoretical insight explains why unconditional (global) alignment has limited adaptability
775   in our context. And the consistency module enforces consistency between object and background features
776   rather than maintaining features of ROI across domains, further enhancing the model’s ability to capture
777   essential density information. Technically, this module ensures that the model maintains consistency be-
778   tween object and background features, promoting accurate counting by preserving the relationships between
779   objects and their surroundings. Theoretically, CM play a crucial role in maintaining the consistency of the
780   feature space, implementing Definition 3. The proposed BiAN is designed to align the feature distribu-
781   tion across domains while preserving the inter-object information by maintaining the condition-independent
782
      partitions.

      Here we provide the detailed training procedure of BiAN. The training process is shown in Algorithm A.2.
      The training process is similar to the standard training process of UDA. The difference is that we introduce
790
      the conditional alignment and the CM module to the training process. The images (xs , xt ) from the source
791
      and target domain are fed into the model h = gs/t ◦ f . The model firstly predicts the counting results
792   ŷs and ŷt . Then model segments the images into foreground and background using the predicted results,
793
      obtaining (xfs , xbs ) and (xft , xbt ). The model then predicts the conditional results (ŷsf , ŷsb ) and (ŷtf , ŷtb ) for the
794   foreground and background. The conditional domain loss Ld is calculated between the conditional results.
795   The pixel loss Lp is calculated between the predicted results and the ground truth. The source loss Lsource
796   and the target loss Ltarget are calculated. The CM loss LCM is calculated between the conditional results and
797   the predicted results. The sum up loss L is calculated. The gradient of the loss is calculated and the model
798   is updated.

1105   In this section, we present the visual results of BiAN in the counting task experiments. As shown in Figure 7,
1106   we randomly select two samples from every cross-domain adaptation. In the visualization figure, we mark
1107   the inaccurate counts in the samples. The low-density samples can be counted in precise amounts, and the
1108   localization is also accurate. However, in microscopy cell images, cells of an overlapped or abnormal size are
1109   not fully recognized. The cell-alike objects (e.g. bubbles) easily distract the model recognition, especially
1110   in the DCC cell images. The conditional alignment mechanism enables BiAN to recognize distinguishing
1111
       features of cells. As for the crowd counting task, human main characters are important cues to lead the
       model to marks. In contrast, the characters of hidden persons are easily missed targets. The results show that
1112
       BiAN is able to retrieve the partial features of humans. It results in significant performance improvements.

       Overall, the visualization demonstrates the proposed model’s recognition ability and learning of the visual
1114   representation of counting targets.

1119   the aforementioned joint error is not the tightest (Zhao et al., 2019). It means the tightest lower bound of

1122   more conditions. Regarding the designed model BiAN, we observed that there exists limited precision in
1123   recognizing and localizing, and counting minor objects. This aspect can be further improved in addition to
1124
       the proposed CM. Nevertheless, our work emphasizes the importance of matching the conditions during the
       adaptation process and provides a promising direction for future research.
