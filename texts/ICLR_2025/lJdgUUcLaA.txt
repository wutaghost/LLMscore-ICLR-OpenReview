009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            We present the first correct-by-construction learning-based system for step-by-step
012            mathematical integration. The key idea is to learn a policy, represented by a GPT
013            transformer model, which guides the search for the right mathematical integration
014            rule, to be carried out by a symbolic solver. Concretely, we introduce a symbolic
015            engine with axiomatically correct actions on mathematical expressions, as well
016            as the first dataset for step-by-step integration. Our GPT-style transformer model,
017            trained on this synthetic data, demonstrates strong generalization by surpassing its
018            own data generator in accuracy and efficiency, using 50% fewer search steps. Our
019
               experimental results with SoTA LLMs also demonstrate that the standard approach
               of fine-tuning LLMs on a set of question-answer pairs is insufficient for solving this
020
               mathematical task. This motivates the importance of discovering creative methods
021
               for combining LLMs with symbolic reasoning engines, of which our work is an
022            instance.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Large language models (LLMs) based on the transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2023) have
028   demonstrated remarkable abilities across diverse tasks, such as language translation, code generation,
029   and engaging human-like conversations (OpenAI, 2024). However, applying these models to mathe-
030   matics presents significant challenges. Their autoregressive nature makes them prone to hallucinations
031   and errors during inference. Advancements such as Chain-of-Thought (CoT), self-consistency, and
032   process supervision help generate more accurate multi-step reasoning (Wei et al., 2023), (Wang
033   et al., 2023), (Lightman et al., 2023). However, unlike general language tasks, mathematics demands
034   absolute rigor and precision, where even minor errors are unacceptable. Mathematical correctness
035
      relies on faultless execution of logical steps and computations. LLMs often fail to achieve this
      consistently and there is no provable method which ensures the correctness of their mathematical
036
      reasoning.
037
038
039
      Mathematical Integration A fundamental mathematical task is one of indefinite integration of
      mathematical expressions, a problem with no straightforward algorithmic solution. Existing methods
040
      for solving this task fall into two categories: those that directly output the antiderivative, and those
041
      that provide step-by-step proofs.
042
043   Lample and Charton (2019) proposed a learning-based approach, training a seq2seq model to generate
044   the antiderivative directly, without steps and with correctness verification left as a separate problem.
045
      Welleck et al. (2021) demonstrated that such models do not generalize well, even though they
      might have high test accuracy, as the neural network needs to mechanically learn how to carry out
046
      complex operations like applying the partial fractions algorithm or dividing two large numbers. The
047
      algorithm proposed by Risch (1969) reduces the integration problem into finding poles of certain
048   algebraic functions. Risch’s method is pseudo-complete for indefinite integration, but it only applies
049   to the restricted setting of functions with elementary antiderivatives and similarly does not produce
050   intermediate steps. The full description of the method is longer than 100 pages and has never been
051   fully implemented. Current symbolic solvers often include a simplified, heuristic version. However,
052   the resulting answers, while always correct, are not very illuminating. Further, in contrast to learning-
053   based approaches, this method does not directly generalize to similar tasks, such as non-elementary
      or multidimensional integration, or general theorem proving.

054
      To generate step-by-step proofs, SymPy’s manualintegrate module can be used, which applies various
055   heuristic techniques recursively to construct a solution. However, this method is slow and prone
056   to failure on simple integrals due to its reliance on manual pattern matching. Another option is
057   leveraging state-of-the-art language models like GPT-4. However, these models lack guarantees
058   of correctness. Our experiments further show that, despite their vast training data and billions of
059   parameters, such models often perform poorly on complex integrals.
060
061   Our Work: correct-by-construction learning-based integration In this work we introduce the
062   first open system which combines the strengths of both symbolic engines and GPT transformer
063   models in order to learn to integrate mathematical expressions in a step-by-step, provable manner.
064   Our approach is inspired by the groundbreaking advancements of AlphaProof and AlphaGeometry
065   (Trinh et al., 2024), where language models interact with a symbolic engine to generate a solution
066   that is guaranteed to be correct. Concretely, we designed a novel symbolic engine and generated
067
      synthetic data used to train a GPT transformer language model capable of sophisticated interaction
      with this engine.
068
069
070
      Main contributions Our key contributions are:
071          • The first dataset for rigorous step-by-step derivation proofs for indefinite integration.
072          • A versatile open-source symbolic engine to interact with mathematical expressions through
073            axiomatically correct actions with a novel encoding.
074          • A (very small) transformer model which surpasses in performance the leading open-source
075            step-by-step integration baseline. Our evaluation also demonstrates that our tool can effec-
076
               tively guide search in a complicated action space and thus surpass its own dataset generator
               in both completeness and efficiency, through strong generalization.

083   2    S YMBOLIC E NGINE WITH PARAMETRIC ACTION S PACE
084
085
      We depart from the typical approach of solving mathematics problems with LLMs done by fine-tuning
086   on question-answer pairs (Shao et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024). Instead, our language model interacts
087   with a symbolic engine exposing a parametric action space. This guarantees that every step taken by
088   the model is correct (or null) since rewrites of mathematical expressions are permitted only through
089   the symbolic engine.
090
      In each step, the symbolic engine takes in a mathematical expression f , a subexpression g, an action
091
      a, and action parameters p1 , . . . , pn , if applicable. It returns an expression that results from applying
092   action a(p1 , . . . , pn ) to subexpression g, along with a boolean that specifies whether the expression
093   was modified or not. The expression is not modified if g is not a valid subexpression of f , or if the
094   action is not valid on this subexpression. Figure 1 shows an example of a successfully executed
095   action. A full list of actions can be found in Table 4 in Appendix A.1.

      4     G ENERATING A S YNTHETIC M ATHEMATICAL I NTEGRATION DATASET
184
185
      We will train a model to derive step-by-step integrals by predicting single-step rule applications. To
      this end, we generate fully synthetic step-by-step integration data, described in this section. Note that
186
      such a rigorous step-by-step integration dataset, based on a well-defined space of possible actions
187
      and on such a broad variety of mathematical expression data, was lacking prior to this work.
188
189
      4.1     R ANDOM M ATHEMATICAL E XPRESSIONS
190
191   To create a large-scale dataset of mathematical expressions, we adopt an algorithm from Lample and
192   Charton (2019), which samples random unary-binary trees and fills the nodes with operators and
193   operands. We generate ∼5M unique expressions with this algorithm described in Appendix C.1.
194
195   4.2     S TEPS OF I NTEGRATION
196
197   Once we have generated a dataset of random mathematical expressions, we pass them through the
198   manualintegrate module of SymPy (Meurer et al., 2017), in order to get a step-by-step solution. Then,
199   we map the solution into a sequence of actions and parameters in our symbolic engine. This results in
200   a sequence of tuples of expression, subexpression, action, and action parameter for each expression.
201
      An example of a full solution in this format, generated by our model, is given in Figure 4. There are,
      of course, many expressions SymPy cannot integrate as it enumeratively tries heuristic methods. Our
202
      hypothesis is that transformers are able to generalize to cases not covered by SymPy.

217
218   In this section, we describe our transformer model architecture and the objective we used for training.
219
220   5.1   A RCHITECTURE AND H YPERPARAMETERS
221
222   We use a decoder-only transformer architecture with 6 layers of multi-head attention with 6 heads
223   and a hidden dimension of 384 (Radford et al., 2019). This results in a tiny model with only 10M
224   parameters. We use the AdamW optimizer with β1 = 0.9, β2 = 0.99, dropout of β = 0.2, and
225   weight decay of λ = 0.1 (Loshchilov and Hutter, 2017). We decay the learning rate linearly from
226   10−3 to 10−4 throughout training, with batch size 256. We use a single A100 GPU for training. We
227
      choose this simple setting as we observed no performance improvements with larger architectures.
228
229
      5.2   T RAINING O BJECTIVE
230
      We would like our model to propose a subexpression, action, and parameters of the action given a
231   mathematical expression to integrate. Then, the model will be repeatedly fed back with the result
232   obtained through the symbolic engine to find solutions for new expressions. To train our model for
233   this, we shuffle all integration steps into lines structured as follows:
234
      START [EXPR] SUBEXPR [SUBEXPR] RULE [RULE] PARAM [PARAM] END
235
236   Here, terms in parentheses are tokenized mathematical expressions (e.g. x2 + sinh(x)) or actions (e.g.
237   PartsRule). We use the standard Cross Entropy Loss objective, where the model predicts extensions
238   of START [EXPR] SUBEXPR. An example from the training dataset looks as follows:
239   START Integral cos + E + x tan INT+ 2 x SUBEXPR Integral cos + E +

262   Given an expression f , we tokenize it and feed it to the transformer. We decode the transformer using
263   beam search with N candidates until we reach the END token, i.e., we heuristically search for the

430
431   Deep Learning for Theorem Proving Deep learning applied to theorem proving has seen significant
      advancements, particularly in premise selection and proof guidance. Early work such as DeepMath


                                                         8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      used CNNs and RNNs to predict the relevance of premises for proving conjectures (Alemi et al.,
433   2016). Later, various papers leveraged decoder-only transformer architectures or pre-trained LLMs
434   to guide proofs through recommending premises and next steps (Polu and Sutskever, 2020) (Yang
435   and Deng, 2019) (Song et al., 2024). These models usually interact with Interactive Theorem Provers
436   (ITPs) such as Lean, Isabelle, or Coq. This usually requires large-scale pre-training data to be
437   successful. On the contrary, we develop a compact language for interaction between the symbolic
438   engine and generative model. This allows us to easily generate synthetic data and circumvent the
439   pre-training required to learn the syntax of the language.
440   Tree Structured Neural Networks A recent body of work has employed neural network structures
441   that have inductive biases for processing tree-structured inputs which is the case when dealing with
442   mathematical expressions or abstract syntax trees. For example, Tai et al. (2015) proposes TreeLSTM,
443   a generalization of LSTMs to tree-structured network topologies. (Huang et al., 2018) uses this
444   architecture to train baseline models for the formalization of the Feit-Thompson Theorem. (Arabshahi
445   et al., 2018) trains the same architecture to model mathematical equations and verify their correctness.
446   In this work, we focus on decoder-only architectures, as they have become more standard, and we
447
      aim to build a system that is not only efficient but also easily transferable to other tasks. This allows
      for greater flexibility and adaptability across various problem domains.

450   9    C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
451
452   We introduced a novel approach for step-by-step integration using a transformer model guided
453   by a custom symbolic engine. The policy captured by the transformer model is learned from
454   a synthetically generated dataset of integration rules. A major advantage of our work is that it
455   guarantees the final expression is always sound. This follows from the fact that the policy always
456
      applies a correct-by-construction integration rule, realized by the symbolic solver. Our experimental
      evaluation demonstrates strong generalization, surpassing its data generator in accuracy and efficiency.
457
      Interestingly, it also provides insights into potential errors found in modern heuristic solvers. We
458
      demonstrated significant improvements compared to direct approaches using LLMs, which typically
459   fine-tune an LLM on a dataset of question-answer pairs. We exhibit better robustness compared
460   to other learning-based approaches and our method naturally generalizes to other settings, where
461   results without intermediate steps may be hard to verify. Limitations include reliance on synthetic
462   data and the scope of integration techniques that we handle. Future work will focus on more general
463   approaches to training such as reinforcement learning, expanding the action space, and extending the
464   model to other mathematical tasks.

468   This work focuses on improving symbolic integration using transformer models, which has limited
469   direct ethical concerns. The methods presented here aim to enhance mathematical problem-solving,
470   primarily for academic and educational purposes. Since the model is designed for symbolic com-
471   putation, the potential for misuse is minimal, and the outcomes are easily verifiable. As with all
472   AI systems, it is important to ensure that results are used appropriately in domains requiring high
473   mathematical rigor, but we see no significant ethical risks associated with this research.

      C     DATASET G ENERATION
703
704
      C.1    G ENERATING R ANDOM M ATHEMATICAL E XPRESSIONS
705
706   The algorithm for generating random mathematical expressions consist of three steps:
707
            1. Sample random unary-binary trees with a uniformly distributed number of nodes between 3
708
               and N , where N = 50.
709         2. Fill the leaves of the tree with a symbol x with probability p = 34 and one of the constants π,
710
               e or number {0, . . . , 10} uniformly at random with probability 1 − p = 41 . This is aimed at
711            generating more difficult expressions effectively by incentivizing symbols as leaves.
712         3. Fill remaining internal nodes with random unary or binary operations. The binary operators
713            are +, −, ×, /, and the unary operators include trigonometric, hyperbolic, their inverses,
714            and the exp and log functions.
715
      When selecting binary operations, addition and multiplication are twice as likely to be chosen over
716
      division and subtraction, as the latter can result in term cancellations and duplicate values. Unary
717   operations are sampled uniformly at random. We do all trivial simplifications (e.g. evaluating x + 2x
718   to 3x or x + 1 + 0 + 3 to x + 4. All duplicates are removed during post-processing.
