                                                  A BSTRACT
013
014
               Spatio-temporal forecasting is crucial in various fields and requires a careful bal-
015
               ance between identifying subtle patterns and filtering out noise. Vector quantization
016            (VQ) appears well-suited for this purpose, as it quantizes input vectors into a set
017            of codebook vectors or patterns. Although VQ has shown promise in various
018            computer vision tasks, it surprisingly falls short in enhancing the accuracy of
019            spatio-temporal forecasting. We attribute this to two main issues: inaccurate op-
020            timization due to non-differentiability and limited representation power in hard
021            VQ. To tackle these challenges, we introduce Differentiable Sparse Soft-Vector
022            Quantization (SVQ), the first VQ method to enhance spatio-temporal forecasting.
023            SVQ balances detail preservation with noise reduction, offering full differen-
024
               tiability and a solid foundation in sparse regression. Our approach employs a
               two-layer MLP and an extensive codebook to streamline the sparse regression
025
               process, significantly cutting computational costs while simplifying training and
026
               improving performance. Empirical studies on five spatio-temporal benchmark
027            datasets show SVQ achieves state-of-the-art results, including a 7.9% improvement
028            on the WeatherBench-S temperature dataset and an average MAE reduction of
029            9.4% in video prediction benchmarks (Human3.6M, KTH, and KittiCaltech), along
030            with a 17.3% enhancement in image quality (LPIPS). Code is publicly available at
031            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/SVQ-Forecasting.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036
      Spatio-temporal forecasting is pivotal in numerous domains ranging from environmental monitoring
      to urban planning, where precisely predicting future dynamics is crucial. The journey to refine
037
      forecasting methods has spanned from traditional feature engineering to the latest explorations in
038
      deep learning. Among various methodologies explored, Vector Quantization (VQ) has distinguished
039   itself primarily in computer vision tasks, showcasing its ability to compress high-dimensional
040   vectors into a compact, discrete form that maintains significant fidelity to the original information.
041   Historically rooted in signal processing, VQ’s breakthrough came with its application in image
042   processing advancements like the Vector Quantised-Variational AutoEncoder (VQ-VAE) van den
043   Oord et al. (2017), which set a precedent in generating high-quality images by learning efficient
044   representations of complex distributions.
045   While VQ has proven effective and has become a nearly default approach in computer vision
046   generation tasks, its potential applications in spatio-temporal forecasting remain less explored.
047   Considering the noise reduction capabilities of VQ, along with the similarities between image/video
048   generation and spatio-temporal forecasting, one could infer that VQ would positively impact the latter.
049   However, our review of existing studies reveals that few have successfully enhanced spatio-temporal
050   forecasting performance using VQ techniques. Our empirical analysis of recent state-of-the-art VQ
051   methods discovered that all of them fell short of expectations, often degrading the performance
052   of baseline forecasting models rather than providing the anticipated improvements, as illustrated
053
      in Figure 1 and detailed in Table 4. They consistently exert a significant negative influence on the
      final MSE or MAE regression accuracy.

057   inherent dynamic nature of spatio-temporal data. The
058   complex temporal evolutions and spatial distributions
059   introduce a level of complexity that traditional VQ
060   methods are not equipped to handle. Specifically, the
061   unsatisfactory results of traditional VQ methods are
062   caused by two reasons:

073   In response to these limitations, this work presents Differentiable Sparse Soft-Vector Quantization
074   (SVQ), a novel technique designed to strike a balance between noise reduction and detail preservation
075   for spatio-temporal forecasting tasks. We solve the aforementioned challenges by:
076
077          • Introducing a differentiable VQ that simplifies gradient computations. This is achieved by
078            approximating sparse regression with an MLP layer, coupled with a codebook, retaining
079            the differentiability crucial for modern deep learning pipelines. A two-layer MLP generates
080
               regression coefficients through nonlinear projections of input vectors. The quantized outputs
               are derived from the dot product of these coefficients and the codebook matrix. Since the
081
               coefficients are generated from input vectors, gradients can flow directly from the quantized
082
               outputs to the input vectors. This straightforward yet effective approach not only enables
083            differentiable VQ, enhancing accuracy, but also addressing the computational challenges
084            often associated with sparse regression in VQ.
085
             • Using Soft-VQ with sparse regression to combine vectors from a large codebook. As shown
086
               in Figure 2, SVQ innovatively integrates sparse regression and allows for the allocation of
087
               input vectors with multiple codebook vectors. This significantly enhances the model’s ability
088            to capture intricate patterns and effectively filter out noise. Compared to hard-VQ, SVQ
089            exhibits a more uniform distribution of codebook vectors, indicating that SVQ is able to
090            preserve more diverse and fine-grained information from the original inputs. Our empirical
091            studies reveal that SVQ possesses intriguing properties, such as effectively utilizing a
092            completely frozen, randomly initialized codebook without sacrificing performance, thereby
093            significantly reducing learning parameters and showcasing its efficiency and robustness.

116   Due to space limitations, here we provide a brief overview of vector quantization, the lineage of
117   sparse coding techniques, and the latest developments in spatio-temporal forecasting algorithms. An
118   extensive review can be found in the Appendix B.
119
      Vector Quantization and Sparse Coding. Instead of using continuous latent, VQ-VAE van den
120
      Oord et al. (2017), a seminal work, incorporates vector quantization to learn discrete latent represen-
121
      tations, typically assigning each vector to the nearest code in a codebook. Subsequent enhancements
122   include Residual VQ Zeghidour et al. (2022), which quantizes the residuals recursively, and Multi-
123   headed VQ Mama et al. (2021b), which adopts multiple heads for each vector. While these methods
124   are effective, they often rely on a relatively small number of codes to represent the original vectors.
125   To address this, SCVAE Xiao et al. (2023) employs sparse coding, allowing vectors to be represented
126   through sparse linear combinations of multiple codes, and achieves end-to-end training via the
127   Learnable Iterative Shrinkage Thresholding Algorithm (LISTA) Gregor & LeCun (2010). However, a
128   significant drawback of the sparse coding method using LISTA (referred to as SVQ-raw here) is its
129   high computational complexity, which scales quadratically with codebook size.
130   Building on these insights and limitations, our work proposes a soft-VQ method applicable to spatio-
131   temporal forecasting tasks. Although it is closely related to a simultaneous research work Tschannen
132   et al. (2023), which employs an infinite cookbook with a linear layer for continuous vector quantization
133   applied in image generation, our approach is largely inspired by sparse regression, as clearly evidenced
134   by our analysis. Specifically, our work focuses on the challenges arising from spatio-temporal
135   forecasting, providing a strong theoretical foundation and effectively addressing the challenges.
136   Spatio-Temporal Forecasting Models. Recent advancements in spatio-temporal forecasting have
137   highlighted a shift from recurrent to non-recurrent frameworks. Despite the forecasting capabilities of
138   models like ConvLSTM SHI et al. (2015), PredRNN Wang et al. (2017), and PredRNNV2Wang et al.
139   (2022), this shift is largely due to the high computational demands of sequential processing in recurrent
140   models. Non-recurrent models, such as MMVP Zhong et al. (2023) and the SimVP family Gao et al.
141   (2022); Tan et al. (2022), have become benchmarks in video prediction by decoupling spatial and
142
      temporal learning through an efficient encoder-translator-decoder structure. This transition is further
      enhanced by innovative features like visual attention in TAU Tan et al. (2023a) and MetaFormers
143
      in OpenSTL Tan et al. (2023b), showcasing the continuous improvements towards more effective
144
      forecasting solutions. Our proposed method is designed for seamless integration as a plugin with the
145   majority of these spatio-temporal forecasting models.

                                                                ′

      5.1   B ENCHMARKS ON VARIOUS FORECASTING TASKS
293
294   We explore both fixed (frozen) and learnable versions of SVQ on various forecasting tasks. Interest-
295   ingly, our findings reveal that with a large codebook size, the performance of a frozen, randomly-
296   initialized codebook is on par with that of a carefully learned codebook. This observation aligns
297   with our intuition: when allowed to choose a very large number of representative vectors to form
298   a codebook, a random choice is often as good as the one that is carefully chosen, which has al-
299   ready been studied in the column subset selection problem in matrix theory Drineas et al. (2008);

      Deshpande & Rademacher (2010). The comparison baselines consist of two categories: 1) Non-

      models including ConvLSTM SHI et al. (2015), PredNet Lotter et al. (2017), PredRNN Wang et al.

303   PhyDNet Guen & Thome (2020), MAU Chang et al. (2021), PredRNNv2 Wang et al. (2022), and
304   DMVFN Hu et al. (2023). Baseline results are copied from the original OpenSTL paper Tan et al.

531
532
      In this work, we present Differentiable Sparse Soft-Vector Quantization (SVQ), a concise yet effective
533
      method for spatio-temporal forecasting enhancement. Unlike other state-of-the-art VQ methods, this
534   is the first approach that demonstrates a boosting effect in spatio-temporal forecasting tasks.SVQ
535   elegantly tackles the inaccuracies in the optimization problem arising from non-differentiability and
536   the restricted representational capabilities associated with hard-VQ.Tested across diverse benchmarks,
537   from weather to traffic and video prediction, SVQ consistently outperforms pure baseline methods,
538   setting new performance standards without complex priors. Its differentiability and seamless inte-
539   gration with baseline models highlight SVQ as a significant advancement for efficient and effective
      spatio-temporal forecasting.

      A.4      E VALUATION OF PERPLEXITY
883
884   Unlike other VQ methods that rely on a single code, our SVQ generates multiple regression weights
885   to merge several codes. To evaluate its perplexity, we first normalize the regression weights and then
886   convert them into binary form using a threshold set at θ times the standard deviation, where θ serves
887   as the threshold value. We utilize two thresholds (2 and 3) to obtain reasonable perplexity.

897   B       E XTENSIVE REVIEW OF RELATED WORK
898
899   B.1      R ECURRENT- BASED FORECASTING MODEL
900
      The majority of spatio-temporal forecasting models leverage techniques such as Conv2D Xu et al.
901
      (2018), Conv3D Wang et al. (2019a), and attention mechanisms Liu et al. (2022a) for spatial
902   modeling. Distinctions among these models primarily arise from how they incorporate temporal
903   information. Recurrent-based models, exemplified by ConvLSTM SHI et al. (2015), have been
904   widely used to capture motion dynamics by iteratively processing multi-frame predictions. Variants
905   like PredRNN Wang et al. (2017) introduce the Spatio-Temporal LSTM (ST-LSTM) unit, integrating
906   spatial appearances and temporal variations within a single memory pool. Further advancements
907   include PredRNN++ Wang et al. (2018) and PredRNNV2 Wang et al. (2022), which deepen the model
908   and expand the receptive field through a cascading LSTM mechanism and a memory decoupling
909   strategy, respectively. MIM Wang et al. (2019b) network utilizes a self-renewed memory module to
910
      exploit differential signals by decomposing non-stationary dynamics. PredNet Lotter et al. (2017)
      improves performance by estimating prediction errors in forward propagation.
911
912
913
      B.2      N ON - RECURRENT FORECASTING MODEL
914   Despite the effectiveness of recurrent-based methods, they suffer from high computational cost caused
915   by their inherent unparallelizable architecture. Recent efforts in spatio-temporal forecasting have
916   shifted towards non-recurrent models that decouple the forecasting task from autoregressive processes.
917
          4
              https://github.com/lucidrains/vector-quantize-pytorch/tree/master


                                                                            17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      Notably, SimVPv1 Gao et al. (2022) and SimVPv2 Tan et al. (2022) separate spatial and temporal
919   learning into distinct phases within an encoder-translator-decoder structure, consistently surpassing
920   recurrent counterparts in video prediction tasks. TAU Tan et al. (2023a) refines the architecture by
921   incorporating a visual attention mechanism into the translator. OpenSTL Tan et al. (2023b) further
922   enhances the translator by MetaFormers.
923
924   B.3   V ECTOR QUANTIZATION
925
926   By partitioning a continuous vector space into a discrete collection of vectors, VQ effectively reduces
927   the data required to characterize a set of values, thereby achieving noise reduction. Following the
928   introduction of VQ-VAE van den Oord et al. (2017), many variants such as VQGAN Esser et al.
929
      (2021), Residual VQ Zeghidour et al. (2022), Multi-headed VQ Mama et al. (2021b), Grouped
      Residual VQ Yang et al. (2023), Finite Scalar Quantization (FSQ) Mama et al. (2021a), and Lookup
930
      Free Quantization Yu et al. (2023a) have been developed to enhance the representational capabilities
931
      of VQ. For instance, FSQ Mama et al. (2021a) simplifies VQ in generative modeling by discretizing
932   scalar values. However, to the best of our knowledge, VQ has seen limited application in spatio-
933   temporal forecasting, which has inspired our research. Traditional hard-VQ tends to eliminate
934   excessive detail, thus impairing forecasting accuracy. While the sparse coding-based variational
935   autoencoder (SC-VAE) Xiao et al. (2023) incorporates sparse coding into the variational autoencoder
936   framework, its application is primarily targeted at image reconstruction and segmentation tasks.
937   Furthermore, in the main text, our experiments have demonstrated that the implementation of the
938   LISTA algorithm Gregor & LeCun (2010) within SC-VAE leads to out-of-memory issues when a
939   large codebook size is employed.

941   C     C ONVERGENCE BEHAVIOR OF SVQ AND TRADITIONAL VQ
942
943   SVQ and traditional VQ are based on sparse regression and clustering, respectively. Since SVQ is
944   fully differentiable, their convergence behaviors can be considered analogous to Backpropagation
945   (BP) and K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN), respectively. We prove that BP’s optimization is smoother
946   than KNN’s due to continuous gradient descent.
947   Backpropagation. The decision function is directly related to the weights and activation functions,
948   which tend to evolve smoothly under gradient descent.
949
950                  f (x) = σ(Wn (σ(Wn−1 (. . . σ(W1 x + b1 ) . . .) + bn−1 )) + bn ),                 (11)
951   with the gradient updates affecting Wi and bi :
952
953                                        Wi ← Wi − η∇Wi J,                                            (12)
954
955                                          bi ← bi − η∇bi J.                                          (13)
956
957   The gradient ∇L(W) is typically smooth and continuous if the loss function L and the activation
958   functions are smooth. Hence, the weights update in a relatively smooth manner, and the path to the
959   minimum of the loss function is traversed in small, continuous steps.
960   K-Nearest Neighbors. Being a non-parametric method, KNN directly relies on the training data to
961   make its predictions. For a new input x, the prediction y is made based on the majority vote among
962   its k nearest neighbors:
963

                                                        i=1
967

      where δ is the Kronecker delta function.
969
970   The decision boundary of KNN is piecewise linear and can change abruptly with small changes in
971   the input data. Consider the case where the input x moves slightly from one side of the decision
      boundary to the other:

979    We can conclude that the optimization process for backpropagation is smoother as compared to KNN

1298
       We investigated the impact of SVQ on the sparsity of regression weights in Figure 7. To further
1299
       investigate its effect on the latent representation, we compared the distribution of batch tensors before
1300   and after applying SVQ. The tensors were transformed into normalized vectors, and their density
1301   distributions were estimated. As depicted in Figure 10, the representation after SVQ demonstrates a
1302   more compact distribution, indicating improved robustness to noise. These results further prove that
1303   SVQ can enhance forecasting performance by effectively handling noise in the data.
1304
       Figures 11, 12, 14, 15, and 13 present the comparison of latent feature maps before and after applying
1305
       SVQ. These figures illustrate that the difference between foreground and background in the feature
1306   maps increases after SVQ. For example, in the KittiCaltech dataset, a clear distinction is observed
1307   between road conditions and sky (Figure 11). Similarly, in the WeatherBench-S temperature dataset,
1308   distinctive regions are identified between high and low latitudes (Figure 12). These findings suggest
1309   that SVQ helps in enhancing the discriminative power of the latent representations, which in turn
1310   contributes to improved downstream forecasting performance.
