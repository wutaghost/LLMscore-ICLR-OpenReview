009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            When finetuning multiple tasks altogether, it is important to carefully weigh them
012            to get a good performance, but searching for good weights can be difficult and
013            costly. Here, we propose to aid the search with fast previews to quickly get a rough
014            idea of different reweighting options. We use model merging to create previews by
015            simply reusing and averaging parameters of models trained on each task separately
016
               (no retraining required). To improve the quality of previews, we propose a Bayesian
               approach to design new merging strategies by using more flexible posteriors. We
017
               validate our findings on vision and natural-language transformers. Our work shows
018
               the benefits of model merging via Bayes to improve multitask finetuning.
019

021   1    I NTRODUCTION
022
023   Multitask finetuning has recently gained popularity due to the success of large pretrained models, but
024   a careful weighting of tasks is crucial to get good performances (Liu et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024;
025   Chung et al., 2024). Similarly to the traditional multi-task learning (Caruana, 1997; Ruder, 2017), the
026   weighting is useful in tackling data imbalance, task interference, negative transfer, and also effects of
027
      variable task difficulty (Raffel et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2023). When left unresolved, these can lead to
      issues, for instance, regarding safety (Jan et al., 2024). Weighting is also useful for pre-finetuning
028
      recently used for multi-lingual transfer and continual pretraining (Aghajanyan et al., 2021; Gemma
029
      Team, 2024a; Martins et al., 2024; Fujii et al., 2024).
030
031   Despite its importance, little has been done to address task weighting for multitask finetuning. For
032   large models, an exhaustive search over weights is out of the question, but even if we could try a few
033
      weighting configurations, which ones should we try? There is no guide for that. Weights are often
      chosen arbitrarily and sometimes heuristically but these are not sufficient; see, for example, Liu et al.
034
      (2023, Sec. 6). The weighting methods used for deep learning and pretraining can be adapted to
035
      search for good weights (Ren et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2018; Raffel et al., 2020; Groenendijk et al.,
036   2021; Du et al., 2022; Yan et al., 2022; Xie et al., 2023; Thakkar et al., 2023), but a quick guidance
037   on reasonable search areas will still be useful to assist the search.
038
039
      In this paper, we propose to aid the search with fast previews of performances, estimated to obtain
      weights that improve accuracy of multitask finetuning. We use model merging to create the previews
040
      where we train and store models on each task separately and reuse them later to create previews by
041
      simply averaging the model parameter for a wide-range of weights (Fig. 1). Our main contribution is
042   a Bayesian approach to design new merging strategies that yield better previews over a wider range
043   of weights. This differs fundamentally from previous work which only focus on the best performing
044   weights (Don-Yehiya et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023; Feng et al., 2024; Stoica et al., 2024; Yang et al.,
045   2024). Such strategies do not always yield good-quality previews; see Fig. 2a for an illustration.
046
      We propose a Bayesian approach where more flexible posteriors yield better previews but with slightly
047
      higher costs (Figs. 2b and 2c). For instance, we show that Task Arithmetic (Ilharco et al., 2023) for
048   LLMs corresponds to isotropic Gaussian posteriors, while better (and slightly more costly) Hessian-
049   based methods (Daheim et al., 2024) employ more flexible Gaussian forms. We generalize this
050   result to generic exponential-family posteriors and present a recipe to derive new merging strategies.
051   We validate our method on several benchmarks on vision and natural-language transformers. For
052   example, we experiment with image classification using Vision Transformers (ViTs) (Dosovitskiy
053   et al., 2021) of 86M parameters and adding new languages to GEMMA with 2B parameters (Gemma
      Team, 2024b) for machine translation. Our results consistently show that more flexible posteriors

      2     W EIGHTED M ULTITASK F INETUNING

097
      Multitask finetuning aims to finetune on multiple tasks altogether. For example, given a Large
      Language Model (LLM) trained for English, we may want to finetune it on multiple languages (Muen-
098
      nighoff et al., 2023), for instance, German, French, Chinese, Japanese, etc. Denoting the loss of each
099
      task by ℓt (θ) for the model parameters θ, we want to finetune over a weighted loss
100

104   We denote the loss-weight vector by α = (α1 , α2 , . . . , αT ) and the finetuned parameters obtained
105   with the weighting by θα . We will generally assume that αt sum to 1 but this is not strictly required.
106   Such multitask finetuning has recently become important for LLM alignment and usability. For
107   example, it is used for improving instruction-following abilities (Chung et al., 2024; Ouyang et al.,
      2022), different kinds of safety tuning (Gemma Team, 2024a), combining coding tasks (Liu et al.,

108
      2023), and mixing coding and math skills into LLMs, which is useful even when they are designed
109   for other tasks like machine translation (Martins et al., 2024).
110
111   In practice, it is important to choose α carefully for reasons that are true for any multi-task learning
112
      problem (Caruana, 1997; Ruder, 2017). For instance, one issue is due to data-imbalance: different
      tasks may contain different types of information and some of higher quality than others. There is
113
      also task interference, for example, a model that does math well, may not necessarily be the best at
114
      languages. Additionally, learning some tasks might hurt the performances in the other tasks, and
115   then there is variability in task difficulty: some tasks are harder to learn and we do not want those to
116   impact the tasks that are relatively easier to learn.
117
118
      The effects of these issues are often felt in practice. For example, adding too much safety data
      can make the model more conservative and reduce its usefulness (Bianchi et al., 2024); too much
119
      instruction finetuning can undo safety alignment and open new vulnerabilities (Qi et al., 2024; Jan
120
      et al., 2024). Such problems can be avoided by careful task weighting. Weighting is also useful
121   during pre-finetuning where we try to balance multiple tasks differently during the last, say, 10% of
122   pretraining (Aghajanyan et al., 2021; Gemma Team, 2024a; Martins et al., 2024; Fujii et al., 2024).
123
124
      Despite its importance, not much work has been done to find good ways to set the weights. Decades
      of work exist for multitask learning but multitask finetuning is a relatively new area and is still
125
      under-explored. Similarly to multi-task learning, an exhaustive search over the whole α-space is not
126
      feasible when T is large. With little guidance, arbitrary values are tried to get an idea, for example,
127   Fujii et al. (2024) try only two values of α for continual pretraining. Sometimes heuristics are
128   used and meta-learning approaches are also adopted, but the results are not always satisfactory, for
129   example, see Liu et al. (2023, Sec. 6) who report such a result for LLMs trained for code generation.
130
      Our goal in this paper is to provide a fast (and cheap) method to assist the search of good α
131
      values. Such a guiding tool is useful to restrict the search to a few values and also to warm-start
132
      the optimization process. For this, we need a fast but accurate approach to quickly estimate the
133   performance of θα for a wide-range of α values. Model merging is a useful tool for this, but the
134   choice of merging strategy matters to get a high quality preview. We show that simple merging
135   methods are not satisfactory because they can be quite inaccurate and may only yield good estimates
136   for a small region in the α space (see Fig. 2a). Our main technical contribution is to address this
137   with a Bayesian approach to expand the region by designing a more accurate merging strategy. The
138   quality is improved at the expense of cost but the approach still remains fast enough to be employed
139   in practice. Existing methods on model merging in this space only focus on the best performing
140   weights (Don-Yehiya et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023; Feng et al., 2024; Stoica et al., 2024; Yang et al.,
141
      2024). Our work instead aims to design merging strategies that work for a wide range of α values.

304   3.5   P RACTICAL A LGORITHMS FOR FAST M ULTITASK F INETUNING P REVIEWS

306   Our methods to generate previews follow the 3-step procedure described in Sec. 3. Below we list
307   three versions where different algorithms are used to generate qt (θ).
308
            1. A DAM W-SG: We train each task using AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019) to get θ t and
309
               use it to build the Laplace posterior qt (θ) = N (θ | θ t , H−1
                                                                            t ). The Hessian is fixed to a
310            diagonal matrix with the diagonal set to squared gradients, which are computed by one extra
311            pass through the data. Previews are then computed using Eq. 10 for all α.
312
            2. IVON-H ESS: We train each task using the variational learning method IVON (Shen et al.,
313
               2024) which yields a Gaussian with diagonal covariance, similarly to AdamW-SG. The
314            advantage here is that no additional pass through the data is required to compute the diagonal
315            Hessian. Again, previews are computed by using Eq. 10.
316
            3. M ULTI IVON-H ESS. For each task, we train using multiple runs of IVON and use them
317
               to construct a mixture-of-Gaussians posterior. The cost is K times the cost of IVON-Hess
318            where K is the number of IVON runs. Previews are obtained using Alg. 1.
319
320   We also show results for using full Hessians on small-scale experiments in Fig. 2 and App. C.1;
321   details are given in App. C.1 and Appendices B.1 and B.2. For MultiIVON-Hess, we only run 5-10
322   iterations of Alg. 1 with K set in the range of 10 to 30 components. The method is still practical but,

515   5               C ONCLUSION
516
517   Multitask finetuning is a crucial ingredient in many neural network training recipes but good weight-
518   ings between tasks are hard and expensive to find. Here, we propose to aid the search for such
519   weightings with previews obtained from model merging, where single task models can be reused
520   for many weight combinations. We show that model merging strategies can be derived using a
521
      Bayesian framework by defining suitable surrogate losses to the multitask objective for exponential-
      family-based distributions. We use this to outline various preview and merging strategies, including a
522
      new mixture-based algorithm for improved model merging. Along various experiments including
523
      image classification with Vision Transformers and machine translation with LLMs we show that
524   model merging can effectively be used to preview multitask finetuning weightings. Flexible model
525   merging can improve the preview quality, but also increase the cost. For example, mixture posteriors
526   sometimes need too many mixture component to get improvements. This is not ideal for large model
527   where storing many models is not possible. We hope to address this limitation in the future.

       C.2   C OMPARISON OF H ESSIAN A PPROXIMATIONS FOR LLM M ERGING
1052   Fig. 10 shows a comparison of using the squared gradient approximation of the (diagonal) Fisher
1053   and the diagonal Hessian approximation obtained with IVON for Hessian-Weighted merging. Both
1054   methods are comparable and provide good previews for multitask finetuning. However, the Hessian
