009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Large-scale vision-language models have achieved remarkable performance on
012            various downstream tasks. Nevertheless, how to efficiently adapt vision-language
013            models to new data distributions without re-training, i.e., domain incremental
014            learning (DIL) of vision-language models, is still under-explored. Existing DIL
015            methods for single modality are either not applicable to multi-modal settings
016            or need exemplar buffers to store previous samples to avoid catastrophic forget-
017            ting, which is not memory-efficient. To address these limitations, we propose
018
               an exemplar-free paradigm to improve DIL of vision-language models based on
               prompt-tuning. We theoretically analyze and decompose the problem into two
019
               optimization objectives. Guided by the theoretical insights, we propose a novel
020
               framework named Multimodal Continual Domain Adaptation (MCDA), which
021            incorporates two strategies: Multimodal Domain Alignment (MDA) and Maximum
022            Softmax Gating (MSG). MDA enhances cross-domain performance by aligning
023            visual and language representation spaces, while MSG improves the accuracy of
024            domain identification by gating through Softmax probability. Extensivev experi-
025            mental results demonstrate that our method outperforms current state-of-the-art
026            approaches.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Large vision-language models have achieved remarkable performance on various downstream
032   tasks (Zhang et al., 2022; Adel et al., 2019; Agarwal et al., 2022; Arani et al., 2021). After large-scale
033
      pretraining, these powerful models can make zero-shot predictions without requiring any task-specific
      training examples. This advantage of large vision-language models is promising for the development
034
      of more general-purpose models without further tuning. However, the zero-shot performance on
035
      certain tasks suffers from a lack of sufficient relevant image-text pairs in the pre-training corpus. For
036   example, images containing domain shift (Aygün et al., 2022; Bhat et al., 2023; Cao et al., 2021)
037   from one domain (e.g.sketch) to another (e.g.cartoon) with certain textural descriptions are difficult
038   to collect, even though it’s crucial for models’ open-domain learning adaptability.
039
      Unfortunately, expanding the knowledge of the vision-language model through re-training from
040
      scratch would incur prohibitively high computational costs. One effective way to alleviate the issue
041
      is to continually fine-tune or prompt-tune the vision-language model on various domains of data,
042   which is known as domain incremental learning (DIL) (Ardywibowo et al., 2022; Benzing, 2022;
043   Boschini et al., 2022b;a;c). In the DIL setting, the set of classes remains constant, but the domains
044   (data distributions) involved commonly vary a lot in sequence while domain indices are not provided
045   at inference time. The continually learned model can handle any image-text input and can be further
046   used for downstream tasks. Nevertheless, domain incremental learning remains challenging for
047   vision-language models. We find that when learning is performed sequentially on multiple domains,
048   pre-trained vision-language models tend to forget most of the knowledge related to previously learned
049   tasks (a task refers to learning on one domain). The phenomenon is commonly known as catastrophic
050
      forgetting.
051   Despite considerable efforts to apply DIL in single-modality settings, these methods either do not
052   apply to multi-modal settings or require exemplar buffers to store previous samples to prevent
053   catastrophic forgetting, which is not memory-efficient. Different from traditional methods that modify
      all or a subset of the network parameters or store examples in a buffer, a new paradigm arises for

054
      continual learning by optimizing a limited number of learnable prompts. As a pioneer working under
055   such a paradigm, S-prompt (Wang et al., 2022a) treats the learning of the prompts independently,
056   which leads to the best performance per domain. It replaces the use of expensive buffers by optimizing
057   per-domain prompts. During training time, they calculate centroids for each domain by applying
058   k-means on the training image features, which are generated with the fixed pre-trained transformer
059   without any prompts. During inference, KNN is used to identify the nearest centroid to the test
060   image and then add the associated domain prompt to the image tokens for classification. Despite the
061   empirical performance gains observed by S-prompting, there is still a gap between empirical success
062   and theoretical analysis.
063   In this paper, we theoretically analyze the paradigm of S-prompting and argue that the design of its
064   component is sub-optimal. The theoretical analysis also shows that its performance is largely limited
065   by wrong-domain prediction and out-of-domain prediction. Motivated by the limitation, we propose
066   a novel framework called Multimodal Continual Domain Adaptation (MCDA) with two strategies:
067   Multimodal Domain Alignment (MDA) and Maximum Softmax Gating (MSG) to tackle the issue.
068   MSG transforms the problem of domain selection into out-of-domain detection. MDA alleviates the
069
      problem of catastrophic forgetting of the vision-language model by forcing the alignment matrix to
      be similar to that of the previous domain.
070
071   Extensive experimental results shows that our approach outperforms state-of-the-art methods. Specif-
072   ically, MCDA outperforms existing advanced methods of L2P and S-liPrompts with the highest
073   average accuracy score of 89.17% and the lowest average forgetting score of -0.17%. This suggests
074   that MCDA is more adept at learning new information without significantly forgetting previously
075
      learned knowledge.
076   In summary, our contributions are as follows:
077
078          • From a theoretical perspective, we analyze the boundedness of a continual learning process
079            of vision-language models, and a clear theorem is presented in Theorem 1.
080          • From a framework perspective, we propose the MCDA as a novel framework using both
081            vision and language adaptation for enhancing continual learning of vision-language models.
082
083          • From an experimental perspective, our proposed MCDA archives new state-of-the-art
084
               performances on the CDDB-Hard, DomainNet and CORe50 datasets.

      Continual learning refers to learning scenarios that require models to adapt to a sequence of
089
      tasks with varying data distributions. One of the major challenges of continual learning is known
090   as catastrophic forgetting, where models tend to forget most of the knowledge they previously
091   learned after adapting to new data. To tackle catastrophic forgetting, numerous methods have been
092   proposed. Regularization-based methods (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017; Zenke et al., 2017; Aljundi et al.,
093   2018; Chaudhry et al., 2018; Zenke et al., 2017) alleviate catastrophic forgetting by adding explicit
094   regularization terms to balance the old and new tasks. Replay-based methods(Vitter, 1985; Chaudhry
095   et al., 2019; Riemer et al., 2018; Borsos et al., 2020; Caccia et al., 2020) try to approximate and
096   recover previous data distributions. Optimization-based methods(Lopez-Paz & Ranzato, 2017; Zeng
097   et al., 2019; Guo et al., 2022; Kong et al., 2022; Liu & Liu, 2021) focus on designing specific
098
      optimization procedures and programs. Architecture-based methods(Xue et al., 2022; Serra et al.,
      2018; Golkar et al., 2019; Jung et al., 2020; Gurbuz & Dovrolis, 2022) focus on constructing
099
      task-specific parameters.
100
101   Domain-incremental learning is one of the most commonly seen scenarios of continual learning. In
102   the setting of DIL, each task (domain) has the same data label space but different distributions. Task
103   (domain) identities are not available at inference time. DIL is involved in many real-world problems
104
      such as autonomous driving, where the vehicle meets varying weather conditions in the wild (Mirza
      et al., 2022).
105
106   Prompt tuning methods are different from traditional methods that modify all or a subset of the
107   network parameters or store examples in a buffer, L2P (Wang et al., 2022d) begins a new paradigm
      for continual learning by optimizing a limited number of learnable prompts. After that, several work

197   3.3   M ULTIMODAL D OMAIN A LIGNMENT
198
      At training time, an independent set of prompts is trained for each domain and is frozen when training
199
      on subsequent domain data. At inference time, the sample needs to identify which domain it comes
200
      and select the corresponding set of prompts for that domain. If the sample successfully identify the
201   domain it belongs to, then forgetting will not happen. However, if the sample fail to identify the
202   correct domain, then it will select a wrong set of prompts. Therefore, the sample will be tested on
203   a model trained on domain data that are different from its original domain. In other words, if the
204   domain identification process goes wrong, then the model will be tested on out-of-distribution (OOD)
205   data. Intuitively, the performance is expected to drop when a model is tested with OOD data. To
206   validate the statement, we use models prompt-tuned with different domain sources to test on data
207   from different domains and the results are in Figure 2 (a).

      3.4      M AXIMUM S OFTMAX G ATING

      At inference time, the test sample needs to identify which domain it comes from and then select the
264   corresponding set of domain-specific prompts. If the sample fails to identify the correct domains
265   it belongs to, then as discussed above, a performance drop will happen. Therefore, improving
266   the domain identification accuracy is another key factor in improving DIL performance under the
267   proposed paradigm. Previous work, such as S-prompt, takes a naive approach by storing prototype
268   centroids at training time and calculating the similarity of testing sample and prototype centroids to
269   select domain at inference time. We argue that this is sub-optimal and that the problem of domain
      identification can be transformed into the problem of OOD detection.

476   5    D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
477
478   In this paper, we theoretically analyze and decompose the problem of DIL into two optimization
479   objectives. Guided by the theoretical insights, we propose two strategies: Multimodal Domain
480   Alignment (MDA) and Maximum Softmax Gating (MSG). MDA improves the model’s cross-domain
481   performance by forcing the alignment between visual and language representation spaces. MSG
482
      improves the accuracy of domain identification by gating through softmax probability. Experiments
      show that our method outperforms existing state-of-the-art methods.
483
484   The limitation of the proposed MCDA is mainly in two aspects. On the one hand, MSG could still be
485   improved to enhance the domain selection accuracy. On the other hand, our method is restricted for
      the setting of DIL. Making MCDA adaptable for all CL setting remains to be explored.
