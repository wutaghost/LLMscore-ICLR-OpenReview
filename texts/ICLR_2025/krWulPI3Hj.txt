009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent advances in Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) have deployed
012            fully-connected multi-layer deep learning architectures to solve partial differen-
013            tial equations (PDEs). Such architectures, however, struggle to reduce prediction
014            error below O(10−5 ), even with substantial network sizes and prolonged train-
015            ing periods. Methods that reduce error further, to O(10−7 ), generally come with
016            high computational costs. This paper introduces a Single-layered Architecture
017            with Feature Engineering (SAFE-NET) that reduces the error by orders of mag-
018
               nitude using far fewer parameters, challenging the prevailing belief that modern
               PINNs are effectively learning features in these scientific applications. Our strat-
019
               egy is to return to basic ideas in machine learning: with random Fourier features,
020
               a simplified single-layer network architecture, and an effective optimizer we call
021            (Adam + L-BFGS)2 , SAFE-NET accelerates training and improves accuracy by
022            reducing the number of parameters and improving the conditioning of the PINN
023            optimization problem. Our numerical results reveal that SAFE-NET converges
024            faster, matches or generally exceeds the performance of more complex optimiz-
025            ers and multi-layer networks, improves problem conditioning throughout training,
026            and works robustly across many problem settings. On average, SAFE-NET uses
027            an order of magnitude fewer parameters than a conventional PINN setup to reach
028            comparable results in 20% as many epochs, each of which is 50% faster. Our find-
029
               ings suggest that conventional deep learning architectures for PINNs waste their
               representational capacity, failing to learn simple but effective features.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Partial Differential Equations (PDEs) form the bedrock of numerous scientific and engineering
035
      fields, but their solution poses fundamental difficulties. Very few PDEs possess analytic solutions,
036   forcing scientists and engineers to rely on numerical methods, which become prohibitively expensive
037   for non-linear and high-dimensional problems. In the past several years, approaches based on ma-
038   chine learning have been developed that have the potential to ameliorate this problem. Amongst the
039   many approaches, one of the most popular is the physics-informed neural network (PINN) (Raissi
040   et al., 2019; Karniadakis et al., 2021; Cuomo et al., 2022), which has the potential to solve both for-
041   ward and inverse problems involving PDEs. PINNs parameterize the solution to a PDE with a neural
042   network, and are often fit by minimizing a least-squares loss involving the PDE residual, boundary
043   condition(s), and initial condition(s). As PINNs do not require a mesh, they have the potential to
044
      efficiently solve high-dimensional and non-linear problems that require massive computing power
      to solve with classical numerical methods.
045
046   Unfortunately, recent works Krishnapriyan et al. (2021); Rathore et al. (2024) have shown that
047   PINNs are extremely difficult to train. The main source of the diffiuclty being the ill-conditioning
048   induced by the differential operator appearing in the PINNs loss. The ill-conditioning makes it very
049   difficult for popular optimizers such as Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2014) and SGD (Bottou, 2010) to
050
      make the loss small enough so as to yield a useful model.
051   To address this issue, Rathore et al. (2024) proposed a custom optimizer, NysNewton-CG (NNCG),
052   which, when combined with Adam and L-BFGS, significantly improves the optimization of the
053   PINNs loss function. This combination achieved error reductions of one to two orders of magnitude
      compared to using Adam and L-BFGS alone.

054
      Despite its effectiveness, NNCG has two significant shortcomings: (1) it is computationally expen-
055   sive and (2) it is difficult to tune. For the wave equation, Rathore et al. (2024) reports that a step of
056   NNCG is over 10× then a step of L-BFGS. It is partly because of this that they recommend making
057   as much progress as possible with Adam and L-BFGS before switching to NNCG. NNCG also has
058   multiple hyperparameters, that are not easily set apriori, which means an expensive grid-search may
059   be required.
060
      In this paper, we ask whether or not a simpler more computationally friendly solution is possible to
061   address the challenges in training PINNs? We argue that the primary issue facing PINNs is that the
062   input representation is inadequate for the network to adequately learn the solution. We show that
063   by incorporating an informative set of features that better capture the inductive bias of the PDE, we
064   can exceed the performance of more complex optimizers and multi-layer networks. In particular,
065   our proposed method SAFE-NET, combines two-layer neural network with feature augmentation to
066   yield a method that is not only faster and computationally less expensive but also more generalizable
067   to a wider range of more complex problems.

089   of experiments aimed at optimizing the PINN loss function for a range of PDE problems including

098   BFGS)2 setups involve tuning the Adam learning rate as previously mentioned, with adjustments in

134
135   Here we review common approaches for PINN training and feature engineering strategies proposed
136   in the literature.
137
138   3.1   C HALLENGES IN LEARNING WITH PINN S
139
140   In order to learn effectively with a PINN model, it is crucial that the model be trained till it reachs
141
      a low loss — indeed, existing generalization bounds Mishra & Molinaro (2023) show the quality of
      the recovered solution increases as the training loss goes down. Unfortunately, training the vanilla
142
      PINN architecture is known to be extremely challenging Krishnapriyan et al. (2021); Rathore et al.
143
      (2024). On a fundamental level, this issue directly arises from of the presence of the differential
144   operator in the loss. The differential operator can be highly ill-conditioned De Ryck et al. (2023),
145   which leads to an ill-conditioned optimization landscape and slow convergence Rathore et al. (2024).
146
147
      In order to address these challenges, a variety of modifications to the vanilla PINN have been pro-
      posed within the literature, many of which attempt to make the optimization problem easier to solve.
148
      Wang et al. (2021a; 2022a;b); Nabian et al. (2021); Wu et al. (2023a;b) propose loss reweight-
149
      ing/resampling to balance different components of the loss, Yao et al. (2023); Müller & Zeinhofer
150   (2023) propose scale-invariant and natural gradient-based optimizers for PINN training, Jagtap et al.
151   (2020a;b); Wang et al. (2023) propose adaptive activation functions which can accelerate conver-
152   gence of the optimizer, and Liu et al. (2024) propose an approach to precondition the PINN loss
153   itself. Other approaches include bespoke loss functions and regularizations E & Yu (2018); Lu et al.
154   (2021); Kharazmi et al. (2021); Khodayi-Mehr & Zavlanos (2020); Yu et al. (2022) and new archi-
155   tectures Jagtap et al. (2020c); Jagtap & Karniadakis (2020); Li et al. (2020); Moseley et al. (2023).
156   These strategies work with varying degrees of success, and no single strategy improves performance
157   across all PDEs.
158   SAFE-NET differs from much of this prior work, in that it does not seek to directly change the opti-
159   mization landscape. Instead, it seeks to better capture the inductive bias of the PDE, by augmenting
160   the initial data representation with relevant features. However, we shall see that the better choice of
161   representation yields can improve conditioning which leads to a better optimization landscape and
      fast training. Thus, SAFE-NET implicitly preconditions the problem with its feature engineering.

      3.2     F EATURE ENGINEERING IN PINNS
163
164   We are not the first work to consider feature engineering PINNs. Several works have considered
165   feature engineering in the context of spectral bias. Spectral bias refers to the inability of neural
166   networks to learn high-frequency functions. Wang et al. (2021b) applies random Fourier features to
167   enhance the network’s ability to learn high-frequency functions more effectively, using their Fourier
168   feature mapping. They use the feature mapping
169                                       γ(v) = [cos(Bv), sin(Bv)]
170
      where v denotes the input coordinates (space, time, etc.) and B ∈ Rm×d is a matrix with elements
171
      from a Gaussian distribution N (0, σ 2 ). By using both cos and sin components in their mapping, they
172   try to have the network capture periodic, high-frequency behavior in the input data. This embedding
173   is much less general then SAFE-NET, and they only explore the case when B is normally distributed.
174
175
      Another recent approach is the RBF-PINN (Burghardt et al.; Zeng et al., 2024). In the RBF-PINN
      method, the authors propose using Radial Basis Functions (RBF) instead of Fourier features. They
176
      use the RBF function:
177                                                       |x − c|2

178                                    ϕRBF (x) = exp −              ,
                                                            2σ 2
179
      where c is the center of the RBF, and σ controls the width of the RBF. They argue that the RBF
180
      kernel can better handle problems with sharp changes or discontinuities, which Fourier features
181   sometimes struggle with due to the Gibbs phenomenon. The restriction to RBF kernels is less
182   general than SAFE-NET, and maybe more computationally intensive due to the need to perform
183   kernel regression.
184
      Moseley et al. (2023) proposed FBPINN, which models the solution as a sum of basis functions
185
      with neural network coefficients. Again this technique was developed to address spectral bias and
186
      the deficiencies of PINNs in capturing multiscale solutions. This approach can be viewed as a feature
187   expansion of the solution, with the basis functions corresponding to features. Judicious choice of
188   the basis functions can be viewed as feature engineering. This approach differs significantly from
189   SAFE-NET, which engineers the input-features to the network to learn better solutions.
190
      In total, most of the literature on feature engineering in PINNS has been focused on addressing
191
      spectral bias. Unlike SAFE-NET These works do not systematically investigate whether or not
192
      augmenting PINNS with physically relevant input features leads to significantly better performance.

      4.2     L OGARITHMIC AND P OLYNOMIAL F EATURES
236
237   Our experiments show that, in addition to the previously discussed Fourier features, adding polyno-
238   mial and logarithmic features to certain problems can improve loss function minimization, enhanc-
239   ing the error’s order of magnitude. A key strength of logarithmic features is the identity:
240                               log(ωx) = log(ω) + log(x) for any ω ∈ R
241
242   This property implies that adding log(ωx) as a feature in the network is effectively the same as
243   adding log(x) plus a constant term log(ω). Since adding a constant has no impact on the network’s
244
      performance, it suffices to include only log(x) and log(t) as features. In certain problems, these
      logarithmic features could make a substantial difference.
245
246   We also found that incorporating polynomial features could be beneficial in specific scenarios. For
247   our experiments, we added x2 , xt, and t2 , as polynomials of higher degrees do not seem to contribute
248   to further noticeable improvements in loss function minimization in our experiments.
