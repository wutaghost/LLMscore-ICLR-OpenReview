009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Selecting an optimal learning rate (LR) is crucial for training deep neural net-
012            works, significantly affecting both convergence speed and final model perfor-
013            mance. Determining this optimal LR typically involves two key challenges:
014            choosing an appropriate initial LR and selecting an LR scheduler for adjusting
015
               the LR during training. This paper focuses on the former challenge—selecting
               the initial LR. Traditionally, this task relies on manual tuning or heuristic meth-
016
               ods, often involving extensive trial-and-error or computationally expensive search
017
               strategies like grid search or random search. We propose an algorithm, Automatic
018            Learning Rate Selection (ALRS), to find the initial LR without the need for man-
019            ual intervention. ALRS leverages the gradient of the LR itself — a less explored
020            approach in the field. ALRS is a computationally lightweight pre-training process
021            that automatically selects the initial LR by iterative refinements using the LR gra-
022            dient, specifically analyzing its sign information, combined with suitable search
023            algorithms. This approach efficiently converges to the optimal LR in a stable and
024            robust manner across various optimizers and network architectures.
025            We evaluate our technique on standard deep learning benchmarks, including
026            MNIST with a CNN and CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 with ResNet-18, using both
027            SGD and Adam optimizers. Our experiments demonstrate that the automatically
028
               determined LRs achieve performance comparable to manually tuned LRs and
               state-of-the-art results.

031   1     I NTRODUCTION
032
033   In recent years, deep learning has revolutionized numerous fields, from image recognition and natu-
034   ral language processing to game playing and scientific discovery. At the heart of these advancements
035   lies the optimization process, where the objective is to minimize a loss function through iterative up-
036   dates of the model’s parameters. This optimization process is driven by optimizers, such as Stochas-
037   tic Gradient Descent (SGD) and Adaptive Moment Estimation (ADAM) (Ruder, 2016), which play
038
      a crucial role in determining the direction and magnitude of parameter updates.
039   One of the most critical hyperparameters in this optimization process is the learning rate (LR) (Ben-
040   gio, 2012). The LR controls the step size taken in the direction of the gradient, significantly influ-
041   encing the convergence speed and stability of the training process. A LR that is too high can cause
042   the model to oscillate or even diverge, while a LR that is too low can lead to slow convergence
043   and suboptimal solutions (Yang & Shami, 2020). Therefore, selecting an appropriate initial LR is
044
      essential for efficient and effective training of neural networks. In addition to choosing an initial
      LR, one often needs to choose a scheduler that adapts the LR during the training process. Choosing
045
      a scheduler can be thought of as a separate problem; the sole focus of this paper is the problem of
046
      choosing the initial LR.
047
048
      1.1   C HALLENGES IN LR S ELECTION
049
050   Traditionally, the selection of the LR has essentially been a manual process, often involving exten-
051   sive trial and error. Researchers and practitioners typically perform grid search or random search
052   over a predefined set of possible values, followed by fine-tuning based on empirical observations
053   (Bischl et al., 2023; Yang & Shami, 2020). This process is time-consuming and computationally ex-
      pensive. Additionally, the optimal LR can vary significantly across different datasets, architectures,


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      and even different stages of training, further complicating the selection process (Nar & Sastry, 2018;
055   Jastrz˛ebski et al., 2017).
056
057   Techniques such as LR schedulers (e.g., step decay, exponential decay) adjust the LR during training
058
      based on predefined rules or gradients of the parameters (Johnson et al., 2024). While these methods
      offer improvements over fixed LRs, they still depend on initial LR settings, thereby making the
059
      choice of initial LR crucial. The dependence of this initial LR on the model, dataset and optimizer
060
      makes the choice of initial LR challenging. The challenge that we aim to address in this paper is
061   finding the initial LR in a computationally lightweight manner.
062
063
      1.2   O UR C ONTRIBUTIONS
064
065   In this paper, we propose an algorithm called Automatic LR Selection (ALRS) (see Section 3) to
066   automatically determine the optimal initial LR. The main ingredient of our approach is the gradient
067   of the LR itself. The use of LR gradient appears in (Almeida, 1998; Baydin et al., 2017). However,
068   we do not directly apply gradient descent to the LR, thus deviating from prior hypergradient descent
069   methods.
070   Specifically, our contributions include:
071
072         1. ALRS algorithm: We calculate the gradient of the loss function with respect to the LR.
073            Rather than following the hypergradient descent method, we propose an algorithm that pro-
074            duces increase and decrease signals depending essentially on the signs of these gradients.
075            The problem of adjusting the LR based on these signs calls for separate search algorithms.
076
               We propose an implementation of one such search algorithm (see Section 3). ALRS is a
               pre-training process with computational costs typically equal to that of one training epoch.
077
078         2. Empirical Validation: We validate our approach through experiments on three benchmark
079            datasets: MNIST with a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN), CIFAR-10 with ResNet-
080
               18, and CIFAR-100 with ResNet-18. Our method demonstrates robust performance across
               different combinations of optimizers and models, achieving results at par with state-of-the-
081
               art performance.
082
083         3. Study of the dependence of ALRS on initial seeds: The ALRS depends on two seeds: a
084
               random seed and a starting LR seed from which the optimal LR is derived. We study the
               dependence of ALRS on these seeds and show that it is within acceptable limits.
085
086   By addressing the challenge of automatic initial LR selection, this work aims to contribute to ad-
087   vancements in finding hyperparameter-less training algorithms in the field of deep learning.
088

099   2.1   H YPERPARAMETER O PTIMIZATION
100
101   Hyperparameter Optimization (HPO) is a crucial aspect of machine learning that involves selecting
102   the best set of hyperparameters for a given model. Hyperparameters are parameters whose values
103   are set before the learning process begins, apart from LR some other hyperpatameters such as batch
104
      size, and the number of layers in a neural network are also optimized via HPO.
105   Several methods exist for HPO, including Grid Search, a brute-force approach that exhaustively
106   searches through a predefined set of hyperparameters. In this approach, an exhaustive search through
107   all possible combinations can be very time-consuming and computationally expensive. Random
      Search randomly samples hyperparameters from a specified distribution (Bergstra & Bengio, 2012).


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      This approach may require a large number of iterations to find optimal hyperparameters, as it does
109   not leverage any information from previous evaluations. Also, it does not systematically explore the
110   hyperparameter space, potentially missing optimal regions. Bayesian Optimization (Wu et al., 2019;
111   Victoria & Maragatham, 2021) uses probabilistic models to predict the performance of hyperparam-
112   eters and iteratively refines the search space. This probabilistic model of the objective function can
113   be complex to implement and tune. Hypergradient descent (Baydin et al., 2017; Almeida, 1998;
114   Donini et al., 2019; Franceschi et al., 2017), which deals with continuous hyperparameters like LR,
115   is closest to our approach; we discuss the similarities and differences in the following subsection.
116
117   2.2   H YPERGRADIENT D ESCENT V S ALRS
118
119
      Hypergradient descent (HD) uses the gradient method to fine tune the LR. The similarity between
      ALRS and HD is that they both use the gradient of the LR. However, the following are the key
120
      differences:
121
122         1. Unlike HD, ALRS does not use the gradient descent method to fine-tune the LR. Instead, a
123            separate algorithm that essentially uses signs of this gradient across batches is used.
124         2. Unlike HD, ALRS is a pre-training algorithm to find the optimal initial LR, after which its
125            role is over. Hence, we expect ALRS to be free from overfitting issues seen in the case of
126            HD (Jin et al., 2021).
127
            3. HD appears to be sensitive to newly introduced parameters in HD viz. Hypergradient LR
128            and also the initial starting LR. ALRS is not sensitive to newly introduced parameters like
129            LR seed, see Section 5.
130

      3     ALRS
133
134
      The ALRS algorithm consists of two separate sub-algorithms. The goal of the first sub-algorithm
      is to point the direction in which the current LR should be adjusted to approach an optimal value,
135
      i.e., to come up with increase and decrease signals. The signs of the LR gradient, as well as the
136
      total magnitude across a set number of batches, are used to come up with these signals. For ease
137   of illustration, we think of the second sub-algorithm as being implemented by an instance of a
138   ParameterSearch class. This class is initialized with a starting or a seed value of a parameter (in
139   our case, LR) and responds to the increase and decrease signals via increase and decrease methods,
140   which adjust the parameter value by magnitude depending on the class implementation. The goal of
141   this ParameterSearch class is to converge to an optimal parameter value. So, the class also provides
142   a method (called done) that detects convergence within a specified tolerance.
143   We now explain the ALRS algorithm, which takes an instance of the parameter class as an input.
144   In the subsequent subsection, we outline a specific implementation of the ParameterSearch class,
145   which we call SBF search.
146
147   3.1   ALRS ALGORITHM
148
149   While the precise algorithm is given in Algorithm 1, we briefly describe the main ideas used in
150   ALRS. It is important to note that ALRS being a pre-training process the weights of the model are
151   not modified throughout the algorithm. We use an instance of an optimizer denoted by OPT (like
152   Adam or SGD). For each training batch, we run the OPT.step() method which temporarily modifies
153
      the weights of the model. However, we store the old weights in a variable and not only restore the
      original weights but also save the difference in a dictionary, which is denoted by ADJUSTMENTS.
154
      This dictionary is initialized to zero in the beginning. In each training batch, instead of using the ac-
155
      tual weights of the model we use WEIGHT + γ· ADJUSTMENT, where γ is a variable initialized
156   to 1. This allows us to calculate the gradient of the loss function w.r.t. γ. Since the ADJUST-
157   MENTS are proportional to the LR for optimizers like Adam or SGD, this gradient is interpreted as
158   the gradient of the loss function w.r.t. LR. An increase signal is generated if 90% of the LR-gradients
159   for a specified number of steps are negative. In our case, this specified number of steps is total train-
160   ing batches divided by 50; the algorithm appears robust towards small changes in this value. The
161   reason for looking at multiple batches before generating a signal is that a single training batch may
      not be sufficiently representative of the data. If an increase signal is not generated, then by default

201   3.2   SBF S EARCH
202
203   ALRS requires an implementation of a ParameterSearch class (see Section 3). Recall that a Param-
204   eterSearch class is initialized with a starting seed value of the parameter and responds to increase
205   and decrease signals with the goal of converging to an optimal value of the parameter. The imple-
206
      mentations of interest in this paper utilize the concept of MOMENTUM, which is calculated as an
      exponential moving average of +1 (for increase signals) and -1 (for decrease signals). The role of
207
      MOMENTUM is to quantify the oscillatory behaviour of the signals.
208
209   SBF Search (SBFS) is a particular implementation of the ParameterSearch class that is used for run-
210   ning experiments in this paper. Other implementations are possible (see Section 6). SBFS combines
211   two separate implementations of ParameterSearch class, starting with Soft Binary Search (SBS) and
212
      switching later to Fractional Search (FS) for better convergence when the parameter value is within
      a certain range.
213
214   SBS is a modified binary search algorithm that uses soft limits instead of hard boundaries. These
215   limits are called soft limits because, after each increase signal, the upper limit is increased depending
      on the size of the MOMENTUM.


                                                            4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      FS is a search algorithm that adjusts the value by a fraction. The fraction depends on the MOMEN -
217   TUM , increasing it for consecutive moves in the same direction and decreasing it for oscillatory
218   moves.
219
220
      SBFS combines these two algorithms in the following manner:
221         1. Initialization: The algorithm initializes SBS with the initial parameter value.
222
            2. SBS Phase: SBS is used to narrow down the search space. The soft limits of SBS are
223
               iteratively adjusted on each increase or decrease signal using a scaling factor between 1.05
224            and 1.5 depending on magnitude of MOMENTUM.
225
            3. Switch to FS: When the difference between the upper and lower limit contracts to less than
226
               5% of the parameter value or after 100 signals, the algorithm switches to FS.
227
228
            4. FS Phase: FS is used to refine the search further, adjusting the parameter value by a small
               fraction, which is between 1% and 5% depending on the magnitude of the MOMENTUM.
229
230         5. Termination: We run FS for 20 iterations.

343   6     C ONCLUSION , L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE D IRECTIONS
344
345   6.1    L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE DIRECTIONS
346
347
      Despite the effectiveness of ALRS, certain limitations of this work highlight important areas for
      future research:
348
349
            1. Alternative ParameterSearch Methods: Implementing a ParameterSearch class based
350
               on reinforcement learning as an alternative to the SBFS (see 3) could potentially yield
351            better performance. This approach warrants further investigation to assess its viability and
352            effectiveness.
353
354
            2. The choice of 1/10: ALRS uses an LR that is one-tenth of the value obtained from the
               ParameterSearch class. While the optimal LR found directly from the ParameterSearch
355
               class can be considered ideal for training the next few batches, determining the optimal
356
               LR after every few batches is computationally expensive. Therefore, using a fraction of
357            the found LR becomes necessary. In this study, a fraction of 1/10 was used, which proved
358            effective across tested configurations. However, a larger fraction could be employed at the
359            expense of more frequent scheduling. The choice of this fraction is closely related to the
360            problem of LR scheduling.
361
            3. LR Scheduling: The issue of scheduling the LR throughout the entire training process
362            remains unaddressed. Applying similar ideas to automate LR adjustments over time could
363            lead to a fully automated training process, which is an important direction for future work.
364
365
            4. Testing on larger models : ALRS remains untested on language models, including large
               language models (LLMs). Evaluating its performance on such models is essential to un-
366
               derstand its scalability and applicability in more complex scenarios.
367
368
369
      6.2    C ONCLUSION
370
      Leveraging the LR gradient offers a promising solution for determining the optimal initial LR when
371
      training deep neural networks. Our experiments demonstrate that ALRS is effective in automating
372   this selection process. We note that, the LRs predicted by ALRS do not necessarily align with
373   standard values; in some cases, they are significantly larger or smaller. Despite these differences, the
374   training loss achieved using ALRS is comparable to that obtained with standard LRs, highlighting
375   the method’s success. This confirms that automating the initial LR selection through ALRS is both
376   feasible and beneficial, enhancing training efficiency and effectiveness. An important direction for
377   future work is to develop a suitable LR scheduling algorithm that can work in tandem with ALRS,
      further improving and fully automating the training process.
