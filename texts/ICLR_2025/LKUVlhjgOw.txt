009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            The physical world dynamics are generally governed by underlying partial deriva-
012            tive equations (PDEs) with unknown analytical forms in science and engineering
013            problems. Neural network based data-driven approaches have been heavily stud-
014            ied in simulating and solving PDE problems in recent years, but it is still chal-
015            lenging to move forward from understanding to controlling the unknown PDE
016            dynamics. PDE boundary control instantiates a simplified but important problem
017            by only focusing on PDE boundary conditions as the control input and output.
018
               However, current model-free PDE controllers cannot ensure the boundary output
               satisfies some given user-specified safety constraint. To this end, we propose a
019
               safety filtering framework to guarantee the boundary output stays within the safe
020
               set for current model-free controllers. Specifically, we first introduce a general
021            neural boundary control barrier function (BCBF) to ensure the feasibility of the
022            trajectory-wise constraint satisfaction of boundary output. Based on a neural op-
023            erator modeling the transfer function from boundary control input to output tra-
024            jectories, we show that the change in the BCBF depends linearly on the change
025            in input boundary, so quadratic programming-based safety filtering can be done
026            for pre-trained model-free controllers. Extensive experiments under challenging
027            hyperbolic, parabolic and Navier-Stokes PDE dynamics environments validate the
028            effectiveness of the proposed method in achieving better general performance and
029
               boundary constraint satisfaction compared to the model-free controller baselines.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Partial derivative equations (PDEs) characterize the most fundamental laws of the continuous dy-
034
      namical systems in the physical world Evans (1998); Perko (1996). Non-analytical PDE dynamics
035
      are often involved in complicated science and engineering problems of computational fluid dynam-
036   ics Kochkov et al. (2021), computational mechanics Samaniego et al. (2020), robotics Heiden et al.
037   (2021), etc. Recently, neural networks have largely boosted the study of numerical PDE solvers
038   using data-driven methods, simulating and characterizing the dynamics Raissi et al. (2019); Brunton
039   & Kutz (2024); Kovachki et al. (2023). However, the PDE control problem still remains challeng-
040   ing without any prior about underlying PDE equations, serving as a huge gap from understanding
041   science to solving engineering problems Yu & Wang (2024).
042   Recent pioneer works Bhan et al. (2024); Zhang et al. (2024a) provide various formulations of PDE
043   control problems and multiple benchmark settings, either in-domain control Zhang et al. (2024b) or
044   boundary control Bhan et al. (2023). Since it is easier to control the PDE boundary in the real world,
045   following Bhan et al. (2024), we focus on the PDE boundary control setting where the control signal
046   essentially serves as the boundary condition and the unknown PDE dynamics itself remains un-
047   changed. Model-based PDE boundary control has been studied for years, and backstepping-based
048   methods have been applied to different PDE dynamics Krstic & Smyshlyaev (2008b). Neverthe-
049   less, the model-based methods cannot work well under the unknown PDE dynamics, suffering from
050
      significant model mismatch. Model-free reinforcement learning (RL) controllers Schulman et al.
      (2017); Haarnoja et al. (2018) have shown impressive results in the benchmark Bhan et al. (2024)
051
      compared to the model-based control methods Pyta et al. (2015).
052
053   Besides, constraint satisfaction is of great importance for the PDE boundary control problems, but
      current safe PDE control methods are typically backstepping-based and require knowledge about the

071   PDE dynamics (Krstic & Bement, 2006; Li & Krstic, 2020; Koga & Krstic, 2023; Wang & Krstic,
072   2023). The constraint considered in this paper is called boundary feasibility, which characterizes
073
      whether the boundary output falls into and stays within the safe set at the end of the finite-time tra-
      jectory, and can be understood as the constraint of finite-time convergence. Under ordinary differ-
074
      ential equations (ODEs) setting, neural network parameterized control Lyapunov/barrier functions
075
      (CLF/CBFs) have been adopted to ensure the convergence and safety of learning-based controllers
076   Boffi et al. (2021); Dawson et al. (2023); Chang et al. (2019); Mazouz et al. (2022), based on the
077   Markov property of the dynamics at each step , i.e., the change of state only depends on the current
078   state and control input. However, the Markov assumption does not generally hold for PDE boundary
079   control due to infinite-dimensional unobserved states along the spatial axis. Hence, it is challenging
080   to adopt ODE CBFs and find the boundary control input at each step for trajectory-wise convergence
081   over boundary constraint satisfaction in the PDE setting.
082   To this end, we introduce a new framework to achieve boundary feasibility within a given safe set
083   for the PDE boundary control problem, as shown in Figure 1. More specifically, we propose neural
084   boundary control barrier functions (BCBFs) over the boundary output to enable the incorporation
085   of the time variable with a finite-time convergence guarantee. Then, we adopt a neural operator
086   to directly learn the mapping from boundary input to output as a transfer function. Combining
087   well-trained neural BCBF and neural operator, we show a linear dependence between boundary
088   feasibility condition and the derivative of boundary control input, making the safety filtering possible
089   by projecting the actions from the nominal RL controller to the safe boundary control input set using
090
      quadratic programming (QP). We conduct experiments on multiple PDE benchmarks and show our
      superiority over RL controllers in terms of general performance and constraint satisfaction. To
091
      the best of our knowledge, we are the first to study the safe boundary control with unknown PDE
092
      dynamics. We summarize our contributions below.
093
094
      • We propose a novel PDE safe control framework with a neural boundary control barrier function
095     to guarantee the boundary feasibility of boundary output within a given safe set.
096
097   • We model the control input and output mapping through a neural operator as a transfer function
098     and prove that it can be used for safety filtering by solving quadratic programming.
099
100   • We show that the performance after safety filtering performs better compared to the original RL
101
        controllers in reward and boundary feasibility rate and time steps on multiple PDE environments.

139
      Control barrier functions (CBFs) are shown to be successful for safe control (Liu & Tomizuka, 2014;
      Ames et al., 2014) and neural networks have been heavily investigated to effectively parameterize
140
      CBFs (Robey et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023) for ODE dynamics. Since the Markov
141
      assumption does not hold for PDE boundary control problem, it is challenging to leverage conven-
142   tional CBF to directly find control input U at time t for the constraint satisfaction of the marginalized
143   output boundary Y (t) := u(0, t) from the underlying PDE dynamics with spatially-continuous un-
144   observed state u(x, t). To mitigate this issue, inspired by Garg & Panagou (2021b), we propose a
145   more general neural boundary control barrier function (neural BCBF), explicitly incorporating time
146   t into neural network parameterized function ϕ(t, Y ) : T × S → R for the time-dependent zero-
147   sublevel set Sϕ,t := {Y (t) | ϕ(t, Y (t)) ≤ 0}. Note that the conventional CBF ϕ(Y ) can be viewed
148   as a specially case of BCBF ϕ(t, Y ) where t remains constant, so we also regard ϕ(Y ) as BCBF.

150
      fined on finite time domain T = [0, T ], which requires higher convergence rate to the safe set than

292
      Environments and model-free controllers. We adopt the challenging PDE boundary control en-

      vironments as well as the model-free reinforcement learning (RL) controllers from Bhan et al. (2024)

      Control for PDE Dynamics. PDE control problems can be in-domain control Botteghi & Fasel
      (2024); Zhang et al. (2024b) or boundary control Krstic & Smyshlyaev (2008b); Smyshlyaev &
465
      Krstic (2010), where the latter is more commonly-seen setting in the real world. As it has been
466
      studied for over a decade, backstepping has become a dominant approach for boundary control
467   with known PDE dynamics Krstic & Smyshlyaev (2008a); Smyshlyaev & Krstic (2004). Recently,
468   learning-based controllers have gotten rid of the requirement of analytical form of unstable PDE
469   dynamics and become a promising solution to the PDE control problems Botteghi & Fasel (2024);
470   Zhang et al. (2024b); Krstic et al. (2024); Qi et al. (2023); Mowlavi & Nabi (2023). However,
471   regarding the safety of constraint satisfaction in the PDE dynamics, current backstepping-based safe
472   PDE control methods (Krstic & Bement, 2006; Li & Krstic, 2020; Koga & Krstic, 2023; Wang &
473   Krstic, 2023) still assume the non-stable PDE dynamics is known. Therefore, we focus on data-
474   driven methods for PDE safe control without any prior knowledge of PDE dynamics.
475
476   Safe Control with Neural Certificate For the control of the ODE dynamical system, there is
477
      rich literature regarding learning-based controllers with safety guarantees or certificates Boffi et al.
      (2021); Dawson et al. (2023); Xiao et al. (2023); Lindemann et al. (2021); Chang et al. (2019);
478
      Mazouz et al. (2022). Neural networks have been used to parameterize the CBFs under complex
479
      dynamics with bounded control inputs Liu et al. (2022); So et al. (2023); Zinage et al. (2023);
480   Dawson et al. (2022); Dai et al. (2022), which result in forward invariance of the user-specified safe
481   set to guarantee the safety with neural certificate for learning-based controllers Choi et al. (2021);
482   Wei et al. (2022); Agrawal & Panagou (2021); Xiao et al. (2022); Hsu et al. (2023), i.e. once the
483   states enter the safe set, they will never go out. However, forward invariance may not hold in the
484   PDE boundary control setting with commonly-seen highly oscillating trajectories. For example,
485   highly-oscillating trajectories may go out of the safe set during the early oscillation and break the
      forward invariance defined by conventional ODE CBFs Liu & Tomizuka (2014); Ames et al. (2014),

515   Neural Operator Learning for PDEs. Neural operator learning has become as a powerful tool for
516   solving PDEs by learning mappings between function spaces rather than pointwise approximations
517   Kovachki et al. (2023); Brunton & Kutz (2024). Recent research has demonstrated the utility of
518   neural operators in multiple science and engineering fields like fluid dynamics, weather forecasting,
519
      and robotics Kochkov et al. (2021); Pathak et al. (2022); Heiden et al. (2021); Raissi et al. (2019).
      There exist multiple architectures for neural operators based on different mathematical properties
520
      of data. Lu et al. (2021) introduces DeepONet with a branch and a trunk network, and NOMAD
521
      Seidman et al. (2022) adopts nonlinear decoder map to learn submanifolds in function spaces, while
522   Green’s function-inspired neural operators Li et al. (2020a;b;c; 2022; 2024) adopt linear integral
523   kernel representation with various kernel implementations. However, for the PDE boundary control
524   problem, current works Bhan et al. (2023); Krstic et al. (2024) only adopt neural operators to learn
525   the integral kernel in backstepping, which does not release the full potential of neural operator for
526   characterizing and controlling unknown dynamics. The proposed work is the first to leverage neural
527   operators to learn the direct mapping from control input to boundary output as a transfer function.

      6   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
530
531
      In this work, we introduce a novel safe PDE boundary control framework using safety filtering with
532
      neural certification. First, BCBF and neural operator are learned from collected PDE boundary input
533
      and output trajectories within a given safe set. Then boundary feasibility is guaranteed by filtering
534   the unsafe boundary conditions using the BCBF. we show that the change in the BCBF depends
535   linearly on the change in input boundary, hence the filtering can be done by solving a quadratic
536   programming problem. Experiments on three challenging PDE control environments validate the
537   effectiveness of the proposed method in terms of both general performance and constraint satisfac-
538   tion. One limitation of the work is that our work does not consider complicated boundary constraint
539   settings and safe sets. Model mismatch between underlying PDE and neural operator is also an
      important but unexplored topic, which is marked as future work.

946   adopting the pre-trained models under default Navier-Stokes equation Bhan et al. (2024). For the
947   data collection in the 1D hyperbolic equation, we evaluate the backstepping-based model Krstic
948
      & Smyshlyaev (2008a), PPO and SAC models with random initial conditions U0 ∈ [1, 10] and
      collect 50k pairs of input and output u(1, t), u(0, t) trajectories for each model. Similarly, for the
949
      1D parabolic equation, we evaluate the backstepping-based model Smyshlyaev & Krstic (2004),
950
      PPO and SAC models with random initial conditions U0 ∈ [1, 10] and collect 50k pairs of input and
951   output u(1, t), u(0.5, t) trajectories for each model. For the Navier-Stokes equation, we evaluate
952   the model-based optimization method Pyta et al. (2015), PPO and SAC models with random initial
953   conditions u0 ∈ [−0.1, 0.1] and default tracking ground truth and collect 10k pairs of input and
954   output u(0.05, 1, t), u(0.5, 0.95, t) trajectories for each model. After the data pairs are collected, we
955   annotate the safety label with pre-defined safe constraints based on the original performance of each
956   policy: for the hyperbolic equation, Y < 1 for PPO and Y < 0 for SAC; for the parabolic equation,
957   Y < 0.6 for PPO and Y > −0.26 for SAC; for the Navier-Stokes equation, |Y − Ygt | < 0.145 for
958   PPO and SAC models. Then we randomly split 90% as a training dataset and leave others as a test
959
      set.

       C    L IMITATION AND D ISCUSSION
1070
1071   Since the proposed method is based on neural operator modeling instead of real PDE dynamics, it
1072   does not directly solve the problem of model mismatch which may hurt the safety filtering perfor-
1073   mance in the implementation. We mark this important point as future work. Also, for PDE dynamics
1074   with higher-dimensional states, it is future work to investigate how BCBF can deal with spatially-
1075   dependent boundaries. Another limitation lies in that we do not adopt online safety filtering under
1076   the real PDE dynamics due to the delay of QP, and it is promising to improve it by filtering-induce
1077   policy which is found offline. It is also interesting to omit the iterative filtering by prediction using
1078   the one-time filtering for the whole trajectory based on Equation (10), which owns challenges of the
1079
       nonlinear dependence of neural operator derivative at initial time.
