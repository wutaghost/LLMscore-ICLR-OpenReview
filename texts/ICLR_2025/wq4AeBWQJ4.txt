011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            Traditional verification methods in chip design are highly time-consuming and
014            computationally demanding, especially for large scale circuits. Graph neural net-
015            works (GNNs) have gained popularity as a potential solution to improve verifi-
016            cation efficiency. However, there lacks a joint framework that considers all chip
017            design domain knowledge, graph theory, and GPU kernel designs. To address
018            this challenge, we introduce GROOT, an algorithm and system co-design frame-
019            work that contains chip design domain knowledge and redesigned GPU kernels,
020
               to improve verification efficiency. More specifically, we redesign nodes features
               utilizing the circuit node types and the polarity of the connections between the in-
021
               put edges to nodes in And-Inverter Graphs (AIGs). We utilize a graph partitioning
022
               algorithm based on the observation that approximately only 10% boundary edges
023            (nodes) between cluster, to divide the large graphs into smaller sub-graphs for fast
024            GPU processing. We carefully profile the EDA graph workloads and observe the
025            uniqueness of their polarized distribution of high degree (HD) nodes and low de-
026            gree (LD) nodes. We redesign two GPU kernels (HD-kernel and LD-kernel), to fit
027            the EDA graph learning workload on a single GPU. We evaluate the performance
028            of GROOT on large circuit designs, e.g., Carry Save Adder (CSA) multipliers,
029            the 7nm technology mapped CSA multipliers and Booth Multipliers. We compare
030            the results with state-of-the-art GNN-based GAMORA and the traditional ABC
031
               framework. Results show that GROOT achieves a significant reduction in memory
               footprint (59.38 %), with high accuracy (99.96%) for a very large CSA multiplier,
032
               i.e. 1,024 bits with a batch size of 16, which consists of 134,103,040 nodes and
033
               268,140,544 edges. We compare GROOT with state-of-the-art GPU-based GPU
034            Kernel designs such as cuSPARSE, MergePath-SpMM, and GNNAdvisor. We
035            achieve up to 1.104×, 5.796×, and 1.469× improvement in runtime, respectively.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
039
040
      Logic synthesis plays a vital role in chip design by converting high-level circuit descriptions into
041
      optimized gate-level implementations and helps to bridge the gap between high-level synthesis and
042   physical design (7). Verification is a critical step in logic synthesis that ensures internal functionality,
043   prevents costly errors, and reduces the time-to-market by identifying and fixing issues early in the
044   design cycle (19). However, traditional verification methods are computationally demanding and
045   increasingly time-consuming, especially for complex designs (8; 28). For example, as measured in
046   (20), the verification process takes more than 100 hours for the booth multiplier using the OneSpin
047   commercial equivalence checker tool. Furthermore, using the open-source verification tool ABC
048   (15), a 2048-bit multiplier requires 8.6 × 105 seconds (more than nine days) (25).
049   Graph neural networks (GNNs) have gained popularity as a potential solution to improving verifi-
050   cation efficiency, e.g., GAMORA (25), since graph is one the most natural ways to represent many
051   fundamental objects in circuits, such as Register Transfer Level (RTL) descriptions, netlists, lay-
052   out, and Boolean functions. In GNN-based methods, GNN is leveraged to classify the graph nodes
053   which significantly reduces the verification time. For example, the 2048-bit multiplier verification
      time reduces from more than nine days to 0.919 seconds when GNN is used (25).

070   Despite their promising results, there are research gaps. First, an effective graph machine-learning

072   pertise and knowledge of graph machine learning. However, existing efforts tend to focus on just
073   one aspect, such as applying GNN algorithms to EDA tasks, and may lack EDA domain expertise.
074   For instance, GAMORA (25) does not distinguish Primary Inputs and Primary Outputs (PO) when
075   creating graph node features, however, PI and PO are inherently different and need to be distin-
076
      guished. Second, processing a large-scale EDA GNN on a single hardware, which is crucial to
      efficient AI, has been largely neglected. Figure 1 shows the memory consumption (on two high-end
077
      GPUs NVIDIA A100 40 GB and 80 GB, and one low-end GPU GeForce RTX2080) required for
078
      the verification of various bit widths multipliers. We observe that even the NVIDIA A100 could not
079   accommodate the 1,024-bit CSA multiplier graph when batch size equals 16. Please note that batch
080   processing is essential to achieve high throughput as GPUs are designed to process parallel data.
081   Third, the state-of-the-art (SOTA) high-performance solutions often use GPU, and simply adopt
082   commercialized multi-GPU solutions (e.g., GAMORA directly uses Pytorch Geometric (5) on two
083   or more GPUs). However, an important aspect that frequently goes unnoticed is the consideration of
084   GPU primitives. This fundamentally limits making single GPU achievable for EDA GNN and the
085   applicability of broadening accessibility in economically disadvantaged districts.
086   In this research, we propose GROOT, Graph Edge Re-growth and Partitioning for the Verification of
087   Large Designs in Logic Synthesis. GROOT is a single-GPU-based framework and simultaneously
088   achieves high accuracy, and low memory footprint at run-time. The classical open-source EDA tool
089   ABC (15) is not capable of obtaining verification results at run-time, and GAMORA (25) faces the
090   out-of-memory issue on large circuit graphs, as summarized in Table 1.
091   Our key contributions are: (i) We create the EDA graph node features. We utilize the circuit node
092   types and the polarity of the connections between the input edges to nodes in And-Inverter Graphs
093   (AIGs), to form the input embedding of the EDA graph. With the addition of more features, our
094   GNN model possesses the capacity to learn from a broader spectrum of circuit characteristics. (ii)
095   At the graph processing level, we utilize a graph partitioning algorithm to divide the large graphs into
096   smaller sub-graphs for GPU processing and develop a boundary edge re-growth algorithm. (iii) We
097   carefully profile the EDA graph workloads and observe the uniqueness of their polarized distribution
098   of high degree (HD) nodes and low degree (LD) nodes. We redesign two GPU kernels (HD-kernel
099
      and LD-kernel), to fit the EDA graph learning workload on a single GPU.

141   3.1        C ONVERTING N ETLISTS INTO T RANSITIONAL G RAPH
142
143   A Boolean network (digital design) can be described as a directed acyclic graph (DAG), where the
144   nodes symbolize logic gates. An And-Inverter Graph (AIG) represents a specific type of combina-
145
      tional Boolean network, comprised of two input AND gates and inverters (14). Essentially, AIG
      graphs are specialized DAGs that encapsulate the logical functionality of Boolean networks. Inter-
146
      estingly, through DeMorgan’s rule, the combinational logic of any given Boolean network can be
147
      easily transformed into an AIG.
148
149   In GROOT, this transformation is accomplished through an open-source EDA tool ABC (15). Figure
150   3 illustrates this process using a two-bit CSA multiplier. The ABC takes a netlist as an input, as
151
      shown in Figure 3 (a), and generates the corresponding AIG representation, shown in Figure 3 (b).
      In AIG representation, inputs a1a0 and b1b0 represent the two-bit binary numbers for the multiplier
152
      and multiplicand, respectively. The multiplication result is represented using m3m2m1m0 bits.
153
      For example, multiplier a1a0 = 10 and multiplicand b1b0 = 11 gives the multiplication result
154   m3m2m1m0 = 0110. The multiplication of the least significant bits (LSB) highlighted in golden
155   color, symbolizing the ‘AND′ operation at node 5 (i.e., m0 = a0 · b0). The additional operation of
156   multiplication is ‘XOR′ (green), containing nodes 6, 7, 8, 9, and 10, and can be represented by the
157   equation m1 = a0 · b1 xor a1 · b0. The ‘NOT′ operations are indicated by dashed lines.

      3.3       PARTITION , N ODE C LASSIFICATION AND P OST-P ROCESSING
201
202
      To deal with the memory footprint challenge caused by large EDA graphs, we use the graph par-
203
      tition, where we divide our graph into sub-graphs as shown in Figure 2 (c), and feed them to our
204   GNN architecture to perform a node classification task (see Appendix 7.2). We use the GraphSAGE
205   framework (6), a “sampling-and-aggregation” approach to generate node representations. We ob-
206   serve that EDA graphs contain approximately only 10% boundary edges (nodes) between clusters.
207   We regenerate boundary edges between disconnected clusters to prevent the loss of features and sup-
208   port effective message passing between inter-cluster nodes (see boundary edge re-growth algorithm
209   in Appendix 7.3).
210   We use GNN to classify the nodes into two categories XOR and MAJ as depicted in Figure 2 (e). We
211   use the algebraic re-writing technique (28; 3) for verification. The algebraic representation of the
212   basic Boolean operators is summarized in the appendix Table 3. Consider the case involving the XOR
213   and MAJ operations. The sub-polynomial expression is x1 + 2x2 + . . . , where x1 = XOR(a, b, c)
214   and x2 = MAJ(a, b, c), where a, b, c are inputs of XOR and MAJ functions. Substituting the algebraic
215   representations of XOR and MAJ into the sub-polynomial, we obtain, x1 + 2x2 + . . . = (a + b + c −
      2ab − 2ac − 2bc + 4abc) + 2(ab + ac + bc − 2abc) = a + b + c.

251   the right-hand feature matrix based on static workload partitioning. Intermediate results are stored

253   buffer (buf 1 to buf 32). In step A-2, we accumulate the results in the 32 buffers using the tree-based
254   accumulation. First, the direction of the warp operation is reset, and the 32 threads in each warp are
255
      responsible for one bit in the buffer with the same number. Then, the tree-shaped accumulation oper-

256

257

258   shown in the example in figure 4, after the first cycle, we get the first accumulation result, that is, the

261   get the second accumulation result stored in buf 1, buf 5, ..., buf 29. After the fifth cycle, the final
262   accumulation result is stored in buf 1. After the tree-shaped accumulation operation is completed,
263   the final output of a row of results is obtained, which can be directly transferred to the corresponding
264   row in the global memory without additional accumulation operations on the global memory, greatly

296   Similarly, nzmax /2, rows at the degree of 2 and nzmax /3, rows with a degree of 3 are assigned
297   to two separate warps. In addition, the partitioning remains is processed recursively to ensure the
298   minimum error in calculation, as mentioned in part 2. The whole partitioning method significantly
299   improves the efficiency of the LD-kernel.
300   The sorting and row-assembling details are described in Fig. 5. The warp-block operation of LD-
301   kernel starts with sorting on the original sparse input by the degree of each row and maps the left-
302   hand side (LHS) rows into an array linearly, whereby the partitioning is executed via dividing the
303   array into blocks of rows according to their degree, sequentially from the smallest to the largest.
304   Then, within the block, warps will operate in parallel to extract non-zeros in the rows to multiply the
305   corresponding right-hand-side (RHS) rows. The resulting product rows are summed up to produce
306   the output. All blocks perform the whole process in parallel. For example, in the lower left part of
307   Fig. 5, warp 1 traverses its non-zeros from the left to the right, meanwhile locating the correspond-
308
      ing RHS row (1,1). The first 1 implies warp 1, and the second 1 refers to the first row, which warp 1
      is responsible for.
309
310   Multiple rows of non-zero elements are assigned to the same warp rather than one row per warp.
311   Since the degree of each row is small, say 3, the number of warps per block in this kernel is set to
312   6m. For rows with degrees of 1, 2, and 3, each warp is responsible for 6m, 3m, and 2m rows, which
313   translates into nzmax , nzmax /2 and nzmax /3 rows in the figure, respectively. This increases the
314
      proportion of active warps. In addition, this significantly reduces the overhead of warp switching and
      improves calculation efficiency. The aggregated organization method enhances access continuity to
315
      global memory when transferring calculation results from shared memory to global memory. On
316
      top of that, We utilize the technique of coalesce dumping to exploit this advantage fully. During step
317   B − 3 called coalesce dumping, it writes the intermediate results of multiple consecutive rows from
318   the same warp into a continuous area in global memory, its efficiency is also improved.

      of the net list which graphs describe, and the datasets where the graphs belong to. Our GROOT-GPU

518   powerful GPUs. GROOT-GPU achieves the highest acceleration ratio of 10.28 for the Booth dataset

522   particularly for complex datasets and higher bit widths, making it a promising choice for various

528
529   In this paper, we introduce GROOT, an algorithm and system co-design framework that contains
530   chip design domain knowledge, graph theory, and redesigned GPU kernels, to improve verifica-
531
      tion efficiency. We redesign nodes features utilizing the circuit node types and the polarity of the
      connections between the input edges to nodes in And-Inverter Graphs (AIGs). We utilize a graph
532
      partitioning algorithm to divide the large graphs into smaller sub-graphs for fast GPU processing.
533
      After profiling EDA graph workloads, we notice their distinct distribution of high-degree and low-
534   degree nodes and tailor the GPU kernel accordingly. We evaluate our framework on large circuit
535   designs, e.g., CSA multipliers, the 7nm technology mapped CSA multipliers and Booth Multipli-
536   ers. We compare the results with state-of-the-arts, e.g., GAMORA and ABC. Experimental results
537   show that GROOT achieves a significant reduction in memory footprint, with high accuracy for a
538   very large CSA multiplier, i.e., 1,024 bits with a batch size of 16. We also compare GROOT with
539   SOTA GPU-based GPU Kernel designs such as cuSPARSE, MergePath-SpMM, and GNNAdvisor,
      and achieve notable runtime improvement.

      7.3           B OUNDARY E DGE R E - GROWTH A LGORITHM
703
704   Our partitioning algorithm, shown in Algorithm 1, divides a large EDA graph into smaller sub-
705   clusters and facilitates the reconnection of edges between these clusters. We observe that EDA
706   graphs contain approximately only 10% boundary edges (nodes) between clusters, and the bound-
707   ary recovering process does not add complexity to the inference stage. This approach focuses on
708   regenerating boundary edges between disconnected clusters to prevent the loss of features and sup-
709   port effective message passing between inter-cluster nodes.
710
711   Algorithm 1 Graph Partition with Boundary Recovery
712   Require: G, V {Input graph as adj. list and embedding}
713    1: [G0 , G1 , ..., Gn ], [V0 , V1 , ..., Vn ] ← METIS(C)
714    2: for p = 0 to n do
715
       3:   Cp , Np ← FIND BOUNDARY CONNECTIONS(Gp ) {Locate all boundary edges/nodes of
            partition Gp }
716
       4:   Gp ← Gp ∪ Cp {Restore the boundary edges}
717
       5:   Vp ← Vp ∪ Np {Restore the boundary nodes}
718    6: end for
719    7: return G0 , G1 , ..., Gn and V0 , V1 , ..., Vn
