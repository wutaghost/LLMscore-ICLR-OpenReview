                                                 Abstract
010
011
                A challenging problem for implementing multi-agent reinforcement learn-
012
                ing (MARL) in real-world applications is ensuring the safety of cooperative
013
                strategies. According to the Protection Motivation Theory (PMT), threat
014             appraisals result in negative emotions and elicit protective behaviors, which
015             are instrumental for coping with security threats. Drawing inspiration from
016             the PMT, we focus on two discrete emotions–fear and regret–to evaluate
017             threat severity and facilitate multiple agents to learn protective behaviors.
018             These can promote cooperative decision-making with fewer safety viola-
019             tions. Specifically, we propose two safety guarantee methods with PMT:
020             fear for safety guarantee (F4SG) and regret for safety guarantee (R4SG),
021             utilizing the active inference technique to model the emotions of fear and
022
                regret separately. The threat severity evaluated by these emotions influ-
                ences the state value and the executed action respectively, which avoids the
023
                potential threat of visiting certain states or taking certain actions. Experi-
024
                mental results demonstrate that our proposed methods are safer and more
025             eﬀicient than state-of-the-art baselines on challenging tasks in safe MARL
026             benchmarks.

029   1     Introduction
030
031   In recent years, Multi-agent Reinforcement learning (MARL) has become an active research
032   field with great success in addressing various complex multi-agent decision-making tasks,
033   such as multi-robot control (Chen et al., 2024), autonomous vehicle coordination (Li et al.,
034   2024), and transportation resource management (Zhang et al., 2023). Although traditional
035   MARL methods show significant performance for completing cooperative tasks, the lack of
036   safety guarantees limits their deployment in the real world. These methods are purely for
037   reward maximization, completely ignoring safety violations (Gu et al., 2023). For example,
038   the autonomous vehicles controlled by MARL agents ignore the risk of collision with others
039
      in order to obtaining high returns through high-speed driving; the robots controlled by
      MARL agents ignore irreversible harm to themselves or environmental elements in order to
040
      quickly completing the required tasks.
041
042   Recently, some works offer effective learning algorithms for ensuring safety in MARL meth-
043   ods: 1) The primal-dual framework-based safe MARL methods (Lu et al., 2021; Ding et al.,
044   2023; Gu et al., 2023). These methods formulate the constrained optimization problem in
045
      safe MARL as the min-max problem between reward and cost, and utilize a primal-dual
      framework (Boyd et al., 2004) or its combination with trust region approach (Schulman
046
      et al., 2015) to find the saddle point. 2) The shielding-based safe MARL methods (Elsayed-
047
      Aly et al., 2021; Melcer et al., 2022; Xiao et al., 2023; Melcer et al., 2024). These methods
048   extend the shielding framework developed in the single-agent setting (Alshiekh et al., 2018)
049   to the multi-agent setting, preventing multiple agents from exploring any unsafe states or
050   actions that violate the safety specification expressed by linear temporal logic (Pnueli, 1977).
051   3) The safe MARL methods with safety layer (Sheebaelhamd et al., 2021; Shi et al., 2023).
052   These methods add the data-driven safety layer for mapping unsafe actions to safe actions,
053   enhancing the capability of correcting unsafe behaviors. 4) Other approach for safe MARL
      methods (Zhu et al., 2020; Jusup et al., 2024). These methods introduce the novel prior

054
      knowledge to ensure the safety, such as gaussian processes and mean field. However, the
055   above works ignore the great potential of utilizing human knowledge about coping with
056   threats in guiding MARL methods to ensure safety, which can effectively achieve the goal
057   of improving rewards while satisfying safety constraints.
058
      The Protection Motivation Theory (PMT (Rogers, 1975)) originated from social psychology
059
      suggests that threat appraisals result in the emotion of fear and impact protective behaviors.
060
      PMT explains human security behaviors to protect themselves when perceiving threats.
061   (Ogbanufe & Pavur, 2022) further extends PMT framework and analyzes the effects of
062   discrete emotions (i.e. fear and regret) for security protection behaviors. Naturally, PMT
063   is also applicable to guide MARL methods in learning safe cooperative strategies in safety-
064   critical scenarios: agents actively infer discrete emotions to evaluate the threat severity and
065   influence the current cooperative decision-making, avoiding the potential threat of visiting
066   certain states or taking certain actions.
067   In this paper, we introduce PMT into the MARL to address the challenge safety. With
068   PMT, we propose two novel safe guarantee methods: fear for safety guarantee (F4SG)
069   and regret for safety guarantee (R4SG). Same as the cognitive mediational process (threat
070   appraisal and coping appraisal) in PMT, each of our methods takes two steps to implement
071   the security protection behaviors elicited by discrete emotions. First, we utilize the active
072   inference technique to model the emotions of fear and regret separately, mapping the state to
073   the severity of fear and the state-action to the severity of regret. Second, the state value and
074   the executed action are respectively influenced according to the threat appraisal, facilitating
075
      the learning of security protection behaviors. Besides, we take Lagrange dual framework
      combined with trust region approach to formulate the constrained optimisation problem as
076
      the min-max problem, accelerating the learning process of safety cooperative strategy.
077
078   To summarize, our main contributions are three-fold: 1) We provide a novel perspective,
079   with the Protection Motivation Theory originated from social psychology, for safe multi-
080   agent reinforcement learning, introducing the human knowledge about facing threats into
081
      MARL to ensure safety. 2) With PMT, we propose two effective safety guarantee methods–
      fear for safety guarantee (F4SG) and regret for safety guarantee (R4SG)–to facilitate mul-
082
      tiple agents learning safety protective cooperative behaviors. 3) Experimental results show
083
      that the two safety guarantee methods outperform the state-of-the-art baselines in terms of
084   balancing performance and constraint satisfaction in several challenging tasks on three safe
085   MARL benchmarks.

      2.2     Protection Motivation Theory
109
110   PMT (Rogers, 1975) is a long-studied and persuasive theory in social psychology, which
111   suggests that threat appraisals result in the emotion of fear and individuals will respond
112   to threat appraisals via protective behavior. Thus, emotions are instrumental for coping
113   with security threats (Liang et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2023). PMT consists of three parts:
114   information sources, cognitive mediational process, and coping patterns. The information
115   source refers to an individual’s cognition of various factors in the environment; The cogni-
116
      tive mediational process is the core part of this theory, which includes two elements: threat
      assessment and response assessment; Coping patterns refer to the behavior of individuals
117
      after undergoing the cognitive mediational process. This theory explains the reasons why
118
      individuals change their behavior due to environmental and social factors through cognitive
119   mediational, and comprehensively elaborates on the process and mechanism of cognition
120   to behavior change. PMT has a wide range of applications, such as information systems,
121   health, and marketing. With the evolution of PMT, many studies consider various emotions
122   affecting protective behavior to explain individuals’ protection motivation (Leary, 2007; Cho
123   et al., 2020; Nehme & George, 2022; Ogbanufe & Pavur, 2022). According to the theoretical
124   underpinning of recently revisited PMT (Ogbanufe & Pavur, 2022), the behaviors that char-
125   acterize individuals’ motivation to protect themselves from a threat are elicited by discrete
126   emotions of fear and regret, which have different effects on security protection behaviors.
127
      Naturally, PMT is also applicable to multi-agent systems in safety-critical scenarios and we
      facilitate multiple agents learning safety cooperative strategy with this theory.

      2.3     Active Inference Technique
131   Active inference technique (Friston et al., 2009) offers a useful computational framework for
132   minimizing the divergence between an unbiased model of the real world and a biased gener-
133   ative model of organisms’ preferences, which arises from the free energy principle (Friston,
134   2010). Given a generative model, it tends the output distribution q(x; θ) (known as the
135   prior in Bayesian) towards the true posterior p(x|y) via minimizing the free energy F:
136
                                           F = DKL [q(x; θ)||p(x, y)]                           (2)
137
138   where DKL (·||·) is the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence. x denotes the hypotheses about
139   the world and y denotes the observation about the world. The model parameters θ are
140   treated as random variables, casting the learning of the model as a process of inference.

142   3     Safe Multi-agent Reinforcement Learning with Protection Motivation
143
            Theory
144
145
      This section describes our proposed safety guarantee methods with protection motivation
146   theory, F4SG and R4SG, that elicit agents’ protective behaviors via discrete emotions of
147   fear and regret. We follow the Centralized Training and Decentralized Execution frame-
148   work, where agents are trained with access to global information but make decisions based
149   on their own local information in execution. Both F4SG and R4SG follow the cognitive
150   mediational process revealed by PMT. We then introduce the details of implementing our
151   safety guarantee algorithm on the representative Actor-Critic style MARL methods.
152
153   3.1     Fear for Safety Guarantee
154
155   3.1.1    Threat Appraisal about Fear
156   Fear increases an individual’s perception of threats and prompts them to take suggested
157   actions to reduce the fear or harm caused by such threats. The fear-based PMT suggests that
158   fear acting as a mediator between threat appraisals and protection motivation can influence
159   individuals’ safety protection behaviors to avoid potential threats. The observations in
160   neurosciences (Herry et al., 2008; Jovasevic et al., 2015) suggest that the fear-inducing
161   underlying mechanism is state dependent. Thus, we propose a fear prior network (FPN)
      learned via active inference that maps agents’ states to their fear severity.

      For the emotion of fear, it can be elicited by certain negative stimuli (LeDoux & Daw, 2018).
163   In safe MARL, the cost function serves as a negative incentive used to punish the unsafe
164   behaviors of the agent, which can be viewed as a form of negative stimulus. Furthermore,
165   the emotion of fear can also be caused by uncertainties in individual perception (Carleton
166   & Nicholas, 2016). Therefore, the FPN considers both the anticipated negative stimuli and
167   epistemic uncertainties simultaneously. Mathematically, the fear severity inferred by FPN
168   for each agent i can be expressed as f˜i = F P N (s; ζ i ). Formally, the fear severity of state s
169   is the probability value at the 1st-index of f˜i , while the probability value at the other index
170   denotes the level of non-fear.
171
      To make the inferred fear severity f˜i about state s incorporate both the elicited factors, the
172
      FPN is optimized by minimizing the following:
173
174                    argmin (DKL [q(f˜i )||p(C i )] − ln[p(C i )]) + DKL [F P N (s), F P N (sD )]                               (3)
175
      where the first term is the free energy simplified by Variational Bayes (Buckley et al., 2017)
176
      and the second term is the epistemic uncertainty between the fear severity of state current
177
      state s and the fear severity of state history state sD (stored in replay buffer D); p(·) and
178   q(·) are the probability distributions; DKL is the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence. Due to
179   the true distribution of cost is binomial distribution, we adopt the reparameterization trick
180   technique (Jang et al., 2017) to sample from q(f˜i ) for generating the binomial distribution
181   about fear severity b(f˜i ). Meanwhile, the second term can be expressed as the cognitive
182
      error, which is replaced by the mean squared loss between f˜i and f˜Di in the implementation.
183   Therefore, the final loss function to learn the FPN is:

      3.2.1    Threat Appraisal about Regret
265   Regret is a negative emotion of not performing a behavior that would positively contribute to
266   one’s personal goals. The regret-based PMT suggests that regret is important in individuals’
267   decision-making to avoid the actual experience of meeting threats via the regret in pre-
268   behaviors. Many works (Bastani et al., 2022; Karpov & Zhang, 2024) utilize the regret
269   theory to facilitate the learning of policy. However, these works learn no-regret policies
      maximizing rewards by regret theory but ignore the great potential of regret in ensuring

452
      The MAPDN environment serves as a suitable framework for distributed active voltage con-
453
      trol, readily accommodating the application of MARL methods. The 33-/141-bus network
454
      scenarios of MAPDN environment are emploied to compare the performance. In 33-bus
455   network, there are 4 regions with 6 agents. In 141-bus network, there are 9 regions with
456   22 agents. The agents (photovoltaics, PVs) in the power distribution network has the po-
457   tential for voltage fluctuations exceeding the power grid standards. The distributed active
458   voltage control task (Shi et al., 2023; Qu et al., 2024) need agents to control the voltage
459   within a safety range around a stationary value, while the reactive power generation is as
460   less as possible. Thus, the following two key evaluation metrics are emploied to assess the
461   performance:
462
463
      • Controllable Rate (CR). It calculates the ratio of the time steps in each episode where
        all buses’ voltages is under control within the safety range from 0.95 per unit to 1.05 per
464
        unit (reference voltage is 1.0 per unit).
465
466   • P ower Loss (PL). It calculates the average of the total power loss for entire power
467     network per time step in each episode.

      5                              Conclusion and Future Work
532
533
      In this paper, we propose two novel safety guarantee methods, fear for safety guarantee
534   (F4SG) and regret for safety guarantee (R4SG), to learn cooperative and safe strategies.
535   These methods are inspired by the Protection Motivation Theory from social psychology,
536   which offers a useful theoretical underpinning for guiding the learning of protective behav-
537   iors. The experimental results demonstrate the F4SG and R4SG achieve advantages in the
538   balance between performance improvement and safety constraint satisfaction compared with
539   state-of-the-art baselines. In the future, we will explore the possibility of extending safety
      MARL methods to offline problems to ensure safety implementation.

      A.1   Related Work
705
706
      Recently, safety has been increasing concerned in the MARL domain and some safety guar-
707   antee algorithms have been studied for MARL methods.
708
709   The primal-dual framework-based safe MARL methods. Safe Dec-PG (Lu et al., 2021) is
710
      the first decentralized policy gradient algorithm for safe MARL, which leverages the min-
      max saddle-point formulation to find the ϵ-first-order stationary points. UCB-CSAPO (Ding
711
      et al., 2023) focuses on the online safe MARL and formulates it as the generalized Lagrangian
712
      policy optimization problem, solved by the developed upper confidence reinforcement learn-
713   ing algorithm. The work (Gu et al., 2023) proposes two safe multi-agent policy gradient
714   algorithms, MACPO and MAPPO-Lagrangian, for multi-robot control. MACPO leverages
715   the multi-agent trust region method and MAPPO-Lagrangian uses Lagrangian multipliers
716   to simplify the repetitive computation in MACPO, which achieves the goal of improving
717   reward while satisfying safety constraints. MA-DELC (Qu et al., 2024) is a MARL method
718   extended from the primal-dual optimization RL method, addressing the challenge of guar-
719   anteeing safety constraints in the active voltage control problem.
720   The shielding-based safe MARL methods. The work (Elsayed-Aly et al., 2021) develops two
721   shielding frameworks, centralized shielding and factored shielding, to ensure multiple agents
722   satisfy the safety specification expressed by linear temporal logic. The work (Melcer et al.,
723   2022) discusses the strong assumptions in previous shielded MARL methods and presents a
724   decentralized shielding algorithm for the decomposition of a centralized shield. And then,
725   the work (Melcer et al., 2024) extends this decentralized shielding algorithm to the shielding
726   method without any assumptions, which is applicable to environments with general partial
727
      observability. MBDS (Xiao et al., 2023) synthesizes distributive shields by an approximate
      world model and allows shields without prior knowledge to dynamically split, merge, and
728
      recompute based on agents’ states.
729
730   The safe MARL methods with safety layer. Safe MADDPG (Sheebaelhamd et al., 2021) ex-
731   tends the idea of linearizing the single-step transition dynamics to the MADDPG framework
732   and adds a safety layer to ensure safety. The work(Shi et al., 2023) proposes two MARL al-
733
      gorithms with a centralized data-driven safety layer, ACPL-MADDPG and ACS-MADDPG,
      to ensure the power system satisfies the security.
734
735   Other approach for safe MARL methods. Multi Safe Q-Agent (Zhu et al., 2020) utilizes
736   a Gaussian Processes-based approach to estimate safety and uncertainty, enabling decen-
737   tralized safe navigation for multiple different agents. SAFE-M3 -UCRL (Jusup et al., 2024)
738   is the first safe model-based mean-field MARL algorithm to solve the vehicle reposition-
739
      ing problem, ensuring pessimistic constraints satisfaction with high probability via using
      epistemic uncertainty and log-barrier approach.
740
741   However, the above works ignore the great potential of utilizing human knowledge about
742   coping with threats in guiding MARL methods to ensure safety. Thus, we leverage the
743   theoretical underpinnings of the Protection Motivation Theory, revealing the individuals’
744   protection behavior motivations when perceiving threats, to develop safety guarantee ap-
745
      proaches for MARL methods.

853   A.6   Computational Complexity
854
855   The computational complexity of both F4SG and R4SG is O(T N EM P ), where T denotes
856   the number of steps, N denotes the number of agents, E denotes the number of PPO-epoch,
857
      M denotes the number of constraints, P denotes the max number of parameters in {actor
      network, and fear/regret prior network}. Due to the P ∝ |S|×2 in fear prior network and the
858
      P ∝ |S| × |A| in regret prior network, the wall-clock times that methods train an episode are
859
      different. For example, the average wall-clock time for training F4SG and R4SG on 2-agent
860   ant walking through the corridor task in Safe MAMuJoCo is approximately 23.5 s/episode
861   and approximately 25.2 s/episode respectively. Fig. 6 shows the steps of our methods and
862   baselines that complete an episode on Ant 2 × 4 task.
