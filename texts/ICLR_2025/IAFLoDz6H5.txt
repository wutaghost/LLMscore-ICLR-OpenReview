010                                                A BSTRACT
011
012            Language models exhibit scaling laws, whereby increasing model and dataset size
013            yield predictable decreases in negative log likelihood, unlocking a dazzling array
014
               of capabilities. At the same time, even the most capable systems are currently
               vulnerable to adversarial inputs such as jailbreaks and prompt injections, despite
015
               concerted efforts to make them robust. As compute becomes more accessible to
016
               both attackers and defenders, which side will benefit more from scale? Will safety-
017            trained frontier models become robust against any but the strongest attacks, or will
018            additional compute make attacks almost impossible to defend against?
019            We attempt to answer this question with a detailed study of robustness on lan-
020            guage models spanning three orders of magnitude in parameter count. We find
021            that increasing base model size alone does not consistently improve robustness.
022            However, larger models benefit more from safety-training, and in particular better
023            generalize from adversarial training to new attacks. We then study the attacker’s
024            perspective, finding predictable improvement in attack success rate as attacker
025            compute is increased against all models studied. Finally, we show that offense
026            widens its advantage as both sides spend more on compute.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Language models have demonstrated a range of impressive capabilities in tasks such as general lan-
031   guage understanding (Hendrycks et al., 2021), graduate-level Q&A (Rein et al., 2023), and code
032   generation (Chen et al., 2021). This growth in capabilities has fueled rapid deployment, with Chat-
033   GPT becoming one of the fastest-growing consumer applications in history (Hu, 2023). Language
034   models are now increasingly integrated into larger systems, enabling them to take actions in the real
035   world using external tools (OpenAI, 2023; Anthropic, 2024; Google, 2024) and to pursue long-term
036   open-ended goals (Richards, 2024; Kinniment et al., 2024).
037   While the advent of language models enables many new tasks to be solved by AI, it also introduces
038   novel classes of security vulnerabilities. A variety of adversarial prompts can bypass safety fine-
039   tuning (Wei et al., 2023; Zou et al., 2023; Anil et al., 2024), unlocking harmful capabilities such as
040   generating misinformation (Spitale et al., 2023; Chen & Shu, 2024). Users of LLM-driven applica-
041   tions are also at risk from attacks like indirect prompt injections (Abdelnabi et al., 2023) that exploit
042   the underlying LLM without the user’s awareness or participation. As models become more capa-
043   ble, the risks from attacks will increase, with future models potentially able to assist with dangerous
044
      actions such as biological weapon development (Mouton et al., 2023). These concerns compound
      as models are given greater affordances to interact with the world (Sharkey et al., 2023).
045
046   Over a decade of research in adversarial robustness (Szegedy et al., 2014) has yet to find a way to
047   reliably defend against adversarial attack, and attackers and defenders remain locked in a game of
048   cat-and-mouse. Taking a step back from the specifics of this game today, what general trends can
049   we identify to inform us about the future? In particular, how will increased access to compute—for
050
      both attackers and defenders—affect the robustness of frontier models?
051   Previous results tell an uncertain story. In computer vision, scaling unlabeled pretraining
052   data (Hendrycks et al., 2019; Carmon et al., 2022; Alayrac et al., 2019) and model size (Xie &
053   Yuille, 2019; Huang et al., 2023; Caballero et al., 2023) improve model robustness. In turn, scaling
      up language models has led to improved capabilities across a variety of settings (Hestness et al.,

092   2   R ELATED W ORK
093
094   Adversarial examples were first identified in image classifiers (Szegedy et al., 2014), and have since
095   been found for systems performing image captioning (Xu et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2020), speech
096   recognition (Cisse et al., 2017; Alzantot et al., 2018; Schönherr et al., 2018), and reinforcement
097   learning (Huang et al., 2017; Gleave et al., 2020; Ilahi et al., 2022).
098   Most recently, many qualitatively different vulnerabilities have been found in language models, from
099   interpretable jailbreaks (Wei et al., 2023) to seemingly gibberish adversarial suffixes (Wallace et al.,
100   2021; Zou et al., 2023). Methods such as perplexity filtering and paraphrasing defend against some
101   of these attacks (Jain et al., 2023), but such defenses can often be bypassed by more sophisticated
102   attacks (Zhu et al., 2023). Adversarial training can in theory be used against any attack, and can be
103   scaled up or down depending on defender compute, so we use it as the basis for defending models
104
      in this study.
105   The determinants of adversarial robustness have been well-studied in computer vision (CV). One
106   line of scholarship proposes a fundamental tradeoff between robustness and accuracy (Tsipras et al.,
107   2019), positing that exploitable models are simply relying on non-robust features (Ilyas et al., 2019),
      which improve training performance but hurt robustness. Other work has emphasized what improves


                                                                           2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      robustness. For example, scaling unlabeled pretraining data (Hendrycks et al., 2019; Carmon et al.,
109   2022; Alayrac et al., 2019), model depth (Xie & Yuille, 2019) and model width (Huang et al., 2023)
110   all improve CV adversarial robustness. However, other work shows that increasing scale alone will
111   not fully solve CV adversarial robustness (Debenedetti et al., 2023; Bartoldson et al., 2024).
112
      Language model scaling laws (Hestness et al., 2017; Rosenfeld et al., 2019; Kaplan et al., 2020;
113
      Hoffmann et al., 2022) have shown that increasing compute improves performance across many
114
      tasks and domains (Chen et al., 2021; Hernandez et al., 2021), leading some to surmise that “per-
115   haps many capabilities simply lie on a spectrum that can be continuously unlocked with increasing
116   scale” (Henighan et al., 2020). Yet we know scaling does not solve all problems—indeed, it makes
117   some worse (Lin et al., 2022; McKenzie et al., 2023). There has been only limited work on scaling
118   laws for adversarial robustness in language models, with mixed results. Larger models are shown to
119   be generally harder to red-team in Ganguli et al. (2022), while Anil et al. (2024) find that in-context
120   learning attacks are more successful on larger models with larger context windows. In turn, Yang
121   et al. (2024) find some improvement to robustness with scale when using a substitution-based attack,
122   though their attack sometimes significantly corrupts inputs. In this work, we systematically study
123
      effects of scale on language model robustness by varying model size, adversarial training, and attack
      strength across a variety of tasks.

126   3    E XPERIMENTAL M ETHODOLOGY
127
128   We study robustness of models spanning three orders of magnitude in size drawn from two families
129   across six classification tasks and one generation task, under three attacks and multiple defenses.
130
131   Metrics We measure robustness by the attack success rate. For binary classification tasks this is
132   simply the proportion of examples correctly classified by the model before attack that are incorrectly
133   classified after attack.1 For generative tasks, a direct definition is not possible as refusal cannot be
134   programmatically checked. We therefore follow StrongREJECT (Souly et al., 2024) and evaluate
135   model responses to harmful questions using an LLM-based judge. For comparability to classification
136   tasks, we evaluate only on examples that the model refused in the pre-attack evaluation.
137
138   Models We primarily study the Pythia model suite (Biderman et al., 2023). Pythia was the most
139   suitable open-weight model family for a systematic study as it provides many different models across
140   three orders of magnitude in size, with comparable architectures, all trained on the same dataset.
141
      Specifically, we use the non-deduped Pythia model family which consists of 10 autoregressive lan-
      guage models ranging from 14M to 12B parameters, pre-trained on the Pile (Gao et al., 2020). To
142
      create classification models, we replace the unembedding matrix with a classification head. After
143
      this replacement, the Pythia models range from 7.6M to 11.6B parameters.2
144
145   In addition to Pythia, we run on the more recent Qwen2.5 family of language models (Qwen Team,
146   2024). The Qwen2.5 family contains multilingual base and instruction-tuned models ranging from
147
      0.5B to 72B. We use a subset of these models, ranging from 0.5B to 7B for our classification tasks
      and from 0.5B to 14B for our generative task. For the base models, as with Pythia, we create
148
      classification models by replacing the unembedding matrix with a classification head.
149
150   We finetune all classification models for three epochs on a task dataset consisting of 20,000 exam-
151   ples, using a linear learning rate schedule that decays from 1e−5 to 0. See Table 1 for worst-case
152   accuracies for the smallest and largest models of each family after finetuning; Figures 8 and 9 show
153
      accuracies for all models sizes in both families. We find that even the smallest 7.6M parameter
      Pythia model achieves high accuracy on most classification tasks, enabling us to study robustness
154
      across a significantly wider scale than is possible in generative tasks.

200   In the RandomToken baseline, the N = 10 tokens are chosen uniformly at random from the
201   model’s vocabulary. We evaluate the model on the attacked text, repeating the process with newly
202   sampled N = 10 random tokens (which replace the old ones) until the model is successfully attacked
203   or an appointed budget for model calls is exhausted.
204   In GCG (Zou et al., 2023), the N = 10 tokens are initialized arbitrarily and then greedily optimized
205   over multiple rounds. In each round, the gradient of the loss function with respect to the attack
206   tokens is computed. This gradient is used to compute a set of promising single-token modifications,
207   from which the best candidate is used in the next round. To make this attack work in the classifi-
208   cation setting, we minimize the cross-entropy loss between the predicted label and the target label.
209   Importantly, we apply GCG to datapoints individually rather than optimizing a single attack across
210   multiple prompts, leading to a very strong attack.

214   candidates achieving the lowest adversarial loss. In the reference implementation, the tokens are
215   sampled from the victim model to keep their perplexity low, but since our victims are classification

247
      Larger models are more robust on average. Figure 2 shows the robustness of our finetuned
      models as a function of model size when attacked with the GCG attack. We observe a noisy and
248
      task-dependent trend. For the Pythia family (left), larger models are generally more robust than
249
      smaller models: for example, on the IMDB task, the attack achieves a median success rate of almost
250   100% against the 7.6M model, while it achieves less than 20% against the 12B parameter model.
251   However, even among tasks where scale appears to help, we observe significant variability across
252   model sizes and tasks. For example, in the Spam task, increasing parameter count over 50x from
253   123.7M (4th blue point from the left) up to 6.7B (3rd blue point from the right) results in a higher
254   attack success rate. Furthermore, in the WordLength task, model size does not appear to confer
255   any additional robustness at all. See Figure 10 for similar results with the RandomToken attack.
256   For the Qwen2.5 family (right), the trend is less pronounced, though this might be in part due to the
257   limited breadth of model sizes. On classification tasks, robustness appears approximately constant
258   across model sizes. On the generative StrongREJECT task, the trend is clear: larger models are
259   consistently more robust. This is likely because the generative Qwen2.5 models we tested are from
260   the Instruct family, and thus have undergone some safety training. Thus, we might expect these
261   results to look more similar to the adversarially trained classification results in Section 5.
262   While increasing model scale improves adversarial robustness on most tasks, this trend is high-
263   variance at best, and non-existent at worst. The effect from scale is also very weak: in early exper-
264   iments, we found that even moderately increasing the number of attack iterations quickly saturated
265   attack success rate near 100%, removing any clear scaling behavior.

      7   L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
494
495
      In this work, we focus on evaluating the robustness of classifiers, which enabled us to study scaling
496   across three orders of magnitude with a clear notion of attack success. Classifiers such as moderation
497   filters are often used in security-critical settings, making their robustness of immediate practical
498   relevance. Furthermore, since generation is a harder task, we expect robustness of classifiers to
499   serve as a lower bound to robustness of generative models. However, studying jailbreaks on open-
500   ended tasks requires generative models. While our initial results on generative models show similar
501   behavior to those on classifiers, it would be valuable to study a wider class of generative models.
502   Additionally, most of our experiments used the same attack method both to find examples to use for
503   adversarial training (defense) and to evaluate robustness (attack). However, in the real world, this
504   is an unrealistic threat model for both attacker and defender. On the attack side, if an attacker has
505   access to the weights of a model, there is no need for adversarial attack—a small amount of finetun-
506   ing is a more effective use of compute to bypass safety training (Pelrine et al., 2023). If the attacker
507   does not have model access, then the attacker must use a black-box attack like BEAST instead of
508   a gradient-based attack like GCG. On the defense side, the defender can afford to do more than
509   adversarially train with GCG. For example, they can use a more compute-efficient attack method,
510
      like Latent Adversarial Training (Casper et al., 2024), to find examples on which to adversarially
      train. Furthermore, they can employ other defenses on top of adversarial training. With this in mind,
511
      we believe it would be of value to determine whether the offense-defense balance remains in the
512
      attacker’s favor under a more realistic threat model.
513
514   Similarly, our analysis focused on asymptotic aspects and quantifiable trends. This is relevant for
515   understanding relative changes to the status quo, but is insufficient to comment on the absolute costs
516
      related to attacks. Increasing the computational cost of an attack by 2 orders of magnitude has
      very different implications for the practicality of an attack that currently costs $0.01 versus one that
517
      costs $10,000. An important direction for future work is to quantify the absolute costs of current
518
      attacks and defense, combining this with our scaling trends to forecast the cost of attacking and
519   defending future models. This would enable defenders to determine the cost of defending against
520   different categories of attacker—and at what point, if at all, attack cost exceeds the cost of the
521   attacker training a model without safeguards.
522
523
524
      8   C ONCLUSION
525
      We find that scaling attack and defense compute significantly and predictably improve attack and
526
      defense performance. Adversarial training is orders of magnitude more compute efficient as a de-
527
      fense than scaling base model size, and larger models generalize better from adversarial training.
528   This suggests substantially more robust models could be trained by diverting a small fraction of
529   pretraining compute towards adversarial training, with increasing benefits for larger models.
530
531
      Given both attack and defense benefit from scale, which has the upper hand? Currently offense is
      winning: an attacker consistently needs to less than double their attack compute in order to maintain
532
      the same success rate against a defender who doubled their adversarial training compute. However,
533
      the offense advantage is slight: even a modest and well-targeted algorithmic improvement could
534   shift the balance towards defense. We propose that actualizing this shift should be the key design
535   goal for new defense methods. Crucially, this will require a shift from today’s common practice of
536   evaluating defenses by a single point on the model size and defense compute frontier, to evaluating
537   defenses by their scaling trends.

       B     A DVERSARIAL ATTACKS
1297
1298   The primary attack we use is GCG from Zou et al. (2023). We use the simple, single-prompt version described
1299   in Algorithm 1 of Zou et al. (2023) with the modifiable subset I set to be the final N tokens of the text before
1300   the Answer: prompt. We use a suffix of length N = 10, batch size B = 128, and k = 256 top substitutions
1301   for all experiments. In Section 5.1 we also use GCG with a slightly different threat model, inserting N tokens
       90% of the way into the part of the prompt that varies among examples in each dataset. For example, in the
1302
       Spam dataset, the varying part of the prompt is everything after “HAM.” but before “Answer:”.
1303
1304   We describe the baseline RandomToken algorithm in Algorithm 2. RandomToken is designed to be similar
       to GCG except that RandomToken does not use gradient-guided search. Instead, for each iteration we replace
1305
       each token in the adversarial suffix with a new token chosen uniformly at random from the vocabulary of the
1306   model. We then evaluate the new prompt to see if it has caused the model to give an incorrect answer and stop
1307   the attack if it has. If no iteration was successful, we return the adversarial suffix from the final iteration. An
1308   iteration of RandomToken is much cheaper than an iteration of GCG, so we use much higher iteration counts
1309   for RandomToken than GCG.

       D.2    A DVERSARIAL T RAINING S ETUP
2107
2108   The adversarial training procedure described in Section 5 and visualized in Figure 21 starts with an empty pool
2109   of attacked examples. Then the algorithm iteratively performs the following steps:
2110           • Adversarially attack a subset of the original training dataset.
2111           • Add those attacked examples to the pool of attacked examples.
2112           • Train the model on a small dataset of clean and attacked datapoints, drawing from the original training
2113             set and the pool of attacked examples.
               • Save model checkpoint for future evaluation.

2134
2135   We begin with the finetuned model trained as in Section 4. In order for each round of adversarial training to
       use the same amount of compute for a given model size, we use a constant dataset size of 1,000 examples for
2136
       each round of adversarial training. Since we are constantly finding new attacked examples, we need a way to
2137   decide which ones to train on each round. In our experiments, we sample from a fixed set of nclean = 20,000
2138   clean examples (the original training dataset) and a growing set of nadv = 200 · r adversarial examples where
2139   r is the round number. From these combined clean and attacked datasets, we sample naug = 1000 datapoints
2140   on which to train each round. We sample sadv = min(80% × 1000, nadv ) from the adversarial dataset, and the
       remaining sclean = naug − sadv from the clean data.
2141
2142   We sample uniformly from the clean data whereas from the adversarial dataset we use exponential sampling to
2143   upweight both recent and successful examples. Before round 4, we take the whole adversarial dataset since we
       have fewer than 800 examples to choose from. After round 4, we rank all of the datapoints by loss (riloss : 0 <
2144
       i < nadv ) and by recency (ritime
                                        : 0 < i < nadv ), then take the simple mean of these two to aggregate to a single
2145   ranking ri = 21 riloss + ritime . We sample adversarial examples with exponential weights exp {λ · ri } where
2146                                             ln(2)
       λ = 0.005 corresponds to a half-life of 0.005   ≈ 140 examples.
2147
2148   As adversarial training continues, generating successful attacks becomes more difficult. In order to compensate
       for this, we employ a linear schedule in order to ramp up the attack strength across rounds of adversarial
2149
       training.4 In round r of a total R rounds, the number of iterations k used for the attack is given by k = kstart +
2150    r
          (kend − kstart ). For GCG, we use kstart = 8, kfinish = 64. For RandomToken, we use kstart = 1024, kfinish =
       R
2151   2048. In order to spend similar amounts of compute at each model size, we set R = 8 for 1B models,
2152   then scale up/down proportionally for smaller/larger models, clipped between 5 and 60 (250 when using the
2153   RandomToken attack) so that the 12B models run for 5 rounds while the 14M models run for 60 (250 for
       RandomToken) rounds.
2154
2155   We evaluate the models using a dataset size of 500 for both clean and attacked validation datasets.
2156       4
             With a fixed attack strength, the model in later rounds of adversarial training is extremely robust to attacks
2157   of that fixed strength and the adversarial attack struggles to succeed at all.

       E     E STIMATED C OMPUTE C ALCULATIONS
2431
2432   To estimate compute costs, we use approximations from Kaplan et al. (2020). To estimate training compute,
2433   we use the
2434                                               Ctrain ≈ 6N D
2435   approximation (where Ctrain is total training FLOPs, N is the number of parameters in the model, and D is the
2436   number of tokens in the dataset). To estimate the forward and backward pass costs, we use Cf orward ≈ 2N D
2437   and Cbackward ≈ 4N D respectively.
2438
2439   E.1    P RETRAINING C OMPUTE C ALCULATION
2440
       In many of our figures, we represent compute as a fraction of the pretraining cost. We do this to allow an
2441   apples-to-apples comparison of attacks of a fixed number of iterations across model sizes. Using GCG or Ran-
2442   domToken for a fixed number of iterations to attack a larger model takes more compute than to attack a smaller
2443   model. This is because the cost of each iteration is proportional to the cost of forward and backward passes
2444   through the target model. For Pythia models, the cost of forward and backward passes is also proportional to
       pretraining compute because all Pythia model sizes were trained on a fixed dataset of 300B tokens (Biderman
2445
       et al., 2023). Thus to compute the pretraining cost, we use Ctrain ≈ (1.8 × 1012 )N , where N is the number
2446   of parameters in the model.
2447
       The exact number of pretraining tokens used for Qwen2.5 is not currently public, but we estimate it by com-
2448
       bining the total number of tokens used for training Qwen2.5 models (18T) with the spread of tokens used
2449   for training Qwen2.5 (12T for Qwen2-0.5B, and 7T for all larger Qwen2 models). This gives 18T tokens for
2450   Qwen2.5-0.5B, and 10.5T tokens for all larger Qwen2.5 models.
2451
2452   E.2    A DVERSARIAL T RAINING C OMPUTE C ALCULATION
2453
2454   The compute cost of adversarial training (Cadv ) consists of two parts: the training cost (Ctrain ), and the
       adversarial example search cost (Csearch ); that is, Cadv = Ctrain + Csearch .
2455
2456   We estimate both Ctrain and Csearch empirically, by recording how many forward and backward passes are
2457   used in each round of adversarial training and applying the Cf orward = 2N D and Cbackward = 4N D
       approximations.
2458
2459   Ctrain and Csearch are not constant across rounds of adversarial training (see Appendix D): we train on more
2460   examples per round, resulting in Ctrain increasing; and we increase the strength of the attack used to search
       for adversarial examples, resulting in Csearch increasing. Despite both increasing, the ratio Ctrain to Csearch
2461
       is not constant across rounds since they increase at different rates.
2462
2463
       E.3    A DVERSARIAL ATTACK C OMPUTE C ALCULATION
2464
2465   The estimated cost Csearch represents the attack compute required to run the attack on the whole dataset, rather
2466   than the attack compute required to attack a single example. For Figure 7, we divide by the size of the dataset
2467
       to get per-example compute, since we are interested in the question of how much compute an attacker would
       have to spend to have a chance of jailbreaking the model once.

2474
       in our code that occasionally resulted in an underestimation of FLOPs spent when evaluating across multiple

       H       ROBUSTNESS T RANSFER
2539
2540   Does adversarial training protect against different attacks? A concern we might have is that at
2541   deploy time, our model is subjected to attacks that were unknown (or did not exist) at train time. Can our
2542   adversarially trained model hope to defend against new attacks? We look for insight into this question by ad-
2543   versarially training our models on the RandomToken attack and then attacking with the GCG attack. Figure 28
       shows models adversarially trained on RandomToken do perform better than undefended models, though the
2544
       effect is quite weak. In this case, adversarial training appears to benefit smaller models more than large mod-
2545   els, with the slope of improvement of small models being steeper. However, only one of the models across
2546   two tasks achieves a below 50% attack success rate, suggesting that the main result of this experiment is that
2547   adversarially training against RandomToken does not confer a meaningful amount of robustness against a
2548   much stronger attack like GCG. This result suggests that it is important to use a similar attack during adversarial
       training as expected at deployment. However, further work is needed to determine whether adversarial training
2549
       on RandomToken fails because it is a different kind of attack, or simply because it is a much weaker attack.
