009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Real-world scenarios frequently involve multi-objective data-driven optimization
012            problems, characterized by unknown problem coefficients and multiple conflicting
013            objectives. Traditional two-stage methods independently apply a machine learn-
014            ing model to estimate problem coefficients, followed by solving the predicted op-
015            timization problem. The independent use of optimization solvers and prediction
016
               models may lead to suboptimal performance due to mismatches between their ob-
               jectives. Recent efforts have focused on end-to-end training of predictive mod-
017
               els that use decision loss derived from the downstream optimization problem.
018
               However, these methods have primarily focused on single-objective optimization
019            problems, thus limiting their applicability. We aim to propose a multi-objective
020            decision-focused approach to address this gap. In order to better align with the
021            inherent properties of multi-objective optimization, we propose a set of novel loss
022            functions. These loss functions are designed to capture the discrepancies between
023            predicted and true decision problems, considering solution space, objective space,
024            and decision quality, named landscape loss, Pareto set loss, and decision loss,
025            respectively. Our experimental results demonstrate that our proposed method sig-
026            nificantly outperforms traditional two-stage methods and most current decision-
027
               focused methods.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Uncertain decision-making is prevalent in various real-life scenarios (Kotary et al., 2021). These
032   scenarios involve a workflow for handling data-driven decision problems where parameter coeffi-
033   cients are predicted based on environmental or historical information, and decisions are made using
034   these predictions. Most traditional approaches decompose this workflow into the prediction phase
035   and the decision phase. Obtaining a perfect prediction model is often unachievable. The problem co-
036   efficients generated by the prediction model are frequently noisy. Given that conventional prediction
037   models prioritize predictive accuracy, they often neglect the structure and attributes of downstream
038   optimization problems. However, in most of optimization problems, the impact of problem coeffi-
039
      cients on the final decision is not uniform. Without a decision-focused approach, decisions derived
      from imperfectly predicted coefficients are more prone to deviate from the optimal solution. Con-
040
      sequently, the separation of the prediction and decision phases often leads to suboptimal outcomes
041
      due to the misalignment of objectives between these phases.(Amos & Kolter, 2017). Addressing this
042   issue, the integration of prediction models with decision problems for unified training has emerged
043   as a promising direction across various domains (Kotary et al., 2022; Vlastelica et al., 2020).
044
045
      The integration of optimization problems into deep learning frameworks poses significant chal-
      lenges, particularly because the mapping from predicted coefficients to the optimal decision vari-
046
      able is non-differentiable. To address the aforementioned issue, numerous decision-focused meth-
047
      ods have been proposed to train a predictive model by minimizing decision loss associated with the
048   downstream optimization task (Kotary et al., 2021). Considering the diverse forms and complex-
049   ity of real-world optimization problems, as well as the time-intensive aspect of iterative problem-
050   solving, prior research has concentrated on two principal directions. The first direction seeks to
051   broaden the applicability of the approach by formulating additional optimization problems of var-
052   ied forms, employing differentiable operators (Amos & Kolter, 2017; Wilder et al., 2019). The
053   second direction (Shah et al., 2022) entails the development of efficient decision surrogate func-
      tions to diminish training time or amplify decision-focused performance. However, previous studies


                                                       1

054
      have predominantly concentrated on the domain of single-objective problems, with less emphasis
055   on extending the learning paradigms into the domain of multi-objective optimization (MOP).
056
057   Numerous data-driven multi-objective problems exist in real-world scenarios (Gunantara, 2018).
058
      The MOP demonstrate increased complexity in the search space compared to their single-objective
      counterparts. Optimal solutions for multi-objective problems are frequently non-unique and cor-
059
      respond to a Pareto front. Additionally, the gradient of multiple objectives often exhibit conflict-
060
      ing directions. Given the challenges and complexity, we aim to propose a novel Multi-Objective
061   Decision-Focused Learning (MoDFL) model to address these gaps.
062
063   To address the mentioned inquiries, we propose three decision-focused loss functions tailored for
064
      MOP. The proposed loss function involves three modules, which measure the distance of objec-
      tive space, solution space as well as the decision quality of representative point. Specifically, we
065
      introduce a landscape loss based on the sample rank maximum mean discrepancy (sRMMD) to
066
      quantify the discrepancy in the objective space across optimization problems. The objective space
067   is considered a manifold within high-dimensional space, represented by the set of objective vectors
068   corresponding to the solutions. We propose a Pareto set loss to directly measure the distance within
069   the Pareto set, aiming to circumvent the homogeneity that may impede model training in certain
070   optimization problems. The decision loss is analogous to other decision-focused losses, utilizing
071   the decision quality of a representative solution as the loss criterion. This representative solution
072   is derived from employing the weighted sum method in this paper. Differentiation of the proposed
073   loss function is achieved by reparameterization method and transforming the multi-objective prob-
074   lem into a corresponding single-objective one. Building on these modules, MoDFL integrates the
075
      predictive model and MOP into a unified pipeline, which enables end-to-end training of the system.
076   The remainder of this paper is structured as follows. Section II provides an overview of the related
077   work. Section III details the formulation of the data-driven multi-objective problem and introduces
078   pertinent definitions. Section IV provides a motivating example on multi-objective decision learn-
079   ing. Section V present three novel loss functions, explore the differentiation of MOP, and explain
080   the integration of the aforementioned modules within MoDFL. The performance of MoDFL is com-
081
      pared to that of two-stage methods and other state-of-the-art DFL method in Section VI. Finally, we
      conclude this paper in Section VII.

086   2.1   D IFFERENTIATION OF O PTIMIZATION P ROBLEMS
087
088   A plethora of studies has investigated the integration of prediction problems with downstream
089   decision-making processes. The scope of this study includes methodologies such as Smart Predict-
090   and-Optimize (SPO)(Elmachtoub & Grigas, 2022), End-to-End Optimization Learning(Kotary et al.,
091   2021), and Decision-Focused Learning (DFL)(Mandi et al., 2021). The central aspect of this topic
092   aims to the differentiable mapping from problem coefficients to optimal decision variables, a concept
093
      we denote as the decision gradient herein. Utilizing the decision gradient enables the formulation
      of many optimization problems as differentiable operators within gradient-based methods like neu-
094
      ral networks. Much of the seminal literature in this field stems from the work of Amos (Amos
095
      & Kolter, 2017). This approach proposed in (Amos & Kolter, 2017) leveraged the Karush-Kuhn-
096   Tucker (KKT) optimality conditions for quadratic programming and the implicit function theorem
097   to construct decision gradients. Nevertheless, this method’s limitation lies in its requisite for a full-
098   rank Hessian matrix of the objective function, constraining its applicability. Subsequent research has
099   addressed this constraint by employing elaborate techniques to generalize the methodology across
100   a broader spectrum of optimization problems. For instance, Wilder et al. (2019) introduced one
101   quadratic regularization term into the objective function to extend the method proposed by Amos
102   (Amos & Kolter, 2017) to linear programming (LP). Ferber et al. (2020) extended DFL to mixed-
103   integer programming (MIP), relaxing MIP to LP by applying a cutting plane at the root LP node
104
      and leveraging Wilder’s method (Wilder et al., 2019) to enable end-to-end training. Xie et al. (2020)
      approached the top-K problem through the lens of the optimal transport problem, extending Mandi
105
      and Guns’s work (Mandi et al., 2020) to tackle it.
106
107   Another category of methods has emerged from the smart predict-and-optimize (SPO) method (El-
      machtoub & Grigas, 2022). This approach primarily focuses on optimization problems with linear


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      objectives and involves predicting the parameters of the optimization problem. It introduces a con-
109   vex surrogate upper bound on the loss, facilitating an accessible subgradient method. Vlastelica
110   et al. (2020) also explore linear objective problems, computing decision gradients by perturbing the
111   predicted problem coefficients. Expanding on this, Niepert et al. (2021) improve the method by
112   incorporating noise perturbations into perturbation-based implicit differentiation to maximize the
113   posterior distribution. Notably, all these methods require iterative addressing optimization prob-
114   lems during training, incurring substantial computational costs. To mitigate this issue, Mulamba
115   et al. (2020) implemented a solution cache to record solutions discovered during model training
116   and devised a surrogate decision loss function based on contrastive loss to reduce the time spent
117
      solving optimization problems. Kong et al. (2022) employed an energy-based model to character-
      ize decision loss, thereby reducing the overhead in end-to-end training by linking minimum energy
118
      with minimum decision loss. Shah et al. (2022) introduced the surrogate convex loss functions to
119
      alleviate the computational burden, such as WeightedMSE, etc. Mandi et al. (2021) leveraged the
120   learning-to-rank concept to devise a proxy for decision loss and introduced four surrogate decision
121   loss functions.

      In single-objective optimization problems, the objective space is one-dimensional, where a partial
      ordering can characterize the relationship between the objective function values of two solutions.
154

155
      quate for representing the distance in objective space across different problems. Moreover, the strin-
156   gent criteria for establishing Pareto dominance often result in numerous objective vectors lacking
157   any dominance relationship, especially for many-objective optimization problems. To address these
158   limitations, we analogize the multi-dimensional objective space to the manifold in high-dimensional
159   space, such as images, video, or audio signals. We use the concept of neighborhood relations in
160   high-dimensional spaces to clearly define how different sets of objectives are connected and to iden-
161   tify common patterns in their overall distribution. Specifically, we utilize the objectives of solu-
      tions found during training model to approximately represent the objective space, and employ the

403   5.2.2   E VALUATION METRIC
404   We evaluate the performance of algorithms by the quality of their decisions in relation to a true
405   optimization problem. While the objective function value is a direct measure, it may be skewed by
406   differing scales across objectives. Therefore, we adopt the average percentage regret as a key metric
407   to judge performance, with a lower value indicating superior outcomes. Additionally, we consider
408   three other metrics commonly employed in MOP analyses: generational distance (GD) (Ishibuchi
409   et al., 2015), maximum Pareto front error (MPFE), and hyper area ratio (HAR). GD gauges the
410   proximity of the predicted Pareto front to the true front, MPFE measures the maximal deviation
411   of approximate solutions from the optimal set, and HAR assesses the relative coverage area of the
412
      Pareto fronts. Collectively, these metrics help determine the efficacy of the proposed solutions in
      approximating the true Pareto front. Detailed formulations and computational approaches for these
413
      metrics are provided in the Appendix.
