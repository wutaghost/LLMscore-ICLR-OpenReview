009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Neutrino telescopes detect rare interactions of particles produced in some of the
012            most extreme environments in the Universe. This is accomplished by instrument-
013            ing a cubic-kilometer volume of naturally occurring transparent medium with light
014            sensors. Given their substantial size and the high frequency of background in-
015            teractions, these telescopes amass an enormous quantity of large variance, high-
016
               dimensional data. These attributes create substantial challenges for analyzing and
               reconstructing interactions, particularly when utilizing machine learning (ML)
017
               techniques. In this paper, we present a novel approach, called om2vec, that
018
               employs transformer-based variational autoencoders to efficiently represent neu-
019            trino telescope events by learning compact and descriptive latent representations.
020            We demonstrate that these latent representations offer enhanced flexibility and
021            improved computational efficiency, thereby facilitating downstream tasks in data
022            analysis.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Neutrino telescopes search for rare interactions caused by neutrinos, an elusive particle central to
028
      many open questions in the Standard Model of particle physics, such as the origin of their masses
      and the matter anti-matter asymmetry of the universe. The leading detector in operation today, the
029
      IceCube Neutrino Observatory, comprises 5,160 optical modules (OMs) arranged on strings deep
030
      within the clear ice of the Antarctic glacier (see Aartsen et al. (2017)). Each OM is designed to
031   precisely record the arrival times of photons, forming a photon arrival time distribution (PATD). The
032   data gathered by IceCube are archived as ”events,” which are triggered when a specific number of
033   coincident light detections occur between OMs in neighboring strings, signaling the presence of a
034   charged particle. Figure 1 is an artistic representation of the photons produced in a neutrino inter-
035   action and their subsequent detection in the OMs. A neutrino telescope typically classifies events
036   into categories based on the morphology of the light detected: cascade-like events, identifiable by
037   their approximately spherical spatial distribution; and track-like events, characterized by their elon-
038   gated, linear spatial morphology. In IceCube, events are recorded at an approximate rate of 3000
039
      per second, constituting a large data rate. Moreover, comparable or even larger optical neutrino
      telescopes are either under construction or planned, such as KM3NeT (Aiello et al., 2019), Baikal-
040
      GVD (Avrorin et al., 2018), P-ONE (Agostini et al., 2020), IceCube-Gen2 (Aartsen et al., 2021),
041
      TRIDENT (Ye et al., 2022), and HUNT (Huang et al., 2023), signaling a need to develop methods
042   for efficiently handling neutrino telescope data.
043
044   The accurate reconstruction and prediction of the interacting particle’s physical properties—
045
      including its energy, direction, and type— from the PATDs recorded by each OM, is essential for
      informing and facilitating downstream physics analyses. A significant challenge in this task is the
046
      incorporation of the full-timing information provided by the PATDs of the OMs, which are char-
047
      acterized by high dimensionality and variable lengths. Interaction events may last several µs, yet
048   ns-scale timing resolution is needed on the PATDs for most downstream physics analyses. Further-
049   more, as illustrated by OM#1 in Figure 1, some OMs near the interaction point can detect up to tens
050   of thousands of photons in the highest-energy and brightest events. On the other hand, many further
051   OMs record only a few photons, resulting in highly irregular events; illustrated in the OM#2 distri-
052   bution on the same figure. In this paper, we introduce a transformer-based variational autoencoder
053   (VAE), termed om2vec, which encodes PATDs into a fixed-size compact latent representation.
      om2vec is trained to reconstruct the input PATD as readout by OMs, using only the information


                                                        1

054
      from its encoded latent space. In this sense, it learns an efficient and more flexible representation
055   of the data recorded in each OM. Our approach provides several significant advantages over exist-
056   ing methods, including improved information retention, greater reliability, faster processing speed,
057   and reduced hyperparameter dependence. We also demonstrate that utilizing transformers for this
058   particular problem is particularly effective, by comparing it to a baseline fully-connected network.
059   We argue that these advantages make this the first “one-size-fits-all” neutrino event representation
060   learner, making it suitable for deployment at the earliest stages of experimental data collection. This
061   will serve to circumvent the challenges inherent in raw neutrino telescope data, and facilitate the
062   rapid adoption of more sophisticated ML techniques from the image-processing community into the
063
      neutrino physics field. Source code, datasets, and pre-trained checkpoints can be found on GitHub.

099   1.1   R ELATED W ORKS
100
      Machine learning techniques have successfully been applied to neutrino reconstruction tasks in re-
101
      cent years, notably in neutrino telescopes such as IceCube (Choma et al., 2018; Abbasi et al., 2021;
102   Huennefeld et al., 2021; Abbasi et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2023; Eller, 2023; Jin et al., 2024; Zhu
103   et al., 2024) and KM3NeT (De Sio, 2019; Aiello et al., 2020; Reck et al., 2021; Guderian, 2022;
104   Mauro & de Wasseige, 2023). Previous studies have employed various approaches to address the
105   challenges of working with neutrino telescope data. One approach uses summary statistics (Abbasi
106   et al., 2021), utilizing key variables that summarize the PATD of a given OM. This method yields
107   greater efficiency and flexibility, as each OM has a fixed-size description of the timing distribu-
      tion, allowing events to be formatted as 2D images with multiple features per pixel. However, the


                                                        2

      summary statistics lose a substantial amount of information present in the original PATD. An alter-
109   native method involves parameterizing the PATD by fitting an asymmetric Gaussian mixture model
110   (AGMM). In this study, we implemented a basic AGMM to serve as a comparison, drawing inspira-
111   tion from Huennefeld et al. (2021). While this retains significantly more information than summary
112   statistics, the optimization process can be slow and prone to failure, with a strong dependence on
113   hyperparameters, e.g., the number of Gaussian components. Our transformer-based VAE approach
114   builds upon these previous methods while addressing their limitations.

145   Since their inception, VAEs have been extensively utilized for learning effective data representations
146   and generative modeling (Kingma & Welling, 2022). More recently, transformers have demonstrated
147
      exceptional performance in handling sequential data (Vaswani et al., 2017). Given the time series
      nature of our dataset, we incorporate transformer layers to enhance feature learning capabilities.
148
      Our model employs the standard encoder-decoder structure, as illustrated in Figure 2. We assume a
149
      Gaussian prior for the latent space and train the encoder to learn the parameters µ and σ, with some
150   added random noise ϵ. The re-parameterization trick is then utilized to construct the latent represen-
151   tation z while maintaining proper gradient flow to these parameters. A key property of VAEs over
152   regular autoencoders is their continuous latent space, meaning that similar representations within
153   this space correspond to similar reconstructed PATDs.
154
      The input data PATDs are formatted as one-dimensional time series sequences of equal length, with
155
      each element corresponding to a specific time in sequence. Each element features a single attribute:
156   the number of photon hits occurring during that particular time window. We first expand the feature
157   space with an input embedding layer. The encoder and decoder are constructed by stacking blocks.
158   Importantly, we employ a hybrid approach that operates across both the feature and sequence dimen-
159   sions. Transformer layers process the feature dimension, followed by a feed-forward network that
160   down-samples or up-samples the sequence length in the encoder and decoder, respectively. Linear
161   layers are then used to flatten the feature dimension into latent representations, as well as to generate
      output logits after the decoder. We use a simple vector of learnable parameters, the ”memory em-


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      bedding,” which acts as the memory input for the transformer decoder layers. After the final linear
163   layer, the outputs are fed through the softmax function to obtain a properly normalized probability
164   density. We interpret the final softmax activation’s output as the probability of detecting a photon
165   hit in each timing bin.

      3    DATASETS
168
169
      Simulations play a key role in neutrino physics experiments, facilitating preliminary testing that
170   informs experiment development and subsequent physics analyses. As such, the field has devel-
171   oped sophisticated simulation tools capable of generating events for incorporation into our training
172   datasets. In this paper, we present results trained and tested on simulated events, but emphasize that
173   the methodology can be readily applied to real detector data. We employ the open-source simulation
174   toolkit Prometheus (Lazar et al., 2024) to emulate an IceCube-like array of OMs within Antarctic
175   ice. We generate four datasets of events based on the different neutrino interaction types, correspond-
176   ing to the distinct flavors of the neutrino, ν. Specifically, these interactions are the charged-current
177   electron neutrino (νe CC), muon neutrino (νµ CC), and tau neutrino (ντ CC) interactions, alongside
178
      the neutral-current (ν NC) interactions. Notably, the νe CC, ντ CC, and ν NC interactions produce
      predominantly spherical cascade-like events, while the νµ CC interactions result in long track-like
179
      events. Additionally, as further detailed in Section 5, the PATDs generated by ντ CC interactions
180
      are of particular interest to physicists due to their potential to exhibit a smoking-gun “double-bang”
181   signature of astrophysical neutrinos.
182
183   Events are simulated, and all OMs detecting at least one photon hit are included in the overall
184
      training dataset. We extract five million PATDs from both νe CC and ν NC interactions, as their
      signals are quite similar. Additionally, 7.5 million PATDs are sourced from νµ CC events, and 3.25
185
      million PATDs from ντ CC interactions, totaling approximately 20 million PATDs for the training
186
      dataset. An additional 4.5 million distributions are reserved for the test dataset, mixed roughly
187   equally from each interaction type. The majority of OMs registering light detect only a few photon
188   hits. For an illustration of this distribution, see the bottom panel in Figure 3. Notably, photon yields
189   from interactions increase significantly at higher energies. The simulated events span a wide range
190   of energies to encompass all types of PATDs that may occur in neutrino telescopes.
191
      As previously mentioned, each PATD in the datasets is pre-processed into a fixed-length vector by
192
      binning photon hits over time. A total of 6400 bins, each 1 ns wide, represent the first 6.4 µs
193   of hits—a readout time frame consistent with IceCube’s OMs (Aartsen et al., 2017). The choice
194   of binning and time window can be adapted to suit the specifications of other neutrino telescopes.
195   Any remaining hits are accumulated in a final overflow bin. The hit counts in each bin are then
196   log-normalized, as they can be extremely large for high-energy neutrino interactions.

452   6   C ONCLUSIONS AND F UTURE D IRECTIONS
453
454   This work introduces om2vec, a novel approach utilizing transformer-based VAEs to efficiently
455   represent neutrino telescope events. Our method addresses the significant challenges posed by the
456   large size, high dimensionality, and variance of data in neutrino telescopes. By learning compact
457   and descriptive latent representations, om2vec offers several advantages for downstream analysis
458   tasks.
459   We have demonstrated that these learned latent representations not only preserve critical information
460   from the original PATDs but also provide enhanced flexibility and substantial computational benefits.
461   Our experiments show that models trained on these latent representations can achieve comparable
462   performance to those using full-timing information in crucial tasks such as angular reconstruction.
463   The enhanced efficiency and adaptability of om2vec encoding for neutrino telescope events paves
464   the way for more sophisticated ML algorithms to be applied to neutrino telescope events. In particu-
465   lar, employing image-based algorithms in neutrino telescopes becomes straightforward by encoding
466   the data using om2vec.
467   Another future direction could involve exploring data throughput rate reduction through latent repre-
468   sentations. In existing active telescopes like IceCube, OM readouts are restricted by data throughput
469   rate limits imposed by various physical and networking factors. Considering that high-energy events
470   can result in tens of thousands of photons hits on a single OM, utilizing latent representations could
471   decrease the throughput rate, enabling experiments such as IceCube to store higher-resolution timing
472   information than is currently feasible.
473   By bridging the gap between the complex, high-dimensional data of neutrino telescopes and efficient
474   ML techniques, om2vec represents an important step forward in our ability to sharpen our view of
475   the neutrino sky. As our telescopes grow larger and data rates increase, our capability to efficiently
476   process events will play an increasingly crucial role in pushing the boundaries of neutrino astronomy.
477   Availability: Source code, datasets, and pre-trained checkpoints for om2vec on Prometheus
478   events is made available on GitHub. Additionally, as an immediate next step, om2vec is being
479   integrated into GraphNeT (Søgaard et al., 2023), an open-source deep learning pipeline widely
480   utilized by various collaborations in the neutrino telescope community.
