009                                                     A BSTRACT
010
011                As machine learning increasingly relies on large amounts of data, concerns about
012                privacy and ethics have grown. Recently, methods for generating synthetic data to
013                augment or replace real datasets have emerged to mitigate these concerns. In this
014                paper, we demonstrate improved performance on a discriminative task when train-
015                ing on a mix of real and synthetic data, compared to training solely on the original
016                real data. Our synthetic data is generated using a novel sampling method based
017                on a conditional generative model and a discriminator, both trained exclusively on
018
                   the original data, with no need for auxiliary data nor pre-trained foundation mod-
                   els. We consider the challenging task of face recognition, which is well known
019
                   for its privacy and ethical issues. Using our augmented dataset, we demonstrate
020
                   consistent improvements over the model trained on the original dataset, on various
021                benchmarks including IJB-C and IJB-B by up to 5% while performing competi-
022                tively with state-of-the-art synthetic data generation 1 .

025   1       I NTRODUCTION
026
027   As machine learning increasingly relies on data for specific applications, the need for high-quality,
028   accurately labeled datasets is becoming a significant challenge. Moreover, the collection of large
029
      datasets required to meet high-performance demands poses even greater challenges when consider-
      ing privacy and ethical concerns, particularly in sensitive domains such as human face images. One
030
      possible solution that recently become popular is synthesizing the data Wood et al. (2021); Azizi
031
      et al. (2023); Rahimi et al. (2024); DeAndres-Tame et al. (2024); Bae et al. (2023). The synthetic
032   datasets are generated from different methodologies including 3D-Rendering Graphics and Genera-
033   tive Models (e.g., GANs and Diffusion Models) to name a few. Sometimes because we can generate
034   many examples using our generation methodologies we can even surpass the performance of the
035   model which is trained using real data. An example of this is the work by Wood et al. (2021), which
036   showed that using a 3D-rendering engine and a mesh-based face model enables the generation of
037   precise labels for dense prediction tasks like face landmark localization. This approach can surpass
038   the models trained on the real data as human annotations for such datasets are usually difficult to
039   gather and real datasets are small. The synthetic data can also be created using generative models.
040
      Since the introduction of VAEs Kingma (2013), GANs Goodfellow et al. (2020); Karras et al. (2019;
      2021), and more recently Diffusion models Song et al. (2020); Karras et al. (2022; 2024); Hooge-
041
      boom et al. (2023); Gu et al. (2024), the pace of generative model development has significantly
042
      accelerated. These models are often just compared with each other using metrics such as Fréchet
043   Distance (FD) Stein et al. (2023); Heusel et al. (2017), which essentially evaluate how likely gener-
044   ators are to generate samples that look alike to their training datasets, or, in the case of text-to-image
045   generative models, using subjective qualitative metrics like user preferences Esser et al. (2024).
046
      In this paper, we emphasize looking at the usage of the generative models as an augmentation
047
      tool not just solely generating images in respect to metrics such as FD. Currently, the trend is to
048
      use generative models Rombach et al. (2022) that are trained on large datasets like LAION-5B
049   Schuhmann et al. (2022). Later, they refine the models for downstream tasks (e.g., classification)
050   using techniques like fine-tuning on separate datasets, prompt engineering, or textual inversion to
051   incorporate augmentation based on generative models Azizi et al. (2023) Trabucco et al. (2024).
052   In the domain of face images, DCFace Kim et al. (2023), authors generated synthetic face images
053

055   CASIA-WebFace for training their method, they employed separate robust face recognition (FR)
056   systems to filter samples based on similarity and other auxiliary networks to balance the generated

      2   BACKGROUND
091
092
      Usage of Synthetic Data in Computer Vision. For a smaller number of class variations, (e.g.,
093   2 or 3 classes for classification target), authors in Frid-Adar et al. (2018) train separate generative
094   models. This approach is not scalable for a higher number of classes and variations of our target
095   (e.g., we have thousands of classes for training an FR system). In Azizi et al. (2023), the authors fine-
096   tuned pre-trained diffusion models on ImageNet classes after training on large text-image datasets,
097   demonstrating improved performance on this benchmark through the synthesis of new samples.
098   Recently authors in Kupyn & Rupprecht (2024) introduced the Instance Augmentation method to
099   augment images by redrawing individual objects in the scene retaining their original shape using
100   pre-trained text-to-image models.
101
      Usage of Synthetic Data in Face Recognition. Authors in DCFace Kim et al. (2023) have used
102   dual condition latent diffusion models (LDM), one for the style and the other for identity. Similar
103   to our approach, they used CASIA-WebFace Yi et al. (2014) for training their method. By applying
104   auxiliary networks based on race and a separate strong face recognition (FR) system, they filtered
105   the generated images to create their dataset. In Sevastopolskiy et al. (2023), the authors collected a
106   large set of unlabeled face images from various ethnicities and pre-trained a StyleGAN2-ADA (SG2)
107   model Karras et al. (2020). They then trained an encoder to map images to SG2’s latent space.
      By transferring the encoder’s weights to the face recognition (FR) network, they demonstrated a


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      bias-mitigated version of the final FR system. GANDiffFace Melzi et al. (2023) uses a pre-trained
109   Stable Diffusion model Rombach et al. (2022) trained on the large LAION-5B dataset Schuhmann
110   et al. (2022). The method involves two steps: first, synthesizing identities using StyleGAN3 Kar-
111   ras et al. (2021) and transforming them in its latent space. Then, by applying Stable Diffusion and
112   DreamBooth Ruiz et al. (2023) fine-tuning, they introduce more intra-class variability. In IDiff-face
113   Boutros et al. (2023), a LDM was conditioned on the embedding space of a face recognition (FR)
114   system trained on the MS1Mv2 dataset to generate their data. Authors in DigiFace1M Bae et al.
115   (2023) used 3D rendering pipelines to generate various intra-class variabilities of different identities
116   by accessorizing them and rendering the final 3D model in different poses, expressions, and lighting
117
      conditions. Recently in Rahimi et al. (2024), authors used off-the-shelf image-to-image transla-
      tion methodologies without any identity information and demonstrated performance improvement
118
      in comparison to the original DigiFace1M.
119
120   As discussed here, while most methods rely on auxiliary datasets and models to show improvement
121   in some datasets, we take a different approach, demonstrating a performance boost without using
122   any additional models or data in most of the FR benchmarks.

      3.1   D ISCRIMINATIVE M ODEL
163
164   Assume that we have a dataset, Dorig , consist of k pairs of image and label,
165   {(X0 , y0 ), (X1 , y1 ), . . . , (Xk , yk )}. Xi is an image of the form RH×W×3 , for simplicity here
166   we assume that the H and W are fixed for all of the k sample pairs in our dataset. yi is a scalar
167   that depicts the label of images from a fixed set of l possible values (e.g., {0, 1, ..., l}), L, which
168   l ≪ k. Here the goal of a discriminative model is to learn the conditional probability p(y|X)
169   using the samples in Dorig . For example here the Dorig can be the ImageNet Russakovsky et al.
170   (2015) or CASIA-WebFace Yi et al. (2014) dataset. The discriminative model’s task is to identify
171
      the most likely class of the image. In most cases this enforces the features (i.e., usually output of
      penultimate layer, e) for the similar images to be closer together according to a measure, m (e.g.,
172
      Cosine Similarity, the lower the output of m(e1 , e2 ) the more similar the features e1 and e2 are).
173
      This is usually learned by a mapping function fθdis : X → y parameterized by θdis which uniquely
174   describes the architecture and the parameters of the f . The θdis is learned through empirical risk
175   minimization as follows:
176                                      ∗
                                        θdis  = argmin E(X,y)∼Dorig [Ldis (fθdis (X), y)]                 (1)
177                                      θdis ∈Θdis
178    Where for a classification problem the, Ldis , is usually in the form of Cross-Entropy and the Ex-
179   pectation here is being calculated by drawing sample pairs from, Dorig . Here we refer to possible
                                                   ∗
180   hyperparameters for calculating the θdis         as hdis , which tries to abstract out the processes such as
181   Learning rates and its schedules, number of epochs for training and other complexities in the real
182   world training of a neural network. As depicted in Figure 2, The outcome of this process is a model,
183   Morig , (i.e., fθdis∗ ), which tries to reflect that the model is trained on the Dorig .

      3.2   G ENERATIVE M ODEL
186   Generative models aim to capture the underlying distribution of the dataset given some samples,
187   such that new samples can be drawn from it. Here we present general problem formulation in the
188   context of the diffusion models, in which we also demonstrate the experiments using these types of
189   models in section 4. The idea of the diffusion models Song et al. (2020); Anderson (1982) is that
190   by sequentially adding noise to the data (i.e., Xi s ) and learning a denoiser/score function, S. This is
191   done to gradually learn to go from a complete white Gaussian noise (i.e., in the sampling process)
192   to the data distribution, by adding noise to the data in different scales and learning the noise that
193
      was added to the data during the training process (i.e., supervision). Following Karras et al. (2024;
      2022) formulation, S, the denoiser function can be learned in two stages. The first stage is that given
194
      a noise level, σ, we optimize the parameters of the denoiser (i.e., θden ) by adding a noise Nh×w×c
195
      which is dependent on σ and removing it by the denoiser function as follows:
196
197              L(Sθden ; σ) = E(X,y)∼Dorig ,N∼N (0,σI) [||Sθden (EVAE (X) + N; c(y), σ) − X||22 ]        (2)
198    Here the image and its corresponding label, (X, y) are sampled from, Dorig . The X is passed to a
199   VAE encoder, Zh×w×c = EVAE (X) as we are working on the latent diffusion paradigm Rombach
200   et al. (2022). Later this Z can be transformed back to image space using the VAE’s decoder, X ≃
201   DVAE (EVAE (X)). Latent diffusion models are mostly popular because of lower computational cost
202   with respect to pixel-level diffusion models, especially in higher resolution. The denoising is done
203   in the latent space of the VAE and later decoded to the pixel space. The denoiser function takes
204   the noisy input, noise scale, and the class condition, c(y) as input and tries to estimate the original
205   latent, EVAE (X). The second stage is to iterate over different data-dependent noise scales which
206
      reduces the final optimization target to:
                                   ∗
207                               θden = argmin Eσ∼N (µ,σ2 ) [λσ L(Sθden ; σ)]                            (3)
                                           θden ∈Θden
208
209    Which λσ is noise scale dependent weight, and µ and σ in N (µ, σ 2 ) are empirically set to focus
210   the training on more important noise levels for the latent space of a VAE Karras et al. (2024; 2022);
211   Rombach et al. (2022).
212   In our formulation, we used one-hot encoding for the c function, meaning that, if the dataset Dorig
213   contains l unique labels, c maps each i ∈ {0, 1, . . . , l} to a unique vector of size l that the value
214   corresponding to the label is 1, while the rest are set to 0. After training the conditional denoiser (i.e.,
215   Sθden ), using Equation 3, as depicted in the middle of Figure 2, we can sample from the generator in
      two ways.

521   5      C ONCLUSIONS
522
      We have shown that by using a generator and discriminative model trained on a single dataset, we
523
      can generate an augmented dataset that will boost the performance of the discriminative model on
524
      several FR benchmarks, without relying on any auxiliary data or pre-trained model. We consistently
525   outperformed the baseline discriminator model on various evaluation benchmarks, unlike other state-
526   of-the-art models whose performance improvements were not consistent across evaluations.
527
528   Future work. Our method can be considered a general formulation of MixUp Zhang (2017) and
529   CutMix Yun et al. (2019), but instead of cropping or blending images, we use a generator to create
530
      a new class. One interesting research direction would be to test if we can reformulate the margin
      losses used in FR to be compatible with the soft labels. Later by establishing a correlation between
531
      the target soft labels and the c∗ , (e.g., for α and β set to 0.7 which increases the mtotal an obvious
532
      choice for soft target labels would be 0.5 and 0.5 for the corresponding source classes) one can study
533   would it be beneficiary to treat the class as a soft-class or a new one.
534
535   Reproducibility. All code for the discriminative and generative models, along with the generated
536   datasets and trained models, will be publicly available for reproducibility.

845   C      D OWNSTREAM P ERFORMANCE VS M ETRICS IN G ENERATIVE M ODELS
846
847   In this section, we examine whether there is a correlation between common metrics for evaluat-
848   ing generative models and the discriminator’s performance when trained on our augmented dataset.
849   We studied the FD Heusel et al. (2017) Precision/Recall Sajjadi et al. (2018); Kynkäänniemi et al.
850   (2019) and Coverage Naeem et al. (2020) which is usually used to quantify the performance of the
851   Generative Models. Calculation of these metrics requires the projection of the images into mean-
852   ingful feature spaces. For feature extraction, we consider two backbones, Inception-V3 Szegedy
853   et al. (2016) and DINOv2 Oquab et al. (2023) which are shown effective for evaluating diffusion
854   models Stein et al. (2023). Both these models were trained using the ImageNet Russakovsky et al.
855
      (2015) in a supervised and semi-supervised manner respectively. Experiments were performed by
      randomly selecting 100, 000 images of both CASIA-WebFace (as the source distribution) and our
856
      generated images by value of α and β using Algorithm 1 (i.e., the same settings as presented in
857
      the section 4). We are reporting four versions of our generated augmentation using a medium-sized
858   generator when it sees 184M, 335M, 603M, and 805M training samples (M for Million). For each
859   of the classes generated from these models we selected 20 samples, based on the observation in Ta-
860   ble 4. Later by mixing the selected images with the original CASIA-WebFace we train FR for each
861   of them and reporting the average accuracies for different thresholds in the IJB-C (i.e., similar to last
862   column in the Table 6). Figure 5 and Figure 6 are showing mentioned metrics for Inception-V3 and
863   DINOv2 feature extractor respectively. We observe no clear correlation between the metrics used
      to evaluate generative models and the performance of a downstream task. This holds when we are
