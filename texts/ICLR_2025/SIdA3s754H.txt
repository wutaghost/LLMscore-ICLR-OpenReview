009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recommender systems play a crucial role in internet economies by connecting
012            users with relevant products or services. However, designing effective recom-
013            mender systems faces two key challenges: (1) the exploration-exploitation trade-
014            off in balancing new product exploration against exploiting known preferences,
015            and (2) context-aware Bayesian incentive compatibility in accounting for users’
016            heterogeneous preferences and self-interested behaviors. This paper formalizes
017
               these challenges into a Context-aware Bayesian Incentive-Compatible Recom-
               mendation Problem (CBICRP). To address the CBICRP, we propose a two-stage
018
               algorithm (RCB) that integrates incentivized exploration with an efficient offline
019
               learning component for exploitation. In the first stage, our algorithm explores
020            available products while maintaining context-aware Bayesian incentive compat-
021            ibility to determine sufficient sample sizes. The second stage employs inverse
022            proportional gap sampling integrated with arbitrary efficient machine learning
023            method
                  √ to ensure sublinear regret. Theoretically, we prove that RCB achieves
024            O( KdT ) regret and satisfies Bayesian incentive compatibility (BIC). Empiri-
025            cally, we validate RCB’s strong incentive gain, sublinear regret, and robustness
026            through simulations and a real-world application on personalized warfarin dosing.
027            Our work provides a principled approach for incentive-aware recommendation in
028            online preference learning settings.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   In the current era of the internet economy, recommender systems have been widely adopted across
033   various domains such as advertising, consumer goods, music, videos, news, job markets, and travel
034
      routes (Koren et al., 2009; Li et al., 2010; Covington et al., 2016; Wang et al., 2017; Zheng et al.,
      2018; McInerney et al., 2018; Naumov et al., 2019; Lewis et al., 2020; Bao et al., 2023). Modern
035
      recommendation markets typically involve three key stakeholders: products, users, and the plat-
036
      form (which acts as a principal). The platform collects and analyzes user data to enhance future
037   distribution services and to respond effectively and promptly to user feedback. In these context-
038   aware markets, the platform serves as the planner and fulfills a dual role: recommending the best
039   available product (i.e., exploitation) and experimenting with lesser-known products to gather more
040   information (i.e., exploration). This exploration is crucial because users often have heterogeneous
041   preferences, and many products may initially seem unappealing. However, exploration feedback can
042   be valuable it provides critical insights into the products and helps determine whether they might
043   be worthwhile for future users with similar interests. Unlike in service-oriented scenarios, these are
044   marketplaces where choices are ultimately made by users rather than imposed by the platform.
045
      The key challenge arises from the fact that heterogeneous users exhibit various interests to explo-
046
      ration and are usually lack incentives to adhere to the platform’s recommendations due to varying
047
      interests. A myopic user is likely to choose products based solely on immediate benefits, demonstrat-
048   ing a bias toward exploitation over exploration. How can the platform effectively keep a balance be-
049   tween exploration and exploitation while taking individualized incentive compatibility into account?
050   In other words, recommender systems commonly face two significant obstacles: (1) exploration-
051   exploitation tradeoff : How can the platform design recommender systems that maximize rewards
052   but also consider that failing to sufficiently explore all available products initially may lead to sub-
053   optimal decisions? (2) context-aware incentive compatibility: How can we strategically address the
      tendency of heterogeneous users to behave myopically?


                                                         1
054
      In this paper, we first formalize those challenges into a Context-aware Bayesian Incentive-
055   Compatible Recommendation Problem (CBICRP). This protocol assumes that the platform can com-
056   municate directly with users, for example, by sending individualized product recommendations, and
057   then observing the user’s actions and the outcomes. The key difference between this protocol and
058   standard bandit algorithm is that user’s actions incorporate not only their personalized interests and
059   a common public prior over all products but also the individualized message sent by the platform.
060   That is, users will continuously evaluate the difference between products after receiving the mes-
061   sage/recommendation sent by the platform which is formalized in a Bayesian way.
062
063
      The basic multi-armed bandit (MAB) model of incentivized exploration has been examined in (Kre-
      mer et al., 2014; Che & Hörner, 2018; Mansour et al., 2020; Sellke & Slivkins, 2023), which model
064
      the recommendation policy within the framework of MAB problems and incorporating incentive
065
      compatibility constraints by agents’ Bayesian priors, but these models assume independent prior
066   preference over products but in reality, these products share correlated prior beliefs. Subsequently,
067   Hu et al. (2022); Sellke (2023) propose BIC recommendation policies for customers with dependent
068   priors with Thompson sampling algorithm. However, Hu et al. (2022) considered the combinatorial
069   semi-bandits which didn’t consider the users’ contextual information and corresponding personal-
070   ized preferences over products. Similarly, Sellke (2023); Kalvit et al. (2024) considered the fixed
071   design setting where feature xi are product-owned and fixed rather than our setting that feature xi,t
072   is user-possessed and online sampled which introduces more technical difficulty since fixed design
073   of x can be transformed into the MAB setting and randomized design of x can not. In addition, their
074
      settings only need to learn one parameter and our setting needs to learn K arms’ parameters (Latti-
      more & Szepesvári, 2020; Bastani & Bayati, 2020). Besides, our framework can easily incorporate
075
      any efficient offline marching learning methods, which greatly strengthens its applicability.
076
077   Recommendation context bandit algorithm (RCB) is composed of a two-stage design’s algorithm. In
078   the first stage, the platform explores all available products, taking into account context-aware incen-
079   tive compatibility, and determines the minimal amount of information (sample size) that needs be
080   collected for the subsequent stage. The second stage employs an inverse proportional gap sampling
081   bandit integrated with any efficient plug-in offline machine learning method. This approach aims to
082   simultaneously ensure sublinear regret and maintain context-aware BIC.
083
084   Our main contributions can be delineated into three parts:
085
086
      1. We formalize the context-aware online recommendation problem under BIC constraints in §3.
         This formulation accommodates context-aware user preferences and incorporates BIC con-
087
         straints.
088
089   2. We introduce a two-stage context-aware BIC bandit algorithm (RCB) for addressing CBICRP (see
090      Algorithms 1 and 2). This algorithm adapts to any efficient offline machine learning algorithm as
091      a component of the exploitation stage. RCB is also a decision length T -free algorithm,

      In §2, we provide related works. In §3, we introduce the heterogeneous recommendation protocol

103   effectiveness and robustness of RCB through simulations and real-data studies.

      In this section, we introduce the Recommendation Contextual Bandit (RCB) algorithm, which is
209
      structure into two stages, the cold start stage and the exploitation stage. The objective during the
210   cold start stage is to develop an algorithm that not only maintains CBIC for users to gain trust but
211   also fulfills the minimal sample size requirement necessary for the subsequent algorithm require-
212   ment for the platform with minimal budget and time cost. In the second stage, the design of RCB
213   focuses on constructing a sampling bandit algorithm that incorporate any efficient offline machine
214   learning methods for the long term goal of the balance of freshness and exploitation. This goal is
215   fulfilled by balancing the ϵ-budget allocation strategically and a carefully designed of sequential
      spread parameter {γm }m over algorithm’s batches m.

449
      We utilize a publicly available dataset from the Pharmacogenomics Knowledge Base (PharmGKB)
450
      that includes medical records of 5,700 patients treated with warfarin across various global research
451
      groups (Consortium, 2009). In the U.S., inappropriate warfarin dosing leads to about 43,000 emer-
452   gency department visits annually. Traditional fixed-dose strategies can result in severe adverse ef-
453   fects due to initial dosing inaccuracies. Our study aims to optimize initial dosages by leveraging
454   patient-specific factors from the cleaned data of 5,528 patients. Detailed data information and pre-
455   proc are provided in Appendix E.2.
456
      Arms Construction: We follow the arm construction as it in (Bastani & Bayati, 2020) and formulate
457
      the problem as a K-armed bandit with covariates (K = 3). We bucket the optimal dosages using
458   the “clinically relevant” dosage differences: (1) Low: under 3mg/day (33% of cases), (2) Medium:
459   3-7mg/day (54% of cases), and (3) High: over 7mg/day (13% of cases). In particular, patients who
460   require a low (high) dose would be at risk for excessive (inadequate) anti-coagulation under the
461   physicians medium starting dose.
462
      Reward Construction: For each patient, the reward is set to 1 if the dosing algorithm selects the arm
463
      corresponding to the patient’s true optimal dose; otherwise, the reward is 0. This straightforward
464
      reward function allows the regret to directly quantify the number of incorrect dosing decisions.
465   Additionally, it is important to note that while we employ a binary reward for simplicity, we model
466   the reward as a linear function. Despite this, RCB demonstrates robust performance in this setting,
467   indicating its applicability for scenarios involving discrete outcomes.
468
      Ground Truth: We estimate the true arm parameters βi using the linear regression with the entire
469
      dataset for specific group. Besides, we scale the optimal warfarin dosing into [0, 1] with minimum
470
      dosing as 0, and maximum dosing as 1. The true mean warfarin dosage is obtained from the inner
471   production of βi (based on the optimal arm) multiples the covariate of this patient. Besides, for the
472   counterfactual arm, the true mean dosage are set to be 0.
473
474   RCB Setup: The total number of trials is set at T = 5528, with reward noise σ̂ = 0.054 estimated
475
      from the true optimal dosing of warfarin after scaling. To create an online decision-making scenario,
      we simulate the process across 10 random permutations of patient arrivals, averaging the results over
476
      these permutations. The exploration budget ϵ is varied among [0.025, 0.035, 0.045]. The minimum
477
      gap τP0 is set at 0.005. The prior variance is defined as Σ = [0.4, 0.6, 0.8]Id , and the prior means
478   are β2,0 = 0.05 × Id , β1,0 = β3,0 = 0d . Further details on hyperparameters are available in §E.2.
479
480   Evaluation Criteria: We apply four criteria to evaluate the warfarin dose decision. (1) Regret:
481
      The regret is optimal mean dose minus 0. (2) ϵ-CBIC Gain. (3) Fraction of Incorrect Decision:
      the fraction of incorrect decision. (4) Weighted Risk Score: the correct decision deserves 1 point
482
      and incorrect decision loss 1 point and multiple the true dosage sample proportion, which is newly

      proposed by us.

533   7   C ONCLUSION
534
535   We propose a new RCB framework to address the context-aware BIC problem, where the informa-
536   tion about the arms needs to be learned. This approach can leverage any sample-efficient machine
537   learning method. We theoretically prove that RCB is regret-optimal in terms of the number of arms
538   K, dimension d, and horizon length T , all in square root order, and satisfies the ϵ-BIC constraints.
539   Furthermore, we experimentally demonstrate that our algorithm achieves sublinear regret, is robust
      to different priors, dimensions, and budgets, and outperforms the state-of-the-art bandit algorithms.
