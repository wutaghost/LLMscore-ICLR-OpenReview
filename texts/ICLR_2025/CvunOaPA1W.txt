011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            The rapidly developing field of large multimodal models (LMMs) has led to the
014            emergence of diverse models with remarkable capabilities. However, existing
015            benchmarks fail to comprehensively, objectively and accurately evaluate whether
016            LMMs align with the diverse needs of humans in real-world scenarios. To bridge
017            this gap, we propose the Multi-Dimensional Insights (MDI) benchmark, which
018
               includes over 500 images covering six common scenarios of human life. Notably,
               the MDI-Benchmark offers two significant advantages over existing evaluations:
019
               (1) Each image is accompanied by two types of questions: simple questions to
020
               assess the model’s understanding of the image, and complex questions to evaluate
021            the model’s ability to analyze and reason beyond basic content. (2) Recogniz-
022            ing that people of different age groups have varying needs and perspectives when
023            faced with the same scenario, our benchmark stratifies questions into three age cat-
024            egories: young people, middle-aged people, and older people. This design allows
025            for a detailed assessment of LMMs’ capabilities in meeting the preferences and
026            needs of different age groups. With MDI-Benchmark, the strong model like GPT-
027            4o achieve 79% accuracy on age-related tasks, indicating that existing LMMs still
028            have considerable room for improvement in addressing real-world applications.
029
               Looking ahead, we anticipate that the MDI-Benchmark will open new pathways
               for aligning real-world personalization in LMMs.

048   1   I NTRODUCTION
049
050   Developing personalized artificial intelligence (AI) assistants to address the diverse needs of dif-
051   ferent users has long been a significant pursuit for humanity (Kobsa & Schreck, 2003; Xiao et al.,
052   2018; Kocaballi et al., 2019; Rafieian & Yoganarasimhan, 2023; Pesovski et al., 2024). In real-
053   world scenarios, an ideal AI assistant should be capable of precisely meeting the specific demands
      of individuals across various age groups, cultural backgrounds, and professional fields.


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      Recently, the field of artificial intelligence has undergone a significant paradigm shift, transitioning
055   from specialized small models designed for specific simple tasks (Rawat & Wang, 2017; Zhao et al.,
056   2019; Minaee et al., 2021; Singh et al., 2017) to unified large multimodal models (LMMs) capable
057   of handling complex tasks (Zhang et al., 2024). This paradigm shift marks a crucial step toward
058   achieving Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) and underscores the potential for LMMs to become
059   personalized human assistants.
060
      To comprehensively evaluate the capabilities of LMMs, researchers have constructed several com-
061   mon visual question-answering benchmarks (Goyal et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2015; Marino et al.,
062   2019; Mishra et al., 2019; Biten et al., 2019) that assess general image-text comprehension and
063   dialogue capabilities of LMMs. However, these benchmarks merely compare answers to standard
064   solutions, offering limited insights into the fine-grained capabilities of models. To address this limi-
065   tation, subsequent multimodal understanding benchmarks are developed (Yu et al., 2023; Liu et al.,
066   2023; Fu et al., 2024a; Ying et al., 2024), covering a broader range of tasks and a larger number of
067   test samples. This refinement enables a more precise evaluation of model capabilities, fostering the
068   development of more robust LMMs. Nevertheless, current benchmarks focus primarily on technical
069
      metrics for specific tasks, neglecting two critical research questions:
070   Q1: Can these LMMs truly align with the actual needs of humans in real-world scenarios?
071
      Q2: Can these LMMs subsequently address the diverse needs of distinct groups?
072
073   To tackle these challenges, we introduce a novel ”Multi-Dimensional Insights” (MDI) benchmark,
074   which encompasses various real-world scenarios, different problem complexities, and diverse age
075   groups. In detail, the MDI-Benchmark consists of more than 500 real-world images and 1.2k human-
076
      posed questions. As shown in Figure 1, it covers six major scenarios of human life: Architecture,
      Education, Housework, Social Services, Sport, and Transport. Furthermore, MDI-Benchmark fo-
077
      cuses on evaluating LMMs from the following two dimensions:
078
079   Question Complexity Dimension. This dimension categorizes human-posed problems into two
080   levels of complexity. The first level assesses the basic capabilities of LMMs, such as object detection
081   and optical character recognition (OCR), etc. The second level evaluates more complex capabilities,
082
      including logical reasoning, mathematical calculation, and knowledge application.
083   Age Dimension. Age is a fundamental criterion for evaluating individual differences, as people of
084   different ages have diverse needs. We categorize individuals into three age groups: young people,
085   middle-aged people, and older people, to assess the effectiveness of LMMs in addressing the varying
086   needs and preferences across these groups. Our goal is to comprehensively assess whether LMMs
087   can meet the diverse needs of humans in practical situations.
088   In summary, our major contributions are listed:
089
090
091          • To align with the actual needs of humans for Large Multimodal Models, we are the first to
092            propose a multi-modal benchmark for providing a thorough assessment of the capacities of
093            LMMs in practical, real-world scenarios.
094
095          • The MDI-Benchmark includes over 500 real-world images and 1.2k human-posed ques-
096            tions, spanning six real-world multimodal scenarios. Each scenario is divided into 3 sub-
097            domains with 2 levels of complexity. Additionally, we incorporate age factors into the eval-
098            uation to guide LMMs in personalizing their responses for different demographic groups.
099
100
             • With the MDI-Benchmark, we conduct a comprehensive evaluation of several mainstream
101
               LMMs. Specifically, GPT-4o achieved the best results across all indicators, but there is
102            still significant room for improvement in addressing the needs of different age groups.
103            Further analysis across dimensions such as Scenario, Complexity and Age provides valuable
104            insights for developing reliable, personalized human assistants.
105
106
107   We hope our research will advance the application of multimodal large models in real-world scenar-
      ios and pave the way for the development of multi-dimensional personalization.

      2.1   M ULTIMODAL DATASET AND B ENCHMARK
111
112
      To evaluate the capabilities of LMMs, a variety of benchmarks from past research have been applied.
113   Among them, Flickr30k (Young et al., 2014), COCO Captions (Chen et al., 2015), and Nocaps
114   (Agrawal et al., 2019) are utilized to evaluate LMMs’ text generation and image description abilities.
115   Vizwiz (Bigham et al., 2010), VQA (Goyal et al., 2017), GQA (Hudson & Manning, 2019), and
116   OK-VQA (Marino et al., 2019) are used to assess LMMs’ comprehension of image information
117   and question-answering abilities. For evaluating OCR capabilities, benchmarks like ST-VQA (Biten
118   et al., 2019) and OCR-VQA (Mishra et al., 2019) are employed. DocVQA (Mathew et al., 2021) is
119   specifically used to evaluate a model’s ability to understand and identify documents.
120   To further explore the fine-grained capabilities of LMMs, recent benchmarks have significantly ex-
121   panded the types of tasks assessed. Examples of such benchmarks include LVLM-eHub (Xu et al.,
122   2023), MM-Vet (Yu et al., 2023), MMBench (Liu et al., 2023), SEED-Bench (Li et al., 2023), MME
123   (Fu et al., 2024a), MMT-Bench (Ying et al., 2024), Video-MME (Fu et al., 2024b), MMMU (Yue
124   et al., 2023), MMMU-Pro (Yue et al., 2024), MathVista (Lu et al., 2024b), Contextual (Wadhawan
125   et al., 2024), We-Math(Qiao et al., 2024), and MMEvol(Luo et al., 2024). Nevertheless, it should
126   be noted that these benchmarks have not fully explored the capability of LMMs to address the di-
127
      verse needs of different individuals. Therefore, we hope to better explore this ability through the
      MDI-Benchmark.

133   2023; Chiang et al., 2023), recent research has combined large language models with visual encoders

      to form LMMs with powerful visual understanding and semantic generation capabilities. Many
      excellent open-source (Hong et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024a; Liu
135
      et al., 2024b; Ye et al., 2023; Abdin et al., 2024) and closed-source (Team et al., 2023; Bai et al.,

      2023; OpenAI, 2023; 2024) projects have been developed. This development has further enhanced

      To achieve personalized AI assistants, large language models (LLMs) are currently attempting to
      combine with users’ personalized outputs to enhance their personalization capabilities and enable
143
      them to generate outputs that conform to users’ preferences (Woźniak et al., 2024; Zhuang et al.,
144
      2024; Baek et al., 2024; Tan et al., 2024). Simultaneously, to further expand the understanding ability

147   models to address personalized needs and provide our insights for future LMMs research.

150   3     MDI-B ENCHMARK
151
152   The benchmark sample design emphasizes the real-world complexity of information, scene vari-
153   ability, and age differences. People’s information concerns often vary by scenario. As shown in
154   Figure 2, a family buying a new house may focus on practical issues that are closely related to
155   them, such as kitchen type, garage capacity, and bedroom amenities. Spectators at sports events
156
      may concern themselves with game details, player achievements, and game progress.

159
160   In contrast to existing work, MDI-Benchmark emphasizes the model’s performance on real-world
161   problems across various ages and complexities within specific task scenarios, it is structured along
      three different dimensions: scenario, age, and problem complexity.

185
186   Scenario Dimension. From the perspective of the scenario, the MDI-Benchmark aims to closely
187   align with the real needs of human life. Unlike the capability evaluation focus of previous LMMs
188   evaluation benchmarks, the MDI-Benchmark is constructed based on real-life scenarios.
189   In response to the various scenarios that humans face in real life, we have drawn on the definitions
190   provided in sociological literature (Tajfel, 1979; Birmingham et al., 2008; Spears, 2021) and ex-
191   panded upon them to identify 30 sub-domain scenarios. On this basis, we conducted a one-month
192   questionnaire survey covering people of different ages, genders, and occupations. A total of 2,500
193   questionnaires were distributed, and 2,374 valid responses were collected. Based on the frequency
194   of sub-domain selection in the questionnaires, we selected the top 18 sub-domains, which were ul-
195
      timately summarized into six main scenarios: architecture, education, housework, social service,
      sports, and transport. We collected images from these subdomains to ensure this benchmark is rich
196
      in scenario information. Examples are in the Appendix B.1.
197
198   Problem Complexity Dimension. In the realm of everyday human activities, the level of com-
199   plexity varies significantly, and the definition of difficulty is often subjective. To streamline this
200   definition, we have quantified the problems hierarchically based on the fundamental capabilities
201
      of the model as the atomic units. Based on this criterion, we have filtered survey questions and
      refined previous evaluation standards. Furthermore, the MDI-Benchmark is categorized into two
202
      levels: (1) The first level involves relatively straightforward problem types that mainly evaluate the
203
      model’s ability to extract scenario information. This includes tasks such as detection, optical char-
204   acter recognition, position recognition, color recognition, and other fundamental capacities. (2) The
205   second level demands that the model skillfully analyze both scenario information and user seman-
206   tic information with logical acuity while integrating relevant knowledge to effectively meet user
207   requirements. Examples are in the Appendix B.2.
208
      Age Dimension. Age is a universal and specific criterion for group classification, making it more
209
      objective compared to classifications based on culture and religious beliefs. As a fundamental at-
210   tribute possessed by everyone, age is easy to quantify and compare. By using age as a classification
211   dimension, we can better understand the needs of various groups and assess the capability of LMMs
212   to meet these diverse needs. For the purposes of assessment and quantification, we identified three
213   distinct age groups: young people (ages 10-25), middle-aged people (ages 35-50), and old peo-
214   ple (ages 60-75). We engaged individuals from these age brackets in real-life scenarios to inquire
215   about their needs. The results of these surveys informed the creation of the initial version of the
      MDI-Benchmark. Examples are in the Appendix B.3.

      3.2       DATA C OLLECTION
217
218   Data Source. Existing LMMs evaluation benchmarks have been widely used to evaluate and train
219   new models. To ensure the accuracy of the evaluation results, we collected over 500 new images
220   that were not included in existing datasets and recruited 120 volunteers from three age groups. From
221   each group, we sampled 10 volunteers to form a 30-person data construction team. The main data
222   collection process was as follows: First, after determining the scenario dimension information, the
223   data construction team wrote detailed scenario information based on their interests. Meanwhile,
224
      we input the scenario dimension information into open-source models (e.g., GPT-4o, Gemini 1.5
      Pro) and closed-source models (e.g., LLaVA-Next, MiniCPM) to generate more personalized, di-
225
      verse, and detailed scenario descriptions. Furthermore, the descriptions created by both humans and
226
      models were used as keywords to search for relevant images on the Internet. Meanwhile, We paid
227   volunteers a sufficient wage, approximately seven dollars per hour. These volunteers were tasked
228   with categorizing the images into six scenario dimensions. To ensure data balance and minimize
229   bias, we ensured diversity within each age group in terms of gender, occupation, and other factors.
230   Detailed classification standards and guidelines were provided to ensure consistency in categoriza-
231   tion. We employed a cross-validation approach, whereby each group of volunteers screened the
232   images, and we retained only those images that were categorized identically by all three groups.
233   Additionally, multiple iterations of validation were conducted. This comprehensive process helped
234   to construct a balanced and reliable data source.
235   Question and Answer Generation. After obtaining the collected images, we used a heuristic
236   method to manually generate questions and problems. The specific process is as follows: (1) Con-
237   struction of Knowledge Base. Specifically, multiple open-source and closed-source models are first
238   used to describe the scenario content in the image and are summarized by human experts. Subse-
239   quently, additional information related to the scenario content was found through an Internet search,
240   and the image and this information were combined to form a knowledge base. (2) Generation of
241
      Difficult Multi-Choice Questions. To ensure the consistency of the generated questions with the
      image content, we invited volunteers from three different age groups who participated in the data
242
      collection phase to submit questions. These volunteers posed questions of varying complexity based
243
      on the image scenarios and knowledge base content and created confusing incorrect options. (3)
244   Question Format. The image-question pairs provided by the volunteers had to follow the format:
245   [Level]-[Age]-[Scenario]. Here, Level includes level 1 and level 2; Age includes old, mid, and
246   young; Scenario includes architecture, education, housework, social services, sports, and transport.
247   Finally, a team of experts screened and evaluated the questions submitted by the volunteers to final-
248   ize the construction of the questions.
249
      Data Statistics. The MDI-Benchmark is collected from three different dimensions: scenarios, age
250   groups, and abilities. It includes a total of 514 images and 1298 questions, all newly collected.
251   Meanwhile, we strived to ensure a balance of data across different scenarios, ages, and question
252   complexities. The detailed information is presented in the Table 1. As shown in Figure 2, the
253   dataset covers six domains, each with three sub-domains, providing a comprehensive and structured
254   construction of data across various fields.

      4.3   S CENARIO D IMENSION A NALYSIS
353
354   The performance of LMMs in daily scenarios still has great room for improvement. To ob-
355   serve the specific performance of different models in various scenarios, as shown in Figure 3, we
356   calculated the accuracy of different models across different fields. We found that these 14 LMMs
357   achieved good performance in Level 1 for the education scenario. The performance is more bal-
358   anced in the architecture, housework, transport and social service scenarios. However, there are
359   some shortcomings in the performance of sports scenarios, which we believe are closely related to
360
      the current training data of LMMs. At present, LMMs research groups focus more on achieving
      better training and testing levels using existing Internet text data and high-quality textbook data, but
361
      they neglect the improvement of datasets and capabilities in everyday life fields. This is where the
362
      MDI-Benchmark comes into play. We believe that the types of problems related to logical reasoning
363   and the required background knowledge in the fields of sport and transport are richer and broader
364   than those in architecture, resulting in increased problem difficulty and a significant gap in reasoning
365   performance.
366
367   4.4   C OMPLEXITY D IMENSION A NALYSIS
368
369   Decreased performance with increased complexity. As the complexity of the problems increases,
370   the model’s performance in every scenario noticeably decreases. The accuracy of answering ques-

      5    C ONCLUSION
528
      In this paper, we propose the MDI-Benchmark, a tool designed to evaluate the capability of Large
529
      Multimodal Models (LMMs) in addressing real-world human demands within multi-dimensional
530
      scenarios. The MDI-Benchmark comprises over 500 images and 1.2k corresponding requirements,
531   encompassing six major aspects of human life. Additionally, we introduce the concept of age strat-
532   ification and sampling questions based on the needs of elderly, middle-aged, and young individuals
533   to ensure comprehensive evaluation. Using the MDI-Benchmark, we evaluated 14 existing LMMs,
534   revealing their performance preferences in different scenarios. While GPT-4o performed best across
535   a variety of metrics, there were gaps in performance across all age groups and scenarios. Therefore,
536   we suggest that future studies should focus on improving the adaptability of LMM to human needs
537   and its ability to generalize across different domains and age groups. This will pave the way for the
538   next generation of LMMs that can effectively meet human needs.

      L IMITATIONS
757
758
      In this paper, we introduce MDI-Benchmark, the first benchmark to incorporate personalized pref-
759   erence requirements, represented by age, into LMM evaluation. However, there are still some limi-
760   tations to this paper.
761   (1) Scope Coverage: Given the infinite possibilities of real-world scenarios, MDI-Benchmark can-
762   not cover all domains. MDI-Benchmark focuses on 18 fine-grained subfields within 6 key domains.
763   (2) Task Format: To achieve automated evaluation, similar to many other benchmarks, we use
764   multiple-choice questions as the sole task format in MDI-Benchmark.
765   (3) Data Scale: MDI-Benchmark consists of 500 meticulously hand-collected and processed images
766   and 1,298 questions. How to automatically construct large-scale, high-quality customized prefer-
767
      ence data remains to be explored.
      (4) Preference Dimensions: MDI-Benchmark selects the most common preference dimension, age,
768
      to evaluate LMM. We leave the exploration of other customized preference dimensions in future
769
      work.
