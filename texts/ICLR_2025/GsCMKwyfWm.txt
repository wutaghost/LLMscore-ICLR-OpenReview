009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Counting is a fundamental skill for various visual tasks in real-life applica-
012            tions, requiring both object recognition and robust counting capabilities. Despite
013            their advanced visual perception, large vision-language models (LVLMs) struggle
014            with counting tasks, especially when the number of objects exceeds those com-
015            monly encountered during training. We enhance LVLMs’ counting abilities using
016            a divide-and-conquer approach, breaking counting problems into sub-counting
017
               tasks. Unlike prior methods, which do not generalize well to counting datasets
               on which they have not been trained, our method performs well on new datasets
018
               without any additional training or fine-tuning. We demonstrate that our approach
019
               enhances counting capabilities across various datasets and benchmarks.

022   1   I NTRODUCTION
023
024   Counting is a key cognitive task with broad applications in industry, healthcare, and environmental
025   monitoring (De Almeida et al., 2015; Guerrero-Gómez-Olmedo et al., 2015; Paul Cohen et al., 2017;
026   Lempitsky & Zisserman, 2010). It improves manufacturing, inventory, and quality control, ensures
027   safety in medical settings, and helps manage resources in environmental efforts (Wang & Wang,
028   2011; Zen et al., 2012; Arteta et al., 2016). Recent advancements in prompt-based models enable
029
      counting of unlimited object varieties without visual exemplars. Although current trained, and text-
      prompt-based, counting models by Dai et al. (2024); Amini-Naieni et al. (2024) perform well on
030
      the datasets they are trained on, they face the following challenges. First, they require fine-tuning
031
      on new datasets. Second, since the concepts in the counting datasets are limited, these models do
032   not generalize well to counting questions that involve complex reasoning. There are also training-
033   free models by Shi et al. (2024), but overall they have weaker performance compared to trained
034   models. On the other hand, large vision-language models (LVLMs), such as GPT-4o (Achiam et al.,
035   2023), which also do not need dataset specific training, show very good performance in counting
036   low numbers of objects, usually less than 20, across most datasets. However, their performance
037   deteriorates for larger numbers of objects, regardless of the dataset.

054
      We enhance the accuracy of LVLMs to count objects in images by leveraging their reasoning power
055   within a divide-and-conquer method. The LVLMs allow us to handle diverse objects and com-
056   plex counting questions, while our divide-and-conquer method alleviates challenges associated with
057   counting large numbers of objects. Inspired by prior work on the rapid and accurate estimation of
058   small quantities by Chattopadhyay et al. (2017), we divide an image into sub-images, and prompt
059   the LVLM to count the objects of interest in each sub-image. The counts from each sub-image are
060   then aggregated to make the final prediction. Our proposed workflow is illustrated in Figure 1.
061   Initially, in our pipeline, the object of interest is extracted from the input question using an LLM. The
062   area containing the object of interest is detected in the image by a grounding model, such as Liu et al.
063   (2023), and then cropped. The cropping step is important since it removes irrelevant context from the
064   image. Secondly, using an object detection model by Liu et al. (2023), and a segmentation model
065   by Kirillov et al. (2023), the segmentation masks of the objects of interest are created. Thirdly,
066   we use a mechanism that divides the image into multiple sub-images without cutting through the
067   objects of interest. We call this mechanism object-aware division. The division positions can be
068   either set manually or determined automatically using unsupervised and non-parametric methods.
069
      In the latter case, we automatically select the division positions based on object masks, then we treat
      the object-aware division as a path-finding problem, avoiding objects as obstacles. A black-white
070
      image is built by converting all the masks into black and the rest of the image into white pixels. The
071
      binary image is converted into a graph where only white pixels are connected as nodes. Using the
072   A∗ algorithm (Russell & Norvig, 2016), a path is found from one end to the other end of the image,
073   ensuring objects remain intact. Finally, using an LVLM as a counting tool, the objects of interest in
074   the sub-images are counted and aggregated. Our contributions are summarized below:
075
076         1. We propose LVLM-Count that leverages the strengths of large vision-language models
077            (LVLMs) in visual perception for counting. Our method is a prompt-based counting ap-
078            proach that, in addition to simple counting problems, can handle complex cases as well.
079            Our method does not require any additional training or fine-tuning on any dataset. LVLM-
080
               Count outperforms existing state-of-the-art models across various datasets and benchmarks.
081         2. We propose a solution for object-aware division. Accurate division is crucial, as parts of
082            cut objects can lead to over-counting (see Figure 2a). To the best of our knowledge, this is
083            the first method to divide images without cutting through objects of interest.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      Early counting models, referred to as class-specific, targeted counting problems for certain cate-
111   gories (Arteta et al., 2016; Babu Sam et al., 2022; Mundhenk et al., 2016; Xie et al., 2018), such as
112   cars, people, or cells. Later, with the emergence of stronger vision models and large-scale datasets,
113   class-agnostic methods were proposed that could count objects from a wide variety of categories.
114   However, most existing class-agnostic, or open-world, models require visual exemplars of the target
115   objects (Ðukić et al., 2023; Gong et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022; Lu et al., 2019;
116   Nguyen et al., 2022; Ranjan et al., 2021; Shi et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2021; You et al., 2023). The
117   concept of divide and conquer has also been used in early work(Xiong et al., 2019; Chattopadhyay
118   et al., 2017). However, this early work requires training dedicated models to utilize this concept.
119   Text-based counting-specific trained models. With the advent of vision-language foundation
120   models such as CLIP and GroundingDINO, text-based open-world methods have been proposed.
121   Leveraging the rich textual and visual feature extraction capabilities of foundation models, obtained
122   through web-scale training, the text-based counting methods by Amini-Naieni et al. (2023); Dai
123   et al. (2024); Kang et al. (2024); Amini-Naieni et al. (2024) have started to demonstrate comparable
124   or superior accuracy. GroundingREC by Dai et al. (2024) is an open-world model built on top of
125
      GroundingDINO (Liu et al., 2023), and introduces an additional task called referring expression
      counting. Concurrently, Amini-Naieni et al. (2024) proposed a method that also builds on Ground-
126
      ingDINO but adds an extra image-text fusion module in the input, enabling the model to accept text
127
      and/or visual exemplars to determine the target.
128
129   Models without counting-specific training. Shi et al. (2024) introduce TFOC, a counting model
130   that does not require any counting-specific training. Instead, they cast the counting problem as
131
      a prompt-based segmentation task, using SAM (Kirillov et al., 2023) to obtain segmentation maps
      that determine the output number. Another group of models that do not need further training to count
132
      are LVLMs. State-of-the-art models such as GPT-4o (Achiam et al., 2023), Gemini 1.5 Pro (Reid
133
      et al., 2024), and Claude 3.5 Sonnet (Anthropic, 2024) show strong performance in counting small
134   numbers of objects, although their performance degrades with larger numbers of objects. In compar-
135   ison to prior work, our method (LVLM-Count) is an open-world, text-based counting approach that
136   does not require any counting-specific training. Although we use pre-trained GroundingDINO and
137   SAM in our method, we differ from prior work by not treating the counting task as a segmentation
138   or detection problem. Instead, we use LVLMs as a counting tool.

      3     LVLM-C OUNT
141
142
      Our proposed method aims to answer counting questions by dividing an image into sub-images
143   while avoiding cuts through objects of interest. LVLM-Count consists of four key stages. First, in
144   the “Area Detection” stage, we localize areas containing relevant objects. Second, in the “Target
145   Segmentation” stage, we identify and segment these local areas. Third, in the “Object-aware Divi-
146   sion” stage, we divide the localized areas into sub-images without cutting through the segmented
147   objects. Finally, the LVLM counts the target objects in each sub-image and aggregates the results.
148   Figure 1 illustrates the workflow of our method, which we will detail in the following subsections.
149   Note that in this section, we illustrate each stage using example images. These images are are used
150   solely for illustration purposes. LVLM-Count may not outperform other methods on these examples.
151
152   3.1   A REA D ETECTION
153
      In this part of the pipeline, we assume that we are given a counting question Q along with an
154
      image. The question Q contains an expression E that specifies a set of objects of interest. The
155
      expression E distinguishes these objects from objects of other categories or the same category but
156   with different attributes present in the image. By employing an LLM, the expression E is extracted
157   from Q. For example, let Q be “How many people are in the boat?”. Q is given to an LLM, which is
158   prompted to return the expression E “people in the boat”, referring to the objects we want to count.
159   After E is extracted, it is given as input to GroundingDINO along with the image. The output
160   of GroundingDINO is a set of bounding boxes that have relevance to E beyond a certain threshold.
161   These bounding boxes often overlap and typically contain repeated objects. Thus, all the overlapping
      output bounding boxes are merged. After merging, a set of non-overlapping areas of interest may

163   passed to the next stage. Note that the area detection stage is important as it extracts the area with

      3.2   TARGET S EGMENTATION
185
186   The cropped images from the first stage contain objects of interest, and the ultimate goal is to di-
187   vide them without cutting through those objects. However, a prerequisite for implementing such a
188   mechanism is to first detect and localize the objects of interest. Each cropped image is fed into an
189   open-world detection model along with E. The output of the open-world detection model produces
190   a bounding box for each object of interest. The bounding boxes are then given as input to a seg-
191   mentation model, which returns segmentation masks for the objects within each bounding box. We
192   illustrate an example of this process in Figure 4.

212   How to determine the bounding boxes. To determine the bounding boxes, we use GroundingDINO
213   and set the bounding box probability threshold to a low value to avoid missing any objects. The
214   bounding boxes alone cannot help with the object-aware division of the cropped images due to their
215   rigid structure, which includes redundant areas in the vicinity of the object and, in the worst case,
      overlaps with other bounding boxes. Our goal is to precisely locate the pixels of an object of interest.

      How to determine the segmentation masks. We use a pre-trained segmentation model, i.e., SAM
217   (Kirillov et al., 2023), for the segmentation task, which accepts bounding boxes as prompts. It
218   produces a mask covering the most prominent object within a bounding box. We run non-maximum
219   suppression on the masks produced by SAM to remove the masks corresponding to less certain
220   bounding boxes that have large overlaps with other masks. The masks produced for each cropped
221   image are then passed to the next stage. For GroundingDINO we set the bounding box threshold to
222   a very low value to avoid missing any objects of interest, especially if the objects belong to a rare
223   category that GroundingDINO has encountered less frequently during pre-training. As shown in
224   Figure 4, a low threshold value can lead to false positives, making the number of masks unreliable
225
      for the counting problem. Nonetheless, the false positive masks have no negative effect on the object-
      aware division in the next stage. The only consequence is that, in addition to the target masks, the
226
      division paths will not cut through the false positive masks either. In other words, extra masks are
227
      of no concern as long as all the target masks are identified.
228
229
      3.3   O BJECT- AWARE D IVISION
230
231
      In this stage, the cropped image is divided into appropriate sub-images so that no object of interest
232   is cut by the dividing paths. The core idea is that the dividing paths should not intersect the pixels
233   covered by the masks corresponding to the objects of interest. This step consists of two sub-steps.
234   First, we decide the starting and ending points of the paths. Second, we draw the paths. Below, we
235   describe how we approach these two sub-steps.
236
      How to determine the starting and ending points of the paths. We consider the first sub-step as a
237
      hyper-parameter, which can be set in various ways depending on the type of dataset. We utilize two
238   approaches in our experiments. The first approach is unsupervised and non-parametric, where we
239   use a clustering method to obtain the start and end points of the paths. We describe the clustering
240   approach in more detail below. The second approach is to simply use a pre-determined number
241   of equidistant points. This is helpful when we know a priori that objects are uniformly distributed
242   across the image. In this case, we fix k equidistant vertical and horizontal coordinates in the image as
243   the points of the dividing paths for the entire dataset. In our experiments, we specify which approach
244   we use for each dataset. Nonetheless, we will illustrate in the experimental results that even if we
245   do not treat this as a hyperparameter and always use the unsupervised and non-parametric approach,
246
      LVLM-Count is quite effective.

270
      non-parametric mean-shift algorithm. Once the clusters are identified, the point between the point
271   with the highest x value in one cluster and the point with the lowest x value in the subsequent
272   cluster is considered the x-coordinate of a vertical division path. Using this technique, we obtain the
273   appropriate coordinates for the vertical paths, as well as the number of paths. For example, if there
274   is only one cluster, no vertical division is required, and if there are two clusters, one vertical path
275   will divide the image into two parts. We illustrate this approach in Figure 5. Note that we chose
276   the vertical axis purely for convenience in illustration. The same process can be applied along the
277   y-axis to obtain the y-coordinates of the horizontal division paths.
278   How to draw the paths. In effect, knowing the x-coordinate of a vertical path means that the
279   coordinates of its endpoints are known. In particular, assuming height h for an image crop, we
280   consider Ps = (x, 0) and Pe = (x, h) as the start and end points, respectively. In an ideal case
281   where there are no masks in the path of a straight line connecting the two points, this line will be
282   drawn by connecting all the pixels on the straight path. However, there are potential masks that can
283   be considered obstacles blocking the path. In other words, beginning from Ps , the line needs to
284   go around these obstacles to reach Pe . Consequently, we treat this as a 2-dimensional path-finding
285
      problem. To solve the problem, we build a 2D binary map, IB , where the pixels covered by the
      masks are turned into black, indicating them as obstacles, and all the other pixels are turned into
286
      white, showing they are open for passage. This binary image IB is mapped into a graph G, where
287
      each white pixel is a node, and it is connected to all of its white neighboring pixels. We use the
288   A∗ (G, Ps , Pe , g) search algorithm to find a path that connects Ps to Pe , where the heuristic g is set
289   to be Manhattan distance. The output of A∗ is a set of connected pixels that go around the obstacles
290   and connect Ps to Pe , creating an object-aware division path, as shown in Figure 6. The path-finding
291   algorithm is run for all division coordinates. Finally, we draw the image contours based on these
292   division paths and take the area surrounded by each contour as a resulting sub-image.

334   FSC-147 (Ranjan et al., 2021). FSC-147 is a counting dataset that contains 6135 images, spanning
335   147 different object categories such as kitchen utensils, office supplies, vehicles, and animals. The
336   number of objects in each image ranges from 7 to 3731, with an average of 56 objects per image.
337   The dataset is split into training, validation, and test sets. A total of 89 object categories are assigned
338   to the training set, 29 to the validation set, and 29 to the test set, with different categories in each
339
      split. The training set contains 3659 images, with the validation and test sets containing 1286 and
      1190 images, respectively. For each image in the test set, a single category name is given, and the
340
      expected output is the number of instances from the category.
341
342   Open-ended Counting Benchmark. TallyQA (Acharya et al., 2019) is an open-ended counting
343   dataset that includes complex counting questions involving relationships between objects, attribute
344   identification, reasoning, and more. TallyQA is quite a large dataset, with the train set having
345
      249, 318 questions and the test set having 22, 991 simple and 22, 991 complex counting questions.
      Please refer to Appendix C and Figure 9 for more information on simple and complex categoriza-
346
      tion of the questions. The number of objects in each image ranges from 0 to 15. The dataset has a
347
      heavy bias towards a low number of objects (see Figure 8 in the Appendix). To alleviate this bias
348   and create a benchmark for efficiently measuring the simple and complex open-ended capabilities
349   of a counting model, we randomly sample 10 questions per ground truth count. This results in 155
350   simple and 149 complex open-ended counting questions in total. It is important to note that the bias
351   in TallyQA is pronounced; for most ground truth values greater than 10, there are fewer than 10
352   samples available in the entire test set.

      5   L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
517
      A limitation of LVLM-Count is the area detection stage. If the cropped areas do not provide enough
518
      context, performance may suffer. This is especially true for complex questions that require under-
519
      standing the relationships between all the objects and the background in an image. This opens up
520   opportunities for future work on improved area detection methods or the use of a context provider
521   to complement the area detection stage. Another limitation arises with images containing thousands
522   of objects. After one iteration of division, a significant number of objects may still remain in the
523   sub-images. A potential solution is to apply additional iterations of division on the sub-images;
524   however, the low resolution of these sub-images may make this infeasible. Developing a solution to
525   maintain resolution is another direction for future work. Finally, in some cases, sub-images do not
526   contain any objects of interest. The LVLM occasionally predicts a non-zero value in such instances.
527   This is a weakness of LVLMs that requires special consideration to improve their performance.

733   Acharya et al. (2019) were among the first to formally categorize counting questions into simple
734   and complex types. They applied a linguistic rule: first, they removed any substrings such as “...in
735   the photo?” or “...in the image?”. Then, they used SpaCy to perform part-of-speech tagging on
736
      the remaining substring. They classified a question as simple if it contained only one noun, no
      adverbs, and no adjectives; otherwise, they deemed it complex. This rule classifies questions such
737
      as “How many dogs?” as simple and “How many brown dogs?” as complex. Following this rule,
738
      they built two splits for the TallyQA dataset: a simple split and a complex split. Since we sample our
739   open-ended counting benchmark from the TallyQA simple and complex splits, we adopt the same
740   classification criteria for this benchmark.

796        D ETECTION AND TARGET S EGMENTATION STAGES OF LVLM-C OUNT
797
798   One of the limitations of LVLM-Count is that if the area detection fails to detect a relevant area,
799   the objects of interest in that area will not be counted. Another limitation arises when the target
800   segmentation fails to segment all the instances of the target category of objects. In such a scenario,
801   non-segmented objects of interest might be cut through by division lines, causing the predicted total
802   number to increase due to being counted multiple times. One simple, yet quite effective, approach
803   that we use to overcome this limitation to a significant degree is setting the detection threshold of
804   GroundingDINO, which is used in both of the mentioned stages, to an extremely low value. This
805   significantly reduces the chance of missing an area or object of interest, although it might lead to
806
      some false positives. However, note that false positives do not harm the performance of LVLM-
      Count, as their only effect is that the object-aware division lines avoid cutting through them.
807

1232   Similar to Chattopadhyay et al. (2017), we choose PASCAL VOC 2007 among other variants. This
1233   variant contains a training set of 2501 images, a validation set of 2510 images, and a test set of 4952
1234   images, with 20 object categories that remain consistent across the splits.
1235

1236   frequent count per object category (as one would expect in everyday scenes) is 0. Moreover, there
1237   is a clear bias toward lower count values. To address this bias and obtain a balanced benchmark,
1238   we first create 20 simple counting questions asking for the number of objects from each of the 20
1239   categories for every image in the test set. Then, we randomly sample five questions for each ground
1240   truth count. Note that the ground truth counts for this sampling are based on the original annotations
1241   of the dataset. The bias toward lower numbers is so significant that for ground truth counts larger
       than 16, there are fewer than five questions for the entire test set. In such cases, we simply use the
