011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent empirical studies show three phenomena with increasing size of language
014            models: compute-optimal size scaling, emergent capabilities, and performance
015            plateauing. We present a simple unified mathematical framework to explain all of
016
               these language model scaling phenomena, building on recent skill-text bipartite
               graph frameworks for semantic learning. Modeling the learning of concepts from
017
               texts as an iterative process yields an analogy to iterative decoding of low-density
018
               parity check (LDPC) codes in information theory. Thence, drawing on finite-size
019            scaling characterizations of LDPC decoding, we derive the compute-optimal size
020            scaling (Chinchilla rule) for language models. Further, using tools from random
021            network theory, we provide a simple explanation for both emergence of complex
022            skills and plateauing of performance as the size of language models scale. We see
023            multiple plateaus.
024

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   To optimally use computational resources when training language models, several recent studies have
029   empirically investigated how model size and dataset size should scale with compute budget (Kaplan
030   et al., 2020; Hoffmann et al., 2022), finding a certain allometric rule much like in mathematical
031   biology (Thompson, 1917; Haldane, 1926). As the sizes of language models continue to increase,
032   large improvements in performance have been observed in certain complex tasks with only a small
033
      improvement in the model’s loss (Wei et al., 2022) (but see Schaeffer et al. (2023)). The larger
      language models are therefore said to exhibit emergent capabilities, a term drawn from statistical
034
      mechanics, where small changes in a macroscopic variable of the system (such as temperature)
035
      around a critical value cause an abrupt change—a phase transition or emergent behavior (Baxter,
036   1982)—in its properties. More recently, there has been prevalent discourse in the AI community that
037   further increases in language model size lead to plateauing of performance (Byrnes, 2023; Ritter &
038   Lu, 2024). Although, there have been attempts to explain one or two of these empirical phenomena, a
039   unified mathematical framework that explains all three of these empirically observed phenomena is
040   lacking.
041
      Here we take an approach that builds on information and coding theory (McEliece, 2002) that does
042   so, and also predicts multiple plateaus. In particular, we draw on mathematical ideas around low-
043   density parity check (LDPC) codes (which achieve Shannon optimality) (Sourlas, 1989; Richardson
044   & Urbanke, 2008) and random graph theory (Barabási, 2016). Though statistical language modeling
045   and information theory were introduced in the same paper (Shannon, 1948), modern connections
046   between the two are still fairly limited, cf. Basu et al. (2023).
047
      To provide simple and insightful explanations of empirical phenomena, several abstract frameworks
048   have been proposed (Arora & Goyal, 2023; Liao et al., 2024; Michaud et al., 2023), all based on a
049   skill-text bipartite graph that operates at a semantic level and captures key real-world properties (Yu
050   et al., 2023a). Arora & Goyal (2023) explain emergent phenomena by assuming a compute-optimal
051   size scaling rule (Chinchilla allometry rule) (Hoffmann et al., 2022). Liao et al. (2024) also assume
052   compute-optimal (Chinchilla) size scaling to explain emergence. Michaud et al. (2023) assume
053   power-law scaling and that each text piece contains only one skill, which may be very different
      than real-world scenarios. Moreover, inverse polynomial loss scaling is interpreted as the average


                                                        1
054
      behavior of emergence at different scales. These existing frameworks explain neither the Chinchilla
055   rule nor the plateau phenomenon. These three frameworks abstract the gradient dynamics of language
056   model training (Arora & Goyal, 2023); an alternate mathematical framework considers dynamics to
057   explain the Chinchilla rule and loss function plateaus but does not consider emergence (Bordelon
058   et al., 2024).
059
      Our information-theoretic approach is inspired by skill-text bipartite graph frameworks of Arora
060
      & Goyal (2023); Liao et al. (2024); Michaud et al. (2023) and is closest to Liao et al. (2024). We
061   make a small modification by separating notions of concepts and skills, as in well-established human
062   cognitive architectures (Newell, 1990) that have simple hierarchies (Laird et al., 1987; Anderson,
063   1993; Kieras & Meyer, 1997). In our framework, skills are not directly learned from text; rather,
064   concepts are learned from texts and skills at different levels are learned from concepts (see Section 2
065   for a detailed description). That is, our framework takes on the notion of skill-quanta from Michaud
066   et al. (2023), and so the number of concepts a language model can learn is proportional to the model
067   size (number of model parameters).
068   The key difference in our work is to have much more detailed and expressive analysis using non-
069   asymptotic techniques rather than asymptotic ones (Di et al., 2002). Indeed, such finitary analysis is
070   necessary to even consider size scaling. Recall that Arora & Goyal (2023); Liao et al. (2024) assume
071   Chinchilla scaling, whereas we derive it without it being built into our framework.
072
      The main contributions of this paper are as follows.
073
074         1. We propose a simple unified mathematical framework that considers a language model’s
075            learning of concepts from texts and composition of skills from concepts.
076
            2. Using this framework and tools from non-asymptotic information theory, we deduce
               compute-optimal scaling in language models.
077
            3. With the help of random network theory, we provide a simple explanation for emergent
078
               abilities of language models in complex tasks when their sizes exceed a certain threshold.
079         4. We show that plateauing of performance with size-scaling is just a consequence of diversity
080            of skills required for a task. Moreover, plateauing indicates the possibility of multiple
081            emergences as language models continue to scale further.
082
      Our work takes a step in grounding empirical phenomena observed in size scaling of language models
083
      on a rigorous mathematical footing. Understanding the origin of these phenomena may yield insights
084
      into better architectures, better datasets, and the limitations of large-scale learning systems. Provable
085   optimality of the Chinchilla rule (as in Proposition 1), however, may indicate that there are no gains
086   from better scaling of data and compute remaining, cf. Ho et al. (2024, Appendix B). Separately, our
087   results may help policymakers develop regulatory policy by providing insight into the relationship
088   between capabilities of concern and controllable resources such as data and compute (Hooker, 2024).

      2     G RAPH - BASED FRAMEWORK
092
093   Our framework is based on the notion of learning as two levels. First, a set of concepts are learnt
094
      from a set of texts with each text involving one or more concepts. Second, learning concepts enables
      the language model to acquire skills, and after encountering a sufficient number of texts with co-
095
      occurring pairs of skills, the model eventually acquires compositional abilities resulting in emergent
096
      phenomena in various complex tasks. The framework naturally leads to information-theoretic analysis
097   in Section 3.

      2.1   T EXTS , CONCEPTS , AND SKILLS
100
101
      A set of tokens constitute a text (similar to a text piece defined in Arora & Goyal (2023)) from which
102   a language model can learn a set of concepts. This is modeled as a concept-text bipartite graph similar
103   to the skill-text bipartite graph in Liao et al. (2024). Note that we consider single epoch training
104   as described in Hoffmann et al. (2022). The total number of concepts a model can learn depends
105   on its size (number of model parameters). Here we consider a hierarchy of skills: basic skills in
106   the first layer and multiple layers of advanced skills. Basic skills are easily acquired from concepts,
107   whereas acquiring advanced skills additionally requires certain prerequisite (less advanced) skills.
      We formalize these semantic learning notions in the sequel. Note that this bipartite graph formulation

      of learning is intimately related to graph-based approaches to data compression (Martinian & Yedidia,
109   2003) and associative memory (Karbasi et al., 2013). Moreover, although this approach to abstract
110   modeling has been tied to Transformer-based language modeling architectures (Yu et al., 2023a), it
111   can describe a variety of quite different learning paradigms (Yu et al., 2023b).

193   2.5   D EFINING EMERGENCE
194
195
      In the context of neural language models, there are several definitions of skill emergence in the
      literature. In most existing frameworks, skills are directly associated with texts. In the skill-text
196
      bipartite graph framework of Arora & Goyal (2023), the fraction of text pieces in error (incorrect
197
      answers to cloze questions) is obtained from the Chinchilla rule, where the error fraction is smaller
198   for larger model sizes. Emergence is defined in terms of the error rate of the skill-tuple, i.e., the
199   fraction of edges to error-marked text pieces from k-tuple of skills, as follows: For a fixed target error
200   threshold, with an increase in model size, emergence is defined as increase in the largest size of the
201   skill tuple k whose error rate is below the threshold. According to this definition of emergence, there
202   is no phase transition and conforms to the notion that emergence is slow.
203
      In Liao et al. (2024), emergence is defined as a function of the ratio of number of text pieces to skills:
204   with increase in the ratio of number of texts to skills, emergence is defined as the increase in the size
205   (normalized) of the largest connected component corresponding to the learnt skills. This definition
206   of emergence exhibits a phase transition around a specific value of text-to-skill ratio. However, this
207   definition of emergence as a function of text-to-skill ratio (not of model size) does not follow the
208   definition of emergence, for example in Wei et al. (2022): “An ability is emergent if it is not present
209   in smaller models but is present in larger models.”
210   A critical view of emergent abilities is given in Schaeffer et al. (2023), arguing that emergence in
211   performance (e.g. in terms of accuracy) is only a consequence of quantization of another metric
212   (e.g. token edit distance) which shows gradual improvement with size scaling, and hence is only a
213   mirage. Although this argument holds, we maintain a more optimistic view. How the performance of
214   the model is measured is important, but corresponding to a performance metric, there is an abstract
215   quantity such as the ability to compose multiple skills, which a language model gains when the model
      size exceeds a certain threshold to exhibit a true phase transition.

217   than directly from text pieces. To describe the composition of skills not seen in training, we begin
218   by asserting transitivity of skill composition for a fixed skill level l: if the training data contains
219                                                         (l) (l)         (l) (l)
      enough text pieces with composition of both pairs (s1 , s2 ) and (s2 , s3 ), then a language model
220                                   (l)      (l)
      is capable of composing skill s1 and s3 . Consequently, a language model successfully performs a
221                                                         (l)
222   sub-task requiring a composition of a set of skills Sθ ⊆ S (l) if there is a path between every pair
                               (l)           (l)
223   of skills belonging to Sθ in graph G2 . As we will see in Section 3.3, the skill graph with nodes
        (l)
224   S is an Erdös-Rényi (ER) random graph with edges indicating pairwise composition of skills seen
225   in training, and the relationship between composition of a set of skills and the presence of giant
226   connected component.
227   For small compute budgets, dataset size corresponding to compute-optimal performance is small, in
228   which case the training data contains composition of only a small number of skill pairs. As compute
229   budget increases, the size of the training data increases, and therefore the number of composed
230   skill pairs seen by the language model during training increases. Beyond a certain compute-budget
231   threshold and due to skill composition transitivity, the ability of the language model to compose most
232   skill pairs emerges, appearing as a phase transition around this compute-budget threshold. As we will
233   see in Section 3.3, this phase transition is related to the appearance of a giant connected component
234
      (GCC) in random graphs with increasing edge probability. Our definition of emergence exhibits phase
      transition as empirically observed in language models, and our finitary analysis helps in conforming
235
      to the definition of emergence in Wei et al. (2022).

513   4     C ONCLUSION
514
515   We presented a simple unified framework to explain all three empirical phenomena observed with
516   size scaling of language models. Existing frameworks assume a compute-optimal scaling rule and
517   only then explain emergent phenomena. We use non-asymptotic information theory to explain both
518   compute-optimal size scaling and emergent abilities of language models. Moreover, we explain the
519   more recent empirical phenomenon of plateauing of performance using random network theory, and
520   also predict that plateauing implies the possibility of multiple emergent phenomena with further size
521   scaling.
522   There are some open questions and considerations worth exploring. We do not take training time into
523   account in our framework. Therefore, we do not explain (or attempt to explain) empirical phenomena
524   such as double descent or grokking (Huang et al., 2024). Perhaps future work can either incorporate
525   training epochs in our framework or propose a different novel framework to explain them. Even
526   though the sequential learning of concepts through peeling process gives certain ordering to concepts,
527   there is no inherent ordering of concepts and we do not consider concept hierarchies (Yu et al.,
528
      2023b;c). One can explore the advantages of doing so. Evidently, the degree distribution of texts is
      related to the model’s architecture. Therefore, optimizing the degree distribution enables a language
529
      model to learn more concepts from text pieces. Further, the quality of the training data is related to
530
      text-to-concept edge deletions in sequential concept learning, which can be incorporated into our
531   framework. Such optimization is a line of future work that has natural analogues in optimization of
532   communication systems and fault-tolerant computation (Richardson & Urbanke, 2008).

642   Low-density parity check (LDPC) codes are a family of error-correction codes, whose noisy code-
643   words can be decoded in a computationally efficient manner using belief propagation. Before getting
644   into deriving the probability that a concept is learnt from text pieces, we provide a very short summary
645   of belief propagation decoding of LDPC codes when codeword symbols are corrupted by erasure. An
646   LDPC code can be graphically represented by a Tanner graph, which is a bipartite graph with a set of
647   variable nodes (codeword symbols) and check nodes (parity checks). Each codeword satisfies all the
      parity checks. Given a degree distribution pair (for variable and check nodes), there is a channel noise


                                                        12

651   erased). Then the erased symbol is determined as the one satisfying the parity. The next iteration
652   starts by finding another check node with only one erased codeword symbol. This process continues
653   until either all the codeword symbols are decoded or the decoder gets stuck with no parity checks
654   containing only one erased symbol. The latter is declared as a decoding failure.
