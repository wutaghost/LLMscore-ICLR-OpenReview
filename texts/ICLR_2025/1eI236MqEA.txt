                                                   A BSTRACT
011
012            Customization generation techniques have significantly advanced the synthesis of
013
               specific concepts across varied contexts. Multi-concept customization emerges as
               the challenging task within this domain. Existing approaches often rely on train-
014
               ing a fusion matrix of multiple Low-Rank Adaptations (LoRAs) to merge various
015
               concepts into a single image. However, we identify this straightforward method
016            faces two major challenges: 1) concept confusion, where the model struggles to
017            preserve distinct individual characteristics, and 2) concept vanishing, where the
018            model fails to generate the intended subjects. To address these issues, we intro-
019            duce LoRA-Composer, a training-free framework designed for seamlessly inte-
020            grating multiple LoRAs, thereby enhancing the harmony among different concepts
021            within generated images. LoRA-Composer addresses concept vanishing through
022            concept injection constraints, enhancing concept visibility via an expanded cross-
023            attention mechanism. To combat concept confusion, concept isolation constraints
024
               are introduced, refining the self-attention computation. Furthermore, latent re-
               initialization is proposed to effectively stimulate concept-specific latent within
025
               designated regions. Our extensive testing showcases a notable enhancement in
026
               LoRA-Composer’s performance compared to standard baselines, especially when
027            eliminating the image-based conditions like canny edge or pose estimations.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031   Diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2021) have significantly advanced the field of image generation,
032   particularly in creating images that adhere to user-specific concepts. The progress made in cus-
033   tomization models (Gu et al., 2023; Kumari et al., 2022; Shah et al., 2023; Ruiz et al., 2022; Yang
034   et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023) play an important role in enriching the landscape of image synthesis.
035   As technologies for single concept customization evolve, users are presented with various meth-
036   ods to personalize content, ranging from fine-tuning U-Net (Ruiz et al., 2022; Kumari et al., 2022),
037   modifying text embeddings (Gal et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023c), to leveraging Low-Rank Adaptations
038   (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021). LoRA is a versatile, plug-and-play module that enables users to customize
039
      their models to generate diverse and lifelike personal images. Its adaptability and accuracy in image
      generation have established LoRA as a preferred method for customization tasks.
040
041   While LoRA excels in single-concept customization, its application to emerging multi-concept cus-
042   tomization presents challenges. Recent developments have explored the integration of multiple-
043   concept LoRAs to infuse images with diverse concepts via fusion tuning (Gu et al., 2023; Wang
044   et al., 2023). However, as illustrated in Fig. 1, these integration strategies often necessitate a variety
045
      of conditions, including textual and image-based inputs (Zhang et al., 2023) (such as human pose
      and canny edge), constraining variation and flexibility. Furthermore, in the process of combining
046
      multiple LoRAs, prior research (Gu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Smith et al., 2023) focused on
047
      training a fusion ratio matrix, which aims to optimally weigh individual LoRAs. However, adjusting
048   LoRA weights in this manner can exacerbate two problems: 1) concept vanishing, where the concept
049   fails to be injected into the figure; and 2) concept confusion, where the model struggles to associate
050   attributes with subjects or fails to capture distinct concept characteristics. Examples illustrating the
051   issues are displayed in the top row of Fig. 1, showcasing outputs from the representative method
052   Mix-of-Show (Gu et al., 2023). The left column shows a clear case of concept vanishing, where the
053   model fails to generate one of the dogs (within the red box). The right column highlights issues with
      incorrect attribute binding, such as the dog’s color being mistaken (within the blue box).

       089     and quantitative assessments. In summary, our contributions are as follows:

109   ages in unprecedented high quality. To further support image generation from fine-grained spatial
110   conditions, like sketches, human keypoints, semantic maps, etc., ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023)

      text description, while discouraging the latent features outside the box from attending to the text.

120   2.2   M ULTI -C ONCEPT C USTOMIZATION
121
      Concept customization aims at generating concepts specified by a few input images. While signif-
122   icant progress has been made in generating a single custom concept (Ruiz et al., 2022; Gal et al.,
123   2022; Tewel et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2023; Han et al., 2023; Nichol & Dhariwal,
124   2021; Li et al., 2023a; Ruiz et al., 2023), the customized generation of multiple concepts remains
125   challenging. A pioneer work, Custom Diffusion (Kumari et al., 2022), jointly finetunes multiple
126   concept images for customization. Cones series (Liu et al., 2023b;d) finds concept-related neurons
127   in pre-trained diffusion models for multi-concept customization. To accelerate the customized gen-
128   eration, FastComposer (Xiao et al., 2023) finetunes diffusion model on massive data to take subject
129   embedding as input and generate the composed image of multiple concepts. Similarly, Paint-by-
130
      Example (Yang et al., 2023) and AnyDoor (Chen et al., 2023) are trained on a significant amount
      of images and can achieve multi-concept generation through image inpainting. Considering the
131
      widespread utilization of LoRA for customization , several recent works (Gu et al., 2023; Wang
132
      et al., 2023; Shah et al., 2023; Zhong et al., 2024) seek to achieve multi-concept customization by
133   combining multiple LoRA weights of individual concepts. For example, Mix-of-show (Gu et al.,
134   2023) proposes gradient fusion to train a composed LoRA weight that mimics the prediction of
135   individual LoRAs. It further leverages T2I-Adaptor (Mou et al., 2023) and sketches or key points
136   for final generation. A concurrent work (Zhong et al., 2024) proposes two variants, namely LoRA
137   Swich and LoRA Composite, to realize LoRA merge during decoding. The former uses multi-
138   ple LoRA weights sequentially, while the latter averages the latent obtained from different LoRA
139   weights. However, they focus on the combination of a single foreground and a single background
140   LoRA weights. By contrast, we tackle the more challenging task of customizing multiple foreground
141
      characters, facing issues of concept confusion and vanishing.
142   Probably the most similar work to ours is Mix-of-Show, but we emphasize the following differences.
143   Firstly, Mix-of-Show requires repeated gradient fusion training for each combination of multiple
144   concepts, while we achieve this on the fly without retraining the LoRA weight. Secondly, Mix-of-
145   Show requires additional image-based conditions, like sketches and keypoints, as input for high-
146   quality image generation, which could be difficult to obtain.

      Simply using text prompts to specify desired concepts may result in missing concepts in the basic
191   Stable Diffusion (Chefer et al., 2023). Although spatial attention guidance methods like BoxD-
192   iff (Xie et al., 2023), Attend-and-Excite (Chefer et al., 2023), and Local control (Zhao et al., 2023)
193   can mitigate the issue of missing objects in multi-concept generation, they fall short in precisely
194   represent user-defined concepts. Additionally, Mix-of-Show (Gu et al., 2023) involves optimizing
195   a combination of lesser LoRA weights to preserve the characteristics of the concepts within the
196   pre-trained model. However, this can diminish LoRA’s capability to represent conceptual features,
197   resulting in diminished concepts, as illustrated in Fig. 1. To tackle these challenges, we introduce
198   the concept injection constraints, which is comprised of two key components: region-aware LoRA
199
      injection and concept enhancement constraints.

      3.3    C ONCEPT I SOLATION C ONSTRAINTS
263
264   While the concept injection constraints effectively guarantee that objects will be placed within user-
265   specified regions, they cannot prevent the potential overlap or infection of customized concepts
266   within these areas (see Fig. 6(d)). To preserve the integrity and distinctiveness of each concept within
267   its designated region, we introduce concept isolation constraints. This approach is divided into
268   two main components: concept region mask and region perceptual restriction. Both elements are
269   integrated within the self-attention of the U-Net block, ensuring that each concept remains isolated
      and unaffected by others, thereby maintaining the purity of concepts in target regions.

      Concept Region Mask. The self-attention mechanism creates connections among all query ele-
271   ments, essential for maintaining the distinct characteristics of each concept. To preserve the dis-
272   tinctiveness of each concept, we adopt the concept region mask strategy, guided by a given layout
273   condition as depicted in Fig. 3(b). This design limits the interaction between queries within a spe-
274   cific concept region and those in other concept regions, as demonstrated in Fig. 3(c). Thus, it ensures
275   the preservation of each concept’s characteristics, free from the influence of neighboring concepts.
276

280   between queries of the foreground and background areas. This technique ensures that each concept

      3.4   L ATENT R E - INITIALIZATION
301
302   We discovered that traditional LoRA is not ideally suited for generating specific local areas, because
303   it is trained without control in location. This discrepancy can result in imprecise locations for con-
304   cept generation (see Fig. 6(c)). To address this issue, we propose re-initializing the latent space to
305   better accommodate the integration of concept-specific LoRAs.
306   Our approach aims to identify the position within the latent space where the object is likely to appear
307   and then align this position with the specified layout. Specifically, before the denoising phase, we
308   initialize the latent space zt with Gaussian noise and apply the LoRA-Composer process for a one-
309   step update using Eq. (9). Afterward, a cross-attention map is generated based on the region latent
310   query Qi and the textual embedding Ki for each local prompt. First, we compute each candidate
311   area with the same shape as the layout. Then, we replace the layout area zt [Mi ] with the latent area
312   corresponding to the highest scoring area among the candidate areas. Finally, the latent is normalized
313   to a standard Gaussian distribution. The aforementioned candidate areas can be expressed as:

      4.5      A BLATION S TUDY
454
455   To demonstrate the efficacy of each component in our method, we choose a challenging scenario
456   with five and four concepts for this section. As illustrated in Fig. 6(c), the positions of the anime girl
457   and the dog within the red box diverge from the layout conditions outlined in Fig. 6(a). This discrep-
458   ancy serves as evidence that omitting latent re-initialization (LR) hampers the precise placement of
459   concepts, mainly because of the lack of spatial priors. Subsequently, as shown in Fig. 6(d), removing
460
      the concept isolation constraints (CI) results in the blending of concept characteristics. This results in
      observable issues such as the confusion in the anime girl’s haircut, and the distortion of the woman’s
461
      arm. Without CI, concepts begin to overlap and influence each other, resulting in a disruption of
462
      harmony and coherence in the overall image composition. Finally, as shown in Fig. 6(e), eliminat-
463   ing the concept enhancement constraints (CE) results in the disappearance of concepts. However,
464   thanks to the presence of region-aware LoRA injection, the model retains the capability to insert
465   concepts, though with diminished precision in their placement and representation. This highlights
466   each element’s critical role in achieving precise and harmonious concept integration.
467
      To substantiate our findings as non-coincidental, we conducted a comprehensive quantitative evalu-
468
      ation. As detailed in Tab. 2, our analysis demonstrates the individual and collective impacts of CE,
469   LR, and CI on performance. CE leads to significant improvements across all performance metrics,
470   showcasing its effectiveness in activating concepts, as evidenced by the largest increase in mean
471   image similarity. LR further contributed to these enhancements by refining region-specific priors.
472   CI played a crucial role in preserving the distinctiveness of concept traits and enhancing model
473   robustness. More ablation results are shown in Appendix C.2.

475   5      C ONCLUSION
476
477
      In this paper, we introduce LoRA-Composer, a novel approach designed to seamlessly integrate mul-
      tiple concepts within a single image. We explore two prevalent issues in multi-concept customiza-
478
      tion: concept vanishing and concept confusion. To this end, we employ concept injection constraints
479
      to combat concept vanishing, while concept isolation constraints alleviate concept confusion. Addi-
480   tionally, we propose latent re-initialization to provide precise region priors. Our experiments high-
481   light the capability of LoRA-Composer to customize entire images, including both background and
482   foreground elements, and to manipulate the interactions and attributes of various concepts through
483   textual prompts. Compared to traditional methods, LoRA-Composer offers enhanced flexibility and
484   usability, allowing users to generate images with fewer conditions and readily accessible LoRA
485   techniques. Furthermore, we demonstrate the method’s ability to achieve high-fidelity combinations
      of multiple concepts, underscoring its practical utility in complex image-generation tasks.

      A    P RELIMINARY
670
      Diffusion Models are famous for their capacity to generate high-quality images. Their framework
671
      operates in two primary phases: the forward phase, where Gaussian noise is progressively added
672   to an image until it fully conforms to a Gaussian distribution, and the reverse phase, which aims
673   to reconstruct the original image from its noised condition. The reverse phase typically employs
674   a U-Net architecture enhanced with text conditioning, enabling the synthesis of images based on
675   textual descriptions during inference. In this work, we employ Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al.,
676   2021), which distinguishes itself by operating in the latent space rather than directly manipulating
677   image pixels through these phases. This approach involves an autoencoder, with an encoder E
678   and decoder D, trained to serve as a bridge between image pixel space x and latent space z, i.e.,
679   D(z) = D(E(x)). In each time step t, given a textual condition τ (P ) and an image x, where P
680
      represents the text prompt and τ denotes the pre-trained CLIP text encoder (Radford et al., 2021).
      The training objective for Stable Diffusion is to minimize the denoising objective by
681
682
                             Lsd = Ez∼E(x),P,ϵ∼N (0,1),t ∥ϵ − ϵθ (zt , t, τ (P ))∥22 ,

683                                                                                                     (11)
684   where ϵθ is the denoising U-Net with learnable parameter θ.
685
      ED-LoRA (Gu et al., 2023) aims to augment the expressiveness of the embedding by employing a
686
      decomposed structure. We use it by default. ED-LoRA implements a layer-wise embedding strategy,
687   following the method described in P+ (Voynov et al., 2023), to forge a multi-faceted representation
688   for the concept token (V = [Vrand , Vclass ]). This involves adding a random variation (Vrand ) and
689   a class-specific component to the base embedding (Vclass ). Furthermore, it integrates a LoRA layer
690   into the linear layers present in all attention modules of the text encoder and U-Net. This integration
691   allows for a flexible adaptation of the model to specific concepts by modifying the linear layers in a
692   low-rank manner, thereby enhancing the model’s ability to encode and synthesize images based on
693   textual descriptions with high fidelity. We use it by default in all experiments.
694
695   B    I MPLEMENTATION D ETAIL
696
697   ED-LoRA Setting. We chose ED-LoRA due to its strong capability in maintaining concept fidelity.
698   In alignment with the single-concept ED-LoRA tuning guidelines from (Gu et al., 2023), we inte-
699   grate the LoRA layer into the linear layers of all attention modules within both the text encoder and
700   U-Net, setting a rank (r) of 4 for all experiments. For optimization, we employ the Adam opti-
701   mizer (Kingma & Ba, 2014), utilizing learning rates of 1e-5 for the text encoder and 1e-4 for U-Net
      tuning. Follow Mix-of-Show (Gu et al., 2023), we use 5-20 images for training each concept.
