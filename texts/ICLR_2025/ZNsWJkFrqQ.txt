009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Despite a widespread success in various applications, large language models
012            (LLMs) often stumble when tackling basic physical reasoning or executing robotics
013            tasks, due to a lack of direct experience with the physical nuances of the real world.
014            To address these issues, we propose a Grounding Large language model with Imper-
015            fect world MOdel (GLIMO), which utilizes proxy world models such as simulators
016
               to collect and synthesize trining data. GLIMO incorporates an LLM agent-based
               data generator to automatically create high-quality and diverse instruction datasets.
017
               The generator includes an iterative self-refining module for temporally consistent
018
               experience sampling, a diverse set of question-answering instruction seeds, and a
019            retrieval-augmented generation module for reflecting on prior experiences. Com-
020            prehensive experiments show that our approach improve the performance of strong
021            open-source LLMs like LLaMA-3 with a performance boost of 2.04 ×, 1.54 ×,
022            and 1.82 × across three different benchmarks, respectively. The performance is
023            able to compete with or surpass their larger counterparts such as GPT-4.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
      Recent advances in Large Language Models (LLMs) is bringing great transformation in various
028
      robotics applications, such as self-driving cars (Mao et al. (2023)), autonomous drones (Vemprala
029
      et al. (2023)), robotics manipulation (Liang et al. (2022)). LLMs are able to enhance robots with rich
030   common-sense knowledge and complex planning capabilities. However, LLM needs to be physically
031   grounded in reality, which includes understanding of the environment dynamics, the task-related
032   constraints, and the consequences of its actions (Gao et al. (2023); Rana et al. (2023)).
033
      Many previous works in robot learning heavily rely on prompting, such as (1) decomposing problem
034
      structures using human priors (Rana et al. (2023); Liang et al. (2022)), (2) self-refinement (Zhang
035
      et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2023a)) and (3) external tools (Mao et al. (2023)). This approach does not
036   alter the weights of the model, instead relying on the pretrained knowledge of the LLMs. However,
037   LLMs are trained with text corpus, lacking the understanding of the fine-grained semantics in physical
038   environments. It also suffers from hallucination problems (Rawte et al. (2023)), and difficulties with
039   understanding time-aware actions (Dhingra et al. (2021)). Moreover, the "heavy prompting" approach
040   often proves effective only in small-scale environments, like a predefined room with fixed sets of
041   objects and available actions (Rana et al. (2023)). The prompts we use may easily overfit to these
042   small-scale environments, making the evaluation results less applicable to open-ended real-world
043   robotic tasks.
044   To overcome those limitations, instruction tuning (Wei et al. (2021)) was proposed as a powerful
045   solution by leveraging a question-answering data to finetune the LLMs and learn new capabili-
046   ties (Zhou et al. (2023)). Studies also show that instruction tuning can mitigate hallucination (Ouyang
047   et al. (2022)), enhance the agent capabilities in using tools (Schick et al. (2023)), and improve
048   understanding of the physical world (Mao et al. (2023)).
049   Recent work (Xiang et al. (2023)) applied instruction tuning to enhance LLM’s capability in embodied
050   tasks with a new learning paradigm, Embodied Experiences from World Model (E2WM), which
051   samples embodied experiences from a world model and fine-tunes LLMs with instruction data derived
052   from those experiences. However, such an approach has several limitations. First, directly sampling
053   experiences from a perfect world model is costly and often unsafe in real-world robotics applications,
      such as autonomous driving. Second, such approaches are less generalizable. E2WM assumes that

071   each specific tasks. For example, E2WM requires manually designing the downstream tasks, such as

083

084

086   augmented generation module for the LLM to reflect on prior collected experiences and enhance
087   the effectiveness of future exploration.
088

089

090

091   two environments: a 2D strategic puzzle game Agent World similar to Tower Of Sorcerer and a

094   a distribution shift between imperfect world model and world model. Our fine-tuned model beats the
095   SoTA close source model GPT-4 by a large margin of 51.7% and 84.5% in Agent World and Urban
096   Driving environments, respectively.

129   World Model. "World model" refers to a digital representation of the physical world, which can
130   simulate real-world changes in response to actions (Ha & Schmidhuber (2018)). A large body
131
      of previous studies on machine learning and cognitive science leveraged world model to enable
      data-efficient learning and strong generalization in unseen scenarios (Allen et al. (2020); Battaglia
132
      et al. (2013)). Humans have an innate world model for predicting outcomes of actions. Recent studies
133
      develop these models in LLMs for enhanced reasoning. Xiang et al. (2023) fine-tuned LLMs with
134   collected experiences in a grounded physics engine, VirtualHome (Puig et al. (2018)), to mirror real
135   conditions. However, those approach is infeasible in real-world robotics tasks due to its high-cost
136   and safety concerns in directly collecting embodied experiences from the real world. Instead, our
137   approach mitigates these issues by enabling learning from imperfect world model, such as simulators.
138
139   Language Model Grounding. Recent research has focused on integrating language models (LMs)
140   with world models to improve task performance. Techniques vary from prompting (Wang et al.
141   (2023a)) and fine-tuning through supervised learning (Wei et al. (2021)) to reinforcement learn-
142   ing (Ouyang et al. (2022)). Previous works (Xiang et al. (2023)) show that LLMs can improve their
143   capabilities by fine-tuning on embodied experiences collected from world models. This process
144   involves adjusting the model’s parameters to better reflect the complexities and nuances of real-world
145   interactions. By simulating scenarios within world models, LLMs can learn to predict outcomes
146
      more accurately and adapt to dynamic environments. This method has proven especially beneficial
      in scenarios where LLMs must interact with physical or simulated environments, enhancing their
147
      decision-making and problem-solving abilities.

211   3.2   G ENERATING G ROUNDED E MBODIED E XPERIENCES W ITH LLM AGENTS
212
213   Instruction tuning (Wei et al. (2021)) is an effective approach to enhance LLMs’ capabilities in
214   domain-specific problems. However, instruction tuning requires a textual question-answering format.
215   To fit this paradigm, previous works manually design formats to extract knowledge from collected
      experiences, such as plan generation and object path tracking (Xiang et al. (2023)). These approaches,


                                                           4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      which integrate human prior knowledge about what is useful in specific tasks, are still unwieldy and
217   difficult to scale in real-world robotics tasks.
218
219   To address this, we propose to use an LLM agent-based framework to interpret embodied experiences
220
      and automatically transform it into instruction tuning format, in a scalable manner. The LLM-based
221   agent framework keeps track of the embodied experiences in the target environment D̂. The LLM
222
      interacts with the embodied experiences as a multi-turn dialogue, and generates grounded instruction
      data. Our framework let the LLM to explore the environment via a self-refining iterative prompting
223
      mechanis (Shinn et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2023a); Yao et al. (2023)).
224
225   Figure 3 illustrates the pipeline for our LLM agent-based system, designed to guide the sampling
226   of embodied experiences and optimize them for instruction tuning. On the left side of the figure,
227   the LLM agent iteratively executes a plan and evaluates whether the defined goal has been achieved.
228
      During each iteration, the agent not only assesses the success of the current plan but also compares
      it against prior experiences. This iterative approach ensures that the agent continuously learns and
229
      adapts from historical data.
230
231   We present the prompt templates that are integral to this process. These templates serve three
232   key functions: (i) enabling self-refinement based on past experiences, (ii) generating rationales or
233   explanations for the agent’s decisions to support chain-of-thought fine-tuning Lightman et al. (2023),
234
      and (iii) encouraging exploration to enhance the diversity of the generated data. These prompt
      templates ensure that the LLM agents produce high-quality, diverse embodied experiences, which
235
      are essential for refining the agent’s decision-making capabilities in dynamic environments. We will
236
      introduce those with more details in the remained section.
237
238
      Agent Integration. We use text-only LLMs in this paper, as we focus on whether the textual
239
      embedding space learned by LLMs is able to transfer the dynamics from imperfect world model to
240   perfect world model, which also helps our future study in multimodal LLMs. To generate text inputs
241   and enable actions for LLMs, we introduce: (1) a tool module, that interacts with the environment
242   to provide necessary information. For example, a neural object detection module, or a collision
243   detection module. LLM agents will call the tool to acquire necessary information for the decision-
244   making. (2) A customizable memory module that explicitly retains previous actions and observations,
245   integrating human prior knowledge about what is necessary past information for future tasks. (3) A
246   skill library that encodes necessary skills for LLMs to act in the physical world. The skills works as a
247   function calling with certain format: e.g., a lane_change(lane_id:int) skill can be called,
248
      and we have a low-level implementation to actuate this skill. The skill library is needed to cope with
      continuous control task.
249

251
      Reasoning. Reasoning is important in sampling experiences, reject trivial experiences and explore
      valuable directions. We use prompt engineering techniques such as Chain-of-thoughts (Wei et al.
252
      (2022)), ReAct (Yao et al. (2023)) and Reflexion (Shinn et al. (2023)) to faciliate reasoning and refine
253
      the LLM’s decisions, and mitigate error accumulation. To enhance the LLMs’ ability in reasoning,
254   we use an iterative prompting method as in (Wang et al. (2023a)) to self-refine and verify proposed
255   action sequences. By performing exploration actions iteratively, the LLM learns to self-reflect and
256   refine its actions. Figure 3 shows a self-consistency example to generate n reasoning data and using
257   votes to select the groundtruth Wang et al. (2023b).

261   Imagine a game in which the player makes a single decision, choosing between option A and option
262   B. The success rate of option A is 100%, while the success rate of option B is 80%. Without any prior

264   adjust the LLMs’ weights based on maximum likelihood principles. As a result, the LLM might
265   slightly favor option A. However, in real-world decision-making, we desire a deterministic policy
266
      that always chooses option A. This example highlights the importance of reflecting on embodied
      experiences along with past experiences.
267

      previous related embodied experiences, and generate instruction data based on this contexts (Lewis

306   3.4   A NNOTATION G ENERATION
307
308   Our pipeline automatically transform embodied experiences to grounded instruction data formats.
309
      Motivated by self-instruct (Wang et al. (2022)), we begins with a small set of task templates. Random
      tasks are sampled from this pool and used to prompt the LLM to generate both new instructions and
310
      corresponding instances. Low-quality or similar generations are then filtered out, and the remaining
311
      high-quality instances are added back to the initial repository of tasks. Such self-refining and
312   bootstrapping process improve the diversity and quality of the generated data. They also constantly
313   receiving the feedback from the simulators to correct possible errors.
314
315
      Notable, our task pool includes the following tasks which significantly improve the finetuned model:
      (1) Rationale Enhancement. Incorporating explanations or rationales has been shown to improve
316
      LLM performance, as indicated by previous studies (Lampinen et al. (2022)). By understanding the
317
      reasoning behind decisions, LLMs can form more robust strategies that better mimic human-like
318   reasoning. (2) Counterfactual Reasoning involves training LMs to consider alternative outcomes
319   or scenarios that didn’t occur but could have, under different circumstances. By engaging in this
320   type of hypothetical reasoning, LMs can develop a deeper understanding of causality and potential
321   consequences in physical environments, leading to more informed and effective decision-making. (3)
322   Episodic Memory Question Answering (Datta et al. (2022)) is a question-answering format that
323   queries pre-recorded embodied experiences to assess the spatial and temporal understanding of a
      scenario. For example, it might ask, "Where is the altar you encountered most recently?".

      We also annotate the grounded instruction dataset by querying on different temporal scale. For
325   example, Figure 3.3 shows the comparison of two high-level plans: getting the hammer and attack
326   the boss. Those actions are carried out with a sequence of low-level actions of up/down/left/right
327   movement. We also design different questions on a shorter temporal scale, e.g. "Is it able to access
328   the yellow key directly?"
329
330   Filtering. The purpose of the filtering is to encourage diverse and high-quality data samples, which
331   is critical for the performance of instruction tuning Ouyang et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2022). We
332   find two approaches effective in our experiments: Ask LLM to determine if the new data is novel
333   with chain-of-thoughts prompting, according to the tagging as mentioned in 3.3. We also leverage
334
      self-consistency to generate multiple samples to evaluate the novelty, and take the majority of the
      voting results. Use domain specific model or generative model to embed the observation, and filter
335
      the samples that are in distribution. For the urban-driving scenario, we use a variational autoencoder
336
      and leverage the reconstruction loss to evict easy samples, a low reconstruction loss means the data is
337   in-distribution, and easy to predict. This is actually similar to the idea of hard negative data mining.
338
339   Training. We take a two-stage finetuning approach: (1) in the first stage, we improve the instruction
340   following capability of our base model as in (Liu et al. (2024)), Chat-QA instruction dataset also
341   specializes in understanding tabular data and text retrieval, which we deem very useful in our
342   embodied setup. (2) in the second stage, we finetune our model with our grounded embodied
343   experiences. To save computation resources and avoid overfitting, we use LoRA techniques (Hu
344   et al. (2021)) to finetune LLMs such as LLaMA-3 (Touvron et al. (2023)) and OPT-13B (Zhang et al.
345   (2022)).

397   particularly in out-of-distribution (OOD) scenarios. This suggests that data from the imperfect world
398   model can serve as an effective "regularization" technique to mitigate overfitting, a common challenge
399   in LLM supervised finetuning (Luo et al. (2024b)).

408   (OOD) question answering data. We also study the exploration capability with an increasing number

      5   L IMITATIONS
487
488
      Due to the limitations of conducting real-world testing in academia, including insufficient resources
489   and costs, our approach is evaluated solely in simulated environments, such as games and self-driving
490   simulators, which may not sufficiently generalize to complex real-world scenarios. Additionally, our
491   LLMs currently accept only text inputs, while robotics applications require perception modalities to
492   comprehend fine-grained environmental semantics. In future work, we aim to extend our approach to
493   multimodal LLMs, as they can incorporate perception capabilities.

      6   C ONCLUSION
496
497
      In this paper, we presented GLIMO, a novel approach to enhance the performance of large language
498   models (LLMs) in robotics applications by leveraging imperfect world models. By using an automatic
499   LLM annotation agent that includes an iterative self-refining module to explore the environment,
500   a diverse set of question-answering instruction sets, and a retrieval-augmented generation module,
501   we demonstrated that GLIMO can effectively fine-tune LLMs using embodied experiences from
502   imperfect world models.

      Agent World. The Tower of the Sorcerer tow is a puzzle game where the player (as a warrior) takes
858   an adventure in a 17-level tower, the player needs to explore the tower, improve itself to get ready for
859   the final boss and save the princess. Players enhance their character’s attack strength, defense, and
860   health by collecting items, such as potions, crystals, and equipment, and by donating gold at altars
861   to acquire experiences or strength. The game features a deterministic combat system where players
862   and monsters take turns striking each other, with damage calculated as the difference between attack
863   and defense strengths. This necessitates thoughtful planning and strategic battle order to efficiently
      progress through levels while conserving resources like health and keys. A interesting tool is the Orb

      intricate setup makes the game an excellent platform for evaluating decision-making algorithms,
869
