009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Offline-to-online reinforcement learning (O2O RL) offers a promising paradigm
012            that first pre-trains an offline policy and fine-tunes it with further online interac-
013            tions. Nevertheless, the distribution shift between the offline and online phase
014            often hinders the fine-tuning performance, sometimes even incurring performance
015            collapse. Existing methods mitigate this by enhancing training robustness with
016            Q-ensemble, training a density ratio estimator to balance offline and online data,
017            etc. But they often rely on components like ensemble and have higher training
018
               costs. In this paper, we address this issue by establishing a concrete performance
               bound for the optimal policies between two consecutive online steps. Motivated
019
               by the theoretical insight, we propose a simple yet effective fine-tuning method,
020
               Prioritized Experience Selection (PES). During the online stage, PES maintains a
021            dynamically updated priority queue containing a portion of high-return trajecto-
022            ries, and only selects online samples that are close to the samples in the queue for
023            fine-tuning. In this way, the distribution shift issue can be mitigated and the fine-
024            tuning performance can be boosted. PES is computationally efficient and com-
025            patible with numerous approaches. Experimental results on a variety of D4RL
026            datasets show that PES can benefit different offline and O2O RL algorithms and
027            enhance Q-value estimate. Our code is available and will be open-source.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Online reinforcement learning (RL) (Sutton & Barto, 1999; François-Lavet et al., 2018) presents a
033   paradigm that the agent learns an optimal policy by interacting with the environment. However, this
034
      trial-and-error manner also imposes inherent risks of high costs or even danger. Offline RL (Levine
      et al., 2020; Prudencio et al., 2023), instead, learns the optimal policy from a previously collected
035
      dataset, which could be sourced from historical data, expert knowledge, or behavior policies. Such
036
      learning paradigm is promising since it eliminates the need for interacting with the environment.
037   Nevertheless, the performance of the offline RL algorithm often suffers from the size and quality
038   of the underlying static dataset, e.g., learning with a small dataset with poor quality makes it chal-
039   lenging to learn superior policies. To leverage the advantages of both online RL and offline RL, the
040   Offline-to-Online (O2O) RL paradigm (Xie et al., 2021; Ball et al., 2023; Wagenmaker & Pacchi-
041   ano, 2023) has been explored, where the agent is first pre-trained on the offline dataset, and then
042   further fine-tuned through online interactions with the environment. While this pre-training + fine-
043   tuning paradigm is widely used and proved effective in computer vision (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020;
044   Radford et al., 2021) and natural language processing (Devlin et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2019b; Brown
045
      et al., 2020; Hu et al., 2021), its effectiveness in RL is generally not as promising. Especially, dur-
      ing the fine-tuning phase, the “unlearning” phenomenon (Nakamoto et al., 2024; Nair et al., 2020;
046
      Uchendu et al., 2023) may occur, which means that the policy improvement is slow, or there might
047
      be a performance drop at the beginning of the fine-tuning phase. One reason for this phenomenon
048   is the distribution shift between offline and online stages (Lee et al., 2022; Uchendu et al., 2023;
049   Nair et al., 2020; Wen et al., 2023), i.e., the agent encounters unseen state-action pairs during online
050   interaction. Due to extrapolation error (Fujimoto et al., 2019; Kumar et al., 2019) and conservatism
051   in value function (Kumar et al., 2020; Lyu et al., 2022b), the agent cannot provide a good Q-value
052   estimate for online samples. There are many attempts to address the distribution shift issue. For
053   example, Wen et al. (Wen et al., 2023) leverage Q-ensemble and robustness regularization to smooth
      the Q-function for policy fine-tuning. However, ensemble method introduces extra computational

054
      burden. Lee et al. (Lee et al., 2022) use balanced replay to select near-on-policy samples for fine-
055   tuning. However, one needs to train a density ratio estimator, which increases the complexity of
056   training. It necessities to develop a general and effective method for better policy fine-tuning.
057
058
      In this paper, we propose a simple yet effective approach for online fine-tuning, Prioritized
      Experience Selection, namely PES. We begin with the theoretical insight that, at the beginning
059
      of the online phase, one should only use online transitions that do not deviate far from the visited
060
      transitions to ensure smooth policy transfer. To further guarantee fast policy adaptation, we only
061   select good online transitions to fine-tune the policy. To that end, we maintain a priority queue
062   containing a portion of high-return trajectories encountered before during the online phase, and only
063   select online samples that are close to the samples in the queue for further fine-tuning. We determine
064   whether the online transition is close to the queue by searching its k-nearest neighbors in the queue
065   and measure their average deviations. We admit the transition if the calculated deviation is small and
066   vice versa. We leverage the KD Tree (Bentley, 1975) for efficient implementation. Meanwhile, we
067   dynamically update the priority queue to ensure that the samples are of high quality in the queue. In
068   this way, we make sure that the samples used for fine-tuning stay close to the previously encountered
069
      samples, thus mitigating the distribution shift. Moreover, since the queue only contains high-return
      trajectories, we also ensure that good online samples are used for fine-tuning, thereby improving
070
      the sample efficiency. To further tackle the underlying over-conservative issue due to partial sample
071
      selection, we adapt the selection threshold throughout the online phase to ensure data diversity.
072
073   PES is general and can be seamlessly integrated into different offline and O2O RL algorithms for
074   efficient online fine-tuning. Experimental results on various D4RL (Fu et al., 2020) datasets demon-
075
      strate that PES can significantly benefit offline and O2O RL algorithms and mitigate distribution
      shift. To ensure reproducibility, we provide the code in the supplementary materials.

      We consider a Markov Decision Process (MDP) (Puterman, 1990) that can be specified by a tuple
081   ⟨S, A, p, r, ρ, γ⟩, where S and A are the state space and action space, respectively, p : S × A → S
082   is the transition dynamics, r : S × A → R is the reward function, ρ is the initial state distribution,
083   and γ ∈ [0, 1) is the discount factor. The goal of reinforcement learning
                                                                          P∞ (RL) is to obtain a policy
084   πθ which maximizes the following object function: η(θ) = Eπθ [ t=0 γ t r(st , at )|s0 ∼ ρ]. In the
085   context of offline RL, the agent is only accessible to a static dataset: D = {(si , ai , ri , si+1 )}N
                                                                                                           i=1 .
086   Since the dataset cannot cover the entire state-action space, training solely on it will constrain the
087   agent’s performance. To further improve the performance of offline RL agents without incurring
088   excessive costs and risks, offline-to-online RL aims to fine-tune offline-trained agents with minimal
089   online interactions. Samples collected online are stored in Donline and training samples are drawn
090
      from D ∪ Donline for fine-tuning.

      3.1   A M OTIVATING E XAMPLE
095
096   Offline-to-online RL suffers from distribution shift during the online phase, which hinders the pre-
097   trained policy from achieving higher returns. Nevertheless, we argue that the distribution shift issue
098   can be effectively alleviated when leveraging the sample selection approach for filtering fine-tuning
099   data, even if the distribution shift is severe. We provide a motivating example to illustrate this point.
100
      We choose IQL (Kostrikov et al., 2022), a popular offline RL algorithm for experiments. We first
101
      pre-train IQL on halfcheetah-medium-v2 dataset for 1M gradient steps. To simulate a severe
102   distribution shift, we use halfcheetah-expert-v2 dataset for fine-tuning. As shown in Figure
103   1 (left), these two datasets exhibit distinct state-action distributions. We consider two approaches for
104   fine-tuning, (a) directly fine-tuning using samples from the halfcheetah-expert-v2 dataset,
105   tagged as IQL-Expert; (b) we construct a priority queue and initialize it with top-10 trajectories in
106   the halfcheetah-medium-v2 dataset, and then use halfcheetah-expert-v2 dataset to
107   fine-tune IQL with the sample selection mechanism, i.e., ignoring samples that deviate far from the
      queue. We denote this variant as IQL-PES.

      estimated MDPs is small, then the learned policy can avoid the abrupt performance drop, facilitating
174   a smooth policy transfer. Motivated by this theoretical insight, one can simply add online samples
175   that are similar to D ∪ Donline to Donline . However, we argue that such an approach is not ideal. On
176   one hand, the replay buffer may contain sub-optimal trajectories with low returns. It is possible that
177   online samples with low-quality would be admitted and used for fine-tuning, resulting in slow policy
178   evolution. On the other hand, since the size of the replay buffer can often be large (containing more
179   than 1M transitions), assessing the similarity between the online samples and the buffer transitions
180   can be a heavy computational overhead. To avoid these drawbacks, we propose prioritized experi-
181   ence selection (PES), which maintains a priority queue that contains visited high-return trajectories
182
      and only favours online samples that are similar to samples in the queue. In this way, the distri-
      bution shift is alleviated and the sample efficiency can be boosted (since only high-quality samples
183
      are selected), the computational load is also significantly reduced as the priority queue holds much
184
      fewer samples compared to the entire replay buffer. Meanwhile, the queue is dynamically updated
185   such that we can consistently include high-quality samples for higher sample efficiency. Note that
186   the agent’s exploration ability may be reduced since PES filters out proportion of online samples.
187   To tackle this issue, we gradually loose the selection threshold throughout the fine-tuning process
188   to ensure data diversity. This is rational since the “unlearning” phenomenon primarily occurs in the
189   beginning of the fine-tuning phase, and we should admit more diverse online samples in the later
190   fine-tuning stage to encourage exploration.
191   The core idea of PES is demonstrated in Figure 2. Compared with the previous offline-to-online RL
192   pipeline, the main difference is that PES maintains an additional priority queue for online transition
193   selection. The overall procedure of PES can be divided into the following steps:
194
      Step 1: Offline Pre-Training. Given an offline dataset D, we first learn a policy π via offline RL
195
      algorithms. Since PES is orthogonal to algorithmic designs, we can adopt a variety of offline RL
196
      algorithms, such as CQL (Kumar et al., 2020), IQL (Kostrikov et al., 2022), etc.
197
198   Step2: Constructing the Priority Queue. After offline pre-training, we first construct a void
199   priority queue Q with capacity N (i.e., how many trajectories can the queue Q hold) to store high-
200
      quality trajectories. We sort the trajectories in D by their returns and push the top N trajectories
      with the highest returns into Q. Note that this step only aims to initialize the priority queue.
201
202   Step 3: Prioritized Experience Selection. This step is the core contribution of PES. For an online
203   sample, PES evaluates its similarity to the experiences in the priority queue Q and adds those with
204   high similarities to Donline . By doing so, PES ensures that the samples in Donline are of high-quality,
205   which in principle should benefit policy improvement. Moreover, to prevent the policy being overly
206
      conservative, we also continuously update Q during the fine-tuning process, as demonstrated in Step
      5. The remaining issue is how to measure the similarity between online samples and those in Q. One
207
      can train neural networks to fulfill that (e.g., train a classifier to determine whether the online sample
208
      belongs to the queue). But it brings heavy training costs and may suffer from training instability. We
209   resort to the k-nearest neighbor distance in the state-action space as the similarity metric. Given an
210   online sample (s, a), we measure the distance between (s, a) and its k-nearest neighbors in Q:

240   We note that PES enjoys the following advantages: (a) Compatibility with existing algorithms:
241   Since PES only involves online sample selection and is independent of the specific algorithmic
242   design, PES can be seamlessly integrated into a variety of offline RL and offline-to-online RL al-
243
      gorithms. This flexibility allows for the enhancement of existing methods without the need to alter
      their core designs; (b) Slight additional training costs: PES leverages an unsupervised learning
244
      method, KD tree, to measure the distance between the online sample and the transitions in the prior-
245
      ity queue, which is quite efficient and it does not introduce much additional training costs, as shown
246   in Appendix G.

295   Baselines. We consider the following baselines: (i) AWAC (Nair et al., 2020): an approach combin-
296   ing dynamic programming with maximum likelihood policy updates via advantage-weighted actor-
297   critic for offline-to-online learning. (ii) IQL (Kostrikov et al., 2022), an offline RL algorithm that
298
      also trains the policy via advantage weighting. (iii) BR (Balanced Replay (Lee et al., 2022)), which
      selects relevant, near-on-policy offline samples for fine-tuning. The difference between PES and BR
299
      lies in that PES selects online samples. (iv) JSRL (Jump Start RL (Uchendu et al., 2023)), which
300
      utilizes a guide-policy for the rollout of the first part of the trajectory, and an exploration-policy for
301   the rest part of the trajectory. (v) O3F (Mark et al., 2022): an optimistic action selection mechanism
302   which encourages exploration by taking actions with higher expected Q-value. For a fair compar-
303   ison, we employ IQL as the base algorithm for BR and O3F (i.e., no Q-ensemble is adopted as in
304   their original papers). All algorithms are run across 5 varied random seeds.
305
      Experimental Results. We summarize in Figure 3 the learning curves of IQL-PES and the above
306
      baselines in the online stage, where we report the normalized score for both MuJoCo tasks and
307   Antmaze tasks. It can be found that PES significantly boosts the fine-tuning performance of IQL,
308   and IQL-PES achieves the highest final performance in 10 out of the 15 datasets among all meth-
309   ods. Moreover, thanks to the online sample selection mechanism, the “unlearning” phenomenon
310   at the beginning of online stage is mitigated (i.e., no abrupt performance degradation occurs in
311   PES). These clearly show the effectiveness of our method. We observe that on random datasets like
312   hopper-random-v2, the performance of IQL-PES is slightly inferior than the vanilla IQL. We
313   believe this is because the data distribution of the random datasets is diverse, making the policy less
314   affected by the distribution shift issue. Meanwhile, the offline samples in random datasets are of
315
      poor quality, and filter out online samples may potentially decrease sample efficiency. However, on
      datasets with a narrow data distribution, such as the medium datasets (e.g., hopper-medium-v2),
316
      PES can bring significant performance improvement. Notably, PES consistently beats the balanced
317
      replay approach on almost all the tasks, emphasizing the greater significance of online sample se-
318   lection over offline sample selection. It can be observed that some fine-tuning methods, such as
319   balanced replay and O3F, could result in a slow performance improvement, which seems to be con-
320   tradictory to their original papers. We argue that the reasons are that these methods are optimistic in
321   their action selection strategy and sampling mechanism. Such optimism could lead to more severe
322   distribution shift, and they employ the Q-ensemble trick to enhance the robustness of Q-networks
323   and the policy. They fail here due to the lack of ensemble Q-networks. In contrast, PES is general
      and does not rely on the Q-ensemble to achieve a good performance.

494   5    R ELATED W ORK
495
496   Offline RL. Offline RL aims to learn a constrained optimal policy with access to a static dataset.
497   Due to the distribution shift and inability to explore (Ladosz et al., 2022; Amin et al., 2021; Liu
498   et al., 2021; Jin et al., 2020; Lambert et al., 2022), offline RL often exhibits severe extrapolation error
499
      (Fujimoto et al., 2019). To address this issue, common strategies adopt importance sampling (Gelada
      & Bellemare, 2019; Liu et al., 2019a; Nachum et al., 2019; Precup et al., 2001; Sutton et al., 2016),
500
      policy constraints (Fakoor et al., 2021; Fujimoto & Gu, 2021; Ghasemipour et al., 2021; Kumar
501
      et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2019), conservative value estimation (Kumar et al., 2020; Lyu et al., 2022b;
502   Kostrikov et al., 2021; Ma et al., 2021), uncertainty quantification (Bai et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2021;
503   Zanette et al., 2021), and learning without querying OOD actions (Kostrikov et al., 2022; Chen et al.,
504   2020; Wang et al., 2018; Xu et al., 2023). There are also some valuable attempts in model-based
505   offline RL (Kidambi et al., 2020; Yu et al., 2020; Lyu et al., 2022a; Zhang et al., 2023b).
506
      Offline-to-Online RL. Several studies has explored how to benefit online learning with offline
507
      data (Vecerik et al., 2017; Hester et al., 2018; Nair et al., 2018; Rajeswaran et al., 2017), which as-
508   sume the datasets contain near-optimal demonstrations. However, most offline datasets are sourced
509   from sub-optimal behavior policies and do not satisfy this assumption. A more practical manner
510   for bridging offline and online learning phase is Offline-to-Online (O2O) RL (Lee et al., 2022; Nair
511   et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2023; Lei et al., 2023), which pre-trains an offline policy
512   and then fine-tunes it in the real environment. O2O RL also exhibits an issue of distribution shift be-
513   tween offline datasets and online samples. Some efforts handle this issue by selecting near-on-policy
514   online samples (Lee et al., 2022), parameter transferring (Xie et al., 2021), policy expansion (Zhang
515   et al., 2023a), guided exploration (Campos et al., 2021; Uchendu et al., 2023), adjusting update fre-
516
      quency (Zhang et al., 2023c; Feng et al., 2024), etc. There are also some researches that directly
      fine-tune the offline pre-trained policy without introducing additional components (Kostrikov et al.,
517
      2022; Lyu et al., 2022b; Tarasov et al., 2024a; Yang et al., 2024), but their fine-tuning performance is
518
      often limited and some of them rely on a careful hyperparameter tuning. Our work is closest to (Lee
519   et al., 2022), but the difference lies in that PES selects online samples for fine-tuning by constructing
520   a priority queue while (Lee et al., 2022) selects offline samples by training a density ratio estimator.
521
522
      Prioritized Experience Replay. Our work is also related to the prioritized experience replay in
      RL, which prefers more essential samples in the replay buffer to benefit off-policy RL algorithms.
523
      PER (Schaul et al., 2015) prioritizes samples with larger TD-error to accelerate training, and many
524
      studies prioritize samples from different perspectives (Horgan et al., 2018; Saglam et al., 2023; Li
525   et al., 2021; Oh et al., 2021; Pan et al., 2022). There are also studies focusing on online RL with
526   offline demonstrations leveraging the idea of prioritized experience replay (Song et al., 2022; Vecerik
527   et al., 2017). Our work is different from these studies in that we focus on the offline-to-online setting
528   and we construct the priority queue for data filtering.
529
530
      6    C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this paper, we propose PES, a simple yet effective online experience selection method to handle
533
      distribution shift for offline-to-online RL. PES maintains a priority queue containing top N highest-
534   return trajectories and only selects online samples close to those in the queue for online fine-tuning.
535   Our method is compatible with different algorithmic forms, and can incur more accurate Q-value es-
536   timate. One limitation of our work is the underlying heavy computational overhead for KNN search
537   in high-dimensional data spaces, such as image inputs, which may harm the training efficiency. One
538   possible solution can be using image encoders to map the original space to a hidden one, where we
539   conduct KNN search, and we leave that for future work.

      B     T HEORETICAL BACKUP FOR PES
923
924
      In this part, we give some theoretical backups for PES’s ability to select high-quality samples. We
925
      have the static offline dataset D, the online buffer Donline , and the priority queue Q in PES. We
926   assume that the behavior policy in D gives µ, the behavior policy in Donline is µb , and the behavior
927   policy in Q is µq .
928
929
      Firstly, we define the Lipschitz function as follows:
930   Definition B.1 (Lipschitz function). A function f : Rm → Rn is called a Lipschitz function if there
931   exists a constant K ≥ 0 such that:
932
                                                ∥f (x) − f (y)∥ ≤ K∥x − y∥                                     (4)
933
934   for any x, y ∈ Rm . ∥ · ∥ represents the norm, and K is called Lipschitz constant.
935
936   We assume that the reward signals, as well as the state space and action space, are bounded. To be
937   specific, we have the following assumption:
938   Assumption B.1. The rewards are bounded, i.e., |r(s, a)| ≤ rmax , ∀ s, a. Furthermore, the state
939   space and the action space are also bounded, i.e., ∥s∥2 ≤ Cs < ∞, ∥a∥2 ≤ Ca < ∞, ∀ s ∈ S, a ∈
940   A, where Cs , Ca are constants.
941
942   The above assumption can be usually satisfied in practice, because it is less likely that we encounter
943   boundless states or actions. The reward function is often manually written and is usually bounded.
944   Given the above assumption, it is not difficult to derive that the Q function satisfies: |Q(s, a)| ≤
      rmax
945   1−γ , i.e., the Q function is also bounded.
946   Denote the learned current policy as π and the corresponding Q function as Q(s, a). We then further
947   make the following assumptions about the behavior policy in the priority queue, µq , and Q(s, a).
948
      Assumption B.2. The behavior policy in the priority queue Q, µq , is deterministic and satisfies the
949
      Lipschitz condition with a Lipschitz constant Kµ , i.e.,
950
951                                     ∥µq (·|s1 ) − µq (·|s2 )∥ ≤ Kµ ∥s1 − s2 ∥                              (5)
952
953   for all s1 , s2 ∈ S.
954   Assumption B.3. The Q-function Q(s, a) is a Lipschitz function with KQ the Lipschitz constant,
955   i.e.,
956                      ∥Q(s1 , a1 ) − Q(s2 , a2 )∥ ≤ KQ ∥s1 ⊕ a1 − s2 ⊕ a2 ∥                   (6)
957
      for all (s1 , a1 ), (s2 , a2 ) ∈ S × A.
958
959
      The Lipschitz assumptions are popular and have been used in many previous RL papers (Asadi
960   et al., 2018; Ran et al., 2023). The assumption on the Q function is valid since it is bounded, and
961   this assumption can be satisfied by properly choosing KQ .
962
963
      For any given online sample (s, a), we follow PES and query its k-nearest neighbors in the priority
      queue Q, and measure the distance d. We denote the nearest neighbors as {(ŝ1 , â1 ), . . . , (ŝk , âk )}.
964
      If the sample resembles the samples in Q, it is guaranteed that d(s, a) ≤ ϵ in PES. We then have the
965

1020   that the expected return starting from (s, a) is lower bounded by KQ (2 + Kµ )ϵ. We can guarantee

1023   lower bound involves KQ Ca , which is a constant and Ca can not be controlled. That being said, Ca
1024   can be quite large. Then, it is hard to tell whether training upon (s, a) can incur a good performance,
1025   and (s, a) can be a quite bad sample. Therefore, we conclude that PES can theoretically guarantee

1141   In this part, we present the details of baseline implementations, PES implementations, and hyperpa-

1143
1144   E.1     BASELINE I MPLEMENTATION
1145
1146   Our baselines include IQL (Kostrikov et al., 2022), CQL (Kumar et al., 2020), TD3-BC (Fujimoto &
1147   Gu, 2021), AWAC (Nair et al., 2020), PEX (Zhang et al., 2023a), Cal-QL (Nakamoto et al., 2024),
1148
       Balanced Replay (Lee et al., 2022), JSRL (Uchendu et al., 2023), and O3F (Mark et al., 2022).
       For the offline pre-training process and online fine-tuning process of IQL, CQL, AWAC and Cal-
1149
       QL, we use the code from CORL2 (Tarasov et al., 2024b), which provides reliable implementations
1150
       for different offline and offline-to-online RL algorithms. For TD3-BC, since CORL only provides
1151   offline training code, we additionally implement our own online fine-tuning code. For PEX, we use
1152   the official code3 to replicate the results in MuJoCo and Antmaze domain. For Balanced Replay,
1153   we do not follow the official code4 that adopts the CQL-based pessimistic Q-ensemble technique.
1154   Instead, we use IQL as the base algorithm. For JSRL, the core idea is to divide the full trajectory into
1155   two parts, and utilize a guide-policy for the rollout of the first part of the trajectory, and a exploration-
1156   policy for the rest part of the trajectory. We use the offline trained policy πoff as the guide-policy,
1157   and current policy πθ as the exploration-policy, and use a linear scheduler that anneals from the max
1158   trajectory length to 0 for decreasing the first part of rollout by πoff . For the implementation of O3F,
1159
       we add 10 random noise samples to each action and select the perturbed action with the highest
       Q-value for execution, without the use of Q-ensemble. Regarding training steps of these baselines,
1160
       we set them uniformly to 1M gradient steps for offline pre-training, and 1M environmental steps for
1161
       online fine-tuning.

       E.2     I MPLEMENTATION OF PES
1165
       We then provide the implementation details of PES. We implement PES upon baselines discussed
1166   above. Our major modifications are (i) we construct a priority queue where the trajectories are
1167   sorted based on their cumulative return. (ii) we select online samples based on their distances to
1168   samples in the priority queue. We do not make any other change to the base algorithms. For the
1169   implementation of (i), we utilize a three-dimensional array to store trajectory samples, with a shape
1170   of (number of trajectories, trajectory length, sample dimension). To
1171   implement prioritized selection, we use the numpy library, i.e., numpy.argsort() function for
1172   sorting returns of the trajectories. For the implementation of (ii), we measure the k-nearest neighbor
1173   distances of online samples against samples in the queue in state-action spaces. In specific, we
1174
       concatenate the state and action dimensions of samples in the queue and construct a KD Tree for
       efficient k-nearest neighbor search. We use the implementation of KD tree from sklearn library,
1175
       i.e., sklearn.neighbors.KDTree. Note that we can directly get the distances when querying
1176
       KD Tree.

1552
        Search Vector. This determines the search space of k-nearest neighbor search for PES. For
1553
        the default setting in our experiments, we search in the state-action space, yielding the search
1554    vector of (s ⊕ a). In addition to (s ⊕ a), one can also choose other search vectors, such
1555    as (s ⊕ a ⊕ s′ ) and (s ⊕ s′ ). To examine whether different search vectors matter for PES,
1556    we change the choice of search vector and conduct extensive experiments on several D4RL
1557    datasets. We present the results in Table 8, and the results show that the impact of different
1558    search vector seems to depend on specific datasets. Some datasets (e.g., hopper-medium-v2,
1559    antmaze-umaze-diverse-v0) prefer the choice of (s ⊕ a), while it is better to use (s ⊕ a ⊕ s′ )
1560    as the search vector for tasks like halfcheetah-medium-v2, walker2d-medium-v2, and
1561    antmaze-large-diverse-v0. However, considering both performance and computational
1562
        burden, we simply use (s ⊕ a) for all the experiments.
1563    Distance Measure. The default distance measure used in PES is Euclidean distance. One
1564    can certainly utilize other common distance measures like Manhattan distance, Chebyshev
