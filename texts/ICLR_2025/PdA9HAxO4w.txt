011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Vision-Language Pre-training (VLP) models have exhibited unprecedented capa-
014            bility in many applications by taking full advantage of the multimodal alignment.
015
               However, previous studies have shown they are vulnerable to maliciously crafted
               adversarial samples. Despite recent success, these methods are generally instance-
016
               specific and require generating perturbations for each input sample. In this paper,
017
               we reveal that VLP models are also vulnerable to the instance-agnostic univer-
018            sal adversarial perturbation (UAP). Specifically, we design a novel Contrastive-
019            training Perturbation Generator with Cross-modal conditions (C-PGC) to achieve
020            the attack. In light that the pivotal multimodal alignment is achieved through the
021            advanced contrastive learning technique, we devise to turn this powerful weapon
022            against themselves, i.e., employ a malicious version of contrastive learning to train
023            the C-PGC based on our carefully crafted positive and negative image-text pairs
024            for essentially destroying the alignment relationship learned by VLP models. Be-
025            sides, C-PGC fully utilizes the characteristics of Vision-and-Language (V+L) sce-
026
               narios by incorporating both unimodal and cross-modal information as effective
               guidance. Extensive experiments show that C-PGC successfully forces adversarial
027
               samples to move away from their original area in the VLP model’s feature space,
028
               thus essentially enhancing attacks across various victim models and V+L tasks.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Vision-Language Pre-training (VLP) models, including ALBEF (Li et al., 2021), TCL (Yang et al.,
034   2022), and BLIP (Li et al., 2022), have recently demonstrated remarkable efficacy in a wide range of
035   Vision-and-Language (V+L) tasks. By self-supervised pre-training on large-scale image-text pairs,
036   VLP models efficiently align cross-modal features and capture rich information from the aligned
037   multimodal embeddings, thereby providing expressive representations for various applications.
038   Adversarial attacks (Carlini & Wagner, 2017), which aim to deceive models during inference time,
039   have attracted extensive attention due to their significant threat to security-critical scenarios (Eykholt
040   et al., 2018). Recent studies have shown that VLP models are also vulnerable to adversarial samples.
041   The pioneering work Co-Attack (Zhang et al., 2022) proposes the first multimodal attack that simul-
042   taneously perturbs both image and text modalities and displays excellent performance. However,
043   Co-Attack only considers relatively easier white-box attacks where victim models are completely
044   accessible. To handle more practical black-box settings, subsequent studies propose various transfer-
045
      able adversarial samples generated on an available surrogate model to fool other inaccessible mod-
      els. Specifically, SGA (Lu et al., 2023) significantly improves the adversarial transferability through
046
      the set-level cross-modal guidance obtained from data augmentations. Subsequently, TMM (Wang
047
      et al., 2024) proposes to jointly destroy the modality-consistency features within the clean image-
048   text pairs and include more modality-discrepancy features in the perturbations to further enhance
049   transferability. While existing methods have achieved great success, they are all instance-specific
050   and need to generate a perturbation for each input pair, which results in substantial computational
051   overhead. Meanwhile, universal adversarial attacks, as an efficient instance-agnostic approach that
052   uses only one Universal Adversarial Perturbation (UAP) to conduct attacks, have not been fully in-
053   vestigated for VLP models. This naturally leads to a question, is it possible to design a universal
      adversarial perturbation that can effectively deceive VLP models across various image-text pairs?

      Motivation. To this end, we make an intuitive attempt to transplant existing renowned approaches
      UAP (Moosavi-Dezfooli et al., 2017) and GAP (Poursaeed et al., 2018) to launch attacks on several
076
      VLP models by maximizing the distance between the embeddings of the adversarial image and its
077
      matched texts. Unfortunately, Figure 1 demonstrates that these methods yield unsatisfactory attack
078   success rates (ASR), especially for black-box attacks. Empirically, this failure stems from their
079   narrow focus on the image modal, disregarding the other modality and the multimodal information
080   that plays a pivotal role in VLP models. To overcome this challenge, we revisit the VLP models’
081   basic training paradigm and emphasize that regardless of the downstream V+L tasks, their achieved
082   outstanding performance is heavily reliant on the well-established multimodal alignment, which
083   draws the embedding of matched image-text pairs closer while distancing those of non-matched
084   pairs. In light of this consideration, we argue that the key core of an effective universal adversarial
085   attack is to obtain a UAP that can fundamentally destroy this learned alignment relationship to
086
      mislead VLP models into making incorrect decisions. Besides, Fig. 1 also shows that the generator-
      based GAP consistently outperforms UAP methods, confirming the superiority of the generative
087
      paradigm, which is also corroborated by numerous studies (Gao et al., 2024; Feng et al., 2023).
088
089   Based on these insights, we propose a novel generative framework that learns a Contrastive-training
090   Perturbation Generator with Cross-modal conditions (C-PGC) to launch universal attacks on VLP
091   models (see Fig. 2). To essentially destroy the multimodal alignment, we devise to utilize VLP mod-
092
      els’ most powerful weapons to attack against themselves, i.e., use the contrastive learning mecha-
      nism to train the generator using our maliciously constructed image-text pairs that completely vi-
093
      olate the correct VL matching relationship, to produce perturbation that pushes the embedding of
094
      matched pairs apart while pulling those of non-matched ones together. Inspired by the multimodal
095   characteristics of V+L scenarios, we modify the generator’s architecture to incorporate cross-modal
096   knowledge through the advanced cross-attention mechanism for better guidance. In addition, we
097   also consider the intra-modal influence and introduce an unimodal distance loss to further enhance
098   the attacks. Since previous studies (Zhang et al., 2022; Lu et al., 2023) achieve impressive improve-
099   ments via multimodal perturbation, we are motivated to generate UAP for both images and texts to
100   utilize the synergy between different modalities. Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
101
102
      • We design a novel cross-modal conditional perturbation generator, which produces effective UAP
        for both image and text modalities to achieve universal adversarial attacks on VLP models.
103
104   • We propose the first malicious contrastive paradigm tailored for multimodal adversarial attacks,
105     which incorporates both unimodal and multimodal guidance to contrastively train the generator
106
        using our meticulously constructed positive and negative pairs for enhanced attack effects.
107   • Extensive experiments on 6 various VLP models across different V+L tasks reveal that our method
        achieves outstanding white-box performance and black-box transferability in different scenarios.

      2.1   V ISION -L ANGUAGE P RE - TRAINING M ODELS
111
112   VLP models are pre-trained on massive image-text pairs to learn the semantic correlations across
113   modalities and serve diverse multimodal user demands (Chen et al., 2023; Du et al., 2022). We next
114   illustrate the basis of VLP models from multiple perspectives.
115
      Architectures. Based on the ways of multimodal fusion, the architectures of VLP models can be
116   classified into two types: single-stream and dual-stream architectures. Single-stream architectures
117   (Li et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2020) directly concatenate the text and image features, and calculate
118   the attention in the same Transformer block for multimodal fusion. On the contrary, dual-stream
119   architectures (Radford et al., 2021; Li et al., 2022) separately feed the text and image features to
120   different Transformer blocks and leverage the cross-attention mechanism for multimodal fusion.
121
      Pre-training Objectives. The pre-training objectives for VLP models mainly include masked fea-
122   tures completion, multimodal features matching, and specific downstream objectives. Masked fea-
123   tures completion (Chen et al., 2020) encourages VLP models to predict the deliberately masked
124   tokens using the remaining unmasked tokens during pre-training. Multimodal features matching (Li
125   et al., 2021) pre-trains VLP models by learning to precisely predict whether the given image-text
126   pairs are matched. Specific downstream objectives (Anderson et al., 2018) directly utilize the train-
127   ing objectives of downstream tasks (e.g., visual question answering) for pre-training VLP models.
128   Downstream Tasks. In this paper, we mainly consider the following multimodal downstream tasks:
129   (1) Image-text retrieval (ITR) (Wang et al., 2016): finding the most matched image for the given text
130   and vice versa, including image-to-text retrieval (TR) and text-to-image retrieval (IR). (2) Image
131   caption (IC) (Bai & An, 2018): generating the most suitable descriptions for the given image. (3)
132   Visual grounding (VG) (Hong et al., 2019): locating specific regions in the image that correspond
133   with the given textual descriptions. (4) Visual entailment (VE) (Xie et al., 2019): analyzing the input
134   image and text and predicting whether their relationship is entailment, neutral, or contradiction.
135
136   2.2   A DVERSARIAL ATTACKS
137
138
      Instance-specific Attacks on VLP Models. The adversarial robustness of VLP Models has already
      become a research focus. Early works (Kim & Ghosh, 2019; Yang et al., 2021) impose perturbations
139
      only on single modality and lack cross-modal interactions when attacking multimodal models. To
140
      address this issue, Co-Attack (Zhang et al., 2022) conducts the first multimodal white-box attacks on
141   VLP models. On the basis of Co-Attack, Lu et al. (2023) extend the attacks to more rigorous black-
142   box settings and propose SGA, which utilizes set-level alignment-preserving argumentations with
143   carefully designed cross-modal guidance. However, Wang et al. (2024) points out that SGA fails to
144   fully exploit modality correlation, and proposes TMM to better leverage cross-modal interactions
145   by tailoring both the modality-consistency and modality-discrepancy features. Nonetheless, these
146   methods are all instance-specific and need to craft perturbations for each input pair.
147   Universal Adversarial Examples. Universal adversarial attacks (Moosavi-Dezfooli et al., 2017;
148   Mopuri et al., 2018) aim to deceive the victim model by exerting a uniform adversarial modification
149   to all the benign samples. These attacks save the redundant procedures of redesigning perturbations
150   for each input sample and are consequently more efficient than traditional attack strategies. Gen-
151   erally, universal adversarial attacks can be categorized into optimization-based methods (Moosavi-
152   Dezfooli et al., 2017; Wang et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023) and generation-based methods (Hayes &
153   Danezis, 2018; Gao et al., 2024; Anil et al., 2024). Benefiting from the powerful modeling abilities
154   of generative models, generation-based methods are more versatile and can produce more natural
155
      samples than optimization-based ones. In this paper, we explore universal adversarial attacks on
      VLP models and manage to generate UAP with excellent attack effects and high transferability.

184   3.1      P ROBLEM S TATEMENT
185
      We define an input image-text pair as (v, t) and denote ev and et as the image and text embedding
186   encoded by the image encoder fI (·) and text encoder fT (·) of the targeted VLP model f (·). Let Ds
187   be an available dataset consisting of image-text pairs collected by a malicious adversary. The attack
188   objective is to utilize Ds to train a generator Gw (·) that is capable of producing a powerful pair of
189   universal image-text perturbations (δv , δt ) that can affect the vast majority of test dataset Dt to fool
190   models into making incorrect decisions. Formally, the attack goal can be formulated as:
191

193   where T (·) denotes the operation that uses the output V+L features to obtain the final predictions,
194   ⊕ indicates the text perturbation strategy (Zhang et al., 2022; Lu et al., 2023) that replaces certain
195   important tokens of the original sentence with crafted adversarial words, and y is the correct pre-
196   diction of the considered V+L task. To ensure the perturbation’s imperceptibility, we constrain the
197   pixel-level image perturbation with l∞ norm of a given budget ϵv . The textual perturbation is token-

199
      altering words in a natural sentence can be easily noticed or detected, we apply a rigorous restriction
      that permits only a single word to be substituted (ϵt = 1). On the premise of imperceptibility, the
200
      attacker attempts to generalize the crafted UAP to a wider range of test data and victim models.

      3.2      OVERVIEW OF THE P ROPOSED F RAMEWORK
203
204   As depicted in Fig. 3, we adopt the multimodal perturbation strategy and generate perturbations on
205   both image and text modalities for enhanced attacks. Given the similarity between the workflows for
206   image and text, we then take the image attacks as an example to illustrate the proposed framework.
207
      Firstly, a fixed noise zv is randomly initialized and subsequently fed into the conditional generator.
208
      For each image v and its descriptions t, the generator Gw (·) then translates the input noise zv into the
209
      adversarial perturbation δv that is of the same size as the image v. During the generation, the network
210   Gw additionally benefits from cross-modal information by integrating the textual embedding, i.e.,
211   δv = Gw (zv ; fT (t)). Next, the generated adversarial noise δv is injected into the clean image to
212   obtain the adversarial image via vadv = v + δv . To better guide the training process, we design
213   two efficient unimodal and multimodal losses as our optimization objectives. Unimodal loss is
214   straightforward and aims to push the adversarial images away from the clean images in the latent
215   embedding space, while multimodal loss is based on contrastive learning and uses our manually
      constructed positive and negative samples to effectively destroy the image-text matching relationship

      obtained from feature alignment. Once we finish training the C-PGC using the proposed losses, the
217   input fixed noise is transformed into a UAP that is of great generalization and transferability.

221   Next, we provide a detailed introduction to each of the proposed designs. Note that we primar-
222   ily discuss the image attack as an example, given that the design of the text attack is completely

225   et al., 2024; Anil et al., 2024) have shown excellent efficacy in fooling the discriminative models.
226   Nevertheless, since existing generative attacks are limited to a single modality, directly utilizing
227   the off-the-shelf generators might fail to leverage the multimodal interactions in these special V+L
228   scenarios. To address this limitation, we additionally introduce cross-modal embeddings as auxiliary
229   information to further facilitate the process of perturbation generation. Specifically, we modify the
230   existing generator’s architecture by adding several cross-attention modules that have been proven
231   effective in tasks with variable input modalities. The obtained textual embeddings et encoded by
232   fT (·) are then incorporated into our generator through:

295   A notable distinction between the image and text attacks is the approach to inject adversarial pertur-
296   bations. Due to the discreteness of text data, we apply the token-wise substitute strategy (Lu et al.,
297   2023; Wang et al., 2024) that replaces certain important words in the original sentence with crafted
298   adversarial words. Accordingly, the conditional generator is utilized to output the adversarial textual
299
      embeddings, which are subsequently mapped back to the vocabulary space to obtain a universally
      applicable word-level perturbation. Prior to implementing the word replacement, a meticulous pro-
300
      cess is undertaken to identify the most optimal position within the sentence to insert the perturbation.
301
      Our strategy intends to identify and replace the words that are more likely to have a greater influence
302   during the decision-making. Concretely, for each word wi within a given sentence, we compute the
303   distance between the embeddings of the original sentence and the wi -masked version encoded by
304   the VLP models to determine its contribution. As aforementioned, we set ϵt = 1 and choose the
305   single word exerting the highest feature distance as the target for replacement.

      4.4   A BLATION S TUDY
469
470   This subsection employs the representative ALBEF (Li et al., 2021) model as the surrogate model to
471   provide sufficient ablation studies on Flickr30K. We begin our analysis on the contribution of each
472   proposed technique. Subsequently, we examine the sensitivity of certain hyperparameters.
473
      The effect of LCL and LDis . To investigate the impact of the proposed loss terms, we introduce two
474   variants C-PGCCL and C-PGCDis that remove LCL and LDis from the training loss respectively.
475   As shown in Table 5, the removal of LCL leads to significant degradation, particularly for black-box
476   transferable attacks. e.g., a 27.12% ASR drop in TR tasks from ALBEF to TCL. This validates the
477   considerable contribution of LCL to guarantee a successful attack. Regarding the influence of LDis ,
478   we demonstrate that this unimodal guidance can further enhance attacks on the basis of LCL , e.g.,
479   a 10.59% increase in the ASR of TR tasks for white-box attacks on ALBEF. The proposed two loss
480   terms complement each other and jointly underpin the generalizability of the generated UAP.
481   The effect of positive sample selection. To validate the farthest selection strategy when construct-
482   ing positive samples, we design another variant C-PGCRand that adopts randomly sampled data
483   points as positive samples. Results in Table 5 reveal the necessity of the proposed farthest selection
484   strategy as it brings an average improvement of 25.96% in white-box ASR and 4.95% in black-box
485   ASR. Moreover, we can also conclude that if the positive samples are not adequately defined, adding
      LCL would even severely harm the white-box performance (see C-PGCCL and C-PGCRand ).

528   5             C ONCLUSION
529
530   This paper delves into the challenging task of launching universal adversarial attacks against VLP
531   models and proposes an effective solution that achieves superior performance using only one uni-
532   versal pair of image-text perturbations. We begin by revealing the unsatisfactory results of existing
533   UAP methods and empirically explaining the underlying reasons. Based on our analysis, we pro-
534   pose to break the crucial cross-modal alignment in VLP models by designing a contrastive-learning
535
      generative UAP framework that leverages both unimodal and multimodal information to enhance
      the attacks. Extensive experiments validate the efficacy of the proposed algorithm on diverse VLP
536
      models and V+L tasks. We highlight that the proposed framework makes a significant step in ex-
537
      ploring the classic universal adversarial attacks in VLP models and deepens our understanding of the
538   mechanism of VLP models. We also hope that this paper can promote future research that explores
539   more sophisticated defenses to strengthen the resilience of VLP models against adversarial attacks.

      B    R ATIONAL BEHIND O UR D ESIGN OF L OSS F UNCTION
728
729   It is widely acknowledged that contrastive learning serves as a powerful and foundational tool for
730   modality alignment in VLP models, establishing a nearly point-to-point relationship between image
731   and text features. Our core idea stems from the general principle:“It’s easier to tear down than to
732   build up.” Since contrastive learning can establish robust and precise alignment, leveraging the same
733   technique to disrupt the established alignments is expected to yield effective performance.
734
      Taking image attack as an example, the principle behind our contrastive learning-based attack can
735   be understood from two perspectives:
736
737   • Leverage the originally matched texts as negative samples to push the aligned image-text pair
738     apart. This broadly corresponds to the common objective of untargeted attacks.
739   • Additionally, our contrastive paradigm introduces dissimilar texts as positive samples to pull the
740     adversarial image out of its original subspace and relocate it to an incorrect feature area.
741
742   By simultaneously harnessing the collaborative effects of push (negative samples) and pull (positive
743   samples), the proposed contrastive framework achieves exceptional attack performance, which has
744   been validated by comprehensive experimental results.

       For Flickr30K and MSCOCO, we randomly sample 30,000 images and their captions from the
993
       training set to train our perturbation generator. For SNLI-VE and RefCOCO+, we learn the C-
994
       PGC directly using their training set with 29,783 and 16,992 images respectively. Since an image
995    corresponds to multiple text descriptions in these datasets, we calculate the average of their textual
996    embedding as the multimodal condition for the cross-attention modules.
997
998
       We initialize the noise variable zv as a 3 × 3 matrix. Meanwhile, the initial noise zt ’s dimensions in
       the text modality depend on the size of the hidden layer within the specific VLP model. Concretely,
999
       we set its dimension to 1 × 3 for ALBEF, TCL, BLIP, and X-VLM, while 1 × 2 for the CLIP model.
1000
       When computing the multimodal contrastive loss LCL , the temperature τ is set as 0.1. The generator
1001   is trained over 40 epochs with the Adam optimizer, utilizing a learning rate of 2−4 . Following
1002   previous works Lu et al. (2023); Wang et al. (2024), we employ the attack success rate (ASR) as
1003   our quantitative measurement of our attack in ITR tasks by computing the extent the adversarial
1004   perturbations result in victim models’ performance deviations from the clean performance.

1008   • Flickr30K (Plummer et al., 2015). Collected from the Flickr website, this dataset describes
1009     different items and activities, which becomes a standard benchmark for various V+L tasks. It
1010     contains 31,783 images, each of which has five associated captions. We use it for ITR tasks.
1011
       • MSCOCO (Lin et al., 2014). The MSCOCO dataset is a rich and diverse dataset consisting of
1012     123,287 images, each of which is annotated with approximately five sentences. We use this dataset
1013     to test the attack performance of ITR and IC tasks.
1014
       • SNLI-VE (Xie et al., 2019). Originally proposed for natural language reasoning tasks, this dataset
1015
         provides large-scale images and descriptions, where each image is annotated with several sen-
1016     tences and their logical relationship labels, including entailment, neutral, and contradiction. This
1017     dataset is used for VE tasks.
1018
       • RefCOCO+ (Yu et al., 2016). An image dataset was selected from MSCOCO, which contains
1019
         19,992 images and 141,564 annotations. It is specially used for visual grounding (VG) tasks.

1022   I   D ISCUSSIONS AND F UTURE D IRECTIONS
1023
1024   Overlook of interactions between perturbations δv and δt . The proposed framework generates
1025   universal perturbations for image and text respectively based on the designed multimodal and uni-
       modal losses. Despite the remarkable attack performance, it does not consider the interactions and


                                                             19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
       synergy between the perturbations δv and δt during optimization, which has been leveraged in sev-
1027   eral previous attacks (Lu et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024) to improve performance. In future research,
1028   this limitation can be explored as a potential mechanism to further strengthen the attacks.
1029
1030
       Textual Semantic consistency. To ensure the stealthiness of text attacks, we set the perturbation
       budget ϵt = 1, i.e., only one word is modified. Despite the superior semantic similarity to previous
1031
       sample-specific methods, there is still room to improve from the proposed C-PGC. Moreover, future
1032
       studies can consider similarity preservation strategies by applying more effective constraints during
1033   the generator training or post-processing adversarial sentences to facilitate a more stealthy attack.
1034
1035   Leveraging Task-level Characteristics. In contrast to the unimodal scenarios, we fully leverage the
1036
       unique characteristics of multimodal scenarios to enhance the modeling of a universal perturbation
       that can effectively generalize to diverse downstream V+L tasks. While this work lies in leveraging
1037
       the shared and joint characteristics of Vision-Language scenarios to present a universal and versatile
1038
       UAP, it is a promising direction for future studies to investigate task-level V+L characteristics to
1039   further enhance attacks for specific downstream tasks.
1040
1041   Synthetic positive samples. Introducing synthetic samples that are maximally distant from the
1042
       anchor as positive samples is a promising direction. A reasonable implementation might involve
       adversarial learning to generate such maximally distant samples. However, this strategy necessi-
1043
       tates synthesizing samples for each input pair, leading to a significant increase in the computational
1044
       overhead. Future works can explore more efficient and effective positive sample strategies.
