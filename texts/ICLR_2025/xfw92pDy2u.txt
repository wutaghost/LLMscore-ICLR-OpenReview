                                                      A BSTRACT
009
               Transformer-based Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable capa-
010
               bilities, yet their autoregressive nature forces sequential token-by-token decoding, leading
011
               to inefficiencies during inference. Furthermore, autoregressive language models lack in-
012            herent self-correction abilities, which hinders their capacity to refine and improve gener-
013            ated content without relying on external prompting or retraining techniques. In contrast,
014            diffusion-based models offer the advantage of fast parallel generation through iterative
015            refinement, while leveraging bi-directional attention to utilize full context at once. How-
016            ever, diffusion models are unable to match their autoregressive counterparts. This moti-
017            vates us to explore the possibility of distilling a pre-trained autoregressive (AR) language
018            model (teacher) into a non-autoregressive diffusion (non-AR) language model (student),
019            combining the best of both worlds. In this work, we present Target Concrete Score (TCS)
020
               distillation, a theoretically grounded framework that bridges autoregressive and diffusion
               paradigms. TCS distillation is broadly applicable to both discrete and continuous diffu-
021
               sion models, with any pre-trained autoregressive teacher model. We propose techniques
022
               to make TCS distillation scalable and efficient for transformer-based models, and show
023            how it can both improve pre-trained diffusion language models and also train new mod-
024            els from scratch. Through comprehensive experiments on language modeling tasks, we
025            demonstrate the effectiveness of our proposed methods.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Autoregressive (AR) architectures are the bread and butter for the modern revolution in Large Language
031   Models (LLMs) (Brown et al., 2020; Touvron et al., 2023; Shoeybi et al., 2019). These models have shown
032   amazing capabilities on a large variety of NLP tasks, but they still suffer from inefficient inference, hal-
033
      lucinations (Ji et al., 2023a; Zhang et al., 2023), overconfidence (Xiong et al., 2023), and “reversal curse”
      (Berglund et al.). These problems probably arise from their causal nature as they are learned in a left-to-right
034
      manner. First, the causal nature of AR models prevents them from generating tokens in parallel, unless spe-
035
      cific multi-token-prediction training strategies has been applied (Gloeckle et al., 2024; Cai et al.). Second,
036   they are unable to undo actions made earlier in generation easily. In some tasks, the ability to refine their
037   generations, for example, through self-reflection (Ji et al., 2023b), or chain of thought (Wei et al., 2022)
038   type approaches, can enhance the performance of autoregressive LLMs. However, this iterative inference
039   process can be time consuming, because iterative improvement is performed by extending the autoregressive
040   generation process, to mimic the “error-correction” training of these models.
041
      Motivated by these limitations, the research community has attempted to use diffusion modeling techniques
042   for language modeling. Diffusion models have been very successful in image generation (Ho et al., 2020;
043   2022; Rombach et al., 2021; Gu et al., 2023) using an implicit progressive denoising technique that is akin to
044   self-refinement. The image generation models, which work in continuous spaces, start from Gaussian noise
045   and progressively turn them into images, by iteratively cleaning up intermediate images that they generate.
046   A large body of works has been developed which extend diffusion models to discrete space to match the

                                                         1

047
      target domain of language (Lou et al., 2024; Campbell et al., 2022; Austin et al., 2021; Dieleman et al.,
048
      2022). These models generate text by starting with a categorical distribution that is easy to sample from and
049   progressively turn them into sensible language; since these discrete diffusion models enable tokens to be
050   generated in parallel, it leads to faster inference speed (higher bandwidth of generated tokens), especially
051   for longer generations. Unfortunately, discrete diffusion models have been challenging to train and do
052   not always achieve optimal fluency and performance. Research, such as the SEDD (Lou et al., 2024) and
053   Plaid (Gulrajani & Hashimoto, 2023), suggests that they are approximately at the level of GPT-2, still lagging
054   behind state-of-the-art AR LLMs.
055   Distillation has long been used to transfer knowledge from stronger models to weaker models (Hinton et al.,
056   2015) because learning from a teacher can be more effective than learning from data distribution because the
057   teacher can provide distributional supervision over the whole space, not just the observed data. In this paper
058   we aim to distill a strong AR teacher into a diffusion language model1 . Since the diffusion model student
059   is a parallel generation model, while the teacher is an AR model, off the shelf distillation techniques do not
060   apply. To address this gap, we made the following contributions in this work:
061
062              • We propose a target concrete score (TCS) distillation objective to bridge the gap between autore-
063                gressive teacher and non-autoregressive student, to combine the benefits of both worlds. We show
064                the connection of gradient-informed estimation to the target score matching in continuous diffusion.
065              • We introduce methods to apply TCS to transformer-based language models, by proposed methods
066                for efficient estimation of the target concrete score from AR teacher model. resulting a family of
067                distillation methods called Distilled Diffusion Language Models (DDLM). To optimize the compute,
068                we propose top-K and gradient-informed estimation techniques.
069
                 • Our proposed methods work for diffusion models that operate in discrete space (e.g. Lou et al.
070
                   (2023)), and for those that map discrete tokens to continuous spaces and learn the model in contin-
071                uous space (e.g. Gulrajani & Hashimoto (2023))
072
073              • We demonstrate through extensive experiments that the proposed methods achieve faster conver-
074                gence, efficient parallel generation and lower perplexity and superior downstream reasoning and
075
                   controlled generation task performance DDLM inherits the strengths of autoregressive models while
                   bringing novel benefits such as iterative refinement during generation, which shines particularly in
076
                   complex tasks like in-filling, arithmetic and arbitrary prompting.

238   In this section, we demonstrate how to apply the TCS objective to a specific setup of interest: distilling a pre-
239   trained transformer-based autoregressive language model qAR to a denoising diffusion language model pθ .
240   We present a set of techniques to facilitate the efficient computation of the target concrete score rqAR (x0 ) in
241   practice for transformer-based language models. We refer to the family of models resulting from this process
242   as Distilled Diffusion Language Models, or DDLM.

245
      4.1   E FFICIENT ESTIMATION OF TARGET C ONCRETE S CORE
246
247
248   To optimize the TCS distillation objective, we need to compute the target concrete score rqAR (x0 ) (x0 ).
249   Naively, this requires (V − 1) × L + 1 log-density evaluations of the teacher autoregressive model for each
250   sequence x, where for each position 1 ≤ l ≤ L, the l-th token is replaced with all other V − 1 tokens,
251
      and the log probability of each altered sequence is explicitly computed by the teacher model to obtain the
      log-density ratio, ultimately resulting in the target concrete score rqAR (x0 ). However, this procedure is
252
      computationally prohibitive. To address this challenge, we propose two practical estimation approaches.
253
      For example, GPT-2 (Radford et al., 2019) has 50257 vocabulary size and Llama3 (Dubey et al., 2024)
254   model has 128_000 vocabulary size. We introduce two approaches to efficiently estimate the target concrete
255   score, top-K estimation and gradient-informed estimation.
256
257
      Top-K Estimation. Empirically, the concrete score is highly sparse. As illustrated in Figure 2, tokens
      with high density ratios closely resemble the one-hot encoding of original tokens in the simplex space, but
258
      enriched with distributional information. This observation motivates approximating the score vector with
259
      only the top-K items, treating the rest as zero, for efficient computation. In particular, we approximate
260   the computation of rqAR (x0 ) (x0 ) by replacing the l-th token only with the top-K most probable tokens,
261   determined by the logits output of teacher model based on the preceding l−1 tokens, estimated by the teacher
262   model itself qAR (xl |x<l ). This approach reduces the total number of sequence log-probability evaluations
263   from (V − 1) × L to K × L + 1, thus eliminating the dependency on vocabulary size. Note that we can read
264   out qAR (xl |x<l ) from the teacher model’s logits output at each position l, which can be done in one batched
265   forward pass with causal attention. Additionally, we employ KV-caching during the teacher model’s forward
266   pass to further reduce computational overhead. The details of the top-K estimation algorithm is described
267   in Algorithm 2. We found this approach to be effective in practice with a relatively small K ≤ 128.
268   Gradient-informed Estimation. We now present another method to estimate the target concrete score
269   rqAR (x0 ) (x0 ). The key insight is that while autoregressive language models operate over discrete state
270   spaces, they are, in fact, continuous and differentiable functions that accept real-valued one-hot encoded
271   input tokens, though they are typically evaluated on a discrete subset of their domain. This observation has
272   been employed in previous work to accelerate the convergence rate of Gibbs sampling in discrete energy-
273   based models (Grathwohl et al., 2021).

313   Building on the TCS objective introduced in Equation (9) and the two practical estimation methods discussed
314   earlier, we present the full training procedure for DDLM, as illustrated in Algorithm 1. In TCS distillation,
315   data examples must be sampled from the target teacher distribution. However, relying exclusively on teacher-
316   policy data for distillation may not yield optimal results. When autoregressive LLMs are trained using the
317   teacher-forcing objective, their learned distributions can become biased and skewed, potentially resulting
318   in less diverse and artificially generated data samples. Alternatively, when real data is available, it can
319   be sampled and evaluated by the teacher model to compute the target concrete score. Indeed, the TCS
320
      distillation objective is also effective for any x0 ∼ q0 (x0 ) with full support over X L , enabling off-policy data
      learning. In practice, to balance data efficiency with sample quality, we sample from a mixture of teacher-
321
      generated data and real data: x0 ∼ ωqAR (x0 ) + (1 − ω)qdata (x0 ), with the default value of ω = 0.5. Similar
322
      to the classical knowledge distillation (Hinton et al., 2015), we combine the TCS distillation loss with the
323   denoising score matching loss of the baseline student model as a weighted sum controlled by λ as shown in
324   Algorithm 1.

344   5            E XPERIMENTS
345
346   In this section, we empirically assess the performance of DDLM with TCS distillation across various language
347   modeling and reasoning tasks to investigate the following research questions: RQ1: Is TCS distillation an
348   effective training objective for distilling a pre-trained autoregressive (AR) language model into a diffusion
349   language model? RQ2: Does such distillation offer novel benefits for AR language modeling? RQ3: What
350   are the limitations of the proposed TCS distillation? Under what conditions does TCS distillation perform
351   best, and when does it fall short? We present a summary of our findings in this section. Detailed descriptions
352
      of the datasets and model configurations can be found in the appendix due to space constraints.
353   Baselines We use state-of-the-art diffusion language models in both discrete and continuous settings as the
354   baseline models, including SEDD (Shi et al., 2024), MD4 (Shi et al., 2024), MDLM (Sahoo et al., 2024) in
355   discte space and Plaid (Gulrajani & Hashimoto, 2023) in continuous space.
356   DDLM Models In our experiments, we consider the following DDLM models: DDLM-Full refers to the
357   model that uses the exact TCS estimation computed by replacing each token with all other tokens in the
358   vocabulary. This is possible when the vocabulary size V is small such as character-level language modeling
359   tasks. DDLM-TopK refers to the model that uses the top-K approximation of the TCS estimation. DDLM-∇
360   refers to the model that uses the gradient-based estimation of the TCS. We include the name of the base
361   student model in the name of the DDLM model for clarity, e.g., DDLM-Student-SEDD meaning that we use
362   SEDD as the diffusion language model formulation to distill the teacher AR model. We use DDLM-from-
363   scratch to refer to the model that is trained from scratch, and DDLM-fine-tune to refer to the model that the
364
      student model is first pre-trained by regular denoising score matching objective and then fine-tuned by our
      TCS distillation.

437               DDLM-fine-tuning with TCS distillation can transfer the reasoning ability of the teacher model to the

      tilled student model on the multi-digit multiplication task from the BIG-bench benchmark (Srivastava et al.,

444
      digit (4 x 4) and five-digit (5 x 5) multiplication problems, as these tasks are particularly difficult to solve

                  While DDLM-from-scratch and DDLM-fine-tune can improve the sample efficiency of the student

                  extensive data augmentation and amount of data.

455
456
      We observe that the benefits of DDLM-fine-tune are task-dependent. Specifically, DDLM-fine-tune does not
      consistently enhance the student model’s performance in terms of perplexity for language modeling tasks. It
457
      is crucial to use ground-truth data during fine-tuning to maintain the teacher model’s perplexity. However,
458
      DDLM-fine-tune can yield better results in terms of generative perplexity. Additionally, we note that when
459   the dataset is large and data augmentation is extensive, as in the case of GSM8K-Aug, the distillation benefits
460   may plateau.

      In this section, we present the pseudo-code for the proposed estimation methods of the target concrete score.
832   Algorithm 2 demonstrate the proposed Top-K estimation. First, we compute the teacher model’s logit
833   output based on the preceding tokens. This can be achieved in a single forward pass using causal attention
834   calculated in parallel. Next, the top-K tokens at each position are selected to compute the log-density ratio,
835   ultimately leading to the estimated concrete score.
836   We also introduce a variant called Top-K with N -Gram estimation. In Algorithm 3, we highlight the differ-
837   ences in blue. This variant employs a distinct procedure for selecting the top tokens. At the l-th position, we
838   use an N-Gram language model to compute n-gram scores and select additional top-K tokens based on these
839   scores, combining them with the original top-K tokens selected from the teacher’s logit. This results in a
840   total of 2K tokens. Specifically, the n-gram score at position l is computed as [p(xl+1 , . . . , xl+N −1 |x)]x∈V
841   with p(xl+1 , . . . , xl+N −1 |x) ∝ p(x)p(x, xl+1 , . . . , xl+N −1 ), where p(x) and p(x, xl+1 , . . . , xl+N −1 ) can
842   be estimated using the empirical distribution. Empirically, we observe that this approach performs similarly
843
      to that in Algorithm 2.
844   We also provide the pseudo-code in Listing 1 for gradient-informed estimation. In this method, we use a
845   first-order Taylor approximation to estimate the concrete score, significantly reducing computational costs.

964   Diffusion models (Austin et al., 2021; Campbell et al., 2022; Sahoo et al., 2024; Lou et al., 2024; Campbell
965   et al., 2024; Gat et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2024; He et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2023), grounded
966   in discrete-time Markov chains within continuous state spaces and employing Gaussian transitions (Sohl-
967
      Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020), have been extended to continuous-time formulations through the
      application of stochastic processes and score matching (Song et al., 2021). A parallel research direction ex-
968
      plores discrete diffusion models operating on discrete data spaces, similarly based on Markov chains (Sohl-
969
      Dickstein et al., 2015; Hoogeboom et al., 2021). D3PM (Austin et al., 2021) investigated discrete-time
970   Markov chains utilizing various transition matrices (uniform, absorbing, discretized Gaussian), deriving a
971   discrete-time variational lower bound (ELBO) that was subsequently generalized to continuous-time Markov
972   chains (CTMCs) (Campbell et al., 2022). This approach leverages mean-parameterization to learn the re-
973   verse transition probability.
974
      An alternative perspective posits that D3PM implicitly learns the ratio of marginal distributions, termed the
975
      "concrete score"—a discrete analogue of the continuous score function (Meng et al., 2022; Lou et al., 2024).
976   This ratio can be directly learned via concrete score matching, mirroring the continuous score matching
977   approach (Meng et al., 2022). However, practical implementation faces challenges due to the incompatibility
978   of the L2 loss with the inherent positivity constraint of this ratio. SEDD (Lou et al., 2024) addresses this
979   challenge by introducing a score entropy objective, providing a theoretically more robust surrogate and
980   establishing a connection between the concrete score and the continuous-time ELBO.
981
      Although SEDD considers both uniform and absorbing transitions, masked diffusion (the absorbing case)
982   exhibits significantly improved empirical performance. This approach introduces a [MASK] token repre-
983   senting an absorbing state and models the transitions between masked and unmasked states, analogous to
984   the mechanism employed in masked language models. Recent work (Shi et al., 2024; Sahoo et al., 2024)
985   further unifies the masked diffusion framework with continuous diffusion principles, resulting in simplified
986   and theoretically grounded training and sampling procedures. This unification not only offers a more co-

       through enhanced parameterization and engineering strategies. The present work primarily adopts this uni-

991    LLM distillation Recent research on LLM distillation (Xu et al., 2024) focus on enhancing the efficiency
992    and performance of smaller models while leveraging the strengths of larger ones. One of the main challenge
993    lies in addressing the discrepancy betweeen training and inference. MiniLLM (Gu et al., 2024) proposes
994    mixing teacher and student distributions to address training-inference mismatches, improving output qual-
995    ity and consistency. DistiLLM (Ko et al., 2024) builds on this by introducing a skew Kullback-Leibler
996    divergence (KLD) loss to stabilize optimization and an adaptive off-policy strategy to enhance training ef-
997    ficiency, significantly reducing the computational burden associated with generating self-generated outputs.
998    Hsieh et al. (2023) uses rationales generated by LLMs to train smaller, task-specific models effectively. This
999
       method highlights the importance of reasoning in distillation, allowing smaller models to achieve competi-
       tive performance even with limited data. Liu et al. (2024) explores a dynamic approach to distillation that
1000
       integrates active learning techniques by iteratively selecting the most informative examples, thereby improv-
1001
       ing the efficiency and effectiveness of knowledge transfer from larger to smaller models. Fundamentally
1002   different from them, our distillation from the LLM to the diffusion model involves transferring knowledge
1003   from a unidirectional model to a bidirectional model. Nevertheless, we have discovered that certain tech-
1004   niques, like mitigating the distribution discrepancy between training and inference is helpful.
