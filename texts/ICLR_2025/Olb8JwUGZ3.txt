                                                    A BSTRACT
008
009
               Many real-world learning tasks have an underlying hierarchical modular structure,
010
               composed of smaller sub-functions. Traditional neural networks (NNs), however,
011            often ignore this structure, leading to inefficiencies in learning and generalization.
012            Leveraging known structural information can enhance performance by aligning
013            the network architecture with the task’s inherent modularity. In this work, we
014            investigate how modular NNs can outperform traditional dense networks by sys-
015            tematically varying the degree of structural knowledge incorporated. We com-
016            pare architectures ranging from monolithic dense NNs, which assume no prior
017            knowledge, to hierarchically modular NNs with shared modules, which leverage
018            sparsity, modularity, and module reusability. Our experiments demonstrate that
019
               incorporating structural knowledge, particularly through module reuse and fixed
               connectivity, significantly improves learning efficiency and generalization. Hier-
020
               archically modular NNs excel in data-scarce scenarios by promoting functional
021
               specialization within the modules and reducing redundancy. These findings sug-
022            gest that task-specific architectural biases can lead to more efficient, interpretable,
023            and effective learning systems.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Real-world learning tasks often exhibit an inherent hierarchical and modular structure, where a com-
029   plex target function can be decomposed into smaller sub-functions arranged hierarchically Simon
030   (1991). Exploiting this structure—either explicitly known or inferred during training—can signifi-
031   cantly improve the efficiency and generalization of neural networks (NNs).
032   Traditional NNs typically treat target functions as undifferentiated input-output mappings, ignor-
033   ing any underlying modular structure. This results in higher training costs and the need for larger
034   datasets. Recent advances in NN architectures, particularly sparse and hierarchically modular NNs,
035   have demonstrated the potential to overcome these challenges (Fernando et al., 2017; Rosenbaum
036   et al., 2017; Shazeer et al., 2017; Kirsch et al., 2018; Goyal et al., 2021; Ponti et al., 2022). These ar-
037   chitectures break the NN into sparsely connected sub-networks or modules, each learning a distinct
038
      sub-function, and arrange them hierarchically to mirror the structure of the task.
039   The key advantage of hierarchically modular NNs is their alignment with the natural task structure,
040   allowing for more efficient learning. However, the challenge remains when the task structure is not
041   explicitly known. In this study, we explore how different degrees of structural knowledge can be
042   leveraged to enhance NN performance.
043   We begin by considering dense NNs with no structural assumptions, followed by random sparse NNs
044   that assume sparsity but not its specific pattern. These fall under the category of monolithic NNs.
045   We then examine hierarchically modular NNs, where modules are explicitly defined and organized
046   hierarchically (Fernando et al., 2017; Ostapenko et al., 2021). In these networks, both module
047   weights and inter-module connections are learned without assuming any prior knowledge about the
048   specific connectivity. We further extend this exploration to modular NNs with fixed inter-module
049   connectivity, representing cases where the exact sub-function connectivity is known.
050   Finally, we introduce module reusability, where the same module can be used in multiple locations
051   within the hierarchy, reflecting the idea that sub-functions may recur throughout the task (Goyal
052   et al., 2021; Ostapenko et al., 2022). In these cases, the NN must learn both connectivity and
053   module selection dynamically from a shared pool of modules. We also consider a variant with fixed
      connectivity and module selection, representing complete structural knowledge.

091   2.1   H IERARCHICALLY M ODULAR B OOLEAN F UNCTIONS
092
093   In this work, we construct hierarchical and modular tasks using Boolean functions. A Boolean
094   function f : {0, 1}n → {0, 1}m maps n input bits to m output bits. The set of gates G includes
095   {∧, ∨, ⊕} (AND, OR, XOR), with edges representing direct connections.
096   A function graph for a Boolean function is represented as a directed acyclic graph (DAG) consisting
097   of n input nodes with zero in-degree, k gate nodes with non-zero in-degree (associated with gates
098   from G), and m output nodes with zero out-degree.
099
      A sub-function or sub-task corresponds to a gate node within the function graph that applies an
100
      operation on its specific inputs. Sub-functions are organized hierarchically, with outputs from certain
101   sub-functions serving as inputs for others. These sub-functions have three fundamental properties:
102
103   1. Input Connectivity and Separability: Sub-functions operate on outputs from previous sub-
104
      functions or input nodes. This connectivity is sparse—each sub-function relies on a subset of preced-
      ing outputs. In our experiments, each sub-function takes exactly two inputs to maintain uniformity
105
      across graphs.
106
107   2. Output Connectivity and Reusability: Outputs produced by a sub-function can be reused by
      multiple sub-functions at higher hierarchical levels, similar to feature reuse in neural networks.

      locations in the function graph. For instance, an XOR gate might be reused in various parts of the
119   graph.
120
121   A task’s hierarchical structure or sub-function organization is defined by the relationships among
122
      these underlying sub-functions.

124   2.2   N EURAL N ETWORK A RCHITECTURES
125
      In this section we describe various NN architectures used in our experiments.
126
127   Monolithic NNs: We consider dense multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs) and sparse MLPs. Sparse
128   MLPs are created by random pruning to introduce sparsity, leveraging a notion of sparsity in the
129   function graph but without its specific pattern.
130   Hierarchically Modular NNs (modular): The modular architecture is arranged in L hierarchical
131   layers, each containing Ml modules, denoted as mil , where i represents the module’s position within
132   layer l. Each module includes a small MLP and an input selection vector sil ∈ RMl−1 . The MLP
133   learns the functional component, while the input selection vector governs inter-module connectivity
134   by selecting inputs from the outputs of modules in the previous layer. The final outputs of the NN
135   are selected from the set of all modules in the last layer using an output selection vector.
136   We explore two scenarios: 1. Modular: Both inter-module connectivity and module MLP weights
137   are learned. 2. Modular-FC: Inter-module connectivity is fixed, inferred from the function graph,
138   while module MLP weights are learned.
139
      Learning Inter-Module Connectivity: Given outputs from layer l − 1, xl−1 ∈ RMl−1 , the Sigmoid
140
      function is applied to sil to produce a score for each potential input, selecting the top-k inputs.
141   We use the straight-through estimator (Bengio et al., 2013) to propagate gradients through non-
142   differentiable selections, facilitating effective learning of inter-module connectivity. We fix k = 2
143   to maintain structural consistency, which also prevents module collapse and optimizes module uti-
144   lization (Goyal et al., 2021; Ostapenko et al., 2022). See Appendix A for additional implementation
145   details and Appendix C.1 for experiments related to learning inter-module connectivity.
146
      Hierarchically Modular NNs with Shared Modules (modular-shared): The modular-shared ar-
147   chitecture extends the modular architecture, treating module positions as slots filled by modules
148   from a shared pool of M modules. Each slot has an input selection vector sil ∈ RMl−1 and a module
149   selection vector vli ∈ RM , which determines the module used in that slot. The final outputs are
150   selected from the set of slots in the last layer using an output selection vector.
151
152
      We explore two scenarios: 1. Modular-shared: The network learns both inter-module connectivity
      and module selection dynamically, alongside module weights. 2. Modular-shared-FCMS: Connec-
153
      tivity and module selection are fixed, with only the module weights being learned.
154
155   Learning Inter-Module Connectivity and Module Selection: Outputs from slots at layer l−1, xl−1 ∈
156   RMl−1 , are selected as inputs similarly to the previous architecture. The chosen inputs are passed
157   to a module determined by the selection vector vli ∈ RM , which is first transformed via a Softmax
158
      function to assign probabilities to each module in the shared pool. The module with the highest
      probability is selected (top-1). The straight-through estimator is used to compute gradients for both
159
      sil and vli (see Appendix A).
160
161   Hereafter, ”hierarchically modular NNs” refers to both modular and modular-shared unless other-
      wise specified.

      E demonstrates that varying the number of modules or slots has minimal impact on performance.
      All module MLPs have a uniform structure, with 2 input units, a hidden layer of 12 units, and 1

304   3.3   FACTORS I NFLUENCING G ENERALIZATION IN M ODULAR N ETWORKS
305
306   In this section, we analyze two key factors influencing the generalization of modular and modular-
307   shared NNs: learning the sub-function organization through inter-module connectivity and achiev-
308
      ing functional specialization within modules, particularly under limited data conditions.
309   Learning the Sub-Function Organization: Unlike monolithic dense NNs, which directly learn
310   input-output mappings, modular and modular-shared NNs need to identify the underlying task
311   structure to perform effectively. We measure how well these NNs capture the true task structure
312   using minimum graph edit distance (Abu-Aisheh et al., 2015), comparing the learned inter-module
313   connectivity to the ground truth function graph.
314   The learned connectivity is represented as a graph with NN input units, output units, and all modules
315   as nodes. The input and output nodes must match, while modules can align with any gate node at the
316   same hierarchical level, ensuring permutation invariance. Figure 5 shows that modular-shared NNs
317   consistently achieve lower graph edit distances compared to modular NNs, indicating a closer match
318   to the ground truth. Notably, the graph edit distance is zero when both modular and modular-shared
319   NNs achieve 100% train and test accuracy.
320   Learning the Underlying Sub-Function: The superior generalization of modular-shared NNs,
321   particularly with lower truth table ratios, suggests an advantage due to module reuse across multiple
322   locations, enabling modules to learn sub-functions more effectively with fewer samples. To quantify
323   functional specialization, we use a metric based on Pearson’s correlation coefficient between module
      outputs and truth table outputs for a specific sub-function.

      5   D ISCUSSION
467
468   Recently, sparse NNs designed at initialization have gained attention for reducing training costs.
469   Methods to identify these sparse NNs often involve using a portion of the training data to determine
470
      optimal sparsity patterns (Lee et al., 2019; Wang et al., 2019) or exploiting structural properties that
      enhance convergence and generalization (Tanaka et al., 2020; Patil & Dovrolis, 2021). However,
471
      these sparse NNs generally underperform in terms of generalization compared to dense NNs, espe-
472
      cially at lower density levels. Accurately capturing the task structure within NNs before training
473   remains a significant challenge.
474
475   Prior studies have examined the structure of trained dense NNs to uncover the hierarchical modu-
476
      larity inherent in tasks (Watanabe et al., 2018; Watanabe, 2019; Filan et al., 2021; Hod et al., 2021;
      Lange et al., 2022). Nonetheless, these methods do not conclusively demonstrate whether the NN’s
477
      acquired structure aligns with the underlying task structure. Other works have proposed techniques
478
      to derive the task structure by training NNs and conditioning them to reveal hierarchical modularity
479   (Malakarjun Patil et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023).
480
481   Our work bridges these two lines of inquiry by exploring how the integration of a task’s hierarchical
482
      and modular structure at initialization influences NN performance. We demonstrate that varying
      degrees of structural knowledge can significantly enhance generalization and training efficiency.
483
484   The concept of hierarchically modular architectures has also been applied in transfer and continual
485   learning, where multiple tasks are learned sequentially, often assuming some level of interdepen-
      dence. Previous work has utilized explicitly defined hierarchically modular NNs to address these


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      challenges (Terekhov et al., 2015; Veniat et al., 2020; Mendez & Eaton, 2020; Ostapenko et al.,
487   2021). Hierarchical modularity offers advantages such as freezing modules to prevent catastrophic
488   forgetting and reusing modules to enhance transfer performance. Similarly, multitask learning ap-
489   proaches have benefited from these frameworks by finding appropriate inter-module connectivity for
490   different tasks, reusing modules for common sub-functions, and learning distinct modules for task-
491   specific sub-functions (Devin et al., 2017; Rosenbaum et al., 2017; Maninis et al., 2019; Kanakis
492   et al., 2020; Ponti et al., 2022).
493   Combinatorial generalization, also known as compositional or systematic generalization, involves
494   the ability of NNs to generalize to unseen input combinations or tasks involving the same sub-
495   functions arranged differently, as in visual question answering (Bahdanau et al., 2018; Lake & Ba-
496   roni, 2018; Hupkes et al., 2020). Hierarchically modular NNs have demonstrated superior perfor-
497   mance in these scenarios by enabling module reuse and reorganization based on specific parts of the
498   input (Andreas et al., 2016; Hu et al., 2017; Wiedemer et al., 2024).

      6   C ONCLUSION
501
502
      This work explored how varying degrees of structural knowledge about a task’s hierarchical modu-
503
      larity impact NN generalization performance and training efficiency. Through experiments involv-
504   ing Boolean functions and a hierarchically modular MNIST task, we showed that networks with
505   modularity and module reusability significantly outperform monolithic and sparse networks, partic-
506   ularly in data-limited scenarios. The improved performance stems from the ability of these NNs to
507   exploit task structure effectively by learning both the specific sub-functions and their organization.
508   Our findings emphasize the importance of explicitly incorporating task structure and modularity into
509   NNs, indicating a promising direction for scalable and efficient learning systems.
510   A potential direction for future research is to explore the theoretical underpinnings of task learnabil-
511   ity, focusing on its function graph and the extent of prior knowledge encoded in the NN architec-
512   ture. Additionally, applying these architectures in the context of transfer learning could be valuable.
513   Investigating how the similarity between pre-training and target tasks impacts both transfer perfor-
514   mance and efficiency could provide key insights for optimizing transfer learning strategies.
