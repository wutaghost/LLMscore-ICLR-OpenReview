009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011               Accelerating scientific discoveries holds significant potential to address some of
012               the most pressing challenges facing society, from mitigating climate change to
013               combating public health crises, such as the growing antibiotics resistance. The
014               vast and complex nature of design parameter spaces makes identifying promising
015               candidates both time-consuming and resource-intensive, rendering conventional
016               exhaustive searches impractical. However, recent advancements in data-driven
017               methods, particularly within the framework of "active learning," have led to more
018               efficient strategies for scientific discovery. By iteratively identifying and labeling
019
                  the most informative data points, these methods function in a closed loop, guiding
                  experiments or simulations to accelerate the identification of optimal candidates
020
                  while reducing the demand for data labeling. Despite these advancements, the
021
                  lack of standardized benchmarks in this emerging field of autonomous scientific
022               discovery impedes progress and limits its potential translational impact. To address
023               this, we introduce BALSA: a comprehensive benchmark specifically designed for
024               evaluating various search algorithms applied in autonomous laboratories within
025               the active learning framework. BALSA offers a standardized evaluation protocol,
026               provides a metric to characterize high-dimensional objective functions, and includes
027               reference implementations of recent methodologies, with a focus on minimizing
028               the data required to reach optimal results. It provides not only a suite of synthetic
029               functions or controlled simulators but also real-world active learning tasks in
030
                  biology and materials science — each presenting unique challenges for autonomous
                  laboratory tasks.1

034   1       I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Designing proteins or materials with specific properties—ranging from antibiotic resistance to
037   superconductivity—represents a crucial frontier in addressing critical scientific and societal challenges
038
      (Hamidieh, 2018; Varmus et al., 2003; Merchant et al., 2023). Traditionally, scientists have approached
      these design processes by generating hypotheses based on prior knowledge and past data. These
039
      hypotheses are then tested using experimental protocols within constrained budgets. However, this
040
      approach is often inefficient, time-consuming, and limited by human ingenuity and errors. In recent
041   years, the integration of data-driven methods with automated laboratory setups has accelerated
042   discovery across various fields, ranging from the design of proteins or DNA sequences in biology or
043   the discovery of functional materials (Coley et al., 2019; Rao et al., 2022; Szymanski et al., 2023;
044   Rapp et al., 2024).
045
      One of the most promising innovations in this field is the self-driving laboratories (SLs), which
046
      leverage active learning (AL) algorithms to autonomously guide experimentation and accelerate
047   scientific discovery (Häse et al., 2019; Kang et al., 2019; Abolhasani & Kumacheva, 2023). Advances
048   in AL offer the potential to significantly enhance the exploration of larger regions within the expansive
049   search space, thus improving efficiency and effectiveness in experimental designs and optimization
050   processes, as shown in Figure 1a. Given that the underlying model of objective function (or the
051   validation source) is often intractable, and only limited data are available, a typical approach is to
052   develop a surrogate model to approximate the distribution of objective function. This surrogate model

      is then used iteratively to optimize the design, serving as a stand-in for the objective function in the
055   optimization process. The key components of SLs (or AL pipelines) are illustrated in Figure 1b.
056
057   Despite significant progress, many strategies to explore the search spaces, including exact and
058
      heuristic approaches, often struggle to adapt and scale to high-dimensional and non-linear scenarios
      found in many science applications (Frazier, 2018). Bayesian Optimization (BO) and its variants
059
      (Shahriari et al., 2016; Bubeck et al., 2011; Springenberg et al., 2016), have emerged as popular
060
      alternatives that learn a Bayesian model of the objective function and sample the best candidates
061   using an uncertainty-based technique such as Thompson sampling (Shahriari et al.). While these
062   approaches perform well in low-dimensional spaces, their effectiveness diminishes in more complex,
063   higher-dimensional settings (Frazier, 2018). More recently, tree search methods, which are the key
064   component of many revolutionary AI algorithms such as AlphaGo (Silver et al., 2016), have been
065   applied to design problems. These methods iteratively partition the search space (Kim et al., 2020a)
066   and employ local surrogate models to approximate the promising search subspace (Eriksson et al.,
067   2019). However, their success is often contingent on the quality of these local models, and they also
068   struggle with the curse of dimensionality (Wang et al., 2020b).
069   Moreover, the intricate interplay between surrogate models and search strategies within AL pipelines,
070   coupled with the growing number of scientific applications, has made it increasingly difficult to
071   compare and track progress effectively. Different methods are often proposed and evaluated on
072   distinct tasks with varying evaluation protocols, leading to inconsistent benchmarks. To the best
073   of our knowledge, no unified benchmark or systematic investigation currently exists to evaluate
074   and compare these algorithms across AL strategies. This paper addresses this gap by proposing a
075
      standardized benchmark that enables a fair comparison of state-of-the-art AL strategies, ensuring
      more consistent progress in scientific discovery.
076
077   Our contributions We conduct a systematic evaluation of the key algorithmic components and their
078   interactions within AL pipelines. Our main contributions are summarized as follows:
079
             • We propose an AL pipeline specifically tailored for real-world self-driving laboratory
080
               environments. The pipeline is designed with the following key objectives: (i) to emulate
081
               the iterative, step-by-step process characteristic of real-world self-driving tasks; (ii) to
082            leverage surrogate models for the efficient approximation of complex systems in data-scarce
083            scenarios; and (iii) to address the unique challenges associated with low-data regimes,
084            ensuring robust performance under limited data availability.
085          • We introduce a suite of 6 standardized synthetic tasks and 11 baseline methods to sys-
086            tematically evaluate a broad range of current AL pipelines and the respective surrogate
087            models.
088          • We design and implement four real-world tasks to evaluate the proposed pipeline: (i) neural
089            network architecture search to optimize model performance; (ii) the lunar landing problem,
090            simulating complex control dynamics; (iii) a biology task utilizing AlphaFold2 as a virtual
091            simulator for protein design, demonstrating applications in computational biology; and (iv) a
092            materials science task focused on resolution optimization of scanning transmission electron
093            microscopes, leveraging professional open-source simulation software for advanced imaging
094            applications.
095          • Through a systematic investigation and a large-scale empirical study, we introduce a novel
096            metric that quantifies the characteristics of objective landscapes across diverse design
097            problems. This metric offers valuable insights into the behavior and performance of AL
098            methods, enabling a more nuanced understanding of their effectiveness in complex settings.
099          • Based on these empirical findings, we highlight three critical areas for advancing self-
100            driving labs within the AL pipeline: (i) understanding the interplay between surrogate model
101            and search strategy in relation to the objective landscape, (ii) ensuring reproducibility of
102
               algorithmic performance across a wide variety of synthetic and real-world tasks, and (iii)
               developing methods that handle the optimization problems with limited data availability.

      Our benchmark suite includes 6 carefully selected functions: Ackley, Rastrigin, Rosenbrock,
208
      Griewank, Schwefel, and Michalewicz. The primary objective for these synthetic functions is
209

212   synthetic functions to mimic the complex data distributions generated by various validation sources.
213   The process is iterative, with each iteration allowing only 20 data points to be sampled from the
214   synthetic function tasks. This constraint necessitates the development of an effective learning-based
215   surrogate model. These synthetic functions can serve as valuable test cases for understanding the
      properties of real-world SL tasks across diverse conditions using different search algorithms with

221   Landscape characterization Understanding the topology of an objective function is crucial for
222   evaluating the performance of learning-based surrogate models within the AL pipeline. For instance,
223   a machine learning model often exhibits a less-satisfactory performance on a flat landscape of an
224   objective function, for which most of the values are at the same level, making it difficult for the model
225   to learn and generalize. A poorly performing surrogate model may mislead the search methods,
226
      ultimately resulting in sub-optimal outcomes. Figure 2 (a-d) visualizes the objective landscapes
      of the corresponding synthetic functions in their 2D forms. Ackley shows a rugged but funneled
227
      topology, while Rosenbrock exhibits a long valley with numerous local minima. Schwefel presents a
228
      complex multi-funnel topology, whereas Michalewicz has sharp drops on a rather flat landscape (The
229   mathematical formula can be found in Supplementary S.1).
230

233

234
      the objective landscape. While the metric provides valuable empirical insights, we acknowledge its
235   limitations in theoretical rigor and aim to explore a more comprehensive analysis in future work.

      Many real-world tasks can be treated as VLs, where high-fidelity simulators are combined with
304   learning models, automatically optimizing designs to achieve better mechanical, physical, or chemical
305   properties within a virtual environment. VLs are essential across a multitude of complex real-world
306   systems, particularly when experiments are associated with prohibitive costs and extensive design
307   spaces. The virtual tasks included in VLs can be framed as typical AL problems. In this work, we
308   focus on four benchmark tasks within SLs: neural network architecture search, lunar landing problem,

      and (iii) they can be executed within reasonable time and computational resources.

      5.1   C YCLIC PEPTIDE DESIGN
346
347   Background Cyclic peptides are a class of compounds that have garnered significant attention as
348   therapeutic agents due to their enhanced stability, high specificity, and excellent membrane permeabil-
349   ity. These properties make them particularly effective in targeting traditionally "undruggable" protein
350   surfaces (Vinogradov et al., 2019). The amino acids (AAs) in cyclic peptides are interconnected by
351   amides or other chemically stable bonds, which can be chosen from the 20 standard AAs or various
352   non-standard ones, creating a high-dimensional and complex sequence design space (Zorzi et al.,
353
      2017). Here, the task is more specific than general protein design: it involves designing a specialized
      type of protein with therapeutic applications. This protein is required to exhibit stronger interactions
354
      with its target, such as higher binding affinity. Such a task can be framed as an optimization problem.
355
      However, even for a relatively simple 16-residue sequence, the combinatorial search space includes
356   1620 possible configurations. The intricate and nonlinear relationship between protein sequence and
357   functional properties further complicates the challenge, making it a suitable benchmark for testing
358   advanced methodologies. An additional advantage of this setup is the availability of natural binders
359   as a reference for comparison. Traditionally, one often needs to conduct high-throughput wet lab
360   experiments, synthesizing thousands of cyclic peptides before discovering one that can specifically
361   bind to a desired protein (Gang et al., 2018). VL can accelerate this discovery process by narrowing
362   the potential candidates to a few dozen, drastically reducing the cost.
363
364   Optimization target The optimization target of cyclic peptide design is defined as follows:
365                                           T arget = SC · dSASA                                           (4)
366
      The SC value ranges from 0 to 1, referring to how well the surfaces of two proteins fit geometrically
367
      together at their interface; dSASA measures the size of the interface (in units of Å2 ). a larger
368
      dSASA reflects a more extensive interface area. Further details regarding the dataset and simulation
369
      settings can be found in Supplementary S.7.

515   7   D ISCUSSION
516
517   Biology and material science applications represent exciting application areas with tremendous
518   potential for the development of self-driving labs. However, the absence of standardized benchmarks
519   and evaluation protocols has hindered the accurate tracking of progress. To address this, we design
520   an active learning pipeline that tailors to self-driving lab settings, including (i) iterative process,
521   (ii) use of surrogate models and(iii) low-data regime. Our benchmark BALSA is a comprehensive
522   resource that includes (i) a codebase, (ii) a suit of synthetic tasks, and (iii) two complex tasks with
523   controlled simulators and two real-world applications in biology and materials science. It features a
524   large-scale empirical evaluation, providing a template for reproducible research and for systematically
525
      advancing the performance of algorithms across disciplines. Virtual labs and high-fidelity simulators
      have the potential to reduce the need for costly and time-consuming real-world experiments. Our
526
      extensive evaluation highlights current limitations and indicates promising directions for future
527
      research, including developing methods for hyperparameter selection with network-based surrogate
528   models and scaling approaches to very high dimensions.

931   S.5    A DDITIONAL N EURAL NETWORK ARCHITECTURE SEARCH DETAILS
932
933
      Dataset and optimization target To benchmark the efficacy of AL algorithms in optimizing neural
      network structures within the context of active learning, we choose the NAS-Bench-101 dataset
934
      (Ying et al., 2019), which contains over 400,000 unique convolutional neural networks along with
935
      their corresponding performance metrics, trained on the CIFAR-10 dataset (Hinton et al., 2012).
936   Each neural network is represented by a 7×7 upper-triangular adjacency matrix with up to 9 edges,
937   where nodes represent specific operations and edges denote the connection relationships between
938   these operations. The first operation represents the input, and the last represents the output, while
939   the remaining five components can be selected from 3×3 convolution, 1×1 convolution, or 3×3
940   max-pooling. The objective of the NAS task is to identify an optimized neural network structure that
941   achieves the highest classification accuracy on the test set (test acc).
942
943   Neural network architecture encoding We adopt a truncated 40-bit path-based encoding scheme
944   (White et al., 2021) to represent the neural network structure, where each bit corresponds to a specific
945   path from the input layer to the output layer, incorporating various operators along the way. For
946   optimization algorithms like CMA-ES, Dual Annealing, LAMCTS, and TuRBO5, which require a
947
      well-defined search domain, we parameterize the neural network structure into a 36-dimensional
      vector within the continuous [0, 1] space, as adopted from prior work (Letham et al., 2020). The first
948
      21 entries correspond to the adjacency matrix, where the largest values set the respective elements in
949
      the matrix to 1. The remaining 15 entries represent the one-hot encoding of 5 components, each with
950   three possible operations. For MCMC and Random Search, optimization is performed directly at the
951   adjacency matrix level.
952
953   Surrogate model We train a 1D-CNN model to map the path encoding into the test acc. The
954   1D-CNN consists of 5 convolutional layers with filter sizes of 128, 64, 32, 16, and 8, respectively,
955   each using a kernel size of 3. It also includes 2 max-pooling layers with a pooling size of 2, 2 dropout
956   layers with a dropout rate of 0.2, followed by a flatten layer, 2 fully connected layers with 128 and 64
957   units, respectively, and a final output layer. The loss function used is mean square error (MSE).
958
959   AL algorithm srtups The optimization process begins by generating 200 random initial data points
960   from NAS-Bench-101, which are used to train the initial surrogate model. In the active learning
961   loop, optimization algorithms then sample 20 optimized successors by refining the surrogate model,
962   expanding the dataset. The updated surrogate model is subsequently used in the next iteration of the
963   loop, continually improving the optimization process.

996    S.7   A DDITIONAL CYCLIC PEPTIDE DESIGN DETAILS
997
998    Dataset Two protein and canonical cyclic peptide complexes, PDBID: 4kel and PDBID: 7k2j, are
999    sourced from the Protein Data Bank (PDB). The former is a 14-amino acid serine protease inhibitor
1000
       targeting human kallikrein-related peptidase 4 (KLK4) (Riley et al., 2019), while the latter is a cyclic
       7-mer peptide interacting with Kelch-like ECH-Associated Protein-1 (KEAP1) (Ortet et al., 2021).
1001
       For simplicity, we only consider standard amino acids. Therefore, each cyclic peptide is represented
1002
       as a sequence of integers ranging from 0 to 19, with each number corresponding to a distinct type of
1003   standard amino acid, making this a discrete optimization task.
1004
1005   Pipeline The pipeline for cyclic peptide VL consists of three components: (1) AlphaFold2 with
1006   cyclic offsets to predict the structure of protein-cyclic peptide complexes (Kosugi & Ohue, 2023); (2)
1007   ProteinMPNN to ensure the diversity of designed cyclic peptide sequences (Dauparas et al., 2022);
1008   and (3) Rosetta’s interface analyzer to evaluate the quality of the designed interface (Leaver-Fay
1009   et al., 2011). Given the structure of the desired protein and the corresponding interaction hotspot,
1010   the pipeline begins with an optimization method that iteratively searches for the sequence yielding
1011   the highest AlphaFold2 pLDDT (predicted Local Distance Difference Test) score, which indicates
1012   the confidence level of the predicted structure. The optimized sequence is fed into ProteinMPNN to
1013
       generate a pool of diverse sequences. Finally, the product of two Rosetta binding metrics—shape
       complementarity (SC) and the change in Solvent Accessible Surface Area (dSASA)—is used to filter
1014
       the output sequences, with the best-fit design likely to have high SC and dSASA values (Muratspahić
1015
       et al., 2023).
1016
1017
       Simulation Settings The structure of the protein and cyclic peptide complex is predicted using
1018   AlphaFold2-multimer with cyclic offsets, as implemented in ColabDesign (Kosugi & Ohue, 2023).
1019   ProteinMPNN is also employed in ColabDesign with a batch size of 128. The SC and dSASA
1020   values for the predicted structure of the protein and cyclic peptide complex are computed using the
1021   PyRosetta Interface Analyzer (Chaudhury et al., 2010).
1022

1024   oracle function evaluations to approximately 1000. The specific settings are detailed as follows.
