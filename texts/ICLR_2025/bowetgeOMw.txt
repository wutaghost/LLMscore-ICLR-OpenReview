009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            As large language models (LLMs) continue to advance, their deployment often
012            involves ﬁne-tuning to enhance performance on speciﬁc downstream tasks. How-
013            ever, this customization is sometimes accompanied by misleading claims about
014            the origins, raising signiﬁcant concerns about transparency and trust within the
015            open-source community. Existing model veriﬁcation techniques typically assess
016            functional, representational, and weight similarities. However, these approaches
017            often struggle against obfuscation techniques, such as permutations and scaling
018
               transformations, that obscure a model’s lineage. To address this limitation, we
               propose a novel detection method that rigorously determines whether a model has
019
               been ﬁne-tuned from a speciﬁed base model. This method includes the ability to
020
               extract the LoRA rank utilized during the ﬁne-tuning process, providing a more
021            robust veriﬁcation framework. This framework is the ﬁrst to provide a formal-
022            ized approach speciﬁcally aimed at pinpointing the sources of model ﬁne-tuning.
023            We empirically validated our method on twenty-nine diverse open-source models
024            under conditions that simulate real-world obfuscation scenarios. We empirically
025            analyze the effectiveness of our framework and ﬁnally, discuss its limitations. The
026            results demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach and indicate its potential to
027            establish new benchmarks for model veriﬁcation.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Recently, as large language models (LLMs) continue to advance, increasingly powerful models are
033
      rapidly emerging, demonstrating exceptional performance across a wide range of tasks. Users fre-
      quently ﬁne-tune these models to enhance their performance for speciﬁc applications. However,
034
      certain model providers have engaged in deceptive practices, exaggerating their technological capa-
035
      bilities for unjust gain. For example, the Reﬂection-70B, marketed by HyperWrite as the worlds
036   leading open-source model, was in fact ﬁne-tuned on Llama3-70B-instruct, not on Llama3.1-70B
037   as originally claimed, as illustrated in Figure 1. Such false claims have raised signiﬁcant concerns
038   regarding the potential misuse of models and the spread of misleading information (Pan et al., 2023).
039
      Current detection methods mainly evaluate functional, representation and weight similarity, as well
040
      as training data properties and program similarity (Klabunde et al., 2023b). However, the criteria
041
      used in these methods lack the necessary rigor and formalization, leading to ambiguity and incon-
042   sistency in determining whether a model is a ﬁne-tuned derivative of a speciﬁc base model. Among
043   these techniques, weight similarity is considered the most effective indicator to verify the relation-
044   ship between models. However, when the model undergoes obfuscation techniques such as permu-
045   tation or scaling transformations (Zhou et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2018), its reliability is compromised.
046   This shortcoming highlights the urgent need for more robust and systematic detection frameworks
047   that can reliably identify ﬁne-tuned models even when intentional obfuscation is involved.
048   To address this challenge, our study introduces a novel detection method that can rigorously deter-
049   mine whether a model has been ﬁne-tuned from a speciﬁed base model. Our approach is the ﬁrst
050   formal framework designed to address the complexity of model ﬁne-tuning for detection, marking a
051   signiﬁcant advance over existing techniques. Crucially, the method remains valid regardless of the
052   permutations used, enabling accurate determination of the basis model for any derivative. Through
053   this research, we aim to establish new standards for model veriﬁcation in the open-source community
      and improve the transparency and trustworthiness of the sources of AI models.

      2   R ELATED W ORKS
085
086   Parameter-Efﬁcient Fine-Tuning. PEFT has emerged as a crucial strategy for optimizing LLMs
087
      for speciﬁc tasks while reducing resource consumption. Techniques such as Low-Rank Adaptation
      (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021; Dettmers et al., 2024), Adapter Layers (Karimi Mahabadi et al., 2021),
088
      and Prompt Tuning (Jia et al., 2022) achieve performance improvements by modifying only a small
089
      subset of parameters, thus capturing task-speciﬁc information while retaining the original model’s
090   foundational knowledge. However, the increasing reliance on these methods raises concerns about
091   transparency and traceability, highlighting the need for robust veriﬁcation techniques to ensure the
092   integrity and reliability of ﬁne-tuned models.
093
      Obfuscation Techniques. To bolster model privacy and hinder unauthorized access, techniques
094
      such as permutation, scaling, and noise addition are employed Elhage et al. (2021). These meth-
095
      ods obscure direct parameter comparisons, complicating the identiﬁcation of derived models. For
096   example, permutation rearranges parameters, scaling alters their magnitudes, and noise addition
097   introduces random variations, all of which mask the model’s characteristics. These obfuscation
098   strategies protect intellectual property and sensitive data from unauthorized access and reverse engi-
099   neering (Youseﬁ et al., 2023), while also preventing misuse.
100
      Detection Methods. Recent researches for identifying model modiﬁcations focus on various similar-
101
      ities, including functional, representational, weight, training data, and procedural aspects (Klabunde
102   et al., 2023a). Functional and representational similarities compare model outputs and internal ac-
103   tivations, respectively, but often struggle against ﬁne-tuning variations and obfuscation techniques
104   like permutations and noise addition (Ethayarajh, 2019; Wu et al., 2020; Kornblith et al., 2019).
105   Weight similarity can effectively detect model lineage but is compromised by permutation-based
106   obfuscation (Wang et al., 2022; Elhage et al., 2021). Techniques examining training data and proce-
107   dural similarities, such as inﬂuence functions, can illuminate ﬁne-tuning practices but often require
      extensive datasets (Grosse et al., 2023; Shah et al., 2023). Additionally, procedural similarity offers

      insights into training methods but is limited by the proprietary nature of training pipelines (Bider-
109   man et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2023). Overall, recent approaches highlight the challenges in detecting
110   model modiﬁcations amid sophisticated obfuscation tactics.

      3.2   O BFUSCATION
135
136   Obfuscation in neural networks refers to the deliberate manipulation of parameters or structures to
137   obscure their original form while retaining functional output. In the context of models, obfuscation
138   techniques such as rearranging parameter matrices in attention and MLP modules are employed to
139   complicate unauthorized access, prevent direct comparisons, and protect model privacy. Despite
140   the internal changes, these techniques ensure that the models functional behavior remains intact,
141
      preserving performance while safeguarding intellectual property.
142   Signiﬁcant studies, such as those by Maron et al. (2020) and Zaheer et al. (2017), have explored
143   obfuscation’s effects in maintaining consistent outputs across different conﬁgurations. These works
144   highlight how obfuscation stabilizes model performance and hinders reverse engineering efforts.
145   Mathematically, given a set S = {s1 , s2 , . . . , sn }, obfuscation is deﬁned through transformations
146   that render the underlying structure opaque. When applied to a weight matrix W ∈ Rm×n , trans-
147   formation matrices P ∈ {0, 1}n×n reorder elements, turning W into W P . These transformations
148   complicate direct analysis without affecting the models output.
149
      In Transformer architectures, the MLP and attention layers, denoted by F and H, undergo obfusca-
150
      tion through transformations Π1 and Π2 , deﬁned as follows:
151
152                                      Fobf ◦ Hobf (X) = F ◦ H(X),
153
      where Fobf = Π1 (F ) and Hobf = Π2 (H). This approach ensures that internal obfuscation does not
154   affect the overall output, maintaining the model’s integrity and safeguarding its internal structure.
155

      3.3   P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
157
158   This research aims to determine whether the candidate model Mc has undergone ﬁne-tuning in its
159   self-attention modules, excluding MLP modules, from the base model Mb using Low-Rank Adap-
160   tation (LoRA), followed by layer-level obfuscation. We consider both models as white boxes, but
161   the obfuscations in Mc complicate comparisons with Mb due to potential parameter transformations
      that may obscure the structural relationships between their parameter matrices.

230   module of base model and address how to resolve obfuscations involved in these processes. The
231   relationship between single decoder-layer of Mc and Mc∗ can be formalized as:
232
                            fc ◦ hc (x) = fc∗ ◦ h∗c (x) and fc = Π2 (fc∗ ), hc = Π1 (h∗c ),

236                                       zc = fc ◦ hc (x) = fb ◦ h∗c (x),

243   This equation describes a non-linear transformation involving both element-wise operations and
244   matrix multiplications, rendering the inverse mapping from the output back to the input analytically
245   intractable. Given the nonlinearity and complexity of this transformation, directly inferring the
246
      intermediate state y from the observed output z poses signiﬁcant challenges. To tackle this, we adopt
      an iterative optimization strategy using gradient descent to approximate the original intermediate
247
      state y that likely produced the observed output. The goal is to minimize the discrepancy between
248
      the MLP output and the actual observed output by adjusting y. The iterative update formula is
249   expressed as:

      Models and Datasets. We consider twenty-nine open-source, LoRA-ﬁne-tuned models with vari-
296
      ous architectures as target models. Speciﬁcally, we select the series of LLaMA2 (7B, 13B, 70B)
297
      Touvron et al. (2023), LLaMA3 (8B, 70B), and Mistral (7B,13B) Jiang et al. (2023), as base mod-
298   els. We constructed a 5k dataset from the Natural Language Toolkit (NLTK) Loper & Bird (2002),
299   ensuring each input was encoded as a single token to maintain a consistent attention score during
300   the self-attention module.
301
      Rank Extraction Method. We extract the LoRA rank by analyzing the differences in intermediate
302
      representations between the target model and its base model. Then, we compute the dimensionality
303
      of this subspace with SVD(Singular Value Decomposition). The rank is determined by a sharp drop
304   (Figure 3) in singular values, seen as a peak (Figure 4) in the differences between consecutive values.
305
306   Implementation Details. To reconstruct the inputs for the MLP, we utilized gradient descent with
307
      the Adam optimizer Kingma & Ba (2014), initiating the process with a learning rate of 1.5e-3. We
      incorporated a StepLR scheduler with a step size of 1 to periodically adjust the learning rate by a
308
      factor of 0.9999, thus ﬁnely tuning the learning rate reduction to balance rapid convergence with
309
      meticulous adjustment of the model parameters. Furthermore, to address potential inaccuracies in
310   reverse-engineered MLP inputs, we conducted 50 iterations of stochastic sampling, each consisting
311   of 520 output evaluations. The minimum rank determined from these iterations forms our ﬁnal
312   estimation, enhancing the overall precision by reducing the inﬂuence of outlier data.

       6                    C ONCLUSION
465
466   This study addresses growing concerns within the open-source community about the misrepresen-
467   tation and misuse of ﬁne-tuned models. Existing detection methods, while valuable, often struggle
468   against sophisticated obfuscation techniques like permutations and scaling transformations, compli-
469   cating the veriﬁcation of modiﬁed models’ lineage and authenticity. We introduce a novel detection
470   methodology aimed at overcoming these challenges through a rigorous and systematic approach to
471   ascertain the provenance of ﬁne-tuned models.
472   Our methodology establishes a new benchmark for transparency and reliability in managing open-
473   source models. By implementing this formalized framework, we enhance the trustworthiness of
474   the ecosystem, ensuring that the origins and modiﬁcations of models are accurately documented,
475   thereby promoting greater accountability in AI technology deployment.
476
      Limitations. Despite its effectiveness, our method has limitations that restrict its broader applicabil-
477
      ity. It is currently designed for scenarios where MLP layers remain unmodiﬁed during ﬁne-tuning;
478   modiﬁcations to these layers, whether through parameter adjustments or architectural changes, re-
479   duce the effectiveness of our detection capabilities in complex model conﬁgurations. Future research
480   will aim to extend this technique to accommodate various ﬁne-tuning strategies, particularly those
481   impacting MLP layers. Additionally, our method struggles with models exhibiting small output
482   norms, which hampers the efﬁciency of reverse engineering intermediate states due to weakened
483   gradient signals during reconstruction. This highlights the need for further reﬁnement of the algo-
484   rithm to ensure robust performance across varying output magnitudes.
