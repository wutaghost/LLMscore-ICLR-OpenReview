009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Datasets often incorporate various functional patterns related to different aspects
012            or regimes, which are typically not equally present throughout the dataset. We pro-
013            pose a novel, general-purpose partitioning algorithm that utilizes competition be-
014            tween models to detect and separate these functional patterns. This competition is
015            induced by multiple models iteratively submitting their predictions for the dataset,
016            with the best prediction for each data point being rewarded with training on that
017            data point. This reward mechanism amplifies each model’s strengths and encour-
018
               ages specialization in different patterns. The specializations can then be translated
               into a partitioning scheme. The amplification of each model’s strengths inverts
019
               the active learning paradigm: while active learning typically focuses the training
020
               of models on their weaknesses to minimize the number of required training data
021            points, our concept reinforces the strengths of each model, thus specializing them.
022            We validate our concept – called active partitioning – with various datasets with
023            clearly distinct functional patterns, such as mechanical stress and strain data in a
024            porous structure. The active partitioning algorithm produces valuable insights into
025            the datasets’ structure, which can serve various further applications. As a demon-
026            stration of one exemplary usage, we set up modular models consisting of multiple
027            expert models, each learning a single partition, and compare their performance
028            on more than twenty popular regression problems with single models learning all
029
               partitions simultaneously. Our results show significant improvements, with up to
               54% loss reduction, confirming our partitioning algorithm’s utility.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Datasets can include multiple sections that adhere to distinct regimes. For instance, in stress-strain
035
      tests of materials, the initial phase exhibits elastic behavior, which is reversible. However, if the
036   material is stretched further, it enters a phase of plastic behavior, resulting in permanent changes.
037   Similarly, self-driving cars face unique challenges when navigating construction zones, which may
038   be specific to certain regions of the parameter space, just as the challenges on highways or country
039   roads. This mixture of different functional patterns within datasets affects how difficult they are
040   for models to learn. Typically, the more diverse the functional patterns within a dataset, the more
041   challenging it is for a model to achieve high accuracy. We present a novel partitioning algorithm
042   aiming to detect such different functional patterns and separate them from one another whenever
043   possible.
044   The development of algorithms for partitioning datasets has a long history. MacQueen introduced
045   the renowned k-means algorithm in 1967 (Macqueen, 1967). However, most approaches define an
046   arbitrary similarity measure for grouping data points. In k-means, spatial proximity is interpreted
047   as the similarity of data points. In contrast, we allow those models that are supposed to learn the
048   dataset to determine which data points they can coherently learn. We believe that for effective dataset
049   partitioning, it is crucial to consider the models themselves. As Jacobs (1990) stated, “the optimal
050
      allocation of experts to subtasks depends not only on the nature of the task but also on that of the
      learner”.
051
052   Our new algorithm is based on the competition between multiple models. These models are itera-
053   tively trained using the data points for which they have made the best predictions, thereby emphasiz-
      ing each model’s strengths and inducing model specialization. The models being trained specifically

      on their strengths, rather than their weaknesses, inverts the traditional active learning strategy, lead-
055   ing us to term this approach active partitioning. The resulting data point distribution to different
056   models is translated into partitions representing different regimes or patterns. Technically, the out-
057   come of this algorithm is the boundaries between these partitions, stored in a support vector machine
058   (SVM).
059
      There are several ways to utilize the resulting partitioning. One notable application is to learn each
060
      partition with a separate model and then combine these expert models into a modular model, allow-
061   ing each expert model to focus on a specific pattern rather than handling all patterns simultaneously.
062   Our experiments demonstrated that such a modular model, based on the results of the partitioning
063   algorithm, significantly outperformed a single model on several exemplary datasets.

066   2     R ELATED WORK
067
068   2.1   PARTITIONING
069
070   Extensive literature surveys on clustering, including partitioning as a special form, can be found in
071   Jain (2010), Du (2010), Aggarwal & Reddy (2013), and Ezugwu et al. (2021). According to Jain
072   (2010), clustering is about identifying groups within datasets such that “the similarities between
073   objects in the same group are high while the similarities between objects in different groups are
074   low.” There are four major categories of clustering algorithms: hierarchical algorithms, partitional
075
      algorithms, density-based algorithms, and heuristic algorithms. This paper focuses on partitional
      algorithms, which dynamically assign data points to clusters in either a hard or soft manner. In hard
076
      partitional clustering, each data point is assigned to exactly one cluster, whereas in soft partitional
077
      clustering, each data point can belong to multiple clusters.
078
079   K-means is the most well-known partitional clustering algorithm. In each iteration, each data point
080   is assigned to the nearest centroid, after which each centroid takes over the main position of all its
081
      data points (Macqueen, 1967). To address weaknesses such as the need to specify the number of
      clusters in advance or the convergence to a local minimum, the algorithm can be run multiple times
082
      with different numbers of clusters and random initializations. A prominent extension of k-means
083
      is fuzzy c-means, which allows for soft partitional clustering (Dunn, 1974). A recent extension is
084   game-based k-means, which increases competition between centroids for samples (Rezaee et al.,
085   2021).
086
087
      In the 1990s, the kohonen network and its specialization, the self-organizing map, were developed.
      Both consist of two neural network layers: an input layer and an output layer, known as the kohonen
088
      layer. The prototypes competing for data points are the neurons in the kohonen layer (Kohonen,
089
      1990). Most approaches that utilize competition for partitioning have entities within one model
090   compete, such as the centroids in k-means or the last-layer neurons in the kohonen network. To
091   our knowledge, Müller et al. are the only exception, aiming to segment temporally ordered data
092   by identifying switching dynamics. They defined an error function and an assumption of the error
093   distribution to trigger competition between neural networks for data points (Müller et al., 1995).
094   Chang et al. extended this framework to use support vector machines instead of neural networks
095   (Chang et al., 2004).
096   A completely different approach for localizing and specializing experts is the iterative splitting of
097   datasets and models, as suggested by Gordon & Crouson (2008). Zhang and Liu combine model
098   splitting and competition in what they call the “one-prototype-take-one-cluster” (OPTOC) paradigm
099   (Zhang & Liu, 2002). New models are created if the accuracy is not yet satisfactory, and these
100   models then compete for data points, which later defines the localization of the experts. Wu et al.
101   adapted this paradigm for clustering gene expression data (Wu et al., 2004).
102   The novelty of our research lies in the development of a flexible partitioning method through the
103   competition of entire models. To the best of our knowledge, no general-purpose partitioning al-
104   gorithm has previously employed competition between entire models. Although the simultaneous
105   training of experts and a gate involves model competition, it has not been used to create a partition-
106   ing (Jacobs et al., 1991). Müller et al. utilized competition for data segmentation, relying on the
107   switching dynamics of temporally ordered data (Müller et al., 1995). In contrast, our approach is
      not constrained by any specific origin or order of data.

      2.2   C OMBINING MODELS
109
110   To demonstrate the effectiveness of our algorithm, we will compare a modular model based on
111   our partitioning approach with a single model. Therefore, we will also review related work on
112   combining multiple models. Comprehensive overviews of model combination techniques can be
113   found in Sharkey (1996), Masoudnia & Ebrahimpour (2014), and Dong et al. (2020). Multiple
114   models can either be fused to an ensemble, meaning that they are trained at least partially on the
115   same data and that their predictions are combined by a weighted average, or they can form a modular
116
      model, meaning that they are trained on different parts of the dataset and for each prediction only
      one responsible model is selected. An approach representing a compromise between these two poles
117
      is the mixture of experts system, which consists of expert models and an additional gating model
118
      mediating the experts, all of which are trained simultaneously. The design of the error function that
119   is minimized is crucial for the extent of localization or specialization of the experts and therefore
120   for the quality and generalization of the overall predictions. Typically, neural networks are selected
121   as models in the mixture of experts system (Jacobs et al., 1991)(Avnimelech & Intrator, 1999). An
122   obvious advantage of a mixture of experts system compared to a single model is the significantly
123   increased capacity to learn large or complex datasets. Shazeer et al. recently combined thousands
124   of neural networks into a sparsely-gated mixture of experts system (Shazeer et al., 2017). Since the
125   gating network only activates a few expert networks per sample, they achieved a dramatic increase in
126   model capacity while even decreasing the computational cost compared to state-of-the-art models.

      A significant asset of our partitioning algorithm is its ability to extend to a pattern-adaptive model
271   type, architecture, and hyperparameter search without incurring additional costs. So far, competing
272   models have been considered similar in terms of their type, architecture, and hyperparameter set-

275   Consequently, the algorithm’s output can not only be a partitioning but also an optimal configura-
276   tion of model type, architecture, and hyperparameters for each partition.

      We designed two-dimensional, section-wise defined functions to serve as test datasets for validating

      the effectiveness of our approach and its implementation. The anomaly-crest function is illustrated in
345   Figure 2a, and the wave-climb function is depicted in Figure 6a. Due to their section-wise definition,

419   5    D ISCUSSION
420
421   As introduced in Section 3, the partitioning algorithm is based on the competition between mul-
422   tiple models: iteratively, each model is trained on the data points for which it provided the best
423   predictions. This approach inverts typical active learning strategies, which usually focus on train-
424   ing models on their weaknesses to enhance generalization. Instead, we emphasize the strengths of
425   each model to induce specialization. We consider our concept to be the first in a new category of
426   partitioning approaches: active partitioning approaches, which invert the traditional active learning
427   paradigm.
428   The application of our active partitioning algorithm to the anomaly-crest function (see Fig. 2)
429   demonstrates that the competition between multiple models is generally effective for developing
430   specialized experts and separating different functional patterns. The primary value of this partition-
431   ing lies in its ability to detect these distinct patterns and provide insights into the dataset’s structure.
      For the anomaly-crest function, the four identified sections clearly differ in their functional charac-


                                                           8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      teristics (see Fig. 2). In the case of the wave-climb function, the algorithm successfully separates
433   the two sinusoidal sections with different frequencies and amplitudes, as well as a final u-shaped
434   section, which seems reasonable (see Fig. 6a). For the porous structure’s stress-strain dataset, it
435   is noteworthy that the first hook is identified as a distinct pattern. Subsequently, all sections with
436   concave curvature are captured by the green model, while all sections with convex curvature are
437   captured by the orange model. This partitioning was surprising, but it appears that the models find it
438   easier to learn either concave or convex curvatures exclusively (see Fig. 6b). The models themselves
439   detecting which functional patterns can be learned well coherently was exactly what we were aiming
440   for.
441   There are several ways to utilize the partitioning, and we found it important to also illustrate a
442   path that leads to measurable improvements by leveraging our partitioning results. In Section 4.1,
443   we introduced modular models that combine multiple experts. Our hypothesis is that for datasets
444   with separable patterns, it may be advantageous to have multiple experts, each focusing on a single
445   pattern, rather than a single model handling all patterns. As expected, the modular model was not
446   superior for all datasets. We believe this is because if a dataset exhibits only one coherent pattern or
447
      if multiple patterns highly overlap, it is more beneficial for a single model to access all data points
      rather than splitting them. However, among the 25 datasets we tested, we identified six datasets that
448
      could be learned more precisely with the modular model utilizing our partitioning results. For the
449
      porous structure’s stress-strain dataset and the energy efficiency dataset, the modular model even
450   achieved a loss reduction of more than 50% (see Fig. 7).
451
452   In Section A.1, we describe a detailed analysis of the factors contributing to the performance of
453
      the modular model. Our findings reveal a correlation between the number of patterns identified by
      the partitioning algorithm and the modular model’s performance: the more distinct patterns in the
454
      dataset, the better the modular model performs relative to the single model. This aligns with our
455
      expectation that not all datasets are suitable for our approach. The partitioning algorithm should
456   primarily be applied to datasets that are expected to exhibit predominant patterns with minimal
457   overlap. The clearer the patterns, the more effective the modular model is expected to be.
458
459
      Additionally, we examined the impact of our pattern-adaptive hyperparameter search, which opti-
      mizes the hyperparameter settings for each pattern. We discovered that tailoring the learning rates
460
      to each partition enhances the modular model’s performance. However, our results indicate that ad-
461
      justing the numbers of layers and neurons per layer for each pattern does not provide any significant
462   advantage.
463
464   Finally, we aimed to verify that the partitioning algorithm identifies substantial patterns rather than
465
      merely separating small and challenging snippets. Our results confirm that the more homogeneous
      the partition proportions, the more successful the modular model tends to be.
466
467   There are numerous potential applications, many of which we may not have yet considered. One
468   application we plan to explore is using the partitioning algorithm for active learning. In the context
469   of expensive data points, the following data collection loop could be advantageous: first, collect a
470   batch of data points; then, apply the partitioning algorithm; and finally, train each partition with
471
      a separate model, akin to the modular model approach. Instead of immediately combining their
      predictions, we could assess each expert’s performance and adjust the collection of new data points
472
      accordingly. Partitions that are more challenging to learn should receive more data points, while
473
      easier partitions should receive fewer. This approach could lead to a more efficient use of the data
474   point budget. The process can be repeated iteratively. For instance, with a budget of 500 data points,
475   we could run this process 10 times, each time distributing 50 data points according to the difficulty
476   of the experts in learning their partitions in the last iteration.

480
481   In this paper, we introduced a novel active partitioning algorithm. To the best of our knowledge, this
482   algorithm is unique in its use of competition between models to generate a general-purpose parti-
483   tioning scheme, without constraints on the dataset’s origin or order. The partitioning is achieved by
484   having multiple models iteratively submit their predictions for all points in the dataset and being re-
485   warded for the best predictions with training on the corresponding data points. This process induces
      specialization in the models, which is then translated into a partitioning. Focusing the training on


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      each model’s strengths practically inverts the active learning paradigm of focusing the training on a
487   model’s weaknesses, leading us to call our concept an active partitioning algorithm.
488
489   We demonstrated that our algorithm is both widely applicable and useful. Its wide applicability
490
      was shown by valuable results across datasets of varying dimensionalities, sparsities, and contexts –
      from student education to engineering stress-strain tests. The utility of our algorithm was illustrated
491
      in two primary ways: first, the partitioning inherently provides insights into the dataset’s structure.
492
      For instance, three distinct patterns were detected in the porous structure’s stress-strain dataset: an
493   initial hook, convex, and concave parts. Second, certain datasets can be learned more accurately with
494   a modular model based on the active partitioning algorithm than with a single model. If a model’s
495   accuracy in learning a dataset is unsatisfactory and the dataset is likely structured along predominant
496   patterns with little overlap, we recommend applying our pipeline of the active partitioning algorithm
497   and modular model. Particularly in the context of expensive data points, improving the model on
498   this path without adding more data points can be financially beneficial. In the future, we will explore
499   a third application: adapting data collection strategies based on the active partitioning algorithm.

690   For each dataset, we computed the mean test loss of the single model over the ten test runs. We then
691   compared the test losses of the modular model against this benchmark. For example, if the single
692   models achieved an average loss of 100 and the modular model achieved a loss of 80, we recorded a
693   performance value of 20%. Conversely, if the modular model achieved a loss of 120, we recorded a
694   performance value of -20%. These performance measures were plotted against potentially influential
695   parameters: the number of experts, the variance in the experts’ learning rates, the variance in the
696   experts’ parameter counts, and the variance in the partition proportions.
697   Firstly, we observed that the performance of the modular model compared to the single model im-
698   proves with an increasing number of experts (see Fig. 8a). Since the number of experts in the
699   modular model corresponds to the number of patterns the partitioning algorithm has separated, this
700   insight is more about the datasets that work well with this approach than about the modular model
701   itself. The more separate patterns are found within one dataset, the better the modular model can
      be expected to work. Given our initial expectation that not all datasets would contain separable

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


702
      patterns, this finding is not surprising. The more clearly a dataset is structured around separable
703   patterns, the more effective our approach appears to be.
704
705   The modular model allows for the adjustment of hyperparameters locally for each expert, unlike
706
      the single model with a global uniform hyperparameter setting. In our experiments, we varied only
      the learning rate, the number of layers, and the number of neurons per layer. For this analysis, we
707
      combined the number of layers and the number of neurons per layer into a single metric: the number
708
      of trainable parameters. We evaluated the impact of locally adapting the hyperparameter settings to
709   each pattern. The more the hyperparameter settings are tailored to each pattern, and the more they
710   differ from a constant setting for the entire dataset, the greater their variance among all experts in a
711   single run. Consequently, we plotted the performance of the modular model compared to the single
712   model versus the variance in experts’ learning rates (see Fig. 8b) and the variance in experts’ train-
713   able parameters (see Fig. 8c). We observed a moderate correlation between the modular model’s
714   performance and the adaptation of learning rates, but no correlation with the adaptation of trainable
715   parameters. Notably, also with small variances in learning rates, modular models outperformed sin-
716   gle models. We conclude that locally adapting learning rates to each pattern is moderately beneficial,
717
      whereas adjusting the number of layers and neurons per layer does not appear to have a significant
      impact.
718
719   Finally, we plotted the performance of the modular model compared to the single model against the
720   variance in partition proportions for each run (see Fig. 8d). Our aim was to verify that the algorithm
721   identifies significant patterns rather than just isolating small, difficult segments. Our findings confirm
722   this hypothesis, indicating that the more uniform the partition proportions, the more effective the
723
      modular model becomes.
