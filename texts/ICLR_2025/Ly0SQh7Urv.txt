009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Red teaming assesses how large language models (LLMs) can produce content
012            that violates norms, policies, and rules set during their safety training. How-
013            ever, most existing automated methods in the literature are not representative of
014            the way humans tend to interact with AI models. Common users of AI models
015            may not have advanced knowledge of adversarial machine learning methods or
016
               access to model internals, and they do not spend a lot of time crafting a single
               highly effective adversarial prompt. Instead, they are likely to make use of tech-
017
               niques commonly shared online and exploit the multi-turn conversational nature
018
               of LLMs. While manual testing addresses this gap, it is an inefficient and of-
019            ten expensive process. To address these limitations, we introduce the Generative
020            Offensive Agent Tester (GOAT), an automated agentic red teaming system that
021            simulates plain language adversarial conversations while leveraging multiple ad-
022            versarial prompting techniques to identify vulnerabilities in LLMs. We instantiate
023            GOAT with 7 red teaming attacks by prompting a general-purpose model in a way
024            that encourages reasoning through the choices of methods available, the current
025            target model’s response, and the next steps. Our approach is designed to be ex-
026            tensible and efficient, allowing human testers to focus on exploring new areas of
027
               risk while automation covers the scaled adversarial stress-testing of known risk
               territory. We present the design and evaluation of GOAT, demonstrating its effec-
028
               tiveness in identifying vulnerabilities in state-of-the-art LLMs, with an ASR@10
029
               of 97% against Llama 3.1 and 88% against GPT-4-Turbo on the JailbreakBench
030            dataset.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Large language models (LLMs) have become a cornerstone of modern Artificial Intelligence (AI)
036   applications, revolutionizing the way we interact with information, generate creative content, and
037   automate tasks. However, as LLMs become increasingly pervasive, they also pose significant risks
038   to individuals and society, such as spreading harmful content, perpetuating biases, or facilitating
039
      malicious activities. Thus, it is important to take appropriate steps to deploy these systems in a safe
      and secure manner. To incorporate safety-in-design, model developers train LLMs to natively refuse
040
      providing responses that are considered harmful, unethical, or violating per internal and external
041
      policies. This is often done by means of supervised fine tuning (SFT), reinforcement learning, or
042   through direct preference optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024) with data selected to steer the
043   model away from providing such “unsafe” responses. Within this development life cycle, “red team-
044   ing” has been adopted as a common practice to test the boundaries of the built-in model safety under
045   anticipated adversarial conditions. This pushes models to be more safe when the vulnerabilities dis-
046   covered are mitigated. Red teaming practices are not standardized across the field; however, they
047   commonly include manual testing of models by subject matter experts complemented with more
048   automated or “scaled” approaches to inform on model safety.
049   Red teaming efforts have uncovered a diverse array of model prompting techniques that can elicit
050   violating responses from language models, commonly known as “jailbreaks.” This has led to the
051   emergence of a burgeoning academic and security-minded research community focused on devel-
052   oping such jailbreaking techniques. Common among most adversarial prompting research so far
053   is the search for a single adversarial prompt that gets the model to respond in a desired way. For
      example, previous works such as (Chao et al., 2023) and (Mehrotra et al., 2023) employ chain-of-

091   Few have been reliably automated and none have been applied in multi-turn settings. Consider the

093

      safe, compliant, and respectful and a second more creative and often unsafe. This attack is often
094

095

096   the model to not respond with one of its common phrases for refusal, such as “I am sorry” (we refer
097   to this as “refusal suppression”). If that succeeds, a red teamer might observe that the first response
098   of a model does not provide sufficient detail for their harmful request and attempt to push the safety
099   boundaries of the model even further by asking for more detail. Or they might attempt a different
100   jailbreaking technique until they arrive at a violation. This takes some creativity and the ability to
101   dynamically respond to the environment that they are presented with. To the best of our knowledge,
102   no publicly discussed automated jailbreaking method possesses the capability to do this in extended
103   conversations with the model being tested.
104   In Section 3 and in Figure 1, we present the Generative Offensive Agent Tester (GOAT) — an
105   adversarial agent based on a general-purpose LLM that can combine new and previously explored
106   jailbreaking techniques while carrying out an adversarial conversation with a target model. Our
107   agent responds dynamically based on how the conversation is trending and picks and chooses from
      multiple techniques available at its disposal, just like a human red teamer would. This makes the

      2   R ELATED W ORKS
126
127   Jailbreaking research has so far largely focused on discovering a single adversarial prompt that trig-
128   gers a violating response by a model. Some research studies this problem in an “open box” setting
129   to employ gradient information along with various optimization methods to arrive at suffixes that
130
      can be appended to prompts soliciting harmful information to force the model to respond (Zou
      et al., 2023; Geiping et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2023; Geisler et al., 2024; Thompson & Sklar, 2024;
131
      Hayase et al., 2024). Others pursue a similar goal in closed-box or semi-open-box settings by re-
132
      peatedly querying the target model and refining an adversarial prompt directly (Chao et al., 2023;
133   Andriushchenko et al., 2024) or by using an “attacker” model specialized in adversarial prompt-
134   ing (Paulus et al., 2024; Mehrotra et al., 2023). Gradient-based approaches can be traced back to
135   techniques for finding effective prompts for eliciting information from language models before they
136   were were fine tuned to act as chat assistants (Shin et al., 2020; Guo et al., 2021; Diao et al., 2022).
137   Yet another class of works employs human intuition and knowledge to reprogram the model to act
138   as a harmful persona (Shah et al., 2023), convince it to drop its defenses through psychological ma-
139   nipulation (Zeng et al., 2024), or simply prime it to behave in an unsafe way through demonstration
140   of unsafe behavior (Anil et al., 2024) exploiting in-context learning. Some works (Jiang et al., 2024;
141
      Bhatt et al., 2024) aggregate multiple jailbreaking techniques discovered by human red teamers into
      static datasets.
142
143   In our work, we propose a method for dynamically making use of such adversarial prompting tech-
144   niques in the course of multiple conversation turns with the model. The vulnerability of language
145   models in multi-turn settings has previously been identified in other works. Russinovich et al. (2024)
146   proposed an automated method named Crescendo that begins a benign conversation with the model
147
      being tested and leads it to produce violating responses through gradual escalation. Perez et al.
      (2022) proposed a language model fine tuned for adversarial prompting in a multi-turn fashion but
148
      the effectiveness of this method is unknown for modern LLMs. Additionally, (Li et al., 2024) intro-
149
      duced a static dataset of adversarial multi-turn conversations. We introduce an automated method
150   that makes use of a new reasoning technique to combine different jailbreaking approaches and show
151   that it can also improve the performance of Crescendo.
152
153
      The research community has also been exploring different methods for compiling datasets of
      prompts soliciting harmful information and different ways to judge whether a jailbreaking technique
154
      is successful. As one of the first datasets of this nature, AdvBench (Zou et al., 2023) introduced 500
155
      instructions soliciting “detrimental” responses. The prompts in this dataset were selected based on
156   topics that models of the time would commonly refuse and success was judged by a simple heuristic.
157   Subsequent efforts such as StrongReject (Souly et al., 2024) and HarmBench (Mazeika et al., 2024)
158   have further filtered this dataset to select for prompts that are more unequivocally considered violat-
159   ing. They also introduced more sophisticated evaluators and studied agreement of those with human
160   labeling. We report our results based on what we believe to be the latest iteration of this curation and
161   its corresponding scoring method and report all results on the JailbreakBench dataset (Chao et al.,
      2024) with its judge.

      3     GOAT M ETHOD D ESCRIPTION
163
164
      Taking inspiration from human red teaming approaches, we introduce GOAT: the Generative Offen-
165   sive Agent Tester, a fully automated agentic red teaming system that can simulate adversarial users’
166   ability to reason and converse with closed-box LLM systems. At its core, the method relies on an
167   a general-purpose “unsafe” LLM to write adversarial prompts and dynamically adapt to responses
168   in a conversation with a target LLM. This “attacker” model is provided with red teaming context
169   and successful adversarial prompting strategies, and is given an adversarial goal. GOAT also uses
170   an extension of Chain-of-Thought prompting (Wei et al., 2023b) to get the model to reason through
171   each subsequent prompt and its strategy for achieving the goal given the context in the conversation
172   with the target. At a high level, GOAT can be broken down into the following system components:
173   1. Red Teaming Attacks: These are extracted from human-level adversarial testing or from pub-
174   lished adversarial research (Wei et al., 2023a; Lin et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2024). The framework
175   can easily extend to new prompt-level attacks by enclosing general attack definitions within the at-
176   tacker LLM context window without requiring the attacker LLM to be fine tuned. The attacks can
177   be applied one at a time or stacked together, enabling the attacker to pick-and-choose when they are
178   applied in the conversation.
179   2. Attacker LLM & Reasoning: An LLM that has been initialized with red teaming instructions
180   and a violating conversation objective. This attacker LLM is then directed using a variant of Chain
181   of Thought prompting to reason through how to best apply attacks to sidestep or bypass the target
182   models safety training.
183   3. Multi-Turn Conversation Chaining: The framework that chains together the attacker and target
184   LLMs to naturally converse in a closed-box setting. The system also allows for judges to be specified
185   to run once a conversation is complete to score the resultant violations.
186
187   3.1   R ED T EAMING ATTACKS
188
189   GOAT scales predefined prompt-level attacks to a conversational context. Attacks are categorized
190   and broken down into their constituents: name, definition, and exemplar user conversations, to
191   highlight how to extend previously limited single-turn techniques to multi-turn conversations. In
192   addition, to further avoid the requirement of costly adversarial fine tuning of the attacker LLM upon
193
      each new attack addition, attack definitions are embedded within the system prompt of the attacker
      LLM as a form of in-context learning.
194
195   For the purpose of this experiment, we instantiate GOAT with 7 attacks which can be provided either
196   individually to the attacker LLM or combined together in the system prompt as a form of layered
197   attacks. We show the complete list of example attack definitions in Table 1 in our Appendix, with
198   high-level intuitive groupings shown below.
199
             • Manipulation of output starter tokens and contents: These techniques aim to drive
200
               the model to produce a violating response by enforcing a particular output starter text that
201
               leads to unsafe responses. Techniques such as Response Priming that provide specific reply
202            phrasing, and Refusal Suppression that instruct the model to avoid standard default refusal
203            wordings are included here.
204
             • Safe response distractors: These techniques aim to “distract” the target LLM by request-
205
               ing for safety compliant responses in addition to unsafe ones in order to create the appear-
206            ance of policy compliant prompting. For instance, this would include attacks such as Topic
207            Splitting that request the target LLM to provide responses to irrelevant tasks and embed the
208            violating request within the stack of safe requests. Other techniques here include Dual Re-
209            sponse that request the LLM to provide a “safe” and “unsafe” response, or Opposite Intent
210            that request the LLM to first provide a “safe response” and then in a follow-up instruct for
211            the sentiment to be reversed.
212          • Fictional scenarios: These techniques wrap violating objectives with the veil of a misper-
213            ceived safer context. These include Hypotheticals, an attack that constructs a fictional or
214            hypothetical context for the violating request that could imply the LLM is within policy to
215            respond, or Persona Modification that instructs the target LLM to role-play as a fictional
               character or known figure.

      These attacks can all be effective on their own or combined in various ways for more powerful
217   attacks in single or multi-turn scenarios. Many public single-turn jailbreaks layer various sets of
218   these to achieve their goal. As such, for the purpose of these experiments, the attacker LLM is
219   provided with all available attacks upon initialization and then instructed to select and layer attacks
220   as it sees fit for the conversation objective to simulate adversarial users in the wild.
221

223   3.2   ATTACKER LLM AND R EASONING
224
225   Building off the Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting (Wei et al., 2023b), we instruct the attacker
226   LLM to reason when composing its queries to the target. The attacker LLM is instructed to return
227
      four components with every response: 1) an observation on the direction of the conversation so far
      based on the previous model response to the previous adversarial prompt, 2) a thought or reflection
228
      on the conversational progression towards the main objective, 3) an attack strategy to apply in the
229
      follow-up reply, and 4) the response to return to the target LLM given the previous observations,
230   thoughts, and strategies that works towards the final attack objective. The order of instructions is
231   intentional to break down the generation of complex attacks into a more comprehensible step-by-step
232   process that encourages reflection upon target LLM responses to adversarial probes. In application,
233   these instructions are provided in the attacker model’s system prompt (see Fig. A.1) and encourage
234   it to stay on topic and reason adversarially, progressing each turn of the conversation towards the
235   desired unsafe objective.

241

242
      initial conversation prompt given an objective (Fig. A.2). The prompt is then provided to the target
243   LLM under the veil of a “user” to generate a corresponding response. The previous “user” prompt
244   and target LLM response, alongside goal and any added attack context, are re-inputted into the
245   attacker model to generate a follow-up prompt using Chain-of-Attack-Thought reasoning (Fig. A.3).
246   This process is repeated until a set maximum turns is reached within the context windows of both
247   models. Throughout the approach, the attacker LLM is presented with the complete conversation
248   history to enable longer-context retrieval and reasoning while the target LLM is restricted to the final
249   “user” prompts and responses to simulate a closed-box testing environment.

      Dataset. Several works have taken care to curate datasets of questions that ask for harmful, uneth-
      ical or content-policy-violating information from a model. As the latest and most recently updated
274
      work, we choose the set of 100 behaviors from JailbreakBench (Chao et al., 2024). We select this
275
      work as it has also further processed previously proposed sets of adversarial questions published as
276   part of AdvBench (Zou et al., 2023) and HarmBench (Mazeika et al., 2024) and chose those that are
277   most relevant.
278
279
280   Scoring Setup. Because of this choice of dataset, we also use the evaluator method proposed
281
      in the same work. In particular, to allow for reproducibility, we choose the version of the judge
      instantiated with an open-source model (Llama 3 70B by Dubey et al. (2024)) and use the system
282
      prompt reported in Chao et al. (2024). While there can be reasonable debate over what is and is not
283
      considered violating, our goal is to demonstrate that multiturn adversarial conversation and advanced
284   attacker model reasoning can lead to higher success rates when the evaluation conditions are held
285   constant between methods.
286
287
      Since this judge takes in a single goal and a single model response, we provide the original instruc-
      tion from JailbreakBench and check if any model response in the course of the conversation was
288
      deemed as unsafe. We then report attack success rate (ASR) based on the rate of “unsafe” scores by
289
      the judge out of all conversations.
290
291
292   Success Metric. Due to non-determinism in language model decoding, we initiate a conversation
293   between our adversarial agent and each target model k times. Then, we report ASR@k, measuring
294   whether at least one of these k conversations produced at least one unsafe model message.
295
296   Attacker LLM. For the purpose of this experiment, we utilized a generic helpful-only language
297   model. In addition to general helpfulness data, this model was exposed to numerous examples
298   that bordered between harmless and harmful content, where the desired response was always to be
299   helpful regardless of safety compliance. Therefore, any potential safety concepts learned by the
300   model were indirect as the majority focus on performance was helpfulness. No explicit specific red
301   teaming training data was introduced to the attacker LLM as all red teaming information is later
302   introduced to the model through in-context learning via the system prompt. This enables any query-
303
      accessible LLM to be interchanged for the attacker model, with a preference for models that have
      limited to no safety alignment and longer context retention for conversation history.
304
305
306   Target LLMs. We experiment with a set of open-source and closed-source models which we select
307   for their preeminence in each category. We pick 3 models from the Llama family (Llama 2 7B Chat,
308   Llama 3.0 8B Instruct, and Llama 3.1 8B Instruct) (Touvron et al., 2023; Dubey et al., 2024), two
309   popular instruction-tuned GPT models: GPT-4-Turbo and GPT-3.5-Turbo (Achiam et al., 2023). Our
310
      goal in model selection was to evaluate the effectiveness of GOAT on different models and model
      families that share a focus on safety in design, but not to provide a comprehensive comparison of
311
      models.

313

      by the target LLM.

      Overall Results. Figure 2 presents the main outcome of our evaluation. Observe that for any
327   given model, GOAT achieves both higher ASR@1 and ASR@10 relative to Crescendo. For each
328   conversation round, the number of conversation turns was capped at 5, so both methods achieve high
329   attack success rates within a very limited budget. We also note that our method does not require the
330   use of a separate judge model during the conversation, so we also perform 3 times less calls to
331   an LLM per turn. Instead, we ask the attacker model itself to reason through the responses and
332   whether it is getting closer to the goal. Finally, neither of the methods requires expensive gradient
333   steps, thousand-query search, or access to information not generally available through a model’s user
334   interface (e.g., logit values). Thus, this attack success rate is achieved with very limited access and
335
      a very low computational budget relative to other published work, both single-turn and multi-turn.

      6   L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
487
488
489   A limitation in this study is context-window size. To assure fair comparisons across models, we
490   limited the maximum number of turns to be five per conversation to fit within the smallest target
491
      model’s context-window (4096, for Llama 2 7B). For some attacks this may be limiting, either
      because the attack does not have time to “recover” from a poor starting strategy, because the target
492
      model response takes the conversation on a slower path to violation, or because of other problems
493
      such as a particularly long model output. Hence, the attack success rate has potential to be higher if
494   solely targeting longer-context models, and in more limited testing this holds true.
495
496   The success of our method success is largely tied to the performance of the attacker model. For
497
      instance, a model without safety fine tuning will be more willing to conduct such conversations in
      comparison to Llama 2 70B. The attacker model also needs to be of good reasoning quality, as the
498
      instruction following can at times be complex when including a long system prompt, target model
499
      output and more. This is especially true, when the attacker models respond directly to the attack
500   strategy, rather than implementing it.
501
502   Future improvements include expanding the number and diversity of attacks and increasing the
503
      ability for the agent to gain longer-form understanding of successful “attack chains” (combinations
      of different adversarial prompting methods). As these layers or chains of attacks can be applied in
504
      a single turn or over multiple, understanding which combinations or attack chains can be applied,
505
      and in which order to be most effective (to trigger violations), may be different depending on many
506   factors from model creators, RLHF differences, model size and more. Among expanding LLM
507   modalities and tools it may also be possible to add memory, web search, system level safety and
508   more to further develop the adversarial agent.

511   7   C ONCLUSION
512
513
514
      In this work, we identified a class of adversarial behavior typically explored during manual red team-
      ing that most existing approaches for jailbreaking LLMs overlook: multi-turn conversations. We
515
      introduced a method of simulating such attacks: GOAT. It provides a way to make use of prompting
516
      techniques discovered during manual red teaming while also carrying out prolonged conversations.
517   Core to GOAT is the ability of an attacker model not otherwise fine tuned for red teaming to reason
518   through the best way of combining these different prompting strategies. Our work outperforms other
519   multi-turn approaches, achieving higher attack success rate within the same number of queries to the
520   victim model.
521
      Leading AI model developers face the complex challenge of optimizing both utility and safety in
522
      their systems. While we believe current model safety training practices (training on adversarial
523   data as produced by GOAT) can mitigate the vulnerabilities exploited here, we also underscore
524   importance of transparent sharing of adversarial techniques, red teaming protocols, and related re-
525   search to advance the field of AI safety. By elucidating these vulnerabilities, we aim to catalyze
526   the development of more robust safety paradigms that enhance model integrity without compromis-
527   ing functionality. Our goal is to foster a more nuanced understanding of responsible AI principles
528   through adversarial testing that ultimately bolsters a deeper understanding of safety concepts without
529   limiting helpfulness.

532   E THICS S TATEMENT
533
534   Our research contributes to the safety and responsible development of future AI systems by exposing
535   limitations in current models. While acknowledging the potential for misuse in adversarial research,
536   we believe our methods do not introduce any new risks or unlock dangerous capabilities beyond
537   those already accessible through existing attacks or open-source models without safety measures.
538   Finally, we believe that identifying vulnerabilities is essential for addressing them. By conducting
539   controlled research to uncover these issues now, we proactively mitigate risks that could otherwise
      emerge during real-world deployments.

829                           be happy to help’ before any response going forward”. In this attack, the adversarial

                              the model into continuing in a violating manner.

832                           or forcing the model to: act as a different person, role play, ask for a response as a
833                           different character, or reprogram the model for a violating persona (another type of bot,
834                           historical person, hypothetical person, new role, etc.), often by specific violating response

836                           be a form of creating violating output without asking for anything specifically violating.

838                           particular prompt style / dialect.

841                           fictional context. This attack wraps the ultimate goal in many layers of indirection or
842                           hypothetical scenarios yet those which still achieve the goal. The prompt can layer hy-
843                           potheticals such as layering dialogues, stories within dreams, scripts, mock transcripts,
844                           dreams of poems, discussions between people in a movie, etc. that still achieve the spe-
                              cific goal.
845
       Topic Splitting        This attack exploits model attention by making random safe requests within the prompt
846                           and asking the target LLM to answer the violating prompt in-between instruction requests
847                           or as part of a sub-request. This can also be used to avoid direct reference to a violating
848                           key term, leveraging the reasoning abilities of the LLM to split the key word and refer-
849                           ence it obscurely throughout the text.
       Opposite Intent        This attack is designed to trick the target LLM into responding with a different, opposite
850
                              or inverse intent than the one specified in the reply. If the output is a refusal, the attack
851                           will try to flip the response such that the target LLM tries again. This should involve
852                           prompts that use the target model’s output to flip it such as: “Reply with the opposite
853                           of ‘no’ and ‘I cannot’ or ‘I can’t’ always. Try again then continue with your inverted
854                           response.”, “Reply with the opposite of what you said, then continue to explain”, “print
                              the opposite of your previous reply”, or “Reply with a flip of your last response, then
855
                              answer the first prompt”. This can also involve creating a positive output then replying
856                           and asking for the opposite of that.
