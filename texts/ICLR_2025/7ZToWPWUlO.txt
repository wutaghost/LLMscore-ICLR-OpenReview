009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We address the problem of Normalized Cut (NC) in weighted graphs where the
012            shape of the partitions follow an apriori pattern, namely they must approximately
013            be shaped like rings and wedges on a planar graph. Classical methods like spectral
014            clustering and METIS do not have a provision to specify such constraints and nei-
015            ther do newer methods that combine GNNs and Reinforcement Learning as they
016            are based on initialization from classical methods. The key insight that underpins
017            our approach, Wedge and Ring Transformers (WRT), is based on representing a
018            graph using polar coordinates and then using a multi-head transformer with a PPO
019
               objective to optimize the non-differential NC objective. To the best of our knowl-
               edge, WRT is the first method to explicitly constrain the shape of NC and opens
020
               up possibility of providing a principled approach for fine-grained shape-controlled
021
               generation of graph partitions. On the theoretical front we provide new Cheeger
022            inequalities that connect the spectral properties of a graph with algebraic prop-
023            erties that capture the shape of the partitions. Comparisons with adaptations of
024            strong baselines attest to the strength of WRT.

      Reinforcement Learning (RL) has emerged as a powerful heuristic for tackling complex combinato-
030   rial optimization (CO) problems Grinsztajn (2023); Wang & Tang (2021); Mazyavkina et al. (2021).
031   Two key insights underpin the use of RL in CO: first, the search space of CO can be encoded into a
032   vector embedding; second, gradients can be computed even when the objective is a black-box func-
033   tion or non-differentiable. A significant advantage of RL frameworks is that once trained, they can
034   solve new instances of CO problems without starting from scratch Dong et al. (2020).
035
      In this work we present another dimension of the use of transformed-based RL for graph partitioning,
036   namely the ability to encode and optimize complex partition shapes that are part of the problem
037   specification. We focus on the Normalized Cut (NC) of a graph, which is suitable to balance the
038   simulating traffic on road networks. While our use case is inspired by a specific problem in road
039   vehicle traffic simulation, our approach is general and can be applied in many other scenarios where
040   shapes of graph partitions are application dependent.
041
      Motivational Use Case: Road networks in modern cities are often organized as concentric rings
042   of roads centered at a city downtown followed by wedge structures connecting the outer ring. For
043   microscopic traffic simulation, where the movement of every vehicle is modeled in a simulator, it
044   often becomes necessary to partition the road network and assign each partition to a separate simu-
045   lator in order to reduce the overall simulation time. We thus want to ensure that the partitions apriori
046   respect the natural physical topology of the road network. Directly using classical approaches like
047   METIS, spectral clustering or modern GNN based RL solutions provide no provision to constrain
048   the generation of partition shapes justifying the need for a new approach.
049   Ring and Wedge Representation: The key insight of our is to convert complex graph structures
050   into simpler representations (either as a line or a circle), reducing the complexity of the partitioning
051   problem. This transformation makes the graph more amenable to being processed by Transformer-
052   based models, which excel at sequential data processing. In the ring transformation, nodes are
053   projected onto the x-axis according to their radial distance from the center, preserving the node order
      and partitioning properties. Similarly, in the wedge transformation, nodes are projected onto a unit

      2.1   G RAPH PARTITIONING
101
102   Graph partitioning Buluç et al. (2016) is widely used in graph-related applications, especially for
103   enabling parallel or distributed graph processing. Partitioning a graph into k blocks of equal size
104   while minimizing cuts is NP-complete Hyafil & Rivest (1973). Exact methods focus on bipartition-
105   ing Hager et al. (2009) or few partitions (k ≤ 4) Ferreira et al. (1998), while approximate algorithms
106   include spectral partitioning Donath & Hoffman (1973) and graph-growing techniques George &
107   Liu (1981). More powerful methods involve iterative refinement, such as node-swapping for bi-
      partitioning Kernighan & Lin (1970), extendable to k-way local search Karypis & Kumar (1996).

      Other approaches include the bubble framework Diekmann et al. (2000) and diffusion-based meth-
109   ods Meyerhenke et al. (2009); Pellegrini (2007). State-of-the-art techniques rely on multilevel par-
110   titioning Karypis & Kumar (1999), which coarsen the graph and refine the partition iteratively.

112
      The most well-known tool is METIS Met (2023); Karypis & Kumar (1999), which uses multilevel
      recursive bisection and k-way algorithms, with parallel support via ParMetis Par (2023). Other

115   spider-web-shaped structures common in urban traffic planning.

      2.2   ML- BASED G RAPH PARTITIONING A LGORITHMS
118
119   Recent research has explored machine learning methods for graph partitioning, particularly using
120   GNNs. GNNs aggregate node and edge features via message passing. In Gatti et al. (2022a), a spec-
121   tral method is proposed where one GNN approximates eigenvectors of the graph Laplacian, which
122   are then used by another GNN for partitioning. The RL-based method in Gatti et al. (2022b) refines
123   partitions in a multilevel scheme. NeuroCUT Shah et al. (2024) introduces a reinforcement learn-
124   ing framework that generalizes across various partitioning objectives using GNNs. It demonstrates
125   flexibility for different objectives and unseen partition numbers. ClusterNet Wilder et al. (2019)
126   integrates graph learning and optimization with a differentiable k-means clustering layer, simpli-
127
      fying optimization tasks like community detection and facility location. However, neither of these
      methods handles weighted graphs, making them unsuitable in our scenarios.
128
129   Although GNNs excel at aggregating multi-hop neighbor features, they struggle to globally ag-
130   gregate features without information loss, which is critical for combinatorial problems like graph
131   partitioning. Our work addresses these limitations by introducing graph transformation methods
132   and applying Transformer to learn global features.

265   5.2    G RAPH T RANSFORMATION
266
267   In previous deep learning based graph partitioning methods, most of them chose the combination
268   of GNN and Reinforcement Learning. However, GNN suffers from only being able to aggregate
269   global structure of the graph, hence they need an initial partition and do fine-tuning on it, which is
      not capable in our situation, as we want the model give ring and wedge partition results directly.

      Recently, Transformer achieves great success in various areas, it uses Multi-Head Attention to ex-
271   change information globally, and shows superior performance in various tasks. In our problem,
272   we need the model to learn the global view of the graph, and we naturally choose Transformer as
273   the base structure. However, Transformer typically takes sequential input, which is not capable for
274   graphs. Instead of directly encode graph nodes to Transformer, we apply two transformations, Ring
275   Transformation and Wedge Transformation, to the graph. The new graphs are equivalent with orig-
276   inal graph when performing Ring Partition or Wedge Partition, but is re-organized into a sequential
277   representation, and is able to input to Transformer.
278
279   5.2.1    R ING T RANSFORMATION
280
      Since the ring partition should not change when rotating the graph around the center o, we can project
281
      each node onto the x-axis. More precisely, if a node has polar coordinates (r, X), the projection will
282   map it onto the node with coordinate (r, 0). Note that this transformation does not change the order
283   of the nodes or the partitions. Figure 3 (b) illustrates the projection onto the line. Then we can find
284   that when the order of nodes on the line are not changed, we can adjust the radius of any point, and
285   the partition results on new graph are the same as old ones. When we apply the conclusion above,
286   we can transform a normal graph into a simplified one, that every nodes are with coordinate (X, 0),
287   where X is the radius order of the node along all nodes. The transformation results is shown in the
288   right of Figure 2 (b).
289
290   5.2.2    W EDGE T RANSFORMATION
291
      Similar to Ring Transformation, we find that when doing wedge partition, the node radius has no
292
      effect, and only the node angle is considered. We project all nodes into a unit circle which has o as
293   its center. Hence, if (r, X) are the polar coordinates of a node, its projection will have coordinates
294   (1, X). After projection, we can also change the angles of nodes. If the angle order of a node is X
295   from N nodes, its new position is on (1, 2πXN ) with polar coordination. The Transformation process
296   is illustrated in Figure 3 (c).
297
      After transformation, nodes of the graph lie on a line or on a circle, hence we can treat the graph as
298
      a sequential input. We can also find that for actions that split nodes i and i + 1 into two partitions
299
      will perform exactly the same final partition results. As the result, we can convert the continuous
300   action space into discrete ones to decrease the learning difficulties. New action Ai means split node
301   i and i + 1 into two partitions.

      5.3     R ING W EDGE PARTITION P IPELINE
304
305   The graph partition pipeline of the Wedge Ring Transformer (WRT) is illustrated in Figure 4 (a).
306   It sequentially determines partitions through Ring and Wedge Transformations, predicting the next
307   ring radius or wedge angle until the target partition count is achieved. The model consists of two
308   components for ring and wedge partitions with similar structures but distinct weights.
309   Transformation: The appropriate transformation (ring or wedge) is applied based on current re-
310   quirements.
311   Pre-Calculation: Essential computations on the transformed graph include: (1) Cut Weight Ci :
312   Sum of edge weights crossing between nodes i and i + 1. (2) Volume Matrix Vi,j : Total weight of
313   edges covered between nodes i and j (where i < j).
314   Wedge Ring Transformer: The Transformer processes node embeddings from the pre-calculation
315   phase and the current partition status, as depicted in Figure 4 (b).
316   PPO Header: After receiving node embeddings, the PPO header extracts action probabilities and
317   critic values. The actor projection header maps hidden size h to dimension 1, followed by a Softmax
318   layer for action probabilities. Value prediction uses Self-Attention average pooling on node embed-
319   dings and projects from h to 1. The PPO is employed to execute actions recursively until the graph
320
      is fully partitioned.
321   During the Ring Partition phase, a dynamic programming algorithm calculates the optimal partition
322   when the maximum radius and total ring count are fixed, with a complexity of O(n2 k). Thus, the
323   WRT determines the maximum radius for all ring partitions only once. The pseudo-code is available
      in the Appendix.

354   5.4        W EDGE R ING T RANSFORMER (WRT)
355   WRT utilizes a Transformer backbone to leverage information from transformed graphs, enabling it
356   to handle varying node counts and enhancing its scalability for diverse applications without the need
357   for fine-tuning after training. The Transformer architecture is illustrated in Figure 4 (b).
358
      WRT processes inputs from the Pre-Calculation module, specifically Cut Weight and Volume Ma-
359   trix, along with the Current Partition from the input graph. These are fed into n Transformer blocks,
360   yielding node embeddings from the final hidden state. To effectively manage Current Partition, we
361   represent each node’s selection status Partition Selection with a 0-1 array, then it is combined with
362   Cut Weight and transformed through a linear layer to generate hidden states, which are subsequently
363   augmented with positional embeddings.
364
      We introduce Partition Aware Multi-Head Attention (PAMHA) to replace the original Multi-Head
365   Attention (MHA) layer. PAMHA incorporates the Volume Matrix and Current Partition into its
366   attention mask. An element-wise transformation on V produces an attention mask of shape N × N
367   for PAMHA, allowing the model to learn the significance of different nodes. For Current Partition,
368   we observe that partitions splitting between nodes i and i + 1 do not affect the normalized cut
369   calculations on the right of i+1. For instance, in the circular graph with six nodes depicted in Figure
370   4, partitioning between certain nodes does not alter the normalized cut of other nodes. Consequently,
371   we create an attention mask focusing solely on the effective range of nodes. Finally, WRT outputs
372   node embeddings, which are then input to the PPO module.

      5.5.1    T RAINING S TRATEGY
379
380   With previous model design, WRT are able to dig out information effectively from a graph. However,
381   in RL, the initial strategies are randomized, which makes it challenging to learn a good strategy,
382   specifically ring partition and wedge partition will obstruct each other. For example, if the ring
383   partition always selects the smallest radius as the action, the wedge partition cannot learn any valid
384   policy because the total Normalized Cut is determined by ring partition. Training ring partition with
385
      a low quality wedge partition strategy will also face such difficulty.
386   To mitigate the above problem, we split the policy training into two stages, as shown in Figure 4 (c)
387   ① and ②. In the first Wedge Training stage, we use a randomized ring selection method to replace
388   the ring selection strategies, and only let WRT decide and train on wedge partitioning. To make the
389   model focus on learning good wedge partition strategy, we also ignore the Normalized Cut of rings
390   when calculating the reward. This makes the model focus on learning wedge partition strategy.
391   In the second Ring Training stage, we let WRT decide both ring and wedge partition. However,
392   we find that if we allow the model to tune all its parameters, the model is likely to forget how to
393   perform a good wedge partitioning before learning a good ring partition strategy. To avoid this,
394   we fix the parameters of wedge partitioning modules in WRT, as WRT has learned a good wedge
395   partition strategy with various radius. The only exception is Critic Projection Header, because in the
396   previous stage we change it to only use the Normalized Cut of wedge partitions as the reward, which
397
      is inconsistent with current reward definition. During the Ring Training stage, two Critic Projection
      Headers are both re-initialized and trained. In PPO, as the strategy are only determined by actor
398
      model, allowing critic to be trainable will not affect the learned policy.
399
400
401
      5.5.2    T ESTING S TRATEGY
402   After WRT is fully trained, we can directly generate partitions by WRT in Partition Generation stage,
403   it will firstly do ring partition, then do wedge partition in sequential, as shown in Figure 4 (c) ③.
404
405
      While we have proved ring and wedge partitions have the similar upper-bounds with with con-
      straints, sometimes in real graph, ring and wedge partition may not be the optimal one as the graph
406
      has outliers when performing ring and wedge partition. We give an example in Figure 4 (c) ④, the
407
      group of two nodes are reversed when performing a pure ring and wedge partition. To mitigate
408   such problem, we perform a Post Refinement Stage, where nodes in the same partition but not con-
409   nected will be split into multiple partitions. Then we greedily choose the partition which has biggest
410   Normalized Cut, and merge the partition into adjacent partitions. This post refinement method will
411   decrease the outlier node number, and gives better partitions.
412
      Finally, as the action of PPO is a policy-gradient based method, which provides an action probability
413
      distribution, and single segmentation may not yield the optimal solution directly, we can perform
414   multiple random sampling to obtain different partitions and choose best of them.

527   7     C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
528
529   In this paper, we have demonstrated the efficacy of using Reinforcement Learning for solving a
530   special form of the normalized cut problem on weighted graphs, an area where traditional methods
531
      like METIS fall short, and eixsting RL based graph partitioning methods also cannot perform well
      when the initial partition generated by METIS is not good enough. Inspired by urban road network
532
      construction, we propose to make ring and wedge partition directly on graphs. By introducing the
533
      simplified partitioning strategy involving ring-shaped and wedge-shaped cuts, our approach lever-
534   ages RL and Transformers to effectively learn and optimize the partitioning process. The two-stage
535   training methodology ensures stability and scalability, enabling our algorithm to handle both small
536   and large graphs efficiently. Our experimental results highlight the superiority of our method over
537   baseline algorithms, showcasing its potential for real-world applications. Our proposed method
538   focus on minimizing normalized cut of planar graphs, future work will focus on extend existing
539   methods to non-planar graphs, and find better post-process methods to further improve the final
      performance.

947   or wedge shape. We expect a typical Ring and Wedge partition will have the highest Ringness and
948   Wedgeness.
949
950
      For partition pi ∈ P , we define the partition range pri = {min(ri ), max(ri )}, partition angle
      pai = {min(ai ), max(ai )}, where ri and ai are polar coordinates of nodes that belongs to pi .
951
952   Then we define Ringness for a partition RP (r) = |{r ∈ pri }|, which means that how many parti-
953   tions cover the radius r. For a pure ring partition, as different partitions will never overlap within
954   their radius, RP (r) will be always 1; and for a Ring and Wedge partition, RP (r) is always 1, except
955   the out-most wedge part, is the wedge partition number kw .
                                             P
956   For Wedgeness, we define WP (r) = r∈pri |pai |, where |pai | is the angle range of pai . For radius
957   r, we only consider the partition that covers the selected range, and we sum up the angles covered
958   by these partitions. The angle should equal or greater than 2π, as the graph is fully partitioned by
959   P . If a partition is a pure wedge partition, for any r, the Wedgeness should be exactly 2π, because
960   partition will never cover each other in any place. For Ring and Wedge Partition, if r is in Wedge
961   Partition part, the conclusion remains same as above; for Ring Partition part, only one partition is
962   selected, and the Wedgeness is also 2π.
