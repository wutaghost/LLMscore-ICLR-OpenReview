009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            The Domain Generalisation (DG) problem setting requires a model trained on a set
012            of data distributions (domains) to generalise to new distributions. Despite a huge
013            amount of empirical study, previous DG methods fail to substantially outperform
014            empirical risk minimisation on rigorous DG benchmarks. Motivated by this, we
015            analyse the DG problem from a learning theoretic perspective and characterise in
016
               which situations DG will succeed or fail. Specifically, we derive upper bounds
               on the excess risk of ERM and lower bounds on the minimax excess risk, for
017
               three settings with different restrictions on how the domains may differ. In the
018
               most unconstrained setting, we show that all learning algorithms converge slowly
019            with respect to number of training domains, potentially explaining the lack of
020            algorithmic progress in this area. We also consider constrained settings including
021            limiting the pairwise domain distances as measured by a broad class of integral
022            probability metrics, and constraining all domains to have the same underlying
023            support. In these constrained cases, DG algorithms can converge more rapidly.
024            Notably, for all three settings, the we demonstrate that ERM has an optimal rate
025            of convergence towards the best possible model. Our analysis guides practitioners
026            interested in knowing when cross-domain generalisation might be reliable, and
027
               suggests strategies for optimising the performance of ERM in each setting.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Machine learning systems have shown exceptional performance on numerous tasks in computer
032   vision, natural language processing, and beyond. However performance drops rapidly when the
033   standard assumption of identically distributed training and testing data is violated. This domain-
034   shift phenomenon occurs widely in many applications of machine learning (Csurka, 2017; Zhou
035   et al., 2021; Koh et al., 2021), and often leads to disappointing results in practical machine learn-
036   ing deployments, since data “in the wild” is almost inevitably different from training sets. Given the
037   practical significance of this issue, numerous methods have been proposed that aim to improve mod-
038   els’ robustness to deployment under train-test domain shift by leveraging several different training
039
      sets for the same task (Zhou et al., 2021), a problem setting known as Domain Generalisation (DG).
      These span diverse approaches including specialised neural architectures, data augmentation strate-
040
      gies, and regularisers. Nevertheless, determining the effectiveness of these methods has proven to
041
      be difficult. A large scale experimental investigation (Gulrajani & Lopez-Paz, 2021) determined that
042   none of the previously proposed methods for addressing the DG problem could reliably outperform
043   Empirical Risk Minimisation (ERM)—the method that simply ignores all of the domain structure in
044   the data and trains a model as normal.
045
      Meanwhile various theoretical analyses have tried to study the DG problem via the derivation of
046
      generalisation bounds, attempting to provide guarantees on the worst case performance of various
047
      learning methods in this setting. Usually these analyses make strong assumptions about either the
048   structure of the model or the underlying data generation process. Algorithms based on kernel meth-
049   ods have received some attention (Muandet et al., 2013; Blanchard et al., 2021), and the behaviour
050   of how well these approaches scale with the number of training examples and training domains is
051   well-understood. There is also substantial work on investigating generalisation in other distribution
052   shift settings (Mansour et al., 2009a; Kpotufe, 2017; Rosenfeld et al., 2021a). However, there is a
053   lack of work on exploring the theoretical properties of sensible baselines the fundamental limits of
      the problem setting.

      In this paper we take a different direction to this existing body of theoretical work, and present the
055   first learning-theoretic study of: (i) excess risk bounds for ERM in the DG setting; and (ii) minimax
056   excess risk bounds that determine the fundamental limit of the best possible algorithm for a partic-
057   ular setting. To help characterize in what circumstances DG is likely to succeed or fail, we study
058   these bounds for both the most popular and challenging unconstrained DG setting, as well as two
059   less common simpler settings with constraints on how different the domains can be. We show that
060   in the most general unconstrained case, the best possible algorithm converges slowly with respect to
061   the number of domains; while it possible to converge more rapidly in constrained settings where we
062   can guarantee the domains’ similarity in terms of pairwise distances, or sharing the same support.
063
      Nevertheless, in all cases the minimax bounds and the worst case bounds for ERM have the same
      scaling behaviour, indicating surprisingly that it is not possible for any learning algorithm to do
064
      substantially better than ERM. Moreover, we show that out-of-domain generalisation is governed
065
      by a similar trade-off between empirical risk and complexity that governs in-domain generalisation.
066   Thus, new architectures or regularisers that improve practical in-domain generalisation of ERM will
067   also improve out-of-domain generalisation, and vice-versa. These results may go some way toward
068   explaining why empirical analyses (Gulrajani & Lopez-Paz, 2021) have shown that the plethora of
069   purpose designed methods (Zhou et al., 2021) struggle to beat ERM when carefully evaluated. Our
070   analysis shows that the search for an alternative learning algorithm to ERM per-se is futile. How-
071   ever, it does provide some specific actionable insights for improving DG by: (1) Regularising more
072   strongly when optimising for out-of-domain generalisation, than would be optimal for in-domain
073   generalisation, and (2) using prior knowledge to develop hypothesis classes that exhibit increased
074
      domain invariance and thus move more toward the IPM-constrained variant of the problem.
075   In summary, we make the following contributions to knowledge:
076
077          • We derive upper bounds on the excess risk of ERM that show, as the quantity and variety of
078            training data increases, ERM is able to better approximate the optimal model in the chosen
079            class of models. This conclusion holds for all three of the DG settings that we consider.
080          • Lower bounds on the minimax excess risk are provided. By comparing these lower bounds
081            with the upper bounds for ERM, we demonstrate that no method can perform substantially
082            better than ERM in the three general purpose DG settings considered in this paper.
083          • Comparing the upper and minimax bounds across problem settings, we delineate some
084            settings where DG is likely to succeed or fail, in terms of achieving a a reasonable expected
085            error on held out domains after seeing a reasonable number of training domains.
086          • Actionable insights are given for how one can optimise the performance of ERM in each of
087            the DG settings via the choice of regularisation strength and ensuring the hypothesis class
088            is well-suited to the underlying problem.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
092
093
094   The DG problem setting was first introduced and analysed by Blanchard et al. (2011), who also
095   provided a learning theory motivated algorithm for addressing it based on kernel methods. Since
096   then, several other works have also gone down the route of using statistical learning theory to derive
097   kernel methods with performance guarantees for DG (Muandet et al., 2013; Blanchard et al., 2021).
098   We note that a more general analysis of the performance of standard baselines and of fundamental
099
      limitations of methods developed for the standard DG setting is missing. However, some work has
      been undertaken for a variant where unseen domains are restricted to convex or affine combinations
100
      of those domains seen during training Rosenfeld et al. (2021a), where it is shown that ERM is
101
      minimax optimal. In any case, empirical evidence suggests that these limitations might be closer
102   than we would like. Gulrajani & Lopez-Paz (2021) compared several state of the art methods using
103   DomainBed, a common benchmark and hyper-parameter tuning protocol. They ultimately defend
104   Empirical Risk Minimization (ERM) over more sophisticated alternatives on the grounds that no
105   competitor consistently beats it. We also broadly defend ERM, and go a step further in claiming that
106   one cannot construct a general purpose DG method that substantially outperforms ERM. However,
107   we provide a deeper analysis into when and why ERM works, rooted in a theoretical analysis of
      generalisation qualities of ERM, unlike the prior purely empirical evaluation.

      Methods based on Invariant Causal Prediction (ICP) (Peters et al., 2016) have also been developed
      for the DG setting, and often come with claims of guaranteed robustness in the face of distribution
129
      shifts due to leveraging the underlying causal structure of a problem. The most popular idea in this
130
      area is Invariant Risk Minimisation (IRM) (Arjovsky et al., 2019), and variants such as Krueger et al.
131   (2020), which use an objective function inspired by ICP to train a deep neural network for DG. Some
132   other prior work makes a similar observation to us, identifying that standard regularisation methods
133   can improve robustness to distribution shift, albeit through a different lens (Janzing, 2019; Sagawa
134   et al., 2020). However, methods based on IRM have been shown to be more fragile than expected
135   in the face of real distribution shifts, rather than those considered in simplified causal diagrams
136   (Gulrajani & Lopez-Paz, 2021; Rosenfeld et al., 2021b).
137   Our work also takes inspiration from other transfer learning settings. In particular, there has been a
138   lot of work analysing the supervised and unsupervised Domain Adaptation problems, where there
139   are now a number of ways to relate the performance on training data from some source domain
140   to new data drawn from some target domain (Mansour et al., 2009a;b; Ben-David et al., 2010;
141   Kpotufe, 2017; Zhang et al., 2019; Kpotufe & Martinet, 2020). The focus of these works is typically
142   to define some notion of distribution divergence and then develop a tractable method for estimating
143   this divergence, or finding a model that best exploits the insights provided by such divergences.
144   There have been some attempts to apply these ideas in the DG setting (e.g., Albuquerque et al.
145
      (2020)). However, in the domain adaptation settings, there is an emphasis on finding divergences
      that are capable of exploiting asymmetries in the direction of transfer (Kpotufe & Martinet, 2020).
146
      In contrast, the standard DG formulation assumes a domain is just as likely to appear at training
147
      time as it is at test time, so it is not clear that we should expect an asymmetric divergence to be
148   well-suited to DG.

235

237   distribution for this algorithm to maximise the excess risk. It provides an indication of how quickly
238   the best possible learning algorithm is able to find the optimal model as a function of the amount
239   of training data (and number of training domains) available. One could argue that E contains many
240   DG problems that are not likely to be of interest in the real world, and that one of the uninteresting
241   problems could be the maxmimiser in the minimax excess risk. In the remainder of the paper we
242   consider three different restricted subsets of E that correspond to popular intuitions discussed in
243   the transfer learning literature: the general case; problems with bounded pairwise distance between
244
      domains; and problems where the marginals for each domain have bounded density ratios.

347   Practical Insights While the results so far appear to be pessimistic, there are actionable insights
348
      that we discuss here. The difference between Theorem 2 and single domain bounds (e.g., Theorem
      1) is the additional dependence on the number of domains n via the additional Rademacher com-
349
      plexity term. This shows that in-domain generalisation and cross-domain generalisation are both
350
      controlled by the same type of complexity term. This means that novel architectures or in-domain
351   regularisation methods (interventions that reduce complexity while maintaining the empirical risk,
352   or vice-versa) will automatically benefit cross-domain generalisation: Seeking models with good
353   in-domain generalisation will also improve DG. This reflects the situation in empirical DG bench-
354   marks where underlying neural architecture improvement has the most substantial influence on DG
355   performance (Gulrajani & Lopez-Paz, 2021; Zhou et al., 2021), as well as in-domain performance.
356
      More specifically, comparing the theorems also shows that when the goal is to generalise to new
357   domains, the risk of overfitting is higher. Therefore a lower complexity model is better for held
358   out domain performance compared for seen domain performance in standard i.i.d. learning. To
359   illustrate this empirically, we train linear SVM on pre-computed DINO (Caron et al., 2021) deep
360   features1 for six popular DG benchmarks. Tight bounds on linear SVM model complexity are known
361   and can be directly controlled by the scalar SVM slack parameter. The objective is also convex so
362   confounding factors in deep network training (stochastic optimisers, early stopping, etc) disappear,
363   and training is fast so that we can densely and exhaustively evaluate a wide range of complexities.
364   We compare conditions corresponding to the two theorems: (i) Training on the train splits of all
365
      domains, and testing on the test splits of all domains (standard i.i.d. learning). (ii) Training on the
      train splits of three domains, and testing on the test splits of a held out domain (i.e., DG).
366
367   The results in Fig. 2 average over 5 random seeds for dataset splitting, and all choices of held-out
368   target domain. From these we can see that: (i) All experiments exhibit the classic trade-off between
369   fitting the data well and constraining hypothesis class complexity appropriately. There is underfit-
370   ting for high regularisation (small C), and overfitting at low regularisation (large C). (ii) Across-
371
      domain/DG condition (red) exhibits lower performance as expected due due to the distribution shift.
      And most importantly (iii) the optimal regularisation for novel-domain performance is stronger than
372
      for seen-domain performance (red vertical lines left of blue). This illustrates our theoretical result
373
      that the ideal model complexity is lower for DG than for conventional i.i.d. learning.

523   5   C ONCLUSION
524
525   This paper provides a theoretical analysis of several DG problem settings through the use of bounds
526   on the excess risk of ERM and the minimax excess risk. Our results delineate two constrained
527   settings where it is possible to generalise with a smaller number of training domains, as opposed
528   to the most general setting where a large number of training domains are required for learning.
529   However, in all cases it is not possible to substantially outperform ERM. Therefore we also provide
530   several insights into how one can maximise ERM performance in each setting. In the unconstrained
531
      setting, the only course of action is to heavily regularise the model, as the model complexity has a
      larger impact on the transfer risk than the usual i.i.d. risk. If the bounded density ratio assumption
532
      holds, it is crucial to specify the “correct” model class—in the sense that the ground truth labelling
533
      function is well approximated by a function in the hypothesis class. The bounded IPM setting
534   is unique in that the modeller can influence whether it holds or not by choosing an appropriate
535   hypothesis class. We have provided two examples, along with an experimental demonstration in
536   Appendix I, that carefully choosing a hypothesis class with the correct invariances for a problem can
537   substantially reduce the number of domains required for good DG performance. Going forward, we
538   anticipate that data-driven approaches to defining hypothesis classes via auxiliary data (e.g., through
539   transfer learning or meta-learning) will enable appropriate hypothesis classes to be constructed with
      minimal manual labour.
