                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
               Recent success of diffusion models has inspired a surge of interest in developing
010
               sampling techniques using reverse diffusion processes. However, accurately esti-
011            mating the drift term in the reverse stochastic differential equation (SDE) solely
012            from the unnormalized target density poses significant challenges, hindering ex-
013            isting methods from achieving state-of-the-art performance. In this paper, we
014            introduce the Diffusion-PINN Sampler (DPS), a novel diffusion-based sampling
015            algorithm that estimates the drift term by solving the governing partial differential
016            equation of the log-density of the underlying SDE marginals via physics-informed
017            neural networks (PINN). We prove that the error of log-density approximation
018            can be controlled by the PINN residual loss, enabling us to establish convergence
019
               guarantees of DPS. Experiments on a variety of sampling tasks demonstrate the
               effectiveness of our approach, particularly in accurately identifying mixing propor-
020
               tions when the target contains isolated components.

023   1    I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Sampling from unnormalized distributions is a fundamental yet challenging task encountered across
026   various scientific disciplines such as Bayesian statistics, computational physics, chemistry, and biology
027   (Liu & Liu, 2001; Stoltz et al., 2010). Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) and variational inference
028   (VI) have historically been the go-to methods for this problem. However, these approaches exhibit
029   limitations when dealing with complex target distributions (e.g., distributions with multimodality or
030   heavy tails). Recently, the success of diffusion models for generative modeling (Song et al., 2020b;
031   Ho et al., 2020; Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021; Kingma et al., 2021) have sparked considerable interest
032   in tackling the sampling problem using the reverse diffusion processes that transport a given prior
033
      density to the target, governed by stochastic differential equations (SDE).
034   In diffusion-based generative models, the score function in the drift term of the reverse SDE is
035   learned based on score matching techniques (Hyvärinen & Dayan, 2005; Vincent, 2011) that require
036   samples from the target data distribution. However, for sampling tasks, we only have access to
037   an unnormalized density function π, making it challenging to estimate the score function for the
038   reverse SDE. From a stochastic optimal control perspective (Tzen & Raginsky, 2019; De Bortoli
039
      et al., 2021), several VI methods that parameterize the drift term with neural network approximation
      have been proposed (Zhang & Chen, 2021; Berner et al., 2022; Vargas et al., 2023b;a). Nevertheless,
040
      these approaches face challenges such as instability during training, the computational complexity
041
      associated with differentiating through SDE solvers, and mode collapse issues arising from training
042   objectives based on reverse Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergences (Zhang & Chen, 2021; Vargas et al.,
043   2023a). On the other hand, Huang et al. (2023) proposed a scheme based on the connection between
044   score matching and non-parametric posterior mean estimation. More specifically, they use MCMC
045   estimation of the scores to potentially alleviate the numerical bias intrinsic in parametric estimation
046   methods such as neural networks. However, this method also introduces noise in the estimates and
047   requires repetitive posterior sampling in each time step of the reverse SDE. Overall, despite their
048   potential, diffusion-based sampling methods have not yet achieved state-of-the-art performance.
049   In addition to its connection with posterior mean estimation, the score function has also been shown to
050   evolve according to a partial differential equation known as the score Fokker-Planck equation (score
051   FPE) (Lai et al., 2023). This discovery has led to a novel regularization technique for enhancing score
052   function estimation in diffusion models (Lai et al., 2023; Deveney et al., 2023). In this paper, we adopt
053   this strategy for diffusion-based sampling methods. While the score function can be recovered by
      solving the score FPE using the score of target distribution π as the initial condition, we demonstrate

054
      that it may fail to identify correct mixing proportions when π has isolated components, a common
055   limitation known as the blindness of score-based methods (Wenliang, 2020; Zhang et al., 2022). To
056   remedy this issue, we propose to solve the log-density FPE, a similar partial differential equation
057   for the log-density function, using physics-informed neural networks (PINN) (Raissi et al., 2019;
058   Wang et al., 2022). The estimated log-density function is then integrated into the reverse SDE,
059   leading to a novel sampling algorithm termed Diffusion-PINN Sampler (DPS). We prove that the
060   error of log-density estimation can be controlled by the PINN residual loss, which allows us to obtain
061   convergence guarantee of DPS based on established results for score-based generative models (Chen
062   et al., 2023b;a; Benton et al., 2023). Experiments on a variety of sampling tasks provide compelling
063
      numerical evidence for the superiority of our method compared to other baseline methods.

065   2    R ELATED WORKS
066
067   Recently, several works have explored the combination of Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINN)
068   and sampling techniques. For instance, Máté & Fleuret (2023); Fan et al. (2024); Tian et al. (2024)
069   address the continuity equation using PINN based on ODEs and achieve flow-based sampling through
070   a linear interpolation (i.e., annealing) path between the target distribution and a simple prior, such
071   as a Gaussian distribution. Besides, Berner et al. (2022) (in the appendix of their paper) and Sun
072   et al. (2024) propose solving the log-density Hamilton–Jacobi–Bellman (HJB) equation via PINN to
073   develop a SDE-based sampling algorithm. However, both approaches lack comprehensive numerical
074   investigation and thorough theoretical analysis. In contrast, our work investigates a limitation of
075
      score-based Fokker-Planck equations (FPE) in identifying the mixing proportions of multi-modal
      distributions, introduces novel computational techniques for solving PDEs via PINN in the context of
076
      diffusion-based sampling, and provides the first complete theoretical analysis of the algorithm. See
077
      more discussion about related works in Appendix D.

140   4     D IFFUSION -PINN SAMPLER
141
142   We consider sampling from aR probability density π(x) = µ(x)/Z with x ∈ Rd , where µ(x) has
143   an analytical form and Z = Rd µ(x)dx is the intractable normalizing constant. Throughout, we
144   only consider the forward process (1) with an explicit conditional density of xt |x0 ∼ πt|0 (·|x0 ). We
145   denote by πt the marginal density of xt associated with (1) from x0 ∼ π0 = π.
146
      Inspired by diffusion models, sampling can be performed by simulating a reverse process (8) targeting
147   at π(x), given an accurate estimate of the perturbed scores sθ (x, t) ≈ ∇x log πt (x),
148
                         dxt = f (xt , t) − g 2 (t)sθ (xt , t) dt + g(t) dB̄t , xT ∼ πprior ,

149
                                                                                                          (8)
150   where πprior denotes the stationary distribution of the forward process (1) and T is large enough such
151   that πT ≈ πprior . However, unlike generative models, sampling tasks lack training data from π, which
152
      hinders the application of denoising score matching for perturbed score estimation. In this section,
      we propose to solve the log-density FPE (6) with PINN to estimate the perturbed scores. While the
153
      score FPE can also be used for this purpose, we find that it may fail to learn the mixing proportions

      5.3    T HEORETICAL COMPARISON BETWEEN DIFFERENT SAMPLING METHODS FOR
389
             COLLOCATION POINTS
390
391   In practice, we typically lack prior knowledge of the high-probability regions of the diffusion path
392   starting from the target distribution. As a result, specifying a sufficiently large support for uniform
393   sampling of collocation points, becomes challenging and inefficient, especially in high-dimensional
394   settings. In contrast, we employ a more sophisticated strategy for generating collocation points that
395   integrates Langevin Monte Carlo (LMC) with the forward pass (see Section 4.2 for details). Similar
396   to Theorem 2 and 3, theoretical guarantee of uniformly sampled collocation points can be established,
397   albeit in a weaker form. Specifically, our results indicate that employing LMC and the forward
398
      pass for sampling collocation points is advantageous over uniform sampling. This is because, in the
      uniform case, the KL bound includes a factor proportional to the volume of the support, Vol(Ω),
399
      which can be prohibitively large in high dimensions. In contrast, our bound depends on the density
400
      ratio πt /νt , which is more manageable due to LMC and converges to 1 as t increases, thanks to the
401   forward process. Detailed results and proofs for uniform collocation points are provided in Appendix
402   B.

530
531   In this work, we proposed Diffusion-PINN Sampler (DPS), a novel method that leverages Physics-
532   Informed Neural Networks (PINN) and diffusion models for accurate sampling from complex target
533   distributions. By solving the log-density FPE that governs the evolution of the log-density of the
534   underlying SDE marginals via PINN, DPS demonstrates accurate sampling capabilities even for
535
      distributions with multiple modes or heavy tails, and it excels in identifying mixing proportions when
      the target features isolated modes. The control of log-density estimation error via PINN residual
536

1306
1307   As we use LMC for collocation generation in DPS, there is a risk of missing modes if short LMC
1308   runs do not adequately cover the high-density domain. In such cases, running LMC for an annealed
1309   path of target distributions or adopting the adversarial training method in Wang et al. (2022) for
1310
       collocation points maybe helpful. Also, solving high dimensional PDEs via PINN can be challenging,
       and we may use techniques such as stochastic dimension gradient descent or the Hutchinson trick to
1311
       scale DPS to high dimensional problems (Hu et al., 2024b;a).

1314   D      M ORE ON R ELATED W ORKS
1315
1316   To sample from an unnormalized target distributions, vanilla methods based on ergodic sampling
1317   using Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) (Kass et al., 1998; Neal, 2012) or stochastic differential
1318   equations (SDE) such as the Langevin dynamics (Roberts & Tweedie, 1996) typically have very slow
1319   convergence rates, making them inefficient in practice. In addition to those simulation-based VI
1320   approaches within the stochastic optimal control framework, Akhound-Sadegh et al. (2024) avoids
1321   the need to back-propagate through an SDE, at the price of introducing a bias into their objective
1322   function. Off-policy training has also been enabled for diffusion-based samplers where a log-variance
1323   objective function is employed instead of the KL divergence (Richter & Berner, 2024).
