009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            The growing demands on GPU memory posed by the increasing number of neural
012            network parameters call for training approaches that are more memory-efficient.
013            Previous memory reduction training techniques, such as Low-Rank Adaptation
014            (LoRA) and ReLoRA, face challenges, with LoRA being constrained by its low-
015            rank structure, particularly during intensive tasks like pre-training, and ReLoRA
016            suffering from saddle point issues. In this paper, we propose Sparse Spectral
017            Training (SST) to optimize memory usage for pre-training. SST updates all
018
               singular values and selectively updates singular vectors through a multinomial sam-
               pling method weighted by the magnitude of the singular values. Furthermore, SST
019
               employs singular value decomposition to initialize and periodically reinitialize low-
020
               rank parameters, reducing distortion relative to full-rank training compared to other
021            low-rank methods. Through comprehensive testing on both Euclidean and hyper-
022            bolic neural networks across various tasks, including natural language generation,
023            machine translation, node classification, link prediction, and image classification,
024            SST demonstrates its ability to outperform existing memory reduction training
025            methods and is comparable to full-rank training in various cases. On LLaMA-1.3B,
026            with only 18.7% of the parameters trainable compared to full-rank training (using a
027            rank equivalent to 6% of the embedding dimension), SST reduces the perplexity gap
028            between other low-rank methods and full-rank training by 97.4%. This result high-
029
               lights SST as an effective parameter-efficient technique for model pre-training, of-
               fering a promising new paradigm for achieving scalable and memory-efficient neu-
030
               ral network training. Our code is available at https://anonymous.4open.
031
               science/r/sparse_spectral_training-6A2C/.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   The development and scaling up of large language models (Kaplan et al., 2020; Brown et al., 2020;
037   Touvron et al., 2023b) pose great challenges to the feasibility of training large language models from
038   scratch. Normal training methods that update all parameters of models become extremely expensive
039   due to their extensive GPU memory requirements.
040
      Recent developments in parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT) methods, such as Low-Rank Adap-
041
      tation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2022), have sought to mitigate the challenge of fine-tuning memory
042   requirements by introducing trainable low-rank matrices that efficiently reduced the memory foot-
043   print. However, limiting the model’s parameter updates to a low-rank subspace can severely restrict
044   the ability of a model to capture and represent complex data patterns, leading to suboptimal perfor-
045   mance, especially in the pre-training stages. Recent advancements such as ReLoRA (Lialin et al.,
046   2024), COLA (Xia et al., 2024), and PLoRA (Meng et al., 2024b) have addressed the limitation of
047   low-rank constraint, by iteratively merging low-rank parameters with frozen parameters. However,
048   they still encounter saddle point issues due to zero gradient of low-rank parameters that occurs after
049   each merging step. This challenge results in slower and less effective convergence compared to
050
      full-rank models during pre-training.
051   In response to these challenges, we introduce Sparse Spectral Training (SST), a new training
052   framework designed to optimize memory consumption while closely approximating the overall
053   learning dynamics and performance of full-rank training. Unlike previous methods (Hu et al., 2022;
      Lialin et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023; Ding et al., 2023) that primarily focus on updating within


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      a low-rank subspace at each step, SST adopts a more effective approach by updating all singular
055   values at each step. SST also leverages the intrinsic spectral properties of the weight matrices,
056   focusing selective updates of singular vectors sampled from a multinomial distribution weighted
057   by the magnitude of the singular values. Additionally, SST uses singular value decomposition
058   to initialize and reinitialize low-rank parameters during training, reducing distortion relative to
059   full-rank training compared to other low-rank methods.
060
      Our comprehensive evaluations cover different tasks, including pre-training large language models
061   on OPT model family, ranging from 125m to 1.3b (Zhang et al., 2022), using Transformer (Vaswani
062   et al., 2017) for machine translation tasks and hyperbolic graph neural networks (Chen et al., 2022)
063   on node classification and link prediction tasks. For the OPT and LLaMA model family, SST
064   reduces the perplexity gap between other low-rank methods and full-rank training by 50%-97.4%.
065   In machine translation with Transformers, SST reduces the BLEU gap by an average of 66.7%.
066   Furthermore, we are the first to incorporate parameter-efficient pre-training process in hyperbolic
067   space, demonstrating that SST is a general technique applicable across various data structures and
068   models. On the hyperbolic Transformer, SST even outperforms full-rank training in most scenarios.
069
      For hyperbolic graph neural networks, SST reduces the performance gap by an average of 73.7% in
      node classification and 82.5% in link prediction.

072   2     R ELATED W ORK
073
074   Low-Rank Adaptation. Low-rank adaptation has become a key strategy for reducing the computa-
075   tional and memory requirements of training large-scale neural networks. Hu et al. (2022) introduced
076   Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA), a technique that fine-tunes pre-trained models by integrating low-rank
077
      matrices to significantly reduce the number of parameters updated during training. Various enhance-
      ments to LoRA have since been developed to improve its efficiency and broaden its application
078
      (Zhang et al., 2023; Dettmers et al., 2024; Zi et al., 2023; Valipour et al., 2023). Lialin et al. (2024)
079
      introduced ReLoRA specifically for the pre-training phase, which requires a full-rank warm-up to
080   achieve performance comparable to full-rank training. A similar approach is also found in COLA
081   (Xia et al., 2024) and PeriodicLoRA (Meng et al., 2024b). Additionally, Zhao et al. (2024) intro-
082   duced GaLore, which projects gradients into a low-rank subspace. Meng et al. (2024a) introduced
083   PiSSA, which applies SVD-based low-rank updates for fine-tuning pre-trained weights by focusing
084   on dominant singular vectors. These advancements highlight the versatility and ongoing evolution
085   of low-rank adaptation techniques in response to the growing complexity of neural network models.
086   Other parameter-efficient training methods are included in Appendix C.
087
088   Hyperbolic Neural Networks. Hyperbolic neural networks are an emerging area in deep learning,
089
      exploiting the unique properties of hyperbolic space that make it ideal for processing hierarchical and
      graph-structured data (Muscoloni et al., 2017; Cannistraci & Muscoloni, 2022). Innovations in this
090
      area have adapted fundamental neural network mechanisms to function within hyperbolic geometries,
091
      as demonstrated by Muscoloni et al. (2017) and Ganea et al. (2018). Further developments by
092   Chen et al. (2022) explore manifold-specific properties to enrich both theoretical understanding and
093   practical deployment. The use of hyperbolic spaces has been shown to significantly improve data
094   representation and generalization across various tasks, marking a notable advancement in managing
095   complex, non-Euclidean data structures (Gulcehre et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2019; Tifrea et al., 2019).

131   3.2   R E L O RA*
132
133   ReLoRA (Lialin et al., 2024), COLA (Xia et al., 2024), and PLoRA (Meng et al., 2024b) address
134   the limitation of fixed low ranks by iteratively merging the low-rank matrices B and A back into
135   the base weight matrix W0 . Although ReLoRA is designed for pre-training, it includes an initial
136   period of full-rank training (referred to as a “warm start”), which prevents it from being fully end-to-
137   end parameter-efficient. Meanwhile, COLA and PLoRA are primarily intended for fine-tuning. In
138
      this paper, we unify these methods into a generalized, end-to-end parameter-efficient pre-training
      paradigm, which we refer to as ReLoRA* and formalize in Algorithm 1.
139
140
      Algorithm 1 ReLoRA*

          Subtracting: Subtract B and A from W to maintain the original model output, W = W − BA
145

150
      with a Kaiming initialization (He et al., 2015). The initial zero setting for B allows the subtraction
151   step to be skipped. Notably, the optimizer states for B and A are reset after each merging step (99%
152   optimizer state is pruned in ReLoRA).
153
154   Limitation of ReLoRA*. Each iteration of ReLoRA* learns only a small subset of singular values.
155   Additionally, its reliance on zero initialization can result in zero gradients of low-rank matrices at
156   each reinitialization, as discussed in Section 4.3. These issues hinder ReLoRA* from achieving the
157   convergence speed and training quality of full-rank training.
158
159   3.3   G A L ORE
160
161   Gradient Low-rank Projection (GaLore) (Zhao et al., 2024) introduces a different approach by
      projecting the gradient using a low-rank projection matrix, rather than the weight matrix, as done by


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      LoRA and ReLoRA*. The projection matrix Pt is obtained by computing the top-r singular vectors
163   of the gradient of the weight matrix W, and it is recalculated every T steps. This matrix Pt is then
164   used to project the gradient of the weight matrix into the low-rank space, allowing the low-rank
165   gradient to update the first and second-order low-rank momentum in Adam. Finally, the low-rank
166   updates calculated by Adam are re-projected back to the original weight shape and used to update the
167   weights.
168
169   Limitations of GaLore. Although GaLore presents a valuable contribution by exploring low-rank
170   gradient projection, it has some limitations. Firstly, Pt is calculated based solely on the SVD of
171   the gradient from a single batch, which can be affected by data sampling noise. Secondly, GaLore
172   always selects the top-r singular vectors, which, combined with the previous limitation, restricts its
173   effectiveness during pre-training with a small r. In our experiments, we observed that with a small r
174
      (less than 1/12 of the dimension, different from the 1/2 to 1/4 used in the GaLore article), GaLore
      showed instability, leading to a sudden increase in loss on OPT-350M. Consequently, we chose to
175
      include the detailed explanation and comparison with GaLore in Appendix G rather than in the main
176
      text.

314   4.4                                        SST BALANCES E XPLOITATION AND E XPLORATION
315
316   From another perspective, SST combines the strategies of exploitation and exploration in spectral
317   domain. LoRA primarily focuses on exploitation by repeatedly adjusting the top-r singular values,
318   as detailed in Section 3.1, while neglecting the remaining spectral vectors. ReLoRA*, on the other
319   hand, emphasizes exploration by periodically reinitializing the matrices B and A after each merging,
320
      thereby constantly seeking new directions for learning but ignoring previously established dominant
      directions.
321
322   SST boosts learning efficiency by updating all magnitudes (Σ) at each step and cyclically revisiting
323   previously established dominant directions. By continuously updating all singular values, SST
      ensures unbiased sampling of U and VT , enabling a thorough exploration of the parameter space. As

      a result, SST balances the exploitation of known critical directions with the exploration of emerging
325   opportunities within the spectrum of matrix decomposition.

356   To validate our Sparse Spectral Training (SST) approach, we conducted experiments on both Eu-

358   neural network architectures and embedding geometries.

360   ReLoRA* and ReLoRA (Lialin et al., 2024) is that ReLoRA includes a full-rank training as “warm
361   start”, which prevents it from being an end-to-end memory-efficient pre-training method.
362
      For all low-rank methods, all linear layers in the baseline models were replaced by low-rank layers.

      Hyperparameters and implementation details are provided in Appendix E.

366   (Zhao et al., 2024) is provided in Appendix G, as GaLore is unstable during OPT pre-training with

      Previous hyperbolic neural network articles have predominantly focused on low-dimensional config-
416
      urations (Ganea et al., 2018; Shimizu et al., 2021; Nickel & Kiela, 2017). A key characteristic of
417   hyperbolic space is its exponential growth in volume with distance from a reference point, which
418   is significantly more rapid than the polynomial growth seen in Euclidean space (Cho et al., 2019).
419   This expansive nature makes hyperbolic spaces particularly prone to overfitting as dimensionality
420   increases. By imposing constraints on the parameter search space of hyperbolic neural networks, SST
421   prevents the overfitting typically associated with such high-dimensional settings.

530   6    C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
531
532   In this work, Sparse Spectral Training (SST) has demonstrated its efficacy as a parameter-efficient
533   pre-training methodology that surpasses other parameter-efficient methods, and better approximates
534   the learning dynamics and performance of full-rank training across diverse architectures, tasks,
535   and embedding geometries. SST introduces a novel approach by updating all singular values and
536   selectively adjusting the singular vectors of network weights. Moreover, SST incorporates SVD
537   both for the initialization and periodic reinitialization of low-rank parameters. Future directions for
538   SST include: (1) Investigating faster convergence approaches that avoid optimizer state reset. (2)
539   Extending the application of SST to the embeddings of large language models (LLMs).

      Apart from low-rank adaptations, researchers have developed a variety of parameter-efficient training
      techniques to optimize resource consumption while preserving learning effectiveness. Prompt tuning
873
      is an effective method that integrates tunable prefixes or soft prompts into the input embeddings of
874
      models. It enables lightweight task-specific adaptations with minimal impact on the model’s overall
875   architecture (Lester et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2021). Dynamic sparse training (DST), through methods
876   like SET (Mocanu et al., 2018), RIGL (Evci et al., 2020), MEST (Yuan et al., 2021), and CHT (Zhang
877   et al., 2024), employs a dynamic prune-and-grow strategy that adjusts network topology during
878   training. This approach optimizes training efficiency and can improve generalization by continuously
879   adapting the network’s sparse structure. This presents a significant shift from static training methods.

1412   optimizing memory efficiency while maintaining strong model performance, making it a practical
1413   choice for resource-constrained pretraining tasks.
