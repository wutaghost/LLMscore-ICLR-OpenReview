009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            World model has recently emerged as a promising approach to reinforcement
012            learning (RL), as evidenced by the recent successes that world model based agents
013            achieve state-of-the-art performance on a wide range of visual control tasks. This
014            work aims to obtain a deep understanding of the robustness and generalization
015            capabilities of world models. Thus motivated, we develop a stochastic differential
016
               equation formulation by treating the world model learning as a stochastic dynamical
               system in the latent state space, and characterize the impact of latent representation
017
               errors on robustness and generalization, for both cases with zero-drift representation
018
               errors and with non-zero-drift representation errors. Our somewhat surprising
019            findings, based on both theoretic and experimental studies, reveal that for the case
020            with zero drift, modest latent representation errors can in fact function as implicit
021            regularization and hence result in improved robustness. We further propose a
022            Jacobian regularization scheme to mitigate the compounding error propagation
023            effects of non-zero drift, thereby enhancing training stability and robustness. Our
024            extensive experimental studies corroborate that this regularization approach not
025            only stabilizes training but also accelerates convergence and improves accuracy of
026            long-horizon prediction.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Model-based reinforcement learning (RL) has emerged as a promising learning paradigm to improve
031   sample efficiency by enabling agents to exploit a learned model for the physical environment. Notably,
032   in recent works Hafner et al. (2019; 2020; 2022; 2023); Kessler et al. (2023); Freeman et al. (2019);
033   Wu et al. (2023); Kim et al. (2020) on world models, RL agents learn a latent dynamics model
034   (LDM) of the environment from observations and action, and then optimize the policy over the
035   learned dynamics model. Different from conventional approaches, world-model based RL takes an
036   end-to-end learning approach, where the building blocks (such as dynamics model, perception and
037   action policy) are jointly trained and optimized to achieve a unified goal. This framework offers
038   significant potential to improve both generalization and robustness to perturbations, making it highly
039
      advantageous for deployment in real-world scenarios. For example, DreamerV2 and DreamerV3
      achieve great progress in mastering diverse tasks involving continuous and discrete actions, image-
040
      based inputs, and both 2D and 3D environments, thereby facilitating robust learning across unseen
041
      task domains Hafner et al. (2019; 2020; 2022). Recent empirical studies have also demonstrated the
042   capacity of world models to generalize to unseen noisy states and dynamics in complex environments,
043   such as autonomous driving Hu et al. (2023). Nevertheless, it remains not well understood when and
044   how world models can generalize well in unseen environments, and how robustness plays a role in
045   this process.
046
      In this work, we make attempts to obtain a systematic understanding of the robustness and gen-
047
      eralization capabilities of world models by examining the impact of latent representation errors.
048   Specifically, we investigate how latent representation errors can enhance robustness against pertur-
049   bations, which in turn often improves generalization Lim et al. (2021). While one may expect that
050   optimizing a LDM prior to training the task policy would minimize latent representation errors and
051   hence can achieve better world model training, our somewhat surprising findings, based on both
052   theoretical and empirical studies, reveal that modest latent representation errors during training may
053   in fact be beneficial for robustness. In particular, the alternating training strategy for world model
      learning, which simultaneously refines both the LDM and the action policy, can improve robustness

      In a nutshell, latent representation errors incurred by latent encoders, if properly managed, may actu-
073
      ally facilitate world model training by enhancing robustness against perturbations, thereby improving
074
      generalization. This insight aligns with recent advances in deep learning, where noise injection
075   schemes have been studied as a form of implicit regularization to enhance models’ robustness. For in-
076   stance, recent study Camuto et al. (2021) analyzes the effects of introducing isotropic Gaussian noise
077   at each layer of neural networks, identifying it as a form of implicit regularization. Another recent
078   work Lim et al. (2021) explores the addition of zero-drift Brownian motion to RNN architectures,
079   demonstrating its regularizing effects in improving network’s stability against noise perturbations.
080
      However, we caution that latent representation errors in world models differ from the above noise
081   injection schemes (Lim et al. (2021); Camuto et al. (2021)), in the following aspects: 1) Unlike
082   the artificially injected noise only added in training (and removed during inference), these errors
083   are inherent in world models, leading to error propagation during rollouts; 2) Unlike the controlled
084   conditions of isotropic or zero-drift noise examined in prior studies, the errors in world models
085   may not exhibit such well-behaved properties in the sense that the drift may be non-zero and hence
086   biased; and 3) additionally, in the iterative training of world models and agents, the error originating
087   from the encoder affects the policy learning and agent exploration, influencing both robustness and
088   generalization.
089   In light of these observations, we develop a continuous-time stochastic differential equation (SDE)
090   formulation by treating the world model learning as a stochastic dynamic system with stochastic
091   latent states. This approach provides a formal characterization of latent representation errors as
092   stochastic perturbations, allowing us to quantify their impacts on robustness and generalization. Our
093   main contributions can be summarized as follows:
094
       • Latent representation errors as implicit regularization: Aiming to understand the robustness
095
         and generalization of world models and improve it further, we develop a continuous-time SDE
096      formulation by treating the world model learning as a stochastic dynamic system in latent state
097      space. Leveraging tools in stochastic calculus and differential geometry, we show that under
098      certain technical conditions, modest latent representation errors can in fact function as implicit
099      regularization and hence result in robustness gain.
100    • Improving robustness and generalization in non-zero drift cases via Jacobian regularization: For
101      the case where latent representation errors exhibit non-zero drifts, we show that the additional
102      bias can degrade the implicit regularization effect, leading to learning instability. Based on
103      the theoretical quantification of the instability caused by non-zero drift, we show that the well-
104      known Jacobian regularization can be employed to address this issue. Our experimental studies
105      demonstrate its efficacy in enhancing robustness and generalization.
106    • Reducing error propagation in predictive rollouts: We explicitly characterize the effect of latent
107      representation errors on predictive rollouts and their impact on robustness. We apply the Jacobian
         regularization technique to controll these effects, and our findings corroborate its ability to

126   Robustness and Generalization in Deep RL. Recent work on robustness and generalization in
127   deep RL has studied zero-shot generalization of learned policies to unseen environments Kirk et al.
128   (2023), often emphasizing task-level generalization through techniques such as task augmentation
129   in meta-RL Yao et al. (2021); Lee and Chung (2021). These approaches focus on ‘policy transfer’
130   across different tasks, whereas our work here aims to understand the robustness and generalization
131
      of world model based RL, under perturbations and variations in observations and dynamics. Unlike
      task-centric methods, our study on the LDM is centered around improving robustness in unseen or
132
      noisy environments where latent representations play a pivotal role in decision-making. Additionally,
133
      while recent studies on RL robustness Panaganti et al. (2022); Liu et al. (2023) introduce new training
134   frameworks aimed at policy safety and robustness, they do not account for the inherent challenges
135   posed by latent representation errors during rollouts.
136
137
      World model based RL. World models have demonstrated remarkable efficacy in visual control
      tasks across various platforms, including Atari Bellemare et al. (2013) and Minecraft Duncan (2011),
138
      as detailed in the studies by Hafner et al. Hafner et al. (2019; 2020; 2022). These models typically
139
      integrate encoders and memory-augmented neural networks, such as RNNs Yu et al. (2019), to
140   manage the latent dynamics. The use of variational autoencoders (VAE) Doersch (2016); Kingma
141   and Welling (2013) to map sensory inputs to a compact latent space was pioneered by Ha et al. Ha
142   and Schmidhuber (2018). Furthermore, the Dreamer algorithm Hafner et al. (2020; 2023) employs
143   convolutional neural networks (CNNs) LeCun et al. (1989) to enhance the processing of both hidden
144   states and image embeddings, yielding models with improved predictive capabilities in dynamic
145   environments.
146   Continuous-time RNNs. The continuous-time assumption is standard for theoretical formulations of
147   RNN models. Li et al. Li et al. (2022) study the optimization dynamics of linear RNNs on memory
148   decay. Chang et al. Chang et al. (2019) propose AntisymmetricRNN, which captures long-term
149   dependencies through the control of eigenvalues in its underlying ODE. Chen et al. Chen et al.
150   (2020) propose the symplectic RNN to model Hamiltonians. As continuous-time formulations can
151   be discretized with Euler methods Chang et al. (2019); Chen et al. (2020) (or with Euler-Maruyama
152   methods if stochastic in Lim et al. (2021)) and yield similar insights, this step is often eliminated for
153   brevity.
154   Implicit regularization by noise injection in RNN. Studies on noise injection as a form of implicit
155   regularization have gained traction, with Lim et al. Lim et al. (2021) deriving an explicit regularizer
156   under small noise conditions, demonstrating bias towards models with larger margins and more stable
157   dynamics. Camuto et al. Camuto et al. (2021) examine Gaussian noise injections at each layer of
158   neural networks. Similarly, Wei et al. Wei et al. (2020) provide analytic insights into the dual effects
159   of dropout techniques.

167   by the following key components: an encoder that compresses high dimensional observations st into
168   a low-dimensional latent state zt (Eq.1), a sequence model that captures temporal dependencies in
169   the environment (Eq.2), a transition predictor that estimates the next latent state (Eq.3), and a latent
170
      decoder that reconstructs observed information from the posterior (Eq.4):

       In this work, we consider a popular class of world models, including Dreamer and PlaNet, where {z,
176
      z̃, s̃} have distributions parameterized by neural networks’ outputs, and are Gaussian when the outputs
177
      are known. It is worth noting that {z, z̃, s̃} may not be Gaussian and are non-Gaussian in general.
178   This is because while z is conditional Gaussian, its mean and variance are random variables which
179   are learned by the encoder with s and h being the inputs, rendering that z is non-Gaussian due to the
180   mixture effect. For this setting, we have a continuous-time formulation where the latent dynamics
181   model can be interpreted as stochastic differential equations (SDEs) with coefficient functions of
182   known inputs. Due to space limitation, we refer to Proposition B.1 in the Appendix for a more
183   detailed treatment.

530   6    C ONCLUSION
531
532   In this study, we investigate the robustness and generalization of world models. We develop an
533   SDE formulation by treating the world model learning as a stochastic dynamical system in the latent
534   state space, and characterize the effects of latent representation errors as implicit regularization,
535   for zero-drift and non-zero drift cases. Our findings, based on both theoretic and experimental
536   studies, reveal that for the case with zero drift, modest latent representation errors can paradoxically
537
      function as implicit regularization and hence enhance robustness. To mitigate the compounding
      effects of non-zero drift, we applied Jacobian regularization, which enhanced training stability and
538
      robustness. Our empirical studies corroborate that Jacobian regularization improves generalization
539
      performance, broadening world models’ applicability in complex environments. This work has


                                                                  10
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


540
      the potential to improve the robustness and reliability of RL agents, especially in safety-critical
541   applications like autonomous driving. Future work can extend this study to other world models such
542   as with transformers-based LDM.
