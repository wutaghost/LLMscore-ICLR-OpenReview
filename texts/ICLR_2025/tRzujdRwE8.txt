                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
                Ensemble methods are known for enhancing the accuracy and robustness of ma-
010
                chine learning models by combining multiple base learners. However, standard
011
                approaches like greedy or random ensembles often fall short, as they assume a
012             constant weight across samples for the ensemble members. This can limit ex-
013             pressiveness and hinder performance when aggregating the ensemble predictions.
014             In this study, we explore employing neural networks as ensemble methods, em-
015             phasizing the significance of dynamic ensembling to leverage diverse model pre-
016             dictions adaptively. Motivated by the risk of learning low-diversity ensembles,
017             we propose regularizing the model by randomly dropping base model predictions
018             during the training. We demonstrate this approach lower bounds the diversity
019             within the ensemble, reducing overfitting and improving generalization capabili-
020
                ties. Our experiments showcase that the dynamic neural ensemblers yield com-
                petitive results compared to strong baselines in computer vision, natural language
021
                processing, and tabular data.

024   1     I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Ensembling machine learning models is a well-established practice among practitioners and re-
027   searchers, primarily due to its enhanced predictive performance over single-model predictions. En-
028   sembles are favored for their superior accuracy and ability to provide calibrated uncertainty estimates
029   and increased robustness against covariate shifts (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017). Combined with
030   their relative simplicity, these properties make ensembling the method of choice for many applica-
031   tions, such as medical imaging and autonomous driving, where reliability is paramount.
032   Despite these advantages, the process of selecting post-hoc models that are both accurate and di-
033   verse remains a challenging combinatorial problem, especially as the pool of candidate models
034   grows. Commonly used heuristics, particularly in the context of tabular data, such as greedy se-
035   lection (Caruana et al., 2004) and various weighting schemes, attempt to optimize ensemble perfor-
036   mance based on metrics evaluated on a held-out validation set or through cross-validation. However,
037   these methods face significant limitations. Specifically, the selection of models to include in the en-
038   semble and the determination of optimal ensembling strategies (e.g., stacking weights) are critical
039   decisions that, if not carefully managed, can lead to overfitting on the validation data. Although neu-
040
      ral networks are good candidates for generating ensembling weights, few studies rely on them as a
      post-hoc ensembling approach. We believe this happens primarily due to a lack of ensemble-related
041
      inductive biases that provide regularization.
042
043   In this work, we introduce a novel approach to post-hoc ensembling using neural networks. Our
044   proposed Neural Ensembler dynamically generates the weights for each base model in the ensemble
045   on a per-instance basis, a.k.a dynamical ensemble selection (Ko et al., 2008). To mitigate the risk
046
      of overfitting the validation set, we introduce a regularization technique inspired by the inductive
      biases inherent to the ensembling task. Specifically, we propose randomly dropping base models
047
      during training, inspired by previous work on DropOut in Deep Learning (Srivastava et al., 2014).
048
049   In summary, our contributions are as follows:
050
051       1. We propose a simple yet effective post-hoc ensembling method based on a neural network that
052
             dynamically ensembles base models.
053       2. To prevent the formation of low-diversity ensembles, we introduce a regularization technique
             that involves randomly dropping base model predictions during training. We demonstrate theo-

178
179   In this section, we elaborate on one risk that might arise during learning ensembles, as a base model
180   might be highly correlated with the target in the validation set. We dub this model a Preferred Base
181   Model.
182

183
      base models predictions Z = {zm |zm ∈ R, m = 1, ..., M }. zp is the Preferred Base Model if it has
184   the highest sample correlation to the target, i.e. ρzp ,y ∈ [0, 1], ρzp ,y > ρzm ,y , ∀zm ∈ Z/{zp }.
185

      3.3   BASE M ODELS ’ D ROP O UT
208
209
      Using Neural Ensemblers tackles the need for dynamical ensembling. Moreover, it gives additional
210   expressivity associated with neural networks. However, there is also a risk of overfitting and diver-
211   sity collapse. As we showed in Section 3.2, this happens when there is a preferred model on which
212   the ensembler mainly relies while neglecting other base model predictions. Although it might effec-
213   tively decrease the validation loss (Equation 5), it does not necessarily generalize to test samples.
214   Inspired by previous work (Srivastava et al., 2014), we propose to drop some base models during
215   training forward passes. Intuitively, this forces the ensembler to rely on different base models to
      perform the predictions, instead of merely using the preferred base model(s).

407   RQ 2: Do Neural Ensemblers need a strong group of base models, i.e. found using Bayesian
408   Optimization?
409   Experimental Protocol. Practitioners use some methods such as greedy ensembling as post-hoc
410   ensemblers, i.e., they consider a set of models selected by a search algorithm such as Bayesian
411   Optimization as base learners. DivBO enhances the Bayesian Optimization by accounting for the
412   diversity in the ensemble in the acquisition function. We run experiments to understand whether the
413   Neural Ensemblers’ performance depends on a strong subset of 50 base models selected by DivBO,
414   and whether it can help other methods. We conduct additional experiments by randomly selecting
415   50 models to understand the impact and significance of merely using a smaller set of base models.
416   We normalize the base of the metric on the single-best base model from the complete set contained
417
      in the respective dataset.
418   Results. We report in Table 4 the results with the two selection methods (random and DivBO) us-
419   ing a subset of common baselines, where we normalize using the metric of the single-best from
420   the whole set of models. We limit the number of baselines for brevity, and the extended version
421   includes more baselines in Table 15. We also include results in separate tables for the two selec-
422   tion methods (Appendix E). We can compare directly with the results in Table 3. We observe that
423
      reducing the number of base models with DivBO negatively affects the performance of the Neural
      Ensemblers. Surprisingly, randomly selecting the subset of base models improves the results in two
424
      metadatasets (TR-Class and NB-1000). We hypothesize that decreasing the number of base models
425
      is beneficial for these metadatasets. With over 1000 base models available, the likelihood of identi-
426   fying a preferred model and overfitting the validation data increases in these metadatasets. Naturally,
427   decreasing the number of base models can also be detrimental for the Neural Ensemblers, as this
428   happens for some metadatasets such as TR-Reg and QT-Micro. In contrast to the Neural Ensemblers,
429   selecting a subset of strong models with DivBO improves the performance for some baselines such
430   as Model Averaging (MA) or TopK (K = 25). In other words, it works as a preprocessing method
431   for these ensembling approaches. Overall, the results in Tables 4 and 15 demonstrate that Neural
      Ensemblers do not need a strong group of base models to achieve competitive results.

460   RQ 3: What is the impact of the DropOut regularization scheme?
461
      Experimental Protocol. Firstly, we do a small experiment to observe how the DropOut rate affects
462   the weights when using Neural Ensemblers in MA mode. For this, we use QT-Micro datasets,
463   and save each model’s weights θm and error after training the neural network. Subsequently, to
464   understand how much the base learners DropOut helps the Neural Ensemblers, we run an ablation
465   by trying the following values for the DropOut rate δ ∈ {0.0, 0.1, . . . , 0.9}. We compute the average
466   NLL for three seeds per dataset and divide this value by the one obtained for δ = 0.0 in the same
467   dataset. Therefore, we realize that a specific DropOut rate is improving over the default network
468   without regularization if the normalized NLL is below 1.
469   Results. We show how the mean of the weights per model is related to the mean error of the models
470   for different DropOut rates in Figure 5. When there is no DropOut some weights are close to one,
471   i.e. they are preferred models. As we increase the value, many models with high weights decrease.
472   If the rate is very high (e.g. 0.9), we will have many models contributing to the ensemble, with
473   weights different from zero. Our ablation study demonstrates that non-existing or high DropOut are
474   detrimental to the Neural Ensembler performance in general. As shown in Figure 6, this behavior is
475   consistent in all datasets and both modes, but TR-Reg metadataset on Stacking mode. In general, we
476
      observe that Neural Ensemblers obtain better performance when using base models’ DropOut.

506   Ensembles for Deep Learning. Ensembles of neural networks (Hansen & Salamon, 1990; Krogh &
507   Vedelsby, 1994; Dietterich, 2000) have gained significant attention in deep learning research, both
508   for their performance-boosting capabilities and their effectiveness in uncertainty estimation. Various
509
      strategies for building ensembles exist, with deep ensembles (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017) being
      the most popular one, which involve independently training multiple initializations of the same
510
      network. Extensive empirical studies (Ovadia et al., 2019; Gustafsson et al., 2020) have shown that
511
      deep ensembles outperform other approaches for uncertainty estimation, such as Bayesian neural
512   networks (Blundell et al., 2015; Gal & Ghahramani, 2016; Welling & Teh, 2011).
513
514   Mixture-of-Experts. Our idea of generating ensemble base model weights is closely connected to
515
      the mixture-of-experts (MoE) (Jacobs et al., 1991; Jordan & Jacobs, 1993; Shazeer et al., 2017),
      where one network is trained with specialized sub-modules that are activated based on the input
516
      data. Batch ensembles (Wen et al., 2020; Wenzel et al., 2020) are also closely related, as they aim
517
      to capture diverse model behaviors by simultaneously training multiple models with shared weights
518   but different random projections. Alternatively, we could include a layer, aggregating predictions by
519   encouraging diversity (Zhang et al., 2020). In contrast to these approaches, our Neural Ensemblers
520   can ensemble any (black-box) model and are not restricted to gradient-based approaches.
521
      Dynamic Ensemble Selection. Our Neural Ensembler is highly related to dynamic ensemble selec-
522
      tion. Both dynamically aggregate the predictions of base models per instance (Cavalin et al., 2013;
523
      Ko et al., 2008). Traditional dynamic ensemble selection methods aggregate the most competent
524   base models by paring heuristics to measure competence with clustering, nearest-neighbor-based,
525   or traditional tabular algorithms (like naive Bayes) as meta-models (Cruz et al., 2018; 2020). In
526   contrast, we use an end-to-end trained neural network to select and weight the base models per
527   instance.

      6   C ONCLUSIONS
530
531
      In this work, we tackled the challenge of post-hoc ensemble selection and the associated risk of
532
      overfitting on the validation set. We introduced the Neural Ensembler, a neural network that dynam-
533   ically assigns weights to base models on a per-instance basis. To reduce overfitting, we proposed
534   a regularization technique that randomly drops base models during training, which we theoretically
535   showed enhances ensemble diversity. Our empirical results demonstrated that Neural Ensemblers
536   consistently form competitive ensembles across diverse data modalities, including tabular data (clas-
537   sification and regression), computer vision, and natural language processing. In the future, we aim
538   to explore in-context learning (Brown et al., 2020), where a pretrained Neural Ensembler could gen-
539   erate base model weights at test time, using their predictions as contextual input. We discuss broader
      impact and limitations in Appendix B.

1353   To better understand the trade-off between performance and computational cost, we analyze the
1354

1355

1356
       trast to methods like DivBO, which sequentially train models during the search, leading to higher
1357   computational demands.

1360

1361
       Neural Ensemblers are faster than traditional machine learning models such as Gradient Boosting,
1362
       Support Vector Machines (SVM), Random Forests, and optimization algorithms like CMAES. While
1363   simpler methods like Top5 and Top50 exhibit faster runtimes, they do so at the expense of reduced
1364   accuracy.

       G    P ROOF - OF -C ONCEPT WITH OVERPARAMETERIZED BASE M ODELS

1391
1392   A key question is whether dynamic ensemblers like our Neural Ensembler (NE) offer benefits when
1393   base models are overparameterized and potentially overfit the data. Specifically, does the advantage
1394
       of the NE persist in scenarios where the base models have high capacity?
1395   To explore this, we extend our Proof-of-Concept experiment by ensembling 10th-degree polynomi-
1396   als instead of 2nd-degree ones, thereby increasing the complexity of the base models and introducing
1397   the risk of overfitting. We follow the same protocol as in Section 4.1, comparing the performance of
1398   the NE with the fixed-weight ensemble method.
1399   As illustrated in Figure 10, even with overparameterized base models that tend to overfit the training
1400   data, the NE (specifically the weighted Model-Averaging version) achieves the best performance on
1401   unseen data. This improvement occurs because the NE dynamically adjusts the ensemble weights
1402   based on the input. Thus, dynamic ensembling is advantageous not only when base models underfit

1588   J                 S ENSITIVITY A NALYSIS OF N EURAL E NSEMBLER H YPERPARAMETERS
1589
1590   While our proposed Neural Ensembler (NE) uses MLPs with 4 layers and 32 neurons per layer by
1591   default, it is important to understand how sensitive the NE’s performance is to changes in its hyper-
1592   parameters. To this end, we conduct an extensive ablation study by varying the number of layers
1593   L ∈ {1, 2, 3, 4, 5} and the number of neurons per layer (hidden dimension) H ∈ {8, 16, 32, 64, 128}
1594   in the stacking mode of the NE.
1595   For each configuration, we compute the Negative Log Likelihood (NLL) on every task in the meta-
1596   datasets and normalize these values by the performance obtained with the default hyperparameters
1597   (L = 4, H = 32). This normalization allows us to compare performance changes across different
1598   tasks and metadatasets, accounting for differences in metric scales. A normalized NLL value below
1599   one indicates improved performance compared to the default setting.
1600   The results of this ablation study are presented in Figures 13a and 13b, we observe that increasing
1601   the hidden dimension beyond H = 32 generally leads to worse performance across all metadatasets.
1602   Decreasing the hidden dimension below H = 32 yields slight performance improvements on several
1603   metadatasets, but also causes significant performance drops on a few others. Similarly, Figure 13b
1604   shows that increasing the number of layers beyond L = 4 provides minimal performance gains on
1605   a few metadatasets, while decreasing L below 4 results in improvements on only three out of seven
1606   metadatasets.
1607   These findings indicate that there is no single hyperparameter configuration that is optimal across
1608   all datasets. However, our default configuration (L = 4, H = 32) strikes a balance, providing
1609   robust performance across diverse tasks without the need for extensive hyperparameter tuning, and
1610   can be effectively applied in various settings without the need for dataset-specific hyperparameter
1611   optimization.

1656
       In our experimental setup (Section 4), we train the base models using the training split and the en-
1657   semblers using the validation split, then evaluate on the test split. An important question is whether
1658   merging the training and validation data could improve the performance of both the base models and
1659   the ensemblers. Specifically, we explore:
1660
1661        (a) Can baseline methods that do not require a validation split, such as Random, achieve better
1662            performance if the base models are trained on the merged dataset (training + validation)?

1664           cial, given that more data might enhance their learning?
1665

1667   Pipelines and FTC. We trained the base models on the merged dataset and also trained the ensem-
1668   blers on this same data. Five representative baselines were compared: NE-MA, NE-Stack, Greedy,
1669   Random and Single-best.
1670   Figure 15b presents the test set performance on the Scikit-learn Pipelines metadataset. Training
1671   on the merged dataset did not improve performance compared to training on the original splits. In
1672   fact, the results are similar to the Random ensembling method, indicating no significant gain. This
1673   suggests that training both the base models and ensemblers on the same (larger) dataset may lead to
       overfitting, hindering generalization to unseen data.

1730
       In Section 3.1, we discussed that the Neural Ensembler in model-evaraging mode (NE-MA) com-
1731   putes weights θm (z; β) that rely solely on the base model predictions z for each instance. Specifi-
1732   cally, the weights are defined as:
1733
1734                                                    exp fm (z; β)
1735                                        θm (z; β) = P                                                (53)
                                                          m′ fm (z; β)
                                                               ′
1736
1737   where z represents the base model predictions for an instance x ∈ X , and X denotes the original
1738   input space. As our experiments encompass different data modalities, x can be a vector of tabular
1739   descriptors, an image, or text.
1740
       An alternative formulation involves computing the ensemble weights directly from the original input
1741
       instances instead of using the base model predictions. This approach modifies the weight computa-
1742   tion to:
1743
1744                           X                           X        exp fm (x; β)
1745                    ŷ =       θm (x; β) · z(x; m) =        P                    · z(x; m)           (54)
                                                                    m′ exp fm (x; β)
                                                                             ′
1746                           m                           m
1747
       where fm now operates on the original input space X to produce unnormalized weights.
1748
1749   However, adopting this formulation introduces challenges in selecting an appropriate function fm
1750   for different data modalities. For example, if the instances are images, fm must be a network capable
1751   of processing images, such as a convolutional neural network. This requirement prevents us from
1752   using the same architecture across all modalities, limiting the generalizability of the approach.
1753   To evaluate this idea, we tested this alternative neural ensembler on the Scikit-learn Pipelines meta-
1754   dataset, which consists of tabular data. We implemented fm as a four-layer MLP. Our results, rep-
1755   resented by the yellow bar in Figure 15b, indicate that this strategy does not outperform the original
1756   approach proposed in Section 3, which uses the base model predictions as input.
1757   We hypothesize that computing ensemble weights directly from the original input space may be more
1758   susceptible to overfitting, especially when dealing with datasets that have noisy or high-dimensional
1759   features. Additionally, this strategy may require tuning the hyperparameters of the network fm for
1760   each dataset to achieve optimal performance, reducing its effectiveness and generalizability across
1761   diverse datasets.
