009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               State-of-the-art medical multi-modal large language models (med-MLLM), such
               as LL AVA-M ED or B IO M ED GPT, leverage instruction-following data in their
012
               pre-training stages. However, those models primarily focus on scaling the model
013
               size and data volume to boost performance while mainly relying on the autore-
014            gressive learning objectives. Surprisingly, we reveal that such learning schemes
015            might result in a weak alignment between vision and language modalities, mak-
016            ing these models highly reliant on extensive pre-training datasets — a significant
017            challenge in medical domains due to the expensive and time-consuming nature
018            of curating high-quality instruction-following instances. We address this chal-
019            lenge with a new multi-graph alignment algorithm, namely L O G RA -M ED, which
020            enforces triplet correlations on the latent embedding space among image modal-
021            ities, conversation-based descriptions, and extended contextual captions. Owing
022
               to this technique, the model is encouraged to capture the semantic meaning of the
               context, handle linguistic variability where the captions or questions may differ
023
               from training instances, and learn cross-modal associations, linking visual ele-
024
               ments with various textual interpretations. To scale our algorithm to the med-
025            MLLM setting, we also design an efficient end-to-end learning scheme based
026            on advanced black-box gradient-estimation techniques that permit fast forward
027            and backward steps through the LLM model (LLaMa 7B). Empirical results show
028            that we can match the performance of LLAVA-Med pre-trained on 600K image-
029            text pairs from PMC-15M for Medical VQA tasks and significantly outperform it
030            when trained on only 10% of the data. For instance, on VQA-RAD, we exceed
031            LLAVA-Med (both trained on 10%) by 20.13% and achieve near parity with the
032            100% pre-training setting (72.52% vs. 72.64%). Additionally, we also surpass
033
               other SOTA pre-training methods and med-MLLM such as B IOMED GPT on vi-
               sual chatbot or R AD FM on zero-shot image classification with VQA, showcasing
034
               the power of multi-graph alignment in improving vision-language integration for
035
               medical-MLLM.

038   1   I NTRODUCTION
039
      Generic Multi-Modal Large Language Models (MLLM) are an emerging field integrating processing
040
      and generation across text, images, and audio. Models like GPT-4V (Achiam et al., 2023), LLaVa
041
      (Liu et al., 2024b), and Next-GPT (Wu et al., 2023b) handle tasks from image captioning to com-
042   plex visual reasoning. A critical component in training these models is instruction-following (IF)
043   data (Lou et al., 2023), which involves complex, multi-turn interactions (Sun et al., 2024) where
044   the model is expected to respond to specific instructions or questions about the image. In the med-
045   ical domain, current efforts have been focused on building medical MLLM by curating specialized
046   IF datasets encompassing medical images, clinical notes, and diagnostic criteria (Xie et al., 2024).
047   These datasets are used to adapt general-purpose MLLM, aiming to transfer foundational knowl-
048   edge of generic concepts and reduce computation costs required in training steps. For instance,
049   in LLAVA-Med, (Li et al., 2024) 600K biomedical image-text pairs are sampled from PMC-15M
050
      (Zhang et al., 2023c) and GPT-4 is used to create instruction data from the text inputs, resulting in
      approximately 60K multi-modal IF data points. In the next step, (i) vision encoders and language
051
      decoders are taken from LLaVa and are first aligned through trainable projection layers before (ii)
052
      the models (with the exception of the vision encoders) are trained together on collected medical IF
053   data. Both steps are called the pre-training phase, where the auto-regressive function is used as the
      primary objective. The model can then be fine-tuned to various downstream tasks.

077   To address this challenge, we present a novel multi-graph alignment algorithm, namely L O G RA -
078   M ED, that improves the model’s ability to learn complex interactions between vision and lan-
079   guage modalities, mitigating the limitations of autoregressive functions when trained on limited
080
      instruction-following data. Specifically, given pairs of input images with instruction data, we use
      GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023) to form a longer version of the instruction, with more in-context expla-
081
      nations for concepts and correlations among entities while preserving the same meaning. These data
082
      pairs are fed into the MLLM, where a vision encoder extracts features for the image while the lan-
083   guage model (LLaMa) computes latent embedding of the instruction data and its extended versions.
084   The embedding vectors obtained from different IF samples in a batch are then used to construct
085   three graphs. The first graph’s vertices are the image features, while the vertices of the two other
086   graphs are the embeddings of the instructions and their extended versions. We subsequently learn
087   feature representation by solving the combinatorial multi-graph alignment problem between these
088   three graphs such that the input embedding of the graph alignment will output the triplet coupling
089   among the image, its instruction data, and the longer extension. By jointly learning this alignment
090   with autoregressive methods, we aim to preserve the LLMs’ sequential generation capabilities while
091
      enhancing the model’s ability to capture deeper semantic relationships. This approach goes beyond
      memorization, increases resilience to linguistic variability, and significantly improves cross-modal
092
      coherence.
093
094   Our method differentiates itself from existing multi-modal alignment techniques for LLM (Park
095   et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023a; Chen et al., 2023a) in two ways. First, while prior contrastive objec-
096   tives primarily focus on learning projections to connect frozen vision encoders with frozen language
097
      models, our algorithm directly trains LLM using the multi-graph framework. Second, we unify and
      generalize pairwise contrastive learning, which focuses on individual image-caption pairs, by in-
098
      corporating global constraints through graph structures. This allows us to integrate both feature
099
      and structural consistencies using graph edges, enhancing robustness for similar entities (whether
100   images or descriptions) commonly found in medical datasets. Although combinatorial graph align-
101   ment is generally non-differentiable and solving multi-graph alignment is computationally expen-
102   sive, we overcome these challenges by leveraging modern implicit maximum likelihood estimation
103   techniques (Niepert et al., 2021; Minervini et al., 2023). This enables efficient gradient estimation
104   and allows for fast forward and backward propagation through large LLMs (e.g., LLaMa-7B), us-
105   ing a barycenter graph (Agueh & Carlier, 2011) for alignment. As a result, the model can scale
106   effectively with extensive datasets on large LLMs while maintaining alignment performance.
107   In summary, we make the following key contributions:

123   Medical Multi-modal LLMs. Recent developments in medical-MLLM like Biomed-GPT (Zhang
124
      et al., 2023a), MedFlamingo (Moor et al., 2023), Med-Dr (He et al., 2024), LLAVA-Med (Li et al.,
      2024), and Med-PaLMs (Singhal et al., 2023; Tu et al., 2024) are transforming healthcare by inte-
125
      grating diverse data types and scaling medical instruction data. Biomed-GPT excels with multiple
126
      biomedical modalities, MedFlamingo focuses on few-shot learning for medical visual question an-
127   swering, and LLAVA-Med leverages large-scale biomedical image-text pairs for improved perfor-
128   mance. Commonly, these models emphasize scaling medical instruction data and increasing model
129   parameters to enhance accuracy and applicability in real-world medical scenarios. In contrast, our
130   approach examines the widely used autoregressive pre-training algorithms and demonstrates that
131   incorporating long-context multi-graph alignment of exist instruction samples can significantly en-
132   hance medical-MLLM performance without requiring larger models or extensive datasets.
133   Visual Instruction Tuning. Visual instruction tuning techniques aim to bridge the gap between
134   frozen vision-language models and frozen LLMs trained on unimodal data, enabling them to work
135   effectively in a multi-modal context. These methods involve (i) learning a multi-layer perceptron
136   (MLP) layer to map embeddings from the vision model to the language model as LLaVa (Liu et al.,
137   2024b), VideoLLM (Chen et al., 2023a); (ii) using adapter-based adjustment as LLaMa-adapter
138   (Zhang et al., 2024), Voxposer (Huang et al., 2023), or (iii) learning multi-modal perceiver by gated
139   cross-attention (Alayrac et al., 2022) or Q-Former as in BLIP-2 (Li et al., 2023a). Pre-training al-
140   gorithms to train these models can be combined with both auto-regressive and contrastive learning
141
      (Park et al., 2024; Zhai et al., 2023) or image-text matching as in (Li et al., 2022; 2023a). Our
      algorithm differs from those by focusing on directly training LLMs rather than lightweight projec-
142
      tors. This requires a fast solver capable of efficiently handling forward and backward passes through
143
      large-scale LLMs with extensive parameters.
144
145   Vision-language Pretraining Algorithm. Pre-training algorithms commonly applied for vision-
146   language models, like CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), follow various strategies. Generative approaches,
147
      such as masked prediction in language models (Devlin, 2018; Song et al., 2020), or autoregressive
      algorithms that predict sequential text in LLMs (Liu et al., 2024b; Zhang et al., 2024), are widely
148
      used. Another direction focuses on discriminative methods, which learn contrastive distances be-
149
      tween image-text pairs (Liu et al., 2023; Zhai et al., 2023; Khan & Fu, 2023), optimal transport
150   (Chen et al., 2022; Nguyen et al., 2024a), or impose clustering constraints (Park et al., 2024). Our
151   function departs from these by generalizing them into a combinatorial graph-matching formulation
152   across cross-domain graphs. While LVM-Med (MH Nguyen et al., 2024) is the most similar to our
153   approach, it targets alignment within vision tasks, whereas we align images, instruction-following
154   data, and extended contextual information.
155
      Graph alignment across K domains (K ≥ 3) is highly computationally intensive. Current meth-
156   ods, such as multi-marginal optimal transport (Lin et al., 2022; Piran et al., 2024), Wasserstein
157   barycenters (Nguyen et al., 2024b), and multi-adjacency matrix assumptions (Bernard et al., 2019;
158   Swoboda et al., 2019), relax the problem but are limited to small-scale tasks and require multiple
159   solver steps, making them inefficient for LLM training. In contrast, our approach utilizes heuristic
160   solvers (Swoboda et al., 2017; Rolı́nek et al., 2020) and modern gradient estimation techniques for
161   black-box optimization (Niepert et al., 2021; Minervini et al., 2023), enabling scalable and efficient
      performance for large language models.

257   3.4 S CALABLE M ULTI - GRAPH A LIGNMENT
258   Our aim is to solve the graph alignment between three graphs Gv , Ga , and Gae to form a triplet
259   constraint between input image embedding, its original instruction embedding, and the long-context
260   extended version ones. However, solving a structure-aware graph alignment between K domains
261   is computationally expensive. One potential solution is to perform pairwise graph alignments K 2
262   times, as shown in Eq. (3), while applying specific constraints to maintain consistency between
263   correspondences (Bernard et al., 2019; Swoboda et al., 2019). However, this method becomes im-
264   practical as K increases, making it unsuitable for larger-scale problems.
265   Another direction leverages the barycenter concept from optimal transport, which identifies a central
266   distribution that minimizes the weighted sum of Wasserstein distances to the given input distribu-
267   tions. We follow this idea to reformulate the alignment of K graphs into K separate alignments
268   with a barycenter graph. Unlike previous unsupervised methods that estimate the barycenter before
269   aligning, we directly define the barycenter using known triplet pairs across the three graphs. This
      significantly reduces complexity, making our solver more efficient in LLM settings.

      4.2 DATA - HUNGRY P ROBLEM OF AUTOREGRESSIVE
368
369
      We begin by presenting the data-intensive nature of autoregressive training. To assess this, we
      used LLaVA-Med, a state-of-the-art multimodal large language model in the biomedical domain.
370
      LLaVA-Med follows a two-stage in their training process: Stage 1 aligns image-text tokens with
371
      biomedical concepts, and Stage 2 fine-tunes the model for instruction-based tasks. We pre-trained
372   LLaVA-Med on varying data amounts (10%, 40%, 70%) and used the checkpoints to fine-tune
373   the visual question answering downstream task. As shown in Figure 1, performance on the VQA-
374   RAD dataset sharply declines when using 10% pre-training data compared with fully-training on
375   100%. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first time that the auto-regressive mechanism’s
376   data dependency issue in medical-MLLM has been revealed. This evidence indicates that without
377   using enough instruction-tuning data, the auto-regressive creates a weak connection between visual
      features and text embeddings in LLM.

381   ing data sizes and utilizing the checkpoints for instruction fine-tuning. Figure 1 demonstrates that

524   5 C ONCLUSION
525
      We have shown that enforcing triplet correlations among image modalities, their instruction data, and
526   the extended contextual captions can improve vision-language alignment, which is often lacking in
527   models trained by auto-regressive, especially given less pre-training data size as demonstrated for
528   the LLaVa-Med model. We also present L O G RA -M ED, a new multi-graph alignment algorithm to
529   handle such requirements, which is efficient in training and matches LLAVA-Med’s performance on
530   just 10% of the training data while outperforming other state-of-the-art methods on various tasks.
531   These findings underscore that selecting the appropriate learning algorithm for training MLLMs is
532   as crucial as scaling model size or data volume.
533
      Limitations and Future Work While we have conducted several experiments using the LLaVa
534   model, it is crucial to validate L O G RA -M ED with other architectures, such as the Flamingo model
535   (Alayrac et al., 2022). Additionally, integrating a vision encoder or LLM specifically trained on
536   medical sources (Chen et al., 2023b; MH Nguyen et al., 2024) holds promise for enhancing perfor-
537   mance and improving robustness—an approach we have not yet evaluated. Lastly, we propose to
538   investigate adaptor-based methods (Hu et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023b) for fine-tuning downstream
539   tasks, as frozen backbone models can significantly reduce memory usage and maximize efficiency.

982    Theorem 2 enables us to characterise the optimal transport problem between two measures as a
983    curve in the space of measures, with the objective of minimising its total length. Furthermore,
984    this formulation is beneficial for deriving global minima results for non-convex particles in gradi-

986    Bach, 2018). By definition, a geodesic between G1 and G2 is a shortest path between these two

       graphs. In particular, the computation of distances along constant speed geodesic paths is a rela-

1223   Evaluation Protocol We evaluate the ability of models to serve as a medical visual chatbot as
1224
       follows: each of the 193 novel questions in the dataset has a corresponding ground-truth answer. We
       generate response to each question from the LMM. Next, we ask GPT-4 to score the helpfulness,
1225
       relevance, accuracy and level of details of the response from the LMM relative to the ground-truth
1226
       answer. The score is therefore in the scale of 0 to 100, where higher score indicates better overall
1227   performance. During our project, we are unable to access the GPT-4 version used by LLaVA-Med
1228   due to deprecation. Therefore, we opt for GPT-4o version as a judge. We also use this GPT version
1229   to reproduce the results reported in LLaVA-Med paper and observe decrease in performance. This
1230   may be due to the fact that GPT-4o serve as a better judge than previous version and thus judge the
1231   model’s response harder.
