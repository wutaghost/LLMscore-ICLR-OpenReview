009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            With the rapid advancement of large language models (LLMs), the diversity of
012            multi-LLM tasks and the variability in their pricing structures have become increas-
013            ingly important, as costs can vary greatly between different LLMs. To tackle these
014            challenges, we introduce the C2MAB-V, a Cost-effective Combinatorial Multi-
015            armed Bandit with Versatile reward models for optimal LLM selection and usage.
016
               This online model differs from traditional static approaches or those reliant on
               a single LLM without cost consideration. With multiple LLMs deployed on a
017
               scheduling cloud and a local server dedicated to handling user queries, C2MAB-
018
               V facilitates the selection of multiple LLMs over a combinatorial search space,
019            specifically tailored for various collaborative task types with different reward mod-
020            els. Based on our designed online feedback mechanism and confidence bound
021            technique, C2MAB-V can effectively address the multi-LLM selection challenge
022            by managing the exploration-exploitation trade-off across different models, while
023            also balancing cost and reward for diverse tasks. The NP-hard integer linear
024            programming problem for selecting multiple LLMs with trade-off dilemmas is
025            addressed by: i) decomposing the integer problem into a relaxed form by the local
026            server, ii) utilizing a discretization rounding scheme that provides optimal LLM
027
               combinations by the scheduling cloud, and iii) continual online updates based on
               feedback. Theoretically, we prove that C2MAB-V offers strict guarantees over ver-
028
               satile reward models, matching state-of-the-art results for regret and violations in
029
               some degenerate cases. Empirically, we show that C2MAB-V effectively balances
030            performance and cost-efficiency with nine LLMs for three application scenarios.

033   1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035   In the digital era of today, large language models (LLMs) such as ChatGPT lead innovations in
036   computational linguistics and cognitive processing Tian et al. (2023). The emergence of numerous
037   high-performance LLMs has sparked significant interest in the challenge of model selection Foster
038   et al. (2019); Wang et al. (2023). Typically, current schemes for selecting LLMs often rely on
039
      identifying the best-performing model in a static setting, such as selecting the LLM that achieves the
      lowest perplexity score Salazar et al. (2019); Peng et al. (2023). However, the diverse capabilities
040
      of various LLMs present an opportunity to adopt a task-specific selection approach, where different
041
      LLMs have their own strengths and weaknesses, e.g., Investlm Yang et al. (2023) is specifically
042   designed for the financial sector and may better suit queries related to investments. Moreover, the
043   limitations of static selection methods become more pronounced due to factors like “generation
044   diversity”, where a less expensive LLM may perform better in certain scenarios Chen et al. (2023), and
045   “data drift”, which refers to changes in the characteristics of answers generated in real-time compared
046   to those of training data Bhardwaj et al. (2022). Consequently, beyond large-scale pre-training
047   methods, these issues highlight the need for an “online” approach to optimize decision-making
048   in selecting the “appropriate” LLMs, which leverage continuous feedback to adapt to the varying
049   performance levels of models and diverse application needs through ongoing user interaction.
050   Furthermore, scenarios combining multiple LLMs (or agents) to complete tasks have commonly
051   emerged, moving beyond using a single LLM. Platforms like Poe (2024) have spearheaded func-
052   tionalities that integrate several bots within a single chat session. Liu et al. (2023c) introduces a
053   dynamic LLM-agent network through dynamic interaction architecture and intelligent agent team
      optimization. Hong et al. (2023) proposes a meta-programming framework that enhances the col-

054
      laboration of multiple LLMs. Gupta et al. (2024) explores the implementation of LLM cascades
055   for generative tasks. However, previous works have not considered optimizations tailored to the
056   characteristics of different tasks, which may feature different forms of rewards. Below, we present
057   three streamlined collaborative combinations of LLMs for different tasks: 1. For user experience
058   enhancement, multiple LLMs may be deployed to ensure satisfactory outcomes. 2. In educational
059   tutoring, subject-specific LLMs operate in parallel, with failures in one not severely impacting others.
060   3. In project development, LLMs manage different sub-modules, where any module’s failure could
061   jeopardize the entire project. These three examples underscore the importance of combining multiple
062   suitable LLMs based on the specific “task structure”.
063   Additionally, it is crucial to recognize that the emergence of LLMs with diverse performance levels
064   introduces varying costs in practical use, an important factor neglected in existing research. The
065   operating expenses for LLMs are high; for example, it is estimated that running ChatGPT costs over
066   $700,000 daily, and deploying GPT-4 for customer service could cost a small business upwards of
067   $21,000 per month Chen et al. (2023). This implies the necessity of incorporating cost considerations
068   into the selection and utilization strategies for LLMs.
069   Based on the discussions above, we introduce the Cost-effective Combinatorial Multi-armed Bandit
070   with Versatile reward models (C2MAB-V), designed to synergize the integration of diverse LLMs
071   across different task types. C2MAB-V manages the dual challenges of selecting LLMs that both
072   achieve high performance and meet cost constraints. Additionally, C2MAB-V adapts to various multi-
073   LLM collaborative tasks by utilizing a “combinatorial model selection strategy” which extends beyond
074   traditional single-model limitations by encompassing a broad spectrum of LLM candidates. Finally,
075
      to address the NP-hard complexities of combinatorial LLM selection under cost considerations, we
      transform the initial problem into a continuous form. This process is executed on the local server,
076
      which is required to handle user queries. Meanwhile, our discretization method, which guarantees
077
      precise evaluation of LLM performance post-relaxation, is implemented on the scheduling cloud
078   where multiple LLMs are deployed. This two-tiered approach can accommodate both the limited
079   resources of the local server and strategically mitigate the scheduling cloud’s workload. We emphasize
080   that this paper focuses on a fully online learning approach that operates without initial information
081   and relies solely on user feedback with robust interpretation. Our method is designed to complement,
082   not compete with, current approaches that utilize GPU resources for offline training, where offline
083   training can streamline the search space and enhance the convergence speed of online learning.
084   In summary, our contributions are as follows.
085
086   Novel Multi-LLM Selection Formulation. We introduce a novel formulation of the cost-effective
087
      multi-LLM selection, designed for tasks requiring collaboration among multiple LLMs with versatile
      reward structures. This formulation emphasizes the essential balance between exploring new models
088
      and exploiting proven effective models, while adhering to long-term cost budget considerations and
089
      securing high rewards across diverse multi-LLM tasks.
090
091   Unique Online Algorithmic Framework. We developed the C2MAB-V framework for online
092   multi-LLM selection, managing diverse collaborative LLM tasks and performance variability due to
093
      generation diversity and data drift. Our approach utilizes a “combinatorial bandit selection strategy”
      with a cost-conscious versatile reward structure. Based on a natural local-cloud architecture, the local
094
      server, with its limited resources, simplifies the selection process and alleviates the computational
095
      load on the scheduling cloud. The cloud coordinates and selects LLMs based on continualized
096   feedback data from the local server, which also gathers user feedback to enhance LLM evaluations.
097
098   In-Depth Regret and Violation Analysis. We conduct a thorough theoretical analysis of our
099
      online C2MAB-V framework, covering three distinct reward models. This analysis addresses the
      trade-offs between reward and violation, and between exploration and exploitation. We identify
100
      common properties across different reward models and employ “martingale construction techniques”
101
      to examine the stochastic properties of our model under varying collaborative tasks. Notably, our
102   results on regret and violation analysis match the state-of-the-art results in several degenerate cases.
103
104   Comprehensive Empirical Validation. Our C2MAB-V framework has been empirically validated
105
      with superior performance outcomes across evaluations involving nine distinct LLMs. These tests
      consistently confirm C2MAB-V’s capacity to adaptively navigate the trade-off dilemmas, resulting
106
      in amplified rewards or decreased costs. Moreover, detailed analysis from exploratory experiments
107
      provides deeper insights into the strategic design and utilization of the multi-LLM approach.

112   Combinatorial Multi-Armed Bandit. The field of online learning problems under the multi-armed
113   bandit (MAB) model has been extensively studied. The MAB model was first introduced by the
114   seminal work Robbins (1952) and has been expanded upon by many other researchers ( Liu et al.
115   (2021c); Slivkins et al. (2019); Liu et al. (2023a)). Traditional MAB models focus on selecting a single
116   arm per trial; however, the more complex scenario of combinatorial MAB (CMAB) involves selecting
117
      multiple arms simultaneously. The stochastic CMAB has received much attention recently Chen
      et al. (2016); Kveton et al. (2015b); Wang & Chen (2017); Merlis & Mannor (2019; 2020). Initial
118
      research on stochastic CMAB is spearheaded by Gai et al. (2012), with subsequent improvements in
119
      regret bounds offered by Kveton et al. (2015c); Combes et al. (2015). Later on, Chen et al. (2016);
120   Wang & Chen (2017) considers probabilistic feedback to extend the feedback model. Recently, Liu
121   et al. (2022) proposes the variance-adaptive algorithm BCUCB-T. Liu et al. (2023a) incorporates the
122   contextual information in CUCB. Our research builds on the CMAB settings but diverges significantly
123   by addressing more complicated trade-off issues. Furthermore, to address various cooperative task
124   types, we explore three different combinatorial reward formulations within versatile reward models.
125   Please refer to the additional literature in Appendix A.2, and the unique challenges in Appendix A.3.
126   Multi-LLM Combination. The combination of multiple LLM models for enhanced performance
127   has received considerable attention, aimed at bolstering output quality Kim et al. (2023). Techniques
128   such as “knowledge distillation” Hinton et al. (2015); Sanh et al. (2019) facilitate the training of
129   compact models to mimic the behavior of larger and more complex models, thus optimizing resource
130   utilization. Meanwhile, “ensemble learning” Hokamp et al. (2020); Huy et al. (2022) leverages the
131   collective predictions of independently trained models for superior results. Nonetheless, the prevalent
132   practice of withholding model internals from commercial LLM, such as OpenAI (2023), restricts
133   knowledge distillation by obscuring the “teacher” model, which can decrease the replication efficacy
134
      of the “student” model. Concurrently, ensemble learning faces increased financial and procedural
      burdens due to the necessity of amalgamating various model outputs, a task complicated by the
135
      absence of open-source models. Madaan et al. (2023); Ding et al. (2024); Chen et al. (2023) primarily
136
      address one of the scenarios we have outlined, i.e., selecting a single LLM that best satisfies the user
137   from multiple options. Moreover, these approaches do not incorporate user feedback. Our approach
138   distinctively accounts for the online aspects of model selection, the diversity of multi-LLM task types,
139   and the associated costs, setting it apart from previous methods.

191   In this section, we introduce our online framework of a cost-effective combinatorial bandit for
192   multi-LLM selection with versatile reward models, namely, C2MAB-V (Please refer to Fig. 3 for
193
      details). A summary of the main symbols is provided in Appendix A.1.
194   Local-Cloud Architecture. Given the large number of parameters and significant storage overhead of
195   LLMs, a typical approach, if not opting for a streamlined but less capable version of LLMs, involves
196   deploying multiple LLMs on a resource-abundant scheduling cloud. When responding to user queries,
197   the local server first handles the requests and synchronizes communication with the cloud to initiate
198   tasks (for discussions on asynchronous handling, see Appendix E.3). Subsequently, user feedback
199
      is stored on the local server.1 While the scheduling cloud serves multiple local servers, for ease of
      presentation, we focus on describing the relationship between a single local server and the cloud.
200
201   Combinatorial LLM Instance. The scheduling cloud orchestrates multiple independent LLMs, to
202   effectively fulfill requests from the local server. Let K = {1, . . . , K} represent the set of all LLMs,
203   where each index k ∈ K corresponds to a specific LLM (i.e., base arm), comprising a total of K LLMs.
204   The system operates in a time-slotted manner, delineated by discrete intervals T = {1, 2, 3, . . . , T }.
205
      During each round t ∈ T , the cloud coordinates and selects a subset St of the available LLMs from
      K. This selection process, termed an “action”, adapts dynamically based on real-time constraints and
206
      availability, with the cardinality |St | ≤ K indicating the number of LLMs selected. Let S represent
207
      the set of all possible combinations of actions.2 For example, high-demand workloads may cause
208   GPT-4 to reach its usage limit, temporarily preventing its selection. Let N = maxS∈S |S|, denoting
209   the maximum number of LLMs that can be simultaneously active.
210
211
      Online Learning Protocol. A combinatorial LLM instance involves the sequential interaction
      between the local server, the scheduling cloud, and user queries within our local-cloud architecture.

      The local server processes user activity and feedback, particularly focusing on the users’ performance
217   feedback regarding the utilized LLMs. This process involves locally recording and updating the
218   performance evaluations of the LLM. Then, the local server will transmit these information to the
219   scheduling cloud. The scheduling cloud undertakes the new coordination and selection of an action
220   St (i.e., selecting a subset of LLMs for service) after receiving the newly updated information from
221   the local server. Such performance evaluations, termed as rewards, could, for instance, be based
222   on the ROUGE-2 score for automatic summarization tasks Lin (2004), and are represented by a
223   random variable vector X t = (Xt,1 , . . . , Xt,|St | ). For simplification, we posit that X t ∈ [0, 1]|St | .
224   Furthermore, for each LLM k ∈ K included in the action St , its associated cost yt,k is observable at
225   round t. A detailed definition of this cost will be provided later.
226   Note that we demonstrate a fully online learning process here without any prior information for a clear
227   description. In real-world scenarios, online learning can often be accelerated by incorporating offline
228   pre-training. For example, for mathematical queries, narrowing the action space by offering fine-
229   tuned LLMs for specific mathematical tasks, rather than selecting from a broad set, can significantly
230   streamline the online selection process.
231   Versatile Reward Models. Let µ = (µ1 , ..., µK ) represent the “initially unknown” mean vector
232   of outcomes for each LLM with µk = E[Xt,k ]. We consider versatile reward models for different
233   combinations of LLMs in multi-LLM tasks below. Q
234   • Any Win Combination (AWC): r(S; µ) = 1 − k∈S (1 − µk ) . As illustrated in Fig. 2, this
235     reward model is designed to safeguard user experience by selecting multiple LLMs to generate
236     answers, with success defined as any LLM’s answer satisfying the user. This reward model aims to
237
        maximize user satisfaction by providing a range
                                                     P of possible solutions.
      • Sum Up Combination (SUC): r(S; µ) = k∈S µk . In this setup, domain-specific LLMs inde-
238
        pendently tackle tasks in parallel. Each LLM earns rewards for correctly answering questions in its
239
        field. This reward model aims to speed up task completion and reduces the workload on each LLM,
240     enhancing overall task effectiveness.
241

      • All In Combination (AIC): r(S; µ) = k∈S µk . This reward model is exemplified in devel-
242     opment tasks, where each LLM is responsible for developing sub-modules simultaneously. The
243     key aspect of this reward model is that the failure of any LLM leads to the failure of the entire
244     development task, thus ensuring the success of the whole collaborative work.
245   For more discussions (e.g., specify LLMs under the SUC and AIC reward models), please refer to
246   Appendix C.1. Compared to previous works Ding et al. (2024); Chen et al. (2023); Madaan et al.
247   (2023); Zhu et al. (2023), which primarily focus on selecting a single LLM to satisfy the user, our
248   versatile reward model accounts for the diversity of tasks, addressing scenarios where multi-LLMs
249   collaborate to complete tasks, besides the distinction being our focus on an online learning setting.
250   Partial LLM Feedback. In the process of selecting and querying LLM in action St , not all LLMs
251   may actually be queried in the muti-LLM tasks. Consequently, the local server often only observes
252   outcomes from a subset of the selected LLMs. Formally, the local server observes feedback from the
253   LLM subset Ft in action St , where the subset’s cardinality is denoted as Ft . If all LLMs in St are
254   queried, then Ft = |St |, which is worst-case scenario under the AWC reward model.
255   Statistically-Based Cost Model. Drawing inspiration from Alibabacloud-Opensearch (2024), which
256   charges based on statistical computational resource utilization (with 1 CU supporting an average of
257   10 interactions), Awan-LLM (2024), which offers unlimited token pricing without the consideration
258   of token limits, and JD-Cloud-Yanxi-AI (2024), which bills based on exclusive resource groups, we
259   propose a statistically-based cost model. Specifically, for any query q from a set Q, LLM k ∈ K
260   processes the query using a deterministic number of input tokens, lkin (q), and generates a random
261   number of output tokens, denoted as lkout (q) ∼ Dkout (q), representing the probabilistic nature of LLMs
262
      Xie et al. (2021); Dalal & Misra (2024). Given a distribution Dq over Q, a user selects a query
263   qt ∼ Dq in each round, and the cost for LLM k is yt,k = (lkin (qt ) + lkout (qt ))Ck , where Ck is the cost
264
      per token. The goal is to estimate the unknown expected cost ck = E[yt,k ].

408   Discretization Rounding for LLM Selection. In our architecture, the scheduling cloud can commu-
409   nicate with each local server, while the local servers do not directly communicate with each other.
410   As described in Section 3, our focus is on elucidating the one-to-one relationship between a local
411   server and the cloud. With fully original user data stored locally, the local server sends the relaxed
412   continuous data Z̃ t to the scheduling cloud, which then coordinates various LLMs and selects a new
413   action in response to requests. Inspired by the works of Chekuri et al. (2009); Gandhi et al. (2006), the
414
      synchronized cloud employs specialized discretization rounding algorithms based on reward models
415
      to convert the relaxed continuous data back into discrete form. By discretizing Z̃ t , the scheduling
      cloud identifies a feasible set of LLMs St to schedule and select for the current round t (line 6 in
416
      Algorithm 1). The corresponding algorithms are tailored solutions for our versatile reward model
417
      and represent a significant effort to find suitable methods for our specific setup. However, since the
418   design of these algorithms is not our core contribution, details are provided in Appendix B. Note that
419   discretization rounding can reduce the complexity of LLM combination selection, as detailed in the
420   computational efficiency comparison with direct discrete constrained optimization in Appendix E.3.

526   7                                                         C ONCLUSION
527
528   In this paper, we introduce C2MAB-V, a cost-effective combinatorial online model with a versatile re-
529   ward structure, designed to efficiently select multiple LLMs based on specific task requirements while
530   adhering to budget constraints. C2MAB-V incorporates continual online feedback, and transform the
531   NP-hard multi-LLM selection problem into a manageable relaxed form, with specified discretization
532   rounding schemes designed to coordinate LLMs within a local-cloud architecture. Theoretical analy-
533   sis of C2MAB-V provides novel and robust guarantees for the efficacy of the framework, including
534   sublinear regret and a rapidly decreasing violation. Moreover, empirical evaluations with nine LLMs
535   demonstrate C2MAB-V’s capability to balance performance with cost efficiency across three distinct
536
      types of collaborative multi-LLM tasks.
537   Looking ahead, there are several compelling directions for future research. For example, further
538   development could focus on enhancing privacy protection and improving communication between
539   multiple local servers within our local-cloud architecture. Additionally, incorporating contextual
      combinatorial multi-armed bandit approaches could more effectively capture user preferences.

      A.3             TACKLING N EW C HALLENGES IN C OMBINATORIAL BANDITS
881
882   The key innovation of our approach lies in its formulation of constraint-effective combinatorial
883   multi-armed bandits, a new variant of combinatorial semi-bandits. While previous CMAB research
884   has made significant progress in minimizing regret, many existing approaches overlook the critical
885   aspect of cost constraints, often allowing for excessive violations. Our work directly addresses
886   this gap by adopting a constraint-effective perspective, where we aim to minimize both regret
887   and constraint violation simultaneously. Specifically, our approach combines traditional regret
888   minimization techniques with the novel challenge of managing cost constraints effectively.
889   A distinguishing feature of our work is the way we tackle the constrained optimization problem.
890   Traditional methods typically treat the constraint as a discrete optimization problem, which is often
891   NP-hard and computationally inefficient. Direct solutions to this problem tend to yield suboptimal
892   approximation ratios. In contrast, we propose a relaxation and rounding (RR) technique, which
893   transforms the problem into a continuous optimization problem. This transformation not only
894   improves approximation guarantees but also offers better time complexity compared to traditional
895   methods. However, implementing RR techniques in an online setting presents new challenges, as
896
      RR methods were originally designed for offline optimization problems. The flexibility in choosing
      relaxation functions and rounding procedures complicates the guarantee of low regret and low
897
      violation in the online case. To overcome this challenge, we propose a detailed analysis that identifies
898
      the conditions under which the RR approach can effectively perform in online scenarios. This involves
899   establishing a connection between regret and over-estimation terms, which can be bounded using
900   standard CMAB analysis. Additionally, we introduce a novel martingale construction technique
901   that allows us to bound long-term violations of the constraints, a critical aspect in addressing online
902   combinatorial optimization problems.
903
      Furthermore, our approach handles non-linear reward functions and partial feedback, which are essen-
904
      tial for addressing real-world LLM task scenarios. These challenges have not been comprehensively
905   tackled in existing CMAB works.

953   examination of set K to identify LLM k such that their corresponding value z̃t,k lies strictly between
954   0 and 1 (line 1). Subsequently, Algorithm 3 progresses by identifying a pair of distinct LLM, k and j,

956   procedure involves computing the probabilities for adjusting the values of z̃t,k and z̃t,j . This is
957   achieved by determining p and q, which represent the minimum increments and decrements needed
958
      for the adjustment, ensuring the discretization stays within bounds (line 3). With these parameters,
      Algorithm 3 probabilistically updates the pair (z̃t,k , z̃t,j ) to either increase z̃t,k and decrease z̃t,j ,
959
      or vice versa, thus balancing the overall distribution (line 4). Finally, Algorithm 3 concludes by
960
      assembling the set St comprised of all k ∈ K for which z̃t,k = 1, marking them as the selected
961   LLM for the given timestep (line 6). This discrete selection process, grounded in the probabilistic
962   adjustments of the z̃ values, systematically refines the selection of LLM.

      One fundamental aspect of the reward r(S; µ) is its demonstration of the “diminishing marginal”
      property. Specifically, when a new LLM k ∈ K is added into action Sj with a relatively larger set

967   submodular function, formally expressed as follows:

969                         r(Si ∪ {k}; µ) − r(Si ; µ) ≥ r(Sj ∪ {k}; µ) − r(Sj ; µ).                           (9)

988    In the context of C2MAB-V, we define ground base arms as K = {1, ..., K} and denote S as
989    combinatorially feasible sets, structured by specific combinatorial frameworks. A set S is linearizable
990    if its convex hull forms a polytope in RK , meaning we can describe it using a finite number of
991    linear constraints in RK , with S representing the integral solutions. Matroids represent a prominent
992    category of linearizable combinatorial sets, characterized by:
993
              • Containment of the empty set: ∅ ∈ S.
994           • Downward closure: For any S ∈ S and S ′ ⊂ S, it follows that S ′ ∈ S.
995           • Exchange property: For S, S ′ ∈ S with |S ′ | > |S|, there exists an k ∈ S ′ \ S such that
996             S ∪ {i} ∈ S.
997
       For any S ∈ S, the rank function rank(S) is defined as the maximal size of independent subsets
998
       in S, and IS indicates a vector in {0, 1}K representing the membership of elements in S. The
999
       polytope induced by S, denoted as P (S), is defined as the convex hull of these characteristic vectors,
1000   encapsulating the feasible integral solutions for S.

1003
       B(S), is the intersection of P (S) with a hyperplane defined by k∈K z̃k = N . Examples of
       matroids include:

1868   Based on our online learning-based multi-LLM selection algorithm C2MAB-V, multiple LLMs are
1869

1870

1874   be entirely synchronous. Therefore, we explore an asynchronous local-cloud architecture with a batch
1875

1876
       re-coordinate multiple LLMs and adjust the multi-model selection strategy. Until then, the previous
1877
       multi-LLM selection strategy remains in use. We investigate the changes in reward and violation
1878   for batch sizes of 10, 50, 100, and 200. As shown in Fig. 14, the largest batch size of 200 exhibits
1879   relatively lower rewards and higher violations, which is intuitive. However, there is no significant
1880   difference in the reward and violation values of C2MAB-V under different batch sizes. This indicates
1881   that the choice of batch size may not critically impact the overall performance within the tested range

1924
       In this section, we explore three representative applications that fit within our framework and
1925
       summarize the results in Table 11, providing comparisons with existing works.
1926
1927   Dynamic Assortment: In this problem Sankararaman & Slivkins (2018), an agent selects N products
1928   from K available options over T rounds to maximize total sales, subject to a cost constraint. Each
1929   product k has an unknown probability µi of being purchased and an expected resource consumption
1930
       ck . The average resource consumption over T rounds must remain below a threshold ρ. For this
       application, the goal is to maximize the total reward, making it suitable for the SUC reward model.
1931
1932   Network Routing: In this scenario Kveton et al. (2015b), an agent selects a path in a network with
1933   K routers to maximize the probability of successful packet delivery. Each router k has an unknown
1934   uptime probability µk and incurs a random cost with an expected value ck . The agent observes
1935   whether the packet is delivered and the costs of the chosen routers, aiming to keep the average
1936
       cost below ρ over T rounds. For this application, if any router in the selected path fails, the entire
       connection fails, making it suitable for the AIC reward model.
1937
1938   Movie Recommendations: In this setting Kveton et al. (2015a), an agent (e.g., Netflix) recommends
1939   a list of up to N movies from K options over T rounds. Each movie k has an unknown probability
1940   µk of being found attractive and incurs a random royalty cost with an expected value ck . Users
1941   sequentially scan the list and click on the first attractive movie, which generates a reward. The agent
1942
       aims to maximize the click probability while keeping the average cost below ρ. For this application,
       users stop browsing as soon as they find a satisfactory movie, making it suitable for the AWC reward
1943
       model.
