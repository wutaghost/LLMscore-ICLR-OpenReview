011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Interpretablility in recent deep learning models has become an epicenter of re-
014            search particularly in sensitive domains such as healthcare, and finance. Concept
015            bottleneck models have emerged as a promising approach for achieving trans-
016            parency and interpretability by leveraging a set of human-understandable con-
017            cepts as an intermediate representation before the prediction layer. However,
018
               manual concept annotation is discouraged due to the time and effort involved.
               Our work explores the potential of large language models (LLMs) for gener- ating
019
               high-quality concept banks and proposes a multimodal evaluation metric to assess
020
               the quality of generated concepts. We investigate three key research questions:
021            the ability of LLMs to generate concept banks comparable to existing knowl-
022            edge bases like ConceptNet, the sufficiency of unimodal text-based seman- tic
023            similarity for evaluating concept-class label associations, and the effectiveness of
024            multimodal information in quantifying concept generation quality compared to
025            unimodal concept-label semantic similarity. Our findings reveal that multimodal
026            models outperform unimodal approaches in capturing concept-class label simi-
027            larity. Furthermore, our generated concepts for the CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100
028            datasets surpass those obtained from ConceptNet and the baseline comparison,
029
               demonstrating the standalone capability of LLMs in generating high-quality con-
               cepts. Being able to automatically generate and evaluate high-quality concepts
030
               will enable researchers to quickly adapt and iterate to a newer dataset with little to
031
               no effort before they can feed that into concept bottleneck models.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
036
037   In recent years, large-scale machine learning models, particularly deep neural networks, have
038   achieved remarkable improvements in accuracy across various domains. However, these advance-
039
      ments have often come at the expense of interpretability and transparency, making it challenging to
      understand the internal decision-making processes of these models. This lack of clarity and inter-
040
      pretability poses significant limitations to their deployment in critical areas where the consequences
041
      of incorrect predictions can be severe. In domains such as medical diagnostics, healthcare, public
042   infrastructure safety, and visual inspection for civil engineering, the ability to explain and justify the
043   decisions made by these models is of utmost importance (Li et al., 2022b). Stakeholders in these
044   fields require a clear understanding of the reasoning behind the model’s predictions to ensure that
045   the outcomes align with established domain knowledge and best practices. Without this level of
046   transparency, the trustworthiness and reliability of these models come into question, hindering their
047   widespread adoption in safety-critical applications (Gao & Guan, 2023).
048   To address this challenge, researchers and practitioners are actively exploring methods to enhance
049   the interpretability of machine learning models while maintaining their impressive performance
050   (Singh et al., 2020). Techniques such as feature importance analysis, SHAP, rule extraction, and vi-
051   sual explanations aim to provide insights into the factors influencing the model’s predictions (Zhang
052   et al., 2021; Bujwid & Sullivan, 2021; Lundberg & Lee, 2017). By bridging the gap between the
053   model’s internal workings and human understanding, these approaches seek to achieve greater con-
      fidence in the use of machine learning in high-stakes scenarios.

      Concept Bottleneck Models (CBMs) is one of the techniques to have gained significant attention in
055   the field of Artificial Intelligence due to their ability to provide interpretable explanations for model
056   predictions. Just before the classification layer, Concept-Bottleneck models have a bottleneck layer
057   that comprise of human-interpretable concepts (Oikarinen et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023). Concept
058   Activation Vectors (CAVs) also provide human-friedly interpretation of the existing classification
059   models (Kim et al., 2018a). A recent modification of CBM known as Counterfactual CBM is pro-
060   posed which uses counterfactual explanations by emphasizing not only on ”why” but also on ”what
061   if” by providing alternate counterfactuals concepts (Dominici et al., 2024). While aforementioned
062   approaches are promising and highly interpretable, they suffer from two major challenges: 1) Con-
063
      cept generation turns out to be a key challenge in concept-bottleneck models as high number of
      related concepts generally tend to produce better bottleneck layer resulting in more interpretabil-
064
      ity; 2) There is limited literature on independently evaluating, and hence improving concept quality
065
      before feeding them to the CBM pipeline.
066
067   In order to overcome the aforementioned challenges, there have been attempts to automate concept
068   generation and quality improvement. For generating high quality concepts, earlier methods have
069
      mostly relied on manual concept annotation (Koh et al., 2020). While this method may generate
      concepts of reasonable quality, it has huge resource limitations and relies completely on human
070
      understanding of the underlying classes. Moreover, this method cannot be generalized across newer
071
      datasets as new class labels will require concept annotation from scratch. To alleviate this problem,
072   some researchers have also proposed the idea of augmenting the base human-labelled concepts by
073   using LLMs via in-context learning (Tan et al., 2024). A few researchers have also focused on
074   leveraging concept annotations in datasets where it is readily available and utilise multimodal models
075   to learn or discover new set of concepts (Wang et al., 2023; Yuksekgonul et al., 2022). Recent
076   advancements in Large Language Models (LLMs) have shown promising results in various natural
077   language processing tasks (Kojima et al., 2022). LLMs, such as GPT-3 have the ability to generate
078   coherent and meaningful text based on a given prompt. This capability can be leveraged to automate
079   the concept generation process in CBMs, reducing the manual effort required and improving the
080
      overall efficiency of the model (Yang et al., 2023).
081   While concept generation has been automated by the use of pre-trained LLMs, the quality assess-
082   ment of the generated concepts still remains to be a challenge. Current approaches rely on run-
083   ning end-to-end pipeline for CBM in order to assess the quality of the generated concepts. Higher
084   scores in CBM classification predictions are automatically interpreted as a generating good con-
085   cepts (Oikarinen et al., 2023; Yuksekgonul et al., 2022). This approach does not directly quantify
086
      the quality of generated concepts. Moreover, it also requires extensive resources as running complete
      end-to-end pipeline is not computationally inexpensive.
087
088   In this research, we propose an unsupervised concept generation and evaluation technique which
089   could help evaluate and iterate on the generated concepts at an early stage of CBM classification.
090   Our method aims to eliminate the reliance on manual annotation and improving the interpretability
091   of CBMs. We also evaluate text-based model for concept quality evaluation to see how well can
092
      it quantify the overall concept quality. Our work is inspired by some of the approaches that have
      achieved success in similar tasks (Semenov et al., 2024; Bhaskar et al., 2017; Kritharoula et al.,
093
      2023).
094
095   We emphasize on the following three Research Questions in the given research:
096
097          • RQ1: Can large language models without visual information generate good enough con-
098            cept bank as compared to the existing knowledge bases such as ConceptNet?
099          • RQ2: Is unimodal text-based semantic similarity enough to evaluate the association be-
100            tween concepts and class labels?
101
             • RQ3: Is multimodal information enough to quantify the quality of the concept generation
102            as opposed to the unimodal concept-labels semantic similarity?

105   2   L ITERATURE R EVIEW
106
107   Concept Bottleneck models have been used to add a layer of interpretability to the black-box deep
      learning-based classification algorithms. However their performance comparatively remains to be


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      limited due to the lack of good quality concepts. There are various approaches for improving per-
109   formance of concept-bottleneck models.
110
111   Early approaches rely heavily on manually hand-crafted concepts which may result in good hu-
112
      man interpretation but are not scalable and require manual labor (Koh et al., 2020). Moreover, the
      concepts are limited to human’s capability and understanding of the domain and are likely to miss
113
      some important concepts (Shang et al., 2024). Iterating and evaluating these concepts requires more
114
      manual intervention which becomes infeasible due to the lack of time and resources. Addition-
115   ally, manual concept generation is subjective and the quality of concepts may rely on the individual
116   brilliance of the annotators.
117
118
      A few researchers propose methods to partially eliminate the manual annotation by proposing to
      learn concepts from the dataset. For instance, (Wang et al., 2023) uses self-supervision to simulta-
119
      neously learn concepts and classification objective. It uses slot attention-based mechanism to spot
120
      the region where corresponding concept is found. It learns a set of k concepts while k is a hyper-
121   parameter and is set to 5. Their experiments demonstrate that contrastive loss with self-supervision
122   really contribute to concept discovery. The authors evaluate the accuracy of their proposed approach
123   by generating synthetic dataset proposed in . There is one limitation of this approach and is as-
124   sociated with tuning the number of k-concepts for each dataset which hurts the scalability across
125   datasets.
126
      Another approach relies on base high-quality human annotated concepts to create a seed concept
127   bank. These concepts are incrementally bootstrapped by learning and optimizing learnable vectors
128   initalized from multimodal model such as CLIP (Radford et al., 2021). These ambiguous and unclear
129   vectors are then translated into potentially meaningful concepts by using concept discovery module.
130   Lastly, they introduce a metric to evaluate concept utilisation efficiency (Shang et al., 2024). While
131   their approach seems to marginally outperform existing models, it relies on high quality initial seed
132   concept bank which requires manual effort.
133   Hierarchical concept learning has also been explored to improve the performance of concept-
134   bottleneck models by aiming to produce better concepts (Sun et al., 2024). The idea here is to
135   avoid information leakage issue by introducing supervised learning in concept prediction. The au-
136   thors establish notable improvements in model performance as concept prediction results in better
137   concepts.
138
      Due to the widening popularity of LLMs for unsupervised learning, recent articles have also dived
139   deeper into utilising them for concept generation. A recent study Oikarinen et al. (2023) proposed
140   a label-free concept bottleneck mechanism to generate models using GPT-3 model. They also filter
141   concepts by utilising vision and text encoders to compute similarities between concepts and classes.
142   A similar approach is proposed by Yang et al. (2023) where the concepts are generated automat-
143   ically by LLM. However, concept filtering is performed by using submodular optimization which
144   tends to be more effective compared to the static rules applied in the former approach. While these
145   methods do alleviate the manual effort of generating concepts, they rely on a paid GPT-3 API. These
146   approaches also evaluate concept quality based solely on the final results of classification which
147
      is resource intensive. Moreover, their models do not outperform existing model such as Standard
      sparse model on CUB-200 dataset.
148
149   Another popular approach is the use of TCAV (Testing with Concept Activation Vectors) for in-
150   terpreting neural network decisions, the researchers demonstrated several key findings (Kim et al.,
151   2018b). TCAV provides a human-friendly linear interpretation of deep learning models, offering
152   insights into model decisions through natural high-level concepts that do not need to be predefined
153
      during training. The approach supports accessibility, customization, plug-in readiness, and global
      quantification, making it a versatile tool that requires minimal machine learning expertise to em-
154
      ploy. Unfortunately, the assumption of linearity between concept and predictions does not always
155
      hold true resulting in performance degradation where non-linear dependencies need to be captured.
156
157   ConceptSHAP improves the assessment of concept importance in model explanations by adapting
158   Shapley values to fairly assign the importance of each concept (Yeh et al., 2020). This adaptation
159
      allows it to uniquely satisfy desired axioms such as efficiency, symmetry, dummy, and additivity.
      Specifically, ConceptSHAP measures how much each individual concept contributes to the over-
160
      all completeness score of the model, which helps in evaluating the importance of each discovered
161
      concept in explaining the model’s decisions. By providing both global attribution and per-class


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      saliency, ConceptSHAP offers a more nuanced and interpretable understanding of how different
163   concepts contribute to model predictions. This approach is validated through metrics and user stud-
164   ies on synthetic and real-world datasets, demonstrating its effectiveness in finding complete and
165   interpretable concept explanations.
166
      Multimodal models have also shown great improvements in tasks that involve multiple modalities
167
      such as vision and text (Li et al., 2022a). The idea of using multimodal models for concept an-
168
      notation by leveraging multimodal models to obtain concept representations is also explored (Yuk-
169   sekgonul et al., 2022). This involves learning the concept bank by training a linear SVM for each
170   concept. The vector normal to the boundary is used to represent the concept. Multimodal model
171   (CLIP Radford et al. (2021)) is then used to map each concept to a vector using text encoder. This
172   method has a number of limitations. Firstly, it necessitates the creation of a predefined library of
173   initial concepts, which may involve concept pruning, requiring human intervention via annotation.
174   Secondly, this approach relies on preexisting knowledge graphs, such as ConceptNet (Speer et al.,
175   2017), to identify relationships between classes and related concepts. While these knowledge graphs
176   provide valuable information, they may not always capture the nuances and context-specific relation-
177
      ships present in the particular task or dataset at hand. Lastly, it also requires supervised training of
      a classifier the tuning of which also requires additional effort when adapting to a newer dataset.
178
      Consequently, the effectiveness of this approach may be limited by the quality and relevance of the
179
      utilized knowledge graphs.
180
181   A recent approach proposes a hypothesis for quantifying concept similarity using an algorithm
182   called Concept Matrix Search (CMS) algorithm (Semenov et al., 2024). It generates concepts using
183
      ConceptNet, a popular freely-available commonsense knowledgebase, and utilises CLIP model for
      computing concept-image and concept-labels similarity matrices. It predicts the class label by us-
184
      ing cosine similarity for k-th image-concept and image-class concept. While the hypothesis seems
185
      reasonable, it relies on the fact that textual embeddings for class-concept have closer semantic ad-
186   herence to the image-concept mapping which may not always be true due to the abstract nature of
187   the concepts. For example, the distance between an image of class label ”apple” and the image of
188   a red apple may be closely associated while the semantics between the label ”apple” and concept
189   ”red” may not be close enough in the embeddings space. Moreover, ConceptNet is does not provide
190   comprehensive concepts for multi-word phrase classes.
191
      In conclusion, various approaches have been proposed to improve the performance of concept-
192   bottleneck models. These methods aim to enhance concept discovery, reduce information leakage,
193   and automate concept generation. While some approaches, such as self-supervision and hierarchical
194   concept learning, have shown notable improvements in model performance, others, like label-free
195   concept bottleneck mechanisms using GPT-3, still face challenges in terms of concept quality and
196   cost-effectiveness. Additionally, methods that rely on predefined concept libraries and knowledge
197   graphs may be limited by the relevance, accuracy, and completeness of these resources. Although
198   there are developments to this field, there remains room for further research and development in this
199   area to address the limitations and improve the scalability and generalizability of concept-bottleneck
200
      models across different datasets and tasks.

      To answer the aforementioned research questions, we need to establish benchmark datasets to quan-
208
      tify and compare the quality of two or more concept generation sources. Once we obtain these sets
209
      of concepts against respective classes, we can apply the proposed metric to compare and evaluate
210   their quality. Our core methodology tends to be completely unsupervised as generate we rely on

212   on exploiting the pretrained knowledge of the multimodal model like CLIP for making the predic-

214   need for additional training and dataset-specific manual labelling. The proposed approach will help
215   us quantify the quality of concept bank and iterate over them fast before feeding them to a larger
      concept-bottleneck based architecture.

228   as many concepts as possible. Due to API limitations, we use Sentence Transformer’s roberta-based
      1

      M ETHODOLOGY FOR I DENTIFYING T OP -C ONCEPTS ACROSS C LASSES
403
404   In order to identify the most representative concepts for each class, we employ a multi-step approach
405   that leverages classwise similarity scores between images and concepts. The process is outlined as
406   follows:
407
408         1. Classwise Similarity Score Computation: For each class, we calculate the similarity
409            scores between the images belonging to that class and the entire set of concepts.
410         2. Classwise Mode Determination: Once the classwise similarity scores are obtained, we
411            determine the mode (most frequently occurring value) for each class in order to get the
412            highest level of similarity across the images within a particular class.
413         3. Top-k Concept Selection: Based on the classwise modes, we select the top-k concepts for
414            each class corresponding their mode values.
415         4. Concept Retrieval: Finally, we retrieve the concepts associated with the top-k mode values
416            for each class.
417
418   By following this methodology, we are able to effectively identify the top-concepts across classes,
419
      providing a concise and meaningful representation of the visual content within each class. This
      approach enables us to gain insights into the key concepts that are statistically most relevant and
420
      discriminative for each class, facilitating further analysis and understanding of the underlying data.
421
422   The identification of top concepts across classes plays a crucial role in various applications, such as
423   image classification, retrieval, and understanding. By focusing on the most representative concepts
424   for each class, we can develop more efficient and accurate models that capture the essential charac-
425
      teristics of the visual data, ultimately leading to improved performance in downstream tasks. Top

459

460   erated concepts using this approach. Our findings demonstrate that multimodal models are indeed
461   necessary for accurately capturing the similarity between concepts and class labels, surpassing the
462   performance of unimodal methods like BERTScore as well as the baseline. Furthermore, our gener-

465   it is worth noting that our generated concepts for the CUB-200 dataset did not surpass those from
466   ConceptNet, highlighting the need for further investigation and improvement in this specific domain.
467   The implications of our research are significant for the field of concept generation and evaluation.

      existing knowledge bases, opening up new possibilities for automated concept generation. More-

470

473   emphasizes the importance of multimodal evaluation metrics. The findings suggest that LLMs have
474

      and accurate means of assessing concept quality. Future work can build upon these insights, explor-
