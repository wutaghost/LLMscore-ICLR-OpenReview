009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) have demonstrated impressive capabil-
012            ities for capturing and reasoning over multimodal inputs. However, these models
013            are prone to parametric knowledge conflicts, which arise from inconsistencies of
014            represented knowledge between their vision and language components. In this
015            paper, we formally define the problem of cross-modality parametric knowledge
016            conflict and present a systematic approach to detect, interpret, and mitigate them.
017            We introduce a pipeline that identifies conflicts between visual and textual an-
018
               swers, showing a persistently high conflict rate across modalities in recent LVLMs
               regardless of the model size. We further investigate how these conflicts interfere
019
               with the inference process and propose a contrastive metric to discern the conflict-
020
               ing samples from the others. Building on these insights, we develop a novel dy-
021            namic contrastive decoding method that removes undesirable logits inferred from
022            the less confident modality components based on answer confidence. For mod-
023            els that do not provide logits, we also introduce two prompt-based strategies to
024            mitigate the conflicts. Our methods achieve promising improvements in accuracy
025            on both the ViQuAE and InfoSeek datasets. Specifically, using LLaVA-34B, our
026            proposed dynamic contrastive decoding improves an average accuracy of 2.24%.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs; OpenAI 2023; Anil et al. 2023; Liu et al. 2024) have
032   demonstrated potent capabilities for perceiving and understanding information across different
033
      modalities. These models typically consist of a visual encoder and a large language model (LLM),
      aligned by a projection layer (Li et al., 2022a; Alayrac et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2024). This alignment
034
      and collaboration mechanism between language and vision components allows users to input text
035
      and images simultaneously, breeding some of the wildest applications, including retrieving informa-
036   tion based on a combination of textual and visual queries (Karthik et al., 2023) and accomplishing
037   complex real-world tasks with multimodal agents (Zhang & Zhang, 2023; Zheng et al., 2024).
038
039
      However, the disentangled training processes and distinct learning resources leveraged by the vision
      and language components of an LVLM, respectively, inherently bring along inconsistencies in their
040
      learned representations, captured knowledge, as well as their influence during inference (Bartsch
041
      et al., 2023; Rabinovich et al., 2023). Given that the visual encoder and the LLM are separately
042   trained on different datasets with distinct training objectives, their parametric knowledge across
043   language and vision modalities is susceptible to conflicts (Wang et al., 2024), potentially leading
044   to hallucinations (Ji et al., 2023) and inconsistencies in prediction (Chang & Bergen, 2024). As
045   illustrated in Fig. 1, we present a conflict case from an LVLM. When asked a question about the
046   same entity presented in two different modalities, the LVLM provides two contradictory answers.
047   Even though the visual encoder is able to recognize the Sydney Opera House , the model still
048   fails to integrate this information coherently across modalities. This phenomenon reveals a crucial
049   challenge: the disparity between the knowledge captured by the vision and language components
050
      of LVLMs. However, there has been limited research on parametric knowledge conflicts within
      these models, especially concerning cross-modality conflicts. Thus, in this paper, we systematically
051
      investigate the phenomenon of cross-modality parametric knowledge conflict as defined in §3.
052
      We aim to address several principled research questions, as further detailed below:
053
      RQ1: How to detect cross-modality parametric knowledge conflicts?

      2    R ELATED W ORK
096
097   Knowledge Conflict. Knowledge conflict is a critical problem in context-specific tasks, such as
098   machine reading comprehension (Longpre et al., 2021; Zhou et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023a) and
099
      information extraction (Wang et al., 2022; Fang et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023b;c)
      In the realm of LLMs, recent studies can be categorized into context-memory conflict, inter-context
100
      conflict, and intra-memory conflict (Xu et al., 2024). The context-memory conflict and the inter-
101
      context conflict are concerned mainly in the process of Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG).
102   They find that LLMs tend to overly rely on their own parametric memory when facing contradictory
103   evidence (Xie et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024). The intra-memory conflict, on the other hand, is rooted
104   in the pre-training corpus which contains inaccurate and misleading information (Bender et al., 2021;
105   Lin et al., 2021; Kandpal et al., 2023). The inconsistency of knowledge causes LLMs to generate
106   outputs that are contradictory to each other when given different prompts with the same information
107   (Elazar et al., 2022; Grosse et al., 2023), undermining their reliability. In this context, prior work
      has not systematically studied this problem for LVLMs, which is exactly the focus of this work.

108
      Robustness Issues of LVLMs. Although LVLMs have demonstrated significant potential in un-
109   derstanding and reasoning over multimodal inputs, they also face several robustness challenges,
110   including language bias (Niu et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024), hallucinations
111   (Huang et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2024), and the visual perception gap (Ghosh et al., 2024). Language
112   bias refers to the tendency of LVLMs to rely on language patterns learned during LLM pretrain-
113   ing (Niu et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024). Hallucinations, which originate from
114   LLMs, pertain to the discrepancies between generated contents and facts from either real-world or
115   user inputs. (Huang et al., 2023; 2024). The visual perception gap refers to the phenomenon that the
116   LVLMs demonstrate proficient knowledge and visual recognition abilities but fail to link their visual
117
      recognition to this knowledge (Lee et al., 2023; Ghosh et al., 2024). These issues often overlook the
      potential conflicts between the visual and textual components of the LVLM, which may contribute
118
      to the aforementioned challenges.
119
120   Inference-time Intervention. Inference-time intervention encompasses a range of techniques de-
121   signed to influence the inference or generation process of LLMs (Damera Venkata & Bhattacharyya,
122   2022; Li et al., 2024b). These techniques either directly manipulate the logits of the generated to-
123
      kens or adjust the parameters of the LLM during inference. One of the most notable strategies in
      this context is contrastive decoding (Li et al., 2022b; Leng et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024), which
124
      mitigates undesired distributions by removing them from the original distribution. Another approach
125
      involves modifying specific layers of the LLMs. For instance, ITI (Li et al., 2024b) adjusts model
126   activation during inference by following a set of directions across several attention heads, enhancing
127   the truthfulness of LLMs. These methods provide a means for training-free adjustments to LVLMs,
128   significantly reducing the cost compared to readjusting model parameters.

138   3.1   D EFINITIONS
139
140   To ground our analysis, we need to define 1) a typical LVLM architecture, and 2) cross-modality
141   parametric knowledge conflicts.
142
      LVLM Architecture. We focus on the general architecture that is adopted by a variety of LVLMs,
143
      including LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024), Blip (Li et al., 2023), and Qwen-VL (Bai et al., 2023). Typi-
144   cally, these models consist of a visual encoder V , a projector F , and a language model LM. Given
145   a multimodal input xm = {xv , xt }, where xv is the visual input and xt is the textual input, LVLM
146   first processes xv with V , resulting in pv = V (xv ). Then, through the projector F , pv is projected
147   into the textual embedding space: ev = F (pv ). Finally, xt is embedded into the embedding space
148   by the embedding layer of the LM, resulting in et = embed(xt ). The language model then gen-
149   erates the output by the probability pLM (y|ev , et ). So, a contemporary LVLM can be defined as
150   pLM (y|F (V (xv )), embed(xt )).
151   Cross-Modality Parametric Knowledge Conflict. Since training a large model from scratch is
152   prohibitively costly, LVLMs typically align a vision encoder onto an existing language model. For
153   example, LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024) aligns the pre-trained CLIP visual encoder ViT-L/14 (Radford
154   et al., 2021) with Vicuna (Chiang et al., 2023), which have been separately trained on different data
155   distributions, leading to potential inconsistent parametric knowledge (Grosse et al., 2023).
156   To elicit parametric knowledge, we propose to use answers from different modalities as the indi-
157   cators of the specific parametric knowledge from each modality. Specifically, given a multimodal
158   input xm = {xv , q}, where q is the question regarding the entity in the image xv , the output ym is
159   generated by pLM (F (V (xv )), embed(qm )), which we define as the visual answer. On the contrary,
160   given a textual input xt = {xe , q}, where xe is the textual description of a named entity and q is the
161   question to the named entity, the output yt is generated by pLM (embed(qt )), which we define as the
      textual answer. If ym ̸= yt , then a parametric knowledge conflict is identified.

272   We conduct experiments with LVLMs following the aforementioned procedure, and the results are
273   presented in Tab. 2. We report the accuracy (Acc) on the complete evaluation set and the recognized

278   visual answers. The most pronounced performance gap in the LLaVA family is observed in the
279   LLaVA-7b model, where the accuracy difference exceeds 20%. This performance gap is attributed

282   the overall accuracy (Acc). This indicates that the models perform better on recognized entities and

294

      5     I NTERPRETING PARAMETRIC KNOWLEDGE CONFLICTS
302
303   The constantly large conflict rate across datasets highlights the phenomenon caused by cross-
304
      modality knowledge conflicts. In this section, we will take a closer look, through the sample-wise
      perspective, at how parametric knowledge in visual components, i.e., the visual encoder V and the
305
      projector F , causes cross-modality parametric knowledge conflict by intervening the inference pro-
306
      cess of the LLM. In particular, we explore how these conflicts influence answer confidence and
307   propose a metric that can serve as an indicator of the presence of such conflicts.
308
309
      5.1     I S PROBABILITY A RELIABLE INDICATOR OF ANSWER CORRECTNESS ?
310
311
      Method. Since the answer probability reflects the model’s confidence in a given response, it is
312   natural to consider how parametric knowledge conflicts might affect this probability. For instance,
313   such conflicts may either reduce confidence in the original answer or introduce a more confident
314   alternative answer. Given that embed(xe ) and F (V (xv )) might encapsulate different knowledge,
315   this discrepancy can affect the probability distribution over possible answers, resulting in a shift in
316   confidence in the final output. To investigate how cross-modality parametric knowledge conflict in-
317   fluences answer confidence, we design experiments to determine whether the answer confidence can
318   serve as an indicator of conflict and whether it can suggest the correctness of the answer.
319   To elicit the answer probability, we calculate the textual answer probability pt and the visual answer
320   probability pv using Eq. 3 and Eq. 4. Since we adopt MCQA as the task format, we extract the logits
321   of the answer token, i.e. “A,” “B,” “C,” and “D” and apply the softmax function to them. Thus, the
322   extracted confidence can be presented as c = softmax(log(p[A]), log(p[B]), log(p[C]), log(p[D])),
323   where p[A] indicates the probability of token “A,” and so on. Then, we use the following strategies
      to understand how visual components influence the inference:

353   Method. To effectively understand how conflicting knowledge affects the inference, we utilize the
354   concept of Contrastive Decoding (Li et al., 2022b). Its objective, which subtracts an undesired
355   distribution from the original distribution, serves as a metric for evaluating the degree of divergence
356   between the two distributions. Given that we are using MCQA as the task format, our focus is
357   specifically on the distribution of the answer token, particularly the first token.
358

359   and a textual input xt = {xe , q}, where xe is the textual description of the named entity in xv , the

361   and (4). The contrastive objective can then be written as:

409   6     M ITIGATING PARAMETRIC KNOWLEDGE CONFLICTS AT INFERENCE TIME
410
411   Having established an understanding of cross-modality parametric knowledge conflicts, we now
412   shift our focus to strategies for mitigating these conflicts. Since the contrastive metric has proven
413
      effective in distinguishing conflicting samples from consistent ones, we first propose a strategy that
      leverages the principles of contrastive decoding. Moreover, we also design an alternative approach
414
      based on prompting for models that do not provide access to logits during inference.

      6.1     DYNAMIC C ONTRASTIVE D ECODING
417
418   Method. In an ideal application of contrastive decoding, we would have an a priori knowledge of the
419   logits, which enables us to define the undesired logits. That is to say, to resolve cross-modality para-
420   metric knowledge conflicts, the logits from the incorrect, conflicting modality should be excluded
421   from those of the correct modality. However, in real-world scenarios, without external validation,
422   it is impossible to definitively determine the correctness of an answer. Therefore, we propose using
423   the model’s answer confidence as a trend for correctness, also treating it as a scaling factor for the
424   original logits. We then apply these scaled logits to the contrastive decoding algorithm, formulating
425   the dynamic contrastive decoding (DCD). This approach adjusts the contrastive decoding objec-
426
      tive by incorporating confidence as a dynamic factor to more accurately measure the difference in
      information embedded by the textual and visual components.
427
428   Specifically, given the textual answer yt with its probabilities pt (yt |xe , q) and the visual answer yv

526   7     C ONCLUSIONS
527
528
529   In this paper, we introduce the concept of cross-modality parametric knowledge conflicts in LVLMs,
530   a significant issue arising from the misalignment between visual and textual modalities. We propose
531
      a systematic approach to detect these conflicts, revealing a persistently high conflict rate across all
      model sizes. Our findings indicate that simply scaling up models does not resolve these conflicts,
532
      highlighting the need for targeted intervention strategies. To address these challenges, we propose
533
      the contrastive metric, which effectively identifies conflicting samples by measuring the information
534   gap between modalities. Building on this, we introduce the dynamic contrastive decoding (DCD),
535   which selectively removes unreliable logits to improve answer accuracy. For models without access
536   to logits, we propose two prompt-based strategies. These approaches collectively improve model
537   performance. On LLaVA-34B, the dynamic contrastive decoding achieves an accuracy improvement
538   of 2.36% on the ViQuAE dataset and 2.12% on the InfoSeek dataset. Our contributions advance the
539   understanding of cross-modality parametric knowledge conflicts in LVLMs and provide practical
      solutions to mitigate these conflicts, leading to more robust and accurate multimodal inference.

      From these memories, several observations can be made:
800
801   1. LLM is better at memorizing date and location. This aligns intuitively with the nature of the
802      LLM’s training process, where such factual knowledge frequently appears in the text corpora. It
803      corresponds well with the expectation that language models acquire structured knowledge from
804      reading-based data.
805   2. Visual components are better at memorizing the correlation between an entity and its
806      names and the relationship among entities. For example, when asked the common name
807      for Calliphora Vomitoria, the LLM fails to answer correctly, while the visual answer
808      is correct. This is likely due to the training objective of aligning visual components with the
809      LLM, during which visual components learn entity-specific knowledge by mapping images to
         the language space.
