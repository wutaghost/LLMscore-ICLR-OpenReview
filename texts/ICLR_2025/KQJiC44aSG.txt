009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Temporal point processes (TPPs) are a powerful framework for modeling event
012            sequences with irregular timestamps, such as those commonly found in electronic
013            health records (EHR), which often involve high-dimensional and diverse event
014            types. However, building generative models for such complex datasets comes with
015            several challenges, including addressing sample inefficiency, accurately capturing
016            intricate event patterns, and producing outputs that are both trustworthy and inter-
017            pretable. In this paper, we present a neuro-symbolic generative model for TPPs
018
               based on the Variational Autoencoder (VAE) framework. Our model incorporates
               a neural-symbolic reasoning layer into the latent space, allowing it to integrate
019
               interpretable, logic-based constraints and perform logical reasoning over learned
020
               representations. This integration enhances the interpretability of the latent space
021            by embedding logic rules directly into the generative process, enabling structured
022            reasoning and improved decision-making based on underlying data patterns. We
023            validate our model on an ICU EHR dataset, as well as other real-world datasets,
024            demonstrating its effectiveness in capturing complex event dynamics with irreg-
025            ular timestamps. In addition to improving sample efficiency and accuracy, our
026            model supports the secure and interpretable generation of synthetic event data,
027            making it a valuable tool for healthcare applications where reliability and trust-
028            worthiness are critical.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Temporal point processes (Daley & Vere-Jones, 2007) are a powerful model for analyzing and pre-
      dicting event sequences occurring at irregular time intervals. These models are particularly well-
034
      suited for capturing the dynamics of events in continuous time, allowing for a flexible representa-
035
      tion of temporal dependencies and event relationships. TPPs are widely applied in various domains,
036   from finance and social media to healthcare (Reynaud-Bouret & Schbath, 2010; Bacry et al., 2015;
037   Zhao et al., 2015; Farajtabar et al., 2017), where understanding the generative process of the event
038   time and types plays a crucial role in understanding underlying patterns and making predictions.
039
      For example, in healthcare, TPPs offer a valuable tool for modeling EHRs (Enguehard et al., 2020),
040
      which contain detailed sequences of medical events such as drug prescriptions, diagnoses, and moni-
041
      toring of vital signs. These sequences are often characterized by irregular time intervals and complex
042   dependencies, making them challenging to model effectively. In this context, generative models hold
043   significant value for several reasons. First, they provide a powerful tool for augmenting limited real
044   datasets, particularly in cases of rare diseases where patient data is scarce (Lee et al., 2022). By gen-
045   erating synthetic data that adheres to clinical logic and medical guidelines, these generative models
046   can facilitate more robust analysis and support the development of treatments or interventions. Sec-
047   ond, generative models can help create secure, de-identified patient data (Libbi et al., 2021; Biswas
048   & Talukdar, 2024; Biswal et al., 2021), ensuring privacy while enabling researchers and clinicians
049   to explore new insights without compromising sensitive information. Additionally, synthetic data
050
      can be used to simulate various medical scenarios, aiding in the testing of predictive models and
      decision-support systems.
051
052   Traditional generative models, such as VAEs (Kingma, 2013), are highly flexible and effective at
053   learning complex distributions. However, they often fall short in providing interpretable latent rep-
      resentations that align with clinical logic. This lack of interpretability can limit their ability to gener-

      ate realistic and clinically relevant data, which is essential for high-stakes applications in healthcare
055   where trustworthiness and alignment with medical knowledge are critical.
056
057   To address these challenges, we propose a neuro-symbolic generative model for TPPs, leveraging the
058
      VAE framework to integrate interpretable logic-based constraints into the latent space. Our model in-

      This approach not only improves the interpretability of the latent representations but also ensures that

      data that is both realistic and useful for research purposes. This capability is crucial for enhancing
067   data privacy, testing clinical hypotheses, and developing treatment strategies, especially in scenarios
068   where real data is limited.

080
081   Temporal Point Processes (TPP) TPP models have emerged as an elegant framework for mod-
082   eling event times and types in continuous time, directly treating the inter-event times as random
083   variables. With the advance of neural network, various neural TPP models have been proposed.
084   Some of them are built on recurrent neural networks (Du et al., 2016; Mei & Eisner, 2017; Xiao
085
      et al., 2017b; Omi et al., 2019; Shchur et al., 2019; Mei et al., 2020; Boyd et al., 2020). Some
      others utilize transformer architecture (Zuo et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2020; Enguehard et al., 2020;
086
      Sharma et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2021). Recently, Li et al. (2020; 2021) proposed
087
      integrating logic rules within the intensity function of TPP model to foster interpretability. We aim
088   to utilize TPP to model the real-world event sequences.
089
090   Variational Auto-Encoder (VAE) VAE models, proposed by Kingma (2013), encodes data to
091   latent (random) variables, and then decodes the latent variables to reconstruct the input data. Recent
092   works resort to VAE to learn a disentangled representation for sequential data. Bowman et al. (2015)
093   succeed in training a sequence-to-sequence VAE and generating sentences from a continuous latent
094   space. Desai et al. (2021) proposed a VAE framework with a decoder design that enables user-
095   defined distributions for generating time-series data. To enhance the disentanglement capability,
096   some works aim to introduce structural patterns in latent representation of VAE to improve efficiency
097
      and the quality of generated data. Hu & Rostami (2023) proposed a binarized regularization for
      VAE to encourage symmetric disentanglement, improve reconstruction quality. Van Den Oord et al.
098
      (2017) incorporated ideas from vector quantisation to learn a discrete latent representation which
099
      model important features that usually span many dimensions in data space. Yang et al. (2020)
100   proposed a new VAE framework which includes a causal layer to transform independent exogenous
101   factors into causal endogenous ones that correspond to causally related concepts in data. However,
102   existing VAE frameworks often lack interpretability in the latent representation, overlooking fine-
103   grained guiding logic rules.
104
105   Neuro-Symbolic Integration Neuro-Symbolic systems aims to transfer principles and mecha-
106   nisms between logic-based computation and neural computation Besold et al. (2021). Serafini &
107   Garcez (2016) demonstrate that the logic can be implemented using neural networks for the ground-
      ings of the symbols. Manhaeve et al. (2018) started from a probabilistic logic programming language

109   that learns literals that represent a linear relationship among numerical input features along with the

142   3.2   RULE L EARNING AND R EASONING
143
      We consider learning logic rules in the form of Horn clauses:
144
145                                       f:     Q ← P1 ∧ P2 ∧ · · · ∧ Ph                                    (1)
146
      Here, P1 , P2 , . . . , Ph are predicates that form the body of the rule, representing conditions that must
147
      hold true, and Q is the head, representing the conclusion that can be inferred when all the predicates
148
      in the body are satisfied.
149
150   Predicates are Boolean variables that take values of either True or False, based on the data. They
151   express properties or relationships between entities. For example, a predicate like HasFever(Patient)
152
      indicates whether a patient has a fever, while UseDrug(Patient) specifies whether a particular drug
      is being administered. These predicates help capture key characteristics and relationships within the
153
      system’s state.
154
155   With learned rules, we can reason to infer new information. Reasoning includes two main methods:
156   forward chaining, which starts from known facts (body predicates) and infers new knowledge step
157   by step, and backward chaining, which starts from a goal (the head) and works backward to find
158
      supporting conditions. In this work, we focus on forward chaining, which is especially useful for
      predicting future events in temporal point processes (TPP).
159
160   For example, given the rule UseDrug ← HasFever ∧ HighTemperature and knowing that
161   HasFever(Patient) and HighTemperature(Patient) are true, we can infer via forward chaining that
      UseDrug(Patient) is true.

171

172

352   4.4   D ECODER
353
354   During the reconstruction phase, we derive the inferred ẑ (H) from the neural-symbolic layer and
355   sample the latent variable z from Bernoulli distribution. The decoder then auto-regressively models
356   the conditional distribution of the next event based on z and the current event history H (ti ) =
357
      pψ (ti+1 , mi+1 | z, H (ti )) until a complete sequence is generated.
358   In the generation phase, after the model being well-trained, we start from an initial state and sample
359   z from inferred ẑ (H) . These are input into a feed-forward neural network to construct intensity, from
360   which inter-event times are sampled. This process is iterated, with each generated event becoming
361   part of the historical events along with the sampled z, until a predefined time horizon is reached.

      5.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
381
382   To evaluate the effectiveness of our proposed framework, we primarily compare the model perfor-
383   mance on prediction and generation tasks. The results indicate that our model outperforms other
384   existing methods in prediction accuracy and demonstrates higher data generation quality. Further-
385   more, we visualize the process of neuro-symbolic forward chaining, which highly enhances the
386   interpretability.
387
388   Datasets We utilized four real-world datasets: i) MIMIC-IV: An electronic health record dataset
389
      of ICU patients, focusing on those diagnosed with sepsis (Saria, 2018). We extracted 2000 samples,
      with an average sequence length of 22.93 events, including lab measurements, drug intake, and other
390
      health-related features. ii) Covid-19 UK: Collected data from the Oxford Covid-19 Government
391
      Response Tracker (Hale et al., 2021), focusing on the UK during 2021. This dataset includes 27
392   samples with an average of 59.22 events per sequence, tracking government policies and their impact
393   on confirmed case reduction. iii) Car-Follow: Derived from the Lyft Level-5 dataset (Li et al., 2023),
394   containing 5000 samples with an average of 4.6 events per sequence, focusing on vehicle driving
395   modes. iv) Epic-Kitchen: A dataset of first-person recordings from kitchen activities, where we
396   extracted 400 samples with an average sequence length of 36.76 events, focusing on cooking-related
397   action verbs. For detailed descriptions and processing information of the datasets, please refer to
398   Appendix.A.
399   We abstract the features in each dataset into high-level concepts and use these concepts to construct
400   ground truth governing logic rules either by experts or large language models (Zhao et al., 2023),
401   with details illustrated in Appendix.B.
402
403   Baselines We choose several state-of-the-art baselines considering three different fields: i) Neural
404   Temporal Point Process Model (Neural TPP): RMTPP (Du et al., 2016), THP (Zuo et al., 2020),
405   PromptTPP (Xue et al., 2023), and HYPRO (Xue et al., 2022) ii) Logic-Based Model: TELLER (Li
406   et al., 2021), and CLNN (Yan et al., 2023) iii) Generative Model: We follow the work of (Lin et al.,
407   2022) and consider history encoder and probabilistic decoder framework for temporal point process
408
      generative model. For the history encoder, we use attention mechanism Vaswani (2017); Zuo et al.
      (2020). For the generative probabilistic decoder, we consider TCDDM (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015),
409
      TCVAE (Pan et al., 2020), TCGAN (Xiao et al., 2017a), and TCCNF (Mehrasa et al., 2019). These
410
      generative models can also be utilized for prediction tasks (Lin et al., 2022). Detailed introduction
411   for the baselines can be found in Appendix.C
412
413   Comparison Metric The evaluation metrics we utilized primarily encompass the following two
414   aspects: i) Prediction tasks: Following common next-event prediction task in TPPs (Du et al., 2016;
415   Zuo et al., 2020), our model as well as other baselines (including all Neural TPP, Logic-based,
416   and generative baselines) attempt to predict next event from history. We evaluate the event type
417   prediction with the Error Rate (ER%) and evaluate the event time prediction with the Root Mean
418   Square Error (RMSE). ii) Generation tasks: To assess the quality of the generated data, we train
419   a classification model to distinguish between the original and synthetic data as a supervised task.
420   Therefore, we can use the discriminative score, which is given by (accuracy - 0.5) on the held-out
421
      set, to evaluate the generative performance. A score close to 0 is better, indicating the generated data
      is hard to distinguish from original data. We also analyse 2-dimensional t-SNE plots of the original
422
      and generated data. Initially, we employ the same embedding to project the sequences into a high-
423
      dimensional space, considering that the sequences encompass both complex event time and event
424   type information. Subsequently, t-SNE is applied to reduce the dimensionality to two components.
425   These comparison metrics were all considered in (Yoon et al., 2019; Desai et al., 2021).

533    6       C ONCLUSION
534
535   We propose a novel VAE framework that integrates a neural-symbolic reasoning layer into the latent
536   space, enabling the incorporation of interpretable, logic-based constraints and logical reasoning on
537   learned representations. Our model addresses the complex task of temporal point process generation,
538   crucial for generating de-identified data and managing missing data. Proficient models can utilize
539   domain expertise to produce semi-synthetic datasets from real data, facilitating transfer learning and
      ensuring secure data generation for privacy protection.

773   A    DATASETS D ETAILS
774
775   We extracted four interesting real-world datasets. Followings are brief introduction to these real-
776   world datasets: i) MIMIC-IV: an electronic health record dataset of patients admitted to the inten-
777   sive care unit (ICU). (Johnson et al., 2023). We considered patients diagnosed with sepsis (Saria,
778   2018), one of the major causes of mortality in ICU due to septic shock. We extract 2000 samples of
779   multiple features with average sequence length of 22.93 events, encompassing lab measurements,
780   drug intake, intravenous fluids, and urine output. ii) Covid-19 UK: COVID-19 is an unprecedented
781
      pandemic and various control measures have been introduced to curb the spread of the virus. The
      Oxford Covid-19 Government Response Tracker (OxCGRT) gathers data on governments’ imple-
782
      mentation of specific measures and their timing. (Hale et al., 2021; 2020). We collected 27 samples,
783
      each with sequence length of 59.22 events, for the United Kingdom during 2021, focusing on the
784   effect of government’s epidemic prevention policies related to containment/closure, the healthcare
785   system, vaccination efforts, and economic impacts on daily cumulative number of confirmed cases
786   reduction. To reduce daily fluctuations, we recorded the cumulative number of confirmed cases over
787   7-day intervals to illustrate the epidemic spread trend. We identified the time points when case num-
788   bers began to decrease. iii) Car-Follow: a dataset processed from Lyft level-5 open dataset (Li et al.,
789   2023; Houston et al., 2021), which includes 1000+ hours of perception and motion data collected
790   over a 4-month period from urban and suburban environments along a fixed route in Palo Alto, Cal-
791   ifornia. We extract 5000 samples with an average sequence length of 4.6 events, which recordings
792
      vehicle driving modes. iv) Epic-Kitchen: This dataset originates from a large-scale, first-person
      (egocentric) vision dataset, featuring multi-faceted, audio-visual, non-scripted recordings in natural
793
      settings, specifically the wearers’ homes. It captures daily kitchen activities over multiple days. We
794
      have utilized the annotated action sequences. focusing only text, and extracted them to create a
795   temporal event history of cooking verbs. This was achieved by omitting the entities that the human
796   subjects interacted with. We specifically focus on ten verbs such as manipulate, move, clean, etc.
797   We concentrated on a subset of 400 samples, each with an average sequence length of 36.76 events.

            • Logic-Based Model
904             – TELLER (Li et al., 2021): It is a non-differentiable algorithm that can be described
905               as a temporal logic rule learning algorithm based on column generation principles.
906               This method formulates the process of discovering rules from noisy event data as a
907
                  maximum likelihood problem. It also designs a tractable branch-and-price algorithm
                  to systematically search for new rules and expand existing ones. The algorithm alter-
908
                  nates between a rule generation stage and a rule evaluation stage, gradually uncovering
909
                  the most significant set of logic rules within a predefined time limit.
910
                – CLNN (Yan et al., 2023): The model learns weighted clock logic (wCL) formulas,
911
                  which serve as interpretable temporal logic rules indicating how certain events can
912               promote or inhibit others. Specifically, the CLNN model captures temporal relations
913               between events through conditional intensity rates guided by a set of wCL formulas
914               that offer greater expressiveness. In contrast to conventional approaches that rely on
915               computationally expensive combinatorial optimization to search for generative rules,
916               CLNN employs smooth activation functions for the components of wCL formulas.
917               This enables a continuous relaxation of the discrete search space and facilitates effi-
                  cient learning of wCL formulas using gradient-based methods.

            • Generative Model: All the temporal point process generative models we consider in our
919           paper are summarized in the work of (Lin et al., 2022). It simplifies the generative model
920           of temporal point processes into an history-encoder-probabilistic-decoder architecture. For
921           the history encoder, we use attention mechanism Vaswani (2017); Zuo et al. (2020). For
922           the generative probabilistic decoder, we consider
923              – TCDDM (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015): Temporal conditional diffusion denoising
924                model (TCDDM) is based on diffusion model. In sampling, given the historical encod-
925                ing, we first sample from the standard normal distribution, then take it and historical
926                encoding as the input to get the approximated noise, and generally remove the noise
927                with different scales to recover the samples. For inference, the prediction is based on
928                Monte Carlo estimation.
929              – TCVAE (Kingma, 2013; Pan et al., 2020): Temporal conditional variational autoen-
930                coder (TCVAE) consists of a variational encoder as a conditional Gaussian distribution
931                for approximating the prior standard Gaussian and a variational decoder to generate
932
                   arrival time samples.
933              – TCGAN (Xiao et al., 2017a): Temporal conditional generative adversarial network
934
                   (TCGAN) decoder is mostly based on Wasserstein GAN in TPPs (Arjovsky et al.,
                   2017; Xiao et al., 2017a). The probabilistic generator is trained via adversarial pro-
935
                   cess, in which the other network called discriminator is trained to map the samples to a
936
                   scalar, for maximizing the Wasserstein distance between the distribution of generated
937                samples and the distribution of observed samples.
938              – TCCNF (Mehrasa et al., 2019): Temporal conditional continuous normalizing flows
939                (TCCNF) is based on Neural ODE (Chen et al., 2018; 2020).
