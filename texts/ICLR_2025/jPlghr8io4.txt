009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Few-shot learning (FSL) is challenging due to limited support data for model train-
012            ing. The situation is much worse when the support data is contaminated with
013            noise. To address this issue, we propose a novel Transformer-based Analogical
014            Reasoning model for Noisy Few-Shot learning (TarNFS), by mimicing the hu-
015            man’s ability of learning by analogy. Concretely, we assume the existence of a
016            large human cultivated or AI-powered knowledge base, and hypothesize that sim-
017            ilar concepts in the knowledge base are visually similar in the latent space as well.
018
               Then we design a transformer-based analogical reasoning model to utilize inter-
               concept connections among these concepts, aiming to build robust and discrimi-
019
               native classification boundaries. In addition, we propose a task-level contrastive
020
               learning to analogically learn from negative tasks to facilitate training with noisy
021            tasks. Experiments demonstrate that our TarNFS enables more effective learning
022            from limited and imperfect data. It not only improves the generalization ability of
023            FSL in different noisy settings but also achieves competitive performance in the
024            common clean FSL settings. Code is publicly available here.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Modern deep learning methods have achieved great success thanks to the huge amount of data avail-
030   able for model training. Yet, these methods are faced with challenges in the scenario where training
031   data is scarce. To alleviate the problem, researchers have recently resorted to few-shot learning
032   (FSL), a task that is officially formalized as a N -way K-shot recognition problem with each of
033
      the N classes having K labeled images, tackling the task from various perspectives like data aug-
      mentation or hallucination, meta-learning for fast knowledge transfer, metric-learning to construct
034
      a transferable latent space, etc (Vinyals et al., 2016; Snell et al., 2017; Sendera et al., 2023; Guo
035
      et al., 2022). While advancements are made, most FSL methods are unconsciously devised to learn
036   from clean data (as in Figure 1(a)), assuming an ideal scenario where all samples in the support set
037   are deliberately selected to represent their class. This assumption is in sharp contrast to real-world
038   settings where even carefully annotated and curated datasets often contain mislabeled samples (as
039   in Figure 1(b)) due to ambiguity, automated data collection, or human error. Liang et al. (2022)
040   have shown that existing FSL methods are quite vulnerable to such label noise.
041
      Sample selection was proposed to handle noisy labels in FSL. By identifying and selecting poten-
042   tially clean data from noisy data, these methods aim to build better prototypes using the chosen
043   clean samples (Que & Yu, 2024). Instead of directly discarding noisy data, some leverage sample
044   weighting to intentionally take the noisy data into consideration during learning (Killamsetty et al.,
045   2020). Specifically, they design a weighting policy to assign higher weights to the clean data and
046   lower weights to the noisy, in which manner the clean data are supposed to contribute more to the
047   final prototypes and the side information in the noisy are properly utilized.
048   Despite their attainments, the aforementioned efforts have been substantially devoted to acquiring
049   data-efficient strategies for constructing better category prototypes out of corrupted intra-class sam-
050   ples, rarely exploring the categories’ connections to the open world. Differently, we human learn
051   new concepts not only from instructions or demonstrations, but also from their rich relations with
052   other known concepts (Brown & Kane, 1988). For example, when we encounter a zebra for the first
053   time, it is not uncommon for us to interpret the species as “a horse with black and white striped
      pattern”. We would also take one step further to guess that a zebra might likewise appear in zoo.

      This ability of learning by analogy, or analogical reasoning, has been proved a fundamental build-
073
      ing block in human learning process (Gentner & Holyoak, 1997; Thibaut et al., 2010; Bartha, 2024;
074
      Zhou et al., 2019). It ensures us to learn fast and faithfully from few examples while not being easily
075   deceived by distracting misinformation.
076
077   To mimic the behavior, in this work we propose a novel Transformer-based Analogical Reasoning
078
      model for Noisy Few-Shot learning (TarNFS). Specifically, we assume a knowledge base,
      e.g.WordNet, that contains lots of connections among concepts, which represents how we human
079
      perceive the world. Given a novel concept, we find its relations with known concepts in the knowl-
080
      edge base to build a semantic analogy graph, and hypothesize that the relations can be analogically
081   applied to the visual space for feature learning. Thus, unlike previous methods that struggle to
082   learn robust category prototypes from few corrupted samples, we propose to learn with analogi-
083   cal reasoning to distill discriminative category boundaries, by taking the noise-independent concept
084   connections as effective clues. The transformer architecture of TarNFS enables a natural manner of
085   processing arbitrary number of related concepts. A task-level contrastive learning is also presented
086   to learn from totally different few-shot tasks. This enables a task-level analogical reasoning, behind
087   which the intuition is that two tasks should be different in the latent visual space if they have different
088   concepts. Experiments on the MiniImageNet and TieredImageNet datasets show that, with analogi-
089
      cal reasoning and task-level contrastive learning, our TarNFS outperforms previous SOTAs on FSL
      with noisy labels (e.g.obtain 8.3% relative improvement on MiniImageNet with 40% symmetric
090
      noise). Notably, when tested on FSL with clean data, TarNFS using Conv4-64/ResNet-12 is able to
091
      achieve 60.75%/70.29% 1-shot accuracy, performing competitively against other FSL methods.

094   2     RELATED WORK
095
096   2.1   F EW-S HOT L EARNING
097
098   Existing FSL approaches can be roughly categorized into four groups, i.e.hallucination-based,
099
      optimization-based, parameter-generating based, and metric-learning based, according to recent
      investigations (Wang et al., 2020; Song et al., 2023a). Hallucination-based approaches learn to
100
      estimate the distributions of novel categories (Hariharan & Girshick, 2017; Luo et al., 2021; Guo
101
      et al., 2022). Methods in this group generate new instances by sampling from the estimated distribu-
102   tions, turning FSL into an easily resolved many shot learning problem. Optimization-based methods
103   (Finn et al., 2017; Nichol et al., 2018; Zhao et al., 2020) perform rapid adaption with a few training
104   samples by learning a good optimizer or learning a well-initialized model. Parameter-generating
105   methods follow the paradigm of hypernetworks (Ha et al., 2016; Andrychowicz et al., 2016), where
106   the weights of the learner (often the classifier) are generated by another hypernetwork conditioned
107   on the few samples of novel classes so that the learner can be rapidly adapted to recognize new
      categories (Gidaris & Komodakis, 2019; Bateni et al., 2020; Sendera et al., 2023). Metric-learning


                                                                2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      based methods tackle FSL differently by learning to compare two examples (Sung et al., 2018; He
109   et al., 2020a) or by learning an embedding space constrained on a selected metric (Snell et al., 2017;
110   He et al., 2022a; Cheng et al., 2023). The main idea is to project instances into an embedding space
111   and utilize the learned or selected metric to estimate distances from a query to candidate categories
112   for classification. The learning often accommodates the episodic training proposed by Vinyals et al.
113   (2016) so that the embedding space has the merit of easily generalizing to new tasks.
114
115   2.2   N OISY L ABEL L EARNING
116
117   Noisy label learning addresses the challenge of training on datasets that contain mislabeled examples
118   or noises. To this end, some methods focus on estimating the latent noisy transition matrix (Liu &
119   Tao, 2016), aiming to understand and model the confusion between classes induced by label noise.
120   In an orthogonal direction, some methods, like MentorNet (Jiang et al., 2018), prioritize learning
121   from samples that are more likely to be correctly labeled by following certain sample weighting
122
      scheme. For instance, Co-teaching (Han et al., 2018) proposes a strategy to train two networks
      simultaneously, with each network providing samples it deems correctly labeled to the other for
123
      training. Li et al. (2019) leverage meta-learning to learn from synthetic noisy labels that simulate the
124
      actual training. However, these methods filter out or purify corrupted labels based on a large amount
125   of clean data, making them unsuitable for noisy label learning in few-shot setting. To tackle the
126   problem, Mazumder et al. (2021) propose to create hybrid features and utilize clustering approach
127   like k-means to build more accurate prototypes. TraNFS (Liang et al., 2022) learn to automatically
128   promote refined prototypes out of corrupted support samples by leveraging a transformer module.
129   In this study, we leverage inter-concept connections that are noise-independent for noisy FSL. By
130   transferring the connections among the novel and the known categories from semantic space to the
131   visual feature space, we manage to build discriminative and noise-resistant prototypes.
132
133   2.3   C ONTRASTIVE LEARNING
134
135   Contrastive learning inherently learns an embedding space in a self-supervised manner where in-
136   stances in positive pairs are close to each other and instances in negative pairs stay apart (He et al.,
137   2020b; Khosla et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2020; He et al., 2022b). Its efficacy in representation learning
138
      has inspired some applications in FSL (Yang et al., 2022; Gidaris et al., 2019; Shirekar et al., 2023;
      Song et al., 2023b). For instance, Gidaris et al. (2019) introduced a rotation-based self-supervised
139
      pretext task into FSL and utilized the corresponding auxiliary loss for model optimization. Shirekar
140
      et al. (2023) designed an end-to-end framework in which they proposed a self-attention based mes-
141   sage passing contrastive learning method to facilitate representation learning for unsupervised FSL.
142   Yang et al. (2022) performed nearest centroid classification on two different views of the same FSL
143   task and adopted the contrastive loss to overcome bias between views, which improves the transfer-
144   ability of representations. One observation is that most FSL methods that leverage contrastive learn-
145   ing actually perform instance-level contrastive constraint, while the task-level contrastive learning
146   is overlooked. In this work, we propose to leverage task-level contrastive learning for FSL. Our
147   method takes two views of the same task as positive and the tasks in an extra queue as negative. By
148   aggregating each task into a task-level representation, we propose a task-level contrastive learning
149
      to push tasks with different categories away from each other and pull tasks with the same categories
      close to each other for model regularization during FSL.

210   4    APPROACH
211
212   To facilitate FSL from noisy labeled data, as shown in Figure 2, we propose a novel method named
213   TarNFS that leverages inter-concept connections from the novel to the known to construct robust
214   and discriminative category prototypes. We further devise a task-level contrastive learning that
215   pulls similar tasks close to each other and pushes dissimilar tasks far apart, to regularize model
      optimization from an extra task-level perspective.

      4.1   FSL WITH ANALOGICAL REASONING
217
218   We humans learn new concept fast from few demonstrations, not only due to our powerful brains but
219   also because of our ability to make abstract connections between the new concept and the known
220   to perform analogical reasoning. To give FSL models the similar talent, we assume there exists
221   a knowledge base, either human-curated or AI-powered, in which connections between concepts
222   (including both known concepts and novel ones) can be obtained. We leverage these connections for
223   analogical reasoning for robust FSL.
224   Concretely, given a novel category in Cnovel , we draw connections between novel categories and the
225   known Cbase in the knowledge base and represent the connections as a semantic analogy graph G =
226   {A, E}. After that, we sample the prior experiences of known categories from a knowledge bank
227   B = {bc }|c∈Cbase to construct more robust and discriminative category prototypes for classification.
228   Note the knowledge bank is supposed to hold prior experiences of all known categories. For each
229   novel category, only those related known categories are selected for FSL.

      5.1   DATASETS AND E VALUATION M ETRICS
325
326   Datasets. MiniImageNet is a subset of ImageNet with 100 classes (600 images per class) and has
327   a total of 60k images of size 84 × 84. The dataset is divided into 64 training classes, 16 valida-
328   tion classes, and 20 test classes. TieredImageNet contains 608 classes that are sampled from 34
329   high-level categories from ImageNet. The 34 top categories are divided into 20 for training, 6 for
330   validation and 8 for testing, resulting 351 training classes, 97 validation classes, and 160 test classes,
331   respectively. TieredImageNet is much larger than MiniImageNet. It contains around 779k images,
332   about 1,300 images per class. The high-level split ensures that the test classes are semantically
333   distinctive enough from the train classes, which provides a more challenging and realistic setting.
334   Evaluation Metrics. Conventionally, FSL considers both 5-way 1-shot and 5-way 5-shot settings.
335   In our study, as the noise exists, we solely consider the 5-way 5-shot setting. Once trained, we
336   randomly sampled 10,000 tasks and consider two types of noise, i.e.the symmetric label swap noise
337   (Van Rooyen et al., 2015) and the paired label swap noise (Han et al., 2018), as mentioned in Sec-
338   tion 3, to construct noisy tasks for evaluation. We conducted experiments at noise proportions of
339   20%, 40%, and 60% in symmetric label swap noise setting. As for the paired label swap noise, we
340
      exclusively conducted experiment at noise proportion of 40%. This is due to that paired label swap
      noise with a noise proportion of 20% is identical to the symmetric label swap noise with the same
341
      noise proportion that is already studied (Liang et al., 2022), and the paired label swap noise with
342
      a noise proportion more than 50% (e.g.60%), would cause the noisy samples outnumber the clean,
343   leading to ambigious class, which is too challenging to be tackled. We report the mean average top-1
344   accuracy ± 95% confidence interval over the 10,000 noisy tasks for comparison.

347   5.2   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
348
      Architecture of the Learner. We follow Liange et al. Liang et al. (2022) to choose Conv4-64,
349
      a simple yet widly adopted convolutional network that comprises 4 convolutional blocks, as the
350
      architecture of the learner. In Conv4-64, each block contains a convolutional layer with 3 × 3
351   kernels, a batch normalization layer, a relu activation layer and a 2×2 max pooling layer. The
352   feature map output by the learner is of size 64 × 5 × 5. For pre-training, we append a linear classifier
353   at the end of the learner, and train the learner on the base dataset to recognize known categories until
354   the accuracy is saturated. As mentioned in Liang et al. (2022), the naive architecture of Conv4-64
355   helps emphasize our method rather than the feature extractor.
356
      FSL with analogical reasoning. We discard the linear classifier and use the pre-trained learner
357   to repersent each image as a tensor of size 64 × 5 × 5. For each known category, we calculate
358   its mean representation as in Equation (3) to initialize the knowledge bank. When a novel class
359   is encountered, we find its top 5 related known categories in WordNet via the Leacock-Chodorow
360   similarity. Then, the mean representations of these selected known categories are retrieved from
361   the bank for refining the drifted prototype of the novel category. The multi-head self-attention is
362   implemented by following Vaswani (2017). The dimensions of the query, key and vlaue in the
363   transformer-based analogy reasonling are equally set to 64.
364   Task-level Contrastive Learning. The dimension of the hidden state of our Bi-LSTM for task
365   feature learning is set to 256. Therefore, each task is represented by a vector of dimension 512.
366   The length of the task queue is set to 256 to ensure that there exist abundant true negative tasks for
367   contrastive learning. In the infoNCE loss, we set the temperature τ to 0.07. We employ the Adam
368   optimizer with an initial learning rate of 0.001 to train our model, by following the formulation in
369   Equation (11). The total number of traning tasks is set to 20,000, and the learning rate decays by
370   half every 2,000 tasks. We set the weight of the task-level contrastive learning loss to 1 (i.e.λ = 1).

463   an auxilary supervision from the task perspective. It allows the model to maintain an overall char-

467   Train on Tasks with Different Noise Ratios. A foundamental hypothesis in FSL is that we should
468   train a model to do fast learning in the environment where it would be tested (Vinyals et al., 2016).
469   The idea inspires that it may be helpful to train the model on noisy tasks to improve its robustness
470   to noise. To verify this, we train our model on tasks that incorporate varying symmetric noise ratios
471
      and test it on tasks with different noise proportions. The results are listed in Table 4. As can be
      seen, training on tasks with a single noise proportion boosts the performance on that noise level
472
      or higher during testing, which is partially consistent with the findings in Liang et al. (2022). By
473
      combining tasks with varying noise ratios for training, we observe a performance drop on clean
474   tasks. Consistent performance boosts on tasks with 60% noise are also observed when compared to
475   the model trained on clean tasks. However, unlike Liang et al. (2022), the performance gains in other
476   two noise levels are not observed in our method when training with hybrid noise levels. Contrarily,
477   we find that training on tasks with 20% noise appears to achieve the best overall performance. Thus,
478   we report the results of our TarNFS trained on tasks with 20% noise in Table 1 for comparison.
479

480   ratio leads to ambugious prototypes and meaningless task representations, thereby dramatically de-
481   creasing performance. Liang et al. (2022) also found that it is not helpful to train on tasks with
482   60% noise. When trained and evaluated on tasks with the same noise ratio, Table 4 shows that our
483   method can consistently surpass TraNFS. For example, our TarNFS trained on clean tasks are strong
484   enough to obtain 68.22%/60.04%/49.55% accuracy when tested on noisy tasks with 20%/40%/60%
485   noise, boosting the performance of TraNFS in the same setting by a margin of 4.66%/7.19%/10.36%
      respectively. It indicates the effectiveness of our method in tackling noisy FSL.

      hand-crafted novel tasks for validation. Results are reported in Table 6. We can observe that, with

526   3%/0.45% when using the Conv4-64/ResNet-12 backbone. Whereas, in the 5-way 5-shot setting, as
527   the prototypes generated are already sufficiently accurate, our method can only achieve a modicum
528   of improvement. This is expected. Though, according to Table 6, we surely conclude that our
529   TarNFS contributes a new robust FSL method to the literature.

      6     CONCLUSION
532
533
      In this paper, we propose Transformer-based Analogical Reasoning model for Noisy Few-Shot learn-
534   ing (TarNFS), a novel model designed to address the challenges posed by mislabeled support sets
535   samples. The TarNFS method has the following features: (1) The analogical reasoning module
536   leverages semantic relationships to construct noise-resilient prototypes, which are effective in both
537   noisy few-shot learning (FSL) and typical FSL scenarios. (2) The implementation of task-level con-
538   trastive learning further optimizes the model, enhancing its generalization capabilities when faced
539   with novel tasks. The experiments on MiniImageNet and TieredImageNet demonstrate the signifi-
      cant success of our method, particularly under conditions of severe data noise or data scarcity.

731   In this appendix, we present experiments on CIFAR-FS with ResNet12 as the learner to showcase
732   the generalization of our method to other datasets, backbones and knowledge bases.
733
      CIFAR-FS is a dataset derived from CIFAR-1001 with images of size 32 × 32 × 3. It contains 100
734   categories with 600 instances in each class. All the classes are split into 64, 16 and 20 for training,
735   validation and test.
736
737
      ResNet12 is a small version of ResNet that is widely explored and exploited in few-shot learn-
      ing (Pan et al., 2024; Fan et al., 2023; He et al., 2022a). Like Conv4-64, ResNet12 consists of four
738
      residual blocks. Each block contains a stack of three convolutional layers with 3 × 3 kernels. Each
739
      convolutional layer is followed by batch normalization and a leaky ReLU non-linearity. A convolu-
740   tional skip with 1 × 1 kernel over the convolutional stack is used in each residual block. Following
741   each residulal block, a leaky ReLU layer and a 2 × 2 maxpooling layer are placed at the end for
742   non-linearity and downsampling. The number of filters used in each block is [64, 128, 256, 512] or
743   [64, 160, 320, 640], respectively. In our experiments, we follow An et al. (2023); Pan et al. (2024)
744   to use the later wide architecture for fair comparison.
745

      categories in CIFAR-FS which hinders our direct adoption to this dataset. To work with CIFAR-FS,
