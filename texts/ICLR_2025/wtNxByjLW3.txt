009                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011            Maximum Inner Product Search (MIPS) is essential for machine learning and informa-
012            tion retrieval, particularly in applications that operate on high-dimensional data, such as
013            recommender systems and retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), using inner product or
014            cosine similarity. While numerous techniques have been developed for efficient MIPS,
015            their performance often suffers due to a limited understanding of the geometric proper-
016
               ties of Inner Product (IP) space. Many approaches reduce MIPS to Nearest Neighbor
               Search (NNS) through nonlinear transformations, which rely on strong assumptions and
017
               can hinder performance. To address these limitations, we propose a novel approach that
018
               directly leverages the geometry of IP space. We focus on a class of special vectors called
019            dominators and introduce the Monotonic Relative Dominator Graph (MRDG), an IP-
020            space-native, sparse, and strong-connected graph designed for efficient MIPS, offering
021            solid theoretical foundations. To ensure scalability, we further introduce the Approximate
022            Relative Dominator Graph (ARDG), which retains MRDG’s benefits while significantly
023            reducing indexing complexity. Extensive experiments on 8 public datasets demonstrate
024            that ARDG achieves a 30% average speedup in search at high precision and reduces index
025            size by 2× compared to state-of-the-art graph-based methods.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Maximum Inner Product Search (MIPS) is foundational in machine learning and information retrieval (Lewis
030   et al., 2020; Seo et al., 2019), especially with the rise of high-dimensional representations based on in-
031   ner product or cosine similarity. Applications such as recommendation systems (Xu et al., 2018), query-
032   answering chatbots (Ahmad et al., 2019), multi-modal retrieval (Wang et al., 2024), and Retrieval Aug-
033   mented Generation (RAG) (Asai et al., 2023) depend on efficiently searching large vector databases to find
034   items that maximize similarity with a query vector. Fast and accurate MIPS leaves more time for complex
035   model inference, thereby enhancing both system performance and user experience.
036   Despite numerous methods developed for MIPS (Morozov & Babenko, 2018; Guo et al., 2020; Zhao et al.,
037   2023; Guo et al., 2016; Liu et al., 2020), the geometric properties of the inner product space require further
038   exploration. This lack of geometric theoretical support has led most existing approaches (Zhao et al., 2023;
039   Zhou et al., 2019; Shrivastava & Li, 2014) to reduce MIPS to the nearest neighbor search (NNS) problem in
040   a transformed space, allowing established advanced NNS methods to address the MIPS problem. However,
041   this reduction is typically achieved through nonlinear transformations, such as the Möbius Transformation
042   (Zhou et al., 2019) or XBOX Transformation (Zhao et al., 2023). These transformations often impose strong
043   theoretical assumptions, introduce data distortion, and struggle with data updates (Morozov & Babenko,
044
      2018), leading to compromised efficiency and scalability, particularly in high-dimensional settings.
045   To tackle these challenges, we propose a novel approach that leverages the intrinsic geometry of the IP
046   space without transforming it into a metric space. Our key insight is to identify a set of vectors termed

074   Notations. Let Rd denote d-dimensional real coordinate space. {·} is use to denote sets. Let D =
075   {x1 , . . . , xn } ⊂ Rd represent a vector dataset with n points. A query vector is denoted by q ∈ Rd . ⟨x, y⟩
076   denotes the inner product (IP) between vector x and y. ∥x∥ gives the Euclidean norm of vector x, and the
077   Euclidean distance between vectors x and y is denoted as ∥x − y∥. The Voronoi cell under IP similarity
078   associated with vector x is represented as Vx . A graph is defined as G = (V, E), where V is the node set and
079
      E is the edge set. We use sup(S) to denote the supremum of a set S. O(·) and o(·) denote the big O and
      small o notations, respectively.
080
081   Problem Definition. The Maximum Inner Product Search (MIPS) problem is defined as follows: Given a
082   query vector q ∈ Rd and a dataset D = {x1 , x2 , ..., xn }, the goal is to find the vector x∗ ∈ D that maximizes
083   the inner product with the query: x∗ = arg maxx∈D ⟨q, x⟩.
084   Similarly, the Nearest Neighbor Search (NNS) problem is defined as finding the vector x∗ ∈ D that is
085   closest to the query q typically under the Euclidean distance: x∗ = arg minx∈D ∥q − x∥.
086
      Challenges in MIPS and Prior Work. Although the MIPS and NNS problems appear similar, the ap-
087
      proaches to solve each are not directly interchangeable. This is because IP is not a typical metric that
088
      accept properties like the triangle inequality, which are fundamental to many NNS algorithms. Methods
089   designed for the NNS problem leverage the triangle inequality to efficiently prune the search space (Malkov
090   & Yashunin, 2018; Tian et al., 2023; Babenko & Lempitsky, 2014; Ram & Gray, 2012; Fu et al., 2019),
091   thereby improving performance. Due to the lack of well-explored geometry favoring search in IP space,
092   most researchers turn to reduce the MIPS problem to an NNS problem and focus on the approximate MIPS
093   problem (Zhao et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2019; Shrivastava & Li, 2014; Yan et al., 2018; Li et al., 2018).

      This relaxed problem allows for an acceptable loss in accuracy in exchange for faster query processing or
095
      to accommodate transformations into metric spaces. The approximate MIPS problem is defined as follows:
096   Given a query q ∈ Rd , a dataset D ⊂ Rd , and an approximation ratio ϵ ∈ (0, 1), let p∗ ∈ D be the exact
097   MIPS solution for q. The goal is to find a vector p ∈ D satisfying: ⟨p, q⟩ ≥ ϵ · ⟨p∗ , q⟩.

169   In this section, we introduce the concept of dominators under the IP space. By focusing on these dominators,
170   we can leverage the intrinsic geometry of the IP space to improve search efficiency.

172   3.1   D OMINATORS AND C ONSTRUCTION OF NAIVE D OMINATOR G RAPH
173
174   We begin by defining dominators and exploring its properties in the context of IP space:
175   Definition 3 (Dominator of Voronoi Cell). Followed by Definition 1, the vector x ∈ D associated with
176   Voronoiip cell Vx is a dominator. Sdom represents dominators in D.
177
178
      Property 1. The Voronoiip cells associated with the dataset D provide a finite full space coverage of Rd .
179   Property 2. For any query q ∈ Rd , its MIPS solution lies within the dominator set Sdom ⊂ D.
180
181   From Property 1 and 2, we conclude that executing MIPS on the entire dataset D can be reduced to MIPS
182
      on the dominator set Sdom . This insight motivates the construction of a graph structure that focuses mainly
      on dominators, given the success of prior graph-based methods (Malkov & Yashunin, 2018; Fu et al., 2019).
183
184
185   Definition 4 (Naive Dominator Graph (NDG)). Given a dataset D ⊂ Rd , a Naive Dominator Graph
186   (NDG) G can be constructed as follows: For each point xi ∈ D, sort the remaining points in descending
187   order of ⟨xi , xj ⟩ to form a list L(xi ). Starting from the beginning of L(xi ), evaluate each point xj using

      In the proof of Theorem 1 (Appendix B.2), we further distinguish between two types of dominators based on
194   their relationship to their Voronoiip cells: A self-dominator resides within its own Voronoiip cell, meaning
195   if for all y ∈ D, ⟨x, x⟩ > ⟨x, y⟩, then x dominates itself and belongs to Vx . An out-dominator does
196   not reside in its own Voronoiip cell, meaning that ∃y ∈ D such that ⟨x, x⟩ ≤ ⟨x, y⟩, then x is potentially
197   dominated by y. Theorem 1 identifies dominators and builds a graph linking nodes to these dominators.
198   Because MIPS solutions reside within the dominator set (Property 2), we can prune the graph by keeping
199   only one self-dominator neighbor for each ordinary point, resulting in a sparser NDG (Figure 1 (c)).
200   Moreover, Sub-figures (e) to (h) in Figure 1 illustrate self-dominators, out-dominators, and ordinary points.
201   We can see that the Voronoiip boundaries are determined by the dominators, while ordinary points do not
202   contribute to the formation of boundaries. Additionally, a self-dominator dominates a capped hyper-cone
203   (sub-figure (h)) according to the definition. The cap is part of the surface of a hypersphere centered at x/2
204   with diameter ∥x∥, due to the condition ⟨x, x⟩ > ⟨y, x⟩, where y is any point potentially dominated by x.
205   The valid dominating region contains at least one point—the self-dominator itself—while out-dominators
206   do not reside in their own dominating regions, potentially leading to vacant dominating regions.
207   With above observations, we are more interested in self-dominators with respect to MIPS for three reasons:
208
209
      (1) Self-dominators tend to have large norms, as their squared norms exceed the IP values with any other
      vectors. Prior studies (Liu et al., 2020; Tan et al., 2019) have also shown MIPS solutions often cluster
210
      around large-norm points theoretically and empirically, making self-dominators strong MIPS candidates. (2)
211
      Using the edge selection from Definition 4, identifying self-dominators is straightforward, ensuring every
212   point is linked to at least one, ensuring efficient graph traversal via self-dominators. (3) Ordinary points in
213   out-dominators’ Voronoiip are not MIPS solutions, while out-dominators likely belong to the Voronoiip of
214   self-dominators. This implies MIPS solutions are concentrated within self-dominators’s Voronoiip cells.

216   3.2   I MPROVE MIPS E FFICIENCY W ITH M ONOTONIC R ELATIVE D OMINATOR G RAPH
217
218   It is practical to assume that queries primarily fall within the union of Voronoiip cells associated with self-
219   dominators. In modern machine learning, query vectors are often trained to align with the distribution of base
220   vectors (Bengio et al., 2013; Chen et al., 2020), making them more likely to be near self-dominators, which
221
      dominate larger regions. For these queries, MIPS can be streamlined by focusing solely on self-dominators.
      Thus, the search efficiency on a graph built around self-dominators depends on two factors: the density of
222
      self-dominators in the dataset and the efficiency of navigating through them.
223

227   is Pdom (x) = Φ(r), where Φ(·) is the cumulative distribution function (CDF) of the standard Gaussian.
228

      4     P RACTICAL A PPROXIMATION : ARDG
283
284
285   While MRDG is efficient for MIPS in theory, constructing it for large-scale datasets is impractical due to its
286   O(dn2 ) indexing complexity. To address this, we propose the Approximate Relative Dominator Graph
287   (ARDG), designed to reduce indexing costs. ARDG focuses on Locality, Connectivity, and Sparsity.
288
289
290   4.1   I NDEXING M ETHODOLOGY
291
292   Locality is a key principle in proximity graphs for vector-based search under common metrics (Wang et al.,
293   2021; Jayaram Subramanya et al., 2019). In top-k MIPS, maintaining locality—by connecting neighbors
294   with the largest IP similarity—enhances retrieval efficiency: If x is the top-1 solution for a query, the re-
295   maining k − 1 solutions are likely in x’s local neighborhood, allowing the algorithm to focus on the most
296   relevant areas. To preserve locality and speedup indexing, we use an efficient algorithm to build an approxi-
297
      mate k-MIP graph as the initial structure. Specifically, we use ScaNN to approximately retrieve k neighbors
      with the largest IP similarity for each point. These k-MIP neighbors are then used as candidates in further
298
      steps, ensuring good locality in the final ARDG, in line with the requirement of Theorem1.
299
300   Connectivity. As per Theorem 1 and the analysis in Section 3.3, it is essential to connect each node to
301   at least one self-dominator and maintain connectivity among self-dominators. To accurately identify self-
302   dominators, we apply dominator-oriented edge selection over a broader range of neighbors. Since building a
303
      large k-MIP graph is costly, we first construct a k-MIP graph with a small k ≪ n and enrich the candidates
      by including 2-hop neighbors. Self-dominators are then selected from the 2-hop neighbors via Theorem 1.

      4.2   C OMPLEXITY A NALYSIS
330
331   Indexing Complexity. ARDG indexing involves two stages: constructing the approximate k-MIP graph
332   and sparsifying it. The k-MIP graph construction, using ScaNN, has an empirical complexity of O(nd log n)
333   (Guo et al., 2020), where n is the number of data points and d is the dimensionality. Since we set a maximum
334   degree limit for each point, we can assume each node’s 2-hop neighbors is capped by a constant r, the edge
335   selection strategy incurs a complexity of O(r2 d) per node, leading to O(nr2 d) for the dataset. Thus, the
336   total indexing complexity is O(nd log n + cndr2 ), where c is a constant. The O(nd log n) term dominates
337   the growth rate as n increases given r ≪ n.
338   Search Complexity. As ARDG approximates MRDG, its search complexity is analytically intractable due
339   to potential effects on dominator accuracy and path length. However, empirical evaluations show that the
340   search complexity for top-k MIPS solutions on ARDG is only slightly higher than O(d log n), demonstrating
341   that ARDG closely approximates MRDG while maintaining scalability.
342   Worst Case Analysis can be found in Appendix.

464

      A    R ELATED W ORKS
659
660
661   Inner Product is crucial in AI and machine learning applications such as representation learning (Wang
662   et al., 2024), classification (Yu et al., 2014), clustering (Fujiwara et al., 2023), language modeling (Asai
663   et al., 2023), knowledge graphs (Xu et al., 2020), computer vision (Radford et al., 2021), and recommender
664   systems (Huang et al., 2020). Efficient MIPS is particularly essential for reducing system latency and im-
665   proving user experience in emerging RAG applications. MIPS methods are generally categorized into Lo-
666
      cality Sensitive Hashing (LSH), tree-, quantization-, and graph-based approaches:
667   LSH-based methods: Traditional LSH, originally designed for Euclidean space, is adapted for MIPS us-
668   ing transformations such as L2 (Shrivastava & Li, 2014), Correlation (Shrivastava & Li, 2015), and XBOX
669   (Bachrach et al., 2014). Range-LSH (Yan et al., 2018) is the first to observe that MIPS results cluster around
670   large-norm vectors. Simple-LSH (Neyshabur & Srebro, 2015) introduce a symmetric LSH that enjoys strong
671   guarantees. Fargo (Zhao et al., 2023) represents the recent state-of-the-art. Tree-based methods: Early
672
      MIPS approaches favored tree structures but struggled with high dimensionality. ProMIPS (Song et al.,
      2021) addresses this by projecting vectors into a lower-dimensional space, though information loss remains
673
      a challenge. LRUS-CoverTree (Ma et al., 2024) improves on this but faces difficulties with negative inner
674
      product values. Quantization-based methods: NEQ (Dai et al., 2020) quantizes the norms of items in a
675   dataset explicitly to reduce errors in norm. ScaNN (Guo et al., 2020) integrates ”VQ-PQ” with anisotropic
676   quantization loss, while SOAR (Sun et al., 2024) employs an orthogonality-amplified residual loss, achiev-
677   ing state-of-the-art performance and is integrated into the ScaNN library. Graph-based methods: Proven
678   effective for NNS, graph-based methods have been adapted for MIPS. ip-NSW (Morozov & Babenko, 2018)
679   first uses IP-Voronoi cell and constructs approximate Delaunay graphs using the inner product similarity. ip-
680   NSW+ improve its graph quality by adding an angular proximity graph, while Möbius-Graph employs a
681   Möbius transformation to perform greedy search on a transformed graph. IPDG (Tan et al., 2019) focuses
682   on identifying extreme points and uses a heuristic pruning strategy for top-1 MIPS. NAPG (Tan et al.,
683
      2021) introduces a non-continuous inner product-based metric, α⟨x, y⟩, with α varying based on the norm
      of neighbors. They modify ip-NSW using this new metric and claim state-of-the-art performance among
684
      graph-based methods.
685
686   Comparison between IPDG and our work. IPDG (Tan et al., 2019) defines extreme points as those domi-
687   nating non-empty Voronoiip cells, a concept intermediate between the out-dominator and self-dominator in
688   our framework. In our definition (Section 3), Voronoiip cells dominated by self-dominators are never empty,
689
      whereas out-dominators may dominate empty Voronoiip cells, as demonstrated in Figure 1. IPDG uses a
      heuristic edge pruning strategy where a neighbor xj is retained if ⟨xj , xj ⟩ > ⟨xi , xj ⟩ for all neighbor xi
690
      inserted before xj . This differs from our strategy (Theorem 1), which also requires that ⟨xi , xi ⟩ > ⟨xi , xj ⟩
691
      for all xi , providing stronger guarantees. From our perspective, IPDG’s approach may incorrectly link out-
692   dominators and even ordinary points as neighbors, resulting in a dense graph that contradicts their goal
693   of connecting only ”extreme points”. Furthermore, IPDG does not provide a theoretical analysis for this
694   heuristic. In contrast, ARDG inherits the theoretical guarantees of MRDG by approximating it, not only
695   through the edge pruning strategy in Theorem 1 to identify self-dominators, but also by sparsifying con-
696   nections among self-dominators using Definition 5, resulting in much sparser graphs with solid theoretical
697   foundations.

      Regarding the definition of the IP-Delaunay graph, there is currently no standardized consensus in the aca-
      demic community. The definition provided by iP-NSW (Morozov & Babenko, 2018) is tailored for the top-1
830
      MIPS problem and proves that greedy search in any graph containing an IP-Delaunay graph (as per their
831
      definition) as a subgraph always converges to the exact solution of the MIPS problem. However, unlike the
832   Delaunay graph in Euclidean space, their IP-Delaunay graph is not a globally connected graph. Thus, their
833   definition does not extend to the top-k MIPS problem, which is crucial in current retrieval scenarios. There-
834   fore, we have defined an IP-Delaunay graph that can query the top-k MIPS by building a strong connected
835   graph. To clarify, we have renamed it the Generalized IP-Delaunay Graph.

      D.4   W ORST C ASE A NALYSIS IN T IME C OMPLEXITY
978
979
      Consider a scenario in very high-dimensional spaces where all data points lie on a great circle (the inter-
      section of a hyperplane passing through the origin and a hypersphere centered at the origin). In this case,
980
      each point on this curve becomes a self-dominator, dominating only itself. When sparsifying the graph,
981
      both MRDG and ARDG will, with high probability, link each node to only its two adjacent neighbors on
982   the curve. Consequently ARDG will fail to accelerate search on such data, resulting in a linear search
983   complexity of O(nd). Additionally, constructing the ARDG incurs an approximate indexing complexity
984   of O(n2 d log n), since ARDG relies on other MIPS methods to construct the base k-MIP graph. In this
985   scenario, all other MIPS methods also fail to accelerate MIPS on this data. However, such extreme cases
986   hardly occur in practice.
