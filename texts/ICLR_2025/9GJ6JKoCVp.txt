009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent advancements in deep learning for neuroimaging have resulted in the de-
012            velopment of increasingly complex models designed for a wide range of tasks.
013            Despite significant improvements in hardware, enhancing inference and training
014            times for these models remains crucial. Through a numerical analysis of convo-
015            lutional neural networks (CNNs) inference, we found that a substantial amount of
016            operations in these models are applied to pure numerical noise, with little to no
017            impact on the final output. As a result, some CNNs consume up to two-thirds of
018            their floating-point operations unnecessarily.
019            To address this inefficiency, we introduce NaN Pooling & Convolution—novel
020            variations of PyTorch’s max pooling and 2D convolution operations. These tech-
021
               niques identify numerically unstable voxels and replace them with NaNs, allow-
               ing models to bypass operations on irrelevant data. We evaluate NaN Pooling and
022
               Convolution on two models: the FastSurfer CNN, a widely used neuroimaging
023
               tool, and a CNN designed to classify the MNIST dataset. For FastSurfer, our ap-
024            proach significantly improves computational efficiency, skipping between 33.24%
025            and 69.30% of convolutions in certain layers while preserving the model’s origi-
026            nal accuracy. On MNIST, our approach skips up to 28.38% of convolutions, again
027            without major impact on the accuracy.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), in particular U-Nets, are transforming neuroimaging by
033   progressively replacing traditional image analysis software with models that deliver comparable
034   performance in a fraction of the runtime. This advancement significantly enhances the field, en-
035   abling the routine processing of larger databases in reasonable timeframes. However, optimizing the
036   inference and training times of these models remains a critical challenge, as improvements in this
037   area could facilitate near-real-time analyses across various applications and support the training of
038
      larger models for tasks currently unattainable with existing approaches.
039   Through previous investigations of numerical stability in CNNs, summarized in Appendix A.5, we
040   identified a numerical instability in the max pooling operation that leads to the propagation of pure
041   numerical noise in approximately two thirds of the embedding values. Consequently, a substantial
042   number of operations—particularly convolutions—end up only processing this noise and could be
043   eliminated to improve efficiency.
044   The source of this instability is illustrated in Figure 1. When the forward calculation of the max
045   pooling operation is applied to a relatively uniform window—where multiple values can achieve the
046   maximum up to an epsilon—the position of the max index becomes undetermined. Multiple values
047   in this window are now eligible for being the maximum value and they each have a different index.
048   This has no immediate impact, but when the unpooling operation is called, the instability arises.
049   Unpooling uses the indices saved from the max pooling operation to restore the maximum values
050
      to their original positions, filling the remaining voxels with zeros. The undetermination in the max
      pooling operation leads to several values in the unpooling operation being assigned either a zero or
051
      a non-zero value, resulting in a total loss of numerical precision.
052
053   Surprisingly, models affected by this numerical issue can still be trained and produce accurate results
      despite the widespread propagation of numerical noise. This suggests that the impacted values

055   efficiency.
056
057   To capitalize on this observation, the execution framework must account for numerical precision,
058
      allowing operations on numerical noise to be bypassed. However, accurately measuring and repre-
      senting numerical precision at the scale of CNN executions is impractical and would significantly
059
      slow down processing. Instead, we represent values with no numerical significance using IEEE NaN
060
      values, which are already supported by existing frameworks. We have modified the max pooling op-
061   eration to generate NaNs in case of numerical instability, and we adjusted the convolution operation
062   to handle tensors containing NaNs, bypassing computations when NaNs exceed a specified thresh-
063   old.
064
      We tested NaN Pooling on two CNNs: FastSurfer, a widely-used neuroimaging U-Net for whole-
065
      brain segmentation Henschel et al. (2020), and a CNN classifying the MNIST dataset LeCun &
066
      Cortes (1998). Our results demonstrate that for FastSurfer, NaN Pooling can bypass up to 69.30%
067   convolutions in some layers, and up to 44% convolutions in a full model while maintaining the
068   accuracy of model outputs. For MNIST, NaN Pooling skips up to 28.38% convolutions, again while
069   achieving comparable accuracy.

      2.1   N UMERICAL I NSTABILITY IN M AX P OOLING
074
075   Max pooling Boureau et al. (2010) is a widely used downsampling technique that replaces a defined
076   window of values with its maximum value. It can optionally return indices that indicate the original
077   locations of these maximum values. During upsampling, max unpooling uses these indices to restore
078   the maximum values to their original positions, filling the remaining voxels with zeros. This process
079   ensures that the spatial structure of the input data is partially reconstructed based on the locations
080   of the selected maximum values. The indices generated during max pooling are especially useful in
081   U-Net architectures, where downsampling and upsampling processes are frequently coupled Zeiler
082   et al. (2010); Çiçek et al. (2016); Lu et al. (2019); Plascencia et al. (2023); De Feo et al. (2021).
083   In our previous work Anonymized, we investigated the numerical uncertainty of CNNs during in-
084   ference. We found that numerical instabilities arose during max unpooling operations due to fluctu-
085   ations in the indices passed to this process. When values within a pooling window are close to each
086   other, even slight noise—introduced, for example, by variations in the execution environment—can
087   lead to index shifts while the maximum value remains unchanged. This instability is particularly ev-
088   ident when upsampling is applied to areas of an image’s background, where uniform values prevail.
089
      Interestingly, we observed that the propagation of this numerical noise did not adversely impact the
      final outputs of the models we tested.
090
091   Unstable voxels contribute no meaningful information to the model. To address this inefficiency, we
092   propose NaN Pooling and Convolution as a way to bypass operations on such irrelevant voxels. In
093   floating-point arithmetic, NaNs (Not-a-Number) are special values defined by the IEEE 754 stan-
094   dard to represent undefined or unrepresentable results, such as 0 divided by 0 or the square root of
095
      a negative number. A NaN is represented by an exponent of all ones and a non-zero mantissa, and
      it is used to flag errors or exceptional conditions in calculations. Leveraging this concept, we use
096
      NaNs to mark numerically irrelevant voxels, effectively skipping over operations that would other-
097
      wise be wasted on data that provides no useful information. This approach enhances computational
098   efficiency by allowing the model to focus on relevant data, without altering the final output or model
099   performance.

      2.4   NA N C ONVOLUTION I MPLEMENTATION
177
178   Implementing NaN Convolution requires modifying PyTorch’s internal handling of 2D convolutions.
179   Instead of processing convolutions on a window-by-window basis, PyTorch executes an entire con-
180   volution layer as a single matrix multiplication. This formulation requires us to adapt the NaN
181   Convolution definition to align with PyTorch’s more efficient computational strategy.
182   PyTorch’s implementation of 2D convolutions lies in the im2col technique, illustrated in the Ap-
183   pendix Figure 7. This operation reshapes the input tensor by extracting overlapping subregions
184   (corresponding to the convolutional kernel’s dimensions) and arranges them into columns, which
185   can then be multiplied with the kernel weights in a single matrix multiplication step. To reduce the
186   occurrence of random memory seeks, the im2col process is typically column-major, meaning data
187   is stored and processed primarily by columns. This technique dramatically increases computational
188   efficiency compared to the naive approach Chetlur et al. (2014).
189   For an input of size (N, C, Hin , Win ) and a kernel of size (C, Hk , Wk ), im2col extracts subre-
190   gions of size (C, Hk , Wk ) and arranges them into columns, repeating the process for each of the N
191   batches. In our NaN Convolution implementation, we reduce the number of columns by removing
192   subregions that exceed a predefined NaN threshold. By eliminating these irrelevant columns, we
193   reduce the memory footprint and the computational load, thus improving overall performance. This
194   optimization aligns with our theoretical expectations, offering a practical approach to reducing the
195   computational inefficiencies while preserving model accuracy.

      3     E XPERIMENTS
232
233
      We evaluated NaN Pooling and Convolution on two different CNNs. The first use case is a popular
      neuroimaging U-Net, FastSurfer, using a representative set of images from a dataset provided by
234
      the Consortium for Reliability and Reproducibility (CoRR) Zuo et al. (2014). Evaluation metrics
235
      for FastSurfer included the ratio of skipped convolutions, and the loss functions utilized to train the
236   original model. The second CNN is built to perform digit classification on the MNIST dataset Le-
237   Cun & Cortes (1998). It is a widely known task that showcases NaN Pooling and Convolutions’
238   applicability beyond neuroimaging specific models.
239
240   3.1   FAST S URFER
241
242   FastSurfer is a CNN model that performs whole-brain segmentation, cortical surface reconstruc-
243   tion, fast spherical mapping, and cortical thickness analysis. The FastSurfer CNN is inspired from
244   the QuickNAT model Roy et al. (2019), which is composed of three 2D fully convolutional neu-
245   ral networks—each associated with a different 2D slice orientation—that each have the same en-
246
      coder/decoder U-net architecture with skip connections, unpooling layers and dense connections
      as QuickNAT. A diagram of the model’s architecture is available in the Appendix Figure 8. The
247
      FastSurfer segmentations were shown to surpass state-of-the-art methods, as well as being general-
248
      izable to unseen datasets and having better test-retest reliability. We used the pre-trained model from
249   FastSurfer available on GitHub fas. We focus exclusively on the task of whole-brain segmentation,
250   defined as the labeling of different anatomical brain regions, which is performed solely by the CNN.
251
252   3.2   MNIST
253
254   To evaluate the performance and behavior of NaN Pooling and NaN Convolution, we conducted
255   experiments on a small CNN trained on the MNIST dataset. The model architecture included alter-
256   nating convolutional and ReLU activation layers, each followed by a pooling layer, repeated three
257   times, and concluded with a final convolutional layer, a pooling layer, and a log-softmax output
258   layer. While this architecture is not a U-Net — considered the ideal setting for applying NaN Pool-
259
      ing and Convolutions — we show that this approach is also effective for other CNN architectures
      utilizing convolution and max pooling operations. The task, a classification problem to identify dig-
260
      its from the input images, is well-established and widely considered solved. This experiment served
261
      as a baseline to demonstrate the feasibility and potential benefits of NaN Pooling in a controlled,
262   well-understood context.

293   4.1   NA N P OOLING AND C ONVOLUTION S AVES 39% OF C ONVOLUTIONS ON AVERAGE
294
295   To quantify the acceleration introduced by NaN Pooling and Convolution, we measured the number
296
      of convolutional operations replaced with NaNs in the FastSurfer model. We tested the techniques
      with several thresholds ranging from 1 to 0.5 depending on the use case. The threshold represents
297
      the ratio of NaNs required to skip an operation, with threshold 1 being the most stringent (skipping
298
      operations only when the input consists entirely of NaNs) and threshold 0.5 being more lenient
299   (skipping operations when 50% or more of the values are NaNs).
300
301   Our results revealed that the numerically unstable voxels impacted by NaN Pooling and Convolution
302
      are typically found in the background of the input data. This often numerically irrelevant background
      can comprise up to two-thirds of the total input space, rendering it ideal for optimization. To quantify
303
      the computational impact, we calculated the ratio of skipped operations relative to the total number
304
      of convolutional operations. This ratio was tracked both across the architectural layers of the models
305   and across brain slices in the neuroimaging data, providing a comprehensive view of the effect of
306   NaN Pooling and Convolution.
307
308
      Figure 2 shows the impact of NaN Pooling and Convolution on FastSurfer during inference. As
      illustrated in Figure 2a, skipped operations only occur between the Encode 1 and Decode 2 blocks.
309
      This is because no NaN Pooling is applied prior to Encode 1, and no NaN Pooling occurs after
310
      the Bottleneck layer, leading to a decline in skipped operations after Decode 2 as NaNs become
311   sparse. As a result, the majority of skipped operations are concentrated in the middle sections of the
312   model with the exception of the Bottleneck block. The drop here is due to the U-Net architecture’s
313   reduced spatial dimensions at this stage, which prioritises the preservation of relevant information
314   while minimising the presence of numerically irrelevant voxels (represented by NaNs).
315
      In Figure 2b, we observe higher skipped operation ratios at the extremes of the brain slice distri-
316
      bution, which implies a higher amount of numerically irrelevant voxels. Upon examination, these
317   slices contain a larger proportion of background than brain matter voxels. This is consistent with
318   preliminary results which suggested background voxels are largely numerically irrelevant.
319
320

      44.19% at thresholds of 1 and 0.5, respectively. When focusing solely on model layers affected by
321
      NaNs, the skipped operations increased significantly to 50.59% and 69.30% for these thresholds.
322

507   5   C ONCLUSION
508
509
510
      This paper introduced NaN Pooling and Convolution, new variations of PyTorch’s max pooling and
      2D convolution operations, which are designed to enhance the efficiency of U-Net models. The
511
      techniques identify and leverage numerically unstable voxels, which don’t contribute to the model’s
512
      output, by replacing them with NaNs. This allows the model to bypass operations on irrelevant data,
513   thereby saving computation time. These potential benefits aren’t limited to neuroimaging and could
514   be advantageous in other fields facing similar challenges related to computational demands and data
515   efficiency.
516
      We evaluated the effectiveness of these methods on two use cases: the widely used neuroimaging
517
      U-Net, FastSurfer, and the MNIST benchmark CNN. For FastSurfer, our NaN Pooling and Convolu-
518
      tion techniques achieved a 39% reduction in total convolutional operations on average, significantly
519   improving computational efficiency without compromising model accuracy. The NaN-FastSurfer
520   demonstrated performance comparable to its default implementation. Similarly, in the MNIST
521   benchmark CNN, we achieved up to a 28.38% reduction in convolutional operations while main-
522   taining comparable accuracy. However, the MNIST model showed greater sensitivity to threshold
523   variations compared to FastSurfer, highlighting the importance of carefully balancing efficiency and
524   performance when determining the optimal threshold.
525   Despite successfully skipping operations, we have not yet observed a direct impact on runtime
526   speed-up, likely due to PyTorch’s inherent computational optimizations. As a result, our analy-
527   sis focuses on the number of skipped convolutional operations as a proxy for computational effi-
528   ciency. Therefore, future work will focus on extending the application of NaN Pooling and Con-
529   volution to the training of models and delivering the expected runtime speed-ups from skipped op-
530   erations in practice. Further investigation is required to evaluate potential hardware limitations,
531   implementation-specific factors or investigating alternative implementations that leverage sparse
532   matrix representations or specific hardware architectures.

      In our previous work Anonymized, we examined the numerical uncertainty of the FastSurfer CNN
689
      during inference, focusing on the stability of the final classification results as well as the embeddings.
690
691   In order to do so, we used Monte Carlo Arithmetic (MCA) Parker (1997), a stochastic arithmetic
692   technique that introduces randomness to assess numerical uncertainty. MCA is implemented through
693
      the Verrou tool Févotte & Lathuiliere (2016), a tool that leverages Valgrind Nethercote & Seward
      (2007) to dynamically instruments binary executables with MCA. Using Verrou, we instrumented
694
      FastSurfer inference to generate 10 iterations of the model’s embeddings, each subjected to random
695
      perturbations introduced during execution. These perturbations allowed us to simulate and analyze
696   numerical uncertainty inherent in the model. To quantify this uncertainty, we computed the number
697   of significant digits across the 10 iterations. Significant digits Sohier et al. (2021) measure numerical
698   uncertainty by determining the number of digits shared in common across multiple MCA samples
699   for a given floating-point value. This analysis was performed for every layer of the FastSurfer
700   model, with the results visualized as heatmaps in Figure10. This revealed that a large fraction of the
701   model embeddings were purely numerical noise (zero significant digits), represented by red-colored
      regions in the figure.
