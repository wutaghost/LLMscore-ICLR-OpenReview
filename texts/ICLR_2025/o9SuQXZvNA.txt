009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models (LLMs) hold great promise to revolutionize current clinical
012            systems for their superior capacities on medical text processing tasks and medical
013            licensing exams. Meanwhile, traditional ML models such as SVM and XGBoost
014            have still been mainly adopted in clinical prediction tasks. An emerging question
015            is Can LLMs beat traditional ML models in clinical prediction? Thus, we build a
016            new benchmark ClinicalBench to comprehensively study the clinical predictive
017            modeling capacities of both general-purpose and medical LLMs, and compare
018            them with traditional ML models. ClinicalBench embraces three common clinical
019
               prediction tasks, two databases, 14 general-purpose LLMs, 8 medical LLMs, and
               11 traditional ML models. Through extensive empirical investigation, we discover
020
               that both general-purpose and medical LLMs, even with different model scales,
021
               diverse prompting or fine-tuning strategies, still cannot beat traditional ML
022            models in clinical prediction yet, shedding light on their surprising but critical
023            deficiency in clinical reasoning. We call for caution when practitioners adopt LLMs
024            in clinical applications. ClinicalBench can be utilized to bridge the gap between
025            LLMs’ development for healthcare and real-world clinical practice. Code is here.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Large Language Models (LLMs) have shown great potential to revolutionize existing clinical systems
030   for their superior capacities on a variety of medical text processing tasks including document classifi-
031   cation, report generation and summarization, and information extraction (Jahan et al., 2024; Chen
032   et al., 2023a; Zhou et al., 2023). Also, LLMs could even rival human clinician performance in medical
033
      licensing exams (Singhal et al., 2023a;b; Gilson et al., 2023) and clinical case challenges (Kanjee
      et al., 2023; McDuff et al., 2023). However, traditional machine learning (ML) models such as
034
      Logistic Regression, SVM, XGBoost, MLP and Transformer have still been predominantly adopted
035
      for clinical prediction tasks in real-world practice (van de Water et al., 2024; Moazemi et al., 2023),
036   which play an essential role in modern health systems to improve patient outcomes and support
037   clinical decision making. Thus, considering LLMs’ profound world knowledge and strong reasoning
038   capacities, an emerging question is: Can LLMs beat traditional ML models in clinical prediction?
039
      In this paper, we propose to build a new benchmark ClinicalBench to comprehensively and system-
040
      atically investigate the capacities of LLMs in clinical prediction tasks. More importantly, we compare
041
      the performance of LLMs with diverse traditional ML models in a head-to-head way to explore
042   the feasibility of adopting LLMs in real-world clinical prediction. Specifically, following previous
043   works (Wang et al., 2020; van de Water et al., 2024), we adopt three common clinical prediction tasks
044   including Length-of-Stay Prediction, Mortality Prediction and Readmission Prediction and two real
045   clinical databases MIMIC-III (Johnson et al., 2016) and MIMIC-IV (Johnson et al., 2020). As for
046   the benchmarked methods, ClinicalBench embraces 11 traditional ML models and 22 LLMs with
047   different scales, which include 14 general-purpose LLMs and 8 medical LLMs.
048   In ClinicalBench, we aim to answer three research questions through extensive empirical studies.
049   The first question is Can directly prompting LLMs beat traditional ML models? As shown in Figure 1,
050   to effectively leverage the language understanding capacities of LLMs, we first convert the original
051   clinical codes in datasets to texts and then let LLMs output the predicted label directly. Comparing
052   the performance of both 9 general-purpose LLMs and 6 medical LLMs with around 7B parameters to
053   traditional ML models, we discover that traditional ML models can mostly outperform LLMs with
      a regular scale by a large margin across three tasks and two datasets. Comparing general-purpose

      The second question is Can LLMs with prompt engineering beat traditional ML models? We studied
074
      the efficacy of four common prompting strategies including Zero-shot Chain-of-Thought (Kojima
075   et al., 2022), Self-Reflection (Pan et al., 2023), Role-Playing (Chen et al., 2024b), and In-Context
076   Learning (Dong et al., 2023) on both general-purpose LLMs and medical LLMs. In general, we find
077   that the effectiveness of different prompting strategies is very limited. Except the improvement
078   brought by In-Context Learning on Llama3-8B, Gemma2-9B, and Internist-7B for Length-of-Stay
079   Prediction, the aforementioned prompting strategies do not enhance the performance of LLMs in
080   clinical prediction across three tasks and two datasets in a noticeable way.
081   The third question is Can fine-tuned LLMs beat traditional ML models? We compare the performance
082   of traditional ML models and LLMs with the same dataset split on training, validation and test sets.
083   With thorough investigation over 2 fine-tuning strategies, 4 LLMs, 3 clinical prediction tasks and 2
084   datasets, we find that fine-tuning is clearly effective for certain tasks though the effectiveness
085   varies across different models. As for Length-of-Stay Prediction and Mortality Prediction, we
086   observe that fine-tuning can evidently enhance the clinical prediction performance of LLMs though
087   the extent of improvement depends on models. As for Readmission Prediction, we do not notice
088   fine-tuning can bring any enhancement. However, most fine-tuned LLMs still cannot surpass
089
      typical traditional ML models such as XGBoost, SVM, Transformer and RNN.
090   Overall, the contributions of this paper can be summarized as follows:
091
      • We built a new benchmark ClinicalBench, embracing 14 general-purpose LLMs, 8 medical LLMs,
092
        11 traditional ML models, three tasks and two databases, which made the first attempt to compare
093
        the clinical prediction capacities of LLMs and traditional ML models in a head-to-head way.
094
      • We discover that both general-purpose and medical LLMs, even with different model sizes, prompt-
095
        ing or fine-tuning strategies, still cannot beat traditional ML models in clinical prediction yet.
096
097   • Our findings demonstrate the surprising but critical deficiency of both general-purpose and
098
        medical LLMs in real-world clinical reasoning and decision-making, which could have almost
        clinician-level performance in medical licensing exams and clinical case challenges. We call for
099
        caution when adopting LLMs in practical clinical applications. ClinicalBench could be leveraged
100
        to bridge the gap between the development of LLMs for healthcare and real-world clinical practice.

      2.1        C LINICAL P REDICTION TASKS
106
107   In ClinicalBench, following previous works (Wang et al., 2020; van de Water et al., 2024), we focus
      on three common clinical prediction tasks including Length-of-Stay Prediction, Mortality Prediction,


                                                                                                2

108
      and Readmission Prediction. Specifically, Length-of-Stay Prediction is simplified as a three-class
109   classification task aiming to predict the length of the current hospital visit as less than one week, 1 ~ 2
110   weeks, or more than two weeks. Mortality Prediction is a binary classification task which intends
111   to estimate whether the patient will decease in the current visit. Readmission Prediction is a binary
112   classification task designed to identify patients who are at high risk of being readmitted to the hospital
113   within a specific time frame. Following PyHealth (Yang et al., 2023), each task requires models
114   to make predictions based on patients’ demographic features (e.g., gender and age) and clinical
115   information including diagnosis, medical procedures, medications and drugs for the current visit.
116
      2.2      DATASET P ROCESSING
117
118   We adopt MIMIC-III-v1.4 (Johnson et al., 2016) and MIMIC-IV-v2.2 (Johnson et al., 2020) in
119   ClinicalBench, which are two large and freely accessible databases and widely adopted in the fields
120   of healthcare data science. They contain de-identified data associated with ICU admissions at the
121   Beth Israel Deaconess Medical Center during 2001-2012 and 2008-2019 respectively.
122
123
      Dataset Filtering We generally follow the filtering criteria in previous literature (Yang et al., 2023;
      Purushotham et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2020; Tang et al., 2020). First, we only keep the samples of the
124
      first-time visits for each patient since other visits require the consideration of patients’ history, which
125
      desire different prediction strategies compared with first-time visits. Second, we filter out patients
126   who are younger than 18 due to the substantial differences between pediatric and adult physiology.
127   Third, we also did not consider patients with missing values following the literature.
128
129   Dataset Division For traditional ML models, we first adopt stratified split to divide the original
130   dataset into training, validation and test sets. Due to the label imbalance of training set for three
131   tasks, we apply under-sampling to rebalance the training set but leave the validation and test sets
132
      unchanged, which can reflect the real-world clinical prediction performance of different models. For
      prompting-based methods, we directly assess the performance of LLMs on the same test set. For
133
      fine-tuning based methods, the division is the same as traditional ML models for fair comparison.
134
135   Code-to-Text Conversion It is worth noting that the information of patient diagnosis, medical
136   procedures, medications and drugs in original MIMIC-III (MIMIC-IV) dataset is stored in the form of
137   ICD-9 (ICD-10) diagnosis codes, ICD-9 (ICD-10) procedure codes and ATC codes respectively. To
138   effectively leverage the natural language understanding and reasoning capacities of LLMs, as shown
139   in Figure 1, we first convert the codes into texts and then design the specific prompts for LLMs.
140
      2.3      B ENCHMARKED M ETHODS
141
142   We compare mainstream traditional ML models and LLMs in ClinicalBench. The traditional ML
143   models embrace XGBoost, Logistic Regression, Decision Tree, Random Forest, AdaBoost, SVM,
144   Naive Bayes and neural network models such as MLP, Transformer and RNN. For LLMs, we first
145   investigated directly prompting both general-purpose and medical LLMs with different decoding
146
      temperatures and model scales. Then, we explored the effectiveness of diverse prompting and fine-
      tuning strategies. The examples of specific prompts for the different strategies are in Appendix D. It is
147
      worth noting that we only evaluate open-source LLMs in ClinicalBench since closed-source models
148
      such as GPT-4 are prohibited for MIMIC-III and MIMIC-IV according to their data use policy 1 .

304

      et al., 2024), Qwen2 (Yang et al., 2024), and Yi-v1.5 (Young et al., 2024) and two medical model

312   Readmission Prediction. Qwen2-1.5B outperforms Qwen2-0.5B and Qwen2-7B for Length-of-Stay
313   Prediction and Readmission Prediction. We also notice that parameter scaling could even hurt the

      and Yi-v1.5-34B for Length-of-Stay Prediction on both MIMIC-III and MIMIC-IV datasets.
316   However, through the shades of red color, we can clearly see that even though the performance of
317   some LLMs is improved for certain tasks as the parameter scales expand, they still underperform
318   typical traditional ML models such as XGBoost, SVM, RNN and Transformer. For example,
319   although the performance is substantially enhanced for Meditron-70B compared to Meditron-7B in

418   Fine-tuning is a common paradigm to adapt LLMs to specific tasks or domains (Zhang et al., 2023;
419   Lou et al., 2024). Our results on medical LLMs in Table 1 have shown that only adapting general-
420   purpose LLMs to medical domain may not improve and could even degrade the clinical reasoning
421   capacities. In this section, we explored whether directly adapting general-purpose LLMs to clinical
422
      prediction tasks can enhance their performance and also compare them with traditional ML models.
423   For a fair comparison, we adopted the same dataset division of training, validation and test sets for
424   fine-tuning LLMs and training traditional ML models. The details of fine-tuning data construction
425   for the three clinical prediction tasks are in Appendix C. Considering the relatively small scale of the
426   training set, we adjusted the original LoRA (Hu et al., 2022) into two fine-tuning strategies named
427   LoRA (Full) and LoRA (Last Layer). We set the training epochs for both LLMs and traditional ML
428
      models as 20. The loss curves of fine-tuning in Appendix B.5 show that LLMs can mostly converge
      within 20 epochs and we leveraged the validation set to select the optimal fine-tuned checkpoint.
429
430   As shown in Figure 4, we can observe that fine-tuning can clearly enhance the clinical prediction
431   performance of LLMs on Length-of-Stay Prediction and Mortality Prediction, though it may
      not improve the performance on Readmission Prediction. More specifically, LoRA (Full) brings

      6     I MPLICATIONS AND H YPOTHESIS
446
447
      It has attracted increasing attention to transform the existing healthcare systems with LLMs (Zhou
448   et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024b). While the high performance in medical licensing exams and question-
449   answering benchmarks such as MedQA (Jin et al., 2021) and MedMCQA (Pal et al., 2022) has
450   shown that LLMs may have clinician-level general medical knowledge, it is still highly under-
451   explored whether they could perform clinical reasoning and decision-making with real-world complex
452   scenarios. ClinicalBench made the first attempt to systematically and comprehensively benchmark
453   the clinical reasoning capacities of both general-purpose and medical LLMs based on three real-
454   world clinical prediction tasks. By comparing their performance with traditional ML models, we
455   have shown LLMs’ surprising but critical deficiency in performing real-world clinical reasoning,
456
      implying their safety and reliability risks in practical adoption and the challenges of integrating
      LLMs into clinical workflows. Our discoveries also echo the recent discussions on LLMs’ potential
457
      limitations (Hager et al., 2024; Han et al., 2024; Gallifant et al., 2024). We hypothesize that LLMs’
458
      limited clinical reasoning abilities could be attributed to the lack of realistic and relevant data in
459   both of the pre-training and post-training stages, considering the sensitivity nature of patients’
460   information. The recent emerging clinical digital twin (Katsoulakis et al., 2024; Das et al., 2023; Sun
461   et al., 2023) and data synthesis (Liu et al., 2024d; Bauer et al., 2024; Tan et al., 2024) techniques
462   could be explored in the future to address this challenge. ClinicalBench could be adopted to facilitate
463   the progress in enhancing LLMs’ clinical reasoning capacities and minimize the gap between the
464   development of LLMs for healthcare and clinical practice in the real world.
465
466   7     R ELATED W ORK
467
468   Clinical Prediction Clinical prediction tasks play a critical role in current healthcare systems
469   with multifaceted significance including improving patient outcomes, optimizing hospital resources,
470   and supporting clinical decision-making (Rajkomar et al., 2019). Length-of-Stay Prediction (Stone
471   et al., 2022), Mortality Prediction (Jentzer et al., 2021), and Readmission Prediction (Artetxe et al.,
472   2018) are among the most common ones. While traditional ML models such as XGBoost, SVM,
473   and RNN have still been widely adopted in these tasks (Moazemi et al., 2023; van de Water et al.,
474   2024), many advanced models have also been developed for clinical predictive modeling (Wang
475
      et al., 2024b). For example, Xu et al. (2023) proposed to leverage hypergraph transformers with
      patients as hyperedges and medical codes as nodes for predictive tasks. Jiang et al. (2024) leveraged
476
      personalized knowledge graphs and attention-augmented graph neural networks for enhancing the
477
      prediction performance. Cui et al. (2024) conducted diffusion-based data augmentation to further
478   improve health risk prediction. Although the emerging LLMs may have been demonstrated to perform
479   well on medical question-answering and hold great promise to transform healthcare systems, their
480   capacities on clinical prediction tasks are largely under-explored. ClinicalBench shows that LLMs
481   cannot beat traditional ML models yet, suggesting their critical limitations in clinical applications.
482
483   Clinical Benchmarks for LLMs The majority of existing clinical benchmarks for LLMs can
484   generally be categorized from two perspectives. The first one aims to assess LLMs’ capacities in
485   clinical text processing tasks (Harris et al., 2024; Jahan et al., 2024; Feng et al., 2024; Chen et al.,
      2023a; Wang et al., 2023a; Luo et al., 2024). For example, Jahan et al. (2024) has comprehensively


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      evaluated four LLMs in different typical biomedical text tasks (e.g., named entity recognition, relation
487   extraction, entity linking, text classification and text summarization with biomedical texts). The
488   second one intends to evaluate LLMs performance in clinical question-answering tasks. Besides
489   MedQA (Jin et al., 2021) and MedMCQA (Pal et al., 2022), many recent benchmarks have been
490   built to test the medical knowledge of LLMs in different aspects (Korgul et al., 2023; Chen et al.,
491   2024a; Vladika et al., 2024; Shoham & Rappoport, 2024). For example, Chen et al. (2024c) and
492   Wang et al. (2024a) have developed QA benchmarks to assess the diagnostic performance of LLMs in
493   rare diseases. Kweon et al. (2024), Kasai et al. (2023), Rosoł et al. (2023), Alonso et al. (2024), Cai
494   et al. (2024a), Wang et al. (2023b), and Liu et al. (2024a;c) designed QA benchmarks with languages
495
      beyond English such as Korean, Japanese, Polish and Chinese. However, benchmarks on LLMs’
      clinical reasoning capacities with real-world complex scenarios are relatively lacking. ClinicalBench
496
      filled the gap through a comprehensive investigation involving three common clinical prediction
497
      tasks, two databases, 14 general-purpose LLMs, 8 medical LLMs and has provided valuable insights.
498

500   8    C ONCLUSION
501
502   In this paper, we have built a new benchmark ClinicalBench to comprehensively and systematically
503   compare the effectiveness of traditional ML models and LLMs in typical clinical prediction tasks in-
504
      cluding Length-of-Stay Prediction, Mortality Prediction, and Readmission Prediction. With extensive
      empirical evidence, we find that general-purpose and medical LLMs, even with different scales of
505
      parameters, diverse prompting or fine-tuning strategies, still cannot beat traditional ML models in
506
      clinical prediction yet. Our findings have illustrated the critical limitations of LLMs in performing
507   real-world clinical reasoning and decision-making with complex scenarios. We urge practitioners to
508   exercise caution when adopting LLMs in real-world clinical applications.
