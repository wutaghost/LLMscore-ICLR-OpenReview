026                                                A BSTRACT
027
028            This paper explores the problem of commonsense-level vision-knowledge conflict
029            in Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs), where visual information contra-
030            dicts model’s internal commonsense knowledge (see Figure 1). To study this issue,
031
               we introduce an automated pipeline, augmented with human-in-the-loop quality
               control, to establish a benchmark aimed at simulating and assessing the conflicts in
032
               MLLMs. Utilizing this pipeline, we have crafted a diagnostic benchmark compris-
033
               ing 374 original images and 1,122 high-quality question-answer (QA) pairs. This
034            benchmark covers two types of conflict targets and three question difficulty levels,
035            providing a thorough assessment tool. Through this benchmark, we evaluate the
036            conflict-resolution capabilities of nine representative MLLMs across various model
037            families and find a noticeable over-reliance on textual queries. Drawing on these
038            findings, we propose a novel prompting strategy, “Focus-on-Vision” (FoV), which
039            markedly enhances MLLMs’ ability to favor visual data over conflicting textual
040            knowledge. Our detailed analysis and the newly proposed strategy significantly
041            advance the understanding and mitigating of vision-knowledge conflicts in MLLMs.
042
               The data and code will be released.
043

      1    I NTRODUCTION
045
046
      Large Language Models (LLMs) (Brown et al., 2020; OpenAI, 2022; Touvron et al., 2023; OpenAI,
047
      2023a; Meta AI, 2024) have reshaped the landscape of deep learning for their comprehensive
048   capabilities in language understanding, reasoning, and generation (Wei et al., 2022a;b; Chen et al.,
049   2023). This evolution has paved the way for the emergence of Multimodal Large Language Models
050   (MLLMs) (Li et al., 2022a; 2023b; Lyu et al., 2023; Dai et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024d; Zhu et al., 2023;
051   Bai et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024b; Wang et al., 2024; OpenAI, 2023b; Liu et al., 2024c; Tong et al.,
052   2024; Gemini Team, 2024; OpenAI, 2024b), which integrate a vision model with the LLM to process
053   and respond to visual information. MLLMs like GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024b) and LLaVA-NeXT (Liu
      et al., 2024c) have exhibited remarkable proficiency across various vision-language tasks such as


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      image captioning (Chen et al., 2015), visual question answering (VQA) (Antol et al., 2015), and
055   visual reasoning (Johnson et al., 2017; Yue et al., 2024).
056
057   However, the persistence of knowledge conflicts in LLMs remains a significant challenge for MLLMs,
058
      often cited as a primary cause of hallucinations in these systems (Liu et al., 2023a; Guan et al., 2024).
      Introducing visual information into MLLMs generates a novel form of discrepancy termed “vision-
059
      knowledge conflict”, where the visual data contradicts the model’s pre-existing parametric knowledge.
060
      Prior research has mainly focused on assessing and comprehending these conflicts through the lens
061   of factual knowledge by generating counterfactual images via image editing techniques (Guan et al.,
062   2024). Nonetheless, the aspect of vision-knowledge conflict related to commonsense knowledge,1
063   which proves more challenging for LLMs and MLLMs due to its implicit and nuanced nature (Li
064   et al., 2022b), remains under-investigated.
065
      To address this gap, we propose an automated pipeline with human-in-the-loop to develop a bench-
066
      mark for simulating and analyzing commonsense-level vision-knowledge conflicts in MLLMs.
067   Illustrated in Figure 2, our pipeline consists of four key modules: knowledge component extraction,
068   counter-commonsense query construction, image generation, and question-and-answer (QA) genera-
069   tion. This framework streamlines the generation of counter-commonsense inputs from scratch and
070   is modularly designed to facilitate the addition of further conflict categories and QA formats in the
071   future. We demonstrate the application of our framework by developing the C ONFLICT V IS bench-
072   mark built on the Open Mind Common Sense dataset (Singh et al., 2002). The benchmark focuses on
073   the critical aspects of commonsense knowledge in the visual scene: Subject, Action, and Place. It
074   consists of 374 original images with 1,122 high-quality QA pairs spanning two conflict targets and
075
      three question difficulty levels, all verified by human annotators.
076   Using the crafted benchmark, we evaluate nine representative MLLMs from five model families,
077   providing insights into model behaviors, the causes of conflicts, and effective methods designs to
078   mitigate the negative effects of conflicts. Notably, when facing knowledge conflicts, MLLMs tend
079   to over-rely on their parametric knowledge for simpler questions. For instance, the top commercial
080   model, Claude-3.5-Sonnet, exhibits a memorization ratio (MR) on parametric knowledge of 43.6%
081
      on Yes-No questions, substantially higher than results on more complex subjective questions; the
      largest open-source model evaluated, LLaVA-NeXT-34B, shows a MR of 26.7% on Yes-No questions,
082
      three times more than that on subjective questions. Our detailed analysis of the failure cases reveals
083
      that MLLMs generally focus more on the textual query than on the visual context. Drawing on these
084   observations, we revisit several existing training-free methods (Leng et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024f;
085   Wei et al., 2022b) to enhance the impact of visual context in answer generation. Interestingly, although
086   Chain-of-Thought prompting (Wei et al., 2022b) improves model’s reasoning abilities, it directs
087   MLLMs to over-utilize the parametric knowledge in the reasoning process, leading to inaccurate
088   conclusions or refusals. From this understanding, we propose Focus-on-Vision (FoV) prompting to
089   instruct MLLMs to prioritize visual information, which markedly improves the performance.
090   Our main contributions are summarized as follows:
091
092   • We introduce an innovative framework to automatically construct counter-commonsense bench-
093     marks from the ground up. This platform allows the flexible definition of conflict categories and
094     QA formats, facilitating the large-scale creation of conflict samples with minimal human effort.
095   • We present C ONFLICT V IS , a pioneering diagnostic benchmark specifically designed to evaluate
096     vision-knowledge conflicts at the commonsense level in MLLMs. The benchmark is meticulously
097     validated by human experts to guarantee the quality of data.
098   • We perform extensive experiments on nine representative MLLMs, uncovering critical insights
099     into model performance and the underlying mechanisms of the conflict. Leveraging these insights,
100     we propose a novel Focus-on-Vision (FoV) prompting as an effective solution to significantly
101     minimize vision-knowledge conflicts in MLLMs.

127   2.1     AUTOMATED P IPELINE
128
129   Commonsense knowledge refers to the information generally accepted by the majority of people
130   about everyday life, encompassing practical principles on how the world functions (Singh et al., 2002).
131   Inspired by the widely-used image captioning dataset (Chen et al., 2015), we explore a specific type of
132
      commonsense knowledge encapsulated as a triplet < s, a, p >, where s is the Subject, a is the Action,
      and p is the Place of the action or subject. For example, the statement “a baby (Subject) drinking
133
      water (Action) on the bed (Place)” illustrates this format. In this structure, the Subject outlines
134
      the main object’s appearance, the Action describes the primary activity and its interactions with
135   other relevant objects, and the Place highlights the background objects and setting. This three-part
136   framework efficiently captures the vital details that are accurately reflected in an image.
137
138
      We then describe the strategies for creating images and corresponding QA pairs that contradict the
      commonsense knowledge based on an input corpus. In general, we construct triplets containing low
139
      co-occurring concepts to serve as the counter-commonsense queries for image generation and fill
140
      pre-defined templates to generate the QA pairs. The pipeline contains the following four steps:
141
142
      1. Extract Knowledge Components from Input Corpus. The process commences with the extrac-
143
      tion of knowledge components from the corpus, which contains human commonsense knowledge in
144   natural language. Drawing inspiration from Li et al. (2021) on the creation of a compositional general-
145   ization test set, we identify frequently occurring components in the corpus and enumerate all possible
146   combinations to generate knowledge triplets. To this end, we employ a transformer model pipeline to
147   extensively annotate the syntactic labels, including dependency (DEP), Part-of-Speech (POS), and
148   Named Entity (NE). Table 3 in Appendix lists the linguistic rules to identify each component:
149
150   • Subject Phrases: Our framework examines noun chunks, selecting those where the head noun
151     functions as the nominal subject, indicated by dependency labels “nsubj” or “nsubjpass”.
152   • Action Phrases: The process starts by identifying the verb token (“VERB”) and then examines its
153     dependents for direct objects marked as “dobj”. Upon finding such objects, our framework includes
154     any determiners or compound modifiers present to form the complete action phrase. In addition,
155     verbs are converted to the "VBG" form to align with the common practice in image captions.
156   • Place Phrases: Distinguishing place phrases from other prepositional phrases relies on more than
157     just syntactic labels. Accordingly, the framework employs semantic role labeling techniques by
158     utilizing a BERT-based model (Gardner et al., 2017) to annotate each word’s semantic role. It then
159     extracts the phrase with a location tag (“ARGM-LOC") that contains 3 to 4 words.
160
161   To develop a high-quality benchmark, our framework filters the top subject (NS ), action (NA ), and
      place (NP ) phrases, respectively, based on the occurrence. It also removes named entities from the


                                                                             3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      subject and place phrases and merges similar phrases (e.g., “a doctor” and “the doctor”) by keeping
163   the most frequently occurring variant.
164
165   2. Construct Counter-commonsense Query. We aim to construct scenes with a single anomalous
166   concept (i.e., target) that seldom co-occurs with the other concepts (i.e., the context pair). For instance,
167   the Action component “fixing a computer” and the context pair (“the baby”, “on the bed”) results in
168   the triplet “the baby on the bed fixing a computer”, which contradicts commonsense knowledge, as it
169   is highly improbable for a baby to fix a computer in reality. Specifically,
170
171
      • The context pair should exhibit a high co-occurrence level, acting as a common background.
172   • The target should display a low co-occurrence with the given context, representing the anomaly.
173
      To find the high co-occurrence context pairs, our framework first groups context pairs by their target
174
      category, i.e., ContextAction consists of (Subject, Place) pairs and ContextP lace comprises (Subject,
175   Action) pairs. These groups help to develop the focus of the questions in the next section. We omit
176   ContextSubject to avoid the ambiguity and potential ethical issues for judging a subject’s identity.
177
178
      We demonstrate how to construct queries with counter-commonsense Actions, and a similar approach
      can be applied to Places. For each subject, we pair it with all the Place components to establish context
179
      candidates and select the contexts based on the co-occurrence level. We use Normalized Pointwise
180
      Mutual Information (NPMI) to evaluate the co-occurrence of the context pairs C = (CX , CY ):
181
182                                    P (CX , CY )                                  PMI(CX ; CY )
             PMI(CX ; CY ) ≡ log2                    ,         NPMI(CX ; CY ) ≡                        .      (1)
183                                   P (CX )P (CY )                               − log2 P (CX , CY )
184   A higher NPMI score indicates a more frequent co-occurrence of the two context components. For
185   each subject, we select Top-K places with highest NPMI scores to form context pairs.
186
      For each selected context pair, we construct counter-commonsense triplets by selecting a target Action
187
      component that is not compatible with the context pair. Concretely, we also use the NPMI metric (i.e.
188   NPMI(T ; C)) to evaluate the co-occurrence of the target T and context pair C. For each context pair,
189   we select Top-M actions with lowest NPMI scores to form counter-commonsense queries.
190
191
      To better match real-world knowledge, we leverage an LLM trained on large-scale web data to
      calculate the probability P (·). For example, to calculate the joint probabilities for multiple co-
192
      occurring components,our framework forms a concatenated expression of the concept phrases (e.g.,
193
      “a baby on the bed” or “a baby on the bed fixing a computer”) and inputs it into a pre-trained LLM
194   to obtain the generative probability. Utilizing an LLM here offers an advantage over frequency
195   counting for addressing the issue of zero frequencies for rare concept compositions, which often
196   arises with limited dataset size. Since large language models are trained on large-scale data to learn
197   the statistical tendencies of human language, their generative probability provides an approximation of
198   the likelihood of certain phrase appearing in reality. For the probability of each individual component,
199   we use its relative frequency within its category (Lin, 1999). Following generation, a manual review
200   is conducted here to ensure the data quality (See Appendix B for further details).
201
202   3. Generate Image. We leverage state-of-the-art text-to-image model DALL·E 3 (OpenAI, 2024a)
203
      to generate image based on the counter-commonsense query. Specifically, we concatenate the
      components of each triplet in order and prefixing them with “an image of”. As shown in Figure 2,
204
      the sample prompt for image generation is “an image of the baby fixing a computer on the bed”.
205
      After image generation, a human annotator is responsible for the quality control, filtering out the
206   low-quality images that are distorted or misaligned with the input prompt. We present detailed
207   filtering guidelines and illustrative examples in Appendix B.
208
209   4. Generate Questions. As illustrated in Figure 3, with the constructed counter-commonsense
210   triplets, the pipeline generates questions across three difficulty levels, from easy to hard:
211
212   • Yes-No Question: For counter-commonsense Actions and Places, our framework utilizes templates
213     “Is/are [Subject Place] [Action]?” and “Is/are [Subject Action] [Place]?”, respectively. The correct
214
        response to this type of question is typically “Yes”.
215   • Multiple-Choice Question: For these questions, the templates include “What is/are [Subject]
        doing [Place]?” and “Where is/are [Subject] [Action]?” for counter-commonsense Action and

      Knowledge Conflicts. Knowledge conflicts in LLMs can be divided into three categories (Xu
474   et al., 2024b): within the retrieved context (Chen et al., 2022), within model’s parametric knowledge
475   (Huang et al., 2023), and between the context and the model’s parametric knowledge (Xie et al., 2024;
476   Wu et al., 2024a). These conflicts can lead to incorrect or inconsistent responses, undermining the
477   model’s trustworthiness (Xu et al., 2024b; Xie et al., 2024). In MLLMs, these conflict types expand
478   to multimodal inputs, where conflicts can occur between the input image and the text instruction
479   (Liu et al., 2024e; Han et al., 2024), or when the image contains counterfactual information that
480   contradicts the model’s parametric knowledge (Liu et al., 2024e; Guan et al., 2024).
481   To evaluate the conflicts between visual information and the parametric knowledge in MLLMs,
482   HallusionBench (Guan et al., 2024) manually edits collected informational graphics to create normal-
483   counterfactual image pairs, and verifies model consistency with simple Yes-No questions. AutoHallu-
484   sion (Wu et al., 2024b) introduces an automated approach to generate counterfactual scenarios by
485   altering correlated objects in images, utilizing Yes-No questions to probe object existence and spatial
      relationships. In the context of commonsense knowledge, PhD (Liu et al., 2024e) generates counter-


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      commonsense images through manual collection and synthesis, employing Subjective questions for
487   assessment. Our proposed C ONFLICT V IS distinguishes itself by:
488
489   • Incorporating a wider range of question types and conflict targets, including Yes-No, Multiple-
490     Choice, Subjective questions and counter-commonsense actions and places. This approach allows
491     for a more thorough evaluation of MLLMs. Our findings indicate that MLLMs tend to disregard
492     visual information more frequently when responding to simpler questions and conflicting action is
493     more challenging for MLLMs to resolve.
494
495   • Automatically generating counter-commonsense images based on the co-occurrence of knowledge
496
        components and crafting questions around these counter-commonsense aspects, ensuring better
        image-question alignment. With the aid of advanced text-to-image models, our framework can
497
        generate instances tailored to specific needs.

499
      Hallucination in MLLMs. Hallucination in MLLMs refers to cases where the model generates
500
      descriptions that conflict with the given visual context. MLLMs’ hallucinations are typically cat-
501
      egorized based on the type of incorrect information, such as non-existent objects, incorrect object
502   attributes, and inaccurate object relations (Liu et al., 2024a). Relevant research has mainly focused
503   on two key areas: developing benchmarks and metrics to detect hallucinations (Rohrbach et al., 2018;
504   Li et al., 2023c; Liu et al., 2023a; Wang et al., 2023) and proposing strategies to mitigate them (Liu
505   et al., 2023a; Leng et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024f).
506
      In the context of knowledge conflicts, hallucination occurs when the model gives precedence to its
507
      intrinsic knowledge rather than the visual context (Liu et al., 2023a; Guan et al., 2024; Liu et al.,
508   2024e;a). Our study explores this issue by simulating knowledge conflicts that MLLMs may face
509   during inference, and evaluates the effectiveness of different strategies to mitigate hallucinations (Leng
510   et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024f) by enhancing the model’s fidelity to visual inputs. Furthermore, we
511   introduce a novel prompting technique that explicitly instructs MLLMs to prioritize visual information,
512   thereby significantly and consistently reducing the occurrence of hallucinations.
513
      Benchmarks for MLLMs. Traditional vision-language benchmarks are designed to assess indepen-
514   dent skills, including image captioning (Chen et al., 2015), visual grounding Rohrbach et al. (2016),
515   and visual question answering (Antol et al., 2015; Hudson & Manning, 2019). However, with the
516   emergence of MLLMs, there is a growing need for more comprehensive and tailored benchmarks.
517   The strong zero-shot abilities and advanced language generation skills exhibited by MLLMs can
518   render traditional benchmarks insufficient, as they may not account for the diversity of responses or
519   the full range of MLLM capabilities. To address these limitations, researchers have developed more
520   complex and comprehensive benchmarks to assess MLLMs across a wider range of capabilities (Yue
521   et al., 2024; Fu et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023a; Liu et al., 2023c). Meanwhile, diagnostic benchmarks
522
      have also been proposed to evaluate specific challenges or traits in MLLMs, such as hallucination
      (Li et al., 2023c; Liu et al., 2023a), social bias (Howard et al., 2024), and model safety (Liu et al.,
523
      2023b). C ONFLICT V IS is a pioneering analytical benchmark to introduce conflicting visual contexts
524
      against the model’s commonsense knowledge, allowing us to investigate model performance in the
525   face of vision-knowledge conflicts.
526

528   5    C ONCLUSION
529
530
531
      This paper introduces an innovative approach to understand and mitigate the significant challenge
      of vision-knowledge conflicts in MLLMs, with a particular focus on commonsense knowledge. By
532
      developing an automated pipeline and the C ONFLICT V IS benchmark, we offer a tool for systematically
533
      evaluating and understanding the nuances of these conflicts. Our extensive experiments across nine
534   representative MLLMs reveal a tendency toward over-reliance on parametric knowledge, especially
535   in simpler queries, which underscores the importance of the visual context in these models’ decision-
536   making processes. Building on these insights, we evaluate three improvement methods and design a
537   new Focus-on-Vision prompting to reinforce the significance of visual input, thereby reducing the
538   impact of conflicts. Our work paves the way for future research aimed at improving the robustness
539   and reliability of MLLMs. Our goal is to ensure these models can effectively integrate and interpret
      multimodal data, mitigating internal knowledge inconsistencies.

785   B     H UMAN Q UALITY CONTROL FOR CONSTRUCTING C ONFLICT V IS
786
787   This section outlines the quality control procedures within our automated pipeline. As shown in the
788   previous Figure 2, there are two required quality control points: (1) after generating knowledge
789   triplets, and (2) after generating images. In practice, to reduce the workload for annotators at the
790   first point, we subdivide it into three steps: (i) after extracting knowledge components, (ii) after
791   generating context pairs, and (iii) after generating knowledge triplets. Below, we detail the guidelines
792   applied at each stage.
793
794   B.1     H UMAN A NNOTATORS
795
      Our annotation team consists of four Ph.D. students majored in computer science. All annotators are
796
      compensated for their contributions.

      B.2     F ILTERING K NOWLEDGE T RIPLETS

800   Filtering knowledge components. This step removes semantically abstract phrases in the Subject,
801   Action, and Place categories, which are difficult to visualize. Annotators classify each phrase based
802   on its concreteness:
803
804   • Concrete (1). The phrase represents tangible objects or actions.
805   • Abstract (0). The Phrase represents abstract ideas or concepts.

      Place phrases that meet the requirements.

865
866   The objective of this step is to select high-quality images that align closely with the text prompt. Each
867   annotator is provided with an image and its corresponding text query, and evaluates the image based
868   on two criteria:
869
870   • Alignment with text prompt (0 or 1). The image should contain the key objects, actions, and
871
        background described in the text. The main focus should clearly represent the scene without
        ambiguity or misinterpretation.
872
873   • Image quality (0 or 1). The image should be clear, free from significant distortions, artifacts, or
874     unnatural effects like warping, blurring, or pixelation.
875
      With these two guidelines, the annotators extensively label all the generated images, and the images
876
      with a total score of 2 are kept in the final dataset.

899   C     G UIDELINES FOR M ANUAL L ABELING O PEN - END R ESPONSES
900
901   To label the correctness of MLLMs responses for the open-ended questions, we design our evaluation
902   criteria following Xu et al. (2024a). Specifically, the annotators are asked to determine the correctness
903
      of MLLM’s responses based on two criteria:
904
      • Relevancy (0 or 1): The content of the response is reflected in the image and addresses the focus
905     of the question.
906
907
      • Responsiveness (0 or 1): The response directly answers the question as instructed.
908
      Responses with a total score of 2 are considered correct for accuracy calculation.
909
910   To classify responses as either knowledge-based or vision-based, annotators grade the semantic
911   closeness between the current response and the reference responses on a scale from 0 to 2:
912
      • 0: The answers are entirely unrelated.
913
914   • 1: The answers share similar concepts but are not identical or synonymous.
915   • 2: The answers are identical or synonymous.
916
917   The candidate response is assigned to the category with a higher score (e.g., Vision or Knowledge).
      If both scores are 0, the response is categorized as “Other”.
