011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent advancements in Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) systems have
014            significantly enhanced the capabilities of large language models (LLMs) by in-
015
               corporating external knowledge retrieval. However, the sole reliance on retrieval
               is often inadequate for mining deep, domain-specific knowledge and for perform-
016
               ing logical reasoning from specialized datasets. To tackle these challenges, we
017
               present an approach, which is designed to extract, comprehend, and utilize domain
018            knowledge while constructing a coherent rationale. At the heart of our approach
019            lie four pivotal components: a knowledge atomizer that extracts atomic questions
020            from raw data, a query proposer that generates subsequent questions to facilitate
021            the original inquiry, an atomic retriever that locates knowledge based on atomic
022            knowledge alignments, and an atomic selector that determines which follow-up
023            questions to pose guided by the retrieved information. Through this approach, we
024            implement a knowledge-aware task decomposition strategy that adeptly extracts
025            multifaceted knowledge from segmented data and iteratively builds the rationale
026
               in alignment with the initial query and the acquired knowledge. We conduct com-
               prehensive experiments to demonstrate the efficacy of our approach across various
027
               benchmarks, particularly those requiring multihop reasoning steps. The results
028
               indicate a significant enhancement in performance, up to 12.6% over the second-
029            best method, underscoring the potential of the approach in complex, knowledge-
030            intensive applications.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Large Language Models (LLMs) have revolutionized the field of natural language processing by
036   demonstrating the capability to generate coherent and contextually relevant text. These advanced
037   models are trained on expansive corpora, equipping them with the versatility to execute a diverse
038   spectrum of linguistic tasks, ranging from text completion to translation and summarization (Achiam
039
      et al., 2023; Bahrini et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023; Anil et al., 2023). Despite their broad capa-
      bilities, LLMs exhibit pronounced limitations when tasked with specialized queries in professional
040
      domains (Ling et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023a), a demand that is particularly acute in practical ap-
041
      plications. This primarily stem from the scarcity of domain-specific training material and an limited
042   grasp of private knowledge and rationale within these domains. As a result, LLMs may produce
043   responses that are not only potentially erroneous but also lack the detail and precision required for
044   expert-level engagement (Bender et al., 2021). Besides the limitations in the domain-specific tasks,
045   another striking issue with LLMs is the phenomena known as “hallucination”, where the model
046   generates information that is not grounded in reality or factual data (Beltagy et al., 2020; Xu et al.,
047   2024). Moreover, the knowledge base of LLMs, being static and crystallized at the point of their
048   last update, introduces temporal stasis (Brown et al., 2020). Further compounding these challenges
049   is the issue of long-context comprehension (Li et al., 2024). Existing LLMs struggle to maintain
050
      an understanding of task definitions across long context, and their performance tends to deteriorate
      significantly when confronted with more complex and demanding tasks.
051
052   To mitigate these issues, Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) (Lewis et al., 2020) has emerged
053   as a promising solution, augmenting LLMs with external knowledge retrieval to anchor generated
      content in factual data. While RAG systems improve the accuracy and reliability of generated re-

      sponses by incorporating relevant external information, they often fall short in handling tasks that
055   demand deep domain-specific reasoning. Current RAG methods predominantly rely on text retrieval,
056   without explicitly focusing on extracting and understanding the underlying knowledge needed to
057   tackle complex, multihop reasoning tasks. In this work, we argue that the key to advancing RAG
058   lies in knowledge-aware processing, where domain-specific knowledge is not only retrieved but also
059   atomized, contextually-decomposed, and reasoned over in a more structured and adaptive manner.
060
      The Importance of Knowledge-aware Processing Addressing complex, logic-driven tasks in spe-
061   cialized domains requires more than surface-level retrieval of relevant text passages. It demands
062   knowledge extraction and comprehension to deeply understand both the user’s information needs
063   and the underlying context of the retrieved data. For example, specialized queries in fields like
064   medicine, law, or finance often involve domain-specific terminology and logic, which generic LLMs
065   may fail to grasp fully. Traditional RAG systems that retrieve text passages based on keyword match-
066   ing (Ram et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023) or embedding similarity (Gao et al., 2023) may retrieve
067   contextually relevant but semantically shallow information, insufficient for answering intricate ques-
068   tions. The challenge lies in ensuring that the retrieved knowledge aligns with the true intent behind
069
      the user’s query and the broader context of the problem at hand.
070   Moreover, effective comprehension of knowledge extends beyond extracting factual data. It also
071   involves understanding the semantics and relationships hidden within the data corpus. To this end,
072   some recent efforts (Raina & Gales, 2024) attempted to rephrase the original questions with integrat-
073   ing hypothestical answers (Gao et al., 2022) or transform text passages into lists of simple yet atomic
074   questions (Raina & Gales, 2024). This is especially crucial when multiple sources of information
075
      must be integrated to form a coherent response. Without a mechanism to extract and comprehend
      knowledge from each chunk of domain data, current RAG systems are limited in their ability to
076
      handle complex tasks that require deep contextual understanding.
077
078   The Need for Iterative Reasoning in multihop Questions In many real-world scenarios, a sin-
079   gle retrieval or answer generation step may not be enough to fully resolve a complex query.
080   multihop reasoning tasks, where the answer depends on synthesizing information from multiple
081
      sources, demand the decomposition of the original query into a series of simpler, interrelated sub-
      questions (Press et al., 2023). Nonetheless, this approach may face obstacles in domains where the
082
      knowledge is not readily accessible to LLMs. We argue that the decomposition in such domains
083
      should be contextual, rather than a standalone operation, meaning that decomposed queries can be
084   answered with the retrieved knowledge and context progressively and evolve into refining subse-
085   quent queries. This iterative approach allows the system to evolve its understanding of the user’s
086   inquiry, ensuring that follow-up questions are informed by the most recent retrieval results.
087
      For example, consider the HotpotQA (Yang et al., 2018) dataset question: “Who was born first,
088
      Erika Jayne or Marco Da Silva?”. A straightforward rewriting strategy might decompose this into
089
      two separate queries: “What is the age of Erika Jayne?” and “What is the age of Marco Da Silva?”.
090   However, if the available knowledge base only contains information such as “Her father left in 1971
091   when Erika Jayne was 9 months old.”, traditional rewriting may not lead to the retrieval of relevant
092   information. Techniques like Think-on-Graph (ToG) (Sun et al., 2024), which apply beam search
093   and iterative reasoning on knowledge graphs (KGs), could help. Yet, the performance of KG-based
094   methods is often hampered by limited richness of the knowledge within their structured triples and
095   the need for a high-quality, domain-specific KG. For instance, GPT-4 might generate triples like (Her
096   father, left, action) and (Erika Jayne, 9 months old, age), which are insufficient for reconstructing
097   the original context, “Her father left in 1971 when Erika Jayne was 9 months old.” 1 .
098   To overcome these limitations, we introduce a novel framework, KAR3 -RAG, that employs a
099   knowledge-aware dual rewriting and reasoning mechanism. Our approach features a dynamic in-
100   teraction between question rewriting and knowledge retrieval, enabling the system to adaptively
101   refine both the query and the retrieved context at each iteration. The core components of our sys-
102   tem include Knowledge atomizer, decomposing raw data into atomic questions for more granular
103   retrieval, Query proposer, generating follow-up questions based on the evolving context, Atomic

      Our key contributions are as follows: 1). We present a novel RAG framework that capitalizes
114
      on the synergistic interaction between interdependent rewriting and reasoning processes, ensuring
115   full utilization of the available context. 2). We enhance retrieval efficacy through a dual rewriting
116   mechanism that modifies both the original questions and the text passages (chunks). Our reasoning
117   process is context-aware, enabling the adaptive formulation of follow-up questions based on the
118   provided context. 3). We report on comprehensive experimental and ablation studies that validate
119   the superior performance of our approach across multiple benchmark datasets, which is up to 12.6%
120   increase over the second-best method.

122   2     R ELATED WORK
123
124   2.1   RAG
125
126   Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) has emerged as a promising solution that effectively incor-
127   porates external knowledge to enhance response generation. With the booming of LLMs (Bahrini
128   et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023), most research in the RAG paradigm has shifted towards a frame-
129
      work that initially retrieves pertinent information from external data sources and subsequently inte-
      grates it into the context of the query prompt as supplementing knowledge for contextually relevant
130
      generation (Ram et al., 2023). To enhance the retrieval quality of the naive RAG, advanced RAG
131
      approaches implement specific enhancements across the pre-retrieval, retrieval, and post-retrieval
132   processes, including query optimization (Ma et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2023), multi-granularity
133   chunking (Chen et al., 2023; Zhong et al., 2024), mixed retrieval (Yang, 2023) and re-ranking (Co-
134   here, 2023). On one hand, efforts focus on query rewriting, either explicitly (Zheng et al., 2024) or
135   implicitly (Gao et al., 2022), to enhance retrieval performance. On the other hand, several studies
136   transform raw data sources into structured data, ultimately converting them into valuable knowl-
137   edge for more effective retrieval and reasoning(Wang et al., 2023b; Zheng et al., 2024; Raina &
138   Gales, 2024). In our system, we introduce atomic rewriting for both queries and chunks, which
139   not only achieves multi-granularity query decomposition but also comprehensively extract inherent
140
      knowledge from chunks. It has been recognized that naive RAG systems is insufficient for tack-
      ling complex tasks such as summarization (Hayashi et al., 2021) and multihop reasoning (Ho et al.,
141
      2020). Consequently, most recent research focuses on developing advanced coordination schemes
142
      that leverage existing RAG modules to collaboratively address these challenges. Iter-RetGen (Shao
143   et al., 2023) and DSP (Khattab et al., 2023) employ retrieve-read iteration to leverage generation
144   response as the context for next round retrieval. FLARE (Jiang et al., 2023) propose a confidence-
145   based active retrieval mechanism that dynamically adjusts query during iterative retrieval processes.
146   Our approach adopts an iteration-based RAG pipeline that leverages context-aware reasoning pro-
147   cess, enabling the adaptive formulation of follow-up questions for each iteration and reducing the
148   difficulty of retrieval and reasoning of complex tasks.

150   2.2   M ULTIHOP QA
151
152   multihop Question Answering (MHQA) (Yang et al., 2018) involves answering questions that re-
153
      quire reasoning over multiple pieces of information, often scattered across different documents or
      paragraphs. This task presents unique challenges as it necessitates not only retrieving relevant infor-
154
      mation but also effectively combining and reasoning over the retrieved pieces to arrive at a correct
155
      answer. The traditional graph-based methods in MHQA solves the problem by building graphs and
156   inferring on graph neural networks(GNN) to predict answers (Qiu & other authors, 2019; Fang &
157   other authors, 2020). With the advent of LLMs, recent graph-based methods (Li & Du, 2023; Panda
158   et al., 2024) have evolved to construct knowledge graphs for retrieval and generate response through
159   LLMs. Another branch of methods dynamically convert multihop questions into a series of sub-
160   queries by generating subsequent questions based on the answers to previous ones (Trivedi et al.,
161   2023; Khattab et al., 2023; Feng et al., 2023). The subqueries guides the sequential retrieval and the
      retrieved results in turn are used to improve reasoning. Treating MHQA as a supervised problem,

      3.3   K NOWLEDGE ATOMIZING
262
263
      Chunked text often contains multifaceted information, and typically, only a subset is needed to ad-
264   dress a specific task. Traditional information retrieval methods, which consolidate all information
265   within a single chunk may not facilitate the efficient retrieval of the precise information required.
266   Recent research have explored the extraction of triple knowledge units from chunked text and con-
267   structing knowledge graphs to facilitate efficient information retrieval (Edge et al., 2024; Panda et al.,
268   2024). However, the construction of these knowledge graphs is costly, and the inherent knowledge
269   may not always be fully explored. To better present the knowledge embedded in documents, we pro-
      pose atomizing the original documents for knowledge extraction, a process we refer as Knowledge

      document chunk to an LLM as context, ask it to generate relevant questions that can be answered by

399   Metrics To ensure consistency with established benchmarks, we adopt F1 as a conventional met-
400   ric in our experimental evaluation. To more accurately assess the the alignment of responses with
401   the intended answers—beyond mere lexical matching—we introduce a novel evaluation metric em-
402   ploying GPT-4. In this process, GPT-4 acts as an evaluator, assessing the correctness of a response
403   in relation to the question and the correct answer labels. We refer to this metric as Accuracy (Acc).
404   Upon manual inspection of a sample set, the judgments rendered by GPT-4 demonstrate complete
405   agreement with human evaluators, affirming the reliability of this metric.
406   Specifically, in cases where multiple correct answer labels are available, we employ a conservative
407   scoring approach for F1 by retaining the highest score achieved. While in the context of computing
408   Accuracy (Acc), all admissible answer labels are furnished concurrently to the evaluation process,
409   resulting in a singular accuracy score. Furthermore, a full evaluation results with Exact Match (EM),
410   Recall and Precision can be found in Appendix A.3.
411
412   Datasets Since we are targeting at solving multihop reasoning tasks, three widely-recognized
413   multihop datasets: HotpotQA(Yang et al., 2018), 2WikiMultiHopQA(Ho et al., 2020), and
414
      MuSiQue(Trivedi et al., 2022) are used in our evaluation. A brief introduction to these datasets
      can be found in Appendix A.1. For each dataset, we randomly sample 500 QA data from the dev
415
      set, disregarding the question type and the number of hops to ensure randomness. We compile the
416
      context paragraphs from all sampled QA data into a single knowledge base for each benchmark,
417   creating a more complex retrieval scenario. This design choice aims to rigorously assess the task
418   decomposition and relevant context retrieval capabilities of our model.

535   approach, particularly in scenarios involving benchmarks with multihop questions. For future work,

      A.1   I NTRODUCTION TO O PEN -D OMAIN B ENCHMARKS
705
706   We provide a brief overview of the multihop QA datasets used in our experiments, noting that our
707   method does not leverage the question type information nor the number of hops information during
708   the solving process, as our approach is designed to be agnostic to such classifications. Table 3
709   outlines the distribution of question types within our sampled sets, offering insight into the variety
710   of reasoning challenges presented in our evaluation, though this does not directly impact our method.
711
712   HotpotQA The HotpotQA dataset is a well-known multihop QA benchmark primarily consist-
713
      ing of 2-hop questions, each associated with 10 Wikipedia paragraphs. Among these, some para-
      graphs contain supporting facts essential to answering the question, while the rest serve as dis-
714
      tractors. The dataset also includes a question type field, which delineates the logical reasoning
715
      required—comparison questions involve contrasting two entities, and bridge questions require in-
716   ferring the bridge entity, or inferring the property of an entity through an intermediary entity, or
717   locating the answer entity (Yang et al., 2018). Although our method operates independently of
718   these types, their description here is to exemplify the nature of questions within the dataset and to
719   contextualize the expected performance variance across different benchmarks.
720
721   2WikiMultiHopQA Inspired by HotpotQA, 2WikiMultiHopQA expands the diversity of question
722   types. It retains the comparison type from HotpotQA and introduces inference and compositional
723   questions that evolve from the bridge type by focusing on entity attribute deduction and entity lo-
724   cation, respectively. Additionally, the bridge comparison type is a novel category that requires a
725
      synthesis of bridge and comparison reasoning. This dataset typically presents 2-hop to 4-hop ques-
      tions, each accompanied by 10 Wikipedia paragraphs containing supporting facts and distractors.
726
      While these types inform the dataset’s structure, they are not utilized by our method, which treats all
727
      questions uniformly regardless of their categorization. For the sake of brevity, 2WikiMultiHopQA
728   is abbreviated to 2Wiki in the result tables throughout this paper.
729
730   MuSiQue Addressing the issue that many multihop questions can be solved via short-
731   cuts—arriving at correct answers without proper reasoning—MuSiQue implements stringent filters
732   and additional mechanisms specifically designed to encourage connected reasoning, as reported by
733   Trivedi et al. (Trivedi et al., 2022). Unlike the other datasets, MuSiQue does not categorize questions
734   by type, but it does provide explicit information on the number of hops required for each question,
735   ranging from 2 to 4 hops. Each question is associated with 20 context paragraphs, which introduce
736   a mix of relevant and irrelevant information, further complicating the task of discerning the correct
737   reasoning path. This explicit hop information, while not used by our method, underscores the com-
738
      plexity of the dataset and the robustness required by models to handle such challenges effectively.

773   and relying only on a specific knowledge base.

775
      The methods evaluated in this study are listed as follows:
776
            • Zero-Shot CoT: Questions are addressed using solely the Chain-Of-Thought (CoT) tech-
777
              nique, which prompts the LLMs to articulate its reasoning process step-by-step without the
778
              aid of example demonstrations or supplemental context. This method assesses the LLMs’
779           intrinsic knowledge and reasoning capabilities in a zero-shot setting.
780
781
            • Naive RAG: This approach employs dense retrieval from a flat knowledge base to procure
              relevant information for each question. The knowledge base consists of pre-embedded
782
              chunks are matched to the original question based on semantic similarity. The retrieval
783
              process is direct, without any intermediate task decomposition.
784
785         • Self-Ask w/ Retrieval: This method employs a task decomposition strategy wherein the
786
              LLMs is prompted to iteratively generate and answer follow-up questions, thereby breaking
              down complex problems into more manageable sub-tasks. General demonstrations illus-
787
              trating the logic and methodology of task decomposition are provided for all benchmarks
788
              to guide the LLMs’ reasoning process. Different to the original setting(Press et al., 2023),
789           where the framework relies solely on LLM’s own knowledge to answer each follow-up
790           question, in this setting, we introduces an additional retrieval component. Relevant chunks
791           are retrieved with the follow-up question as the query from a flat knowledge base to provide
792           a reference context. What’s more, we also limit the decomposition process to raise up to N
793           follow-up questions to align with other methods.
794         • IRCoT: This approach iteratively prompts LLMs to generate one more sentence of ratio-
795           nale with retrieved passages, and retrieves new passages with the newly generated reason.
796           The original setting limit the process with a maximum token number(Trivedi et al., 2023).
797           In our experiments, we limit the total number of iterations to the constant N we used for
798           our methods.
799         • Iter-RetGen: This method iteratively answers questions with retrieved passages, and uses
800           the newly generated rationale and answer for the next-round retrieval. In this setting, we
801           also limit the total number of iterations to the same N .
802

803

804
805         • GraphRAG Global: The knowledge base is pre-processed to construct a knowledge graph
806
              in accordance with the public guidance. The evaluation is inferred in global mode.
807         • KAR3 (Ours): The proposed knowledge-aware decomposition method iteratively decom-
