014
015
              The trend towards large language models (LLMs) for guardrailing against
              undesired behaviors is increasing and has shown promise for censoring user
              inputs. However, increased latency, memory consumption, hosting expenses
016
017
              and non-structured outputs can make their use prohibitive. In this work,
018           we show that task-specific data generation can lead to fine-tuned classi-
019           fiers that significantly outperform current state of the art (SoTA) while
020           being orders of magnitude smaller. Secondly, we show that using a single
021           model, MultiTaskGuard, that is pretrained on a large synthetically gen-
022           erated dataset with unique task instructions further improves generaliza-
023           tion. Thirdly, our most performant models, UniGuard, are found using our
024           proposed search-based model merging approach that finds an optimal set
025           of parameters to combine single-policy models and multi-policy guardrail
026
              models. On 7 public datasets and 4 guardrail benchmarks we created,
              our efficient guardrail classifiers improve over the best performing SoTA
              publicly available LLMs and 3rd party guardrail APIs in detecting unsafe
027
028
              and safe behaviors by an average F1 score improvement of 29.92 points
029           over Aegis-LlamaGuard and 21.62 over gpt-4o, respectively. Lastly, our
030           guardrail synthetic data generation process that uses custom task-specific
031           guardrail policies leads to models that outperform training on real data.

034   1   Introduction
035   The widespread use of large language models (LLMs) in both the public and private do-
036   mains has led to an increasing concern around guardrailing against prompts that are mali-
037   cious or violate user-specified disallowed behaviours (Biswas & Talukdar, 2023; Zheng et al.,
038   2024; Yao et al., 2024). While there has been a concerted effort to defend against misuse
039   of LLMs, current guardrailing and safety alignment approaches can lead to considerable
040   performance degradation on safe and non-malicious prompts, reducing the models general
041   capabilities (Qi et al., 2023; Jain et al., 2023) Manczak et al. (2024). In contrast, guardrails
042
      that are independent of the main LLM being used avoid the issue of safety alignment de-
      grading generalization performance. However, it is desirable that an independent guardrail
043
      model adds little inference time and storage overhead to the LLM. While 3rd party API
044
      services and publicly available models (e.g PromptGuard and LlamaGuard (Inan et al.,
045   2023)) offer different solutions to this issue of guardrailing while not diminishing the LLMs
046   general capabilities, they are limited in performance, inference speed and adaptability (i.e
047   lacks transferability, requires retraining).
048   In this paper, we show that fine-tuning a sub 1GB classifier on high quality synthetic data
049   from our synthetic data pipeline can significantly outperform current state of the art (SoTA)
050   while being orders of magnitude smaller in size. We demonstrate the effectiveness of these
051   classifiers on various safety, toxicity and prompt injection public benchmarks and show major
052   improvements over LLamaGuard-[1,2,3]-7b (Inan et al., 2023), Nemo Guardrails (Rebedea
053
      et al., 2023), Azure Content Safety, GPT-3.5-turbo/4/4o OpenAI (2023a), Meta Prompt-
      Guard (Inan et al., 2023) and OpenAIs Content Moderation API (OpenAI, 2023b).

      2   Related work
105
106   Content moderation. Ensuring safety has been an active area of research for several years.
107   Bert-based classifiers have been used to detect offensive or toxic inputs (Vidgen et al., 2020;
      Deng et al., 2022). More more recent work has focused on the use of LLMs through APIs


                                                    2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      such as Perspective API (Lees et al., 2022), OpenAI Content Moderation API (Markov et al.,
109   2023) (categories including toxicity, threat, harassment, and violence) and Azure Content
110   Safety API Microsoft (2023) (categories include hate and violence) that provide a severity
111   score between 0-6. While bert-based classifiers have the benefit of being much smaller than
112   current LLMs, to date they have lacked the necessary training data to be robust against
113   guardrail domains and topics of interest. Our work addresses these shortcomings.
114
      Model Merging. Techniques for merging multiple models have been proposed as efficient
115   ways to benefit from the capabilities of multiple LLMs without retraining or accessing the
116   original datasets. In Model Soup Averaging (MSA) (Wortsman et al., 2022), they first pro-
117   pose to combine models with weight averaging, showing improved performance compared
118   to a single model. Ilharco et al. (2022) build on this by performing task arithmetics, i.e
119   element-wise operations on model parameters to edit their behavior towards specific tasks.
120   Similar alternatives are RegMean Jin et al. (2022), and Fisher Merging (Matena & Raffel,
121   2022). Model merging in non-linear spaces showed improved results, as in SLERP White
122   (2016). TIES Yadav et al. (2024) reduce merging interference due to redundant weights
123
      and sign disagreements by resolving sign disagreements and only combining sign-aligned
      weights. In contrast, DARE (Yu et al., 2024) prunes weights with little change post fine-
124
      tuning and rescales the remaining weights to have similar output activation. Model Bread-
125
      crumbs (Davari & Belilovsky, 2023) also use sparse masks for improved model merging.
126   EvoMM (Akiba et al., 2024) and LM-Cocktail Xiao et al. (2023) automate the merging pro-
127   cess by using downstream task-specific data. Unlike our work, none of the above consider
128   efficient Bayesian search techniques to explore weightings to combine model parameters.

150   3.2   Custom Policy Guardrailing
151
      Given the synthetic data generation process described by p(D|P; G), we first fine-tune a
      policy-specific classifier, known as TaskGuard on D. Let fθ denote our base classifier with
152
153
      parameters θ, which can be instantiated from a pre-trained language models We fine-tune
154   fθP to create a policy-specific classifier fθP that maximizes performance on the task defined
155   by policy P. We optimize the classifer with binary cross entropy loss, LCE for safe and
156   unsafe labels. By minimizing LCE (θ) the classifier learns nuances of D given the policy P.

158   3.3   Multi-Policy Guardrailing
159   In contrast to TaskGuard, in this section we describe MultiTaskGuard, a novel approach
160   designed to create a single, versatile model capable of performing well across data generated
161   from all policies. This approach not only improves efficiency but also enables cross-task
      learning, improving performance on individual tasks through shared representations.

288   4.2        Model Details
      Baseline Models. For 3rd party API services we use 1) OpenAI GPT models such as gpt-
289

      3.5-turbo, gpt-4 and gpt-4o (OpenAI, 2023a)) OpenAI Content Moderation (OpenAI,
291   2023b), 3) Azure Content Safety and 4) Nemo Guardrails using gpt-4o as the genera-
292   tor. For the GPT-models we use batch completion through litelllm2 library to reduce
293   API call response time. For our public SoTA LLMs, we use LlamaGuard-1/2/3 (Inan
294   et al., 2023), Meta-Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct (Dubey et al., 2024), nvidia/Aegis-AI-
295   LlamaGuard (Ghosh et al., 2024) and Prompt-Guard-86M (AI, 2023) (see appendix for
296   prompt templates).
297   Finetuning Setup. The base models used in finetuning and benchmarking TaskGuard
298   and MultiTaskGuard are RoBERTALarge (777MB in bfloat16) (Liu et al., 2019) and
299   Multilingual-E5Large -Instruct (1.1GB) (Wang et al., 2024). The former is a standard well-
300   established masked monolingual language model (MLM) model, while the latter is a multi-
301   lingual MLM that has been trained from instructions to produce high quality embeddings.
302
      Model Merging Settings. We compare 4 well-established model merging methods when it
303   used with and without our MMS. Namely, SLERP, TIES, MSA and DARE aforementioned
304   in section 2. For all proceeding experiments when applying MMS we run a maximum of
305   50 iterations and a maximum of the top 6 most performant models to find the optimal
306   combination of either attention-only parameter merging, base model only merging or full
307   model (includes classification layer merging) merging and the associated weights given to the
308   models being merged. We carry out either through random search or a Bayesian (Thompson
309   sampling) search. See the supplementary material for further details.
