009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Understanding how real data is distributed in high dimensional spaces is the key to
012            many tasks in machine learning. We want to provide a natural geometric structure
013            on the space of data employing a deep ReLU neural network trained as a classifier.
014            Through the data information matrix (DIM), a variation of the Fisher information
015            matrix, the model will discern a singular foliation structure on the space of data.
016
               We show that the singular points of such foliation are contained in a measure zero
               set, and that a local regular foliation exists almost everywhere. Experiments show
017
               that the data is correlated with leaves of such foliation. Moreover we show the
018
               potential of our approach for knowledge transfer by analyzing the spectrum of the
019            DIM to measure distances between datasets.
020

022   1    I NTRODUCTION
023
024   The concept of manifold learning lies at the very heart of the dimensionality reduction question, and
025   it is based on the assumption that we have a natural Riemannian manifold structure on the space of
026   data (Tenenbaum, 1997; Hinton & Roweis, 2002; Tenenbaum et al., 2000; Fefferman et al., 2013).
027
      Indeed, with such assumption, many geometrical tools become readily available for machine learning
      questions, especially in connection with the problem of knowledge transfer (Bozinovski, 2020; Weiss
028
      et al., 2016), as geodesics, connections, Ricci curvature and similar (Ache & Warren, 2019). In
029
      particular, the fast developing field of Information Geometry (Amari, 1998; Martens, 2020), is now
030   providing with the techniques to correctly address such questions (Sun & Marchand-Maillet, 2015).
031
032   However, the practical situation, arising for example in classifying benchmark datasets as MNIST (Le-
033
      cun et al., 1998), Fashion-MNIST (Xiao et al., 2017) and similar, with CNNs (Convolutional Neural
      Networks), is generally more complicated and does not allow for such simple description, at least
034
      in the cases most relevant for concrete applications, and calls for more sophisticated mathematical
035
      modeling, that we shall explore here replacing the notion of manifold with that of singular foliation.
036   Deep Learning vision classifier models, via their intrinsic hierarchical structure, offer a natural
037   representation and implicit organization of the input data (Olah et al., 2017). For example, Hinton
038   & Salakhutdinov (2006) show how a multilayer encoder network can be successfully employed to
039   transform high dimensional data into low dimensional code, then to retrieve it via a “decoder”.
040
      In this paper, we want to provide the data space of a given dataset with a natural geometrical structure,
041
      and then employ such structure to extract key information. We will employ a suitably trained CNN
042   model to discern a foliation structure in the data space and show experimentally how the dataset our
043   model has been trained with is strongly correlated with its leaves, which are submanifolds of the
044   data space. The mathematical idea of foliation is quite old (see (Reeb, 1961; Ehresmann, 1963) and
045   refs therein); however, its applications in control theory (see (Agrachev & Sachkov, 2004) and refs
046   therein) via sub-Riemannian geometry and machine learning have only recently become increasingly
047   important (Tron et al., 2022; Grementieri & Fioresi, 2022).
048   The foliation structure on the data space, discerned by our model, however, is non-standard and
049   presents singular points. These are points admitting a neighbourhood where the rank of the distribu-
050   tion, tangent at each point to a leaf of the foliation, changes. Moreover, in the presence of typical non
051   linearities of the network, as ReLU, there are also non smooth points, thus hindering in such points
052   the smooth manifold structure itself of the leaf. However, we prove that both singular and smooth
053   points are a measure zero set in the data space, so that the foliation is almost everywhere regular
      and its distribution well defined. As it turns out in our experiments, the samples belonging to the


                                                         1
054
      dataset we train our model with are averagely close to the set of singular points. It forces us to model
055   the data space with a singular foliation that we call data foliation in analogy with the data manifold.
056   Applications of singular foliations were introduced in connection with control theory (Sussmann,
057   1973); their study is currently an active area of investigation (Lavau, 2018). As we shall show in our
058   experiments, together with their distributions, singular foliations provide with an effective tool to
059   single out the samples belonging to the datasets the model was trained with and at the same time
060   discern a notion of distance between different datasets belonging to the same data space.
061   Our paper is organized as follows. In Sec. 2, we recap the previous literature, closely related to
062   our work. In Sec. 3, we start by recalling some known facts about Information Geometry in 3.1.
063   Then, in Sec. 3.2 we introduce the data information matrix (DIM), defined via a given model, and
064   two distributions D and D⊥ naturally associated with it, together with some illustrative examples
065   elucidating the associated foliations and their leaves. In Sec. 3.3 we introduce singular distributions
066   and foliations and then we prove our main theoretical results expressed in Lemma 3.4 and Theorem 3.6.
067   Lemma 3.4 studies the singularities of the distribution D. Theorem 3.6 establishes that the singular
068   points for D are a measure zero set in the data space. Hence our foliation, though singular, acquires
069
      geometric significance. Finally in Sec. 4 we elucidate our main results with experiments. Moreover,
      we show how the foliation structure and its singularities can be exploited to determine which dataset
070
      the model was trained on, and its distance from similar datasets in the same data space. We also make
071
      some "proof of concept" considerations regarding knowledge transfer to show the potential of the
072   mathematical singular local foliation and distribution structures in data space.

075   2     R ELATED W ORKS
076
077   The use of Deep Neural Networks as tools for both manifold learning and knowledge transfer has
078
      been extensively investigated. We give a brief overview on the literature closer to our contribution.
079   Latent manifold. The question of finding a low dimensional manifold structure (latent manifold) into
080   high dimensional dataset spaces starts with PCA and similar methods to reach the more sophisticated
081   techniques as in (Fefferman et al., 2013; Sun & Marchand-Maillet, 2015), (see also (Murphy, 2022)
082   for a description of the most popular techniques in dimensionality reduction and (Burges, 2010) for
083   a complete bibliography or the origins of the subject). Such understanding was applied towards
084
      the knowledge transfer questions in (Cook et al., 2007; Bengio, 2012) and more recently Dikkala
      et al. (2021). Moreover, distances between datasets were also studied through Optimal Transport in
085
      (Alvarez-Melis & Fusi, 2020; Hua et al., 2023). Another important technique of knowledge transfer
086
      is via shared parameters as in (Maurer et al., 2016). The idea of using techniques of Information
087   Geometry for machine learning started with Amari (1998). More recently, it was used for manifold
088   learning in (Sun & Marchand-Maillet, 2015) (see also refs therein).
089
090
      Foliations. Employing foliations to reduce dimensionality is not per se novel. In (Grementieri &
      Fioresi, 2022), the authors introduce some foliation structure in the data space of a neural network,
091
      with the aim of approaching dimensionality reduction exploiting the low rank of the equivalent of the
092
      Fisher matrix on the dataspace. Observations on the low rank of the Fisher matrix, and its key role in
093   CNN parameter space, appear also in (Sun & Nielsen, 2017; 2024). In (Tron et al., 2022), orthogonal
094   foliations in the data space are used to create adversarial attacks, and provide the data space with
095   curvature and other Riemannian notions to analyse such attacks. In (Szalai, 2023) invariant foliations
096   are employed to produce a reduced order model for autoencoders. Also, in considering singular points
097   for our foliation, we are led to study the singularities of a neural network. This was investigated, for
098   the different purpose of network expressibility in (Hanin & Rolnick, 2019b).The partitioning of the
099   data space into connected components after removing non-smooth points of ReLU CNNs has been
100   studied in (Hanin & Rolnick, 2019a) and in (Montúfar et al., 2014).

148
      distribution C on M is integrable if for every x ∈ M , there exists a connected immersed submanifold
149
      N of M , with Ty N = Cy for all y ∈ N . In other words, the distribution defines at each point the
150   tangent space to a submanifold. Whenever a distribution is integrable, we have a foliation, that
151   is, M becomes a disjoint union of embedded submanifolds called the leaves of the foliation. If
152   dim Cx = dim Cy for all x, y ∈ M we say that the foliation, or the corresponding distribution, has

      3.3   S INGULAR F OLIATIONS
247
248   A foliation on a manifold M is a partition of M into connected immersed submanifolds, called leaves.
249   A foliation is regular if the leaves have the same dimension, singular otherwise. Notice that the map
250   x 7→ dim(Lx ), which associates to x ∈ M the dimension of its leaf Lx , is lower semi-continuous,
251   that is, the dimensions of the leaves in a neighbourhood of x are greater than or equal to dim(Lx )
252   (Lavau, 2018). Whenever we have an equality, we say that x is regular, otherwise we call x singular.
253   It is important to remark that, adhering to the literature (Lavau, 2018), the terminology singular point
254
      here refers to a point that has a neighbourhood where the leaves have non constant dimension. We can
      associate a distribution to a foliation by associating to each point, the tangent space to the leaf at that
255
      point. Such distribution has constant rank if and only if the foliation is regular. Frobenius Theorem
256
      3.2 applies only to the case of constant rank distributions, however, there are results extending part of
257   Frobenius theorem to non constant rank distributions. In (Hermann, 1963) and Nagano (1966), the
258   authors give sufficient conditions for integrability in this more general setting (see (Lavau, 2018) for
259   correct attributions on statements and proofs).
260
      In the practical applications we are interested in, foliations may also have non smooth points, that is
261
      points where the leaf through the point, belonging to the foliation, is not smooth. For example, Fig. 4
262
      shows a Xor network with ReLU non linearity displaying both singular and non smooth points.
263
264   The singular point of each picture is in the center, while non smooth points occur only for Xor
265   with ReLU non linearity. As previously stated, this is a degenerate case, where the involutivity and
266
      integrability of D is granted automatically by low dimensionality, though for (b) in Fig. 4, we cannot
      define D at non smooth points.

269   D, generated by the image of the DIM D, is both non smooth and singular. It then becomes important
      to understand where such non smooth and singular points are located. The aim of this section is

      points (see Thm 3.6) for the distribution D, generated by the image of the DIM D (see Def. (2)), in
393
      knowledge transfer questions. The emergence of a singular foliation structure in an dense open set
394
      of the dataspace, granted by Thm 3.6, via Frobenius Thm 3.2 (see also Obs. 3.5) is related with the
395   inner structure of datasets in such dataspace. Indeed, we will show that an effective way to measure
396   the "distance" between different datasets embedded in the same dataspace, is related with the rank of
397   D, or equivalently D, and the (relative) magnitude of D’s eigenvalues.

402   CIFAR10 dataset (Krizhevsky, 2012) cropped and transformed to be 28 × 28 gray-scale pictures.
403   Our neural network is similar to LeNet, with two convolutional layers, followed by a Maxpool and
404   two linear layers with ReLU activation functions, see Fig. 5. This is slightly more general than our
405   hypotheses in Sec. 3.3. The model is then trained on MNIST, reaching 98% of accuracy.4

515   5                       C ONCLUSIONS
516
517   We propose to complement the notion of data manifolds with the more general one of data foliations.
518   The (integrable) distribution D, defined via the data information matrix (DIM), a generalization of
519   the Fisher information matrix to the space of data, indeed allows for the partition of the data space
520   according to the leaves of such a foliation via the Frobenius theorem. Examples and experiments
521   show a correlation of data points with the leaves of the foliation: moving according to the distribution
522
      D, i.e. along a leaf, the model gives a meaningful label, while moving in the orthogonal directions
      leads to greater and greater classification errors. The data foliation is however both singular (drop
523
      in rank) and non smooth. We prove that singular points are contained into a set of measure zero,
524
      hence making the data foliation significant in the data space. We show that points in the dataset the
525   model was trained with have lower DIM eigenvalues, so that the distribution D allows successfully
526   to determine whether a sample of points belongs or not to the dataset used for training. We make
527   such explicit comparison with similar datasets (i.e. MNIST versus FashionMNIST, KMNIST etc).
528   Then, we use the lowest non-zero eigenvalue of the DIM to measure the distance between data sets.
529   We test our proposed distance by retraining our model on datasets belonging to the same data spaces
530   and checking the validation accuracy. Our results are not quantitatively conclusive in this regard, but
531   they represent a "proof of concept", to encourage further investigation. We believe that our empirical
532   examples show a great promise as a first step to go beyond the manifold hypothesis and exploiting
533
      the theory of singular foliations to perform dimensionality reduction and knowledge transfer.
