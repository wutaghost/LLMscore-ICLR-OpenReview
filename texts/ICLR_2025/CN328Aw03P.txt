009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Urgent applications like wildfire management and renewable energy generation
012            require precise, localized weather forecasts near the Earth’s surface. However,
013            weather forecast products from machine learning or numerical weather models
014            are currently generated on a global regular grid, on which a naive interpolation
015            cannot accurately reflect fine-grained weather patterns close to the ground. In this
016            work, we train a heterogeneous graph neural network (GNN) end-to-end to down-
017            scale gridded forecasts to off-grid locations of interest. This multi-modal GNN
018
               takes advantage of local historical weather observations (e.g., wind, temperature)
               to correct the gridded weather forecast at different lead times towards locally ac-
019
               curate forecasts. Each data modality is modeled as a different type of node in
020
               the graph. Using message passing, the node at the prediction location aggregates
021            information from its heterogeneous neighbor nodes. Experiments using weather
022            stations across the Northeastern United States show that our model outperforms
023            a range of data-driven and non-data-driven off-grid forecasting methods. Our ap-
024            proach demonstrates how the gap between global large-scale weather models and
025            locally accurate predictions can be bridged to inform localized decision-making.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   In recent years, machine learning (ML) has been widely used in weather forecasting applications.
031   This popularity stems from its fast inference speed and ability to model complex physical dynamics
032   directly from data. Some high-profile ML weather forecasting models include FourCastNet (Pathak
033
      et al., 2022), GraphCast (Lam et al., 2023), and Pangu-Weather (Bi et al., 2023). These ML weather
      models can generate forecasts thousands of times faster than traditional numerical weather predic-
034
      tion (NWP) models, while at the same time being more accurate and flexible, freed from the NWP
035
      model’s sometimes restrictive physical constraints (Pathak et al., 2022; Lam et al., 2023; Kochkov
036   et al., 2024).
037
038   To date, most ML weather models have been trained with gridded numerical weather reanalysis
039
      products like ERA5 (Hersbach et al., 2020). However, reanalysis products have been shown to have
      a systematic bias relative to the weather station measurements (Ramavajjala & Mitra, 2023). Here,
040
      we verify the existence of a substantial bias in ERA5’s near-surface wind estimates (Figure 1). ERA5
041
      systematically overestimates the inland near-surface wind speed and is much smoother across space
042   than the actual wind field as measured by weather stations. ML models trained to predict reanalysis
043   products inherit this significant bias and are unable to make accurate localized predictions.
044
045
      This presents a challenge, as accurate off-grid weather forecasts are critical for applications like
      wildfire management and sustainable energy generation. To bridge this gap, we train a multi-modal
046
      graph neural network (GNN) end-to-end to downscale gridded forecasts to localized off-grid coor-
047
      dinates. First, we curate a dataset that contains both global weather reanalysis (ERA5) and local
048   weather station observations (MADIS), spanning 2019–2023 and covering the Northeastern United
049   States. We then construct a heterogeneous graph containing gridded ERA5 and off-grid weather
050   stations as two different types of nodes. The GNN operates on this graph and makes forecasts at
051   each weather station. It preserves off-grid station nodes’ irregular geometry and theoretically infinite
052   spatial resolution. When making predictions at a station location, the GNN aggregates information
053   from neighboring weather stations and ERA5 nodes using message passing (Gilmer et al., 2017). As
      a result, the prediction is informed by both global atmospheric dynamics and local weather patterns.

      We evaluate our model’s ability to forecast real data from weather stations, focusing on wind in
055   particular; near-surface wind dynamics are very complex and poorly captured by ERA5. Our GNN
056   method outperforms a variety of other off-grid forecasting methods, including ERA5 interpolation
057   and time series forecasting without spatial context.

      Gridded Weather Forecasting Weather forecasting has long been a challenging problem in at-
101
      mospheric sciences, with efforts dating back centuries. Since the advent of numerical weather pre-
102   diction (NWP) in the mid-20th century, most forecast simulations have been conducted on a regular
103   grid, dividing the atmosphere into evenly spaced discrete points to solve complex partial differen-
104   tial equations. This grid-based approach has remained the foundation of many numerical weather
105   forecasting models such as the Integrated Forecast System (ECMWF, 2022) and High-Resolution
106   Rapid Refresh (Dowell et al., 2022). In recent years, machine learning (ML) has gained traction
107   as a promising tool in weather forecasting (Bauer et al., 2015), offering new techniques to improve
      accuracy and computational efficiency. These ML weather models can be roughly divided into two


                                                      2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      categories: end-to-end models and foundation models. FourCastNet (Pathak et al., 2022), Graph-
109   Cast (Lam et al., 2023), Pangu-Weather (Bi et al., 2023), AIFS (?) and NeuralGCM (Kochkov
110   et al., 2024) are end-to-end models trained directly to make weather forecasts. In contrast, At-
111   moRep (Lessig et al., 2023), ClimaX (Nguyen et al., 2023), Aurora (Bodnar et al., 2024) and Prithvi
112   WxC (?) are foundation models that are first trained with a self-supervision task and then fine-tuned
113   for weather forecasting. However, the training data for ML models largely stem from traditional
114   gridded numerical simulations such as ERA5 (Hersbach et al., 2020). As a result, the ML models
115   themselves still typically maintain the grid-based paradigm even within their more modern forecast-
116   ing approach. One major disadvantage of gridded weather forecasting is that it is usually limited
117
      by its fixed resolution such that it cannot accurately reflect fine-grained local weather patterns (al-
      though efforts towards limited area modeling have recently been made (Oskarsson et al., 2023)).
118
      Other works focusing on increasing the forecast resolution (Harder et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023;
119
      Prasad et al., 2024) exist, but their methods are mostly tested on synthetic datasets. Work meant
120   to correct ERA5 forecasts exists (Mouatadid et al., 2023), but focus on sub-seasonal forecast at
121   coarse spatial resolution rather than local weather forecasting. In this work, we propose a multi-
122   modal graph neural network which can effectively downscale gridded weather forecasting to match
123   real-world local weather dynamics.
124
125   Off-Grid Weather Forecasting Even though gridded weather forecasting is the main focus of
126   the ML community, there have been several attempts to forecast weather off-grid. Bentsen et al.
127   (2023) applied a GNN to forecast wind speed at 14 irregularly spaced off-shore weather stations,
128   each of which was treated as a node within the graph. The model input is the historical trajectory of
129   weather variables recorded at each station. This work has two limitations: the forecasting region is
130   small, only covering 14 stations, and it only considers a single input modality of station historical
131   measurements. MetNet-3 (?) takes another approach to off-grid weather forecasting. It trains a
132   U-Net-like transformer (Ronneberger et al., 2015) model that takes multi-modal inputs including
133
      weather station observations, satellite imagery, and assimilation products to predict weather at sta-
      tions. However, both input and output station data are re-gridded to a high resolution mesh (4 km
134
      × 4 km), which distorts the off-grid data’s original granularity. To address the aforementioned dis-
135
      advantages, we construct a multi-modal GNN that makes predictions at raw off-grid locations over
136   358 stations in the Northeastern US, with both numerical weather simulation and station observation
137   as inputs.
138
139
      Graph Neural Network for Physical Simulation Graph neural networks (GNNs) are a type of
140
      deep learning model designed to operate on data structured as graphs, where entities are repre-
141   sented as nodes and their relationships as edges. GNNs provide flexibility to process data with non-
142   Euclidean structures. A GNN learns to capture relationships between nodes by iteratively passing
143   and aggregating information between neighboring nodes, and updating node representations based
144   on their connections. Recently, GNNs have been widely used in physical system simulation. For
145   example, the 2D Burgers’ equation can be effectively solved on both a regular and an irregular mesh
146   with GNNs such as MAgNet (?) and MPNN (?). Sanchez-Gonzalez et al. (2020) used a GNN to
147   simulate particle dynamics in a wide variety of physical domains, involving fluids, rigid solids, and
148   deformable materials interacting with one another. GraphCast (Lam et al., 2023) even showed that a
149
      3D GNN is capable of simulating a global gridded atmospheric system. These successful use cases
      of GNNs motivate us to apply a graph network to our task for localized off-grid weather forecasting.

153
154
      We train a message passing neural network (MPNN, Gilmer et al. (2017); Pfaff et al. (2021)), a type
155
      of GNN (Scarselli et al., 2009), to forecast weather at the station level with the aid of global weather
156   predictions. At its core, the method uses past local weather station observations to forecast the
157   weather variables of interest at different lead times into the future. This structure is then augmented
158   by forming a heterogeneous graph with the gridded output of a global weather model (could be
159   NWP or ML) known to provide accurate forecasts globally, but lacking accuracy at fine scales.
160   For instance, global models largely neglect surface friction when modeling wind fields (Figure 1).
161   By integrating global forecasts with localized weather data, we can view the task as a correction of
      global forecasts rather than forecasting de novo; that is, our model aims to correct the global forecast


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      toward the local reality based on prior local observations. This setup enables our model to achieve
163   accurate off-grid near-surface weather forecasting.

197

198   weather station graph is constructed with k-nearest neighbor, that is, to connect each node i to a set
199   of nodes N (i), the k closest neighbors.
200
201

314   ERA5 as “Perfect Forecast” For our task of point-level weather forecasting, we integrate histori-

462   5   D ISCUSSION
463
464   Discrepancies arise between weather station observations and reanalysis data, such as ERA5, due
465   to the latter’s limitations in modeling terrain and land cover. These local surface characteristics sig-
466   nificantly influence local weather patterns, particularly wind, but are not adequately represented in
467   gridded global datasets. The high ME associated with MADIS persistence underscores the inherent
468   chaotic nature of wind dynamics; current wind conditions do not reliably indicate future conditions.
469   However, this unpredictability has a less pronounced effect on ME at low wind speeds, typically
470   observed inland and in sheltered coastal regions. Given these complexities, accurate wind forecast-
471
      ing requires a model that accounts for both the discrepancies between global and local conditions
      and the inherent variability of wind across space and time. ERA5 and historical station data provide
472
      complementary information on wind dynamics. We thus in this work seek a method that can inte-
473
      grate these two datasets successfully for forecasting. ERA5 offers large-scale atmospheric patterns,
474   but it fails to capture low wind speeds in areas that are sheltered from wind due to topography or
475   land surface characteristics.
476
477
      Machine learning methods trained on station data can adapt to local conditions like low wind speeds,
      but struggle to account for changes between low and high wind speeds (i.e., ramp-ups and ramp-
478
      downs) and do not effectively capture the dynamics associated with longer lead times. When pre-
479
      dicting local weather using only local data (i.e., in forecasting mode), the MLP and MPNN achieve
480   respectable performances. However, with the introduction of ERA5, the MLP and MPNN, both suc-
481   cessfully incorporates large-scale atmospheric dynamics from ERA5, thereby improving predictions
482   at longer lead times. The slightly superior performance of the MPNN suggests that the spatial struc-
483   ture of the GNN, along with the proposed heterogeneous message passing structure, can be suitable
484   for correcting a globally gridded weather dataset to reflect local conditions. Even without ERA5,
485   the inductive bias of the station’s spatial correlation within the MPNN does, on its own, enhance
      forecasting performance.

524   6          C ONCLUSION
525
526   This work demonstrates the use of a multi-modal GNN for downscaling gridded weather forecasts
527   and improving the accuracy of off-grid predictions. Our model addresses the inherent bias in grid-
528   ded reanalysis products like ERA5. By incorporating both ERA5 and MADIS weather station data
529   within a heterogeneous graph, our GNN predicts off-grid weather conditions by leveraging both
530   large-scale atmospheric dynamics and local weather patterns. In our evaluation using a surface wind
531
      prediction task, the GNN outperformed all baseline models. For instance, it achieved a 80% re-
      duction in ME compared to ERA5 interpolation and a 3.6% improvement over the best-performing
532
      multi-Layer perceptron (MLP). An ablation study, where ERA5 input was removed, resulted in an
533
      ME increase from 0.54 to 0.72 m/s, highlighting the importance of incorporating global atmo-
534   spheric dynamics for accurate local predictions. This finding motivates the exploration of additional
535   modalities, such as radar measurements and satellite imagery, which could further enhance local
536   forecast accuracy. This research has significant implications for improving weather forecasting, par-
537   ticularly in high-value regions where weather stations can be installed. More accurate off-grid pre-
538   dictions can enhance weather-dependent decision-making in various sectors, including agriculture,
539   wildfire management, transportation, and renewable energy. Future work will focus on expanding
      the study area and exploring the integration of our GNN model with weather foundation models.
