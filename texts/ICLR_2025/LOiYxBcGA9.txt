011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            The rising successes of RL are propelled by combining smart algorithmic strate-
014            gies and deep architectures to optimize the distribution of returns and visitations
015
               over the state-action space. A quantitative framework to compare the learning
               processes of these eclectic RL algorithms is currently absent but desired in prac-
016
               tice. We address this gap by representing the learning process of an RL algorithm
017
               as a sequence of policies generated during training, and then studying the policy
018            trajectory induced in the manifold of state-action occupancy measures. Using an
019            optimal transport-based metric, we measure the length of the paths induced by the
020            policy sequence yielded by an RL algorithm between an initial policy and a final
021            optimal policy. Hence, we first define the Effort of Sequential Learning (ESL).
022            ESL quantifies the relative distance that an RL algorithm travels compared to the
023            shortest path from the initial to the optimal policy. Further, we connect the dy-
024            namics of policies in the occupancy measure space and regret (another metric to
025            understand the suboptimality of an RL algorithm), by defining the Optimal Move-
026
               ment Ratio (OMR). OMR assesses the fraction of movements in the occupancy
               measure space that effectively reduce an analogue of regret. Finally, we derive
027
               approximation guarantees to estimate ESL and OMR with finite number of sam-
028
               ples and without access to an optimal policy. Through empirical analyses across
029            various environments and algorithms, we demonstrate that ESL and OMR provide
030            insights into the exploration processes of RL algorithms and hardness of different
031            tasks in discrete and continuous MDPs.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   In recent years, significant advancements in Reinforcement Learning (RL) have been achieved in
037   developing exploration techniques that improve learning (Bellemare et al., 2016; Burda et al., 2019;
038   Eysenbach et al., 2019) along with new learning methods (Lazaridis et al., 2020; Müller et al., 2021;
039
      Li, 2023). With growing computational resources, these techniques have led to various successful
      applications of RL, such as playing games up to human proficiency (Silver et al., 2017; Jaderberg
040
      et al., 2019), controlling robots (Ibarz et al., 2021; Kaufmann et al., 2023), tuning databases and
041
      computer systems (Wang et al., 2021; Basu et al., 2019), etc. However, there remains a lack of
042   consensus over approaches that can quantitatively compare these exploratory processes across RL
043   algorithms and tasks (Seijen et al., 2020; Amin et al., 2021; Ladosz et al., 2022). This is attributed
044   to some methods being algorithm-specific (Tang et al., 2017), while others provide theoretical guar-
045   antees for very specific settings (Lattimore & Szepesvári, 2020; Agarwal et al., 2022). Thus, com-
046   paring the exploratory processes of these eclectic algorithms across the multi-directional space of
047   RL algorithm design, emerges as a natural question. However, the present literature lacks a metric
048   to compare them except regret, which is often hard to estimate (Ramos et al., 2017; 2018).
049   This paper aims to address this gap based on two key observations. First, we observe from the lin-
050   ear programming formulation of RL that solving the value maximization problem is equivalent to
051   finding an optimal occupancy measure (Syed et al., 2008; Neu & Pike-Burke, 2020; Kalagarla et al.,
052   2021). Occupancy measure is the distribution of state-action pair visits induced by a policy (Alt-
053   man, 1999; Laroche & des Combes, 2023). Under mild assumptions, a policy maps uniquely to
      an occupancy measure. Second, we observe that any RL algorithm learns by sequentially updating

054
      policies starting from an initial policy to reach an optimal policy. The search for an optimal policy
055   is influenced by the exploration-exploitation strategy and functional approximators, both of which
056   impact the overall performance of the agent by determining the quality of experiences from which
057   it learns (Zhang et al., 2019; Ladosz et al., 2022). Hereby, we term collectively the learning strategy
058   and the exploration-exploitation interplay as the exploratory process.
059
      Contributions. 1. A Framework. Motivated by our observations, we abstract any RL algorithm
060
      as a trajectory of occupancy measures induced by a sequence of policies between an initial and a
061   final (optimal) policy. The occupancy measure of a policy given an environment corresponds to the
062   data-generating distribution of state-actions. Thus, we can quantify the effort of each policy update,
063   i.e. the effort to shift the state-action data distributions, as the transportation distance between their
064   occupancy measures. The total effort of learning by the algorithm can be measured as the total
065   distance covered by its occupancy measure trajectory. We provide a mathematical basis for this
066   quantification by proving that the space of occupancy measures is a differentiable manifold for
067   smoothly parameterized policies (Section 3). Hence, we can compute the length of the occupancy
068   measure trajectory on this manifold using Wasserestein distance as the metric (Villani, 2009).
069   2. Effort of Sequential Learning. In contrast to RL, if we knew the optimal policy we could update
070   our initial policy directly via supervised or imitation learning. Effort of this learning is represented
071   by a direct, shortest (geodesic) path from initial to optimal policy on the occupancy measure mani-
072   fold. To quantify the cost of the exploratory process to learn the environment, we define the Effort
073   of Sequential Learning (ESL) as the ratio of the (indirect) path traversed by an RL algorithm in the
074   occupancy measure space to the direct distance between the initial and optimal policy (Section 3.1).
075
      Lower ESL implies more efficient exploratory process.
076   3. Efforts to learn that lead to Regret-analogue minimization. Regret is a widely used optimality
077   measures for reward-maximizing RL algorithms (Sutton & Barto, 2018). It measures the total de-
078   viation in the value functions achieved by a sequence of policies learned by an RL algorithm with
079   respect to the optimal algorithm that always uses the optimal policy (Sinclair et al., 2023). We show
080   that regret is related to the sum of distances between the optimal policy and each policy in the se-
081
      quence learned by the RL algorithm, in the occupancy measure space. We can define an analogue
      of instantaneous regret (at any one step during learning rather than cumulative), in the occupancy
082
      measure space, as the geodesic distance between the occupancy measure of the policy at this step in
083
      the learning sequence, and the optimal one. We find that not all policy updates lead to a reduction
084   in this analogue of immediate regret, and thus define another index Optimal Movement Ratio that
085   measures the fraction that do (Section 3.2).
086
087
      4. Computational and Numerical Insights. We prove sample complexity guarantees to approximate
      ESL and OMR in practice as we do not have access to the occupancy measures but collection of
088
      rollouts from the corresponding policies (Section 4). We show the relation of empirical OMR and
089
      ESL to the true ones if the optimal policy is never reached by an algorithm. We conduct experiments
090   on multiple environments, both discrete and continuous, with sparse and dense rewards, comparing
091   state-of-the-art algorithms. We observe that by visualizing aspects of the path traversed (and by
092   comparing ESL and OMR), we are able to compare and provide insights into their exploratory
093   processes and the impact of task hardness on them (Section 5). The results confirm the ubiquity and
094   effectiveness of our approach to study the exploratory processes of RL algorithms.

349   agent is stuck in suboptimal policies, 2) the coverage area of the exploration processes, and 3) their
350   varied characteristics over time. We further compare ESL and OMR of different algorithms on a few
351   environments in Section 5.2. Finally, we show in Section 5.3 that ESL scales proportionally with
352   task difficulty, and thus, reflects the effects of task difficulty on exploration and learning.

      6   R ELATED W ORKS
487
488   Several prior works have utilized various components leveraged in our work, namely Wasserstein
489   distance, occupancy measures, and the trajectory of RL on a manifold, but for different purposes.
490   Here, we summarise them and elucidate the connections.
491   In supervised learning, Alvarez-Melis & Fusi (2020) proposed an optimal transport approach,
492   namely Optimal Transport Dataset Distance (OTDD), to quantify the transferability between two
493   supervised learning tasks by computing the similarity (aka distance) between the task datasets. Here,
494   we conceptualise and define the effort of learning for RL, as a sequence of such supervised learning
495   tasks. We observe that the total effort of sequential learning can be computed as the sum of OTDD
496   distances between consecutive occupancy measures. Recently, Zhu et al. (2024) have developed
497   generalized occupancy models by defining cumulative features that are transferable across tasks. In
498   future, one can generalize our indices for the cumulative features constructed from some invertible
499
      functions of the step-wise occupancy measures.
500   Optimal transport-based approaches are also explored in RL literature. These works broadly be-
501   long to two families. First line of works uses Wasserstein distance over a posterior distribution of
502   Q-values (Metelli et al., 2019; Likmeta et al., 2023) or return distributions (Sun et al., 2022) to
503   quantify uncertainty, and then to use this Wasserstein distance as a loss to learn better models of
504   the posterior distribution of Q-values or return distributions, respectively. The second line of works
505
      uses Wasserstein distance between a feasible family of MDPs as an additional robustness constraint
      to design robust RL algorithms (Abdullah et al., 2019; Derman & Mannor, 2020; Hou et al., 2020).
506
      Here, we bring a novel concept of using Wasserstein distance between occupancy measures to un-
507
      derstand the exploratory dynamics. Incorporating this insight into better algorithm design would be
508   an interesting future work. Recently, Calo et al. (2024) relate Wasserstein distance between reward-
509   labelled Markov chains to bisimulation metrics which abstract state spaces. In the same spirit, we
510   could use reward as the cost-function in computing our nested Wasserstein distance (OTDD) to ob-
511   tain a reward- or value-aware OTDD to define broader bisimulation metrics with abstract state-action
512   spaces, instead of just state spaces.
513
      As a parallel approach to optimal transport, the information geometries of the trajectory of an RL
514   algorithm under different settings are studied. These approaches use mutual information as a met-
515   ric instead of Wasserstein distance. Basu et al. (2020) study the information geometry of Bayesian
516   multi-armed bandit algorithms. They consider a bandit algorithm as a trajectory on a belief-reward
517   manifold, and propose a geometric approach to design a near-optimal Bayesian bandit algorithm.
518   Eysenbach et al. (2021); Laskin et al. (2022) study information geometry of unsupervised RL and
519   propose mutual information maximization schemes over a set of tasks and their marginal state distri-
520   butions. Yang et al. (2024) extend this approach with Wasserstein distance and demonstrate benefits
521   of using Wasserstein distance than mutual information. We use Wasserstein distance as a natural
522
      metric in occupancy manifold that also allows comparison of hardness of different tasks. It would
      be interesting to extend our framework to understand the dynamics of unsupervised RL algorithms.
523
524
525   7   D ISCUSSION AND F UTURE W ORKS
526
      Our work introduces methods to theoretically and quantitatively understand and compare the learn-
527
      ing strategies of different RL algorithms. Since learning in a typical RL algorithm happens through
528   a sequence of policy updates, we propose to understand the learning process by visualizing and
529   analysing the path traversed by an RL algorithm in the space of occupancy measures corresponding
530   to this sequence.
531
      We show the usefulness of this approach by conducting experiments on various environments. Our
532
      results show that the indices ESL and OMR provide insight into the agent’s policy evolution, re-
533
      vealing whether it is steadily approaching the optimal policy or mostly meandering. Additionally,
534   this allows us to understand how the learning process of the same algorithm changes with different
535   rewards and transitions structures, and task hardness. A key limitation of our indices is that they are
536   based on assumption that the final policy reached at the end of training is an optimal one, though we
537   could still derive some benefit from our approach even if not (see Appendix B.7). In the future, it
538   would be interesting to use this approach to benchmark and compare the learning dynamics of dif-
539   ferent RL algorithms on further environments. In addition, it would be useful to study whether the
      occupancy measures trajectory of an algorithm provides insights to improve its exploratory process.

1325   A.9   WASSERSTEIN S PACES AS G EODESIC S PACES
1326
       Given probability measures µ, ν ∈ P(X ) on a metric space X ⊂ RB with metric dX (x, x′ ), the
1327
       Wasserstein distance Wp (µ, ν) is the minimal transport cost for c(x, x′ ) = (dX (x, x′ ))p with p ≥
1328   1 (Villani, 2009). The Wasserstein distance Wp (µ, ν) takes a distance on X and creates out of it
1329   a distance on P(X )(Peyré, 2019). Proposition 5.1 in (Santambrogio, 2015) asserts that Wp is a
1330   distance over P(X ).
1331
       Definition A.9 (Wasserstein Space). (Santambrogio, 2015) Given a Polish space X , for each p ∈
1332
       [1, ∞), the space P(X ) endowed with the distance Wp is a Wasserstein space Wp of order p.
1333
1334   Theorem 5.27 in (Santambrogio, 2015) states that if X is a convex space, then the space Wp is a
1335   geodesic space (length space). Thus, the geodesic (shortest path distance) between µ, ν ∈ P(X ) is
1336   given by Wp (µ, ν) (Kolouri et al., 2017). It was mentioned in Appendix A.7.1 that the RL problems
1337   we consider consist of the state-action space Z = S × A ∈ RB : B ≥ 2 (subsets of the Euclidean
1338   space). Given that Euclidean spaces are convex spaces (Boyd & Vandenberghe, 2004), our space of
1339
       occupancy measures M is a Wasserstein space Wp = (M, W1 ) and thus a geodesic space. There-
       fore, W1 (µ, ν) measures the shortest path on the surface of the manifold M between probability
1340
       distributions µ and ν.

1548   B.7   E XTENDED D ISCUSSION OF U SEFULNESS OF ESL AND OMR
1549
1550
       The quantities like regret and number of updates (UC) are outcomes of the exploratory processes,
       and thus reflect only a partial view of the underlying exploration mechanisms. We propose ESL and
1551
       OMR to complement regret and number of updates as metrics but not to replace them.
1552
1553   1. Complementarity of ESL and OMR with respect to UC:
1554   a. Case 1. Let us consider two RL algorithms that reach optimality with the same number of updates,
1555   i.e. they have the same UC. How would one be able to distinguish the exploratory processes of these
1556   algorithms? ESL and OMR are the summary metrics of the policy trajectory during learning. These
1557   can reveal which algorithm’s exploratory process is more direct versus meandering, smooth versus
1558   noisy, or has large versus small coverage area in the policy space (Figures 3 and 4, top rows).
1559   Therefore, ESL and OMR quantify with granularity the characteristics of the exploratory process of
1560   an RL algorithm for any given environment.
1561   b. Case 2. Let us consider the case when optimality is not reached but the maximum number
1562   of updates is attained by two RL algorithms. How would one be able to evaluate the exploratory
1563   processes of these algorithms and systematically uncover which exploratory process demonstrate
1564   desired characteristics? Looking into the training trajectories of RL algorithms in an environment
1565   and corresponding higher/lower ESLs (ηsub , Section 4.2), we can make a knowledgeable choice
       of an RL algorithm exhibiting desired characteristics (e.g. high coverage, smooth exploration).


                                                       29
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1566
       We have shown in Section 4.2 and results in Appendix B.5 that ranking based on suboptimal ESL
1567   is aligned with true ESL, and additionally, the visualization of the training trajectories (Figures 3
1568   and 4) can indicate the characteristics of corresponding RL algorithms even when optimal policy is
1569   not reached.
1570
       c. Experimental Evidence. UCRL2 is known to be provably regret-optimal and is designed to
1571
       continuously explore. SAC does not have such rigorous theoretical guarantees but is known to
1572
       be practically efficient. In Table 1, by UC, we observe that SAC is significantly suboptimal than
1573   UCRL2. But SAC has lower ESL than UCRL2 as its exploration is smoother. Additionally, OMR
1574   for SAC is higher than that of UCRL2. They together indicate that SAC takes smoother but larger
1575   number of policy transitions aligned to optimal direction for exploration, while UCRL2 exhibits
1576   bigger policy changes and in diverse manner trying to cover the environment faster.
1577
       2. Complementarity of ESL and OMR with respect to Regret:
1578
1579   UCRL2 and PSRL have the same order of regret bound (Osband et al., 2013). But PSRL leads to
1580   smoother policy transitions that are much more orientated towards optimality (as shown in Figure 3),
1581   while UCRL2 leads to less smooth policy transitions that do not taper as it approaches optimality.
1582
       This information is not evident from regret but from corresponding ESLs and OMRs (Table 1).
1583   3. Insights for Algorithm Design:
1584
       Knowing ESL (or suboptimal ESL) and OMR can assist with developing algorithms that emphasize
1585
       certain exploratory characteristics. We can develop algorithms with grades of coverage or directness,
1586   while also being able to visualize this. Ultimately, depending on the environment, we can choose
1587   which characteristics of exploratory process are well suited. In contrast, looking only at the final
1588   outcomes of RL algorithms like regret and number of updates does not include these nuances.
