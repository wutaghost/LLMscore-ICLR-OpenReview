011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            Achieving efficient data augmentation (DA) in time series classification is not a
014            trivial task due to the high complexity of temporal data. Generative models, such
015
               as GANs (Generative Adversarial Networks), diffusion models, and Variational
               Autoencoders (VAEs), are powerful techniques to address the generative learn-
016
               ing trilemma of producing (1) high-quality samples, (2) fast sampling, and (3)
017
               diversity. These methods vary in their ability to address the trilemma. Diffusion
018            models allows for high diversity and high quality samples, while GAN allows for
019            high quality samples and fast sampling, and VAE for high diversity and fast sam-
020            pling. In this paper, we introduce a novel generative method, ASCENSION (VAE
021            and GMM-controlled latent space class expansion), that retains the strengths of
022            VAE in terms of diversity and fast sampling, while enabling controlled and quan-
023            tifiable exploration of uncharted regions in the latent space. This approach not
024            only enhances classification performance but also yields higher quality (more re-
025            alistic) samples. ASCENSION leverages the probabilistic nature of the VAE’s
026
               latent space to represent classes as Gaussian mixture models (GMMs). By mod-
               ifying this mixture, we enable precise manipulation of class probability densi-
027
               ties and boundaries. To ensure intra-class compactness and maximize inter-class
028
               separation, we apply clustering constraints. Empirical evaluations on the UCR
029            benchmark (102 datasets) show that ASCENSION outperforms state-of-the-art
030            DA methods, achieving an average classification accuracy improvement of ap-
031            proximately 7% and excelling in all aspects of the generative learning trilemma.

034   1    I NTRODUCTION
035
036   The complexity of time series data, represented as X = x1 , x2 , . . . , xN , where each sample xi be-
037   longs to a class yi ∈ 1, 2, . . . , C, combined with limited availability of real-world data due to privacy
038   concerns, poses challenges for effective machine learning training. Data augmentation (DA) helps
039
      mitigate this issue by generating synthetic data to enhance the training set. DA involves creating an
      augmented dataset Xaug , which adds new, diverse samples that remain consistent with their respec-
040
      tive classes, with the goal of improving the efficiency of the classification model. Formally, let Dtrain
041
      represent the original training dataset and Daug the augmented dataset. The conventional approach
042   aims to achieve Dtrain ∪ Daug ∼ dtrue , where dtrue denotes the true underlying data distribution.
043
044   DA methods fall into two categories: Traditional and Generative DA models/methods (Iglesias et al.,
045
      2023b). Traditional DA methods, such as AutoAugment (AA) Cubuk et al. (2019) and Fast Au-
      toAugment (FAA) (Lim et al., 2019), automate the application of predefined transformations like
046
      window slicing, jittering, or scaling (Iglesias et al., 2023a). However, the reliance on these pre-
047
      defined transformations – often adapted from the computer vision domain – restricts the ability to
048   maintain intra-class consistency and preserve the original data semantics, which diminishes the over-
049   all effectiveness of the augmentation process. Generative DA models like GANs, diffusion models
050   and VAEs (Cheung & Yeung, 2020) address the generative learning trilemma of producing (1)
051   high-quality samples, (2) fast sampling, and (3) diversity. While GAN-based DA methods, such as
052   TimeGAN (Zhang et al., 2022), TS-GAN and LatentAugment (Tronchin et al., 2023), excel at gen-
053   erating high-quality samples with speed, they often fall short in terms of diversity (see Figure 1(a)).
      This limitation arises because these models tend to interpolate within the existing data or introduce

082   In this research work, we assume that a controllable progressive expansion mechanism is crucial
083   to prevent the exploration of regions with a high risk of class overlap, which would degrade sample
084   quality. Despite the advances in state-of-the-art generative DA methods, as outlined in Appendix A

086

087

088

089   expansion (shown by successive dashed shapes) reflects different mixture values. Additionally, to
090   ensure that the GMMs faithfully capture the data distribution and retain statistical significance, we
091   impose clustering constraints that enhance the structural properties of the VAE’s latent space. These
092   constraints foster intra-class compactness while maximizing inter-class separation.

      2                       ASCENSION METHOD
132
133   ASCENSION builds on a VAE-based generative model, leveraging its fast sampling and diversity
134
      capabilities, while introducing a controllable mechanism for progressive expansion of the latent
      space. Instead of focusing solely on class-consistent augmentation, ASCENSION approximates
135
      each class distribution with a GMM. By gradually increasing the variances of GMM components,
136
      it expands the space for each class, enabling boundary exploration while minimizing overlap risk.
137   Figure 2 shows ASCENSION’s architecture, featuring (i) a deep clustering VAE that learns latent
138   representations, and (ii) a GMM that models the latent space and generates new samples. Encoder
139   and Decoder architectures vary based on data type: fully connected networks for univariate time
140   series, and CNNs or RNNs for multivariate data. The ASCENSION augmentation process involves
141   three steps, outlined in sections 2.1 to 2.3.
142
143   2.1                      VAE TRAINING
144
145   The VAE fVAE to learn a low-dimensional representation of the input time series data. It consists of
146   an encoder fenc that maps the input data to a latent space, and a decoder fdec that reconstructs the
147   input data from the latent space. It is worth noting that we implement the re-parametrization trick
148   to differentiate the encoder and decoder during training. In summary: fVAE = fdec ◦ fenc ∶ x ↦ x̂,
149   where x̂ is the reconstructed version of the input x.
150
151   2.1.1                      L ATENT SPACE
152
153
      We denote the latent space as Z = {z1 , z2 , . . . , zN }, where each latent point zi corresponds to the
      encoded vector of the input sample xi . We denote K as the dimension of the latent space. Optimally,
154
      K should be chosen as low as possible to capture the essential features of the data while reducing
155
      the risk of overfitting. The VAE models the posterior distribution over the latent variables given the
156   input data through the variational distribution qϕ (z∣x). This distribution is typically assumed to be
157   Gaussian and is parameterized by the encoder network with parameters ϕ. Specifically, qϕ (z∣x) is
158   defined as:
159                                    qϕ (z∣x) = N (z; µϕ (x), Σϕ (x)),                                   (1)
160
161   where µϕ (x) and Σϕ (x) represent the mean and covariance of the Gaussian distribution, respec-
      tively, both of which are functions of the input x and are learned by the encoder network.

      2.1.2    C LUSTERING CONSTRAINTS
163
164   Hypothesis 1 (Latent space clustering) [H1] We hypothesize that adding a clustering constraints
165   during VAE training will create a more structured latent space, improving class-consistent sample
166   generation and classification performance.
167
      To ensure that the latent space representations learned by the VAE are semantically meaningful and
168
      aligned with the classification task, we introduce clustering constraints during the training process.
169   These constraints encourage samples from the same class to cluster together while maintaining a
170   significant distance from samples of other classes. This approach is vital for generating synthetic
171   samples that are class-consistent and reflect the underlying data distribution. The constraints are
172   incorporated as additional loss terms in the VAE training process, penalizing pairwise distances
173   between samples from the same class in the latent space. By minimizing this clustering loss, the
174   VAE learns to encode the input data in a manner that promotes the generation of diverse and class-
175   specific synthetic samples. In this context of multiple loss terms, normalizing these terms is essential
176   to ensure proper model convergence, preventing any single loss term from dominating the training
177
      process. Consequently, we augment the standard VAE loss function with an additional term:
178                                     L = Lrecon + LKL + Lclass + Lcluster                              (2)
179   where Lrecon represents the MSE between the VAE’s input and output, LKL denotes the Kullback-
180   Leibler divergence loss, Lclass is the classification loss, and Lcluster is defined as in (3).
181                                                 N    N

182                                      Lcluster = ∑ ∑ δyi ,yj ⋅ d(zi , zj )                               (3)
                                                   i=1 j=1

184   Given the high dimensionality of the data, we use cosine similarity as the distance metric for d.
185   Examples showing how the latent space is evaluating through the learning phase in ASCENSION
186   (using specific UCR datasets) are given and discussed in Appendix F.

188   2.2     GMM MANIPULATION
189
190   Hypothesis 2 (Distribution discrepancies) [H2] We hypothesize that current state-of-the-art gen-
191   erative DA methods are hindered by significant discrepancies in distance between the training and
192
      testing set distributions.
193   Hypothesis 3 (Consistency through expansion) [H3] We hypothesize that adjusting class distri-
194   bution to expand training set boundaries will improve accuracy, especially in datasets with discrep-
195   ancies between training and testing distributions.
196
197   ASCENSION approximates the distribution of each class yi using a GMM, denoted as GM M (yi ).
198
      The augmentation process generates synthetic samples by sampling from these GMMs while grad-
      ually expanding the class boundaries by increasing the covariance matrices Σ of the Gaussian com-
199
      ponents. Statistically, samples are generated according to the following formula:
200

204   ponent with mean µk , and αΣk is a scaled covariance matrix. The scaling factor α is used to flatten
205   the distribution. Figure 3 illustrates the evolution of class distributions for three different classes as
206   the parameter α changes (α = 1, 2 and 3). As the covariance increases, the overlap between classes

210                                             yi = arg max Pj (x)                                          (5)

274   Train/Test datasets: Experiments were conducted using the UCR Time Series Archive, which com-
275   prises 120 univariate time series datasets from various applications and domains, including sensors,
276   ECG, etc. (a complete list of the dataset types is provided in Table 4).
277   Classification models: Classifiers selected for our experiments were chosen based on the findings
278   of Fawaz (2020), which reports that ResNet-50 and Fully Connected Networks (FCN) are the two
279   most effective classifiers (out of 9 evaluated for the UCR datasets. We use the architectures from
280   (Koonce & Koonce, 2021) and (Scabini & Bruno, 2023) for these two classifiers. Additionally, we
281   also incorporate: (i) the embedded classifier of ASCENSION, denoted ASCENSIONEmbCl. ; (ii) a
282   combination of ASCENSION’s embedded classifier with the state-of-the-art classifiers denoted by
283   ASCENSIONc-EmbCl. with c ∈ {ResNet, FCN} in our experiments. The augmentation is defined as
284   the difference between the maximum baseline accuracy (i.e., without augmentation), either VAE’s
285
      classifier or standalone classifier c, and the maximum accuracy achieved by ASCENSIONEmbCl. or
      classifier c, given by the formula:
286
287             AccASCENSIONc-EmbCl. = max(AccASCENSIONEmbCl. , Accc ) − max(AccBaselinec , AccVAE )     (6)
288
289   Benchmarked DA methods: ASCENSION is compared with several state-of-the-art methods,
290   including one traditional DA method (FAA) and three generative methods (TTS-GAN, LA,
291
      MODALS). More details on these methods can be found in Appendix A. FAA was selected due to its
      comparable performance with other traditional DA methods (incl., RA and DAA), while MODALS
292
      was chosen for its architectural similarity to ASCENSION. TTS-GAN and LA were included as the
293
      most recent generative DA methods with publicly available code (cf., Figure 7). However, bench-
294   marking MODALS on the UCR datasets is not feasible since its code, released in 2020, is no longer
295   functional, and the authors informed us they do not plan to repair it. Therefore, we propose to
296   benchmark ASCENSION by evaluating it on the same dataset originally used by Cheung & Yeung
297   (2020) for assessing MODALS.

      4   C ONCLUSION
487
488
      A key challenge in time series data augmentation is addressing the generative learning trilemma
489   (see Figure 1). Generative DA methods, such as GAN, diffusion models and VAEs vary in their
490   ability to address this trilemma while maintaining high classification performance. In this paper,
491   we introduce a novel method called ASCENSION, which builds on the strengths of VAEs in terms
492   of diversity and fast sampling, while enabling controlled and quantifiable exploration of uncharted
493   regions in the latent space. ASCENSION uses the probabilistic nature of the VAE’s latent space to
494   model classes as GMMs, and through the manipulation of these mixtures, allows for precise adjust-
495   ments to class probability densities and boundaries. Clustering constraints are applied to maintain
496   intra-class compactness and maximize inter-class separation. Overall, ASCENSION addresses the
497
      challenges of high-dimensional sequential data by enhancing the representativeness of the training
      set and expanding decision boundaries in a controlled manner. This is particularly useful when there
498
      is a significant discrepancy in distance between the distributions of training and testing sets.
499
500   Our empirical study on 102 UCR benchmark datasets shows that ASCENSION outperforms state-
501   of-the-art DA techniques. It is evaluated on multiple metrics, including (i) classification accuracy,
502   (ii) diversity, (iii) sample quality, and (iv) fast sampling speed, excelling in all areas compared to
503
      benchmarked DA methods. Furthermore, an in-depth analysis identifies the types of time series data
      that benefit most from augmentation with each DA method. This study highlights ASCENSION’s
504
      advantage in handling datasets with high discrepancies between training and testing distributions.
505
506   Future research could explore extending ASCENSION to other types of sequential data, such as
507   natural language or spatio-temporal datasets, but also non-sequential data such as images, due to
508   its highly flexible architecture. We could also explore new clustering and sampling strategies to
509
      enhance generalization across different domains, along with expansion mechanisms (e.g., beyond a
      single α factor).

620   A    R ELATED WORK
621
622   Iglesias et al. (2023b) and Iwana & Uchida (2021) divide DA for time series into two categories:
623   Traditional vs. Generative DA methods. Figure 7 offers an overview of the evolution of these meth-
624   ods, emphasizing their ability to address and manage the generative learning trilemma (diversity,
625   high-quality samples, fast sampling) and whether their associated codes are publicly available.
626   Traditional DA methods, such as window slicing, jittering, and scaling (Iglesias et al., 2023a), are
627   primarily adapted from computer vision and rely on transformation strategies like cropping, rotation,
628   scaling, drifting, and so forth. However, the complex nature of time series data often renders these
629   methods sub-optimal, as they can disrupt the semantic integrity of the original data. For instance,
630   while a slightly flipped image of a cat remains recognizable, reversing the time axis of an electro-
631   cardiogram sequence can render it meaningless. In response to these challenges, more advanced DA
632   techniques were developed to automate the sequence of transformations to be performed. A first
633
      method, named AutoAugment (AA) Cubuk et al. (2019), uses reinforcement learning to explore
      transformation pipelines/policies. A second method named Fast AutoAugment (FAA) (Lim et al.,
634
      2019) uses density matching for a faster search strategy, eliminating the need for back-propagation.
635
      Subsequent methods such as RandAugment (Cubuk et al., 2020), Deep AutoAugment (Zheng
636   et al., 2022), and Trivial Augment (Müller & Hutter, 2021) were introduced to further simplify
637   and refine the augmentation search strategy. RandAugment streamlines the augmentation process
638   by removing the exhaustive search phase, instead applying a fixed number of random transforma-
639   tions with adjustable magnitudes. Deep AutoAugment incorporates a deep reinforcement learning
640   model that dynamically combines transformation policies based on the specific characteristics of the
641   dataset. Trivial Augment introduces an even simpler approach by applying a minimal set of random
642   transformations, emphasizing ease of use and computational efficiency. Despite all these advance-
643   ments, all these methods rely on predefined transformations, which is suboptimal for preserving
644
      intra-class consistency and the semantic characteristics of the original time series data, thereby lim-
      iting the effectiveness of data augmentation.
645
646   Generative DA models such as Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) (Goodfellow et al.,
647   2020), diffusion models (Yang et al., 2023a), and VAEs (Kingma & Welling, 2013) represent power-
      ful techniques capable of learning a probabilistic representation of data distributions. These models

687   architecture to augment time series data in the context of class imbalance by adjusting the loss with
688   mutual information term and using a spectral normalization. LISGAN generates high quality syn-
689   thetic data and significantly increases classification performance with industrial internet of things
690   datasets. Diffusion models, a more recent class of generative models, have garnered significant
691   attention for their capability to model complex data distributions. Unlike GANs, which rely on ad-
692   versarial training, diffusion models generate data by progressively refining noise toward the target
693
      data distribution. This denoising approach has yielded remarkable results in high-fidelity image

694

695

697   synthetic sample quality through meta-attribute conditioning, and Time-DDPM, which integrates
698   a diffusion denoising probabilistic model with CNN-LSTM networks to enhance sample quality.

905
      α parameter: As discussed in section 3.2.2, performance improvement relation to α seems diffi-
906   cult to generalize while remaining relatively stable. Increasing α can lead to better boundary explo-
907   ration, as shown in Figures 12 and 11 but can also make the performance drop for too high values of
908   α. While pinpointing the exact α values and iterations for optimal results across all datasets is not
909   trivial, the general trend suggests selecting α ∈ [1, 3] to expand class boundaries without venturing
910   into areas that risk class overlap, which could negatively impact classification accuracy.
911
912   Number of iterations: In Figures 11-13, and 15, we observe that a higher number of iterations
913   can have either a positive or negative impact on performance, whereas in Figure 8, the number of
914   iterations does not play a significant role in performance improvement. This ambivalent behavior
915   is closely related to the class distribution within the dataset. As the number of iterations increases,
916   classes in the latent space may become closer due to the increase in the α parameter at each iteration,
917   which leads to the expansion of covariances αΣk (cf., section 2.2). Therefore, we recommend
      carefully adjusting the number of iterations in relation to the chosen α parameter.
