009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Representation learning is an approach that allows to discover and extract the fac-
012            tors of variation from the data. Intuitively, a representation is said to be disentan-
013            gled if it separates the different factors of variation in a way that is understandable
014            to humans. Definitions of disentanglement and metrics to measure it usually as-
015            sume that the factors of variation are independent of each other. However, this is
016            generally false in the real world, which limits the use of these definitions and met-
017            rics to very specific and unrealistic scenarios. In this paper we give a definition of
018
               disentanglement based on information theory that is also valid when the factors are
               not independent. Furthermore, we demonstrate that this definition is equivalent to
019
               having a representation composed of minimal and sufficient variables. Finally, we
020
               propose a method to measure the degree of disentanglement from the given defini-
021            tion that works when the factors are not independent. We show through different
022            experiments that the method proposed in this paper correctly measures disentan-
023            glement with independent and non-independent factors, while other methods fail
024            in the latter scenario.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   In the jargon of representation learning, data are considered to be completely defined by factors of
030   variation (or simply factors from now on) and nuisances. The difference between them is that the
031   former are relevant for a given task and the latter are not. For example, if we had a dataset composed
032
      of images of fruits and the task were to describe the fruits in the images, the factors could be the
      type of fruit, the color or the size. On the other hand, nuisances could be the background color or the
033
      shadow of the fruit. In multiple works it has been proposed that it is desirable for a representation to
034
      be disentangled, i.e., it separates the different factors of variation (Schmidhuber, 1992; Bengio et al.,
035   2013; Ridgeway, 2016; Lake et al., 2017; Achille & Soatto, 2018a). Disentangled representations
036   have different application, such as: (i) interpreting and explaining the representations and predictions
037   (Hsu et al., 2017; Worrall et al., 2017; Gilpin et al., 2018; Zhang & Zhu, 2018; Liu et al., 2020; Zhu
038   et al., 2021; Nauta et al., 2023; Almudévar et al., 2024), (ii) making fairer predictions by mitigating
039   or eliminating biases in sensitive attributes (such as gender or race) (Calders & Žliobaitė, 2013;
040   Barocas & Selbst, 2016; Kumar et al., 2017; Locatello et al., 2019a; Sarhan et al., 2020; Chen et al.,
041   2023), or (iii) giving generative models the ability to create new data with concrete attributes (Yan
042   et al., 2016; Paige et al., 2017; Huang et al., 2018; Dupont, 2018; Kazemi et al., 2019; Antoran &
043   Miguel, 2019; Zhou et al., 2020; Shen et al., 2022; Almudévar et al., 2024).
044   The definition in the previous paragraph allows us to understand the concept of disentangled rep-
045   resentation in an intuitive but imprecise way. Two main questions arise from this definition: what
046   does it exactly mean to separate the factors, and how can we assess whether the different factors
047   are actually separated? We review in section 2 different works that have tried to clear these doubts.
048   Most of these works assume that the different factors are independent of each other and of the nui-
049   sances. Therefore, the definitions and metrics they propose are assuming this independence. Most
050
      of the datasets where these definitions and metrics are assessed do have their factors independent
      (or nearly independent) (LeCun et al., 2004; Liu et al., 2015; Reed et al., 2015; Matthey et al., 2017;
051
      Burgess & Kim, 2018; Gondal et al., 2019), so the definitions and metrics are valid (or nearly valid).
052
053   However, in the real world it is uncommon for factors to be independent of each other and of the
      nuisances. In the previous example, it is easy to see that the type of fruit is not independent of the


                                                         1

054
      color, the size or the shadow of the fruit. It is very likely to have a red strawberry, but the probability
055   of having a red banana tends to zero. When the factors are independent of each other, understanding
056   the concept of separating them seems obvious. However, it is not so obvious to understand what it
057   means for a representation to separate dependent factors. For example, we might ask whether it is
058   possible to separate the concept of a strawberry from the color red and what the implications of this
059   are. If we can separate the factors, we could create an image of a red banana or avoid classifying a
060   yellow strawberry as a banana or a lemon just for the fact that it is yellow, for example. In this work,
061   we focus on defining and measuring disentanglement when the factors are not independent of each
062   other and of the nuisances. Our key contributions can be summarized as follows:
063          • We propose a set of four properties based on information theory that are desirable for a
064            disentangled representation when the factors are not independent of each other and of the
065            nuisances. We connect these properties to those most accepted in the literature.
066          • We demonstrate that these properties are fulfilled if and only if the representations are
067            minimal and sufficient. Then, we argue that measuring minimality and sufficiency is more
068            interesting than measuring the previous properties separately.
069
             • We give a method for measuring the degree of minimality and sufficiency, which allows to
070            evaluate the level of disentanglement of a representation. We evaluated these methods on
071            different datasets to illustrate their appropriateness in a variety of scenarios.

074   2    R ELATED W ORK
075   Definition of Disentanglement Despite being a topic of great interest, there is no general con-
076   sensus on the definition of disentangled representation. Intuitively, a disentangled representation
077   separates the different factors of the data (Desjardins et al., 2012; Bengio et al., 2013; Cohen &
078   Welling, 2014; Kulkarni et al., 2015; Ridgeway, 2016). Originally, disentanglement was evaluated
079   via visual inspection (Kingma & Welling, 2013). However, there is a need to better specify what
080
      it means for a representation to be disentangled, as this provides insight into the scope and limi-
      tations of their different applications. One of the first definitions of disentanglement was given in
081
      Bengio et al. (2013) and long the most widely accepted in the literature (Higgins et al., 2017; Kim
082
      & Mnih, 2018; Kim et al., 2019; Suter et al., 2019), says that a disentangled representation is one
083   in which a change in one part of the representation corresponds to a change in a factor of variation,
084   while being relatively invariant to changes in other factors. Other authors claim that a disentangled
085   representation is one in which a change in a single factor of variation translates into the change in
086   a single part of the representation (Kumar et al., 2017; Locatello et al., 2019b). Others propose to
087   define a disentangled representations based on a list of properties that they must satisfy. Ridgeway
088   & Mozer (2018) and Eastwood & Williams (2018) stand out in this by proposing virtually simul-
089   taneously three properties that a representation must satisfy in order to be considered disentangled.
090   Although they refer to the same ideas, the two papers refer to these properties by different names.
091
      These properties are: (i) modularity (disentanglement in Eastwood & Williams (2018)), that is, each
      variable in the representation captures at most one factor of variation; (ii) compactness (complete-
092
      ness in Eastwood & Williams (2018)), i.e., a factor of variation is captured by only one variable of
093
      the representation; and (iii) explicitness (informativeness in Eastwood & Williams (2018)), that is,
094   the representation captures all the information about the factors that is present in the input. All of
095   the above definitions present two problems: (i) they do not analyze the invariance to some nuisances
096   possibly present in the input; and (ii) they assume that the factors of variation are independent of
097   each other and of the nuisances, which is in general false. In this paper we give a list of properties
098   that a representation must satisfy to be considered disentangled considering these two last aspects.
099   Metrics for Disentanglement As a consequence of the fact that there is no consensus on the
100   definition of disentanglement, there is no consensus on how to measure it. In Carbonneau et al.
101   (2022) they organize the methods in the literature into three groups according to the principle of
102   their operation. Below we explain an overview of each of these groups.
103           • Intervention-based Metrics. These create subsets in which their elements have in com-
104             mon a factor of variation while the rest are different. Subsequently, the representations of
105             these elements are obtained and compared in different ways to obtain a score. Some of
106             the methods in this family are: β-VAE (Higgins et al., 2017), FactorVAE (Kim & Mnih,
107             2018) and R-FactorVAE (Kim et al., 2019). These methods are intended to measure only
                modularity, but not compactness and explicitness (Sepliarskaia et al., 2019).

                                                          2

108
             • Information-based Metrics. These methods estimate the mutual information between the
109            factors of variation and each variable in the representation. Some of the most widely used
110            are Mutual information Gap (MIG) (Chen et al., 2018) or Robust MIG (Do & Tran, 2019).
111            These methods are designed to measure only the compactness. Modifications on these
112            methods have been proposed to also capture modularity (Do & Tran, 2019; Li et al., 2020;
113            Ridgeway & Mozer, 2018; Sepliarskaia et al., 2019).
114          • Predictor-based Metrics. These train regressors or classifiers to predict the factors of
115            variation from the different variables in the representations. Subsequently, the predictor is
116            inspected to analyze the importance of each variable to predict each factor. The first method
117            of this family proposed was Separated Attribute Predictability (SAP) (Kumar et al., 2017),
118            which measures only compactness. Soon after, as we have already explained, it was pro-
119            posed to measure disentanglement through different properties. In Eastwood & Williams
120            (2018) the properties disentanglement, completeness and informativeness (DCI) and ways
121
               to measure them are proposed. Simultaneously, the properties modularity, compactness
               and explicitness and ways to measure them are proposed in Ridgeway & Mozer (2018).
122
123
      Our proposal would fall into the latter group, since we train predictors to measure the different
      properties we describe. However, unlike the methods mentioned above, ours allows us to measure
124
      the degree of disentanglement even when the factors are dependent between them or of the nuisances.
125
126   Disentanglement under Dependent Factors of Variation As mentioned, disentanglement under
127   dependent factors is an underexplored field. However, there are some works related to this idea,
128   among which the following stand out: Suter et al. (2019), that gives a causal perspective on repre-
129   sentation learning that addresses disentanglement and domain shift robustness; Choi et al. (2020),
130   which disentangles variations common to all classes and variations exclusive of each class; Montero
131   et al. (2020; 2022), which excludes combinations of factors during training and at test time they ask
132
      the models to reconstruct the missing; Träuble et al. (2021), that artificially introduces dependencies
      between pairs of factors; Roth et al. (2022), which considers the use of Hausdorff Factorized Sup-
133
      port (HFS) criterion allowing for arbitrary distributions of the factors over their support, including
134
      correlations between them; Ahuja et al. (2023), that explores how interventional data can help causal
135   representation learning by revealing geometric patterns in latent variables; and von Kügelgen et al.
136   (2024), which focuses on inferring latent causal variables and relations from high-dimensional data
137   using a non-parametric approach without restrictive assumptions.
138
139   Information Bottleneck This a technique introduced by Tishby et al. (2000) that is designed to
140
      find a trade-off between fidelity and compression of representations. The representation that is max-
      imally faithful is said to be sufficient and the one that is maximally compressed is called minimal.
141
      The ideal representation should contain only (minimal) all (sufficient) the information necessary to
142
      solve a given task. Formally, given an input x, a task y and a representation z, the sufficient represen-
143   tation z maximizes I(z; y) and the minimal representation minimizes I(z; x). Different works use
144   this technique to derive regularizers in representation learning (Alemi et al., 2016; 2018; Achille &
145   Soatto, 2018b;a). Others have optimized it to obtain disentangled representations. Vera et al. (2018);
146   Yamada et al. (2020); Jeon et al. (2021); Gao et al. (2021). However, to the best of our knowledge,
147   the present is the first work formally defining the concept of disentanglement through that of mini-
148   mal sufficient representation. We do it in a way in which we consider each of the factors as a task
149   and the representation as a set of representations. Concretely, if each of these representations is
150   minimal and sufficient for a given factor, then we consider the representation to be disentangled.

      4       D ISENTANGLEMENT THROUGH M INIMALITY AND S UFFICIENCY
217
218   In representation learning, given an input x and a task y, the representation z that maximizes I(z; y)
219   is called sufficient. By the definition of representation, we know by the Data Processing Inequality
220   (DPI) (Beaudry & Renner, 2011) that I(z; y) ≤ I(x; y). Since this is the unique upper bound of
221   I(z; y), the sufficient representation satisfies that I(z; y) = I(x; y) or, equivalently, the Markov
222
      chain x ↔ z ↔ y, i.e., y is fully described by z. On the other hand, the representation z that
      minimizes I(z; x) is called minimal. From the definition of representation and the DPI, we know
223
      that I(z; x) ≥ I(z; y). Since this is the unique lower bound of I(z; x), the minimal representation
224
      satisfies that I(z; x) = I(z; y) or, equivalently, the Markov chain x ↔ y ↔ z, i.e., z is fully
225   described by y. From the above, we have that a representation is sufficient and minimal if it satisfies
226   that I(x; y) = I(z; y) = I(z; x).

250   4.2       W HY MEASURING D ISENTANGLEMENT THROUGH M INIMALITY AND S UFFICIENCY ?
251
      In section 3 we have provided four desirable properties for a disentangled representation. These
252
      properties hinge on those most widely accepted in the literature and are adapted to the scenario in
253   which the factors may be non-independent. In section 4.1 we connect this properties to the concepts
254   of minimality and sufficiency. Next, we argue that it makes more sense to evaluate minimality and
255   sufficiency than the four properties of 3 separately to measure the degree of disentanglement.
256
257            1. Minimality vs. Factors-Invariance + Nuisance Invariance: When we evaluate whether
258               a representation is disentangled, we really want to analyze whether each of its variables is
259               affected only by a single factor regardless of the other factors or the nuisances as a whole. It
260
                  is no use having a variable that is only affected by one of the factors if the nuisances affect
                  it to a large extent. Similarly, if a variable is nuisances-invariant, but the rest of the factors
261
                  affect it, we could not consider that we have an disentangled representation. Therefore,
262
                  we argue that it makes sense to measure these two properties jointly and, as proposed in
263               Proposition 1, this can be done through measuring the minimality.
264
               2. Sufficiency vs. Representation-Invariance + Explicitness: When we evaluate whether a
265
                  representation is disentangled, we want to analyze if we can describe a factor using only
266
                  one variable of the representation and whether we can describe it completely. If we had a
267               representation in which only one variable affected a factor, but could describe it to a very
268               low extent, it would not be fulfilling the most elementary objective of a representation,
269

355   We propose in this section a set of experiments in which we artificially define the representations
356
      based on the factors. This allows us to have exact knowledge of the relationship between the factors

472   independent. However, Träuble et al. (2021) propose a modification
                                                                             to introduce correlation between

                                                                                          2   , i.e., the lower
474   the value of σ the stronger the correlation. Subsequently, Roth et al. (2022) propose to introduce
475   this correlation scheme between multiple pairs of factors. Here, we propose a similar scheme.
476   Concretely, the results include scenarios with uncorrelated factors (original datasets), correlations
477
      between multiple pairs of factors (a factor is never correlated with more than one factor) and shared
      confounders (one factor correlates to all others). As in the previous experiment, we artificially
478
      define the representation from the factors, so that we can have exact knowledge of the level of
479
      disentanglement. We define z = Ay, so that A = (aij ), aii = 1 − α, aij ∼ U[0, α) if i ̸= j and
480   α ∈ [0, 0.5]. Thus, the representation will be fully disentangled when α = 0. We use this scheme
481   for Shapes3D (Burgess & Kim, 2018), dSprites (Matthey et al., 2017), MPI3D (Gondal et al., 2019)
482   and CelebA(Liu et al., 2015). The results for MPI3D are shown next and those corresponding to
483   Shapes3D, dSprites and CelebA can be found in Appendix D due to space limitations and the fact
484   that the conclusions drawn are similar to those of MPI3D. We compute the experiments 10 times and

527   6      C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this work we have presented two problems that the different definitions of disentanglement suffer:
530   (i) they do not analyze the invariance to the nuisances; and (ii) they assume that the factors of
531
      variation are independent of each other and of the nuisances. Consequently, the metrics used to
      measure disentanglement do not capture it accurately when any of these two factors are present. To
532
      address these problems, we have first proposed four properties from the point of view of information
533
      theory that serve to define a disentangled representation considering the nuisances in the input and
534   the scenario in which the factors can be dependent. Furthermore, we have related these properties
535   to the concepts of minimality and sufficiency of a representation. In fact, we have argued that it
536   is more convenient to measure the degree of sufficiency and minimality of a representation rather
537   than measuring the four properties individually. Subsequently, we have proposed metrics to measure
538   sufficiency and minimality and derived estimators of them. Finally, we have compared our metrics
539   with others in the literature to illustrate that our metrics are able to correctly capture the level of
      disentanglement under a wider variety of scenarios.

      Proposition 1. Let y = {yl }nl=1 be some factors and z = {zj }m    j=1 a representation. If zj is a min-
759   imal representation of yi , then zj is factors-invariant for yi and nuisances-invariant. Equivalently,
760   we have that:
761                        (x ↔ yi ↔ zj ) ⇐⇒ (ỹi ↔ yi ↔ zj ) ∧ (n ↔ yi ↔ zj )
762
763
      Proof. First, we demonstrate that (x ↔ yi ↔ zj ) =⇒ (ỹi ↔ yi ↔ zj ): Given this Markov chain,
      by the DPI, we have that I(zj ; x) ≤ I(zj ; yi ). Since zj is a representation of x, we also have by DPI
764
      that I(zj ; y) ≤ I(zj ; x). Therefore, it follows that I(zj ; y) ≤ I(zj ; yi ) On the other hand, by the
765
      chain rule of mutual information, we have that I(zj ; y) = I(zj ; yi , ỹi ) = I(zj ; yi ) + I(zj ; ỹi |yi ).
766   By the non-negativity of mutual information, we are left with I(zj ; y) ≥ I(zj ; yi ). Therefore, we
767   have that I(zj ; yi ) = I(zj ; y) = I(zj ; yi , ỹi ) or, equivalently, ỹi ↔ yi ↔ zj .
768
769
      Second, we demonstrate that (x ↔ yi ↔ zj ) =⇒ (n ↔ yi ↔ zj ): Given this Markov chain, we
      know from the DPI that I(zj ; x) ≤ I(zj ; yi ). On the other hand, since zj is a representation of x,
770
      we have the Markov chain (n, yi ) ↔ x ↔ zj . Equivalently, we have that I(zj ; n, yi ) ≤ I(zj ; x).
771
      The chain rule of mutual information tells us that I(zj ; n|yi ) = I(zj ; n, yi ) − I(zj |yi ). According
772   to the above two points, we are left with I(zj ; n|yi ) ≤ I(zj ; x) − I(zj |yi ). Therefore, because of
773   the non-negativity of mutual information, it is only possible that I(zj ; n|yi ) = 0 or, equivalently,
774   n ↔ yi ↔ zj .
775
      Finally, we demonstrate that (ỹi ↔ yi ↔ zj ) ∧ (n ↔ yi ↔ zj ) =⇒ (x ↔ yi ↔ zj ): We
776
      know by the Bayes’ theorem that p(zj , y, n) = p(zj |y, n)p(n|y)p(y) = p(n|zj , y)p(zj |y)p(y) =
777
      p(n|zj , y)p(zj |yi )p(y). Thus, p(zj |x) = p(zj |y, n) = p(zj |yi ) if and only if p(n|y) = p(n|zj , y),
778   which is equivalent to having p(zj |y) = p(zj |y, n) = p(zj |x) and since p(zj |y) = p(zj |yi ), we
779   are left with p(zj |x) = p(zj |yi ), and thus, (x ↔ yi ↔ zj ).

783   tation of yi if and only if zj is representations-invariant for yi and z is explicit for yi . Equivalently,
784   we have the that:
785                          (x ↔ zj ↔ yi ) ⇐⇒ (z̃j ↔ zj ↔ yi ) ∧ (x ↔ z ↔ yi )
786
787   Proof. First, we demonstrate that (x ↔ zj ↔ yi ) =⇒ (z̃j ↔ zj ↔ yi ): Given this Markov chain,
788
      by the DPI, we have that I(yi ; x) ≤ I(yi ; zj ). Since z is a representation of x, we also have that
      I(yi ; z) ≤ I(yi ; x). Therefore, it follows that I(yi ; z) ≤ I(yi ; zj ) On the other hand, by the mutual
789
      information chain rule, we have that I(yi ; z) = I(yi ; zj , z̃j ) = I(yi ; zj ) + I(yi ; z̃j |zj ). By the
790
      non-negativity of mutual information, we are left with I(yi ; z) ≥ I(yi ; zj ). Therefore, we have that
791   I(yi ; zj ) = I(yi ; z) = I(yi ; zj , z̃j ) or, equivalently, z̃j ↔ zj ↔ yi .
792
793   Second, we demonstrate that (x ↔ zj ↔ yi ) =⇒ (x ↔ z ↔ yi ): Since zj is a representation of
794
      x, we have that p(yi |x, zj ) = p(yi |x). Moreover, since zj is sufficient, we have that p(yi |x, zj ) =
      p(yi |zj ). Putting the above two terms together, we are left with p(yi |x) = p(yi |zj ). As we have
795
      already shown, if zj is sufficient, then we have the Markov chain (z̃j ↔ zj ↔ yi ), so p(yi |z) =
796
      p(yi |zj ). Therefore, we are left with p(yi |z) = p(yi |x). Finally, since z is a representation of x,
797   we know that p(yi |x) = p(yi |x, z). Putting all of the above together, we are left with p(yi |x, z) =
798   p(yi |z) or, equivalently, x ↔ z ↔ yi .
799
800
      Finally, we demonstrate that (z̃j ↔ zj ↔ yi ) ∧ (x ↔ z ↔ yi ) =⇒ (x ↔ zj ↔ yi ): Since
      zj and z are representations of x, we have that p(yi |x, zj ) = p(yi |x, z) = p(yi |x). Furthermore,
801
      given the Markov chain x ↔ z ↔ yi , we know that p(yi |x, z) = p(yi |z). Equivalently, given
802
      z̃j ↔ zj ↔ yi , we have that p(yi |zj ) = p(yi |zj , z̃j ) = p(yi |z). Recapitulating the above, we are
803   left with p(yi |zj ) = p(yi |z) = p(yi |x, z) = p(yi |x, zj ). Therefore, we have that x ↔ zj ↔ yi .
