009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Motion prediction is a crucial task in autonomous driving. Existing motion predic-
012            tion models rely on high-definition (HD) maps to provide environmental context
013            for agents. However, offline HD maps require extensive manual annotation, mak-
014            ing them costly and unscalable. Online mapping-based methods still require HD
015            map annotation to train the online mapping module, which is costly as well and
016            may also suffer from the issue of out-of-distribution map elements. In this work,
017            we explore conducting motion prediction with standard-definition (SD) maps as
018
               substitution, which are more readily available and offer broader coverage. One
               crucial challenge is that SD maps have low resolution and poor alignment accu-
019
               racy. Directly replacing HD maps with SD maps leads to a significant drop in
020
               performance. We introduce end-to-end learning and specially tailored modules
021            for SD maps to solve the problems. Specifically, we propose ESDMotion, the
022            first end-to-end motion prediction framework that uses SD maps without any HD
023            map supervision. We integrate BEV features obtained from raw sensor data into
024            existing motion prediction models, with tailored designs for anchor-based and
025            anchor-free models respectively. We find that the coarse and misaligned SD maps
026            bring challenges to feature fusion of anchor-free model and on anchor generation
027            of anchor-based model. Thus, we design two novel modules named Enhanced
028            Road Observation and Pseudo Lane Expansion to address these issues. Bene-
029
               fiting from the end-to-end structure and new modules, ESDMotion outperforms
               the state-of-the-art online mapping-based motion prediction methods by 13.4%
030
               in motion prediction performance and narrows the performance gap between HD
031
               and SD maps by 73%. We will open source our code and checkpoints.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   The autonomous driving system is composed of multiple modules, including perception, motion
037   prediction, and planning. The motion prediction module forecasts the future states of agents based
038   on their historical data and environmental context, providing critical insights for planning and con-
039   trol. In providing environmental context for motion prediction models, High-Definition (HD) maps
040   play a crucial role. These maps offer detailed and precise road geometry information, such as lane
041   dividers, centerlines, pedestrian crossings, and stop lines, and are widely employed in motion pre-
042   diction models (Gu et al., 2021; Shi et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2022).
043   However, obtaining HD maps is costly. As in Fig. 1 (a), Creating HD maps requires extensive data
044   collection and manual annotation, with updates required every 2-3 months (Li et al., 2022a). It
045   constrains the applicability of methods that rely on HD maps. To reduce dependence on HD maps,
046   as in Fig. 1 (b), online mapping models (Liao et al., 2023a; Li et al., 2024b; Yuan et al., 2024) are
047   developed. These models predict HD map elements around the vehicle using sensor data in real time,
048   providing input for downstream modules. Nevertheless, the training of these supervised models still
049   depends on ground-truth annotations, facing the challenge of annotation and generalization.
050   By comparison, standard-definition (SD) maps, like those provided by Google Maps and Baidu
051   Maps, are available at low cost and cover a wide range of areas (Jiang et al., 2024). Commonly used
052   in human driving, SD maps provide information on road direction and intersection structures, and
053   assist in route planning and driving maneuvers like turning. However, compared with HD maps,
      SD maps have two major weaknesses: (1) Low Resolution. SD maps only indicate the general

      The development of end-to-end autonomous driving architectures (Hu et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023)
082
      presents an attractive way to capture task-specific information directly from raw sensor data. These
083
      features encompass environmental information around the agent, serving a similar function to maps,
084   thereby reducing reliance on map precision. During the process, SD maps could serve as a rough
085   guide for the feature aggregation, providing an understanding of the road’s general layout, which
086   could potentially achieve performance comparable to using HD maps with detailed road information.
087
      Based on the motivation, we propose ESDMotion, an end-to-end motion prediction framework that
088
      uses SD maps without any HD map input or supervision, as in Fig 1 (c). The framework is com-
089
      patible with both anchor-based and anchor-free motion prediction models (We use DenseTNT (Gu
090   et al., 2021) and HiVT (Zhou et al., 2022) in this paper). To construct the end-to-end model for SD
091   maps, we extract BEV features from raw data with an encoder, and incorporate several modules, in-
092   cluding a BEV-agent encoder, BEV-lane encoder, and Road Observation into base motion prediction
093   models, to efficiently fuse BEV features with agent features and SD map features.
094
      We point out two problems caused by the usage of SD maps on anchor-based and anchor-free mod-
095
      els and design new modules to address them. Anchor-based models like DenseTNT select candidate
096   goal points (anchors) form maps. The low resolution and poor alignment accuracy results in poor
097   distributions of goal points, for example, there may be no goal point around agents. We solve this
098   issue by introducing an anchor generation method called Pseudo Lane Expansion, which generates
099   extra pseudo anchors parallelly based on original SD map instances to improve anchor distributions.
100   For anchor-free models such as HiVT, due to sparse and misaligned SD maps, limited or unhelp-
101   ful BEV features could be fused with SD map features in our original Road Observation module
102   which uses standard deformable attention. Thus, we modify the multi-head deformable attention by
103   adding reference points and introducing a head weighting mechanism. We propose Enhanced Road
104
      Observation to sample a wider range of BEV features around SD instances.
105   Benefit from the end-to-end architecture and modules specially designed for SD maps, our models
106   achieves superior accuracy with SD maps as the only map information, surpassing state-of-the-art
107   online HD mapping-based motion prediction models (Gu et al., 2024a;b) with a reduction of 13.4%
      (minADE) and 7.8% (minFDE) on anchor-free model. We also pay attention to the performance gap

      2.1        E2E S TRUCTURE FOR M OTION P REDICTION WITH SD MAPS

      2.1.1          OVERALL S TRUCTURES
157
158   To reduce the performance gap between SD maps and HD maps, we extend motion prediction mod-
159   els in an end-to-end manner. We use an existing encoder (Li et al., 2022b) to obtain BEV features
160   from raw sensor data. Then we propose modules including BEV-Agent encoder, BEV-Lane en-
161   coder, and Road Observation for feature fusion and we incorporate them into appropriate positions
      in anchor-free and anchor-based models.


                                                                                  3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      The anchor-free model. For anchor-free model HiVT (Zhou et al., 2022), the feature of each
163   agent is obtained from original agent-agent interaction and temporal transformer. Then, the agent
164   features are enhanced with the SD lane feature, BEV feature, and BEV-lane feature in Agent-Lane
165   interaction, BEV-Agent Encoder, and Road Observation module. After a global interaction that
166   exchanges the information among agents, BEV-agent fusion is applied again. Finally, a decoder
167   generates future trajectories from the agent features. The process is shown in Fig. 2.
168
169   The anchor-based model. For anchor-based model DenseTNT (Gu et al., 2021), the sparse goal
170   points is generated from maps first. The features of SD maps and agents are extracted by a sparse
171   context encoder, which is based on VectorNet (Gao et al., 2020) and enhanced with BEV-Agent
172   encoder and BEV-Lanes. The sparse goal is scored and selected with extracted features. Then,
173   the dense goal candidates are generated from selected sparse ones in the dense goal encoder and
174   their probability distribution is computed. Finally, a decoder predicts target points based on the
175   distribution and decodes the future trajectory. The process is shown in Fig. 3.
176
177   2.1.2   SD M AP O RIENTED E2E M ODULES
178
179   BEV encoder. We adopt BEVFormer (Li et al., 2022b) as the BEV encoder, which extracts fea-
180
      tures from multi-view images using an image backbone (e.g., ResNet50). Then, it uses BEV2PV
      look-up to construct BEV features B ∈ RH×W ×C with the intrinsic and extrinsic of each camera.
181
      Note that other BEV encoders could work as well, for example, LSS (Philion & Fidler, 2020).
182
183
184
      BEV-Agent encoder and BEV-Lane encoder. Since BEV features are spatial, it is common to
      sample BEV features by positions of agent or lane and apply feature fusion. Previous study (Gu
185
      et al., 2024b) explores the fusion of agent features with BEV features in motion prediction models.
186
      It uses an approach similar to the Vision Transformer (ViT) (Dosovitskiy, 2020) to handle offline
187   BEV features. In the method, BEV features are projected into coarse patches, with the patch cor-
188   responding to the agent’s location serving as the query, and other patches acting as keys and values
189   in an attention mechanism. The resulting features are then concatenated with the agent features.
190   However, the projection can lead to the loss of fine-grained details, and the simple concatenation
191   could limit the direct interaction between the two types of features.
192
      To this end, we propose a BEV-Agent encoder that uses deformable attention (Zhu et al., 2020) for
193   more direct and efficient feature fusion. We donate BEV feature B ∈ RH×W ×C , agent feature
194   QA ∈ RN ×D , and 2D positions of agents p ∈ RN ×2 , where N represents numbers of agents and D
195   denotes the hidden dimension. BEV-Agent encoder fuse feature through:
196
197                                FBEV-Agent = DeformAtt(QA , Tref (p), B)                             (1)
198
199   Where Tref is the translation from ego vehicle coordinate system to BEV grids, FBEV-Agent is the
200   resulting BEV-Agent feature. The BEV-Lane encoder is similar to the BEV-Agent encoder. It just
201   replaces agent features with SD lane features. These two encoders sample BEV features spatially
202   close to the agent or lane, and fuse it with the agent or lane feature, enabling the agent or lane to
203   capture information about its surroundings from raw data.
204
205   Road Observation. The Road Observation module applies integration among the BEV features,
206   SD map features, and agent features. It first uses a BEV-Lane encoder to get BEV-SD map features,
207   then fuses them into agent features via attention. These operations enrich SD map features with
208   visual information and interact them with agent features, providing agents with the visual and geo-
209   metrical context of roads and thus named Road Observation. Specifically, We denote the vectorized
210
      SD map as m ∈ RNm ×2 , with the encoded map features represented by Fmap ∈ RNm ×D , where
      Nm is the total number of points constituting the polylines. We obtain the BEV-SDmap feature via

      2.2        E NHANCE FEATURE FUSION WITH SD MAP IN ANCHOR - FREE MODEL
234
235
      Challenges in feature fusion with SD maps. In the Road Observation, we utilize deformable
236
      attention to fuse SD map features with BEV features, generating road features. The 2D coordinates
237   of the polylines in the vectorized map are selected as reference points. Standard deformable attention
238   initializes sample points around these map points, sampling BEV features near the polylines. This
239   process works well with HD maps whose polylines are accurate and dense. The sampling area of
240   HD map lines almost covers the entire road, providing information of a wide range.
241
      However, using SD maps as input introduces challenges. Due to low resolution, SD maps contain
242
      fewer polylines and reference points. The sample points are in a limited region and can not cover the
243   entire road, as shown in Fig. 2 (lower left). Even worse, the misaligned SD lines may fuse useless
244   BEV features around it. For example, it may capture features far away from the road.
245
246
247   Enhanced Road Observation. We observe that SD maps accurately indicate road direction de-
248
      spite alignment errors. Thus, we aim to expand reference points by creating parallel lines. In this

249

250

285   2.3     IMPROVE ANCHOR DISTRIBUTION FROM SD MAP IN ANCHOR - BASED MODEL
286
287
      Challenges in anchor generation with SD maps. In the anchor-based model DenseTNT, the
      challenges of using SD maps lie in anchor generation. The model densely samples points around
288
      candidate target points from a vectorized map, then predicts the probability for each, selecting the
289
      final target point based on these probabilities. As a result, the map has a direct impact on the distri-
290   bution of candidate target points. Unfortunately, due to the low resolution and alignment accuracy of
291   SD maps, there may be no candidate points near the agent or its future trajectory, which significantly
292   reduces prediction accuracy. Fig. 3 shows the matter.
293
294   Pseudo Lane Expansion. To address this issue, adding extra pseudo anchors is a straightforward
295   way. Since DenseTNT is designed around lane and goal features, we directly input the expanded
296   SD lines into the model. We denote a single polyline in the SD map containing Nd 2D points
297   p ∈ RNd ×2 , the unit normal vector of the vector from the i-th point to the (i+1)-th point ni . The i-th
298   point p̂ji of the j-th expanded line is calculated as:
299                                                pˆji = pi + dj ni                                        (6)
300   Where dj is the distance between the j-th expanded line and the original line. Because DenseTNT’s
301   original lane scoring or goal scoring modules predict weights for each polyline or target point, we no
302   longer predict weights for each parallel line as Enhanced road observation. This simple but effective
303   anchor generation method greatly improves prediction performance with SD maps. We demonstrate
304   the process as in Fig. 3 (right).
305
306   Adaptive Pseudo Lane Expansion. Because of the variability in road structures and the distri-
307   bution of SD maps, using fixed parameters, including the number and distances of the extended
308
      parallel lines in Pseudo Lane Expansion, often fails to achieve optimal performance across diverse
      scenarios. Thus, we adapt the parameters based on the distance of the SD map lane relative to the
309
      vehicle to predict, and the density of SD map lanes. (1) Distance. If the closest distance between
310
      the SD map and the target vehicle is large, it likely indicates poor alignment of the SD map. In such
311   case, we generate more pseudo lanes on the side closer to the vehicle while reducing the number
312   on the opposite side to decrease the creation of irrelevant lines, as in Fig. 5 (left). (2) Density. For
313   sparsely distributed SD lanes (only one or two SD lanes in the range), we increase the number of
314   pseudo lanes to ensure better coverage of the road. Conversely, in dense areas (e.g., intersections),
315   we decrease the number and spacing of pseudo lanes to avoid overlap and interference as in Fig. 5
316   (right). Furthermore, we design a set of pseudo lanes for special situation that there is no SD lanes
317   in the range. The detail is discussed in C.2.

354   minimum Average Displacement Error (minADE), minimum Final Displacement Error (minFDE),
355   and Miss Rate (MR). We predict multiple future trajectories and calculate the ADE and FDE for
356   the trajectory closest to the ground truth, referred to as minADE and minFDE. ADE measures the
357   average L2 distance between predicted and ground-truth trajectories, while FDE measures the L2
358
      distance between the final points of the predicted and ground-truth trajectories. Miss Rate is the
      proportion of samples where FDE exceeds a certain threshold.

396   els and the usage of end-to-end framework influence the performance gap between HD maps and
397   SD maps on our anchor-based and anchor-free model. We show the results in Table 2. For base
398   motion prediction models, the anchor-based model DenseTNT is affected more greatly by the
399   precision of the maps than the anchor-free model HiVT. Compared within ESDMotion, the mi-
400   nADE gap of maps on the anchor-based model is 0.0431(5.7%), while on the anchor-free model it
401   is just 0.0035(1.1%). Compared with HD map based online methods in Table 1, the anchor-free
402   model achieves larger improvement than the anchor-free model. We speculate that this is due to the
403
      different ways these models utilize and depend on maps. Anchor-free model HiVT are designed
      around agent features, with map features serving as auxiliary information, integrated through cross-
404
      attention. This makes the model less dependent on the map, allowing it workers even without any
405
      map input. Additionally, the robust map encoder and cross-attention mechanism can still generate
406   useful map features from lower-precision SD maps. As a result, the performance gap between HD
407   and SD maps is relatively small. In contrast, anchor-based model DenseTNT directly uses vector-
408   ized maps to extract goal points, predict their probability distribution, and select the optimal goal
409   point based on this distribution. The accuracy of the map directly affects the plausibility of goal
410   point distribution and, consequently, the accuracy of the predicted target. This makes the model
411   highly dependent on map precision, widening the performance gap between HD and SD maps.
412   To investigate how the usage of the end-to-end framework influences the gap, we conducted experi-
413   ments using different map inputs in both end-to-end (our method) and non-end-to-end architectures
414   (original HiVT and DenseTNT models). We found that the end-to-end approach not only im-
415   proved overall performance but also reduced the gap between HD and SD map performance.
416   For the anchor-free model, the gap between minADE and minFDE narrows by 73% (from 0.0130
417   to 0.0035) and 44% (from 0.0144 to 0.0081). For the anchor-based model, the gap decreases by
418   87% on minADE and 93% on minFDE. Notably, the narrowed gap does not mean that maps are
419   unnecessary, because an anchor-free model without maps has significantly poorer performance. It
420
      states that the precision of maps is less important in end-to-end architecture. This aligns with our
      hypothesis that under the end-to-end architecture, the motion prediction model directly incorporates
421
      visual scene information. It may capture features such as lane markings or road boundaries from
422
      BEV features, so the BEV feature partially compensates for the absence of high-precision maps. As
423   a result, maps primarily provide coarse priors, such as road direction and general layout, making
424   map precision less critical to the model’s performance.

527   4     C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this paper, we introduce ESDMotion, a novel end-to-end motion prediction framework that ex-
530   clusively utilizes SD maps without any HD map supervision. This framework effectively integrates
531   with both anchor-based and anchor-free motion prediction models. To address the low resolution
532
      and alignment accuracy of SD maps, we designed two modules called Enhanced Road Observation
      and Pseudo Lane Expansion. Experiments demonstrate that our model achieves performance com-
533
      parable to, or even better than online HD mapping-based models. We focused on the performance
534
      gap between HD and SD maps, analyzing how the type of motion prediction model and the adoption
535   of end-to-end architectures influence the gap.

      A.1   HD M APS AND SD M APS
682
683   HD Maps. High-definition(HD) maps contain detailed road information, but their creation re-
684   quires extensive manual annotation and ongoing maintenance, making them expensive and unscal-
685   able (Li et al., 2022a). This leads to the development of various online map estimation methods,
686   which estimate HD maps from camera or LiDAR data. Recent approaches such as MapTR(Liao
687   et al., 2023a; Li et al., 2024b) are mostly based on an encoder-decoder architecture, where BEV
688   (bird’s-eye view) features are extracted from sensor data, and a transformer decoder is used to pre-
689   dict various map elements. Moreover, MapEX (Sun et al., 2023) encodes map elements into query
690
      tokens and refines the matching algorithm. This results in better performance and robustness. Online
      map estimation enables autonomous vehicles to operate in areas without Offline HD map coverage,
691
      reducing dependency on HD maps. However, these methods still require HD map ground truth for
692
      supervised training, making it challenging to obtain sufficient HD map data for large-scale training.
693   Additionally, online HD map estimation consumes computational resources and time.

695
      SD Maps. Since obtaining HD maps is costly, easily accessible, and scalable standard-definition
696   (SD) maps garner increasing attention. Currently, many methods utilize SD maps as priors to predict
697   HD maps (Li et al., 2024a). For example, PriorDrive uses a unified vector encoder to effectively
698   encode diverse vector prior maps including SD maps to enhance the robustness and accuracy of
699   online HD map construction. (Zeng et al., 2024) Some studies in this field (Jiang et al., 2024; Zhang
700   et al., 2024) merge SD maps from Open Street Maps (OSM) (Haklay & Weber, 2008) into widely
701   used datasets like nuScenes (Caesar et al., 2019) and OpenLane-V2 (Wang et al., 2023) to make
      them more available.

      A.2   M OTION P REDICTION WITH M APS
703
704   Motion prediction with offline HD maps. Many motion prediction models use offline HD maps
705   to obtain environmental information. Early models typically use rasterized HD maps and encode
706   them with Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) (Marchetti et al., 2020; Biktairov et al., 2020;
707   Casas et al., 2020; Gilles et al., 2021). However, high-resolution rasterized maps incur significant
708   storage and computational costs. Recent approaches shift towards vectorized representations of HD
709   maps. In terms of map utilization, some methods such as LaneGCN(Liang et al., 2020), GOHOME
710   (Gilles et al., 2022) and HiVT(Zhou et al., 2022), employ Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) to encode
711
      the influence of map elements on vehicle interactions. Other methods like MTR (Shi et al., 2022) and
      QCNet(Zhou et al., 2023) directly use transformer architectures, leveraging cross-attention mecha-
712
      nisms to fuse map and vehicle features. Some target-based approaches (Zhao et al., 2021; Gu et al.,
713
      2021) generate candidate target points based on the map, leading to a stronger dependency on the
714   map. However, most methods rely on HD maps, which are costly to obtain. ViP3D(Gu et al., 2023)
715   combines detection, tracking, and prediction in an end-to-end structure, gets agent information di-
716   rectly from sensor data, but still uses offline HD maps. Currently, there is a lack of motion prediction
717   methods specifically designed for or adapted to SD maps.
718
719   Motion prediction with online HD maps. Directly inputting online HD maps into motion pre-
720   diction models is a basic method of online mapping-based motion prediction. However, the error
721   between estimated HD maps and GT HD maps leads to errant behaviors in motion prediction. To
722   address this issue, a highly rated work (Gu et al., 2024a) (CVPR24 best paper candidate) extends
723   online map estimation methods to additionally estimate uncertainty, to provide information about
724   potential errors of maps for downstream models. Another study (Gu et al., 2024b) directly uses
725   BEV features generated by online map estimation models as substitutes for vectorized HD maps. In
726   this way, decoding HD maps in upstream models and encoding in the downstream model is omitted,
727
      which results in increased speed, decreased information loss, and better performance. However,
      in this work, the upstream model and downstream model are trained separately and the latter uses
728
      stored offline BEV features obtained by the former. This makes the system non-differentiable. Op-
729
      positely, in our method, we train all modules in an end-to-end way demonstrating a performance
730   enhancement, and we only use SD maps as map input.

733   B     I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
734
735   We strictly follow the benchmark protocol in state-of-art online mapping-based motion prediction
736   methods (Gu et al., 2024a;b). For some samples without SD maps in the range, we use an all-zero
737   vector as input. We train ESDMotion on 8 RTX 4090 GPUs with the batch size of 1 on each GPU.
738   We set the learning rate to 2 × 10−4 and the number of epochs to 48, with no dropout for faster
739   convergence.
740   For the BEV encoder, we adopt the official configuration of BEVFormer-base (Li et al., 2022b).
741   The encoder takes a temporal queue of 4 samples as input and obtains BEV features with 6 encoder
742   layers. The BEV feature has a size of 200x200x256 and is in the Lidar coordinate system.
743
      For the motion prediction models, we strictly align the setting of original modules in HiVT and
744   DenseTNT with previous works (Gu et al., 2024a;b) for fair comparison. Specifically, we use a
745   4-layer temporal transformer, a 1-layer local interaction module, and a 3-layer global interaction
746   module of HiVT. We only add new modules to these two base models without removing existing
747   modules.
