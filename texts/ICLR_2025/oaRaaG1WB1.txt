                                                   A BSTRACT
013
014
               Trilevel learning (TLL) found diverse applications in numerous machine learning
015            applications, ranging from robust hyperparameter optimization to domain adapta-
016            tion. However, existing researches primarily focus on scenarios where TLL can
017            be addressed with first order information available at each level, which is inade-
018            quate in many situations involving zeroth order constraints, such as when black-
019            box models are employed. Moreover, in trilevel learning, data may be distributed
020            across various nodes, necessitating strategies to address TLL problems without
021            centralizing data on servers to uphold data privacy. To this end, an effective dis-
022            tributed trilevel zeroth order learning framework DTZO is proposed in this work to
023
               address the TLL problems with level-wise zeroth order constraints in a distributed
               manner. The proposed DTZO is versatile and can be adapted to a wide range of
024
               (grey-box) TLL problems with partial zeroth order constraints. In DTZO, the cas-
025
               caded polynomial approximation can be constructed without relying on gradients
026            or sub-gradients, leveraging a novel cut, i.e., zeroth order cut. Furthermore, we
027            theoretically carry out the non-asymptotic convergence rate analysis for the pro-
028            posed DTZO in achieving the ϵ-stationary point. Extensive experiments have been
029            conducted to demonstrate and validate the superior performance of the proposed
030            DTZO, e.g., it approximately achieves up to a 40% improvement in performance.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
034   Trilevel learning (TLL), also known as trilevel optimization, pertains to nested optimization prob-
035   lems involving three levels of optimization, thus exhibiting a trilevel hierarchical structure. Trilevel
036   learning has been widely used in many machine learning applications, such as robust hyperparame-
037   ter optimization (Sato et al., 2021), domain adaptation (Choe et al., 2023), robust neural architecture
038   search (Guo et al., 2020; Jiao et al., 2024), and so on. The general form of a trilevel learning problem
039
      can be expressed as,
                                       min f1 (x1 , x2 , x3 )

042                                         s.t. x3 = arg min f3 (x1 , x2 ′ , x3 ′ )
043                                                     x3 ′
044                                   var.        x1 , x2 , x3 ,
045   where f1 , f2 , f3 denote the first, second, and third level objectives, and x1 ∈ Rd1 , x2 ∈ Rd2 , x3 ∈
046   Rd3 are variables. Existing trilevel learning approaches focus on scenarios where TLL problems
047   can be addressed with first order information available at each level. However, situations where
048   first order information is unavailable (i.e., ∇f1 , ∇f2 , ∇f3 are non-available), such as when black-
049   box models are employed, remain under-explored. Additionally, in trilevel learning applications,
050
      data may be distributed across various nodes, necessitating strategies to address trilevel learning
      problems without centralizing data on servers in order to uphold data privacy (Jiao et al., 2024).
051
052   Complexity of Addressing TLL with Zeroth Order Constraints: The complexity involved in
053   solving problems characterized by hierarchical structures with three levels is significantly greater
      than that of bilevel learning problems (Blair, 1992; Avraamidou, 2018). It is worth mentioning that

054
      even finding a feasible solution in TLL problem is NP-hard since it necessitates addressing the inner
055   bilevel learning problem, which is NP-hard (Ben-Ayed and Blair, 1990; Sinha et al., 2017). Existing
056   approaches are not applicable for addressing TLL with zeroth order constraints, as they either rely
057   on the first order information to solve the TLL problems (Jiao et al., 2024; Sato et al., 2021) or focus
058   on single-level and bilevel zeroth order learning problems (Fang et al., 2022; Qiu et al., 2023).
059
      To this end, an effective Distributed Trilevel Zeroth Order learning (DTZO) framework is proposed
060
      in this work. Specifically, we first introduce the cascaded zeroth order polynomial approximation for
061   the trilevel learning problems, which consists of the inner layer and outer layer polynomial approx-
062   imation. Next, how to generate the novel zeroth order cuts without using gradients or sub-gradients
063   to gradually refine the cascaded polynomial approximation is discussed. Zeroth order cut is a type of
064   cutting plane that does not rely on first order information during generation. Finally, the distributed
065   zeroth order algorithm is developed to address trilevel zeroth order learning problems (i.e., TLL
066   with level-wise zeroth order constraints) in a distributed manner. Theoretically, we demonstrate that
067   the proposed zeroth order cuts can construct a polynomial relaxation for TLL problems, and this re-
068   laxation will be gradually tightened with zeroth order cuts added. Additionally, we also analyze the
069
      non-asymptotic convergence rate, i.e., iteration and communication complexities, for the proposed
      DTZO to achieve the ϵ-stationary point. The contributions of this work are summarized as follows.
070
071   1. Different from the existing works on single-level and bilevel zeroth order learning, this work
072   takes an initial step towards addressing trilevel zeroth order learning. To the best of our knowledge,
073   this is the first work to address the trilevel zeroth order learning problems.
074   2. An effective framework DTZO with novel zeroth order cuts is proposed for tackling trilevel zeroth
075   order learning problems in a distributed manner. Different from the existing methods, the proposed
076   DTZO is capable of constructing the cascaded zeroth order polynomial approximation without using
077   gradients or sub-gradients.
078
      3. Extensive experiments on black-box large language models (LLMs) trilevel learning and robust
079   hyperparameter optimization substantiate the superior performance of the proposed DTZO.

084   2.1   D ISTRIBUTED Z EROTH O RDER O PTIMIZATION
085
      Zeroth order optimization is widely-used for addressing machine learning problems where obtaining
086
      explicit gradient expressions is challenging or impractical (Liu et al., 2018c; Chen et al., 2019; Wang
087   et al., 2018b; Chen et al., 2017; Héliou et al., 2021; Cai et al., 2021; Gao and Huang, 2020; Yue et al.,
088   2023; Li et al., 2022; Ren et al., 2023; Nikolakakis et al., 2022; Tu et al., 2019; Rando et al., 2024).
089   In practical applications of zeroth order optimization, data may be distributed across different nodes.
090   To address zeroth order optimization problems in a distributed manner, the distributed zeroth order
091   optimization methods have recently garnered significant attention, e.g., Lian et al. (2016); Tang
092   et al. (2020); Fang et al. (2022); Chen et al. (2024a); Akhavan et al. (2021); Sahu et al. (2018); Shu
093   et al. (2023). Furthermore, to tackle the bilevel zeroth order optimization problems in a distributed
094   manner, the federated bilevel zeroth order optimization method FedRZObl (Qiu et al., 2023) has
095
      been proposed. However, how to address the higher-nested zeroth order optimization problems,
      e.g., trilevel, in a distributed manner remains under-explored. To the best of our knowledge, this is
096
      the first work that considers how to address the trilevel zeroth order optimization problems.

      2.2   T RILEVEL L EARNING
100
      Trilevel learning has found applications in various fields within machine learning. A robust neural
101
      architecture search (NAS) approach that integrates adversarial learning with NAS is introduced in
102   Guo et al. (2020). The robust NAS can be viewed as a trilevel learning problem, as discussed in
103   Jiao et al. (2024). A trilevel learning problem comprising two levels pretraining, fine-tuning and
104   hyperparameter optimization, is explored in Raghu et al. (2021). In Garg et al. (2022), the trilevel
105   learning problem involves data reweight, architecture search, and model training is investigated. In
106   Sato et al. (2021), the robust hyperparameter optimization is framed as a trilevel learning problem,
107   and a hypergradient-based method is proposed to address such problems. In Choe et al. (2023), a
      general automatic differentiation technique is proposed, which can be applied to trilevel learning

108
      problems. Additionally, a cutting plane based distributed algorithm is proposed in Jiao et al. (2024)
109   for trilevel learning problems. Nevertheless, existing methods predominantly rely on first order
110   information to solve trilevel learning problems. This is the first framework that can be used to
111   solve trilevel learning problems without relying on first order information.
112
113   2.3   C UTTING P LANE M ETHOD
114   Cutting plane methods are widely used in convex optimization (Bertsekas, 2015; Franc et al., 2011),
115   robust optimization (Yang et al., 2014; Bürger et al., 2013), and so on. Recently, there has been no-
116   table interest in leveraging cutting plane methods to tackle distributed nested optimization problems.
117   It is shown in Jiao et al. (2023) that the nested optimization problem can be transformed into a de-
118   composable optimization problem by utilizing cutting plane method, which significantly facilitates
119   the design of distributed algorithms for nested optimization. In Jiao et al. (2023), the cutting plane
120   method is employed to tackle bilevel optimization problems in a distributed manner. Similarly, Chen
121   et al. (2024c) utilizes the cutting plane method to address distributed bilevel optimization problems
122
      within downlink multi-cell systems. Furthermore, Jiao et al. (2024) applies the cutting plane method
      to solve distributed trilevel optimization problems. However, the existing cutting plane methods for
123
      nested optimization rely on the gradients or the sub-gradients to generate cutting planes, which is not
124
      available in zeroth order optimization. In this work, the proposed framework is capable of generating
125   zeroth order cuts for nested optimization problems without using gradients or sub-gradients.

532   6     C ONCLUSION
533
      In this work, a distributed trilevel zeroth order learning (DTZO) framework is proposed to address
534   the trilevel learning problems in a distributed manner without using first order information. To our
535   best knowledge, this is the first work that considers how to tackle the trilevel zeroth order learning
536   problems. The proposed DTZO is capable of constructing the cascaded polynomial approximation
537   for trilevel zeroth order learning problems without using gradients or sub-gradients by utilizing the
538   novel zeroth order cuts. Additionally, we theoretically analyze the non-asymptotic convergence rate
539   for the proposed DTZO to achieve the ϵ-stationary point. Experiments on black-box LLMs trilevel
      learning and robust hyperparameter optimization demonstrate the superior performance of DTZO.

923

924
      Appendix E, and the discussions about ϕin and ϕout are also conducted in this part. In Appendix F,
925   details of the experimental setting and additional experimental results are provided. The discussions
926   about Assumptions 1 and 2 are offered in Appendix G, we show that both Assumptions 1 and 2 are
927   mild and widely-used in machine learning. In Appendix H, the reasons why we choose the exterior
928   penalty method in the proposed framework are discussed, and we demonstrate the close relationship
929   between the original constrained optimization problem and the unconstrained optimization problem.
930   In Appendix I, we show that the proposed framework can be applied to a wide range of TLL prob-

1767   Soft constraint. A soft constraint refers to a constraint that can be partially violated without render-
1768   ing the optimization problem meaningless (Kautz et al., 1996; Régin, 2011; Wilson et al., 2022). It is
1769   shown in many bilevel and trilevel learning works that the lower-optimization problem often serves
1770   as a soft constraint to the upper-level optimization problem. Examples are provided as follows.
1771
1772            * In bilevel neural architecture search (Liu et al., 2018a), rather than computing the optimal
1773              solution for the lower-level optimization problem, the result obtained after a single gradient
1774              descent step can be used as an approximation of the optimal solution.
1775
                * In bilevel meta-learning (Ji et al., 2021; Finn et al., 2017), instead of solving the lower-level
1776
                  optimization problem to optimality, the results obtained after multiple gradient descent
1777
                  steps can serve as an approximation.
1778
1779            * In bilevel adversarial learning (Madry et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2022), which is a min-max
1780              optimization problem, instead of solving the maximization problem to obtain the optimal
1781              solution, the results after several projected gradient descent steps are used as the approxi-
                  mation.

1852
1853   In this section, we provide the details of the experimental setting. In the experiment, all the models
1854   are implemented using PyTorch, and the experiments are conducted on a server equipped with two
1855   NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPUs.
1856   In the experiment, we compare the proposed method with the state-of-the-art distributed zeroth order
1857   learning method FedZOO (Fang et al., 2022) and state-of-the-art distributed bilevel zeroth order
1858   learning method FedRZObl (Qiu et al., 2023), which are introduced as follows. FedZOO (Fang
1859   et al., 2022) is a derivative-free federated zeroth-order optimization method, which can be applied
1860   to solve the single-level optimization problems in a distributed manner. In FedZOO, clients perform
1861   several local updates based on gradient estimators in each communication round. After receiving
1862
       local updates, the servers will perform the aggregation and update the global parameters. FedRZObl
       (Qiu et al., 2023) is designed for zeroth order bilevel optimization problems. In each communication
1863
       round, FedRZObl involves the following steps: clients first compute the estimated optimal solution
1864
       to the lower-level optimization problem and the inexact implicit zeroth-order gradient. They then
1865   update the local parameters and transmit them to the server. Upon receiving the updates, the server
1866   aggregates them to obtain the global parameters.

       J.1    C UTTING P LANE M ETHOD
2292
       Cutting plane method, also called polyhedral approximation (Bertsekas, 2015), is widely used in
2293
       convex optimization (Franc et al., 2011; Boyd and Vandenberghe, 2007) and distributed optimiza-
2294   tion (Bürger et al., 2013; Yang et al., 2014). The rationale behind cutting plane method is to use
2295   the intersection of a finite number of half-spaces (e.g., P = {x|aTl x ≤ bl , l = 1, · · · , L}, where
2296   {x|aTl x ≤ bl } represent a half-space (Boyd and Vandenberghe, 2004)) to approximate the feasible
2297   region of the original optimization problem (e.g., x ∈ X ) . The approximation can be gradually re-
2298   fined by generating additional half-spaces (Bertsekas, 2015). Recently, cutting plane methods have
2299   proven effective in tackling distributed multilevel optimization problems. By leveraging these meth-
2300   ods, such problems can be transformed into decomposable optimization problems, which greatly
2301   simplifies the design of distributed algorithms for nested optimization, as discussed in (Jiao et al.,
2302   2023; 2024). In (Jiao et al., 2023), cutting plane methods are applied to solve bilevel optimization
2303   problems within a distributed framework. Likewise, (Chen et al., 2024c) utilize the cutting plane
2304
       method to tackle distributed bilevel optimization challenges in downlink multi-cell systems. Build-
       ing on this, (Jiao et al., 2024) further extend the approach to address distributed trilevel optimization
2305
       problems. However, existing cutting plane methods for multilevel optimization rely on the first-order
2306
       information to generate cutting planes, which are not available in zeroth-order optimization. In this
2307   work, we propose a framework capable of generating zeroth-order cuts for multilevel optimization
2308   problems without the use of first-order information.

2346   This study is the first work that considers how to address the trilevel zeroth order optimization

2348   zeroth order learning problems but can also be applied to a broad class of TLL problems, e.g., TLL
