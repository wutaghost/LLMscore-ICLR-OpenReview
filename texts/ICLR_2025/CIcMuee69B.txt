009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               We define a congruence that copes with null next-symbol probabilities that arise
012            when the output of a language model is constrained by some means during text
013            generation. We develop an algorithm for efficiently learning the quotient with
014            respect to this congruence and evaluate it on case studies for analyzing statistical
015            properties of LLM.
016
017
018   1   I NTRODUCTION
019
020
      Many works have studied neural language models, such as Recurrent Neural Networks (RNN) and
021   Transformers, through the analysis of surrogate automata of different sorts obtained from the former
022   in a variety of ways, with the purpose of verifying or explaining their behavior Wang et al. (2018);
023   Weiss et al. (2018); Khmelnitsky et al. (2021); Mayr et al. (2023); Muškardin et al. (2023).
024
      Recently, several papers proposed to analyze neural sequence-processing models by composing
025
      them with automata or regular expressions in order to verify properties on-the-fly while learn-
026
      ing Mayr et al. (2021), assess the existence of memorization, bias, or toxicity Kuchnik et al. (2023),
027   and guide text generation Willard & Louf (2023). However, they have not been applied to language
028   models, but language recognizers, this is the case of Mayr et al. (2021), or they lack formalization.
029
030
      An important problem that arises when synchronizing a neural language model with a guiding au-
      tomaton or constraining text generation with common sampling strategies, such as top-k, is the
031
      occurrence of symbols with null probabilities. A consequence of this, for instance, is that generation
032
      may not terminate. Moreover, this implies the model does not define a probability distribution over
033   finite strings.
034
035   The contribution of the paper is threefold: 1) the definition of a Myhill-Nerode-like congruence over
036
      strings which takes into account the occurrence of zero-probabilities, that provides an underlying
      formal basis for learning of probabilistic deterministic finite automata (PDFA) Vidal et al. (2005)
037
      from neural language models constrained by automata and sampling strategies; 2) the development
038
      of the Omit-Zero algorithm for learning the quotient with respect to this congruence, which shows to
039   be more efficient than other algorithms for the experiments carried out; 3) a framework for analyzing
040   statistical properties of LLM based on the previous two.
041
042
      In Sec. 2, we address the question of dealing with null next-symbol probabilities that appear when
      constraining the output of a language model by composing it with an automaton and/or a sampling
043
      strategy, such as the top k most likely symbols. We do this by defining an appropriate congruence
044
      that induces a quotient PDFA without zero-probability transitions. In Sec. 3, we adapt the learn-
045   ing algorithm of Mayr et al. (2023) to efficiently learn the quotient PDFA. In Sec. 4, we discuss
046   issues that arise when analyzing real large language models, in particular the role of tokenizers, and
047   apply the algorithm on problems discussed in Kuchnik et al. (2023); Willard & Louf (2023) when
048   generating text with GPT2. Experimental results show the interest of our approach.

143   to approximating the behavior of language models with probabilistic automata in order to group in
144   a single state strings which continuations slightly differ in probability. For instance, in Weiss et al.

150   infinitely many distributions, while keeping the error of the approximation as small as desired or

      5    C ONCLUSIONS
514
515   This work was motivated by the need of understanding LLM when their operation is controlled by
516   external artifacts, such as grammars, to generate text following a specific format. An important
517   question that arise in this context is how to deal with 0-probabilities that appear when restricting
518   their output. To start up with, we revised the congruence (2) in order to make constructing the
519   quotient less dependent on P by expressing it in terms of the output of the language model. The
520   first consequence of this operational view is to allow a generalization of the congruence capable
521   of dealing with equivalences on distributions. Besides, it led to developing a variant of the QNT
522   active-learning algorithm to efficiently learn PDFA by avoiding to check for 0-probability transitions
523
      as much as possible. This is essential to make it computationally feasible by reducing the number
      of queries to the LLM.
524
525   The experimental results support the viability of our approach for analyzing and validating statistical
526   properties of LLM, such as bias in text generation. Besides, they provided evidence that distributions
527   resulting from generation of a guided LLM could be well approximated by a learnt PDFA. This
528   opens the door to make these analyses less dependent on sampling by studying properties of the
529
      PDFA.

667   We will now prove by induction on the length |w| that L(uw) = L(vw) whenever 1L (uw) =
668   1L (vw) = 1. We already proved the claim for |w| = 0, so suppose it holds true for length ≤ n. Let
669   w be such that |w| = n + 1 and let σ ∈ Σ be such that 1L (uwσ) = 1L (vwσ) = 1. Since all terms
670   involving the products in (15) are positive, and by induction hypothesis L(u w<i ) = L(v w<i ) for
671
      all i = 1, . . . , n, all these terms cancel out leaving the equality L(uw)(σ) = L(vw)(σ). Since
      σ ∈ Σ is arbitrary and L(uw) and L(vw) are probability distributions, we see again that they must
672
      be equal.
673
674   Assume now 1L (u) = 1L (v) and ∀w ∈ Σ∗ . 1L (uw) = 1L (vw) = 1 =⇒ L(uw) = L(vw).
675   If 1L (u) = 1L (v) = 0, then the quotients in (2) are undefined and equality holds trivially for all
676   w ∈ Σ∗ .
677
678
      Let us suppose then that 1L (u) = 1L (v) = 1. We first prove that 1L (uw) = 1L (vw) for all
      w ∈ Σ∗ . In fact, if on the contrary there exists w ∈ Σ∗ so that 1L (uw) ̸= 1L (vw), then there exists
679
      w′ ∈ pref(w) with 1L (uw′ ) = 1L (vw′ ) = 1 but L(uw′ ) ̸= L(vw′ ) because they have different
680
      support. This contradicts our assumption.
681
682   Let w ∈ Σ∗ be so that 1L (uw) = 1L (vw) = 1. Then for all prefix w<i we also have 1L (uw<i ) =
683   1L (vw<i ) = 1, and therefore L(uw<i ) = L(vw<i ). In particular, all the terms in (15) are equal
684
      and therefore (2) holds.
685   This completes the proof that u ≡ v.
