009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) have been studied in text generation
012            to tackle the exposure bias problem. Despite their remarkable development, they
013            adopt autoregressive structures so suffering from high latency in both training and
014            inference stages. Although GANs have potential to support efficient generation by
015            adopting non-autoregressive (NAR) structures, their explorations in NAR models
016
               are extremely limited. In this work, we conduct pioneering study of building lan-
               guage GANs based on NAR structures. We identify two issues that constrain the
017
               performance of GAN-based NAR models. Firstly, existing methods of incorporat-
018
               ing latent variables provide highly similar representations which cannot describe
019            the diversity of different words in sentences. We tackle this problem by proposing
020            Position-Aware Self-Modulation, providing more diverse and effective representa-
021            tions. Secondly, the attention mechanism in Transformer cannot accurately build
022            word dependencies in the unstable training of GANs, and we adopt Dependency
023            Feed Forward Network to enhance the model capacity in dependency modeling.
024            Armed with these two facilities, we propose a GAN-based NAR model, Adver-
025            sarial Non-autoregressive Transformer (ANT). The experimental results demon-
026            strate that ANT can achieve comparable performance with mainstream models in
027
               a single forward pass and has great potential in various applications like latent
               interpolation and semi-supervised learning.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) (Goodfellow et al., 2014) denote a powerful family of
033   generative models. Unlike diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Saharia et al., 2022) and autoregres-
034   sive (AR) models (Zhang et al., 2021; Yu et al., 2022) requiring multiple inference steps, GANs can
035   produce high quality samples in a single forward pass, and thus have much lower latency (Sauer
036   et al., 2023; Kang et al., 2023). GANs were first applied to text generation to tackle the notorious
037   exposure bias problem (Bengio et al., 2015), which arises from the discrepancy between training and
038   inference processes in Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE)-based AR models. These language
039
      GANs (Yu et al., 2017; de Masson d’Autume et al., 2019; Ren & Li, 2023) keep the AR structures
      and tackle the exposure bias problem by using previously generated words as input in both training
040
      and inference stage. When providing consistent training and test processes, they do not support par-
041
      allel computation and have high latency. The high efficiency nature of image GANs are completely
042   lost in existing language GANs. In theory, the global optimality of GANs is achieved if and only if
043   the learned distributions are exactly same with the real distributions (Goodfellow et al., 2014). More
044   importantly, their convergence does not rely on specific structures. GANs can theoretically obtain
045   high quality samples in a single forward pass. Even so, their explorations in non-autoregressive
046   (NAR) text generation are extremely limited.
047
      In this paper, we unlock the power of GANs in building NAR text generative models. Instead of
048   using AR structures like existing language GANs (Yu et al., 2017; Lin et al., 2017; Che et al., 2017;
049   de Masson d’Autume et al., 2019; Ren & Li, 2023), our model adopts NAR structures supporting
050   high efficiency parallel computation. When benefiting from the generation efficiency, we observe
051   clear gaps between the performance of existing AR language GANs and our NAR language GANs.
052   The main obstacles come from two problems. Firstly, GANs rely on latent variables (which are
053   always from a pre-defined distribution) to support sampling, while existing methods of incorporat-
      ing latent variables provide highly similar representations. These representations cannot describe

      the diversity between words and thus leading to inaccurate sentence generation. Secondly, Trans-
055   former (Vaswani et al., 2017), widely used in NAR models (Gu et al., 2018; Ghazvininejad et al.,
056   2019), establishes word dependencies solely through the attention mechanism. However, the dy-
057   namic weight assignment process becomes unstable during the fragile training of GANs, causing
058   the loss of word dependencies and ultimately resulting in ungrammatical outputs.
059
      Regarding the first problem, we propose Position-Aware Self-Modulation which can provide di-
060
      verse hidden representations for the model to obtain various words in sentences. For the second
061   problem, we replace the original Feed-forward Network (FFN) module in Transformer to be our
062   proposed Dependency Feed-forward Network (Dependency FFN). Different with the attention
063   mechanism whose dependency is easily lost with poor weight assignment, Dependency FFN pro-
064   vides more stable methods for dependency modeling. Armed with these two facilities, we propose
065   an Adversarial Non-autoregressive Transformer (ANT). The contributions of this work are sum-
066   marized as follows:
067
            • We conduct pioneering work of building language GANs in NAR structures. To support
068
              the generation of various words in sentences, we propose Position-Aware Self-Modulation
069
              which can obtain diverse representations to describe the diversity of different words and
070           improve generation quality. Furthermore, Dependency FFN is proposed to support more
071           stable dependency modeling. Different with the attention mechanism, which is easily in-
072           fluenced by the unstable training process of GANs, Dependency FFN can help the model
073           build more accurate dependencies and thus obtain more grammatical results.
074         • Utilizing these two facilities, we propose a GAN-based NAR text generative model—ANT.
075           Existing language GANs employ AR structures, which leads to high latency due to their
076           reliance on previously generated words. ANT, however, generates all words in parallel
077           and support high efficiency generation. It inherits the advantages of GANs, enabling the
078           generation of high-quality samples in a single forward pass.
079         • The experimental results demonstrate that ANT achieves performance comparable to ex-
080           isting models, but with significantly lower latency, in both unconditional and conditional
081           generation tasks. Besides, we also explore the potential of ANT in applications like latent
082           interpolation and semi-supervised learning. To the best of our knowledge, it is the first
083           work demonstrating the effectiveness of GANs in building NAR text generative models.

085   2   BACKGROUND
086
087   GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2014) are initially transferred to text generation to tackle the notorious
088   exposure bias problem (Bengio et al., 2015). More specifically, existing text generative models
089   adopt autoregressive structures as backbones and use Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE) as
090   training objectives. These methods use ground truth as input during training, but reads previously
091   generated words during inference. When the model makes mistakes in generation, these mistakes
092
      will be fed into the model as input and the model will be in the state space it has never met during
      training (Bengio et al., 2015). These mistakes will thus be quickly amplified, leading to a sharp
093
      decrease in the quality of the generated samples.
094
095   The training of GANs does not need ground truth as input, so they can use generated tokens in
096   both training and inference stage. It tackles the exposure bias problem by providing a consistent
097   generation manner in training and test procedures. In text generation, the generator often models
098
      the output word probabilities and sample specific words from these probabilities. This sampling
      operation, however, is non-differentiable and stops the gradients from being passed through to the
099
      generator.
100
101   Early study tackles this problem by using either R EINFORCE (Yu et al., 2017; Lin et al., 2017; Che
102   et al., 2017; Guo et al., 2018; de Masson d’Autume et al., 2019) or continuous relaxations (Nie et al.,
103   2019). However, R EINFORCE Williams (1992) is in high variance, while continuous relaxations like
104
      Gumbel-softmax Jang et al. (2017) are biased estimators. Models based on these two methods rely
      on pre-training techniques to obtain acceptable performance (Ren & Li, 2023).
105
106   Another method is to transform words into representations, and train the generator to obtain these
107   representations. This method avoid the non-differentiable sampling operation during training, so
      the gradients can be passed to the generator directly. Different with R EINFORCE and continuous

131   they are denoted as representation modeling methods (Ren & Li, 2023). This method can obtain
132   satisfied performance without using any pre-training techniques. We thus build our model based on

134
      NAGAN (Huang et al., 2021), which also adopts GANs to build NAR models, is the most similar
135
      work to ours, yet the performance of their model is significantly limited by the biased straight-

139
      Furthermore, existing MLE-based NAR models (Gu et al., 2018; Ghazvininejad et al., 2019) suffer
140
      from the multi-modality problem Gu et al. (2018), which tends to mix words in different candidates
141
      and obtain ungrammatical results. Huang et al. (2022a) reveal that the KL divergence between their
142   learned distributions and the real distributions remains non-negative lower bounds. In theory, the
143   learned distribution cannot be exactly same with the real distributions unless words in sentences are
144   independent to each other (which does not match the real situation). Thus, existing NAR models
145   are mainly developed in several specific tasks, while their explorations in more general tasks (like
146   unconditional generation) are extremely limited. This further underscores the pressing necessity of
147   investigating more promising approaches for building NAR models, thereby enabling their applica-
148   tion across diverse domains.

      3.1   M ODEL S TRUCTURE
154
      In this paper, we propose an Adversarial Non-autoregressive Transformer (ANT) which generates
155
      text in a fully NAR manner. ANT is based on the representation modeling framework (Ren & Li,
156   2023). As shown in Figure 1, there are three parts in ANT: Aligner, Discriminator and Generator.
157   The aligner maps words into representations, and the generator tries to recover these representa-
158   tions. The discriminator needs to identify whether input representations are from the aligner or the
159   generator. We adopt Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017) as the backbones of all the three parts to
160   support highly parallel computation. An input is firstly added with a positional encoding and fed
161   into encoder layers. Each encoder layer has a multi-head attention (MHA) module and feed forward
      network (FFN) module. A layer normalization is added after each module.

188   A non-autoregressive generator cannot input previously generated words, so trainable representa-
189   tions are adopted as input. The generator then gives output representations ri in different positions
190
      and uses the same linear transformation layer FLT in the aligner to transform these representations

191
      erator (µxi and ri ) as input. Different from image GANs whose discriminators give a single scaler
192
      output for an image, our discriminator gives output for each representation. During training, the
193   aligner will be trained first, and its parameters are fixed during the training of the discriminator and
194   the generator. The representations given by the generator need not be transformed into words in
195   training process, so the gradients from the discriminator can directly pass through to the generator.
196
      Causal masks are adopted in both the discriminator and the generator to break the possible symmetry
197
      in the input. We use Wasserstein distance (Arjovsky et al., 2017) as the training objective and adopt
198
      Lipschitz penalty (Petzka et al., 2018) to regularize the discriminator. However, there is still a gap
199   between our basic model and existing autoregressive models and we further propose Position-Aware
200   Self-Modulation and Dependency Feed Forward Network (Dependency FFN) to improve model
201   performance.

204   3.2   P OSITION -AWARE S ELF -M ODULATION
205
206   An effective sampling method plays a key role in the success of GANs. Transformer based im-
207   age GANs (Lee et al., 2021) generate different samples by adopting self-modulation (Chen et al.,
208   2019) to incorporate latent variables. Self-modulation assigns the same shift and scale factors to the
209   normalized results in different positions, which leads the representations in various positions to be
210   highly similar even with positional encodings (as shown in Figure 2 (a)). However, the output of
211   the generator (i.e., word representations in different positions) are of high diversities. Similar input
212
      representations cannot describe the diversity among different words and thus leading to inaccurate
      sentence generation.
213
214   To tackle this problem, we propose Position-Aware Self-Modulation. As shown in Figure 3 (a),
215   this method adopts different mapping layers for the calculations in different positions so as to gain
      diverse results. In practice, a parallel implementation is adopted to improve the computation effi-

      3.3   D EPENDENCY F EED F ORWARD N ETWORK
247
      Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017) builds word dependencies by dynamically assigning weights in
248
      the attention mechanism. This process, however, is unstable under the training of GANs. It will lead
249
      the models to lose word dependencies, and finally result in ungrammatical sentences. We tackle
250   this problem by proposing Dependency Feed Forward Network (Dependency FFN) to strengthen
251   the FFN module with the capacity of dependency modeling. The structure of Dependency FFN is
252   shown in Figure 3 (b), and calculated as follows:
253
254                                         st = σ(xt Ws + bs )
                                                                                                            (3)
255                                         ot = st−1 Wa + st Wb + bo
256
257   where σ(·) is an activation function which is GELU in this work. With causal masks, st−1 and st
258   contain the information of first (t−1) and t words, respectively. Using the sum of these two variables
259   can help the model to explicitly build stable dependencies between the t-th word and previous (t−1)
260   words in the fragile training process of GANs.

      3.4   E XTENSION TO C ONDITIONAL G ENERATION
263
264   Besides unconditional generation, conditional generation is frequently employed in a variety of
265   tasks. We thus also extend ANT to conditional generation. Given a condition representation c, the
266   generator can consider it by shifting the original latent variable z. We find that using trainable factors
267   to assign weights to z and c can slightly improve the performance: ẑ = α1 ◦ z + α2 ◦ c, where α1
268   and α2 are two trainable variables. For the discriminator, we use the sum of word representations
269   xdt and conditional representations c as the input: x̂dt = xdt + c. Then, x̂dt is fed into the remaining
      modules of the discriminator.

291   4.1     E XPERIMENT S ETUP
292
293   The experiment covers both unconditional generation and conditional generation to evaluate model
294   performance comprehensively. For the unconditional generation, the task is to generate sentences
295   whose distribution can be as close as to the target sets. We follow the settings of previous
296   work (de Masson d’Autume et al., 2019; Ren & Li, 2023) and use sentences from two datasets:
297   the COCO Image Caption Dataset (Lin et al., 2014)1 and the EMNLP 2017 News Dataset2 . The
298
      size of training sets of the COCO dataset and the EMNLP dataset are set to be 50,000 and 200,000,
      respectively. The COCO dataset can support evaluations in short sentence generation, while the
299
      EMNLP dataset focuses on long sentence generation. For the conditional generation, we randomly
300
      select 100,000 sentences from the Yelp Dataset3 as training data and use emotion labels (positive or
301   negative) as conditions.
302
303
      4.2     E VALUATION M ETRICS
304
305   The evaluation is conducted at both embedding level and token level. In embedding level, we use
306   Universal Sentence Encoder4 (Cer et al., 2018) to transform sentences into embeddings. Then, we
307   calculate both Fréchet Embedding Distance (FED) (de Masson d’Autume et al., 2019) and Least
308   Coverage Rate (LCR) (Ren & Li, 2023) to evaluate the overall similarity and the fine-grained
309   similarity of two distributions, respectively.
310   In token level, we use Inverse-BLEU (I. BLEU) to evaluate model performance in terms of quality
311   and diversity together. Besides, we also draw a curve of BLEU (Papineni et al., 2002) and Self-
312   BLEU (Zhu et al., 2018) by tuning the temperature of the model (Caccia et al., 2020). In the case
313   of conditional generation, Accuracy (Acc.) is also employed to assess whether the models produce
314   sentences that align with the input labels.

481   5    C ONCLUSION
482
483   In this work, we firstly reveal the limitations in existing language GANs based on AR structures. The
484   global optimality of GANs do not rely on specific structures and NAR structures can obtain similar
485   performance with much lower latency. Then, we reveal two problems in building GAN-based NAR
      models: 1) Existing methods of integrating latent variables obtain similar representations which


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      cannot describe the diversity of different words. 2) The attention mechanism in Transformer cannot
487   build word dependencies stably. We tackle these two problems by proposing Position-Aware Self-
488   Modulation and Dependency Feed Forward Network, respectively. Armed with these two facilities,
489   we propose an Adversarial Non-autoregressive Transformer (ANT), a GAN-based NAR model. The
490   experimental results demonstrate that ANT obtains comparable performance as other AR models
491   but with much lower decoding latency. Besides, we also demonstrate the great potential of ANT in
492   various tasks like smooth latent interpolation and semi-supervised learning.

      A.2   I MPLEMENTATION OF NAT AND CMLM
649
650   Both NAT (Gu et al., 2018) and CMLM (Ghazvininejad et al., 2019) are designed for machine
651   translation, so their original structures contains an encoder to encode input in source language.
652   However, there may be no meaningful input in our experiment (e.g., unconditional generation), so
653   this structure cannot be used in the experiment directly. We thus use the idea of VAE to obtain
654   hidden representations, so they can be transferred to the tasks in our experiments.
655   More specifically, a Transformer-based encoder is adopted to encode the sentences into hidden rep-
656   resentations during training. Then, these representations are fed into the decoder to reconstruct the
657   input sentences. During inference, representations sampled from the standard normal distribution
658   will be fed into the decoder, and the decoder will generate sentences based on the sampled represen-
659   tations. For NAT, the representations are fed into decoder as input. For CMLM, the representations
660   are concatenated with the embeddings of input tokens (masked or unmasked words). The iteration
661   number of CMLM is set to be 10 as in previous work (Ghazvininejad et al., 2019; Huang et al.,
662
      2022b).

665
      Fréchet Embedding Distance (FED) (de Masson d’Autume et al., 2019) is same with the Fréchet
666   Inception Distance (FID) (Heusel et al., 2017) except for the encoding model. The encoding model is
667   changed to be fit into text generation. We adopts Universal Sentence Encoder following the settings
668   of the previous work (de Masson d’Autume et al., 2019). It is calculated as follows:
669
670                           F ED = ||µ1 − µ2 ||22 + T r(c1 + c2 − 2(c1 c2 )1/2 )                      (4)
671   where µ1 and µ2 are the mean, and c1 and c2 are the covariance.
672
673   Least Coverage Rate (LCR) (Ren & Li, 2023) is proposed to be a compliment when the FED of two
674
      models are too close to each other, since LCR is more sensitive to the change of data quality (Ren
      & Li, 2023). Given two sets of sentence Xia ∈ Xa and Xib ∈ Xb , LCR is calculated as follows:
675
676                                  Sij = Sim(Emb(Xia ), Emb(Xjb ))

                                                  |Xa |    |Xb |
678                                          1 X X
                                     Ra =               δ(       Sij ≥ τ )
679                                         |Xa | i=1 j=1

681                                        1 X X

                                          |Xb | j=1 i=1
