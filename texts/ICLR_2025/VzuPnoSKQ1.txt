009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Preference-based Reinforcement Learning (PbRL), which optimizes reward func-
012            tions using preference feedback, is a promising approach for environments where
013            handcrafted reward modeling is challenging. Especially in sparse-reward envi-
014            ronments, feedback-based reward modeling achieves notable performance gains
015            by transforming sparse feedback signals into dense ones. However, most PbRL
016
               research has primarily focused on single-agent environments, with limited attention
               to multi-agent environments. In this paper, we propose Dual Preferences-based
017
               Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning (DPM), which extends PbRL to multi-agent
018
               tasks by introducing dual preferences comparing not only whole trajectories but
019            also individual agent contributions during transitions. Furthermore, DPM replaces
020            human preferences with those generated by LLMs to train the reward functions. Ex-
021            perimental results in the StarCraft Multi-Agent Challenge (SMAC) and SMACv2
022            environments demonstrate significant performance improvements over baselines,
023            indicating the efficacy of DPM in optimizing individual reward functions and
024            enhancing performances in sparse reward settings.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Cooperative multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) has demonstrated strong performance
029
      across various domains (Du & Ding, 2021; Oroojlooy & Hajinezhad, 2023). However, reinforcement
030   learning-based decision-making methodologies have limited performance in environments where
031   the reward signals are rarely given hence learning the optimal policy is challenging. The situation
032   worsens in multi-agent scenarios since exploration is much harder due to larger state and action space,
033   and each agent’s behavior can be assessed only with a shared reward signal.
034
      Preference-based Reinforcement Learning (PbRL) is one notable approach to addressing sparse
035
      reward challenges. By training a reward model based on human preferences, PbRL can transform a
036   sparse reward environment into a dense reward environment, thereby allowing for the facile resolution
037   of issues arising from sparse rewards. Recent works have demonstrated that PbRL effectively solves
038   single-agent reinforcement learning tasks in sparse reward setting or even without rewards from the
039   environments, proving PbRL to be an effective alternative (Lee et al., 2021a; Kim et al., 2023).
040
      However, a challenge in applying PbRL to MARL arises from the limitation in optimizing the
041
      reward function and its application in MARL has been explored in only a few studies (Zhu et al.,
042   2024). Common methods that rely on a single preference type comparing trajectory pairs struggle to
043   accurately assess the contributions of individual agents, making it difficult to optimize the reward
044   functions. In particular, trajectory preferences pose the challenging temporal credit assignment
045   problem, where it is difficult to identify states or actions within a trajectory that influence the reward
046   (Wirth et al., 2017). This issue is further exacerbated by the expansive space in MARL. For example,
047   as depicted in Figure 1 (Left), even within a single trajectory, cooperative and non-cooperative
048   behaviors are mixed, so identifying non-cooperative actions through trajectory comparison alone
049   becomes challenging.
050   In this paper, we present the Dual Preferences-based Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning (DPM), a
051   model that utilizes Preference-based Reinforcement Learning (PbRL) to tackle the sparse reward issue
052   in Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning (MARL). DPM provides agent-specific reward information
053   by facilitating comparisons not only between trajectories but also across agents. By integrating
      preferences that assess the contributions of individual agents, DPM enhances the optimization of

      2.1   A C OOPERATIVE M ULTI - AGENT R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING
090
091
      A cooperative MARL task can be formulated as a Dec-POMDP (Oliehoek et al., 2016) which consists
092   of a tuple ⟨S, A, P, R, O, Ω, n, γ⟩. s ∈ S is the global environment state. At each time step, each
093   agent i ∈ N ≡ {1, ..., n} obtains an observation oi ∈ O with the observation function Ω(s, i) :
094   S × N → O, and selects an action ai ∈ A which forms a joint action a = {a1 , · · · , an } ∈ An . Then
095   the environment follows the transition function P (s′ |s, a) : S × An × S → [0, 1] and all the agents
096   share the same reward function r(s, a) : S × AnP   → R. The objective is to learn a joint policy π to
                                                          ∞
097   maximize the expected return Est+1:∞ ,at+1:∞ ∼π [ k=0 γ k rt+k |st , at ] with γ ∈ [0, 1).

      2.2   S PARSE R EWARD P ROBLEM AND S OLUTIONS IN R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING
100
101
      In sparse-reward setting, non-zero rewards r(s, a) are rarely given (e.g., when the given task is
102   completed). To address the sparse-reward challenge, various approaches have been proposed. A
103   common solution is reward shaping in which subgoal-based methods adopt a hierarchical architecture
104   to decompose the given task to smaller sub-tasks (Tang et al., 2018; Jeon et al., 2022). Influence-based
105   methods quantify the influences caused by each agent’s action to choose optimal actions (Jaques et al.,
106   2019; Li et al., 2022). DPM instead utilizes preference data to address sparsity. Intrinsic motivation
107   for exploration gives incentives for visiting diverse environmental states (Gronauer & Diepold, 2022).
      DPM assigns intrinsic rewards for choosing preferable actions.

108
      There are various attempts to solve the sparse reward problem using LLMs. One proposed method
109   leverages the coding abilities of LLMs to design reward functions (Xie et al., 2023), while another
110   involves independently generating multiple reward functions with LLMs and selecting the best-
111   performing one (Ma et al., 2023). Moreover, Sun et al. (2024) enhances the performance of the
112   LLM coder by adding an evaluator that generates preference feedback, resulting in the creation of a
113   robust reward model. The method for identifying key states involves using LLMs to extract critical
114   states, enabling effective exploration of the expansive state-action spaces in MARL (Qu et al., 2024).
115   DPM shares the aspect of using LLMs, but it differs in that LLMs are used to obtain preferences
116   rather than to extract key states or generate code for reward models.
117
118   2.3   P REFERENCE - BASED R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING
119
120   Preference-based Reinforcement Learning (PbRL) is an alternative approach for complex tasks
121   where designing a suitable reward function is difficult. In PbRL, the agent’s learning is also guided
122
      by a preference between difference behaviors rather than just a single scalar feedback from the
      environment. The source of preferences could be human feedback (Christiano et al., 2017; Casper
123
      et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2021b;a), a scripted teacher which assigns preferences according to true task
124
      rewards or AI feedback such as that generated by Large Language Models (Bai et al., 2022; Lee
125   et al., 2023; Klissarov et al., 2023).
126

      tiano et al., 2017). A segment σ is a sequence of observations and actions during k timesteps

      3     M ETHOD : DPM
146
147
      In this section, we present Dual Preferences-based Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning (DPM),
      which applies preference-based learning to multi-agent systems based on dual type preferences. DPM
148
      not only offers a solution to the sparse reward problem but also replaces human preferences with large
149
      language model-based preferences, thereby addressing the issues associated with human preferences.
150   DPM is based on an off-policy online learning MARL algorithm such as QMIX (Rashid et al., 2018).
151
152
153
      3.1   OVERVIEW
154
      The overall structure of DPM is illustrated in Figure 2. DPM comprises reward models, which
155
      generate intrinsic rewards, learned from preference feedback. Transition data from the environment
156   and intrinsic rewards are stored in the replay buffer and utilized in the policy training. DPM trains
157   the reward models based on two types of preference feedback. One involves comparing trajectory
158   pairs, while the other entails ranking the actions of agents in a scene. Preferences are obtained
159   using an LLM. To utilize an LLM, vector-based transition information must be transformed into
160   text-based prompts. Therefore, a prompt generator is utilized to convert transition information into
161   text format for input into the LLM. The LLM utilizes the provided information to generate preferences
      or rankings. The generated preferences (or rankings) are used to train the reward models.

182   3.2   D UAL T YPE P REFERENCES
183
184   DPM utilizes two types of preferences to train the reward models. One is trajectory comparison
185
      preference feedback, which selects the better trajectory through comparison. Trajectory comparison
      is similar to a common approach in PbRL and consistent with Section 2.3. The other is agent
186
      comparison preference feedback, which ranks the actions of agents in a single step. In MARL,
187
      since agents have different trajectories even within the same episode, comparing entire trajectories
188   at a macro level cannot provide appropriate rewards for each agent’s actions. To resolve this, it is
189   necessary to evaluate at a micro level whether the actions of agents at each step are appropriate. To
190   ensure proper evaluation at the micro level, the actions of agents at a step are ranked, and the rankings
191   are used as preferences.
192
      Trajectory Comparison: In a multi-agent concept, a trajectory segment includes observations
193
      compared to a single RL segment σ = {(s0 , o0 , a0 ), · · · , (sk , ok , ak )}. Two trajectory segments are
194   sampled from the replay buffer to generate a preference label (y) and we save the pair and the label
195   into the dataset DT = {(σ 1 , σ 2 , y)}.
196
197
      Agent Comparison: Agent comparison involves preferences in the form of rankings. In a given step,
      the actions of agents (a1t , a2t , . . . , ant ) are ranked according to their contributions. Therefore, when
198
      the state st and the actions at are provided, the LLM generates ranking labels z = {z1 , z2 , . . . , zn }
199
      based on the contributions and we save the dataset DA = {(st , at , ot , z)} to the buffer.

      3.3   P REFERENCE C OLLECTION
202
203
      Trajectory Selection Strategy: Selecting appropriate trajectories for preference feedback is crucial.
204   To achieve this, common PbRL research (Lee et al., 2021b) has used ensemble-based sampling
205   techniques. In DPM, since the contributions of agents can be ranked, we select trajectories (or scenes)
206   based on the agreement of rankings assigned by the reward functions using Kendall’s Tau (Kendall,
207   1938). Kendall’s Tau is used to assess the consistency of ranking data generated by reward models
208   when they create rewards for one step and then rank them based on the generated rewards. A low
209   Kendall’s Tau value indicates a disagreement among reward models, suggesting that the reward
210   models are encountering unseen trajectory. Therefore, DPM picks the trajectory with a low average
211   Kendall’s Tau value across its steps. For detailed explanation, refer to the Appendix C.
212   Prompt Generation: To obtain preference feedback using a Large Language Model (LLM), prompt
213   generation is essential. However, most environments provide information in the form of vectors
214   or images rather than text. We use a prompt generator to convert vector-based states into text-
215   based prompts that an LLM can understand. The prompt generator employs a template-based
      approach, where the provided information is substituted into the corresponding sections of the


                                                           4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      template. Specifically, A predefined prompt contains placeholders and the placeholders are filled
217   with the corresponding information from the state or observation. The prompt generator effectively
218   converts vector data into text format. However, it is limited in its ability to include all transitions
219   of trajectories in the prompt. Therefore, for trajectory comparison, the prompt only includes the
220   information of the initial state and the end state. Prompt examples can be found in the Appendix E.
221
      LLM Choice: The LLM generates preferences for the given comparison dataset using the prompts
222
      created by the prompt generator. We utilize the GPT-3.5 and GPT-4o (Achiam et al., 2023) as the
223   preference generation model. This model is considered to possess human-level judgment, enabling it
224   to make decisions at a level comparable to that of humans (Bai et al., 2022; Lee et al., 2023),

228   Structure: In contrast to common MARL approaches that utilize a global (team) reward function,
229   DPM generates rewards individually for each agent by leveraging preferences based on agent
230   comparisons. These reward functions take as input the transition and state information of the agent
231   (st , st+1 , oit , oit+1 , ait ) and produce corresponding an intrinsic reward (r̂ti ). This reward generation
232   process enables DPM to tailor rewards to the specific contributions of each agent, enhancing its
233   effectiveness in multi-agent environments. For more details of the reward models’ structure, please
234
      refer to Appendix A.4. Furthermore, DPM adopts multiple reward models, then the intrinsic reward
      is defined as the average of rewards generated by the reward models.

395   4.3   P ERFORMANCE ANALYSIS OF D UAL T YPE P REFERENCES
396
397   DPM optimizes the reward model using two types of preference feedback. In this subsection, we
398   compare the performance differences between using dual type preferences and a single preference
399   type, to highlight the advantages of dual type feedback. Additionally, we compare the results with
400   those obtained by training the team reward functions using trajectory feedback. Figure 5 illustrates
401   the performances of models using only one type preference versus those incorporating both trajectory
402   and agent comparison preferences.
403   The experiments are conducted on EASY scenarios in SMAC: 3m, 2m vs 1z, 2s vs 1sc, and 8m.
404   For both the single and dual preference types, the total number of feedback used is the same. When
405   using only trajectory comparison feedback, some scenarios did not converge to a high win rate or
406   failed to solve the problem entirely. In contrast, using both types of preferences feedback(red line)
407   leads to a convergence to an almost 100% win rate in all scenarios. Specifically, in the 2m vs 1z
408   and 8m scenario, the single type feedback approach showed no performance improvement at all.
409   Furthermore, when using only agent comparison preference feedback and when training team reward
410
      functions, the performance is significantly lower, compared to DPM(both).
411   In online learning, unlike offline RL, the policy is trained from scratch, making initial policy training
412   crucial. If the reward model is not well-optimized from the beginning, it is difficult to achieve good
413   performance. Using only one type of preference often causes the reward function to fall into a local
414   optimum. Consequently, the policy fails to learn effectively, and the quality of transitions collected
415   subsequently is poor, making it increasingly difficult to acquire appropriate preferences in the next
416
      iteration. Therefore, using a single type of preference limits both policy and reward model training.
417   Case study : To verify the efficacy of dual type preferences in optimizing the individual reward
418   function, we conduct a case study on a single episode of the 8m scenario. The top of Figure 6 depicts
419   six selected scenes within the episode, describing the states and actions at these steps. The bar graphs
420   display normalized individual reward values, scaled between 0 and 1, generated by reward models

519   5   C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATION
520
521
      We propose a novel approach called Dual Preference-based Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learn-
522   ing(DPM) for applying preference-based learning to multi-agent reinforcement learning. DPM
523   leverages a reward model trained on preferences to transform sparse reward environments into ones
524   akin to dense reward settings, thus addressing the sparse reward problem. Moreover, it addresses
525   issues inherent in traditional human-based preference methods by utilizing a large language model to
526   obtain preferences instead of relying solely on human input.
527
      DPM differs from conventional models that solely utilize trajectory comparison feedback by intro-
528   ducing a preference type that compares agents’ contributions through ranking. This addition helps to
529   better optimize the reward models. We evaluate DPM in SMAC and SMACv2, which are prominent
530   environments in multi-agent reinforcement learning. DPM demonstrates significant performance
531   improvement compared to baselines in sparse reward settings, and its performance is comparable
532   to that in dense reward settings. This confirms that DPM effectively addresses the sparse reward
533   problem in MARL.
534   However, there exists a constraint to generate prompts due to the utilization of an LLM. It is limited to
535   encapsulate information such as state, observation, actions, etc., within the prompt. To convert vector
536   or image data into text form, additional prepossessing is required. Therefore, we aim for DPM to be
537   more generally applicable across various environments through future research, such as exploring
538   the utilization of Vision-Language Models (VLMs) to effectively substitute non-vector data such as
539   image format data into prompts. This includes investigating methods to seamlessly incorporate data
      in forms other than vectors into prompts, thereby enhancing the generality of DPM.

783   A.2   BASELINES
784
             • VDN (Sunehag et al., 2017) : VDN is a value-based method and introduces a method to
785            decompose joint value functions into individual agent value functions, addressing challenges
786            in cooperative multi-agent reinforcement learning with a single team reward signal.
787
             • QMIX (Rashid et al., 2018) : QMIX, one of the most widely used methods in MARL, is
788
               an extension of VDN with a mixing network for monotonic value function factorization,
789
               allowing decentralized policies to be trained centrally for better coordination and efficiency.
790
791          • QPLEX (Wang et al., 2020) : QPLEX is also a value-based method and takes a duplex
792
               dueling network for learning environment randomness and cooperative randomness indepen-
               dently.
793
794          • MASER (Jeon et al., 2022) : MASER is a method proposed to address sparse reward
795            problems by generating sub-goals for agents from an experience replay buffer and providing
796            intrinsic rewards based on these sub-goals to effectively solve the sparse reward challenge.
797
               MASER utilizes the intrinsic rewards to train both individual Q-functions and the global
               Q-function.
798
799          • FOX (Jo et al., 2024) : FOX is another approach to addressing the spare reward problem by
800            proposing a formation-aware exploration framework, which provides intrinsic rewards to
801            guide agents toward forming diverse formations, thereby reducing the exploration space and
802
               increasing efficiency.
803          • ICES (Li et al., 2024) : ICES introduces individual contributions as intrinsic exploration
804            scaffolds, utilizing Bayesian surprise and privileged global information during training to
805            address sparse reward challenges and improve cooperative exploration.
806          • LAIES (Liu et al., 2023) : LAIES distinguishes agent states into internal states, directly
807            related to the agent, and external states, associated with external information, defining lazy
808            agents based on their interaction with the environment. LAIES generates intrinsic rewards to
809            prevent lazy agent behaviors, encouraging agents to actively interact with the environment
               and improving overall performance.

       C     T RAJECTORY S ELECTION S TRATEGY
985
986
       To obtain high-quality preference data, it is crucial to select comparison pairs appropriately. In
987
       prior PbRL research (Lee et al., 2021b), ensemble-based sampling techniques are employed. This
988    involves assuming rewards generated by multiple reward models as preferences and selecting pairs of
989    trajectories where the preferences do not align.
990
991
       On the other hand, in DPM, individual reward functions are utilized, necessitating optimization based
       on individual rewards rather than global rewards which are the sum of individual rewards. However,
992
       if trajectory-based sampling, similar to single-RL, is employed, the global reward becomes the
993
       criterion, making it challenging to select appropriate trajectories. To address this issue, DPM employs
994    Kendall’s Tau (Kendall, 1938) to calculate the degree of consensus among ranking data generated
995    from individual reward functions. The ranking is determined based on the rewards generated by the
996    reward functions, with higher-ranked agents having higher rewards.
997
       Since Kendall’s Tau calculates the concordance between pairs of ranking data, to assess the consensus
998
       among multiple reward functions, pairwise combinations is performed, followed by averaging the
999
       results. If the value is lower than the threshold, the trajectory is added to the list for comparison.
1000   Otherwise, the trajectory is excluded from the comparison. The threshold varies with each iteration,
1001   decreasing as the iterations progress.

       D     PREFERENCE FEEDBACK COLLECTION SETTING AND RESULTS
1005
1006
       D.1   LLM FEEDBACK COLLECTION SETTING

1007   DPM fundamentally uses preference feedback generated by LLMs. In online learning, the timing of
1008   feedback acquisition and the amount of feedback are crucial settings. By default, DPM is configured
1009   to repeatedly collect feedback every 1,500 episodes, gathering 150 pieces of feedback at a time. When
1010   using both types of feedback, 75 pieces are collected for each type. If only one type of feedback is
1011   used, 150 pieces are collected at a time. This process is repeated a total of 10 times, resulting in 1500
1012   pieces of feedback used to train the reward models.
1013
1014   D.2   HUMAN FEEDBACK COLLECTION SETTING AND RESULTS
1015
1016   All human feedback was collected by the authors, who are domain experts familiar with the MARL
1017
       task and the environments. Similar to the LLM feedback collection, feedback is collected in 10
       iterations, with total 150 pieces of feedback gathered in each iteration.
1018
1019   In the process of obtaining human feedback, preferences are determined based on the following
1020   criteria. First, in trajectory comparisons, preferences are determined by assessing scenarios where
1021   allied units retain more health while enemy units sustain significant damage. When comparing the
1022   actions of agents, the following criteria are applied. First, agents demonstrating cooperative behavior
1023
       are given higher rankings (e.g., agents engaged in focused attacks are prioritized). Second, agents
       with high health that do not participate in engagements are assigned lower rankings. Third, agents for
1024
       whom ranking is difficult are left unrated. Preferences are collected based on these criteria.
