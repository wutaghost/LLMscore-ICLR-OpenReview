009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The impressive capabilities of large language models (LLMs) come at the cost
012            of substantial computational resources during deployment. While KV Cache can
013            significantly reduce recomputation during inference, it also introduces additional
014            memory overhead. KV Cache quantization presents a promising solution, strik-
015            ing a good balance between memory usage and accuracy. Previous research has
016
               shown that the Keys are distributed by channel, while the Values are distributed by
               token. Consequently, the common practice is to apply channel-wise quantization
017
               to the Keys and token-wise quantization to the Values. However, our further inves-
018
               tigation reveals that a small subset of unusual tokens exhibit unique characteristics
019            that deviate from this pattern, which can substantially impact quantization accu-
020            racy. Furthermore, these tokens often have higher attention scores, exacerbating
021            their quantization errors. To address this, we develop a simple yet effective method
022            to identify these tokens accurately during the decoding process and exclude them
023            from quantization, significantly improving overall accuracy. Extensive experi-
024            ments show that our method achieves significant accuracy improvements under
025            2-bit quantization and can deliver a 6.4× reduction in memory usage and a 2.3×
026            increase in throughput. Our code will be released upon acceptance.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Large language models (LLMs) have significantly impacted various industries due to their powerful
031   capabilities (Achiam et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023a;b; Dubey et al., 2024; Jiang et al., 2023).
032   However, their auto-regressive nature makes the generation process slow. While using KV Cache
033   can reduce decoding complexity from O(n2 ) to O(n) by storing the Keys and the Values computed
034   during inference, it introduces substantial memory overhead. This overhead scales with sequence
035   length, batch size, and hidden dim, often creating a memory bottleneck and placing considerable
036   pressure on resources during deployment. As a result, optimizing KV Cache management to enhance
037   resource utilization and improve model throughput remains a critical challenge.
038   KV Cache affects throughput in two primary ways. First, its memory usage limits the scalability of
039   batch sizes, reducing parallelism during decoding and thus lowering throughput. Second, attention
040   computation is delayed while waiting for the KV Cache to be transferred from memory to the com-
041   putation unit. As the KV Cache size grows, the transmission time increases, decreasing throughput.
042   Existing approaches mainly address this issue by optimizing hardware scheduling (Aminabadi et al.,
043   2022; Dao et al., 2022; Sheng et al., 2023; Kwon et al., 2023) and reducing the size of the KV Cache
044
      (Liu et al., 2024b; Hooper et al., 2024; Kang et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023; Xiao et al., 2024). In
      this paper, we focus on the latter approach—KV Cache compression.
045
046   One method to reduce the size of the KV Cache is to reduce the number of values that need to be
047   stored. The shape of the KV Cache is [num layers, batch size, num heads, sequence length,
048   head dim]. There are various compressing methods across each dimension, including layer-wise
049   KV Cache sharing (Wu & Tu, 2024; Brandon et al., 2024; Zuhri et al., 2024; Mu et al., 2024), prefix
050
      sharing (Juravsky et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2024), head-wise KV Cache sharing (Shazeer, 2019;
      Ainslie et al., 2023), token eviction (Xiao et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023; Ge et al.), and low-rank
051
      projection (Wang et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2024; Chang et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024a).
052
053   Another strategy for reducing the size of KV Cache is quantization. However, unlike weight quan-
      tization, KV Cache quantization poses unique challenges due to the uneven distribution of the Keys

054
      and Values (Kang et al., 2024). To enhance quantization accuracy, various methods have been
055   proposed, including using low-rank matrices to approximate the error before and after quantiza-
056   tion (Kang et al., 2024), smoothing Key distributions through specific mappings (Ashkboos et al.,
057   2024; Chang et al., 2024), channel-wise Key and token-wise Value asymmetric quantization (Liu
058   et al., 2024b; Hooper et al., 2024), non-uniform quantization (Hooper et al., 2024; Dettmers et al.,
059   2022), mixed-precision quantization (Dong et al., 2024), and Block Floating Point (BFP) quantiza-
060   tion (Trukhanov & Soloveychik, 2024). Among these methods, channel-wise Key and token-wise
061   Value asymmetric quantization has garnered much attention for its high accuracy and tuning-free
062   nature. This technique operates under the assumption that some channels of the Keys have huge
063
      magnitudes and that the distribution of the Keys within the same channel is relatively uniform.
064   However, our further exploration reveals that a few unusual tokens deviate from this assumption.
065   Moreover, we find that these tokens exhibit remarkably high attention scores—often referred to as
066   attention sinks (Xiao et al., 2024). Notably, unlike previous studies, we find that these attention
067   sinks can occur at any position within a sentence rather than being confined to the initial positions.
068   Based on these observations, we propose Sink-aware KV Cache Quantization (SinkQ), a simple
069
      yet effective method that identifies these tokens and excludes them from the quantization process,
      thereby improving quantization accuracy. With hardware-friendly implementation, SinkQ achieves
070
      significant accuracy improvements under 2-bit quantization, resulting in a 6.4× reduction in memory
071
      usage and a 2.3× increase in throughput.
072
073   Overall, our contributions are summarized as follows:
074
             • We investigate the outlier channels of the KV Cache and identify that some tokens deviate
075
               from the previous assumptions.
076
077
             • We introduce Sink-aware KV Cache Quantization (SinkQ), a simple yet effective method to
               dynamically identify and exclude these tokens during quantization, thus improving overall
078
               quantization accuracy.
079
080
             • Our method achieves significant accuracy improvements under 2-bit quantization, yielding
               a 6.4× reduction in memory usage and a 2.3× increase in throughput, thereby enhancing
081
               model efficiency.

085
086   Implementation of KV Cache. Transformer-based (Vaswani, 2017) LLMs typically utilize KV
087   cache to prevent the redundant calculation of the attention scores and accelerate auto-regressive
088
      decoding. The generation process of LLMs with KV cache is divided into the prefill phase and
      the decoding phase (Patel et al., 2024). Given a prompt X = {x0 , x1 , . . . , xn−1 } and tensor X ∈
089
090
      Rb×n×d after embedding, where b is the batch size, n is the length of the prompt, and d represents
      the hidden size, we will briefly describe the calculation process of the attention block, and we omit
091
      the number of heads in the multi-head attention mechanism.
092
093   i) During the prefill phase, the Keys K<n and Values V<n are computed and cached by transforming
094   X through the Key and Value weight matrices Wk , Wv ∈ Rd×d of each layer, which can be
095   formulated as:
096
                                         K<n = XWk , V<n = XWv .
097   ii) During the decoding phase, only the Keys and Values of the new token xn need to be calculated,
098   which are then combined with the cached Keys and Values to compute the new attention scores and
099   outputs. For the current input tensor Xn ∈ Rb×1×d , we update the KV cache as follows:
100                                   K = K<n ∥Kn ,         V = V<n ∥Vn ,
101
      where Kn = Xn Wk and Vn = Xn Wv . We calculate the new attention output ATT as follows:

107   Necessity of compression. While KV cache reduces the computational complexity from O(n2 ) to
      O(n), it introduces substantial GPU memory overhead, particularly with long sequence lengths and


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      large batch sizes. For example, in the case of LLaMA3-8B (Dubey et al., 2024), where the number
109   of layers nlayers is 32, the number of heads h is 8, the head dimension d is 512, the input length l is
110   8192, and the batch size b is 64, performing inference with fp16 precision (which uses 2 bytes per
111   value) requires 4bhdlnlayers bytes to store the KV cache—equivalent to 256GB of memory. Thus,
112   effectively compressing the KV cache is crucial to reducing GPU memory usage.
113
      Uniform Quantization. In this paper, we focus on compressing the KV cache by reducing the
114
      bit-width needed to represent cached tensors. A straightforward approach is Uniform Quantization
115   (Jacob et al., 2018), which maps continuous numerical data to a discrete domain. Specifically, to
116   quantize a high-precision matrix (e.g., float32) X to a matrix X ′ with b-bit precision, we first
117   determine the quantization step size q. Each element Xi,j ∈ X can then be quantized to Q(Xi,j ) as
118   follows:
119                    Q(Xi,j ) = ⌊(Xi,j − Xmin )/q⌋, q = (Xmax − Xmin )/(2b − 1),                   (2)
120   where ⌊·⌋ is the rounding function.
121
122
      Group Quantization.          However, Uniform Quantization does not fully exploit the distribution
      characteristics of the data, which can lead to significant quantization errors, especially when there
123
      are outliers. A more advanced technique is Group Quantization (Yao et al., 2022), which divides
124
      the matrix into multiple groups, expecting the data within each group to share similar distribution
125   characteristics. Unlike Uniform Quantization, Group Quantization allows each group to have dif-
126   ferent quantization parameters, such as step size. This flexibility enables the method to better adapt
127   to the local characteristics of the data, thereby reducing quantization errors while maintaining a low
128   bit-width. The channel-wise Key quantization and token-wise Value quantization proposed by KIVI
129   (Liu et al., 2024b) is a type of Group Quantization.

207   3.2    SinkQ: S INK - AWARE KV C ACHE Q UANTIZATION
208
209   From these observations, we note that a few outlier tokens exhibit exceptionally high attention
210   scores, consistent with the findings from StreamingLLM (Xiao et al., 2024). These tokens are
211   referred to as attention sinks, and we will use this term later. Previous work suggesting that attention
212
      sinks occur only in the initial tokens; however, our observations reveal that they can appear at any
      position within a sentence. Moreover, the distribution of the Keys of attention sinks differs signifi-
213
      cantly from that of other tokens, which can substantially impact overall quantization performance.
214
215   Based on the insights above, we propose Sink-aware KV Cache Quantization (SinkQ). Figure 2 il-
      lustrates an overview of our method. SinkQ consists of two components: quantization and decoding.

230   Figure 2: Overview of SinkQ. Top: Decoding stage. Multiply the Query by each type of the Keys
231   and concatenate the results to obtain the attention scores. Multiply the attention scores by each type
232   of the Values and sum the results to get the attention output. Bottom: Quantization stage. Before
233
      quantization, process the sinks first, then quantize the Keys by channel and the Values by token.
234
235
      Quantization We define a fixed-size sink pool with a capacity of sink num to store the Keys
236
      and Values of the attention sinks. To fulfill group quantization, we quantize KV Cache every G
237   steps, which means group size, a hyper-parameter in group quantization. When the group is full,
238   tokens selected for quantization will obtain a position in the sink pool based on the rules described in
239   Section 3.1. Once selected, the Keys and Values of these tokens are replaced with the mean values of
240   all tokens to eliminate their impact on quantization. We adopt the quantization strategy from KIVI,
241   applying channel-wise quantization for the Keys and token-wise quantization for the Values.
242
      Decoding We maintain three types of KV Cache: the quantized KV Cache, the full-precision KV
243   Cache, and the KV Cache stored in the sink pool. First, the Query is multiplied by all three types
244   of Keys, and we concatenate the results to produce the attention scores. Next, we multiply these
245   scores by their corresponding Values from each type and sum them to generate the final attention
246   output. To enhance decoding efficiency, we utilize a CUDA fused kernel to multiply full-precision
247   and quantized matrices efficiently.
248
      Following KIVI, we have group tokens and recent tokens. We trigger a new quantization step when
249   the full-precision KV Cache reaches a certain length (necessary for channel-wise key quantization).
250   When the group is not full, we must keep these group tokens in full-precision. KIVI also maintain
251   the local tokens in full-precision because these tokens are found important in many studies, we
252   follow this operation and call these tokens recent tokens.

402   4.4       A BLATION
403
404   Group size and residual length. Group size and residual length are critical hyperparameters in
405
      SinkQ. Theoretically, a larger group size allows more values to be quantized at each step, which
      can reduce quantization accuracy due to the increased range of Xmax − Xmin . On the other hand,
406
      a larger group size decreases memory usage by requiring fewer quantization coefficients to be re-
407
      tained. Conversely, increasing the residual length requires more memory since a more full-precision
408   KV Cache must be retained, but it also improves accuracy. Thus, selecting an appropriate group size
409   and residual length is critical to balancing memory usage and accuracy.
410
411
      We explore the impact of group size and residual length with group sizes of {32, 64, 128} and
      residual lengths of {0, 8, 16, 32, 64, 128}. Table 3 reports the results for LLaMA3-8B-Instruct
412
      on Gsm8k 8-shot and 8-shot CoT under different configurations. When the group size is fixed,
413
      we observe a clear upward trend in accuracy as the residual length increases. However, when the
414   residual length is fixed, the effect of group size shows no clear pattern, likely because the token
415   distribution is relatively uniform, meaning that increasing group size has a limited impact. Since
416   increasing the group size can improve the compression ratio (if not consider the group tokens), we
417   tend to choose a larger group size. For our main experiments, we choose a group size of 128 and a
418   residual length of 32 to balance performance and compression ratio.
419
      The number of sinks. We explore the effect of varying the number of sinks (sink num) from 0
420   to 6, keeping all other settings unchanged. Table 4 presents the results for LLaMA3-8B-Instruct

422   icantly improve performance, but further increases in sink num yield diminishing returns, even-
423   tually plateauing performance. However, the increase in sink num may result in more memory
424   overhead, leading to a decrease in compression ratio. Considering that a small sink num is already
425   sufficient to significantly improve the accuracy, we set sink num = 3 for our main experiments,

451   5    R ELATED WORK
452
453   Efficient Inference of LLMs. Large Language Models often have enormous parameters, leading
454   to significant computational costs during inference. To address this, some researchers have em-
455   ployed parameter pruning techniques to eliminate redundant or less important parameters, thereby
456   compressing LLMs (Ma et al., 2023; Xia et al., 2024; Frantar & Alistarh, 2023). Other studies have
457   focused on quantizing model weights, reducing their size and the number of arithmetic operations
458   required for inference. For example, GPTQ (Frantar et al., 2022) uses second-order information to
459
      quantize models to 3 or 4-bit precision while maintaining accuracy. AWQ (Lin et al., 2024) preserves
      critical weights based on the activation distribution, quantizing the remaining weights to lower bit
460
      precision. These methods can be combined with KV Cache compression to achieve a better memory
461
      usage and a higher throughput.
462
463   KV Cache Compression. KV Cache compression can significantly reduce the size of KV Cache
464   with minimal accuracy loss. Liu et al. (2024b) find that some outlier channels in the Keys have
465
      very large magnitudes, resulting in a significant loss when quantifying Keys by token. They further
      discover that Keys are distributed by channel, while Values are distributed by token. Based on these
466
      observations, they introduce KIVI, a tuning-free 2-bit quantization method that improve quantization
467
      accuracy by applying channel-wise quantization to the Key cache and token-wise quantization to
468   the Value cache. Hooper et al. (2024) find that quantizing the Key cache before applying rotary
469   positional embeddings reduces the negative impact of quantization. GEAR (Kang et al., 2024)
470   compensates for compression-induced errors by combining low-rank and sparse matrices, achieving
471   near-lossless results in 4-bit quantization, but it will bring additional calculations due to the low-rank
472   matrix calculation. Additionally, evicting some tokens during inference helps reduce the excessive
473   memory usage caused by storing KV Cache. Xiao et al. (2024) propose StreamingLLM, which
474   retains the initial and final tokens of the input, leveraging the concept of the attention sink. Moreover,
475   Zhang et al. (2023) consider that only a minority of tokens significantly influence the output and that
476
      these critical tokens are positively correlated with their frequency of occurrence. They propose H2O,
      which retains recent tokens while dynamically evicting less important ones.
477
478
479   6    C ONCLUSION
480
481   In this paper, we start from the assumptions of KIVI and further explore the distribution of the
482   Keys in the outlier channels. We observe that several unusual tokens deviate from the assumptions
483   of KIVI and exhibit exceptionally high attention scores. Quantizing these tokens has detrimental
484   effects, as it increases the quantization errors of other tokens, and their high attention scores lead to
485   more significant quantization errors reflected in the attention output. Building on these observations,
      we propose Sink-Aware KV Cache quantization (SinkQ), which leverages the magnitude of the


                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Keys to dynamically track these tokens during decoding, excluding them from the quantization
487   process while retaining their full-precision values. Extensive experiments show that our method
488   achieves significant improvements in accuracy, along with substantial reductions in memory usage
489   and increases in throughput.

      7   L IMITATIONS & F UTURE WORK
493
      Although SinkQ has achieved excellent results, there are still some limitations:
494
495          • Due to the presence of the group and recent tokens, we cannot ensure that all tokens are
496            quantized at every moment. When the sequence length is very short and the batch size is
497            very large, the compression ratio of SinkQ is reduced. In extreme cases, when the sequence
498            length is shorter than the group size, SinkQ does not perform any compression.
499          • SinkQ occasionally still incurs a little loss on specific datasets under 2-bit quantization.
500            This may be related to the difficulty of the datasets and the required generation length.
501            When the generation length is very long, SinkQ may face an unacceptable risk of loss due
502            to error accumulation.
503
504   Additionally, there are still some points worth exploring:
505
             • Mitigating error accumulation: When the generation length is excessively long, KV Cache
506
               quantization may lead to unacceptable errors due to error accumulation. Developing tech-
507
               niques to alleviate this issue is a promising direction.
508
509
             • Combining with other KV Cache compression techniques: KV Cache quantization is com-
               patible with other compression methods. Combining it with other KV Cache compression
510
               techniques could achieve State-of-the-art results.
