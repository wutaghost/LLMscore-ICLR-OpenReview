009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Image generation models have encountered challenges related to scalability and
012            quadratic complexity, primarily due to the reliance on Transformer-based back-
013            bones. In this study, we introduce MaskMamba, a novel hybrid model that inte-
014            grates Mamba and Transformer architectures, utilizing Masked Image Modeling
015            for non-autoregressive image synthesis. We meticulously redesign the bidirec-
016            tional Mamba architecture by implementing two key modifications: (1) replac-
017            ing causal convolutions with standard convolutions to better capture global con-
018            text, and (2) utilizing concatenation instead of multiplication, which significantly
019            boosts performance while accelerating inference speed. Additionally, we explore
020
               various hybrid schemes of MaskMamba, including both serial and grouped paral-
               lel arrangements. Furthermore, we incorporate an in-context condition that allows
021
               our model to perform both class-to-image and text-to-image generation tasks. Our
022
               MaskMamba outperforms Mamba-based and Transformer-based models in gener-
023            ation quality. Notably, it achieves a remarkable 54.44% improvement in inference
024            speed at a resolution of 2048 × 2048 over Transformer.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   In recent years, the field of generative image models in computer vision has witnessed significant ad-
031   vancements, particularly in class-to-image (Gao et al. (2023); Sun et al. (2024); Sauer et al. (2022))
032   and text-to-image tasks (Yu et al. (2022; 2023); Bao et al. (2023)). Traditional autoregressive gen-
033
      erative models, such as VQGAN (Esser et al. (2021)) and LlamaGen (Sun et al. (2024)), demon-
      strate excellent performance in class-conditional generation. In the realm of text-conditional gener-
034
      ation, models like Parti (Yu et al. (2021; 2022)) and DALL-E (Ramesh et al. (2021)) convert images
035
      into discrete tokens using the image tokenizer and project the encoded text features to caption em-
036   beddings via an additional MLP (Chen et al. (2023)), operating in an autoregressive manner for
037   both training and inference. Concurrently, non-autoregressive methods, including MAGE (Li et al.
038   (2023)) and MUSE (Chang et al. (2023)), leverage Masked Image Modeling, transforming images
039   into discrete tokens during training and predicting randomly masked tokens.
040
      Another prominent approach to image generation involves diffusion models (Song & Ermon (2019);
041
      Song et al. (2020); Ho et al. (2020); Dhariwal & Nichol (2021); Nichol et al. (2021)), such as LDM
042   (Rombach et al. (2022)) with an UNet backbone. Although these models demonstrate high gener-
043   ation quality, their convolutional neural network architecture imposes constraints that hinder scala-
044   bility. To address this challenge, Transformer-based generative models, such as DiT (Peebles & Xie
045   (2023)), enhance global modeling capabilities through attention mechanisms and significantly im-
046   prove generation quality. However, the computational complexity of attention mechanisms increases
047   quadratically with sequence length, which constrains both training and inference efficiency.
048   Mamba (Gu & Dao (2023)) presents a state-space model (Gu et al. (2022; 2021)) characterized
049   by linear time complexity, offering substantial advantages in managing long sequence tasks. Con-
050   temporary image generation efforts, including DiM (Teng et al. (2024)), ZigMa (Hu et al. (2024)),
051   and diffuSSM (Yan et al. (2024)), primarily replace the original Transformer block with a Mamba
052   module. These models enhance both efficiency and scalability. Nevertheless, generating images
053   based on diffusion models typically requires hundreds of iterations, which can be prohibitively time-
      consuming.

      To eliminate the quadratic complexity growth with sequence length in Transformer models and the
068   excessive generation iterations in autoregressive models, we introduce MaskMamba that integrates
069   Mamba and Transformer architectures and utilizes non-autoregressive Masked Image Modeling (Ni
070   et al. (2024); Lezama et al. (2022)) for image synthesis. We meticulously redesign Bi-Mamba (Mo
071   & Tian (2024); Zhu et al. (2024)) to render it suitable for masked image generation by replacing the
072   causal convolution with standard convolution. Meanwhile, we select concatenation instead of mul-
073   tiplication in the final stage of Bi-Mamba to reduce computational complexity, notably improving
074   the inference speed by 17.77% compared to Bi-Mamba (Zhu et al. (2024)).
075   We further investigate various MaskMamba hybrid schemes, including serial and grouped parallel
076   schemes (Shaker et al. (2024)). In serial schemes, we explore alternating layer-by-layer arrange-
077   ments, as well as placing the Transformer in the last N/2 layers. For grouped parallel schemes, we
078   assess the effects of partitioning the model into two or four groups along the channel dimension. Our
079   findings indicate that placing the Transformer in the final layers significantly enhances the model’s
080   ability to capture global context. Additionally, we implement an in-context condition that allows our
081   model to perform both class-to-image and text-to-image generation tasks within a single framework
082
      as show in Fig.1. Meanwhile, we investigate the placement of condition embeddings (Zhu et al.
      (2024)) by inserting them at differnent positions of the input sequence including head, middle, and
083
      tail. The results indicate that placing condition embedding at the middle yields optimal performance.
084
085   In the experimental section, we substantiate the generative capabilities of MaskMamba through two
086   distinct tasks: class-conditional generation and text-conditional generation, utilizing various model
087   sizes for each task. For class-to-image generation task, we execute training over 300 epochs on
088
      the ImageNet1k (Deng et al. (2009)) dataset, benchmarking our MaskMamba against Transformer-
      based and Mamba-based models of analogous size. The results demonstrate that our MaskMamba
089
      outperforms both counterparts with respect to generation quality and inference speed. Furthermore,
090
      we train and evaluate on CC3M (Sharma et al. (2018)) dataset, attaining superior performance on
091   CC3M and MS-COCO (Lin et al. (2014)) valid datasets.
092
093   In summary, our contributions include:
094
                           1. We redesign Bi-Mamba to improve its suitability for masked image generation tasks by
095
                              replacing causal convolution with standard convolution. Additionally, we substitute multi-
096                           plication with concatenation at the final stage, resulting in a significant performance boost
097                           and a 17.77% increase in inference speed compared to Bi-Mamba.
098
                           2. We introduce MaskMamba, a unified generative model that integrates redesigned Bi-
099
                              Mamba and Transformer layers, enabling class-to-image and text-to-image generation tasks
100                           to be performed in the same model through an in-context condition.
101
                           3. Our MaskMamba surpasses both Transformer-based and Mamba-based models in terms of
102
                              generation quality and inference speed on the ImageNet1k and CC3M datasets.

      Mamba Vision. The Transformer (Vaswani (2017)), established as a leading network architecture,
      is extensively utilized across various tasks. Nonetheless, its quadratic computational complexity
124
      presents significant obstacles for the efficient handling of long sequence tasks. In recent develop-
125
      ments, the advent of a novel State-Space Model (Gu et al. (2021)), denominated as Mamba (Gu
126   & Dao (2023); Dao & Gu (2024)), has shown substantial promise in tackling long sequence tasks,
127   capturing significant interest within the research community. The Mamba architecture has effec-
128   tively supplanted conventional Transformer frameworks in multiple domains, delivering noteworthy
129   results. The Mamba family (Gao et al. (2024); Hatamizadeh & Kautz (2024); Lieber et al. (2024);
130   Pilault et al. (2024)) encompasses a broad spectrum of applications, including text generation, ob-
131   ject recognition, 3D point cloud processing, recommendation systems, and image generation, with
132   numerous implementations based on frameworks such as Vision-Mamba (Zhu et al. (2024)), U-
133   Mamba (Ma et al. (2024)), and Rec-Mamba (Yang et al. (2024)). Vision-Mamba employs a bidirec-
134
      tional state-space model structure in conjunction with a hybrid Transformer (Hatamizadeh & Kautz
      (2024)). However, Mamba has not yet been explored in the context of non-autoregressive image
135
      generation. Presently, the majority of Mamba-based generative tasks adhere to the diffusion model
136
      paradigm (Hu et al. (2024); Teng et al. (2024)), which entails complexities related to training and
137   the number of inference iterations. Addressing these challenges, we have designed a novel hybrid
138   Mamba structure aimed at extending the application of Mamba in non-autoregressive image gener-
139   ation (Li et al. (2023); Chang et al. (2022; 2023)) tasks, integrating it with Masked Image Modeling
140   (He et al. (2022)) for both training and inference, thereby enhancing the efficiency of these pro-
141   cesses.

146   3.1    M ASK M AMBA M ODEL : OVERVIEW
147
      Overview. As illustrated in Fig.2, our MaskMamba fundamentally consists of three components.
148
      Firstly, the image pixels x ∈ RH×W ×3 are quantized into discrete tokens q ∈ Qh×w via an im-
149
      age tokenizer (Yu et al. (2021); Van Den Oord et al. (2017); Esser et al. (2021)), where h = H/r,
150   w = W/r, and r represents the downsample ratio of the image tokenizer. These discrete tokens
151   q ∈ Qh×w serve as indices of the image codebook. Then, we randomly sample the masking ratio
152   mr (range from 0.55 to 1.0), and mask out mr · (h · w) tokens, replacing them with a learnable
153   mask token [M ]. Secondly, we transform the class id into a learnable label embedding (Peebles
154   & Xie (2023); Esser et al. (2021)), denoted as {cls}. On the other hand, regarding the text con-
155   ditions, we first extract features using a T5-Large Encoder (Colin (2020)) and then map the ex-
156   tracted features to caption embeddings (Chen et al. (2023)), denoted as {t1 , t2 , . . . , tN }. Lastly,
157   we concat condition embeddings {cond} with the image token embeddings {q1 , q2 , . . . , qh·w } at
158
      middle, where {cond} represents {cls} or {t1 , t2 , . . .}, and add positional embedding to these
      {q1 , q2 , . . . , [M ], . . . , qi , cond, qj , [M ], . . . , qh·w }. The training objective is to predict the token in-
159
      dices of the masked regions utilizing cross-entropy loss (Zhang & Sabuncu (2018)).
160

      text-conditional models. In accordance with the standards established by prior work (Radford et al.

      Convolution Replacement. As illustrated in Fig.3 (c), we redesign the original Bi-Mamba (Zhu
204   et al. (2024)) architecture to better accommodate tasks associated with masked image generation. We
205   substitute the original causal convolution with a standard convolution. Given the non-autoregressive
206   nature of masked image generation task, the causal convolution only permits unidirectional token
207   mixing, which hinders the potential of non-autoregressive image generation. In contrast, the stan-
208   dard convolution enables tokens to interact bidirectionally across all positions in the input sequence,
209   effectively capturing the global context.
210   Symmetric SSM Branch Design. We incorporate a symmetric SSM branch to better accommodate
211   masked image generation. In the symmetric branch, we first flip the input x before the Backward
212   SSM, then flip it back after the Backward SSM to amalgamate it with the results of the Forward SSM.
213   Additionally, compared to the right-side branch of Bi-Mamba, we employ an extra convolution to
214   mitigate feature loss. To fully exploit the advantages of all the branches, we project the input into
215   a feature space of size C/2, thereby ensuring that the final concatenated dimensions are consistent.
      Our output can be denoted as Xout , which is computed using the following Eq.1.

248   Group Scheme Design. As displayed in Fig.4 (a) and Fig.4 (b), we design two group mixing
249   schemes. In group scheme v1, the input is divided into two groups along the channel dimension,
250
      which are then processed separately by our Bi-Mamba-v2 layer and Transformer layer. The pro-

251
      Norm and Project layers. In group scheme v2, the input is divided into four groups along the chan-
252
      nel dimension. Two of these groups are processed by our Bi-Mamba-v2 layer in the Forward SSM

256
      schemes. In serial scheme v1, we alternate layer-by-layer arrangements of our Bi-Mamba-v2 and

257
      former in the last N/2 layers. Due to the attention mechanism of Transformer, which can better
258
      enhance feature representation, Transformer layer are placed after Mamba layer in all serial modes.

      3.3     I MAGE G ENERATION B Y M ASK M AMBA
262
      We employ masked image generation methods (Li et al. (2023); Chang et al. (2022)) for image syn-
263
      thesis. During the forward pass, we first initialize 256 masked tokens for generating a resolution of
264   256 × 256 image. Subsequently, we concatenated the condition embeddings with mask tokens at the
265   middle position. Inspired by the iterative generation approach of MUSE (Chang et al. (2023)), our
266   decoding process also adopts a cosine schedule (Chang et al. (2022)) that chooses a fixed propor-
267   tion of the highest-confidence masked tokens for prediction at each step. These tokens are then set
268   unmasked for remaining steps and the set of masked tokens is correspondingly reduced. Through
269   this methodology, we can infer 256 tokens utilizing merely 20 decoding steps, in contrast to the 256
      steps necessitated by autoregressive methods (Touvron et al. (2023); Sun et al. (2024)).

315   Training Setup. All of class-to-image generation models are trained for 300 epochs on the Ima-
316   geNet 256 × 256 dataset, with consistent training parameter settings across all models. Specifically,

319   set to, including for conditions. During training, the mask rate varies from 0.5 to 1. All training and
320
      inference of the models are conducted on V100 GPUs with 32GB of memory.

322   employing Inception Score (Salimans et al. (2016)) (IS) and Inception Score standard deviation (IS-

      on the CFG and evaluate all models using the aforementioned metrics.

525   5     C ONCLUSION .
526
527   In this work, we propose MaskMamba, a novel hybrid model that integrates Bi-Mamba-v2 and
528   Transformer architectures, utilizing Masked Image Modeling for non-autoregressive image synthe-
529
      sis. We not only redesign a new Bi-Mamba-v2 structure to enhance its suitability for image gen-
      eration but also investigate the effects of different model mixing strategies and the placement of
530
      condition embeddings, ultimately identifying the optimal settings. Additionally, we provide a series
531
      of class-conditional image generation models and text-conditional image generation models within a
532   single framework that incorporates an in-context condition. Our experiment results indicate that our
533   MaskMamba surpasses both Transformer-based and Mamba-based models in terms of generation
534   quality and inference speed. We hope our Masked Image Modeling for non-autoregressive image
535   synthesis in MaskMamba will inspire further exploration in Mamba image generation tasks.
