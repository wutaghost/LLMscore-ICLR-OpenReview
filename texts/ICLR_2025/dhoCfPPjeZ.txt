009                                                                       A BSTRACT
010
011                               Creating hypotheses for new observations is a key step in the scientific process
012                               of understanding a problem in any domain. A good hypothesis that is inter-
013                               pretable, reliable (good at predicting unseen observations), and data-efficient; is
014                               useful for scientists aiming to make novel discoveries. This paper introduces an
015                               automatic way of learning such interpretable and reliable hypotheses in a data-
016                               efficient manner. We propose DiSciPLE (Discovering Scientific Programs using
017                               LLMs and Evolution) an evolutionary algorithm that leverages common sense and
018
                                  prior knowledge of large language models (LLMs) to create hypotheses as Python
                                  programs for scientific problems. Additionally, we propose two improvements: a
019
                                  program critic and a program simplifier to further improve our method to produce
020
                                  good hypotheses. We evaluate our method on four different real-world tasks in
021                               two scientific domains and show significantly better results. For example, we can
022                               learn programs with 35% lower error than the closest non-interpretable baseline
023                               for population density estimation.

026   1                       I NTRODUCTION
027
028   The scientific process involves the collection of data, the creation of hypotheses to explain the data,
029   and experimental testing and validation of the hypothesis to produce scientific laws. This process is
030   manual, laborious and time consuming. With advances in LLMs, there is an opportunity to automate
031   the scientific pipeline to greatly accelerate scientific discovery and progress. While prior work in
032   automating science has looked at efficient data collection (Van Horn et al., 2015; Sener & Savarese,
033
      2018), the possibility of automatic hypothesis generation from observational data has been less
      explored. This is especially important because in many domains like earth science, there are vast
034
      troves of data waiting to be analyzed. A method that could automatically scour through this data to
035
      identify hypotheses for scientists to explore can significantly speed up the scientists’ workflow. Our
036   goal is to address this gap and produce an automatic hypothesis generation framework (fig. 1).
037
038   What are good desiderata for an automatic hypotheses generation framework? First, clearly the
039
      generated hypothesis should fit the observations well. This requires that the framework should be
      able to search through a large and expressive search space. Second, the generated hypotheses should
040
      be reliable, in that it should correctly predict data even outside of the observations used to generate
041
      the hypotheses. Third, a good hypothesis is one that is interpretable by scientists, since only then
042   can scientists understand what is being discovered, and frame experiments to test the hypothesis.
043   Finally, the hypothesis generation framework should be sample efficient. In other words, scientists
044   coming up with a hypothesis should not need to collect a lot of observations to come up with a good

      hypothesis. In this paper, we ask: How can we automatically discover expressive, interpretable and
055   reliable hypotheses in a sample-efficient way?
056
057   Existing approaches that discover hypotheses or formulae are not up to the task. Interpretable ML
058
      techniques such as concept-bottlenecks (Koh et al., 2020) can produce simple bag-of-words pro-
      grams for visual classification but are not expressive enough to produce more sophisticated hypothe-
059
      ses. At the other end of the spectrum, black-box neural networks are expressive but offer no insight
060
      to the scientists for exploration. Symbolic regression (Cranmer, 2023) techniques are interpretable
061   and can produce sophisticated formulae, but are very slow to converge because of the large search
062   space. This issue is particularly severe in scientific domains where the hypothesis may reference
063   any concept or variable in the scientific vocabulary, making the search space effectively unbounded.
064   Neurosymbolic program learning (Johnson et al., 2017) address this search space with reinforcement
065   learning, but this requires exceptional amount of training data and is not sample-efficient.
066
      To be able to search through a large and expressive search space, recent works have looked at lever-
067   aging the common sense abilities of large language models (LLMs) (Surı́s et al., 2023). However,
068   while zero-shot generation from LLMs can work well for well-known problems, they are insufficient
069   for scientific domains with novel problems and data. Recent work has looked at leveraging LLMs
070   as the mutation operator in evolutionary algorithms (Romera-Paredes et al., 2024; Chiquier et al.,
071   2024). We leverage the common sense and prior knowledge of LLMs in tandem with an evolution-
072   ary algorithm-based symbolic regression method to generate programs as hypotheses, resulting in
073   a significantly smaller search space. The interpretable programs are learned on a set of lower-level
074   primitives, including neural modules such as open-world segmentation. Our framework produces
075
      neuro-symbolic programs as good hypotheses.
076   While performing crossover and mutation with LLMs can produce meaningful programs, several
077   improvements can be made on the framework to further improve the search. First, unlike symbolic
078   regression, the hypotheses evaluation in the evolution loop does not have to be limited to a single re-
079   gression score. The LLM can leverage more information about the current hypothesis to better guide
080   the search. Therefore, we propose to add a critic to our framework that can provide fine-grained in-
081
      formation about the hypothesis to better guide the search. Our critic divides the training observation
      set into fine-grained categories and can provide feedback on categories that the hypothesis does well
082
      on or does badly on. This results in programs that can have an all-round better performance. Second,
083
      while the LLM is good at exploring the search space, many programs produced by the framework
084   contain frivolous variables, that may not aid the hypotheses. Having such redundant features/parts
085   in programs can result in more complex final hypotheses as well as a larger search space during evo-
086   lution. Therefore, we also add an analytical (non-llm-based) program simplification method, that
087   can remove such frivolous parts of the program.
088
      We run experiments in two different expert domains (demography and climate science) on four real-
089
      world problems and show that we can produce good hypotheses by leveraging LLMs. In all of these
090   cases, the programs generated are reliable and result in better generalization than deep neural nets.
091   Moreover, in a few cases, we also observe better performance on even unseen in-distribution data.

             • We present two key components: a critic and a program simplification method to DiSci-

               domains and four different problems and show that our learned hypotheses are indeed more
               interpretable, reliable, and data-efficient compared to baselines.

105
106   Concept bottlenecks. Concept bottleneck (Koh et al., 2020) is an approach used to create
107   interpretable-yet-powerful classifiers. The key idea is to train a deep model to predict a set of
      low-level concepts or bottlenecks and then learn a linear classifier. Such concept bottlenecks have


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      the basis of methods in several areas such as fine-grained recognition (Ferrari & Zisserman, 2007;
109   Huang et al., 2016; Zhou et al., 2018; Tang et al., 2020) and zero-shot learning (Lampert et al., 2013;
110   Akata et al., 2015; Kodirov et al., 2017). However in order to train these models, expensive data is
111   needed to be collected for the bottleneck concepts themselves. One way to reduce this annotation
112   cost is to sequentially ask question in an information theoretically optimized way (Chattopadhyay
113   et al., 2024a;b). Researchers have also automated this pipeline by using large-language models as a
114   knowledge base to propose concept bottleneck models (Menon & Vondrick, 2023; Pratt et al., 2023;
115   Han et al., 2023). Chiquier et al. (2024) proposed an evolutionary algorithm with LLMs as the muta-
116   tion operation to discover interpretable concept bottleneck models without prior information. While
117
      these models are interpretable, they are very simple in terms of expressive power. In this work, we
      instead evolve programs, which are more expressive than bag of words, while being interpretable.
118
119
120
      Symbolic regression. Symbolic regression (Cranmer, 2023) is a powerful technique for learning
      such programs on evolution through evolutionary search. Several methods have been proposed to
121
      improve the search (Makke & Chawla, 2024), however most symbolic regression techniques cannot
122
      solve problems beyond simple mathematical formula. This is partly because the search space of
123   possible hypotheses is too large due to combinatorial explosion. Like ours, recent work in symbolic
124   regression (Grayeli et al., 2024) have also looked at using LLMs to better guide the search. However,
125   they also only test this method on mathematical formula, with limited set of primitives. In real-world
126   problems, the primitives functions are more complex than mathematical operations and can even be
127   open-world for example a text-to-image segmentor.
128
129   Neuro-Symbolic Program Learning. (Mao et al., 2019; Dong et al.) is another avenue for learn-
130   ing programs as hypotheses for observation datasets or answering questions. These methods typi-
131   cally try to learn both discrete program structures together with neural networks. However, since
132   this optimization is non-differentiable these methods require reinforcement learning (Johnson et al.,
133   2017) or complex non-differentiable optimization techniques (Ellis et al., 2021). The hard opti-
134
      mization issue makes the problem of learning programs sample inefficient in real-world settings.
      We alternatively use LLMs ability to program to better guide the search for such programs.
135
136
      Program synthesis with LLMs. Several works have utilized LLM coding ability in different ap-
137
      plications such as VQA (Surı́s et al., 2023; Gupta & Kembhavi, 2022) and robot manipulation (Liang
138
      et al., 2023). While the zero-shot inferred code work very well on domains well-known to the inter-
139   net, they tend to perform poorly on problems in scientific domains, as shown by our results.
140
141
      Scientific applications. Researchers in numerous scientific domains have looked machine learn-
142   ing tools to build predictive models/hypotheses for their quantities of interest. In this work, we
143   experiment in two such scientific domain of: demography and climate Science. In demography, we
144   focus on the problems of socioeconomic indicator prediction (Yong & Zhou, 2024), namely popula-
145   tion density and poverty estimation (Metzger et al., 2024; Xie, 2017). Similarly in climate science
146   we focus on two problems of aboveground biomass prediction (AGB) (Nathaniel et al., 2023) and
147   Contiguous Solar Induced Chlorophyll Fluorescence(CSIF) forecasting (Zhang et al., 2018).

246        at the start of the process, we provide the LLM with a prompt for the objective to generate the
247        initial programs. To leverage the prior knowledge of LLMs, we use a prompt po that mentions the
248        expert specified description of the quantity of interest: “Given a satellite image, write a function
249
           to estimate ⟨descr⟩”. While an LLM cannot answer such a difficult scientific question without
           leveraging the observations D, the prompt prevents the evolutionary algorithm from searching in

           completely random directions. As a result, our initial population is not entirely random.

      3.3   F EATURE SET PREDICTION
271
272   Hypotheses for many problems require combination of multiple feature. Optimizing for the correct
273   combination of these features is challenging for an LLM, as we are not doing gradient-based opti-
274   mization. Therefore instead of prompting the LLM to directly generate a predictor for y, we prompt
275   is to create a list of predictive features. We can learn a linear regressor on top of this list and use the
276   regressor with the list of features as hypotheses. Both the program and regressor are interpretable,
277   therefore our hypothesis is also interpretable.
278
279   3.4   P ROGRAM C RITIC
280
      The only form of supervision our method gets is through the metric score s(h; D) created by evaluat-
281
      ing the program h on the observations. However, since we perform crossover and mutation through
282   a language model, we can provide finer-grained information to LLM in order to aid the search.
283
284   More specifically, we propose a critic that performs a finer-grained evaluation of the program that
285
      we get after crossover/mutation. Our critic performs a stratified evaluation by partitioning the ob-
      servation data into multiple categories and evaluating the program on individual strata.
286

289   In all the applications where we use satellite images, we partition the observation dataset into land-
290   use categories, using an open-world segmentation model. The critic obtains per-partition score
291   s(h; di ) and prompts the LLM to improve the program on categories the model is bad. The addition
292   of a critic improves the programs on data overlooked by programs, resulting in reliable programs.

294   3.5   P ROGRAM S IMPLIFICATION
295
      Successive steps of crossovers and mutations of programs result in large programs with many redun-
296
      dancies, hurting interpretability. We propose an analytical approach to simplify the programs and
297
      remove the redundant parts of it. Our hypothesis are programs without loop, they can be represented
298   as a directed acyclic graphs (DAG) (we use the abstract syntax tree (AST); see fig. 5). In these
299   DAGs, all the constants and the arguments of the function are root nodes. Only the return statement
300   and the unused variables are the leaf nodes. Any leaf node that is not a return statement, is a piece
301   of code that is not needed and can be removed. We then recursively remove all the leaf nodes that
302   are not return statements.
303
      While removing such nodes (unreachable by the return statement) is useful, there could still be fea-
304   tures that are returned by the program but are not contributing. Recall that we use linear regression
305   on the list of features returned by the program. The weights assigned to individual features by the
306   regression model are useful indicators of which features are redundant. We remove the features that
307   have a significantly smaller weight compared to the largest weight in the regression (a threshold of
308   5% works well). Removing these features from the return statement results in numerous newly cre-
309   ated leaf nodes. We, therefore, redo the recursive leaf node removal to further simplify the program.
310   Each program generated by mutations and crossover is first improved through the critic and then
311   simplified before adding back to the population. Algorithm 1 shows the complete algorithm.

357   Observation Dataset: The problem seeks to predict the population density by observing the satellite

364   The predicted population densities are therefore also aggregated at the county block group level.
365   The metric is the per-block group level average L2 error after applying a log transformation. Along
366   with the arithmetic, and logical primitives (appendix B), we use open-vocabulary segmentation as a

      nightlight intensity, and elevation at the input location.

499   4.5   A BLATIONS
500
501   How important is the role of feature-set prediction, critic, and simplification? Table 3 mea-
502   sures the performance of our model on the task of population density as we successively add these
503   components to the evolutionary algorithm. The addition of feature set prediction instead of a single
504   feature helps, as it allows our method to learn expressive linear regression parameters instead of let-
505
      ting the LLM come up with them. Further adding critic results further improvement as the programs
      start covering nicher concepts resulting in better unseen and OOD generalization. Finally adding
506
      in simplification also improves hypothesis. We posit that simplification removes irrelevant features
507
      preventing the LLM to focus on them when performing crossovers.
508
509   How important are common sense and prior knowledge of LLMs? The two major differences
510   or our method from symbolic regression are: 1) better crossover and mutation as LLMs can under-
511   stand the meaning of the primitive functions. 2) use of prior knowledge for better guided search.
512   Therefore we remove these two sources of information and test how well can our method perform.
513   To remove the understanding of functions we rename them with meaningless terms and remove the
514   descriptions. To remove the context of the problem we remove objective prompt. Without common
515   sense, the search cannot even progress away from the initial random programs, resulting in worse-
516   than-mean results (L1 error of 0.84 vs 0.26 for DiSciPLE on density estimation). This suggests that
517
      symbolic regression models, that have no understanding of open-world primitives, would struggle
      to search. If we just remove the context of the problem the model does slightly better and can obtain
518
      results better than the mean and zero-shot hypotheses (L1 error of 0.45). This suggests that while the
519
      search is moving in the objective’s direction, it is slow. (see appendix D.3 for the complete table).
520   We also present more ablations of our method in appendix D.

522
523   5     D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
524
525   Limitations: A limitation of DiSciPLE is that we can only differentiably optimize learnable pa-
526
      rameters in the last computational layer. This could miss out on hypotheses with useful parameters
      in some intermediate computation layers. We attempted to make the whole pipeline differentiable,
527
      however the model performance did not improve much. Many of the operations in our pipeline even
528
      though differentiable have zero-gradient in large part of input space, making gradient optimization
529   challenging. Moreover, a completely differentiable hypothesis is even slower to optimize result-
530   ing in much slower evolution. In future work, we plan to use initialization tricks for non-linear
531   optimization and second-order optimization to obtain even more expressive models.
532
533   Conclusion: We present DiSciPLE – an evolutionary algorithm that leverages the prior-knowledge
534   and common sense abilities of LLMs to create interpretable, reliable and data-efficient hypotheses
535   for real-world scientific problem. This allows us to create hypotheses that are more powerful than
536   existing interpretable counterparts and more insightful than deeper uninterpretable models while
537   being on-par in terms of performance. We shows its prowess on 2 different expert scientific domain
538   on 4 different problems. We believe that using DiSciPLE in tandem with a human expert can rapidly
539
      speed up the scientific process and result in numerous novel discoveries.
