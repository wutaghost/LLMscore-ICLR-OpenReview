                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               Transformer-based language models have become the de facto standard in natural
               language processing. However, they underperform in the tabular data domain
011
               compared to traditional tree-based methods. We posit that current models fail to
012
               achieve the full potential of language models due to (i) heterogeneity of tabular
013            data; and (ii) challenges faced by the model in interpreting numerical values. Based
014            on this hypothesis, we propose the Tabular Domain Transformer (TDTransformer)
015            framework. TDTransformer has distinct embedding processes for different types
016            of columns. The alignment layers for different column-types transform these
017            embeddings to a common space. Besides, TDTransformer adapts piece-wise linear
018            encoding for numerical values for better performance. We test the proposed method
019            on 76 real-world tabular classification datasets from the OpenML benchmark.
020            Extensive experiments indicate that TDTransformer significantly improves the state-
021
               of-the-art methods. We release our code in https://anonymous.4open.
               science/r/tdtransformer.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Deep learning methods have achieved state-of-the-art (SOTA) performance in various areas including
      vision (Rombach et al., 2022; He et al., 2022; Zou et al., 2024), language (Radford et al., 2019;
028
      Touvron et al., 2023), and multimodal processing (Radford et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2023). Even though
029
      deep learning methods have shown great potential in many domains, their performance for tabular
030   data has so far been unimpressive. This has led to the question as to whether deep learning is a
031   fundamentally superior approach for tabular data (Shwartz-Ziv & Armon, 2022; Grinsztajn et al.,
032   2022; Borisov et al., 2022; McElfresh et al., 2024). Experimental benchmarks (Grinsztajn et al., 2022;
033   Borisov et al., 2022) have shown the broad superiority of tree-based methods over deep learning.
034   Among deep learning methods, the generalization of transformer-based architectures (Vaswani et al.,
035   2017) to tabular data has shown some promise — however, they continue to lag tree-based methods
036   such as XGBoost (Chen & Guestrin, 2016).
037   The broad-based success of transformers in learning high-dimensional representations, especially
038   in NLP, is evidence of their potential. A natural question arises as to what makes transformer-
039   based architectures underperform tree-based methods. Based on prior studies, we posit that this
040   phenomenon is a result of (i) difficulty in learning irregular patterns of the target function owing
041   to data heterogeneity (Shwartz-Ziv & Armon, 2022; Mathov et al., 2022; Borisov et al., 2023; Yan
042   et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024a), and (ii) the challenges faced by the model in interpreting numerical
043   features (Gorishniy et al., 2021; 2022).
044   On the one hand, spectral analysis of neural networks indicates that neural networks tend to learn the
045   low-frequency components of a function in lieu of relatively high-frequency components (Rahaman
046   et al., 2019; Xu et al., 2019; Beyazit et al., 2024). Owing to the different types of columns, the
047   feature spaces in the tabular data domain are generally heterogeneous. On the other hand, numerical
048   reasoning is known to be a formidable challenge for language models (Lu et al., 2022; Lee et al.,
049   2023; Shen et al., 2023; Testolin, 2024; Ahn et al., 2024).
050   We propose a framework named Tabular Domain Transformer (TDTransformer) that overcomes the
051   aforementioned obstacles in the way of achieving the full potential of transformer-based architectures.
052   TDTransformer embeds different types of table columns using different approaches to obtain the
053   hidden representations. For each column type, we use an alignment layer to map the hidden
      representation to a common embedding space. Alignment layers for different column types are


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      independent of one another. This design is inspired by multimodal models such as CLIP (Radford
055   et al., 2021) where one alignment layer transforms the hidden dimension of the image branch di to
056   a dimension d, ψi : di → d. The other one transforms the hidden dimension of the text branch dt
057   to d, ψt : dt → d. To enhance the model’s understanding of numerical values, we use a piecewise
058   linear encoding (PLE) that directly maps scalars to high-dimensional embeddings. Compared to
059   conventional tokenization and embedding, PLE introduces an inductive bias that is beneficial to the
060   training process. Our use of the PLE is inspired by the pioneering work of (Gorishniy et al., 2022).
061   We adapt PLE such that the hidden representation is close to the conventional hidden representation
062   of transformer-based architectures. We combine hidden representations as the input to the backbone
063
      model. The pipeline of the training process is the pre-training model followed by fine-tuning.
064   We examine the performance of TDTransformer on the standard tabular data benchmark OpenML 1 .
065   Extensive experiments on more than 70 tabular data sets show the superiority of TDTransformer. In
066   summary, the main contributions of this work are as follows:
067
068             • To avoid the performance degradation caused by the heterogeneous nature of tabular data,
069
                  we design different embedding approaches to obtain the hidden representations of columns.
                  Alignment layers are applied to hidden representations to ensure that embeddings for
070
                  different types of columns are in the same embedding space.
071
072             • We adapt the piece-wise linear encoding to improve the representation of numerical values
073               so that the model can interpret them well. These encoded representations are combined with
074
                  those of categorical and binary columns and then input to the backbone model.
075             • We propose a column-type dependent corruption for pre-training. We also propose a column-
076               type-aware positional encoding that further boosts the performance of TDTransformer.

      Tabular deep learning A key line of work in tabular deep learning focuses on the use of graph
081
      learning to enhance the understanding of relations among columns. An auxiliary knowledge graph
082   is used to regularize a multilayer perceptron (Ruiz et al., 2024). (Chen et al., 2024b) utilizes a
083   hypergraph to capture tabular structures. With the development of large-scale foundational models,
084   significant research has emerged on adapting foundation models in the tabular data domain. (Zhang
085   et al., 2023) uses parameter-efficient fine-tuning to adapt the pre-trained LLaMA 2 model to the
086   tabular domain (Touvron et al., 2023). (Zhu et al., 2024) converts tables to formats that are consistent
087   with the pre-training data (e.g. markdown format). The converted input data are directly fed to the
088   pre-trained LLaMA 2 model (Touvron et al., 2023). (Deng et al., 2024) treat tables as images and
089   utilize the multimodal capability of GPT-4 (Achiam et al., 2023) and Gemini (Team et al., 2023).
090
      (Hegselmann et al., 2023) serializes column names and values into a natural language string. Input
      strings are used for fine-tuning pre-trained large language models.
091
092
093
      Numerical reasoning Large language models mainly focus on NLP and code generation. However,
      their application to numerical reasoning has turned out to be less successful (Lewkowycz et al., 2022;
094
      Imani et al., 2023; Ahn et al., 2024; Romera-Paredes et al., 2024). This difficulty arises for multiple
095
      reasons: (i) numerical reasoning might require intricate intermediate steps internally. Language
096   models map scalars to high-dimensional embeddings. The intermediate steps with high dimensional
097   embeddings turn out to be inractable; (ii) there is no built-in mechanism within transformer-based
098   architectures to perform mathematical operations; (iii) numerical values are continuous, whereas
099   transformer-based architectures are inherently designed for (discrete) word toens; (iv) there are
100   repeated patterns in tokenized numerical values, and each token holds equal significance (while
101   omitting unimportant tokens).
102   (Geva et al., 2020) enhances numerical reasoning by adding automatically generated synthetic
103   numerical data to the pre-training process. (Lee et al., 2023) incorprates ideas from chain-of-
104   thought (Wei et al., 2022), with intermediate step results. (Shen et al., 2023) focuses on data format
105   modification to the boost model’s understanding of numerical values. (McLeish et al., 2024) helps
106   models track the position of each digit by adding an embedding that encodes its relative position.

310
      Baseline methods XGboost (Chen & Guestrin, 2016) is an end-to-end tree boosting system. It
      uses a sparsity-aware algorithm and weighted quantile sketch. Compared to XGBoost, CatBoost
311
      (Prokhorenkova et al., 2018; Dorogush et al., 2018) has the inherent capability to process categorical
312
      features without relying on one-hot encoding. Besides, it introduces ordered boosting to avoid target
313   leakage. SubTab (Ucar et al., 2021) divides input features into multiple subsets to perform multiview
314   representation learning. Scarf (Bahri et al., 2022) uses vanilla self-supervised contrastive learning to
315   improve classification accuracy in the fully-supervised learning setting. SwitchTab (Wu et al., 2024)
316   uses an asymmetric encoder-decoder framework to decouple mutual and salient features, which can
317   address the issue of lacking dependencies between samples.
318
319   Datasets We use 56 real-world tabular classification datasets in the standard OpenML bench-
320   mark (which are manually curated for effective benchmarking). The train/validation/test splits is
321   72%/8%/20% for each OpenML dataset. We use accuracy as the metric to measure the performance
322   for all classification data sets. Additionally, we use the area under the curve (AUC) to evaluate binary
323   classification and the F1 score t evaluate multiclass classification. The details of the tables are given
      in Section A.3 of the Appendix.

      Experimental details TDTransformer uses pre-trained BERT tokenizer (Devlin, 2018) and Adam
325   optimizer (Kingma, 2014) without weight decay. The hidden dimension is 512 and model depth is 12.
326   The number of quantiles for PLE is 64. In both the pre-training and fine-tuning process, we use an
327   early stopping strategy (Yao et al., 2007) with a patience of 10. The maximum number of training
328   epochs is 200 with batch size of 128. The corruption parameter of pre-training process is set to 0.5.
329   When there are empty cells in a column, we replace empty cells with the most common values in that
330   column. We conducted all epxeriments using a single A40 Tensor Core GPU and EPYC 7232P CPU.

      5   D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
499
      Our results advocate a rethink of the power of language models in the tabular data domain. A direct
500
      way of applying language models to the tabular data domain is to represent tables using sequences of
501
      word tokens. However, the heterogeneity property of tables hinders models from learning effective
502   representations (Shwartz-Ziv & Armon, 2022; Mathov et al., 2022; Borisov et al., 2023; Yan et al.,
503   2023; Chen et al., 2024a). TDTransformer explicitly uses distinct embedding processes for different
504   types of columns. Owing to the difference in embedding processes, the embedding spaces of
505   different types of columns are different. Specifically, TDTransformer uses PLE to encode the
506   statistical information of numerical columns in high-dimensional vectors while maintaining the
507   continuity of numerical values in the codomain of PLE function. Alignment layers are used to convert
508   embeddings of different types of columns to a common embedding space. TDTransformer utilizes the
509   good semantic understanding of language models. Some baseline methods with transformer-based
510
      architectures use one-hot encoded representation for categorical columns, which inherently loses
      semantic information. Those baselines lag behind tree-based methods. We find that language models
511
      might have unfavorable performance when a table has categorical columns that lack semantics. In
512
      addition, we find that positional encoding is important for the TDTransformer framework. The
513   embeddings of numerical and binary columns are essentially column-wise, while those of categorical
514   columns are token-wise. Based on this observation, we propose CTA positional encoding, which
515   can boost the performance of TDTransformer. Overall, TDTransformer is able to to overcome the
516   incapability of classical transformer-based architectures in interpreting heterogeneous data and to
517   enhance the ability of the model to interpret numerical values.
