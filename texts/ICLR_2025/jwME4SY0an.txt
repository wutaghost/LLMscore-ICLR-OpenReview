009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Training models to act as agents that can effectively navigate and perform actions in
012            a complex environment, such as a web browser, has typically been challenging due
013            to lack of training data. Large language models (LLMs) have recently demonstrated
014            some capability to navigate novel environments as agents in a zero-shot or few-shot
015            fashion, purely guided by natural language instructions as prompts. Recent research
016            has also demonstrated LLMs have the capability to exceed their base performance
017            through self-improvement, i.e. fine-tuning on data generated by the model itself. In
018
               this work, we explore the extent to which LLMs can self-improve their performance
               as agents in long-horizon tasks in a complex environment using the WebArena
019
               benchmark. In WebArena, an agent must autonomously navigate and perform
020
               actions on web pages to achieve a specified objective. We explore fine-tuning on
021            three distinct synthetic training data mixtures and achieve a 31% improvement in
022            task completion rate over the base model on the WebArena benchmark through a
023            self-improvement procedure. We additionally contribute novel evaluation metrics
024            for assessing the performance, robustness, capabilities, and quality of trajectories
025            of our fine-tuned agent models to a greater degree than simple, aggregate-level
026            benchmark scores currently used to measure self-improvement.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated impressive capabilities in a variety of natural
032   language processing (NLP) tasks such as summarization and question answering (Radford et al.,
033
      2019; Raffel et al., 2020; Brown et al., 2020) through zero-shot and few-shot prompting techniques
      (Ouyang et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2021). However, prompting techniques alone are insufficient to
034
      enable LLMs to act as agents and navigate environments in order to solve complex, multi-step, long-
035
      horizon tasks (Yao et al., 2023). Fine-tuning LLMs to perform such tasks is also infeasible due to the
036   scarcity of training data suitable for these tasks. Acquiring data for sequential decision-making and
037   complex interactions is not only time-consuming, but also costly. Additionally, automatic evaluation
038   of trajectories (or sequences of actions) taken by an agent is also difficult (Dinu et al., 2024). The
039   absence of metrics that accurately capture the efficacy of each step in a sequence complicates the
040   assessment of incremental improvements or degradations in an agent’s performance.
041
      A number of proposed self-improvement techniques have demonstrated that LLMs can use zero-
042   shot and few-shot prompting to achieve performance above the baseline without any additional
043   supervised training data (Huang et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2024). In place of supervised data as a
044   learning signal, many of these techniques use a self-critique technique (Weng et al., 2022; Yuan
045   et al., 2024), or obtain a critique through interactions with tools or environments (Gou et al., 2024).
046   While self-improvement techniques have shown promise on standard NLP benchmark tasks like
047   machine translation or question answering (Han et al., 2021; Huang et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2024),
048   their efficacy has not yet been thoroughly investigated for long-horizon tasks that require multi-step
049   interactions with a complex and realistic environment.
050   WebArena (Zhou et al., 2023) is a recently proposed benchmark wherein an LLM agent is required
051   to solve tasks using a web browser. One example WebArena task is to use the OpenStreetMap
052   website to answer the question “What is the minimum travel time by car from CMU to University of
053   Pittsburgh?”. Such a task requires an agent to complete a sequence of steps on the website, including
      entering a start location, entering a destination location, submitting a form, and then, reasoning

      In summary, our contributions are:
086
087          • We propose and detail procedures for collecting and generating synthetic training examples
088            for complex, multi-step tasks involving interaction with an environment. We explore
089            collecting in-domain synthetic examples of trajectories as well as generating synthetic
090            examples of solution trajectories for novel, out-of-domain tasks.
091          • We propose auxiliary metrics to understand the effect self-improvement has with respect to
092            acquiring new capabilities and to evaluate variable-length trajectories produced by agents
093            through an extension of the VERTEX score. These metrics provide nuanced insights not
094            captured by aggregate-level benchmark scores currently used to evaluate self-improvement,
095            allowing us to better assess the effect self-improvement has on multiple dimensions: perfor-
096            mance, robustness, capability acquisition, and the quality of generated trajectories.
097          • We show that the performance of LLM agents improves after fine-tuning on this synthetic
098            data, demonstrating that self-improving techniques work for a new class of tasks. We
099            analyze three synthetic training data mixtures and find all three mixtures improve capability
100            acquisition, with the best performing mixture yielding a 31% improvement over the base
101            LLM agent on the WebArena benchmark. While we find models can self-improve once with
102
               our procedure, we find models can not iteratively self-improve.

105
106   Self-improvement techniques for large language models typically involve using the model’s own gen-
107   erations to create synthetic few-shot examples (Han et al., 2021) or synthetic fine-tuning data (Huang
      et al., 2022). These techniques amplify knowledge, correct behaviors, and introduce regularization

      (Pham et al., 2022), often leading to an overall boost in performance. The self-generated examples
109   are often filtered, post-edited, or ranked with a set of unsupervised techniques such as self-critique to
110   introduce a signal for learning and improvement (Weng et al., 2022; Patel et al., 2023; Chen et al.,
111   2024; Yuan et al., 2024). For multi-step agent tasks, the environment itself can additionally provide
112   the LLM agent a way to detect failure in a fully unsupervised manner, which provides another useful
113   signal for learning (Gou et al., 2024; Yuan et al., 2023; Song et al., 2024).
114
      Using the WebArena benchmark (Zhou et al., 2023), we define and experiment with both in-domain
115   synthetic training examples and out-of-domain synthetic training examples for web agent tasks, and
116   fine-tune on three different synthetic data mixtures: Mixture A (in-domain synthetic examples only),
117   Mixture B (both in-domain and out-of-domain synthetic examples), and Mixture C (out-of-domain
118   synthetic examples only). Figure 1 illustrates our process.

      the base model. We filter out any trajectories where the model self-detected failure (self-critique)
123

127   set of plausible trajectories can serve as reasonably high-quality in-domain synthetic examples for
128   fine-tuning. Similar to the self-improvement prior work we discuss earlier, the collection of this data
129   is completely unsupervised and no ground-truth labels are utilized for filtering and selection.

144   The WebArena environment can be formulated as a partially observable Markov decision process:
145   E = ⟨S, A, O, T ⟩, where S represents the state space, A represents the action space, O represents
146   the observation space, and T : S × A → S is the deterministic transition function (Zhou et al.,
147   2023). An agent model M produces a next action at ∈ A provided an objective represented by some
148   natural language intent i, the current observation ot ∈ O, and the previous action taken at−1 ∈ A:
149   (i, ot , at−1 ). This continues for T time steps until the agent produces a stop action or the environment
150
      produces an error or stop condition. The models M we select for our experiments are the Qwen-1.5-
      72B-Chat model (Bai et al., 2023) and the Llama-3-70B-Instruct model (Dubey et al., 2024), which
151
      at the time of this work are highly ranked 1 and competitive open source LLMs (Chiang et al., 2024)
152
      that are accessible for fine-tuning. Further choice of inference parameters and other configuration
153   details can be found in Appendix A.

      3.1    F UNCTIONAL C ORRECTNESS S CORE
271
272   Functional correctness is the standard metric proposed by the WebArena benchmark that is a simple
273   binary task completion score (0 or 1) averaged over all 812 tasks in the benchmark.
274
275   3.2    C APABILITY S CORE (N EW )
276
277   While WebArena contains 812 unique task instances, these 812 tasks are instantiated using natural
278   language intent templates like “What is the minimum travel time by car from {{location1}} to
279
      {{location2}}?”. Therefore, many tasks actually test the same capability. Aggregate-level metrics
      like the functional correctness score may be misleading since improvements may only be due to the
280
      model becoming more robust at solving capabilities it already could solve versus demonstrating the
281
      ability to solve new capabilities that were previously unsolvable. There are 241 unique templates
282   in WebArena that are used to instantiate 812 tasks. Moreover, some of these templates are simple
283   paraphrases of each other. For example, “What is the estimated driving time between {{city1}}
284   and {{city2}}?” is a paraphrase of the prior template. Using a sentence similarity model,2 we
285   iteratively group these templates into a set of unique capabilities. Each template is grouped with
286   any existing capability if it has a similarity of > 0.60 with any template in the group, otherwise the
287   template is added to a new capability group. This results in 136 unique capabilities (see Appendix F).
288   A model receives a score of 1 for each capability group with at least one successful task completed,
289   otherwise it receives a score of 0.3 The capability score is then the averaged over all 136 capabilities.
290   We note, however, that a number of tasks in the WebArena benchmark are trivial tasks and can be
291   solved by a trivial baseline agent or weak model that performs no actions and only immediately exits
292   by always generating stop [N/A]. In the capability score computation, we do not count such trivial
293   tasks as evidence a model can perform the capability as these are degenerate cases of the capability.
294
295   3.3    VERTEX DTW S CORE (N EW )
296
297   Both functional correctness and the capability score only evaluate task completion, however, they do
298
      not assess the quality of entire trajectories, therefore, a measure that is sensitive to incremental im-
      provements and degradations in trajectories, independent of task completion, is desirable. We extend
299
      the recently proposed VERTEX score (Dinu et al., 2024), which measures the similarity of two rela-
300
      tional trajectories by using embeddings to compare node distributions within a computational graph.
301   The VERTEX score integrates the semantic meaning across the distributional path by computing at
302   each node the cross-similarity between the generated embeddings and embeddings sampled from a
303   reference distribution. An ideal reference distribution would be ground-truth reference trajectories
304   produced by humans for all of the WebArena tasks. In absence of this, we use a larger, stronger
305   model, GPT-4 (OpenAI et al., 2024), to collect three reference trajectories for each task.
306
      One obstacle to the straightforward application of the VERTEX score is the assumption that both
307   trajectories are of the same length. Agents operating in complex environments, however, are not
308   constrained to a fixed-length for the trajectories they produce. Therefore, we propose modification in
309   the computation of the VERTEX score that enables comparison of sequences with different lengths.
310   Our extension consists of an additional alignment step prior to calculating the VERTEX score for
311   the aligned trajectories. First, we embed all steps of a trajectory X as et = f (ot , at ) ∈ Rd , where f
312   is an embedding model 4 with embedding dimension d. The embedding model f is independent of
313   both the model that generated the reference trajectories as well as the model that generated the test
314   trajectories. Then, we use Dynamic Time Warping (DTW) (Berndt and Clifford, 1994) to align two
315   embedded trajectories X̃m = (e0 , . . . , ei , . . . , em ) ∈ Rm×d and X̃n = (e0 , . . . , ej , . . . , en ) ∈ Rn×d
316
      with length m and n, respectively. Consequently, we refer to our proposed measure as VERTEXDTW .
317   DTW returns an alignment path ν of length T , where each ei ∈ X̃m is aligned with a corresponding
318   ej ∈ X̃n , preserving the order in their respective trajectory. This order preservation occurs because
319
      once a node is matched, it is excluded from potential new matches, maintaining the integrity of the
      temporal alignment. As a scoring function for DTW, we choose cosine distance. In addition to the

399   Can models self-improve at web agent tasks? We find fine-tuning on both Mixtures A and B
400   improve overall benchmark performance with the best performing mixture, Mixture B, completing 18
401   more tasks correctly, a 31% relative improvement (7.14 → 9.36). Training on all Mixtures A, B, and
402   C demonstrate self-improvement on at least one metric, with MC showing a gain on capability score.
403
404   Do self-improved agents acquire new capabilities? We find agent models can acquire new
405   capabilities through self-improvement, however, they also may lose the ability to perform some
406
      capabilities. In net, all of our self-improved agents acquire more capabilities than they lose. We find
      fine-tuning on both Mixtures A and B improve the capability score equally and lead to the largest
407
      net acquisition of capabilities demonstrating 5 more capabilities than the base agent model, a 24%
408
      relative improvement (15.44 → 19.12). We find all agent models demonstrate at least one new
409   capability that no other agent model demonstrates, for example, only MC successfully completes
410   the “Fork {{repo}}” capability on the GitLab website. Interestingly, we find that the majority
411   of capabilities acquired by MA and MC are mutually exclusive, suggesting in-domain synthetic
412   examples and out-of-domain synthetic examples improve acquisition of different capabilities. We list
413   all capabilities MA , MB , and MC acquire and lose compared to M in Appendix C.
414
415   Are self-improved agents more robust? For MB , we find a larger improvement in functional
416   correctness (31%) than in capability score (24%), which supports that the agent model is improving
417   at more consistently succeeding at tasks belonging to the same capability, an indicator of one type of
418   robustness. MC is less robust by the same measure. Moreover, the capability analysis in Appendix
419   C also shows both MA and MB after self-improvement still demonstrate the majority of capabilities
420
      demonstrated by the base agent model M, whereas MC only demonstrates a minority. This would
      indicate MA and MB more reliably maintain the capabilities of the base agent model after self-
421
      improvement, a measure of robustness that would be useful in deployed settings where users of agent
422
      models may desire stability in performance.
423
424
      Is there an effect on the quality of generated trajectories? Fine-tuning on Mixtures A and B
425
      show no degradation in the quality of generated trajectories and show small improvement towards
426   the reference on VERTEXDTW . Fine-tuning on Mixture C degrades the the quality of generated
427   trajectories from the reference. Training on the out-of-domain synthetic examples allows MC
428   to demonstrate some unique capabilities no other agent model demonstrates, however, inspecting
429   trajectories from MC , we find this comes with trade-offs. For example, compared with MA , we find
430   MC produces longer trajectories (~1.6x) and produces more invalid actions (~3.9x). In comparison
431   with M, MA and MB do not greatly increase trajectory length (~1.1x and ~1.3x) or the rate of
      invalid actions (~1x and ~1.3x), further explaining the quality difference VERTEXDTW highlights.

      Due to lack of human reference, the reliability of this evaluation is limited which we discuss in Section

436   Can models iteratively self-improve at web agent tasks? Our results are consistent with prior
437   works such as Chen et al. (2024) and Feng et al. (2024) and we find diminishing returns to successive
438   rounds of self-improvement and training on synthetic data. While the agent model after a second
439   round of self-improvement outperforms the base agent model, it does not perform any better than
440   agent models with a single round of self-improvement. We analyze the set of plausible trajectories
441   in the second round in Appendix E and find that while more synthetic training examples can be
442   collected, they are of lower quality and contain a higher proportion of failed trajectories.

444   6    R ELATED W ORK
445
446   Self-Improvement A number of techniques have been proposed for self-improving LLMs (Huang
447   et al., 2022; Weng et al., 2022; Madaan et al., 2023, inter alia). Some self-improvement techniques
448   (Han et al., 2021; Gulcehre et al., 2023; Singh et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024; Yuan et al., 2024)
449   involve self-distillation (Zhang et al., 2019), a special form of knowledge distillation (Hinton et al.,
450   2015) where the teacher and student are the same model. A growing trend of works (Wang et al.,
451   2023; Gunasekar et al., 2023) similarly prompt LLMs to generate synthetic fine-tuning data.
452
453   LLM Agents A number of prompting techniques proposed (Kojima et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2022;
454   Yao et al., 2023; Shinn et al., 2023) can improve an LLM agent’s performance, however, these
455
      techniques are orthogonal to self-improvement fine-tuning. Chen et al. (2023) introduces a technique
      for supervised fine-tuning of LLM agents. Sodhi et al. (2024) and Lai et al. (2024) introduce
456
      handcrafted subprompts or supervised techniques that improve performance on WebArena.
457
458
      Self-Improving Agents Bousmalis et al. (2023) demonstrates self-improving embodied agents
459
      for complex robotics tasks. Aksitov et al. (2024) introduces a method for self-improving agents on
460   a simpler multi-step question answering task. Concurrently, Song et al. (2024) proposes a similar
461   procedure of filtering trajectories and fine-tuning, but primarily focuses on supervised filtering, does
462   not explore generating novel tasks and synthetic data, and evaluates on less realistic and complex
463   benchmarks. Pan et al. (2024) explores using vision models for critique to improve on WebArena.
464

      7    L IMITATIONS AND B ROADER I MPACTS
467
      While we find self-improvement fine-tuning techniques can improve performance by reinforcing
468
      correct actions and decisions of an underlying model, these techniques can also further reinforce
469
      incorrect actions and biases of the underlying model. Some human or supervised filtering may
470   mitigate this drawback, however, in this paper we focus our investigation on the efficacy and quality
471   of unsupervised self-improvement as producing datasets for such complex tasks is difficult and
472   expensive. Our analysis of capabilities is limited by our method to group tasks by the intent template
473   used and cosine similarity. It is possible other strategies may produce more optimal groups to measure
474   capabilities. Our VERTEXDTW score utilizes a stronger model’s generations (GPT-4) as a reference,
475   however, human references would significantly improve the reliability of this evaluation. While
476   WebArena spans many different types of realistic tasks and websites (shopping, online forums, maps,
477   etc.), a future direction for this work might involve evaluation on larger, and more diverse benchmark.
478
      Some hyperparameter choices and parameter choices are chosen arbitrarily, within the limits of
      what was computationally feasible during our experiments and future work may seek to explore the
479
      sensitivity of such techniques to hyperparameters.

482   8    C ONCLUSION
483
484   In this work, we explore whether large language models can self-improve beyond their base perfor-
485   mance at complex, long-horizon web agent tasks. We conclude self-improvement can increase the
      performance and robustness of agent models and allow agent models to acquire new capabilities.

      We also find it is possible for self-improvement to yield these benefits with minimal degradation

488   towards boosting the performance of LLMs in complex, multi-step agent environments such as web
489   environments, without relying on supervised training data. We release our code, evaluation metrics
490   with references, synthetic datasets, and model trajectories.
