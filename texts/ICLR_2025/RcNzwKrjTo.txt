009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Standard conformal prediction offers a marginal guarantee on coverage, but for pre-
012            diction sets to be truly useful, they should ideally ensure coverage conditional on
013            each test point. However, it is impossible to achieve exact, distribution-free condi-
014            tional coverage in finite samples. In this work, we propose an alternative conformal
015            prediction algorithm that targets coverage where it matters most—in instances
016            where a classifier is overconfident in its incorrect predictions. We start by dissect-
017
               ing miscoverage events in marginally-valid conformal prediction, and show that
               miscoverage rates vary based on the classifier’s confidence and its deviation from
018
               the Bayes optimal classifier. Motivated by this insight, we develop a variant of con-
019
               formal prediction that targets coverage conditional on a reduced set of two variables:
020            the classifier’s confidence in a prediction and a nonparametric trust score that mea-
021            sures its deviation from the Bayes classifier. Empirical evaluation on multiple image
022            datasets shows that our method generally improves conditional coverage properties
023            compared to standard conformal prediction, including class-conditional coverage,
024            coverage over arbitrary subgroups, and coverage over demographic groups.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Machine learning models are envisioned to inform decision-making in high-stakes applications such
030   as medical diagnosis (Kumar et al., 2023; Elfanagely et al., 2021; Caruana et al., 2015). Consequently,
031
      there is a critical need for actionable and useful uncertainty estimates to mitigate the risks associated
      with incorrect decisions influenced by overconfident models. Conformal prediction (Vovk et al.,
032
      2005) is a framework for constructing prediction sets that provide finite-sample marginal coverage
033
      guarantees without making any modeling or distributional assumptions beyond exchangeability; i.e.,
034   the sets contain the correct output with a probability specified by the user. Given a calibration dataset
035   {(Xi , Yi )}ni=1 and a new test point (Xn+1 , Yn+1 ), split conformal prediction (referred to as simply
036   “conformal prediction” from here on) constructs a prediction set C(Xn+1 ) ⊆ Y that satisfies,
037
                                          P(Yn+1 ∈ C(Xn+1 )) ≥ 1 − α,                                       (1)
038
039   for α ∈ (0, 1). However, marginal validity does not necessarily ensure that the prediction sets are ac-
040   tionable in arbitrary contexts, as coverage can be unacceptably poor for individual predictions.
041
      To highlight the limitations of marginal validity, consider a scenario where a model is used to diagnose
042   a disease in a population where 90% of the cases are straightforward to diagnose, while 10% are
043   challenging. Prediction sets that only cover in the straightforward cases may achieve 90% marginal
044   coverage, but may be useless for the cases where uncertainty estimates are most critical for clinicians.
045   Ideally, one would like to construct prediction sets that satisfy conditional coverage; i.e., P(Yn+1 ∈
046   C(Xn+1 )|Xn+1 = x) ≥ 1 − α, ∀x. Unfortunately, however, it is well known that it is impossible to
047   attain distribution-free exact conditional coverage in a meaningful sense (Lei & Wasserman, 2014;
048   Vovk, 2012; Barber et al., 2021). The question becomes one of approximate conditional coverage.
049   In this work, we introduce a relaxed objective for conditional coverage and a variant of conformal
050   prediction that ensures prediction sets adapt to the true uncertainty of test instances. Since the feature
051   space X ⊆ Rd can be high-dimensional, achieving approximate X-conditional coverage is challeng-
052   ing. Gibbs et al. (2023) proposed a method that can theoretically achieve conditional coverage with
053   respect to any function class; however, implementing this with a function class that corresponds to
      X-conditional coverage is infeasible. Our key idea is to instead identify a lower-dimensional variable

098

105    Group-conditional conformal prediction. A widely adopted relaxation of conditional coverage in
106    prior work is based on group-conditional guarantees of the form P(Yn+1 ∈ C(Xn+1 ) | Xn+1 ∈ G) ≥
107    1 − α for all groups G ∈ G (Vovk et al., 2003; Barber et al., 2021; Jung et al., 2023). This concept
       is often motivated by the idea that instead of X-conditional coverage, one can ensure the coverage

108
      guarantee holds over predetermined subgroups that might otherwise be underserved by marginal
109   coverage (Romano et al., 2020a). Mondrian conformal prediction (Vovk et al., 2003) achieves
110   exact group-conditional coverage in finite samples when the groups in G are disjoint. The proce-
111   dure involves splitting the calibration data into subgroups and then separately calibrating on each
112   group. Within this framework, Romano et al. (2020a) focus on achieving equal coverage over disjoint
113   protected groups of interest. Barber et al. (2021) propose an approach that allows groups in G to
114   overlap; however, this method can be highly conservative and result in wide prediction intervals that
115   over-cover. To provide practical, “multivalid” coverage guarantees over arbitrary subgroups, (Jung
116   et al., 2023) propose learning P
                                     quantile multiaccurate predictors by minimizing the pinball loss over
117   the class of functions F = { G∈G βG 1{x ∈ G} : β ∈ R|G| }. While Jung et al. (2023) provide
118
      PAC-style guarantees, Gibbs et al. (2023) propose a conditional conformal procedure that yields exact
      coverage guarantees over arbitrary collection of groups in finite samples, and also extends beyond the
119
      group setting to finite-dimensional classes. That said, as noted earlier, it is not feasible to run this
120
      method with a function class that can guarantee approximate X-conditional coverage. Our work is
121   complementary to this line of work: we propose a practical way to construct a function class that
122   guarantees an interpretable notion of conditional coverage broader than group-conditional coverage
123   over pre-specified groups.
124
      Learning features from data for improved conditional coverage. We note there are some recent
125
      works relevant to us that share similar motivation of learning features from data to improve conditional
126
      coverage; however our methodology presented herein differs greatly. Yuksekgonul et al. (2023)
127   propose a density-based atypicality notion to improve calibration and conditional coverage with
128   respect to input atypicality. They implement a special case of Mondrian conformal prediction to
129   improve coverage in high atypicality or low confidence groups. In our work, the goal is to improve
130   conditional coverage more generally beyond the chosen statistic V and our evaluation reflects the
131   same. Our method is also very different from Mondrian conformal prediction as we discuss. (Kiyani
132   et al., 2024) propose to learn partitioning of the covariate space such that points in the same partition
133   are similar in terms of their prediction sets in order to improve conditional validity. They present
134   an algorithm that iteratively updates the partitioning and prediction sets over a given calibration
135
      data set. While they learn low-dimensional features from the calibration data, we study general
      patterns of miscoverage in standard conformal prediction and propose a two-dimensional statistic that
136
      consistently shows to be effective. It would be interesting to explore their approach in our evaluation
137
      setup that extends beyond pre-defined groups. In the regression setting, (Sesia & Romano, 2021)
138   learn conditional histograms from the data to detect the skewness of Y | X and estimate the quantiles
139   of the conditional distribution. Guan (2022) propose a localized conformal prediction framework to
140   adapt to the heterogeneity of the conditional distribution. They propose weighting the conformal
141   scores differently based on the observed feature value Xn+1 of the test sample.
142
      Other approaches for improving conditional coverage. With the goal of achieving approximate X-
143
      conditional coverage, previous works have proposed new conformity score functions (Romano et al.,
144   2019; 2020b; Angelopoulos et al., 2021) that yield significant practical improvements. Ding et al.
145   (2023) focus on achieving class-conditional coverage instead, and introduce a clustered conformal
146   prediction method for Y -conditional coverage. As argued by Angelopoulos et al. (2021) and Ding
147   et al. (2023), we note that in high-signal problems like image classification where Y is perfectly
148   determined by X, X-conditional coverage is less interpretable as an objective.

      Consider a scenario where the feature space X is discrete, and we have access to a large calibration set
226   that includes all possible values in X . In this case, one approach to constructing conditionally valid
227   sets is to select a distinct threshold q̂ in (3) for each x ∈ X . This procedure would assign a larger q̂ for
228   instances where the model fˆ is more prone to errors, and a smaller q̂ where errors are less likely. How-
229   ever, in practice, this procedure is infeasible because X is typically high-dimensional or continuous.
230   More generally, Lei & Wasserman (2014) and Barber et al. (2021) have shown that distribution-free
231   conditionally valid predictive inference is impossible to attain meaningfully.
232
      The key idea behind our proposed method is to condition on a lower-dimensional statistic V ∈ V,
233
      rather than the full feature space X , where |V| ≪ |X |. We select this statistic as a proxy to identify
234   test instances prone to error, enabling the selection of a distinct threshold q̂ for each v ∈ V, rather than
235   conditioning on each individual point in X . While this procedure does not achieve X-conditional
236   coverage, it provides coverage conditioned on the likelihood of under-coverage by STANDARD. The
237   miscoverage patterns discussed in Section 2.1 motivate the selection of V as V = {Conf, Rank}.
238   Consequently, instead of strict conditional coverage, P(Yn+1 ∈ C(Xn+1 ) | Xn+1 = x) ≥ 1 − α, for
239   all x ∈ X , we adopt a more relaxed notion of conditional coverage as follows:
240
                  P(Yn+1 ∈ C(Xn+1 ) | Conf(Xn+1 ) = c, Rank(Xn+1 , Yn+1 ) = r) ≥ 1 − α,                        (6)
241
242   for all c ∈ [0, 1] and r ∈ {1, . . . , |Y|}. However, the label Yn+1 is not available at test time, and thus
243   Rank(Xn+1 , Yn+1 ) cannot be used to construct the prediction sets. In the next section, we propose an
244
      alternative to the Rank(X, Y ) variable that can be computed using calibration data.

401

402

403   performance (lowest CovGap) across different binning schemes in nearly all experimental settings.
404
405   Focusing our attention on the Conf × Trust and Conf × Rank CovGap metrics, we observe that

406

407

408

409   NAIVE having access to Conf × Trust bin information during the calibration phase. The improved
410   performance of CONDITIONAL compared to STANDARD as well as NAIVE empirically validates
411   our choice of V . We can see this improved conditional coverage is achieved with sets that are not
412   significantly larger than STANDARD (and, in fact, smaller in Places365-LT).
413

414

418
      Conf × Trust and Conf × Rank , we also measure approximate conditional coverage via a notion of

419

420
      the figure from [rmin , rmax ] (see Appendix B.4 for further details on the choice of r and simulation

      5            D ISCUSSION
473
474   Conformal prediction guarantees exact coverage marginally across test samples in finite samples. How-
475   ever, for uncertainty sets to be truly meaningful and useful, they should provide coverage conditional
476   on test instances where uncertainty is higher or where the model is more likely to err. Unfortunately,
477
      achieving X-conditional coverage in finite samples is impossible without making distributional as-
      sumptions. In this paper, we propose a relaxed notion of conditional coverage that improves the practi-
478
      cal utility of prediction sets by ensuring coverage where it matters most—specifically, in cases where a
479
      classifier is overconfident in its incorrect predictions. To assess a classifier’s overconfidence, we
480   use its reported confidence (softmax probabilities) in combination with a nonparametric trust score
481   that measures its alignment with the Bayes classifier. We develop a practical variant of conformal
482   prediction that achieves approximate conditional coverage with respect to these two variables, and
483   demonstrate that it improves conditional coverage properties in a general sense, including subgroup-
484   level and class-conditional coverage. By reducing the coverage gap across relevant subpopulations,
485   our resulting prediction sets can lead to fairer and improved downstream decision-making, especially
      in high-stakes applications where miscoverage can be consequential.


                                                                                                      9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      While we show trust scores significantly improve conditional coverage in conformal prediction, they
487   also come with limitations. Particularly, in cases where the trust scores are not a good approximate of
488   the rank of the true class, our method may not improve conditional coverage properties over standard
489   conformal prediction. To add, our function class is susceptible to computational difficulties at higher
490   polynomial degrees beyond a threshold. Future work can explore more sophisticated function classes
491   to achieve our proposed conditional coverage objective.

697   B.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
698
699   We set α = 0.1 for our empirical evaluation. We run all our experiments with 10 random seeds
700   {1, . . . , 10} and report the standard errors in our results. The randomness in our experiments is
701   over splitting the validation set into calibration and evaluation data and fitting the temperature
      parameter (Guo et al., 2017).


                                                                13
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


702
      Evaluation Metrics. For our conditional coverage evaluation, we use the notion of CovGap
703   (inspired by class coverage gap in Ding et al. (2023)) to measure the coverage gap across multiple
704   bins in the feature space. We propose three binning schemes: Conf × Trust, Conf × Rank, and
705   Class-conditional CovGap. To apply two-dimensional binning, we split the samples into evenly
706   spaced bins based on Conf and then split each confidence bin into equal-size bins based on Trust
707   score or Rank depending on the binning scheme. For both two-dimensional binning schemes, we
708   split the samples into 10 evenly spaced confidence bins and then 4 equal-size bins based on trust
709   score or rank. We choose this splitting to have appreciable granularity while also ensuring most bins
710   have sufficient number of samples in all cases. As a special case for ImageNet, we manually edit the
711
      Rank bins as ∼ 75% test samples have accurate predictions.

      We follow the data processing steps and pretrained models used by Yuksekgonul et al. (2023) in our

      ImageNet. ImageNet (Russakovsky et al., 2015) is a large-scale image classification dataset with

      1000 classes. ImageNet has roughly balanced class distributions. We use the ImageNet-1k version

721

725   use the validation split as our calibration set and use the test set for evaluation. Following Yuksekgonul

729   Places365. Places365 (Zhou et al., 2018) contains 10 million images from 365 classes. For

733   Places365-LT. Places365-LT is the long-tailed version of Places365 (Liu et al., 2019). Places365-
734   LT was constructed from the original dataset using a Pareto distribution with a power value α = 6,
735   and has a maximum of 4980 images per class and a minimum of 5 images per class. We use the
736   validation split as our calibration set and use the test set for evaluation. Following Yuksekgonul et al.
737
      (2023), we use the ResNet152 model trained on Places365-LT by (Zhisheng Zhong & Jia, 2021) in
      our experiments.

739
740
      Fitzpatrick 17k. Fitzpatrick 17k (Groh et al., 2021) is a dataset of clinical images with skin

      training script provided by Groh et al. (2021) to train a ResNet18 model (pretrained on ImageNet)
742

743   group-conditional coverage evaluation, we consider Fitzpatrick skin types 1 through 6 as well as the

        B.4                             D ETAILS ON APPROXIMATE X- CONDITIONAL COVERAGE EVALUATION
760
761    We share details regarding the experimental setup for evaluating approximate X-conditional coverage
762    (Figure 2). In this experiment, we evaluate the coverage gap between randomly sampled ℓ2 balls in
763    the feature space. We compute the Euclidean distance between features Xi and Xj from the model’s
764    penultimate layer. We vary the radius r of the ℓ2 balls, where r is evenly spaced in the interval
765    [rmin , rmax ]. rmin and rmax are estimated as the minimum and 90th percentile of the distribution of
766    distances between randomly sampled pairs of points for each dataset, respectively. For a fixed r, we
767    randomly sample 100 test points and find Euclidean balls with radius r around the test point such that
768    every ball should have at least 10 neighboring points. Then, we compute the coverage gap over these
769
       regions using Eq. 15. We perform 100 trials of this procedure for each r for all datasets, and report
       the standard errors by error bars.
