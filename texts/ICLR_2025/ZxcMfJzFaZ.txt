009                                                            A BSTRACT
010
011               Adversarial training (AT) is a common technique for enhancing neural network
012               robustness. Typically, AT updates all trainable parameters, but such comprehensive
013               adjustments can lead to overfitting and increased generalization errors on clean data.
014               Research suggests that fine-tuning specific parameters may be more effective; how-
015               ever, methods for identifying these essential parameters and establishing effective
016
                  optimization objectives remain unclear and inadequately addressed. We present
                  CLAT, an innovative adversarial fine-tuning algorithm that mitigates adversarial
017
                  overfitting by integrating "criticality" into the training process. Instead of tuning
018
                  the entire model, CLAT identifies and fine-tunes fewer parameters in robustness-
019               critical layers—those predominantly learning non-robust features—while keeping
020               the rest of the model fixed. Additionally, CLAT employs a dynamic layer selection
021               process that adapts to changes in layer criticality during training. Empirical results
022               demonstrate that CLAT can be seamlessly integrated with existing adversarial
023               training methods, enhancing clean accuracy and adversarial robustness by over 2%
024               compared to baseline approaches.

066   layers which have learned non-robust features dominantly. Subsequently, our algorithm meticulously
067   fine-tunes these critical layers to remove their non-robust features and reduce their criticality, while
068   freezing the other, non-critical layers. Dynamic selection of critical layers is conducted during the

087   2.1   A DVERSARIAL TRAINING
088
      Adversarial Training (AT) was first introduced by (Goodfellow et al., 2015), who demonstrated how
089   the integration of adversarial examples into the training process could substantially improve model
090   robustness. This idea evolved into a sophisticated minimax optimization approach with Projected
091   Gradient Descent Adversarial Training (PGD-AT) (Madry et al., 2019), which employs PGD attacks
092   in training. Regarded as the gold standard in AT, PGD-AT generates adversarial training samples
093   using multiple steps of projected gradient descent, leading to substantially improved empirical
094   robustness (Carlini & Wagner, 2017; Athalye et al., 2018; Croce & Hein, 2020). Further refining
095   this approach, TRADES (Zhang et al., 2019) optimizes a novel loss function to balance classification
096   accuracy with adversarial robustness. Recent enhancements in AT, including model ensemble and data
097
      augmentation, have also produced notable improvements in model resilience. (Xie et al., 2020; Yang
      et al., 2020; Carmon et al., 2022). Inkawhich et al. (2019) propose “Activation Attacks” (AA) which
098
      underscore the efficacy of leveraging intermediate model layers for generating stronger adversarial
099
      attacks, suggesting that incorporating AA in adversarial training could fortify defenses. Their findings
100   provide a foundation for our method which integrates these intermediate critical layers into our
101   adversarial training strategy.
102
103   2.2   A DVERSARIAL TRAINING IMPROVEMENTS AND ROBUST OVERFITTING
104
105   Adversarial training methods like PGD-AT and TRADES are computationally expensive and prone
106   to overfitting, requiring multi-step adversary generation, complex objectives, and extensive model
107   tuning (Shafahi et al., 2019). To improve efficiency, Shafahi et al. (2019) proposed “Free” AT, which
      accelerates training by using a single backpropagation step for both training and PGD adversary


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      generation. However, gradient alignment issues led to reduced robustness and increased overfitting.
109   Similarly, Wong et al. (2020) introduced “Fast” AT, but it also suffered from similar weaknesses
110   (Andriushchenko & Flammarion, 2020), prompting the development of GradAlign. Unfortunately,
111   GradAlign tripled training time due to second-order gradient computation. Later efforts sought to
112   address robust overfitting. RiFT (Zhu et al., 2023) improved general performance by leveraging
113   layer redundancies but was constrained by heuristic redundancy measurements. Zhang et al. (2024)
114   mitigated overfitting by disentangling natural and adversarial objectives, yet model-wide adjustments
115   still limited robustness. In contrast, CLAT uses a theoretically grounded, dynamic, critical layer
116   selection mechanism, resulting in improved robust generalization by tuning a critical subset of layers.
117
      Furthermore, CLAT is agnostic to attack generation methods in the AT processing, making it an ideal
      complement to existing fast-AT methods to mitigate overfitting.

120   3     M ETHODS
121
122   Building on prior attack and defense research (Inkawhich et al., 2019; 2020; Zhu et al., 2023) which
123   demonstrates that not all model layers equally learn non-robust features and having all layers learn
124   robust features leads to overfitting, we aim to improve model robustness by identifying and fine-tuning
125   only those critical layers that are prone to learning non-robust features, while keeping the non-critical
126
      layers frozen. In this section, we begin by defining and identifying critical layers, then outline our
      objectives for reducing their criticality. Finally, we present our complete CLAT algorithm, which
127
      effectively mitigates overfitting and enhances model robustness.

      3.1   L AYER C RITICALITY
130
131   Consider a deep learning model with n layers, and an input x, defined as:
132
133                                        F (x) = fn (fn−1 (. . . f1 (x))),                                  (1)
134   where the functionality of the i-th layer is denoted as fi . During the standard training process, all
135   layers learn useful features which contribute to the correct outputs of the model. We denote the
136   hidden feature learned at the output of the i-th layer as Fi (x) = fi (fi−1 (. . . f1 (x))).
137

138

139

140   computation, we consider the worst-case feature difference under a fixed input perturbation budget of
141   ϵ. This leads to our definition of the ϵ-weakness of layer i’s feature as:

156   For the purpose of mitigating overfitting, we want to identify the layers that are the most critical to the
157   lack of robustness, characterized by their increased susceptibility to perturbations from adversarial
158   inputs. Other already-robust layers shall then be fixed to avoid further overfitting to the adversarial
159   training objective. We therefore provide the following definition:
160   Definition 3.1. Critical layer: A layer is considered critical if it exhibits a greater propensity to
161   learn non-robust features or demonstrates diminished robustness to adversarial input perturbations
      relative to other layers in the model.

      3.3   CLAT ADVERSARIAL TRAINING
217
218   We design CLAT as a fine-tuning approach, which is applied to neural networks that have undergone
219   preliminary training. The pretraining phase allows all layers in the model to capture useful features,
220   which will facilitate the identification of critical layers in the model. Notably, CLAT’s versatility
221   allows it to adapt to various types of pretrained models, either adversarially trained or trained on a
222   clean dataset only. In practice, we find that models do not need to fully converge during the pretraining
223   phase to benefit from CLAT fine-tuning. For example, in case of the CIFAR-10 dataset, 50 epochs of
224   PGD-AT training would be adequate. We consider the number of adversarial pretraining epochs as a
225   hyperparameter and provide further analysis on the impact of pretraining epochs in Section 4.3.1.
226   After the pretraining, CLAT begins by identifying and selecting critical layers in the pretrained model.
227   We then fine-tune critical layers only while freezing the rest of the layers. This process is illustrated
228   in Fig. 1, and the pseudocode is provided in Algorithm 1 in Appendix A for greater clarity. As
229   fine-tuning progresses, the critical layers will be updated to reduce their criticality, making them less
230   critical than some of the previously frozen layers. Subsequently, we perform periodic reevaluation of
231   the top k critical indices, ensuring continuous adaptation and optimization of the layers that are the
232
      most in need in the training process. Through hyperparameter optimization, we find 10 epochs to be
      adequate to optimize the selected critical layers for all models that we tested.

238   4.1   EXPERIMENTAL SETTINGS
239
240   Datasets and models We conducted experiments using two widely recognized image classification
241   datasets, CIFAR10 and CIFAR100. Each dataset includes 60,000 color images, each 32×32 pixels,
242
      divided into 10 and 100 classes respectively (Krizhevsky & Hinton, 2009). For our experiments, we
      deployed a suite of network architectures: WideResnets (34-10, 70-16) (Zagoruyko & Komodakis,
243
      2017), ResNets (50, 18) (He et al., 2016b), DenseNet-121 (Huang et al., 2017), PreAct ResNet-18(He
244
      et al., 2016a), and VGG-19 (Simonyan & Zisserman, 2015). In this paper, these architectures are
245   referred to as WRN34-10, WRN70-16, RN50, RN18, DN121, Preact RN18 and VGG19 respectively.
246
247
248   Training and evaluation Since CLAT can be layered over clean pretraining, partial training, or
249   other adversarial methods, results incorporating CLAT are denoted in our tables as "X + CLAT,"
250   where "X" refers to the baseline method applied prior to CLAT. Typically, this baseline method is
251
      run for the first 50 epochs, followed by fine-tuning during which CLAT is applied for an additional
      50 epochs. The total number of epochs is in line with the 100 epochs used in previous PGD-based
252
      adversarial training work (Zhang et al., 2019; Zhu et al., 2023).
253
254   For our baseline, we use PGD for attack generation during training, following a random start (Madry
255   et al., 2019), with an attack budget of ϵ = 0.03 under the ℓ∞ norm, a step size of α = 0.007, and
256   10 attack steps. The same settings apply to PGD attack evaluations. AutoAttack evaluations (Croce
257
      & Hein, 2020) also use a budget of ϵ = 0.03 under the ℓ∞ norm, with no restarts for untargeted
      APGD-CE, 9 target classes for APGD-DLR, 3 target classes for Targeted FAB, and 5000 queries for
258
      Square Attack. These settings remain consistent unless explicitly noted otherwise.
259
260   Experiments were conducted on a Titan XP GPU, starting with an initial learning rate of 0.1,
261   which was adjusted according to a cosine decay schedule. To ensure the reliability of robustness
262   measurements, we conducted each experiment a minimum of 10 times, reporting the lowest adversarial
263
      accuracies we observed.
264
265   CLAT settings We select critical layers as described in Section 3.1. Table 8 outlines the Top-5 most
266   critical layers for some of the models and corresponding datasets at the start of the CLAT fine-tuning,
267   after adversarially training with pgd-at for 50 epochs. In customizing the CLAT methodology to
268   various network sizes, we select approximately 5% of layers as critical through hyperparameter
269   optimization. For instance, DN121 uses 5 critical layers, while WRN70-16, RN50, WRN34-10,
      VGG19, and RN18 use 4, 3, 2, 1, and 1 critical layers, respectively.

      4.2     CLAT PERFORMANCE
271
272   White-box robustness Table 1 and Table 2 present white-box evaluation results using the PGD and
273   Auto attack frameworks, respectively. These tables illustrate CLAT’s versatility and effectiveness
274   when combined with various standard adversarial training methods, including state-of-the-art bench-
275   marks from RobustBench (Croce et al., 2020). CLAT effectively mitigates the overfitting typically
276   observed in traditional adversarial training, thereby enhancing both clean and adversarial accuracy
277   compared to baseline methods.
278   Table 1 further highlights that reducing trainable parameters alone does not necessarily lead to
279   improved performance. CLAT surpasses other parameter-efficient fine-tuning methods, such as
280   LoRA (Aleem et al., 2024) and RiFT (Zhu et al., 2023), due to its ability to precisely identify
281   critical layers and eliminate non-robust features from these layers. Additionally, we show that fast
282   adversarial training techniques, as discussed by Wong et al. (2020), can be applied to address the
283   inner maximization problem in the CLAT training objective described in Eq. (5). The "CLAT (Fast)"
284
      method not only enhances performance but also improves robustness compared to Fast-AT baselines.
285   Notably, CLAT models are trained with PGD-like attacks on hidden features without seeing Auto
286   Attacks directly, but their robustness persists under these attacks (see Table 2). This suggests
287   that different attacks across various networks share similarities in exploiting non-robust features.
288   By addressing these non-robust features through critical layer fine-tuning, CLAT ensures that the
289   robustness is adaptable across different attack settings and models. Lastly, we assess the robustness
290
      across various attack strengths in Appendix B.
291
292   Black-box robustness Table 3 and Table 4 evaluate the robustness against black-box attacks (Auto
293   Attack and PGD-AT respectively) between models trained solely using PGD-AT and those augmented
294
      with CLAT. Attack settings are the same as those of the white-box attacks. As a sanity check, the
      accuracies under black-box attack surpass those observed under white-box scenarios, indicating that
295
      gradient masking does not appear in the CLAT model, and that the white-box robustness evaluation
296
      is valid. More significantly, models trained with CLAT consistently outperform those trained with
297   PGD-AT, maintaining superior resilience in both black-box and white-box settings, regardless of the
298   attack method or models employed.

302   4.3.1    A BLATING ON PRETRAINING EPOCHS BEFORE CLAT
303
304   As discussed in Section 3.3, we apply CLAT after the model has been adversarially trained for some
305   epochs. Here, we analyze how the number of pretraining epochs affects CLAT performance. Fig. 2
306   shows the training curves for different allocations of PGD pretraining epochs and CLAT fine-tuning
307
      epochs within a 100-epoch training budget. The overfitting of PGD-AT is evident as adversarial
      accuracy plateaus and declines towards the end, as documented in previous research (Rice et al.,
308
      2020). In contrast, CLAT continues to improve adversarial accuracy, effectively addressing this
309
      issue. Including CLAT at any stage of training results in higher clean accuracy and robustness at
310   convergence. Additional results on pretrained clean models are provided in Appendix C.
311
312   Furthermore, an intriguing aspect of our experiments involves running CLAT from scratch (0 PGD-AT
313
      epochs). Although CLAT ultimately surpasses PGD-AT with sufficient epochs, using CLAT without
      any prior adversarial training results in significantly slower model convergence. We believe this
314
      suggests that “layer criticality” emerges during the adversarial training process, allowing critical
315
      layers to be identified as the model undergoes adversarial training. This phenomenon supports our
316   theoretical insight that criticality can be linked to the curvature of the local minima to which each
317   layer converges during adversarial training.

464   4.4   OVERHEAD AND STABILITY ANALYSIS
465
466   The cost of optimizing the CLAT training objective in Eq. (6) is similar to that of the standard
467   adversarial training given its min-max formulation. Here, we show that the computational overhead
468   for determining critical indices is negligible. We verify in Table 10 that criticality indices can be stably
469
      computed with a single training batch as small as 10, with top-ranking layers consistent with those
      achieved with a larger batch. We further conducted over 1000 runs for each network to randomly
470
      select the data used for criticality estimation, where we find remarkable consistency in computed
471
      critical layers, differing in less than 5% of cases, typically involving only one layer change among
472   the top five critical layers. The stability means we can use a mere 0.0002% of the training data for
473   criticality estimation every 10 epochs, which introduces a neglectable 0.4% additional time to the
474   standard adversarial training process as measured in Table 10.

      5     C ONCLUSIONS
516
      In this work, we introduce CLAT, an innovative adversarial training approach that addresses robust
517
      overfitting issues by fine-tuning only the critical layers vulnerable to adversarial perturbations. This
518
      method not only emphasizes layer-specific interventions for enhanced network robustness but also
519   sheds light on the commonality in non-robust features captured by these layers, offering a targeted
520   and effective defense strategy. Our results reveal that CLAT effectively selects less than 4% critical
521   trainable variables to achieve significant improvements in clean accuracy and adversarial robustness
522   across diverse network architectures and baseline adversarial training methods. We limit the scope
523   of this work to improving the empirical robustness of the model by utilizing the critical layers. In
524   this sense, open questions remain on why these specific layers become critical, whether they can
525   be identified more effectively, and whether the issues can be resolved with architectural or training
526   scheme changes. We leave a more theoretical understanding of these questions as future work.

1000
       The main paper defines feature weakness based on the feature variation under worst-case perturbation.
1001
       However, due to the non-linear and non-convex nature of the neural network model, the weakness
1002
       measurement may not be precise in more complicated model architectures with a mixed layer
1003   type, such as the Vision Transformer model. To this end, this section provides a more accurate
1004   curvature-based formulation on feature weakness, and shows how the proposed weakness metric is
1005   an approximation. We leave the utilization of the curvature-based weakness measurement on more
1006   complicated models as future work.
1007
