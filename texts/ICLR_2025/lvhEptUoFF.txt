009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent retrieval-augmented models enhance basic methods by building a hierar-
012            chical structure over retrieved text chunks through recursive embedding, cluster-
013            ing, and summarization. The most relevant information is then retrieved from both
014            the original text and generated summaries. However, such approaches face lim-
015            itations with dynamic datasets, where adding or removing documents over time
016            complicates the updating of hierarchical representations formed through cluster-
017            ing. We propose a new algorithm to efficiently maintain the recursive-abstractive
018
               tree structure in dynamic datasets, without compromising performance. Addition-
               ally, we introduce a novel post-retrieval method that applies query-focused recur-
019
               sive abstractive processing to substantially improve context quality. Our method
020
               overcomes the limitations of other approaches by functioning as a black-box post-
021            retrieval layer compatible with any retrieval algorithm. Both algorithms are val-
022            idated through extensive experiments on real-world datasets, demonstrating their
023            effectiveness in handling dynamic data and improving retrieval performance.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Large Language Models (LLMs) have established themselves as powerful tools across a wide range
029   of natural language processing (NLP) tasks, thanks to their ability to store vast amounts of factual
030   knowledge within their parameters (Petroni et al., 2019; Jiang et al., 2020). These models can be
031   further fine-tuned to specialize in specific tasks (Roberts et al., 2020), making them highly versatile.
032   However, a key limitation of LLMs lies in their static nature: as the world evolves and new informa-
033
      tion emerges, the knowledge encoded within an LLM can quickly become outdated. A promising
      alternative to relying solely on parametric knowledge is retrieval augmentation (Gao et al., 2023).
034
      This approach involves the use of external retrieval systems to supply relevant information in real-
035
      time. Instead of encoding all knowledge directly into the model, large text corpora are indexed,
036   segmented into manageable chunks, and dynamically retrieved as needed (Lewis et al., 2020; Gao
037   et al., 2023). Retrieval-augmented methods not only improve model accuracy but also offer a prac-
038   tical solution for maintaining performance as knowledge evolves over time.
039
      However, retrieval-augmented approaches also have limitations. Many existing methods only re-
040
      trieve short, specific chunks as context, which restricts the model’s ability to answer questions re-
041
      quiring a broader understanding of the text. To address this, RAPTOR was introduced (Sarthi et al.,
042   2024). It recursively embeds, clusters, and summarizes text chunks, enabling the retrieval of rel-
043   evant information from both original document chunks and generated summaries. Yet, RAPTOR
044   introduces new challenges, especially with dynamic datasets where documents are frequently added
045   or removed. The clustering component makes the tree structure sensitive to these updates, requiring
046   a full re-computation of the tree after each change, which is computationally expensive.
047
      To address these limitations, we introduce adRAP (adaptive Recursive Abstractive Processing),
048   an algorithm designed to efficiently update RAPTOR’s recursive-abstractive structure as new doc-
049   uments are added or removed. By incrementally adjusting the structure, adRAP avoids full re-
050   computation, preserving retrieval performance while significantly reducing computational overhead.
051   Furthermore, both RAPTOR and adRAP introduce memory overhead and require periodic mainte-
052   nance when used with dynamic datasets. As an alternative, we propose postQFRAP, a post-retrieval
053   method that applies query-focused recursive abstractive processing as a black-box layer, as illus-
      trated in Figure 1. This post-processing method integrates seamlessly into any retrieval pipeline

077   2     R ELATED W ORK
078
079   Retrieval Algorithms In the context of LLMs, retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) involves
080   retrieving relevant information from external sources and appending it to the LLM’s context along-
081   side the original query (Ram et al., 2023). Naı̈ve RAG methods (Gao et al., 2023) address this
082   challenge by converting documents into text, splitting it into chunks, and embedding these chunks
083   in a vector space where semantically similar chunks are mapped to nearby vectors. The query is sim-
084
      ilarly embedded, and the k-nearest vectors are retrieved to augment the LLM’s context. However,
      segmenting text into contiguous chunks may fail to capture its full semantic richness, and retrieving
085
      overly granular segments can overlook key information (Gao et al., 2023).
086
087   The recursive-abstractive summarization method by Wu et al. (2021) addresses this issue by break-
088   ing down tasks to summarize smaller text segments, which are then integrated to form summaries
089   of larger sections. While effective at capturing broader themes, it may miss finer details. RAPTOR
090
      (Sarthi et al., 2024) improves on this technique by recursively grouping and summarizing similar
      chunks, retaining both summaries and initial chunks. This approach captures a representation of the
091
      text at multiple levels of detail while preserving inter-dependencies within the text.
092
093
      Post-Retrieval Algorithms To optimize retrieval algorithms, post-retrieval strategies are com-
094   monly employed. These include re-ranking retrieved chunks and compressing the context (Gao
095   et al., 2023), as large contexts fed directly into LLMs often result in information loss, particularly
096   in the middle sections (Liu et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2024). The closest approach to our setting is the
097   query-focused summarization algorithm by Zhang et al. (2024). They retrieve relevant documents
098   which they also summarize using a prompt designed to extract key information before generating
099   the summary. The latter is then passed as context to the LLM. In contrast, we construct a hierarchi-
100   cal tree over the retrieved documents, allowing us to recursively filter noise by focusing on smaller,
101   manageable chunks at each step. This yields a more refined and relevant summary.

      3.2     D IMENSIONALITY R EDUCTION WITH UMAP
129
130   Clustering algorithms often struggle with the curse of dimensionality, where data becomes sparse,
131   and distances between points lose distinction in high dimensions. To address this, Uniform Mani-
132   fold Approximation and Projection (UMAP) (McInnes et al., 2018) reduces the dimensionality of
133   embeddings, significantly enhancing clustering performance (Allaoui et al., 2020). UMAP learns a
134   low-dimensional representation that preserves both local and global structures, with the key param-
135   eter n neighbors controlling the trade-off between local and global structure preservation.
136
137   3.3     R ECURSIVE -A BSTRACTIVE T REE C ONSTRUCTION
138
139
      The process of building the recursive-abstractive tree is outlined first, as it is key to understanding
      our new algorithms. The construction, based on Sarthi et al. (2024) with minor adjustments, consists
140
      of four steps: dataset chunking, clustering, summarizing, and recursive construction.
141
142
      Dataset Chunking Given a dataset, the first step is to divide the text into sentences using the
143   NLTK Punkt Sentence Tokenizer1 . These sentences are then grouped into chunks of up to 250
144   tokens, with a 50-token overlap between consecutive chunks, resulting in chunks of up to 300 tokens.
145   To maintain coherence, sentences are kept intact between chunks: if a sentence exceeds 250 tokens,
146   it is included in the next chunk. Sentences longer than 250 tokens are split at punctuation marks.
147   Token counts are determined using the cl100k base tokenizer from the tiktoken2 library.
148
      Note that we use 250 tokens with a 50-token overlap instead of the 100-token chunks used by (Sarthi
149   et al., 2024). Preliminary experiments (not included in this work) suggest that the larger chunk size
150   with overlap improves output quality.
151
152   Clustering The goal is to group n chunks c1 , . . . , cn into k clusters C1 , . . . , Ck , where k is to be
153   determined. Clustering is performed on the embeddings, not the raw text. So, using an encoder
154   model, embeddings v1 , . . . , vn are generated for the chunks. Then, dimensionality reduction is
155   performed using UMAP, followed by clustering with Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs). This
156   process is repeated twice, varying UMAP’s n neighbors parameter to create a hierarchical clustering,
157   an approach shown to be effective for this task (Sarthi et al., 2024).

159   applied, yielding global clusters C1g , . . . , Ckgg . Next, refinement occurs within each global cluster.

      3.4   R ETRIEVING D OCUMENTS
188
189   Given a query and a tree constructed over a relevant dataset, the goal is to retrieve k documents
190   that are helpful in answering the query. Sarthi et al. (2024) compared tree-based retrieval with
191   a collapsed-tree approach, where all nodes are considered simultaneously. The latter performed
192   better, and is the method used in our experiments.
193   In the collapsed-tree approach, the tree is flattened, and the k most similar documents are retrieved
194   using cosine similarity on the embeddings. This method can be seen as augmenting the dataset with
195   document summaries, followed by applying naı̈ve RAG to the expanded dataset. The pseudo-code
196   for the retrieval algorithm is provided in Appendix A.

      4.1   OVERVIEW
201
202   The problem we are addressing can be formally described as follows. Let T0 represent a recursive-
203   abstractive tree built on an initial dataset D0 . Given an updated dataset D = D0 ∪D1 , where |D0 | ≫
204   |D1 |, let T be the tree constructed over D. The goal is to efficiently update T0 to approximate T
205   without fully recomputing the tree on D.
206
      To achieve this, UMAP is used to reduce the dimensionality of the new documents, which are then
207   assigned to clusters, potentially updating the existing clustering. We first examine these components
208   individually, then explain how they are combined to create a dynamic data structure.
209
210
      4.2   A DAPTIVE UMAP
211
212   Let d ∈ D1 be a new document with embedding v. The first step is to reduce the dimensionality of
213   v to 10. To do this, we find the n neighbors nearest neighbors of v in the original high-dimensional
214   space and interpolate their positions in the previously learned low-dimensional embedding to obtain
215   the reduced embedding v ′ . This preserves the local relationships of v with its neighbors, maintaining
      the structure learned during fitting. Given |D0 | ≫ |D1 |, we assume this property holds for all new

250

251

254   larger values of k and fit a new GMM from scratch, selecting the k that optimizes the BIC.

299   5.1   M OTIVATION
300
301   Maintaining adRAP is costly, as it requires updating clusters and summaries with each new docu-
302   ment. Moreover, when many documents are added, the entire tree has to be recomputed to maintain
303   solution quality. Integrating this system poses significant development challenges, and companies
304
      with established retrieval algorithms may be hesitant to adopt a completely new system.
305   To address this, we propose a modified version of the recursive-abstractive tree as a black-box post-
306   retrieval solution that can be integrated with retrieval algorithms handling dynamic datasets (e.g.,
307   naı̈ve RAG). This approach enhances the initial construction by incorporating query-focused sum-
308   maries, improving the context relevance of the output.
309
310   5.2   POST QFRAP A LGORITHM
311
312   Let R be a retrieval algorithm that takes as input an integer k ∈ N+ and a query q, and returns k
313   documents relevant to the query. A simple example is the naı̈ve RAG algorithm (Gao et al., 2023).
314   We augment R as follows. First, we retrieve k0 documents without imposing a token limit. Then, we
315   apply a query-focused version of Algorithm 1 to these k0 documents to build a recursive-abstractive
316   tree. The key modification is using query-focused summarization (see prompt in Appendix, Table 5).
317   Since the tree is constructed to answer q, summarizing information relevant to q ensures that key
318   details are preserved while recursively filtering out irrelevant content. Additionally, we modify the
319   clustering to rely solely on local embeddings, as retrieving k0 documents already serves as a global
320
      filtering step. In other words, we assume the retrieved documents belong to the same global cluster.
      We demonstrate in Appendix B that using the simpler one-step clustering preserves the quality of
321
      the generated context compared to the two-step approach.
322
323   Finally, a summarization step is applied to all nodes at the last layer of the tree, instead of using a
      top-k retrieval approach, to reduce redundancy in the results. This process is detailed in Algorithm 3.

335   5.3    K EY P ROPERTIES
336
337   postQFRAP can be seamlessly integrated as a black-box solution with any retrieval algorithm that
338   handles dynamic datasets. A prime example of the latter is the naı̈ve RAG algorithm, where adding
339   documents is easy: chunk the new documents, embed each chunk, and add them to the vector
340
      database. Removing documents is equally simple—just delete them from the database.
341   Moreover, by recursively applying query-focused summarization, postQFRAP continuously extracts
342   information relevant to answering the question. Then, the final summarization step removes redun-
343   dancy and serves as a last denoising phase, producing a highly relevant and coherent context. It is
344   important to note that increasing the hyperparameter k0 enables the model to handle broader ques-
345   tions without expanding the generated context size, though it increases inference time.
346   Furthermore, postQFRAP avoids relying on a summarization model with a large context length
347   due to its recursive structure, which focuses on small chunks at each step. This enables the use of a
348   distilled model for greater inference efficiency (e.g., the abstractive compressor of Xu et al. (2023)).

352   6.1    DATASETS
353
354   We evaluate our methods on three question-answering datasets: MultiHop, QASPER, and QuALITY.
355
      MultiHop consists of news articles published between 2013 and 2023 (Tang & Yang, 2024). Al-
356
      though the original questions focus on retrieving and reasoning across multiple documents, they
357   primarily target explicit fact retrieval. To create more challenging questions requiring a broader un-
358   derstanding, we construct a RAPTOR tree on the dataset, sample chunks/summaries from the tree,
359   and ask an LLM to generate questions based on those chunks. Details are provided in Appendix H.3.
360
      QASPER consists of 1,585 NLP papers with associated questions (Dasigi et al., 2021). Each question
361
      seeks information from the full text and is written by an NLP practitioner who has only seen the title
362
      and abstract. For our experiments, we use the first 300 questions and their relevant papers. To make
363   each question context-independent, we include the paper’s name in the question (e.g. instead of
364   asking ”What are the observed results?”, we ask ”In paper X, what are the observed results?”)
365
366
      QuALITY consists of multiple-choice questions paired with context passages averaging 5,000 tokens
      (Pang et al., 2022). This exceeds the size of the context generated by the retrieval algorithms in our
367
      experiments. We also select the first 300 questions along with their corresponding context passages.
368
369   We present the results of an additional dataset in Appendix H.4. Moreover, dataset sizes and an
370   analysis of the recursive-abstractive trees constructed for each dataset are provided in Appendix H.6.

      Some generated answers may lack coherence, either in their internal structure or in relation to the
385   question. Providing summarized content as context may help the model generate more coherent
386   responses. To evaluate this, we introduce a novel metric called Human Coherence Rating, which
387   prompts an LLM to assess whether an answer is coherent and resembles one that could plausibly be
388   generated by a human expert. The specific prompt used for this evaluation is shown in the appendix
389   (Table 6), with a qualitative analysis of the metric provided in Appendix C.
390
      To gain deeper insights into our algorithms, we conduct a head-to-head evaluation. Given our focus
391   on questions requiring a global understanding, we adopt the evaluation metrics from Edge et al.
392   (2024), which assess comprehensiveness, diversity, empowerment, and directness. For each
393   comparison, the evaluator LLM is given the question, a prompt describing the target metric, and two
394   answers. The LLM evaluates which answer is superior or if it is a tie, providing a rationale for its
395   decision. To mitigate position bias (Zheng et al., 2024), the evaluation is repeated for each pair of
396   answers with their positions swapped. If the same answer wins both trials, it is declared the winner;
397   otherwise, the result is a tie.
398   We also conduct a qualitative analysis of postQFRAP, detailed in Appendix D.
399
400
401   6.3     H YPERPARAMETER S ELECTION
402
403   A key hyperparameter to consider is the context size, or equivalently, the number of documents to
404   retrieve. Sarthi et al. (2024) evaluated different context lengths on a subset of the QASPER dataset,
405
      finding that 2,000 output tokens yielded the best results. Based on this, we set the output context
      size to 2,000 tokens for all algorithms unless stated otherwise.
406
407   To select k0 for the postQFRAP algorithm, we compare different values on two validation datasets.
408   We choose k0 = 20, as larger values increase computational complexity without significant quality
409   gains, while smaller values substantially reduce context relevance. Details of this study are provided
410   in Appendix E.

413
414   We compare the adRAP algorithm against Naı̈ve RAG, RAPTOR, and a greedy variant of adRAP,
415   which assigns each new point to its most probable cluster without updating the GMM fit. To compute
416   adRAP, we first construct a full tree using 70% of the dataset. The remaining
                                                                                  p30% is added using the
417   adRAP algorithm (Section 4.4) where we set τc = 11 and τn = max(100, |D0 |) for Algorithm 2.
418   The choice of τc is based on the average cluster size in the full RAPTOR tree, which is always less
419   than 10 (see appendix, Table 12). For the greedy variant, a similar procedure is used. To simulate a
420   challenging scenario, we remove the last 30% of documents instead of random sampling.
421
      We compare postQFRAP with other post-retrieval methods: no processing (naı̈ve RAG) with
422   k = 7, 20 retrieved documents, one-shot summarization, re-ranking, and postRAP. One-shot sum-
423   marization uses the controller from Zhang et al. (2024) to directly generate 2,000 tokens (see prompt
424   in Appendix, Table 8). For re-ranking, we use the ms-marco-MiniLM-L-12-v24 cross-encoder
425   from HuggingFace, retrieving 20 documents via naı̈ve RAG, then re-ranking them to keep the top 7.
426   Finally, postRAP is a variant of postQFRAP without query-focused summarization which retrieves
427   the top-k most similar chunks from the tree built on the k0 chunks.
428   We also tried adding query expansion (Jagerman et al., 2023) to our postQFRAP algorithm, but this
429   barely affected the results. So, we report the details of those experiments in Appendix F.

521   7        L IMITATIONS
522
523   While adRAP is more efficient than repeatedly recomputing the full RAPTOR tree for dynamic
524   datasets, it introduces some overhead. It requires extra memory to store multiple UMAP and GMM
525   models and adds complexity to the retrieval pipeline, as it must be triggered when new documents
526
      are added. Additionally, a full tree recomputation may still be needed if a large volume of new
      documents is introduced, increasing implementation effort. With postQFRAP, generated summaries
527
      can make it harder to trace original sources. Additionally, multiple summarization calls are required
528
      during inference, although this follows the current trend of shifting more computational workload
529   to inference time, as seen with OpenAI’s o1 model (OpenAI, 2024; Brown et al., 2024).

532   8        C ONCLUSION
533
534   In this paper, we introduced adRAP, an adaptive extension of the RAPTOR algorithm, designed to
535
      efficiently approximate clustering when documents are added or removed. Our experiments show
      that adRAP performs comparably to RAPTOR, making it a viable solution for dynamic datasets.
536
537   We also presented postQFRAP, a novel post-retrieval algorithm that applies query-focused,
538   recursive-abstractive processing to refine large contexts. By filtering out irrelevant information,
539   postQFRAP produces highly relevant summaries. Our results demonstrate that postQFRAP consis-
      tently outperforms traditional methods, proving its effectiveness for post-retrieval processing.

651   Algorithm 4 Querying the Recursive-Abstractive Tree
652    1: Input: Query q, Recursive-Abstractive Tree T , Integer k, Token Threshold τ
653    2: Output: List of retrieved documents
654    3: Compute the embedding v for the query q.
655    4: Calculate the cosine similarity between v and the embeddings of all nodes in T .
656    5: Select the k most similar nodes, sorted by decreasing similarity.
657
       6: Add the nodes’ content to the output in order, stopping if the token threshold is reached.

      Algorithm 5 adRAP Algorithm
660
661    1: Input: Tree T0 with GMM and UMAP models, new document d
662    2: Output: Updated tree T
663
       3: Compute the embedding v of document d using the appropriate model.
       4: Create a leaf node in T0 for (d, v).
664
       5: Compute the global reduced embedding vg of v using the global UMAP model.
665
       6: Assign vg to the most probable cluster in the global clustering, denoted Cg∗ .
666    7: Compute the local reduced embedding vl of v using the local UMAP model of Cg∗ .
667    8: Update the local clustering of Cg∗ using the online GMM procedure (Algorithm 2).
668    9: for all clusters that changed do
669   10:     Regenerate the tree summary.
670   11:     Recompute the embedding for the cluster node.
671   12:     Repeat the process for its ancestors in the tree.
672   13: end for
673   14: for all newly created clusters do

      15:     Recur to the next layer (treating it as the leaf layer) and repeat the process.
      16: end for

678   B    C OMPARING ONE - VS TWO - STEP C LUSTERING FOR POST QFRAP
679
680   We compare postQFRAP, which uses a two-step hierarchical clustering algorithm as described in
681   Section 3.3, with a one-step approach that only applies local clustering (i.e., setting the UMAP
682
      parameter n neighbors to 10 and using GMMs once).
683   As shown in Figures 10,the results across all four datasets indicate that the difference between one-
684   step and two-step clustering is minimal. Furthermore, Figure 11 demonstrates that one-step clus-
685   tering performs better in QASPER and QuALITY, worse in NarrativeQA, and is comparable to
686   two-step clustering in MultiHop. Overall, the differences in performance between the two algo-

897         F               Q UERY E XPANSION
898
899         A possible extension to the postQFRAP algorithm is to first expand the query q before retrieving
900         k0 initial documents. This would lead to broader documents being retrieved before the clustering-
901         summarization process, potentially leading to an improved context.
902
            To test this approach, we use the query expansion algorithm from (Jagerman et al., 2023) with the
903
            Q2E/PRF prompt. It consists of first retrieving the top-3 documents using naı̈ve RAG. Then, we ask
904         an LLM to extract key words from those documents relevant to the question. We append those to
905         the query that is duplicated 5 times to get the augmented query q ′ :
906
907                                                      q ′ = Concat(q, q, q, q, q, LLM(promptq ))
908         where LLM(promptq ) is the output of the Q2E/PRF prompt. The latter is found in Table 9. Then
909
            we use postQFRAP as before, by replacing q with the augmented query q ′ .
910
911         We use this particular query expansion algorithm instead of the simpler Q2D/ZS algorithm that just
912         asks an LLM to answer the query and use that answer as a new query because we do not want
913
            to exploit the LLM’s parametric knowledge. Instead, we ground the extension on the retrieved
            documents.
914
915         We present the results in Figures 13 and 14. We observe that adding Query Expansion to postQFRAP
916         does not improve performance and, in fact, reduces the comprehensiveness and coherence of the
917         generated answers.
