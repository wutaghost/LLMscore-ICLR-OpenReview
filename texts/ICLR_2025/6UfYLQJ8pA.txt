009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011              Long-horizon event forecasting is critical across various domains, including re-
012              tail, finance, healthcare, and social networks. Traditional methods, such as
013              Marked Temporal Point Processes (MTPP), often rely on autoregressive mod-
014              els to predict multiple future events. However, these models frequently suffer
015              from issues like converging to constant or repetitive outputs, which limits their
016              effectiveness and general applicability. To address these challenges, we intro-
017              duce DeTPP (Detection-based Temporal Point Processes), a novel approach in-
018              spired by a matching-based loss function from object detection. DeTPP em-
019
                 ploys a unique matching-based loss function that selectively prioritizes reliably
                 predictable events, improving the accuracy and diversity of predictions during
020
                 inference. Our method establishes a new state-of-the-art in long-horizon event
021
                 forecasting, achieving up to a 77% relative improvement over existing MTPP and
022              next-K methods. Furthermore, DeTPP enhances next-event prediction accuracy
023              by up to 2.7% on a large transactions dataset and demonstrates high computational
024              efficiency during inference. The implementation of DeTPP is publicly available
025              on GitHub1 .

028   1       I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Data from various domains, including internet activity, e-commerce transactions, retail operations,
031   and clinical visits, is often recorded as timestamps and associated information. When ordered by
032   their timestamps, these data points form event sequences, and it is crucial to develop methods capa-
033   ble of handling these complex data streams. Event sequences differ fundamentally from other data
034   types. Unlike tabular data (Wang & Sun, 2022), events include timestamps and have an inherent
035   order. In contrast to time series data (Lim & Zohren, 2021), event sequences are characterized by
036   irregular time intervals and additional attributes. These distinctions necessitate the development of
037
      specialized models.
038   The primary task in the domain of event sequences is predicting future event types and their occur-
039   rence times. Indeed, the ability to accurately forecast sequential events is vital for applications such
040   as stock price prediction, personalized recommendation systems, and early disease detection. The
041   simplified domain, represented as pairs of event types and times, is typically called Marked Tempo-
042   ral Point Processes (MTPP) (Rizoiu et al., 2017). Additionally, structured modeling of dependencies
043
      between different data fields (McDermott et al., 2024) can be considered an extension of MTPP.
044   Practical applications often require predicting multiple future events within a specified time horizon.
045   This task presents unique challenges that differ from traditional next-event prediction. The conven-
046   tional approach typically relies on autoregressive models, which predict the next event step by step.
047   While these models are effective for immediate next-event forecasting, their performance tends to
048   deteriorate as the prediction horizon extends (Karpukhin et al., 2024).
049   This study identifies and addresses significant limitations of the autoregressive approach in the con-
050   text of long-horizon prediction. In particular, autoregressive models accumulate errors over time,
051   leading to constant or repetitive outputs. Additionally, their inference parallelism is limited due to
052   dependencies on the latest predictions. To address these limitations, we propose DeTPP, a novel

      Marked Temporal Point Processes. MTPP is a stochastic process that consists of a sequence
091
      of time-event pairs (t1 , l1 ), (t2 , l2 ), . . . , where t1 < t2 < . . . denote the times of events, and
092   li ∈ {1, . . . , L} are the corresponding event type labels (Rizoiu et al., 2017). Traditional MTPP
093   models primarily focus on predicting the next event in the sequence. A straightforward approach
094   is to independently predict the time and type of the next event, while more sophisticated methods
095   model the temporal dynamics of each event type separately. These models often rely on Temporal
096   Point Processes, which describe the stochastic generation of event times.

      MTPP models. Traditional MTPP models, such as Poisson and Hawkes processes (Rizoiu et al.,
      2017), rely on strong assumptions about the underlying generative processes. Recent advancements
100
      have shifted towards more flexible and expressive models that leverage neural architectures. These
101
      include classical Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) (Du et al., 2016; Xiao et al., 2017; Omi et al.,
102   2019; Shchur et al., 2019), as well as more advanced architectures like transformers (Zhang et al.,
103   2020; Zuo et al., 2020; Wang & Xiao, 2022; Yang et al., 2022). Additionally, continuous-time mod-
104   els such as Neural Hawkes Processes (Mei & Eisner, 2017), ODE-RNN (Rubanova et al., 2019),
105   and their variants (Jia & Benson, 2019; De Brouwer et al., 2019; Kidger et al., 2020; Song et al.,
106   2024; Kuleshov et al., 2024) have been developed to capture the dynamics of event sequences bet-
107   ter. Moreover, some researchers have adapted generative models, including denoising diffusion and
      Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), for use in TPPs (Lin et al., 2022).

      Next-K models. Previous research has explored models that predict multiple future events simul-

118   3     L IMITATIONS OF AUTOREGRESSIVE I NFERENCE
119
120   Previous studies have identified several challenges associated with autoregressive models for long-
121   horizon predictions (Karpukhin et al., 2024). These models often exhibit reduced prediction uncer-
122   tainty over extended horizons, even though the task becomes increasingly difficult. As illustrated
123   in Fig. 1.c, the predicted label sequences often have constant or repetitive outputs. This behavior
124   likely stems from the model’s reliance on its predictions as input for subsequent predictions, which
125   can amplify errors and lead to repeated events. Notably, even the next-K approach, despite avoiding
126
      autoregressive dependencies, exhibits repetitive patterns. This may be attributed to each head in the
      next-K model predicting the entire distribution of labels, leading to a bias toward the most frequent
127
      classes during inference.
128
129   In contrast, our proposed DeTPP approach exhibits greater diversity in its predictions and, as we
130   will demonstrate, achieves superior performance in long-horizon prediction tasks. The details of the
131   proposed method are discussed in the following section.
132
133   4     E VENT D ETECTION WITH D E TPP
134
135   This section introduces DeTPP, a novel approach to MTPP modeling that incorporates the loss func-
136   tion, motivated by DeTR object detection approach (Carion et al., 2020). DeTPP begins by utilizing
137   a backbone model to extract embeddings from historical data. In the next stage, DeTPP predicts
138   K future event candidates within a specified time horizon H, where K is larger than the typical se-
139
      quence length. During training, the model aligns its predictions with ground truth and computes
      pairwise losses, as illustrated in Fig. 2. At inference time, the model retains only candidates with
140
      high prediction scores. Below, we provide a detailed overview of the event prediction head, the
141
      sequence model, and the associated training and inference procedures.
142

146   a probabilistic framework. Specifically, DeTPP predicts the probability of an event occurring, the
147   distribution of event labels, and the distribution of the time shift relative to the last observed event.
148

      4.4   C ALIBRATION AND I NFERENCE
238
      Inference with DeTPP involves two steps: filtering and sorting. First, predicted events are filtered
239   based on their occurrence probabilities ôi . The remaining events are then sorted according to their
240   predicted timestamps, forming the final output sequence. However, in practice, an additional cali-
241   bration step is necessary. Without calibration, the model tends to predict a small number of events
242   due to a bias in the predicted occurrence probabilities ôi toward the matching frequency of each
243   head, which is typically below the 0.5 threshold. Calibration aims to determine optimal prediction
244   thresholds for all ôi , aligning the prediction rates with the matching probabilities. This calibration is
245   performed on the fly by tracking matching frequencies and computing the corresponding quantiles
246   using a streaming algorithm. The proposed calibration algorithm is outlined in Algorithm 1.

265
266   DeTPP relies on two key hyperparameters: the maximum number of predictions K and the horizon
267   H. We set the horizon H to match that of the T-mAP metric, ensuring consistency in evaluation.
268   The selected value is also sufficient to include the necessary number of events for calculating the
269   OTD metric. The selection of K typically requires tuning for each dataset. It controls the maximum
      number of predictions, and we recommend setting K to approximately four times the average se-

      quence length. In our experiments, K values ranged between 32 and 64, depending on the dataset’s
271   characteristics. The value of λ from Eq. 7 was set to 4 in all experiments.
272
273   Additionally, we found it beneficial to adjust the weight of each loss function during alignment to
274
      accommodate the number of model outputs, dataset classes, and the average time step. In practice,

      5.3                        P REDICTIONS DIVERSITY
404
405   As we qualitatively demonstrated in Section 3, autoregressive methods tend to produce repetitive
406   outputs. In this section, we provide additional quantitative results that further highlight the low
407   output diversity of traditional approaches.
408
      A popular way to make diverse predictions is to adjust temperature during sampling. When the
409
      temperature approaches zero, the model predicts the label with the maximum output probability, as
410
      previous experiments did. When the temperature increases, the model samples from the uniform
411   label distribution, leading to the highest possible entropy. We, therefore, need to study the impact
412   of the temperature on both the predictions diversity and long-horizon quality. We do this by varying
413   temperature from 0 to 10 and measuring the average entropy of predicted event types within the hori-
414   zon. We also evaluate the long-horizon prediction quality regarding OTD, as T-mAP doesn’t depend
415   on temperature. The results from Fig. 6 show, that DeTPP achieves the optimal balance between
416   diversity and prediction quality on 4 out of 5 datasets. Certain methods benefit from sampling-based
417   approaches over maximum probability predictions; however, they fail to achieve the prediction qual-
418   ity of DeTPP. Further details on DeTPP’s output diversity can be found in Appendix D.

      6                  L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
512
513   While DeTPP effectively addresses both next-event and long-horizon prediction tasks, it has certain
514   limitations. First, DeTPP relies on a fixed horizon size, H. In this study, we selected H based
515   on the hyperparameters of the OTD and T-mAP metrics. A change in the evaluation metric typi-
516   cally requires adjusting DeTPP’s parameters. A potential direction for future research could explore
517
      combining horizon-based and autoregressive inference, enabling DeTPP to make predictions across
      arbitrary horizons without requiring modifications to the model.
518
519   Another limitation of DeTPP is its independent prediction of events within the horizon. Future
520   research could investigate integrating rescoring techniques, such as those in Xue et al. (2022), or ap-
521   plying beam search to improve DeTPP’s predictive performance. Additionally, better time modeling
522   might be achieved by integrating intensity-based approaches, like NHP or RMTPP, with DeTPP, of-
523
      fering another promising direction for research.
524   Furthermore, some techniques from DeTPP could be adapted for object detection in computer vi-
525   sion Carion et al. (2020). DeTPP introduces a probabilistic framework that unifies different loss
526   functions, using the same objective during both matching and backpropagation, which enhances op-
527   timization robustness. Notably, DeTPP employs a presence score during matching, which, as shown
528   in our ablation studies, significantly improves the model performance on most datasets.

531   7                  C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this work, we introduced DeTPP, a novel event prediction method that adapts a set-based training
534   objective from object detection to address the challenges of long-horizon forecasting. Our experi-
535   ments demonstrate that DeTPP effectively overcomes the limitations of traditional autoregressive ap-
536   proaches. Notably, DeTPP generates more diverse predictions, significantly enhancing long-horizon
537   prediction accuracy. Moreover, DeTPP is computationally more efficient than most MTPP methods,
538   as it predicts multiple future events in parallel. This approach paves the way for advancing the
539   modeling of Marked Temporal Point Processes, offering new opportunities across a wide range of
      practical applications.

      Temporal mean Average Precision (T-mAP). T-mAP introduces two key parameters: the horizon
793
      length H and the maximum allowed time error δ. From each pair of predicted and ground truth
794   sequences, T-mAP extracts subsequences that fall within the specified time horizon H. Unlike the
795   Optimal Transport Distance (OTD), T-mAP compares sequences of variable lengths. Additionally,
796   T-mAP evaluates the predicted probability distribution of labels p̂(l) rather than the hard labels,
797   which typically correspond to the label with the highest probability. T-mAP computes the Average
798   Precision (AP) for each class, which is equivalent to the area under the precision-recall curve. The
799   AP values for all classes are then averaged with equal weights, a process commonly referred to as
800   macro averaging.
801   To compute T-mAP for a specific class l, consider some decision threshold τ . T-mAP selects a
802   subset of predicted events with probability p̂(l) exceeding the threshold τ . A prediction ŷi = (t̂i , l) is
803   considered a true positive (TP) if there exists a ground truth event yj = (tj , l) such that |t̂i − tj | ≤ δ.
804   Each ground truth event can be matched to at most one prediction, so the number of true positives
805   does not exceed the total number of targets. Predictions not matched to any ground truth event are
806   classified as false positives (FP), while unmatched ground truth events are considered false negatives
807   (FN). For a given threshold τ , precision and recall can then be calculated as:

      future events and a novel loss function. To study the impact of these modifications, we evaluated
842
      DeTPP with a simple pairwise loss. Unlike matching, this loss is computed between events on

      corresponding positions in the prediction and ground truth. As shown in Table 5, pairwise loss
844   demonstrates a low long-horizon prediction quality, even worse than a simple IFTPP. A possible
845   reason for this failure can be incorrect alignment between predictions and ground truth, leading to
846   noisy gradients and reduced prediction quality. We therefore conclude that our matching loss is an
847   essential part of the training pipeline.
