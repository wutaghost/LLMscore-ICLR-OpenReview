009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            We present OmniBooth, an image generation framework that enables spatial
012            control with instance-level multi-modal customization. For all instances, the multi-
013            modal instruction can be described through text prompts or image references. Given
014            a set of user-defined masks and associated text or image guidance, our objective is
015            to generate an image, where multiple objects are positioned at specified coordinates
016
               and their attributes are precisely aligned with the corresponding guidance. This
               approach significantly expands the scope of text-to-image generation, and elevates
017
               it to a more versatile and practical dimension in controllability. In this paper, our
018
               core contribution lies in the proposed latent control signals, a high-dimensional
019            spatial feature that provides a unified representation to integrate the spatial, textual,
020            and image conditions seamlessly. The text condition extends ControlNet to provide
021            instance-level open-vocabulary generation. The image condition further enables
022            fine-grained control with personalized identity. In practice, our method empow-
023            ers users with more flexibility in controllable generation, as users can choose
024            multi-modal conditions from text or images as needed. Furthermore, thorough
025            experiments demonstrate our enhanced performance in image synthesis fidelity
026            and alignment across different tasks and datasets.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Image generation is flourishing with the booming advancement of diffusion models (Ho et al.,
031   2020; Rombach et al., 2022). These models have been trained on extensive datasets containing
032   billions of image-text pairs, such as LAION-5B (Schuhmann et al., 2022), and have demonstrated
033   remarkable text-to-image generation capabilities, exhibiting impressive artistry, authenticity, and
034   semantic alignment. An important feature of such synthesis is controllability – generating images
035   that meet user-defined conditions or constraints. For example, a user may draw specific instances in
036   desired locations. However, in the context of text-to-image, users mostly generate favored images by
037   global text description, which only provides a coarse description of the visual environment, and can
038
      be difficult to express complex layouts and shapes precisely using language prompts.
039   Previous methods have proposed to leverage image conditions to provide spatial control, but have
040   been limited in their ability to offer instance-level customization. For example, ControlNet (Zhang
041   et al., 2023) and GLIGEN (Li et al., 2023b) employ various types of conditional inputs, including
042   semantic masks, bounding box layouts, and depth maps to enable spatial-level control. These methods
043   leverage global text prompts in conjunction with plain image conditions to guide the generation
044
      process. While they effectively manage the spatial details of the generated images, they fall short in
      offering precise and fine-grained descriptions to manipulate the instance-level visual character.
045
046   In this paper, we investigate a critical problem, “spatial control with instance-level customization”,
047   which refers to generating instances at their specified locations (panoptic mask), and ensuring their
048   attributes precisely align with the corresponding user-defined instruction. To achieve comprehensive
049   and omnipotent of controllability, we describe the instruction using multi-modal input such as
050
      textual prompts and image references. In this manner, the user is empowered to freely define the
      characteristics of instance according to their desired specifications.
051
052   While this problem has been identified as a critical area of need, it remains largely unexplored in the
053   existing literature. This problem is usually designed as two distinct tasks. The first is instance-level
      control for image generation. Prior works provide extra prompt control for each instance in an

054
      image. They learn regional attention (Zhou et al., 2024b) or modified text embedding and cross
055   attention (Wang et al., 2024b) to perform controllable generation. The second task is subject-driven
056   image generation. Several works (Ma et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2024) leverage image as condition and
057   generate images with the same identity. Optimization-based methods such as DreamBooth (Ruiz et al.,
058   2023) require fine-tuning the diffusion model using multiple target images, and can not generalize
059   to new instances. Some of them treat the whole images as reference, and can not generate multiple
060   instances with spatial control. Consequently, these limitations significantly impede the practical
061   applicability of these methods in real-world scenarios.
062   To achieve spatial control with instance-level customization, we propose a unified framework that
063   enables controllable image synthesis through multi-modal instruction. The core contribution of
064   our method is our proposed latent control signal, denoted as lc. We first use lc to represent the
065   input panoptic mask for spatial control. Then, by painting text embedding or warping image
066   embedding into the unified lc, we form a unified condition comprising different modalities of control.
067   Furthermore, as image modality contains a more complex structure, we propose spatial warping to
068   encode and transform irregular image identity into lc. Finally, we develop an enhanced ControlNet
069
      framework (Zhang et al., 2023) to learn feature alignment of the latent input rather than spatial image.
      Through extensive experiments and benchmarks in MS COCO (Lin et al., 2014) and DreamBooth
070
      dataset (Ruiz et al., 2023), our method achieves better image quality and label alignment than prior
071
      methods. In summary, our contributions include:
072
073          • We present OmniBooth, a holistic image generation framework to attain multi-modal con-
074            trol, including textual description and image reference. Our unified framework unlocks a
075            spectrum of applications such as subject-driven generation, instance-level customization,
076            and geometric-controlled generation.
077          • We propose latent control signal, a novel generalization of spatial control from RGB-image
078            into the latent domain, enabling highly expressive instance-level customization within a
079            latent space.
080          • Our extensive experimental results demonstrate that our method achieves high-quality image
081            generation and precise alignment across different settings and tasks.

085   2.1 D IFFUSION M ODELS FOR T EXT- TO -I MAGE G ENERATION
086
      Recently, diffusion-based image generation has developed rapidly. Image Diffusion (Song et al.,
087   2020a;b) learn the process of generating images by progressive denoising a random variable drawn
088   from a Gaussian distribution. Latent diffusion (LDMs) (Rombach et al., 2022) further leverage a
089   UNet (Ronneberger et al., 2015) and perform diffusion process in the latent space of a Variational
090   AutoEncoder (Kingma & Welling, 2013), significantly improving computational efficiency.
091
092   2.2   C ONTROLLABLE I MAGE G ENERATION THROUGH M ULTI - MODAL I NSTRUCTION
093
      In this paper, we explore instance-level controllable image synthesis using two primary modalities:
094
      text prompts and image conditions, which serve as the two most commonly used modalities by users.
095
096   Text-Control Text-controlled image generation typically involves learning a cross-attention module
097   that builds interaction between image features and text embeddings. These text embeddings can be
098   extracted using CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) or T5 (Raffel et al., 2019) text encoders. While this
099   approach effectively controls the high-level appearance or style of generated images, it often struggles
100   to manage detailed spatial contents and preserve subject identity. As a result, we further incorporate
101
      image references as additional conditions to control the specific instance identity.
102   Subject-Control Subject-driven image synthesis typically leverages image references to control
103   and customize generation. DreamBooth (Ruiz et al., 2023) fine-tunes the entire UNet network using
104   target images to capture the identity of target objects. To enable zero-shot personalization, some
105   works treat the identity as the text embedding to the diffusion model. IP-Adapter (Ye et al., 2023b)
106   and InstantBooth (Shi et al., 2024) learning an image encoder to extract the identity of objects.
107   Relevant work (Wang et al., 2024a; Dahary et al., 2024) further learning multi-object generation
      through regional attention.

152
153   Given the textual description Ti of each instance in a scene, we aim to generate an image with
154

155

156
      regional control, which will be discussed in Sec. 3.2.

182   Learning a zero-shot image customization is challenging. Consequently, it would be beneficial and
183   necessary to leverage the pre-trained vision foundation model to extract the identity information of
184   the target object. Previous works (Ma et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2024; Ye et al., 2023b) chose to use
185   a pretrained CLIP image encoder to embed the target object. However, given that CLIP is trained
186   through a contrastive loss between images and high-level textual description, it only encodes the
187
      semantic meaning of objects while neglecting the identity details. To extract the discriminative
      spatial identity, we use DINOv2 (Oquab et al., 2023) as the feature extractor. Trained through
188
      patch-level objectives via random masking, the feature extracted by DINOv2 preserves discriminative
189
      information, thus it is expressive for conditions. The output of the image encoder is a spatial
190   embedding si ∈ R26×26×1024 that contain patch features, and a global embedding gi ∈ R1024
191   contain global information. These embeddings are further fused with text embedding to perform
192   multi-modal controlled generation.

242   Image Control: Spatial Warping Incorporating image conditions into image generation poses a
243   distinct challenge compared to text-based conditions. Utilizing a 1D embedding for image identity is
244
      suboptimal, as it fails to capture the geometric intricacies of the image. To preserve spatial details,
      we introduce a simple and effective technique termed spatial warping to encode image embeddings.
245
246   We provide a detailed illustration of spatial warping in Fig. 2. Given a target region (instance mask),
247   we first align the bounding rectangle of the mask with the input DINO feature maps using box
248   coordinates. Subsequently, we use the dense grids of the mask within this rectangle to interpolate
249   features from the input DINO features. This process is similar to ROI Align (He et al., 2017), but the
250
      resolution of ROI is flexible and follows the size of the mask. The features will form an ROI feature
      (orange region in Fig. 2) of the latent control signal. Finally, to further incorporate global embedding,
251
      we randomly drop 10% of the spatial embedding si and replace it with the DINO global embedding
252
      gi . As our target mask may have a different silhouette from the shape of input image, injecting global
253   embedding enhances the model’s generalizability and adaptability across diverse image conditions.
254   The final ROI feature is then warped into the original region of latent control signal.

      3.3        F EATURE A LIGNMENT
263
      After obtaining the latent control signal in spatial and latent dimension, we develop a simple but
264   effective feature alignment network to inject our conditions into latent features. As we represent our
265   latent control signal as latent features with the same width and height as the latent diffusion, we first
266   use a 1×1 convolution to align the channel dimension. Then, we employ a UNet encoder to extract
267   features and integrate them into the diffusion UNet via adding, following the standard ControlNet
268   approach (Zhang et al., 2023). Since we perform controllable generation through feature adding, we
269   don’t need to fine-tune the diffusion UNet or inject specific layers. Consequently, our method not only
      achieves computational efficiency, but is also naturally compatible with various community plugins.

295   3.4        E NHANCE S TRUCTURE S UPERVISION BY E DGE L OSS
296
      In the training process of diffusion model, each pixels in an image contribute equally during super-
297
      vision. However, in the scenes characterized by challenging hierarchy and overlapping object, the
298
      importance of object edges may differ markedly from that of the plain background pixels. To enhance
299   the supervision over the high-frequency area and improve generation quality, we propose edge loss.
300   We first extract the edge map of the input image through Sobel filtering. Then apply Progressive
301   Foreground Enhancement (detailed in Sec. C) from SyntheOcc (Li et al., 2024b) to the edge pixel.
302   Since we perform edge detection in the latent size of input images, we regard the edge map as a loss
303   weight map to enhance structure supervision. The detailed formulation is:
304
305                               w(x, m, n) = Progressive Enhance(Edge(x), m, n),                             (6)
306
      where x is the groundtruth image, n is the current training step, and w is the loss reweight map which
307
      has the same spatial resolution as the latent space of the diffusion model.

310   3.5        T RAINING PARADIGM
311
312   Multi-Scale Training The training of the diffusion model typically leverages a fixed-resolution
313   scheme. This behavior limits the application in generating images with different resolutions and
314   aspect ratios. In this paper, we introduce a multi-scale training scheme that enhances the model’s
315   flexibility and generalizability across various resolutions and ratios. In detail, we split the training
316   images into different groups of ratios. During training, we sample images from the same group, and
317
      resize them to the same resolution as the training images in a batch, thereby enabling a robust and
      adaptable image generation during inference.
318
319   Random Modality Select To enable a unified image generation framework that accommodates
320   multi-modal conditions, we employ a stochastic selection process during training. We randomly
321   choose either a textual description Ti or an image reference Ii as the conditional input of each
322   instance, each with 50% probability. The image reference is copy-pasted from ground-truth image
323   using instance mask. This random approach ensures flexibility and adaptability in the controllable
      generation, allowing the model to effectively integrate various types of conditions.

      4      E XPERIMENT

344   Basic Setup We experiment with our model on the MS COCO dataset (Lin et al., 2014). To obtain
345   panoptic annotations for the images, we utilize the COCONut annotation (Deng et al., 2024). As the
346
      annotation only contains category-level captions of instances, we employ BLIP-2 (Li et al., 2023a) to
      generate textual descriptions for each instance in the COCO dataset.
347
348   Networks We use Stable Diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022) XL as initialization. During training,
349   we only train our feature alignment network while keeping the diffusion UNet and DINO network
350   frozen. Additionally, we train two separate Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) layers to extract features
351   from both text and image embeddings, which are subsequently integrated into latent control signal.
352
353
      Hyperparameters During training, we resize the height of training image to 1024 and keep the
      aspect ratio. We train our model in 12 epochs with batch size = 16. The learning rate is set at 4e−5 .
354
      The training phase takes around 3 days using 8 NVIDIA A100 80G GPUs. We use the classifier-free
355
      guidance (CFG) (Ho & Salimans, 2022) that is set as 7.5. For each image reference, we perform a
356   random horizon flip and random brightness as data augmentation to simulate multi-view condition.
357
358   Baselines We compare our method with prior methods in Tab. 1. SpaText uses a CLIP text encoder
359   or CLIP image encoder to extract embedding from conditional input. Then it concatenates the spatial
360   feature to the latent feature in diffusion UNet to control the image generation. ControlNet leverages
361   an RGB image as condition input to provide layout guidance. The conditions include canny edge,
362
      semantic map, and depth map. They either comprise a predefined and fixed vocabulary or focus
      solely on geometric controls. At the same time, we argue that ControlNet can be hard to precisely
363
      control the individual instances through global prompts (see Tab. 1), due to their fixed length and
364
      lack of spatial correspondence in global prompts. On the other hand, InstanceDiffusion enables
365   instance-level control through modified global textual embedding. The textual embedding contains
366   the point coordinates of boxes or masks for each instance, along with instance descriptions. Detailed
367   comparison will be provided in Sec. 4.2.

      5       L IMITATION AND B ROADER I MPACTS
508
509   Granularity Conflict In our research, we aim to achieve a unified controllable image synthesis
510   by utilizing multi-modal instructions. We consider both textual descriptions and image references
511   as equivalent conditional descriptors for representing instances. However, it is crucial to note that
512   textual descriptions operate within a global representation, treating all relevant instances uniformly.
513   This approach contrasts with the use of image references, which offer distinctive features tailored to
514   individual instances. The amalgamation of these two types of descriptors into a single latent space,
515   latent control signal, can inadvertently lead to a blending of inconsistent levels of detail, potentially
516
      undermining the fine-grained distinctions necessary for precise image synthesis.
517   3D Conditioning and Overlapping Objects In scenarios where the input layout features multiple
518   objects in close proximity or overlapping, discerning individual elements can be challenging for our
519   method. A suboptimal solution is to use “another” description in the instance prompt, to distinguish
520   the overlapping instances with the same textual property. Future work can enhance our model by
521   integrating 3D conditioning techniques, such as depth cues or multiple plane images (MPIs) (Li et al.,
522   2024b), to enrich the spatial context and improve the model’s ability to discern overlapping objects.
523
      Future Research Our framework provides application in dataset generation, enabling the creation
524
      of free annotated panoptic masks. It allows for the conditioning of these masks to produce aligned
525
      images, thereby offering a rich resource for free-labeled data. Additionally, we envision adapting
526   our framework to facilitate controllable video generation, which would empower users to precisely
527   manipulate video content following their specifications. We hope this property will offer substantial
528   benefits in applications of content creation and robotics (Yang et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024c).

      A.2     C OMPARISON WITH C ONTROL N ET-BASED C ONTROL
777
778   In this section, we draw a comparison between our approach and ControlNet, examining its two
779   primary variants. The first is to condition an RGB semantic mask. This is a degradation of our
780   method, where each color in the semantic mask represents a category in a fixed vocabulary. This
781   method, while effective for instances aligning with a predefined vocabulary, is limited in its scope. It
782   does not accommodate open-vocabulary generation or subject-driven generation.
783
      The second variant uses geometry layout as conditions, then leverages global prompts to instruct the
784   specific instance with open-vocabulary control. The geometry layout can be a depth map or edge
785   map without semantics. This approach is efficacious in scenarios with a limited number of instances
786   or simple topological structures. However, its efficacy diminishes when confronted with complex
787   layouts and a multitude of instances, as the token length may surpass the text encoder’s maximum
788   capacity of 77 tokens. Additionally, the weak spatial reference in global prompts poses a challenge in
789   accurately targeting each instance with its description. Furthermore, the depth map and edge map
790   can be difficult for users to draw. Consequently, we consider these ControlNet variants to be less
791   effective baselines for our method.
