011                                                  A BSTRACT
012
013             In-context learning (ICL) is an important yet not fully understood ability of pre-
014             trained large language models (LLMs). It can greatly enhance task performance
015             using a few examples, termed demonstrations, without fine-tuning. Although effec-
016
                tive in question answering, ICL often underperforms in long-form generation tasks
                such as summarization. Under appropriately realistic assumptions, we empirically
017
                and theoretically show that ICL demonstrations alone are insufficient to teach
018
                LLMs the task’s language and format distributions for generation.
019
                We argue for explicit exposure to the task distributions and hypothesize that defin-
020
                ing them by prompting enhances model performance. To this end, we present
021             LongGuide, which efficiently generates two parallel streams of guidelines captur-
022             ing task language and format properties: (i) Metric Guidelines (MGs) that instruct
023             models to optimize self-evaluated metrics; and (ii) Output Constraint Guidelines
024             (OCGs) that constrain generation at both token and sentence levels. LongGuide
025             automatically selects the best combination of guidelines, improving both strong
026             open- and closed-source LLMs by over 5% in both zero- and few-shot settings.
027             We show that LongGuide is generalizable, learnable by weak models to enhance
028             strong ones, and integrates synergistically with automatic prompt optimizers.

033   In recent years, pre-trained large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated impressive instruction-
034

035

098   A long-form generation dataset with n samples for a task T is defined as D = {(xti , yit )}ni=1 where xti
099   and yit are input contexts and ground-truth sentence- or paragraph-long responses (Fan et al., 2019).
100   For such tasks, preserving language and format properties of task-specific data during generation
101   is essential for aligning outputs with ground truth. This is unlike classification, where outputs are
102
      predefined. We now demonstrate that ICL fails to enable LLMs to implicitly capture and maintain
      these properties during generation.

        Findings. We present the k = 5 demonstration results in Table 1 and the case of Mistral-7B-it-v.02
140     in Figure 2 with metrics (1)–(7)1 . We derive three surprising findings. Firstly, the ICL models do
141     not achieve a 100% score of 5 on any metric and instruct models generally outperform non-instruct

143     excel while for critical summarization metrics such as INF and COV, they achieve only up to 20% to
144     40%. Notably, although all demonstrations contain 17 output tokens, fewer than 5% answers achieve
145     this property. Secondly, increasing # demonstrations does not rectify this issue; the same trends
146     persist across 3, 5, and 10. Finally, by adding a simple guideline shown in Figure 2, the percentages
147     of answers maintaining the metrics are mostly improved, especially (7) and (8), verifying that adding
148
        guidelines is indeed helpful for instruct models to maintain these properties. Additionally, without
        ICL (and without instruction in our consideration), the model is entirely unable to solve the task.

         Theoretical intuitions. Recall that PM and PT are the probability functions of M and the task
151
         data, we prove the Remark A.1 which establishes that when M does not capture the true task data
152
         distribution (i.e., PM ̸= PT ), demonstrations alone fail recover the desired alignment in the limit. As
153      a result, certain task language and format properties, even when well-presented in demonstrations,
154      may not be implicitly captured and preserved during LLM generation, as observed in Figure 2. We
155      term this unsolved issue the text property transfer (PT) challenge: ensuring that M captures and
156      preserves specific desired text properties observed in a limited set of labeled data to responses.
157
        We hypothesize that explicitly guiding the model to optimize essential textual language and format
158
        properties at their levels matching the task data mitigates the mismatch between PM and PT , leading
159     to improved performance. This hypothesis is formalized as Hypothesis A.1, and we present our
160     complete theoretical intuition in §A.

179   3    L ONG G UIDE : A N E FFICIENT G UIDELINE G ENERATION A LGORITHM

181
      Motivations. As we have seen, providing textual guidelines instructing LLMs to optimize certain
182
      text property metrics can enhance them on responses, possibly because LLMs are optimizers (Yang
183   et al., 2024). Inspired by §2, we propose LongGuide (Figure 3 and Algorithm 1), an algorithm that
184   efficiently generates guidelines for LLMs to optimize self-evaluated text properties during generation.
185   Specifically, Steps 1–3 focus on generating the Metric Guideline (MG) capturing the intrinsic language
186   properties of the task via reference-free metrics. In parallel, Step 4 analyzes the answer format of the
187   task and translates it to Output Constraint Guideline (OCG). The best combination of MG and OCG
188   is selected for inference (Step 5). To ensure LongGuide’s generalizability to new tasks, we assume
189   access to at most 50 training samples: Dtrain = {(xti , yit )}ni=1 .
190
191   Step 1: Metric Collection & Selection. To learn a task’s language properties, this step reasons to
192   select appropriate language evaluation metrics for self-evaluation. For this purpose, we first construct
193   a pool of evaluation metrics, S, applicable to any text generation task. S consists of 27 distinct
194
      metrics from 4 main sources (Appx.-Table 13 for details). Specifically, we collect 3 metrics from
      ABC’s of Communication (Wagner, 1963), 12 metrics from (Fu et al., 2023) for dialogue generation,
195
      summarization, data2text generation, and machine translation, and propose 12 more metrics for a
196
      broader evaluation coverage. We do not collect LM-based metrics, such as FactScore (Min et al.,
197   2023), because it is challenging for LLMs to define and self-evaluate them. Additionally, we do not
198   gather definitions of collected metrics, as their interpretations may vary across different tasks.
199
200
      With Dtrain and S, we then perform K iterations to select the metrics. At each iteration, we randomly
      sample a batch of data from Dtrain and instruct M to generate the top-5 most important metrics
201
      in S for evaluating batch data properties via Chain-of-Thought prompting (Wei et al., 2022). We
202
      implement the top-5 constraint to avoid excessive metrics being selected. The final set of selected
203   metrics, denoted by M , consists of the metrics chosen across all iterations sorted in alphabetic order.
204
205
      Step 2: Metric Score Collection via Self-evaluation. This step focuses on evaluating the selected
206   metrics from M on Dtrain to capture the task properties. Motivated by prior studies (Wang et al.,
207   2023a; Ren et al., 2023), we utilize M to score the metrics on a scale of 1–5. Specifically, for each
208   train sample, M scores its ground-truth answer on all M’s metrics via Self-Consistency (Wang
209   et al., 2022a). The final metrics’ scores, denoted as scoresM , are the average of scores over all train
210   samples. Note that we separate this step from Step 1’s metric selection because we want to evaluate
211   each chosen metric on Dtrain instead of the samples that led M to select it.
212
213   Step 3: Generating Metric Guideline (MG). This step aims to generate a textual metric guideline
214   (MG) that guides M to align generation outputs with task-specific properties from scoresM . MG is
215   formed by concatenating metrics’ definitions generated by M and tailored by scoresM via the LLM
      instruction “Based on these scores on a scale of 5...define the expected quality of the output for each

      Step 4: Output Constraint Guideline (OCG). Research on controllable generation has extensively
      proposed constraints including ones on the length, which are broadly applicable, as well as linguistic
251
      or keyword, which are more task-specific (Fan et al., 2018a; He et al., 2022). In this step, we aim

      to establish a robust set of output constraints that apply universally to long-form generation tasks.
253   We focus on six key constraints related to two distributions: the number of sentences and tokens in
254   ground-truth answers. These constraints include minimum, maximum, and average counts, serving as

260   optimal MG and OCG combination by evaluating model performance on Dtrain across configurations.
261   The best-performing guideline configuration is then selected as the final output of LongGuide.

510   6     R ELATED W ORK
511
512   Automatic prompt design for long-form generation. Long-form generation tasks are essential
513   and have been studied extensively (Li et al., 2024). With LLM advancements, adapting these models
514   for such tasks using prompt-based methods is critical yet challenging. Previous studies (Bang et al.,
515   2023; Yang et al., 2023a; Hadi et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023b; Pan et al., 2024) highlight the limited
516
      efficacy of LLMs in producing outputs that resemble ground truths, as evaluated by ROUGE-L (Lin,
      2004). Our approach autonomously composes supplementary contexts, integrating text evaluation
517
      metrics and format constraints. In addition, studies regarding enhancing instructions for LLMs (Wang
518
      et al., 2022b; Yin et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023b), automatic prompt optimization (Zhou et al., 2023a;
519   Pryzant et al., 2023), and demonstration selection (Yang et al., 2023b; Qin et al., 2023) are also
520   related areas that can be developed in parallel and combined with our work (§C.3).
521
522   Controllable generation with LLMs. Controllable generation during fine-tuning has been studied
523   extensively (Fan et al., 2018a; Lakew et al., 2019; Martin et al., 2020; He et al., 2022). More recently,
524   researchers have explored prompting methods to control LLM generation. For instance, Sun et al.
525   (2023b) found that LLMs struggle to meet fine-grained hard constraints, while Fonseca & Cohen
526   (2024) proposed controlling stylistic features like keywords and narrative during generation, leading
527   to improved LLM summarization outcomes. Although (Lu et al., 2023; Fonseca & Cohen, 2024) are
528   closely related to our OCG, our approach goes beyond summarization and open-ended only features,
529   as discussed in §3. We focus on universally applicable features across multiple tasks.
530
531   7     C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this paper, we demonstrate that in-context learning (ICL) falls short in implicitly ensuring that large
534   language models (LLMs) consistently preserve essential language and format properties in long-form
535   generation tasks. To address this challenge, we introduce LongGuide, an efficient algorithm that
536   automatically learns the critical language and format properties from task-specific data, converting
537   them into textual guidelines for LLMs. Our results show that LongGuide significantly improves
538   LLM performance across seven generation tasks and is highly generalizable, offering strong potential
539   for various downstream applications with minimal data. This work paves the way for adaptive,
      task-specific prompt generation, advancing LLM adaptation.

      G ENERALIZABILITY AND C USTOMIZATION OF L ONG G UIDE
541
542
      LongGuide facilitates flexible generalization that allows customization and extension of guidelines
543
      MG and OCG for specific tasks, which we strongly recommend. For instance, in summarization,
544
      MG can focus on only 4-5 standard metrics from S while integrating summary-specific metrics
545   like “Summary Structure” and “Retention of Core Supporting Evidence.” Simultaneously, OCG
546   can impose stricter constraints on topics, keywords, grammar, or tones (Fan et al., 2018a; Lakew
547   et al., 2019; Martin et al., 2020). Although LongGuide is primarily presented for general long-form
548   generation, we strongly advise for these customizations to enhance its effectiveness.

      L IMITATIONS
552
553   Our study has several limitations. Firstly, our theoretical analysis focuses solely on the task language
554
      distribution which is PM (X) or PM (X|Df ) instead of the actual output distribution, which is
      arg maxy∈Y PM (Y = y | X) or arg maxy∈Y PM (Y = y | Df , X). In our study, while leveraging
555
      the task language distribution allows us to hypothesize and highlight the limitations of demonstrations,
556
      shifting focus to the actual output distribution could yield more insights.
557
558   Secondly, LongGuide’s learned guidelines are based on task-level and average statistics rather
559   than sample-based details. We designed our framework at the task level to address limited data
560
      constraints, as we found that sample-based learning under these conditions leads to high errors.
      While task-level guidelines already demonstrate significant improvements for LLMs, sample-based
561
      guidelines could offer more tailored guidance, potentially leading to optimal results. Moreover,
562
      this average guidance approach may be ineffective for tasks with high variance in the statistics that
563   LongGuide learns. In such cases, the final step of LongGuide can prevent performance decline by
564   likely choosing no guideline. For example, we found this applies to Code2Text (Richardson et al.,
565   2017) & StoryGeneration (Fan et al., 2018b).
566
      Thirdly, LongGuide relies on models having a certain level of task knowledge to perform self-
567
      evaluation effectively, and LongGuide necessitates LLMs with strong instruction-following capabili-
568
      ties. However, we anticipate that cutting-edge AI language models will overcome this limitation both
569   now and in the near future.
570
571   Lastly, the guidelines learned by LongGuide may not be useful for the tasks the models are trained
572
      on. This is because these guidelines might introduce out-of-distribution context relative to the
      training data, thereby reducing the effectiveness of the testing inference. For instance, while we see
573
      notable enhancements on the CommonGen-Challenge dataset (Lin et al., 2020), it’s intriguing that
574
      we don’t observe any improvements on the WebNLG (Gardent et al., 2017) and E2E NLG (Puzikov
575   & Gurevych, 2018) datasets, despite their expected similarity. Given the popularity of these datasets,
576   we suspect the models we tested may have been previously trained on them.
577
578
579   E THICAL C ONSIDERATIONS
580
581   This method could be misused to optimize prompts for harmful purposes such as generating misin-
582   formation, hate speech, or privacy violations. While our method is not intended for such uses, it is
583   impossible to completely prevent misuse. Although our method could enhance the efficiency and
584
      efficacy of bad actors, we do not anticipate that LongGuide is inherently more effective in these
      negative contexts than in positive applications. Finally, we employ annotators at an hourly rate of
585
      $20, which exceeds the local minimum wage requirements.
