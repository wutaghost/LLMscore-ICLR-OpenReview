                                                  A BSTRACT
013
014
               In recent years, deep neural networks have showcased their predictive power
015
               across a variety of tasks. Beyond natural language processing, the transformer
016
               architecture has proven efficient in addressing tabular data problems and challenges
017            the previously dominant gradient-based decision trees in these areas. However, this
018            predictive power comes at the cost of intelligibility: Marginal feature effects are
019            almost completely lost in the black-box nature of deep tabular transformer networks.
020            Alternative architectures that use the additivity constraints of classical statistical
021            regression models can maintain intelligible marginal feature effects, but often fall
022            short in predictive power compared to their more complex counterparts. To bridge
023            the gap between intelligibility and performance, we propose an adaptation of tabular
024            transformer networks designed to identify marginal feature effects. We provide
025
               theoretical justifications that marginal feature effects can be accurately identified,
               and our ablation study demonstrates that the proposed model efficiently detects
026
               these effects, even amidst complex feature interactions. To demonstrate the model’s
027
               predictive capabilities, we compare it to several interpretable as well as black-box
028            models and find that it can match black-box performances while maintaining
029            intelligibility. The source code is vailable at https://anonymous.4open.
030            science/r/nmfrmr-B086.

033   1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Interpretability has emerged as one of the core concepts of tabular data analysis. Especially in
036   high-risk domains such as healthcare, where understanding the data’s underlying effects is of crucial
037   importance, this leads researchers to commonly rely on identifiable and interpretable generalized
038   additive models (GAMs) (Hastie, 2017), instead of powerful neural networks or decision trees
039
      (Erfanian et al., 2021; Ravindra et al., 2019; Prata et al., 2020). In applications where predictive
      power is a central objective, researchers often resort to model-agnostic methods that try to explain
040
      model predictions via local approximation and feature importance like Locally Interpretable Model
041
      Explanations (LIME) (Ribeiro et al., 2016), or Shapley values (Shapley, 1953) and their extensions
042   (Sundararajan & Najmi, 2020). While these methods are very effective for, e.g., image classification
043   tasks, they can be hard to interpret for tabular regression problems.
044
045
      Although predictive modeling in the domain of tabular data is traditionally dominated by tree-
      based bagging and boosting approaches (Breiman, 2001; Chen & Guestrin, 2016; Prokhorenkova
046
      et al., 2018), several relatively recent results show that deep-learning based techniques can be
047
      highly competitive in general or even superior on specific tabular datasets (McElfresh et al., 2024).
048   In particular models utilizing the transformer architecture stand out in terms of their predictive
049   power (Gorishniy et al., 2021; 2023; Hollmann et al., 2022). The most performant models, Tabular
050   (bayesian) prior-fitted transformer models (Hollmann et al., 2022), can only be used for smaller
051   datasets. However, FT-Transformers have robustly proven to be performant on tabular problems
052   (Gorishniy et al., 2021; Grinsztajn et al., 2022; McElfresh et al., 2024). Nevertheless, despite the use
053   of [ CLS ] tokens, allowing for heuristic interpretability of feature importance, these models remain
      black boxes and do not provide insights into marginal feature effects.

054
      To bridge the gap in performance seen with traditional statistical models while preserving inter-
055   pretability, recent efforts have focused on enhancing visual interpretability by incorporating additivity
056   constraints into neural network architectures (Agarwal et al., 2021; Chang et al., 2021; Enouen & Liu,
057   2022). Similar to GAMs each feature is fit with a separate shape function. Neural additive models
058   (NAMs) (Agarwal et al., 2021) and their extensions have emerged as a powerful yet interpretable
059   solution for tabular data problems. Depending on the model, shape functions vary from Multi-layer
060   Perceptrons (MLPs) (Agarwal et al., 2021; Radenovic et al., 2022; Thielmann et al., 2023) to neural
061   oblivious decision trees (Chang et al., 2021), splines (Luber et al., 2023; Rügamer et al., 2023; 2021;
062   Dubey et al., 2022), ensemble decision trees (Nori et al., 2019) or transformer networks (Thielmann
063
      et al., 2024). While these models offer visual interpretability, they come with inherent downsides:
      I) There is a performance gap relative to fully connected black-box models and even to simple MLPs.
064
      II) The networks can become parameter-dense, depending on the complexity of the marginal effects,
065
      as each feature is modeled with a distinct shape function (Agarwal et al., 2021; Thielmann et al.,
066   2023; 2024). III) The complexity of the model structure grows rapidly with the number of features,
067   especially when accounting for feature interactions, leading not only to a potentially suboptimal
068   inductive bias, but also a vast hyperparamter space making effective hyperparamter tuning com-
069   putationally expensive or even impossible. IV) Additionally, higher-order feature interactions can
070   negatively impact the model’s identifiability and are thus are often simply excluded (Kim et al., 2022;
071   Siems et al., 2024).
072   We propose to leverage the existing proven, and highly performant architectures for deep tabular
073   learning and introduce a new architecture to bridge the gap between high-performing tabular models
074   and inherently interpretable models using the flexible tabular deep learning architecture from Gorish-
075   niy et al. (2021). More specifically, we use target-aware embeddings (Gorishniy et al., 2022) and fit
076   shallow one layer neural networks on uncontextualized embeddings while accounting for all higher
077   order interaction effects.
078   Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
079
080         I. We introduce the NAMformer, a fully connected tabular deep learning architecture that
081            combines a powerful FT-Transformer with interpretable feature networks.
082
           II. We demonstrate that this straightforward approach yields intelligible and identifiable
083
               marginal feature effects, while perfectly maintaining the predictive power of FT-
084            Transformers and adding an almost negligible amount of additional parameters to the
085            model.
086
           III. We show that identifiability can be achieved by employing strategic feature dropout.

089   2    M ETHODOLOGY
090
091   The core of the NAMformer architecture is given by an FT-Transformer in combination with a
092   shallow MLP that both take uncontextualized embeddings as their input. In a nutshell, all numerical
093   features are encoded and all categorical features are tokenized. Subsequently all features are fed
094   through data type dependent embedding layers. The embeddings are passed through a stack of
095   transformer layers, after which the [ CLS ] token embedding is processed by a task specific model
096
      head. The uncontextualized embeddings, before being passed through the transformer layers, are fed
      to shallow, one-layer independent neural networks. The final prediction is gained by summation over
097
      all shallow feature networks as well as the task specific head. The model is trained end-to-end. An
098
      overview over the models structure is given in figure 2. The model architecture, the reasoning for
099   leveraging the uncontextualized embeddings for intelligibility, as well as the identifiability constraints
100   are explained in detail below. In summary, we present a model that achieves identical performance
101   to FT-Transformers (Gorishniy et al., 2021) while maintaining intelligibility with marginally more
102   trainable parameters.

174   From Uncontextual Embeddings to Marginal Predictions To understand the potential of uncon-
175   textualized embeddings as direct representations of raw input features in non-textual data settings,
176   our investigation first examines their role within tabular data models. Importantly, using target aware
177   encodings for preprocessing introduces non-linearity to numerical features, similar to neural spline
178   expansions (Luber et al., 2023).
179   State-of-the-art language models, leveraging the Transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017) first
180   create context-insensitive input token representations. In Natural Language Processing, these are the
181   raw, uncontextualized word embeddings. Subsequently, they compute L layers of context-dependent
182   representations, finally resulting in contextualized embeddings of the raw word representations (Peters
183   et al., 2018). The proposed NAMformer architecture employs these uncontextualized embeddings
184   directly to generate identifiable marginal feature predictions in a tabular context, necessitating a
185
      thorough analysis of their capability and effectiveness. By leveraging the uncontextualized embed-
      dings, we aim to evaluate their utility in the proposed model architecture. This exploration is critical
186
      as it may reveal that raw, minimally processed embeddings can sufficiently capture and represent
187
      the essential characteristics of the features, potentially simplifying the model architecture while
188   maintaining high predictive accuracy and interpretability.
189
190   In the context of tabular transformer networks,
191
      which do not employ positional encodings, the
      nature of context differs significantly from that
192
      in transformer models trained on textual data.
193
      The context that is added in the transformer lay-
194   ers, consists of the feature interactions (Huang
195   et al., 2020). The uncontextualized, raw em-
196   beddings, however, are seldom used and are
197   merely a byproduct of the model architecture.
198   Since the input data for numerical features is not
199   tokenized, token identifiability (Brunner et al.,
200   2019) directly applies to the tabular input data.

      Additivity Constraint Since we have verified that the uncontextualized embeddings almost per-
217   fectly preserve the original single feature information, we use them to include marginal feature
218   predictions in the model. To achieve this, the uncontextualized embeddings are passed through single
219   layer neural networks, similar to neural splines (e.g. (Luber et al., 2023; Rügamer et al., 2021).
220   Subsequently, we make use of a simple additivity constraint following GAMs. Given a link function
221   g(·) that connects the linear predictor to the expected value of the response variable, accommodating
222   different types of data distributions, a GAM in its fundamental form can be expressed as follows:

      5    C ONCLUSION
487
488
      We present NAMformer, an effective adaptation to the FT-Transformer architecture. We can ef-
489   fectively incorporate marginal feature effects and show theoretical justification of our approach.
490   With minimally more parameters compared to the FT-Transformer architecture, NAMformer achieve
491   identical performance while also generating identifiable marginal feature predictions. The reasoning
492   for including an additivity constraint and fitting shallow feature networks in tabular transformers is
493   thus that without loss of generalizability and without loss of performance, we can get an interpretable
494   model at the cost of - depending on the embedding size and the number of features - marginally more
495   parameters. Our theoretical justification of identifiable marginal feature effects is also seamlessly
496   applicable to models incorporating unstructured data (Rügamer et al., 2023; Reuter et al., 2024).
497
      Therefore, the achieved results are a further step into intelligible deep learning models beyond tabular
      data analysis.

      6    L IMITATIONS
515
516
      The interpretability of NAMformer, while significantly better than that of black-box models, still
517
      does not match the inherent statistical inference capabilities of classical GAMs. True interpretability
518   in the form of significance statistics is still a problem for further research.
519
520   Additionally, this paper solely focuses on single marginal feature effects. While we account for
521
      high-order feature interactions, we do not explicitly account for, e.g., second order feature interactions
      as models like EB2 M do. Hence, interesting interaction effects as the ones between, e.g., longitude
522
      and latitude are not specifically accounted for. However, the introduced identifiability constraint
523
      seamlessly enables to account for any amount and order of feature interactions that one wants to
524   account for. Simple feature interaction networks can be easily incorporated and fit on a combination
525   of the uncontextualized embeddings.

      A    L ITERATURE R EVIEW
703
704
      This manuscript can be categorized into two main areas of literature: tabular deep learning and
705
      additive interpretable modeling, the latter being largely inspired by classical statistical models. An
706
      additive model, as the name suggests, learns marginal feature effects and derives its final prediction
707   by summing over these effects (e.g., see Hastie (2017)). These models originate from simple linear
708   regression, but instead of relying on linear effects, generalized additive models (GAMs) allow for the
709   learning of more complex relationships through shape functions, as shown in Equation 1.
710
      A key aspect of additive models is that they allow interpretation of marginal feature effects, which
711
      quantify the isolated impact of each individual feature on the prediction while holding all other
712
      features constant. Marginal effects provide a clear, interpretable mapping of feature-to-outcome
713   relationships, making them especially valuable in domains such as healthcare, finance, and policy-
714   making, where understanding why a model makes a specific prediction is critical (e.g. (Hastie &
715   Tibshirani, 1995; Barrio et al., 2013; Mize et al., 2019)). By offering transparency into the model’s
716   reasoning, interpretable marginal effects allow practitioners to validate predictions, build trust in the
717   model, and gain actionable insights for decision-making.
718
      Classical GAMs (e.g., (Hastie, 2017; Wood, 2017)) often use splines for basis expansions. For an
719   introduction, see (Fahrmeir et al., 2013). This approach offers significant advantages, particularly in
720   terms of interpretability and intelligibility. However, detecting complex feature effects, especially
721   those involving interactions, can be challenging for spline-based models. This limitation has motivated
722   the development of Neural Additive Models (NAMs) (Agarwal et al., 2021), which replace splines
723   with shape functions modeled via multi-layer perceptrons (MLPs) optimized through gradient descent.
724
      Extensions of NAMs, such as those proposed in (Thielmann et al., 2023; 2024; Luber et al., 2023;
725   Kim et al., 2022; Chang et al., 2021), build on this simple additive modeling concept. Although these
726   models outperform classical GAMs, they often lag behind the performance of models like XGBoost
727   or FT-Transformers (Gorishniy et al., 2021).
728
      Tabular deep learning models, such as the FT-Transformer, do not impose the additivity constraint
729
      from Equation 1. As a result, they can effectively capture higher-order feature interactions. The
730
      underlying approach is straightforward: all features are passed jointly through the architecture. In the
731   case of FT-Transformers, features are first embedded into a higher-dimensional space via embedding
732   layers (Gorishniy et al., 2022), then processed through the transformer blocks. Finally, the outputs
733   are pooled and passed through a task-specific model head to derive the final prediction.
734
      Although tabular deep learning models and (tabular) additive models are closely related, to the best
735
      of our knowledge, no existing model achieves both interpretable marginal effect modeling and the
736
      performance of state-of-the-art models like XGBoost or FT-Transformers.
