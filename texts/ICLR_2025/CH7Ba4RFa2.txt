009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Monocular 3D lane detection is a fundamental yet challenging task in autonomous
012            driving. Recent advancements primarily rely on constructing 3D surrogates from
013            monocular images and camera parameters. However, misalignment is introduced
014            in current methods due to the lack of dense depth information in datasets, coupled
015            with the inherent depth ambiguity of monocular images. To address this issue,
016            we propose Seg-LaneDet, a simple but effective end-to-end 3D lane detector. We
017            frame the task of 3D lane detection as an elevation from 2D to 3D detection.
018
               Specifically, we leverage a pre-trained 2D lane detector to obtain instance seg-
               mentation of lanes, of which the segmentation maps serve as the sole prior for
019
               the 2D-to-3D module. This allows us to achieve a straightforward 3D lane rep-
020
               resentation based on front-view segmentation maps. Our method demonstrates
021            performance comparable to state-of-the-art (SOTA) F1 scores on the OpenLane
022            and the Apollo datasets.

111   2.1   2D L ANE D ETECTION
112
113
114
      Significant progress has been made in 2D lane detection, widely applied in autonomous driving.
      These deep learning methods can be categorized into four main approaches: row-wise methods,
115
      pixel-wise segmentation, anchor-based methods, and parametric methods. Some methods (Cho
116
      et al., 2020); (Chen et al., 2021) set the row anchors in the row direction and the column cells in the
117   column direction to represent the 2D lanes. These methods turn the detection into a classification
118   problem of row vectors. Some works (Li et al., 2022); (Luo et al., 2018); (Cai et al., 2021) consider
119   the 2D lane detection as a segmentation task based on pixel-wise, which has the flexibility. However,
120   the computing cost is more expensive than the row-wise methods. Inspired by region-based object
121   detectors, anchor-based methods (Wang et al., 2018); (Badue et al., 2021b) employ different kinds
122   of anchors to localize lanes. parametric methods (Feng et al., 2022); (Badue et al., 2021a) rethink
123   lane detection as a curve-fitting problem. These methods use priors about lane shapes to represent
124   the parametric representations.
125
126
127   2.2   3D L ANE D ETECTION
128
129
130
      To address the growing demand for precise 3D lanes in autonomous driving, researchers have a
      notable interest in researching 3D lane detection. 3D-LaneNet (Cohen et al., 2019) is the pioneer
131
      of the task, which firstly transforms the front-view features to bird-eye-viewed for 3D lane detec-
132
      tion. The framework uses two pipelines to fuse information from different views of FV and BEV.
133   Gen-LaneNet (Chen et al., 2020) proposes a two-stage model that first encodes an input image and
134   then only decodes its lane segmentation map. Due to the compression of information compared to
135   the original image, the semantic segmentation graph is difficult to support the 2D-to-3D process.
136   PersFormer (Chen et al., 2022) proposes a novel transformer-based (Vaswani et al., 2017) to realize
137   spatial transformation of features, unifying 2D and 3D lane detection. The other great contribution
138   of PersFormer is the OpenLane dataset, the first large-scale realistic 3D lane dataset. Unlike the
139   above methods, BEV-LaneDet (Cao et al., 2023) utilizes MLP to transform FV features to BEV,
140   which is a simple but effective convolutional neural network (CNN) framework. Furthermore, some
141
      studies (Bai et al., 2023); (Dai et al., 2023); (Cui et al., 2023) aim to directly regress 3D lanes from
      front-view features while incorporating designed anchors with the designed anchors. These meth-
142
      ods offer improvements in accuracy, while the dense anchors are needed to mitigate the perspective
143
      geometric distortion. Recently, PVALane (Gao et al., 2024) presented a view-agnostic feature align-
144   ment architecture, thinning anchors by 2D priors. LaneCPP (Condurache et al., 2024) introduces an
145   elegant representation based on parametric splines.

149   3     M ETHODOLOGY
150
151
152   As shown in Figure 2, the whole simple network architecture consists of three parts: 1) Pre-trained
153   Two-dimensional Lane Detector (PTD): a pre-trained detector for 2D prior; 2) U-shaped Lifting
154   Module (ULM): Lifting the 2D input to 3D; 3) 3D Lane Head (3DLH): a postprocessing method to
155   obtain the 3D lanes.
156   Given an input image, our model first detects 2D lanes using a pre-trained 2D lane detector. The
157   output consists of 2D lane instance segmentation, which includes a semantic segmentation map, a
158   clustering map, and a classification. Subsequently, the semantic segmentation map, the correspond-
159   ing image, and the 2D position embeddings are concatenated and fed into the U-shaped Lifting
160   Module (ULM). Following this, the 3D estimations of the entire scene are processed by the 3D
161   Lane Head. During training, these estimations are handled as 2D position estimates and 3D lane
      instances; however, during testing, the 2D position estimates utilized for training are not computed.

      3.3   U- SHAPED L IFTING M ODULE
202
203
      Inspired by previous methods (Cui et al., 2023); (Cao et al., 2023), we posit that spatial transfor-
204   mation is more advantageous for reverse-projecting front-view information into ground space than
205   traditional depth estimation techniques. Accordingly, we design a simple lifting module with a U-
206   shaped architecture. Previous studies (Belongie et al., 2017) have suggested that a pyramid structure
207   can leverage different resolutions to balance global and local information, thereby implicitly incor-
208   porating scale. Consequently, we utilize a U-Net architecture as the backbone of ULM. Since we
209   do not employ anchors, we construct a lightweight Absolute Scale Module (ASM) based on MLP to
210   introduce absolute scale information. The ULM consists of the U-Net and the ASM, as illustrated
211   in Figure 3.
212   To incorporate absolute scale information of the scene, we propose a simplified Absolute Scale
213   Module. This module combines the feature map channels from the bottom of the U-shaped structure
214   with the width dimension and performs a fully connected operation on the height. Since lanes in
215   the image extend along the elevation, the absolute scale differences between individual pixels are
      significant, making them easier to learn.

220   scene, lane, and point. Specifically, the point-level loss imposes constraints on the absolute errors of
221   lane positions, the lane-level loss regulates the relative shape of lane lines, and the scene-level loss

      4.1   DATASETS AND M ETRICS
302
303   OpenLane is the first real-world, large-scale 3D lane dataset, sourcing valuable content from the
304   Waymo dataset (Caine et al., 2020). This dataset comprises 200,000 frames, encompassing vari-
305   ous challenging scenarios such as Up&Down, Curve, Extreme Weather, Intersection, Merge&Split,
306   and Night cases, all at a resolution of 1280×1920. OpenLane includes 880,000 annotated lanes,
307   categorized into a total of 14 distinct types.
308
      Apollo Synthetic constructed to stimulate the development and evaluation of 3D lane detection
309   methods, consisting of 10,500 examples from diversified scenarios of highway, urban, and rural
310   environments. The data is split into three subsets, 1) Standard (simple) scenarios, 2) Rare Scenes,
311   and 3) Visual Variations.
312
      Evaluation Metrics. We employ the official inspection methods (Chen et al., 2022) to validate our
313
      model across the above datasets. The assessment metrics include the F1 score and the X/Z error.
314
      Initially, lanes are normalized and subsequently matched using the minimum-cost flow algorithm. A
315   lane is deemed if at least 75% of its points fall within a predefined threshold of 1.5 meters from the
316   ground truth. Furthermore, the error is categorized into close and far errors in the X/Z directions.
317   The close error corresponds to the Y direction within the range of [0, 40] meters, while the far error
318   applies within the range of [40, 100] meters.

419   4.4    A BLATION S TUDY
420
421   We conducted extensive experiments on the OpenLane dataset to validate the design of our model.
422
      Specifically, we performed ablation studies on data preprocessing, ULM, and losses. All experi-
      ments and training maintained consistent configuration settings.
423
424   Data Preprocessing. We concatenate the original image with corresponding 2D position embed-
425   dings and lane segmentation maps of the same size, employing this as a straightforward data aug-
426   mentation method. The effectiveness has been validated by training it with the individual masking
427   of both the 2D position embeddings and the lane segmentation maps. As shown in Table 3, our
428
      SegLaneDet fails to function effectively without the segmentation map input, underscoring that our
      methodology is fundamentally based on the segmentation maps. Furthermore, the two-dimensional
429
      position embedding significantly enhances performance, which improves our model on both sides.
430
431   U-shaped Lifting Module. Referencing BEVLaneDet, we constructed a scale pyramid encoder
      and a decoder of the same scale for comparison with our U-shaped Lifting Module (ULM). As


                                                         8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      shown in Table 4, the experimental results demonstrate that the ULM outperforms the scale pyramid
433   architecture. ASM played a part in helping SegLaneDet make some gains in F1 scores but sadly did
434   little to improve the absolute X/Z accuracy.
435
436
      Losses. To validate the rationale and effectiveness of our method’s loss functions, we experimented
      with various loss combinations, as detailed in Table 5. The experiment utilizes the point-level loss
437
      function, revealing that the lane-level and scene-level loss functions separately result in increases of
438
      4.8 and 8.4 in the F1 score, respectively. When both loss functions are incorporated together, the
439   improvement is 8.7. Our analysis indicates that the internal factors contributing to the enhancements
440   provided by these two loss functions partially overlap, which explains why the combined effect is
441   less than the sum of their individual contributions.

      5   C ONCLUSIONS
445
      In this work, we propose Seg-LaneDet, a front-view and simple 3D lane detector. We innovatively
446
      decompose 3D lane detection into two sub-tasks: 2D lane detection and dimensional lifting. Fur-
447   thermore, we introduce a frontal-view-based representation of 3D lanes, bypassing the need for 3D
448   proxies and cumbersome anchors. Our proposed ULM effectively integrates both relative and ab-
449   solute scale cues. Additionally, we introduce a hierarchical loss function tailored for the monocular
450   modality. Extensive experiments demonstrate the simplicity and efficacy of Seg-LaneDet. However,
451   our performance on the X/Z error metric is suboptimal, which may reflect a common issue associated
452   with segmentation-based methods. We hope that our work will spark further advancements.
