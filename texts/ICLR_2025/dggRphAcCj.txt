011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Compositional generalization, referring to the capacity to generalize novel com-
014            binations of fundamental and essential concepts, is thought to be the mechanism
015
               underlying a human’s remarkable ability of rapid generalization to new knowl-
               edge and tasks. Recent research on brain neural activation space has found that
016
               the geometric structure of neural representations is highly related to human com-
017
               positional generalization capability. In this paper, we extend the above obser-
018            vations from neuroscience to deep neural networks to validate the potential re-
019            lationship between the geometric structure of representations and compositional
020            generalization capability. In particular, we first construct a new compositional
021            generalization benchmark from the existent datasets, which aims to discriminate
022            multiple concepts simultaneously through a powerful representation. Meanwhile,
023            for the aforementioned geometric constraint, the parallelism score is formally de-
024            fined for deep neural networks. Subsequently, we decompose the deep neural net-
025            work into two parts: the featurizer and the classifier, to investigate the relationship
026
               between compositional generalization capability and parallelism score separately.
               Our proposed method, Geometric Constraint (GeoCon), involves distance vari-
027
               ance minimization on the classifier and parallelism score maximization on the fea-
028
               turizer. Experiments on synthetic and real-world datasets demonstrate significant
029            improvement of our approach, verifying the effectiveness of our neuroscience-
030            inspired GeoCon approach towards human-like superior generalization ability.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Humans exhibit a remarkable capacity for generalization by transferring existing limited prior
036   knowledge to novel contexts. One underlying mechanism is hypothesized to be compositional gen-
037   eralization (Cole et al., 2013; Frankland & Greene, 2020; Hupkes et al., 2020), the ability to sys-
038   tematically disentangle learned concepts and recombine them into unseen compositions (e.g., red
039
      apple and yellow banana can be decoupled and recomposed into a new composition yellow apple).
      This ability, described by Chomsky (2014) as “the infinite use of finite means”, is considered an
040
      essential characteristic of human intelligence. Despite the substantial advancements accomplished
041
      by deep neural networks (Li et al., 2021; Han et al., 2022), they still struggle with generalization per-
042   formance and have been criticized for lacking compositional generalization capability, even when
043   provided with extensive training data (Zhang et al., 2023). Consequently, it is a significant yet chal-
044   lenging research topic to study the compositional generalization mechanism of deep neural networks
045   (Lin et al., 2023), which is crucial for advancing toward artificial intelligence.
046
      Related Work. In previous research on compositional generalization in computer vision, one impor-
047
      tant related research field is disentangled representation learning (Higgins et al., 2017; Wang et al.,
048   2022), which aims to extract independent underlying concept factors from mixed representations
049   and recombine them to generate novel concept compositions predominantly on synthetic datasets.
050   However, it remains unclear whether disentanglement can assist in compositional generalization,
051   while some studies suggest that there is currently no evidence that explicitly decoupling input com-
052   positional factors substantially improves the learning efficiency or generalization capacity of models
053   (Montero et al., 2020; Schott et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2022), whereas some claim to find a correlation
      between disentanglement and compositional generalization (Higgins et al., 2017; Esmaeili et al.,

082   To demystify the mechanism of compositional generalization from a neuroscience perspective,
083   Bernardi et al. (2020) investigates the neural activation space in the hippocampus and prefrontal
084   cortex, proposing the compositional additive representation extracted from neural signals, identified
085
      as the abstract representation. They further propose the parallelism score to quantify the parallelism
      of the geometric structure for the abstract representation, which is positively related to the com-
086
      positional generalization capability, as shown in Fig.1.(a). The subsequent study (Ito et al., 2022)
087
      leverages the parallelism score to measure the fMRI activity signals of the human brain during the
088   execution of tasks that necessitate logical decision, semantic comprehension, and motor response.
089
090   Our Contributions. Motivated by the aforementioned observations from neuroscience research,
091
      we would like to validate whether this pattern is consistent in deep neural networks. Initially, we
      establish a novel compositional generalization task from the existent datasets and provide a formal
092
      definition of the parallelism score. Afterward, we partition the deep neural network into the featur-
093
      izer and the classifier, proposing regularization techniques: distance variance minimization on the
094   classifier and parallelism score maximization on the featurizer, to constrain the representation space.
095   As a result, we introduce the Geometric Constraint (GeoCon) to strengthen the visual compositional
096   generalization capability. Experimental results demonstrate that our GeoCon method surpasses the
097   current baselines across multiple datasets. This research endeavor has the potential to serve as a valu-
098   able investigation into the mechanisms underlying compositional generalization, thereby advancing
099   the development of deep neural networks toward achieving human-like intelligence.

257   variance minimization regularization on the classifier to enhance the CG capability.

259   combination samples, due to neglecting the highly parallel geometric structures of representations.

262   ating excessively from the (a2 , b2 )-center points, based on which we propose Distance Variance
263   Minimization (MinDV) regularization to achieve this insight.

288   The MinDV regularization promotes model consistency in the distance from the decision boundary
289   across all samples. The idea of “balance enhances generalization” has also been applied in domain
290   generalization. For instance, the Invariant Risk Minimization (IRM) (Arjovsky et al., 2019) algo-
291   rithm aims to achieve similar classification performance across diverse domains while maintaining
292   overall classification efficacy. In summary, both MinDV and IRM are based on the assumption that
293   there exists a substantial disparity between the observed training distribution and the unseen testing
294   distribution. They strive to ensure that the model performs similarly across all visible distributions,
295   rather than significantly outperforming on a specific distribution.

      Datasets. As described in Section 2.1, the CG benchmark requires each sample to have at least two
412
      concept labels. Therefore, domain generalization datasets that concurrently annotate class and do-
413
      main labels would serve as an feasible solution. We select the PACS (Li et al., 2017), Office-Home
414   (Saenko et al., 2010), DomainNet (Peng et al., 2019), and NICO (He et al., 2021) datasets, consid-
415   ering their class and domain labels as two separate concepts, to construct our CG task, among which
416   PACS, Office-Home, and DomainNet have concepts labeled as content and style, while NICO’s
417   concepts are labeled as object and environment. Refer to the Appendix D for more details.
418
      Due to the significant disparity in the quantity of classes compared to domains in the Office-Home,
419
      DomainNet, and NICO datasets, there exists a substantial difficulty gap between classifying the two
420   targets. To address this issue, we divide the datasets by class into several subsets, each containing a
421   comparable number of classes as the number of domains, and record the average performance across
422   these subsets. For each class, one domain is selected as the test set, while the remaining domains
423   serve as the train set, ensuring that all domains have appeared in the train set.
424
      For deeper analysis, we additionally leverage the synthetic dataset 3D Shapes (Kim & Mnih, 2018),
425
      composed of 6 factors: floor hue, wall hue, object hue, scale, shape, and orientation. We identify
426   shape and object hue as the concepts to be predicted, while regarding the other factors as noise,
427   providing a reasonable simulation of the real world. We randomly sample 1000 images for each
428   concept combination to construct our dataset. Half of the concept combinations are used for training,
429   while the remaining combinations are employed for testing. Each image is flattened into a one-
430   dimensional vector, and a three-layer fully connected neural network is employed, wherein the first
431   two layers are shared by two concepts, serving as a featurizer. Subsequently, two linear classifiers
      are connected to classify shape and object hue, respectively.

      4.3   D ISCUSSIONS
508
509   We conduct a more comprehensive analysis on the 3D Shapes dataset, leveraging supervised learn-
510   ing without the addition of any regularization as the baseline method. Fig.8 illustrates the variation
511   of metrics during the training process, wherein our approach demonstrates a significant improve-
512   ment of 22.29% compared to the baseline. When MaxPS is not used, the model converges rapidly
513   but experiences a decrease in accuracy after 25 epochs. It is noteworthy that the improvement in
514   accuracy aligns precisely with the uplift of PS, while the decline in accuracy corresponds to the
515
      stagnation of PS. Maintaining consistency with MaxPS, the application of MinDV leads to lower
      DV and improved accuracy, without which DV will ascend.
516
517   Fig.9.(a) and Fig.9.(b) display the t-SNE visualization results. The representations of the baseline
518   model still tend to cluster together but exhibit poor separability. In contrast, our GeoCon method
519   produces highly separable representations characterized by distinct linear discriminability.
520   To investigate the impact of pre-training on CG, we initially train a featurizer supervised by shape
521   solely and subsequently fine-tune it according to the CG setting. Surprisingly, the pre-trained models
522   demonstrated lower accuracy compared to models trained from scratch, as depicted in Fig.9.(c). This
523   could be attributed to biases introduced during the pre-training phase that are challenging to entirely
524   eliminate during fine-tuning. This observation suggests that simply fine-tuning existing pre-trained
525   models may not be efficacious for enhancing CG performance. The development of algorithms with
526   stronger CG capability necessitates consideration from the pre-training stage.
527
528   5     C ONCLUSIONS
529
530   Conclusions. We formally introduce the parallelism score from neuroscience to deep learning, re-
531   vealing a strong positive correlation between it and compositional generalization from a geometry
532   perspective. Our framework, GeoCon, consisting of MinDV for the classifier and MaxPS for the fea-
533   turizer, aims to enhance CG capability by constraining the geometric structures of representations
534   and forcing the decision boundaries to conform to the well-organized structure. Experiments show
535   that GeoCon outperforms traditional approaches. This neuroscience-inspired representation mecha-
536
      nism may elucidate the fundamental nature of human-like intelligence in deep neural networks.
537   Limitations. Our GeoCon currently relies on aligned concepts, presenting challenges when scaling
538   up to more complex and productive tasks. Furthermore, the computation of PS requires a substantial
539   quantity of concept combinations inside each batch, which is of relative inefficiency. This issue
      could potentially be addressed by introducing a memory bank to mitigate computational complexity.

       E.2   BATCH S IZE
1109
1110   To get an absolutely accurate PS, it needs to calculate all samples of the dataset. According to
1111   Eq.14, the minimal sample quantity required to calculate PS is 4 with the target concepts of (a1 , b1 ),
1112   (a1 , b2 ), (a2 , b1 ), and (a2 , b2 ).
1113
       As stated in Section 3.2, we hope to generate an estimation of PS as accurately as possible. The batch
1114
       size depends on the specific dataset, including the number of concept factors and the number of target
1115   categories. More concept factors and target categories imply a larger batch size. In practice, we set
1116   the batch size as 256. Compared to the domain quantity of no more than 6 and the class quantity
1117   of no more than 7, the batch size is big enough to generate an accurate estimation. However, when
1118   the batch size is limited, we can employ an exponential smoothing method to reduce the estimation
1119   variance, thus resolving the challenge.
