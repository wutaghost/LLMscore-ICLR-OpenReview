009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Pre-trained vision-language models (VLMs), such as CLIP, have exhibited re-
012            markable performance across various downstream tasks by aligning text and im-
013            ages in a unified embedding space. However, due to the imbalanced distribution
014            of pre-trained datasets, CLIP suffers from the bias problem in real-world appli-
015            cations. Existing debiasing methods struggle to obtain sufficient image samples
016            for minority groups and incur high costs for group labeling. To address the lim-
017            itations, we propose a Text-Only Debiasing framework called TOD, leveraging
018
               a text-as-image training paradigm to mitigate visual biases. Specifically, this ap-
               proach repurposes the text encoder to function as an image encoder, thereby elim-
019
               inating the need for image data. Simultaneously, it utilizes a large language model
020
               (LLM) to generate a balanced text dataset, which is then used for prompt tun-
021            ing. However, we observed that the model overfits to the text modality because
022            label names, serving as supervision signals, appear explicitly in the texts. To ad-
023            dress this issue, we further introduce a Multi-Target Prediction (MTP) task that
024            motivates the model to focus on complex contexts and distinguish between tar-
025            get and biased information. Extensive experiments on the Waterbirds and CelebA
026            datasets show that our method significantly improves group robustness, achiev-
027            ing state-of-the-art results among image-free methods and even competitive per-
028            formance compared to image-supervised methods. Furthermore, the proposed
029
               method can be adapted to challenging scenarios with multiple or unknown bias
               attributes, demonstrating its strong generalization and robustness.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      In recent years, pre-trained VLMs have made significant progress. Benefitting from large amounts of
035
      image-text data, the VLMs, such as CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) and ALIGN (Jia et al., 2021), learn
036   rich visual and textual representations. Specifically, these models construct a joint visual-language
037   embedding space where semantically relevant images and text are encoded as close features. This
038   aligned embedding space enables CLIP to excel in various downstream tasks, such as zero-shot
039   classification (Guo et al., 2023b), image-text retrieval (Baldrati et al., 2022) and image captioning
040   (Mokady et al., 2021).
041
      While CLIP demonstrate impressive capabilities, it inherits biases or spurious correlations from
042   the inappropriate pre-training data. This behavior leads to poor group robustness in downstream
043   tasks—the model performs significantly worse on some groups where the correlations do not hold.
044   The lack of group robustness not only weakens the generalization ability of the model but also has
045   the potential to exacerbate existing social biases such as racism or gender discrimination. Therefore,
046   addressing the bias and robustness challenges in CLIP models has become a critical issue.
047
      Many works aim to improve group robustness and have been applied to vision-language models.
048   Previous methods typically retrain or fine-tune the model on downstream visual tasks with group
049   annotations (Sagawa et al., 2019; Byrd & Lipton, 2019; Idrissi et al., 2022; Kirichenko et al., 2023).
050   Other works have explored debiasing of CLIP without group labels, such as deriving them from
051   zero-shot predictions(Liu et al., 2021; Nam et al., 2020), to lower annotation costs. However, ac-
052   quiring sufficient images for minority group (such as waterbirds on land) can be expensive or even
053   infeasible, while the training process on image may be computationally intensive. Therefore, recent
      works (Chuang et al., 2023) have proposed training-free debiasing methods, but achieved inferior

054
      performance compared to those using image supervision. To address these limitations, we propose
055   a Text-Only Debiasing framework called TOD, which leverages CLIP’s ability to align across both
056   image and text modalities, exploiting textual data to mitigate visual biases.
057
058
      To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work to leverage a text-as-image (TaI) training paradigm
      (Guo et al., 2023a) to mitigate visual biases. TOD comprises two stages: balanced text data genera-
059
      tion and text-only training. Given a set of categories and biased attributes, we first employ GPT-4o
060
      to generate textual descriptions that contain the target and attribute names, yielding a distribution-
061   balanced training dataset. In this way, group labels can be directly derived from target and attribute
062   names. Then, the text descriptions serve as alternatives to images in prompt tuning, and the learned
063   prompt can classify images during inference. Compared to images, our approach is more cost-
064   effective and scalable by utilizing the capabilities of LLMs to automatically generate balanced text
065   data and group labels.
066
      While this method reduces the reliance on costly image data, we observe that text-only training can
067   lead to overfitting to the text modality, exhibiting poor generalization when transferred to classify
068   images as shown in Figure 2. This is primarily because the classification in text-only training can be
069   easily made by matching the class names in prompts with similar words in text descriptions, which
070   is less influenced by bias information compared to image-text matching. To address this issue, in-
071   spired by human visual perception which processes multiple objects in parallel (Brandman & Peelen,
072   2017; de Wit et al., 2011), we intoduce a Multi-Target Prediction (MTP) task. This task involves
073   simultaneously predicting target attributes and bias attributes within a single prompt. By compelling
074   the model to actively focus on different attribute words or image regions, MTP increases task dif-
075
      ficulty, thereby mitigating overfitting. Unlike previous works that remove bias information from
      representations (Chuang et al., 2023; Berg et al., 2022), our method guides the model to distinguish
076
      between the concepts of target attributes and biased attributes, achieving a more robust debiasing
077
      effect. Experiments on the Waterbids and CelebA datasets show that our method significantly out-
078   performs existent image-free methods and even surpasses image-supervised methods. Moreover, the
079   approach can be naturally adapted to more challenging scenarios, delivering robust performance in
080   the presence of multiple bias attributes or when the bias attributes are unknown.
081
      Our main contributions are summarized as follows:
082
083         1. We propose a novel Text-Only Debiasing framework (TOD), which mitigates bias in CLIP
084            by utilizing LLMs to generate balanced text datasets, circumventing the need for visual
085            data.
086
            2. We introduce an effective solution for modal overfitting in text-only training: Multi-Target
087
               Prediction (MTP) with prompt tuning. This significantly enhances the model’s debiasing
088            performance when applied to image data.
089
            3. Our method significantly improve robustness group robustness on the Waterbirds and
090
               CelebA datasets, achieving performance comparable to state-of-the-art image-supervised
091
               methods. Besides, TOD can be seamlessly extended to scenarios with multiple bias at-
092            tributes and unknown bias attributes, demonstrating its strong generalization and robust-
093            ness.

096   2   R ELATED WORK
097
098   Group Robustness of Vison Models. Many works use supervision of group labels in training to
099
      improve the group robustness of vision models. This includes strategies such as minimizing the
      loss of the worst group for robustness optimization (Sagawa et al., 2019), importance reweighting
100
      (Byrd & Lipton, 2019; Shimodaira, 2000) or subsampling to balance the samples of major and minor
101
      groups (Idrissi et al., 2022; Kirichenko et al., 2023). All of these methods require group labels for
102   the entire training set, which can be extremely expensive. Recent research has circumvented the
103   need of group annotations during training, instead relies only on a small validation set with group
104   labels for hyperparameter selection. A common approach is to first train a model to infer the group
105   labels of the samples and then train a second robust model. For example, BPA (Seo et al., 2022)
106   automatically infers group labels and performs robust training by clustering sample points. JTT (Liu
107   et al., 2021) and LfF (Nam et al., 2020) reweight difficult or misclassified samples. CNC (Zhang
      et al., 2022) learns similar representations for samples from different groups of the same class by


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      contrastive loss. However, all of these methods rely on the downstream training set and often train
109   the visual model twice, which is costly when applied to large foundation models.
110
111   Debiasing Vison-Language Models. Recently, the bias issue in vision-language models (e.g.,
112   CLIP) has garnered increasing attention (Hall et al., 2023; Agarwal et al., 2021; Slyman et al.,
113   2023). Considering the huge number of parameters in VLMs, many studies have focused on elim-
114   inating spurious correlations to the bias attribute without updating the entire network weights. For
115   example, Berg et al. (2022) mitigates bias in CLIP via adversarial prompt tuning, Zhang & Ré (2022)
116   uses contrastive learning to train an adapter (a 2-layer MLP) in the representation space. Meanwhile,
117   Seth et al. (2023) introduces an additional residual module to separate protected attribute informa-
118
      tion from the visual representation. Dehdashtian et al. (2024) employs a kernel approach to debias
      CLIP, removing bias without the need for attribute annotations. However, these methods require a
119
      downstream image training set. Our approach requires only the target category and bias information
120
      without any training images. The closest related works aim to eliminate biases in vision-language
121   models in a zero-shot manner. Chuang et al. (2023) defines the directions of spurious correlation
122   with biased prompts and projects them out from the text embedding. However, these methods have
123   a large gap in performance with the trained methods, while our method has a competitive or even
124   surpassing effect compared to the methods trained on real data.
125
126   Text as Image Training. The CLIP encoder maps images and text into a shared embedding space
127   where semantically similar images and text are aligned with each other. This allows the use of
128   features from another modality in place of the original modality during the training phase. Since
129   text data is more accessible and contains richer vision concepts which facilitate model to learn
130   generalized representations, many works use text as a substitute for images in training. For example,
131
      Nukrai et al. (2022); Li et al. (2023); Fei et al. (2023) train a decoder to reconstruct the masked text
      and generate image captions by replacing the text input with images in the inference phase. Guo
132
      et al. (2023a); Zhu et al. (2023) exploited the inherent property of textual descriptions which contain
133
      semantic dependencies and spatial relationships between objects, to perform text-only training for
134   multi-labeled image recognition. Additionally, several works have employed text-as-image training
135   for tasks such as composed image retrieval (Gu et al., 2024), visual question answering (VQA) (Gu
136   et al., 2023), and visual entailment (Gu et al., 2023). Our work further explores the application
137   scenarios of text-as-image training by applying it to debiasing tasks for the first time.

141   Notation. In this work, we consider group robustness as the measure of bias, following the main-
142   stream practice (Sagawa et al., 2019; Creager et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2021; Kirichenko et al., 2023).
143   We denote the input image as x ∈ X, the target category as y ∈ Y , and the bias attribute category
144   as b ∈ B. Take the Waterbirds dataset (Sagawa et al., 2019) as an example, where the target task
145   is to recognize birds with category y ∈ {landbird, waterbird}, and the bias attribute is the back-
146   ground b ∈ {land, water}. The combination of the target category and the bias attribute category
147   divides the dataset into groups, denoted as g ∈ G, with the group labeling denoted as G = Y × B.
148   When the attribute s affects the prediction of y but lacks a causal relationship, it is considered as
149
      bias correlation. For example, in the Waterbirds dataset, 95% of the samples with y = waterbird
      have the bias attribute b = water. As a result, models trained on this dataset may rely heavily on
150
      the background (water) to predict the category (waterbirds), leading to incorrect predictions on the
151
      minority group g = (water, landbird). To measure the debiasing effect, we use the worst group
152   accuracy (WG) and the gap between the average accuracy and the worst group accuracy (Gap) as
153   our evaluation metrics.
154

158   convert high-dimensional image X and text sequences T , respectively, into embedding vectors in
159   a shared low-dimensional space. Specifically, this objective aims to maximize the cosine similarity

198
199   We propose a novel debiasing method for CLIP: multi-target prediction based on text as image
200   training, with the framework shown in Figure 1. Initially, we generate a balanced text dataset for the
201   downstream classification task using LLM in Section 4.1. During the training phase, we fine-tuning
202   the model on the text dataset in Section 4.2 (Training Phase). Particularly, we employ a multi-target
203
      prediction task that simultaneously predicts target and bias categories to mitigate overfitting and
      imitate human visual perception. During the inference phase, we perform multi-target prediction on
204
      the image input and select the combination of the target and bias categories with the highest logits,
205
      where the target category serves as the final classification result in Section 4.2 (Inference Phase).

      4.1       T EXT DATASET G ENERATION
209
      Attribute Description Generation. The first step of our method is to determine the attributes con-
210   tained within the dataset. Taking the Waterbird dataset as an example, we defined two key attributes:
211   the target attribute (bird) and the bias attribute (background). The target attribute is divided into two
212   categories: waterbird and landbird, while the bias attribute is divided into landscape and waterscape.
213   We applied the GPT-4o to generate descriptions of these attributes. The specific procedure was
214   as follows: we instructed the model to generate 1,000 short image descriptions of waterbirds, and
215   the instruction used was, “please help me to generate 100 very short images captions of waterbird.
      Be careful to describe only the appearance of the bird, not the background or environment.” This


                                                                                       4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      process was repeated 10 times. We generated descriptions for each attribute class, including 1,000
217   descriptions each for waterbird, landbird, landscape, and waterscape.
218
219
      Attribute Combination with Automatic Annotation. After generating independent attribute de-
220
      scriptions, we compose the attributes to construct a complete image description. Unlike the cou-
221
      pling between features in images, feature composition can be achieved in text by just concatenation.
222   Specifically, we randomly select a bird description from the waterbird or landbird description sets
223   and a background description from the landscape or waterscape description sets. These two parts are
224   then concatenated to form a complete image description that included both the foreground and the
225   background. To ensure a balanced training dataset, we generated an equal number of descriptions
226   for each attribute composition.
227
      This design automates the data annotation process since the source of each attribute description is
228   predefined with the corresponding label. When the bird description and background description
229   come from the waterbird description set and landscape description set respectively, we can directly
230   assign the target label T=1 (for waterbird) and the bias label B=0 (for landscape). Meanwhile, the
231   group label G is defined as the composition of attributes, e.g., G=1×0. The high annotation cost of
232   traditional image datasets is effectively avoided.
233
      Following the steps, we generated balanced text datasets containing 10,000 descriptions each for
234   the Waterbirds and CelebA datasets. Dataset construction details for CelebA are provided in the
235   Appendix A.2.

293   Dataset.   We conduct experiments on commonly used datasets for bias and fairness assessment.
294
             • Waterbirds (Sagawa et al., 2019) combines the Caltech-UCSD Birds-200-2011 (CUB)
295
               dataset (Wah et al., 2011) and the Places dataset (Zhou et al., 2017), placing images of
296            birds on different backgrounds to simulate different biases. The task is to classify fore-
297            ground birds (waterbird or landbird) and the background (land or water landscape) is a bias
298            attribute. Statistically, the minimum group in the training set contains only 53 images.
299
             • CelebA (Liu et al., 2015) contains over 200,000 portraits of celebrities, each annotated
300            with 40 binary attribute labels such as hair color, gender and age. The task on this dataset
301            is to classify hair color (blond or non-blond) and gender (male or female) is a bias at-
302            tribute. Statistically, the women examples is more than 94% in the blond hair of training
303            set. Additionally, we consider more complex scenarios including multiple bias attributes
304            and unknown bias attributes on the CelebA.

309   setup, we use the worst group accuracy of the image validation set to select the stop epoch and

      hyperparameters. In scenarios where the bias attribute is unknown, we select the stop epoch and

      6   CONCLUSION
517
518
      In this paper, we propose a text-only debiasing method for CLIP. We perform prompt tuning on a
519
      balanced text dataset generated by a large language model. Further, we observe that text-only train-
520
      ing may lead to overfitting. To address this issue, we propose multi-target prediction that predicts
521   both target and bias attributes. Experiments show that our method achieves comparable performance
522   to image-supervised methods and can seamlessly extend to multi-bias and bias-unknown scenarios.
523   Future work may apply text-only training to address bias issues in other vision tasks, such as image-
524   text retrieval.
