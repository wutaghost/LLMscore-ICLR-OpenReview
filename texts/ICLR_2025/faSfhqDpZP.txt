009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            This paper tackles the challenge of automatically constructing realistic surgical
012            simulation systems from readily available surgical videos. Recent efforts have
013            successfully integrated physically grounded dynamics within 3D Gaussians to
014            perform high-fidelity simulations in well-reconstructed static simulation environ-
015            ments. However, they struggle with the geometry inconsistency of simulation
016            environments and unrealistic physical deformations of soft tissues when it comes
017            to dynamic and complex surgical processes. In this paper, we propose SurgiSim, a
018            novel automatic simulation system to overcome these limitations. To build a sur-
019
               gical simulation environment, we maintain a canonical 3D scene composed of 3D
               Gaussians coupled with a deformation field to represent a dynamic surgical scene.
020
               This process involves a multi-stage optimization with trajectory and anisotropic
021
               regularization, enhancing the geometry consistency of the canonical scene which
022            serves the simulation environment. To improve the realism of physical simula-
023            tions, we implement a Visco-Elastic deformation model based on the Maxwell
024            model, effectively restoring the complex deformations of tissues. Additionally,
025            we infer the physical parameters of tissues by minimizing the discrepancies be-
026            tween the input video and simulation results guided by estimated tissue motion,
027            ensuring realistic simulation outcomes. Experiments on various surgical scenarios
028            and interactions demonstrate SurgiSim’s ability to perform realistic physics-based
029            simulation of soft tissues among surgical procedures, showing its enormous po-
030
               tential for enhancing surgical training, planning, and robotic surgery systems.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Realistic surgical simulation systems are pivotal in enhancing clinical training, offering substantial
036   benefits for training surgeons, and advancing the development and deployment of robotic surgery
037   systems (Huang et al., 2023; Long et al., 2023). Currently, mainstream surgical simulation systems,
038   such as LaparoS TM from VIRTAMED, are commercial products that primarily utilize mesh-based
039
      technology. While these systems provide highly realistic simulations, their performance and reli-
      ability heavily depend on labor-intensive mesh modeling and manual physical parameter settings.
040
      This dependence on the skills of modelers and animators limits the variety of possible simulation
041
      scenarios. In this paper, we aim to develop a high-quality and flexible simulation system capable
042   of automatically converting real surgical videos into surgical simulation environments as well as
043   performing high-fidelity and physically-accurate simulations.
044
045
      Building high-quality surgical simulation systems from readily available surgical videos poses sig-
      nificant challenges, including the reconstruction of simulation environments from monocular videos
046
      with complex rigid and non-rigid deformations and accurately modeling the soft tissues for re-
047
      alistic surgical simulation. Fortunately, recent advancements in scene reconstruction and phys-
048   ically grounded dynamics offer plausible solutions. Methods based on Neural radiance fields
049   (NeRF) (Wang et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2023b;a) and 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) (Wang et al.,
050   2024; Yang et al., 2024b; Liu et al., 2024b) have demonstrated impressive capabilities in recon-
051   structing surgical scenarios. Besides, PhysGaussian (Xie et al., 2024a) combines continuum me-
052   chanics with 3DGS and employs the Material Point Method (MPM) to simulate realistic motions
053   in reconstructed models under simple point load. However, despite these technological strides, two
      significant challenges still obstruct the realization of the target system.

069
070   Firstly, current methods can’t reconstruct geometrically consistent simulation environments. Re-
071   cent advances in dynamic tissue reconstruction have shown promising capabilities by modeling tis-
072   sues using canonical scenes with deformation fields (Xie et al., 2024b; Liu et al., 2024b; Wang et al.,
073   2022; Yang et al., 2023b;a). However, these methods typically treat dynamic tissue reconstruction
074   as a task of synthesizing high-fidelity images at specific timestamps. Under this definition, the de-
075   formation field is optimized to produce visually plausible results for each individual frame rather
076
      than maintaining consistent geometry across time. Consequently, the geometry mapping between
      the canonical scene and different timestamps becomes physically implausible. In surgical simula-
077
      tions, these geometric inconsistencies lead to significant artifacts such as fragmentation and messy
078
      deformations. Thus, it is crucial to develop a method capable of efficiently transforming surgical
079   videos into high-quality, geometric, consistent, and simulation-ready environments.
080
081   Secondly, current methods fail to simulate complex tissue deformations. Current representative
082
      simulation methods, such as PhysGaussian (Xie et al., 2024a), primarily employ a Plastic-Elastic
      model with uniform simulation parameters assigned manually across the entire subject. This design
083
      falls short in capturing the complicated deformation behaviors inherent in real surgical scenarios.
084
      Moreover, the manual assignment of physical parameters to the simulation subject compromises
085   the realism of the simulation. Recent research (Zhang et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024a) propose to
086   leverage diffusion priors, either in the form of generated videos or through score distillation sampling
087   (SDS, Poole et al. (2022)) to estimate physical parameters and avoid the need for manually assigned
088   parameters. While these diffusion-based methods are effective in simple scenarios such as handling
089   natural fluctuation, they fail in more complex surgical situations that involve external forces from
090   operations like pulling or cutting. Moreover, current medical video generation models (Li et al.,
091   2024a; Sun et al., 2024) are still in the early stages of development (Cho et al., 2024), and the
092   quality of their output is insufficient to provide accurate guidance for simulations.
093   To address the aforementioned challenges, we propose SurgiSim, an automated system for accurate,
094   high-quality surgical simulation leveraging readily available surgical videos. SurgiSim incorpo-
095   rates a novel reconstruction module that employs 3D Gaussians (Kerbl et al., 2023), an explicit 3D
096   representation capable of real-time rendering, to construct a canonical scene from surgical video
097   footage. This module utilizes multi-stage optimization with trajectory and geometric regularization
098   to enhance geometry consistency, complemented by a surface thickening method to enrich recon-
099
      structed tissue content for improved simulation fidelity. To accurately model tissue deformations
      across various surgical scenarios, we implement a Visco-Elastic deformation model based on the
100
      Maxwell model (Johnson & Quigley, 1992). We further employ a physics-guided parameter estima-
101
      tion method to acquire accurate physical parameters of tissues, leveraging observed physical effects
102   in the input video. Specifically, we utilize point tracking (Le Moing et al., 2024) and depth esti-
103   mation (Yang et al., 2024a) to derive the tissue motions induced by external forces, with physical
104   parameters optimized through differentiable simulation and rasterization. This method ensures high-
105   accuracy simulation with realistic physical effects, closely mimicking the complexities of real sur-
106   gical scenarios. Extensive experiments across diverse surgical procedures demonstrate SurgiSim’s
107   capability to realistically simulate soft tissue interactions, highlighting its potential for enhancing
      surgical training, planning, and robotic surgery systems.

119   2   R ELATED W ORK
120
121   Dynamic Scene Representations. The rapidly advancing field of Neural Radiance Filed (NeRF,
122   Mildenhall et al. (2021)) and 3DGS (Kerbl et al., 2023) has garnered significant interest for dynamic
123   scene reconstruction. Early works based on NeRF Bansal et al. (2020); Cao & Johnson (2023);
124   Fridovich-Keil et al. (2023); Li et al. (2022a) model scenes with implicit representations, which are
125   difficult to interact with and therefore unsuitable for simulation. Later works based on 3DGS (Wu
126
      et al., 2024; Luiten et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024c; Yang et al., 2023c) use explicit
      representation, but require multi-view images or videos with severe movement of cameras, which is
127
      hard to obtain in the field of surgery. Recently, some other research (Wang et al., 2022; Yang et al.,
128
      2023b;a; 2024b; Liu et al., 2024b; Xie et al., 2024b; Li et al., 2024b) has focused on the recon-
129   struction of dynamic surgical scenes. However, these studies either still use implicit representation,
130   or primarily aim to reconstruct the dynamic scenes at specific timestamps, instead of the geometric
131   continuity and consistency of the reconstructed canonical model.
132
133   Material Point Method. Material Point Method is a hybrid Lagrangian/Eulerian discretization
134   scheme for solid mechanics (Hu et al., 2018b). The MPM system’s inherent ability to handle topol-
135   ogy changes and frictional interactions makes it well-suited for simulating a wide range of mate-
136   rials (Xie et al., 2024a). including elastic objects, sand, cloth, hair, snow, lava, and viscoelastic
137   fluids De Vaucorbeil et al. (2020); Daviet & Bertails-Descoubes (2016); Fu et al. (2017); Han et al.
138   (2019); Hu et al. (2018a); Jiang et al. (2017); Wolper et al. (2019). In addition, modern implemen-
139
      tations of MPM utilize the parallel ability of GPUs to achieve advanced efficiency(Dong & Grabe,
      2018). Recently, Xie et al. (2024a) uses GPU-based MPM to efficiently incorporate dynamics into
140
      different scenarios using a unified particle representation within the Gaussian Splatting framework.
141
      However, their methods rely on manually assigned physical parameters, and assume the parameters
142   to be the same over large regions. Follow-up works by Zhang et al. (2024); Liu et al. (2024a) fur-
143   ther utilize the differentiable ability of the simulation process to estimate the parameters in MPM
144   guided by Diffusion Models (Blattmann et al., 2023). These methods extract the physical prior from
145   generative models, which is unreliable in the surgery field.
146
147   Surgical Simulation Systems. Medical simulation systems are based on the deformation models
148   used to handle tissue motion under the interaction of external forces (Meier et al., 2005). Early
149   systems apply mainly heuristic approaches like deformable spines, spring-mass models, and linked
150   volumes (Cover et al., 1993; Keeve et al., 1996; Gibson et al., 1997; De Casson & Laugier, 1999).
151   These systems are limited by the computing hardware, and only perform simple simulations. With
152   the development of computer graphics technology, later methods and products either base the sim-
153
      ulation on manually built mesh models and predefined animation or replay real videos captured for
      VR simulators (Li & Li, 2022; Lesch et al., 2020). They require expensive human labor or advanced
154
      hardware or VR video capture but provide limited interaction. Recently, Yang et al. (2024c) pro-
155
      posed SimEndoGS, a data-driven system based on 3DGS and MPM. However, they use an elastic
156   model to capture tissue motion and only support minor interactions like tiny force impulses.

346   Tissue Motion Estimation. To obtain accurate physical parameters, we involve extracting the
347   physical priors of tissues from surgical videos. This process comprises two main steps: 1) recovering
348   the tissue motion induced by external forces from instruments as captured in the input video, and
349   2) refining physical parameters by minimizing the discrepancies between the simulated dynamics
350   guided by the tissue motion and input video.
351   One straightforward way to recover motion in MPM simulation is to estimate the forces involved.

410   4     E XPERIMENTS
411
412   4.1   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS .
413
414   Dataset. We evaluate our method using the EndoNeRF dataset (Wang et al., 2022), which comprises
415   several surgical video clips totaling 807 frames. Each clip, captured by stereo cameras from a single
416   viewpoint, spans 4-8 seconds and shows typical soft tissue scenarios encountered in robotic surgery,
417
      including complex non-rigid deformations. We utilize 5 of these clips to establish our simulation
      environments, excluding one clip that involves significant tissue cutting. Based on these 5 clips, we
418
      set up a simulation environment for each and perform a total of 10 typical operations.
419
420   Environment and Simulation Setup. Our environment setup employs a multi-stage optimization
421   process consisting of 5000 iterations. The initial stage, comprising the first 50% of iterations, fo-
422   cuses on optimizing the canonical model G 0 and the deformation field D. This is followed by a
423
      trajectory regularization stage from 50% to 70% of the total iterations. A refinement period then oc-
      curs from 70% to 90%, allowing for further optimization of the deformation field after its correction
424
      by trajectory optimization. In the final stage, occupying the last 10% of iterations, we freeze the de-
425
      formation field and fine-tune the canonical model. For the simulation, we adapt the MPM framework
426   from Xie et al. (2024a); Zong et al. (2023). The thickened model Gd0 is normalized into a 2-unit cube,
427   overlaid with an Eulerian grid of resolution 50 × 50 × 50. To synchronize our physical simulation
428   with real-world time, we established a conversion rate where 10,000 simulation steps correspond
429   to one second of video at 25 fps. For each operation in the simulation, we conduct 80k simulation
430   steps to form an 8-second video. All experiments were conducted on a machine equipped with a
431   Core i7-13700K CPU and a single NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPU, running Ubuntu 24.04. The code will
      be made public to promote the virtual surgery.

      5   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
527
528
      In this paper, we present SurgiSim, an automated and flexible method for transforming monocular
529
      surgical videos into simulation-ready scenes, enabling accurate physics surgical simulations within
530   these environments. SurgiSim employs multi-stage optimization with trajectory and anisotropic
531   regularization to construct a geometrically consistent simulation environment. By incorporating a
532   Visco-Elastic deformation model and precise physical parameters extracted from real videos, Sur-
533   giSim shows highly realistic and accurate tissue deformations during simulation. We hope that
534   SurgiSim could contribute to the development of more diverse and realistic surgical simulations.
535
536   Limitations. In the simulation environment setup, our method could not handle the invisible part
537   on the side of the tissues, which caused flaws in the texture formed by our thickening method. As
538   for simulation, firstly, the physical prior in the videos is sometimes not enough for only part of the
539   tissues are actually moving and contribute to the parameter estimation. Secondly, our method does
      not yet support more complicated operations like cutting and topological inversions of structure.
