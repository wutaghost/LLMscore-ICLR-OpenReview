011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013
               Over the past decade, breakthroughs in both general-purpose and specialized hard-
               ware have propelled the success of large-scale machine learning. However, the
014
               advancements in general-purpose hardware are not keeping pace with those in
015
               specialized hardware. Consequently, operations conducted on the general-purpose
016            hardware have become the primary performance bottleneck. Notably, data loading
017            significantly lags behind the gradient computation during training. To address this
018            issue, the technique of data echoing has been introduced in practice, whereby the
019            current batch of samples is reused for gradient computation to minimize idle time
020            while waiting for new data. However, this approach can lead to overfitting on the
021            current batch, and it remains unclear whether convergence benefits from this prac-
022            tice. In this paper, we provide a sharper analysis on a stochastic formulation of data
023            echoing and show that it obtains linear speedup proportional to the number of reuse
024
               times. Additionally, we investigate the impact of the communication bottleneck
               in data parallelism to data echoing, and propose a new communication-efficient
025
               data echoing algorithm via reducing the frequency of model averaging. We then
026
               show that it is possible to perform data echoing without additional communication
027            cost with data parallelism. Finally, we perform empirical experiments to verify
028            our analysis on the data echoing and the proposed efficient algorithm for data
029            parallelism.

      Besides, we also investigate the data echoing algorithm in the data parallelism setting. To speed up
079
      the training of large-scale models, it is common to divide the examples across different nodes. In each
080   training step, nodes first compute gradients in parallel and then average these gradients collectively.
081   Note that the communication volume of the average step is at the order of O(dK) (d is the number of
082   model parameters and K is the number of nodes), which is comparable to that of the data loading.
083   As a result, the benefit of doing data echo could disappear in the data parallelism setting, where the
084   amount of communication among GPU/TPU nodes increase as more gradient steps are performed.
085   To tackle this challenge, we propose a new communication-efficient data echoing algorithm in the
086   data parallelism setting. More specifically, for each training step t, nodes only average gradients with
087   a predefined probability p(c) > 0. By carefully selecting the value of p(c) , we show that it is possible
088   to eliminate the communication overhead when we apply data echoing with data parallelism.

      The term of data echoing is proposed in Choi et al. (2019), where authors consider the full training
111
      pipeline of a machine learning algorithm, including reading from the disk, shuffling the data, data
112
      augmentation, batching etc. Then in data echoing, each stage simply reuses the current available data
113   instead of waiting for operations from the last stage to finish. In our work, we focus on the echoing of
114   data batches similar to Agarwal et al. (2020). Agarwal et al. (2020) is a pioneered work in analyzing
115   the convergence of data echoing. Its analysis consists of two parts: the first involves establishing
116   bounds on the progress made with respect to an objective defined over a mini-batch, while the second
117   part connects the decrease on a batch to the full dataset objective. The first part can be performed
118   by standard regret analysis in optimization, while the second part utilizes the concept of uniform
119   stability. Note that Agarwal et al. (2020) considers the set of convex optimization problems with
120   bounded gradient. We study the general non-convex setting and do not require the bounded gradient
121
      assumption, instead we require a less restrictive bounded bias assumption (Even, 2023). Our analysis
      to data echoing is from the view of Markov chain gradient descent, while our communication-efficient
122
      data echoing algorithm is inspired by the Local Gradient Descent (i.e. FedAvg) (McMahan et al.,
123
      2017). We introduce some recent developments in these two directions below:
124
125   Markov-Chain Gradient Descent. Algorithms similar to stochastic gradient descent (Robbins &
126   Monro, 1951) have achieved significant success in large-scale machine learning. Typically, these
127
      algorithms rely on access to i.i.d. examples to estimate the gradient. However, in some applications
      such as decentralized optimization (Johansson et al., 2007; 2010) and reinforcement learning (Sun
128
      et al., 2018), i.i.d. samples may not be readily available or may be costly to obtain. A well-studied
129
      alternative (Johansson et al., 2007; 2010; Sun et al., 2018; Doan et al., 2020; Even, 2023) involves
130   sampling examples from a Markov chain. The example sequence in data echoing (Choi et al., 2019)
131   can also be viewed as a Markov chain. The convergence of Markov chain gradient descent is analyzed
132   under various assumptions to the properties of the involved chains. Under the ergodic assumption,
133   Johansson et al. (2007) shows that O(ПөвҲ’2 ) iterations are needed to achieve an Пө error. Sun et al. (2018)
134   shows for both reversible and non-reversible chains that O(ПөвҲ’(1/(1вҲ’q)) ), q вҲҲ (0.5, 1) iterations are
135   needed for non-convex problems. Even (2023) improves over the analysis in Sun et al. (2018) by
136   removing the bounded gradient assumption. Besides the convergence analysis, Wang et al. (2022)
137   studied the generalization property of Markov chain gradient descent using the tool of algorithmic
138   stability (Hardt et al., 2015); Adibi et al. (2024) studied the effect of delayed update to Markov chain
139   gradient descent.
140   Local Gradient Descent. The idea of reducing communication cost by performing local gradient
141   descent is used in Federated Learning (FL) (McMahan et al., 2017), which is a novel paradigm for
142   performing machine learning tasks over distributed, privacy sensitive data. In FL, communication
143   is the major performance bottleneck (Wen et al., 2017) and Local Gradient Descent is proposed to
144   reduce the frequency of client-server communication. Our communication-efficient data echoing for
145   data parallelism is inspired by the similar idea. Besides, various compression techniques (Wen et al.,
146
      2017; Lin et al., 2017; Stich et al., 2018; Karimireddy et al., 2019) can also be applied to reduce
      the communication cost and can be combined with data echoing (we leave this as a future work to
147
      explore).

      In this work, we explore the properties of the data echoing technique. First, we provide a tighter
532
      analysis of data echoing, demonstrating that its stochastic formulation achieves linear speedup with
533
      respect to data loading probability. Next, we examine the application of data echoing in the context of
534   data parallelism and propose a novel algorithm that reduces the frequency of model averaging, thereby
535   improving communication efficiency. Theoretically, we show that our communication-efficient data
536   echoing algorithm lowers the communication cost in data parallelism while preserving the benefit of
537   reduced data loading operations. Finally, we empirically validate the effectiveness of data echoing
538   and introduce a cosine diminishing schedule for data loading probability. We also conduct extensive
539   numerical experiments to confirm the efficacy of our communication-efficient algorithm in the data
      parallelism setting.

       In Figure 1, we measure the FLOPS rate vs Arithmetic Intensity under different data loading speed
1158   levels. In particular, the term "arithmetic intensity" comes from the Roofline model (Williams et al.,
1159   2009) of the computer systems community. Formally, Arithmetic Intensity is the ratio of total floating-
1160   point operations (FLOPS) to the total data movement (Bytes) required to support those FLOPS. The
1161   main observation from the figure is: For a given data loading speeding, a certain arithmetic intensity
1162   is necessary to reach the maximum FLOPS rate.
1163
       We perform synthetic experiments to get Figure 1. More specifically, we train a ResNet-18 model to
1164   fit the CIFAR-10 dataset on an A5000 GPU. Firstly, we vary the arithmetic intensity by changing
1165   the number of gradient steps (denoted as k) performed for each sample. In other words, we perform
1166   k consecutive gradient steps for each sample loaded to the memory. Note that by the definition of
1167   arithmetic intensity, it increases as the value of k increases. As for the data loading speed, we repeat
1168   the data loading operation i times per sample to achieve different levels of loading speed. Increase
1169   the value of i leads to slower data loading
