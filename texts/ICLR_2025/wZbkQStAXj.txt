009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Role-playing in large language models (LLMs) has become a crucial area of re-
012            search, enabling models to simulate diverse personas and tailor responses, signif-
013            icantly impacting natural language understanding and human-computer interac-
014            tion. However, while advanced LLMs like GPT-4 are used to evaluate role-playing
015            methods, their reliability in providing accurate assessments remains uncertain, es-
016            pecially in distinguishing nuanced role-playing characteristics. In this paper, we
017            introduce PersonaEval, a benchmark designed to assess the effectiveness of LLMs
018
               in role-playing evaluation tasks. We frame the problem as a classification task to
               determine whether an LLM evaluator can distinguish between sentences from dif-
019
               ferent levels of expertise based solely on linguistic cues. Using real-world data
020
               from the Wired 5 Levels video series—where experts explain concepts to five dis-
021            tinct audiences: a child, a teenager, a college student, a graduate student, and
022            another expert—we design three evaluation settings that correspond to commonly
023            used LLM evaluation approaches: single answer role grading, pairwise role com-
024            parison, and reference-guided role grading. These settings aim to capture various
025            aspects of how effectively LLMs evaluate role-playing performance. Our study
026            highlights the limitations of current LLMs in persona evaluation tasks and under-
027            scores the need for further research to enhance their evaluation capabilities. We
028            provide a foundation for future work aimed at improving the accuracy and profes-
029
               sionalism of LLM evaluators in role-playing contexts.

      1   I NTRODCUTION
033
      Role-playing has rapidly developed into a crucial area in the research and application of large lan-
034
      guage models (LLMs) (Shao et al., 2023; Tao et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024). The
035
      ability of LLMs to simulate diverse personas and adapt their responses accordingly holds signif-
036   icant implications for natural language understanding, human-computer interaction, and various
037   downstream tasks (Tseng et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024a;b). Most current research on role-playing
038   employs state-of-the-art LLMs, such as GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023), to evaluate different role-playing
039   methods and models (Tang et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024a). These advanced LLMs are often pre-
040   sumed capable of accurately assessing whether a role player’s output aligns with the target role.
041
      However, the reliability of LLM evaluators in providing correct assessments remains uncertain.
042   Some studies have begun to investigate this issue, such as testing the evaluator’s instruction-
043   following abilities (Murugadoss et al., 2024; Wei et al., 2024) or task feasibility (Zhang et al.,
044   2024). Yet, research specifically focused on evaluating LLMs’ effectiveness in role-playing eval-
045   uation is still limited. Can LLMs accurately distinguish user levels or make nuanced judgments
046   between different roles? Moreover, can their evaluations, even with reference guidance, be trusted
047   to reflect true role consistency and authenticity? Despite their widespread use for such tasks, more
048   research is needed to explore their accuracy and professionalism as role-playing evaluators.
049   To address this gap, we propose a benchmark called PersonaEval to evaluate LLMs effectiveness
050   in role-playing evaluation tasks, as shown in Figure 1. Framed as a classification problem, our
051   benchmark assesses whether an LLM evaluator can reliably distinguish between sentences originat-
052   ing from different levels of expertise. The goal is to determine if the LLM can accurately classify
053   these sentences based solely on linguistic cues, providing a deterministic approach to evaluate its
      capability in identifying nuanced role-playing characteristics.

073   To construct our benchmark, we leveraged real-world data from the popular Wired 5 Levels video
074   series1 . In this series, experts explain complex concepts to five distinct audiences: a child, a teenager,
075
      a college student, a graduate student, and another fellow expert. The series is renowned for its ability
      to adapt explanations to varying levels of prior knowledge, making it an ideal source for evaluating
076
      the capability to adjust language complexity.
077
078   Our PersonaEval encompasses 26 diverse topics and includes 130 unique speakers, resulting in dia-
079   logues that average over 17 turns of interaction. By focusing on these specific roles, we aim to assess
080   the nuanced understanding and discriminatory abilities of LLMs in tailoring language use across dif-
081
      ferent age groups and educational backgrounds. Furthermore, the dialogues in our benchmark are
      structured and goal-oriented rather than random exchanges. Each conversation is centered on ex-
082
      plaining a concept at varying levels of complexity, meticulously tailored to match the audience’s
083
      understanding. This setup presents a meaningful and structured challenge for LLMs, testing their
084   ability to modulate explanations appropriately. Since the roles in the “5 Levels” series represent
085   common personas, the LLMs evaluate familiar character archetypes. This familiarity reduces the
086   risk of compromised performance due to unfamiliarity with the roles, allowing for a more accurate
087   assessment of the models’ capabilities in language adaptation and audience-specific communication.
088
      To comprehensively evaluate the performance of LLMs in role-playing tasks using our PersonaEval
089
      benchmark, we designed three distinct evaluation settings: single answer role grading, pairwise role
090   comparison, and reference-guided role grading. These settings correspond to established evaluation
091   methodologies for LLMs (Zheng et al., 2023): single-answer grading, pairwise comparison, and
092   reference-guided evaluation, respectively. In the single answer role grading setting, we assess the
093   model’s ability to recognize and categorize user inputs across different proficiency levels, testing its
094   nuanced understanding of varied user interactions. The pairwise role comparison setting examines
095   how effectively the model can distinguish between different roles, highlighting its adaptability to
096   diverse contextual personas. In the reference-guided evaluation, we introduce reference examples to
097   guide the LLM’s judgments, evaluating its capacity to leverage prior examples to enhance evaluation
098
      accuracy. By employing these varied evaluation settings, we aim to capture multiple facets of how
      LLMs can effectively assess role-playing performance.
099
100   Our study reveals significant limitations in current LLMs when tasked with evaluating role-playing
101   performances, underscoring the need for continued research to enhance their evaluative capabili-
102   ties. The proposed PersonaEval benchmark serves as a foundational tool for future efforts aimed
103   at improving the professionalism and precision of LLM evaluators in role-playing contexts. By ad-
104
      dressing these limitations, we contribute to the advancement of models that are proficient not only
      in generating role-specific content but also in evaluating such content with a high degree of accuracy
105
      and reliability.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
109
110
      Role-playing Evaluation The use of LLMs as evaluators in role-playing tasks has gained trac-
111   tion due to their efficient and scalable capabilities in assessing performance, providing feedback,
112   and aiding in decision-making processes (Gusev, 2024; Wang et al., 2024b). Three primary eval-
113   uation approaches have emerged in this context: single-answer grading, pairwise comparison, and
114   reference-guided grading (Zheng et al., 2023).
115
      For the single answer grading, an LLM evaluates a single response and directly assigns a score based
116
      on predefined metrics such as coherence, role alignment, or content accuracy. MMRole (Dai et al.,
117   2024) introduces a reward model to better align LLM-generated evaluations with those of human
118   judges. Pairwise comparison is commonly used to judge subtle differences between responses from
119   two baseline models and identify which one better meets the intended criteria. RoleLLM (Wang
120   et al., 2024a) uses GPT-4 to compare and rank generated samples, determining metrics such as win
121   rates and average rankings for baseline models to evaluate instruction following and role general-
122   ization. Reference-guided grading provides a reference solution to the LLM evaluator. Neeko (Yu
123   et al., 2024) leverages GPT-3.5 as a judge, prompting step-by-step scoring of dialogue performance
124   based on predefined metrics.
125   These approaches aim to assess how effectively LLMs play specific roles, contributing to the overall
126   understanding of their performance in role-playing scenarios. However, is the evaluating results
127   reliable and stable, challenges remain in ensuring that the evaluations are reliable and accurately
128   reflect the nuances of role adherence. Current approaches to using LLMs in role-playing evaluations
129   are not without flaws.
130
131   Evaluating LLM Evaluator The reliability of LLMs as evaluators is gaining increasing attention
132   in the community (Son et al., 2024; Wei et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024). Several studies test the
133   instruction-following abilities of LLMs in their role as evaluators. Judging LLM-as-a-Judge (Zheng
134
      et al., 2023) addresses this issue by using advanced LLMs to assess performance on open-ended
      questions. To verify alignment between LLM judgments and human preferences, they introduce
135
      two benchmarks: MT-Bench, a multi-turn question set, and Chatbot Arena, a crowdsourced bat-
136
      tle platform. Wang et al. (2023) approach ChatGPT as a human-like evaluator by providing task-
137   specific (e.g., summarization) and aspect-specific (e.g., relevance) instructions to prompt ChatGPT
138   for evaluating the outputs of various NLG (Natural Language Generation) models. They conduct
139   experiments on five NLG meta-evaluation datasets, covering tasks like summarization, story gen-
140   eration, and data-to-text conversion. Evaluating the Evaluator (Murugadoss et al., 2024) explores
141   whether LLM assessments are based purely on prompt instructions or also reflect inherent prefer-
142   ences for high-quality data similar to their fine-tuning data. Their dataset spans tasks such as text
143   summarization, conversation quality, task solution quality, and generated story quality.
144   However, despite these explorations, the evaluation of LLMs specifically as role-playing task evalu-
145   ators remains underexplored. Current work often lacks the focus on assessing the nuanced abilities
146   of LLMs to differentiate role levels and align with role-specific contexts, highlighting a gap in un-
147   derstanding LLMs’ capacity for accurate role-play assessment.

152
      to act as role-playing evaluators. Our objective is to assess how effectively LLMs can classify and
153   distinguish specific roles based on language usage associated with different roles. This classifi-
154   cation capability is crucial for determining whether role-playing evaluators can discern contextual
155   differences in interactions across a diverse range of audiences.

      3.2     E VALUATION S ETTING
191
192   To systematically assess the effectiveness of LLM evaluators in role-playing scenarios, we have
193   designed three benchmark settings, as shown in Figure 2. Each setting corresponds to a common
194   use case of LLM evaluators, as identified by Zheng et al. (2023): (1) Single Answer Grading: LLM
195   judger assigns a score to a single response; (2) Pairwise Comparison: LLM judger is presented with
196   a question and two possible answers, determining which is better or if they are equally suitable; (3)
197   Reference-Guided Grading: LLM evaluates a response against a provided reference solution when
198
      applicable.
199
200   3.2.1      S INGLE A NSWER ROLE G RADING
201
      The first evaluation setting involves a five-level classification task. The LLM is presented with sev-
202   eral turns of dialogue and must determine to which level the responses correspond: child, teenager,
203   college student, graduate student, or expert. This task aligns with the single-answer grading ap-
204   proach, requiring the LLM to assign appropriate classifications based on the input, akin to accurately
205   scoring a response.
206
      We employ the PersonaEval benchmark, a dataset specifically designed to test the LLM’s ability
207
      to discern varying levels of expertise in user input. By evaluating whether LLM evaluators can
208   accurately identify the user’s knowledge level from the context, we can assess the model’s under-
209   standing of nuanced differences in language and content. The goal is to ensure that the LLM can
210   tailor explanations appropriately for different audiences, enhancing the effectiveness of personalized
211   communication.
212

214
215   The second setting focuses on pairwise role comparison, corresponding to the binary choice evalu-
      ation approach. In this scenario, the LLM is given two responses generated from different roles and

302   The five-level classification experiments are conducted from two perspectives: the explainer side
303
      and the audience side. For each perspective, we use two input settings: five turns of dialogue and all
      available dialogue turns from that side. This approach enables us to evaluate how well LLMs, acting
304
      as evaluators, can classify role levels based on linguistic cues from either the explainer’s language
305
      or the language tailored to different audiences. By comparing performance across these settings, we
306   aim to understand how the amount of dialogue context and the perspective taken affect the model’s
307   ability to accurately identify the correct role level in this multi-class classification task, shedding
308   light on the LLMs’ role-playing evaluation capabilities.
309
      Tables 2 and 3 compare classification performance based on explainer-side and audience-side dia-
310
      logues. For explainer-side dialogues (Table 2), models like GPT-4o show improvement when given
311
      full context, with macro-averaged precision rising from 48.9% to 58.2% and recall from 47.1% to
312   54.6%. However, while gpt-3.5-turbo excels in precision for “Child”, its recall remains low across
313   levels, and the Qwen models maintain moderate precision but struggle with recall, especially for
314   the “Expert” role. DeepSeek-v2.5 performs well for “Child” but similarly falls short on recall for
315   nuanced roles, indicating challenges in distinguishing subtleties using explanation-focused cues.
316
      In contrast, audience-side language (Table 3) provides clearer distinctions, leading to better model
317
      performance overall. Full context enhances the precision and recall for all models, with GPT-4o
318   reaching a macro average of 69% in both metrics, demonstrating adaptability to role-specific au-
319   dience cues. Compared to explanation-side language, audience-side prompts yield higher precision
320   and recall across models, as seen in the moderate but consistent gains for gpt-3.5-turbo and the Qwen
321   models. DeepSeek also benefits from audience context, though its recall varies across roles. These
322   results indicate that the language style directed at different audience levels is more informative for
323   role classification, suggesting the value of considering the listener’s perspective to improve LLM
      role-playing evaluations.

      5     U SER S TUDY
487
488
      To evaluate human performance on the tasks defined by our PersonaEval benchmark and to gain
489   insights into the human experience with these tasks, we conducted a comprehensive user study. This
490   study aimed to assess how well humans can classify dialogues according to target levels and to
491   compare their performance with that of large language models like GPT-4o.
492
493   5.1   S ETTINGS
494
495   We recruited 15 volunteers, including 10 undergraduates and 5 PhD students from various academic
496   disciplines. The study consisted of two main tasks: (1) Single Answer Role Grading: Participants
497   classified 60 dialogues from our PersonaEval benchmark (30 from the explainer perspective and 30
498   from the audience perspective) into one of five levels based on complexity and target alignment. (2)
499   Pairwise Role Comparison: The 5 PhD students also compared pairs of dialogues, selecting the one
500
      that best matched a target level. Each participant answered 40 questions (20 for each perspective).
      No time limits were set, allowing participants to complete them at their own pace. This approach
501
      was intended to minimize pressure and encourage careful consideration in their responses.
502
503
504
      5.2   A NALYSIS
505
      In the Single Answer Role Grading Task, participants achieved an average accuracy of 48% on the
506   explainer perspective, closely aligning with GPT-4o’s accuracy of 47%. On the audience perspec-
507   tive, participants attained a higher average accuracy of 54%, whereas GPT-4o achieved 67%. The
508   higher accuracy on the audience side suggests that audience dialogues were slightly easier to clas-
509   sify, possibly due to more relatable language or clearer indicators of comprehension. A comparison
510   between educational backgrounds revealed that PhD students outperformed undergraduates in both
511   roles, suggesting that advanced education may improve the ability to recognize subtle differences in
512   dialogue complexity and appropriateness. Importantly, participant accuracy significantly exceeded
513   the chance level of 20%, confirming that humans can identify patterns within the dialogues that
514
      enable them to distinguish between levels effectively. This finding underscores the validity of the
      benchmark and its alignment with human cognitive abilities.
515
516   In the Pairwise Role Comparison Task, PhD participants achieved an average accuracy of 87% on
517   the explainer role and 86% on the audience role, closely matching GPT-4o’s accuracy of 90% on
518   both roles. The high accuracy rates in this task indicate that when directly comparing dialogues, both
519   humans and the language model can effectively assess alignment with target levels. Despite strong
520
      performance, evaluating long dialogue pairs may have posed cognitive challenges for participants,
      as processing and comparing large amounts of information could lead to occasional inaccuracies.
521
      However, human evaluators generally maintained consistency in their judgments across different
522
      contexts. In contrast, language models like GPT-4o may exhibit inconsistencies due to the nature
523   of prompt-based evaluations. Since the model assesses the same dialogues with varying prompts
524   for different target levels, it may assign different levels in separate evaluations of the same dialogue
525   pair. This discrepancy arises because the model does not retain context between evaluations, unlike
526   human participants who can integrate information holistically.

      6     C ONCLUSION
530
531
      We explore the role-playing capabilities of large language models (LLMs) by introducing a bench-
      mark that assesses their ability to evaluate dialogues across varying levels of complexity. Using
532
      real-world data from the Wired 5 Levels video series, we introduce PersonaEval with three eval-
533
      uation settings, single answer role grading, pairwise role comparison, and reference-guided role
534   grading, to comprehensively analyze LLMs’ effectiveness in role adherence and comparison tasks.
535   Our results show that while advanced models like GPT-4o demonstrate strong performance, they
536   still face challenges in accurately distinguishing between closely related role levels, highlighting
537   limitations in their evaluation capabilities. Few-shot learning enhances performance, particularly
538   for more advanced models, but its impact varies across LLMs, indicating room for improvement.
539   Our PersonaEval underscores the need for continued research to refine LLM evaluators, with the
      goal of developing models that can more reliably and accurately assess role-playing consistency.

952
953          • Explainer Side: Participants observed that when addressing lower levels like Child and

957            in-depth explanations that presumed foundational knowledge of the subject.
958          • Audience Side: For lower levels like Child and Teen, audience responses were typically
959            brief and focused on seeking clarification. As levels progressed to Grad Student and Expert,
960            responses became more detailed, often engaging critically with the content. Higher-level
961            audiences not only demonstrated a deeper understanding but also frequently discussed top-
