009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Game-playing agents like AlphaGo have achieved superhuman performance
012            through self-play, which is theoretically guaranteed to yield optimal policies in
013            competitive games. However, most language tasks are partially or fully coopera-
014            tive, so it is an open question whether techniques like self-play can effectively be
015            used to improve language models. We empirically investigate this question in a
016
               negotiation game setting known as Deal or No Deal (DoND). Crucially, the ob-
               jective in DoND can be modified to produce a fully cooperative game, a strictly
017
               competitive one, or anything in between. We finetune language models in self-
018
               play over multiple rounds of filtered behavior cloning in DoND for each of these
019            objectives and evaluate them in self-play and in collaboration with humans. We
020            find that language models improve substantially in self-play, achieving 14-17×
021            higher scores in task reward after finetuning. Further, the trained models general-
022            ize to both cooperation and competition with humans, scoring 2.5-6× higher than
023            base models. We view these results as an early promising sign for language model
024            self-play in cooperative settings, despite a lack of theoretical guarantees.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Many of the greatest achievements in artificial intelligence have occurred in two-player zero-sum
029
      (2p0s) games such as Go (Silver et al., 2016), chess (Silver et al., 2018), and heads-up poker (Brown
030   & Sandholm, 2018). One key technique enabling these breakthroughs has been self-play, in which
031   identical copies of a model are pitted against each other and used to generate new training data. By
032   iteratively training on their own data from games of self-play, models like AlphaGo were able to
033   continue improving long past the threshold of human performance. In certain types of 2p0s games,
034   self-play is theoretically guaranteed to produce optimal policies, given sufficient model capacity and
035   compute (Bai & Jin, 2020; Bai et al., 2020). However, in settings that involve collaboration with
036   humans, self-play is no longer guaranteed to yield optimal policies (Strouse et al., 2021).
037   It is an open question whether the same principles that led to the success of models like AlphaGo can
038   be applied to language models. Empirically, previous work on training agents to communicate via
039   self-play has shown that they invent uninterpretable communication strategies (Kottur et al., 2017);
040   even when initialized with natural language data, self-play can cause models to gradually diverge
041   from human-interpretable language (Lewis et al., 2017). As a result, much work has focused on
042   mitigating these challenges, e.g., by regularizing with models trained on human data (FAIR, 2022).
043   In this work, we examine the effect of game objectives on self-play between language models.
044   We run a series of experiments on a negotiation task known as Deal or No Deal (Lewis et al.,
045   2017) and train language models for multiple rounds of self-play across three different objectives
046   on this task, ranging from fully cooperative, to semi-competitive, to strictly competitive. Contrary
047   to expectations, we find that self-play leads to large improvements in both the cooperative and semi-
048   competitive settings. These results generalize to human experiments, where scores improve by up to
049   2.5× in the cooperative setting and 6× in the semi-competitive setting. In contrast, we find minimal
050
      improvements in the strictly competitive setting, where models tend to overfit during self-play.
051   We then investigate the reasons behind these improvements, finding that models trained with self-
052   play better follow task instructions, hallucinate less, and obtain a higher agreement rate with humans.
053   However, at the same time, self-play causes model dialogues to become less diverse and does not
      appear to teach high-level strategic reasoning or negotiation tactics in our experiments. Although

      2   C OOPERATIVE AND C OMPETITIVE G AMES
080
081
      Can language model self-play be effective under both cooperative and competitive objectives? To
082
      address this question, we conducted experiments on Deal or No Deal (DoND; Lewis et al., 2017), a
083   two-player negotiation game in which players decide how to divide a shared pool of items through
084   natural language dialogue. Although introduced as a semi-competitive game, DoND has the special
085   property that it can be readily adapted into either a cooperative or strictly competitive (i.e., zero-
086   sum) game, with minimal modifications to its rules. Below, we describe the rules of DoND, how we
087   modify its objective, and how we convert it into an environment for evaluating language models.
088
089   Game Setup Following Lewis et al. (2017), we present two players with a shared collection of
090   books, hats, and balls (with 5-7 total objects). Each player is assigned their own private value
091   function, mapping each item type to an integer point value. Value functions are selected according
092   to the following criteria: (1) each item is valued by at least one player, (2) the maximum score
093   either player can receive is 10, and (3) at most one player can achieve the maximum score. Players
094
      must divide the objects; if they fail to reach an agreement, they both receive zero points. These
      rules ensure that the game is semi-competitive: players have conflicting objectives, but if they fail to
095
      cooperate at all then they will end up without any points.
096
097
      Game Rules The game is divided into two phases. In the first phase, players send messages dis-
098
      cussing which items they would like to receive. At any point, either player may end this phase by
099   submitting a private proposal, delineating which items they would like to claim from the shared
100   collection. During the second phase, no additional messages can be sent, and the other player must
101   respond by submitting a proposal of their own, which ends the game. If players submit complemen-
102   tary proposals (i.e., adding up to the total number of objects in the shared collection), then players
103   receive rewards according to their respective objectives. Hence, players should aim both to reach an
104   agreement and to optimize the value of that agreement.
105
106   Game Objectives In the original formulation, players receive a reward equal to the inner product
107   of their value function and proposed set of objects. However, we observe that this objective can
      be modified to convert DoND into a cooperative game, a strictly competitive one, or anything in

197
        To evaluate whether model improvements generalize beyond self-play, we built a web interface
198

      5.2   S ELF -P LAY F INETUNED M ODELS
233
234   Despite weak performance of the initial models, language model self-play was highly effective, as
235   shown in Figure 2. When evaluated against another copy of the same model, self-play finetuning
236   increased scores by as much as 14× in the semi-competitive setting (0.4 → 5.8) and 17× in the
237   cooperative setting (0.7 → 12.1). Although these experiments were conducted on GPT-3.5, these
238   scores are significantly higher than those of a baseline GPT-4 model, as reported in Section 5.1.
239   Improvements from self-play generalized to collaboration and competition with humans as well,
240   with scores increasing by 6× in the semi-competitive setting (0.8 → 4.9) and 2.5× in the cooperative
241   setting (4.6 → 11.6). In the cooperative setting, we note that human-LM scores peaked at 11.6, but
242   began to decline before the 10th iteration of self-play. We do not report scores for any model after
243   the 10th iteration, as they tended to stabilize or even decline.
244
245   The Case of Strict Competition Between fully rational agents, communication is not useful in a
246   2p0s game (Crawford & Sobel, 1982). Additionally, for the strictly competitive setting, due to the
247   zero-sum nature of the game, it is not informative to report mean scores in self-play, as the average
248   score in this setting will always be zero. We instead evaluated the quality of trained models based
249   on how well they performed against a separate model, GPT-4. Additionally, due to a sparsity of
250   positive-scoring games, we modified the filtering criteria for strictly competitive self-play to also
251
      include samples from zero-scoring games in which a valid agreement was reached. While models
      improved at reaching valid agreements in self-play, we found they generalized poorly against other
252
      agents. Our preliminary results indicated that even the best-performing models for this objective
253
      would routinely fail to reach agreements with humans, so we instead ran 100 games between each
254   iteration’s model and GPT-4, confirming that the model failed to improve outside of self-play. We
255   report results for this objective, along with further implications, extensively in Appendix D.

      6.1   E RRORS , AGREEMENTS , AND PARETO O PTIMALITY
305
306   In order to understand the effectiveness of language model self-play, we analyzed the frequency of
307   errors and successful agreements over the course of training. We also analyzed the rate of Pareto-
308   optimal outcomes, i.e., those where neither player’s score can improve without reducing the other’s.
309
      Prior to self-play finetuning, GPT-3.5 frequently made errors such as submitting invalid proposals
310
      or sending messages and proposals at the same time. When this happened, the game environment
311
      would provide an error message, as detailed in Appendix A, and the model would be given another
312   chance to generate a message. With the baseline model, errors were generated in 14% of semi-
313   competitive games and 56% of cooperative games. If the model received five error messages in
314   a row, the game aborted, and both players received a score of zero; this outcome occurred in just
315   1% of semi-competitive games but in 22% of cooperative games. After self-play finetuning, errors
316   occurred in less than 1% of games, suggesting that models effectively learned the game rules. We
317   present additional results on error and abort frequencies in Appendix E.
318   Models also improved in their ability to achieve valid agreements and Pareto-optimal game scores,
319   both in self-play and in human experiments, as shown in Table 2. Trained models achieved almost
320   perfect agreement rates in self-play, with 96.4% of games ending in an agreement in the semi-
321   competitive setting. While the agreement rates are lower in collaboration with humans, this can
322   partially (but not wholly) be attributed to human error. For example, we observed that humans
323   sometimes failed to read overly long messages in full, resulting in failed proposals; however, such
      errors may also be viewed as the fault of models failing to communicate efficiently.

386   6.3   D IALOGUE L ENGTH AND D IVERSITY
387
388   One natural question is whether self-play finetuning has any adverse effects on language quality.
389
      We qualitatively observed that dialogues in the semi-competitive setting became less diverse over
      the course of self-play finetuning. We quantified this in two ways: (1) by average dialogue length
390
      and (2) by the number of unique words produced during each iteration, as reported in Figure 3.
391
392   We first computed the average dialogue length over 500 hundred games of self-play during each
393   iteration of model training in the semi-competitive and cooperative settings. We found that self-play
394   caused dialogues to become substantially longer in the cooperative setting but shorter in the semi-
395
      competitive one. We hypothesize that this discrepancy may occur because agents in the cooperative
      setting are incentivized to share all information; qualitatively, we often observed models sharing
396
      exact details of their private value functions. Additionally, we found that models which argue with
397
      each other for too long are more likely to “go off the rails” and fail to reach an agreement at all;
398   because these games receive scores of zero, this behavior may be filtered out over the course of
399   self-play under the semi-competitive objective.
400
401
      We also computed the vocabulary size of each iteration by counting the number of unique word types
      produced during 500 games of self-play. We observed a similar trend of decreasing vocabulary size
402
      over the course of self-play in the semi-competitive setting, supporting the hypothesis that the semi-
403
      competitive objective leads to convergence in model behavior. However, as shown in Section 5.2,
404   these models performed well in both self-play and in human generalization experiments, suggesting
405   that they may not need very diverse communication strategies to achieve high scores. We include
406   additional results on n-gram entropy in Appendix E, where we observe mostly similar trends.
407

      7     R ELATED W ORK
409
410
      Grounded Dialogue Much prior work on goal-oriented dialogue has focused on collaborative
411
      settings. In tasks such as Cards (Djalali et al., 2011; Potts, 2012), CerealBar (Suhr et al., 2019),
412   OneCommon (Udagawa & Aizawa, 2019), and DialOp (Lin et al., 2024), two or more agents must
413   collaborate via natural language dialogue to achieve a shared goal within an environment. In many of
414   these tasks, models are often evaluated via self-play, which serves as a proxy for human evaluation.
415
      Another line of work has focused on the case where agents have conflicting goals. A handful of
416
      grounded dialogue tasks are focused on bartering or negotiation, including Deal or No Deal (DoND;
417
      Lewis et al., 2017), which is based on the multi issue bargaining task from DeVault et al. (2015),
418   as well as CaSiNo (Chawla et al., 2021) and the fruit trading game from Gemp et al. (2024). These
419   games are all structurally similar and differ primarily in the number and types of objects they use,
420   as well as the public availability of human data. In the Craigslist Bargaining task (He et al., 2018),
421   agents negotiate on the price of a object for sale. Recently, several new game environments have
422   been proposed for benchmarking language model agents (Chalamalasetti et al., 2023; Qiao et al.,
423   2023; Li et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024a; Gong et al., 2024). Fried et al. (2023) provides additional
424   discussion of grounded dialogue tasks and modeling approaches.
425
426   Self-Improving Language Models Lewis et al. (2017) trained GRU-based language models on
427
      the Deal or No Deal task using REINFORCE (Williams, 1992). In contrast to our work, Lewis et al.
      (2017) did not learn a model tabula rasa but instead interleaved reinforcement learning from self-
428
      play with supervised learning on task-specific data to avoid divergence from human-interpretable
429
      language. Divergence issues abound in other settings where models are trained via self-play, such
430
          2
431         In order to validate this method, the authors manually annotated 100 randomly selected messages across
      iterations and compared their predictions with those of GPT-4, obtaining 92% agreement.


                                                           8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      as in emergent communication (Kottur et al., 2017; Lowe et al., 2019; Tomlin & Pavlick, 2019),
433   including some work on negotiation settings (Cao et al., 2018; Noukhovitch et al., 2021).
434
435   A wave of recent work has focused on methods for autonomously improving large language models
436
      at training (Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022; Abdulhai et al., 2023) or inference (Shinn et al.,
      2023; Yao et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024b) time. Methods like StaR (Zelikman et al., 2022) and Rest-
437
      EM (Singh et al., 2024) iteratively train models on their own filtered outputs to improve reasoning
438
      capabilities. Closely related to our work is Pan et al. (2024), which iteratively trains models for
439   device-control tasks using filtered behavior cloning; however, in contrast to our work, Pan et al.
440   (2024) studies a single agent interacting with an environment, rather than multiple agents interacting
441   with one another. Another closely related paper is Fu et al. (2023), which uses self-play to refine
442   language models for a distributive bargaining task; in contrast to our work, Fu et al. (2023) use in-
443   context learning rather than iterative finetuning, leading to less major performance improvements.
444   Finally, the recently proposed SOTOPIA-π (Wang et al., 2024) trains models via a similar filtered
445   BC method on a benchmark of social tasks (Zhou et al., 2024).
446
447   Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning Training agents against copies of themselves is a long-
448   standing technique in reinforcement learning (Littman, 1994), popularized in the past decade by
449
      models like AlphaGo (Silver et al., 2016) and AlphaZero (Silver et al., 2017). Experiments on
      games such as Overcooked (Carroll et al., 2019) and Hanabi (Bard et al., 2020) have shown that poli-
450
      cies learned via self-play often fail to generalize to collaborative or imperfect information games.
451
      Methods such as fictitious self-play and population play have been proposed to address these issues
452   (Heinrich et al., 2015; Strouse et al., 2021), but have primarily been applied in games without lan-
453   guage components. In games with language, a KL-regularization objective is often used to prevent
454   language from drifting too far from human-written training data (Jaques et al., 2019; FAIR, 2022).
455

      8   C ONCLUSION & D ISCUSSION
457
458
      Our experiments showed that language model self-play can lead to significant performance improve-
459
      ments in both semi-competitive and cooperative games. This finding contradicts existing wisdom
460   that self-play is ineffective in collaborative domains (Strouse et al., 2021), or that models need to be
461   trained on task-specific human data to avoid divergence from human-interpretable language (Lewis
462   et al., 2017; FAIR, 2022). One hypothesis is that because we observed significant model improve-
463   ments after just ten rounds of self-play, the model may not have had time to overfit to cooperation
464   with itself. Another hypothesis is that better language models might be more robust to the nega-
465   tive effects of self-play. Given a model with good generalization abilities, finetuning on self-play
466   games might be able to elicit model capabilities which are not directly present in the self-play data.
467   Furthermore, self-play with pretrained language models might actually function more similarly to
468
      population play, since large language models are trained on text from a population of users and may
      simulate different personas in different contexts (Pataranutaporn et al., 2021; Park et al., 2023).
469
470   Although game scores increased significantly after self-play, this increase can be almost entirely
471   attributed to an increase in the percentage of completed games, rather than better strategic reasoning
472   or negotiation tactics. We anticipate that future work may be able to obtain even larger improvements
473   by combining self-play with approaches other than filtered BC, such as natural language reflections
474
      (Shinn et al., 2023). Another possible approach is described by Srivastava et al. (2024), in which a
      language model is used to describe distributional differences between good and bad trajectories.
475
476   Finally, the effectiveness of methods like self-play is completely dependent on a reward signal,
477   which in this work was obtained from the game environment. To apply similar methods in real-world
478   settings, we anticipate that models will need to rely on feedback from general-purpose, learned eval-
479   uators (e.g., as in Du et al., 2023; Pan et al., 2024). We leave further investigation of the challenges
480
      associated with bringing self-play into real-world application domains to future work.

      A     E NVIRONMENT I MPLEMENTATION
757
758
      We implemented an environment for the task under which language models can play the game with
759
      each other or against another human. For initializing a new instance of the game, we sample from
760   a list of 4,186 valid game contexts (shared item counts and private value functions for each player),
761   provided by Lewis et al. (2017). The environment then takes turns prompting each player to either
762   send a message (prepended by [message]) or submit a proposal (prepended by [propose]).
763   After detecting a submitted proposal, the environment forces the other player to submit a proposal
764   of their own. This is enforced by error correction, as described below. Once a game is completed,
765   the environment uses the game context and the submitted proposals to determine the final score of
766   each player, conditioned on the objective under which the players were instructed to play.
767
768   Error Correction This environment comes with comprehensive error-handling to correct models’
769   errant outputs. Specifically, the environment will reply with instructions for correcting errors when
770   errors are detected, providing the model with the opportunity to format its output correctly. The
771   errors that we check for, and their corresponding correction messages, can be found in Table 3. If a
772
      model generates five errant outputs in a row, then the environment aborts the game, and both players
      receive zero score.

804   Zero-Shot Prompting Across every setting, the initial models are zero-shot prompted with the
805   game’s rules and the instructions for sending messages and submitting proposals with the correct
806   syntax. The choice of this approach over few-shot prompting was motivated by the concern that few-
807   shot examples might influence strategies chosen by the model during inference. Our preliminary
808   experiments found that models would closely match the negotiation techniques used in few-shot
809   examples; for example, if prompted with dialogues where players shared their exact value functions,
      the model would consistently share its own values, whether doing so was advantageous or not.

818   B     M ODEL T RAINING AND H YPERPARAMETERS
819
820
      All models were finetuned using the OpenAI API, with parameters n epochs=3,
821   batch size=1, and learning rate multiplier=8. For model inference, we gen-
822   erated outputs with temperature=1. These parameters were all default values chosen by
823   the OpenAI API, except for the learning rate multiplier, which defaults to 2. Our preliminary
824   experiments with default learning rate multipliers yielded models that not only failed to improve
825   but also devolved significantly in quality. We hypothesize that this occurred because parameters
826   are set dynamically based on the quantity of finetuning data. Because language model self-play
827   requires sequential rounds of finetuning, it may be important to choose initial parameters based on
828   the expectation of future finetuning rounds.
829   To ensure that GPT-3.5’s low initial scores were not a result of suboptimal prompting, we manually
830   crafted 10 alternative semi-competitive prompts (and their cooperative counterparts) and ran 100
831   games of self-play using GPT-3.5 for each one. For the semi-competitive setting, the mean score
832   across prompts was 0.382, with a minimum of 0.18 and a maximum of 0.52. For the cooperative
833   setting, the mean score across prompts was 0.636, with a minimum of 0.25 and a maximum of 1.17.

852   workers from the United States with a 98+% HIT approval rate and at least 500 completed HITs,

      C.3   I NCENTIVE S TRUCTURE
919
920   We paid $1.00 for picking up the HIT and $0.10 per game completed. The majority of pay was
921   distributed through bonuses. We paid a bonus of $0.20 per point earned in the semi-competitive
922   setting and $0.10 per point earned in the cooperative setting, since scores in the cooperative game
923   are on average twice as high. We also paid workers $0.25 in cases where models aborted due to
924   repeatedly generating ill-formed messages.
925   In contrast, Lewis et al. (2017) paid workers $0.10 per game and $0.05 in bonus pay only when
926   workers achieved the maximum score of ten points. We found that this approach incentivized workers
927   to end the game as quickly as possible, as maximizing the number of games played was more
928   lucrative than attempting to achieve a high score.

      D     R ESULTS FOR S TRICT C OMPETITION
951
952
      When we applied LM self-play to Deal or No Deal under the strictly competitive objective, the model
953
      failed to improve its performance, instead learning strategies that adversely impacted its ability to
954   perform outside of self-play. Since the mean score in self-play for every iteration will always be
955   zero (because the game is zero-sum), we instead evaluated the quality of model self-play through
956   agreement rates. As shown in Figure 7 and Figure 8, while agreement rate trends show that the
957   model’s performance in self-play improves, these models fail to generalize to competition with other
958   models, such as GPT-4. Our preliminary human experiments also showed that the model failed to
959   reach agreements in roughly 95% of games.
960   In our qualitative analysis of successive iterations of the model under the strictly competitive ob-
961   jective, we found that it learned to replicate an inverted proposal strategy; specifically, the model
962   learned a strategy where it submits a proposal based on what the opposing player should receive,
963   rather than what the model itself should receive. While the model optimized this strategy in self-
964   play well enough to arrive at valid agreements at a competitive rate with itself, this strategy does
965   not generalize to competition with humans or GPT-4. We found this to be a consequence of the
966   aggressive nature of the strictly competitive objective leading to a smaller proportion of games end-
967   ing in valid agreements to derive reward signal from. With a smaller number of samples providing
968
      reward signal, we risk an outcome where the few samples that are isolated for finetuning achieve
      their non-zero reward through undesirable strategies (inverted proposals, in this instance) that do not
969
      generalize well to human interaction.
970
971   Our experiments under this objective illustrate an important takeaway regarding the failure modes
      of LM self-play. For this strategy to be effective, there must be confidence that the initial model is

        capable of achieving high performance through desired strategies with significant probability. Ad-
973     ditionally, we speculate that this strategy is most effective in environments with continuous reward.
