011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            Diffusion models undergo phase transitions during the generative process where
014            data features suddenly emerge in the final stages. The current study aims to
015            elucidate this critical phenomenon from the geometrical perspective. We employ
016
               the concept of “injectivity radius”, a quantity that characterises the structure of the
               data manifold. Through theoretical and empirical evidence, we demonstrate that
017
               phase transitions in the generative process of diffusion models are closely related
018
               to the injectivity radius. Our findings offer a novel perspective on phase transitions
019            in diffusion models, with potential implications for improving performance and
020            sampling efficiency.
021

023   1    I NTRODUCTION
024
025   Generative models (Bond-Taylor et al., 2022) address the fundamental challenge of approximating
026   and sampling from complex probability distributions. Diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015;
027   Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2021), a prominent class of generative models, incorporate two primary
028   processes: a forward (diffusion) process, wherein data points are perturbed by incrementally adding
029
      noise to the data, mapping a complex distribution into an analytically tractable prior distribution, and
      a backward (reverse diffusion) process, where noise is denoised back into a sample from the data
030
      distribution by reversing the noise perturbation. The reverse process involves estimating the “score
031
      vector”, the gradient of the log-density of the perturbed data distribution.
032
033   Recent findings report that diffusion models exhibit critical phenomena, the abrupt emergence of
034   distinctive features during the generative process (Ho et al., 2020; Meng et al., 2022; Choi et al.,
035
      2022; Zheng et al., 2023; Raya & Ambrogioni, 2023; Georgiev et al., 2023; Sclocchi et al., 2024;
      Biroli et al., 2024; Li & Chen, 2024), a critical phenomenon we refer to as a “phase transition”.
036
      Elucidating phase transitions is expected to help distinguishing between irrelevant information (noise)
037
      and relevant information, or memorisation and generalisation. This distinction offers valuable insights
038   into optimising the sampling process and developing better conditional diffusion models, such as
039   for tasks involving language-conditioned image generation. While experimental evidences of such
040   phenomena have been provided in various studies, theoretical frameworks still remain limited and
041   under development. Raya & Ambrogioni (2023) have defined local energy and examined its stability;
042   however, their approach is limited to simple data structures, such as hyperspheres, for which the
043   potential is known. We extend this inquiry by exploring tubular neighbourhoods, applicable to data
044   manifolds with more complex geometries, to better understand phase transitions in diverse contexts.
045   Building on this foundation, we propose a novel geometric interpretation of phase transitions in
046   diffusion models, grounded in the behaviour of the score vectors. As demonstrated in prior re-
047   search (Stanczuk et al., 2024), the score vectors at the final time step of the generative process are
048   orthogonal to the tangent plane of the data distribution. This implies that score vectors map noisy
049   data points to their nearest points on the noise-free data distribution at the final stage of the generative
050   process. However, these points may not always be unique and depend on the data geometry. Moreover,
051   the uncertainty of such generative trajectories is expected to increase as they move farther from the
052
      data manifold. To address this interpretation, we employ the concept of the “injectivity radius” —
      the supremum distance within which the nearest point on the data distribution is always uniquely
053
      determined. We define the region within the injectivity radius as the tubular neighbourhood (Fig. 1).

130   2.2   F ROM THE M ANIFOLD H YPOTHESIS TO T UBULAR N EIGHBOURHOODS
131
132   Data often concentrates around a lower-dimensional manifold, a concept known as the manifold
133   hypothesis (Fefferman et al., 2013; Loaiza-Ganem et al., 2024). We work in this paper based on
134   this hypothesis. For simplicity, we will assume all data manifolds are compact and embedded in the
135   Euclidean space Rd . In principle, any Riemannian manifolds can be isometrically embedded into
136
      some Euclidean space (the Nash embedding theorem).
137   A tubular neighbourhood of a manifold is roughly speaking a set of points near the manifold and
138   every point of the set has a unique projection onto it (see Appendix C.3 for the formal definition).
139   It is theoretically known that every manifold embedded in Rd has a tubular neighbourhood. In fact
140   if we take a sufficiently small neighbourhood of a manifold, we may find a tubular neighbourhood.
141   On the other hand, it is easy to imagine that we cannot take a too large neighbourhood as a tubular
142
      neighbourhood. See also Appendix A for previous studies which inspired our perspective.

330
      Remark 5.1. Throughout this section, given a manifold M embedded in Rd , the tubular neighbour-
331
      hood of M means that with the injectivity radius R(M), for short.
332
333   In this section, we empirically demonstrate the presence of phase transitions at the boundary of the
334   tubular neighbourhood during the generative process of diffusion models. In particular, we analyse
335   the proportion of particles outside the tubular neighbourhood at each time step using the standard
336   DDPM setup (T = 1000) and investigate the corresponding changes in the Wasserstein distance
337   between the training data distribution and the generated distribution for varying initial times T . The
338   Wasserstein distance is evaluated using the late initialisation scheme (Raya & Ambrogioni, 2023),
339
      where the generation process begins at different initial time steps. During inference, we adopted
      values of T ranging from 1 to 1000, with the initial state set to xT ∼ N (0, I). Our experiments show
340
      that during late initialisation, the Wasserstein distance undergoes a sharp shift at certain time step,
341
      indicating the onset of phase transitions as particles enter the tubular neighbourhood. The number and
342   timing of these transitions vary based on the curvature of the underlying manifold. For hyperspheres,
343   one phase transition occurs, aligned with a rapid decrease in the proportion of particles outside the
344   tubular neighbourhood and a sharp rise in the Wasserstein distance. For ellipses and tori, the timing of
345   transitions varies due to regions with higher curvature. In the following subsections, we make detail
346   experiments on various geometries, including hyperspheres (see 5.1), ellipses, tori (see 5.2), and also
347   disjoint arcs (see 5.3), to provide a comprehensive understanding of how tubular neighbourhoods
348   affect the generation process. Additionally, we demonstrate that embedding real-world datasets into
349   a hypersphere improves sampling efficiency(see 5.4). The detailed experimental setup, including
350
      DDPM parameters and configurations, is provided in Appendix J.

      As shown in the case of S 20 embedded in R48 (Figure 5), the hypothesis breaks down when the
396   ambient space is increased. This phenomenon can be attributed to the growing distance between
397   the data manifold and the initial Gaussian distribution as the ambient space dimension increases.
398   Generally, when the ambient space dimension becomes larger, the expected region where the Gaussian
399   distribution is concentrated moves further away from the origin, scaling with the square root of the
400   dimension if the initial state follows a standard normal distribution. In contrast, in our experiments,
401   the data manifold is a unit hypersphere, and its average position remains fixed at a constant distance
402   of 1 from the origin. Consequently, a significant discrepancy emerges between the two distributions.
403   This discrepancy indicates the presence of a phase in the generation process during which the data
404
      distribution experiences a substantial average shift. As a result, when sampling from a Gaussian
      distribution under late initialisation, the difference from the expected distribution at the original time
405
      step becomes significantly larger, making accurate reconstruction more challenging (see J.3).
406
407   We now focus on two manifolds with “mixed” curvatures in some sense — ellipses and tori. We
408   can verify that the injectivity radius of the ellipse with major axis 2R and minor axis 2r is given by
409   r2 /R, and that of the torus with major radius R and minor radius r is given by min{R − r, r} (see
410
      J.4). During the experiments, we observed multiple phases of increase in the Wasserstein distance
      under late initialisation. Moreover, in standard diffusion models, we confirmed that the time at which
411
      particles begin to enter the tubular neighbourhood corresponds to the final sharp increase in the
412
      Wasserstein distance. This suggests that the timing of the last spontaneous symmetry breaking can be
413   inferred from the particles within the tubular neighbourhood. Furthermore, these findings imply that
414   the concept of injectivity radius corresponds to a mathematical quantity representing the region of the
415   data manifold with the highest curvature.

513   6    D ISCUSSION AND CONCLUSION
514
515   In this study, we employed the concept of the injectivity radius to understand the generative process
516   of diffusion models and analysed it theoretically and experimentally from the perspective of the
517   geometric structure of data manifolds. Specifically, we provided a new perspective on a phenomenon
518   where certain features emerge rapidly over a short time interval, referred to as a phase transition.
519   However, the concept of the injectivity radius, which may correspond to the region of the manifold
520   with the largest curvature, only partially explains the phase transition phenomenon. To address
521   this limitation, defining a mathematical quantity that corresponds to the smallest curvature of the
522   manifold may remove the assumption of constant curvature, potentially leading to a more general
523   theory applicable to a wider range of datasets. This would allow for more efficient sampling methods
524   that better reflect the geometric properties of the data manifold, thereby enhancing the overall
525
      performance of diffusion models. Moreover, from the perspective of nonequilibrium thermodynamics,
      the system’s free energy, defined by weighting the energy at each point with the probability distribution
526
      function, can provide a more comprehensive macroscopic understanding of phase transitions (for
527
      details, see Appendix G). This approach could enable a more accurate discussion of phase transitions
528   as a macroscopic phenomenon, complementing the microscopic geometric analysis.
529
530   By exploring the relationship between the data’s geometric structure and the score vectors, as
531
      discussed in Section 4.2, it may also be possible to design optimal noise scheduling strategies that
      are tailored to the geometry of the data manifold. Clarifying how score vectors interact with the
532
      manifold’s geometric properties could further optimise the generative process. In addition, our
533
      experiments with real-world datasets suggest that modifying the VAE embedding into hyperspheres
534   could extend the applicability of the tubular neighbourhood concept to more complex datasets. This
535   modification is expected to not only improve sampling efficiency but also increase the flexibility
536   of diffusion models in handling a wider variety of real-world data. These discussions lie at the
537   intersection of concepts from differential geometry, particularly singularities, statistical physics,
538   especially phase transitions in non-equilibrium thermodynamics, and computer science, specifically
539   diffusion models. This interdisciplinary approach represents an important step forward, and further
      theoretical development and practical applications in this direction hold promising potential.

       A     R ELATED WORK
973
974
       Diffusion models (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2021) have emerged
975    as a powerful class of generative models (Bond-Taylor et al., 2022), demonstrating remarkable
976    performance in various domains including text-to-image synthesis (Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021; Dhari-
977    wal & Nichol, 2021; Ding et al., 2021; Ramesh et al., 2022; Nichol et al., 2022; Rombach et al.,
978    2022; Saharia et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2022; Ho et al., 2022a), text-to-speech (Chen et al., 2021;
979    Kong et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2023), video generation (Ho et al., 2022b; Singer et al., 2023; Xing
980    et al., 2023), natural language processing (Li et al., 2022; Lou et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023b), robot
981    manipulations (Janner et al., 2022; Zhu et al., 2023), inverse problems (Daras et al., 2024), and
982    protein interactions modelling (Abramson et al., 2024).
983    Motivations and related works. Our work is motivated by several recent theoretical advance-
984    ments (Yeğin & Amasyalı, 2024) and practical challenges (Chen et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024):
985
986        • Optimisation of Diffusion Time: Some empirical studies report existence of an optimal
987          diffusion time that enhances model efficiency (Franzese et al., 2023).
988
           • Critical Phenomena and Statistical Thermodynamics of Diffusion Models: There are some
             empirical and theoretical studies report heterogeneity/non-uniformity, critical phenomena during
989
             generation (Ho et al., 2020; Meng et al., 2022; Choi et al., 2022; Zheng et al., 2023; Raya &
990
             Ambrogioni, 2023; Georgiev et al., 2023; Sclocchi et al., 2024; Biroli et al., 2024; Li & Chen,
991          2024; Yu & Huang, 2024; Kadkhodaie et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024). Raya & Ambrogioni
992          (2023) reveals a spontaneous symmetry breaking in diffusion models, dividing the generative
993          dynamics into two phases: a linear steady-state around a central fixed point and an attractor
994          dynamics towards the data manifold. They linked the fixed points of the Fokker-Planck equations
995          to moments of spontaneous symmetry breaking in the Hessian of the potential functions and
996          demonstrated an end-to-end asymptotic analysis in a simple discrete distribution supported on
997          two points and some other toy examples. The authors also propose a Gaussian late initialisation
998          scheme which improves model performance, generation efficiency, and increases sample diversity.
999
             The concurrent work (Li & Chen, 2024) introduces a theoretical framework to understand phase
             transitions (they coined the term “critical windows” to describe the narrow time intervals in the
1000
             generation during which specific features of the final image sample emerge, such as image class
1001
             or background colour). The authors propose a formal non-asymptotic framework to study these
1002         windows, focusing on data from a mixture of strongly log-concave densities. They show that
1003         these windows can be provably bounded in terms of certain measures of inter- and intra-group
1004         separation. Biroli et al. (2024) employs statistical physics to identify three distinct dynamical

1840   In previous studies (Raya & Ambrogioni (2023)), the training of diffusion models was performed
1841   using DDPM. The number of timesteps is set to 1000, and the noise schedule coefficient β linearly
1842   increases from 1.0 × 10−4 to 2.0 × 10−2 . A key difference from prior work is that, for denoising,
1843   the MLP layers have been replaced with a 1D U-Net. This adjustment is necessary to handle
1844   higher-dimensional data, such as 16D, 24D, and 48D, where a more complex model is required.
1845   The model is trained using the mean squared error (MSE) loss function, with AdamW as the optimizer.
1846   The learning rate is set to 1 × 10−3 , and the batch size is 32. For toy data experiments, the training
1847   dataset consists of 50,000 points sampled from a uniform distribution. The model is trained without
1848   using any advanced samplers like DDIM, relying solely on the standard DDPM reverse process.

2027   In Section 5.4, we performed experiments by embedding real-world datasets such as MNIST and
2028
       Fashion MNIST onto a hypersphere, subsequently mapping them into a high-dimensional Euclidean
       space. For efficient generation and sampling in diffusion models, it is a common practice to reduce
2029
       the dimensionality into a latent space—similar to the approach in Latent Diffusion—and consider
2030
       the transitions of the diffusion model within this space. While Latent Diffusion utilizes VQ-VAE for
2031   embedding, we employ a Hyperspherical VAE in our methodology.
2032
2033   In our approach, several key modifications were made to the original hyperspherical VAE
2034
       (sVAE) (Davidson et al., 2018) setup used in prior studies. One significant change was transi-
       tioning from binary data representation, where data was handled as binary values, to continuous
2035
       data representation. As a result, we replaced the Binary Cross-Entropy (BCE) loss function with
2036
       the Mean Squared Error (MSE) loss function. This modification allows for more accurate modeling
2037   and reconstruction of continuous data, particularly when working with datasets such as MNIST and
2038   Fashion MNIST.
2039
2040
       Furthermore, to address the limitations of the previously used MLP layers for reconstructing natural
       images, we enhanced the model by adopting CNN-based layers. This adjustment is particularly
2041
       beneficial for reconstructing images from higher-dimensional latent spaces.
2042

2044   the hyperspherical VAE (sVAE), focusing on the ELBO (Evidence Lower Bound) and log-likelihood

2046

2133   There are limitations regarding embedding onto hyperspheres. Previous studies have shown that
2134   as the dimensionality of the embedding onto the hypersphere increases beyond a certain value, the
2135
       accuracy of the embedding decreases. This phenomenon is related to the fact that the surface area
       of a hypersphere approaches zero in the high-dimensional limit. Therefore, although we conducted
2136
       experiments with MNIST and Fashion MNIST, for larger datasets, the accuracy of the embedding
2137
       would deteriorate to the point where considering a diffusion model would no longer be meaningful.
2138
2139   However, despite these current challenges, there are potential solutions. Previous research focused
2140   on
       √ embeddings onto unit hyperspheres, but it is possible to consider hyperspheres with      √ a radius of
2141
         n. When the dimensionality is n, the surface area of a hypersphere with a radius of n increases
       monotonically, suggesting that the embedding could remain effective even as the dimensionality
2142
       increases. In this case, efficient generation using diffusion models that leverage the concept of tubular
2143
       neighborhoods could become meaningful.
