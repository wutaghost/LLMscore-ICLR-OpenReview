                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
012
               This paper studies fast adversarial training against sparse adversarial perturbations
               bounded by l0 norm. We demonstrate the challenges of employing 1-step attacks
013
               on l0 bounded perturbations for fast adversarial training, including degraded per-
014
               formance and the occurrence of catastrophic overfitting (CO). We highlight that
015            CO in l0 adversarial training is caused by sub-optimal perturbation locations of
016            1-step attack. Theoretical and empirical analyses reveal that the loss landscape of
017            l0 adversarial training is more craggy compared to its l∞ , l2 and l1 counterparts.
018            Moreover, we corroborate that the craggy loss landscape can aggravate CO. To
019            address these issues, we propose Fast-LS-l0 that incorporates soft labels and the
020            trade-off loss function to smooth the adversarial loss landscape. Extensive exper-
021            iments demonstrate our method can overcome the challenge of catastrophic over-
022            fitting, achieve state-of-the-art performance, and narrow down the performance
023
               gap between 1-step and multi-step adversarial training against sparse attacks.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Deep neural networks have been shown vulnerable to adversarial perturbations (Szegedy et al.,
029
      2013). To achieve robust models, comprehensive evaluations (Athalye et al., 2018; Croce & Hein,
030   2020b; Croce et al., 2020) have demonstrated that adversarial training (Madry et al., 2018) and
031   its variants (Croce & Hein, 2020a; Sehwag et al.; Rebuffi et al., 2021; Gowal et al., 2021; Rade
032   & Moosavi-Dezfooli, 2021; Cui et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023) are the most effective methods.
033   However, adversarial training is generally computationally expensive because generating adversarial
034   perturbations in each training step needs multiple forward and backward passes of the model. Such
035   efficiency issues hinder the scalability of adversarial training to large models and large datasets.
036   Improving the efficiency of adversarial training is tricky. Some works (Shafahi et al., 2019; Zhang
037   et al., 2019a; Wong et al.; Sriramanan et al., 2021) employ faster but weaker 1-step attacks to gen-
038   erate adversarial perturbations for training. However, such methods may suffer from catastrophic
039   overfitting (CO) (Kang & Moosavi-Dezfooli, 2021): the model overfits these weak attacks instead
040   of achieving true robustness against adaptive and stronger attacks.
041
      On the other hand, most existing works (Madry et al., 2018; Tramer & Boneh, 2019; Jiang et al.,
042   2023) focus on studying adversarial perturbations bounded by l∞ , l2 or l1 norms. In these scenarios,
043   the set of allowable perturbations is convex, which facilitates optimizing adversarial perturbations
044   and thus adversarial training. However, there are many scenarios in real-world applications where
045   sparse perturbations, bounded by the l0 norm, need to be considered (Modas et al., 2019; Croce
046   & Hein, 2019; Croce et al., 2022; Zhong et al., 2024). Since the l0 norm is not a proper norm,
047   the set of all allowable perturbations in this case is not convex. Consequently, from an optimiza-
048   tion perspective, obtaining robust models against sparse perturbations becomes more challenging.
049   Compared with the l∞ , l2 and l1 counterparts, more steps are needed to generate strong l0 bounded
050
      perturbations, making the corresponding adversarial training even more computationally expensive.
051   Among algorithms aiming at obtaining robust models against sparse perturbations, sAT and
052   sTRADES (Zhong et al., 2024) stand out as the most effective ones. These methods employ ad-
053   versarial training against Sparse-PGD (sPGD) (Zhong et al., 2024). However, they still require 20
      steps to generate adversarial perturbations in each training step to achieve decent performance. As

063   In this work, we investigate the challenges associated with fast adversarial training against sparse
064   perturbations, including training instability caused by catastrophic overfitting (CO) and performance
065
      decline in both robust accuracy and clean accuracy. Specifically, we highlight that CO in l0 adver-
      sarial training is caused by sub-optimal perturbation locations of 1-step attack. Our observation
066
      indicates that adjusting the perturbation magnitudes alone cannot help mitigate CO in this context,
067
      so existing CO mitigation methods (Kim et al., 2020; Andriushchenko & Flammarion, 2020; Zheng
068   et al., 2019; Huang et al., 2023) used in other cases do not work in the l0 scenario. Following, we
069   provide empirical and theoretical evidence to illustrate that the loss landscape of adversarial training
070   against l0 bounded perturbations is notably more craggy compared to its l∞ , l2 , and l1 counterparts.
071   Remarkably, these observations hold true even when only a single pixel is perturbed. Furthermore,
072   we corroborate that the craggy loss landscape further aggravates CO in l0 adversarial training.
073
      Drawing from these insights, we propose to utilize soft labels and a trade-off loss objective function
074   to enhance the smoothness of the adversarial loss objective function, thereby improving the perfor-
075   mance of fast adversarial training against sparse perturbations. In addition to the performance, we
076   showcase that these techniques can also eliminate CO, thus improving training stability. Finally,
077   our extensive experiments demonstrate that smoothing the loss landscape can effectively narrow the
078   performance gap between 1-step adversarial training and its multi-step counterparts.
079
      To the best of our knowledge, this work is the first to investigate fast adversarial training in the
080   context of l0 bounded perturbations. We summarize the contributions of this paper as follows:
081
082   1. We highlight that catastrophic overfitting (CO) in fast l0 adversarial training is caused by sub-
083      optimal perturbation locations of 1-step attack. Popular techniques in fast l∞ , l2 and l1 adversarial
084      training are ineffective in the l0 case.
085   2. We theoretically and empirically demonstrate that the adversarial loss landscape is more craggy
086      in the l0 cases than in other cases, which further aggravates CO in l0 adversarial training. In this
087      regard, we propose Fast-LS-l0 which incorporates the techniques of soft labels and the trade-off
088      loss function to provably smooth the adversarial loss landscape.
089   3. Our comprehensive experiments demonstrate that smoothing the adversarial loss landscape
090      greatly narrows the performance gap between 1-step l0 adversarial training and its multi-step
091      counterpart. Our method establishes a new state-of-the-art performance for efficient adversarial
092      training against sparse perturbations.

      2    R ELATED W ORKS
109
110
      Adversarial Attacks: The existence of adversarial examples is first identified in Szegedy et al.
111
      (2013), which focuses on l2 norm-bounded adversarial perturbations. Fast gradient sign method
112
      (FGSM) (Goodfellow et al., 2014) introduces an efficient approach by generating perturbations
113   bounded by its l∞ norm in a single step. Furthermore, projected gradient descent (PGD) (Madry
114   et al., 2018) extends and improves FGSM (Kurakin et al., 2017) by iterative updating and random
115   initialization. In addition to these white-box attacks where the attackers have full access to the mod-
116   els, there are also several black-box attacks (Dong et al., 2018; Andriushchenko et al., 2020) where
117   the attackers’ access is restricted. AutoAttack (AA) (Croce & Hein, 2020b) is an ensemble of both
118   white-box and black-box attacks to ensure a more reliable evaluation of model’s robustness.
119   Adversarial Training: Adversarial training (Madry et al., 2018; Croce & Hein, 2020a; Sehwag
120   et al.; Rebuffi et al., 2021; Gowal et al., 2021; Rade & Moosavi-Dezfooli, 2021; Cui et al., 2023;
121   Wang et al., 2023) has emerged as a popular and reliable framework to obtain robust models (Athalye
122   et al., 2018; Croce & Hein, 2020b). Under this framework, we first generate adversarial examples
123   and update model parameters based on these examples in each mini-batch update. Different ad-
124   versarial training variants, such as TRADES (Zhang et al., 2019b) and MART (Wang et al., 2020),
125   may have different loss objective functions for generating adversarial examples and updating model
126   parameters. Furthermore, compared with training on clean inputs, adversarial training is shown to
127
      suffer more from overfitting (Rice et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2021a). In this regard, self-adaptive training
      (SAT) (Huang et al., 2020), which utilizes historical predictions as the soft label, has demonstrated
128
      its efficacy in improving the generalization.
129
130   Sparse Perturbations: Adversarial budget defined by l1 norm is the tightest convex hull of the one
131   defined by l0 norm. In this context, SLIDE (Tramer & Boneh, 2019) extends PGD and employs
132   k-coordinate ascent to generate l1 bounded perturbations. Similarly, AutoAttack-l1 (AA-l1 ) (Croce
133
      & Hein, 2021) extends AA to the l1 case. However, AA-l1 is found to generate non-sparse pertur-
      bations that SLIDE fails to discover (Jiang et al., 2023), indicating that l1 bounded perturbations are
134
      not necessarily sparse. Therefore, we use l0 norm to strictly enforce sparsity. It is challenging to
135
      optimize over an adversarial budget defined by l0 norm, because of non-convex adversarial budgets.
136   While naively applying PGD in this case turns out sub-optimal, there are several black-box attacks,
137   including CornerSearch (Croce & Hein, 2019) and Sparse-RS (Croce et al., 2022), and white-box at-
138   tacks, including Sparse Adversarial and Interpretable Attack Framework (SAIF) (Imtiaz et al., 2022)
139   and Sparse-PGD (sPGD) (Zhong et al., 2024), which address the optimization challenge of finding
140   l0 bounded perturbations. Ultimately, Sparse-AutoAttack (sAA) (Zhong et al., 2024), combining
141   the most potent white-box and black-box attacks, emerges as the most powerful sparse attack.
142   Fast Adversarial Training: While effective, adversarial training is time-consuming due to the use
143   of multi-step attacks. To reduce the computational overhead, some studies (Shafahi et al., 2019;
144   Zhang et al., 2019a) employ faster one-step attacks in adversarial training. However, the training
145   based on these weaker attacks may suffer from catastrophic overfitting (CO) (Kang & Moosavi-
146   Dezfooli, 2021), where the model overfits to these weak attacks instead of achieving true robustness
147   against a variety of attacks. CO is shown to arise from distorted decision boundary caused by
148   sub-optimal perturbation magnitudes (Kim et al., 2020). There are several methods proposed to
149   mitigate CO, including aligning the gradients of clean and adversarial samples (Andriushchenko &
150
      Flammarion, 2020), adding stronger noise to clean sample (de Jorge Aranda et al., 2022) , adap-
      tive step size (Huang et al., 2023), regularizing abnormal adversarial samples (Lin et al., 2024b),
151
      adding layer-wise weight perturbations (Lin et al., 2024a), and penalizing logits discrepancy (Li &
152
      Spratling, 2023). Furthermore, compared to its l2 and l∞ counterparts, CO is caused by overfitting
153   to sparse perturbations during l1 adversarial training (Jiang et al., 2023). To address this issue, Fast-
154   EG-l1 (Jiang et al., 2023) is introduced to generate l1 bounded perturbations by Euclidean geometry
155   instead of coordinate ascent. In this work, we investigate fast adversarial training against l0 bounded
156   perturbations.

      versarial training, which introduces significant computational overhead. To accelerate training, we

      3.1              C ATASTROPHIC OVERFITTING IN FAST l0 A DVERSARIAL T RAINING
168
169   We plot the learning curves of adversarial training using 1-step sPGD in Figure 1. Specifically, we
170   adopt the multi-ϵ strategy (Jiang et al., 2023; Zhong et al., 2024) and allow for different adversarial
171   budget sizes, i.e., ϵ, during training and testing. The results in Figure 1 indicate that CO happens in
172   all configurations. Moreover, our observations of CO in l0 cases are different from other cases in
173   several aspects. First, random initialization of adversarial perturbation, proven effective in l∞ , l2 and
174   l1 cases, does not yield similar results in the l0 case. In addition, Figure 1 showcases that the training
175
      accuracy on the inputs perturbed by 1-step sPGD is even higher than their clean counterparts. What’s
      more, when CO happens in l∞ , l2 and l1 cases, the model sharply achieves perfect robustness against
176
      1-step attacks but zero robustness against multi-step attacks, both in few mini-batch updates. Such
177
      phenomenon is not observed in l0 cases. By contrast, we observe dramatic performance fluctuations
178   on clean examples throughout the training process, even in the fine-tuning phase. Such training
179   instability indicates a non-smooth landscape of the loss function in the parameter space: a subtle
180   change in parameters θ leads to abrupt fluctuation in the loss.

      factor α controls the balance between the loss on the clean inputs and the loss on the adversarial
379   inputs. On one hand, a smaller value of α results in a smoother loss objective function, but it assigns
380   less weight to the loss of the adversarial inputs and potentially hurts the robustness of the obtained
381   model. On the other hand, a bigger value of α assigns more weight to the adversarial loss to focus
382   on robustness, but it makes the corresponding adversarial loss objective function more challenging
383   for optimization. Furthermore, compared with l1 , l2 and l∞ cases, the trade-off loss function is
384   particularly useful and necessary in the l0 case. This is supported by the analyses in Section 3.2
385   and Appendix D, which demonstrate that ∥δ1 − δ2 ∥ is much larger in l0 bounded perturbations
386   than other cases. Therefore, we expect the trade-off loss function Lϵ,α can help mitigate CO by
387
      improving smoothness.

389   smooth activations, where Assumption 3.3 is not strictly satisfied. We provide a more detailed
390   analysis in Appendix C to demonstrate the generality of our conclusions.
391   In summary, soft labels and the trade-off loss function can improve the first-order and second-order
392   smoothness, respectively. Therefore, we can stabilize and improve the performance of fast adversar-
393   ial training against l0 bounded perturbations by combining both techniques together.

395
      ing (SAT) (Huang et al., 2020) and TRADES (Zhang et al., 2019b). Specifically, SAT utilizes the
396
      moving average of previous predictions as the soft label to calculate the loss. TRADES combines
397   the soft label and the trade-off loss function. It utilizes the trade-off loss function to balance the
398   clean and robust accuracy and employs the prediction on the clean inputs as the soft label when cal-
399   culating the loss for adversarial inputs. In Appendix A, we provide the pseudo-codes of both SAT

531
532
      In this paper, we highlight the catastrophic overfitting (CO) in the fast l0 adversarial training is
533
      induced by sub-optimal perturbation locations of 1-step attacks, which is distinct from the l∞ , l2
534   and l1 cases. Theoretical and empirical analyses reveal that the loss landscape of l0 adversarial
535   training is more craggy than other cases, and the craggy loss landscape strongly correlates with CO.
536   To address these issues, we propose Fast-LS-l0 that incorporates soft label and trade-off loss function
537   to smooth the adversarial loss function. Extensive experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of our
538   method in mitigating CO and narrowing down the performance gap between 1-step and multi-step l0
539   adversarial training. The models trained with our method exhibit state-of-the-art robustness against
      sparse attacks in the context of fast adversarial training.
