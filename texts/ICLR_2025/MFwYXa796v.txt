011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Preference-based reinforcement learning (PbRL) can help avoid sophisticated re-
014            ward designs and align better with human intentions, showing great promise in
015            various real-world applications. However, obtaining human feedback for pref-
016            erences can be expensive and time-consuming, which forms a strong barrier for
017            PbRL. In this work, we address the problem of low query efficiency in offline
018            PbRL, pinpointing two primary reasons: inefficient exploration and overoptimiza-
019
               tion of learned reward functions. In response to these challenges, we propose a
               novel algorithm, Offline PbRL via In-Dataset Exploration (OPRIDE), designed to
020
               enhance the query efficiency of offline PbRL. OPRIDE consists of two key features:
021
               a principled exploration strategy that maximizes the informativeness of the queries
022            and a discount scheduling mechanism aimed at mitigating overoptimization of the
023            learned reward functions. Through empirical evaluations, we demonstrate that
024            OPRIDE significantly outperforms prior methods, achieving strong performance
025            with notably fewer queries. Moreover, we provide theoretical guarantees of the al-
026            gorithm’s efficiency. Experimental results across various locomotion, manipulation,
027            and navigation tasks underscore the efficacy and versatility of our approach.

      Reinforcement learning (RL) has proven effective across a range of sequential decision-making
      tasks, from mastering games like Go (Silver et al., 2016) to controlling complex systems such as
034
      robots (Ahn et al., 2022) and plasma reactors (Degrave et al., 2022). However, in many real-world
035
      applications, designing an appropriate reward function is a daunting challenge, as these tasks often
036   involve objectives that are difficult to formalize with numerical rewards.
037
038   Preference-based RL (PbRL) (Akrour et al., 2012; Christiano et al., 2017) has emerged as a promising
039
      paradigm, leveraging human feedback in the form of pairwise preferences, which are inherently
      more interpretable yet still information-rich. This paradigm allows agents to learn from relative
040
      judgments rather than numerical reward signals, significantly reducing the complexity of reward
041
      design. Recent advancements in PbRL have illustrated its efficacy in enabling agents to learn novel
042   behaviors (Christiano et al., 2017; Kim et al., 2023) and in achieving better alignment with human
043   preferences (Ouyang et al., 2022), which are often difficult to encapsulate in a reward function.
044   Despite these advantages, PbRL methods still face critical challenges, particularly in acquiring human
045   feedback efficiently. Querying human preferences is both time-consuming and resource-intensive,
046   limiting the scalability of PbRL in real-world applications.
047
      To address this challenge, we propose Offline PbRL via In-Dataset Exploration (OPRIDE), a
048   novel algorithm designed to systematically enhance the query efficiency of offline PbRL, as depicted
049   in Figure 1. OPRIDE introduces a principled exploration strategy that identifies the most informative
050   queries by analyzing value differences between trajectories, ensuring that each query maximally
051   contributes to learning the optimal policy. Additionally, to prevent overoptimization of the learned
052   reward function (Gao et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2024), particularly in regions with high uncertainty,
053   we incorporate a discount factor scheduling mechanism that dynamically adjusts the discount based
      on the variance in the reward estimation. Based on the pessimistic property of the smaller discount

094
      Preference-based RL. Various methods have been proposed to leverage human preferences (Akrour
095
      et al., 2012; Ibarz et al., 2018) and have demonstrated success in tackling complex control tasks (Chris-
096   tiano et al., 2017; Lee et al., 2021) and in aligning large language models (Stiennon et al., 2020;
097   Ouyang et al., 2022; Rafailov et al., 2023; 2024). In the realm of offline Preference-based Rein-
098   forcement Learning (PbRL), a benchmark including several baselines (e.g., disagreement based
099   method) is introduced by OPRL (Shin et al., 2023), which selects queries based on disagreement
100   between the reward models and is inefficient in determining the optimal policy. Kim et al. (2023)
101   apply Transformer models to effectively capture preferences for better credit assignment. Kang et al.
102   (2023) present a direct approach to learning policy based on preferences. A recent work by Lindner
103   et al. (2021) proposes an information-directed query selection method for PbRL, using the Laplacian
104
      approximation and the Hessian matrix for posterior computation. In contrast, our method selects
      queries to maximize the information gain about the optimal policy rather than the reward function,
105
      ensuring higher query efficiency.
106
107   In addition to empirical achievements, prior studies have also explored the theoretical aspects of
      PbRL. Pacchiano et al. (2021) propose a provable PbRL algorithm tailored for linear MDPs. Chen

213   In this section, we present our proposed algorithm, Offline Preference-based Reinforcement Learning
214   with In-Dataset Exploration (OPRIDE), illustrated in Figure 1. The key idea of OPRIDE is to
215   enhance the query efficiency of offline PbRL by conducting optimistic exploration with in-dataset
      queries and then utilizing the learned reward function pessimistically with discount factor scheduling.

222   3.1   O FFLINE Q UERY S ELECTION WITH I N -DATASET E XPLORATION
223
      Generating informative queries is crucial for calibrating the reward function. Various methods
224
      have been proposed to generate queries for offline preference-based RL, like disagreement-based
225   approaches (Christiano et al., 2017) and information-gain-based approaches (Wilson et al., 2012; Shin
226   et al., 2023), but they can still be inefficient in determining the optimal policy. This naturally leads
227   to the idea of employing an exploration objective (Akrour et al., 2011) into offline query selection,
228   where we maximize the information gain about the optimal policy rather than the reward function.
229
      Inspired by principled exploration strategies for PbRL, analyzed in Section 4, we propose to use
230
      the difference of value differences as the exploration criteria. Specifically, we first train a set of
231   reward functions {rθi }M                                                                    M

232   offline algorithms like IQL (Kostrikov et al., 2021) with the reward functions. Finally, we select two
233   trajectories τ1 and τ2 that maximize the difference of value differences between the two trajectories:

531   6                     C ONCLUSION
532
533
      This paper proposes a new framework, in-dataset exploration, to improve query efficiency in offline
534   PbRL. Compared with disagreement-based approaches, using an exploration strategy helps reduce the
535   burden of learning an accurate reward function in the low-return region, improving learning efficiency.
536   Our proposed algorithm, OPRIDE, conducts in-dataset exploration by weighted trajectory queries,
537   and a principled exploration strategy deals with pairwise queries. Our method has provable guarantees,
538   and our practical variant achieves strong empirical performance on various tasks. Compared to prior
539   methods, our method significantly reduces the required queries. Overall, our method provides a
      promising and principled way to reduce queries required from human labelers in PbRL.

1186   Ablation about In-Dataset Exploration module The choice to emphasize value functions over
1187   reward functions is crucial due to their ability to guide policy optimization effectively. Intuitively,
       while maximizing the information gain concerning the reward function (e.g., difference over the

1188
       reward function) can help learn a well-calibrated reward function, it can still be sample inefficient in
1189   determining the optimal policy since we are not interested in the accuracy of the reward function in
1190   low-return regions. For instance, suppose we have actions a1 and a2 that lead to a terminal state s0 ,
1191   and their immediate rewards are highly uncertain, ranging from [−1, 1]. And we have actions a3 and
1192   a4 that lead to high return states s1 but yield a known fixed immediate reward of zero. By maximizing
1193   the reward differences, we will compare a1 and a2 , but such comparison contains no information in
1194   determining the optimal policy, which will not choose a1 and a2 at all. Theoretically, maximizing the
1195   information gain with respect to the reward function is insufficient to derive a performance guarantee
1196   for PbRL.
1197   We conduct additional ablation studies for these two mechanisms. The experimental results in Table 8
1198   show that maximizing information gain about the optimal policy can achieve better performance than
1199   the reward function.

       Ablation about Variance-based Discount Scheduling module The choice of using a pessimistic
1213
       discount factor in offline RL draws on theoretical guarantees discussed in prior works (Jiang et al.,
1214   2015; Hu et al., 2022). While prior methods may utilize a smaller fixed discount factor (Jiang
1215   et al., 2015) or tuned values in imitation learning (Liu et al., 2023), our approach innovatively
1216   employs variance-based discount scheduling to mitigate reward overestimation issues specific to
1217   offline Preference-based RL.
1218
       A smaller discount factor serves a dual purpose: it regulates optimality against sample efficiency
1219
       trade-offs (Hu et al., 2022) and aligns with model-based pessimism principles, ensuring robust policy
1220   learning. Conversely, multiplicative adjustments to rewards lack theoretical grounding and often
1221   yield suboptimal performance, as evidenced in Table 9.
