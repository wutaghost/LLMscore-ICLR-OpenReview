011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Collaborative perception (CP) is a promising method for safe connected and au-
014            tonomous driving, which enables multiple connected and autonomous vehicles
015            (CAVs) to share sensing information with each other to enhance perception per-
016
               formance. For example, occluded objects can be detected, and the sensing range
               can be extended. However, compared with single-agent perception, the open-
017
               ness of a CP system makes it more vulnerable to malicious agents and attack-
018
               ers, who can inject malicious information to mislead the perception of an ego
019            CAV, resulting in severe risks for the safety of autonomous driving systems. To
020            mitigate the vulnerability of CP systems, we first propose a new paradigm for
021            malicious agent detection that effectively identifies malicious agents at the fea-
022            ture level without requiring verification of final perception results, significantly
023            reducing computational overhead. Building on this paradigm, we introduce CP-
024            GuardBench, the first comprehensive dataset provided to train and evaluate vari-
025            ous malicious agent detection methods for CP systems. Furthermore, we develop
026            a robust defense method called CP-Guard+, which enhances the margin between
027
               the representations of benign and malicious features through a carefully designed
               mixed contrastive training strategy. Finally, we conduct extensive experiments on
028
               both CP-GuardBench and V2X-Sim, and the results demonstrate the superiority
029
               of CP-Guard+.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      The development of collaborative perception (CP) has been driven by the increasing demand for
034
      accurate and reliable perception in autonomous driving systems (Chen et al., 2019b;a; Li et al.,
035
      2022; Hu et al., 2024d;a; 2023; Fang et al., Aug. 2024; Xu et al., 2022). Single-agent perception
036   systems, which rely solely on the onboard sensors of a single CAV, are restricted by limited sensing
037   range and occlusion. On the contrary, CP systems incorporate multiple CAVs to collaboratively
038   capture their surrounding environments. Specifically, The CAVs in a CP system can be divided
039   into two categories: the ego CAV and helping CAVs. The helping CAVs send complementary
040   sensing information (most methods send intermediate features) to the ego CAV, and the ego CAV
041   then leverages this complementary information to enhance its perception performance (Balkus et al.,
042   2022; Han et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2024c; Wang et al., 2020). For example, the ego CAV can detect
043   occluded objects and extend the sensing range after fusing the received information.
044   Despite the many advantages of CP outlined above, it also has several crucial drawbacks. Com-
045   pared to single-agent perception systems, CP is more vulnerable to security threats and easier to be
046   attacked, since it requires receiving and fusing information from other CAVs, which expands the at-
047   tack surface. In particular, malicious agents can directly send intermediate features with adversarial
048   perturbations to fool the ego CAV or a man-in-the-middle who can capture the intermediate feature
049   maps and manipulate them. Figure 1a illustrates the vulnerability of CP to malicious agents. In ad-
050
      dition, several attack methods have been designed to fool CP. For example, Tu et al. (Tu et al., 2021)
      developed a method to generate indistinguishable adversarial perturbations to attack the multi-agent
051
      communication in CP, which can severely degrade the perception performance.
052
053   The inability of the ego CAV to accurately detect and eliminate malicious agents from its collabora-
      tion network poses significant risks to CP, potentially resulting in compromised perception outcomes

      To address the security threats in CP, some previous works have investigated the defense mecha-
082
      nisms against malicious agents. For example, Li et al. (Li et al., 2023) leveraged random sample
083   consensus (RANSAC) to sample a subset of collaborators and calculate the intersection of union
084   (IoU) of the bounding boxes to verify whether there is any malicious agent among the collabo-
085   ration network. Zhao et al. (Zhao et al., 2024) designed a match loss and a reconstruction loss
086   as statistics to measure the consensus between the ego CAV and the collaborators. In addition, our
087   previous work, CP-Guard, which is currently under review, defends against malicious agents by iter-
088   atively checking the anomaly of the collaborative segmentation results from different collaborators.
089   However, these methods all follow a hypothesize-and-verify paradigm, which requires generating
090   multiple hypothetical perception results and verifying the consistency between the ego CAV and the
091
      collaborators. This process is computation-intensive and time-consuming, which hinders its scala-
      bility. This limitation prompts us to explore a new paradigm:
092
093
                       Is it feasible to detect malicious agents directly at the feature level?
094
095
      As illustrated in Figure 1b, the new paradigm shifts the focus to feature-level detection, eliminating
096   the need to generate multiple hypothetical perception results. This direct approach can significantly
097   reduce the computational overhead, thereby enhancing the efficiency of malicious agent detection in
098   CP systems.
099
      Although this idea is concise and appealing, there are still some challenges in realizing it. Firstly,
100
      to detect malicious agents at the feature level, we need to train a deep neural network (DNN) model
101
      on a large-scale dataset to help it learn the features of benign and malicious agents. However, there
102   is a lack of a benchmark dataset for feature-level malicious agent detection in CP systems. The ex-
103   isting datasets for CP, such as V2X-Sim (Li et al., 2022) and OPV2V (Xu et al., 2022), contain only
104   benign agents and do not include malicious agents. Therefore, it is difficult to train a robust DNN
105   model for malicious agent detection in CP systems without a well-annotated dataset. Secondly, in
106   CP scenarios, the environments are highly dynamic and complex, making it unrealizable to directly
107   use a classifier to classify the received intermediate features for detecting malicious agents. This is
      because dynamic environments will cause a high false-positive rate (FPR). Additionally, the adver-

      sarial perturbations are indistinguishable at the feature level, and the feature distribution of malicious
109   agents and benign agents are highly similar. These factors make it difficult to train a robust model
110   to distinguish malicious agents from benign agents.
111
112
      To address the aforementioned challenges, we first generate a new dataset, CP-GuardBench, which
      is the first dataset for malicious agent detection in CP systems. Then, we propose CP-Guard+, a
113
      robust malicious agent detection method for CP systems. CP-Guard+ can effectively detect mali-
114
      cious agents at the feature level without verifying the final perception results, significantly reducing
115   computational overhead and enhancing defense efficiency. Moreover, we design a mixed contrastive
116   training strategy to tackle the stealthy challenges and further enhance the robustness.
117
118
      In summary, we investigate the malicious agent detection problem in CP systems and propose
      a brand new paradigm, feature-level malicious agent detection. Additionally, we construct CP-
119
      GuardBench, the first benchmark for malicious agent detection in CP systems. Furthermore, we
120
      propose CP-Guard+, a robust malicious agent detection method with high robustness and computa-
121   tional efficiency. Finally, we conduct extensive experiments on CP-GuardBench and V2X-Sim, and
122   the results demonstrate the superiority of our CP-Guard+.

126   2.1   F ORMULATION OF C OLLABORATIVE P ERCEPTION
127
128
      In this section, we formulate collaborative perception and give the pipeline of our CP system. Specif-
      ically, Let X N denote the set of N CAVs in the CP system. CAVs in X can be divided into two
129
      categories: the ego CAV and helping CAVs. The ego CAV is the one that needs to perceive its
130
      surrounding environment, while helping CAVs are the ones that send their complementary sensing
131   information to the ego CAV to help it enhance its perception performance. Thus, each CAV can be
132   an ego one and helping one, depending on its role in a perception process. We assume that each
133   CAV is equipped with a feature encoder fencoder (·), a feature aggregator faggregator (·), and a feature
134   decoder fdecoder (·). For the i-th CAV in the set X , the raw observation is denoted as Oi (such as
135   camera images and LiDAR point clouds), and the final perception results are denoted as Yi . The
136   CP pipeline of the i-th CAV can be described as follows.
137
138
            1. Observation Encoding: Each CAV encodes its raw observation Oj into an initial feature
139
               map Fj = fencoder (Oj ), where j ∈ X N .
140         2. Intermediate Feature Transmission: Helping CAVs transmit their intermediate features to
141            the ego CAV: Fj→i = Γj→i (Fj ), j ∈ X N , j 6= i, where Γj→i (·) denotes a transmitter
142            that conveys the j-th CAV’s intermediate feature Fj to the ego CAV, while performing a
143            spatial transformation. Fj→i is the spatially aligned feature in the i-th CAV’s coordinate.
144         3. Feature Aggregation: The ego CAV receives all the intermediate features and fuses them
145            into a unified observational feature Ffused = faggregator (F0→i , {Fj→i }j6=i, j∈X N ).
146         4. Perception Decoding: Finally, the ego CAV decodes the unified observational feature
147            Ffused into the final perception results Y = fdecoder (Ffused ).

194
      To facilitate feature-level malicious agent detection in CP systems, we propose to develop CP-
195
      GuardBench, the first benchmark for malicious agent detection in CP systems. It provides a com-
196   prehensive dataset for training and evaluating malicious agent detection methods. In this section, we
197   will introduce the details of CP-GuardBench, including the automatic data generation and annotation
198   pipeline in Section 3.1, and the data visualization and statistics in Section 3.2.
199
200   3.1   AUTOMATIC DATA G ENERATION AND A NNOTATION
201
202   We build CP-GuardBench based on one of the most widely used datasets in the CP field, V2X-Sim
203   (Li et al., 2022), which is a comprehensive simulated multi-agent perception dataset for V2X-aided
204   autonomous driving. In this section, we introduce the automatic data generation and annotation
205   pipeline of CP-GuardBench. The pipeline is shown in Figure 2. It consists of three steps: 1) inter-
206
      mediate feature generation, 2) attack implementation, and 3) pair generation and saving.
207   Specifically, we firstly train a robust LiDAR collaborative object detector, which consists of a con-
208   volutional backbone, a convolutional decoder, and a prediction head for classification and regression
209   (Luo et al., 2018). As for the fusion method, we adopt mean fusion method to fuse the intermediate
210   features from different collaborators. Subsequently, the backbone is retained for extracting interme-
211   diate features, which are then transmitted and utilized by an ego CAV as supplementary information.
212   Secondly, the attacks are implemented and applied to the intermediate features. The detection head
213   and decoder are then frozen to generate the attacked detection results and optimize the adversarial
214   perturbations. As shown in Figure 2, several iterations are required to optimize the perturbations, and
215   the loss function differs for different attack types. In our CP-GuardBench, we consider five types of
      attacks, including Projected Gradient Descent (PGD) (Madry et al., 2018), Carini & Wagner (C&W)

267   agents, it may not always accurately estimate the latent distribution, particularly when the input is
268   too noisy to perform effective dimension reduction. For instance, object detectors often have feature
269   maps that include complex information from both the foreground objects and the noisy background
      before aggregation.

      5.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
353
      Dataset and Baselines. In our experiments, we consider two datasets: CP-GuardBench and V2X-
354   Sim (Li et al., 2022). We designate CAV #1 as the ego CAV and randomly select adversarial col-
355   laborators from the remaining CAVs. Additionally, we use ROBOSAC (Li et al., 2023) and MADE
356   (Zhao et al., 2024) as baselines, which are two state-of-the-art CP defense methods based on the
357   hypothesize-and-verify paradigm.
358
      Attack Settings. We assess different CP defense methods targeted at five attacks: PGD attack
359
      (Madry et al., 2018), C&W attack (Carlini & Wagner, 2017), BIM attack (Kurakin et al., 2017),
360   FGSM attack (Goodfellow et al., 2015), and GN attack. We set different perturbation sizes ∆ ∈
361   {0.1, 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 1.0}. The number of malicious attackers varies in {0, 1, 2} and all the attackers
362   are randomly assigned from the collaborators, where 0 attacker indicates an upper-bound case. For
363   PGD, BIM and C&W attacks, the number of iteration steps is 15 and the step size is 0.1.
364
      Implementation Details. The CP-Guard+ system is implemented using PyTorch, and we utilize the
365
      object detector described in Section 3.1. For each agent, the local LiDAR point cloud data is first
366   encoded into 32 × 32 bird’s eye view (BEV) feature maps with 256 channels prior to communica-
367   tion. For our CP-Guard+, we use ResNet-50 (He et al., 2016) as the backbone, and the training is
368   performed for 50 epochs with batch size 10 and learning rate 1 × 10−3 . Our experiments are con-
369   ducted on a server with 2 Intel(R) Xeon(R) Silver 4410Y CPUs (2.0GHz), 4 NVIDIA RTX A5000
370   GPUs, and 512 GB DDR4 RAM. For mixed contrastive training, we utilize the output of the fully
371   connected layers preceding the final output layer in the backbone to form a one-dimensional feature
372   vector for each agent, the dimension of which is 2048.
373   Evaluation Metrics. We use a variety of metrics to evaluate the performance of our CP-Guard+
374   model. For malicious agent detection on our CP-GuardBench dataset, we consider Accuracy, True
375   Positive Rate (TPR), False Positive Rate (FPR), Precision, and F1 Score. For CP defense on the
376   V2X-Sim dataset, we use metrics including average precision (AP) at IoU=0.5 and IoU=0.7. Ad-
377   ditionally, to assess the computation efficiency of different CP defense methods, we introduce the
      metric frames-per-second (FPS). The definition of the metrics are provided in Table 3 in Appendix.

      A     R ELATED W ORK
703
704
      A.1   C OLLABORATIVE P ERCEPTION
705
706   Collaborative Perception (CP) significantly extends the field-of-view (FoV) for individual agents,
707   thereby enhancing the comprehensiveness and accuracy of perception outcomes (Han et al., 2023;
708   Hu et al., 2024b). In CP systems, CAVs utilize various fusion methods tailored at distinct stages
709   of data processing. Early fusion at the raw data level and late fusion at the output level often re-
710   sult in either high communication loads or increased perceptual noise. Conversely, intermediate
711   fusion, which involves the transmission of intermediate features among CAVs, achieves an optimal
712   balance by minimizing communication overhead while maximizing perceptual accuracy. Based on
713   intermediate-level collaboration, recent progress in CP have addressed a wide array of challenges,
714
      including communication overhead (Fang et al., Aug. 2024; Tao et al., 2024), robustness (Lu et al.,
      2023), system heterogeneity (Lu et al., 2024), and domain generalization (Hu et al., 2023). Ro-
715
      bustness, in particular, has become a pivotal area of focus, tackling issues such as communication
716
      disruptions (Ren et al., 2024), pose noise correction (Lu et al., 2023), and system latency (Lei et al.,
717   2022). Despite comprehensive research, the vulnerability of these systems to malicious attacks has
718   not been adequately addressed. In this paper, we delve into the robustness of CP systems, specifi-
719   cally considering the impact of malicious agents, and propose strategies to enhance system security
720   and integrity.
721
722
      A.2   A DVERSARIAL C OLLABORATIVE P ERCEPTION
723
724   Adversarial attacks on single-vehicle perception systems typically utilize methods such as GPS
725   spoofing (Li et al., 2021), LiDAR spoofing (Hallyburton et al., 2022), and deploying physically
726   realizable adversarial objects (Tu et al., 2020). However, in multi-vehicle collaborative perception,
727   adversarial strategies differ markedly across collaboration stages. For early-stage collaborative per-
728   ception, Zhang et al. (Zhang et al., 2024) have identified attacks that involve object spoofing and
729   removal, exploiting vulnerabilities through simulated object presence or absence and advanced re-
730
      construction of LiDAR point clouds. Conversely, late-stage collaboration, which mainly involves
      sharing object locations (Schiegg et al., 2020), offers adversaries opportunities to manipulate these
731
      data points. Attacks at the intermediate stage are more complex, typically requiring white-box ac-
732
      cess to perception models. Such access allows attackers to precisely manipulate system outputs,
733   though these systems are generally less vulnerable to simple black-box strategies like ray-casting
734   due to the protective nature of benign feature maps that diminish the impact of these attacks. Pio-
735   neering work by Tu et al. (Tu et al., 2021) introduced untargeted adversarial attacks aimed at gener-
736   ating inaccurate detection bounding boxes by altering feature maps in intermediate-fusion systems.
737   Building on this, Zhang et al.(Zhang et al., 2024) have enhanced these techniques by incorporating
738   perturbation initialization and feature map masking, enabling more realistic and targeted attacks in
739   real-time scenarios. Our research focuses on identifying and mitigating adversarial threats within
740   the intermediate-level collaborative perception framework to bolster system resilience against these
741
      sophisticated attacks.
742
743   A.3   D EFENSIVE C OLLABORATIVE P ERCEPTION
744
745   To enhance the resilience of intermediate-level CP against adversarial threats, contemporary re-
746   search has primarily focused on the detection of malicious agents at the output level. Li et al. (Li
747
      et al., 2023) developed the Robust Collaborative Sampling Consensus (ROBOSAC) method that se-
      lects a random subset of collaborators for consensus verification. Additionally, Zhao et al. (Zhao
