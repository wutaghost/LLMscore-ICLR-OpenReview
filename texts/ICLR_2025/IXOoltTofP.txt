                                                     A BSTRACT
010
011
                  Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs) exhibit impressive capabilities
012               across a variety of tasks, especially when equipped with carefully designed vi-
013               sual prompts. However, existing studies primarily focus on logical reasoning and
014               visual understanding, while the capability of MLLMs to operate effectively in 3D
015               vision remains an ongoing area of exploration. In this paper, we introduce a novel
016               visual prompting method, called 3DAxisPrompt, to elicit 3D understanding capa-
017               bilities of MLLMs in real-world scenes. More specifically, our method leverages
018               the 3D coordinate axis and masks generated from the Segment Anything Model
019               (SAM) to provide explicit geometric priors to MLLMs and then extend their im-
020
                  pressive 2D grounding/reasoning ability to real-world 3D scenarios. Besides, we
                  also provide a thorough investigation of the potential visual prompting formats
021
                  and conclude our findings to reveal the potential and limits of 3D understand-
022
                  ing capabilities in GPT-4o. Finally, we build evaluation environments with four
023               datasets, i.e. ShapeNet, ScanNet, FMB, and nuScene datasets, covering various
024               3D tasks. Based on this, we conduct extensive quantitative and qualitative ex-
025               periments, which demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed method. Overall,
026               our study reveals that GPT-4o, with the help of 3DAxisPrompt, can effectively
027               perceive an object’s 3D position in real-world scenarios. Nevertheless, a single
028               prompt engineering approach does not consistently achieve the best outcomes for
029               all 3D tasks. This study highlights the feasibility of leveraging MLLMs for 3D
030               vision grounding/reasoning with prompt engineering techniques.

      1       I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      In recent years, significant advancements and breakthroughs have been made in large language mod-
035
      els (LLMs) (Brown et al., 2020; Chowdhery et al., 2022; Touvron et al., 2023; OpenAI et al., 2024).
036   By aligning the representations with visual (and other) encoders, LLMs have been extended to multi-
037   modal large language models (MLLMs1 ) (GeminiTeam, 2024; OpenAI, 2024), which are capable of
038   handling richer visual modalities. These studies have attracted significant interest from researchers,
039   with numerous works continuously being proposed to enhance the reasoning capabilities of MLLMs
040   in various aspects. For example, Yang et al. (2023a) leverage the SoM prompting to enable the vi-
041   sual grounding of GPT-4v, and Wu et al. (2024b) achieve accurate object detection with MLLMs in
042   a Chain-of-Thought (Wei et al., 2023) manner. By leveraging the advanced reasoning capabilities
043   of the language model component, MLLMs have been explored for perception and interaction with
044
      a variety of applications.
045   However, existing MLLMs are mainly pretrained with 1D data (e.g. texts) and 2D data (e.g. images),
046   while the real-world challenges are inherently spatial and require spatial grounding in the context of
047   3D scenes. In this context, a critical question emerges:
048       Do vision-language-based MLLMs possess the capability for 3D grounding and reasoning?
049
      Although lots of studies have explored the application of MLLMs in 3D scenarios, these works
050
      have not directly leveraged the 3D grounding and reasoning capabilities of MLLMs. For example,
051   some work (Wen et al., 2024; Cui et al., 2024) apply MLLMs in the field of autonomous driving,
052   however, they primarily leverage MLLMs for decision-making rather than 3D scene understanding.

      Besides, PointLLM (Xu et al., 2023) empowers MLLMs to understand 3D point clouds with ad-
055   ditional point-text instruction training. Although this solution aligns high-level representations of
056   points and texts, it only supports specific comprehension tasks (e.g. classification and captioning for
057   single 3D objects) and does not truly activate the fine-grained 3D perception capabilities of MLLMs.
058   Overall, existing studies do not fully answer the question we raised, and further in-depth exploration
059   is required.
060
      In this paper, we aim to investigate how to extend the exceptional 1D/2D grounding and reasoning
061   capabilities of MLLMs into the 3D world space without further fine-tuning. Based on this require-
062   ment and the inspiration of visual prompts, e.g. (Yang et al., 2023a; Wu et al., 2024b), we propose
063   a new prompting mechanism, called 3DAxisPrompt. Specifically, given the point cloud of a real
064   scene, we first embed the 3D coordinate axis and meshes in this scene in an automatic manner to
065   provide the 3D geometric priors. Then calibrated scene will be rendered into observation images
066   from different angles. Furthermore, to introduce object-level semantic cues, we overlay the masks
067   generated by the Segment Anything Model (SAM) (Kirillov et al., 2023) with numerical or alpha-
068   betic marks, similar to SoM (Yang et al., 2023a). In this way, we can extend MLLMs impressive 2D
069
      grounding/reasoning capabilities to real-world 3D scenarios.
070   Besides, for the first time, we present a comprehensive exploration of potential visual prompt for-
071   mats, such as coordinate axis, masks, bounding boxes, marks, color highlights, etc., in MLLMs
072   for 3D understanding. Based on our investigation, we also conclude some findings to reveal the
073   potential and limits of 3D understanding capabilities in MLLMs. For example, multi-view visual
074   prompting cannot directly activate the 3D reasoning capabilities of MLLMs, but tri-view prompting
075
      can. Finally, we construct evaluation environments using four datasets—ShapeNet, ScanNet, FMB,
      and nuScene—covering a range of 3D tasks. We then conduct extensive quantitative and qualitative
076
      experiments, demonstrating the effectiveness of the proposed approach.
077
078   Overall, our objective is not to achieve perfect zero-shot performance with GPT-4o, but to explore
079   its limitations and potential in zero-shot inference for 3D grounding/reasoning. We expect that
080   future improvements to the MLLMs will lead to further quantitative gains on the actual tasks. To
081
      summarize, our main contributions are:
082

096   LLMs and MLLMs. Significant progress has been witnessed in LLMs (Chowdhery et al., 2022;
097   Touvron et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2022; OpenAI et al., 2024). Trained on internet-scale data, LLMs
098   are effective commonsense reasoners (Zhao et al., 2023). MLLMs (Liu et al., 2023a; Lu et al.,
099
      2024; Bai et al., 2023) integrate vision encoders (Radford et al., 2021) into LLMs, allowing them
      to reason over visual input directly. State-of-the-art MLMMs like GPT-4V, Gemini (GeminiTeam,
100
      2024), Claude (The), and GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024) have excelled in general vision-language tasks
101
      (Wu et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2023c; Fu et al., 2023). Leveraging the advanced vision-language
102   reasoning ability, the exploration has been made of MLMMs in perception and interacting with the
103   physical world (Lu et al., 2024), including autonomous driving (Wen et al., 2024; Cui et al., 2024),
104   anomaly detection (Cao et al., 2023), robotic control and learning (Collaboration et al., 2024; Brohan
105   et al., 2023), which requires fine-grained 3D spatial grounding that remains to be explored (Chen
106   et al., 2024). To promote the connection of MLMMs to the real physical world (Chen et al., 2024),
107   we aim to find a strategic prompting method to elicit and promote the 3D grounding and reasoning
      in MLMMs regarding a real 3D world, such as to reason about the 3D location of an object.

      Visual prompting. Prompt engineering has emerged as a promising approach to improve MLLMs
109   across multiple domains, such as in-context learning (Brown et al., 2020; Dong et al., 2024), Chain-
110   of-Thought and Tree-of-Thought (Wei et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2023). Consequently, numerous
111   prompting methods have been developed to improve visual grounding in MLLMs. Colorful prompt-
112   ing tuning (CPT) (Yao et al., 2022) overlays color-based co-referential markers in both images and
113   text and enables strong few-shot and even zero-shot visual grounding capabilities. RedCircle (Sht-
114   edritski et al., 2023) guides the vision model to an enclosed region by adding a red circle. Blur Re-
115   verse Mask (Yang et al., 2023b) blurs the area outside the target mask to leverage the precise mask
116   annotations to reduce focus on weakly related regions while retaining spatial coherence. These two
117
      methods promote fine-grained visual grounding. Furthermore, Lei et al. (2024) enhances the vision-
      language coordination by SCAFFOLD prompting that scaffolds coordinate on images. Set-of-Mark
118
      (Yang et al., 2023a) add a set of visual marks on top of image regions. Both these two methods indi-
119
      cate the emergent 2D spatial grounding (Mitra et al., 2024; Islam et al., 2023) in MLLMs, including
120   2D position and relation inference. To enhance the 3D spatial grounding, Nasiriany et al. (2024) pro-
121   pose an iterative prompting method (PIVOT) to infer the robot action considering spatial relation.
122   COARSE CORRESPONDENCES (Liu et al., 2024) prompts the MLLMs to elicit the 3D spacetime
123   understanding. These two methods concentrate on 3D spatial relation instead of 3D spatial position,
124   showing limited performance in instance-level tasks that demand precise 3D localization and recog-
125   nition. Our study strives to extend the 2D spatial grounding (Lei et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2023a)
126   to 3D grounding by formulating a visual prompting method, promoting spatial position inference in
127   MLLMs.
128   GPTs and grounding. Generative Pretrained Transformers (GPTs) (Brown et al., 2020; OpenAI
129   et al., 2024) have led to a breakthrough in the realm of natural language processing. As a leading
130   LMM, GPT-4V has significantly expanded the boundaries of MLLMs capabilities and shown abil-
131   ities to understand visual annotations (Yang et al., 2023c) and solve visual reasoning tasks, such as
132   web navigation (Yan et al., 2023a; Zheng et al., 2024), autonomous driving (Wen et al., 2024; Cui
133   et al., 2024), and medicine diagnostics (Yan et al., 2023b; Liu et al., 2023b). Furthermore, GPT-4o
134
      (OpenAI, 2024) is the latest development in a string of innovations to MLLMs, which has shown
      significant performance in multiple tasks (Joe et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024a; Shahriar et al., 2024;
135
      Hu et al., 2024). Our study is to explore a prompting method to promote the 3D spatial ground-
136
      ing in MLLMs. Since GPT-4V is proven to outperform the other models in visual grounding when
137   equipped with visual prompts (Yang et al., 2023a) and GPT-4o shows significant improvement in
138   3D spacetime understanding (Liu et al., 2024), we believe the GPT-4o can present representative 3D
139   spatial grounding abilities in MLLMs and conduct our experiments and analysis using the GPT-4o.

      3     3DA XIS P ROMPT
143
144
      Unlike previous 2D visual prompts that primarily focus on planar object relationships, we aim to
      introduce a 3D prompts method to enable effective 3D spatial reasoning and grounding in GPT-4o for
145
      real 3D environments. In this section, We revisit approaches for incorporating 3D information into
146
      visual prompts and propose an effective method 3DAxisPrompt, to enhance 3D spatial information
147   through visual prompts.

205   Visual prompts, such as marks (Yang et al., 2023a; Liu et al., 2024), masks (Yang et al., 2023b),
206   colors (Yao et al., 2022), scaffolding points (Lei et al., 2024), arrows (Nasiriany et al., 2024), and
207   red circles (Shtedritski et al., 2023), have been shown to provoke 2D spatial grounding in GPT-4o.
208   These visual prompts can be seen as integrating spatial information into images for grounding in
209   image-text pairs (Brown et al., 2020; Li et al., 2022), leading to 2D spatial grounding. To extend 2D
210   spatial grounding to 3D space, we propose encoding additional 3D cues into observation images to
211   trigger 3D perception in GPT-4o. Based on this, we explore effective methods for representing these
212
      3D cues.
213   3D axis integration in scenes. We found that adding a 3D axis to the point cloud of a 3D instance
214   and rendering observation images with the x, y, and z axes as visual prompts enables GPT-4o
215   to reason about 3D positions, as shown in Figure 2. This approach allows GPT-4o to associate
      semantics with spatial locations defined by the 3D axis, thereby facilitating 3D spatial grounding.

239   3D coordinates information. Based on our findings during depth compensation, we believe that
240   encoding all 3D information solely within visual prompts is overly challenging (Liu et al., 2024).
241   Additional 3D cues are necessary beyond just visual prompts. Furthermore, we discovered that
242   GPT-4o can recognize point clouds formatted as coordinates in the input text, as demonstrated in
243   Appendix A. However, when these points are combined with a 3D Axis visual prompt, GPT-4o
244
      effectively incorporates them for reasoning about 3D spatial positions. Consequently, we consider
      the point cloud in text format to be an essential input for the model.

246   Multiview and tri-view images. Inspired by Structure from Motion (SFM) (Schönberger & Frahm,
247   2016), which can reconstruct 3D structures from a series of 2D images, and tri-plane methods (Shue

249   gate the multiview and tri-view images of an actual scene. As shown in Figure 2, we render the
250
      images with the 3D axis of the actual scene from different angles. Additional results are provided
      in Appendix A1. Our findings indicate that the multi-view image sequence can only trigger 3D
251
      spatial grounding in GPT-4o when combined with text-formatted point clouds. In contrast, the tri-
252
      view images successfully provoke 3D spatial grounding in GPT-4o even without the text-formatted
253   point cloud input. However, when reasoning about complex scenes, tri-view encounters significant
254   occlusion issues, leading to considerable inaccuracies.

      4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
300
      Implementation. Our method does not require model training. However, due to the limited and
301   costly GPT-4o API quota, we must exhaustively send 3DAxisPrompt-augmented images to the Chat-
302   GPT interface. To efficiently manage experiments and evaluations, we employ a divide-and-conquer
303   strategy, opening a new chat window for each scene to prevent context leakage. All reported results
304   are obtained in a zero-shot manner.
305
      Benchmarks. Given the limited GPT-4o quota, we could not fully evaluate the validation set for
306
      each task. Instead, we randomly selected 20 scenes from each test dataset as validation data. We
307   aimed to cover as many diverse scenes as possible across all datasets to preserve their original
308   diversity. For each instance, we applied the 3DAxisPrompt to the observation images of the point
309   cloud using our custom toolbox.

463   5   D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
464
465
      Where dose the 3D spatial grounding comes from? Our understanding is derived from experi-
466
      mental observations. We hypothesize that the 3D Axis offers essential scale information and spatial
467
      cues that serve as a foundation for localization. Interestingly, even without the 3DAxisPrompt, GPT-
468   4o can make rough estimates of distances between objects when provided with an observation image
469   of a real scene. However, by incorporating the 3D Axis, these estimates become more precise, as the
470   axis ticks unify the units of measurement, allowing for a more accurate perception of distance. Ad-
471   ditionally, the axis origin and direction act as reference points, supporting the localization process.
472   In this way, the 3DAxisPrompt reinforces 3D spatial grounding by offering crucial 3D cues.
473
      The essential factors in 3DAxisPrompt. The axis ticks and the highlighted contour of an object
474   in the observation images are essential in 3DAxisPrompt. More specifically, the axis ticks provide
475   an essential ruler to measure the world, while the contours marked in the observation images can
476   significantly improve the localization performance. Also, we find that the localization performance
477   can be further enhanced if given the precise coordinates of the objects (reference points) around the
478   queried one. We think this is the same as human perception; the additional reference point makes
479   the coordinate easier to read.
480   Conclusion. In this paper, we propose a visual prompt scheme called 3DAxisPrompt for MLLMs,
481   particularly GPT-4o, aimed at enhancing 3D spatial grounding. By overlaying visible 3D axis,
482   markers, and region edges on observation images from different angles, 3DAxisPrompt enables
483   tasks like localization and spatial reasoning. Our study shows how various 3D visual prompts help
484   GPT-4o interpret 3D space, with qualitative results indicating fine-grained perception and reasoning
485   in real-world scenarios. We hope this work inspires future research on applying MLLMs to real-
      world interactions and advancing AI in everyday life.
