                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               It is common to reject undesired outputs of Large Language Models (LLMs);
010
               however, current methods to do so require an excessive amount of computation,
011
               or severely distort the distribution of outputs. We present a method to balance the
012            distortion of the output distribution with computational efficiency, allowing for the
013            generation of long sequences of text with difficult-to-satisfy constraints, with less
014            amplification of low probability outputs compared to existing methods. We show
015            through a series of experiments that the task-specific performance of our method
016            is comparable to methods that do not distort the output distribution, while being
017            much more computationally efficient.
018
019
020   1     I NTRODUCTION
021
022   Large Language Models (LLMs) are able to perform many complex text manipulation tasks, and
023   embody an incredible amount of world knowledge, but their output is unpredictable. Language
024
      models sometimes generate undesirable outputs, such as syntactically-incorrect code, hallucinated
      PII, or profanity, rendering their use potentially unsafe for certain applications. For example, if
025
      the LLM is used as part of a larger automated system, where its output must conform to a specific
026
      format, and it may have a set of tools which it may invoke. Many undesirable outputs, or deviations
027   from an expected format, which we collectively refer to as errors or constraint violations for the
028   remainder of the paper, can be detected with incremental parsers, regular expression matching, or
029   even simple substring searches.
030
      Each individual task that a LLM is used for may have a unique set of constraints. However, re-
031
      training a LLM to accommodate the constraints of every task is expensive, and may still not fully
032
      protect against violations. Therefore, the community has developed several methods that attempt to
033   mitigate constraint violations without the need to retrain the language model. However, a practical
034   method that does not deviate a lot from the original output distributions is still needed.
035
036
      Our contributions are as follows. First, we analyze several existing methods for avoiding constraint
      violations in text generated from autoregressive language models, and compare the strengths and
037
      weaknesses of each method. Second, we present a method that allows for a useful midpoint in
038
      the tradeoff between computational efficiency and maintenance of the output distribution, without
039   the need for any additional training or fine-tuning step. Finally, we run a series of experiments
040   showing that our method obtains excellent task-specific performance on both synthetic and real-
041   world domains, without introducing an unreasonable level of inference overhead.
042
043   1.1   R ELATED W ORK
044
045   Language models based on a Transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2023) have steadily become
046   more popular with increased parameter counts, with consumer chatbot products such as OpenAI
047   ChatGPT (OpenAI, 2024a) and Anthropic Claude (Anthropic, 2024), or code generation tools such
048   as GitHub Copilot (Github, Inc., 2023) and Amazon Q Developer (AWS, Inc., 2024).
049   While such tools often use RLHF (Kaufmann et al., 2024) to fine-tune for safety and helpfulness,
050   several have introduced features such as generation according to a schema (OpenAI, 2024b). For
051   those willing to run local inference on a language model, however, there are a vast array of tools
052   for constraining the output of a model to follow a template (Microsoft, 2023b; Sengottuvelu, 2023;
053   Automorphic, 2023; Microsoft, 2023a; SRI, 2023; Athiwaratkun et al., 2024), produce syntactically
      valid code (Jones, 2023; Slatton, 2023; Willard & Louf, 2023; Takerngsaksiri et al., 2023; Melcer


                                                        1

054
      et al., 2024), or conform to exotic poetry constraints (Roush et al., 2023). However, these works
055   almost universally use constrained generation to achieve this outcome. As we will discuss in the rest
056   of this paper, there are several additional ways to control the output of a LLM.
057
058
      One set of methods, constrained generation (Beurer-Kellner et al., 2024; Geng et al., 2024; Melcer
      et al., 2024), avoids errors by disabling the generation of any token that immediately leads to such
059
      an error. While this method is effective, it can lead to the amplification of low-probability outputs.
060
061   Another class of methods avoids errors without any amplification of low-probability outputs, at
062   the cost of additional computation. Rejection sampling is the simplest such method; i.e. if the
063   output contains an error, simply generate another sample until the output is acceptable. Adaptive
064
      Sampling with Approximate Expected Futures (ASAp) (Park et al., 2024) provides a performance
      improvement over rejection sampling while maintaining the output distribution by effectively sam-
065
      pling without replacement, but there are still many situations in which it may converge too slowly.
066
      A third class of methods (Yang & Klein, 2021; Lew et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024), avoids errors
067   by estimating the posterior probability of an error occurring for a given prefix, and decreasing the
068   probability of generating prefixes that are more likely to lead to an error. These methods are usually
069   able to quickly generate a sample with little amplification of low-probability outputs, but rely on
070   being able to accurately estimate the posterior probability of an error.
071
      Even when not controlling the output of a LLM, their autoregressive nature can lead to high in-
072
      ference latency. One method to combat this, Speculative Decoding (Leviathan et al., 2023; Miao
073   et al., 2024), reduces latency by transforming the inherently sequential generation problem into a
074   parallelizable verification problem, at the expense of potentially wasting some computation. Several
075   extensions such as Medusa (Cai et al., 2024) and EAGLE (Li et al., 2024a;b) have improved the la-
076   tency and efficiency of speculative decoding, and a variant, Mentored Decoding (Tran-Thien, 2024)
077   further increases the speed of speculative decoding by allowing for some deviation from the LLM’s
078   probability distribution.

226

232   3.3   E XISTING A PPROACHES : P OSTERIOR E STIMATION
233
234   We note three additional methods that, although they use very different formalizations and
235   implementations from each other, rely on a similar core idea to approximately sample from
         B
236   P̂
      P.        In all cases, for any given prefix x1...n , these methods create an estimator of
237       xn+1...m ∈Σ∗ P (xn+1...m |x1...n ) × 1x1...m ∈B ; i.e. the likelihood of an error in all sequences that
238   begin a specific prefix, weighted by the probability of generating each sequence. This posterior
239   probability estimation is used to sample from P̂ B . The difference between each method lies in how
240   they each perform the posterior estimation:
241
      FUDGE (Yang & Klein, 2021) involves training a discriminator, usually a neural network or com-
242   bination of several networks, to directly estimate this probability. SMC Steering (Lew et al., 2023)
243   creates this estimate using Monte Carlo sampling. This method additionally incorporates optimiza-
244   tions such as sampling without replacement, and aggressive pruning of low-probability branches. In
245   contrast, Ctrl-G (Zhang et al., 2024) first distills a LLM into a Hidden Markov Model (HMM) with
246   a tractable number of states (thousands or tens of thousands). If the constraint can be expressed as a
247   Deterministic Finite Automaton (DFA) over tokens, Ctrl-G takes the product of the DFA and HMM,
248   and then calculates the probability of an error in this product system.
249   While these methods exhibit impressive results on many tasks, they may face issues in domains
250   where the posterior probability is close to 1, or where the probability has little to do with the content
251   of the prefix itself. We further discuss considerations for choosing a specific method in Section 6.1.

      While Section 4.2 shows that our method performs well in a simulated domain, the following exper-

      5.1   L IPOGRAMS (T EXT G ENERATION WITH L ETTER E XCLUSIONS )
351
      It is common in poetry or creative writing exercises to write text without using a specific letter; a
352
      product of this exercise is called a lipogram. Lipograms where the excluded letter is a vowel tend to
353   be more difficult to create than with other letters. Large language models often fail at this task, and
354   more generally, most tasks dependent on individual letters rather than entire tokens.
355
356
      We use Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2 (Jiang et al., 2023) to generate lipograms with vowels as the ex-
      cluded letter. We prompt the LLM to perform one of five simple tasks (detailed in Appendix A).
357
      Each task is appended to instructions to avoid using one of the five vowels, resulting in 25 prompts.
358
359   For each prompt, we generate a completion with four sampling methods: unconstrained generation,
360   constrained generation, ASAp, and AprAD, for up to 200 tokens. If the process reaches 2000 model
361   invocations, generation is interrupted, and the last sequence before an error was detected is returned.
362   We then randomized the generations, hid the labels of which generation correspond to each method,
363   and asked human raters to score each completion on quality, regardless of if the constraint was
364   followed, on a scale of 1-5. If the forbidden letter is detected in the output, we then mark the
365   constraint as violated. Otherwise, we also ask the human raters to decide if the output violates
366   the intent of the constraint; i.e. by answering in a foreign language, adding unnecessary accents,
367   swapping for Cyrillic lookalike characters, or misspelling words to avoid the vowel. Additional
368
      information about the rating process is provided in Appendix A.
369   The results of this evaluation are provided in Table 2, and a representative sample of the outputs are
370   provided in Figure 2. All outputs and rater scores are included in the supplemental material, and

372   As shown by these results, AprAD consistently produces high-quality outputs, nearly matching
373   the readability of unconstrained generation. Additionally, it consistently follows the intent of a

      6     D ISCUSSION
458
459
460   As introduced in Section 3.3, there are several methods to control the output of a LLM based on
461   estimating the posterior probability of constraint violation; we collectively term these posterior
462
      estimation-based techniques. In contrast, AprAD, as well as ASAp and constrained generation, are
      sampling-based techniques. A high-level overview of each method is presented in Table 4.
463
464
465   6.1      P OSTERIOR E STIMATION - BASED M ETHODS
466
467   While posterior estimation-based techniques excel at many tasks, they tend to struggle when the
468   probability of a constraint violation does not necessarily depend on a given text prefix. For example,
469   the probability of a LLM generating text without the letter ‘e’ is close to 0 regardless of if the prefix
470   is “Long ago”, or if the prefix is “In a galaxy far away,” as the probability of generating a specific
471   vowel mostly depends on the arbitrary behavior of a language model. It is unlikely that a learned
472   discriminator or a HMM would capture this specific behavior, and it would require an extraordinary
473   number of Monte Carlo samples to accurately calculate the posterior probability.
474   In contrast, during code generation, the posterior probability of generating a hallucinated method
475   name may depend on the prefix text, and so a practitioner may wish to consider a posterior
476   estimation-based method. For example, a misleading comment that mentions a specific method
477   all but ensures that this method will be generated on the next line. If FUDGE is able to learn a
478   discriminator to predict hallucinated methods, it would be possible to use that method to control the
479   generation. SMC Steering would work as well, but may require a large amount of computation, even
480   on tasks with a relatively sparse constraint. However, we note that it would be difficult to represent
481
      a constraint on hallucinated method names as a DFA for use with Ctrl-G.

500   6.2     A S PECTRUM OF S AMPLING - BASED M ETHODS
501
502   Sampling-based methods are able to generate text that does not violate a constraint, even in domains
503   where it is difficult to obtain an accurate estimate of the posterior probability. The choice of specific
504   method depends on the user’s desired tradeoff between computational overhead, and conformance
505   to the LLM’s original distribution. As our experiments show, a mild deviation from the LLM’s
506
      distribution is not fatal to generation quality, but it should be kept to a manageable level.
507   As stated previously, AprAD lies at a midpoint between ASAp and constrained generation. We
508   observe that all three algorithms may be characterized as one algorithm, parameterized by its back-
509   tracking behavior (Appendix C). However, a user may wish to obtain behavior with slightly lower
510   overhead than AprAD, or with greater conformance to the LLM’s distribution, without moving all
511   the way to either extreme of ASAp or constrained decoding. In this case, it may be possible to
512
      introduce a hyperparameter h to AprAD.
