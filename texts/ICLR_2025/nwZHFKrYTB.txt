                                                         A BSTRACT
010
011
                   We study continued training and supervised-fine-tuning (SFT) of a language model
012                (LM) to make effective use of long-context information. We first establish a reliable
013                evaluation protocol to guide model development—instead of perplexity, we use a
014                broad set of long-context tasks, and we evaluate models after supervised fine-tuning
015                (SFT) with instruction data as this better reveals long-context abilities. Supported
016                by our robust evaluations, we run thorough experiments to decide the data mix
017                for continued pre-training, the instruction tuning dataset, and other design choices
018                such as position extrapolation. We find that (1) code repositories and books are
019                excellent sources of long data, but it is crucial to combine them with high-quality
020
                   short data; (2) training with a sequence length beyond the evaluation length boosts
                   long-context performance; (3) for SFT, using only short instruction datasets yields
021
                   strong performance on long-context tasks. Our final model, ProLong-8B, which
022
                   is initialized from Llama-3 and trained on 40B tokens, demonstrates state-of-the-
023                art long-context performance among similarly sized models at a length of 128K,
024                outperforming Llama-3.1-8B on the majority of long-context tasks despite having
025                seen 5% as many tokens during long-context training. Additionally, ProLong can
026                effectively process up to 512K tokens, one of the longest context windows of
027                publicly available LMs.1
028
029
030       Takeaways for continued training of long-context models
031
032       • Evaluation (§2): We target a range of long-context downstream tasks instead of perplexity
033         or needle-in-a-haystack, while checking if the short-context performance is preserved. We
034
            evaluate models after SFT, which produces a clearer signal on long-context tasks.
035       • Data engineering (§3): We conduct a series of ablations at a 5B-token scale. We find
036         that using code repositories and long books as long-context data and mixing them with
037         high-quality short-context data is crucial for both long-context performance and retaining
038
            the short-context capabilities of the pre-trained model.
039       • Scaling the data and the length (§4): We scale up the training to 20B tokens at a 64K
040         training length and 20B tokens at a 512K training length. Surprisingly, training on contexts
041         longer than the evaluation length yields additional benefits.
042       • Supervised fine-tuning (§5): We find that SFT with standard, short-context instruction
043         datasets is sufficient for achieving good performance. Contrary to previous study, long
044         synthetic instruction data does not boost the result in our setting.
045       • ProLong models (§6): We present our final recipe and evaluation results here. All our
046         code, data, and models will be made publically available.

049   1       I NTRODUCTION
050
051   The ability of language models (LMs) to process extremely long inputs (for example, 128K tokens)
052   has enabled new applications, such as book summarization or learning new tasks on the fly from many
053

              All our code, data, and models will be made publically available.

054
      examples. However, adapting LMs to process long contexts is challenging from an infrastructure and
055   data perspective, and many design decisions are not well understood by open-source practitioners.
056
057   While many works have focused on extending the context length of pre-trained LMs with minimal
058
      training (Chen et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2024), Fu et al. (2024) find that the above methods cannot
      even perform the simple needle-in-a-haystack (NIAH; Kamradt, 2024) task and it is necessary to
059
      continually train the LM on long documents for billions of tokens. Frontier open-source models,
060
      such as Llama-3.1 (Dubey et al., 2024) and Jamba (Lenz et al., 2024), also adopts a long-context
061   continued training stage, followed by supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on instruction data. We adopt the
062   same setting and study continued training and SFT of a pre-trained LM for effective long-context use.
063
064
      We first establish a reliable evaluation protocol to provide meaningful signal for model development.
      Most existing work relies on either perplexity or NIAH for ablating training recipes. We demonstrate
065
      that neither is robust for guiding the development and opt for a broad range of downstream applica-
066
      tions, such as retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), long-document summarization, and many-shot
067   in-context learning (ICL). We also conduct our evaluations after performing SFT, as we observe that,
068   on some long-context tasks, performance gains only emerge after SFT.
069
070
      Guided by our evaluation protocol, we run comprehensive experiments with Llama-3-8B (8K original
      context window; Dubey et al., 2024) to study each component of long-context continued training,
071
      including data mixture, data and length scaling, supervised fine-tuning, and many other design choices
072
      such as position extrapolation. Many of our findings are surprising or contradictory to existing claims,
073   for example, (1) training only on long data hurts long-context performance, (2) training on longer
074   sequences than the evaluation length helps, and (3) SFT on only short instruction data is sufficient for
075   good long-context performance. We outline our main takeaways and the structure of the paper in the
076   takeaway box at the beginning of this section.
077
      Our final model, ProLong, achieves the best performance at a 128K context length among 10B-
078
      parameter models, while taking only 5% of the data budget compared to Llama-3.1’s long-context
079   training (Dubey et al., 2024). ProLong has a maximum context length of 512K tokens, making it one
080   of the longest-context LMs available.2

      2       G UIDE M ODEL D EVELOPMENT W ITH M EANINGFUL E VALUATIONS
084
085
      A pre-requisite for training a strong LM is having a robust evaluation suite that can guide model
      development while tracking its utility in real-world applications. While synthetic benchmarks like
086
      needle-in-a-haystack (NIAH; Kamradt, 2024) and RULER (Hsieh et al., 2024) have gained much
087
      popularity due to their simplicity and controllability, we are interested in a wider range of tasks that
088   reflect practical usage, such as the ability to reason over the whole document. In the following, we
089   describe our evaluation protocols and showcase why they are critical to our model development.
090
091   2.1       E VALUATE ON DIVERSE AND REALISTIC TASKS
092
093   We first make the decision to use HELMET (Yen et al., 2024b) as our main evaluation suite, as it is
094   one of the most comprehensive long-context benchmarks, covering the following task categories:
095
096   • Recall: Given a JSON file with random key-values pairs, retrieve the value for a specific key.
097   • RAG: Answer a question given many retrieved Wikipedia documents (NQ, HotPotQA, PopQA).
098   • Re-rank: Produce a top 10 ranking from a long and shuffled list of documents (MSMARCO).
099   • ICL: Learn classification tasks from many in-context examples, where the number of classes
100
        ranges from 6 to 151; average of 5 datasets (TREC coarse/fine, NLU, Banking77, Clinc-150).
101
      • QA: Answer a question given a full-length book (NarrativeQA).
102
      • Summarization: Summarize long legal documents (Multi-LexSum).
103
      Overall, these diverse tasks reflect a range of long-context abilities including recall, reasoning,
104   learning from context, and robustness to noisy inputs. Yen et al. (2024b) also show that HELMET
105   produces model performance trends that are more consistent with human perceptions unlike other
106   long-context benchmarks.

      2.2             E VALUATE AFTER SUPERVISED FINE - TUNING
134
135
      Supervised fine-tuning (SFT; Ouyang et al., 2022) is an additional training stage that fine-tunes the
136   model on a small amount of natural-language instructions and corresponding responses; it enables
137   a base LM to address user queries in a chat format and has become a standard step for producing
138   frontier LMs. Here, we consider the difference between evaluating a model before or after SFT.
139
      In preliminary experiments, we continue training Llama-3-8B-Base on 5B-token subsets from the
140
      data mix by Fu et al. (2024). The mix is based on SlimPajama (Soboleva et al., 2023) and upsamples
141
      long documents to constitute roughly 70% of tokens, while retaining the original domain proportions.
142   Then we conduct SFT on several intermediate checkpoints with UltraChat (Ding et al., 2023).
143
144   We show the benchmarking results before and after SFT in Figure 2. Long-context evaluation shows
145
      clearer signals when it is conducted after SFT: (1) SFT shows that the model continues to improve
      with more training tokens on RAG and re-ranking, while the improvement is less clear or does not
146
      exist when evaluated before SFT. (2) SFT enables evaluation on realistic applications like QA and
147
      summarization, which require instruction following and have low performance before SFT. We also
148   note that the variance from two random training runs is not substantially higher after the additional
149   SFT phase. Therefore, unless otherwise specified, we report the long-context performance after SFT.

232   3.2                T RAINING ONLY ON LONG DATA HURTS LONG - CONTEXT PERFORMANCE
233
234   The ratio between short/long data is another crucial factor for downstream performance. Prior work
235   either trains only on long data (Peng et al., 2024) or adds some short training data (Yen et al., 2024a;
236   Fu et al., 2024). However, we are the first to systematically study the impact of short/long ratio.
237   Figure 3 shows that short task performance monotonically decreases as the long data increases. The
238   trends for long-context vary by tasks and are further complicated by SFT: On tasks like recall and

240   SFT drastically deteriorates with more long data. We hypothesize that specializing the model only on
241   long data makes it a poor initialization for generic SFT—highlighting the importance of evaluating
242   checkpoints after SFT (§2.2). While some long-context tasks benefit from more long data consistently
243   (ICL) or show no clear pattern (re-ranking), the best average performance is achieved at 60% long

294   Training for more steps is well-known to improve downstream tasks in regular pre-training, but little

304   short-context tasks, we observe the average performance initially drops from the initialization, but

      5   S UPERVISED F INE -T UNING FOR L ONG -C ONTEXT LM S
341
342
      In this section, we study how to best enable long-context language models to follow instructions. We
      focus on supervised fine-tuning on instruction datasets (Ouyang et al., 2022) and leave reinforcement
343
      learning and preference optimization for future work.
344
345   All our experiments in this section use the ProLong base model, which was trained for 40B tokens at
346   a maximum sequence length of 512K. In comparison, open-source instruction data are very short, e.g.,
347   UltraChat (Ding et al., 2023) conversations have 1.2K tokens on average and 4.1K tokens maximum.
348
      To bridge this gap, several works (Xiong et al., 2023; Dubey et al., 2024; Xiong et al., 2024) have
      proposed to generate long-context instruction tuning data synthetically.
349

415
      We summarize the training recipe for ProLong in Table 9. Our final model starts from the Llama-3-
416
      8B-Instruct model and is trained on 64K sequence length for 20B tokens. It is then further trained on
417
      512K sequence length for 20B tokens (ProLong base), which we achieve using sequence parallelism
418   (Li et al., 2023). We obtain the final ProLong model via SFT of the base model on UltraChat. One
419   small difference on the data mixture between our ablations and the final model is that we mix in
420   3% high-quality textbooks (Chevalier et al., 2024), as book-like data are shown to be beneficial for
421   long-context (§3.1) and textbooks are also highly educational. You can find more details about our
422   data processing (§B.2) and the training stack (§B.3) in the appendix.

429   8 × 106 for the 64K setting and 1.28 × 108 for the 512K setting.

431   Disabling cross-document attention. Ding et al. (2024a) show that masking out attention across
      document boundaries improve model performance and this was also used during Llama-3 pre-training

501   7    R ELATED W ORK
502
503   Adapting existing LMs for long contexts. Many works explore extending the LM context windows
504   with minimal training, either by position extrapolation (Chen et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2024; Chen
505   et al., 2024; Ding et al., 2024b; Liu et al., 2024a; Zhang et al., 2024b; Zhu et al., 2024; Zhao et al.,
506   2024; Wu et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2024b) or manipulating the attention patterns (Chen et al., 2024;
507   Xiao et al., 2024b;a; Bertsch et al., 2023; Jin et al., 2024). Yoshida et al. (2020); Choromanski et al.
508   (2021); Chevalier et al. (2023) instead explore the idea of compressing the long contexts into shorter
509   forms. However, Fu et al. (2024); Lu et al. (2024) show that using full attention, applying simple
510   position extrapolation, and fine-tuning the model on long documents reach much stronger results.
511   Llama 3.1 (Dubey et al., 2024) and Jamba (Lieber et al., 2024) achieve long-context capabilities
512   by adding a long-context continued training stage between standard pre-training and supervised
513   fine-tuning, which is the setting we follow. Fu et al. (2024) study the data engineering for this setting
514   and argue that 0.5B tokens of domain-balanced, length-upsampled data is sufficient for acquiring the
515   long-context recall ability—which we show is not sufficient if a more holistic evaluation is taken.
516   Xiong et al. (2023); Dubey et al. (2024); Lieber et al. (2024); Xiong et al. (2024); An et al. (2024b);
517
      Bai et al. (2024a) also adopt synthetically-generated long data in the SFT stage; however, we find that
      using standard, short-context instruction data achieves the best long-context results in our setting.
518
519
      Efficient long-context architectures. There have been many efforts in designing more efficient
520
      architectures, for example, linear attention/RNNs (Gu & Dao, 2023; Dao & Gu, 2024; Ma et al., 2022;
521   Sun et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024b), and alternative attention architectures (Rubin
522   & Berant, 2023; Sun et al., 2024; Yen et al., 2024a). However, they often require training from scratch
523   and many have the inherent limitations in terms of long-context recall (Jelassi et al., 2024; Arora
524   et al., 2024). Recent works explore hybrid models (Waleffe et al., 2024; Lieber et al., 2024)) or
525   distilling existing LMs into hybrid models (Wang et al., 2024) and show promising results.
526
527   Long-context evaluation. Many benchmarks have been proposed for long-context evaluation
528   (Shaham et al., 2023; Hsieh et al., 2024; Krishna et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024a; An et al., 2024a;
529   Bai et al., 2024b) There are works studying particular aspects of long-context LMs as well, such as
530
      positional bias (Liu et al., 2024b), in-context learning (Bertsch et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024b), and
      book-length summarization (Kim et al., 2024). In this work, we follow Yen et al. (2024b) for its
531
      diverse application coverage and reliable evaluations.
532
533
534   8    C ONCLUSION
535
536   We study the problem of given a short-context pre-trained LM, how to most effectively continually
537   pre-train and SFT the model to be long-context. We conduct thorough ablations on each component
538   and many of our findings contradict existing practices or beliefs. We use all the findings to produce
539   ProLong, a new state-of-the-art long-context LM. We release all our code, data, and models publicly
      and hope that our findings will boost research and applications of long-context LMs.

       A     L IMITATIONS
973
974
       Although we try to ablate the major components of our training recipe, due to resource limitations,
975    we cannot exhaust all aspects, such as the optimization hyperparameters and additional data mixtures.
976    We also limit ourselves to the 10B-scale regime and the Llama-3 base models, which may limit
977    the generalizability of our recipe to our base models. Another concern is that we are overfitting to
978    the tasks chosen for model development—however, we do not directly train on those datasets and
979    guiding model development with benchmark tasks has become a common practice in pre-trained LM
980    development. We also show that our final recipe and model perform well on an additional evaluation
981    dataset, NoCha.

1007
       2024b). We highlight some of the evaluation protocol improvements that HELMET implemented
1008   compared to previous benchmarks here:
1009
1010    • Sufficient context lengths and fine-grained control. HELMET can evaluate models at a context
1011      length of 128K tokens and beyond. The evaluation protocol also allows for reporting results at
1012      different lengths, giving developers fine-trained controls for different needs of long contexts.
1013    • Better synthetic recall tasks. As shown in HELMET, needle-in-a-haystack (Kamradt, 2024)
1014      is mostly saturated because of its simplicity—the model only needs to find a needle in some
1015      irrelevant context. We instead use the more challenging JSON KV task, first proposed in Liu et al.
1016      (2024b) and included in HELMET, where the model is required to find the corresponding value
1017      to a given key among a large JSON file.
1018    • Using class-balanced demonstrations and abstract labels for ICL. To disentangle models’
1019      ability of learning from demonstrations from their pre-training bias of the task or the dataset
1020      label distribution (Pan et al., 2023), HELMET samples the same number of demonstrations for
1021      each class and uses number labels (1, 2, ...) instead of natural-language labels (e.g., location,
1022      description, ...).
1023    • Model-based evaluation for long-context QA and summarization. Instead of using traditional
1024      metrics like ROUGE (which has shown to be poorly indicative of the real model performance:
1025      Deutsch & Roth, 2021; Deutsch et al., 2022; Goyal et al., 2023; Chang et al., 2024), HELMET
          uses model-based evaluations to compare the reference answer and the model output. For QA,


                                                         19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
           HELMET uses GPT-4o to score the model output given the question and the reference answer at a
1027       0-3 scale. For summarization, HELMET takes a similar approach as Zhang & Bansal (2021); Gao
1028       et al. (2023): it first uses GPT-4o to decompose the reference summary and the model summary
1029       into atomic claims; then it uses GPT-4o to check whether each reference atomic claim is covered
1030       by the model output (recall) and whether each model atomic claim is covered by the reference
1031       summary (precision). Yen et al. (2024b) show that the model-based evaluation correlates with
1032       human perceptions significantly better than traditional metrics.

1077
1078   Technical stack. We use various open-source packages and tools for the ProLong training and
1079   evaluation. We use PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2019) and Hugging Face transformers (Wolf et al., 2020)
       for the model training. We use mosaic-streaming (Mosaic ML, 2022) for loading and mixing the data


                                                          20
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1080
       and FlashAttention 2 (Dao, 2024) for efficient attention implementation. We implement sequence
1081   parallelism based on DeepSpeed-Ulysses (Jacobs et al., 2023). For long-context evaluation, we use
1082   HELMET (Yen et al., 2024b) and for short-context evaluation, we use lm-eval-harness (Gao et al.,
1083   2021).
1084
1085   Attention and batching. Since we do document masking in attention §6, we use the variable-length
1086   attention implementation from FlashAttention 2 (Dao, 2024) to speed up long-context training: for
1087   sequences that are concatenations of multiple short documents, instead of computing the full attention
1088   with masking, we instead compute the attention for each individual document. Since the complexity
1089   of attention is quadratic to the sequence length, this improves the training speed. However, the
1090
       improvement is negligible in a distributed training setting with FSDP, since GPUs processing short
       sequence batches have to wait on other GPUs processing long sequences. We therefore implement
1091
       a smart batching algorithm: In our setting, a gradient step usually consists of multiple gradient
1092
       accumulation steps, where each device processes a smaller minibatch. We sort all the minibatches
1093   per training step by the sum of the squared lengths of documents in the sequence. This leads to
1094   more balanced sequence lengths across the GPUs and effective speedups, as can be seen in Table 15,
1095   without affecting the gradient updates or loss during training. However, the efficiency gains are
1096   diminished when training with more GPUs, as this reduces the number of gradient accumulation
1097   steps.

1192   Xiong et al. (2023); emozilla (2023) show that changing the RoPE frequency base to a larger value in
1193   continual long-context pre-training or in inference time can improve the long-context performance.
                                                                                             d
1194   emozilla (2023) suggests that one should scale the frequency base by a factor of t d−2 , where t is
1195   the ratio between the target sequence length and the original LM length, and d is the attention head
1196   dimension.
1197
       We conduct ablation studies, at both 64K (same as our standard ablation setting as specified in

       §B.4) and 512K (starting from ProLong-64K and training with the 512K data mixture for 5B tokens)

       sequence lengths, on what frequency bases we should use. Table 18 and Table 19 show the results. We
1200   first see that using the original 500,000 frequency base from Llama-3 leads to significant performance
1201   degradation. While dynamic NTK suggests 4 × 106 , we find that further scaling it to 8 × 106 leads to
1202   better performance. Similar, we see that when scaling the 64K model to 512K, while dynamic NTK
1203   suggests a 64 × 106 frequency base, much larger frequency bases (128 × 106 and 256 × 106 ) lead to
1204   better performance. We use 8 × 106 for 64K and 128 × 106 for 512K for our final ProLong models.
