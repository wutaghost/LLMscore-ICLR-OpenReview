009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Editing 4D scenes using text prompts is a novel task made possible by advances in
012
               text-to-image diffusion models and differentiable scene representations. However,
013
               conventional approaches typically use multi-view images or videos with camera
014            poses as input, which causes inconsistencies when editing monocular videos due
015            to the reliance of these tools on iteratively per-image editing and the absence
016            of multi-view supervision. Furthermore, these techniques usually require exter-
017            nal Structure-from-Motion (SfM) libraries for camera pose estimation, which can
018            be impractical for casual monocular videos. To tackle these hurdles, we present
019            4DEditPro, a novel framework that enables consistent 4D scene editing on casual
020            monocular videos with text prompts. In our 4DEditPro, the Temporally Propa-
021            gated Editing (TPE) module guides the diffusion model to ensure temporal coher-
022
               ence across all input frames in scene editing. Furthermore, the Spatially Propa-
               gated Editing (SPE) module in 4DEditPro introduces auxiliary novel views near
023
               the camera trajectory to enhance the spatial consistency of edited scenes. 4DEdit-
024
               Pro employs a pose-free 4D Gaussian Splatting (4DGS) approach for reconstruct-
025            ing dynamic scenes on monocular videos, which progressively recovers relative
026            camera poses, reconstructs the scene, and facilitates scene editing. We have con-
027            ducted extensive experiments to demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach,
028            including both quantitative measures and user studies.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   In recent years, notable progress has been made in differentiable scene representations from multi-
035   view images (Mildenhall et al., 2021; Pumarola et al., 2021; Kerbl et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024) as
036   well as text-to-image (T2I) diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022; Hertz et al., 2023; Brooks et al.,
037   2023; Zhang et al., 2023). By integrating these two lines of research, a variety of approaches (Poole
038   et al., 2023; Wang & Shi, 2023; Park et al., 2024; Cheng et al., 2024) have been proposed to facili-
039
      tate the generation and editing of 3D contents from text or multi-view images, demonstrating great
      potential for various applications such as VR/AR and the MetaVerse. Some methods (Shao et al.,
040
      2023; Mou et al., 2024) has taken a step further to explore the editing of 4D dynamic scenes, and
041
      Control4D (Shao et al., 2023) has leveraged Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) (Goodfellow
042   et al., 2020) to support diffusion models in producing consistent outcomes from editing. Further-
043   more, Instruct 4D-to-4D (Mou et al., 2024) treats 4D scenes as pseudo-3D scenes and employs a
044   video editing approach to iteratively generate coherent edited datasets.
045
      Despite these significant advancements, 4D scene editing using only casual videos (i.e., monocu-
046
      lar videos with unknown camera poses) remains relatively under-explored, and directly integrating
047
      T2I diffusion models with differentiable 4D representations presents several challenges. Firstly,
048   maintaining temporal and spatial consistency is essential for high-quality 4D editing. Previous
049   work (Haque et al., 2023; Shao et al., 2023) has relied on iteratively updating the edited scene until
050   convergence. Yet, the absence of multi-view information in monocular videos significantly reduces
051   the coherence of the edited scene. Secondly, existing 4D editing approaches heavily depend on
052   camera pose estimation techniques (e.g., COLMAP (Schonberger & Frahm, 2016)), which not only
053   introduce redundancy during model initialization, but also cause 4D editing not directly applicable
      to casual monocular video input.

069   In this paper, we address these challenges in 4DEditPro, our framework that integrates diffusion
070   models and 4D Gaussian Splatting (4DGS) for text prompt-guided 4D scene editing of casual
071
      monocular videos. 4DEditPro adapts two key techniques to ensure the temporal and spatial con-
      sistency of the editing results. (i) We present the Temporally Propagated Editing (TPE) module
072
      to enhance temporal editing coherence. Specifically, we convert all video frames into latent to-
073
      kens using DDIM inversion (Song et al., 2021) and partition the latent tokens into several batches.
074   Within each batch, we select a reference token. Utilizing the diffusion model with an extended
075   self-attention module based on the text prompt, we extract attention features from reference tokens
076   and propagate these features to align with those of the remaining tokens, ensuring a consistent edit-
077   ing style throughout the entire sequence. (ii) We introduce the Spatially Propagated Editing (SPE)
078   module to mitigate the lack of multi-view information in monocular videos. First, we interpolate
079   novel views near the original camera trajectory. Similar to TPE, we propagate attention features
080   from known views to novel views at a given timestamp, refining the visual quality of novel views.
081   Moreover, we introduce a progressive dynamic representation utilizing 4DGS for its efficiency in
082   training and rendering. Specifically, we introduce local 3DGS to progressively capture relative
083   pose changes for updating the current pose. Furthermore, we model the global 4DGS with time-
084   dependent opacity, position, and rotation, incorporating the camera pose input from the local 3DGS.
085   We conduct extensive experiments on a diverse set of monocular videos without camera pose in-
086   put, employing various text prompts to demonstrate the efficacy of our approach. As illustrated in
087   Fig. 1, we utilize both local editing (e.g., editing specific objects or regions) and global editing (e.g.,
088
      applying a style or weather to the scene) to present the editing ability of our approach. The evalu-
      ation demonstrates the effectiveness of our method in producing high-quality rendering results and
089
      maintaining temporal-spatial consistency in 4D editing.
090
091   In summary, our primary contributions can be outlined as follows:
092

099            • Extensive experiments on a range of 4D scenes demonstrate the fidelity and consistency of
100              our method in global and local editing.

      Diffusion-based Editing. Stable Diffusion (SD) (Rombach et al., 2022) is a leading text-to-image
122
      diffusion model that operates within a latent image space. SD encodes RGB images into the latent
123
      image space and utilizes a decoder to reconstruct the latent representations into high-resolution
124   images. The core of SD is based on a U-Net architecture (Ronneberger et al., 2015) incorporating
125   residual, self-attention, and cross-attention blocks. Building upon SD, several diffusion models that
126   integrate additional U-Net encoders have been developed (Zhang et al., 2023; Brooks et al., 2023).
127   These U-Net encoders enable image generation controlled by various types of information, such as
128   depth, edges, or specific regions based on prompts. The majority of current 3D or 4D scene editing
129   methods (Haque et al., 2023; Mou et al., 2024) utilize 2D diffusion models with given prompts to
130   edit datasets. These edited datasets are then utilized as training targets to reconstruct the 3D scene.
131   2.2   R ELATED W ORK
132
133   4D Neural Scene Representation. Neural representations (Sitzmann et al., 2019; Aliev et al.,
134   2020; Thies et al., 2019) have been applied in various 3D tasks, with Neural Radiance Fields
135   (NeRF) (Mildenhall et al., 2021) being a groundbreaking technique that utilizes volume render-
136
      ing to optimize 3D modeling with only 2D supervision. However, the rendering process in NeRF
      is time-consuming. Recently, 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) (Kerbl et al., 2023) has shown im-
137
      pressive rendering quality and speed in 3D reconstruction. The efficient differentiable rendering
138
      implementation and explicit representation of 3DGS allow fast training, making it widely used for
139   4D reconstruction and generation tasks (Yang et al., 2024; Katsumata et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024;
140   Gao et al., 2024). However, 3DGS typically requires a point cloud generated by COLMAP (Schon-
141   berger & Frahm, 2016) for estimation, making it not directly applicable to reconstructing casual
142   videos, thus adding additional steps for downstream tasks. Recent progress has been seen in pose-
143   free 4D Gaussian representations (Wang et al., 2024; Chu et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024), but these
144   representations rely on deformable networks to model time-varying parameters, resulting in extra
145   computational cost for generation or editing tasks. In our approach, we propose a progressive and
146   efficient 4D Gaussian Splatting framework that does not rely on COLMAP initialization, simplifying
147
      and optimizing the 4D editing process.
148   Diffusion-Based Scene Editing. Diffusion models iteratively transform random samples into data
149   resembling the target data (Song et al., 2021; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021), as widely used in various
150   generation tasks such as text-to-image generation or editing (Meng et al., 2022; Couairon et al.,
151   2022). However, in 3D and 4D scene reconstruction and editing, directly applying these models
152   has issues on spatial and temporal consistency. To address these issues, recent diffusion-based 3D
153
      editing techniques (Kamata et al., 2023; Haque et al., 2023; Dong & Wang, 2024; Chen et al.,
      2024; Yu & Liu, 2024) utilize self-distillation as a 2D prior to modify scene appearance, yielding
154
      impressive results. Most of these editing approaches incorporate InstructPix2Pix (IP2P) (Brooks
155
      et al., 2023), an image-conditioned diffusion model, for instruction-based 2D image editing. For 4D
156   scene editing, Control4D (Shao et al., 2023) proposes to construct a continuous 4D space by training
157   a 4D GAN (Goodfellow et al., 2020) from ControlNet (Zhang et al., 2023) to address inconsistent
158   supervision signals in 4D portrait editing. In comparison, Instruct 4D-to-4D (Mou et al., 2024)
159   regards 4D scenes as pseudo-3D scenes and utilizes warping to propagate editing outcomes. As such,
160   Instruct 4D-to-4D relies heavily on the accuracy of optical flow and may result in some artifacts.
161   Furthermore, these 4D methods may struggle to reconstruct and edit 4D scenes from sparse views
      or monocular videos, potentially overlooking consistency in both temporal and spatial views.

184   We define the task of 4D scene editing on Gaussian Splatting from casual monocular videos as
185   follows: Given a video sequence I, ˆ a text description p̂v of the video, and a text prompt p̂e describing
186   the target editing, generate a 4D scene based on I,ˆ aligning with the text prompt p̂e , and maintaining
187   temporal and spatial consistency. As illustrated in Fig. 2, we employ the TPE module to generate a
188   temporally consistent sequence Ê from the input video sequence Iˆ with p̂v and p̂e . Subsequently,
189   we progressively reconstruct the 4D scene based on 4DGS. To improve the spatial consistency of
190   the 4D scene, we introduce the SPE module to enhance the visual quality of novel views near the
191   original camera trajectory. In the following, we describe the key components of our pipeline.
192
193   3.1       T EMPORALLY-S PATIALLY P ROPAGATED E DITING
194
195
      Previous studies on 4D editing (Mou et al., 2024; Shao et al., 2023) have incorporated 3D warping
      or GAN assistance to achieve consistent editing results. However, when dealing with monocular
196
      videos that lack multi-view information, these methods struggle to accurately edit corresponding
197
      regions across frames. This limitation often leads to visual artifacts and unstable editing outcomes,
198   reducing the editing performance.
199
200   To address these challenges, we draw inspiration from recent studies (Geyer et al., 2024; Liu et al.,
201
      2024) and propose a novel approach called Temporally Propagated Editing (TPE), leveraging latent
      tokens from DDIM inversion to ensure editing consistency across all frames in monocular videos.
202
      Also, we introduce Spatially Propagated Editing (SPE) to refine 4D scene reconstruction by enhanc-
203
      ing spatial editing from known views to novel views near the camera trajectory. Our approach aims
204   to enhance both temporal and spatial editing consistency, boosting the overall performance of 4D
205   scene reconstruction.
206

289   3.2   P ROGRESSIVE 4D G AUSSIAN S PLATTING
290
291
      In 3D or 4D editing tasks, it is tedious for users to compute the camera poses with SfM libraries such
      as COLMAP (Schonberger & Frahm, 2016). Moreover, COLMAP may not be able to accurately
292
      estimate poses from various types of casual videos, particularly those featuring dynamic scenes
293
      with highly dynamic objects, poorly textured surfaces, and rotating camera motions that make it
294   challenging to match features. Thus, we propose an efficient 4D scene representation that eliminates
295   the need for users to compute camera poses, accommodates casual monocular videos as input, and
296   streamlines the entire editing process.
297

298
      estimate the relative camera pose between two consecutive frames, which is then utilized as input for
299
      the 4DGS pose. This technique involves estimating the relative camera pose by applying a learnable
300
      SE-3 affine transformation Pi to the 3D Gaussian Ĝli reconstructed from the current frame n to
301
      obtain the 3D Gaussian representation for the subsequent frame i + 1, denoted as Gli+1 = Pi ⊙ Gli .

334   Progressive 4D reconstruction. To begin the training process, we utilize a pre-trained monocular
335   depth estimator (Ranftl et al., 2021) to generate the depth map Di from the edited frame Ei . This
336   depth map offers robust geometric information independent of camera parameters. We initialize our
337   4D representation with points lifted from the monocular depth using camera intrinsic and orthog-
338   onal projections. Subsequently, we train the 4D Gaussians with all attributes by minimizing the
339   photometric loss and depth loss between the rendered image and the current edited frame En :
340                      G∗n = arg min(λr Lrgb (R(Gn ), En ), λd Ldep (R(Gn ), Dn )),               (11)

      where λr and λd are the coefficients for photometric loss and depth loss respectively.

345   where λ = 0.2 is empirically set for all experiments. The depth loss can be represented as:

348   During the progressive training process, we calculate the relative camera pose between consecutive
349
      frames using the local 3DGS, which serves as the initial camera pose for our 4DGS. Subsequently,
      the 4DGS updates the set of 4D Gaussians with all attributes based on the learnable camera poses
350
      Pi obtained from the local 3DGS. Recognizing that accumulated pose errors from the local 3DGS
351
      estimations could impede the optimization of a global scene, we iteratively update the rendered im-
352   ages and camera poses within observed frames. Additionally, we interpolate the novel pose between
353   the observed frames using SPE, and use the refined results of novel views from SPE to supervise
354   the editing of the original novel views, as outlined in Eq. 6. We provide the pseudocode for our
355   4DEditPro in the Appendix.

376   to these evaluation metrics, we conduct a user study to assess the quality of 4D scene editing on the
377   DAVIS and Tanks & Templates datasets. This user study involves a two-way or three-way voting
      process to compare our method with other state-of-the-art approaches.

531   5     C ONCLUSION
532
533   This paper introduces a novel 4D editing framework, named 4DEditPro, which utilizes casual
534   monocular video input along with text prompts for editing. We introduce TPE and SPE modules
535   to aid the diffusion model in producing 4D consistent editing results in both temporal and spatial as-
536   pects. Furthermore, we have developed a progressive 4D Gaussian Splatting pipeline to effectively
537   reconstruct the edited 4D scene while estimating the camera pose. Through extensive experiments
538   on three public datasets with multiple evaluation metrics, we demonstrate the effectiveness of our
539
      method.

      A.1     M ORE I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
714
715   Details for Diffusion Models. We employ DDIM inversion with a classifier-free guidance scale of
716   1, consistent with TokenFlow (Geyer et al., 2024). We employ DDIM deterministic sampling with
717   50 steps in TPE and 20 steps in SPE. We downsample 2x of the input sequences or images in TPE
718   and SPE to reduce the computational cost and improve the editing speed.
719
      Details for Temporally Propagated Editing (TPE). In the TPE module, we begin by partitioning
720
      the entire video sequence into multiple batches. Within each batch, we select Kb frames, where Kb
721   is the largest integer between 1 and 10 that can evenly divide the total number Nv of frames. After
722   determining the number of batches as NB = N   Kb , we proceed to randomly select one reference token
                                                      v

723   from each batch. This selection yields Nk reference tokens, where Nk = NB , and Np remaining
724   frames, with Np = Nv − Nk .
725
726
      Details for Related Pose Estimation. In pose estimation, we introduce local 3DGS to analyze pose
      changes between consecutive frames. However, in dynamic scenarios, the motion of some dynamic
727
      objects can hinder precise pose estimation in local 3DGS, particularly when movements are signif-
728
      icant. To address this problem, we obtain dynamic object masks Md to exclusively consider static
729   regions for more accurate pose estimation. Specifically, we first estimate the fundamental matrix
730   using optical flow (Teed & Deng, 2020) between consecutive frames. Subsequently, by computing
731   the threshold for the Sampson distance with the epipolar line, we obtain dynamic object masks Md
732   to refine the accuracy of pose estimation in 4D dynamic scenes. We provide the pseudocode for our
733   4DEditPro in Alg. 1.

      A.5     L IMITATIONS
883
884   Our 4DEditPro framework effectively edits 4D
885   scenes from casual monocular videos without
886   requiring camera pose input or model initial-
887   ization. Nevertheless, it has several limitations.
888   Firstly, the 4D Gaussian representation in our
889   4DEditPro is developed by combining time-
890   dependent functions or parameters to learn the
891
      attributes of Gaussians, which may struggle to
      fit the scene with complex motions. Introducing
892
      a more sophisticated modeling method, such as
893
      using Multilayer Perception (MLP) to learn dy-
894   namic attributes, may be necessary. Another
895   issue is that 4DEditPro depends on the gener-
896   ative and editing abilities of the base diffusion
897   model. Editing efficacy may be compromised if
898   the base model is unable to handle certain edit-
899   ing tasks effectively.
900   Discussion on DDIM Inversion. In editing
901   tasks, we incorporate DDIM inversion to ex-
902   tract latent tokens, enabling the acquisition of
903   consistent initial noise that aligns with tem-
904   poral and spatial editing outcomes for coher-
905   ence. However, during the process of inverting
906   the original video to latent tokens, additional
907   uncontrolled noise might be introduced. This
908
      noise can manifest as subtle disturbances when
      reconstructing these latent tokens back to recre- Figure 13: An example of our user preference study.
909
      ate the original video. Fine-tuning strategies
910
      may be applied to constrain and mitigate such noise artifacts.
911
912   Discussion on Progressive 4D Gaussian Splatting. In the progressive reconstruction of 4D edited
913   scenes, drift errors may arise from estimating long video sequences using local 3DGS. To mitigate
914
      this problem, keyframe selection can be implemented to detect and optimize the learnable camera
      pose when progressively learning video sequences of a certain length. Furthermore, we are exploring
915
      pose estimation methods of higher robustness to enhance scene reconstruction accuracy.
