009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Membership Inference (MI) refers to the task of determining whether or not a
012            document is included in the training data of a given model. MI provides an
013            effective post-training alternative for analyzing training datasets when the access
014            to them is restricted, including studying the impact of data choices on downstream
015            performance, detecting copyrighted content in the training sets, and checking for
016            evaluation set contamination. However, black-boxed Language Models (LMs)
017            only providing the loss for the document may not provide a reliable signal for
018
               determining memberships. In this work, we leverage the insight that documents
               sharing certain attributes (e.g., time of creation) are all expected to be in a training
019
               set or none of them is, and develop methods that aggregate membership predictions
020
               over these documents. We apply our set assumption on five different domains
021            (e.g., Wikipedia, Arxiv), and find that our method enhances prior MI methods by
022            0.14 in AUROC on average. We further analyze the impact of different language
023            model sizes, training data deduplication, and methods of aggregating membership
024            predictions over sets and find that our method is more effective on undeduplicated
025            and larger models with more documents available in each set and longer sequence
026            sampled for each document, and show our method’s robustness against noises in
027            the set assumption under practical settings.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Language Models (LMs) have demonstrated impressive performance in a range of applications, owing
033
      largely to being trained on web-scale texts. However, the increase in the size of the datasets used to
      train LMs comes at the cost of transparency without releasing access to the datasets, since it becomes
034
      increasingly difficult (in the case of open models) or impossible (for closed models) to determine
035
      whether a given document is included in the pretraining dataset (Achiam et al., 2023; Team et al.,
036   2023; Touvron et al., 2023).
037
038   Membership Inference (MI) refers to the problem of determining whether a document is included
039
      in the training data (member) or absent from it (non-member) given only a trained model. Thus, a
      successful MI approach lets researchers gain knowledge about the presence or absence of training data
040
      from black-boxed LMs and better understand the generalization in language models by enabling post-
041
      hoc analyses such as: detecting membership of test sets in training data to avoid unfair evaluation of
042   the LMs; inferring the time information of the training data to correlate it with the LMs’ downstream
043   performance (K Nylund, 2023); and detecting the presence of copyrighted or licensed content in
044   the training data. Previous MI methods, which we refer as Individual-MI, focus on determining
045   the membership of individual documents relying on the logits from a trained LM (Shokri et al.,
046   2017; Yeom et al., 2018; Carlini et al., 2022a). However, the effectiveness of MI is limited by LMs’
047   pretraining data (Shi et al., 2023). We hypothesize that the loss of individual documents often does
048   not provide a strong enough signal for inferring its membership because LMs are often trained on
049   trillions of tokens and only see most of their training documents only once (Touvron et al., 2023).
050   In this work, we leverage the insight that there exist many sets that are expected to satisfy the set
051   assumption: either all documents in the set are present in the training dataset or none of them are,
052   and reform the problem into deciding the membership of entire sets. For example, as shown in
053   Figure 1, Arxiv papers created on the same date are likely to be present or absent from the training
      data, depending on the data collection date of the LM. We argue that the set assumption are expected

      effectiveness of MI on LMs.

      2     BACKGROUND
109
110
      Membership inference (MI) in deep learning models is formalized by Shokri et al. (2017) as an
111   adversarial attack to investigate the privacy risk of the models, which is widely used as a component
112   to reconstruct training data (Carlini et al., 2021; Mireshghallah et al., 2022b).1 While there are work
113   on less difficult settings assuming the access to model parameter (Leino & Fredrikson, 2020) or
114   training loss (Liu et al., 2022), these assumptions limit the application of these methods. We therefore
115   focus on the black-box setting where only the loss score of the target model is available.
116
      MI has been vastly studied with supervised machine learning models (Yeom et al., 2018; Wat-
117   son et al., 2021; Carlini et al., 2022a). It is also applied to NLP models on pre-training Shi et al.
118   (2023); Duan et al. (2024); Mireshghallah et al. (2022a) and fine-tuning data targeting tasks including
119   text-generation (Mattern et al., 2023; Mahloujifar et al., 2021; Mireshghallah et al., 2022a), trans-
120   lation (Hisamoto et al., 2020), and classification (Shejwalkar et al., 2021). All of these prior work
121   focus on membership on an individual level, as their target of inference is a single sequence instead
122   of a set with a given particular attribute. Jagannatha et al. (2021) proposes using the loss signal as a
123   measure of membership for individual sequences in language models, but they report their results on
124   both an individual and a patient level, where they average the individual sequence signal over all the
125
      records belonging to a patient. Our method, however, does not rely on the unique structure of clinical
      supervised datasets they use and finds natural existing “sets” in the pretraining data originating from
126
      the web. We also experiment with more methods rather than simply using the model loss and explore
127
      alternative ways to aggregate the signals.
128

      2.1   P ROBLEM F ORMULATION
130
131   Given a language model LM trained on pretraining data T (where T is typically unknown), and a
132   dataset D = {d1 , d2 , · · · , dn } as a collection of documents, the goal of membership inference is to
133   predict whether each document di ∈ D is a member of T or not, given only the model’s probability
134   over a document LM (di ) and no access to T . MI methods typically define a function F that outputs
135   a score F(LM, di ), representing the membership of di in T . In Section 2.2, we provide an overview
136   of various instances of scoring functions used in prior work.

      Practicality of the Set Assumption. We can generally expect many naturally-occurring sets that
      satisfy the set assumption at different levels of granularity in training datasets, as the training data
199
      are often chosen based on some inclusion criteria (Albalak et al., 2024), which can be shared across
200
      different documents. For example, articles published on the same date, documents with the same
201   license, and instances from an existing smaller dataset all compose sets that are likely to be entirely
202   present in the trainings sets of LMs. Figure 1 shows an example where sets composed by Arxiv papers
203   created on the same date satisfies the set assumption. However, practical factors like LMs’ filtering
204   procedure on its training data and the potential duplication across different documents (Albalak
205   et al., 2024; Elazar et al., 2023) might cause the set assumption to not hold in some cases. We
206   argue that even in such cases, Set-MI can still yield significant benefits. Firstly, we provide evidence
207   that our approach is robust to membership noise (see Section 6 where we evaluate under simulated
208   membership noise). Secondly, the applications of Set-MI are not affected by the deviation between
209
      the perfect set assumption and the practice, including revealing the time cutoff of LMs’ training data
      collection, checking for potential dataset contamination post-hoc, learning the composition of the
210
      alignment data, etc. Based on these insights, we construct benchmarks in the following Section 4.

408   quantify the impact of the size of the set, we evaluate Set-MI on Wikipedia with varying sizes of sets,
409
      from 1 to 100. Note that when the set size is 1, Set-MI is equivalent to Individual-MI.
410   Results are shown in Figure 4 (right). The performance of Set-MI increases as the number of available
411   documents increases. This is likely because aggregation over the bigger set provides stronger signals
412   about the membership of the sets. We also find the set size does not have to be very big in order to
413   benefit from Set-MI, e.g., the set size of 3 already provides significant gains over Individual-MI (the
414   set size of 1) .

416   6     ROBUSTNESS OF S ET AGGREGATION
417
418
      As discussed in Section 3, due to practical issues like filtering and duplication, the set assumption
419
      can rarely hold perfectly in real-world. We thus conduct an carefully controlled analysis to explicitly
420   explore how the noise in Set-MI datasets, which breaks the set assumption, impacts the performance
421   of aggregation methods, and we provide insights about which aggregation method to choose based
422   on prior knowledge about potential types of noise.
423
      We start off by constructing a clean version of Wikipedia, targeting at the deduplicated version of
424
      the Pythia 2.8B model, where all documents’ memberships are correctly labeled according to their
425
      13-gram overlap with the model’s training data (i.e., the Pile). This simulates a ideal dataset where
426   our set assumption perfectly holds. To simulate noise, we replace a portion of documents in a set with
427   documents of the opposite membership. We investigate the performance of Loss Attack under the
428   settings where the noise is included in either member or non-member sets with noise ratios ranging
429   from 0.0 to 0.9, as well as noise in both sets with ratios ranging from 0.0 to 0.5. We compare three
430   aggregation methods: 1) MAX: averaging the highest 30% of membership scores in each set, 2)
431   MIN: averaging the lowest 30% of membership scores in each set, and 3) FULL: average all the
      membership scores in each set.

461   7    C ONCLUSION
462
463   Membership inference (MI) lets researchers infer the composition of training datasets from trained
464   language models and trace specific downstream behaviors back to the training data of these models.
465   However, the performance of existing loss-based MI methods is not good enough for practical use,
466   likely because the loss from a trained model does not provide sufficiently strong signals to distinguish
467   members from non-members. This paper introduces Set-MI, a new MI method that identifies a set of
468   documents that shares the membership (e.g., arXiv papers posted on the same date) and aggregates MI
469   scores over documents within the set. Set-MI is orthogonal to previous MI methods (Individual-MI)
470
      and can added on top of them to improve their performance. On five different new benchmarks,
      Set-MI leads to significant improvements in AUROC scores, consistently across four previous MI
471
      methods. Our analyses demonstrate that Set-MI effectively exploits stronger membership signals
472
      from the target models, e.g., when target models memorize more due to their larger size and the
473   duplication in the training data. Our study further shows that Set-MI is still robust against the noise
474   potentially introduced by sets with imperfect set assumptions.
475
476
      Our work makes an assumption that the metadata about the dataset of interest (D) is available,
      enabling the identification of sets that satisfy the set assumption. We highlight that this may not
477
      always be the case in practical MI scenarios, and leave relaxing this assumption for future work.
478

480   E THICAL C ONSIDERATIONS
481
482   Although our work deliberately drops the ”attack” term from ”Membership Inference Attack” as our
483   motivation is to provide an alternative method to learn about LM pretraining data composition for
484   research or copyright purposes, it can be used in an adversarial way to threaten users’ privacy as prior
485   work have shown. However, due to the nature of our method, we argue that it is difficult to apply our
      method to enhance adversarial attacks without access or metadata to user’s private information.

      important. In this ablation, we aim to characterize a good reference model for LiRA in language
733

734

735   with different sizes from Pythia family and GPT-Neo family (Black et al., 2021) trained on the Pile,
736   OPT family (Zhang et al., 2022) trained on RoBERTa+the Pile+PushShift.io Reddit, and GPT-2
737   family (Radford et al., 2019) trained on WebCrawl, a filtered corpus from the CommonCrawl. Results
738   are shown in Figure 6.
739
740   Good reference models represent the training data distribution of the target model. LiRA
741   normalizes the calculated membership scores using a reference model so that a ‘surprise’ to the
742   target model should mostly come from a non-member document rather than a difficult one. Thus,
743   the reference model should be able to measure the difficulty of a document to the target model
744   with a similar training data distribution. Due to the size and complexity of the pretraining data of
745
      LMs, it is difficult to find two pretraining datasets with similar composition, resulting in different
      data distributions. In order to find models that can reflect the training distribution of the target
746
      model, choices include using models from the same family or other models training on the same
747
      data. LiRA with Pythia models, GPT-Neo models, and OPT models, with the training data for
748   Pythia-12b-deduped (the Pile) included in their training data, significantly improves over the random

750   disjointed and different from the Pile, and thus GPT-2 models fails to represents the distribution of
