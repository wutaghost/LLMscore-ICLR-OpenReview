009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Unsupervised semantic segmentation aims to assign a semantic label to each pixel
012            in an image, identifying the object or scene class without any supervision. However,
013            the task becomes particularly difficult due to factors like unclear or overlapping
014            boundaries, intricate object textures, and the presence of multiple objects within the
015            same region. Traditional unsupervised models often suffer from class misalignment
016            and poor spatial coherence, leading to fragmented and imprecise segmentation, of-
017            ten employing postprocessing with Conditional Random Fields (CRFs) to improve
018            their results. Additionally, deterministic models lack the ability to capture predic-
019            tion uncertainty, making their outputs particularly prone to errors in ambiguous
020
               regions. To address these issues, we propose a probabilistic unsupervised semantic
               segmentation framework that enhances the robustness and accuracy of segmenta-
021
               tion by refining predictions through uncertainty modeling and spatial smoothing
022
               techniques. We also introduce a novel loss function that encourages the model to
023            focus on learning similarities within pixels by leveraging feature information from
024            pre-trained vision transformer backbones. We also provide theoretical analyses
025            of our proposed loss function, highlighting its favorable properties in relation to
026            the optimization of our models. Our method demonstrates superior accuracy and
027            calibration, outperforming various baselines across multiple unsupervised semantic
028            segmentation benchmarks including COCO, Potsdam, and Cityscapes. In con-
029            clusion, our framework offers a foundation for more reliable, uncertainty-aware
030            segmentation models, advancing research in unsupervised semantic segmentation.

033   1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Semantic segmentation is a powerful tool in the field of computer vision, providing a complex and
036   nuanced understanding of images down to the pixel level. As a dense prediction problem, semantic
037   segmentation surpasses simple object detection and image classification by offering a detailed,
038   granular perspective of an image’s content. Unsupervised image segmentation builds upon this, by
039   aiming to segment images without the use of labeled training data.
040
      Despite recent progress in self-supervised and unsupervised segmentation techniques, considerable
041
      challenges remain. Noisy image regions or blurry edges, often caused by low resolution or motion
042   blur, may result in regions of poor spatial coherence, leading models to output inconsistent and
043   fragmented labels for adjacent parts of the same object. Additionally, images arising in real-world
044   tasks often contain multiple instances of the same object class varying drastically in size and available
045   contextual information. Models must be capable of capturing fine-grained details while still remaining
046   capable of labeling large objects coherently. Moreover, variability in the appearance of the objects
047   present in the images themselves, due to changes in color, lightning, or texture, renders it difficult to
048   consistently identify and distinguish objects. Situations like this often arise in dense urban scenes,
049   where objects may also overlap one another like people on sidewalks or trees in the background of
050
      an urban environment. Together, these challenges pose significant obstacles for robust and accurate
      segmentation models.
051
052   Existing unsupervised segmentation frameworks primarily leverage clustering algorithms, such as
053   K-means (Lloyd, 1982; MacQueen et al., 1967), Fuzzy C-means (Bezdek et al., 1984), or Mean-
      Shift (Comaniciu & Meer, 2002), which group pixels based on similarity in color, texture, or intensity.

095   Our approach differs fundamentally from these methods. Rather than applying additional postpro-
096   cessing, we leverage the coarse segmentation maps produced by these models as pseudo-labels for
097   our SNGP model. Moreover, existing deterministic models lack the capability to quantify uncertainty
098
      in their predictions, making them particularly vulnerable to errors in ambiguous or complex regions
      of the image.
099
100   To address these limitations, we propose a post-hoc probabilistic framework, combined with a novel
101   loss function, which can be seamlessly integrated into existing methods (Kim et al., 2023; 2024;
102   Hamilton et al., 2023). Our framework enhances semantic segmentation by making it uncertainty-
103   aware, improving accuracy, and demonstrating robustness against noisy regions and blurry edges

105   function that enables a more precise definition of class boundaries and improves the robustness of
106   prediction by unsupervised image semantic segmentation. (ii) Our distance-aware uncertainty loss and
107   probabilistic embedding enable the model to better capture the underlying uncertainty in the data. By
      accounting for spatial relationships and variations in the embedding space, our method improves the

      2   BACKGROUND AND RELATED WORKS
121
122   Learning Unsupervised Representation and Semantic Pseudo-label Assignment Recent state-
123   of-the-art unsupervised segmentation techniques exploit self-supervised learning models like
124   DINO (Caron et al., 2018) as feature extraction for pixel embedding. These methods utilize pretrained
125   Vision Transformer (ViT) models to generate rich, semantic representations that can be leveraged
126
      for downstream tasks. For instance, STEGO (Hamilton et al., 2023) introduces an approach to
      distill unsupervised features into semantic labels, achieving improved spatial coherence in segmen-
127
      tation results. EAGLE (Kim et al., 2024) enhances this by utilizing the eigenvectors of a similarity
128
      matrix-based Laplacian, allowing for the discovery of semantic relations between objects. Similarly,
129   CAUSE (Kim et al., 2023) advances the field by incorporating a discretized concept clusterbook and
130   concept-wise self-supervised learning to refine the segmentation process. These approaches focus
131   primarily on label assignment by deriving pseudo-labels from high-level feature embeddings. Despite
132   the progress, there remains a challenge in managing uncertainties and noise inherent in unsupervised
133   methods. Our work addresses these limitations by proposing a probabilistic framework that integrates
134   seamlessly with existing pseudo-labeling techniques, introducing a novel loss function to account for
135   uncertainty in predictions. This not only improves the accuracy of segmentation but also makes it
136   more robust to noisy and ambiguous boundaries.
137   Spectral Normalized Gaussian Process (SNGP) (Liu et al., 2020) improves the uncertainty quantifi-
138   cation capabilities of deep learning models by introducing two key modifications: spectral normaliza-
139   tion and a Gaussian process output layer. Spectral normalization regularizes the network’s weights,
140   ensuring smooth representations that preserve distances between input samples. The Gaussian process
141   output layer models the uncertainty in predictions using a probabilistic framework. This makes SNGP
142   suitable for supervised learning tasks, where the goal is to learn a mapping from input data to
143
      desired outputs. The SNGP model can be expressed as f (x) = GP (µ(x), k(x, x′ )), where µ(x) and
      k(x, x′ ) represent the mean and covariance functions of the GP, respectively. These functions are
144
      parameterized by a neural network, with spectral normalization applied to each layer to control the
145
      Lipschitz constant, ensuring ∥W ∥σ ≤ 1 where W is the weight matrix of each layer and ∥.∥σ denotes
146   the spectral norm. Inspired by SNGP (Liu et al., 2020), we propose a Gaussian neural embedding to
147   generate probabilistic embeddings at the pixel level to capture the uncertainty and variability in the
148   data and expand SNGP for unsupervised setting.

      Our method integrates several key components to achieve robust segmentation result. First, it utilizes
154
      vision transformers for representation learning and feature extraction which result in patch embedding
155
      (3.1). The patch embedding are then passed through a pseudo-labeling process (using existing
156   approaches such as EAGLE (Kim et al., 2024), STEGO (Hamilton et al., 2023), CAUSE (Kim et al.,
157   2023), or KNNs) to assign labels to each pixel. To further refine these predictions, we introduce
158   a probabilistic clustering refinement network (3.2). We leverage a modified SNGP method that
159   generates Gaussian neural embeddings for each pixel and enables capturing the inherent uncertainty
160   in the unsupervised setting. The pixel embeddings are then meticulously segmented through clustering,
161   allowing for precise delineation and isolation of distinct elements within the image. Finally, metric
      learning facilitates the recognition and categorization of different object classes (3.4).

      comprehensive understanding of various objects; (2) Cityscapes(Cordts et al., 2016), which captures

      Evaluation Metrics for Quantitative Results We report the performance of our method using
341
      the following metrics: Top-1 Accuracy ↑: This metric refers to the proportion of pixels in the test
342
      set that the model correctly predicts as belonging to the correct class. In the context of semantic
343   segmentation, it measures how many pixels are assigned the correct label, providing an overall
344   indication of segmentation quality. Mean Intersection over Union (mIoU) ↑: This metric quantifies
345   the overlap between the predicted segmentation and the ground truth. It is calculated as the average
346   of the intersection over union for all classes, defined as the ratio of the intersection area to the union
347   area. This provides a robust measure of the model’s accuracy in delineating different object regions.

351   with our probabilistic framework, allowing us to assess uncertainty in prediction. Additionally, we
352   compared our approach to existing benchmark models across the various datasets. The performance
353   results presented were averaged over five independent runs to ensure robustness and reliability.

498   6    C ONCLUSION
499
500     In this paper, we presented a novel approach to probabilistic unsupervised segmentation that uses
501   Gaussian embeddings and a newly designed loss function to provide a more robust representation for
502   the task of unsupervised semantic segmentation. We also presented a theoretical analysis to justify the
503   convergence properties of the proposed loss function. In addition, we demonstrated that our post-hoc
504   framework and loss function can be integrated with existing deterministic unsupervised segmentation
505   methods to improve both performance and robustness. Our experimental results confirm that our
506   approach achieves state-of-the-art performance, demonstrating its potential for improving accuracy
507
      and stability in unsupervised segmentation tasks.
508
509
      Limitation and Future Work A limitation of our work lies in the dimensionality of the probabilistic
      embeddings. While our results indicate that increasing the dimensionality improves performance,
510
      likely due to the model’s ability to capture more complex patterns and subtleties in the data, this comes
511
      with potential trade-offs. Higher-dimensional embeddings increase computational costs and may
512   also lead to overfitting, particularly when embedding sizes become very large. Future work should
513   investigate these trade-offs to determine optimal embedding sizes that balance model complexity
514   with computational efficiency and generalization ability.
515   In future work, it would be interesting to develop our probabilistic framework for other tasks such
516   as object detection. Extending this approach could allow for more robust uncertainty quantification
517   in object localization and classification. Additionally, adapting the framework for tasks like video
518   analysis might provide deeper insights into complex temporal and spatial dependencies. This
519   extension could also help address challenges in real-time applications where uncertainty estimation is
520
      crucial, such as autonomous driving or medical imaging, where the framework’s probabilistic nature
      can lead to more reliable decision-making.

661   quantifies model uncertainty (e.g., entropy or predictive variance) that quantifies the distance between
662   x and the training data with respect to ∥. ∥X , i.e.,

668

669

670   Additionally, the predictive uncertainty of the GP can be expressed using  the posterior variance
671   u(x∗ ) = var(g(x∗ )) = 1 − k ⊤ V k ∗ , where ki∗ = exp −(1/2l) |x∗ − xi |2 X , and VN ×N is a fixed

676   A.2    S PECTRAL N ORMALIZATION
677
678   When the output layer g is replaced with a Gaussian process, it allows the model logit(x) =
679
      g ◦ h(x) to become aware of the distance in the hidden space |h (x1 ) − h (x2 )|H . To ensure the
      meaningful correspondence between distances in the hidden space |h(x) − h (x′ )|H and the input
680
      space |x − x′ |X , it is crucial that the hidden mapping h is distance preserving. Many modern deep
681
      learning models, such as ResNets and Transformers, are composed of residual blocks. Specifically,
682   the hidden mapping can be expressed as h(x) = hL−1 ◦ · · · ◦ h2 ◦ h1 (x), where hl (x) = x + gl (x).
683
684   To ensure distance preservation, a simple method is employed by bounding the Lipschitz constants
685   of all nonlinear residual mappings {gl }L−1
                                              l=1 to be less than 1. This constraint guarantees that the
686
      distances in the hidden space remain consistent with the distances in the input space, thus preserving
      meaningful relationships between the data points. This result can be formally seen below:
687
688   Proposition 1 (Lipschitz-bounded residual block is distance preserving) Let h : X → H be a hidden
689   mapping with residual architecture h = hL−1 ◦. . . h2 ◦h1 where hl (x) = x+gl (x). If for 0 < α ≤ 1,
690   all gl’s are α-Lipschitz, i.e., ∥gl (x) − gl (x′ )∥H ≤ α ∥x − x′ ∥X ∀ (x, x′ ) ∈ X . Then:
691
692
693                          L1 ∥x − x′ ∥X ≤ ∥h(x) − h (x′ )∥H ≤ L2 ∥x − x′ ∥X ,                           (16)
694
695   where L1 = (1 − α)L−1 and L2 = (1 + α)L−1 , i.e., h is distance preserving.
696
      Proof is provided in Appendix E.1 of Liu et al. (2020). To maintain the distance-preserving property
697   of the hidden mapping h, it is sufficient for the weight matrices of the nonlinear residual block
