011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            This paper addresses domain adaptation challenges in graph data resulting from
014            chronological splits. In a transductive graph learning setting, where each node
015            is associated with a timestamp, we focus on the task of Semi-Supervised Node
016            Classification (SSNC), aiming to classify recent nodes using labels of past nodes.
017            Temporal dependencies in node connections create domain shifts, causing signif-
018            icant performance degradation when applying models trained on historical data
019
               into recent data. Given the practical relevance of this scenario, addressing do-
               main adaptation in chronological split data is crucial, yet underexplored. We pro-
020
               pose Imposing invariance with Message Passing in Chronological split Tempo-
021
               ral Graphs (IMPaCT), a method that imposes invariant properties based on re-
022            alistic assumptions derived from temporal graph structures. Unlike traditional
023            domain adaptation approaches which rely on unverifiable assumptions, IMPaCT
024            explicitly accounts for the characteristics of chronological splits. The IMPaCT
025            is further supported by rigorous mathematical analysis, including a derivation of
026            an upper bound of the generalization error. Experimentally, IMPaCT achieves
027            a 3.8% performance improvement over current SOTA method on the ogbn-mag
028            graph dataset. Additionally, we introduce the Temporal Stochastic Block Model
029            (TSBM), which replicates temporal graphs under varying conditions, demonstrat-
030
               ing the applicability of our methods to general spatial GNNs.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   The task of Semi-supervised Node Classification (SSNC) on graph often involves nodes with tem-
035   poral information. For instance, in academic citation network, each paper node may contain infor-
036   mation regarding the year of its publication. The focus of this study lies within such graph data,
037   particularly on datasets where the train and test splits are arranged in chronological order. In other
038
      words, the separation between nodes available for training and those targeted for inference occurs
      temporally, requiring the classification of the labels of nodes with the most recent timestamp based
039
      on the labels of nodes with historical timestamp. While leveraging GNNs trained on historical data
040
      to classify newly added nodes is a common scenario in industrial and research settings (Liu et al.,
041   2016; Bai et al., 2020; Pareja et al., 2020), systematic research on effectively utilizing temporal
042   information within chronological split graphs remains scarce.

077   2.1   G RAPH N EURAL N ETWORKS
078   Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have gained significant attention across various domains, including
079   recommender systems (Ying et al., 2018; Gao et al., 2022), biology (Barabasi & Oltvai, 2004), and
080   chemistry (Wu et al., 2018). Spatial GNNs, such as GCN (Kipf & Welling, 2017), GraphSAGE
081   (Hamilton et al., 2017), GAT (Velickovic et al., 2017) and HGNN (Feng et al., 2019), derive topo-
082   logical information by aggregating information from neighboring nodes through message passing.
083
084                       Mv(k+1) ← AGG({Xu(k) , ∀u ∈ Nv })                                              (1)
085
                          Xv(k+1) ← COMBINE({Xv(k) , Mv(k+1) }), ∀v ∈ V, k < K                           (2)
086
087   Here, K is the number of GNN layers, Xv0 = Xv is initial feature vector of each node, and the final
088   representation XvK = Zv serves as the input to the node-wise classifier. The AGG function performs
089   topological aggregation by collecting information from neighboring nodes, while the COMBINE
090
      function performs semantic aggregation through processing the collected message for each node.
      Scalability is a crucial issue when applying GNNs to massive graphs. Ego graph, which defines the
091
      scope of information influencing the classification of a single node, exponentially increases with the
092
      number of GNN layers. Therefore, to ensure scalability, algorithms must be meticulously designed
093   to efficiently utilize computation and memory resources (Hamilton et al., 2017; Shi et al., 2022; Zeng
094   et al., 2019). Decoupled GNNs, whose process of collecting topological information occurs solely
095   during preprocessing and is parameter-free, such as SGC (Wu et al., 2019), SIGN (Rossi et al., 2020),
096   and GAMLP (Zhang et al., 2022), have demonstrated outstanding performance and scalability on
097   many real-world datasets. Furthermore, SeHGNN (Yang et al., 2023) and RpHGNN (Hu et al., 2023)
098   propose decoupled GNNs that efficiently apply to heterogeneous graphs by constructing separate
099   embedding spaces for each metapath based on HGNN (Feng et al., 2019).
100   2.2   D OMAIN A DAPTATION
101
102   A machine will learn from a train domain in order to perform on a test domain. Domain adaptation
103   is needed due to the discrepancy between train and test domains. That is, we can not guarantee
104
      that a model which performed well on the train domain, will perform well on the test domain. The
      performance on the test domain is known to depend on the performance of the train domain and the
105
      similarity between two domains (Ben-David et al., 2006; 2010; Germain et al., 2013; 2016).
106
107   For feature space X and label space Y, the goal is to train a predictor function f : X → Y to
      minimize the risk Rtr (f ) = E(X,Y )∼Ptr [L(f (X), Y )] where Ptr is the distribution of the train


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      feature-label pairs, and L is a loss function L : Y × Y → R. We are interested in minimizing
109   Rte (f ) = E(X,Y )∼Pte [L(f (X), Y )] where Pte is the distribution of the test feature-label pairs.
110
111   The domain adaptation bound, or upper bound of generalization error was firstly proposed for a
112
      binary classification task by defining the set of all trainable functions F, symmetric hypothesis
      class F∆F (Ben-David et al., 2006), and a metric for distributions, namely dF ∆F . dF ∆F is the
113
      factor which represents the similarity between two distributions. Recently, theories and applications
114
      as setting up the metric between two distributions as the Wasserstein-1 distance, W1 instead of
115   dF ∆F have been developed (Lee et al., 2019; Shen et al., 2018; Arjovsky et al., 2017; Redko et al.,
116   2017). For brevity, we omit the assumptions introduced in (Redko et al., 2017) and simply state the
117   theoretical domain adaptation bound below.
118                                    Rte (f ) ≤ Rtr (f ) + W1 (Dtr , Dte )                            (3)
119   where Dtr and Dte are marginal distributions on X of Ptr and Pte , respectively.

121   2.3   P RIOR S TUDIES
122   Despite its significance, studies on domain adaptation in GNNs are relatively scarce. Notably, to our
123   knowledge, no studies propose invariant learning applicable to large graphs. For example, EERM
124   (Wu et al., 2022) defines a graph editor that modifies the graph to obtain invariant features through
125   reinforcement learning, which cannot be applied to decoupled GNNs. SR-GNN (Zhu et al., 2021)
126   adjusts the distribution distance of representations using a regularizer, with computational com-
127   plexity proportional to the square of the number of nodes, making it challenging to apply to large
128   graphs. This scarcity is attributed by several factors: data from different environments may have
129   interdependencies, and the extrapolating nature of environments complicates the problem.

      3     M ETHOD E XPLANATION
132   3.1   M OTIVATION OF O UR M ETHOD : I MPOSING I NVARIANCE WITH M ESSAGE PASSING
133
134   The distribution of node connections depends on both timestamps and labels. As a result, even
135   if features from previous layers are invariant, features after the message passing layers belong to
136
      different distributions: Dtr for training and Dte for testing. Imposing invariance here means aligning
      the mean and variance of Dtr and Dte . While it may seem straightforward to compute and align the
137
      mean and variance for each label, this is impractical in real settings since test labels are unknown
138
      during prediction. To overcome this, we analyzed real-world temporal graphs and identified practical
139   assumptions about connection distributions. Based on this, we propose a message passing method
140   that corrects the discrepancy between Dtr and Dte , ensuring feature invariance at each layer.
141

312   adding or removing edges in the graph, GenPMP requires modifying the model to reflect real-valued
313   edge weights during the message passing process. Moreover, when the number of nodes per times-
314   tamp is equal, GenPMP behaves similarly to PMP. Theoretically, if the ratio Ptmax (∆)/Pt̃ (∆) is too
315   large for a fixed ∆, the variance of aggregated message will increase by a factor roughly proportional
316   to the square of the ratio by definition. Therefore, V defined in Section 4.3 will increase substan-
317
      tially, and hence the theoretical upper bound of generalization error will also increase substantially
318
      since there is a factor V 1/3 in the bound. In conclusion, GenPMP is a method for exceptional usage
      on graph data which shows large differences of node numbers with specific timestamps.

399
400   Temporal Stochastic Block Model(TSBM). To assess the robustness and generalizability of pro-
401   posed IMPaCT methods on graphs satisfying assumptions 1, 2, and 3, we conducted experiments
402   on synthetic graphs. In order to create repeatable and realistic chronological graphs, we defined
403
      the Temporal Stochastic Block Model(TSBM) as our graph generation algorithm. TSBM can be
      regarded as a special case of the Stochastic Block Model(SBM) that incorporates temporal infor-
404
      mation of nodes (Holland et al., 1983; Deshpande et al., 2018). In the SBM, the probability matrix
405
      Pyỹ represents the probability of a connection between two nodes i and j, where y and ỹ denote
406   the communities to which the nodes belong. Our study extends this concept to account for temporal
407   information, differentiating communities based on both node labels and time. In the TSBM, the
408   connection probability is represented by a 4-dimensional tensor Ptt̃yỹ . We ensured that assump-
409   tions 1, 2, and 3 were satisfied. Specifically, the feature assigned to each node x ∈ Rf was sampled
410   from distributions depending solely on the label, defined as xi = µ(y) + ky Zi . Here, Zi ∈ Rf is
411   an IID standard normal noise and ky represents the variance of features. To satisfy assumption 2,
412   the time and label of each node were determined independently. To satisfy assumption 3, we first
413   considered the possible forms of g(y, ỹ, |t − t̃|) and then determined Ptt̃yỹ accordingly. We used an
414   exponentially decaying function with decay factor γy,ỹ , defined as:
415                                                                    |t−t̃|
                                         g(y, ỹ, |t − t̃|) = γy,ỹ g(y, ỹ, 0), ∀|t − t̃| > 0                                        (21)

      whether the methods proposed in this study could improve the performance of general spatial GNNs.

511

523   7    C ONCLUSIONS
524
525
      In this study, we addressed the domain adaptation challenges in graph data induced by chronolog-
      ical splits by proposing invariant message passing functions, IMPaCT. We analyzed and tackled
526
      the domain adaptation problem in graph datasets with chronological splits, presenting robust and
527
      realistic assumptions based on observable properties in real-world graphs. Based on these assump-
528   tions, we proposed IMPaCT, which preserves the invariance of both the 1st and 2nd moments of
529   aggregated messages during the message passing step. We demonstrated its adaptability and scala-
530   bility through experiments on both real-world citation graphs and synthetic graphs. Notably, on the
531   ogbn-mag citation graph, we achieved substantial performance improvements over previous state-
532   of-the-art methods, with a 3.0% increase in accuracy using the 1st moment alignment method and a
533   3.8% improvement when combining 1st and 2nd moment alignment. Furthermore, we validated the
534   generality of our assumptions through experiments on the ogbn-arxiv and ogbn-papers100m.
535   Limitations. From an experimental standpoint, further investigation is needed to demonstrate the
536   robustness and generalizability of IMPaCT across a wider variety of baseline models. Additionally,
537   exploring the parallel implementation of JJ NORM to assess how efficiently it can be applied to gen-
538   eral spatial GNNs could be a topic for future work. From a theoretical perspective, our discussions
539   were limited to spatial GNNs and assumed that the semantic aggregation satisfies the G-Lipschitz
      condition. Future work could focus on deriving tighter bounds under more realistic constraints.

      Combining Assumption 1 and Assumption 2, we derive Pte (X, Y ) = Ptr (X, Y ). This is a funda-
665
      mental assumption in machine learning problems, implying that the initial feature distribution does
666   not undergo significant shifts. However, in the real world graph dataset such as ogbn-mag dataset,
667   features are embeddings derived from the abstracts of papers using a language model. It is crucial
668   to verify whether these features remain constant over time, as per our assumption. Assumption 3
669   is a specific assumption derived from a close observation of the characteristics of temporal graphs,
670   which requires justification based on actual data. To address these questions, we conducted a vi-
671   sual analysis based on the real-world temporal graph data from the ogbn-mag dataset. Statistics for
672   ogbn-mag are provided in Appendix A.2.

675

      First, to verify whether the distribution of features change over time, we calculated the average

677
      demonstrate that the distance between mean features of nodes with the same label but different
678
      times is significantly smaller than the distance between mean features of nodes with different labels.
679   Given that the features are high-dimensional embeddings, using simple norms as distances might be
680   inappropriate. Therefore, we employed the unsupervised learning method t-SNE (Van der Maaten
681   & Hinton, 2008) to project these points onto a 2D plane, verifying that nodes with the same label
682   but different times form distinct clusters. For the t-SNE analysis, we set the maximum number of
683   iterations to 1000, perplexity to 30, and learning rate to 50.

      We computed the mean feature for each community defined by the same (label, time) pair. Points

       A.13    S CALABILITY OF INVARIANT MESSAGE PASSING METHODS
1789
1790   First moment alignment methods such as MMP and PMP have the same complexity and can be
1791   easily applied by modifying the graph. By adding or removing edges according to the conditions,
1792   only O(|E|) additional preprocessing time is required, which is necessary only once throughout the
1793   entire training process. If the graph cannot be modified and the message passing function needs to
1794
       be modified instead, it would require O(|E|f K), which is equivalent to the traditional averaging
       message passing. Similarly, the memory complexity remains O(|E|f K), consistent with traditional
1795
       averaging message passing. Despite having the same complexity, PMP is much more expressive than
1796
       MMP. Unless there are specific circumstances, PMP is recommended for first moment alignment.
1797
1798   In PNY, estimating the relative connectivity P̂y,t (ỹ, t̃) requires careful consideration. If both
1799   t ̸= tmax and t̃ ̸= tmax , calculating the relative connectivity for all pairs involves O((N + |E|)f )
1800   operations, while computing for cases where either time is tmax requires O(|Y |2 |T |2 ) computa-
1801   tions. Therefore, the total time complexity becomes O(|Y |2 |T |2 + (N + |E|)f ). Additionally,
1802   for each message passing step, the covariance matrix of the previous layer’s representation and
1803
       the aggregated message needs to be computed for each label-time pair. Calculating the covari-
       ance matrix of the representation from the previous layer requires O((|Y ||T | + N )f 2 ) operations.
1804
       Subsequently, computing the covariance matrix of the aggregated message obtained through PMP
1805
       via relative connectivity requires O(|Y |2 |T |2 f 2 ) operations. Diagonalizing each of them to cre-
1806   ate affine transforms requires O(|Y ||T |f 3 ), and transforming all representations requires O(N f 2 ).
1807   Thus, with a total of K layers of topological aggregation, the time complexity for applying PNY
1808   becomes O(K(|Y ||T |f 3 + |Y |2 |T |2 f 2 + N f 2 ) + |E|f ). Additionally, the memory complexity in-
1809   cludes storing covariance matrices based on relative connectivity and label-time information, which
1810   is O(|Y ||T |f 2 + |Y |2 |T |2 ).
1811
       Now, let’s consider applying PNY to real-world massive graph data. For instance, in the ogbn-mag
1812   dataset, |Y | = 349, |T | = 11, N = 629571, and |E| = 21111007. Assuming a representation
1813   dimension of f = 512, it becomes apparent that performing at least several trillion floating-point
1814   operations is necessary. Without approximation or transformations, applying PNY to large graphs
1815   becomes challenging in terms of scalability.
1816
       Lastly, for JJ NORM, computing the sample mean of aggregated messages for each label and time pair
1817
       requires O(N f ) operations. Based on this, computing the total variance, variance of the mean, and
1818   mean of representations with each time requires O(N f ) operations. Calculating each α̂t requires
1819   O(|T |) operations, and modifying the aggregated message based on this requires O(N f ) operations,
1820   resulting in a total of O(N f + |T |) ≃ O(N f ) operations. For GNNs with nonlinear node-wise
1821   semantic aggregation function with a total of K layers, layer-wise JJ NORM have to be applied,
1822   which results in O(N f K) operations. Additionally, the memory complexity becomes O(|Y ||T |f ).
1823   Considering that most operations in JJ NORM can be parallelized, it exhibits excellent scalability.
1824   In experiments with synthetic graphs, it was shown that invariant message passing methods can be
1825   applied to general spatial GNNs, not just decoupled GNNs. For 1st moment alignment methods
1826   such as PMP and MMP, which can be applied by reconstructing the graph, they have the same
1827   time and memory complexity as calculated above. However, for 2nd moment alignment methods
1828   such as JJ NORM or PNY, transformation is required for each message passing step, resulting in
1829   a time complexity multiplied by the number of epochs as calculated above. Therefore, when using
1830   general spatial GNNs on real-world graphs, only 1st moment alignment methods may be realistically
1831   applicable.
1832   Guidelines for deciding which IMPaCT method to use. Based on these findings, we propose
1833   guidelines for deciding which invariant message passing method to use. If the graph exhibits differ-
1834   ences in environments due to temporal information, we recommend starting with PMP to make the
1835   representation’s 1st moment invariant during training. MMP is generally not recommended. Next,



                                                         34
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1836
       if using Decoupled GNNs, PNY and JJ NORM should be compared. If the graph is too large to
1837   apply PNY, compare the results of using PMP alone with using both PMP and JJ NORM. In cases
1838   where there are no nonlinear operations in the message passing stage, JJ NORM needs to be applied
1839   only once at the end. Using 2nd moment alignment methods with General Spatial GNNs may be
1840   challenging unless scalability is improved.
1841
       Caution is warranted when applying invariant message passing methods to real-world data. If As-
1842
       sumptions do not hold or if the semantic aggregation functions between layers exhibit loose Lip-
1843   schitz continuity, the differences in the distribution of final representations over time cannot be
1844   ignored. Therefore, rather than relying on a single method, exploring various combinations of the
1845   proposed invariant message passing methods to find the best-performing approach is recommended.
