                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011
               This paper presents a novel method that simultaneously achieves model prun-
012            ing and low-bit quantization through Bayesian variational inference to effectively
013            compress deep neural networks (DNNs) while suffering minimal performance
014            degradation. Unlike previous approaches that treat pruning and quantization as
015            separate, sequential tasks, our method explores a unified optimization space, en-
016            abling more efficient compression. By leveraging a spike-and-slab prior com-
017            bined with Gaussian Mixture Models (GMM), we can achieve both network spar-
018            sity and low-bit representation. Experiments on CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and
019            SQuAD datasets demonstrate that our approach achieves compression rates of
020
               up to 32x with less than a 1.3% accuracy loss on the CIFAR datasets and a 1.66
               point decrease in F1 score on SQuAD. Additionally, we show that the Bayesian
021
               model average of neural networks can further mitigate the impact of quantization
022
               noise, leading to more robust compressed models. Our method outperforms exist-
023            ing techniques in both compression efficiency and accuracy retention, offering a
024            promising solution for compressing DNNs.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) have emerged as a leading approach in various machine learning
030   tasks due to their superior performance across domains such as computer vision (He et al., 2016;
031   2017; Dosovitskiy et al., 2021), natural language processing (Devlin, 2018; Xu et al., 2020; Tou-
032   vron et al., 2023), and speech recognition (Hinton et al., 2012; Zhang et al., 2023). However,
033
      this remarkable performance comes with a significant increase in computational and memory de-
      mands (Simonyan & Zisserman, 2014; He et al., 2016; Vaswani, 2017; Radford, 2018; Xu et al.,
034
      2020; Touvron et al., 2023). Various techniques like pruning (LeCun et al., 1989; Han et al., 2015),
035
      weight quantization (Courbariaux et al., 2015; Rastegari et al., 2016; Sze et al., 2017; Frantar et al.,
036   2022; Lin et al., 2024), knowledge distillation (Park et al., 2019; Gou et al., 2021) and neural ar-
037   chitecture search (Liu et al., 2018a;b; Wang et al., 2020b) have been proposed to improve DNNs
038   efficiency and enhance the widespread of DNNs in AI systems.
039
      Model compression techniques, including pruning and quantization, have proven effective in de-
040
      ploying cost-efficient DNNs (Buciluǎ et al., 2006; Choudhary et al., 2020). Pruning involves selec-
041
      tively removing DNN connections (i.e., setting the corresponding weights to zero), whereas weight
042   quantization entails reducing the bit-width of weight representations. Pruning methods are typically
043   categorized into structural pruning (Ding et al., 2019; You et al., 2019), which zeroes out groups
044   of weights, and unstructured pruning (Guo et al., 2016; Dong et al., 2017), which zeroes out in-
045   dividual weights without altering the model’s architecture. As for the quantization method, recent
046   studies (Wang et al., 2018; Banner et al., 2018; Sun et al., 2019) have demonstrated that under 8-bit
047   training techniques, it can effectively accelerate the training of various models, including VGG (Wu
048   et al., 2018), ResNet (Banner et al., 2018), LSTMs, Transformers (Sun et al., 2019), and vision-
049   language models (Wortsman et al., 2023).
050   Han et al. (2015) proposed a model compression pipeline that sequentially applies pruning and
051   weight quantization, achieving significant compression rates without sacrificing much accuracy,
052   however, the sequential application fails to explore the complementarity of pruning and quanti-
053   zation Bai et al. (2023). Recent studies have demonstrated that integrating pruning and quantization
      into a single process not only conserves computational resources but also achieves state-of-the-art

099   2.1   P RUNING AND S PARSE DNN
100
      The concept of weight pruning was initially introduced by LeCun et al. (1989), with further develop-
101
      ment by Hassibi et al. (1993) through a mathematical method known as the Optimal Brain Surgeon
102   (OBS). This approach selects weights for removal from a trained neural network using second-order
103   information. Subsequent improvements, as indicated by studies (Dong et al., 2017; Wang et al.,
104   2019; Singh & Alistarh, 2020), have adapted OBS for large-scale DNNs by employing numerical
105   techniques to estimate the second-order information required by OBS for extensive model parame-
106   ters. Meanwhile, Louizos et al. (2018b) has introduced an l0 regularized method to enhance sparsity
107   in DNNs. Frankle & Carbin (2019) established a critical insight that within a randomly initialized
      DNN, an optimal sub-network can be identified and extracted. More recently, amidst the rise of large


                                                                                     2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      language models (LLMs), the work by Xia et al. (2024) has illustrated that structured pruning, com-
109   bined with targeted retraining, can significantly reduce computational costs while preserving robust
110   performance. Concurrently, researchers (Deng et al., 2019; Blundell et al., 2015; Bai et al., 2020)
111   have employed spike-and-slab distributions and show the power of Bayesian Neural Networks in
112   promoting sparsity in DNNs. Their efforts include comprehensive theoretical analysis that bridges
113   the theoretical foundations with practical applications, thus advancing our understanding of model
114   efficiency. Empirically, many of the aforementioned methods would require incremental pruning
115   followed by retraining to preserve satisfactory performance.
116
117   2.2   Q UANTIZATION
118
119
      Quantization has emerged as a pivotal technique for enhancing the efficiency of deep neural net-
      works (DNNs) (Sze et al., 2017; Frantar et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2024). Research in this domain
120
      generally follows two approaches: discontinuous-mapping quantization (Gupta et al., 2015; Hubara
121
      et al., 2018; Wu et al., 2018) and continuous-mapping quantization (Louizos et al., 2017; Ullrich
122   et al., 2017; Dong et al., 2022; Shayer et al., 2018; Roth & Pernkopf, 2018). Discontinuous quan-
123   tization involves a rounding function that projects full-precision weights onto a low-bit grid (Gupta
124   et al., 2015; Wu et al., 2018; Louizos et al., 2018a; Hubara et al., 2018; Courbariaux et al., 2015;
125   De Sa et al., 2018; Marchesi et al., 1993). To address the non-differentiability of discontinuous-
126   mapping quantization, researchers have adopted the straight through estimator (STE) to facilitate
127   backpropagation in networks with quantized, discrete weights (Courbariaux & Bengio, 2016; Cour-
128   bariaux et al., 2015; Hubara et al., 2018; Rastegari et al., 2016). However, the STE can generate
129   pseudo-gradients that may deviate weights from optimal values and increase training instability (Yin
130
      et al., 2019). Meanwhile, many researchers propose post-training quantization methods that have
      limited access to the training dataset (Wang et al., 2020a; Hubara et al., 2021; Li et al., 2021; Frantar
131
      & Alistarh, 2022; Frantar et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2024). BitSplit (Wang et al., 2020a) incremen-
132
      tally constructs quantized values using a squared error metric based on residual errors. In contrast,
133   AdaQuant (Hubara et al., 2021) utilizes STE for direct optimization. BRECQ (Li et al., 2021) in-
134   tegrates Fisher information into the optimization process and focuses on the joint optimization of
135   layers within individual residual blocks. Extending the Optimal Brain Surgeon (OBS) framework,
136   Exact Optimal Brain Quantization (OBQ) (Frantar & Alistarh, 2022) adapts second-order weight
137   pruning methods to quantization tasks. With the rise of LLMs demanding substantial computational
138   resources, GPTQ (Frantar et al., 2022) employs second-order information for error compensation
139   on calibration sets to speed up generative models. Additionally, AWQ (Lin et al., 2024) implements
140   activation-aware quantization, selectively bypassing the quantization of key weights.
141   In contrast to the discontinuous-mapping quantization, continuous-mapping quantization avoids
142   pseudo-gradients and thus would provide a more stable and accurate solution (Yin et al., 2019).
143   Various studies have established specific prior distributions to approximate the quantized discrete
144   distribution through variational learning (Ullrich et al., 2017; Louizos et al., 2017; Shayer et al.,
145   2018) and Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods (Roth & Pernkopf, 2018). However, these
146   methods either need manual setting of priors (Ullrich et al., 2017; Louizos et al., 2017; Shayer et al.,
147
      2018) or would increase memory footprint (Roth & Pernkopf, 2018). DGMS (Dong et al., 2022)
      is an automated quantization method that utilizes Gaussian Mixture that avoids the aforementioned
148
      problem. Our method is similar to the DGMS (Dong et al., 2022), but further, enhances the compres-
149
      sion rate by unifying pruning and quantization, and boosts performance by utilizing the property that
150   the Bayesian average of DNNs are particularly robust to the quantization noise (Wang et al., 2024).

169

      effect of DNNs weights (Han et al., 2015), DGMS (Dong et al., 2022) has proposed a trainable
170
      quantization method, where each weight is quantized to one of the representations in the adaptive
171
      quantization set QA = {µ1 , · · · , µK } where µk ∈ R is also trained within the overall optimization
172   process. Let θ denote the weights vector in RT indicating the set of weights, with T being the
173   total number of weights in the DNN. Rather than storing T full-precision weights, the weights
174   are quantized into a few discrete values (i.e., shared full precision value), and only a small index
175   indicating which shared value in QA is assigned is stored for each weight, where the T is the
176   total number of weights. This technique not only reduces memory footprint but also accelerates
177   DNN inference through caching and weight reuse (Dong et al., 2022; Han et al., 2015; Xiao et al.,
178   2019). Trivially, a smaller K results in higher quantization noise; on the other hand, when K
179   is as large as the total number of DNN weights, the quantization set QA can accurately replicate
180
      the full-precision DNN weights under appropriate settings. Note that inevitably, any quantization
      function introduces noise to the DNN weights, i.e., the gap between the full-precision number and
181
      its quantized value, hence harming the predictive performance. In addition, the discontinuity of
182
      the quantization mapping suffers from non-differentiability, posing difficulties to the optimization
183   process.

      3.2   VARIATIONAL L EARNING
186
187   Given the observed dataset D, a Bayesian procedure aims to infer from the true posterior distribution
188   π(θ|D) ∝ π(θ)p(D; θ), where π(θ) is the prior and p(D; θ) is the likelihood. Since the posterior is
189   usually intractable, variational inference (Jordan et al., 1999; Blei et al., 2017) tries to approximate
190   the true distribution by the closest member in terms of Kullback–Leibler (KL) divergence (Csiszár,
191   1975) from the variational family of distributions F:
192                                  q ∗ (θ) = arg min DKL (q(θ)||π(θ|D)) .                               (1)
193                                            q(θ)∈F
194
      The optimization (1) is equivalent to minimize the negative Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO) defined
195
      as:
196
                                 Ω = −Eq(θ) [log p(D; θ)] + DKL (q(θ)||π(θ)),                      (2)
197
198   where the first term in (2) is the expected log-likelihood which measures how well the variational
199
      distribution q(θ) aligns with the likelihood of the observed data. The expected log-likelihood usually
      cannot be integrated analytically and thus we employ a further soft-max approximation described
200
      in the later context. The second term works as regularization, and by setting a spike-and-slab prior
201
      distribution, it can promote and enforce sparsity in the weight distribution, encouraging the model
202   to favor sparse solutions.

      6   C ONCLUSION
576
577
      In this paper, we proposed a unified framework for compressing deep neural networks (DNNs) by
578   combining pruning and quantization into one integrated optimization process through variational
579   inferences. Our approach addresses the limitations of sequential pruning and quantization methods
580   by exploring a broader solution space, enabling more efficient compression with minimal perfor-
581   mance degradation. Additionally, by leveraging Bayesian model averaging which is robust to the
582   quantization noise, we enhance the model’s resilience to potential performance degradation. We
583   demonstrated the effectiveness of our method on multiple datasets, including CIFAR-10/100 and
584   SQuAD which supports that our method not only improves performance but also provides a more
585   robust solution for compressing modern DNNs. Our results outperform existing methods in both
586
      compression rates and accuracy retention, making it a promising direction for efficient model com-
      pression in resource-constrained environments.
587
588   In future work, we aim to conduct theoretical analysis to bridge the gap between theory guarantees
589   and empirical successes. We also plan to test our method on computationally demanding models,
590   such as large-scale language models.
