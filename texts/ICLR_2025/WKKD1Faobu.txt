                                                     A BSTRACT
037
038
               We introduce HouseCrafter, a novel approach that can lift a floorplan into a com-
               plete large 3D indoor scene (e.g., a house). Our key insight is to adapt a 2D
039
               diffusion model, which is trained on web-scale images, to generate consistent
040
               multi-view color (RGB) and depth (D) images across different locations of the
041            scene. Specifically, the RGB-D images are generated autoregressively in batches
042            along sampled locations derived from the floorplan. At each step, the diffusion
043            model conditions on previously generated images to produce new images at nearby
044            locations. The global floorplan and attention design in the diffusion model ensures
045            the consistency of the generated images, from which a 3D scene can be recon-
046            structed. Through extensive evaluation on the 3D-Front dataset, we demonstrate
047            that HouseCrafter can generate high-quality house-scale 3D scenes. Ablation stud-
048            ies also validate the effectiveness of different design choices. We will release our
049
               code and model weights.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
055
056
057   High-fidelity 3D environments are crucial for delivering truly immersive user experiences in AR,
058   VR, gaming, and beyond. Typically, this process has been labor-intensive, demanding meticulous
059   effort from skilled human artists and designers, especially for intricate indoor settings with numerous
060   furniture pieces and decorative objects. The development of automated tools for generating realistic
061   3D scenes can significantly improve this process, streamlining the creation of complex virtual
062   environments, which enables faster iteration cycles and empowers novice users to bring their creative
063   visions to life. Such tools hold immense potential across industries like architecture, interior design,
064
      and real estate, facilitating rapid visualization, iteration, and collaborative design.
065   Recent advances in denoising diffusion models (Ren et al., 2023; Ju et al., 2023) show great promise
066   toward developing 3D generative models using 3D data. In contrast to the abundant availability of
067   2D imagery (Schuhmann et al., 2022), 3D data requires intensive labor to create or acquire (Dai
068   et al., 2017; Chang et al., 2017; Fu et al., 2021; Ge et al., 2024; Behley et al., 2019; Yeshwanth et al.,
069   2023). Thus, using 2D generative models (Rombach et al., 2022; Saharia et al., 2022) is a promising
070
      direction for 3D generation. In Song et al. (2023); Tang et al. (2023), 2D diffusion models are used to
      texturize a given 3D scene with only the geometry. However, generating the untextured 3D scene as
071
      input for these methods is not trivial. Alternatively, 3D contents can be estimated based on generated
072
      multi-view observations (Liu et al., 2023b; Ye et al., 2023; Weng et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023c; Shi
073   et al., 2023b;a; Long et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024; 2023a; Kant et al., 2023; Szymanowicz et al., 2023;
074   Kant et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024; Zheng & Vedaldi, 2023; Hu et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2023;
075   Voleti et al., 2024). However, the majority of existing works focus on investigating object-centric
076   generation which has relatively simple camera positions and all images can be generated in one batch
077   due to the small scale. It is non-trivial to extend them for complex large-scale scene generation.
078
      To tackle 3D scene generation, text-to-image diffusion models are employed to create room panora-
079   mas (Song et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2023), offering visually appealing results. However, converting
080   these panoramas into 3D assets without additional information, e.g. geometry, is challenging. Other
081   works (Höllein et al., 2023; Chung et al., 2023; Shriram et al., 2024) obtain 3D assets of the scene
082   by continuously generating 2D images of the environment and projecting them to 3D space using
083   depth provided by monocular depth estimation models (Piccinelli et al., 2024; Ke et al., 2024). While
084   achieving good results on small-scale scenes which can be covered by a few views, these methods
085   struggle to scale up to bigger scenes, as they tend to produce repeated content and distorted geometry.
086   Instead of relying on textual descriptions, layout maps provide better global guidance for scene
087
      generation. Several studies have explored this approach at the room scale, demonstrating the benefits
      of incorporating layout information (Schult et al., 2023; Fang et al., 2023; Bahmani et al., 2023).
088
      However, extending this method to house-scale generation poses challenges, as the current strategy
089
      of generating all scene content in one batch becomes impractical for larger, more complex scenes.
090
091   In this paper, we present HouseCrafter, an autoregressive pipeline for house-scale 3D scene genera-
092   tion guided by 2D floorplans, as shown in Fig. 1. Our key insight is to adapt a powerful pre-trained
093
      2D diffusion model (Rombach et al., 2021) to generate multi-view consistent images across different
      places of the scene in an autoregressive manner to reconstruct the 3D house. Specifically, we sample
094
      a set of camera poses within the scene based on the given floorplan. A novel view synthesis model
095
      is developed to generate images at these poses in a batch-wise manner. For each batch, the model
096   takes the target poses and the already generated images at neighboring poses (initially empty) as
097   reference to generates images at the target poses, guided by the local views of the floorplan. With all
098   the generated images inside the house, we use the TSDF fusion (Zeng et al., 2017) to reconstruct the
099   scene, providing explicit meshes for downstream applications (e.g., in an AR/VR application). With
100   guidance from the floorplan, our method ensures global realism and consistency of images across
101   batches, leading to high-quality scene generation.
102   Unlike existing novel view synthesis approaches (Kong et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2023b; Hu et al., 2024;
103   Liu et al., 2023c), our proposed model incorporates depth into both the reference and the novel/target
104   views, where we consider both color and depth (RGB-D) images in the input and output. This design
105   choice offers two main advantages: (i) enhancing multi-view consistency within a single batch and
106   across different batches in the autoregressive RGB-D image generation process and (ii) facilitating
107   the final 3D scene reconstruction using the generated depth. Compared with previous approaches
      (Höllein et al., 2023; Chung et al., 2023), which suffer from depth scale ambiguity from monocular

      depth estimation models, our model outputs metric depth that can be directly used to reconstruct the
109   scene. It is worth noting that RGB-D novel view synthesis has also been explored in Hu et al. (2024).
110   However, their approach focuses on generating low-resolution depth maps for better object-centric

113
      We evaluate our model on the 3D-Front dataset Fu et al. (2021). Through our experiments, we
114
      demonstrate the effectiveness of our RGB-D novel view synthesis model in generating images at the
115   novel views that are consistent not only with the input reference views and floorplan, but also among

118
      In summary, our key contributions are summarized as follows.
119
120       • We introduce a novel method HouseCrafter, which can lift a 2D floorplan into a 3D house.
121         Compared with existing room-scale methods (Höllein et al., 2023; Bahmani et al., 2023), our
122         approach can generate globally consistent house-scale scenes.
123       • We present a RGB-D novel synthesis method, which takes nearby RGB-D images as reference to
124         generate a set of RGB-D images at novel views, guided by the floorplan. Compared to existing
125         RGB generation methods (Kong et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2024), our approach generates semantically
126         and geometrically consistent multi-view RGB-D images, enabling high-quality and efficient 3D
127         scene reconstruction.
128       • Through both quantitative and qualitative evaluations, we demonstrate that our approach can
129         generate globally coherent house-scale indoor scenes and faithful to the floorplan. Regarding the
130         generated images, we demonstrate the effectiveness of our model in producing images that are
131         faithful to both reference images and floorplan.

      3D Object Generation. Recent advancements in 2D image generation (Rombach et al., 2021;
136
      Blattmann et al., 2023) have inspired attempts to use diffusion models for 3D generation. Some
137
      works (Poole et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2023; Yi et al., 2024) optimize 3D representations (Mildenhall
138   et al., 2021; Kerbl et al., 2023) by leveraging the denoising capabilities of diffusion models. However,
139   these models struggle to maintain a single object instance across denoising updates and are unaware
140   of camera poses, limiting the quality of the optimized 3D representations.
141
      Alternatively, some works convert generated images into 3D models (Liu et al., 2023b; Ye et al., 2023;
142
      Weng et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023c; Shi et al., 2023b;a; Long et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024; 2023a; Kant
143
      et al., 2023; Szymanowicz et al., 2023; Kant et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024; Tochilkin et al., 2024;
144   Zheng & Vedaldi, 2023; Hu et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2023). Liu et al. (2023b) demonstrated that
145   diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2021) fine-tuned on large-scale object datasets (Deitke et al., 2023;
146   2024) can generate consistent multi-view RGB images, enabling 3D model reconstruction. Building
147   on this, subsequent research has focused on enhancing multi-view image quality by integrating 3D
148   representations (Yang et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023c; Kant et al., 2023; Weng et al., 2023; Shi et al.,
149   2023b; Liu et al., 2024; 2023a; Hu et al., 2024) or using cross-view attention (Zheng & Vedaldi,
150   2023; Blattmann et al., 2023; Kong et al., 2024; Shi et al., 2023b; Voleti et al., 2024).
151   Inspired by these approaches, we aim to generate multi-view images at the scene level. Our model
152   uses multi-view RGB-D images and 2D floorplan as conditions to generate new multi-view RGB-D
153   images. Integrating depth enhances multi-view consistency and provides explicit scene geometry
154   for 3D reconstruction. Unlike Kong et al. (2024), which only outputs multi-view RGB images, and
155   Hu et al. (2024), which denoises depth images with RGB latents, our model denoises both RGB and
156   depth images in the latent space. This maintains geometry awareness and produces high-resolution
157   depth images and high-quality 3D reconstructions, ensuring geometric and semantic consistency
158
      across views.

      with existing scenes, using monocular depth estimators to project 2D images into 3D space, but

      Layout-guided 3D Scene Generation. Complimenting to text, the layout provides the detailed
197
      position of objects in the scene. Early work (Vidanapathirana et al., 2021) is able to uplift a 2D

201

202

203

205   our work, uses 2D layout guidance to produce a 3D neural radiance field, enabling textured mesh

207   room-scale methods may not have. For examples, open spaces that combine the living room, kitchen,
208   and dining area, or cases where two rooms are connected by large transparent objects, such as glass
209   doors or windows, require a holistic view of the entire space. Our method effectively uses 2D layout
210   guidance to scale to larger scenes, such as entire houses.
211   Other works. Other approaches treat indoor scene generation as an object layout problem (Wen
212   et al., 2023; Feng et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024). These works focus on predicting floor layouts
213   and furniture placement using with language model, and retrieving suitable objects from a database.
214   Alternatively, Ge et al. (2024) create augmented layouts from templates, while others use procedure
215   generation (Deitke et al., 2022; Raistrick et al., 2024) These approaches complement our pipeline, as
      we can use predicted floorplans to generate the scene’s texture and geometry accordingly.

243   3.1                   G ENERATION P IPELINE
244
245   Our goal is to lift a 2D floorplan to a 3D scene that we can interact with, where explicit scene
246   representation is desired, e.g., in terms of meshes and textures. If we had enough 3D data, training a
247   generative model that outputs the desired 3D asset would be the most straightforward solution. In
248
      practice, 3D data is harder to acquire and thus far more scarce than 2D imagery. Therefore, in this
      paper, we resort to generating multi-view 2D observations of the scene first and then reconstructing
249
      it in 3D. It allows us to harness the powerful generative prior of recent advances in diffusion-based
250
      models that are trained using a large set of 2D images.
251
252   As shown in Fig. 2, we sample camera locations uniformly across the free space based on the
253   2D floorplan and then construct a connected graph from these locations (The details of location
254
      sampling and graph construction are in Appendices E). For each location, we define a set of camera
      orientations to cover the surrounding. The batches in the generation sequence are decided by the
255
      order of traversing the graph (e.g. breadth-first search). To generate the first batch, thanks to the
256
      classifer-free guidance (Ho & Salimans, 2022), we only take the floorplan as condition to generate
257   RGB-D images. When traversing the graph and encountering a node v whose images have not been
258   generated, we choose images at visited nodes within δr hops from v as reference views, and views at
259   unvisited nodes within δn hops from v that as novel views (details in Appendices F). After exhausting
260   these locations, we use TSDF fusion (Zeng et al., 2017) to reconstruct a detailed 3D vertex-colored
261   mesh from the generated RGB-D images.

      Given RGB and depth latents xnj and dnj of a novel view, instead of denoising them separately, we
289
      concatenate them along the channel dimension as znj = [xnj , dnj ] and denoise them jointly. In this way,
      the model can effectively fuse the information of RGB and depth images into a single representation
290
      to ensure the semantic consistency between them at a single view. We double the input and output
291
      channels of the UNet to accommodate znj . When we denoise a set of latents {znj }N   n
                                                                                         j=1 simultaneously,
292
      it ensures consistency across RGB and depth images both semantically and geometrically across
293
      different views and thus leads to higher-quality generation as shown in the experiments.
294
295   To leverage the frozen VAE for depth images, we process the depth image to have the 3 channels and
296   the same value range as RGB image. To obtain the depth latent, we replicate the depth image to 3
297
      channels, clip the depth to a preset of near and far planes (e.g. [0, 3] meters), then map to the range
      [−1, 1] before passing to the VAE encoder. From the depth latent, we decode it then average over
298
      3 channels before unnormalizing the value to the depth range. In this way our model can generate
299
      absolute depth within a pre-defined range. As long as the camera poses are dense enough, the whole
300   scene should be covered.

344   In addition to being faithful to the input floorplan, the generated RGB-D images should be consistent

      4.2.2    R ESULTS
496
497   We provide a detailed quantitative analysis in Fig. 7 and Table 1 and quanlitative comparisons in
498   Fig. 6, Fig. 13, and Fig. 14. Both human (Fig. 7) and automated (Table 1) evaluations show that
499   our method performs better in generating faithful results to the floorplan guidance. However, IS
500   greatly favors Text2Room and BlockFusion over our method and CC3D, while the users significantly
501   prefer our results regarding the visual quality of the generated mesh. We believe the higher IS of
502   Text2Room is due to the more diverse scenes generated by the text-to-image model (Rombach et al.,
503
      2022) trained on the web-scale dataset. BlockFusion also has high Inception Score, which is due to
      the more diverse texture obtained from diverse text prompts. Although our results are less diverse
504
      due to fine-tuneing on a smaller dataset, it can produce more realistic rooms with information from
505
      the floorplan, as recognized by users. The floorplan compliant evaluation result of BlockFusion is
506   low, despite the un-textured geometry appears reasonable (Fig. 14). We believe it is caused by the
507   textures produced by Meshy.

533   5     C ONCLUSION
534
535   In this work, we present HouseCrafter, a pipeline that transforms 2D floorplans into detailed 3D
536   spaces. We generate dense RGB-D images autoregressively and fuse them into a 3D mesh. Our key
537   innovation is an image-based diffusion model that produces multiview-consistent RGB-D images
538   guided by floorplan and reference RGB-D images. This capability enables the generating of house-
539   scale 3D scenes with high-quality geometry and texture, surpassing previous approaches which could
      only generate scenes at the room scale.

      A      D ETAILS OF F LOORPLAN C ONDITIONING
820
821
      For a novel view with the latent feature znj ∈ RC×H×W (where C is the feature dimension and
822
      H × W the spatial dimensions), we obtain the floorplan information lj ∈ RM ×C×H×W at the (latent)
823
      pixel-level by casting rays through the pixels and encoding semantic and geometric information at
824
      every intersection point between the projected ray and floorplan components.
825
826   Subsequently, we use cross-attention at the ray-level where each pixel feature the in znj is the query
827   and the floorplan features along the ray are the keys and values, meaning the attention for each ray
828
      is performed independently. To illustrate the operation we added the batch dimension B and use
      einops (Rogozhnikov, 2022) notation:
829
830                             znj ← rearrange(znj , B C H W → (B H W) 1 C)
831
                                 lj ← rearrange(lj , B N C H W → (B H W) N C)
832
833                             znj ← MHA(q = znj , k = lj , v = lj )
834                             znj ← rearrange(znj , (B H W) 1 C → B C H W ),
835
836
      where MHA() is multihead attention layer. The floorplan information injection is applied in the first
      block of each feature level in the UNet blocks of the base diffusion model. Since each level operates
837
      at a different resolution, this process effectively injects the encoded floorplan at multiple scales.
838
839   In the design described above, we choose to inject into each pixel the information from a single ray.
840   Although alternatively, a receptive field with kernel size K > 1 can provide more spatial information,
841   the quadratic growth O(K 2 ) of the sequence length of keys and values is expensive for the attention
842
      operation. We argue that local information exchange between pixels is effectively managed by the
      network’s convolution layers, eliminating the need for a larger kernel size. Furthermore, attention
843
      to intersection points from a single ray omits the need for 3D positional encoding, which relies on
844
      arbitrary world coordinates, as the ray’s depth alone distinguishes these points. In addition to depth,
845   we also incorporate height relative to the floor, which helps the model identify visible objects and is
846   easy to compute, given that the ’up’ direction is a well-defined canonical reference in indoor scenes.
847

849   B     BASELINE FOR FLOORPLAN ENCODING
850
851   We experimented with a baseline embedding approach that does not explicitly use the geometry
852   information. Each object in the scene is represented by a vector (encoded with object category and
853   2D bounding box). These object vectors control the image content through the cross-attention with
854
      image tokens. For each view, the objects are filtered using camera frustum.
855   In the baseline method, each image token is given all the objects in the view’s candidate objects, and
856   it is up to the model to learn which object is visible in the region of the image token. In contrast, our
857   method exploits the camera model to obtain ray’s candidate objects for each image token, hence
858   reducing the candidate list and simplifying the learning task.
859   Compared to the baseline, the proposed method has higher precision (mAP @25) and recall (AP @25)
860   in layout detection, which shows the effectiveness of our proposed method. However, the multi-view
861   consistency evaluation of the proposed method is relatively lower than the baseline, especially the
862   depth (Table 4). We hypothesize that the higher multiview consistency of the baseline is due to the
863   simpler contents in the views as its recall in the layout evaluation is significantly lower, suggesting
      more objects are absent from the scenes.

984    H.2    F LOORPLAN E VALUATION
985
       The floorplan evaluation protocol is the “inverse” of HouseCrater where we predict the top-down
986    2D bounding boxes of objects in the generated scene. The predicted 2D bounding boxes are then
987    compared with 2D boxes from the given floorplan using mean Average Precision at the intersection-
988    over-union threshold of 0.25, mAP@25. Specifically, we use ODIN (Jain et al., 2024), a 3D instance
989    segmentation method that takes multi-view posed RGB-D images as input and predicts instance
990    segmentation of the point cloud accumulated from input images. Then, top-down 2D boxes are
991    extracted from the segmented instances. As a scene may have up to 2000 images based on its size,
992    we cannot pass all the images to ODIN at once. Instead, these images are partitioned by room, we
993    do segmentation per room. This strategy does not affect the evaluation results since an object in
994
       the scene do not span in more than one room. We finetune ODIN on 3D-Front dataset to make the
       segmentation results more reliable since both HouseCrafter and CC3D are trained on this dataset.

       We conduct a user study to evaluate the results produced by Text2Room, CC3D, and our method. In
999    the study, we ask 12 participants to rate the results in a pair-wise manner. Specifically, we present

1011
1012   We initialize our model from StableDiffusion v1.5 (Rombach et al., 2021). For the first layer
1013
       of the UNet, we duplicate the pre-trained weights and divide the weights by two to accommodate
       the depth’s latent and to reduce the change of the output scale. For the last layer of the Unet, we
1014
       only duplicate the pre-trained weights. The model is trained for 15, 000 iterations in 2 days with an
1015
       effective batch size of 256 (4 samples per GPU ×8 GPUs ×8 gradient accumulation steps). Each
1016   data sample contains 3 reference views and 3 novel views with the resolution of 256. We use Adam
1017   optimizer with a learning rate of 10−4 . All training is conducted on a machine with 8 A6000 48GB

       J     L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE D IRECTIONS
1022
       Our work is the first that can generate textured meshes of 3D scenes at the house-scale, and yet
1023
       without limitations, allowing intriguing future directions.
1024
1025   First, the employed TSDF fusion method produces reasonable results in fusing generated RGB-D
       images and robust their inconsistency. However, it cannot model the view-dependent color, baking


                                                          19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
       the lighting effect into the mesh texture, and thus giving unsatisfactory results. To address this issue,
1027   a reconstruction method that is robust to the inconsistency of generated multi-view images and able
1028   to model view-dependent color is required.
1029
1030
       Second, while using image generation models gives the advantages of using large-scale image data
       as prior for 3D generation, the current pipeline has a lot of redundancy from the high overlap of
1031
       multiview images. Thus an effective poses sampling strategy that can balance the view overlap for
1032
       consistency and efficiency is a promising direction.
1033
1034   Lastly, in our proposed method of injecting the floorplan guidance to the generation process, only the
1035   geometry and semantics of the object are leveraged, while the information about the object instance
1036
       is omitted. We believe that instance-awareness can give better scene understanding thus generating
       scene more faithful to the floorplan.

1224   locations based on their distance (2); and then connecting the connected components to a connected
1225
       graph per room (3). In the last step, we use the door locations to connect the room graphs (4).
       Specifically, for each door, we add an edge between the nearest locations in the two adjacent rooms.
1226
       By creating graphs at the room scale then connecting them using the door location, we avoid making
1227

1228   of each step (2,3,4) are highlighted in white.
