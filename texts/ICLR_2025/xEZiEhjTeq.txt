009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep learning involves navigating a high-dimensional parameter space guided
012            by the loss landscape. In the process, complex computational structures form
013            and re-form inside the neural network, leading to shifts in input–output behavior.
014            It is a priority for the science of deep learning to uncover principles governing
015            the development of neural network structure and behavior. Drawing from the
016            framework of singular learning theory, we propose that model development is
017            governed by the local geometry of the loss landscape. We investigate this link by
018
               monitoring the geometry of the loss landscape throughout training for transformers
               trained as language models or for a synthetic in-context regression task. We divide
019
               training into “developmental stages” marking discrete shifts in loss landscape
020
               geometry. We then confirm that these stages coincide with significant changes in
021            the internal computational structure and the input–output behavior of our models.
022            Our findings provide new insights into transformer development and underscore
023            the potential of a geometric perspective for understanding modern deep learning.
024

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   In modern deep learning, a striking phenomenon is the event of sudden shifts in a model’s internal
029   computational structure and associated changes in generalization behavior (e.g., Wei et al., 2022;
030   Olsson et al., 2022; McGrath et al., 2022). As large models become more deeply integrated into
031
      real-world applications, understanding this phenomenon is a priority for the science of deep learning.
032   While the scale and importance of this phenomenon is new, the idea that neural network learning
033   proceeds in discrete developmental stages goes back decades (Raijmakers et al., 1996). In the case
034   of deep linear networks, we know that training proceeds through stages in which the model learns
035   progressively higher-rank approximations of the data distribution (Baldi and Hornik, 1989; Rogers
036   and McClelland, 2004; Saxe et al., 2019). Unfortunately, it is not clear how this perspective on
037
      stagewise development can be generalized to large nonlinear models such as transformers which
      exhibit more complex internal computational structure and are trained on more complex datasets.
038
039   We propose that the key to understanding stagewise development is the geometry of the loss landscape.
040   This perspective on neural network development is motivated by Singular Learning Theory (SLT;
041   Watanabe, 2009). SLT proves that, for Bayesian neural networks, the posterior develops in discrete
042   developmental stages in a so-called singular learning process governed by the local geometry of the
043
      model likelihood (Watanabe, 2009; Chen et al., 2023). While there are many differences between
      Bayesian deep learning and modern neural network training, we believe that the link between local
044
      geometry and stagewise development is fundamental to learning.
045
046   In this paper, we contribute a thorough empirical investigation into the relationship between loss
047   landscape geometry and stagewise development. In particular, we train transformers in two distinct
048   learning settings, and we demonstrate changes in their loss landscape geometry can be associated with
049
      changes in their internal computational structure and input–output behavior. This finding provides
      evidence that loss landscape geometry is closely linked to neural network development in modern
050
      deep learning.

081   In more detail, we propose the following geometry-based stage identification methodology:
082
            1. Transformer training (Section 2): We train transformers in two learning settings, namely
083
               language modeling, a transformer with around 3M parameters modeling a subset of the
084            Pile (Gao et al., 2020; Xie et al., 2023), and in-context linear regression, a transformer with
085            around 50k parameters modeling synthetic regression data following Garg et al. (2022).
086
            2. Local geometry tracking (Section 3): We quantify the local geometry of the loss landscape
087
               at frequent checkpoints by estimating the Local Learning Coefficient (LLC; Lau et al., 2024),
088            a measure of local geometric complexity derived from SLT (Watanabe, 2009).
089
            3. Geometry-based stage division (Section 4): Motivated by the singular learning process in
090
               Bayesian inference (Watanabe, 2009; Chen et al., 2023), we search for critical points in the
091
               LLC curve across training time and use these as boundaries to divide training into stages.
092
093
            4. Stage validation (Sections 5 and 6): To show that these divisions are meaningful, we track
               shifts in each model’s internal computational structure and input–output behavior across
094
               checkpoints, quantified using various setting-specific metrics.
095
096   Following this methodology reveals that each transformer’s training can be clearly divided into
097   several developmental stages (see Figure 1). Moreover, we find that these divisions are meaningful in
098   that the stages coincide with significant, interpretable shifts in the internal computational structure
099   and input–output behavior of the models (Sections 5 and 6).
100
      These results have several implications, discussed further in Section 8. Both our methodology for
101
      stage identification as well as the specific stages we identify for these transformers contribute to
102   the growing literature on understanding transformer behavior, structure, and training dynamics.
103   Furthermore, we find several stages in which the loss landscape geometry becomes increasingly
104   degenerate, indicating the model becoming simpler—a phenomenon not predicted by prior models of
105   saddle-to-saddle dynamics. Finally, the fact that simply monitoring the local geometry of the loss
106   landscape reveals meaningful developmental stages for modern transformer models in two distinct
107   learning settings points to a fundamental link between local geometry and development, and the
      crucial role geometry has the potential to play in advancing our understanding of deep learning.

164   We track the evolution of degeneracy in the local geometry of the loss landscape throughout training
165   by estimating the Local Learning Coefficient (LLC; Watanabe, 2009; Lau et al., 2024) at model
166   checkpoints. In this section we review the LLC and the estimation procedure of Lau et al. (2024).
167
168   The Local Learning Coefficient (LLC). Given a local minimum w∗ of the population loss ℓ, the
169   LLC of w∗ , denoted λ(w∗ ), is a positive rational number measuring the amount of degeneracy in the
170   geometry of ℓ near w∗ (Watanabe, 2009; Lau et al., 2024). Intuitively, the geometry is more degenerate
171   (lower LLC) at a parameter w∗ if there are more ways in which w can be varied near w∗ such that
172   ℓ(w) remains equal to ℓ(w∗ ). More formally, the LLC of a local minimum w∗ of the population loss

      ℓ (a negative log likelihood) can be defined based on the asymptotics of the volume V (ϵ) of the set

      4    T HE SINGULAR LEARNING PROCESS AND STAGEWISE DEVELOPMENT
217
218   We use critical points (that is, plateaus, where the first derivative vanishes) in the LLC curve to define
219   stage boundaries that divide training into developmental stages. This approach to stage identification
220   is motivated by the singular learning process in Bayesian inference. In this section we review the
221   singular learning process and outline our stage identification approach in more detail.
222
223   The singular learning process. Watanabe’s free energy formula (Watanabe, 2018, Theorem 11),
224   generalized to a local setting by Chen et al. (2023), gives an asymptotic expansion in the number of
225   samples n of the Bayesian free energy Fn of some neighborhood W ∗ surrounding a local minimum
226   of ℓ, w∗ ∈ W ∗ , in terms of the empirical loss ℓn , the LLC λ, and the local multiplicity m:
227                 Fn (W ∗ ) = nℓn (w∗ ) + λ(w∗ ) log n − (m(w∗ ) − 1) log log n + Op (1).                (4)
228   The free energy of a neighborhood is related to the integral of the Bayesian posterior on the neigh-
229   borhood by a negative logarithm, so the lower the free energy the more likely the neighborhood is
230   according to Bayes’ rule (the prior contributes only in the sub-leading terms as long as it is positive).
231   The coefficients of the linear and logarithmic terms are the empirical loss (a negative log likelihood)
232   and the LLC, respectively. This creates a trade-off between accuracy (ℓn ) and degeneracy (λ) that
233   changes as n increases. At certain critical dataset sizes the neighborhood having the lowest free
234   energy may rapidly change, causing the Bayesian posterior to suddenly “jump” from concentrating
235   around one local minima to another (illustrated in Figure 3). The sequence of discrete posterior jumps
236   in Bayesian inference is referred to as the singular learning process (Watanabe, 2009, §7.6).

253   LLC plateaus separate developmental stages. The connection between the singular learning
254   process in Bayesian inference and stagewise development in deep learning is not understood in
255   general, but has been studied in small autoencoders by Chen et al. (2023). Chen et al. (2023)
256   showed that in these models both Bayesian inference and stochastic gradient descent undergo sudden
257   transitions between various encoding schemes, reflected as sudden changes in the estimated LLC, as
258   predicted by Watanabe’s free energy formula.
259   This perspective suggests that changes in the local geometry of the population loss, as measured by the
260   LLC, reflect qualitative changes in the network parameter. However, in larger models, we expect these
261   changes to be more gradual. Accordingly, instead of phase transitions, we speak of developmental
262   stages separated by stage boundaries at which the posterior is stably concentrated around a given
263   local minima. What remains is that plateaus in the estimated LLC curve indicate boundaries between
264   distinct qualitative changes in the network parameter. This motivates our approach of using such
265   plateaus to divide training into developmental stages.
266   In our experiments, we identify plateaus in the estimated LLC over checkpoints as follows. We first
267   lightly smooth the LLC curve with a Gaussian process (to facilitate stable numerical differentiation).
268   We then numerically differentiate the smoothed curve with respect to log training time. We identify
269   plateaus as approximate zeros of this derivative, namely local minima of the absolute value of the
      derivative that fall below a small threshold. Appendix A.5 discusses this procedure in more detail.

295   5     R ESULTS FOR LANGUAGE MODELING
296
297   Plateaus in LLC estimates (Figure 1a) reveal five developmental stages over the training of our
298   language transformers. We associate these stages with the development of bigrams (LM1), n-grams
299   (LM2), previous-token heads (LM3), and induction heads (LM4). There may be other interesting
300   developmental changes; we do not claim this list is exhaustive. We did not, for example, discover
301   significant changes in stage LM5.
302
      It was previously known that small attention-only transformer language models learn bigrams (0-layer
303   models), skip-trigrams (1-layer models), and induction circuits (2-layer models) (Olsson et al., 2022).
304   This represents a progression of internal structure in the final trained transformer, as a function of
305   increasing depth. An original contribution of the present paper is to clearly identify the following
306   progression of developmental stages over training in this small language model:
307
                     Bigrams → n-grams → Previous-token heads → Induction heads.

430   Stage LR4 differs from LR3 in the layer normalization collapse expanding from the unembedding to
431   earlier layer norms, particularly the layer normalization before the first attention block. This affects a
      smaller fraction of weights than the unembedding collapse (Appendix C.4.4).

      7   R ELATED WORK
433
434
435   Loss landscape geometry in deep learning. Given the central role played by the loss landscape
436
      in deep learning it is unsurprising that there have been many attempts to study its geometry. One
      approach is to visualize low-dimensional slices of the loss landscape (Erhan et al., 2010; Goodfellow
437
      et al., 2014; Lipton, 2016; Li et al., 2018; Notsawo et al., 2024). Unfortunately, a random slice
438
      is with high probability a quadratic form associated to nonzero eigenvalues of the Hessian and is
439   thus biased against geometric features that we know are important, such as degeneracy. Antognini
440   and Sohl-Dickstein (2018) have emphasized the difficulty of probing the loss landscape of neural
441   networks with dimensionality reduction tools.
442
      Standard methods of quantifying the geometry of the loss landscape, such as by studying the Hessian
443
      itself, also fail to quantify important aspects of degeneracy. For example, the Hessian trace or
444
      maximum eigenvalues quantify the curvature of a critical point but ignore its degenerate dimensions.
445   In Figure 6b and Appendix C.2.2 we show that these metrics are unable to detect most of the stage
446   boundaries detected by the LLC in our in-context linear regression setting. The Hessian rank accounts
447   for degenerate directions but fails to distinguish between directions by the order of their degeneracy.
448   In contrast, the LLC is a quantitative measure of loss landscape geometry that directly accounts for
449   different kinds of degeneracy (see Appendix A.1).
450
451
      Stagewise development and geometry in nonlinear dynamics and developmental biology. Neu-
452
      ral network training is a stochastic dynamical system, in which the governing potential (the population
453   loss) encodes the structure of the data distribution along with the constraints of the network archi-
454   tecture. It is well-understood in nonlinear dynamics that the local geometry of a potential can give
455   rise to stagewise development of structure in the system (Waddington, 1957; Thom, 1988, see also
456   Franceschelli, 2010). This connection between geometry and stagewise development has been ob-
457   served in biological systems at significant scale and in the presence of stochasticity (Freedman et al.,
458   2023). We have emphasized changes in geometry over a stage whereas in developmental biology
459   the focus, in the mathematical framework of bifurcation theory, is more on the singular geometry at
460   stage boundaries (Rand et al., 2021; MacArthur, 2022; Sáez et al., 2022).
461
462   “Developmental stages” versus “phase transitions.” Many works on emergent phenomena use
463   the term “phase transition” to label a rapid change in model structure or behavior. Our developmental
464   stages can also be modeled as phase transitions (between phases inhabited by the model at stage
465   boundaries). We adopt the terminology from biology to avoid confusion, since in some cases our
466   developmental stages occur over a large number of training steps, whereas the term “phase transition”
467   usually connotes a rapid change. However, we note that phase transitions need not be rapid in time:
468   from a mathematical point of view (Gilmore, 1981; Chen et al., 2023), a phase transition is a shift in
469
      the configuration of a system (or a distribution over such configurations) from the neighborhood of
      one critical point of a potential to that of another, occurring rapidly as a function of some control
470
      variable. The control variable may be time, but it need not be. In developmental biology, there are
471
      carefully modeled phase transitions that take place over days in real time, but rapidly with respect to
472   an inferred developmental time (Freedman et al., 2023).

475   8   D ISCUSSION
476
477
      In this paper, we have contributed a detailed examination of the development of transformer models
478
      in two distinct learning settings. We quantified the changing degeneracy of the geometry of the
479
      population loss throughout transformer training by estimating the local learning coefficient (LLC).
480   Based on an analogy introduced by Chen et al. (2023) between deep learning and the singular
481   learning process, we divided these training runs into developmental stages at critical points of the
482   LLC curve. We also monitored the internal computational structure and input–output behavior of our
483   transformers throughout training, as quantified through a range of setting-specific metrics. We found
484   an approximate correspondence between developmental stages identified based on changes in loss
485   landscape degeneracy and significant structural and behavioral changes taking place in each model.
      In this section, we discuss several implications of these findings.


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Towards a geometric understanding of deep learning. Our main finding is an approximate
487   correspondence between developmental stages identified via loss landscape geometry and significant
488   structural and behavioral changes identified via setting-specific analyses. This correspondence is
489   evidence that the development of our transformers is closely linked to the geometry of the loss
490   landscape, underscoring the potential of loss landscape geometry, and particularly its degeneracy as
491   quantified by the LLC, as a crucial lens through which to study the development of deep learning
492   models. While we have illustrated this correspondence in two distinct learning settings, including
493   language modeling with a nontrivial transformer architecture, it remains necessary for future work to
494   verify this link for larger-scale models across a more diverse range of emergent phenomena.
495
496   Developmental interpretability. We have shown that a geometry-based stage identification method-
497
      ology can reveal meaningful changes in our transformers. We note that our analysis is not exhaustive.
      Indeed, we expect that in general only certain “macroscopic” changes, such as the emergence of
498
      in-context learning, will be delineated by plateaus in the estimated LLC. Building on our approach,
499
      Wang et al. (2024) have shown promising results by measuring the LLC with respect to certain
500   network components and with different data distributions, providing a refined picture of model
501   development. We are optimistic that further work in this direction will lead to geometry-based tools
502   that can offer insights into the development of larger models.
503
      Monitoring model development through loss landscape geometry offers an alternative to monitoring
504
      progress measures such as those derived by Barak et al. (2022) or developed using mechanistic
505
      insights from similar models by Nanda et al. (2023). Both approaches can reveal developments
506   not visible in the loss curve. Monitoring loss landscape geometry is setting-agnostic and able to
507   detect developments before having mechanistically understood the end result. Of course, once a
508   development is detected through a change in geometry, it remains to interpret what has changed.
509
510   Cases studies in transformer development. The specific developments we observe in each setting
511   contribute to the growing empirical literature on the emergence of in-context learning in transformers.
512   We replicate the emergence of induction heads in two-layer attention-only transformer language
513   models (Elhage et al., 2021; Olsson et al., 2022). Moreover, we show that before induction heads
514   form our transformer adopts simpler strategies, following a progression akin to that found by Olsson
515   et al. (2022) for fully-developed models with increasing depth, or that found by Edelman et al. (2024)
516
      within the development of a single transformer in a Markovian sequence modeling setting.
517   For in-context linear regression, we see that before in-context learning emerges, the model predicts
518   all outputs as roughly zero (the optimal context-independent prediction). Moreover, while the
519   transformer can partially adapt to out-of-distribution regression tasks when in-context learning first
520   emerges, this capability deteriorates in later stages. This deterioration is possibly an artifact of the
521   “forgetting” phenomenon studied by Panwar et al. (2024).
522
523
      Development and model complexity. While we have introduced the LLC as a measure of geometric
      degeneracy, it can also be understood as a measure of model complexity (cf. Appendix A.1). It is quite
524
      natural that certain changes in a model’s internal computational structure would show up as a change
525
      in complexity. For example, Chen et al. (2024) showed that the emergence of a certain structure in a
526   transformer coincided with a spike in two model complexity measures, namely the model’s Fisher
527   information and the intrinsic dimension (Facco et al., 2017) of the model’s embeddings.
528
529
      A noteworthy aspect of our experiments are stages in which the LLC decreases, corresponding to a
      simplification of the internal computational structure of the model. As an empirical phenomenon, such
530
      model simplification has precedent, for instance with Chen et al. (2024) and the recent literature on
531
      grokking (Power et al., 2022; Nanda et al., 2023; Notsawo et al., 2024). In our case, the mechanistic
532   nature of the simplification is not yet clear, with the collapse of layer normalization, embedding, and
533   attention patterns arising as candidates in the in-context linear regression setting.
534
535
      This phenomenon is noteworthy because it is currently not accounted for by theories of neural network
      development. In the theory of saddle-to-saddle dynamics, deep linear networks learn progressively
536
      more complex approximations of the data (Saxe et al., 2019). The singular learning process as
537
      outlined in Section 4 also describes transitions for which the LLC increases, though decreasing the
538   LLC while holding the loss constant would be another way to decrease the free energy according to
539   equation (4). Providing a theoretical account of these developmental stages is an open problem.

721   A.1   I NTERPRETATIONS AND EXAMPLES OF THE LLC
722
723   In Section 3, we introduced the LLC as a quantification of geometric degeneracy. In this section,
724   we discuss an additional perspectives on the LLC as a count of the “effective” dimensionality of a
725   parameter, and we give additional examples of the LLC. We refer the reader to Watanabe (2009) and
726   Lau et al. (2024) for more discussion.
727   The LLC has some similarity to an effective parameter count. If the population loss ℓ looks like a
728   quadratic form near w∗ then λ(w∗ ) = d2 is half the number of parameters, which we can think of as
729   d contributions of 12 from every independent quadratic direction. If there are only d − 1 independent
730   quadratic directions, and one coordinate wi such that small variations in wi near wi∗ do not change
731   the model relative to the truth (this dimension is “unused”) then λ(w∗ ) = d−12 .
732
      The situation becomes more intricate when certain dimensions are degenerate but not completely
733
      unused, varying to quartic or higher order near the parameter (rather than being quadratic or flat).
734
      While every unused coordinate reduces the LLC by 12 , changing the dependency on a coordinate
735
      from quadratic (wi2 ) to quartic (wi4 ) (increasing its degeneracy while still “using” it) reduces the
736
      contribution to the LLC from 12 to 14 .
737
738   As a source of intuition, we provide several examples of exact LLCs:
739
             • ℓ(w1 , w2 , w3 ) = aw12 + bw22 + cw32 with a, b, c > 0. This function is nondegenerate, and
740
               λ(0, 0, 0) = 12 + 21 + 12 = 32 . This is independent of a, b, c. That is, the LLC λ does not
741
               measure curvature. For this reason, it is better to avoid an intuition that centers on “basin
742            broadness” since this tends to suggest that lowering a, b, c should affect the LLC.
743
             • ℓ(w1 , w2 , w3 ) = w12 + w22 + 0 in R3 is degenerate, but its level sets are still submanifolds
744
               and λ(0, 0, 0) = 21 + 12 . In this case the variable w3 is unused, and so does not contribute to
745
               the LLC.
746
             • ℓ(w1 , w2 , w3 ) = w12 + w24 + w34 is degenerate and its level sets are, for our purposes, not
747
               submanifolds. The singular function germ (ℓ, 0) is an object of algebraic geometry, and the
748
               appropriate mathematical object is not a manifold or a variety but a scheme. The quartic
749            terms contribute 14 to the LLC, so that λ(0, 0, 0) = 21 + 14 + 14 = 1. The higher the power
750            of a variable, the greater the degeneracy and the lower the LLC.
751
752   Figure 2 offers several additional examples, from left to right:
753

             • A quadratic potential ℓ1 (w1 , w2 ) = w12 + w22 , for which the LLC is maximal in two

             • A quartic potential ℓ2 (w1 , w2 ) = w14 + w24 , for which the LLC is λ2 (0, 0) = 1/2.

793   A.3    LLC ESTIMATES AWAY FROM LOCAL MINIMA
794
      Our methodology for detecting stages is to apply LLC estimation to compute λ̂(w∗ ) at neural network
795
      parameters w∗ = wt across training. In the typical case these parameters will not be local minima of
796
      the population loss, violating the theoretical conditions under which the LLC is defined.
797
798   It is not surprising that the estimator appears to work if w∗ is approximately a local minima. (Lau et al.,
799   2024) validated their estimator at both parameters constructed to be local minima of the population
800
      loss and also at parameters found through training with stochastic gradient descent (possibly not
      local minima of the empirical loss, let alone the population loss). They showed that in both cases the
801
      estimator recovers the true learning coefficient associated with the global minimum of the population
802
      loss. On the other hand, if w∗ is far from any local minima, it is a priori quite surprising that the
803   SGLD-based estimation procedure works at all, as in this situation one might expect the chains to
804   explore directions in which the loss decreases.
805
806
      Nevertheless, Chen et al. (2023) found that, empirically, LLC estimation away from local minima
      appears to give sensible results in practice. In our case, with sufficient localization we see stable
807
      estimates throughout training.

      A.5        S TAGE DISCOVERY
857
858   To identify stage boundaries, we look for plateaus in the LLC: checkpoints at which the slope of λ̂(wt )
859   over t vanishes. To mitigate noise in the LLC estimates, we first fit a Gaussian process with some
860   smoothing to the LLC-over-time curve. Then we numerically calculate the slope of this Gaussian
861   process with respect to log t. The logarithm corrects for the fact that the learning coefficient, like the
862   loss, changes less as training progresses. We identify stage boundaries by looking for checkpoints at
863   which this estimated slope equals zero. The results of this procedure are depicted in Figure B.2 for
      language and Figure C.1 for linear regression.


                                                                             16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      At a local minima or maxima of the estimated LLC curve identifying a plateau from this estimated
865   slope is straightforward, since the derivative crosses the x-axis. However at a saddle point, the slope
866   may not exactly reach zero, so we have to specify a “tolerance” for the absolute value of the derivative,
867   below which we treat the boundary as an effective plateau.
868
      In this case, we additionally require that the plateau be at a local minimum of the absolute first
869
      derivative. Otherwise, we may identify several adjacent points as all constituting a stage boundary.
870
871   To summarize, identifying stage boundaries is sensitive to the following choices: the intervals between
872   checkpoints, the amount of smoothing, whether to differentiate with respect to t or log t, and the
873   choice of tolerance. However, once a given choice of these hyperparameters is fixed, stages can be
874
      automatically identified, without further human judgment.

955   B.3.2    n- GRAM SCORES
956
957   In stage LM2 we consider n-grams, which are sequences of n consecutive tokens, meaning 2-grams
958   and bigrams are the same. Specifically, we consider common n-grams, which is defined heuristically
959   by comparing our 5,000 vocab size tokenizer with the full GPT-2 tokenizer. We use the GPT-2
960   tokenizer as our heuristic because its vocabulary is constructed iteratively by merging the most
961   frequent pairs of tokens.
962   We first tokenize the tokens in the full GPT-2 vocabulary to get a list of 50,257 n-grams for various n.
963   The first 5,000 such n-grams are all 1-grams, after which 2-grams begin appearing, then 3-grams,
964   4-grams, and so on (where 2-grams and 3-grams may still continue to appear later in the vocabulary).
965   We then define the set of common n-grams as the first 1,000 n-grams that appear in this list for a
966   fixed n, n ≥ 2.
967   If we track the performance on n-grams and see it improve, we may ask whether this is simply a
968   function of the model learning to use more context in general, rather than specifically improving
969   on the set of n-grams being tracked. We measure performance against this baseline by defining an
970   n-gram score. For a fixed n, we obtain the average loss ℓngram of the model on predicting the final
971   tokens of our set of 1,000 n-grams and also obtain the average loss ℓntest of the model on a validation
      set at position n of each validation sequence. The n-gram score is then defined to be ℓntest /ℓngram .

1002
1003   In Figure B.3, we measure the effect of the positional embedding on model performance by comparing
1004   the model’s performance at particular context positions on a validation set over the course of training
1005   against performance on the same validation set but with the positional embedding zero-ablated. The
1006   full context length is 1024, and we measure test loss at positions 1, 2, 3, 5, 10, 20, 30, 50, 100, 200,
1007   300, 500, and 1000. In the transition from stage LM1 to LM2, the model begins using the learnable
1008   positional embedding to improve performance. The difference between test loss with and without the
1009   positional ablation is negligible at all measured positions until the LM1–LM2 boundary.
1010   Structurally, we might predict that the positional embeddings should organize themselves in a
1011   particular way: in order to understand relative positions, adjacent positions should be embedded close
1012   to each other, and far-away positions should be embedded far apart.
1013
       In Figure B.4, we examine the development of the positional embedding itself over time from two
1014   angles. The first is to take the embeddings of each position in the context and to run PCA on those
1015   embeddings. The result is that as training progresses, the positional embedding PCAs gradually
1016   resolve into Lissajous curves, suggesting that the positional embeddings might look like a random
1017   walk (Antognini and Sohl-Dickstein, 2018; Shinn, 2023). However, if we look to the explained
1018   variance, we see that it grows very large for PC1, reaching 94.2% at training step 6400. This is much
1019   higher than we would expect for Brownian motion, where we expect to see about 61% explained
1020   variance in PC1 (Antognini and Sohl-Dickstein, 2018).
1021   The second perspective we use is to look at how the magnitudes of positional embeddings over the
1022   context length develop. In this case, we observe that the magnitudes seem to have a fairly regular
1023   structure. In conjunction with the PCAs and explained variance, we might infer that the positional
1024   embeddings look approximately like a (possibly curved) line in dmodel = 256 dimensional space. A
1025   positional embedding organized in this way would make it easier for an attention head to attend to
       multiple recent tokens, which is necessary if a single head is to learn n-grams.

1148
1149   The prefix matching score from Olsson et al. (2022) is defined similarly to the previous-token
1150   matching score. Given a sequence [A][B] . . . [A], the prefix matching score of a particular attention
1151
       head is how much the attention head attends back to the first instance of [A] when at the second
       instance of [A].
1152
1153   We compute this score using a synthetic data-generating process. We first generate 10k fixed random
1154   sequences of length 128. The first token is always a special “beginning of string" token and the [A]
1155   and [B] tokens are selected and placed randomly. One [A] token is placed in the first half of the
1156   sequence, the other is placed in the second half, and the [B] token is placed directly after the first [A]
1157
       token. The remaining tokens are randomly sampled from the tokenizer vocabulary, excluding the [A],
       [B], and beginning of string tokens.
1158
1159   For each sample in this synthetic dataset, we measure the attention score that each attention head
1160   assigns to the earlier instance of [A] from the latter instance of [A]. These scores are averaged across
1161   the dataset to produce the prefix matching score for that attention head at a given checkpoint. The
1162   progression of prefix matching scores over time can be seen in Figure 4b.

       C.3.2         ICL
1335
1336
       We consider two variants of the ICL score: ICL1:D , and ICLD:K .
1337
1338   If the noise term σ 2 equals zero and both tasks t and inputs xk are normalized (i.e., t ∈ S D−1 ),
1339   then D − 1 observations of input-output pairs are enough to precisely identify t. Therefore, ICL1:D
1340
       measures how successful the model is at initially locating the task. The fact that the tasks and inputs
       are not normalized changes this only slightly: the task will still sit near S D−1 within a shell of
1341
       vanishing thickness as D → ∞.
1342
1343   Once localized, ICLD:K measures how successfully the model refines its internal estimate of t with
1344   additional examples, which it can use to reduce the error due to noise.
1345   In terms of implementation, it’s not necessary for the model to internally make a distinction between
1346   locating and refining its estimate of the task. For example, ridge regression makes no distinction.
1347   Still, we find it useful for reasoning about the progression of the model. In particular, we note that
1348   early in stage LR2, while the model begins to develop ICL for early tokens, it becomes worse at
1349   ICL over tokens late in the context. Later, at around 23k steps, ICLD:K stabilizes, while ICL1:D
       continues improving over the entire training run.

1775   C.4.6    D EGENERACY AND DEVELOPMENT
1776
1777   In the previous subsections, we provide a set of theoretical arguments for how (un)embedding
1778
       collapse (Appendix C.4.1), layer normalization collapse (Appendix C.4.4), and attention collapse
       (Appendix C.4.5) can lead to an increase in degeneracy, even while leaving the implemented function
1779
       unchanged.
1780
1781   The free energy formula tells us that, for two different solutions (sets of weights) with the same loss,
       the Bayesian posterior will asymptotically prefer the model that has the lower learning coefficient


                                                                33
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1782
       (i.e., higher degeneracy). This suggests that these different forms of collapse may be driven by a bias
1783   towards higher degeneracy, as captured in the free energy formula.
1784
1785   Actually establishing a causal link between increasing degeneracy and structure development is
1786
       beyond the scope of this paper. For one, the theoretical arguments hinge on the collapse being
       complete, that is, the components that go to zero must become exactly zero in the limit, where we
1787
       take the number of samples to compute the loss to infinity. In practice, we expect there to be some
1788
       threshold ϵ below which we can treat weights as effectively zero. Second, even if these explanations
1789   are correct, we do not know that they account for all of the empirically observed decrease in the
1790   LLC during these stages. There may be other drivers we missed. Finally, establishing a causal link
1791   requires theoretical progress in relating the Bayesian learning process to the SGD learning process.
1792   The arguments are suggestive, but currently only a source of intuition for how structure and geometry
1793   can be related, and a starting point for future research.

       D.3.1        VARYING THE TEMPERATURE
2124
2125   In Lau et al. (2024), the inverse temperature β is set to a fixed “optimal” value β ∗ = 1/ log n, where
2126   n is the number of training samples. In practice, we find that it can be advantageous to sample at a
2127   higher temperature.
2128
       Since β always shows up in a product with n (in (8) for the SGLD step and in (3) for the local
2129   learning coefficient), we can view the inverse temperature as a multiplier that adjusts the effective
2130   size of your dataset. In a Bayesian setting, β = 2 would mean updating twice on each of the samples
2131   in your dataset.
2132
       The problem with the default choice of β ∗ is that as we increase n we have to decrease the SGLD step
2133
       size ϵ to prevent the update from becoming ill-conditioned, and this eventually causes the gradient
2134
       term to suppress the noise term. This, in turn, leads to requiring larger batches to suppress the gradient
2135   noise and requiring longer chains to sufficiently explore the local posterior (Appendix D.3.3).
2136

       D.3.2        S EEDING THE RANDOM NOISE
2140
2141   To smooth out the λ̂t curves, we reset the random seed before LLC estimation run at each checkpoint.
2142   This means the sequence of injected Gaussian noise is the same for LLC estimation runs at different
2143   checkpoints. Additionally, if the batch size is held constant, the batch schedule will also be constant
2144   across different estimation runs. Figure D.5 shows that this does not affect the overall shape of the
2145   learning coefficient curves; it simply smooths it out.
