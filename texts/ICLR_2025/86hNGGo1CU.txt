009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011              This paper introduces a novel kernel learning framework toward efficiently solving
012              nonlinear partial differential equations (PDEs). In contrast to the state-of-the-art
013              kernel solver that embeds differential operators within kernels, posing challenges
014              with a large number of collocation points, our approach eliminates these operators
015              from the kernel. We model the solution using a standard kernel interpolation
016              form and differentiate the interpolant to compute the derivatives. Our framework
017
                 obviates the need for complex Gram matrix construction between solutions and
                 their derivatives, allowing for a straightforward implementation and scalable com-
018
                 putation. As an instance, we allocate the collocation points on a grid and adopt
019
                 a product kernel, which yields a Kronecker product structure in the interpolation.
020              This structure enables us to avoid computing the full Gram matrix, reducing costs
021              and scaling efficiently to a large number of collocation points. We provide a proof
022              of the convergence and rate analysis of our method under appropriate regularity
023              assumptions. In numerical experiments, we demonstrate the advantages of our
024              method in solving several benchmark PDEs.

      1     Introduction
027
028   Solving partial differential equations (PDEs) stands as a central task in scientific and engineering
029   domains. Recently, machine learning (ML)-based solvers have garnered significant attention. Unlike
030   traditional numerical methods, ML-based solvers eliminate the need for complex mesh designs and
031   intricate numerical techniques, enabling simpler, faster, and more convenient implementation and
032   use. Among these solvers, kernel methods or Gaussian processes (GPs) (Williams and Rasmussen,
033   2006) hold great promise due to their solid mathematical foundations, offering high expressiveness,
034
      robustness, and the ability to quantify and reason under uncertainty. Recently, Chen et al. (2021a)
      introduced a general kernel method to approximate solutions of nonlinear PDEs. They augment
035
      the representation by incorporating differential operators (more generally, linear operators) into the
036
      kernels, and jointly fit the solution values and their derivatives of the PDE on a set of collocation
037   points. Their approach has demonstrated promising performance in solving several benchmark
038   nonlinear PDEs, backed by a rigorous error analysis, including both convergence and convergence
039   rates (Chen et al., 2021a; Batlle et al., 2023).
040
      Despite its success, the methodology requires manual construction of a Gram matrix between the
041
      solution and its derivatives (which show up in the PDEs). It enumerates combinations between the
042
      operators on the kernel to compute different sub-blocks. The process enlarges the size of the Gram
043   matrix (as compared to the number of collocation points), and the computation becomes challenging
044   with a large number of collocation points, a crucial factor for capturing complex PDE solutions such
045   as with potential roughness (or even non-smoothness) and higher frequencies.
046
      In response, this work proposes an alternative kernel-based framework for solving nonlinear PDEs
047
      with several key contributions:
048
049            • Framework: We remove the basis functions associated with the differential evaluation
050              functionals in the approximation and use a standard kernel interpolation for solution mod-
051              eling. To approximate solution derivatives, we directly differentiate the interpolant. By
052              minimizing the RKHS norm along with a boundary and residual loss, we estimate the solu-
053              tion without the need for manually constructing a complex Gram matrix. Implementation is
                 straightforward and convenient with the aid of modern automatic differential libraries.

054
             • Computational Method: Our framework allows an immediate use of many existing efficient
055            Gram matrix computation and/or approximation techniques. As an instance, we propose to
056            place collocation points on a grid and employ a product kernel over each input dimension.
057            This choice induces a Kronecker product structure within both the kernel interpolation and
058            its differentiation, effectively bypassing the need for computing the entire Gram matrix.
059            Such modification results in a substantial reduction in computational costs, enabling the
060            efficient processing of tens of thousands or even millions of collocation points. This is
061            achieved without resorting to any sparse and/or low-rank approximations.
062          • Theorem: We provide a rigorous analysis of our framework. We show the convergence and
063            convergence rate of our method under appropriate PDE stability and regularity assumptions
064            that are very similar to the assumptions used in the prior work (Batlle et al., 2023). However,
065            our results are not a trivial extension of the prior work in that while our framework uses a
066            reduced model space for efficient computation, our convergence results are as comparably
067            strong as those in the prior work (Chen et al., 2021a; Batlle et al., 2023) that employs a richer
068
               model space. This is achieved through a much more sophisticated proof. We construct an
               interpolate of the true solution as an intermediate bridge connecting the true solution and our
069
               approximation, via which we prove our learning objective is not only feasible, the learned
070
               approximation also has a bounded RKHS norm. Next, via using domain decomposition,
071            sampling inequality and mean inequality, we are able to bound the L2 norm of the error
072            w.r.t the PDE operators. Combined with the bounded RKHS norm of our approximation, we
073            establish convergence and convergence rate. The results theoretically affirm the efficacy of
074            our method in yielding accurate solutions.
075
             • Experiments: Evaluation on Burgers’, nonlinear elliptical, Eikonal, and Allen-Cahn equa-
076            tions have demonstrated our method’s efficacy. For less challenging scenarios where a small
077            number of collocation points, e.g., 1000, is sufficient, our method achieves comparable or
078            sometimes smaller errors than the existing methods. In more challenging scenarios, such as
079            Burgers’ equation with a viscosity of 0.001, our method seamlessly scales to tens of thou-
080            sands of collocation points, yielding low errors on the order of 10−3 to 10−6 , underscoring
081            its robustness and accuracy across a range of problem complexities.

      3   Our Framework
143
144   Despite the success of (Chen et al., 2021a), it requires computation of a Gram (covariance) matrix
145   (see (4)) with dimensions typically exceeding the number of collocation points. This can exacerbate
146   computational challenges, particularly when addressing complex PDEs that demand a considerable
147   number of collocation points (Cho et al., 2024; Florido et al., 2024). Moreover, the construction
148   of the Gram matrix relies on the particular set of linear operators present in the PDE, rendering it
149   cumbersome for implementation and the adoption of efficient approximations, if needed.
150   We therefore propose an alternative kernel learning framework for nonlinear PDE solving, which
151   simplifies the Gram matrix construction and computation. Specifically, we are inspired by the
152   standard kernel/GP regression. Suppose the solution values at the collocation points are known,

      5   Related Work
271
272
273   The prior works of Graepel (2003); Raissi et al. (2017) propose Gaussian Processes (GPs) models
274   for solving linear PDEs in the presence of noisy measurements of source terms. Recently, Wang
275   et al. (2021) delve into the rationale and guarantees of using GPs as a prior for PDE solutions. The
276   effectiveness of the product kernel is also justified in terms of sample path properties.
277   Chen et al. (2021a) introduced a kernel method capable of solving both linear and nonlinear PDEs.
278   The solution approximation is constructed by both kernels and kernel derivatives (more generally,
279   the linear operators of the PDE over the kernels). Hence, the differentiation operators need to be
280   embedded into the kernels to construct the Gram matrix whose dimension is typically much greater
281   than the number of collocation points. In (Batlle et al., 2023), a systematic theoretical framework is
282   established to analyze the convergence and the convergence rate of the method of Chen et al. (2021a).
283
      To alleviate the computational challenge for massive collocation points, Chen et al. (2023) adapted
      the sparse inverse Cholesky factorization (Schafer et al., 2021) to approximate the Gram matrix
284
      of (Chen et al., 2021a). An alternative approach was proposed by Meng and Yang (2023), which
285
      adjusted the Nyström method (Jin et al., 2013) to obtain a sparse approximation of the Gram matrix.
286   Despite the success of these methods, the construction of the sparse approximation needs to carefully
287   handle different sub-blocks in the Gram matrix, where each sub-block corresponds a pair of linear
288   operators over the kernels, and hence it is complex and relatively inconvenient for implementation. In
289   our work, the solution is approximated by a standard kernel interpolation, and the Gram matrix is
290   therefore just the kernel matrix over the collocation points. The size of the Gram matrix is smaller.
291   More important, the existent sparse approximation methods can be readily applied to our model,
292   without the need for complex adjustments or novel development.
293   The computational efficiency of Kronecker product structures has been recognized in various works
294   (Saatcci, 2012; Xu et al., 2012; Wilson and Nickisch, 2015; Izmailov et al., 2018; Zhe et al., 2019).
295   Wilson et al. (2015) highlighted that utilizing a regular (evenly-spaced) grid results in Toeplitz-
296   structured kernel matrices, facilitating O(n log n) computation. However, in typical machine learning
297   applications, data is not observed on a grid, limiting the utility of the Kronecker product. In contrast,
298   for PDE solving, estimating solution values on a grid is natural, making Kronecker products combined
299   with kernels a promising avenue for efficient computation. The recent work (Fang et al., 2023) uses a
300
      similar computational method to solve high-frequency and multi-scale PDEs. The major contribution
      is to introduce a spectral mixture kernel in each dimension to capture the dominant frequencies in the
301
      kernel space. This work can be viewed as an instance of our proposed framework. We in addition
302
      give a theoretical analysis about the convergence of our framework. For more extensive discussions
303   on Bayesian learning and PDE problems, readers are referred to (Owhadi, 2015).
304
305   Our work is also connected to the radial basis function (RBF) method for solving PDEs (Hardy, 1971;
306
      Kansa, 1990; Tolstykh and Shirobokov, 2003; Shu et al., 2003; Fornberg et al., 2011; Safdari-Vaighani
      et al., 2015). The RBF method typically    approximates the PDE solution as a linear combination of
307                                       P
      RBF bases, i.e., kernels, u(x) ≈ j αj κ(x − xj ) where {xj } are collocation points. To estimate
308
      the coefficients {αj }, the RBF method typically converts the PDE into a linear system Aα = b
309
      and accordingly solves α = (α1 , α2 , . . .)⊤ . Our method differs mainly in two folds. First, we use
310
      kernel regression form (6) to approximate the solution, and do not explicitly estimate the coefficients
311
      α. In other words, we have implicitly α = K−1    M M η. We instead estimate η — the solution values
312   at the collocation points. Second, we do not convert the PDE solving into solving a linear system;
313   instead, we convert it into solving an optimization problem (7) or (8). While we can also use the RBF
314   formulation to approximate the solution in our framework, we found that, optimizing α (instead of
315   η) will severely degrade the performance, which might be due to the complexity of the optimization
316   procedure.

      7   Conclusion
529   We have proposed a new kernel method for nonlinear PDE solving. We use a standard kernel
530   interpolation to model the solution estimate, which allows more convenient implementation and
531   efficient computation. Our method can easily scale to massive collocation points, which are necessary
532   for challenging PDEs. The performance on a series of benchmarks is encouraging. In the future, we
533   plan to develop more efficient optimization, e.g., Gaussian-Newton, to further accelerate our method.

830   C.2    Ablation Study on Grid Shape
831
832   We investigated how the grid shape can influence the performance of our method. To this end, we
833   tested on Burgers’ equation with ν = 0.02 and ν = 0.001. For the former case, we fixed the number
834
      of collocation points to be 4800 and varied the time resolution from 10 to 80, and the spatial resolution
      is obtained by dividing 4800 by the time resolution and rounding up to an integer. Similarly, for
835
      ν = 0.001, we fixed the number of the collocation points to 120K, and varied the time resolution
836
      from 100 to 600. We show the L2 error of using each grid shape in Table 5. It can be seen that the
837   grid shape does influence the error. In particular, when ν is small, i.e., ν = 0.001, the higher the
838   spatial resolution, the smaller the error. The smallest error is achieved when we use the space-time
839   resolution 1200 × 100. On the other hand, the time resolution seems to have much less effect on the
840   solution accuracy. This is reasonable, because on Burgers’ equation, a smaller viscosity (ν) increases
841   the sharpness of the shock wave (spatial function). Naturally, the higher the spatial resolution, the
842   more accurate the sharpness can be captured. In summary, we believe that in general the grid shape
843   should be viewed as an influence factor in running our method, which needs to be carefully selected.
844   The appropriate choice may also connect to the intrinsic property of the PDE itself.

      C.3    Irregularly-Shaped Domains
846
847     We tested on solving the nonlinear elliptic PDE (16) and the Allen-Cahn equation (18) with a = 15.
848   For the nonlinear elliptic PDE, the domain is an inscribed circle within [0, 1] × [0, 1]. For the Allen-
849   Cahn equation, the domain is a triangle with vertices at at (0, 0), (1, 0) and (0.5, 1). The solution
850   is prescribed as in our paper, with boundary conditions derived from the solution. For both PDEs,
851   our method (SKS) used a virtual grid on [0, 1] × [0, 1] that covers the domain. For DAKS and PINN,
852   we sampled the same number of collocation points from the domain. For a fair comparison, all the
853   methods used the same set of 192 uniformly sampled collocation points on the boundary. The error
854   of each method is given in Table 6. The point wise error is shown in Fig. 4. As we can see, on
855
      irregularly-shaped domains, our method SKS still obtains a reasonably good accuracy for both cases
      (note that the Allen-Cahn case is much more challenging).

          D                 Limitation and Discussion

912      Currently, the most effective training for SKS is fulfilled by stochastic optimization, namely ADAM.
913      We need to run a large number of ADAM epochs to achieve a promising solution accuracy. It means
914      that the PDE solving procedure is slow. The second-order optimization methods, such as L-BFGS,
915      neither improve the solution accuracy nor accelerate the convergence. We have also tried the relaxed
916      Gauss-Newton approach as used in DAKS. However, this method can only achieve good performance
917      on the nonlinear elliptic PDE, and easily diverges on the other cases. This might stem from that
