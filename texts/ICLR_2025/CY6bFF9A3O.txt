009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Overfitting to training partners is a common problem in cooperative multi-agent
012            reinforcement learning, leading to poor zero-shot transfer to novel partners. A
013            popular solution is to train an agent with a diverse population of partners. However,
014            previous work lacks a principled approach for selecting partners from this popula-
015            tion during training, usually sampling at random. We argue that partner sampling is
016            an important and overlooked problem, and motivated by the success of regret-based
017            Unsupervised Environment Design, we propose Regret-based Adaptive Curricula
018
               for Cooperation (R ACCOON), a novel method which prioritises high-regret partners
               and tasks. We test R ACCOON in the Overcooked environment, and demonstrate that
019
               it leads to sample efficiency gains and increased robustness across diverse partners
020
               and tasks, compared with strong baselines. We further analyse the nature of the
021            induced curricula, and conclude with discussions on the limitations of cooperative
022            regret and directions for future work.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Many real-world problems require collaboration between two or more agents to achieve a common
028   goal, where agents may be autonomous machine learning agents or humans (Dafoe et al., 2021). A
029   popular approach to training such agents is cooperative multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL),
030   in which multiple agents interact and learn to maximise a common reward (Albrecht et al., 2024;
031   Oroojlooy & Hajinezhad, 2023; Papoudakis et al., 2020). However, such agents also need to be able
032   to adapt to partners with diverse preferences and abilities (Siu et al., 2021). This is the problem of
033
      ad-hoc teamwork: developing agents which can efficiently and robustly succeed with unseen partners
      (Stone et al., 2010; Mirsky et al., 2022).
034
035   While self-play (SP, Tesauro et al. (1995)) has demonstrated its effectiveness for training agents
036   with strong transfer to unseen opponents in two-player zero-sum settings such as Go (Silver et al.,
037   2017), Diplomacy (Bakhtin et al., 2022) and poker (Brown & Sandholm, 2019), SP alone fails to
038   produce robust team players in fully cooperative settings Carroll et al. (2019); Charakorn et al. (2020).
039
      Common-reward games, unlike two-player zero-sum games, can admit a number of incompatible
      equilibria, and SP policies trained on collaborative tasks tend to rely on efficicient but arbitrary
040
      conventions which render them poor teammates when paired with novel partners (Lowe et al., 2019;
041
      Hu et al., 2020).
042
043   A popular alternative is to train an agent—which we refer to as the student—with diverse pre-trained
044   partners for a given task, under the assumption that exposure to diverse partner behaviours during
045
      training leads to better generalisation. As a result, much work on ad-hoc teamwork is concerned
      with obtaining a diverse, high-quality set of training partners. A central open challenge is generating
046
      meaningful diversity—ensuring that behaviours don’t differ in merely superficial ways—while
047
      maintaining reasonable behaviour. Previous approaches include relying on diversity introduced
048   by varying initial seeds and architectures Strouse et al. (2021), maximising statistical divergence
049   between actions or trajectories (Lupu et al., 2021; Zhao et al., 2023) or minimising cross-play (XP)
050   performance between partners in the population (Charakorn et al., 2022; Cui et al., 2022; Sarkar et al.,
051   2024; Rahman et al., 2022). However, while generating partner diversity is an important problem, this
052   is not the problem we address in this paper. Instead we ask: given a population of partners, how
053   can we best use this population to promote the student’s learning? Even in a diverse, reasonable
      partner pool, it is extremely unlikely that all partners are equally useful for learning throughout

054
      training—in other words, that their learning potential is uniform across the population and static
055   across time—as the student improves. However, previous work implicitly makes this assumption by
056   sampling partners uniformly at random during the student’s training (Strouse et al., 2021; Sarkar et al.,
057   2024; Charakorn et al., 2022). In addition, current methods for generating diverse partner populations
058   are likely to be imperfect, for example including duplicates of certain behaviours or uncooperative
059   partners, since it is still unclear what constitutes relevant diversity for a given problem. This further
060   implies that all partners should blindly not be assigned equal weight. We argue that deciding how to
061   prioritise partners requires further careful consideration.
062   Inspired by recent successes of regret-based Unsupervised Environment Design (UED) in single-agent
063   RL and two-player zero-sum MARL (Dennis et al., 2020; Jiang et al., 2021; Samvelyan et al., 2023),
064   we propose an autocurriculum that prioritises high-regret partners, where a partner’s regret at a given
065   time is defined as the difference between the optimal and current XP return with that partner. Our
066   novel replay-based method, R ACCOON (Regret-based Adaptive Curricula for Cooperation), uses a
067   relative regret metric to estimate the learning potential of partners and tasks in a way which reflects
068   their relative difficulty. We empirically demonstrate the robustness and sample efficiency gains
069
      of R ACCOON—compared with baselines which randomise over partners or maximise worst-case
      performance—in the two-player fully cooperative Overcooked environment, a popular cooperative
070
      benchmark in which players must collaborate to cook and deliver soup (Carroll et al., 2019).1 We
071
      additionally analyse the induced curricula and perform an ablation which verifies the importance of
072   using relative rather than absolute regret for improved ad-hoc teamwork.
073
074   As R ACCOON can be paired with any partner population and set of tasks, our method complements
075
      work on diverse partner generation. We hope that our work motivates the problem of finding
      effective curricula over partners for cooperation, and provides a starting point for further research on
076
      cooperative autocurricula.

079   2       BACKGROUND
080
081   Cooperative MARL We consider the fully cooperative setting in which a number of agents
082   interact in an environment and receive common rewards. Since we also allow for varying tasks or
083   “levels” in the environment, following the UED literature we model this as an n-agent decentralised
084   underspecified partially observable Markov decision process (Dec-UPOMDP), described as a tuple
085
      ⟨S, A, T , R, γ, T, Θ⟩, where S is the state space, A = {Ai }1≤i≤n is the joint action space, T :
      S × A × S → [0, 1] is the transition function with reward function R : S × A → R, γ is the reward
086
      discount factor, T the horizon and Θ the set of free parameters of the environment. In this case, the
087
      transition function T additionally takes an environment configuration θ ∈ Θ as an argument.
088
089
      Ad-hoc teamwork The aim of ad-hoc teamwork, introduced by Stone et al. (2010), is to achieve
090   collaboration without prior coordination. Two common approaches are modelling other agents and
091   training with diverse partners (Mirsky et al., 2022). We consider the latter approach in this work, in
092   which an agent, which we refer to as the student, is trained with a population of pre-trained agents,
093   which we refer to as the partners (Charakorn et al., 2020; Lupu et al., 2021; Cui et al., 2022; Rahman
094   et al., 2022; Sarkar et al., 2024). These partners are typically trained via SP with some form of
095   diversity regularisation. A related problem is zero-shot coordination, which additionally assumes that
096   agents are trained independently using the same algorithm, and thus measures a particular algorithm’s
097   ability to break symmetries in non-arbitrary ways (Hu et al., 2020).
098
099   Unsupervised Environment Design Given an environment with configurable parameters—each
100
      defining a separate level (i.e., task)—UED frames curriculum generation as a game between a teacher
      and a student where the teacher iteratively chooses levels for the student to train on (Dennis et al.,
101
      2020). In regret-based UED, the teacher’s objective is to maximise regret of the student—how much
102
      worse the student performs as compared with an optimal policy—while the student aims to maximise
103   return as usual. This has been shown to induce a curriculum which presents the student with the
104   simplest tasks it cannot yet solve (Dennis et al., 2020). Prioritised Level Replay (PLR), the prevailing
105   UED method, curates a curriculum for the student by storing and replaying the highest-regret levels
106   drawn from a random level generator (Jiang et al., 2021).

110   3.1   C OOPERATIVE REGRET AS LEARNING POTENTIAL
111
112   Given a fixed population of partners and a task space, we desire two properties for an effective
113   curriculum: it should adapt to the student’s changing ability throughout training, and it should reflect
114   each (partner, task) pair’s relative learning potential for the student—a term we use informally to
115   denote how much progress the student can make towards improved ad-hoc teamwork by training with
116   that partner on that task.
117   Randomising uniformly over partners lacks both these properties; while successful (Strouse et al.,
118   2021; Sarkar et al., 2024), it likely leaves sample efficiency gains on the table as certain partners
119   become easy to cooperate with and are unnecessarily resampled. Following UED literature, we refer
120   to this approach as domain randomisation (DR), where here the domain is the partner pool.
121   An alternative is to prioritise partners with whom the student currently has lowest XP return. This
122   is employed in Zhao et al. (2023) and partly addresses the intuition that partners who pose a more
123   difficult coordination problem should be sampled more frequently. In the UED framing, this teacher
124   is a minimax adversary. However, in task-based UED, the minimax adversary prioritises levels which
125   are impossible to solve, and therefore confer no learning benefit. Similarly, in the current setting, a
126   minimax adversary will prioritise partners with whom it’s impossible to cooperate. In addition, the
127   minimax adversary does not reflect differences in maximum achievable returns with each partner. If
128   there are partners with whom it’s impossible to succeed, then the minimax adversary will continue to
129
      prioritise those partners at the expense of the student training with other, more useful partners.
130   We propose a third sampling method: maximising regret. For a given decision problem, the regret
131   of a policy is defined as the difference between the best possible outcome which could have been
132   obtained in that problem, and the actual outcome obtained by the policy (Savage, 1951). For a fixed
133   task in the two-player multi-agent setting, we define the regret of a policy π with partner π ′ as
134                               Regret(π, π ′ ) = U (BR(π ′ ), π ′ ) − U (π, π ′ )
135   where U (π, π ) is the expected return obtained by (π, π ′ ), and BR(π ′ ) denotes a best response to π ′ .
                    ′

136
      A curriculum which prioritises high-regret partners has both our desired properties: it adapts to the
137
      student’s ability, and it and provides a reasonable measure of learning potential by measuring the gap
138
      between current performance and optimal performance with each partner. Furthermore, at equilibrium
139   in the teacher-student game, regret-based UED provably results in the student implementing a minimax
140   regret policy, which performs near-optimally with everyone if it is possible to do so (Dennis et al.,
141   2020). Arguably, for ad-hoc teamwork, we want to find a policy which succeeds with as many
142   partners as possible, even if it doesn’t get the highest return with each individual one.
143
      It’s worth nothing that the effectiveness of regret requires that there is a single policy which can
144
      succeed with all partners with whom success is possible (Dennis et al., 2020), which may not exist in
145   settings where partners display incompatible conventions such that it is impossible to cooperate with
146   everyone under uncertainty about the partner’s policy. We discuss limitations of regret in Section 6.
147
148   3.2   RACCOON
149
150   Motivated by the above discussion, we propose R ACCOON (Regret-based Adaptive Curricula for
151   Cooperation), a replay-based method which considers distinct tasks as well as partners for maximal
152   generality. Following Samvelyan et al. (2023), R ACCOON maintains a joint buffer over partners Π
153   and tasks Θ, with the key difference being that we use pre-trained policies as partners, rather than past
154
      checkpoints of the student policy. The buffer stores tasks and associated scores S for each partner,
      where as scores we use regret estimates (see more in Section 3.3). At the beginning of each episode,
155
      the scores are used to obtain a distribution over partners, ∆S (Π), from which a partner is sampled.
156
      We use a rank-based prioritisation, in which the partners’ mean scores are ranked, and the probability
157   of sampling a partner πi is proportional to the inverse of its rank ni , adjusted by temperature β:
158
                                                          (1/ni )1/β
159                                         P (πi |S) ∝ P              .
160                                                         (1/ni )1/β
161   A task is either replayed from the sampled partner πi ’s buffer Λi or randomly generated, controlled by
      a replay probability hyperparameter. If replaying from Λi , the scores Si in the buffer are ranked, and

185   3.3   E STIMATING PARTNER REGRET
186
187
      In practice, we typically do not have access to a best response to each partner policy and instead need
      to estimate the highest achievable return with each partner in order to estimate regret. We discuss
188
      using a learned best response and other methods in Appendix A, but we find the most effective
189
      method estimates regret of partner π ′ (on a task) as the difference between the maximum return ever
190   achieved with π ′ (on that task) and the current return. In order to reflect different achievable returns
191   for different partners and tasks, we normalise to obtain relative regret, defined as

228   Baselines and ablations. We compare R ACCOON—using the score described in Section 3.3—with
229   two strong baselines: domain randomisation (DR, Jakobi (1997)), which uniformly randomises over
230   partners and tasks, and minimax adversary (Minimax, Pinto et al. (2017); Morimoto & Doya (2005)),
231
      where we replace the regret estimate with the negative return as the score in R ACCOON. All students
      are trained and evaluated with the same sets of training and test partners. We also investigate the
232
      importance of using relative regret by performing an ablation, which we denote as R ACCOON− , in
233
      which we use absolute (unnormalised) regret as the score.

235
236   Generating partners. Following Strouse et al. (2021), we obtain diverse partners by training
237
      policies in SP with different initial seeds, and using checkpoints from the beginning, middle and end
      of training to cover a range of skill levels, reflecting the fact that novel partners such as humans are
238
      unlikely to behave optimally. For each layout L, we train a population of partners ΠL on L. We
239
      choose to train partners in this “specialist" way, rather than training on all layouts simultaneously, as
240   we find it results in higher quality policies for each layout, and further allows for faithful selection of
241   partners of the three skill levels.
242
243
      We form the training partner population Πtrain
                                               L by taking five seeds from ΠL and checkpoints from the
      beginning of training, at convergence and when returns are half the final return, resulting in “low-”,
244
      “medium-” and “high-skilled” partners, following the convention in Strouse et al. (2021). Note that
245
      this means that the low-skilled training partners follow random policies. Similarly, we form the
246   held-out partner population Πtest
                                    L using five different seeds and three checkpoints, but we use slightly
247   later checkpoints than for Πtrain
                                   L to model the assumption that unseen partners in ad-hoc teamwork
248   should be at least better than random at the task. We use PPO and an actor-critic network with a
249   shared convolutional layer for both student and partner policies; for more details see Appendix C.
250
      Despite the existence of more complicated methods for generating diversity, varying initial seeds has
251
      been shown to be effective despite its simplicity (Charakorn et al., 2020), and we expect the range
252
      of skill levels afforded by different checkpoints to pose a particularly interesting setting in which to
253   investigate curricula, due to the strong asymmetries in learning potential between partners.

      6    D ISCUSSION
423
      To our knowledge, our work is the first detailed investigation of autocurricula over partners for ad-hoc
424
      teamwork in cooperative MARL. Our experiments which train students on all layouts simultane-
425
      ously show that R ACCOON is able to navigate settings with diverse challenges and sparse learning
426   opportunities—thereby learning a more versatile policy—where baselines fail to learn to solve harder
427   tasks. In particular, we show that using relative regret is key for adapting to high variance in the
428   difficulty of tasks. We additionally show that R ACCOON maintains sample efficiency as we scale
429   the number of partners, while that of DR steadily declines. While Overcooked is a relatively simple
430   environment, these results in combination suggest that the benefits conferred by R ACCOON may
431   truly shine in large and complex task and partner spaces, where randomisation can have no hope of
      efficiently filtering useful learning opportunities.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      Limitations of the environment We test our method in the Overcooked environment because
433   it presents distinct collaborative challenges while being relatively interpretable and fast to train
434   in, and because it is commonly used in work on generating partner diversity for ad-hoc teamwork
435   (Strouse et al., 2021; Sarkar et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2023; Charakorn et al., 2022). However, the
436   tasks in Overcooked are still much simpler than real-world collaborative tasks. Another limitation
437   of Overcooked is that collaboration is not strictly necessary for reasonable performance on most
438   of the five classic layouts, as evidenced by the high return achievable with random policies. This
439   brings into question whether agents are truly learning meaningful collaborative skills, and whether
440   the demonstrated efficacy of including low-skilled partners in the training population (Strouse et al.,
441
      2021) is due to improved cooperation or simply improved individual skill at the task.
442
443
      Limitations of cooperative regret In regret-based UED, a minimax regret policy will have regret at
      most equal to the minimax regret bound on all tasks in the training domain, but there are no guarantees
444
      on the behaviour of the policy on tasks where the regret is not at this bound. Regret stagnation occurs
445
      when it is possible to improve performance on levels which are not at the minimax regret bound, but
446   the student no longer trains on those levels because the UED teacher only plays the highest regret
447   levels (Beukman et al., 2024). This may be particularly problematic in cooperative settings, where
448   diverse partners may employ incompatible policies such that, under uncertainty about its partner,
449   the student is unable to simultaneously achieve low regret with all partners. While Overcooked is a
450   relatively simple environment and good ad-hoc teamwork seems feasible, the problem of incompatible
451   policies is especially notable in Hanabi (Cui et al., 2022), and we encourage further work to investigate
452   and mitigate the limitations of regret in such settings.
453
454   Limitations of relative regret score One limitation of the score described in Section 3.3 is that
455
      using the maximum return ever achieved with a partner on a task as an estimate for optimal return
      doesn’t account for noise in the rewards. For example, it is occasionally possible to make a single
456
      delivery with a randomly behaving partner in Forced Coordination if the partner happens to take the
457
      right sequence of actions, even though most of the time it’s impossible to collaborate with such a
458   partner. As a result, relative regret would score such a partner highly, even though they don’t provide
459   meaningful learning. A more nuanced regret estimate should identify such situations and correctly
460   prioritise only partners with whom cooperation is meaningfully possible.
461
462   Future work A natural follow-up is to pair R ACCOON with different partner population generation
463   methods and larger environment spaces, particularly open-ended or procedurally generated environ-
464   ments (Fontaine et al., 2021). It would also be interesting to test the robustness of R ACCOON with
465   adversarial partner populations, for example those which are particularly sparse on useful learning
466   opportunities or may contain “rogue” partners. Finally, a vision for future work is to combine the
467
      processes of training the partners and the student into a single adaptive process, for example by using
      learning potential to inform partner generation and selection in an evolutionary search method such
468
      as MAP-Elites (Mouret & Clune, 2015; Xue et al., 2022; Parker-Holder et al., 2020).
469
470
      Impact statement Our work aims to make autonomous agents better at collaborating with and
471
      adapting to partners on problems where all agents have a common goal, which can make them more
472   helpful to humans, both in collaborating with humans directly or more efficiently achieving goals
473   specified by humans for an autonomous multi-agent system. It’s worth noting that, in general, agents
474   trained purely to be cooperative may be vulnerable to exploitation when transferred to real-world
475   settings, but we believe that at this stage—given the simplicity of the simulations in which we are
476   working—our work does not pose any apparent negative consequences.
477
478   7    R ELATED WORK
479
480   Diverse partners for ad-hoc teamwork A number of methods have been used to generate diverse
481   training partners for cooperation. A simple but effective method is Fictitious Co-Play (Strouse et al.,
482   2021), which forms a population from different seeds and checkpoints of agents trained in SP. This is
483   the method we use to train the partners for our experiments. Other methods use an auxiliary loss to
484   regularise diversity. TrajeDi (Lupu et al., 2021) trains agents in SP while maximising a statistical
485   divergence term between trajectories, while LIPO (Charakorn et al., 2022), CoMeDi (Sarkar et al.,
      2024), ADVERSITY (Cui et al., 2022) and BRDiv (Rahman et al., 2022) model policy compatibility


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      as XP return and aim to minimise this. However, unlike R ACCOON, these methods train a best
487   response student by sampling partners from the population uniformly at random. Notably, R ACCOON
488   is not intended as a competitor to these methods: indeed, it can be paired with any of these partner
489   populations, and therefore complements work on diverse partner generation.
490
      Maximum Entropy Population-Based Training (Zhao et al., 2023) uses a population entropy bonus
491
      to promote diversity, and uses a prioritised sampling method for sampling partners during the
492
      training of the student. However, unlike our method, partners with lowest XP return are prioritised,
493   corresponding to a minimax adversary, which we show is less robust than regret as it does not reflect
494   whether cooperation is achievable with each partner, in particular performing poorly on Forced
495   Coordination.
496
497   Curricula in cooperative MARL Automatic curriculum learning (Leibo et al., 2019) has enjoyed
498   successes across RL. Self-play—in which an agent plays against itself, typically in a competitive
499   setting (Tesauro et al., 1995)—forms a natural curriculum of increasing difficulty as the agent
500   improves, and has been effective in achieving human-level performance in a number of games
501   (Silver et al., 2017; Bakhtin et al., 2022). Fictitious self-play (FSP, Heinrich et al. (2015)) uses a
502   uniform mixture of past checkpoints as training partners to avoid cycles and forgetting. Vinyals
503
      et al. (2019) introduced prioritised fictitious self-play (PFSP), which weights FSP partners as a
      function of the probability of beating them to identify partners which provide the best learning signal.
504
      While the motivation behind PFSP is similar to R ACCOON, PFSP is applied in a competitive setting,
505
      whereas R ACCOON is designed for cooperative settings and uses pre-trained rather than FSP partners.
506   Similarly, MAESTRO (Samvelyan et al., 2023) combines UED and self-play to induce a curriculum
507   over joint partner-task pairs, and is the most similar to R ACCOON, again with the major difference
508   that we use pre-trained partners for collaborative tasks. Additionally, MAESTRO scores partners and
509   tasks using an estimate of absolute regret, rather than relative regret, as we do.
510
      Bhati et al. (2023) also investigate teammate selection in Overcooked, but use hand-crafted curricula
511
      over skill levels in a single easy layout, whereas our curriculum automatically adapts to the student
512   throughout training and is compatible with multiple tasks.

      8    C ONCLUSION
516
517
      In this work, we introduce R ACCOON, a novel cooperative autocurriculum method which provides a
      complement to diverse partner generation for ad-hoc teamwork. In particular, we define a relative
518
      regret metric to score the learning potential of partners and tasks. We demonstrate the increased
519
      robustness of R ACCOON in the Overcooked environment by presenting diverse partners and tasks,
520   finding that R ACCOON learns to collaborate on the most difficult tasks where baselines fail. Our results
521   on sample efficiency further suggest that R ACCOON remains effective as we scale partner populations
522   and task spaces. We conclude by discussing limitations of regret as well as potential avenues for future
523   work. We hope that our work demonstrates the unexplored potential of autocurricula in cooperative
524   MARL, and provides initial methods and results on which future work can build.

653   A     A LTERNATIVE METHODS FOR ESTIMATING COOPERATIVE REGRET
654
655
      In addition to the relative regret metric outlined in 3.3, we considered and tested a number of methods
656   for estimating cooperative regret with a partner.
657
658
      Training a best response policy Since we want to estimate optimal return possible with each
659
      partner, we could try to train a best response policy BR(πi ) to πi for each πi ∈ Πtrain , and use the
660   maximum return achieved as the estimate of optimal return with πi . While this was fairly effective
661   for easier layouts, we found that this approach frequently underestimates optimal return with each
662   partner, leading to negative scores—the student trained with Πtrain generally outperforms the best
663   response policy with each training partner πi despite (or rather, because of) being trained with all
664   partners.
665
666   Treating the partner’s SP as a best response Another approximation is to treat a partner’s SP
667   return as the optimal return with that partner. While this is more likely to be accurate when training
668   with highly skilled (i.e., converged partners), it falls short in cases where partners are low-skilled (i.e.,
669   partially trained), on tasks where it is possible for one of the team members to “pick up the slack” of
670   a less skilled partner. As a result, we found this method to be effective only on Forced Coordination,
671   which does require both partners’ contributions.

755   Coordination Ring, Counter Circuit and Forced Coordination, we shape the reward with an additional
