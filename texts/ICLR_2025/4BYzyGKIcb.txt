009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Out-of-distribution (OOD) detection ensures safe and reliable model deployment.
012            Contemporary OOD algorithms using geometry projection can detect OOD or
013            adversarial samples from clean in-distribution (ID) samples. However, this setting
014            regards adversarial ID samples as OOD, leading to incorrect OOD predictions.
015            Existing efforts on OOD detection with ID and OOD data under attacks are mini-
016            mal. In this paper, we develop a robust OOD detection method that distinguishes
017            adversarial ID samples from OOD ones. The sharp loss landscape created by
018            adversarial training hinders model convergence, impacting the latent embedding
019
               quality for OOD score calculation. Therefore, we introduce a Sharpness-aware
               Geometric Defense (SaGD) framework to smooth out the rugged adversarial
020
               loss landscape in the projected latent geometry. Enhanced geometric embedding
021
               convergence enables accurate ID data characterization, benefiting OOD detection
022            against adversarial attacks. We use Jitter-based perturbation in adversarial training
023            to extend the defense ability against unseen attacks. Our SaGD framework sig-
024            nificantly improves FPR and AUC over the state-of-the-art defense approaches in
025            differentiating CIFAR-100 from six other OOD datasets under various attacks. We
026            further examine the effects of perturbations at various adversarial training levels,
027            revealing the relationship between the sharp loss landscape and adversarial OOD
028            detection. The implementation code will be released upon paper acceptance.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Advancements in artificial intelligence (AI) go beyond mere model accuracy. One critical aspect is
      the AI model’s capability to identify and reject unfamiliar samples, ensuring reliable AI deployment.
034
      The technical field of detecting out-of-distribution (OOD) samples [45, 53] has raised substantial
035
      attention. The aim is to distinguish disjoint OOD samples from the in-distribution (ID) training
036   samples. For example, an image classifier should recognize unfamiliar input images outside training
037   classes to avoid generating unreliable predictions.
038
039
      The deep neural network is known to be vulnerable to adversarial attacks [18], which are intentionally
      manipulated perturbations in a subtle way that is malicious to mislead model predictions. A handful of
040
      adversarial defense studies are proposed to secure the model prediction against the attacks [40, 38, 15].
041
      Notably, adversarial training and hyperspherical geometry learning effectively alleviate adversarial
042   situations in image classification tasks. In the case of OOD detection, existing studies typically
043   predict adversarial samples as OOD samples [29], leading to substantial alarms for adversarial ID
044   cases shown in Figure 1. These studies have still not yet explored how to distinguish adversarial
045   ID from adversarial OOD samples and thus are still not resilient to attacks in realizing real OOD
046   applications. We aim to ensure the OOD detection system robustly operates in clean and adversarial
047   conditions.
048   The task of differentiating OOD itself is hard due to the widespread new data pattern to the model [16],
049   and suffering from adversarial attacks increases the complexity of OOD detection. ATOM is a
050   pioneering framework for dealing with attacks on open-set samples [5]. Recently, Azizmalayeri
051   et al. [1] found that adversarial attacks on both ID and OOD data significantly degrade detection
052   accuracy. They introduced an Adversarial Training Discriminator (ATD) with an outlier exposure
053   strategy that simulates both adversarial ID and OOD samples. The outlier exposure method highly
      depends on the auxiliary OOD datasets which are expected to be excessively large. This requirement

      leads to inefficient and impracticality in real-world applications. We target the defense against
055   challenging white-box attacks on both ID and OOD data and seek effective perturbation strategies
056   without relying on additional large outlier datasets.

058
      In this work, we aim to tackle this robust OOD de-
      tection issue by combining two perspectives, includ-
059
      ing geometry optimization and loss landscape smooth-
060
      ing. First, the hypersphere and hyperbolic geome-
061   tries can learn compact representations for OOD de-
062   tection [36], but we still empirically observe a high
063   false positive rate. Therefore, we further examine the
064   ability of the multiple-geometric learning method [30]
065   in accommodating ID data variability under adversarial
066   attacks. Second, sharp loss has been observed in prior Figure 1: Problem scope of the adversarial
067   research [14, 56] which is caused by adversarial sam- OOD detection
068   ples. These samples increase the gradient norm and the
069
      subsequent local minimum, sharp loss, and challenges in convergence. The GAN-like structure in
      ATD is also known for its loss convergence issues.
070
071   Therefore, we introduce a sharpness-aware adversarial training framework that effectively allevi-
072   ates the sharp loss landscape, achieving robust latent geometry learning. Our backbone network
073   learns a Multi-Geometry Projection (MGP) [30] by incorporating two Riemannian (hypersphere and
074   hyperbolic) geometries with distinct curvatures to fully characterize the complex ID data. In the
075
      adversarial training procedure, we propose to utilize the Riemannian Sharpness-aware Minimization
      (RSAM) [55, 47], which improves the multiple Riemannian geometry convergence by flattening the
076
      adversarial loss landscape. We empirically find that performing adversarial training based on the
077
      Jitter attacks [41] demonstrates generalizability in defending against other attacks.
078
079   Our experiments comprehensively investigate mainstream OOD detection approaches with and
080   without adversarial training. We use CIFAR-10 and CIFAR-100 as ID datasets and perform OOD
081
      evaluation using six other datasets. We compare the proposed SaGD against ATD [1], the state-of-
      the-art (SoTA) defense approach for OOD detection, and show our improvements. Additionally, we
082
      examine the effects of different adversarial training approaches to reveal the generalization ability
083
      of SaGD in defending other types of attacks. In contrast to other OOD studies [1, 5, 6], which only
084   present one type of Projected Gradient Descent (PGD) attack, our results are comprehensively from
085   the average of six conditions, including the case without attack and five other cases under different
086   attacks. We report the area under the ROC curve (AUC) along with the false positive rate at 95%
087   true positive rate (abbreviated as FPR95 ) as evaluation metrics. The FPR95 is a common metric for
088   OOD detection; however, it is not reported in [1]. In the adversarial OOD detection experiments
089   using the CIFAR-10 ID dataset, our SaGD robustly reduces 14.91% FPR95 and enhances 7.47% AUC
090   over the SoTA approach. We also achieved a 17.71% average FPR95 reduction and 10.18% AUC
091   improvement using CIFAR-100 as the ID dataset.
092   Our contribution is summarized as follows:
093
094   • We introduce a novel sharpness-aware method for improving OOD detection in adversarial training.
095     Our method investigates the combination of Riemannian geometries under adversarial conditions.
096     This expansion of geometry space sharpens our defense against adversarial attacks and avoids
097     reliance on large OOD datasets for auxiliary training.
098
099   • We examine different perturbation techniques (not limited to PGD) for adversarial training to
100     identify their effectiveness for robust OOD detection.
101
102   • We investigate various adversarial attacks on different OOD detection approaches and report results
103     on FPR95 and AUC. Our SaGD sets a new SoTA for OOD detection, excelling in FPR95 and AUC
104
        metrics, both with or without attacks.
105
106
      • We analyze the relations between the minimization of a sharp loss landscape and OOD detection
        performance under various adversarial conditions.

      2.1   OOD D ETECTION
131
132   Post-processing OOD detection. Model-agnostic OOD detection methods [45, 53] formulate scoring
133   functions based on prediction probability and energy score. Determining prediction confidence can
134
      take various forms, such as utilizing softmax outputs [21], energy-based scores [33], or entropy
      functions [4]. To avoid re-training or excessive tuning of the given model, recent advancements
135
      focus on introducing perturbation [31], conducting pruning [12], and generating an unknown novel
136
      class [48] to enhance the distinction between OOD and in-distribution (ID) samples.
137
138   Model training OOD detection. Other OOD studies have sought to enhance fixed-model post-
139   processing by incorporating network constraints during training to improve OOD detection. Sophis-
140
      ticated designs are devised for network space projection and embedding distance measurement to
      effectively train models for OOD detection. Noteworthy examples include SSD [42] and KNN+[46],
141
      employing contrastive loss for latent embedding learning and calculating Mahalanobis[29] and non-
142
      parametric KNN distances, respectively. A recent addition to this line of work is the CIDER frame-
143   work [36], which has demonstrated improved OOD detection performance by imposing constraints
144   on samples using a hypersphere-based loss function. The hyperbolic embedding also demonstrates
145   the enhanced ability for OOD detection [19]. Despite the impressive results achieved by these OOD
146   detection approaches, their performance is not robust when facing adversarial samples in practice.
147
148   2.2   A DVERSARIAL D EFENSES
149
      Adversarial training [34] stands out as a key defense against adversarial attacks. This method
150   involves integrating adversarial samples into model training to bolster the network’s resilience against
151   perturbations. The goal is to approximate potential perturbations in adversarial samples, using them
152   to enhance model accuracy. However, the incorporation of adversarial samples unavoidably leads to a
153   degradation in prediction accuracy due to the introduction of various noises. To this end, Helper-based
154   Adversarial Training (HAT) seeks balance and reduces harm from adversarial samples by tailoring
155   network architecture and loss designs [40]. Notably, works such as [43, 44] train dual-attentive
156   denoising layers, leading to clean reconstructed samples from adversarial ones. Originally devised
157   for addressing the open-set detection problem, these techniques find application in OOD detection
158
      scenarios under adversarial attacks [1].
159   Outlier exposure [22, 39] emerges as a strategy in OOD detection, broadening its capabilities by
160   incorporating outliers during training. Although these techniques can also foster a robust learning
161   space for adversarial outliers [6], their practical utility is constrained by the uncertainty surrounding
      the optimal inclusion of outliers and the types of adversarial that should be artificially introduced.

      2.3   R EGULARIZATION AND A DVERSARIAL ROBUSTNESS
163
164   Regularizing neural networks proves effective against adversarial attacks by preventing the adop-
165   tion of overly complex parameters, and avoiding suboptimal convergence at saddle points. When
166   attacked samples have distributions vastly different from the training space, it significantly biases
167   the network parameters. Implementing regularization based on various network designs, such as
168
      angular and margin regularization on hypersphere geometry, enhances adversarial robustness [38, 15].
      Prioritizing more regularization for vulnerable samples minimizes the robustness risk and improves
169
      generalizability [52]. The underlying reason is that adversarial training can generate rugged sample
170
      space and thus hinder model convergence. Such a sharp loss landscape hinders the training process
171   with scattered gradients and increased curvatures [32].
172
173   Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM) [17] is a well-known technique for its regularization ability
174
      to mitigate training overfitting on a sharp loss landscape. A recent study [49] delves into SAM’s
      potential for adversarial robustness and empirically establishes a lightweight alternative to PGD
175
      adversarial training without significant sacrifices in clean sample accuracy. However, the integration
176
      of SAM-based regularization with adversarial training, especially in OOD detection, remains limited.
177   The exploration of geometric projection associations, such as RSAM operating on the Riemannian
178   manifold [55, 47], is largely uncharted. This paper advances current research by combining RSAM
179   with multi-geometry learning techniques for OOD detection. We also investigate the effects of various
180   adversarial training types in experiments.

184   Figure 2 overviews our proposed SaGD framework, where the training phase consists of adversarial
185   training using Jitter-based adversarial samples (§3.1), multi-geometry projection (§3.2), and sharpness-
186   aware optimization (§3.3). The multi-geometry backbone combines the hypersphere and hyperbolic
187   branches in a multi-task joint loss optimization scheme. We first introduce the architecture along with
188   the scoring function for OOD score calculation. We then show how the Riemannian Sharpness-aware
189   Minimization (RSAM) optimizes the framework with adversarial training.
190   Problem setup. Given labeled data (x, y) from a distribution D, we consider a model fθ with
191   parameters θ. The training and testing data are denoted as Ds and Dt , respectively, where Dt contains
192   both in-distribution (Did ) and out-of-distribution (Dood ) test data. We assume Did is drawn from the
193   same distribution as Ds , while Dood is from a different distribution that needs to be distinguished.
194   The standard procedure for OOD detection is as follows: (1) Train a model fθ with Ds . (2) Fix model
195   parameter θ during test time. For each test sample x, derive embedding z using fθ . (3) Calculate
196   OOD score s(x) and differentiate OOD samples with a threshold λ. To protect the model against
197   adversarial attacks, we focus on the first step to strengthen the model’s robustness.

      3.2   M ULTI -G EOMETRY P ROJECTION (MGP)
217
218   Our backbone network fθ incorporates a dual-stream geometry projection to capture diverse latent
219   structures in ID data. Each geometry stream is defined by its specific loss function for joint op-
220   timization. In this context, we introduce hypersphere and hyperbolic geometry, both Riemannian
221
      manifolds with positive and negative curvature, respectively. The curvature serves as an indicator of
      deviation from the Euclidean space. Hyperspherical geometry has shown its effectiveness in OOD
222
      detection [36]. The hyperbolic space has been used in open-set recognition [11] that can model the
223
      hierarchical structures found in real-world vision data [13], as evident in datasets like Imagenet.
224
225   We assume parameter θ resides on a Riemannian manifold M with the Riemannian metric tensor
226   g M . The tensor g M : Tθ M × Tθ M consists of inner products in its tangent space Tθ M. A
227
      retraction map Rθ provides transformations from M to the tangent space Tθ M. The tangent space
      can be regarded as a measure of small deviation γ near parameter θ, and the metric g M smoothly
228
      varies across θ ∈ M, resulting in the geodesic distance. The deviation γ on Tθ M is considered as
229
      the perturbation generated for adversarial training (as discussed in §3.1), which will be utilized in
230   Riemannian manifold optimization (§3.3).

272   Learning complex latent geometries may face undesirable peaks in the loss minimization process.
273   Inspired by SAM [17] which was originally crafted for model generalization, we employ an improved

275   tion of multiple geometries in the network represents various manifolds that might not consistently
276
      converge in the same gradient direction. The recent work [36] only accounts for a single hypersphere
      geometry, which limits the ability to represent the OOD space. In our scenario, we aim to utilize the
277
      Riemannian manifold optimization strategy to strenthen multiple geometries.

293   3.4   OOD S CORING F UNCTION
294
295   With a trained network f in the MGP framework, we extract the penultimate layer output as an L2
296   normalized embedding z of the sample x to compute its OOD score. To distinguish OOD from ID
297
      samples, we calculate the embedding distance between each input sample and the training ID samples
      and specify the k th nearest neighbor as a reference embedding zk . The OOD score is based on the
298
      L2 distance, S(z) = ||z − zk ||2 . An OOD sample is detected using a threshold λ on the score S(z).

      5   C ONCLUSION
473
      In this paper, we address the robustness issue for out-of-distribution (OOD) detection by investigating
474   various types of adversarial attacks. We propose a novel SaGD framework that leverages the Jitter
475   attack for adversarial training and optimizes the multi-geometry network using RSAM to enhance
476   model convergence. The sharpness minimization strategy mitigates the rugged loss landscape induced
477   by adversarial examples, resulting in improved OOD detection performance under attacks. Our
478   OOD detection experiments encompass two in-distribution (ID) datasets and six OOD datasets tested
479   against five types of attacks. SaGD achieves significantly low FPR95 and high AUC on average. Our
480   ablation study shows the critical role of Jitter-based adversarial training, highlighting the potential
481   risk of employing popular perturbation approaches like PGD and FGSM. Our analysis shows the
482
      importance of using FPR95 for evaluation as it tends to be impacted by increased attacks.
483   Future work includes the exploration of loss convergence conditions during adversarial geometry
484   learning and improving the generalization of OOD detection capability under various adversarial con-
485   ditions. We anticipate this work can initiate a novel direction to investigate an in-depth understanding
      of the relation between geometric loss optimization and robust OOD detection.
