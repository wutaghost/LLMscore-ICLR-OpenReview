                                                    A BSTRACT
007
                 Large language models have demonstrated impressive performance when inte-
008
                 grated with vision models even enabling video understanding. However, evalu-
009              ating these video models presents its own unique challenges, for which several
010              benchmarks have been proposed. In this paper, we show that the currently most
011              used video-language benchmarks can be solved without requiring much temporal
012              reasoning. We identified three main issues in existing datasets: (i) static infor-
013              mation from single frames is often sufficient to solve the tasks (ii) the text of the
014              questions and candidate answers is overly informative, allowing models to an-
015              swer correctly without relying on any visual input (iii) world knowledge alone
016              can answer many of the questions, making the benchmarks a test of knowledge
017
                 replication rather than visual reasoning. In addition, we found that open-ended
                 question-answering benchmarks for video understanding suffer from similar is-
018
                 sues while the automatic evaluation process with LLMs is unreliable, making it an
019
                 unsuitable alternative. As a solution, we propose TVBench, a novel open-source
020              video multiple-choice question-answering benchmark, and demonstrate through
021              extensive evaluations that it requires a high level of temporal understanding. Sur-
022              prisingly, we find that most recent state-of-the-art video-language models perform
023              similarly to random performance on TVBench, with only a few models such as
024              Qwen2-VL, and Tarsier clearly surpassing this baseline.

026     1   I NTRODUCTION
027
        Vision Language Models have gained popularity, benefiting from both the progress made in natural
028
        language processing and the surge of foundation models for vision tasks with strong generalization
029
        capabilities. Recently, video-language models have been introduced (Xu et al., 2021; Lin et al.,
030     2023; Xue et al., 2023), aiming to replicate the success achieved in the image domain. To evaluate
031     their performance, visual question answering has emerged as a key task requiring both textual and
032     visual reasoning. With the rapid model development and release cycles, having a reliable and robust
033     benchmark is crucial in measuring progress and guiding research efforts.
034
        There are two main approaches to designing question-answering benchmarks for videos: multiple-
035
        choice question answering (MCQA) (Wang et al., 2024c; Li et al., 2024b) and open-ended question
036     answering (Yu et al., 2019; Xu et al., 2017). The most popular and commonly used benchmark
037     for MCQA is MVBench (Li et al., 2024b), which is the most downloaded video dataset with over
038     104K monthly downloads as of September 2024 and the second most downloaded across both im-
039     age and video visual question-answering benchmarks on Hugging Face despite being released only
040     recently. As such, its reliability as a valid benchmark is of utmost importance. But how much video
041     understanding does MVBench truly measure?
042     Previous analysis in image question answering benchmarks (Goyal et al., 2017b) has demonstrated
043     that poorly formulated benchmarks could bias the development of new models towards learning
044     strong text representations while ignoring visual information. This is especially relevant for the
045     video-language community, where benchmarks must account not only for visual but also for tempo-
046     ral understanding.
047
        In this work, we conduct a comprehensive analysis of widely used video question-answering bench-
048     marks, revealing that temporal information is poorly evaluated. Furthermore, in MCQA tasks, prior
049     world knowledge, combined with overly informative questions and answer choices, often allows
050     questions to be answered solely through text, without the need for visual input. Our results also
051     indicate that automatic open-ended evaluation is unreliable, with significant evaluation discrepan-
052     cies in results across different models for the same task. We assess the substantial shortcomings of
053     MVBench and propose as a solution a new benchmark TVBench that requires temporal understand-
        ing to be solved, providing an effective evaluation tool for current video-language models:

089   2   R ELATED WORK
090
091   Traditional video evaluation benchmarks focused on specific tasks such as action recognition (Goyal
092   et al., 2017a; Kay et al., 2017) or video description (Das et al., 2013; Xu et al., 2016; Wang et al.,
093
      2019). With the emergence of Vision Language Models (VLMs), there is a growing need for more
      comprehensive evaluation protocols to effectively evaluate models with increasingly advanced gen-
094
      eralization capabilities. Current video-language benchmarks focus on solving QA pairs that require
095
      a certain level of multimodal understanding. There are two major trends in the QA format: open-
096   ended QA and multiple-choice QA (MCQA).
097
098   Open-ended question answering. Evaluating open-ended QA introduces new challenges, as tra-
099
      ditional evaluation metrics such as ROUGE (Lin, 2004), METEOR (Banerjee & Lavie, 2005), and
      CIDEr (Vedantam et al., 2015) fail to analyze discrepancies of more complex and elaborated an-
100
      swers. Alternatively, Maaz et al. (2023) introduces a novel quantitative evaluation pipeline for open-
101
      ended QA datasets. The proposed method relies on GPT-3.5 to determine the correctness of the
102   predicted answer and provides a matching score with the ground truth. Commonly used datasets
103   for evaluating models in this context include MSRVTT-QA (Xu et al., 2017), MSVD-QA (Xu et al.,
104   2017), TGIF-QA (Jang et al., 2017) and ActivityNet-QA (Yu et al., 2019). In general, any open-
105   ended QA benchmarks can be evaluated following this protocol. As shown in our analysis, Large
106   Language Model (LLM) based evaluations are prone to hallucinations, leading to unreliable con-
107   clusions. In contrast, MCQA benefits from a more straightforward evaluation process, based on the
      accuracy score.

136   3     P ROBLEMS IN V IDEO MCQA B ENCHMARKS
137   In this section, we identify two key shortcomings in current video multiple-choice question-
138   answering benchmarks, as demonstrated on MVBench. First, we show that this benchmark contains
139   strong spatial bias, meaning that questions can be answered without temporal understanding. Sec-
140   ondly, we demonstrate that MVBench also contains a strong textual bias, as many questions can be
141   answered without even looking at the visual input. In Appendix A.2.1, we extend our analysis to
142   NextQA (Xiao et al., 2021).
143

145   Video benchmarks must define tasks that cannot be solved using solely spatial information to ef-
146   fectively evaluate the temporal understanding of a model. In video MCQA, this means questions
147   should not be answerable using spatial details from a single random frame or multiple frames e.g.
148   after shuffling them. However, if no understanding of the sequence of events and temporal local-

150   information which we define as spatial bias.

      3.2    D OES V ISION M ATTER ?
203
204   Video benchmarks must be designed to prevent questions from being answered solely through com-
205   mon sense reasoning. Modern LLMs possess strong reasoning skills, which can exploit the informa-
206   tion within the question and candidate sets in MCQA video language evaluation benchmarks. This
207   creates textual bias, enabling models to answer questions without leveraging the video content.
208
      We analyze the impact of textual bias on MVBench in Table 2. We evaluate the performance of state-
209
      of-the-art text-only LLMs, Llama 3 (MetaAI, 2024), and multi-modal LLMs such as Gemini 1.5
210   Pro (Gemini, 2024), GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024) and Tarsier (Wang et al., 2024a). Our findings reveal
211   that LLMs can eliminate incompatible candidates easily, greatly outperforming the random baseline.
212   Models using only text achieve competitive results compared to video-language models across these
213   four tasks (Action Count, Unexpected Action, Action Antonym, and Episodic Reasoning). For
214   instance, Gemini 1.5 Pro achieves an average performance of 62.3% using text-only, compared
215   to Tarsier-34B’s 67.4% using videos. Additionally, we verify an 85.3% agreement between Tarsier-
      34B’s correct text and video responses, confirming its strong reliance on textual biases in MVBench.

286
      Contrary to multiple-choice question answering (MCQA), open-ended question answering can be
      seen as an alternative to solving the aforementioned issues. Without a predefined candidate answer
287
      set, the model cannot rely on textual information to eliminate implausible candidates. However,
288
      open-ended evaluation presents new challenges compared to MCQA. Following Maaz et al. (2023),
289   LLMs have been widely used for the evaluation of open-ended question-answering in datasets such
290   as MSVD-QA (Wu et al., 2017), MSRVTT-QA (Xu et al., 2016) and ActivityNet QA (Yu et al.,
291   2019). Specifically, Maaz et al. (2023) proposed GPT-3.5 as the evaluator model, which makes
292   the entire evaluation process rely on a private API model. The evaluation model is prompted to
293   determine if the predicted answer is correct given the question and the ground-truth answer. In
294   addition, the evaluator also computes a score to measure the answer quality.
295   We conducted a comparative analysis to assess the influence of the evaluation model on the results.
296   Table 3 shows the accuracy and average score for different models on three open-ended datasets,
297   using two evaluators: GPT-3.5 and Llama3-70B. The evaluators produced significantly different
298   results for the same method on the same dataset, with discrepancies of more than 20 points. Specif-
299   ically, Llama3 highly increases the accuracy of text-only and single-image models, while providing
300   similar or even lower results than GPT-3.5 for video models. It is also evident that Llama3 assigns
301   better metrics to predictions made by the same model. If both the prediction model and the evaluator
302   contain similar biases, the hallucinations in the predictions may be classified as correct responses by
303
      the evaluator ignoring the correct answer. These findings raise doubt about the reliability of these
      evaluations, as different models give completely different results.
304
305   Moreover, as shown in Table 3 open-ended QA does not solve the main issues of MCQA. The
306   performance of text-only models is surprisingly strong; state-of-the-art LLMs can guess the answer
307   solely from the question text for a significant number of questions, even without a candidate list.
308   This includes questions such as Which hand of the person in black wears a watch? or What color
309
      is the pants of a person wearing black clothes?, which correct answers are Left hand and Black.
      The first question can be answered just with prior knowledge as people commonly wear the watch
310
      on the left hand, while in the second one, the question gives too much information, the person is
311
      wearing black clothes. Similar to the findings for MCQA on spatial bias in Sec. 3.1, when using
312   a single random frame for image-text models such as GPT-4o, performance reaches 60.6% and
313   46.4%, approaching the video-language model’s 80.3% and 61.6%, respectively. In addition, the
314   performance of Tarsier-34B does not significantly drop—on average less than 3%—when the input
315   videos are shuffled, indicating the low temporal understanding required for solving the benchmarks.
316   These results show that open-ended video-language benchmarks also exhibit strong spatial bias, not
317   requiring temporal understanding to be solved.

352   5     TVB ENCH : A TEMPORAL VIDEO QUESTION ANSWERING B ENCHMARK
353
354   We propose TVBench, a new benchmark specifically created to evaluate temporal understanding in
355
      video QA. We adopt a multiple-choice QA approach to prevent the problems of open-ended VQA
      described in Sec. 4. The main design principles of TVBench are derived from, and address, the
356
      problems listed in Sec. 3. Appendix A.2 provides an overview of the tasks, questions, and answers
357
      candidates used in our benchmark. We verify our choice of tasks and QA templates in Sec. 5.3 by
358   the performance of multi-modal LLMs with solely a random frame or shuffled videos.
359
360
      5.1   D EVELOPING A TEMPORAL VIDEO - LANGUAGE BENCHMARK
361
362
      This section explains the key strategies implemented in TVBench to address the issues identified in
363   Sec. 3 of current video MCQA evaluation benchmarks.
364
365   Strategy 1: Define Temporally Hard Answer Candidates. To address Problem 1, it is crucial
366
      that the temporal constraints in the question are essential for determining the correct answer. This
      involves designing time-sensitive questions and selecting temporal challenging answer candidates.
367
368   1. We select 10 temporally challenging tasks that require: repetition counting (Action Count), prop-
369      erties of moving objects (Object Shuffle, Object Count, Moving Direction), temporal localization
370      (Action Localization, Unexpected Action), sequential ordering (Action Sequence, Scene Transi-
371      tion, Egocentric Sequence), and distinguishing between similar actions (Action Antonyms).
372   2. We define hard-answer candidates based on the original annotations to ensure realism and rele-
373      vance, rather than relying on LLM-generated candidates that are often random and easily disre-
374      garded, as seen in MVBench. For example, in the Scene Transition task (Fig. 5), we design a QA
375      template that provides candidates based on the two scenes occurring in the videos for this task,
376      rather than implausible options like “From work to the gym.” Similarly, for the Action Sequence
377      task, we include only two answer candidates corresponding to the actions that actually occurred
         in the video. More details for the remaining tasks in Appendix A.2.

399   Strategy 2: Define QA pairs that are not overly informative. Contrary to LLM-based generation,
400   we apply basic templates to mitigate the effect of text-biased QA pairs, mitigating Problem 2.
401   1. We design QA pairs that are concise and not unnecessarily informative by applying task-specific
402      templates. These templates ensure that the QA pairs lack sufficient information to determine the
403      correct answer purely from text. An example of Unexpected Action is illustrated in Fig. 6. QA
404      pairs require the same level of understanding for the model to identify what is amusing in the
405      video, but without providing additional textual information. Unlike MVBench, the model cannot
406      simply select the only plausible option containing a dog. We use the same candidate sets across
407      tasks like Action Count, Object Count, Object Shuffle, Action Localization, Unexpected Action,
408
         and Moving Direction, to ensure balanced datasets with an equal distribution of correct answers,
         keeping visual complexity while reducing textual bias. Appendix Table 5 provides an overview of
409
         all tasks, demonstrating that the QA templates are carefully crafted without unnecessary textual
410
         information.
411
412   2. Solving the overreliance on world knowledge requires providing questions and candidates that
413
         contain only the necessary information, specifically removing factual information that the LLM
         can exploit. We remove tasks such as Episodic Reasoning, that are based on QA pairs about TV
414
         shows or movies.

      TVBench, where visual data alone is insufficient to outperform random chance, unlike MVBench.

      In addition, we analyze the effect of reversing videos instead of shuffling them. This significantly
461

462   the best models. For instance, Tarsier-7B and Tarsier-34B perform worse than random by 6.1% and
463   4.9%, respectively. This is expected, as top models on TVBench rely on temporal information.
464   Reversing frames misleads them with incorrect cues while shuffling disrupts the temporal structure
465   entirely. These results demonstrate that temporal understanding is crucial for TVBench which is in
466   contrast to MVBench where reversed and non-reversed videos obtain the same performance.
467
      Does vision matter? For TVBench, state-of-the-art LLMs with text-only perform at random levels,
468   highlighting the effectiveness of our Strategy 2 for Problem 2. Notably, Llama 3 achieves the best
469   performance, just 1.4% above random chance on TVBench, whereas it performs 10.8% better on
470   MVBench. This indicates that LLMs cannot determine the answer solely by analyzing the question
471   and answer candidates or by relying on prior world knowledge. Thus, visual information becomes
472   key for solving TVBench.

      6   D ISCUSSION
475   A sobering view on current models. With our new TVBench, we can accurately assess the tem-
476   poral understanding of existing video-language models. Surprisingly, we find that recent state-of-
477   the-art and highly popular models, such as VideoChat2, ST-LLM, PLLava, VideGPT+, GPT-4o,
478   VideoLLaMA2 7B, mPLUG-Owl3 perform close to random chance on our temporal benchmark,
479   with the largest gain being only +8.6%. The Gemini 1.5 Pro, Qwen2-VL, LLaVA-Video, IXC-2.5,
480   and Tarsier models outperform the random baseline, achieving up to +20.5%. The primary distinc-
481   tion between Tarsier and previous open-sourced models lies in its substantial increase in pretraining
482
      data, leveraging 3.8 million vision-text samples, of which only 1 million are images. From these re-
      sults, we observe that TVBench amplifies the performance gaps between models with the strongest
483
      temporal understanding and those with weaker capabilities, as a good benchmark should.

713   A.2.1    B ENCHMARK C REATION
714
715
      Table 5 provides the template used to generate the QA pairs for each task on TVBench. For the tasks
      Action Count, Object Count, Object Shuffle, Action Localization, Unexpected Action, and Moving
716
      Direction, we use the same set of options for every question. For Action Sequence and Scene Tran-
717
      sition, we generate candidates by selecting an action and a scene from the video to create negative
718   candidates. The Action Antonym candidate set consists of two similar actions that are difficult to
719   distinguish without time understanding, such as ’Wear a jacket’ and ’Take off a jacket.’ Finally, for
720   Egocentric Sequence, we generate random modifications to the correct sequence of actions to create
721   three negative candidates. Table 5 also details the datasets from which videos for each task were
722   sourced. Here we give more details for each task and their template:
723
      Action Antonym. We source videos from the NTU (Liu et al., 2019) RGB+D 120 (action classi-
724   fication dataset). Videos are filtered to retain only those belonging to one of 16 selected categories
725   (e.g., take off bag, put on bag, putting something into a bag, take something out of a bag). For each
726   QA, we select the correct option as the label and the alternative option as the opposite action (e.g.,
727   take off bag vs put on bag).
728
      Action Count. Questions are sourced from the Perception (Patraucean et al., 2024) dataset. QAs
729
      are filtered to always include the same candidate set with four options, each being correct for 25%
730   of the questions.
731
732   Action Localization. Questions are sourced from MVBench(Li et al., 2024b) The candidate set is
733
      balanced, with four options, each being correct 25% of the time.
734   Action Sequence. Videos are selected from the STAR (Wu et al., 2024) dataset, containing two
735   actions. The question is always: ”What did the person do first?” Two candidates are provided, each
736   representing one of the actions depicted in the video.
737   Egocentric Sequence. We ask for the correct order of tasks performed in egocentric videos taken
738   from CSV (Qian et al., 2022). The question is: ”What is the sequence of actions shown in the
739   video?” The correct order is taken from the original annotations. Three negative candidates are
740   generated by swapping two tasks in the correct order.
741
      Moving Direction. Videos and annotations are taken from CLEVRER(Yi et al., 2019), including
742
      annotations for the positions and directions of each object in the video. The question is: ”Which
743   direction does the {yellow sphere} move in the video?” Candidates are always the same four options,
744   each being correct for 25% of the questions: 1. Down and to the left. 2. Down and to the right. 3.
745   Up and to the right. 4. Up and to the left.
746
      Object Count. Videos and annotations are taken from CLEVRER(Yi et al., 2019) The question is:
747
      ”How many moving cylinders are there when the video begins?” The candidate set always consists
748
      of four options, each being correct for 25% of the questions. QAs are selected so that ignoring the
749   time constraint (e.g., ”when the video begins”) would lead to incorrect answers (e.g., the number of
750   objects at the beginning is different from at the end).
751
752
      Object Shuffle. Questions are sourced from Perception(Patraucean et al., 2024) QAs are filtered to
      always include the same candidate set with four options, each being correct for 25% of the questions.
753
754   Scene Transition. Questions are sourced from MVBench(Li et al., 2024b) (with videos originally
755   from MoVQA). The original question and correct answer are retained, while incorrect options are
      discarded. A hard negative is generated by reversing the sequence in the correct answer.
