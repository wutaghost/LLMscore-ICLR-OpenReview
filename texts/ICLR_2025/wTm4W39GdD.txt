009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            As large language models (LLMs) increasingly power conversational agents, un-
012            derstanding how they represent, predict, and influence human emotions is crucial
013            for ethical deployment. By analyzing probabilistic dependencies between emo-
014            tional states in model outputs, we uncover hierarchical structures in LLMs’ emo-
015            tion representations. Our findings show that larger models, such as LLaMA 3.1
016            (405B parameters), develop more complex hierarchies. We also find that better
017            emotional modeling enhances persuasive abilities in synthetic negotiation tasks,
018
               with LLMs that more accurately predict counterparts’ emotions achieving supe-
               rior outcomes. Additionally, we explore how persona biases, such as gender and
019
               socioeconomic status, affect emotion recognition, revealing frequent misclassifi-
020
               cations of minority personas. This study contributes to both the scientific under-
021            standing and ethical considerations of emotion modeling in LLMs.

085   The Psychology of Emotion Representation in Humans. The organization of emotions in hu-
086   mans is a subject of considerable debate. Hierarchical models propose that emotions are structured
087
      in tiers, with basic emotions branching into more specific ones (Shaver et al., 1987; Plutchik, 2001).
      Conversely, dimensional models like the valence-arousal framework position emotions within a con-
088
      tinuous space defined by dimensions such as pleasure-displeasure and activation-deactivation (Rus-
089
      sell, 1980). The universality of emotions is also contested; while Ekman (1992) identified basic
090   emotions that are universally recognized, others argue for cultural relativity in emotional experience
091   and expression (Barrett, 2017; Gendron et al., 2014). Additionally, Ong et al. (2015) explored lay
092   theories of emotions, emphasizing how individuals conceptualize emotions in terms of goals and
093   social interactions. Our work acknowledges these diverse perspectives and focuses on hierarchical
094   structures as one approach to modeling emotions within LLMs.
095
      Emotional Understanding in Language Models. Recent advancements in language models have
096   led to significant progress in understanding and generating emotionally rich text. Large language
097   models demonstrate strong capabilities of capturing subtle emotional cues in text (Felbo et al., 2017),
098   generating empathetic responses (Rashkin, 2018), and detecting emotion in dialogues (Zhong et al.,
099   2019; Poria et al., 2019). A number of recent works have used LLMs to infer emotion from in-
100   context examples (Broekens et al., 2023; Tak & Gratch, 2023; Yongsatianchot et al., 2023; Houlihan
101   et al., 2023; Zhan et al., 2023; Tak & Gratch, 2024; Gandhi et al., 2024). We follow the direction
102   of representation engineering to study cognition in AI systems (Zou et al., 2023) and build on the
103   prompt-based approaches to study LLM’s capability and bias in emotion detection (Mao et al., 2022;
104
      Li et al., 2023). Beyond existing research on LLM’s ability to recognize and generate emotional
      content, our work systematically explores hierarchical emotion relationships, emotional bias across
105
      demographic identities, and emotion dynamics in conversation.
106

      work is related to unsupervised hierarchical representation learning in language processing. Topic

      3         H IERARCHICAL R EPRESENTATION OF E MOTIONS
134
135
      We define a hierarchical structure of emotions by identifying probabilistic relationships between
136
      broad and specific emotional states. For example, optimism can be seen as a specific form of joy, as
137   LLMs often label a scenario as “joy” with high probability when “optimism” is likely, though the
138   reverse may not always hold. These relationships are captured in a directed acyclic graph (DAG),
139   revealing dependencies between emotional states. We then analyze these hierarchies across models
140   of different sizes.
141
142   3.1          G ENERATING H IERARCHY FROM THE M ATCHING M ATRIX
143
144   Figure 2 summarizes the procedure we use to compute the matching matrix of different emotions.
145   Given a sentence followed by the phrase “The emotion in this sentence is”, we have the model
146
      output the probability distribution of the next word. Then, we consider the entries corresponding
      to emotion words, using a list of 135 emotion words from Shaver et al. (1987). For N sentences,
147
      we assembly a matrix Y with dimension N × 135, with row n representing the probability of each
148
      emotion words for the nth sentence. We define the matching matrix as C = Y T Y . Each element,
149          PN
      Cij = n=1 Yni Ynj , is a measure of the degree to which emotion i and emotion j are produced
150
      in similar contexts. Under the assumption that the next word probability is equal to the model’s
151   estimate of the likelihood of the corresponding emotion, the elements in C capture joint probabilities
152   of emotions co-occurring across sentences. We defer the formal statements to Appendix A.
153
154

      goal is to identify pairs of emotions where a implies b. In implementation, we set a threshold,
155
      0 < t < 1, that determines whether we include a certain edge between the two emotions. Emotion
156
      a is considered a child of b if,

      In the previous section, we established that LLMs exhibit a solid understanding of the hierarchi-
247
      cal structure of emotions like humans. Our next question is: does this understanding translate into
248
      real-world behavior, enabling LLMs to perceive human emotions? In psychology, research on emo-
249   tion differentiation typically involves participants reporting on emotional state several times across
250   a variety of circumstances, allowing researchers to assess individuals’ ability to differentiate be-
251   tween emotions (Barrett, 2004; Pond Jr et al., 2012). Drawing from this approach, we introduced
252   Llama 405B to a range of personas and scenarios designed to evoke various emotional cues. We
253   then prompted the model to identify the emotions relevant to each scenario (See Figure 5 for our
254   experimental design).
255   We employed diverse personas representing variations in gender, race, socioeconomic status (in-
256   cluding income and education), age, religion, and their combinations to analyze how these factors
257   influence emotion recognition in LLMs. We also explored connections to psychological conditions,
258   providing a cognitive science perspective to interpret our findings.

408   4.1      H OW LLM S REFLECT HUMAN EMOTION PERCEPTION
409
410   This subsection explores how LLMs’ emotion recognition aligns with human perception. We inves-
411   tigate its capabilities through a user study comparing its performance to humans, experiments using
412
      realistic datasets, and analysis of psychological conditions. The results reveal that Llama 405B
      mirrors human biases in emotion recognition, such as demographic-based disparities and misclassi-
413
      fication patterns, while also replicate insights from psychological research.
414
415
      User Study: Comparing emotion recognition in humans and LLMs. We conduct a user study
416
      to compare emotion recognition accuracy between humans and LLMs. Using Prolific2 , we recruited
417
      60 participants and randomly selected question from each of the 135 categories. Participants were
418   then asked to identify the emotion they felt most closely matched each sentence. Figure 9(a) presents
419   emotion recognition accuracy across six broad emotion categories for humans and Llama 405B. We
420   find that LLM struggles to recognize the emotion of “surprise.” With Llama, the ground truth label
421   “surprise” is often misclassified as “excitement” or “fear,” a tendency that becomes more pronounced
422   when personas are introduced (see Figure 22 in the Appendix). Other than this, Llama generally
423   shows a stronger ability to perceive emotions compared to humans, achieving an average accuracy
424   of 87.8% across six broad emotion categories, whereas human participants reach an average accu-
425   racy of 73.5%. As shown in Figure 9(b)-(c), Llama exhibits human-like biases in misclassification
426
      patterns across various demographic groups. However, these biases are more pronounced among hu-
      man participants. For instance, in Figure 9(b), both Black participants and Black personas modeled
427
      by Llama are more likely to misinterpret fear as anger. Similarly, as shown in Figure 9(c), female
428
      participants and female personas modeled by Llama tend to make the opposite error, misinterpreting
429   anger as fear.

      6    D ISCUSSION
516
517
      Our study provides several key findings on how LLMs comprehend and engage with human emo-
      tions, with important implications for future AI development and deployment. As LLMs scale, they
518
      develop increasingly intricate hierarchical representations of emotions that align closely with estab-
519
      lished psychological models. This suggests that larger models are not merely processing language
520   but internalizing emotional structures, enabling more nuanced and human-like interactions.
521
522   Additionally, our findings highlight that the personas adopted by LLMs can significantly bias their
523
      emotion recognition. When LLMs assume personas defined by attributes like gender or socioeco-
      nomic status, their perception and classification of emotions shift. This raises concerns about the
524
      reinforcement of stereotypes and the amplification of social biases in AI systems.
525
526   We also show a direct correlation between an LLM’s ability to recognize emotions and its success
527   in persuasive tasks, such as negotiations. In our “acorn sales” task, LLMs with stronger emotional
528   modeling secured higher prices, suggesting that emotionally intelligent models can more effectively
529
      influence behavior. This finding raises ethical concerns about the potential for AI agents to manipu-
      late emotions and decisions without users’ awareness or consent.
530
531   These findings have important implications for the future of AI. While LLMs’ ability to form hier-
532   archical emotional representations could enable more empathetic and emotionally intelligent appli-
533   cations, persona-induced biases require proactive mitigation through diverse training data and bias
534   detection algorithms. Furthermore, the potential for AI to manipulate emotions calls for the develop-
535
      ment of ethical guidelines and regulatory frameworks to protect user autonomy and prevent misuse.
      Future research should focus on understanding how LLMs develop emotional representations and
536
      creating tools to promote ethical behavior, ensuring that these systems are not only advanced but
537
      also aligned with human values and societal norms.
