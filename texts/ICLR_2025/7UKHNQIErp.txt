009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Recent direct preference alignment algorithms (DPA), such as DPO, have shown
012
               great promise in aligning large language models to human preferences. While this
013
               has motivated the development of many new variants of the original DPO loss,
014            understanding the differences between these recent proposals, as well as develop-
015            ing new DPA loss functions, remains difficult given the lack of a technical and
016            conceptual framework for reasoning about the underlying semantics of these al-
017            gorithms. In this paper, we attempt to remedy this by formalizing DPA losses
018            in terms of discrete reasoning problems. Specifically, we ask: Given an existing
019            DPA loss, can we systematically derive a symbolic expression that characterizes
020            its semantics? How do the semantics of two losses relate to each other? We pro-
021            pose a novel formalism for characterizing preference losses for single model and
022
               reference model based approaches, and identify symbolic forms for a number of
               commonly used DPA variants. Further, we show how this formal view of pref-
023
               erence learning sheds new light on both the size and structure of the DPA loss
024
               landscape, making it possible to not only rigorously characterize the relationships
025            between recent loss proposals but also to systematically explore the landscape and
026            derive new loss functions from first principles. We hope our framework and find-
027            ings will help provide useful guidance to those working on human AI alignment.

      Our study attempts to remedy this problem by formalizing the corresponding loss functions in terms
055   of logic. Such a formalization is based on trying to answer the question: Given an existing loss
056   function, such as DPO (see Figure 1), can we derive a symbolic expression that captures the core
057   semantics of that loss function (i.e., one that we can then systematically compile back into the exact
058   loss)? In treating loss functions as discrete reasoning problems, ones that abstract away from lower-
059   level optimization details and tell us about high-level model behavior, it becomes possible to study
060   them using conventional semantic notions from logic (e.g., entailment), relate it semantically to
061   other programs, or even modify its underlying logical semantics to derive entirely new algorithms.
062   To do formalization, we devise a novel probabilistic logic based on a generalization of the notion of
063   semantic loss (SL) (Xu et al., 2018) coupled with a provably correct mechanical procedure for trans-
064   lating existing DPA losses into programs in our logic. As in SL, losses are produced from symbolic
065   programs by counting the weighted propositional models of those programs, reducing the problem
066   to one of probabilistic inference (Chavira & Darwiche, 2008). In contrast to the kinds of symbolic
067   programs commonly used with SL, however, empirically successful DPA losses impose systematic
068   conditional constraints on the types of models that should be counted, which shape the structure
069
      of the underlying probability distribution. We express these constraints through a new primitive in
      our logic called a preference structure that also addresses various technical and conceptual issues
070
      involved with modeling pairwise preference symbolically. It is through such constraints that certain
071
      semantic relationships between existing losses can be easily observed and new losses can be derived.
072
073   Our formal view of preference learning sheds much light on the size and structure of the DPA loss
074   landscape. Under modest assumptions motivated by the structure of existing DPA losses and our
075
      new logic, we see that the number of definable DPA losses is doubly exponential over   n
                                                                                                 the number
      (n) of unique predictions (i.e., forward model calls) made in a loss function, or 42 . This results
076
      in, for example, close to 4.3 billion unique variations of the original DPO loss, which leaves much
077
      room for exploration. While big, we show how this space is structured in interesting ways based on
078   formal connections between relationships that hold in the semantic space among formalized DPA
079   losses (e.g., logical entailment, equivalence) and their monotonicity properties in the loss space.
080
081
      These formal results also provide practical insights into how to effectively search for new DPA
      losses. For example, one can start with empirically successful loss functions, use the formalization
082
      to understand their semantics, then modify their semantics to arrive at novel variants that are either
083
      more constrained or less, then experiment accordingly.

086   2   R ELATED WORK
087
088   Language model alignment While traditional approaches to language model alignment have em-
089   ployed reinforcement learning (Ziegler et al., 2019; Christiano et al., 2017), we focus on DPA ap-
090   proaches such as DPO (Rafailov et al., 2024) and SliC (Zhao et al., 2023) that use closed-form loss
091   functions to tune models directly to offline preferences.
092   We touch on two recent areas in this space: formal characterizations of DPA losses (Azar et al.,
093   2023; Tang et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2024) and work on devising algorithmically enhanced variants
094   of DPO (Amini et al., 2024; Hong et al., 2024; Meng et al., 2024; Pal et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024;
095   Ethayarajh et al., 2024; Park et al., 2024). In contrast to this work on formal characterization, which
096   focuses on the optimization properties of DPA losses and particular parameterizations like Bradley-
097   Terry, we attempt to formally characterize the semantic relationships between these variants of DPO
098
      in an optimization agnostic way to better understand the structure of the DPA loss landscape.
099   Neuro-symbolic modeling For formalization, we take inspiration from work on compiling symbolic
100   formulas into novel loss functions (Li et al., 2019; Fischer et al., 2019; Marra et al., 2019; Asai &
101   Hajishirzi, 2020, inter alia), which is used for incorporating background constraints into learning to
102   improve training robustness and model consistency. In particular, we focus on approaches based on
103   probabilistic logic (Manhaeve et al., 2018; Ahmed et al., 2022; 2023; van Krieken et al., 2024).
104   In contrast to this work, however, we focus on the inverse problem of decompilation, or deriving
105   symbolic expressions from known and empirically successful loss functions to better understand
106   their semantics (see Friedman et al. (2024) for a similar idea). Work in this area has mostly been
107   limited to symbolically deriving standard loss function such as cross-entropy (Giannini et al., 2020;
      Li et al., 2019), whereas we look at deriving more complex algorithms for LLMs.

      Relationships between predictions are expressed as symbolic formulas Relationships between
217   model predictions will take the form of symbolic constraints expressed as formulas of propositional
218   logic P defined by applying zero or more Boolean operators over probabilistic predictions. For
219   example, in Figure 3 (A), the top formula, which we later show is fundamental to the semantics
220   of many DPA approaches, uses the implication operator (Implies) to express the constraint that
221   model M should never deem the loser yl to be a valid generation (M(x, yl )) without deeming the
222   winner yw to also be valid (M(x, yw )). The bottom formula tells us instead that only the winner yw
223   should be deemed valid using the conjunction and negation operators (And, Not).2
224   When grounded to model behavior via the proposition weights, such constraints tell us about the
225   structure of a model’s output probability distribution, as visualized in Figure 3 (B). Semantically,
226   we assume that what constitutes a valid generation is any probabilistic prediction whose weight
227   exceeds some threshold ϵ in that distribution, similar to ϵ-truncated support in Hewitt et al. (2020).
228   While our results later will not depend on making any direct assumptions about ϵ, such a definition

531
532
      Despite the routine use of a variety of DPA algorithms to align LLMs with human preferences,
533
      knowing what exactly the losses underlying these algorithms capture and how they relate to each
534   other remains largely unknown. We presented a new technique for characterizing the semantics
535   of such losses in terms of logical formulas over boolean propositions that capture model predic-
536   tions. Key to our approach is the decompilation procedure, allowing one to derive provably correct
537   symbolic formulas corresponding to any loss function expressed as a ratio of disjoint multilinear
538   polynomials. Our approach provides a fresh perspective into preference losses, identifying a rich
539   loss landscape and opening up new ways for practitioners to explore new losses by systematically
      varying the symbolic formulas corresponding to existing successful loss functions.
