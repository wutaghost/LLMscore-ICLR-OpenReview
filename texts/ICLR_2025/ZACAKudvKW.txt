009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            We identify a new task for watermarking – namely the simultaneous identification
012            of text as being automatically generated alongside the identification of the LLM
013            user. We show that a naı̈ve approach that treats a text as artificially generated if
               a user is correctly identified is prone to problems of false positives arising from
014
               multiple hypothesis comparison. We propose a novel approach 1 that is much
015
               more robust to the number of users. We derive theoretical bounds, and empically
016            validate our approach.

019   1    I NTRODUCTION
020
021   Large Language Models (LLMs) (Radford et al., 2018; 2019; Brown et al., 2020; Achiam et al.,
022   2023; Touvron et al., 2023a;b) have emerged as the dominant technology across a wide range of
023
      natural language processing tasks. While these models have significantly advanced the field, their
      misuse has raised numerous ethical concerns. Of particular concern is the use of LLMs to imperson-
024
      ate human text, and to generate text that appears to represent a sincere effort to engage, but is in fact
025   automatically generated. Example use cases include the automatic generation of homework; of sci-
026   entific papers, grants, and reviews; and the automatic generation of spam and astroturfing (Wachter
027   et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2023; Nikiforovskaya et al., 2020). The primary concern here is that such
028   automatically generated text can flood the environment, making it impossible to identify sincere
029   texts that are worth reading and responding to.
030   In response to these harms, researchers have suggested the use of digital watermarking (Aaronson &
031   Kirchner, 2023; Fernandez et al., 2023; Kirchenbauer et al., 2023b). These approaches modify the
032   sampling step of the LLM, allowing for the automatic identification of texts generated by a particular
033   sampler. If the use of watermarked samplers becomes common, it would be much easier to identify
034   and manage LLM generated content. However, this is not the only use of digital watermarking. As
      experimentally shown in Fernandez et al. (2023), digital watermarking can also be used to identify
035
      individual users of an LLM.
036
037   This use of watermarking, which we refer to as user watermarking, caries with it the risk of privacy
038   violations. For example, if LLMs are used as part of a pseudoanonymization process to rephrase
039
      text, user watermarking could allow the text to be traced back to the account that rephrased the
      text. However, if appropriately disclosed, it could replace more intrusive governance measures. For
040
      example, user watermarking would allow LLM service providers to identify and shut down accounts
041   used for generating spam without requiring the monitoring of every customer’s API call.
042
043
      However, user watermarking cannot be simultaneously performed alongside standard watermarking
      (i.e. identifying if a text is LLM generated). While the most obvious option is to simply say that
044
      a text is LLM generated if it is consistent with any user watermark – this is a form of multiple
045   hypothesis testing (i.e. either the text was generated with user 1, or user 2, or ...), and if the test is
046   fixed at a particular sensitivity, we find that the number of false positives increases as the number of
047   users considered grows, a phenomenon we refer to as the false recognition problem. As discussed
048   in the research by Liu et al. (2023b); Giboulot & Teddy (2024), false positives are considerably
049   more critical than false negatives, as erroneously identifying human-generated texts as watermarked
050
      can result in more severe adverse consequences, including the wrongful suspension or shutdown of
      user accounts. We demonstrate, both theoretically and empirically, that as the user size expands,
051
      the false recognition bound increases exponentially. This rapid increase leads to failed watermark

      To address this issue, we propose a novel Dual Watermark Inspector (DWI) designed to detect the
      information embedded within the watermark while mitigating the false positive recognition problem.
058
      Our approach involves a joint encoding of indicator variable and key information into the text.
059   Specifically, the indicator variable is a binary variable that signals if the text is watermarked. In
060   contrast, the key information contains user identity encoded as an integer key, following the approach
061   of previous work (Fernandez et al., 2023). This approach can be easily extended to accommodate
062   more complex types of information. We use a hash decision function to determine whether the
063   current token encodes indicator or key information. Then, we follow Aaronson & Kirchner (2023);
064   Fernandez et al. (2023) to use the Gumbel trick to generate the current token. Afterwards, we
      calculate statistics for the indicator variable and key information respectively, mark the text with
065
      high indicator score as watermarked, and return the key with the maximal score as the encoded key.
066
067   Furthermore, we perform a rigorous analyses of the false positive error bounds associated with tra-
068   ditional methods compared to our proposed method. We prove a false positive error bound of a
069
      standard threshold method (Fernandez et al., 2023) as well as Multibit Yoo et al. (2023b). Our the-
      ory shows that the False Positive Ratio (FPR) can grow uncontrollably for threshold-based methods
070
      (We refer to this as Full Key Encoding (FKE) in this paper) particularly when the key space, corre-
071   sponding to the number of user identities, is large or the text length is short. We analyze our method
072   theoretically and demonstrate that it significantly outperforms the FKE method. Specifically, we
073   prove that if the key space is larger than around 100, our method has a better FPR upper bound than
074   FKE. Additionally, we propose a Hybrid Dual Watermark Inspector (HDWI) strategy that combines
075   the DWI method and the original FKE method, with another theoretical bound to guarantee perfor-
076
      mance. Both our theoretical and empirical findings indicate that our proposed approach surpasses
      existing methods and substantially decreases the false recognition rate.
077
078   The contributions of this paper are as follows: (1) We conduct a rigorous theoretical and empiri-
079   cal analysis of the false recognition problem in watermarking for LLMs; (2) We propose a novel
080
      analysis of the false positive rate, and illustrate the potential severity of this problem under specific
      conditions, such as variations in text length, key space size, and other relevant factors; (3) We intro-
081
      duce a novel method DWI to reduce the false recognition rate, while preserving a high true positive
082   rate, and empirically demonstrate the effectiveness of our proposed method.

086   2   R ELATED W ORKS
087
088   We categorize existing watermarking methods into two categories: Watermark Indicators and Infor-
089   mation Conveyance. Watermark Indicators encode a specific indicator within LLM-generated text,
090   that indicates whether the text is generated by a watermarked LLM or not. Aaronson & Kirchner
091   (2023); Fernandez et al. (2023); Fu et al. (2024) suggest employing the Gumbel trick to generate
092   a corresponding random variable with a distinct distribution for watermarked text. Kirchenbauer
093   et al. (2023a;b) propose dividing the vocabulary into red and green lists based on preceding tokens.
      Christ et al. (2024) introduce the concept of embedding undetectable watermarks in language model
094
      outputs using cryptographic techniques. Zhao et al. (2023) propose the Unigram-Watermark method
095   to improve the detection accuracy and robustness of watermarks in LLM-generated text.
096
097   On the other hand, Information Conveyance methods embed much richer information within the
      generated text. Most existing methods embed an integer key into the generated text, which could
098
      be used to represent user ID. Fernandez et al. (2023) propose utilizing a hash key to represent
099
      essential information. Yoo et al. (2023a;b); Wang et al. (2023); Boroujeny et al. (2024); Qu et al.
100   (2024) suggest encoding multibit information in the watermark. Abdelnabi & Fritz (2021) advocate
101   for adversarially encoding information into the watermark. However, these approaches determine
102   whether the text is watermarked by reusing the key information. To the best of our knowledge, no
103   prior work has analyzed how such reusing may result in false recognition as key capacity increases.
104   In general, the distinction between Indicator and Information Conveyancing is somewhat artificial.
105   Most indicator methods Aaronson & Kirchner (2023); Fu et al. (2024) draw on work on cryptograph-
106   ically secure hashing, and their behaviour depends on a hidden salt. Instead of randomly selecting
107   salts, they can simply be chosen to encode multibit data, and this approach was used by (Fernandez
      et al., 2023) to encode user IDs.

      3     D UAL WATERMARK I NSPECTOR
109
110
      We propose a novel approach termed the Dual Watermark Inspector (DWI) to address the false
111
      recognition problem when embedding keys with capacity K into LLM watermarking. Here, capacity
112   K refers to K distinct keys that can be encoded, such as a user ID. Instead of relying on all tokens to
113   encode the key information, we selectively require certain tokens to encode the indicator as well as
114   the key information. Additionally, we propose a Hybrid Dual Watermark Inspector (HDWI) method
115   that further leverages the key information to assist in detecting the watermark.
116   Our work builds upon Aaronson & Kirchner (2023); Fernandez et al. (2023), who deliberately con-
117   structed a statistic to guide both the generation and detection strategies. Although the original pa-
118   per does not explicitly identify this method as the Gumbel-Max trick (Gumbel, 1954; Maddison
119   et al., 2014; Jang et al., 2016), we show that this method is essentially a Gumbel-Max trick (Sec-
120   tion 3.3).We offer additional details to enhance the understanding of how and why the Gumbel-Max
121
      trick contributes to effective watermark detection. This formulation underpins our analysis in Sec-
      tion 4. In our discussion, we refer to the method introduced by Fernandez et al. (2023) as Full Key
122
      Encoding (FKE), where all tokens are used to encode a single key.

275
276   To provide a thorough understanding of the false recognition problem inherent in the traditional Full
277   Key Encoding (FKE) method, and to demonstrate how our proposed DWI and HDWI approaches
278
      address this issue, we derive the false positive bound for these methods. We denote the capacity
      of key information as K (which can be used to represent the total number of user IDs), the gener-
279
      ated sequence length as T , and the user-specified threshold as s. We compute three false positive
280   bounds. The first bound, presented in Theorem 1, provides a theoretical understanding of the base-
281   line FKE method, which considers a document as watermarked if it matches any one of the key ID
282   ξ. Next, we conduct the second analysis in Theorem 2 to demonstrate how and why our proposed
283   DWI method effectively addresses the false recognition problem. Following this, we perform addi-
284   tional analysis in Theorem 3, illustrating how the HDWI method can further enhance performance.
285
      Then, we conduct a numerical experiment based on these bounds that empirically demonstrates the
      differences between FKE and our proposed approach DWI. We also present an theoretical analysis
286
      of multi-bit methods (Yoo et al., 2023b; Wang et al., 2023), based on Kirchenbauer et al. (2023a) in
287   Appendix A.12, showing that false recognition persists as key capacity increases.

       5.5       WATERMARKED T EXT R ATIO
467
468    The thresholds τk and τd are critical in determining whether a text is watermarked, with their op-
469    timal values varying according to the watermarked text ratio. The metrics presented in the main
470    experiment are averaged across datasets with differing ratios of watermarked text. To provide a
471    comprehensive analysis of performance across various watermarked text ratios, we examine the
472    relationship between the watermarked text ratio and the metrics Accu-I, Accu-O, and FPR. As illus-
       trated in Figure 4, the following observations are made: (1) As the watermarked text ratio increases
473
       from 0% to 100%, Accu-I initially decreases and then rises after the 50% mark. This trend occurs
474    because, when the watermark ratio is 0%, tuning the threshold on the development set to a very high
475    value results in classifying all samples as unwatermarked, thereby leading to optimal performance.
476    A similar situation arises when the watermark ratio nears 100%, tuning the threshold to classify all
477    samples as watermarked yields the best performance on both the development set and the test set.
478    (2) Accu-O decreases as the watermark ratio increases, due to models “overfitting” to predict all
479    samples as unwatermarked when the ratio is 0%. As the ratio increases, predicting the exact key
480
       ID becomes more challenging than merely predicting whether the text is watermarked. However,
       Accu-O slightly increases as the watermark ratio approaches 100% for FKE and PKE. This improve-
481
       ment occurs because the models are tuned to avoid predicting any samples as unwatermarked. (3)
482    With an increasing watermarked text ratio, FPR also increases. This is because, at low watermark
483    ratios, the tuned thresholds are set very high, making it unlikely for any sample to be classified as
484    watermarked, thus eliminating false positive inferences. (4) It is evident that our proposed DWI and
485    HDWI models outperform the FKE model across nearly all watermarked text ratios, demonstrating
       the effectiveness of our approach in various scenarios.

531   6            C ONCLUSION
532
533   In this paper, we address the false recognition problem in watermarking methods for text gener-
534
      ated by LLMs. We establish a rigorous theoretical bound demonstrating the inherent inevitability
      of false positive errors in Information Conveyance watermarking techniques like FKE. To mitigate
535
      this problem, we introduce a novel DWI method that jointly encodes indicator and key information.
536   Furthermore, we present a analysis of our proposed method and validate it through extensive em-
537   pirical experiments. Our results, both theoretical and empirical, indicate that the DWI method and
538   its variants effectively reduces the false positive ratio, thereby alleviating the false recognition prob-
539   lem. This enhancement in watermarking reliability can significantly promote the trustworthiness of
      LLM-generated content.

1283
       Yoo et al. (2023b); Wang et al. (2024) extended Kirchenbauer et al. (2023a)’s method to support
1284   multi-bit encoding. Their approach detects if a text is watermarked by use of a binomial statistic
1285   (Yoo et al., 2023b). However, since the statistic is based on the maximal value of multiple binomial
1286   variables, it should no longer be considered a measure of a binomial distribution, but instead an
1287   approximate Gumbel distribution (Kotz & Nadarajah, 2000; Haan & Ferreira, 2006).
1288   As the parameter for the Gumbel distribution is challenging to compute, we directly derive a novel
1289   bound for the composed extreme variable. Our analysis reveals that this method continues to suffer
1290   from the false recognition problem.

1293   compute the position pt of the message m for the t-th token, denoted as ρt = m[pt ], where pt ∈ [b]
1294   and ρt ∈ [r]. Here, b is the message length, and r indicates the number of bits each position encodes.
