009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            This paper introduces a pioneering methodology, termed StructTuning, to effi-
012            ciently transform foundation Large Language Models (LLMs) into domain spe-
013            cialists. It significantly reduces the training corpus requirement to a mere 0.3%,
014            while achieving an impressive 50% of traditional knowledge injection perfor-
015            mance. Our method is inspired by the educational processes of human students,
016
               particularly how structured domain knowledge from textbooks is assimilated
               and subsequently applied to tackle real-world challenges through specific ex-
017
               ercises. Based on this, we propose a novel two-stage strategy for knowledge
018
               injection and alignment: Structure-aware Continual Pre-Training (SCPT) and
019            Structure-aware Supervised Fine-Tuning (SSFT). In the SCPT phase, we auto-
020            matically extract the domain knowledge taxonomy and reorganize the training
021            corpora, enabling LLMs to effectively link textual segments to targeted knowl-
022            edge points within the taxonomy. In the SSFT phase, we explicitly prompt
023            models to elucidate the underlying knowledge structure in their outputs, lever-
024            aging the structured domain insight to address practical problems. Our ulti-
025            mate method has undergone extensive evaluations across model architectures
026            and scales, using closed-book question-answering tasks on LongBench and MMed-
027
               Bench datasets. Furthermore, we have investigated the scalability of structure-
               aware knowledge injection across varying sizes of training corpora, which lays
028
               a foundation for scaling up our StructTuning for stronger domain-specific LLMs
029
               with comprehensive data utilization. Code is available at this anonymous URL:
030            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/StructTuning/.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Large language models (LLMs) have recently seen extensive deployment across various applica-
036   tions (Vaswani et al., 2017; Achiam et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023; Bi et al., 2024). When adapting
037   foundational models (e.g., Llama series (Touvron et al., 2023a;b; Dubey et al., 2024)) for specialized
038   AI assistants in distinct domains (Qiu et al., 2024; Guo et al., 2024), developers usually employ
039
      two techniques to enhance LLMs’ proficiency: retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) (Lewis et al.,
      2020) and domain knowledge injection (Gururangan et al., 2020). While RAG effectively utilizes
040
      an external knowledge base to augment information, the retrieval process’s inherent noise poses
041
      challenges to generating reliable responses, especially in scenarios requiring logical reasoning where
042   there is a semantic gap between the user’s query and the knowledge base.(Zhang et al., 2023; Chen
043   et al., 2023). Thus, another avenue of research focuses on injecting new knowledge into models via
044   training techniques (Gu et al., 2021; Hu et al., 2021; Mecklenburg et al., 2024).
045
      Continual pre-training (Sun et al., 2020; Ibrahim et al., 2024) has been preferred for integrating new,
046
      domain-specific knowledge into existing LLMs (Cui et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023b; Qiu et al., 2024).
047
      Nevertheless, it often entails resource-intensive auto-regressive training on billions of tokens from
048   the internet to learn fragmented knowledge points, rather than absorbing structured knowledge from
049   a few domain-specific textbooks (Jin et al., 2020). For example, MMedLM (Qiu et al., 2024) curates
050   25.5B tokens to derive a medical model, and DeepSeek-Coder (Guo et al., 2024) uses 2T tokens for
051   coding adaptation. The common failure to learn effectively from limited textbook content has been
052   attributed to insufficient data diversity (Zhu & Li, 2023a), which however violates the observation
053   during the human education process in Fig. 1: students gain knowledge by sequentially studying from
      textbooks, reviewing knowledge points and structures, and applying this knowledge through proper

072   exercises. In this process, all the new data to learn are textbooks (structured content) and exercising
073   examples (question-answering pairs), and students just adopt their world knowledge to memorize,
074   understand, and apply the knowledge to become domain experts (Krathwohl, 2002; Yu et al., 2023).
075   Inspired by this, we propose to inject the domain knowledge from textbooks into LLMs, as educating
076   a human student, through a novel two-stage training strategy: Structure-aware Continual Pre-Training
077   (SCPT) and Structure-aware Supervised Fine-Tuning (SSFT).
078
      In the SCPT stage, we argue that high-quality textbook data can adequately infuse the domain
079   knowledge (Gunasekar et al., 2023), where the organization of training corpora is crucial. In
080   conventional paradigms, as illustrated in Fig. 1, text corpora are simply concatenated and divided into
081   chunks of 2048 (Qiu et al., 2024) or 4096 (Guo et al., 2024), while the inherent structure of the texts
082   (e.g., catalogs of textbooks) is disregarded. Instead, we propose an automatic approach to maintain
083   each chunk’s knowledge structure. We view each chunk as a knowledge point, and employ advanced
084   LLMs to efficiently extract domain knowledge taxonomy from the corpus, bypassing the need for
085   manual annotation. Subsequently, LLMs are trained to predict the textual content (corresponding
086   to the knowledge point) under the condition of the knowledge path within the domain structure,
087
      linking individual training chunks with the entire knowledge architecture. Finally, models are asked
      to memorize the knowledge structure to review the whole domain knowledge system.
088
089   In the SSFT stage, the goal shifts from knowledge injection to enabling LLMs to recall and utilize
090   their acquired knowledge to tackle real-world challenges. We explicitly elicit knowledge paths in
091   LLMs’ responses, as a beacon for models to targeted information retrieval or logical reasoning for
092   reliable responses. To this end, we derive a scalable strategy to generate question-answer pairs as
093
      practice exercises by powerful LLMs such as GPT4 (Achiam et al., 2023) or LLaMA3 (Dubey et al.,
      2024). In the scenarios with existing QA pairs like MMedBench (Qiu et al., 2024), we retrieve related
094
      knowledge structure and content, instructing LLaMA3 to provide explanations from questions to
095
      answers based on the knowledge paths. For datasets lacking specific QA samples like LongBench (Bai
096   et al., 2023), we randomly select knowledge paths from the domain taxonomy and prompt LLaMA3
097   to craft question-answer-explanation triplets for training exercises.
098
099
      Our ultimate approach, termed StructTuning, outperforms conventional methods in domain knowledge
      injection by emulating human learning processes through SCPT and SSFT phases. We extensively
100
      evaluate StructTuning’s effectiveness across different model architectures and sizes. For domain-
101
      adapted language models, we first examine their capability to recall the injected knowledge through
102   open-ended QA on the LongBench (Bai et al., 2023) dataset, then assess their application of this
103   knowledge in addressing real-world issues via multiple-choice QA on MMedBench (Qiu et al.,
104   2024). Both evaluations underscore the superiority of StructTuning. Remarkably, we achieve a 50%
105   improvement in knowledge injection compared to the SOTA MMedLM2 in the medical domain,
106   using merely 0.3% of the training data requirement. And StructTuning illustrates the potential of
107   comparable performance with only 5% of training costs. These findings reveal the scalability of our
      method for enhancing domain-specific AI assistants with further comprehensive data utilization.

      Our contribution is summarized as follows:
109
110       • We proposed a novel two-stage training strategy, SCPT and SSFT, to inject domain knowledge
111         into LLMs by preserving and utilizing the inherent structure of the training corpus.
112       • We developed a scalable data construction framework to generate structure-aware training

114       • We conducted extensive investigations on our StructTuning strategy on various data and model

      2    R ELATED W ORK
119
      Domain Adaptation for Large Language Models. While pre-trained LLMs possess promising
120
      capabilities, their performance is often hampered by the scope and recency of their training data,
121
      which particularly affects smaller models in downstream applications (Zhao et al., 2023; Wang et al.,
122   2023a). Continual Pre-Training (CPT) addresses this by perpetually updating a pre-trained model
123   with domain-specific content (Sun et al., 2020; Xu et al., 2023b). , with parameter-efficient tuning
124   methods devised to curtail training costs (Hu et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2024c). To keep pace with the
125   latest information, models can be fine-tuned with supervised instruction-response pairs (SFT), thus
126   staying current with the advancing knowledge landscape (Mecklenburg et al., 2024; Qiu et al., 2024).
127   Existing literature confirms that combining CPT and SFT is effective for LLMs to remain precise and
128   up-to-date in dynamic fields like law (Cui et al., 2023; Nguyen, 2023) , finance (Wu et al., 2023; Li
129   et al., 2024), medicine (Wang et al., 2023b; Qiu et al., 2024), and coding (Roziere et al., 2023; Guo
130
      et al., 2024). Our study builds upon this CPT-SFT framework, innovating with SCPT-SSFT strategies
      to efficiently and effectively infuse domain knowledge with the inherent structure hierarchy.
131
132   Structure-aware Knowledge Aggregation. Knowledge structure has been widely explored in the
133   recent LLM community. A branch of researchers follows the conventional paradigm to extract
134   entity-relation-entity triplets from texts to construct knowledge graphs (Pan et al., 2024), to enhance
135   LLMs’s factual knowledge and logical reasoning by feature aggregation (Liu et al., 2020; Zhang et al.,
136
      2022), prompt engineering (Wen et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023c), information searching (Logan IV
      et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2022), training data synthesis (Tang et al., 2024), etc. In these cases, each
137
      node corresponds to either a specific entity or an abstract concept, lacking the capability to present
138
      an informative and self-contained knowledge point. Some works have recently related a piece of
139   descriptive text to a knowledge point, and constructed the knowledge structure for LLMs’ retrieval-
140   augmented generation (Sarthi et al., 2024; Dong et al., 2024), where the top-to-down retrieval
141   provides precise information-seeking paths along the knowledge structure. In this paper, we extend
142   the structure-aware knowledge aggregation to LLMs’ training phase, injecting the whole domain
143   knowledge structure into LLMs’ by linking the training samples into corresponding knowledge points
144   and reasoning paths.
145   Data Augmentation and Synthesis. Due to the lack of high-quality datasets, data augmentation
146   has emerged as a promising solution to mimic real-world patterns (Liu et al., 2024b). Traditional
147   methods aim to artificially expand the training dataset size (Xu et al., 2023a; Mukherjee et al., 2023)
148   or generate entirely new samples that could help models learn better or adapt to specific tasks (Tang
149   et al., 2024). Yet, they often overlook the structured nature of domain knowledge, and the aimlessly
150   generated samples may also lack diversity (Ovadia et al., 2023; Mecklenburg et al., 2024), leading to
151   potentially suboptimal training outcomes when applied for domain adaptations (Mecklenburg et al.,
152   2024; Tang et al., 2024). By contrast, our SSFT design is an innovative departure to address the
153
      challenge of retaining and utilizing the structured knowledge inherent in domain-specific content.
154

189   3.1      AUTOMATIC E XTRACTION OF K NOWLEDGE S TRUCTURE
190
191   For web-crawled corpus, previous data pre-processing focuses on quality assessment for individual
192   documents (Bi et al., 2024), while the meta-info of knowledge structures (e.g., the table content
193
      for a textbook) is usually neglected or filtered out, and all we have are those sequentially arranged
      text segments (e.g., page-by-page-chunked content). As shown in Fig. 2 (a), we aim to extract (or,
194
      recover) the knowledge structure from the raw corpus for subsequent domain knowledge injection.
195
196   First, we use spaCy1 to split the content from a textbook at the paragraph-level, and merge the
197   sentences to form training chunks within a maximum size (e.g., 2048 tokens (Qiu et al., 2024)). After
198   that, we prompt the advanced Llama3-70B (Dubey et al., 2024) model to summarize the title for each
199
      chunk, where the textual content with the abstractive title jointly contributes to a “knowledge point”.
200   Then, we automatically aggregate knowledge points and extract the inherent structure hierarchy
201   by leveraging advanced language models. Inspired by Liu et al. (2024a), we take the title list to
202   instruct a specifically developed 7B model to identify the inherent knowledge structure within the
203   text chunks. A prompt example is displayed in Fig. 3, and the detailed implementation is presented in
204   Appendix B.1. In particular, Appendix B.1 and Appendix B.3 verify that our specialized 7B model
205
      can identify sufficiently precise knowledge structure for effective and efficient domain adaptation,
      as more powerful LLMs like LLaMA3-70B (Dubey et al., 2024) and GPT-3.5 (Brown et al., 2020)
206
      cannot bring significant enhancement while largely increase the inference costs.
207
208   Fig. 3 presents an example of the extracted structure, where we use the tree-like mindmap struc-
209   ture (Wen et al., 2023) to present the knowledge taxonomy from a textbook. And Fig. A1 showcases
210   how to deal with non-textbook data. The whole process does not involve human annotation, which
211
      reduces the cost and makes our method scalable for larger domain training corpora.
212   After automatically extracting the domain knowledge structure and associating the original training
213   chunks to related knowledge points, we delve into injecting domain knowledge through structure-
214   aware continual pre-training (SCPT) and structure-aware supervised fine-tuning (SSFT).

      3.3         S TRUCTURE - AWARE S UPERVISED F INE -T UNING
287
      In traditional knowledge injection paradigms, supervised fine-tuning aims to align the (continually)
288   pre-trained models to interactive ChatBots through massive question-answering exercises (Cui et al.,
289   2023; Qiu et al., 2024). However, most QA data augmentation strategies focus on enlarging the
290   quantity and enhancing the diversity of training samples (Xu et al., 2023a; Mukherjee et al., 2023;
291   Liu et al., 2024b), which neglects the nature of the highly structured domain knowledge. Therefore,
292   our structure-aware supervised fine-tuning (SSFT) technique focuses on eliciting models’ structured
293   knowledge learned during the SCPT stage, adapting LLMs to interactive and reliable domain experts.
294   Fig. 2 (c) illustrates the idea of synthesizing SSFT samples guided by domain knowledge structures.
295   Specifically, we use the random walk algorithm to create knowledge paths with 1 to l branches in
296   the original mindmap (the illustration of knowledge paths and branches is displayed in Fig. A2).
297   For paths linking to a single knowledge point, we use the corresponding text content to prompt
298   Llama3-70B (Dubey et al., 2024) to generate knowledge-intensive question-answering pairs. For
299   paths with two or more branches, we prompt Llama3-70B with the knowledge path and textual
300   contents to synthesize 2- or multi-hop QA samples, which require specific reasoning along the
301   knowledge structure to derive from questions to answers. Fig. 5 presents several examples.
302   For every synthesized QA sample (z), we will prepend the relevant mindmap hierarchy to the answer,
303   and add a CoT prompt in the question to construct another type of QA data (z ′ ) for SFT alignment.
304   This design explicitly elicits the learned knowledge in models’ responses, teaching them how to apply
305   the structured knowledge to address real-world problems. We use the two types of QA samples for
306   training, as recommended by Qiu et al. (2024). During testing, we can either use the vanilla question
307   as input to efficiently gather models’ answers, or take the CoT prompt to probe to what extent LLMs
308
      can memorize and leverage the injected knowledge to answer the questions.
309   Integrating with SCPT and SSFT, our StructTuning approach translates into remarkable efficacy and
310   efficiency in domain knowledge injection, as comprehensively evaluated in the following sections.

396   Datasets and Tasks. MMedBench (Qiu et al., 2024) is a multilingual medical multi-choice QA
397   benchmark, with 45,048 QA pairs for adapting LLMs to medical experts and 8,518 for testing. We
398   collect 76M textbook corpora from MedTextBooks (Jin et al., 2020) and MMedC (Qiu et al., 2024) for
399   medical knowledge injection and use the training/test split from MMedBench for SFT/evaluation. We
400   also curate another two sizes of training sets (with 30M and 132M tokens) to validate our method’s
401
      scalability. Detailed setup is in Appendix A.2.
402   Evaluation Metrics. For multi-choice QA in MMedBench (Qiu et al., 2024), we follow the default
403   setting to calculate the accuracy on six language subsets, as well as the averaged scores. Metrics are
404   computed by lexical exact-matching on models’ responses, rather than maximum token probabilities.
405   Investigated Models. For MMedBench, we extend the investigated LLMs across model scales and
406   architectures including Llama2-7B/13B (Touvron et al., 2023b), InternLM2-7B (Zheng et al., 2024),
407   and the recent Llama3-8B (Dubey et al., 2024). We also compare our knowledge-injected models with
408   other popular domain-specified LLMs, such as MedAlpaca (Han et al., 2023), ChatDoctor (Yunxiang
409   et al., 2023), PMC-LLaMA (Wu et al., 2024), and MMedLM (Qiu et al., 2024) models.
410
      Implementation Details. We first train LLMs for 3 epochs on medical textbooks with a batch size of
411   128. Then, we ask Llama3-70B to create structure-aware explanations for existing 45K QA samples
412   in MMedBench’s training split and 33K extra entries by traversing extracted knowledge structures.
413   Syntheses with overlapped options in the test set are removed. In the SFT phase, the learning rate is
414   set as 1e-6 to avoid overfitting on such an amount of SFT samples, as suggested by Qiu et al. (2024).
415
      Main Results. In Tab. 3, we present the overall performance across a series of LLMs on the
416
      testing split of MMedBench. The results demonstrate the promising enhancement achieved by our
417   StructTuning technique, which translates into consistent improvements on the advanced InternLM2-
418   7B (Zheng et al., 2024) and Llama3-8B (Dubey et al., 2024) models, and largely outperforms
419   the previous domain-specific LLMs like PMC-LLaMA (Wu et al., 2024) and MedAlpaca (Han
420   et al., 2023). Notably, our structure-aware knowledge injection approach, using merely 76M tokens
421   curated from medical textbooks, achieves over 50% performance (4.46% v.s. 8.71%, 2.57% v.s.

528   5     C ONCLUSION
529
530   This work pioneers in incorporating structure-aware methodologies to enhance domain knowledge
531
      injection into large language models. Through a novel SCPT-SSFT paradigm, we have set a new
      precedent for adapting LLMs to specialized domains, and the promising and scalable results under-
532
      score the viability and potential of our method. We hope to inspire further research in efficient and
533
      effective domain adaptation, moving a step closer to models that can truly emulate human intelligence.
534
535   Limitation. A noteworthy limitation of our work is the under-explored full potential of our StructTun-
536   ing strategy for knowledge injection. Although we have estimated a scaling law in Eq. (3), the data
537
      requirement to achieve 100% of the desired effectiveness remains unverified. Besides, our method
      introduces negligible data preprocessing costs to extract the domain knowledge structure. However,
538
      Appendix B.3 indicates our method can still reduce the overall adaptation cost to 10% since only a
539
      small part of training corpus is required. We will delve into the investigations in our future work.

814   Dataset Composition. To focus on the investigation of knowledge injection, we choose 7 subsets
815   from LongBench (Bai et al., 2023) across single- and multi-document QA tasks in English and
816   Chinese, and the remaining synthetic or code-orientated tasks are eliminated:
817
          • Single-Doc QA. For single-document QA, we take three subsets from LongBench: (1)
818
            Qasper (Dasigi et al., 2021), featured by question-answering over NLP technical papers and
819         annotated by NLP practitioners; (2) MultiFieldQA (Bai et al., 2023), manually curated from
820         multiple data sources and annotated by Ph.D. students; and (3) MultiFieldQA-zh, the Chinese
821         split also provided by Bai et al. (2023), covering multiple Chinese scenarios. MultiFieldQA
822         contains 150 Context-Question-Answer triplets to test, and the others adopted subsets include
823         200 pieces of test samples respectively.
824       • Multi-Doc QA. Multi-document QA requires LLMs to extract and combine information from
825         multiple documents to derive the answer, which is generally more challenging than single-doc
826         QA. We take four multi-hop QA datasets: (1) HotpotQA (Yang et al., 2018), containing 2-hop
827         questions written by native speakers given two related paragraphs; (2) 2WikiMultihopQA (Ho
828         et al., 2020), involving up to 5-hop questions synthesized through manually designed templates
829
            on Wikipedia passages; (3) MuSiQue (Trivedi et al., 2022), carefully composed with up to
            4-hop reasoning on an increased number of supporting and distracting context evidence; and (4)
830
            Dureader (He et al., 2017), developed based on Baidu Search and Baidu Zhidao and filtered
831
            by Bai et al. (2023) to reduce the data noise. Each subset has 200 test samples.
832
833   For data entries in Single-Doc QA, we extract the knowledge structure for each single passage; in
834   Multi-Doc QA, we identify the knowledge structure across multiple passages for each test sample.
835
      There are ultimate 1350 question-answer-passage(s)-(knowledge)structure quadruples to evaluate
      knowledge injection approaches on LongBench.

840   the SFT stage, we can ensure the knowledge is injected at the CPT stage and SFT only enhances the
841
      instruction-following capability. In practice, merely 13 out of 2700 (around 0.5%) synthetic data
      have over 0.5 F1-Score and are thus filtered out from the SFT data.
