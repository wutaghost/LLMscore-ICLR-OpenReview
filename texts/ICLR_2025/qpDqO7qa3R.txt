011                                                                   A BSTRACT
012
013
014                                This paper introduces a method for zero-shot video restoration using pre-trained
015                                image restoration diffusion models. Traditional video restoration methods often
016                                need retraining for different settings and struggle with limited generalization across
017                                various degradation types and datasets. Our approach uses a hierarchical latent
018                                warping strategy for keyframes and local frames, combined with token merging
019
                                   that uses a hybrid correspondence mechanism that integrates spatial information,
                                   optical flow, and feature-based matching. We show that our method not only
020
                                   achieves top performance in zero-shot video restoration but also significantly
021
                                   surpasses trained models in generalization across diverse datasets and extreme
022                                degradations (8× super-resolution and high-standard deviation video denoising).
023                                We present evidence through quantitative metrics and visual comparisons on various
024                                challenging datasets. Additionally, our technique works with any 2D restoration
025                                diffusion model, offering a versatile and powerful tool for video enhancement tasks
026                                without extensive retraining.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
055
056   Diffusion models have recently achieved remarkable success in image restoration tasks (Xia et al.,
057   2023; Lin et al., 2024). These models can generate realistic details, overcoming the limitations of
058   traditional regression-based methods that often produce blurry outputs without fine details (Fig. 2
059
      (a)). The state-of-the-art methods employing convolutional neural networks (CNNs) (Albawi et al.,
      2017; Kalchbrenner et al., 2014; O’shea & Nash, 2015) or transformers (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021; Liu
060
      et al., 2021b; Vaswani et al., 2017) trained on large-scale data have shown incredible effectiveness in
061
      image restoration.
062
063   Given the success of diffusion models in image restoration, a natural extension is to apply them
064   to video restoration. Video restoration, which typically involves denoising, super-resolution, and
065
      deblurring, is a valuable field that transforms low-quality videos into high-quality ones. However,
      directly applying image-based diffusion models to video restoration presents significant challenges.
066
      Notably, performing per-frame inference on videos using these models often results in severe
067
      flickering (Fig. 2 (b)), especially when using Latent Diffusion Models (LDMs).
068
069   Surprisingly, the application of image restoration diffusion models to video restoration remains largely
070   unexplored. While some attempts have been made to adapt these models for video tasks, they typically
071
      involve fine-tuning with 3D convolution and temporal attention layers. However, such approaches
      require extensive computational resources (e.g., 32 A100-80G GPUs for video upscaling (Zhou et al.,
072
      2023)) and task-specific retraining, limiting their practicality and generalizability.
073
074   In this paper, we present a novel, training-free approach to leverage pre-trained image restoration
075   diffusion models for video restoration. Our method introduces two key modules: hierarchical latent
076   warping and hybrid flow-guided spatial-aware token merging. These modules work in tandem to
077
      enforce temporal consistency in both latent and token (feature from the attention layer) spaces,
      enabling high-quality video restoration without any additional training or fine-tuning. Our method
078
      (Fig. 2 (c)) achieves both realistic and temporally consistent results without any additional training,
079
      leveraging an image diffusion model to restore videos effectively.
080
081   Fig. 1 illustrates our method’s capability to generate temporally consistent restored videos across
082   various tasks, including denoising, super-resolution, and depth estimation, without any further training.
083
      Our zero-shot video restoration framework can be applied to any pre-trained image diffusion model,
      offering a versatile solution that can adapt to various restoration tasks.
084
085   While inspired by recent advances in diffusion-based generation models like VidToMe (Li et al.,
086   2024) and TokenFlow (Geyer et al., 2023), our work goes beyond combining existing techniques.
087   Our main contributions are:
088          • First zero-shot video restoration using diffusion models, balancing temporal consistency
089            and detail generation across various image-based models.
090          • Training-free framework manipulating latent and token spaces with hierarchical latent
091            warping and improved token merging.
092          • State-of-the-art results in extreme scenarios, surpassing traditional methods in generalizabil-
093            ity and robustness.

097   Video Restoration. Video restoration aims to restore high-quality frames from degraded videos,
098   addressing issues such as noise, blur, and low resolution (Chan et al., 2021a;c; Isobe et al., 2020;
099
      Li et al., 2023; Youk et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2018; Liu et al., 2019; 2021a). This task is more

100

101

102   et al., 2017; Tian et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2019; 2020; Zhu et al., 2019), and attention mechanisms to
103   handle temporal dependencies (Cao et al., 2021; Li et al., 2020; Liang et al., 2022; Zamir et al., 2022).
104   Major limitations include dependency on paired HQ-LQ data (Chan et al., 2022b; Xie et al., 2023;
105   Yang et al., 2021), assumptions of predefined degradation processes (Kim et al., 2017; 2016; Kong
106   et al., 2023; Li et al., 2020; Liang et al., 2022), and the need for retraining for different degradation
107   levels (Liu & Sun, 2013; Nah et al., 2019; Yi et al., 2019; Youk et al., 2024). These factors reduce
      effectiveness in real-world applications and lead to poor generalization. Additionally, these methods

133   Diffusion Models for Image Restoration. With significant advancements in diffusion models (Choi
134   et al., 2021; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Hertz et al., 2023; Ho et al., 2020; Rombach et al., 2022),
135   many diffusion-based approaches have been proposed for image restoration (Fei et al., 2023; Ho

      et al., 2023), as seen in StableSR (Wang et al., 2023) and DiffBIR (Lin et al., 2024). Despite their
141
      effectiveness in image restoration, these methods face challenges in video restoration due to temporal
142
      inconsistencies caused by the diffusion process’s randomness. In contrast, our method allows these
143   approaches to work on video without any training, addressing the temporal consistency issue while
144   leveraging the strengths of image restoration diffusion models.

146   Video Editing Methods for Temporal Consistency. Recent research has extended pre-trained
147   image diffusion models to video tasks (Esser et al., 2023; Ho et al., 2022a;b; Hu et al., 2023; Lu et al.,
148   2023; Luo et al., 2023; Mei & Patel, 2023; Kara et al., 2024). Various methods have been proposed to
149   enhance temporal consistency in video editing, which can be categorized based on the level at which
150   they operate:
151                       • Latent Space Level: Approaches working at the latent space level include Rerender-A-
152                         Video (Yang et al., 2023a), which employs latent warping (Teed & Deng, 2020; Xu et al.,
153                         2022) and frame interpolation. While these methods aim to maintain consistency in the
154                         latent representations of consecutive frames, they may struggle with semantic consistency
155                         in demanding restoration tasks. Our method introduces a novel hierarchical latent warping
156                         technique that addresses these limitations.
157                       • Token Level: Methods operating at the token level include VidToMe (Li et al., 2024) and
158                         TokenFlow (Geyer et al., 2023), which enhance temporal consistency by merging attention
159                         tokens across frames. Token merging (Bolya et al., 2023) is another technique used at this
160                         level. However, these techniques often produce blurry outputs in restoration tasks. Our
161                         approach improves upon these methods by introducing a hybrid flow-guided spatial-aware
                            token merging technique that maintains sharpness while ensuring temporal consistency.

253   Latent Warping. Some methods (Zhou et al., 2023) perform warping in the latent space to maintain
254   consistency between frames. This is done by warping the latent representations of adjacent frames.
255   Limitations in video restoration. Existing video editing techniques face challenges in video
256   restoration, often prioritizing temporal consistency over detail preservation. Early-stage denoising
257   produces noisy latents, making traditional similarity measures unreliable, especially in UNet’s
258   downsample blocks (Fig. 5, top). Most methods focus on frame-to-frame consistency and missing
259   global-local coherence, while high merging ratios can lead to over-smoothing. Our approach combines
260   hierarchical latent warping with hybrid flow-guided spatial-aware token merging to address these
261   limitations. This combination provides multi-scale temporal consistency, balances detail preservation
262   with consistency, and adapts to various degradation types. Latent warping handles large-scale
263
      inconsistencies in early stages, while token merging ensures fine detail consistency as features
      become more meaningful. By leveraging both optical flow and similarity measures, our method aims
264
      for superior zero-shot video restoration without task-specific training or computational resources.

280   3.3   H YBRID F LOW- GUIDED S PATIAL - AWARE T OKEN M ERGING
281
      While latent manipulation can achieve a certain degree of consistency, manipulating latents during the
282
      later stages of the denoising process would result in blurry outcomes. Additionally, the token space is
283
      highly semantically related to the image. Therefore, we propose hybrid flow-guided spatial-aware
284   token merging to achieve consistency in the token space.
285
286   Flow-guided. Our hybrid correspondence mechanism integrates spatial information, optical flow,
287   and feature-based similarity. In early denoising stages, latents are noisy, making cosine similarity
288   unreliable, especially in UNet’s downsample blocks (Fig. 5, top). However, optical flow from low-
289
      resolution inputs provides better guidance. As denoising progresses (e.g., steps 30-40), flow-based
      and similarity-based methods often identify different matches (Fig. 5, bottom), suggesting the benefit
290
      of a hybrid approach. Even with low-quality video, we can identify correspondences between frames
291
      based on color. We use flow for correspondences in UNet downsample blocks and employ forward-
292   backward consistency check as a criterion to determine r most similar paired source token Tsrc and
293   target token Ttar :

455   4.1              C OMPARISONS WITH S TATE - OF - THE -A RT M ETHODS
456
      To verify the effectiveness of our approach, we compare it with several state-of-the-art methods,
457   including BasicVSR++ (Chan et al., 2022a), RVRT (Liang et al., 2022), and FMA-Net (Youk et al.,
458   2024) for video super-resolution, and Shift-Net (Li et al., 2023) for video denoising. We also compare
459   our method to per-frame restoration and the application of VidToMe (Li et al., 2024), a zero-shot video
460   editing method, onto the same model as ours. We also try to compare with Upscale-A-Video (Zhou
461   et al., 2023), which is a diffusion-based video restoration model fine-tuned from an image-based
462   diffusion model. However, we are unable to run their inference code on our available hardware (one
463   A6000 GPU, 48GB memory) due to persistent out-of-memory (OOM) issues, even with their default
464   configuration. Therefore, we conduct experiments on the same test cases used in their paper.
465   Our zero-shot video restoration framework is designed to be highly adaptable and capable of leverag-
466   ing a wide range of pre-trained image diffusion models. This flexibility allows easy adaptation from
467   image to video models without extensive retraining, enabling the application of various restoration
468   tasks by simply switching the underlying image diffusion model.
469
      Video Super-resolution. As shown in Tab. 1, regression-based methods like FMA-Net (Youk et al.,
470
      2024) struggle with large motion or severe degradation. VidToMe (Li et al., 2024) can generate highly
471   consistent results, but they are often very blurry, leading to poor visual quality. In contrast, our method
472   enhances temporal consistency while maintaining the generation quality of the original diffusion
473   model, making it the most competitive approach. Fig. 6 provides visualizations of two challenging
474   VSR cases. FMA-Net fails to produce sharp results due to domain gaps between training and testing.
475   Diffusion-based image restoration method DiffBIR (Lin et al., 2024) and SD×4 upscaler (sdx, 2023)
476   can generate sharp results with details, while per-frame processing makes the result video temporal
477   inconsistent and jitters across frames. On the contrary, our zero-shot video restoration framework
478   restores a low-quality input video into a temporally consistent high-quality video. The qualitative
479
      comparisons with Upscale-A-Video are provided in Fig. 7. The results demonstrate that our method
      produces more detailed outputs that better preserve the content of input frames. This advantage
480
      stems from our approach of leveraging pre-trained diffusion priors and zero-shot adaptation to video,
481
      compared to their fine-tuning strategy.
482
483   Video Denoising. Video denoising, compared to VSR, is a simpler task for regression models,
484   as they can often find the correct pixel value given a sufficiently large batch size. However, our
485   method consistently outperforms others in terms of visual quality (LPIPS) and remains highly robust
      even as degradation becomes severe. Fig. 8 visualizes the denoising results on the REDS30 dataset.

530   5          C ONCLUSION
531
532   We introduce a novel zero-shot video restoration framework utilizing pre-trained image-based dif-
533   fusion models, eliminating the need for extensive retraining. Our approach integrates hierarchical
534
      latent warping and hybrid flow-guided, spatial-aware token merging, significantly enhancing temporal
      consistency and video quality under various degradation conditions. Experimental results demonstrate
535
      that our framework surpasses existing methods both in quality and consistency.
536
537   Limitations. Our framework has two main limitations: (1) LDM decoder sensitivity can cause
538   flickering in dynamic scenes. (2) Extreme degradation may yield unsatisfactory results. Future work
539   will address these issues by stabilizing decoder output, and enhancing extreme degradation handling.
      Our framework’s adaptability allows for the integration of future, more powerful diffusion models.

849

851   to consider the computational requirements in comparison to other approaches. Tab. 3 provides an

853
      As shown in the table, our method stands out by not requiring any training or fine-tuning, which
854
      significantly reduces the computational resources needed. This is in stark contrast to other methods
855   that require multiple high-end GPUs and several days of training time. For inference, our method
856   introduces some computational overhead due to the hierarchical latent warping and hybrid token
857   merging processes. However, this overhead is relatively small compared to the resources required for
858   training or fine-tuning video models. Specifically, our method adds only approximately 6 seconds to
