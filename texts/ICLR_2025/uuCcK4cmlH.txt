009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Emerging threats to IoT networks have accelerated the development of intrusion
012
               detection systems (IDSs), characterized by a shift from traditional approaches
013            based on attack signatures or anomaly detection to approaches based on machine
014            learning (ML). However, current ML-based IDSs often lack result explanations
015            and struggle to address zero-day attacks due to their fixed output label space. In
016            this paper, we propose IDS-Agent, the first IDS based on an AI agent powered
017            by large language models (LLMs). For each input network traffic and a detec-
018            tion request from the user, IDS-Agent predicts whether the traffic is benign or
019            being attacked, with an explanation of the prediction results. The workflow of
020            IDS-Agent involves iterative reasoning by a core LLM over the observation and
021
               action generation informed by the reasoning and retrieved knowledge. The action
               space of IDS-Agent includes data extraction and preprocessing, classification,
022
               knowledge, and memory retrieval, and results aggregation – these actions will
023
               be executed using abundant tools, mostly specialized for IDS. Furthermore, the
024            IDS-Agent is equipped with a memory and knowledge base that retains infor-
025            mation from current and previous sessions, along with IDS-related documents,
026            enhancing its reasoning and action generation capabilities. The system prompts of
027            IDS-Agent can be easily customized to adjust detection sensitivity or identify
028            previously unknown types of attacks. In our experiments, we demonstrate the
029            strong detection capabilities of IDS-Agent compared with ML-based IDSs and
030            an IDS based on LLM with prompt engineering. IDS-Agent outperforms these
031            SOTA baselines on the ACI-IoT and CIC-IoT benchmarks, with 0.97 and 0.75
032
               detection F1 scores, respectively. IDS-Agent also achieves a recall of 0.61 in
               detecting zero-day attacks, outperforming previous approaches specially designed
033
               for this task.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      In recent years, the Internet of Things (IoT) has emerged as a transformative technology, increasingly
      adopted across a wide range of applications (Chataut et al., 2023). Alongside its rapid development,
040
      security concerns have arisen within IoT networks due to the typically large number of devices with
041
      potential trustworthiness issues (Alghofaili & Rassam, 2022). Therefore, the deployment of Intrusion
042   Detection Systems (IDSs) has become essential, as they play a critical role in monitoring network
043   traffic and identifying malicious activities (Khraisat et al., 2019).
044
045
      Many traditional IDS employ signature-based methods, which rely on signatures of known attacks
      stored in databases (Ioulianou et al., 2018; Nagaraju et al., 2021; AlYousef & Abdelmajeed, 2019).
046
      They suffer from high false negative rates when variations in attack methodologies do not exactly
047
      match the existing signatures. Alternatively, IDS can also be designed as an anomaly detector to
048   identify distributional deviations from normal traffic (Khraisat et al., 2018; Jia et al., 2021). However,
049   defining the normal behavior in a network can be challenging, especially in diverse and dynamic
050   environments where normal activity can change over time. Machine learning (ML)-based IDS was
051   then proposed to address these problems by leveraging the representation power of ML models,
052   such as deep neural networks (DNNs), to capture complex attack patterns from extensive training
053   data (Maseer et al., 2021). However, ML-based IDS still face limitations due to constraints in the
      model architecture and training data, which hinder their detection capabilities, especially when


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      addressing zero-day attacks (Ullah et al., 2019). Furthermore, the detection results from ML-based
055   IDS often lack clear explanations, which can diminish their credibility, particularly in safety-critical
056   IoT scenarios where understanding the rationale behind alerts is crucial (Catarinucci et al., 2015;
057   Ahmad & Zhang, 2021).
058
      Recently, AI agents empowered by large language models (LLMs) have been created to handle
059
      complicated tasks in various application domains (Yu et al., 2023; Abbasian et al., 2024; Shi et al.,
060
      2024; Li et al., 2024; Deng et al., 2023; Gur et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2024). These agents are
061   characterized by their integrated capabilities to observe the environment, generate a reasonable
062   plan, and then take appropriate actions according to the user’s requests. Typically, LLM agents are
063   equipped with a knowledge/memory base and a toolbox that includes tools such as local functions
064   and third-party APIs. They utilize one or more LLMs for knowledge/memory-based reasoning and
065   planning, including the selection of the most effective tools to employ. The intelligence of LLM
066   agents in reasoning (as well as analysis and criticism (Shinn et al., 2023)) makes them both powerful
067   operators and effective intermediaries between task execution and human users.
068   In this paper, we propose IDS-Agent, the first LLM agent designed for intrusion detection, featuring
069   capabilities for explanation, customization, and adaptation to zero-day attacks. IDS-Agent takes
070   an input request for intrusion detection with a target raw data flow, and outputs detection results
071   with a detailed explanation. The agent adopts a reasoning-followed-by-action pipeline ( (Yao et al.,
072   2023)) with a specialized action space for intrusion detection. Specifically, knowledge-enabled
073   reasoning based on long-term memory from previous sessions is performed by the core LLM of the
074   agent to decide the optimal tools (and their settings/parameters) for data extraction, preprocessing,
075
      classification, and results aggregation.
076   Compared with existing ML-based IDSs, IDS-Agent achieves a stronger detection performance
077   and better interpretability by harnessing the power of multiple ML models and external knowledge in
078   a comprehensive way. It aggregates the classification results from multiple ML models by prompting
079   an LLM with the top-k label predictions and their confidence scores for each model. The prompt
080   also includes external knowledge (e.g. regarding particular attack types) obtained by calling a search
081
      engine and additional instructions, for example, to customize detection sensitivity or to reveal new
      attack types. The LLM is instructed to produce structured outputs, including detection results and an
082
      explanation. Our main technical contributions are summarized below:
083
084   • We propose IDS-Agent, the first LLM-powered agent for intrusion detection, featured by its
085     capabilities of results explanation, detection customization, and revealing of zero-day attacks.
086   • We propose a reasoning-followed-by-action pipeline for IDS-Agent with an action space and
087     toolbox specialized for network traffic processing and intrusion detection results aggregation.
088
      • We demonstrate the effectiveness of IDS-Agent on two IDS benchmarks, ACI-IoT’23 and CIC-
089     IoT’23. IDS-Agent achieves higher detection accuracy compared with the latest LLM-based
090     method, various ML models, and the majority voting classifier.
091   • Experiments show that when classifiers produce discrepancy predictions, IDS-Agent can utilize
092     inherent and external knowledge to help the decision-making. IDS-Agent also demonstrates
093     clearly better performance than existing approaches in detecting zero-day attacks. Moreover, we
094     find that IDS-Agent effectively follows the sensitivity instructions without requiring expert
095     intervention or additional tuning.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
098
099
      Conventional IDS An Intrusion Detection System (IDS) is designed to detect malicious activities
100
      on computer systems, helping to ensure system security (Khraisat et al., 2019). IDSs are generally
101
      classified into two main types: Signature-based Intrusion Detection Systems (SIDS) and Anomaly-
102   based Intrusion Detection Systems (AIDS). SIDS relies on pattern-matching techniques to identify
103   known attacks (Ioulianou et al., 2018; Nagaraju et al., 2021; AlYousef & Abdelmajeed, 2019).
104   However, the rise in zero-day attacks has increasingly diminished the effectiveness of SIDS, as these
105   new attacks lack existing signatures (Ullah et al., 2019). In contrast, AIDS constructs a model of
106   normal system behavior using machine learning, statistical, or knowledge-based techniques. Any
107   significant deviation from the constructed model is flagged as an anomaly, potentially indicating
      an intrusion (Khraisat et al., 2018; Jia et al., 2021). Knowledge-based AIDS requires creating a


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      knowledge base that reflects the legitimate traffic profile by using techniques such as description
109   language (Studnia et al., 2018) and expert system (Kim et al., 2014). However, in a dynamically
110   changing environment, a time-consuming regular update on the knowledge base is needed. Compared
111   with these conventional IDS, our IDS-Agent can adopt a search engine to obtain up-to-date
112   knowledge and can handle zero-day attacks, as shown by our experiments.
113
      ML-based IDS Many machine learning models, such as MLP (Bajaj & Arora, 2013), KNN (Li et al.,
114
      2014), Decision Tree (Guezzaz et al., 2021), SVM (Mohammadi et al., 2021), have been explored for
115   anomaly-based intrusion detection. For IoT intrusion detection, Verma & Ranga (2020) conducted a
116   comprehensive comparison of ensemble and individual classifiers, including Random Forest (RF),
117   AdaBoost (AB), and Gradient Boosted Machines (GBM). Roy et al. (2022) proposed a lightweight
118   IDS model utilizing machine learning to detect cyber-attacks and anomalies in resource-constrained
119   IoT systems. Davis et al. (2024) advanced this line of research by applying a quantum-annealing
120   approach for feature selection in IoT intrusion detection. Compared with ML-based IDSs, our
121   IDS-Agent not only achieves better empirical performance but also provides a detailed explanation
122   of each intrusion detection result.
123   LLM-based IDS Large language models (LLMs), especially generative pre-trained commercial trans-
124   formers, like GPTs, have recently demonstrated outstanding ability in information comprehension and
125   reasoning tasks. This has motivated some studies in applying LLMs to abnormal detection tasks, such
126   as compiler optimization (Gu, 2023) and software vulnerability detection (Guo et al., 2024). Zhang
127   et al. (2024) is the first to use LLMs for IDS by employing a straightforward in-context learning
128   approach with GPT-4, which provides it with a few labeled examples. Their method achieved over
129
      90% accuracy on a simple dataset containing only five types of attacks. However, in this paper, we
      demonstrate that the performance of their method drops significantly when tested on more complex
130
      and diverse datasets. Different from this LLM-based IDS, our IDS-Agent uses LLM for reasoning,
131
      action planning, and tool selection, leading to a huge performance gain on diverse datasets.

      3.1   OVERVIEW
137
      IDS-Agent is designed to produce a prediction result with an explanation for each user request for
138   IoT traffic inference, i.e., to determine if the traffic is benign or belongs to any particular attack type.
139   We also allow IDS-Agent to handle requests for customized detection sensitivities or to detect
140   new attack categories from the given IoT traffic flow. IDS-Agent is equipped with a) an abundant
141   toolbox containing special IDS tools such as ML models for classification and general tools such
142   as search engines to retrieve external knowledge and b) a memory and knowledge base storing the
143   current session information, long-term memory from previous sessions, and supportive documents.
144   These tools, memory, and external knowledge will be integrated to guide the decision-making of
145   IDS-Agent in a structured manner, as detailed in the sequel.
146
147   3.2   P IPELINE OF IDS-A G E N T
148
      The pipeline of our IDS-Agent is inspired by the ReAct agent (Yao et al., 2023). The user request
149
      is fulfilled by executing a sequence of action steps {a1 , a2 , · · · }, where each action step is generated
150   by a core LLM based on previous reasoning and observations. For any input user request with a
151   specification of the traffic flow to be inferred, an initial observation o0 is constructed by concatenating
152   the user request with a system prompt, including a description for each available tool. This initial
153   observation serves as the context for the agent to understand the task, facilitating subsequent reasoning
154   and action generation.
155
      Specifically, IDS-Agent iterates over the following three steps after the construction of o0 :
156   1) Reasoning: The core LLM generates a thought (in plain text) about the next action by ri =
157   LLM(si ), where si = {o0 , {r1 , a1 , o1 }, · · · , {ri−1 , ai−1 , oi−1 }} is the short-term memory of the
158   current session up to the (i − 1)-th iteration (with s1 = {o0 }). Reasoning can optionally adopt
159   long-term memory from previous sessions for in-context demonstration.
160   2) Action generation: The action is generated based on the reasoning/thought by ai = LLM(ri , si ).
161   Notably, each action we generate is a structured JSON file containing an action name and an action
      input. The action input consists of the name of the tool(s) to be used and the associated settings or

209   3.3   ACTION S PACE AND T OOL D ESIGN
210
211   Our IDS-Agent is designed with a comprehensive action space, allowing it to handle various tasks
212
      in the pipeline of data processing and classification through iterative reasoning and execution. The
      action space includes the key actions described below.
213
214   Data Extraction: The goal is to accurately extract network traffic records x specified in the user
215   request from the dataset for further analysis. We design the data extraction tool as a function that
      takes the given line number or the flow ID as the input and outputs a structured traffic sample.


                                                       4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      Preprocessing: The goal is to clean, normalize, and transform the extracted data into a standard
217   format for classification. Our preprocessing tools are designed as functions for diverse data analysis
218   operations, including feature scaling, data encoding, handling missing values, and selecting important
219   features for classification.
220
      Classification: This action applies machine learning models to the preprocessed data to obtain
221
      classification results, i.e., to predict whether the traffic is benign or falling into any malicious category.
222
      The inputs to the classification tool include the preprocessed traffic features and a classifier, while the
223   outputs include the top-k labels and their corresponding confidence scores. Note that our classification
224   tool is more than a classifier; it advances ML-based IDS by adapting to diverse model types and
225   incorporating more information from the classification results into the inference procedure. The types
226   of classifiers used include Random Forest, SVM, MLP, Decision Tree, KNN, etc. It is important to
227   note that the classification toolbox is extensible. In real-world deployments, users can easily add
228   new classifiers to the toolbox without the need to fine-tune the LLM. This flexibility allows for easy
229   updates and adaptation to new attack patterns or changes in the network environment. Moreover,
230   the classifiers can also be open-sourced models trained by third parties (callable through APIs), or
231
      models locally trained based on the data collected by the user.
232   Knowledge Retrieval: This action aims to obtain knowledge regarding the particular types of attacks
233   predicted by the classifiers. The knowledge can be either external knowledge and retrieved by calling
234   search engines such as Google and Wikipedia API, or stored knowledge base or predefined rules and
235   retrieved by RAG. The retrieved knowledge will be used to guide the aggregation step.
236   Long Memory Retrieval: Long Memory carrying information from previous sessions (discussed in
237   Sec. 3.4). We add an instruction in the system prompt in o0 such that long-term memory retrieval
238   will be activated (through reasoning).
239
      Aggregation: This action aims to comprehensively integrate the results from multiple steps of
240
      classification action (based on different classifiers) to generate a structured final inference decision.
241   The core of the aggregation tool is an LLM where the prompt is designed to include 1) the detailed
242   results from the classifiers, 2) the short-term memory, and 3) demonstrative inputs and outputs
243   aggregated by the LLM from previous sessions. Note that the short-term memory also includes the
244   external knowledge previously extracted and the system prompts in o0 . This system prompt, as shown
245   in Fig. 3, includes a specification for the detection sensitivity; it can also include an instruction for
246   revealing new attack categories. Compared to naive aggregation, such as majority voting, our method
247   incorporates more information to resolve any discrepancies between different model outputs in a
248   more comprehensive way.
249
250   3.4    M EMORY AND K NOWLEDGE BASE
251
252
      The memory and knowledge base of IDS-Agent stores 1) the short-term memory, 2) the long-term
      memory, and 3) supportive documents for IDS.
253
254   Short-term Memory (STR). The STR, as described in Sec. 3.2, includes the historical reasoning
255   trace, actions, and observations of the current session in a structured format, and is renewed after
256   each observation update. The major goal of the STM here is to track the agent’s iterative reasoning
257   process and ensure consistency between steps in real-time.
258   Long-term Memory (LTM). The LTM consists of agent decisions and contextual information from
259   previous use cases (Wang et al., 2024; Zhong et al., 2024). Here, a structured LTM example is defined
260   by ϕ = {t, x, R, A, O, ŷ}, where t is a timestamp, x is the feature vector after preprocessing, R =
261   [r1 , · · · , rn ] is the reasoning trace, A = [a1 , · · · , an ] contains all the action steps, O = [o1 , · · · , on ]
262   are the observations, and ŷ is the final label prediction. The long-term memory base can be initialized
263   by running the agent on a validation dataset. During the inference, only sessions with correct agent
264
      decisions will be stored in the long-term memory base. In a real-world intrusion detection scenario,
      such correctness can be validated by human experts.
265
266   In our framework, LTM retrieval provides the agent with additional information while aggregating the
267   results for individual classifiers. Here, we set the LTM retriever input as the current timestamp t and ob-
268   servations from previous data processing and classification actions, denoted by Õ = [o1 , . . . , om−1 ],
269   where m is an arbitrary iteration where the LTM retrieval kicks in. The retriever obtains the top-k
      relevant final reasoning based on the weighted sum of timestamp distance and the cosine similarity

      4.1   E XPERIMENT S ETTINGS
297
      Dataset. This paper focuses on intrusion detection in the IoT environment, which presents more
298   complexities and challenges than traditional networks. We consider the following two datasets
299   commonly used in previous works.
300   1) ACI-IoT’23 (Bastian et al., 2023): This dataset contains both benign and malicious network traffic
301   captured from a variety of IoT devices. The dataset includes simulations of several attack types,
302   such as Reconnaissance (e.g., Host Discovery, OS Scan, Ping Sweep, and Port Scan), DoS (e.g.,
303   ICMP Flood, SYN Flood, UDP Flood, and Slowloris) and Brute Force (e.g., Dictionary Attacks).
304   We randomly select 10% of the data to train the ML-models for classification. For evaluation, we
305   construct a test dataset from the remaining samples in ACI-IoT’23 by randomly selecting 200 benign
306
      samples and 20 samples per attack category.
      2) CIC-IoT 2023 (Neto et al., 2023): This dataset simulates large-scale, real-time IoT environments
307
      and comprises 33 distinct types of attacks, which is even more difficult for intrusion detection than
308
      ACI-IoT’23. The dataset includes network traffic from a broad IoT topology with 105 devices. From
309   the dataset, we selected the 24 most common attack types along with benign samples to create our
310   training and testing datasets. The remaining 9 attack types were excluded from the training data and
311   designated as unknown attacks for evaluating zero-day attack detection performance. Again, we use
312   10% of the data for training the machine learning classifiers. The test dataset is constructed with 100
313   benign samples and 10 samples for each attack type.
314
315   Evaluation Metrics. We are interested in the performance of IDS-Agent in both the binary
316   classification of benign and malicious flows and the multi-classification that also requires recognizing
317   the specific attack type when a flow is deemed malicious. For binary classification, we use accuracy,
318   and false alarm rate (FAR) as the evaluation metrics. Accuracy is the ratio of correctly predicted
319   samples to the total number of samples in the dataset, measuring the overall effectiveness of the
320
      IDS-Agent in detecting both benign and malicious flows. FAR measures the proportion of false
      positives (benign flow incorrectly classified as malicious).
321
322   For multi-classification, we treat each attack category (and the benign category) as a class. We use the
323   per-class precision, recall, and F1-score as the evaluation metrics. Detailed results for the detection
      of each attack category are deferred to the appendix due to space limitations. In the main paper, we


                                                         6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      report the macro-averaged precision, recall, and F1-score across all classes as the overall performance
325   of the IDS being evaluated. This macro-average is computed by averaging the metric over all classes
326   with equal weights.

      4.2   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
329
330   The core LLM of IDS-Agent. In our experiments, we consider three LLM choices: GPT-3.5-
331   Turbo, GPT-4o-mini, GPT-4o.
332
333   Tool Design. 1) Data preprocessing tool. We implement Python functions that will be called
334   sequentially for data preprocessing. First, we remove from each flow the fields irrelevant to the traffic
335   features, including the label, time-stamp, and flow ID. Second, we encode the non-numeral fields
336   into numbers, including the connection type and protocol type. Third, we conduct feature selection
337   based on an F-test for linear dependency between features and labels. Finally, the extracted features
338   are standardized. 2) Classification tool. The core of the classification tool is an ML model for
339   intrusion detection, which can be self-trained or an off-the-shelf model trained by a third party. Here,
340   we pretrained six (multi-)classifiers, including random forest (RF), K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN),
341
      logistic regression (LR), decision tree (DT), multi-layer perceptrons (MLP), and support vector
      classifier (SVC) using the training set from our benchmarks. The output of the classification tool is
342
      the top-3 label predictions with their confidence scores. 3) Knowledge retrieval tool. We construct a
343
      knowledge base for various IoT attacks by collecting 50 online blogs and 50 research papers. These
344   documents are then split into chunks of 1000 tokens with an overlap of 200 tokens. These chunks
345   were embedded using the OpenAI encoder and stored in a vector database powered by ChromaDB.

      4.3   BASELINES
348
349   1) Machine learning methods. We compare IDS-Agent with the state-of-the-art ML-based IDS,
350   which uses a quantum-annealing method for feature selection (Davis et al., 2024). In Appendix A.3,
351   we also compare our IDS-Agent with six IDSs using the six ML models we pretrained, respectively.
352
      2) LLM-based methods. We compared our IDS-Agent with the latest GPT-4-based intrusion
353
      detection approach (Zhang et al., 2024), which leverages the model’s reasoning capabilities and in-
354
      context demonstrations. The authors particularly demonstrate that providing GPT-4 with a few labeled
355   examples can improve the accuracy of intrusion detection. We further improve the performance of
356   this baseline by first clustering their in-context examples using a Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM)
357   and then selecting in-context examples from different clusters to cover as many attack cases as
358   possible. Compared to this baseline with a fixed set of demonstrations, IDS-Agent retrieves LTM
359   for demonstration dynamically based on input similarity.
360
      3) Ensemble learning methods (majority vote). We create a strong baseline by ensemble the results
361   from the six ML models we pretrained through majority voting.

529   5    C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this paper, we propose IDS-Agent, the first LLM-powered agent for intrusion detection. We
      design an iterative reasoning-followed-by-action pipeline for IDS-Agent to extract data from the
532
      network traffic, preprocess the data, consult different machine learning models for classification results
533
      and details, retrieve both internal and external knowledge, and summarize the final detection inference.
534   These agent actions are facilitated by a memory module and a wide array of tools for intrusion
535   detection and general purposes. Empirically, IDS-Agent outperforms diverse types of SOTA IDSs
536   on ACI-IoT’23 and CIC-IoT’23. We find that when classifiers produce discrepancy predictions,
537   IDS-Agent can utilize inherent and external knowledge to assist decision-making. Moreover,
538   IDS-Agent can be easily adapted to detect zero-day attacks, exhibiting better performance than
539   existing methods. Finally, we find that IDS-Agent effectively follows the sensitivity instructions in
      detection without requiring expert intervention or additional tuning.

      A.5     T HE Z ERO - DAY ATTACK D ETECTION D ETAILS
893
894   We prompt GPT-4o to classify an example as an unknown attack if multiple classifiers output low
895   confidence for their top predictions or if there are conflicting predictions among different classifiers.
896   This is based on our observation that, for unknown attacks, machine learning models typically exhibit
897   relatively low confidence levels, as shown in Figure 8. Specifically, we instruct the LLM to consider
898   an example as a potential unknown attack if more than two models have low confidence (e.g., below
899
      a threshold of 0.7). Moreover, if more than two models have low confidence or if different models
      produce significantly divergent predictions, we direct IDS-Agent to search the knowledge base for
900
      characteristics of the most probable predicted attacks. If the traffic features do not match these attack
901
      characteristics, we confirm the example as an unknown attack and provide this as the final output.
