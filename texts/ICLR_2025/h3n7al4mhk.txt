009                                                      A BSTRACT
010
011                We present SoundMorpher, a sound morphing method that generates perceptu-
012                ally uniform morphing trajectories using a diffusion model. Traditional sound
013                morphing methods models the intractable relationship between morph factor and
014                perception of the stimuli for resulting sounds under a linear assumption, which
015                oversimplifies the complex nature of sound perception and limits their morph
016                quality. In contrast, SoundMorpher explores an explicit proportional mapping
017                between the morph factor and the perceptual stimuli of morphed sounds based on
018                Mel-spectrogram. This approach enables smoother transitions between interme-
019
                   diate sounds and ensures perceptually consistent transformations, which can be
                   easily extended to diverse sound morphing tasks. Furthermore, we present a set
020
                   of quantitative metrics to comprehensively assess sound morphing systems based
021
                   on three objective criteria, namely, correspondence, perceptual intermediateness,
022                and smoothness. We provide extensive experiments to demonstrate the effective-
023                ness and versatility of SoundMorpher in real-world scenarios, highlighting its po-
024                tential impact on various applications such as creative music composition, film
025                post-production and interactive audio technologies 1 .

028   1       I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Sound morphing is a technique to create a seamless transformation between multiple sound record-
031   ings. The goal is to produce perceptual intermediate sounds that gradually change from one sound to
032   another. Sound morphing has a wide range of applications, including music compositions, synthe-
033   sizers, psychoacoustic experiments to study timbre spaces (Caetano & Rodet, 2011; Hyrkas, 2021),
034   and practical applications such as film post-production, AR or VR interactive games, and adaptive
035   audio content in video games (Qamar et al., 2020; Siddiq, 2015).
036   Traditional sound morphing methods used the interpolation principle of sound synthesis technique,
037   which relies on interpolating the parameters of a sinusoidal model (Tellman et al., 1995; Osaka,
038   1995; Williams et al., 2014). Others make use of digital signal processing techniques to explore
039   high-level audio features in the time-frequency domain to achieve more effective and continuous
040   morphing (Williams et al., 2014; Brookes & Williams, 2010; Caetano & Rodet, 2010; 2011; Roma
041   et al., 2020; Caetano, 2019). However, these methods are limited to applications such as produc-
042   ing inharmonic and noisy environmental sounds (Gupta et al., 2023; Kamath et al., 2024). Despite
043   the increasing interest in applying machine learning to sound generation, there has only been lim-
044
      ited exploration in sound morphing. Recent approaches (Zou et al., 2021; Gupta et al., 2023; Kim
      et al., 2019b; Kamath et al., 2024) have shown their superior effectiveness compared to traditional
045
      methods in various scenarios. However, we observed several critical limitations of those existing
046
      methods. Firstly, they are primarily designed for static or cyclostationary morphing (see Sec. 3.1),
047   limiting their applicability to dynamic sound transformations. Secondly, these approaches often lack
048   sufficient quantitative evaluation, limiting further analysis of their effectiveness. Thirdly, they re-
049   quire training on task-specific datasets, which limits their application in different scenarios. Most
050   importantly, they typically assume a linear relationship between morphing factors and sound per-
051   ception, and achieve smooth morphing by gradually changing the morph factor. This assumption
052   oversimplifies the complex nature of sound perception, as gradually changing morph factors does
053

056
057   In this paper, we introduce SoundMorpher, a sound morphing method that produces perceptually
058

059          • SoundMopher is the first open-world sound morphing method based on a pre-trained diffu-

069            may offer insights for analyzing and comparing future sound morphing methods.
070          • We provide extensive experiments to demonstrate that SoundMorpher can be effectively
071            applied to several potential applications in broader real-world scenarios, including musical
072            instrument timbre morphing, music morphing and environmental sound morphing.

075   2   R ELATED W ORK
076
      In this section, we first present a detailed review of related works on sound morphing task. Then,
077
      we also briefly introduce tasks that are similar to sound morphing and clarify the differences.
078
079   Sound morphing. Traditional sound morphing methods rely on interpolating parameters of a sinu-
080   soidal sound synthesis model (Tellman et al., 1995; Osaka, 1995; Williams et al., 2014; Primavera
081   et al., 2012). To achieve more effective and continuous morphing, Williams et al. (2014); Brookes
082
      & Williams (2010); Caetano & Rodet (2010; 2011); Roma et al. (2020); Caetano (2011) target on
      exploring perceptual spectral domain audio features by digital signal processing techniques, such
083
      as MFCCs, spectral envelope, etc.. Others such as Kazazis et al. (2016) involve a hybrid approach
084
      that extracts audio descriptors to morph accordingly and interpolate between the spectrotemporal
085   fine structures of two endpoints according to morph factors. Machine learning sound morphing
086   methods offer advantages such as high morphing quality by leveraging semantic representation in-
087   terpolation within a model instead of traditional audio feature interpolation. Zou et al. (2021) pro-
088   poses a non-parallel many-to-one static timbre morphing framework that integrates and fine-tunes
089   the machine learning technique (i.e., DDSP-autoencoder (Engel et al., 2020)) with spectral feature
090   interpolation (Caetano & Rodet, 2013). Kim et al. (2019b) targets synthesizing music correspond-
091   ing to a note sequence and timbre, which uses non-linear instrument embedding as timbre control
092   parameters under a pretrained WaveNet (Engel et al., 2017) to achieve timbre morphing between
093
      instruments. Luo et al. (2019) learns latent distributions of VAEs to disentangle representations for
      pitch and timbre of musical instrument sounds. Tan et al. (2020) uses a GM-VAE to achieve style
094
      morphing to generate realistic piano performances in the audio domain following temporal style
095
      conditions for piano performances, which morphs the conditions such as onset roll and MIDI note
096   into input audio. MorphGAN (Gupta et al., 2023) targets on audio texture morphing by interpolat-
097   ing within conditional parameters, and trained the model on a water-wind texture dataset. A recent
098   concurrent work by Kamath et al. (2024) uses a pre-trained AudioLDM (Liu et al., 2023) to morph
099   sound between two text prompts. In contrast, we focus on classical sound morphing, where the mor-
100   phing process is performed directly between two given audios rather than between text prompts. A
101   key advantage of our method is its ability to provide precise guidance during the morphing process,
102   as the target audio delivers exact information on how the source sound should evolve—something
103   that text prompts cannot always achieve, for example, morphing between two music compositions.
104   Synthesizer preset interpolation. Synthesizer preset interpolation achieves sound morphing by
105   developing models that compute interpolations within the domain of synthesis parameters for a
106   black-box synthesizer (Le Vaillant & Dutoit, 2023; Dutoit et al., 2023; Le Vaillant & Dutoit, 2024).
107   Unlike classical sound morphing, which perceptually blends two audio files into an intermediate
      sound, synthesizer preset interpolation treats the synthesizer as a non-differentiable black box, with


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      presets composed of both numerical and categorical parameters. By smoothly interpolating between
109   these presets, the task aims to achieve seamless morphing of synthesized sounds.
110
111   Text-to-audio editing. Text-to-audio editing is the process of using text queries to edit audio. With
112
      the success of diffusion models in image editing tasks, recent works target zero-shot audio editing
      with text instructions (Manor & Michaeli, 2024; Zhang et al., 2024; Lan et al., 2024) involving tasks
113
      such as inpainting, outpainting, timbre transfer, music genre transfer, or vocals removal.
114
115   Timbre transfer. Timbre transfer is a specific task that aims at converting the sound of a musical
116   piece by one instrument (source) into the same piece played by another instrument (target). This
117   concerns the task of converting a musical piece from one timbre to another while preserving the
118
      other music-related characteristics (Comanducci et al., 2024; Jain et al., 2020; Li et al., 2024).
119   Voice conversion and morphing. Voice conversion (VC) involves modifying vocal characteristics
120   of a source speech to match a target speaker, either by using target speeches or text (Li et al., 2023;
121   Yao et al., 2024; Niu et al., 2024; Sheng et al., 2024). The primary objective of VC is to alter the
122   vocal identity to closely resemble the target voice style, while preserving the linguistic content of the
123   source speech. Voice morphing is a broader scope, focusing on blending or transforming one voice
124
      into another. This often involves creating an intermediate voice that incorporates characteristics of
      both source and target voices, allowing for gradual transitions between them (Sheng et al., 2024).

      3.1   S OUND M ORPHING
129
130   Sound morphing aims to produce intermediate sounds as different combinations of model source
131   sound Ŝ1 and target sound Ŝ2 (Caetano & Rodet, 2010; 2011), which can be formulated as
132
                                       M (α, t) = (1 − α(t))Ŝ1 + α(t)Ŝ2                                  (1)
133
134   Each step is characterized by one value of a single parameter α, the so-called morph factor, which
135   ranges between 0 and 1, where α = 0 and α = 1 produce resynthesized source and target sounds,
136   respectively. Due to the intrinsic temporal nature of sounds, sound morphing usually involve
137   three main types: dynamic morphing, where α gradually transfers from 0 to 1 over time dimen-
138
      sion (Kazazis et al., 2016), static morphing, where a single morph factor α leads to an intermediate
      sound between source and target (Sethares & Bucklew, 2015), and cyclostationary morphing where
139
      several hybrid sounds are produced in different intermediate points (Slaney et al., 1996).
140
141   To solve the limitation on previous works that target on expensive perceptual evaluation only, Cae-
142   tano & Osaka (2012) proposes three objective criteria for sound morphing techniques: (1) Corre-
143   spondence. The morph is achieved by a description whose elements are intermediate between source
144
      and target sounds, highlighting semantic level transition; (2) Intermediateness. The morphed objects
      should be perceived as intermediate between source and target sounds, evaluating perceptual level
145
      correlation; (3) Smoothness. The morphed sounds should change gradually (i.e., ‘smoothly’) from
146
      source to target sounds, by the same amount of perception increment. Under the assumption of
147   linear perceptual stimuli, adding the same factor should increase the same amount of perception. In
148   this study, we evaluate SoundMorpher according to the three criteria by a series of comprehensive
149   objective quantitative metrics in addition to perceptual evaluation.

310   5.1   E VALUATION M ETRIC

527   6       C ONCLUSION
528
      We propose SoundMorpher, a sound morphing method base on a pretrained diffusion model that pro-
529
      duces perceptually uniform morphing trajectories. Unlike existing methods, we refined the sound
530
      morphing problem and explored an explicit connection between morph factor and perceptual stimuli
531   of morphed results which offers better flexibility and higher morphing quality, making it adaptable
532   to various morphing methods and real-world scenarios. We validate SoundMorpher by a series
533   of objective quantitative metrics as well as mean opinion score study following criteria proposed
534   by Caetano & Osaka (2012). These quantitative objective metrics may help to formalize future
535   studies on sound morphing evaluation. Furthermore, we demonstrated that SoundMorpher can be
536   applied to wide range of real-world applications in our experiments and conducted in-depth discus-
537   sions. SoundMorpher also has the potential to achieve voice morphing, as its foundational model
538   AudioLDM2 supports speech generation; however, we leave this exploration for future work.

1008   8.3   I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS FOR COMPUTING FAD AND FD
1009
1010   FAD and FD are commonly used in audio generation tasks to measure the quality of synthesized
1011   audio. Given two branches of audio groups, synthesized audios and real audios, these metrics offer
1012
       a comprehensive assessment of the global quality by evaluating how closely the synthesized audio
       matches the distribution of real audio.
1013
1014   In our experiment, we calculate FAD and FD between morphed audios {x(αi ) }N    i=1 and sourced
1015   audios to reflect correspondence of morphing and audio quality of morphed results. Smaller the
1016   FAD and FD values indicate the morphed audios has smaller semantic distribution gap between real
1017   sourced audios, suggesting that the morphed audios are more natural and exhibit semantic consis-
1018
       tency.
1019   Specifically, in the timbral morphing task, we categorize source audios based on groups of musical
1020   instruments, such as piano-guitar, harp-kalimba, etc. We then compute FAD and FD values be-
1021   tween a consentive morphed path {x(αi ) }N i=1 and the corresponding instrument group to which the
1022   endpoint audios belong. Similarity, in the case of environmental sound morphing task, we classify
1023   source audios according to sound scene categories and compute FAD and FD values between a con-
1024   sentive morphed path {x(αi ) }N
                                     i=1 and the corresponding sounds to which the endpoint audios belong
1025   to the categories. And in music morphing task, we calculate FAD and FD values between morphed
       audios {x(αi ) }N
                       i=1 with 20 samples of sourced music.

1338   11.4     FAILURE CASE
1339
1340   Although SoundMorpher produces high-quality sound morphing results, abrupt transitions can occur
1341   when the source and target sounds have significant temporal structure differences. A clear example
1342   of this is attempting to morph continuous environmental sounds with sounds that contain more
1343   silence. One obviouse example is to morph continuous environmental sounds and sounds contains
1344   more slience as Figure 6 shows.
1345   Environmental sounds often consist of discrete and temporally separated events, such as a dog bark-
1346   ing or a cat meowing, which have distinct and abrupt characteristics. These are inherently different
1347   from the more continuous and harmonically structured nature of music, where elements blend more
1348   fluidly over time. As a result, creating smooth transitions between such disjointed environmental
1349   sounds can be more challenging, leading to the perception of more abrupt or less natural transitions
       in the morphing process.
