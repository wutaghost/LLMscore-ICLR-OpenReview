061   1    I NTRODUCTION
062
063   In the field of deep learning, data collection and annotation have always been challenging. This is
064
      especially true for underwater images. Due to the difficulties in collecting and annotating underwater
      data, existing underwater datasets (Fu et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2021; Pedersen et al.,
065
      2019; Song et al., 2023a; Lian et al., 2023) often lack diversity. This is primarily reflected in the
066
      fact that many datasets are composed of similar frames extracted from the same video. As a result,
067   these datasets exhibit low diversity in appearance such as luminance, dynamic range, and color cast,
068   and typically only reflect the characteristics of specific water regions at similar depths. This lack
069   of variability significantly limits the performance of underwater perception models (Xu et al., 2023;
070   Yeh et al., 2021; Jia et al., 2022; Wang & Xiao, 2023). Meanwhile, the development of generative
071   models has provided an effective means to expand existing datasets (Fang et al., 2024; Hao et al.,
072   2024). Generative models have been widely used as a form of data augmentation (Zhang et al.,
073   2024). A natural approach is to leverage these models to augment underwater datasets and mitigate
074   the limitations imposed by the low diversity of existing data.
075   However, this approach does not yield the expected results. Due to the refraction and absorption of
076   light in underwater environments, underwater images exhibit a wide range of variations and signif-
077   icant appearance discrepancy compared to terrestrial images (Raveendran et al., 2021; Zhang et al.,
078   2022; Peng et al., 2023). Specifically, these discrepancy manifest in three main aspects: 1) lumi-
079   nance: As depth increases, light attenuates rapidly, resulting in gradually decreasing brightness. 2)
080   dynamic range: Suspended particles in the water scatter light, causing the image’s dynamic range
081
      to vary over a wide range in different water conditions. 3) color cast: Due to the different absorp-
      tion rates of light wavelengths in water, longer wavelengths like red attenuate faster than shorter
082
      wavelengths like blue and green. This causes underwater images at greater depths to typically dis-
083
      play a blue shift or green shift, while images in shallower areas maintain relatively normal colors.
084   These significant variations in appearance are exactly why generative models pre-trained on terres-
085   trial images (Rombach et al., 2022; Podell et al., 2023) perform poorly when generating underwater
086   images.
087
      To mitigate the negative impact of these appearance discrepancies, our research focuses on precise
088
      control of underwater image appearance. We model the appearance of underwater images using
089
      three parameters: luminance, dynamic range, and color cast, referred to as appearance attributes,
090   and turn the appearance control of underwater images into a parameter-controlled conditional gen-
091   eration problem.
092
093
      To address this issue, we propose a straightforward yet effective solution called S EA D IFF. Our ap-
      proach employs a Symmetrical Parameter Control framework to achieve precise control over the ap-
094
      pearance of underwater images. This framework consists of two main modules: Appearance Writer
095
      (A-Writer) and Appearance Reader (A-Reader). 1) The A-Writer module encodes appearance at-
096   tributes and utilizes a cross-attention mechanism (Vaswani et al., 2017) to inject these attributes into
097   the U-Net encoder (Ronneberger et al., 2015) . This enables the model to dynamically adjust for
098   appearance discrepancies, significantly enhancing the visual consistency and realism of the gener-
099   ated underwater images. 2) The A-Reader module reads the feature maps from each layer of the
100   U-Net decoder, predicting appearance attributes and providing deep supervision. This ensures that
101   the appearance attributes predicted at different layers align with the expected appearance, maintain-
102   ing high consistency between the generated images and the desired attributes. S EA D IFF, through
103   the combined use of these modules, effectively improves the controllability of underwater image
104
      appearance. As shown in Fig. 1, the proposed S EA D IFF significantly enhances precise control over
      underwater image appearance while maintaining image quality and layout controllability.

120   2     R ELATED W ORK
121
122   Diffusion models. Diffusion models have shown strong progress as generative models in recent
123   years. Early models like DDPM (Ho et al., 2020) and DDIM (Song et al., 2020) generate clear
124   images by progressively denoising Gaussian noise. This process transforms noise into high-quality
125   images. Diffusion models are widely applied in tasks such as text-to-image synthesis (Nichol et al.,
126   2021; Ramesh et al., 2022), image-to-image translation (Saharia et al., 2022a;b), image inpainting
127
      (Wang et al., 2022), and text-guided image editing (Nichol et al., 2021; Hertz et al., 2022). How-
      ever, they struggle with underwater image generation due to challenges like light refraction and
128
      absorption.
129
130
      Layout-to-image generation This area focuses on generating realistic images from layout infor-
131
      mation. Early methods like Layout2Im (Zhao et al., 2019) combined VAE (Kingma & Welling,
132   2013) and LSTM, using adversarial loss for realism and consistency. Recent approaches using
133   GANs, such as LostGAN (Sun & Wu, 2019) and LAMA (Li et al., 2021), improve image qual-
134   ity. LAMA introduces a local perceptual mask adaptation module for handling overlapping object
135   masks. Diffusion models have also made progress in this area. GLIGEN (Li et al., 2023) adds
136   gated self-attention layers to a pre-trained diffusion model for better layout control, while Control-
137   Net (Zhang et al., 2023) improves detail precision by incorporating semantic segmentation masks.
138   ReCo (Yang Z & et al., 2023) uses both text and bounding boxes for text-to-image generation, and
139   GeoDiffusion (Chen et al., 2023) supports geometric control with foreground prior re-weighting.
140
      However, these methods struggle with precise appearance control in underwater image generation,
      especially in terms of brightness, contrast, and color cast.
141
142
      Underwater image generation Underwater image generation aims to create realistic underwater
143
      scenes. Early methods applied color correction techniques (Reinhard et al., 2001; Nguyen et al.,
144
      2014) to existing images. More recent work, such as GAN-based models (Liu et al., 2018), generates
145   underwater images from terrestrial ones. (Desai et al., 2021) proposes a method that uses depth
146   data to generate underwater images. Research using diffusion models (Zhang et al., 2024) has also
147   explored generating realistic underwater images with terrestrial depth data. Additionally, (Desai
148   et al., 2024) incorporates downwelling irradiance and direct light scattering for realistic synthetic
149   underwater images. However, these methods still lack control over underwater image appearance.

184   3.2   A PPEARANCE ATTRIBUTE
185
186
      As discussed in the introduction, underwater images differ significantly from their terrestrial coun-
      terparts due to the unique effects of light refraction and absorption in aquatic environments. Specif-
187
      ically, these differences manifest in three primary aspects: luminance, dynamic range, and color
188
      cast. Understanding and controlling these attributes are crucial for generating realistic underwater
189   images, as conventional models trained on terrestrial datasets often struggle to replicate the under-
190   water appearance accurately. These challenges arise from the inherent discrepancies in appearance
191   between terrestrial and underwater environments.
192
      To address these challenges, we quantitatively model the three primary aspects as appearance at-
193
      tributes:
194
195
196   Luminance. Due to the rapid attenuation of light with increasing depth, underwater images exhibit
197
      significantly lower luminance compared to terrestrial images. To quantify this effect, we calculate lu-
                                      PN
198   minance as follows: kL = N1 i=1 Ii , where N is the total number of pixels, and Ii is the grayscale
199
      value of the i-th pixel. This formulation captures the overall light intensity of the underwater image,
      reflecting decreased visibility at greater depths.
200
201
202   Dynamic range. In underwater environments, light scattering caused by suspended particles re-
203
      duces dynamic range in images, making them appear flatter than terrestrial images. To quantify this
                                                                   PN         ¯ 2 , where I¯ represents the
204   effect, we calculate dynamic range as follows: kDR = N1 i=1 (Ii − I)
205
      average grayscale value of the image. This metric measures the variation in brightness, capturing
      the extent to which light scattering affects the dynamic range in underwater images.
206
207
208   Color cast. Due to the differential absorption of light wavelengths in water, longer wavelengths,
209   such as red, attenuate more rapidly than shorter wavelengths like blue and green. This results in
210
      underwater images often appearing biased towards blue or green hues. We express color cast as
                           PN
211   follows: kCC = N1 i=1 Hi , where Hi represents the hue value of the i-th pixel. This attribute
212
      describes how the colors in the image deviate from the actual scene colors, reflecting the color
      distortion caused by underwater conditions.
213
214   By accurately modeling these attributes, we transform the control of underwater image appearance
215   into a parameter-based conditional generation problem. This approach allows us to effectively ad-
      dress discrepancies in appearance and generate more realistic underwater images.

      4.1      P RELIMINARY: C ONDITIONAL D IFFUSION M ODELS
244
      Conditional diffusion models represent a significant advancement over traditional generative models
245   such as GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2014) and VAEs (Kingma & Welling, 2013). These models
246   learn the underlying data distribution through a T -step denoising process, beginning with normally
247   distributed noise. This approach can be conceptualized as the inverse process of a fixed-length T
248   Markov chain (Ho et al., 2020).
249
      In this framework, the model ϵθ (xt , t) is trained to reconstruct the clean data x from a noisy input
250
      xt at a specific time step t ∈ {1, ..., T } by estimating the noise added at that time. The training
251   objective is defined as:
252                                 LDM = Ex,ϵ∼N (0,1),t ∥ϵ − ϵθ (xt , t)∥2 .                            (1)
253
254   An extension of this concept is found in Latent Diffusion Models (LDM) (Rombach et al., 2022),
255   which perform the diffusion process in the latent space of a pre-trained Vector Quantized Variational
256   AutoEncoder (VQ-VAE) (Van Den Oord et al., 2017). In LDM, the input image x is first encoded
                                                                ′    ′   ′
257   into the VQ-VAE latent space, yielding z = E(x) ∈ RH ×W ×D . This latent representation serves
258   as the clean sample in Eqn. 1.
259   To facilitate conditional generation, LDM introduces a conditional encoder τθ (·), modifying the
260   objective to:
261                             LLDM = EE(x),ϵ∼N (0,1),t ∥ϵ − ϵθ (zt , t, τθ (y))∥2 ,              (2)
262   where y represents the condition applied, such as text in the context of LDM (Rombach et al., 2022).

      4.2      M ETHOD OVERVIEW
265
266   The core of our proposed S EA D IFF method lies in using Symmetrical Parameter Control to achieve
267   precise control over underwater image appearance. It consists of two main modules: A-Writer and
268   A-Reader. The A-Writer module (see Sec. 4.3) encodes appearance attributes and injects them into
269   the U-Net via a cross-attention mechanism, allowing for fine control over the image features. Mean-
      while, the A-Reader module (see Sec. 4.4) predicts appearance attributes by reading feature maps

      from the U-Net decoder, providing deep supervision to ensure better alignment with the desired at-
271   tributes and improving image quality. The combination of these modules enhances both appearance
272   and layout control. For layout control, we use a grid-based method, where each grid position is
273   represented by a unique position token encoded using the CLIP text encoder (Radford et al., 2021).

277   We utilize the Appearance Writer (A-Writer) module to regulate appearance in the diffusion model.

282   model these discrepancies. The appearance attributes KD are initially encoded into tokens using an

      5.1   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
338
339   Dataset. Our experiments were conducted on the RUOD dataset (Fu et al., 2023). To enhance
340   the validity of our experiments, we annotated the appearance attributes introduced in Sec. 3.2 based
341   on the RUOD dataset (Fu et al., 2023). However, the dataset contains multiple frames from the
342   same video, leading to potential data leakage between training and testing sets due to the similarity
343   of adjacent frames. To address this issue, we re-divided the RUOD dataset. Specifically, we used
344   the first 10,000 images as the training set and the following 4,000 images as the testing set. Since
345   the remaining 4,000 images still contained many similar images, we randomly selected 310 images
346   from this subset for the final test set.
347
348   Optimization. We initialized our model using the pre-trained Stable Diffusion v1.5 model, which
349   leverages the LDM (Rombach et al., 2022) text-to-image diffusion framework. All parameters in the
350
      U-Net (Ronneberger et al., 2015) were frozen, except for the K and V matrices. For training, we
      used the AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019) optimizer, with a batch size of 32. The learning rate
351
      was set to 4 × 10−5 for the U-Net and 3 × 10−5 for the CLIP text encoder (Radford et al., 2021).
352
      All experiments were conducted on dual NVIDIA L40 GPUs, resulting in a total training time of 12
353   hours.
354
355
      Comparative experimental setting. Given that our focus is on controlling both appearance and
356
      layout, and considering that most existing generative models do not support direct control over ap-
357   pearance, so we employ the layout-controllable generative models as comparative methods. We
358   utilize their text interface to input appearance attributes. We construct the text prompts using the
359   following template: “brightness: kL , color cast: kCC , contrast: kDR .” Fur-
360   thermore, all training hyper-parameters are aligned with those specified in the original papers for the
361   comparative methods. Furthermore, we do not include certain layout-controllable generative models
362   that do not support text input, such as the classical work LayoutDiffusion (Zheng et al., 2023), in
363   our comparisons.
364
365   Evaluation metrics. We use five metrics to evaluate the model’s performance in terms of appear-
366   ance controllability, generation quality, and layout controllability. For appearance controllability,
367   we propose the Appearance MSE (A-MSE) metric, which measures the mean squared error (MSE)
368
      between the generated images and real images acrossP the luminance,    dynamic range, and color cast

529   6     C ONCLUSION
530
      This paper addresses the challenge of controlling underwater image appearance through three key
531
      attributes: luminance, dynamic range, and color cast. We introduce S EA D IFF, a novel method that
532
      includes two main components: the Appearance Writer (A-Writer) and the Appearance Reader (A-
533   Reader). Our experimental results demonstrate that S EA D IFF enhances control over underwater
534   image appearance, allowing precise adjustments in three key attributes while maintaining image
535   quality and layout consistency.
536
537   Limitations. Although this study yields promising results, it only focuses on the appearance at-
538   tributes of luminance, dynamic range, and color cast, without addressing the complex factors of
539   underwater environments. Future research should explore how to model these factors for more pre-
      cise control over underwater images.

      Enhancing Downstream Tasks. We conducted experiments on four representative object detection
