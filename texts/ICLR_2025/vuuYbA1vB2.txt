011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Enhancing the mathematical capabilities of large language models (LLMs) is cru-
014            cial for applications requiring precise and rigorous mathematical reasoning. Cur-
015            rent models, even when trained with methods like Direct Preference Optimization
016
               (DPO), often struggle to effectively differentiate between correct and erroneous
               mathematical responses, especially when errors occur in multi-step solutions. Tra-
017
               ditional approaches focusing on token or logit-level analysis fail to capture the nu-
018
               anced semantic differences in mathematical reasoning. To address this challenge,
019            we propose leveraging the rich semantic information embedded in the hidden state
020            space of LLMs. Our novel approach, Focused Differentiation Training (FDT),
021            fine-tunes the model by emphasizing the differences between the hidden states of
022            correct and incorrect responses, rather than their common features. Unlike other
023            methods that detect errors at the token or logits level and often rely on human
024            input or more powerful models, our approach enhances mathematical reasoning
025            capabilities using only the model’s inherent abilities. This methodology promotes
026            a more accurate alignment with mathematical correctness, thereby improving the
027
               model’s ability to evaluate and generate precise mathematical responses. Experi-
               mental results demonstrate that our algorithm substantially outperforms traditional
028
               alignment methods in mathematical tasks, offering a robust solution for enhancing
029
               the mathematical reasoning capabilities of language models.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) has emerged as a powerful technique for
035
      aligning large language models (LLMs) with human preferences, significantly enhancing their us-
036   ability and reliability across various applications. While traditional approaches to training LLMs
037   often rely on vast amounts of data and heuristic methods, potentially leading to misalignment with
038   human values and intentions, RLHF addresses this issue by directly incorporating human feedback
039   into the training process, thereby ensuring that models better reflect human preferences.
040
      Existing methods for RLHF, such as Direct Preference Optimization (DPO), have demonstrated
041
      significant improvements in aligning LLM outputs with human preferences in general language
042   tasks. However, when it comes to mathematical reasoning tasks, especially those involving multi-
043   step problems, these methods often fall short. Traditional RLHF approaches typically focus on
044   general language understanding and response generation, which may not adequately capture the
045   specific nuances required for precise mathematical task performance. This oversight can result in
046   models that, although aligned with general linguistic preferences, often fail to address the structured
047   and logical demands unique to mathematical reasoning and problem-solving effectively.
048   The primary challenge with existing RLHF techniques in mathematical contexts is their inability to
049   accurately identify and differentiate errors within multi-step solutions. For instance, if a response
050   contains a single incorrect step amidst otherwise correct reasoning, it is often labeled entirely wrong.
051   This labeling approach discourages models from recognizing the complexity and partial correctness
052   within mathematical arguments, a critical skill for advanced mathematical reasoning. Furthermore,
053   current methods rely heavily on token or logit-level analysis, which often proves inadequate for
      capturing the subtle semantic differences crucial in mathematical reasoning ?. These methods rely


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      on the assistance from human or more advanced models, which is costly and labor-intensive. Addi-
055   tionally, these approaches may not always provide consistent or reliable interpretations, especially
056   in complex multi-step mathematical problems where nuanced understanding is essential.
057
058
      To address these shortcomings, we propose leveraging the hidden states of LLMs to more effectively
      differentiate between correct and incorrect mathematical responses. The hidden state space of LLMs
059
      contains rich semantic information that is more concentrated and intact compared to surface-level
060
      token representations. By focusing on this dense embedding space, we can capture and utilize
061   semantic divergences more effectively, allowing for a deeper analysis of mathematical reasoning
062   processes. Our approach is inspired by recent advancements in semantic analysis of embedding
063   spaces, as described by ?. This method ensures that the model engages with underlying semantic
064   structures rather than merely responding to explicit linguistic cues. Moreover, as highlighted by ?,
065   representing semantic divergence in embedding space provides a promising solution to the problem
066   of semantic equivalence and linguistic invariances, which are particularly relevant in mathematical
067   contexts.
068   Building on these insights, we introduce a novel training algorithm called Focused Differentiation
069   Training (FDT). FDT operates by fine-tuning the weight updates in the model’s output layer, with
070   a specific emphasis on distinguishing the differences between correct and incorrect mathematical
071   responses rather than their common features. This approach is grounded in the observation that
072   the common parts of hidden states between correct and incorrect answers often represent shared
073   mathematical concepts or problem setups, while the differences are more likely to indicate critical
074   points of divergence in reasoning. By specifically targeting how the model perceives and processes
075
      mathematical logic through hidden state analysis, FDT aims to enhance the model’s ability to dissect
      and understand the underlying mathematical structure. This method can be seamlessly integrated
076
      into existing RLHF frameworks, thereby improving the model’s performance on tasks that require
077
      high levels of mathematical accuracy and reasoning. The key contributions of our work are as
078   follows:
079
080          • We introduce FDT, a novel algorithm that leverages hidden state analysis to fine-tune the
081            weight updates in the model’s output layer, enhancing mathematical reasoning capabilities.
082            This method can be plugged into existing RLHF frameworks to improve the model’s ability
083            to distinguish between correct and incorrect mathematical responses, particularly in multi-
084            step problems.
085          • We present a theoretical analysis of how FDT improves the model’s ability to distinguish
086            between correct and incorrect mathematical responses.
087          • We provide empirical evidence demonstrating the superiority of FDT over traditional
088            RLHF methods in mathematical reasoning tasks, showcasing significant improvements in
089            accuracy over a range of mathematical tasks and several models.

      2.1   R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING FROM H UMAN F EEDBACK
095
      Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) has become a central approach for align-
096   ing large language models (LLMs) with human values by incorporating human evaluations to refine
097   model outputs iteratively (?????). Unlike traditional reinforcement learning, which relies on prede-
098   fined rewards, RLHF uses qualitative feedback from human evaluators to guide the model toward
099   more human-like and ethical responses. However, its implementation poses challenges due to the
100   variability and subjectivity of human-generated feedback, which can introduce inconsistencies into
101   the reward model (?).
102   Due to the various limitations of RLHF, researchers have started exploring new paradigms for align-
103   ing large models. In particular, DPO (?) marks a significant advancement in direct policy optimiza-
104   tion, addressing the complexities of balancing model behavior through a more refined approach to
105   reward function optimization. Subsequently, numerous variants of DPO have emerged. SimPO (?)
106   observed that during DPO training, the curve representing the change in probabilities for the model’s
107   generated responses does not align with the implicit reward curve. To address this, SimPO proposed
      directly amplifying the probability gap between the chosen and rejected responses. KTO (?) re-

      structured DPO’s loss function, removing the dependence on pairwise datasets during the alignment
109   process. TDPO (?) re-derived the RLHF problem from a token-level perspective, achieving a better
110   balance between model alignment and generation diversity. ORPO (?), from a more lightweight
111   perspective, further eliminated the reliance on reference models during the alignment process.

114   2.2   M ATHEMATICAL R EASONING
115
116   Large language models (LLMs) have exhibited substantial mathematical reasoning abilities. How-
117
      ever, when faced with complex mathematical problems that require fine-grained reasoning, LLMs
      still struggle to perform effectively. In such cases, LLMs may even exhibit severe hallucination is-
118
      sues. One common approach to addressing this issue is to impose stricter constraints on the model by
119
      requiring more detailed, step-by-step reasoning, thereby enhancing the model’s Chain-of-Thought
120   (CoT) capabilities (?????). While this approach has proven effective in certain tasks, it does not
121   fundamentally improve the model. Moreover, due to the inherent limitations of the model’s archi-
122   tecture, the potential improvements are quite limited. When presented with questions in different
123   formats, the model’s responses can still display hallucinations, indicating that the root cause of the
124   hallucination problem has not been addressed.
125
      Another approach focuses on significantly improving the model’s mathematical reasoning capabil-
126   ities through continued pre-training (CPT) or supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on large-scale, high-
127   quality mathematics-related datasets (????????). During this process, various data augmentation
128   techniques, such as rephrasing, expansion, and evolution, are widely applied to further enrich the
129   datasets, helping the model achieve better performance during CPT or SFT. While, these datasets
130   are collected off-policy with respect to the model itself, which limits their ability to correct some of
131   the model’s intrinsic errors.
132   Reinforcement learning (RL) is another class of methods that can significantly enhance the logi-
133   cal reasoning capabilities of LLMs (???). By progressively strengthening the model’s reasoning
134   abilities and reducing hallucinations during inference, RL improves the reliability of the reasoning
135   process. Recent studies have shown that combining reinforcement learning with mathematical rea-
136   soning tasks can effectively improve the model’s accuracy, particularly for complex mathematical
137   problems. This category of methods includes RLHF, DPO, and DPO-like approaches. Among these,
138   Step-DPO (?), a DPO-like method, stands out for its ability to significantly enhance mathematical
139   reasoning by aligning the reasoning process step-by-step in long-chain reasoning tasks, thereby cor-
140
      recting specific errors in LLMs’ mathematical reasoning.

501
502
      In this paper, we presented FDT, an innovative training methodology developed to bolster the math-
503
      ematical reasoning capabilities of LLMs. FDT harnesses the dense semantic information embedded
504   in the hidden states of LLMs to effectively discriminate between correct and incorrect mathematical
505   responses, particularly in complex multi-step scenarios. Unlike other methodologies that depend
506   on human intervention or more sophisticated models for response evaluation at the token or logits
507   level, our approach autonomously enhances mathematical reasoning by capitalizing on the inherent
508   capabilities of the model itself. This self-sufficient method improves performance without external
509   aid, making full use of the model’s native capacities. Our empirical evaluations on the GSM8K,
510   MATH, and MMLU-redux datasets have demonstrated that FDT not only significantly enhances
511   accuracy but also deepens reasoning capabilities. It notably excels in providing detailed feedback
512
      on partially correct solutions, a feature particularly valuable in educational settings. Additionally,
      FDT’s seamless integration with existing RLHF frameworks underscores its versatility.
