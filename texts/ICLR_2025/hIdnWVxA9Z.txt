009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            We tackle the task of customized image editing using a text-conditioned Diffusion
012            Model (DM). The goal is to fuse the subject in a reference image (e.g., sunglasses)
013            with a source one (e.g., a boy), while retaining the fidelity of them both (e.g., the
014            boy wearing the sunglasses). An intuitive approach, called LoRA fusion, first sep-
015            arately trains a DM LoRA for each image to encode its details. Then the two Lo-
016
               RAs are linearly combined by a weight to generate a fused image. Unfortunately,
               even through careful grid search or learning the weight, this approach still trades
017
               off the fidelity of one image against the other. We point out that the evil lies in
018
               the overlooked role of diffusion time-step in the generation process, i.e., a smaller
019            time-step controls the generation of a more fine-grained attribute. For example, a
020            large LoRA weight for the source may help preserve its fine-grained details (e.g.,
021            face attributes) at a small time-step, but could overpower the reference subject
022            LoRA and lose the fidelity of its overall shape at a larger time-step. To address
023            this deficiency, we propose TimeFusion, which learns a time-step-specific LoRA
024            fusion weight that resolves the trade-off, i.e., generating the source and reference
025            subject in high fidelity given(a)
                                               their respective prompt. Then we can customize
026            image editing using this weight and a target prompt. Codes are in Appendix.

      To address this, a natural extension to the above editing paradigm is LoRA fusion Ryu (2023):
073
      first, learn a source and reference LoRA to reconstruct I s and the subject in I r , respectively; then
074
      linearly combine their guidance in the reverse process using a fusion coefficient (β s , β r ), e.g., a
075   larger β s increases the guidance strength from the source LoRA. However, as shown in Figure 2(a),
076   no combination of coefficients maintains the fidelity of both I s and I r , i.e., increasing β r preserves
077   the visual attributes of the reference subject but sacrifices the fidelity of I s .
078
      Therefore, the crux of customized image editing lies in finding a more precise method for fusing Lo-
079
      RAs that accommodates the visual attributes of both I s and I r . This motivates us to improve LoRA
080
      fusion by considering the diffusion time-step (along which the reverse process generates an image).
081   This is based on two key observations: 1) Diffusion time-step is theoretically and empirically linked
082   to visual attributes Yue et al. (2024a;b), where the reverse process at a smaller time-step is respon-
083   sible for generating a more fine-grained attribute. For example, as we will later show in Figure 4,
084   providing LoRA guidance at smaller time-steps modifies more fine-grained attributes. 2) Thus, a
085   time-step-specific fusion coefficient provides the required precision, e.g., using a large β s at a small
086   time-step to preserve the fine-grained facial details in I s , while increasing β r at a larger time-step
087   to maintain the more coarse-grained shape of the sunglasses in I r (more examples in Figure 13).
088   Building on the above analysis, we propose a time-step specific LoRA fusion strategy called Time-
089   Fusion. Specifically, we first learn a source and reference LoRA using a standard technique Avra-
090   hami et al. (2023) (preliminaries provided in Appendix). Then our coefficients for LoRA fusion
091   consist of two parts, as shown in Figure 2(b): 1) the aforementioned time-step specific ones (βts , βtr )
092   for each diffusion time-step t ∈ {1, . . . , T }. 2) a patch-level one αs , αr ∈ R8×8 for the 8 × 8 latent
093   patches in each layer where LoRA is injected (layer index omitted for simplicity). The patch-level
094   coefficient acts as a modifier to the time-step one, further refining the fusion by considering spatial
095
      information, e.g., applying a large source LoRA coefficient on a background patch to faithfully re-
      construct I s . Overall, during the reverse process at time-step t, the LoRA fusion coefficient for a
096
      patch at spatial location (i, j) will be (αsi,j βts , αri,j βtr ). The paradigm of TimeFusion is summarized
097
      in Figure 2(c). We train the coefficients so that the fused LoRA reconstructs I s and I r according
098   to their respective prompt, i.e., accommodating the visual attributes of both images. In inference,
099   using the learned LoRA fusion, we achieve customized image editing by simply supplying the target
100   prompt. Figure 3 showcases our results. Our contributions include:
101
      • We tackle the challenging task of customized image editing with a text-conditioned DM (Sec-

        tion 2.1), by improving the current LoRA fusion (Section 2.2).
103
104
      • Motivated by the connection between diffusion time-step and visual attribute (Section 2.1), we

168   2.1   T EXT-C ONDITIONED D IFFUSION M ODEL (DM)
169
170   A text-conditioned DM is a generative model that allows textual control. It uses a forward process
171   that incrementally adds noise to input data to learn a reverse process, where the model is trained
172
      to reconstruct clean data from noisy one and its text description. In this work, we focus on Stable
      Diffusion (SD) Rombach et al. (2022), where each input data z0 is an image feature. Specifically,
173
      z0 is the latent variable of a pre-trained image autoencoder, which is encoded from an image to
174
      reconstruct it. Hence we use z0 and image interchangeably when the context is clear.
175
176   Forward Process. It progressively adds Gaussian noise to each image z0 in T time-steps, producing
177   noisy images z1 , . . . , zT , with the subscript denoting time-step. Each zt adheres to its noisy image
178
      distribution q(zt |z0 ), which gradually collapses towards a pure noise with reducing mean and in-
      creasing variance as t increases (details in Appendix). Recent works Yue et al. (2024a;b) show that
179
      the forward process connects diffusion time-step with the visual attributes of an image. In a nutshell,
180
      an increasing t causes a large overlap between noisy image distributions, essentially collapsing dif-
181   ferent images into similar ones by losing the visual attributes that differentiate them. In particular,
182   the theory suggests that fine-grained attributes (i.e., those affecting local appearances, such as ex-
183   pression) become lost at a smaller time-step compared to coarse-grained ones (i.e., those affecting
184   global appearances, such as background). This pattern of attribute loss has interesting implications
185   in the following reverse process.
186
      Reverse Process. It corresponds to a learned Gaussian transition pθ (zt−1 |zt , P ) conditioned on a
187   text prompt P and parameterized by θ. The term is computed in two steps: first reconstructing z0 as
188   z′0 by a learnable denoising network d(zt , P, t; θ) with parameter θ, then computing q(zt−1 |zt , z′0 ),
189   which has a closed-form solution given in Appendix. In training, we minimize the reconstruction
190   error:
191                                              T

194   where zt is sampled from the forward process at a random time-step t. In inference, to generate
195   an image conditioned on a prompt P , we first set zT as a random noise, and recursively run the
196   reverse process pθ (zt−1 |zt , P ) until obtaining z0 . Then for SD, it has a decoder that maps z0 to an

198   in the forward process, the reverse process must correspondingly make up for the lost attribute in
199   each step to accurately reconstruct z0 . Hence as fine-grained attributes are lost at smaller time-steps,
200   the reverse process is responsible for generating them at smaller time-steps correspondingly. The
201
      technique introduced in the next section will help us visualize this.

203   2.2   L OW-R ANK A DAPTATION (L O RA)
204
205   Training a DM with a randomly initialized θ by minimizing Equation 1 can be extremely expensive.
206
      Hence the common approach is to initialize θ from a DM pre-trained on diverse image data (e.g.,
      SD), and fine-tune it to a downstream task. LoRA is the most mainstream fine-tuning method Hu
207
      et al. (2021); Song et al. (2024); Gu et al. (2024).
208
209   LoRA refers to a set of low-rank matrices, each of which is injected into a corresponding layer of DM
210   to update its weight. Specifically, let W ∈ Rm×n denote the pre-trained weight matrix of a layer in
211   DM. The LoRA matrice injected to this layer is given by ∆W = AB, where A ∈ Rm×r , B ∈ Rr×n ,
212
      such that the rank of ∆W equals a small r ≪ min(m, n). After injection, the weight of this layer
      becomes W + ∆W .
213
214   LoRA Training. In fine-tuning, the original DM weights are frozen, and only the injected LoRA
215   is trained. We denote the LoRA matrices as θl (or LoRA θl for short), and the weight of DM after
      LoRA injection as θ ⊕ θl . Given a downstream dataset D where each sample (z0 , P ) comprises of

245   rank matrix in the i-th LoRA and its coefficient, respectively. An example use case of LoRA fusion
246   is synthesizing a subject in a specific style Shah et al. (2023), where a LoRA trained on images
247   of a subject (e.g., one specific dog) is fused with the other LoRA on one image of a style (e.g.,
248
      watercolor). However, as we analyze in the introduction, the current way of fusing LoRAs does not
      have the required precision to tackle the customized image editing task (Figure 2(a)). In the next
249
      section, we propose TimeFusion to fix this.

318   Image Editing are mostly Text-Based (TBIE). To maintain the fidelity of the user-provided image,
319   a line of works Cao et al. (2023); Tumanyan et al. (2023) adopt DDIM inversion Song et al. (2020)
320   or its enhanced versions Pan et al. (2023); Wallace et al. (2023) for accelerated reconstruction. How-
321   ever, such methods are unstable and prone to errors. To improve the reconstruction, fine-tuning SD
322   or injected LoRA layers Kawar et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2023) provides a promising solution.
323   Besides reconstruction, several techniques are proposed to improve editing, e.g., by adjusting at-
      tention maps Hertz et al. (2022), interpolating learnable text embeddings Kawar et al. (2023) and


                                                                            6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      encoding semantic change in the text LoRA Song et al. (2024). However, customized image editing
325   is under-explored. The most relevant work SpecRef Chen & Huang (2023) is based on DDIM inver-
326   sion, where the reference image feature is extracted in the inversion stage, and incorporated when
327   adjusting attention maps for editing. Yet its performance is bottlenecked by DDIM inversion. We
328   improve it by extending LoRA fusion for customized image editing.
329
      LoRA Fusion is mainly used for multi-concept customization, which synthesizes novel combina-
330
      tions of concepts, without the need to perverse the fidelity of any user-provided images. Existing
331   works focus on improving LoRA training Po et al. (2023) or fusion strategy Zhong et al. (2024).
332   For the latter, some notable improvements include Mix-of-Show Gu et al. (2024), which proposes
333   gradient fusion that directly learns the combined LoRA weights without losing the capability for
334   single concept generation; MoLE Wu et al. (2023a), which leverages learnable gating functions for
335   each LoRA layer to fulfill the combination; and ZipLoRA Shah et al. (2023), which claims that the
336   columns of two LoRA weights should be orthogonal to avoid identity loss and optimizes orthogonal
337   coefficients for each column of two LoRAs. However, we find existing LoRA fusion strategy lacks
338   the required precision in customized image editing. Hence we extend it with time-step-specific and
339
      patch-specific fusion coefficients.

343   Implementation Details. Following prior editing works Kawar et al. (2023); Song et al. (2024);
344   Zhang et al. (2023), we adopt Stable Diffusion Rombach et al. (2022) as our DM and use its default
345   parameters. When learning LoRA θs to reconstruct I s , the learning rate is set as 1e-4 and the
346   optimization iteration is 800. We use the approach in Avrahami et al. (2023) to learn the subject in a
347   single image I r . For time-step-specific coefficients, we equally divide the time-steps into 20 splits,
348
      i.e., K = 20. When learning with Equation 4, we set the learning rate as 5e−2 and training iterations
      as 100. Experiments are conducted on an NVIDIA A100 GPU with a batch size of 1. Computation
349
      analysis is included in Appendix.

517   Ablation on Time-step Coefficient. We ablation the number of splits K for the time-step coeffi-
518   cient. As depicted in Figure 11, as K increases from 5 to 20, we observe consistent improvements
519   in fidelity, e.g., the baby’s posture at K = 5 and the baby’s leg at K = 10 are not preserved. This
520   is because when K is small, a long range of time-steps shares the same time-step coefficients, yet
521   corresponds to multiple visual attributes. Hence the fusion may be imprecise to lose fidelity. How-
522
      ever, further improving K to 50 leads to a slight loss of fidelity, e.g., the shade of the baby’s clothes.
      We conjecture that the reason is inadequate learning for coefficients: as the optimization iteration of
523
      fusion is fixed to 100, each time-step coefficient is only expected to be trained twice when K = 50.
524
      Yet increasing the iteration increases the training time. Hence we choose K = 20 to balance the
525   editing results and compute.

528   6       C ONCLUSION
529
530   We presented TimeFusion, which enables customized image editing with the text-conditioned Sta-
531
      ble Diffusion (SD). The crux is to first learn two SD LoRAs for encoding the visual attributes of the
      source image and reference subject, respectively, and then fuse them in a precise way to maintain
532
      the fidelity of both. Motivated by the connection between diffusion time-step and visual attributes,
533
      we extend the current LoRA fusion with learnable time-step-specific and patch-specific coefficients.
534   They are trained such that SD, after LoRA fusion, can still reconstruct the source image and ref-
535   erence subject. We show that the additional coefficients enable LoRA fusion to simultaneously
536   accommodate the visual attributes of the user-provided source and reference images. Hence we
537   significantly improve the fidelity in customized image editing, compared to the current state-of-the-
538   arts. As a future direction, we will speed up the LoRA learning process in training and the SD
539   reverse process in editing by exploring Fast Diffusion Model Wu et al. (2023b) and Consistency
      Models Song et al. (2023).

726   A.3   G ENERALIZATION A NALYSIS
727
728   Our TimeFusion adopts time-step-specific and patch-specific coefficients to fuse source image LoRA
729   and reference subject LoRA. It is worth noting that these two coefficients are learned such that
730
      the fused LoRA can reconstruct the source image and the reference subject with their respective
      prompts, i.e., not overfitting to any of the two images. Take the reference patch coefficient αr for
731
      example, the reference subject having a large weight αr on some positions does not imply that it is
732
      fixed to its layout and pose in the final generation. This is because the generation is affected not only
733   by reference patch coefficient αr , but also by source patch coefficient αs and time-step coefficients
734   βts and βtr , e.g., αr does not affect source image reconstruction given source prompt. In fact, having
735   both patch and time-step coefficients gives the model enough flexibility to accommodate source and
736   reference image attributes, allowing the final generation to be faithful to the user’s prompt.
737   Furthermore, in the LoRA fusion methods for multi-concept customization, the user prompt is not
738   known a priori. To enable fair comparison with existing works, we choose to learn prompt-agnostic
739   coefficients. To follow the user-specified prompt after fusion, we simply leverage SD’s extrapolating
740   power to imagine the composition of the source image and reference subject, which is empirically
741
      justified and extensively leveraged in any SD-based image editing works Kawar et al. (2023); Tu-
      manyan et al. (2023). Actually, prompt-agnostic fusion has the added benefit of quickly adapting to
742
      different prompts given the same source and reference images. As shown in Figure 12, we could
743
      add the clock or replace the banana with the clock, and make the squirrel hold the candle or next to
744   the candle.
