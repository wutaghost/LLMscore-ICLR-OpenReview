011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013              Building upon advancements in Large Language Models (LLMs), the field of au-
014
                 dio processing has seen increased interest in training audio generation tasks with
                 discrete audio token sequences. However, directly discretizing audio by neural
015
                 audio codecs often results in sequences that fundamentally differ from text se-
016
                 quences. Unlike text, where text token sequences are deterministic, discrete audio
017              tokens can exhibit significant variability based on contextual factors, while still
018              producing perceptually identical audio segments. We refer to this phenomenon as
019              Discrete Representation Inconsistency (DRI). This inconsistency can lead to a
020              single audio segment being represented by multiple divergent sequences, which
021              creates confusion in neural codec language models and results in omissions and
022              repetitions during speech generation. In this paper, we quantitatively analyze the
023              DRI phenomenon within popular audio tokenizers such as EnCodec. Our ap-
024              proach effectively mitigates the DRI phenomenon of the neural audio codec. Fur-
025
                 thermore, extensive experiments on the neural codec language model over Lib-
                 riTTS and large-scale MLS dataset (44,000 hours) demonstrate the effectiveness
026
                 and generality of our method. The demo of audio samples is available online 1 .

029   1       I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Recently, speech Large Language Models (LLMs) (Zhan et al., 2024; Anastassiou et al., 2024;
032   Du et al., 2024b) have demonstrated significant strides in generating high-quality speech, largely
033   due to the contributions of neural audio codecs in high-fidelity audio reconstruction (Zeghidour
034   et al., 2021; Défossez et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2023). The neural codec language model (Wang
035   et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024) employs the neural audio codec as the audio
036   tokenizer to quantize continuous audio signals into discrete tokens, and it can generate discrete
037
      tokens autoregressively (Zhang et al., 2023a; Yang et al., 2024), and then detokenize them back to
      audio signals by the neural audio codec. Despite the advantages of autoregressive modeling can
038
      assist those works to achieve better zero-shot performance and naturalness, the synthesized speech
039
      frequently yields higher Word Error Rate (WER) due to the issue of instability in discrete token
040   generation (Song et al., 2024; Xin et al., 2024; Du et al., 2024a).
041
042   The discrete sequence of text is context-independent. In contrast, acoustic discrete representations
043
      are encoded by integrating the contextual information. The advantage of this approach is that dis-
      crete audio tokens consider a larger receptive field of information, thus achieving a higher compres-
044
      sion ratio of information. However, the drawback is that the representation itself becomes more
045
      fragile, sensitive, and easily affected by minor signal changes, leading to drastic drifts in the entire
046   sequence as demonstrated in Figure 1.
047
048   The previous work (Yang et al., 2024) has noticed that audio segments containing the same sound
049
      events aren’t encoded into completely consistent discrete acoustic tokens by the neural audio codec.
      In this paper, we call this phenomenon Discrete Representation Inconsistency (DRI), and further
050
      dig into the problem with Vector Quantization (VQ) (Défossez et al., 2022) based acoustic tokens
051
      due to its popularity as an audio tokenizer and its high-quality reconstruction capabilities. We com-
052   pare the consistency of the discrete sequences of audio segments with and without context on a large

140   2     A NALYSIS ON CONSISTENCY OF NEURAL AUDIO CODECS
141
142   In this section, we extract discrete audio tokens from audio segments with and without context using
143   popular neural audio codecs (Défossez et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023b; Du et al.,
144
      2024c; Kumar et al., 2024; Ju et al., 2024) to analyze the DRI phenomenon. First, we introduce the
      overall experiment design. Then we propose using consistency accuracy as an evaluation metric to
145
      conduct quantitative analyses. Finally, we analyze the results and discuss the potential implications
146
      of the DRI phenomenon.
147
148
      2.1   E XPERIMENTAL D ESIGN O N DRI P HENOMENON
149
150   Recent advancements on neural audio codecs have adopted an encoder-decoder architecture com-
151   bined with the RVQ module to effectively compress continuous audio signals into discrete audio
152   tokens (Défossez et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023b; Du et al., 2024c; Kumar et al.,
153   2024; Ju et al., 2024), which is typically composed of 3 components: (1) An encoder, composed of
154   convolutional layers to capture contextual information, maps the audio signal into a latent represen-
155   tation Z. (2) An RVQ module contains N quantization layers to quantize the latent representation
156   Z into the discrete audio tokens at each time step. (3) A decoder reconstructs the quantized latent
157   representation back to the audio signal.
158   To analyze the DRI phenomenon, we use popular neural audio codecs (Défossez et al., 2022; Yang
159   et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023b; Du et al., 2024c; Kumar et al., 2024; Ju et al., 2024) as audio tok-
160   enizers to quantize both the entire audio and an audio segment within that audio, and then compare
161   the results of their corresponding discrete audio tokens. Obviously, these two audio segments are
      exactly identical with the only difference being whether there is context, and we expect that both


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      discrete audio tokens should be identical after quantization. But the encoders in current neural audio
163   codecs introduce the contextual information that gives rise to the DRI phenomenon, leading to both
164   discrete audio token sequences showing significant differences.

179   2.3   R ESULTS A ND A NALYSIS
180
181   Audio tokenizer vs. text tokenizer. As shown in Figure 1 (a), regardless of whether the context is
182   included, the same text is tokenized into the same text tokens, indicating that the text tokenizer is
183
      context-independent. In contrast, Figure 1 (b) demonstrates that using a neural audio codec as the
      audio tokenizer produces different discrete audio token sequences for identical audio segments. Al-
184
      though it is difficult for human auditory perception to distinguish the reconstructed audio from both
185
      sequences, the many-to-one mapping caused by the DRI phenomenon still increases the difficulty
186   for model training, leading to a decline in speech reconstruction and generation performance.
187
188   The results of consistency accuracy. To quantitatively analyze the DRI phenomenon, we calculate
189
      the consistency accuracy for popular neural audio codecs under different layers and slice lengths.
      The results are shown in Figure 2 and the low consistency accuracy reveals that the DRI phenomenon
190
      is present in the current neural audio codecs (Défossez et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2023; Zhang et al.,
191
      2023b; Du et al., 2024c; Kumar et al., 2024; Ju et al., 2024). Furthermore, we find that with deeper
192   layers of codebooks, neural audio codecs demonstrate lower consistency. This may be attributed
193   to the fact that audio tokens in shallow layers exhibit a high alignment with context-independent
194   semantic information, resulting in better consistency. In contrast, deeper layers focus on more fragile
195   and sensitive acoustic information that can easily change due to minor perturbations, leading to a
196   decrease in consistency accuracy (Zhang et al., 2023b).
197
      The potential implications of the DRI phenomenon. There are many minor perturbations that can
198   cause the DRI phenomenon, such as contextual information and phase perturbation (Lee et al., 2023)
199   that do not alter the auditory perception of the reconstructed audio but can lead to changes in the
200   discrete audio token sequences, which can greatly confuse models. Especially when neural codec
201   language models need to predict different audio tokens due to the DRI phenomenon, this confusion
202   can cause the predicted probability distributions of the next token to converge towards uniformity,
203   resulting in inaccurate predictions and negatively impacting overall performance. Therefore, it is
204   necessary to ease the many-to-one mapping problem to improve the consistency of neural audio
205   codecs, which in turn enhances the performance of downstream speech generation.

      According to the analysis in Section 2, we can draw a conclusion that an ideal neural audio codec
210   should balance the trade-offs between high audio reconstruction quality and addressing the many-
211   to-one problem. To achieve this objective, we introduce two consistency constraint methods: the
212   slice-consistency method and perturbation-consistency method, which enhance the consistency
213   of the neural audio codec from two particular perspectives. Since these methods can be integrated
214   into any neural audio codec, we demonstrate their application using a neural audio codec based on
215   RVQ which utilizes an encoder to transform the audio signal into the latent representation Z and
      reconstructs the waveform from the quantized latent representation.

      3.1   C ONSISTENCY C ONSTRAINT M ETHODS
235
      Slice-consistency requests that audio segments with and without context should be encoded into
236   consistent latent representations. To achieve this object, as shown in Figure 3, we slice a segment of
237   audio from the original audio, and then encode it using the encoder in the neural audio codec to ob-
238   tain the latent representation Z slice . Compared with the latent representation Z from the entire audio,
239   Z slice is not influenced by contextual information. To reduce the influence of context on the latent
240   representation Z, we use Mean Squared Error (MSE) as a constraint to enhance the consistency
241   between Z slice and the corresponding latent representation in Z:
242
243                                                  T
                                                  1X
244                                    Lslice =         MSE(Z slice [t], Z[t]).                            (2)
                                                  T t=1
245
246
247
      As analyzed in Appendix 8.1 about the receptive field, the convolutional layers in the encoder of
      neural audio codecs introduce contextual information, leading to identical audio segments being
248
      tokenized into different discrete audio token sequences. It is clear that reducing the kernel size of
249
      the convolutional layers in the encoder can enhance consistency, but this can also result in a decline
250   in both reconstruction efficiency and quality. Therefore, applying the slice-consistency method is
251   necessary to maintain the original receptive field while enabling models to balance the trade-offs
252   between audio reconstruction quality and alleviating the DRI phenomenon.
253
      Perturbation-Consistency refers to the latent representations of audio, which should remain consis-
254
      tent before and after being applied imperceptible perturbations to human ears. Specifically, as shown
255
      in Figure 3, we slightly adjust the phase of the the original audio without significantly altering the
256   waveform structure, and encode it using the encoder in the neural audio codec to obtain the latent
257   representation Z perception . Since human ears have a limited ability to directly perceive phase changes,
258   we hope that the robustness of the model can also eliminate inconsistency caused by such slight per-
259   turbations. Therefore, we utilize MSE to maintain consistency of both latent representations with
260   and without phase perturbation (Lee et al., 2023):
261
262
                                         Lperception = MSE(Z perception , Z).                              (3)
263
264   It is evident that the perturbation-consistency method differs from audio-based data augmentation
265   methods. Data augmentation methods such as SpecAugment (Park et al., 2019) and environment
266   noise (Snyder et al., 2015) significantly alter the original audio to create new audio. The newly
267   generated audio has a considerable difference in perception compared to the original audio, which
268   aims to expand the training data and increase the robustness of models. In contrast, the perception-
269   consistency method requires that changes to audio should be imperceptible to human ears to avoid
      severe perturbations that disrupt the audio reconstruction quality. Since the phase is difficult to be

270
      perceived by human, we apply phase perturbation (Lee et al., 2023) as a slight perturbation method,
271   which can enhance the perturbation-consistency without expanding the training data.

      6   R ELATED WORK
487
488
      Discrete speech representations. Discrete speech representations can be categorized into semantic
489   and acoustic tokens. Discrete semantic tokens are extracted from self-supervised speech models
490   like HuBERT (Hsu et al., 2021) and WavLM (Chen et al., 2022), or Automatic Speech Recognition
491   (ASR) models like SenseVoice (SpeechTeam, 2024). K-means or VQ models serve as information
492   bottlenecks, filtering out paralinguistic information while retaining semantic information. In con-
493   trast, discrete acoustic tokens are encoded by neural audio codecs, preserving complete acoustic
494   information and aiming to reconstruct high-fidelity audio. SoundStream (Zeghidour et al., 2021)
495   and EnCodec (Défossez et al., 2022) adopt RVQ framework to encode speech into multi-level dis-
496   crete acoustic tokens. SingleCodec (Li et al., 2024) and Disen-TF-Codec (Jiang et al., 2023) utilize
497
      a reference encoder to capture global time-invariant information, thereby reducing the number of
      codebooks. SpeechTokenizer (Zhang et al., 2023b) and FACodec (Ju et al., 2024) decouple speech
498
      into different attributes, making discrete tokens more suitable for downstream speech modeling
499
      tasks. However, the synthesized speech from the neural codec language model, which relies on
500   discrete audio tokens, often leads to a higher WER (Song et al., 2024; Xin et al., 2024; Du et al.,
501   2024a). This is because these discrete audio tokens are fragile and sensitive, easily affected by minor
502   changes in the audio signal. Inspired by the context-independent text tokens, we propose enhancing
503   the consistency of audio token sequences to address the many-to-one mapping problem and improve
504   the stability of discrete audio tokens.
505
      Audio tokenizers and neural codec language models. After tokenizing continuous audio signals
506   into discrete tokens by a neural audio codec, a neural codec language model can be trained on these
507   discrete audio tokens. VALL-E (Wang et al., 2023) and SpearTTS (Kharitonov et al., 2023) employ
508   EnCodec (Défossez et al., 2022) and SoundStream (Zeghidour et al., 2021) as audio tokenizers to
509   extract discrete acoustic tokens, aiming to retain all acoustic information. SpearTTS (Kharitonov
510   et al., 2023) and SoundStorm (Borsos et al., 2023) adopt a coarse-to-fine approach to generate dis-
511   crete acoustic tokens. VoiceCraft (Peng et al., 2024) rearrange audio tokens through an autoregres-
512   sive way to perform speech generation and editing tasks. LLM-Codec (Yang et al., 2024) represents
513   audio tokens with words or subwords from the vocabulary of LLMs, aligning audio modality with
514
      text modality. Although LLM-Codec (Yang et al., 2024) has noticed that even when audio segments
      contain the same sound events, the discrete tokens generated by the audio tokenizer may still ex-
515
      hibit inconsistency. Therefore, to address this DRI phenomenon, we propose the slice-consistency
516
      method and perturbation-consistency method to enhance the consistency within neural audio codecs,
517   thereby improving the performance of downstream speech generation.

      7   C ONCLUSION
521
      We conduct a detailed analysis on the consistency of the discrete audio token sequences, and shed
522
      light on the Discrete Representation Inconsistency (DRI) phenomenon within the existing neural
523
      audio codecs. To mitigate the DRI phenomenon, we propose two consistency enhancement meth-
524   ods: (1) The slice-consistency method requires that the representation from a randomly sliced audio
525   segment should match the corresponding representation from the entire audio. (2) The perturbation-
526   consistency method aims to align the representation obtained from the audio after applying slight
527   spectral perturbations with that from the original audio. Experimental results indicate that our pro-
528   posed methods can successfully increase the consistency of discrete audio token sequences, thereby
529   enabling the neural codec language model based on these audio tokens to outperform the SOTA
530   speech generation model and show better performance by scaling up data. For future work, we plan
531   to try more consistency enhancement methods and apply our method to various modalities to assess
532
      its generalizability.

844   8.7   M ODEL PARAMETERA AND SCALABLE MODEL SIZE
845
846   Our neural audio codec utilizes 8 codebooks, each with a vocabulary size of 1024, and a base
847   dimension of 128. It operates at a frame rate of 50Hz for audio sampled at 16kHz, resulting in a
848   total parameter count of 66 M. On average, the speech to be encoded has a duration of 7 seconds,
849   leading to an estimation of FLOPs of approximately 2.57 × 1012 .
850   For the neural codec language model, both the AR model and NAR model are built upon the same
851   Transformer architecture. This setup includes 12 layers, 16 attention heads, an embedding dimen-
852   sion of 1024, and a feedforward layer dimension of 4096, collectively comprising about 365M
853   parameters. Typically, in the speech teneration task, the phoneme sequence has a length of 100. The
854   audio tokenizer extracts a sequence of audio tokens with a length of 150 from a 3-second prompt
855   audio, and the generated audio token sequence has a length of 550. This results in a computation of
856   FLOPs approximately equal to 1.66 × 1011 .
857   As shown in Table 8, we conducted experiments with models of varying parameter sizes. The
858   observed performance improvements further demonstrate the effectiveness of our method across
859   different model parameter settings.
