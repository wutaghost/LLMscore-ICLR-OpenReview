010
011
               With the rising interest in research on Large Multi-modal Models (LMMs) for
012
               video understanding, many studies have emphasized general video comprehen-
013
               sion capabilities, neglecting the systematic exploration into video quality un-
014            derstanding. To address this oversight, we introduce Q-Bench-Video in this pa-
015            per, a new benchmark specifically designed to evaluate LMMs’ proficiency in
016            discerning video quality. a) To ensure video source diversity, Q-Bench-Video en-
017            compasses videos from natural scenes, AI-generated Content (AIGC), and Com-
018            puter Graphics (CG). b) Building on the traditional multiple-choice questions for-
019            mat with the Yes-or-No and What-How categories, we include Open-ended ques-
020            tions to better evaluate complex scenarios. Additionally, we incorporate the video
021            pair quality comparison question to enhance comprehensiveness. c) Beyond
022
               the traditional Technical, Aesthetic, and Temporal distortions, we have expanded
               our evaluation aspects to include the dimension of AIGC distortions, which ad-
023
               dresses the increasing demand for video generation. Finally, we collect a total
024
               of 2,378 question-answer pairs and test them on 12 open-source & 5 proprietary
025            LMMs. Our findings indicate that while LMMs have a foundational understand-
026            ing of video quality, their performance remains incomplete and imprecise, with a
027            notable discrepancy compared to human performance. Through Q-Bench-Video,
028            we seek to catalyze community interest, stimulate further research, and unlock the
029            untapped potential of LMMs to close the gap in video quality understanding.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   As the field of artificial intelligence (AI) continues to evolve, Large Multi-modal Models
035   (LMMs) (Ye et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024a; Chen et al., 2024; Ke et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024) are pro-
036   gressively utilized in high-level video understanding tasks. These models have shown remarkable
037   capabilities in analyzing and interpreting the semantic content of videos, such as classifying objects,
038   identifying actions, and recognizing events. However, the aspect of video quality, which is vital
039
      for optimizing compression and transmission systems, enhancing viewer experience, and es-
      tablishing standards for high-quality video generation, has received less attention. Although
040
      numerous LMM video benchmarks (Fu et al., 2024; Fang et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024a) have been
041
      developed to assess the semantic understanding of videos by LMMs comprehensively, benchmarks
042   systematically targeting video quality are still lacking. Additionally, while semantic understanding
043   is closely linked to high-level video information, the perception and understanding of low-level in-
044   formation are crucial in video quality (Chikkerur et al., 2011; Li et al., 2024b). Thus, current video
045   benchmarks fail to adequately evaluate the video quality understanding capabilities of LMMs.
046
      To address this gap, we introduce Q-Bench-Video, a novel benchmark specifically designed to sys-
047
      tematically evaluate the video quality understanding of LMMs. As illustrated in Fig. 1, our bench-
048   mark encompasses a wide range of video content, including natural scenes, AI-generated Content
049   (AIGC), and Computer Graphics (CG), ensuring diversity in video sources. In addition, to maintain
050   a reasonable distribution of source video quality, we employ uniform sampling from video datasets
051   that contain subjective quality annotations. This approach guarantees comprehensive coverage of
052   the quality spectrum while avoiding imbalanced quality distributions. Moreover, we extend beyond
053   traditional video evaluations by incorporating both multiple-choice questions (MCQs) and open-
      ended questions. This enables a more thorough analysis of LMMs’ ability to discern video quality

121   2.1    L ARGE M ULTI - MODAL V IDEO M ODELS AND B ENCHMARKS
122
      The rapid advancement of Large Multi-modal Models (LMMs) in recent years (Liu et al., 2023b;a;
123
      2024a; Chen et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023a; Ye et al., 2023a) has showcased their remarkable
124
      perception and cognitive abilities across various multimodal benchmarks for images (Liu et al.,
125   2023d; Wu et al., 2024b; Fu et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024d; Marino et al., 2019). As development
126   progresses, the focus of visual analysis has gradually shifted from images to videos. Early efforts (Li
127   et al., 2023a; Lin et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023c; Xu et al., 2024) aimed at unlocking the video
128   understanding potential of LMMs have yielded promising results. However, initial video-based
129   benchmarks (Li et al., 2023b; Wang et al., 2023; Mangalam et al., 2023) typically concentrated on
130   specific aspects of video comprehension, falling short of fully capturing the performance of these
131   models due to limitations such as a lack of diversity in video types and inadequate coverage of
132   temporal dynamics. In response, more recent video benchmarks (Fu et al., 2024; Fang et al., 2024)
133
      have moved toward a more comprehensive evaluation of LMMs. Nonetheless, these efforts primarily
      focus on high-level semantic understanding without systematic exploration of video quality.
134
135
136
      2.2    V IDEO Q UALITY A SSESSMENT
137
      Video Quality Assessment (VQA) is a task aimed at quantifying video scores based on visual qual-
138   ity. Initially, early VQA methods employ hand-crafted features extracted from videos and regress the
139   features into quality scores (Zheng et al., 2022; Vu et al., 2011; Li et al., 2018). With the emergence
140   of deep neural networks, a shift occurr as numerous methods adopted deep learning techniques for
141   VQA tasks (Li et al., 2019; Sun et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022; Wen et al., 2024). As the field progresses,
142   newer methods begin incorporating considerations for both temporal dynamics and aesthetic quali-
143   ties, leading to a more holistic approach to video quality analysis (Wu et al., 2022a; 2023a; Zhang
144   et al., 2023b; Ahn & Lee, 2018; He et al., 2024a). Moreover, the evolution of large-scale models has
145   further revolutionized VQA methodologies. Many recent approaches have redefined the traditional
146
      quality assessment process into a quality question-answering format (Wu et al., 2024c; Ge et al.,
      2024; Zhang et al., 2024b). This adaptation leverages the substantial prior knowledge embedded in
147
      large models to enhance the precision of quality quantification (Zhang et al., 2024c). Despite these
148
      technological advances, VQA still grapples with challenges in providing interpretable quality scores
149   and deepening the understanding of how models perceive and analyze video quality.

      3.1    B ENCHMARK P RINCIPLE
155
      The Q-Bench-Video is designed based on three guiding principles: (1) It encompasses a broad
156   spectrum of video content, including natural scenes, AIGC, and CG videos. (2) It ensures a
157   comprehensive and representative sampling process across a wide quality range, enhancing the
158   benchmark’s overall effectiveness. (3) It primarily focuses on the aspects of video quality that
159   significantly influence the viewing experience, including technical, aesthetic, temporal, and AIGC
160   distortions. This significantly differs from other video benchmarks that prioritize semantic under-
161   standing. Additionally, the video pair quality comparison is integrated to address the challenges
      associated with comparing video quality. The construction can be overviewed in Fig. 1.

      3.2     S OURCE V IDEOS C OLLECTION
163
164   As shown in Table 1, the source videos are primarily gathered from video quality assessment
165   datasets. We selected videos from these datasets for two main reasons: (1) These datasets have
166   inherently considered the diversity of quality features during video selection; (2) These datasets
167   possess quality annotations that adhere to ITU standards (itu, 2000) (with the exception of Vide-
168   oFeedback), which allow us to accurately and authentically sample videos.
169   Our sampling method primarily employs a uniform approach, extracting videos evenly from each
170   dataset based on the quality range. Moreover, considering the current popularity of AIGC and CG
171   videos, we have also incorporated a selection of these video types. For a detailed description of the
172   datasets and the sampling procedure, please refer to Appendix A and Appendix B.
173
174   3.3     B ENCHMARK D ESIGNS
175
176   In this section, we provide a detailed description of the design of Q-Bench-Video. In this bench-
177   mark, the meta-structure tuple (V,Q,A,C) of each data item can be decomposed into several com-
178   ponents: the video object V (which can be a single video or a pair of videos), the video quality query
179   Q, the set of possible answers A, and the correct answer C. The question samples are listed in Fig. 2.
180
181   3.3.1    Q UESTION T YPES
182
      Yes-or-No Questions. The basic Yes-or-No questions are designed to prompt LMMs to make binary
183
      judgments on video quality queries, typically limited to the answers Yes or No. To address the
184   potential bias in LMMs that may skew towards yes responses, we employ a rigorous annotation
185   process. This process ensures that the distribution of correct answers, either Yes or No, remains
186   balanced at about 50%/50% ratio (see Appendix D). This balanced approach allows for a more
187   accurate assessment of LMMs’ performance on Yes-or-No questions.
188
      What-How Questions. The What-How questions are commonly utilized in benchmarks for LMMs.
189
      The What questions focus on identifying specific distortions (e.g., What is the most apparent dis-
190   tortion in this video?). On the other hand, the How questions are employed to distinguish the finer
191   details of distortion levels (e.g., How is the overall clarity of this video?). Including both What and
192   How questions allows Q-Bench-Video to thoroughly and meticulously evaluate LMMs’ ability on
193   identifying video distortions and evaluating the distortion levels.
194
      Open-ended Questions. It’s important to note that the two types of questions previously mentioned
195
      require LMMs to select the correct answer from a predefined set. However, in many real-world
196   scenarios, Open-ended Questions, which do not restrict responses to a predefined set, are often more
197   necessary and challenging for LMMs (e.g., What are the possible factors that lead to the low clarity
198   of this video? Please list and explain.). By adopting this form of questioning, we can better assess
199   an LMM’s ability to perceive video quality in real-world conditions.
200
201   3.3.2    Q UALITY C ONCERNS
202
203   It’s important to recognize that video quality can be influenced by multiple factors on some occa-
204   sions. Therefore, a query tuple (V,Q,A,C) does not need to be restricted to a single concern. It
205   can address multiple concerns simultaneously. For instance, the question Is this video clear and
206
      well-composed? can be seen as evaluating both technical and aesthetic quality understanding.
207   Technical Distortions. Technical distortions refer to the low-level degradation in video quality that
208   arises from the limitations of recording, compression, and transmission (Su et al., 2021; Ying et al.,
209   2021). These distortions often include artifacts such as blurring, noise, compression artifact, expo-
210   sure, etc., which are directly tied to the technical processes used in video production and delivery.
211   Aesthetic Distortions. Aesthetic distortions involve deviations from the intended visual style, artis-
212   tic design, or creative intent that negatively affect the viewer’s perception of the video (Wu et al.,
213   2022b; Huang et al., 2024). These distortions can include aspects such as confusing color, poor
214   composition, lighting inconsistencies, or distracting elements that reduce the overall aesthetic ap-
215   peal. Unlike technical distortions, aesthetic distortions are subjective and might be affected by
      viewer preference, cultural context, or artistic norms.

      Temporal Distortions. Temporal distortions are related to the degradation of visual quality over
271   time, impacting the fluidity and consistency of the video (Seshadrinathan & Bovik, 2009). These
272   distortions manifest as issues like screen shake, flickering, motion inconsistency, frame drops, and
273   stuttering that result from unstable shooting devices, dramatically changing lighting conditions, and
274   unstable bitrate environments (Wu et al., 2023b). Such disruptions hinder the viewer’s natural per-
275   ception of the video, leading to a disjointed and unpleasant viewing experience.
276
      AIGC Distortions. AIGC distortions pertain to imperfections and unnaturalness specifically aris-
277   ing from the generation of video content through AI models (Liu et al., 2024b; Zhang et al., 2024c).
278   These distortions may include unnatural textures, inconsistent lighting, uncanny facial features, or
279   unrealistic object behavior that result from limitations or biases in the training data, model archi-
280   tecture, or generative process. These distortions are unique to AI-generated content and require
281   specialized evaluation metrics that consider both the technical and perceptual quality aspects.
282
283   3.3.3    S INGLE V IDEOS & V IDEO PAIRS
284
285   Accurately comparing and jointly analyzing the quality of video pairs is sometimes more crucial
286   than assessing the quality of a single video, especially in scenarios such as performance tests in
287   video compression and quality control in video generation (In which it is more important to find out
288   Which video is better in visual quality and why?) (Zhu et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024d). Therefore, in
289   Q-Bench-Video, we include video quality queries for both single videos and video pairs.
290   Single Videos. Queries related to single videos can primarily be categorized into two types: a)
291   Global perception, which involves questions about the overall visual quality of the video, such as
292   How is the overall contrast of this video? b) Referring perception, which focuses on the visual
293   quality of specific elements within the video, like querying What is the most apparent distortion
294   when the player strikes the ball? Through these approaches, we aim to comprehensively evaluate
295   the LMMs’ ability to perceive both the overall and localized aspects of video quality.
296   Video Pairs. Firstly, to ensure that the comparison of video pairs is clear and meaningful, compar-
297   isons are only made between videos from the same source, such as videos from natural sources
298   being paired together while CG videos and AIGC videos are not being paired. There are mainly two
299   types of video pair categories: a) Joint analysis, which involves understanding the shared quality
300   features of the video pairs, for example, such as asking Are both videos blurry? b) Comparative
301   analysis, which involves comparing the quality dimension across two videos, such as How does the
302
      level of brightness in the first video compare to that in the second one? It is important to note that
      we further categorize comparisons based on the difference in quality labels of the videos involved
303
      into coarse-grain (with relatively more significant visual quality differences) and fine-grain (with
304
      relatively minor visual quality differences) comparisons. More details can be found in Appendix J.

307   3.3.4    Q UESTIONS & A NSWERS A NNOTATION
308
      The annotation process of Q-Bench-Video is conducted in a well-controlled laboratory environ-
309
      ment. A total of 8 experts are employed and trained to ensure the consistency of the annotations.
310   The experts are required to watch the videos in their entirety before making annotations. Each anno-
311   tated question-answer pair is then reviewed by at least three other experts to ensure its validity and
312   accuracy. The annotation details and GUI visualization are presented in Appendix C.

      3.4     B ENCHMARK S ETTING & E VALUATION
315
316
      Unless specifically stated otherwise, for Video LMMs we typically analyze by uniformly sampling 16
317
      frames from the video, while for Image LMMs, the sampling is reduced to 8 frames. For Yes-or-No
318   and What-How questions, if the LMMs can accurately respond with the options, we directly record
319   the accuracy of the responses as results. If the LMMs cannot provide option-based answers, we
320   implement a GPT-assisted evaluation strategy to help judge the accuracy of the answers. For Open-
321   ended questions, since the answers are open-ended and cannot be directly quantified for accuracy,
322   we also employ the GPT-assisted evaluation strategy. This involves GPT scoring the responses
323   based on their accuracy, completeness, and relevance compared to the annotated answer. Details
      about benchmark setting and evaluation can be found in the Appendix E and Appendix G.

466
      video pairs have more pronounced quality differences, than in the Compare-fine subcategory. This
467
      highlights that LMMs are more adept at comparing video quality than analyzing the quality of sin-
468   gle videos. This advantage in comparative assessment can be attributed to the inherent clarity in
469   pairwise comparisons, which provide explicit contrasts, as opposed to the more ambiguous nature
470   of evaluating a single video. Both humans and LMMs exhibit enhanced performance in comparative
471   tasks. Although there is still a significant accuracy gap between LMMs and humans, LMMs show
472   promising potential as effective tools for comparing video quality.

      5   C ONCLUSION
476
477
      In this paper, we introduce Q-Bench-Video, the first comprehensive benchmark explicitly designed
      to evaluate Large Multi-modal Models’ (LMMs) understanding of video quality. Our benchmark
478
      includes a diverse range of video types, questions that challenge multiple aspects of video quality,
479
      and a holistic evaluation framework encompassing Technical, Aesthetic, Temporal, and AIGC dis-
480   tortions. Through extensive experimentation with 17 open-source and proprietary LMMs, we find
481   that while LMMs show promise in discerning video quality, their performance remains significantly
482   below human-level understanding, especially when addressing Open-ended questions and AIGC-
483   specific distortions. These findings highlight the current limitations of LMMs in video quality per-
484   ception and underscore the need for further advancements in this area. By offering Q-Bench-Video,
485   we aim to stimulate future research and drive improvements in the field, ultimately bridging the gap
      between LMM and human video quality assessment capabilities.

      A    S OURCE V IDEO DATASET I NTRODUCTION
757
758
      In this section, we briefly introduce the video quality assessment (VQA) datasets as follows:
759
760          • LSVQ (Ying et al., 2021): The LSVQ dataset is currently the largest VQA dataset, com-
761            prising over 39,000 real-world videos and 5.5 million human perceptual quality annota-
762            tions. It primarily focuses on both spatial and temporal aspects of technical visual quality.
763          • MaxWell (Wu et al., 2023b): The MaxWell dataset presents a comprehensive subjective
764            study, gathering over two million human opinions on 13 distinct quality factors across 4,543
765            in-the-wild natural scene videos. These quality factors include technical aspects (sharp-
766            ness, focus, noise, motion blur, flicker, exposure, compression artifacts, fluency) as well as
767            aesthetic aspects (content appeal, composition, color, lighting, and camera trajectory).
768          • WaterlooSQoE-III (Duanmu et al., 2018): The WaterlooSQoE-III dataset comprises 20
769            RAW HD reference videos and 450 simulated streaming videos. To generate meaningful
770            and representative test videos, a series of DASH video streaming experiments are con-
771            ducted, capturing relevant streaming activities and reconstructing the streaming sessions
772            using video processing tools. The WaterlooSQoE-III dataset primarily focuses on assess-
773
               ing the quality of experience (QoE) in streaming video.
774          • WaterlooSQoE-IV (Duanmu et al., 2020): The WaterlooSQoE-IV dataset is currently
775            the largest subject-rated VQA dataset for quality of experience, featuring 1,350 adaptive
776            streaming videos. These videos are generated from a diverse range of source content, video
777
               encoders, network conditions, adaptive bitrate (ABR) algorithms, and viewing devices.
778          • T2VQA-DB (Kou et al., 2024): The T2VQA-DB dataset utilizes 9 different video gener-
779            ation models to create 10,000 AIGC videos. A total of 27 subjects are invited to assess
780
               the perceptual quality of each video, focusing on two main aspects: text-video alignment
               and video fidelity. Text-video alignment refers to how well the generated video content
781
               corresponds to the given text description, while video fidelity encompasses factors such as
782
               distortion, saturation, motion consistency, and content coherence.
783
784
             • VideoFeedback (He et al., 2024b): The VideoFeedback dataset contains human-provided
               multi-aspect scores for 37.6K synthesized videos generated by 11 different video generative
785
               models. The scores assess various aspects, including visual quality, temporal consistency,
786
               dynamic realism, text-to-video alignment, and factual consistency.
787
             • LIVE-YT-Gaming (Yu et al., 2023): The LIVE-YT-Gaming dataset consists of 600 real
788
               user-generated gaming videos. A subjective human study is conducted on this dataset,
789
               resulting in 18,600 quality ratings provided by 61 participants. The primary focus of the
790            study is on evaluating the visual quality of the videos.
791
792   We mainly sample the videos from these VQA datasets for the following reasons: 1) The VQA
793   datasets mentioned above feature well-designed video selection processes and rigorous human anno-
794   tation standards. The quality labels can help us control the quality distribution of Q-Bench-Video.
795   2) Moreover, these datasets are mostly focused on quality issues (close to low-level information)
796   and therefore usually isolated from high-level multi-modal video datasets. Thus sampling videos
797
      from VQA datasets can help prevent overlap with pre-training data used by LMMs to minimize the
      possibility of data leakage. As a result, these VQA datasets are well-suited to serve as sources for
798
      Q-Bench-Video, contributing to a more robust and accurate evaluation.

       In this section, we focus on discussing how to categorize the annotations into Compare-fine and
1245   Compare-coarse classifications. We collect videos from the VQA dataset that already have annotated
1246   quality scores. Since our comparisons are confined to video pairs from the same VQA dataset source,
1247   the quality scores between video pairs are valid and meaningful. Within single VQA dataset, we
1248   randomly select video pairs without repetition, and then rank all video pairs based on the differences
1249   in their quality scores. A median value is then chosen as the threshold. Pairs with a difference
1250   exceeding this threshold are labeled as Compare-coarse, while those with a difference below it are
1251   labeled as Compare-fine. The pseudocode for this procedure is detailed in Algorithm 1.

1254
1255   Limitations. 1) Subjectivity in Evaluation: Although the benchmark includes efforts to minimize
1256   subjective bias by using expert annotations, aesthetic aspects such as visual appeal and composition
1257   inherently involve subjective judgments. Even among trained experts, there might be variations in
1258   opinions on what constitutes high or low aesthetic quality. 2) Rapid Evolution of AIGC Distortions:
1259   The benchmark includes evaluation specifically tailored to AIGC distortions. However, given the
1260   fast-paced advancements in AI-generated video technology, future generations of generative models
1261   may produce fewer visible distortions or entirely new types of artifacts. This implies that the current
1262
       version of Q-Bench-Video might partly become outdated in the future.
1263   Social Impact. By focusing on video quality understanding, this benchmark encourages the devel-
1264   opment of LMMs that can discern not only the semantic content but also the technical and aesthetic
1265   quality of videos. This has broad applications, from improving video compression algorithms to en-
1266   hancing user experience in media platforms. Ultimately, Q-Bench-Video could lead to the creation
1267   of better tools for optimizing video quality across diverse industries.
