009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Auction-based Federated Learning (AFL) has emerged as an important research
012            field in recent years. The prevailing strategies for FL data consumers (DCs) assume
013            that the entire team of the required data owners (DOs) for an FL task must be assem-
014            bled before training can commence. In practice, a DC can trigger the FL training
015            process multiple times. DOs can thus be gradually recruited over multiple FL
016            model training sessions. Existing bidding strategies for AFL DCs are not designed
017            to handle such scenarios. Therefore, the problem of multi-session AFL remains
018
               open. To address this problem, we propose the Multi-session Budget Optimization
               Strategy for forward Auction-based Federated Learning (MultiBOS-AFL). Based
019
               on hierarchical reinforcement learning, MultiBOS-AFL jointly optimizes inter-
020
               session budget pacing and intra-session bidding for AFL DCs, with the objective
021            of maximizing the total utility. Extensive experiments on six benchmark datasets
022            show that it significantly outperforms seven state-of-the-art approaches. On aver-
023            age, MultiBOS-AFL achieves 12.28% higher utility, 14.52% more data acquired
024            through auctions for a given budget, and 1.23% higher test accuracy achieved by
025            the resulting FL model compared to the best baseline. To the best of our knowledge,
026            it is the first budget optimization decision support method with budget pacing
027            capability designed for DCs in multi-session forward auction-based FL.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032
      Federated Learning (FL) Yang et al. (2019; 2020); Goebel et al. (2023) has emerged as a useful
      collaborative machine learning (ML) paradigm. In contrast to the traditional ML paradigm, FL
033
      enables collaborative model training without the need to expose local data, thereby enhancing data
034
      privacy and user confidentiality. Prevailing FL methods often assume that data owners (DOs, a.k.a, FL
035   clients) are ready to join FL tasks by helping data consumers (DCs, a.k.a, FL servers) train models. In
036   practice, this assumption might not always hold due to DOs’ self-interest and trade-off considerations.
037   To deal with this issue, the domain of auction-based federated learning (AFL) has emerged Jiao et al.
038   (2019); Deng et al. (2021); Zhang et al. (2021).
039
      As shown in Fig. 1, the main actors in AFL include the auctioneer, DOs and DCs. The auctioneer
040
      functions as an intermediary, facilitating the flow of asking prices from DOs and DCs. DCs then
041   determine their bid prices to be submitted to the auctioneer. The auctioneer then consolidates the
042   auction outcomes and informs the DOs and DCs about the match-making results. The auctioneer
043   undertakes a pivotal role in orchestrating the entire auction process, managing information dissemina-
044   tion, and ultimately determining the auction winners. Once FL teams have been established through
045   auctions, they can carry out collaborative model training following standard FL protocols.
046
      AFL methods can be divided into three categories Tang et al. (2024b;a): 1) data owner-oriented
047   (DO-oriented), 2) auctioneer-oriented, and 3) data consumer-oriented (DC-oriented). DO-oriented
048   AFL methods focus on helping DOs determine the amount of resources to commit to FL tasks, and set
049   their respective reserve prices for profit maximization. Auctioneer-oriented AFL methods investigate
050   how to optimally match DOs with DCs as well as provide the necessary governance oversight to
051   ensure desirable operational objectives can be achieved (e.g., fairness, social cost minimization). DC-
052   oriented AFL methods examine how to help DCs select which DOs to bid and for how much, in order
053   to optimize key performance indicators (KPIs) within budget constraints, possibly in competition
      with other DCs.

067   can recruit DOs over multiple training sessions. This is especially useful in continual FL Yoon
068   et al. (2021) settings where DOs’ local data are continuously updated over time. Existing AFL
069   approaches designed to optimize KPIs within a single auctioning session cannot be directly applied
070   in multi-session AFL scenarios, especially in scenarios with multiple DCs competing to bid for DOs
071   from a common pool of candidates. This is primarily due to the limitation that they are unable to
072   perform budget pacing, which pertains to the strategic dispersion of a limited overall budget across
073   multiple AFL sessions to achieve optimal KPIs over a given time frame.
074   To bridge this important gap, we propose a first-of-its-kind Multi-session Budget Optimization
075   Strategy for forward Auction-based Federated Learning (MultiBOS-AFL). It is designed to em-
076   power a DC with the ability to dynamically allocate its limited budget over multiple AFL DO recruit-
077   ment sessions, and then optimize the distribution of budget for each session among DOs through
078   effective bidding. The ultimate goal is to maximize the DC’s winning utility. MultiBOS-AFL is
079   grounded in Hierarchical Reinforcement Learning (HRL) Pateria et al. (2021) to effectively deal with
080
      the intricate decision landscape and the absence of readily available analytical remedies. Specifically,
      MultiBOS-AFL consists of two agents for each DC: 1) the Inter-Session Budget Pacing Agent
081
      (InterBPA), and 2) the Intra-Session Bidding Agent (IntraBMA). For each auctioning session,
082
      each DC’s InterBPA opportunistically determines how much of the total budget shall be spent in
083   this session based on jointly considering the quantity and quality of the currently available candidate
084   DOs, as well as bidding outcomes from previous sessions. Then, the DC’s IntraBMA determines
085   the bid price for each data resource offered by DOs in the AFL market within the session budget.
086
      To the best of our knowledge, MultiBOS-AFL is the first budget optimization decision support
087
      method with budget pacing capability designed for DCs in multi-session forward auction-based
088
      federated learning. Extensive experiments on six benchmark datasets show that it significantly
089   outperforms seven state-of-the-art approaches. On average, MultiBOS-AFL achieves 12.28%
090   higher utility, 14.52% more data acquired through auctions for a given budget, and 1.23% higher test
091   accuracy achieved by the resulting FL model compared to the best baseline.

094   2    R ELATED W ORK
095
096   Existing methods for DC-oriented issues can be further divided into two subcategories: i) reverse
097   auction-based methods, and ii) forward auction-based methods.
098   Reverse Auction-based Methods: Developed primarily for monopoly AFL markets where there is
099   only one DC facing multiple DOs, reverse auction-based methods Deng et al. (2021); Zhang et al.
100   (2021); Jiao et al. (2020); Zeng et al. (2020); Ying et al. (2020); Le et al. (2020; 2021); Roy et al.
101   (2021); Zhang et al. (2022); Zhang, Jingwen and Wu, Yuezhou and Pan, Rong (2022); Tan & Yu
102   (2023) address the challenge of DO selection through reverse auctions. The key idea of these methods
103   is to optimally resolve the DO selection problem, targeting the maximization of KPIs specific to the
104
      target DC. Particularly relevant in scenarios where disparate DOs vie for the attention of a sole DC,
      these methods have progressed by integrating diverse mechanisms such as graph neural networks,
105
      blockchains, and reputation assessment.
106
107   Forward Auction-based Methods: These methods are designed for situations where multiple DCs
      compete for the same pool of DOs Tang & Yu (2023b). The key idea of these methods lies in


                                                                 2

108
      determining the optimal bidding strategy for DCs. The goal is to maximize model-specific key
109   performance indicators. A notable example is Fed-Bidder Tang & Yu (2023b) which assists DCs
110   to determine their bids for DOs. It leverages a wealth of auction-related insights, encompassing
111   aspects like DOs’ data distributions and suitability to the task, DCs’ success probabilities in ongoing
112   auctions and budget constraints. However, this method ignores the complex relationships among
113   DCs, which are both competitive and cooperative. To deal with this issue, Tang & Yu (2023a) models
114   the AFL ecosystem as a multi-agent system to steer DCs to bid strategically toward an equilibrium
115   with desirable overall system characteristics.
116   MultiBOS-AFL falls into the forward auction-based methods category. Distinct from existing
117   methods which focus on optimizing the objectives within a single auctioning session, it is designed to
118   solve the problem of multi-session AFL budget optimization.

      3    P RELIMINARIES
121
122
      AFL Market: Generally, an AFL market consists of three types of participants Tang et al. (2024b):
123
      1) Data Owners (DOs): entities possessing potentially sensitive yet valuable data, who are willing
124   to share or sell access to their data resources for FL task training in exchange for appropriate
125   compensation. 2) Data Consumers (DCs): organizations or individuals requiring data to train their
126   machine learning models via FL. 3)Auctioneer: a trusted third-party entity orchestrating the auction
127   process between DOs and DCs. It facilitates the exchange of data resources for FL training tasks
128   through an auction mechanism, such as the Second-Price Sealed-Bid (SPSB) auction.
129
      When a DO is ready to offer its services for FL task training, it notifies the auctioneer, specifying its
130   bid request and the reserve price.1 The auctioneer then announces the auction to all DCs currently
131   participating in the AFL market. Any DC whose required the corresponding data resources aligns
132   with the DO’s offering submits a bid for the auction.
133
      Multi-Session Budget Constrained AFL Bidding: During the course of FL model training, a DC
134
      can initiate the FL training procedure (i.e., a training session) on multiple occasions, with the aim of
135
      recruiting DOs to improve model performance. Consider the scenario of multiple banks engaging in
136   FL. The dynamic nature of user data within these banks sets in motion a perpetual cycle of updates,
137   with continually refreshed data stored locally by each bank. As a result, these banks systematically
138   engage in repeated sessions of federated model training periodically, during which the standard FL
139   training protocol is followed. Let S denote the number of training sessions for the target DC, who has
140   a budget B for all training sessions [S]. In each FL training session s (s ∈ [S]), there are Cs available
141   qualified DOs, which can help train the FL model of the target DC. Each DO i ∈ [Cs ] possesses a
                                        |D |
142   private dataset Di = {(xj , yj )}j=1i .
143
      Following Tang & Yu (2023b), we assume that each DO i become gradually available over time.
144
      Each DO i can trigger the following auction process: 1) Bid Request Initiation: DO i ∈ [Cs ]
145   generates a bid request about itself (e.g., identity, data quantity, etc.) and sends it along with the
146   the reserve price (i.e., the lowest price it is willing to accept for selling the corresponding resources
147   Vincent (1995)) to the auctioneer. 2) Bid Request Dissemination: The auctioneer disseminates the
148   received bid request to the relevant DCs whose FL tasks are relevant to the data resources of the DO
149   being auctioned. 3) Bidding Response: Each relevant DC evaluates the potential value and cost of
150   the received bid request, and decides on a bid price based on its bidding strategy. The DCs submit
151   their bids to the auctioneer. When a DC has exhausted its budget, it will forfeit future auctions. 4)
152   Outcome Determination: Upon receiving bids from relevant DCs, the auctioneer determines the
153
      winning price based on an auction mechanism. It then compares the winning price with the reserve
      price set by each DO. If the winning price is lower than the reserve price, the auctioneer terminates
154
      the auction and informs the DO to initiate another auction for the same resources. Otherwise, the
155
      auctioneer informs the winning DC about the cost (i.e., the winning price) it needs to pay, informs
156   the losing DCs, and informs the DO about the winning DC it shall join.
157
158   When the auctioning process for session s has been completed or the DC has exhausted its budget, it
159
      initiates FL model training with the recruited DOs. Each DC pays the corresponding market prices to
      the DOs it has recruited.

196   4    T HE P ROPOSED MultiBOS-AFL A PPROACH
197
198   Our primary objective is to help DCs recruit DOs across multiple sessions while adhering to budget
199
      constraints, with the overarching goal of maximizing the total utility. To accomplish this, we must
      tackle two fundamental challenges: 1) Budget Allocation: Determining the allocation of the total
200
      budget B to a given session s, Bs ; 2) Bidding Strategy: Determining the bid price bis for any given
201
      DO i in session s under the session budget Bs . Since the AFL market is highly dynamic, it is difficult
202   for DCs to obtain a closed-form analytical solution for the above two problems. Therefore, we design
203   MultiBOS-AFL based on RL Sutton & Barto (2018) to solve these problems without requiring
204   prior knowledge.
205

206
      objective outlined in Eq. equation 1, we design MultiBOS-AFL based on HRL Pateria et al.
207
      (2021). It consists of two HRL-based budget allocation agents: 1) Inter-session Budget Pacing Agent
208   (InterBPA), and 2) Intra-session Bidding Agent (IntraBMA). An overview of MultiBOS-AFL
209   is shown in Figure 2.

      5.1       E XPERIMENT S ETTINGS
338
339   Dataset: The performance assessment of MultiBOS-AFL is conducted on the following six widely-
340   adopted datasets in federated learning studies: 1) MNIST3 , 2) CIFAR-104 , 3) Fashion-MNIST (i.e.,
341   FMNIST) Xiao et al. (2017), 4) EMNIST-digits (i.e., EMNISTD), 5) EMNIST-letters (i.e., EMNISTL)
342   Cohen et al. (2017) and 6) Kuzushiji-MNIST (i.e., KMNIST) Clanuwat et al. (2018). The FL models
343   used are the same as those employed in Tang & Yu (2023b).
344   Comparison Approaches: We evaluate the performance of MultiBOS-AFL against the following
345   seven AFL bidding approaches in our experiments: Constant Bid (Const) Zhang et al. (2014),
346   Randomly Generated Bid (Rand) Zhang et al. (2021); Zhang, Jingwen and Wu, Yuezhou and Pan,
347   Rong (2022), Below Max Utility Bid (Bmub), Linear-Form Bid (Lin) Perlich et al. (2012), Bidding
348   Machine (BM) Ren et al. (2017), , Reinforcement Learning-based Bid (RLB) Tang, Xiaoli and Yu,
349   Han (2023), FedBidder-sim (FBs), and Fed-Bidder-com (FBc) Tang & Yu (2023b). Details can be
350   found in Appendix A.3.
351   Experiment Scenarios: We compare MultiBOS-AFL with baselines under two main experiment
352   scenarios with each containing 10,000 DOs: 1) IID data, varying dataset sizes, without noise: In
353   this scenario, the sizes of datasets owned by various DOs are randomly generated, ranging from 500
354   to 5,000 samples. Additionally, all the data are independent and identically distributed (IID), with no
355   noise. 2) Non-IID data, with noise: In this experimental scenario, we deliberately introduce data
356   heterogeneity by adjusting the class distribution among individual DOs. Following the methodology
357   outlined in Shi & Yu (2023), we implement the following Non-IID setup. We designate 1 class (on
358
      datasets other than EMNISTL) or 6 classes (on EMNISTL) as the minority class and assign this
      minority class to 100 DOs. As a result, these 100 DOs possess images for all classes, while all other
359
      DOs exclusively have images for the remaining nine classes, excluding the minority class. In this
360
      experiment scenario, each DO holds 3,000 images. Additionally, we simulate scenarios in which the
361   minority DOs contain 10% or 25% noisy data.
362
363   To evaluate the effectiveness of MultiBOS-AFL, we create nine DCs, each utilizing one of the
364
      aforementioned bidding approaches to join the auction for each bid request (i.e., each DO) in
      each session s. Following Tang & Yu (2023b), bid requests are delivered in chronological order.
365
      Upon receiving a bid request, each DC derives its bid price based on its adopted bidding strategy.
366
      Subsequently, the auctioneer gathers the bid prices, identifies the winner, and determines the market
367   price using the SPSB auction mechanism. The winning DC pays the market price to the DO. The
368   process concludes when there are no more bid requests or when the budget is depleted.
369
370
      MultiBOS-AFL utilizes fully connected neural networks with three hidden layers each containing
      64 nodes to generate bid prices for a target DO on behalf of their respective DCs. The replay buffer
371
      D of both the InterBPA and the IntraBMA are set to 5,000. During training, both agents explore
372
      the environment using an ϵ-greedy policy with an annealing rate from 1.0 to 0.05. In updating both
373   Qintra and Qinter , 64 tuples uniformly sampled from D are used for each training step, and the
374   corresponding target networks are updated once every 20 steps. In our experiments, we use RMSprop
375   with a learning rate of 0.0005 to train all neural networks, and set the discount factor γ to 1. In

468     Lin and Bmub typically outperform Const and Rand due to the use of utility in the bidding process.
469     However, Bmub is less effective than Lin due to the reliance on randomness. Meanwhile, the more
470     advanced methods BM, FBs, FBc, RLB and MultiBOS-AFL perform significantly better than the
471     simpler approaches. This is largely due to the inclusion of auction records (including auction history
472     and bidding records) and the use of advanced learning methods.
473
       RLB and MultiBOS-AFL both outperform BM, FBs, and FBc, due to their ability of adaptive
474    adjustment to the highly dynamic auction environment. While BM does consider market price
475    distribution, it derives this distribution by learning the prediction of each bid request’s market price
476    density, which may lead to overfitting. In contrast, FBs and FBc obtain the market price distribution
477    via a predefined winning function, which helps predict the expected bid costs more accurately.
478    However, BM, FBs and FB are still static bidding strategies. They are essentially represented
479    by linear or non-linear functions whose parameters are derived from historical auction data using
480    heuristic techniques. Subsequently, these parameters are applied to new auctions, even if the dynamics
481    of these new auctions may vary significantly from those in the historical data. The inherent dynamism
482
       of the AFL market poses a considerable challenge for these static bidding methods, making it hard
       for them to consistently achieve desired outcomes in subsequent auctions.
483
484    While RLB employs dynamic programming to optimize its bidding process, it is susceptible to the
485    drawback of immediate reward setting, which might result in indiscriminate bidding for data samples
       without considering their associated costs. This issue is effectively addressed by MultiBOS-AFL.

531   6   C ONCLUSIONS
532
533
      In this paper, we propose the Multi-session Budget Optimization Strategy for forward Auction-based
534   FL (MultiBOS-AFL). It is designed to empower FL DCs with the ability to strategically allocate
535   budgets over multiple FL training sessions and judiciously distribute the budget among DOs within
536   each session by bidding with different bid prices, in order to maximize total utility. Based on the
537   hierarchical reinforcement learning, MultiBOS-AFL jointly optimizes inter-session budget pacing
538   and intra-session bidding for DCs in the AFL ecosystem. To the best of our knowledge, it is the first
539   budget optimization decision support method with budget pacing capability designed for DCs in
      multi-session forward auction-based FL.

725   A.2     R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING BASICS
726
727
      A Markov Decision Process (MDP) is a mathematical framework for modeling decision-making
      in which an agent interacts with an environment through discrete time steps. MDP is formally
728
      defined by the tuple ⟨S, A, P, R, γ⟩: 1) S represents the possible states in the environment, denoted
729
      as s ∈ S. 2) A encompasses the feasible actions the agent can take. 3) P : S × A × S → [0, 1] is the
730   transition probability function for the likelihood of transitioning between states when an action is
731   taken, capturing environmental dynamics. 4) R : S × A × S → R is the reward function, specifying
732   immediate rewards upon state transitions due to specific actions, with the agent’s aim to maximize
733   cumulative rewards. 5) γ ∈ [0, 1] serves as the discount factor, reflecting the agent’s preference for
734   immediate rewards versus future rewards.
735
      During the MDP process, the agent interacts with the environment across discrete time steps. At each
736   time step, it selects an action a ∈ A based on policy π : S → A, subsequently receiving a reward r,
737   and the environment undergoes state transitions according to P .
738
739
      The goal of MDP is to identify an optimal policy hπ : S → A that   i maximizes the expected sum
                                                          PT       t−1 t
740   of discounted rewards over time, given by maxπ E       t=1 γ    r . This entails finding the policy
741   maximizing expected cumulative rewards. The value function V π : S → R is associated with each
742   policy, quantifying expected cumulative rewards. The optimal value function V ∗ : S → R represents
743   the maximum achievable expected cumulative reward achievable with the best policy from each state.

745   A.3     C OMPARISON A PPROACHES
746
747          1. Constant Bid (Const) Zhang et al. (2014): An DC presents the same bid for all DOs,
748             whereas the bids offered by different DCs can vary.
749          2. Randomly Generated Bid (Rand) Zhang et al. (2021); Zhang, Jingwen and Wu, Yuezhou
750             and Pan, Rong (2022): This approach, commonly found in AFL, involves DCs randomly
751             generating bids from a predefined range for each bid request.
752          3. Below Max Utility Bid (Bmub): This approach is derived from the concept of bidding
753             below max eCPC Lee et al. (2012) in online advertisement auctioning. It defines the utility
754             of each bid request from a DO as the upper limit of the bid values offered by DCs. Therefore,
755             for each bid request, the bid price is randomly generated within the range between 0 and
                this upper bound.


                                                          14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
            4. Linear-Form Bid (Lin) Perlich et al. (2012): This strategy generates bid values which
757            are directly proportional to the estimated utility of the bid requests, typically expressed as
758            bLin (v i ) = λLin v i .
759
            5. Bidding Machine (BM) Ren et al. (2017): Commonly used in online advertisement auction-
760
               ing, especially in real-time bidding, this method focuses on maximizing a specific buyer’s
761
               profit by optimizing outcome prediction, cost estimation, and the bidding strategy.
762
763         6. Fed-Bidder Tang & Yu (2023b): This bidding method is specifically designed for DCs in
764
               AFL settings. It guides them to competitively bid for DOs to maximize their utility. It has
               two variants, one with a simple winning function, referred to as Fed-Bidder-sim (FBs); and
765
               the other with a complex winning function, referred to as Fed-Bidder-com (FBc).
766
767         7. Reinforcement Learning-based Bid (RLB) Tang, Xiaoli and Yu, Han (2023): It regards
768            the bidding process as a reinforcement learning problem, utilizing an MDP framework to
769
               learn the most effective bidding policy for an individual buyer to enhance the auctioning
               outcomes.

772   A.4   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
773
      In our experiments, we faced the challenge of not having a publicly available AFL bidding behaviour
774
      dataset. To address this issue, we track the behaviors of DCs over time during simulations to gradually
775   accumulate data in four different settings. Each setting contains 160 DCs who adopted one of the
776   eight bidding strategies listed in the Compared Approaches section.
777
778
      In the first setting, each of the eight baseline bidding methods is adopted by one eighth of the DCs.
      In the second setting, as BM, Fed-Bidder variants (FBs and FBc) and RLB have AI techniques
779
      similar to MultiBOS-AFL, these four bidding strategies are adopted by three sixteenths of the total
780
      population, while the remaining four baselines are adopted by one sixteenth of the total population.
781   In the third and fourth settings, as both Fed-Bidder variants and MultiBOS-AFL are designed
782   specifically for AFL, we set the percentage of DCs adopting FBs and FBc to be higher than those
783   adopting the other six baselines. Specifically, under the third setting, 50 DCs adopt FBs and FBc,
784   while 10 DCs adopt each of the other six baselines. Under the fourth setting, 65 DCs adopted FBs
785   and FBc, while 5 DCs adopted each of the other six baselines. We adopt the second-price sealed-bid
786   (SPSB) auction mechanism in our experiments. By tracking the behaviors of DCs over time, we
787   can gradually accumulate data in the absence of a publicly available dataset related to AFL bidding
788   behaviours.

790   A.5   E VALUATION M ETRICS
791
792
      To evaluate the effectiveness of all the comparison methods, we adopt the following three metrics:
      1) The number of data samples won by the DC (#data) is defined as the cumulative number of data
793
      samples owned by all DOs recruited by the corresponding DC until the budget or session limits are
794
      reached. 2) The utility obtained by the DC (utility) is defined as the cumulative reputation of DOs
795   recruited by the corresponding DC until the budget or session limits are reached. 3) The test accuracy
796   (Acc) is determined as the accuracy of the final FL model for the respective DC, up to the point where
797   either the budget or session limits are reached.
