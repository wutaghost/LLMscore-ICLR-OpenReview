009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Knowledge Graph Completion (KGC) attempts to predict missing facts in a
012            Knowledge Graph (KG). Recently, there’s been an increased focus on designing
013            KGC methods that can excel in the inductive setting, where a portion or all of the
014            entities and relations seen in inference are unobserved during training. Numerous
015            benchmark datasets have been proposed for inductive KGC, all of which are sub-
016            sets of existing KGs used for transductive KGC. However, we find that the current
017            procedure for constructing inductive KGC datasets inadvertently creates a shortcut
018
               that can be exploited even while disregarding the relational information. Specifi-
               cally, we observe that the Personalized PageRank (PPR) score can achieve strong
019
               or near SOTA performance on most datasets. In this paper, we study the root cause
020
               of this problem. Using these insights, we propose an alternative strategy for con-
021            structing inductive KGC datasets that helps mitigate the PPR shortcut. We then
022            benchmark multiple popular methods using the newly constructed datasets and
023            analyze their performance. The new benchmark datasets help promote a better
024            understanding of the capabilities and challenges of inductive KGC by removing
025            any shortcuts that obfuscate performance.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Knowledge Graph Completion (KGC) attempts to predict unseen facts given an existing knowledge
031   graph (KG). KGC has numerous applications including drug discovery Zeng et al. (2022), personal-
032   ized medicine Chandak et al. (2023), and recommendations Wang et al. (2021). Traditionally, most
033
      research on KGC was focused on the transductive setting, where the same sets of entities and rela-
      tions are seen during training and testing. Most methods Bordes et al. (2013); Trouillon et al. (2016);
034
      Schlichtkrull et al. (2018) generally focus on learning embeddings for all entities and relations to
035
      facilitate the prediction of new facts.
036
037   In recent years, interest in KGC has shifted towards designing methods that can generalize to new
038   entities or relations not seen during training. This task, known as “inductive KGC”, requires a
039
      method to train on a graph Gtrain and perform inference on a different graph Ginf , where the inference
      graph contains either new entities, relations, or both. Because of this, methods for inductive KGC
040
      don’t rely on fixed embeddings for entities or relations, instead opting for more flexible techniques
041
      that can inductively learn representations based on a given graph Teru et al. (2020); Zhu et al. (2021);
042   Lee et al. (2023). To asses the ability of methods for this task, new datasets have been constructed
043   that require methods to reason inductively. All inductive datasets Teru et al. (2020); Galkin et al.
044   (2022); Lee et al. (2023) are constructed from existing transductive KGC datasets. This is done
045   by sampling two graphs, one each for train and inference, which contain disjoint entities. Multiple
046   methods Zhu et al. (2021); Zhang & Yao (2022); Lee et al. (2023) have reported tremendous promise
047   on these newer benchmark datasets.
048   However, we find that on almost all inductive datasets, we can achieve competitive performance
049   by using the Personalized PageRank Page et al. (1999) (PPR) score to perform inference. We
050   note that PPR is a non-learnable heuristic and ignores the relational information in the graph. In
051   Figure 1, we compare the performance of PPR against the supervised SOTA performance on both
052   inductive and transductive datasets. We can see that when performing KGC on inference graphs
053   with either new entities (E) or new entities and relations (E, R), PPR performs only roughly 25%
      worse than SOTA. However, this is generally not true for transductive datasets, where PPR usu-

103   2    BACKGROUND AND R ELATED W ORK
104
105   Throughout this study we denote a knowledge graph as G = {V, R, E} where V are the set of
106   entities (i.e., nodes), R the set of relations (i.e., edge types), and E the set of edges (i.e., triples) of
107   the form (s, r, o) where s and o are entities and r a relation. Lastly, we note that the task of KGC is
      formulated as the following where given a query (s, r, ∗), we attempt to predict the correct entity ∗.

108
      Inductive KGC Datasets: In inductive KGC, we are given a training graph Gtrain =
109   {Vtrain , Rtrain , Etrain } and an inference graph Ginf = {Vinf , Rinf , Einf }. A method is trained on Gtrain
110   and evaluated on Ginf . Most datasets consider the setting that is only disjoint on the entities such that
111   Rinf ⊆ Rtrain and Einf ∩ Etrain = ∅. This setting is referred to as the (E) setting. Another setting,
112   which we denote as (E, R) further allows Rinf to contain relations not in Rtrain .
113
      All existing inductive datasets are sampled from existing transductive datasets. Furthermore, the
114
      majority of inductive datasets Teru et al. (2020); Lee et al. (2023) are further created via the same
115   procedure introduced by Teru et al. (2020). We now give a brief overview of this procedure. Given a
116   transductive dataset, which we denote as G, k seed entities are randomly chosen from G. The 2-hop
117   neighborhood is then extracted around each individual seed entity. To prevent exponential growth,
118   the number of neighbors sampled at any hop is capped at 50 for each seed entity. The resulting
119   edges are then combined to create Gtrain and are subsequently removed from the original graph. The
120   inference graph is then sampled in a similar manner from the resulting graph G \Gtrain . One exception
121   to this procedure are the ILPC datasets, introduced by Galkin et al. (2022), which instead sample
122   p% of the nodes from G and use them to create Gtrain . The rest of the nodes are then used to construct
123
      Ginf . In practice, we find that both methods tend to produce similar graphs.
124   Inductive KGC Methods: NeurlLP Yang et al. (2017) and DRUM Sadeghian et al. (2019) consider
125   combining the path representations of different length between both entities in a triple. However,
126   since they explicitly consider each path, they are often limited to only considering paths of up to
127   length 2 or 3. Conditional MPNNs Huang et al. (2024) are a more efficient alternative to encoding
128   higher-order path information. They work by conditioning the message passing mechanism on the
129
      known entity, allowing the implicit aggregation of all paths up to a length L (which is equal to the
      number of GNN layers). The value of L is typically 5/6. Prominent examples include NBFNet Zhu
130
      et al. (2021) and RED-GNN Zhang & Yao (2022). More scalable alternatives have been proposed
131
      that prune the propagated messages Zhu et al. (2022); Zhang et al. (2023); Shomer et al. (2023b).
132   NodePiece Galkin et al. (2021) is concerned with parameter efficiency, using an anchor-based ap-
133   proach to learn a more compact set of entity and relation representations. All previous methods
134   assume that a fixed set of relations exist between the train and inference graphs. To account for new
135   relations in inference, InGRAM Lee et al. (2023) introduces the concept of a “relation graph”, which
136   inductively encodes the representation of each relation. Gao et al. (2023) introduces the concept of
137   “double permutation- equivariant representations” as a way to model KGs that are equivariant to
138   permutations of both the entities and relations. They theoretically show that capturing this property
139   is essential for proper generalization across KGs. To this point these introduce a new methodIS-
140
      DEA/ISDEA+ that can satisfy this property. They further introduce a variant of InGRAM Lee et al.
      (2023), DEq-InGram, that endows it with the ability to compute double equivariant representations.
141
      Note that we omit subgraph methods that have been to shown to prohibitively expensive Teru et al.
142
      (2020); Liu et al. (2021); Mai et al. (2021); Xu et al. (2022); Geng et al. (2023) or those that require
143   the use of external textual information Gesese et al. (2022); Daza et al. (2021).
144
145   Personalized PageRank: PageRank Page et al. (1999) computes the probability of finishing a ran-
146
      dom walk of arbitrary length at some node u, when there is equal probability of beginning the walk
      at any node in the graph. Personalized PageRank (PPR) Page et al. (1999) is a version of PageRank
147
      that is “personalized" to some root node s, where at each step there is a probability α of teleporting
148
      to s. The set of PPR scores for a root node s is given by prs ∈ R|V| and can be formulated as the
149
      weighted sum of all random walk probabilities between two nodes Chung (2007):

      In this section, we examine the performance on common inductive KGC datasets. We first show that
185
      the Personalized PageRank (PPR) can often serve as a good baseline on most datasets. From this
186
      observation, we attempt to answer two important questions: (1) When can the PPR scores perform
187   well on KGC? and (2) Why can PPR sometimes perform well for KGC?

189
      3.1    P ERSONALIZED PAGERANK (PPR) IS A S TRONG BASELINE FOR I NDUCTIVE KGC
190
191
      We begin by obtaining the PPR matrix of the inference graph for use in evaluation. See Section 2 for
192   more details on how this is done. Given this new graph, for a query (s, r, ∗), we calculate the PPR
193   score for all possible entities o ∈ V. Using these scores, we can obtain the rank of the true entity for
194   our query. We emphasize that PPR is (a) a non-learnable heuristic, (b) ignores the relations in the
195   KG, and (c) has no basis in KG literature as a method to perform KGC.
196
      In Figure 1, we show the performance when using the PPR versus the SOTA performance among
197
      supervised methods. The SOTA method is dataset dependent, with the specific methods listed in
198   Appendix A.1. We split the datasets by transductive, (E), and (E, R) inductive. We include those
199   datasets most often used in each task, comprising 25 in total. Please see Appendix B.1 for more on
200   the datasets chosen. The full set of results can also be found in Appendix A.1.
201
      We observe that on both types of inductive datasets, the PPR score does reasonably well, perform-
202
      ing on average only 25-29% less than SOTA. This is surprising as PPR is both non-learnable and
203
      ignores the relational aspect of the KG. We also find that on some datasets like the WN or ILPC
204   inductive splits, the performance nearly matches or exceeds the supervised performance. On the
205   other hand, for the transductive datasets, the performance disparity is often much larger. Interest-
206   ingly, we note that PPR still performs well on some transductive datasets, including the popular
207   WN18RR. This tells us that this phenomenon is not necessarily unique to inductive datasets, but is
208   most apparent there.
209
      Furthermore, we find that for the inductive datasets, their PPR performance is much higher than
210   their transductive parents. We detail this in Figure 2a where for different inductive datasets we see
211   the percent increase in PPR performance from transductive to inductive. For example, on FB15k-
212   237 the PPR Hits@10 is 2.7%, however the mean performance on the four FB (E) splits is 42.7%,
213   representing a 1481% increase. We can see that the % increase on each inductive dataset is large,
214   with the smallest being 42% on WN. This suggests that there is some change in the underlying
215   inductive graphs that are causing the performance of PPR to increase. Lastly, we further explore
      other potential shortcuts in Appendix A.3, finding that PPR is by far the most severe.

233   3.2   W HEN D OES PPR P ERFORM W ELL AND W HY ?
234
235   In the previous section we detailed that PPR score performs well on the inductive setting. Further-
236   more, the performance of PPR on inductive datasets is much higher than their transductive counter-
237   parts of which they’re derived from. This raises the question – why can PPR perform well on some
238   datasets but not others?
239   We find that the answer lies in comparing the mean shortest path distance (SPD) between
240   positive and negative samples. For a query (s, r, ∗), let’s denote o+ as the correct answer and
241   o− ∈ V − (s, r) as the set of negative answers for the query. We compute the SPD on the infer-
242   ence graph for both the o+ and V − (s, r). This is repeated for each test sample (s, q, ∗) ∈ Einf .
243   We then calculate the mean SPD across all positive and negative test samples, which we de-
244   note as SPD+ and SPD− , respectively. The difference in mean SPD is correspondingly given by
245
      ∆SPD = SPD− − SPD+ . This tells us, on average, how much shorter the distance between entities
      in positive samples are relative to those in negatives.
246
247   In Figure 2b we show the relationship between ∆SPD and the Hits@10 when using PPR. The results
248   are across 25 different datasets, for both inductive and transductive KGC. Calculating the Pearson
249   correlation, we can observe it is 0.87, which indicates a strong relationship between the two metrics.
250   Therefore, there exists a basic pattern to distinguish positive and negative samples in many KGC
251
      datasets. Put simply, the SPD between entities in positive test samples tend to be lower than that
      in negative samples. The larger the discrepancy between the mean distances, the better PPR can
252
      perform. This suggests that the PPR scores can exploit this pattern in the datasets, to achieve good
253
      performance, even while completely ignoring the relational information.
254
255   But, why can PPR exploit this pattern? As shown in Eq. equation 1, the PPR score between two
256   nodes is the weighted sum of walks between them. Furthermore, walks of shorter length are weighed
257
      more heavily than those of longer length. Therefore, nodes with a shorter distance between them
      will be able to benefit from these highly-weighted walks, while those of larger distance will not. For
258
      example, when α = 0.15, the highest weight for a walk when SPD = 2 is 0.72 while when SPD = 5
259
      it is 0.44. As such, the PPR score will invariably favor those node pairs with a lower SPD.
260
261   Note that we use PPR as opposed to SPD in our experiments since all-pairs SPD is costly to calculate
262   and the full PPR matrix can be efficiently approximated via Andersen et al. (2006). Furthermore,
263
      as we show in Section 3.3, even when controlling for the SPD, the PPR score can help differentiate
      between positive and negative samples.

339   4     C ONSTRUCTING I NDUCTIVE DATASETS THROUGH G RAPH PARTITIONING
340
341   In the previous section, we showed that the PPR score can achieve strong performance on most
342   inductive KGC datasets. Furthermore, we demonstrate that this is due to how inductive datasets
343   are sampled from existing transductive datasets. This sampling strategy engenders a shift in the
344   underlying properties of the graph that allows for PPR to perform well. This naturally causes us to
345   ask – How can we mitigate this problem when constructing newer inductive datasets? In the next
346   subsection, we introduce our strategy which utilizes graph partitioning to alleviate this problem.
347
348   4.1   PARTITION -BASED DATASET S AMPLING
349
350   We’ve previously covered in Section 3 that the existing procedure for creating inductive datasets
351
      leads to suboptimal subgraphs. This is because the resulting subgraphs tend to have much different
      properties than the original graph, such as the distance distribution, which can potentially lead to
352
      shortcuts when performing KGC.
353
354   We note that the task of constructing inductive datasets from an existing graph G can be framed as
355   a graph partitioning problem. Formally, we want to sample two non-overlapping partitions from
356   the graph such that Gtrain , Ginf ⊆ G and Gtrain ∩ Ginf = ∅. Given the analysis in Section 3.2, we
357
      hope to sample subgraphs such that ∆SPD(Gtrain ) ≈ ∆SPD(Ginf ) ≈ ∆SPD(G). But, how do we
      find subgraphs that satisfy this property? Intuitively, we want to sample each subgraph so that it’s
358
      removal has little effect on the initial graph’s structure. We give an example in Figure 5 where we
359
      sample two subgraphs from an existing graph. As we can see, even though the graph is partitioned,
360   the relationship between entities in the same partition remain roughly the same before and after the
361   partition. This is because there already exists little relationship between the two partitions in the
362   original graph.
363
      Multiple popular approaches Shi & Malik (2000); Blon-
364
      del et al. (2008) exist that attempt to divide the graph
365
      into optimal partitions. The guiding principle in these ap-
366   proaches is that the partitions should be internally dense
367   but only sparsely connected to one another. Because of
368   this, the entities in different partitions should only be
369   weakly connected and have little impact on one another.
370   Therefore, the relationship between entities in the same
      partition are minimally affected by outside entities or            Figure 5: Sampling Two Subgraphs

373   ties in that partition. Also, since the partitions are created at the same time, we avoid sampling the

      6   C ONCLUSION
529
530
      In this paper we study the problem of constructing datasets for inductive knowledge graph comple-
      tion. Upon examination, we find that we can achieve competitive performance on most inductive
531
      datasets through the use of Personalized PageRank Page et al. (1999), which ignores the relational
532
      structure of the graph. Through our study, we uncover that this shortcut is due to how inductive
533   datasets are created. To remedy this problem, we propose a new dataset construction process based
534   on graph partitioning that empirically mitigates the impact of the studied shortcut. We then construct
535   new benchmark datasets using this new procedure and benchmark various methods. Examining the
536   results, we observe that the relative performance decreases on most datasets and methods, indicating
537   that the newer benchmark datasets are harder than the previous ones. For future work, we plan to
538   explore creating inductive KG datasets that aren’t sampled from existing transductive datasets.

       In this section we give a basic overview of the graph partitioning problem. Graph partitioning seeks
975    to partition a graph G = (V, E) into N subgraphs with disjoint entities Buluç et al. (2016). Formally
976    this is formulated as the following where Vi is the set of entities in the i-th partition.
977
                                              V1 ∪ V2 ∪ · · · ∪ VN = V,                                      (4)
978
979                                           V1 ∩ V2 ∩ · · · ∩ VN = ∅.                                      (5)
980    However, an exact solution to above problem is infeasible in all but simple cases, as graph partition-
981    ing is known to be NP-Hard. Therefore, approximations are typically used to partition the graph.
982
983    Several popular approaches exist to solving this problem. The common thread between them is that
984    they seek to extract partitions that contain many links between nodes of the same partition, but few
985    between nodes of other partitions. As such, this allows us to discover distinct “communities” in the
986    graph, that contain nodes that more often interact with one another. Spectral clustering Shi & Malik
987    (2000) is a popular approach to graph partitioning that uses the Fiedler vector (i.e., the eigenvector
988    of the 2nd smallest eigenvalue) to partition the graph. This is due to its connection the normalized
989    cut of the graph. Another popular approach is the Louvain method Blondel et al. (2008). Louvain
990    uses a specially designed objective, referred to as modularity, to identify partitions that are internally
991
       dense but feature few links between partitions.

995    Training Settings: Each model was trained on either a: Tesla V100 32Gb, NVIDIA RTX A6000
996    48Gb, NVIDIA RTX A5000 24Gb, or Quadro RTX 8000 48Gb. All models were implemented in
997    PyTorch Paszke et al. (2019) and Torch-Geometric Fey & Lenssen (2019).
998    Hyperparameters: Due to the different types and ranges of hyperparameters used for each method,
999    the exact hyperparameters and their ranges differ by method. For each method, we tried to follow
1000   the recommended ranges used by the authors. For InGram Lee et al. (2023) we tuned the learn-
1001   ing rate in {1e−3 , 5e−4 } and the number of entity layers in {2, 4}. For NodePiece Galkin et al.
1002   (2021) we tuned the learning rate from {1e−3 , 1e−4 } and the loss margin from {15, 25}. For RED-
1003   GNN Zhang & Yao (2022), we tuned the learning rate from {5e−3 , 5e−4 } and the dropout from
1004   {0.1, 0.3}. In their experiments, NBFNet Zhu et al. (2021) uses the same hyperparameters across
1005
       all datasets. We therefore do the same. For Neural LP Yang et al. (2017) we also use the default set
       of hyperparameters that is shared across datasets.
1006
1007   Evaluation: For a given test triple (s, r, o), we strive to predict both s and o individually. This
1008   is framed as a ranking problem where we want the probability of the correct entity (e.g., o) to
1009   rank higher than all possible negative entities. We can calculate the rank of the true entity over all
1010   negatives by the following where p(·) denotes the model probability for a triple:
                                                      X
1011                         Rank(s, r, o) = |V| −         1 (p(s, r, o) > p(s, r, v)) .                 (6)
1012                                                 v∈V\{o}
1013
1014
       We note that a lower rank, indicates better performance. In practice, some of the negative entities
       will be true triples observed during training. As such, following Bordes et al. (2013) we use the
1015
       filtered setting and remove those entities from the ranking. Once we obtain the rank of the true
1016
       entity (i.e., “how many negative entities does it have a higher probability than”) we compute the
1017   Hits@K%. Hits@K% computes the percentage of sample where the following inequality holds
1018   true, Rank ≤ K. We follow previous work Galkin et al. (2023); Zhang & Yao (2022); Lee et al.
1019   (2023) and evaluate against all possible negative entities.

1051   (E) we observe a consistently large decrease in performance across all methods. The % difference
1052   ranges from 27-61% with a mean of 40.6%. This suggests that our new WN18RR datasets are much
1053   harder. For FB15k-237, the performance of most methods decrease, with a mean decrease of 9.5%.
1054   However, we do see a small increase in the performance of some methods. Nonetheless, this doesn’t
1055   imply that these new datasets are “easier” for those methods for a few reasons. First, for the older
1056   datasets we use the performance reported by Lee et al. (2023). The results are only reported over
1057   1 random seed while ours is over 5. This makes them difficult to compare, as the performance
1058
       of just one random seed is unreliable. Second, the increase in performance for RED-GNN and
       NBFNet is small at < 10%. Given that the older performance is only over one seed, this difference
1059
       is not statistically significant. Lastly, for Neural LP on FB15k-237 (E, R), the standard deviation in
1060
       performance is very high at 9.9 and 12.8, respectively on both inference graphs (see Table 4). As
1061   such, since the increase in performance relative to the older splits is only 3.8, this change is also not
1062   statistically significant.

1066
1067   When evaluating ULTRA Galkin et al. (2023) we use the 0-shot setting. By default, we use the
1068   checkpoint they provided that was trained on the transductive datasets: FB15k-237 Toutanova &
1069   Chen (2015), WN18RR Dettmers et al. (2018), CoDEx-M Safavi & Koutra (2020), and NELL-
1070   995 Xiong et al. (2017). However, since we create our own inductive splits from FB15k-237,
1071   CoDEx-M, and WN18RR, there is risk of test leakage. That is, some triples that may have been
1072
       in the transductive training graph, may now be a test sample in one of our splits. This gives ULTRA
       an unfair advantage as it has already seen that triple.

1075   used to created that specific inductive dataset. This includes:

1107   et al. (2023), (b) PPR performance, (c) % difference in performance, and (d) ∆SPD. Note that in

1109   negative entities used in ours. To account for this, we compute the PPR performance using both
1110   evaluation settings. The results for the PediaTypes and WikiTopics datasets are in Tables 20 and 21,
1111   respectively.
1112   From the results we can make multiple observations. First, PPR performs well under both settings.
1113   Under the all negatives setting, the mean PPR performance is 32.5% and 38.3% on PediaTypes
1114   and WikiTopics, respectively. Second, under 50 negatives, there is little difference in the PPR and
1115   Neural performance. Specifically, the % difference in performance is 0.4% and 14.2%, respectively,
1116   on PediaTypes and WikiTopics. This suggests that neural methods can only slightly outperform PPR
1117   under this setting. Lastly, the ∆SPD is high on both sets of datasets. This further corroborates our
1118
       findings in Section 3.
1119   This analysis shows that the WikiTopics and PediaTopics datasets introduced by Gao et al. (2023)
1120   fall prey to the same shortcut found in other inductive datasets studied in Section 3.

1157   K    L IMITATIONS
1158
1159   One potential limitation of our study is that the new inductive datasets are still being created from
1160
       existing transductive datasets. While our method can help create better and more realistic inductive
       scenarios, this is limiting as we are still reliant on good quality transductive datasets. Future work
1161
       can focus on creating inductive KGC datasets directly from existing large-scale knowledge graphs,
1162
       thereby bypassing the need to sample from existing transductive datasets.

1165   L    I MPACT S TATEMENT
1166
1167   Our method contributed positively to the field of knowledge graph reasoning. By introducing newer
1168   and better inductive datasets, we better align the task of KGC to it’s real-world applications. This is
1169
       essential, because if we want to perform inductive KGC in real-world tasks, we have to be certain
       that the methods actually work well. As such, the new datasets can serve as a stronger barometer
1170
       of actual performance on inductive KGC and will help spur the development of newer and more
1171
       effective KGC techniques. Since KGC has applications in many different fields including question
1172   answering, biology, and recommender systems, there is a strong need for methods that can perform
1173   well. We have also carefully considered the broader impact of our work from different perspectives
1174   and find that there is no apparent risk.
