011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Dataset quantization (DQ) is an innovative coreset selection method to choose
014            representative subsets from large-scale datasets, such as ImageNet. Although DQ
015            has made significant progress, it heavily relies on large pre-trained models (like
016
               MAEs), leading to substantial additional computational overhead. We first iden-
               tify that removing this pre-trained MAE model degrades DQ’s performance and
017
               increases the variance in model training. Where MAE plays a crucial role in in-
018
               troducing prior knowledge and implicit regularization into the training process.
019            Second, we investigate a data augmentation scheme that can simulate the steps of
020            pixel compression and reconstruction in DQ by simply using a randomly initial-
021            ized ResNet model. This randomly initialized ResNet model can take advantage
022            of the inductive bias of CNNs to locate the semantic object region and then re-
023            place the other region with other images. Therefore, we can use a random model
024            or trained model in the early training stage to enhance semantic diversity while
025            selecting important samples. We remove the module that contains the pre-trained
026            MAE model and integrate the data augmentation scheme into the DQ pipeline,
027
               which formulates a new simple but efficient method, called DQ v2. Our method
               achieves performance improvements across multiple datasets, such as ImageNette,
028
               CUB-200, and Food-101.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Deep learning has become the golden standard for many computer vision and machine learning
034
      tasks (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021), which have seen rapid growth due to increasing model sizes and
035
      dataset volumes. However, training emerging deep models, e.g., vision transformers (ViTs) (Doso-
036   vitskiy et al., 2021), on large-scale datasets like ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009) and LIAON (Schuh-
037   mann et al., 2021) requires substantial computational resources, including high-performance GPUs,
038   large memory capacity, and high-speed storage (Bartoldson et al., 2023). These requirements pose
039   a significant barrier to entry for many researchers and practitioners, especially those in resource-
040   constrained environments. Thus, how to efficiently train large-scale deep learning models with
041   limited resources has become a common concern in both academia and industry.
042   Recent research has shown that large-scale datasets have many redundant and irrelevant samples
043   (Xia et al., 2024; He et al., 2024), which can be compressed into a smaller representative subset
044   without losing model performance. Thus, either coreset selection or dataset distillation, as crucial
045   methods to address this issue, aims to choose or synthesize a representative subset from large-scale
046   datasets to reduce computational complexity while maintaining model performance (Guo et al.,
047   2022; Bartoldson et al., 2023). However, most existing methods face challenges in maintaining gen-
048   eralization and low scalability for larger datasets (Guo et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2023). To this end,
049   Dataset Quantization (DQ) is a recently proposed method that effectively addresses these challenges
050
      while maintaining high performance under all data keep ratios (Zhou et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2024).
      We will quickly review the DQ method in the following section.
051
052   DQ is heavily based on various pre-trained models, that is, Masked Autoencoders (MAE) (He et al.,
053   2022) and a pre-trained ResNet model (He et al., 2016). The ResNet model controls the dataset bin
      generation step, while the MAE model is used for image reconstruction in the pixel quantization and

      First, we observe that removing the MAE reconstruction step will significantly increase the variance

093   DQ V2 can eliminate the drawbacks of DQ’s dependence on pre-trained models while achieving
094   performance improvements across multiple datasets.

      2.1   DATASET Q UANTIZATION
100
      Coreset selection is a crucial technique for reducing the computational complexity of deep learning
101
      models by selecting a representative subset from large-scale datasets. Many efforts have been made
102   to address this issue, including geometry-based methods (Agarwal et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2012;
103   Sener & Savarese, 2018), uncertainty-based methods (Coleman et al., 2019), error-based methods
104   (Toneva et al., 2019; Paul et al., 2021), decision boundary-based methods (Ducoffe & Precioso,
105   2018; Margatina et al., 2021), gradient matching-based methods (Mirzasoleiman et al., 2020; Kil-
106   lamsetty et al., 2021), and submodularity-based methods (Iyer et al., 2021). On the other hand,
107   dataset distillation (Sachdeva & McAuley, 2023) is another important technique for compressing
      large-scale datasets, which aims to synthesize a representative subset from the original dataset.


                                                       2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      However, recent research has shown that regardless of coreset selection or dataset distillation, most
109   existing methods face challenges in low scalability to larger datasets (Guo et al., 2022; Zhou et al.,
110   2023). Therefore, Dataset Quantization (DQ), a combination of coreset selection and dataset distil-
111   lation, has been proposed to address these challenges (Zhou et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2024). It can ef-
112   fectively select a representative subset from large-scale datasets while maintaining high performance
113   under all data keep ratios. However, DQ-based framework is heavily based on various pre-trained
114   models, that is, Masked AutoEncoder (MAE) (Zhao et al., 2024) and a pre-trained ResNet model.
115   These pre-trained models dominate the computational complexity and stability of the DQ method.
116   Directly removing them leads to performance degradation and increased variance in trained model
117
      under different random seeds. In this work, we rethink the necessity of these pre-trained models in
      the DQ method and propose a new method that achieves efficient coreset selection without relying
118
      on any pre-trained models.
119
120
121
      2.2   DATA AUGMENTATION
122
      Data augmentation (Shorten & Khoshgoftaar, 2019) plays an essential role in improving model ro-
123   bustness and generalization ability. Traditional data augmentation methods focus mainly on simple
124   image transformations, such as rotation, flipping, and color adjustment. Recent studies have ex-
125   plored more advanced data augmentation strategies, such as random erasing (Zhong et al., 2020),
126   Mixup (Zhang et al., 2018), CutMix (Yun et al., 2019), and ”Copy and paste” (Dwibedi et al., 2017;
127   Ghiasi et al., 2020). These methods have achieved significant success in enhancing the performance
128   and stability of vision models. Although these data augmentation methods have achieved significant
129   success in improving model performance, they generally lack consideration of image semantic struc-
130   ture. Cao & Wu (2022) introduce a novel data augmentation method that leverages the inductive bias
131
      of random CNNs to preserve semantic objects while mixing up the background. How to design data
      augmentation strategies that can both maintain image naturalness and effectively enhance model
132
      learning ability remains an open question. In this work, we first observe that the pre-trained MAE
133
      model is actually equivalent to a data augmentation method, which introduces prior knowledge and
134   implicit regularization into the training process. Thus, this observation motivates us to explore a
135   new data augmentation strategy that can replace the MAE model in the DQ method.

140   3.1   P RELIMINARIES
141
      Suppose that we have a large dataset D = {(xi , yi )}Ti=1 , where xi is the i-th image and yi is
142
      the corresponding label, and T is the total number of training samples. Coreset selection aims to
143
      choose a optimal small subset DS from a large-scale dataset D, where DS ⊂ D and |DS | ≪ |D|.
144   The model trained on DS can achieve comparable performance to the model trained on the entire
145   dataset D. Finally, the model trained on the coreset DS can be used to make predictions on the
146   test set. As discussed above, most coreset selection and dataset distillation methods suffer from
147   some obvious drawbacks, such as poor generalization and low scalability. Therefore, Zhou et al.
148   (2023) proposed a new method, Dataset Quantization (DQ), which consists of three main steps: 1)
149   dataset bin generalization, 2) selection of subset bin , and 3) image pixel quantization. The entire
150   DQ pipeline is shown in Figure 1 (a).
151   The first step aims to generate multiple non-overlapping dataset subsets (referred to as bins), each
152   containing representative and diverse samples. Where DQ leverages the traditional coreset selection
153   method, i.e., GraphCut method (Iyer et al., 2021) to select the most representative samples. Here, a
154   pre-trained ResNet model is used to extract features for all images, and the GraphCut score is cal-
155   culated for each unselected sample when added to the current bin. The second step involves random
156   sampling of the generated bins to form the final compressed dataset. This design introduces addi-
157   tional randomness, contributing to improved model robustness and generalization. The final step is
158
      to further reduce storage requirements and enhance data quality. This process involves image patch-
      ing, importance scoring, patch selection, and image reconstruction. Here the pre-trained Masked
159
      Autoencoder (MAE) decoder is used to reconstruct the complete image.
160
161   Since DQ can achieve state-of-the-art performance on various datasets, especially large-scale
      datasets like ImageNet, it has attracted significant attention from the research community. Despite

      3.2   T HE EFFECT OF P RE - TRAINED M ODELS
191
192   Our goal is to ablate the role of the pre-trained models in the DQ method. Thus, we conducted
193   a series of controllable experiments to investigate the impact of pre-trained models, particularly
194   the Masked Autoencoder (MAE) model, on the performance and stability of the DQ method. Our
195   results reveal that removing the MAE model significantly degrades DQ’s performance and increases
196   the variance in model training.
197
      We remove the pixel quantization step in the original DQ method and directly use the selected images
198   from the second step to train the model. This process is equivalent to removing the pre-trained
199   MAE model in the DQ method. We conducted experiments on both CIFAR-10 and ImageNette
200   datasets with different random seeds, and we report the mean accuracy and variance of the trained
201   models in Figure 2. The results show that the pre-trained MAE model contributes significantly to
202   reducing the variance in model training, which makes the final trained model achieve consistent
203   performance under different random seeds. This also helps researchers reproduce the results and
204   compare different methods more effectively. However, removing the MAE model will not affect the
205   mean accuracy, but the accuracy increases slightly in CIFAR-10. We think this is because the image
206
      scale of CIFAR-10 is relatively small, and the MAE model may introduce some noise that affects
      the model’s performance. But, on ImageNette, the MAE model indeed helps to improve the model’s
207
      performance.
208
209   In general, these results reveal an interesting phenomenon: MAE has a crucial impact on DQ’s
210   performance and stability of DQ for larger datasets, while its role is relatively minor for smaller
211   datasets. Therefore, we further analyze the underlying reasons for this phenomenon.
212   In the third step of DQ, the training images are first removing the less-informative patches based
213   on the importance scores and then reconstructing the complete image using the pre-trained MAE
214   model. See Figure 3. In fact, the MAE model is pre-trained on a large-scale dataset, which makes
215   it encapsulate extensive image prior knowledge. Since the removing region usually locates in the
      background or less important regions, the MAE reconstruction actually infers the missing regions

      based on the semantic structure of the object in the image. We argue that the MAE reconstruction is
217   equivalent to a data augmentation method which introduces prior knowledge and implicit regulariza-
218   tion into the training process. It can also improve the diversity and quality of the training samples,
219   thus improving the performance and stability of the model.
220
      However, this enhancement comes at the cost of increased computational complexity and depen-
221
      dence on large pre-trained models. This raises a crucial question: Can we design a more efficient
222
      method that achieves or surpasses DQ’s performance without relying on large pre-trained models?
223   In the following sections, we will answer this question by introducing a novel data augmentation
224   strategy that can replace the MAE model in the DQ method.

      3.3   S EMANTICALLY AWARE DATA AUGMENTATION
227
228   As discussed before, the pixel quantization step mainly preserves the semantic object while dropping
229   the background regions that are less-informative or less important for the model training. However,
230   the reconstruction process not only imputes the missing regions, but also slightly modifies the orig-
231   inal image, which can be viewed as a form of data augmentation. This process is similar to the
232   classical data augmentation method, i.e., CutMix (Yun et al., 2019). CutMix generates new training
233   samples by cutting and pasting patches between training images while adjusting the corresponding
234
      labels proportionally, enhancing the model’s robustness and generalization.
235   However, CutMix may randomly cut foreground objects, which cannot hold the semantic object.
236   We simply replace CutMix with the MAE reconstruction step in the DQ method and directly apply
237   the composed data augmentation to the DQ pipeline. We find that the results on the ImageNette
238   dataset show that the CutMix method has a significant performance drop compared to the MAE
239   reconstruction method. See the results reported in the following section. Thus, we need to design
240
      a more effective data augmentation strategy that not only maintains the information of semantic
      objects but also does not rely on any pre-trained models.
241
242   We investigated two data augmentation strategies in recent work that help to relieve the dependency
243   on the pre-trained models in the DQ method. Tobias leverages the inductive bias of randomly ini-
244   tialized CNNs to preserve semantic objects while mixing up the background. This method can
245   effectively maintain the naturalness of the image and introduce beneficial variations. See Figure 5a.
246
      In addition, we mix the Tobias data and the original images to further enhance the diversity of the
      training data. We also report that using appropriately mixing rates can further improve the model’s
247
      performance. While this strategy offers the following advantages:
248
249          • Semantic Consistency: By preserving the image’s main object region, it ensures that aug-
250            mented images maintain the original semantic information.
251
             • Diversity Introduction: The replacement of background regions introduces new visual con-
252
               texts, increases data diversity, and improves model generalization.
253
254
             • Computational Efficiency: Compared to using large pre-trained models (like MAE), this
               method has lower computational overhead and requires no additional model dependencies,
255
               making it suitable for resource-constrained environments.

      3.4   O UR P ROPOSED F RAMEWORK : DQ V 2
259
      By integrating the Tobias data augmentation strategy into the DQ framework without the MAE
260   model, we addressed the stability issues of the original DQ method while reducing computational
261   complexity. Our improved method includes the following key steps:
262
263
      1) Mask Generation: Use an early-trained model1 (e.g., ResNet-50) to generate masks for each
      image in the training set, localizing the regions of the main objects. This step leverages CNN’s
264
      inductive bias to effectively identify the main objects in the images. 2) Tobias Data Augmentation:
265
      Based on the generated masks, augment the training data. Specifically, we retain the main object
266   part of the image while replacing the original background with randomly selected backgrounds from
267   other images, creating an expanded training set. This step maintains the original image’s semantic
268
         1
269        The early-trained model is the model trained by several epochs (like 10 epochs), which is similar to the
      scheme in (Paul et al., 2021).

270
      information while introducing new visual contexts. Then, we mix the Tobias data and the original
271   data in an appropriate ratio to build a new training dataset. 3) Dataset Binning: Use the early-trained
272   model to extract the visual feature and then apply the GraphCut method (Iyer et al., 2021) to split
273   the mixed training set, generating multiple non-overlapping bins. This step ensures that the selected
274   samples are representative and diverse, keeping the core advantages of the DQ method. 4) Bin
275   Sampling: Randomly select a certain percentage of images from each bin to form the final core set.
276   This random sampling process further increases the diversity of the data, allowing users to flexibly
277   adjust the proportions of the data to suit the different task requirements. 5) Model Training: Train
278   the model using the selected core set. As the coreset contains both original and augmented images,
279
      the model can learn richer and more robust feature representations, enhancing model performance
      and stability.
280
281   The pipeline of our proposed method, named Dataset Quantization V2 (DQ V2), is illustrated in
282   Figure 1. Through this design, our method not only resolves the original DQ method’s dependence
283   on large pre-trained models but also improves performance and efficiency in multiple aspects. Ex-
284   perimental results show that without using the MAE model, our method can achieve or even surpass
285
      the performance of the original DQ, providing an efficient and practical solution for core set selec-
      tion and data compression.

406   4.3   A BLATION S TUDY
407
408   In this subsection, we mainly study our method by changing one component while keeping the others
409   fixed. We study the impact of Tobias augmentation, bin split strategy, and mixing ratio between
410
      original images and Tobias-generated images on the performance of our proposed method.
411   Impact of Tobias Size. First, we investigate the impact of the Tobias size that is a key parameter in
412   the Tobias data augmentation strategy. The Tobias size determines the granularity of the image patch
413   division. A higher value indicates a finer division, which better preserves the semantic structure. See
414   Figure 5a as an example. We evaluated five different Tobias sizes on the ImageNette dataset, and
415   the results are shown in Figure 5b.
416   The results show that the Tobias size has a significant impact on the performance of our proposed
417   method. First, we observe that smaller Tobias sizes (e.g., 4×4, 8×8) lead to performance degrada-
418   tion, possibly due to insufficient granularity resulting in incorrect segmentation of the main subject.
419   Second, as Tobias size increases, performance generally shows an upward trend, reaching optimal
420   at 40×40. Finally, larger Tobias sizes (e.g., 32×32, 40×40) not only improve average accuracy
421   but also reduce variance, indicating more stable performance. This phenomenon may stem from
422
      more detailed background replacement almost eliminating semantic information in the background,
      allowing the model to better focus on foreground features while reducing dependence on and over-
423
      fitting to specific backgrounds. Based on these results, we recommend using a Tobias size of 40×40
424
      for optimal performance and stability.

474   5   C ONCLUSION
475
476   In this paper, we introduced DQ V2, an enhanced version of the Dataset Quantization (DQ) method,
477   which integrates the Tobias data augmentation strategy to address the limitations of the original DQ
478   method. Our proposed framework eliminates the dependency on large pre-trained models, thereby
479   reducing computational complexity and improving training efficiency. Through extensive experi-
480   ments on multiple datasets, we demonstrated that DQ V2 achieves superior performance and stabil-
481   ity compared to the original DQ method and other state-of-the-art coreset selection techniques.
482   Our findings suggest that intelligent data augmentation and selection strategies can significantly
483   enhance model performance without relying on extensive pre-training. Future research directions
484   include exploring more advanced semantic-aware data augmentation techniques, extending DQ V2
485   to other visual tasks, and developing adaptive parameter adjustment strategies to further improve its
      flexibility and applicability.

      A     T HE T OBIAS DATA AUGMENTATION S TRATEGY
595
596
      The Tobias data augmentation strategy includes the following steps:
597
598         1. Mask Generation: Use a randomly initialized or lightweight pre-trained CNN to generate
599            masks for each image, identifying the main object regions.
600
            2. Image Segmentation: Divide the image into fixed-size patches.
601
602         3. Background Replacement: Based on the generated mask, the patches are retained in the
603
               main object region and the background patches are replaced with those of other images.
604
605   B     D ISCUSSION AND F UTURE P ROSPECTS
606
607   Based on our experiments and analysis, this paper summarizes the following key findings.
608
609          • No reliance on large pre-trained models: DQ v2 achieves comparable or superior per-
610            formance to DQ without relying on large pre-trained models.
611          • Computational resources and implementation simplicity: DQ v2 requires fewer com-
612            putational resources and is simpler to implement.
613
             • Excellent performance across multiple datasets and scales: DQ v2 performs well on
614            various datasets with different image sizes.
615
             • Significant improvement in training stability: DQ v2 significantly improves the stability
616
               of model training, reducing performance variations between different random seeds.
617
618          • Good complementarity with data augmentation: DQ v2 demonstrates good comple-
619            mentarity with other data augmentation methods (e.g., Mixup).
620
      Based on these observations, we offer insight into several important questions, with the aim of
621
      inspiring the community to rethink core set selection and data augmentation methods.
622
623
624
      B.1   A RE LARGE PRE - TRAINED MODELS NECESSARY ?
625
      No. The prior knowledge provided by large pre-trained models is not absolutely necessary. Our
626   experiments show that DQ v2 can achieve or even surpass DQ’s performance without using large
627   pre-trained models like MAE. As mentioned in (He et al., 2019), the prior knowledge from pre-
628   trained models does not necessarily improve model performance on target data. Through simple
629   processing of target data, DQ v2 can achieve effects similar to using expensive pre-trained large
630   models.
631
632   B.2   I S THE DQ METHOD USEFUL ?
633
634   Yes. DQ, as a core set selection method, provides a crucial auxiliary tool for the computer vision
635   community. Not only do it saves computational resources required for large-scale data, but also it
636
      shortens research cycles, making it easier to obtain encouraging results. We believe these advantages
      will continue to make DQ play an important role in computer vision research. However, DQ v2’s
637
      success indicates that there is still room for further improvement in efficiency and performance
638
      through smarter data processing methods.
639
640
      B.3   D O WE NEED BIG DATA ?
641
642   Yes, but the cost of data collection and cleaning needs to be balanced. Although large-scale datasets
643   have advantages in some tasks, general large-scale classification pre-training sets are not always
644   ideal, for example, the cost of collecting ImageNet is largely overlooked. If the benefits of large-
645   scale classification-level pre-training show diminishing returns, collecting data in the target domain
646   might be more efficient. The success of DQ v2 demonstrates that existing data can be used more
647   effectively through intelligent data augmentation and selection strategies to improve model perfor-
      mance.
