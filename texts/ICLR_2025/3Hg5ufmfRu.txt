009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Machine learning (ML) models are proving to be vulnerable to a variety of attacks
012            that allow the adversary to learn sensitive information, cause mispredictions, and
013            more. While these attacks have been extensively studied, current research predom-
014            inantly focuses on analyzing each attack type individually. In practice, however,
015            adversaries may employ multiple attack strategies simultaneously rather than re-
016            lying on a single approach. This prompts a crucial yet underexplored question:
017            when the adversary has multiple attacks at their disposal, are they able to mount
018
               or enhance the effect of one attack with another? In this paper, we take the first
               step in studying the intentional interactions among different attacks, which we de-
019
               fine as attack combos. Specifically, we focus on four well-studied attacks during
020
               the model’s inference phase: adversarial examples, attribute inference, member-
021            ship inference, and property inference. To facilitate the study of their interactions,
022            we propose a taxonomy based on three stages of the attack pipeline: preparation,
023            execution, and evaluation. Using this taxonomy, we identify four effective attack
024            combos, such as property inference assisting attribute inference at its preparation
025            level and adversarial examples assisting property inference at its execution level.
026            We conduct extensive experiments on the attack combos using three ML model
027            architectures and three benchmark image datasets. Empirical results demonstrate
028            the effectiveness of these four attack combos. We implement and release a modu-
029
               lar reusable toolkit, ACE. Arguably, our work serves as a call for researchers and
               practitioners to consider advanced adversarial settings involving multiple attack
030
               strategies, aiming to strengthen the security and robustness of AI systems.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Recently, machine learning has gained momentum in multiple fields, achieving success in real-
036   world deployments, such as image classification (Devlin et al., 2019; Bao et al., 2020; Zhang et al.,
037   2021), face recognition (Zheng et al., 2017; Kemelmacher-Shlizerman et al., 2016), and medical
038   image analysis (Kourou et al., 2015; Stanfill et al., 2010; Burlina et al., 2011). Nevertheless, prior
039   research has shed light on the vulnerability of ML models to various attacks, such as adversarial ex-
040   amples (Iyyer et al., 2018; Ribeiro et al., 2018; Alzantot et al., 2018), membership inference (Shokri
041   et al., 2017; Nasr et al., 2018; Salem et al., 2019; Li & Zhang, 2021), and backdoor attacks (Chen
042   et al., 2017; Gu et al., 2017; Liu et al., 2018). These vulnerabilities prompt significant security and
043   privacy risks. As a result, investigating, quantifying, and mitigating these various attacks on ML
044
      models have become increasingly important topics.
045   Currently, most research in this field focuses on developing or optimizing more powerful attacks,
046   e.g., higher attack success rates or greater stealthiness, and proposing corresponding countermea-
047   sures. More precisely, these studies typically focus on individual attacks. While some measurement
048   or benchmark papers exist that consider multiple attacks, e.g., ML-Doctor (Liu et al., 2022b) or Se-
049   curityNet (Zhang et al., 2024), they still implement each attack individually. In other words, studying
050
      attacks in isolation is actually the most common practice in the existing ML security domain.
051   However, this practice may not accurately reflect real-world scenarios, where adversaries often pos-
052   sess multiple attack strategies and can potentially synergize or leverage them simultaneously. When
053   focusing solely on individual attacks, researchers may overlook the potential for adversaries to am-
      plify the impact of one attack by leveraging knowledge or capabilities gained from another attack.

081   Combo Taxonomy. First, we propose a taxonomy for attack combinations based on the attack
082   pipeline (RQ1), divided into three levels: preparation, execution, and evaluation. The former en-
083   compasses all preliminary activities before the main attack, including tool setup, data collection, and
084   configuration. The execution level covers the attack’s actual implementation, involving malicious
085   queries, responses, and vulnerability exploitation. Finally, the evaluation level assesses the attack
086   impact, including system disruption, goal achievement, and any post-exploitation activities.
087   Combo Methodology. Based on the taxonomy, we conduct an extensive exploration of attack com-
088   bos across four representative inference-time attacks (RQ2). Specifically, we identify four effective
089   attack combos: one at the preparation level, two at the execution level, and one at the assessment
090   level. At the preparation level, we propose using property inference to assist attribute inference
091   (PropInf2AttrInf). By determining the attribute distribution in the victim model’s training dataset
092   through property inference, we use it to create a balanced attack training dataset for attribute in-
093
      ference. At the execution level, we propose two attack combos: using adversarial examples to
      assist membership inference (ADV2MemInf) and property inference (ADV2PropInf), respectively.
094
      Adversarial examples can search for different noise magnitudes for various membership or prop-
095
      erty statuses, which are then integrated into their original information for improved attack perfor-
096   mance. At the evaluation level, we leverage property inference to assist membership inference
097   (PropInf2MemInf). After the membership inference process ends, we use the property distribution
098   determined by property inference to calibrate its attack output.
099
      Combo Evaluation. We conduct extensive experiments across three popular ML model archi-
100
      tectures and three benchmark image datasets (RQ3). We here summarize our analysis using
101
      ResNet18 (He et al., 2016) trained on CIFAR10 (Krizhevsky, 2009) as an example. First, property
102   inference significantly enhances attribute inference at its preparation level. For instance, AttrInf
103   achieves an accuracy of 0.500 while PropInf2AttrInf achieves an empirical accuracy of 0.894 and
104   a theoretical accuracy of 0.872. Second, adversarial examples improve both membership inference
105   and property inference. For instance, the black-box MemInf with shadow model and PropInf achieve
106   an accuracy of 0.664 and 0.890, respectively, while the attack combos yield significantly improved
107   results, with accuracies of 0.851 and 0.960, respectively. Finally, the black-box MemInf with partial
      training dataset achieves an accuracy of 0.631, compared to PropInf2MemInf’s accuracy of 0.669.

      outline the methodologies for specific attack combinations, designating one as the primary attack
145

148
      3.1   ATTACK C OMBO TAXONOMY
149
      To address RQ1, which examines the approaches for designing and implementing attack combina-
150   tions, we propose a taxonomy based on the attack pipeline. This taxonomy serves several purposes:
151   (1) Most attack pipelines consist of multiple phases, allowing integration and combination of differ-
152   ent attacks at various phases. (2) It is both domain- and model-agnostic, making it easily adaptable to
153   other areas, such as graph data, NLP, and transformer-based models. (3) It offers future researchers
154   a clear framework for studying attack combinations, providing potential benefits to the community.
155
      Preparatory Level. In the preparation stage, the adversary gathers information, sets up the en-
156   vironment, and develops the necessary tools. This includes collecting data about the target ma-
157   chine learning system, such as input-output pairs, model parameters, and any accessible metadata,
158   to understand its architecture. The adversary develops or selects appropriate attack algorithms, like
159   FGSM (Goodfellow et al., 2015) in adversarial example attack, and sets up frameworks and libraries,
160   like PyTorch (https://pytorch.org) or CleverHans (Papernot et al., 2018). Additionally, the adversary
161   prepares the computational infrastructure, including high-performance GPUs or cloud services, and
      may train a shadow/surrogate model to simulate the target system.


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Execution Level. During the execution phase, the actual attack is executed against the target ma-
163   chine learning system. For example, the adversary may deploy the attack by generating adversarial
164   examples through perturbing input data to mislead target models or replicating the target model via
165   model extraction. Throughout this phase, the adversary collects outputs and logs detailed data from
166   the target system for subsequent analysis.
167
      Evaluation Level. In the evaluation phase, the adversary analyzes the outcomes, assesses the attack
168
      performance, and identifies areas for improvement. This involves defining and measuring success
169   metrics such as misclassification rates or confidence reductions, and assessing the broader impact
170   on system performance and security. Post-attack analysis includes examining the types of errors
171   induced by the attack and studying changes in model behavior to understand vulnerabilities. Insights
172   gained during this phase guide the refinement and iteration of the attack strategy, enhancing its
173   effectiveness in subsequent attempts.

      3.2   P REPARATION L EVEL
176
177   We first introduce attack combinations at the preparatory stage. Here, the support attack supports
178   the primary attack during preparation before the primary attack is executed.
179
      PropInf2AttrInf. The first attack combination is enhancing AttrInf (primary attack) by using
180
      PropInf (support attack) during its preparatory stage. Specifically, adversaries in AttrInf often over-
181
      look a key issue: creating a more effective auxiliary dataset for training attack models. The target
182   attribute bias of the target model’s training dataset can complicate the auxiliary dataset, making it
183   crucial to address this bias during preparation. Therefore, we enhance AttrInf by employing PropInf
184   to assist in dataset construction during the preparatory phase.
185
      In general, our intuition is that PropInf can better assist in determining the proportion of the target
186
      attribute in the training dataset. For AttrInf, we believe that adversaries will not really care about
187
      the proportion of the target attribute in the auxiliary dataset. They can never fully eliminate the
188   influence of the bias in the target model without knowing the property information. Therefore, we
189   first determine the distribution of the target attribute in the training dataset using PropInf and further
190   sample the auxiliary dataset, significantly enhancing the effectiveness of AttrInf. In general, the
191   PropInf2AttrInf can be defined as:
192                     PropInf2AttrInf : xtarget , MW , Daux , PropInf → {target attributes}                (1)
193
      More concretely, we have two different scenarios for utilizing PropInf, i.e., empirical and theoretical
194
      settings. 1) For the empirical setting, we use the real posterior of the PropInf attack model as the
195
      confidence for sampling the AttrInf training dataset. For the proportion of the property p, given the
196   confidence c, the ratio of sampling is c × (1 − p). 2) On the other hand, for the theoretical setting,
197   we directly use the predicted label from PropInf into the sampling function. In general, when
198   enough shadow models are trained, such as 1,000 for each label, the empirical setting becomes the

266   of different inference-time attacks.

      5.3   ATTACK M ODELS
333   Attribute Inference. At the preparatory level, the assistant from PropInf will not influence the types
334   of inputs. Therefore, our attack model is a 2-layer MLP where its input is the embeddings from the
335   second-to-last layer of the target model. We use cross-entropy as the loss function and Adam as the
336   optimizer with a learning rate of 1e-2. The attack model is trained for 100 epochs. We use accuracy
337   and F1 score for the evaluation metrics.
338
      Membership Inference. Recall that there are four different scenarios for MemInf; we establish two
339   types of attack models: one for the black-box and the other for the white-box setting. For black-
340   box settings, our original attack model has two inputs: the target sample’s ranked posteriors and a
341   binary indicator on whether the target sample is predicted correctly. Each input is first fed into a
342   different 2-layer MLP. Then, the two obtained embeddings are concatenated and fed into a 4-layer
343   MLP. For the white-box, we have four inputs for this attack model, including the target sample’s
344   ranked posteriors, classification loss, gradients of the parameters of the target model’s last layer, and
345   one-hot encoding of its true label. Each input is fed into a different neural network, and the resulting
346   embeddings are concatenated as input to a 4-layer MLP. We use ReLU as the activation function for
347
      the attack models. For the attack scenario assisted by ADV, the inputs of both the black-box and
      white-box attack models expand the L2 distance between each image and its adversarial example in
348
      the auxiliary dataset. The original attack model remains the same for the attack scenario assisted by
349
      PropInf, but the encoder for choosing λ is a 4-layer MLP. The attack model is trained for 50 epochs
350   by using the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 1e-5. We adopt accuracy, F1 score, AUC score,
351   and TPR @0.1% FPR as the evaluation metrics.
352
353
      Property Inference. Recall that the algorithm level needs to add additional information during
      the attack phase. For PropInf, the attack model is a meta-classifier; its inputs are organized from
354
      the unified overall outputs of each target (shadow) model by feeding the test auxiliary dataset with
355
      different proportions of another property. For the assisted PropInf, the inputs also expand a one-
356   dimensional vector combo of the L2 distance between each image and its adversarial example in the
357   test auxiliary dataset. We adopt accuracy as the evaluation metric on 100 models.

      6.5   TAKEAWAYS
490
491   Overall, our evaluations demonstrate that combining different attack types significantly improves
492   the effectiveness of primary attacks, leading to higher accuracy and success rates. These results
493   confirm our earlier intuition about the benefits of attack combinations. Specifically, using ADV to
494   assist MemInf and PropInf, as well as PropInf to assist AttrInf and MemInf, notably enhances the
495   ability to identify training data and infer sensitive information.
496
497   7     R ELATED W ORK
498
499   More closely related to our work are studies focusing on the relationships between different types of
500   attacks. Li & Zhang (2021) find a positive correlation between a sample’s membership status and its
501   robustness to adversarial noise. They leverage the differing adversarial noise magnitudes of mem-
502   bers and non-members to mount a membership inference attack. However, our work significantly
503   differs from theirs as we integrate one attack into another at different phases, using information from
504   one attack to enhance or amplify another, while Li & Zhang (2021) relies on adversarial example
505   information as the only signal for membership inference, without incorporating its original signal.
506   Recently, Wen et al. (2024) proposed a method to strengthen membership inference through training-
507
      phase data poisoning attacks. However, data poisoning is a training-time attack, while membership
      inference occurs during the inference phase. We emphasize that although an attacker can launch
508
      attacks during both the training and inference phases, this assumption is prohibitively strong. As
509
      the first to systematically study the interactions between different attacks, we start only with the
510   inference-time attack, as this is the most realistic scenario. Finally, Zhou et al. (2022) shows that
511   property inference could enhance the performance of membership inference on GANs. However,
512   their study focuses solely on GANs and proposes only one case study of attack combination. Fur-
513   thermore, even though they provide valuable insight and inspire us to build ACE, their work lacks a
514   high-level, systematic analysis of the intentional interactions among a more diverse set of attacks.
515
516
      8     C ONCLUSION
517
518
      This paper provides the first step in exploring the intentional interaction between different types
519
      of attacks. Specifically, we focus on four extensively studied inference-time attacks: adversar-
520   ial examples, attribute inference, membership inference, and property inference. To facilitate the
521   study of their interactions, we establish a taxonomy based on three levels of the attack pipeline:
522   preparation, execution, and evaluation, and propose four different attack combos: PropInf2AttrInf,
523   ADV2MemInf, ADV2PropInf, and PropInf2MemInf. Extensive experiments across three model
524   architectures and three benchmark datasets demonstrate the superior performance of the proposed
525   attack combos.
526   Additionally, we introduce a reusable modular framework named ACE to integrate our attack com-
527   bos. In this framework, we build four distinct modules to systematically examine the attack combi-
528   nations. We believe that ACE will serve as a benchmark tool to facilitate future research on attack
529   combos, enabling the seamless integration of new attacks, datasets, and models to further explore
530   ML model vulnerabilities.

724   are designed at the sample level, while the last one aims to infer the general information at the dataset
725   level. Different attacks can be applied to different threat models; see Table 4. For each attack and

734   A.1   A DVERSARIAL E XAMPLES
735
736   Adversarial examples (ADV) (Szegedy et al., 2014; Goodfellow et al., 2015; Carlini & Wagner,
737   2017; Goodfellow et al., 2015; Papernot et al., 2016; Madry et al., 2018; Iyyer et al., 2018; Ribeiro
738   et al., 2018; Alzantot et al., 2018; Belinkov & Bisk, 2018) are a type of ML security threat where ma-
739
      licious inputs are deliberately designed to deceive ML models. These inputs, known as adversarial
      examples, are typically crafted by making small, often imperceptible modifications to target data to
740
      cause the model to predict incorrectly. More formally, given a target data sample xtarget , (the access
741
      to) a target model M, an adversarial example xadv can be generated by applying a perturbation δ
742   such that xadv = xtarget + δ. To ensure it remains subtle, the perturbation is usually constrained by a
743   norm ∥δ∥p ≤ ϵ. The goal is to maximize the loss function ℓ (Mθ (xadv ), y) In general, an adversarial
744   attack can be defined as:
745                                               ADV : xtarget , M → {xadv }                               (5)
746
      In general, this type of attack can be categorized into two types based on the knowledge of the
747
      adversary: black-box and white-box attacks (M ∈ {MB , MW }).
748
749   Black-Box ⟨ADV, MB , xtarget ⟩ (Andriushchenko et al., 2020). Black-box attacks operate under
750   the assumption that the adversary has no internal knowledge of the models. Instead, the adversary
751   can only observe the outputs from the model. This scenario is more common in the real world, where
752
      internal details are inaccessible. They usually leverage trial-and-error to approximate the gradient
      of the target model (Chen et al., 2020b) or randomized search schemes to approximate the boundary
753
      of the data samples (Andriushchenko et al., 2020).
754
755   White-Box ⟨ADV, MW , xtarget ⟩ (Madry et al., 2018). White-box attacks assume the adversary
      has complete knowledge of the model, including its architecture, parameters, and training data. It


                                                              14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      allows the adversary to precisely calculate the most effective perturbations to maximize errors of
757   ML models, often employing gradient-based methods to manipulate the input data directly, such as
758   C&W (Carlini & Wagner, 2017), FGSM (Goodfellow et al., 2015), JSMA (Papernot et al., 2016),
759   and PGD (Madry et al., 2018).
760
761   A.2   ATTRIBUTE I NFERENCE
762
763   An ML model may inadvertently learn additional information during the training process unrelated
764   to its original tasks. For instance, a model used to predict the ages from the profile photographs may
765   also unwittingly acquire the capability to predict races (Melis et al., 2019; Song & Shmatikov, 2020;
766   Liu et al., 2022b). Exploiting such unintended information leakage is known as attribute inference
767   (AttrInf). State-of-the-art attacks usually rely on the embeddings of a target sample (xtarget ) obtained
768
      from the target model to predict the sample’s target attributes. Thus, the adversary is assumed to
      have white-box access to the target model. Formally, attribute inference is defined as:
769
770                                AttrInf : xtarget , MW , Daux
                                                             S
                                                                 → {target attributes}                      (6)
771   where Daux is an auxiliary dataset with the second attribute. The adversary is assumed to know
772   the target attributes of the auxiliary dataset. They then use the target attribute embeddings of the
773   auxiliary dataset to train the classifier to infer the actual dataset.
774
775   A.3   M EMBERSHIP I NFERENCE
776
777   Membership Inference attacks(MemInf) (Shokri et al., 2017) against ML models involve an adver-
778   sary aiming to determine whether or not a target data sample is used to train a target ML model.
779
      More concretely, given a target data sample xtarget , (the access to) a target model M, and an auxil-
      iary dataset Daux , a membership inference attack can be defined as:
780
781
                                 MemInf : xtarget , M, Daux → {member, non-member}                          (7)
                        B    W                  P      S
782   where M ∈ {M , M           } and Daux ∈ {Daux , Daux }.
783
      Membership inference has been extensively studied in literature (Shokri et al., 2017; Nasr et al.,
784
      2018; Salem et al., 2019; Jia et al., 2019; Sablayrolles et al., 2019; Li & Zhang, 2021; Chen et al.,
785   2020a; Leino & Fredrikson, 2020; Chen et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022b). Inferring membership of a
786   target sample prompts severe privacy threats; for instance, if an ML model for drug dose prediction
787   is trained using data from patients with a certain disease, then inclusion in the training dataset inher-
788   ently leaks the individuals’ health status. Overall, membership inference often signals that a target
789   model is “leaky” and can be a gateway to additional attacks (Cervi, 2020).
790
      In the following, we illustrate how to implement membership inference (MemInf) under different
791   threat models.
792
793   Black-Box/Shadow ⟨MemInf, MB , Daux      S
                                                 ⟩ (Salem et al., 2019). We start with the most common
794
      and difficult setting for the attack (Shokri et al., 2017; Salem et al., 2019), whereby the adversary
      has black-box access (MB ) to the target model and a shadow auxiliary dataset (Daux S
                                                                                             ).
795
796   The adversary first splits the shadow dataset into two parts and uses one to train a shadow model on
797   the same task. Next, the adversary uses the entire shadow dataset to query the shadow model. For
798   each querying sample, the shadow model returns its posteriors and the predicted label: if the sample
799   is part of the shadow model’s training set, the adversary labels it as a member and, otherwise, as
800
      a non-member. With this labeled dataset, the adversary trains an attack model, which is a binary
      membership classifier. Finally, to determine whether a data sample is a member of the target model’s
801
      training dataset, the sample is fed to the target model, and the posteriors and the predicted label
802
      (transformed to a binary indicator on whether the prediction is correct) are fed to the attack model.
803
804   Black-Box/Partial ⟨MemInf, MB , Daux   P
                                                ⟩ (Salem et al., 2019). If the adversary has black-box ac-
805   cess to the target model and a partial training dataset, the attack method is very similar to that for
806
      ⟨MemInf, MB , Daux S
                            ⟩. However, the adversary does not need to train a shadow model; rather, they
      use the partial training dataset as the ground truth for membership and directly train their attack
807
      model.
808
809   White-Box/Shadow ⟨MemInf, MW , Daux     S
                                                ⟩ (Nasr et al., 2019). Nasr et al. (Nasr et al., 2019) in-

      A.4       P ROPERTY I NFERENCE
820
821   Property inference attacks (PropInf) (Melis et al., 2019; Ganju et al., 2018; Mahloujifar et al., 2022;
822   Zhou et al., 2022) aim to infer general information about the training dataset, such as the proportion
823   of data with a specific property unrelated to the main classification task. For example, the gender
824   ratio in the training dataset can be inferred when a model for classifying race is given. Previous
825   works require access to the training process of the model (e.g., via gradients (Melis et al., 2019))
826   or to model parameters (Ganju et al., 2018). These methods are easy to implement for a few layers
827   of neural networks. However, once the model becomes complex, the vast computational and mem-
                                                                                                      Q
828   ory resources are difficult to achieve. In addition, we build the query auxiliary datasets Daux    with
829
      different proportions of property. Therefore, in this paper, given a target model M, the adversary
                                                                         S
      first trains the shadow models by shadow auxiliary datasets Daux       with different proportions of the
830
      target property. Next, they query these shadow models to get the outputs of each proportion and
831
      concatenate these results together to train a meta-classifier for the property inference. We only need
832   black-box access for this attack. Thus, the property inference can be defined as:
833
                                        PropInf : MB , Daux
                                                        T      S
                                                            , Daux → {target property}                                 (8)
834
835   The global properties of a dataset are confidential when they relate to the proprietary information
836   or intellectual property that the data contains, which its owner is not willing to share. This ex-
837   posure can lead to severe privacy violations, especially if the data is protected by regulations like
838   GDPR (European Union, 2016).

      B       L IMITATION & D ISCUSSION
842
843   Limitations. Naturally, our work is not without limitations. First, we focus on four inference-time
844
      attacks in the image domain. While attacks exist during the training phase, e.g., enhancing member-
      ship inference through backdoor attacks (Wen et al., 2024) or poisoning attacks (Chen et al., 2022),
845
      their settings are more complex, especially in real-world scenarios. More specifically, they typically
846
      require stronger adversarial assumptions, e.g., interfering with the training process or owning the
847   training dataset.
848
849   We currently focus on image datasets because the types of attacks and their implementations are
850
      more detailed and comprehensive in image datasets. We also do not consider model stealing attacks,
      as they primarily, to some extent, convert black-box models to white-box models, which indeed can
851
      enhance the success rate of many attacks. Since we aim to explore the impact of attack combos
852
      during the attack’s different phases, we emphasize that we do not change the overall attack process
853   and the main attack approach.
854
855   Potential Countermeasures. A possible defense strategy against the attacks we consider is ro-
856
      bust adversarial training, where models are trained on adversarial examples to improve robustness.
      Differential privacy techniques can also protect sensitive information by adding noise to the data,
857
      mitigating the risk of attribute, membership, and property inference attacks. Model ensembling,
858
      where predictions are aggregated from multiple models, can increase robustness by making it harder
859   for adversaries to exploit vulnerabilities in a single model. However, we emphasize that, currently,
860   no single defense can protect against all ML model attacks, and effective defenses against property
861   inference or attribute inference are lacking (Liu et al., 2022b). As we focus on providing new in-
862

      sights and techniques for enhancing model security through attack combos, we leave the in-depth
865   exploration of more effective defense mechanisms against them to future work.
