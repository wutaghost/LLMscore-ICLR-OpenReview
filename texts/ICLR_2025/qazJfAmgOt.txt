009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011              This paper addresses the critical challenge of unlearning in Vertical Federated
012              Learning (VFL), an area that has received limited attention compared to horizon-
013              tal federated learning. We introduce the first approach specifically designed to
014              tackle label unlearning in VFL, focusing on scenarios where the active party aims
015              to mitigate the risk of label leakage. Our method leverages a limited amount of
016              labeled data, utilizing manifold mixup to augment the forward embedding of in-
017
                 sufficient data, followed by gradient ascent on the augmented embeddings to erase
                 label information from the models. This combination of augmentation and gra-
018
                 dient ascent enables high unlearning effectiveness while maintaining efficiency,
019
                 completing the unlearning procedure within seconds. Extensive experiments con-
020              ducted on diverse datasets, including MNIST, CIFAR10, CIFAR100, and Model-
021              Net, validate the efficacy and scalability of our approach. This work represents
022              a significant advancement in federated learning, addressing the unique challenges
023              of unlearning in VFL while preserving both privacy and computational efficiency.

061   training (Bourtoule et al., 2020; Foster et al., 2023) or Boundary unlearning (Chen et al., 2023), in
062   this context pose a risk of leaking unlearned labels during the unlearning process. Typically, the
063   active party, which retains the labels, must either inform the passive party about the samples that
064   require unlearning or transfer the gradients associated with the unlearned label. This practice may
065   inadvertently expose sensitive label information to the passive party (see Fig. 1 and Sect. 3.2).
066
      To address this challenge, we propose a few-shot unlearning method that effectively erases labels
067   from both the active model and passive model in VFL by leveraging a limited amount of private data
068   (see Sect. 4). Specifically, our method employs manifold mixup (Verma et al., 2019) to augment
069   the forward embeddings of each passive party. The active party then performs gradient ascent on
070   the mixed embeddings to unlearn the active model and subsequently transfers the inverse gradients
071   to the passive party to facilitate the unlearning of the passive model independently. This approach
072   offers three key advantages: first, it necessitates only labels from a small amount of private data,
073   significantly reducing the risk of label privacy leakage; second, by utilizing the manifold mixup
074   technique, it enhances unlearning effectiveness with minimal data; and third, it is highly efficient,
075
      completing the unlearning process within seconds.
076   The primary contributions of this work are as follows:
077
078         1. To the best knowledge, this is the first work to address the unlearning of labels in VFL.
079         2. We systematically elucidate the label privacy leakage that may occur when directly apply-
080            ing traditional machine unlearning methods.
081         3. We propose a few-shot label unlearning method that effectively erases labels from both the
082            active and passive models in VFL, utilizing a limited amount of private data. Moreover,
083            this approach leverages only a small number of data to mitigate the risk of label privacy
084            leakage while employing manifold mixup to enhance unlearning effectiveness.
085
            4. We conduct extensive experiments on multiple benchmark datasets, including MNIST,
086            CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100, and ModelNet, demonstrating that our method rapidly and ef-
087            fectively unlearns target labels compared to other machine unlearning methods.

      2   R ELATED W ORKS
091
092   Machine Unlearning & Horizontal Federated Unlearning. Machine unlearning (MU) was ini-
093
      tially introduced by (Cao & Yang, 2015) to selectively remove some data from model without retrain
      the model from scratch (Garg et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2021). MU can be categorized into exact un-
094
      learning and approximate unlearning. Exact unlearning methods such as SISA (Bourtoule et al.,
095
      2020) and ARCANE (Yan et al., 2022) split data into sections and train sub-models for each data
096   section and merge all sub-models. During unlearning, retrain the affected data section and merge all
097   sub-models again. In approximate unlearning, techniques such as fine tuning (Golatkar et al., 2020a;
098   Jia et al., 2024) (fine tune with Dr ), random label (Graves et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2023) (fine tune
099   with incorrect random label of Du ), noise introducing (Tarun et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2021), gra-
100   dient ascent (Goel et al., 2023; Choi & Na, 2023; Abbasi et al., 2023; Hoang et al., 2023) (maximise
101   loss associate with Du ), knowledge distillation (Chundawat et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023c; Kur-
102   manji et al., 2023) (train a student model) and weights scrubbing (Golatkar et al., 2020a;b; 2021;
103   Guo et al., 2023; Foster et al., 2023) (discarding heavily influenced weights) are used.
104   Meanwhile, in federated unlearning, most of the existing works are focused in the horizontal envi-
105   ronment (Wu et al., 2022; Gu et al., 2024a; Zhao et al., 2024a; Romandini et al., 2024; Liu et al.,
106   2024; Zhang et al., 2023b; Su & Li, 2023; Ye et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2022; Cao et al., 2022; Yuan
107   et al., 2022; Alam et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Halimi et al., 2023; Xia et al., 2023; Wang et al.,
      2023; Dhasade et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2024b; Wang et al., 2022; Gu et al., 2024b).

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Only very limited research works focus in the vertical environment. For instance, (Zhang et al.,
109   2023a) introduce vertical federated unlearning (VFU) in gradient boosting tree. (Wang et al., 2024)
110   introduce passive party unlearning on deep learning model with fast retraining on remaining parties,
111   and (Deng et al., 2023) introduce passive party unlearning on logistic regression model.
112
      Most if not all existing VFU work have been primarily focused on passive parties unlearning (Zhang
113
      et al., 2023a; Wang et al., 2024; Deng et al., 2023). Hence, a significant gap arise when an active
114
      party seeks for a collaboration from passive parties for a single class unlearning while all parties
115   remaining engaged in VFL. Unfortunately, current VFU approaches do not address this specific sce-
116   nario, as they do not explore class unlearning within VFL setting. In contrast to prior works focusing
117   on class unlearning in centralise machine unlearning and horizontal federated unlearning settings,
118   this paper uniquely addresses class unlearning of classification model within the VFL paradigm.
119   This distinction arises because traditional class unlearning methods in centralised and horizontal
120   federated learning setting are impractical for VFL settings, where all parties have different features
121   of data and different computational power.
122
123   Vertical Federated Learning & Privacy Leakage. VFL is introduced to meet the needs of enter-
124   prises looking to utilize features distributed across multiple parties for improved model performance,
125
      compared to models trained by a single entity, all while preserving data privacy (Yang et al., 2019).
      In VFL, privacy is of utmost importance because the participants are typically companies that handle
126
      valuable and sensitive user information. Hence, privacy protection during VFU is also an important
127
      criteria. We explain the risk of label leakage during VFU in Sect. 3.2.

      5.1   E XPERIMENT S ETUP
303   VFL Setting. We stimulate a VFL scenario including one active party owned the active model and
304   multiple passive parties (ranges from one(1) to eight(8)) owned the passive model (see more details
305   in Appendix A.3).
306
307   Datasets & Models. We conduct experiments on six datasets: MNIST (Lecun et al., 1998), CI-
308   FAR10, CIFAR100 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009), ModelNet (Wu et al., 2015), Brain Tumor MRI (Wang
309   et al., 2024) and Yahoo Answers dataset (Fu et al., 2022a). We adopt ResNet18 (He et al., 2015) on
310   dataset MNIST, CIFAR10, CIFAR100, ModelNet and Brain Tumor MRI. We adopt MixText (Chen
311   et al., 2020) on Yahoo Answers dataset. We do extend our experiments with Vgg16 (Simonyan &
312   Zisserman, 2015) on dataset CIFAR10 and CIFAR100. Experiments are repeated over five random
313
      trials, and results are reported as mean and standard deviation. Experiment results on Brain Tumor
      MRI and Yahoo Answer datasets and further details are available in Appendix A.3. For the MNIST,
314
      CIFAR10, and CIFAR100 datasets, each image feature is divided among K parties, where K rep-
315
      resents the number of passive parties. For the ModelNet dataset, we generate K 2D multi-view
316   images per 3D mesh model by placing two virtual cameras evenly distributed around the centroid.
317   Each passive party is assigned one of the K generated 2D multi-view images.
318
319   Evaluations Metrics. We evaluate the utility of unlearning by measuring accuracy of Dr before
320   and after unlearning. The higher accuracy on Dr indicates stronger utility. To evaluate the unlearning
321   effectiveness, we construct a simple MIA from (Shokri et al., 2017) to test Attack Success Rate
322   (ASR) and measuring the accuracy of Du before and after unlearning. MIA seeks to determine if a
323   specific data record was included in the training of a target machine learning model. Time efficiency
      is evaluated by comparing the runtime of each baseline.

      adversarial examples from Df and assign new nearest incorrect adversarial label to shrink the Df to

      6                             C ONCLUSIONS
532
      In conclusion, this paper presents a pioneering approach to label unlearning within VFL domain,
533
      addressing a critical gap in the existing literature. By introducing a few-shot unlearning method that
534   leverages manifold mixup, we effectively mitigate the risk of label privacy leakage while ensuring
535   efficient unlearning from both active and passive models. Our systematic exploration of potential
536   label privacy risks and extensive experimental validation on benchmark datasets underscores the
537   proposed method’s efficacy and rapid performance. Ultimately, this work not only advances the un-
538   derstanding of unlearning in VFL but also sets the stage for further innovations in privacy-preserving
539   collaborative machine learning practices.

      A.1   D ISCUSSION FOR U NLEARNING E FFECTIVENESS
762
763   Consider a scenario where the active party seeks to unlearn the label yu with the corresponding
764   feature xu and embedding Hu = Gθ (xu ). The gradient ascent approach aims to remove the label
765   information yu from both the active model θ and the passive model ω.
766   1) Unlearning effectiveness for Gradient Ascent (GA). Using the first-order Taylor expansion of
767   ℓ(ω; Hu , yu ) around the initial parameter ωt , we obtain:
768
769                   ℓ(ωt+1 ; Hu , yu ) ≈ ℓ(ωt ; Hu , yu ) + ∇ω ℓ(ωt ; Hu , yu )⊤ (ωt+1 − ωt ).
770   Substituting the gradient ascent update ωt+1 = ωt + η∇ω ℓ(ωt ; Hu , yu ) (as defined in Eq. (5) of the
771   main text), this becomes:
772
773                        ℓ(ωt+1 ; Hu , yu ) ≈ ℓ(ωt ; Hu , yu ) + η∥∇ω ℓ(ωt ; Hu , yu )∥2 .
774   Since η > 0, the loss ℓ(ω; Hu , yu ) increases with each gradient ascent step, effectively reducing the
775   contribution of the label yu to the active model ω. Similarly, for the passive model θ, we derive:
776
777                    ℓ(θt+1 ; xu , yu ) ≈ ℓ(θt ; xu , yu ) + ∇θ ℓ(θt ; xu , yu )⊤ (θt+1 − θt )
778                                       = ℓ(θt ; xu , yu ) + η∇θ ℓ(θt ; xu , yu )⊤ (∇H ℓ∇θ H)
779
                                          = ℓ(θt ; xu , yu ) + η∥∇θ ℓ(θt ; xu , yu )∥2 ,
780
781   where the first equation is due to the Eq. (6) of the main text and second equation is according to the
782   chain rule. Thus, the contribution of the label yu is effectively removed from the passive model θ.
783   2) If the loss function ℓ is β-smooth, we can further derive:
784
785
                          ∥∇ω ℓ(ωT ; Hu , yu )∥ ≤ β∥ωT − ω0 ∥

                                X                                  X                                     (7)
787                        =∥          ∇ω ℓ(ωt ; Hu , yu )∥ ≤ βη          ∥∇ω ℓ(ωt ; Hu , yu )∥,
788                             t=0                                t=0

789   where the second equation follows from Eq. (5) in the main text. This result indicates that the
790   convergence of gradient ascent depends on the learning rate η. For instance, when the learning
                                                                                                1

796   in Section 5 validate this approach.
