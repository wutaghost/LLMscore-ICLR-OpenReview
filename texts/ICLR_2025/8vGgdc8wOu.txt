009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            It is widely assumed that typographic attacks succeed because multimodal pre-
012            trained visual models can recognize the semantics of text within images, allowing
013            text to interfere with image understanding. However, the assumption that these
014            models truly comprehend textual semantics remains unclear and underexplored.
015            We investigate how the CLIP encoder represents textual semantics and identify
016            the mechanisms through which text disrupts visual semantic understanding. To
017            facilitate this analysis, we propose a novel ToT (Texture or Textual) dataset, which
018
               includes a paronyms-synonyms pair of subsets that disentangles orthographic
               forms (i.e., the visual shape of words) from their semantics. Using Intrinsic Di-
019
               mension (ID) to assess layer-wise representation complexity, we examine whether
020
               the representations are built on texture or textual information under typographic
021            manipulations. Contrary to the common belief that semantics are progressively
022            built across layers, we find that texture and semantics compete in the early layers.
023            In the later layers, semantic accuracy improves mainly through texture learning that
024            aids orthographic recognition, while a semantically driven representation emerges
025            only in the final block.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   While visual models trained with vision-language supervision demonstrate the ability to interpret
031   text within images, a fundamental question remains: do these models capture the semantic meaning
032   of the text, or do they simply recognize it as a visual pattern? This distinction is critical, as textual
033
      elements, despite their inherently different texture properties compared to visual objects, are often
      encoded in a similar manner. Such encoding raises the possibility that these models achieve only a
034
      superficial alignment of textures rather than a deeper cross-modal understanding. Furthermore, as
035
      neural networks abstract textural information through successive layers, the point at which textual
036   input begins to influence the semantic interpretation of an image remains uncertain. These challenges
037   form the core motivation for our study, which aims to disentangle textual and textural representations
038   to better understand the semantic mechanisms of vision models.
039
      One significant manifestation of these uncertainties is found in typographic attacks (Goh et al., 2021),
040
      which expose the vulnerabilities inherent in contemporary vision-language models when interpreting
041
      text within images. These attacks involve embedding misleading text into images, resulting in
042   substantial impacts on recognition and classification accuracy. For instance, an image of a dog
043   superimposed with the word "laptop" may be misclassified as an electronic device (Lemesle et al.,
044   2022). The consequences of such misclassifications depend significantly on the characteristics of
045   the text and image. As models like GPT-4 (vision) (Yang et al., 2023) become more advanced, their
046   susceptibility to these attacks raises important security concerns. These typographic manipulations
047   can lead to unintended command executions, akin to ’jailbreaking’ the model (Gong et al., 2023;
048   Robey et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023).
049   While typographic attacks may not strictly qualify as traditional attacks, they demonstrate how pre-
050   trained models effectively learn multimodal representations. Models trained on diverse image-text
051   datasets implicitly learn correlations between text and its real-world meanings (Cao et al., 2023). For
052   instance, a model might link the image of a ’cat’ with the word and concept of a cat, suggesting a
053   unified representation of textual and conceptual semantics. However, this theory requires further
      empirical validation, and alternative explanations should also be explored.

054
      In this work, we use Intrinsic Dimension (ID) as a measure of the complexity of data representation,
055   capturing the required degrees of freedom for accurate encoding. ID is particularly useful in under-
056   standing how subtle image perturbations, including those introduced by typographic attacks, affect
057   visual models (Amsaleg et al., 2017; Ma et al., 2018). Unlike pixel-level perturbations, typographic
058   attacks involve semantic changes that impact how models represent text in images. Our study extends
059   the use of ID to examine how these semantic variations influence model representations across
060   different layers.
061   We introduce the ToT (Textural or Textual) typographic attack dataset, comprising words that are
062   consistent, irrelevant, or nonsensical in relation to image content. This dataset enables an examination
063   of how a vision-language pre-trained model processes these varying types of texts. Additionally,
064   We create a subset of 10 paronyms-synonyms pairs to investigate how representations progressively
065   form to distinguish visual similarities from semantics. Our findings reveal a non-linear pattern in
066   representation; while texture representation evolves gradually in the earlier layers, significant shifts
067   in semantic understanding occur only in the final network block. Specifically, the main contributions
068   of this work are as follows:
069
             • We present a detailed analysis of typographic attacks on visual models, examining their
070
               processing of textual content within images. Through intrinsic dimension (ID), we find
071            that texture and semantic representations share significant features in most layers. Initially,
072            Texture and textual representations compete in the early layers. As the layers progress, the
073            complexity of the representation increases, and texture representation grows rapidly. While
074            the complexity begins to decrease, semantic understanding improves but relies on texture
075            learning for orthographic recognition. Notably, a semantically driven representation emerges
076            only in the final block.
077          • Building on these observations, we defend against typographic attacks by simply fine-
078            tuning only the final block of the model to better distinguish between textural and textual
079            representations. Experimental results show that our strategy effectively balances the perfor-
080            mance between the original image and the typographic classification, achieving significant
081            improvements across diverse defense scenarios.

085   2.1   T YPOGRAPHIC ATTACK
086
087   CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) is known for its ability to joint understanding of language and vision.
088   Due to its large amount and spin of training images, many of which incorporate both visual and
089   textual features, it can read visually presented text, or scene-text (Materzyńska et al., 2022; Cao et al.,
090   2023). A notable aspect of CLIP is its tendency, in certain instances, to rely predominantly on text for
091
      image classification. This reliance can lead to what’s termed a typographic attack (Goh et al., 2021),
      where misclassification occurs due to overemphasis on text.
092
093   In response to such vulnerabilities, various defense strategies have been explored. Materzyńska
094   et al. (2022) implement a linear transformation to bifurcate the model into two distinct streams: one
095   dedicated to visual information and the other to textual data. Azuma & Matsui (2023) introduce the
096   Dense-Prefix token in conjunction with prompt learning, placing it before class names to significantly
097
      enhance accuracy against real-world typographic attack scenarios. PAINT Ilharco et al. (2022)
      involves a method that interpolates between a model’s pre- and post-fine-tuning weights, showing
098
      notable success in mitigating typographic attacks. Cao et al. (2023) takes a different route by filtering
099
      out dataset samples containing text regions within images, leading to not only improved defense
100   against typographic attacks but also heightened accuracy in other tasks.

107   (Zhang et al., 2022), visual question answering (VQA) (Song et al., 2022), and image captioning
      (Mokady et al., 2021). The treatment of visually presented text within these models sparks debate in

109   of the model (Materzyńska et al., 2022; Cao et al., 2023). In contrast, other researchers underscore

118   model’s capability to discern physical objects from scene text, thereby enriching its understanding
119   and interpretation of both visual and textual components in a unified and coherent manner.

121   2.3     I NTRINSIC D IMENSION IN ADVERSARIAL ATTACK
122
123   The Intrinsic Dimension (ID) is the minimum number of dimensions required to represent data effec-
124   tively (Levina & Bickel, 2004). In neural networks, ID is derived from the model’s representations,
125
      indicating the fewest parameters needed to capture specific features (Amsaleg et al., 2015). Ansuini
      et al. (2019) demonstrated a correlation between the final layer’s ID and the model’s accuracy, noting
126
      that ID typically follows a hunchback-shaped curve across layers, reflecting the learning process
127
      (Ansuini et al., 2019). Moreover, ID is crucial for interpreting learned representations and exploring
128   its relationship with neural network training (Aghajanyan et al., 2020; Pope et al., 2021).
129
130   Amsaleg et al. (2017) and Ma et al. (2018) used local ID to assess adversarial robustness, finding
131
      that LID increases with noise in adversarial perturbations. This connection emphasizes how ID
      influences a model’s vulnerability. Tulchinskii et al. (2024) further explored ID in textual data,
132
      revealing that human-generated texts have an average ID of 7 to 9, while AI-generated texts often
133
      fall below 1.5. This distinction enables classifiers to effectively differentiate between human and
134   AI-generated content.

422   between accuracy and ID_max as text size increases. This suggests that ID_max captures textural
423   complexity, whereas ID_last reflects both textural and textual features. CLIP’s representation of text
424   involves a complex interaction between these elements, with semantics heavily influencing accuracy,
425   yet no single layer fully captures this correlation.

527   6    C ONCLUSION
528
529   We explore how visual models process textual semantics in the context of typographic attacks. By
530   introducing the ToT dataset and applying Intrinsic Dimension (ID) analysis, we reveal that early
531   layers of visual models primarily rely on texture features rather than true semantic understanding.
532   Only in the final block do models construct a semantically focused understanding after significant
533
      compression of textural information. Furthermore, we demonstrate an effective defense strategy by
      fine-tuning the final block, which enhances the model’s ability to distinguish between textural and
534
      textual elements. This approach significantly improves performance across various defense scenarios,
535
      offering a practical solution to typographic attacks.
