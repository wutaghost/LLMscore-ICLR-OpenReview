008                                               A BSTRACT
009            Discrete diffusion models, like continuous diffusion models, generate high-quality
010            sequence data by gradually undoing noise applied to datapoints via a Markov pro-
011            cess. Gradual generation in theory comes with many conceptual benefits; for
012            example, inductive biases can be incorporated into the noising Markov process.
013            In practice however, the best performing discrete diffusion model is consistently
014            masking, which does not denoise gradually. Here we explain the performance of
015
               masking diffusion by noting that it makes use of a fundamental difference between
               continuous and discrete Markov processes: discrete Markov processes evolve by
016
               discontinuous jumps at a fixed rate and, unlike other discrete diffusion models,
017
               masking diffusion builds in the known distribution of jump times and only learns
018            where to jump to. We show that we can similarly bake in the known distribution
019            of jump times into any discrete diffusion model; despite their simplicity, our new
020            models – schedule-conditioned diffusion (SCUD) – generalize classical discrete
021            diffusion and masking diffusion. By applying SCUD to models with noising pro-
022            cesses that incorporate inductive biases on images, text, and protein data, we build
023            diffusion models that outperform masking.
024
025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026   Discrete diffusion models are state of the art models for conditional generation of discrete sequences.
027   In biological sequence design, for example, they allow one to generate sequence flexibly conditioned
028   on protein structure (Luo et al., 2022), DNA function (Sarkar et al., 2024), protein family (Alamdari
029   et al., 2023), and other properties (Gruver et al., 2023; Nisonoff et al., 2024). They are also nearing
030   state-of-the-art generation on language data (Sahoo et al., 2024). To define a diffusion model, one
031   proposes a “forward” process by which data is gradually transformed token-by-token into noise and
032   then learns a “backward” transformation that turns noise into data by optimizing an ELBO. In prin-
033   ciple, the quality of the learned model should benefit from a forward process that captures structure
034
      in the data distribution. For example, works have suggested forward processes that are more likely to
      transform tokens into similar tokens – therefore the noising process is more “gradual” (Austin et al.,
035
      2021; Alamdari et al., 2023) ; as well as “state-dependent” processes that transform certain tokens
036
      more quickly than others (Shi et al., 2024). Surprisingly, these methods are all outperformed by
037   “masking diffusion” which has the simplest possible forward process – one transforms each token
038   into a masking token at a uniform rate (Austin et al., 2021; Alamdari et al., 2023; Shi et al., 2024).
039
040
      Here we propose that this is because masking diffusion benefits from a parameterization that forces
      the distribution of corruption / transition events, the “transition schedule”, in the backward process
041
      to match the distribution in the forward process. We use this insight to build models that unlock the
042
      benefits of structured and state-dependent processes in practice. First in Sec. 3 we provide a new
043   decomposition of the ELBO that includes a term describing the mismatch in the distribution of the
044   schedules of the forward and backward processes. Then in Sec. 4 we describe how to efficiently train
045   models that build in the transition schedule (Fig. 1) to set this term to 0. We call our models schedule
046   conditioned diffusion (SCUD). In Sec. 5 we show that when SCUD is applied to discrete diffusion
047   with a uniform forward process, the result is masking diffusion, explaining its superior performance.
048   Finally in Sec. 7 we unlock the potential of structured and state-dependent discrete diffusion by
049   building SCUD versions of these methods and see that they finally beat masking diffusion (Fig 2).
050   We release our code at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/SCUD-3844/.

      3    L EARNING WHEN AND WHERE TO TRANSITION
135   To fit a discrete diffusion model, the backward process should match the forward in both when it
136   transitions and where it transitions to. One should expect that learning where to transition is hard;
137   on the other hand, since the distribution of when to transition is simple and known a priori in many
138   cases, one should expect learning when to transition should be trivial. We see however in Fig. 3 that
139   this is not necessarily true – state of the art published diffusion models have detectable differences
140   in the transition rates of their forward and backward processes.
141   Unlike previously derived forms of the ELBO which are written as an integral of the discrepancy of
142   the flow at each moment t, with some algebra, we break up the ELBO into discrepancy of when and
143   where to transition. Define the “transition schedule”, S = {t1 , t2 , . . . , tM }, as the set of times at
144   which xt transitions.

      In this section, motivated by Eqn. 2, we describe how to incorporate information about when to
168
      transition into a discrete diffusion model. Ideally we could set q(S) = p(S); however, in general, L
169
      may not have constant transition rates at each state, in which case S may be correlated with x0 and
170   p(S) may be a complex distribution. Instead of looking directly at transitions then, we introduce
171   latent “events” which will act as transitions did above – they occur with constant rate and often result
172   in transitions; in some cases we discuss below, they will coincide exactly with transitions. S will
173   describe the schedule of these events and this is what we’ll condition on.
174
      In Sec. 4.1 we will describe models that condition on these event schedules, SCUD. Next in Sec. 4.2
175
      we will write the loss in a form that is easy to train on high dimensional data. Finally in Sec. 4.3 we
176   will describe how to parameterize and sample from SCUD.

      5     S CHEDULE CONDITIONING TO CONDITION ON TRANSITIONS
296   To incorporate information about transitions into qθ , we wish to condition on the schedule. We
297   described how conditioning on “events” in the previous section allow us to incorporate this structure.
298   However not every event corresponds to a transition. The amount of information about the transitions
299   that we bake into our model depends on the diagonal of K – the probabilities of no transition at an
300   event. In turn the diagonal of K will depend on our choice of the rate of events r. For a fixed L, we
301   can choose any rate r ≥ r∗ = maxb −Lb,b . Let’s parameterize our choices of rate with a parameter
302   γ: let r = γ −1 r∗ . When γ is 1, the rate of events is as slow as possible; when γ → 0, the rate of
303   events goes to ∞.
304   γ controls the diagonal of K and therefore how much we condition on the schedule. We can write
305   K = γL/r∗ + I; the larger γ is, the smaller the diagonal of K. When L is “normalized” so that
306   every entry on the diagonal is the same, γ = 1 coincides with K with zero diagonal; in this case,
307   every event is a transition and we’ve fully conditioned on the transition schedule. On the other hand,
308   as γ → 0, the diagonals of K get closer to 1, so that almost no events result in a transition.
309   We now show that when L is uniform and γ = 1 − 1/D, that is, we nearly fully condition on the
310   schedule, our process is equivalent to masking diffusion. On the other hand, as γ → 0, we learn a
311   backwards process while baking in no information about transitions; we show this recovers classical
312   discrete diffusion exactly.

350   6       R ELATED WORK
351
      Diffusion generative models are state of the art for images and other continuous data (Ho et al.,
352   2020; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Peebles & Xie, 2022), but have so far lagged behind autoregressive
353   models on discrete sequence data like text. Inspired by its success on continuous modalities, a
354   number of works have attempted to extend diffusion to discrete domains. D3PM (Austin et al.,
355   2021), for example, adapts Ho et al. (2020)’s continuous framework and extends early work by
356   Hoogeboom et al. (2021) by introducing a family of categorical noise processes based on structured
357   discrete transition matrices. Our method takes inspiration from the diverse noise processes explored
358   in D3PM but is ultimately more flexible, as our formalism can use any L which converges to a
359   stationary distribution and does not require doubly stochastic matrices.
360   To allow for more flexible sampling and principled model development, a number of methods
361   have extended diffusion from discrete time to continuous time. For example, τ LDR (Campbell
362   et al., 2022) intro a continuous-time Markov chain formulation and a corresponding continuous-
363   time ELBO. In related work, SEDD (Lou et al., 2023) introduced score-matching loss for discrete
364   spaces, intended to parallel score-matching for continuous spaces (Song & Ermon, 2019), which
365   allows flexible continuous time sampling. These models differ primarily in how they are parame-
366
      terized and how they estimate the ELBO objective. SCUD on the other hand is more flexible as it
      only requires that one can calculate matrix vector products with K, or equivalently L. Recently,
367
      many works have chosen to focus purely on masking state diffusion, proposed weighted losses that
368
      have pushed compression metrics closer and closer to numbers obtained from autoregressive models
369   (Sahoo et al., 2024; Shi et al., 2024; Ou et al., 2024). While SCUD is closely related to recent work
370   in continuous-time discrete diffusion, we find that schedule conditioning allows structured noise
371   processes to improve performance and thereby leads to non-masking diffusion with state-of-the-art
372   performance.
373
      In the realm of sampling, Chen et al. (2023) also considered an accelerated procedure for simple
374
      diffusion models in which the transition schedule is sample sampled first followed by the transi-
375   tions conditioned on the schedule, which shares similar motivations with SCUD. SCUD, however,
376   describes how to build in this information into training a model.

      Lastly, inspired by flow-matching developments in image modeling, many authors have begun to
379   propose flow-matching frameworks for discrete data. Campbell et al. (2024b) for instance propose a
380   flow-matching framework that accommodates joint modeling of discrete and continuous modalities,
381   enabling applications in protein design. Similarly, Gat et al. (2024) presents a general framework
382   for learning probability paths on discrete sequences and trains large-scale models on text datasets.
383   Unlike papers on discrete flow matching, we still employ a diffusion framework and use an ELBO
384   loss, but it’s possible that our investigation of schedule conditioning or structured forward processes
385   could yield insights that are also useful for score matching, as many of the underlying modeling
386   methods are shared.

388   7     R ESULTS
389
390   We show that by incorporating information about transitions, SCUD better fits the forward process.
391   We first demonstrate the results of Sec. 5 that SCUD with a uniform forward process interpolates
392   between uniform and masking discrete diffusion. We next show that applying SCUD to state of
393   the art classical discrete diffusion models without schedule conditioning improves their likelihoods
394   on images, text, and protein data. Finally, by building SCUD with forward processes that build in
395   inductive biases, we also show scale that we can improve over SCUD uniform which is similar to
396   masking (Sec. 5.1), thereby unlocking the potential of structured discrete diffusion. Throughout this
397   section, SCUD refers to γ = 1.
398   The structured forward processes we build for each modality will be inspired by those from Austin
399   et al. (2021). However Austin et al. (2021) used processes in discretized time that are not equivalent
400   to and continuous time Markov process; thus we describe new structured processes for continuous
401   time in terms of L or K.
402   In all cases we try to make only minor modifications to the architecture and training parameters
403   from previous models so that differences in scores are due to schedule conditioning. We employed
404   a few strategies so that moving from classical discrete diffusion to SCUD did not add substantial
405   computational overhead, summarized in App. D.5. Other experimental details are in App. D.

       In this section we will describe the structured continuous time Markov processes we used in Sec. 7.
975    Our processes are inspired by those from Austin et al. (2021) and Alamdari et al. (2023); however
976    those works framed the process in discrete time in such a way that they are not related to any contin-
977    uous time Markov model, requiring us to design new processes. Note also that those works modified
978    their processes to ensure that the transition matrix at every time-point was doubly stochastic; this
979    was so that all transition matrices would have the same stationary distribution – a uniform distribu-
980    tion. In our case, we are free to pick any L that converges to a stationary distribution, even if it is
981    not uniform.

989    C.2   N EAREST NEIGHBOUR PROCESS FOR LANGUAGE
990
991    Our vocabulary in the language result was approximately 30’000 tokens from the Bert-base-uncased
992
       tokenizer (Devlin et al., 2018). It is prohibitively expensive to compute a 30′ 000 × 30′ 000 matrix
       K to take matrix vector products during training. Instead, we pick a sparse K built using the
993
       embeddings from Devlin et al. (2018); for the most frequent 1000 words (which make up 95%
994
       of tokens seen in the data) i, j we computed their similarity as viT vj where vi is the normalized
995    embedding of word i. For each word we found the 10 nearest neighbours; we noticed restricting to
996    the top 1000 words resulted in nearest enighbours which were much more semantically similar. We
997    next set, for nearest neighbours,
998                                            L̃i,j = exp(viT vj /0.3).
999
1000
       We next normalized L̃ so that the diagonal is 1 – this ensures that every word has an identical
       transition rate, avoiding the case where a word never transitions because it has no nearby neighbours.
1001
1002   We noticed that it often took a long time for particles to reach a stationary distribution with this
1003   process, so we added occasional transitions across the nearest neighbour graph; we called p the nor-
1004   malized frequencies of the top 1000 words in the data and define the uniform transition infinitesimal
1005   generator
1006
                                               Lunif = 1 ⊗ p − I,
1007   where 1 is the vector of all 1’s; this transitions tokens to a random token based on the final token’s
1008   frequency in the data. We combine our two processes by defining
1009
                                               L = L̃ + 0.4 × Lunif
1010
1011   and normalizing so that the smallest value on the diagonal was −1. We do not store this matrix
1012   explicitly, and only perform matrix operations with sparse matrix products and multiplication with
1013   1 or p.
1014   For tokens outside of the most frequent 1000, we transition using Lunif .

1094   D.5   C OMPUTATIONAL COMPLEXITY
1095
1096   In terms of computational complexity, the major differences between SCUD and classical discrete
1097   diffusion are (A) replacing operations of L with operations of K, and (B) replacing the time t in the
1098   argument of x̃0,θ with the number of transitions S. We discuss how (B) does not result in a large
1099   increase of computational complexity below, and note that (A) does not change the computational
1100
       complexity except when the number of tokens B is large, when it actually enables strategies that
       reduce complexity.
1101
1102

1103

1104   exponentiation exp(tL) (Luo et al., 2022). When B is small, both these calculations have negligible
1105   complexity and can be calculated similarly quickly by precomputing an eigen-decomposition of
1106   K or L. But when B is large, as in the language modeling case, these calculations become very
1107   expensive; Luo et al., 2022 settled for very simple L, masking and uniform, such that exp(tL) can
1108   be easily analytically calculated; SCUD is able to build in a richer forward process by picking a
1109   sparse + low rank K so that matrix vector products are very fast.

1120

1121   replacement were negligible compared to the operations and memory spent on operations on the
1122   D-dimensional xt . Above we used previous architectures modified so that each operation on t was
1123   also applied to each dimension of S. As well, for the architectures we chose, whenever a function of
1124   t was added or multiplied to a set of activations, say at layer ℓ, hℓ,θ , the activations had a dimension
1125   D, so we could perform the same operation with element-wise addition or multiplication with S, i.e.
1126            hdℓ+1,θ = f1,θ (t)hdℓ,θ + f2,θ (t) was replaced with hdℓ+1,θ = f1,θ (sdt )hdℓ,θ + f2,θ (sdt ).
1127   Thus, adapting x̃0,θ for SCUD in this way adds no extra parameters. The overhead of this change
1128   is that every call to fθ is replaced by D calls, D-times the activations fθ (sdt ) must be stored, and
1129   D-times more gradients must be calculated for fθ (sdt ). fθ is however a set of linear functions and
1130   activations. The operations on the corrupted data xt involve convolutions and attention, which have
1131   much larger memory and computational costs. In big-O notation, the cost of calculating x̃0,θ (xt , t)
1132   and x̃0,θ (xt , S) are therefore identical – at worst, the constant in front of the largest term changes.
1133   Therefore, in our experiments, we ran all models for roughly equal time with the same batch sizes
       and did not observe any substantial difference in computation.
