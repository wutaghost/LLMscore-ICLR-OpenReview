011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Options are widely used financial derivatives for risk management and corporate
014            operations. Option hedging aims to mitigate investment risks from asset price
015            fluctuations by buying and selling other financial products. Traditional hedging
016            strategies based on the Black-Scholes model face practical limitations due to the
017            assumptions of constant volatility and the neglect of transaction costs. Recently,
018
               reinforcement learning(RL) has gained attention in the study of option hedging
               strategies, but several challenges remain: current methods rely on real-time mar-
019
               ket data (e.g., underlying asset prices, holdings, remaining option term) to de-
020
               termine optimal positions, underutilizing the potential value of historical data;
021            existing approaches focus on the expected hedging cost, overlooking the compre-
022            hensive distribution of costs; In the aspect of training data generation, commonly
023            used single simulation methods perform well under specific conditions but strug-
024            gle to ensure the robustness of the model across diverse datasets. To address these
025            issues, we propose a novel distributional RL option hedging method that incorpo-
026            rates historical information. Historical states are included in the state variables,
027            with a gated recurrent unit (GRU) network layer extracting historical information.
028            This is then combined with current information from fully connected layers to
029
               inform subsequent network layers, ensuring the agent considers both current and
               historical market information when learning hedging strategies. The output of the
030
               value network is set as a series of quantiles, with the Quantile Huber Loss func-
031
               tion fitting their distribution to evaluate strategies based on distribution rather than
032            expected value. To diversify data sources, we use a combination of the Black-
033            Scholes model, the Binomial model, and the Heston model to simulate a large
034            volume of option data. Experimental results show that our method significantly
035            reduces hedging costs and demonstrates strong adaptability and practicality under
036            various market conditions.

039   1   I NTRODUCTION
040
041
      Options are tradable financial derivatives that grant the holder the right, but not the obligation, to
042   buy or sell a certain asset at a specified price at a future date. They play a significant role in risk
043   management and corporate operations(Xiao et al., 2021). In options-related trading, option hedging
044   is one of the most closely watched issues(Mandelli et al., 2023), primarily aimed at controlling
045   investment risk. Option hedging primarily refers to the process where the option seller takes certain
046   measures to reduce potential risks after earning a premium from selling the option. When the option
047   expires, if the buyer exercises the option, the seller must sell or buy the asset at the agreed price,
048   which may be unfavorable for the seller. To mitigate this potential risk, sellers often adopt option
049   hedging strategies, involving the buying or selling of other related financial instruments to offset the
050
      risk caused by asset price fluctuations.
051   Traditional hedging strategies, such as delta hedging based on the Black-Scholes (BS) model(Black
052   & Scholes, 1973), use delta values to obtain the optimal position of the underlying asset. However,
053   the assumptions of the BS model regarding constant volatility, continuous trading, and frictionless
      markets do not hold in reality, as the volatility of the underlying asset is constantly changing and


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      there are transaction costs and the inability to trade continuously in the actual market. Therefore,
055   such methods have significant limitations in practical applications.
056
057   To address the aforementioned issues, in recent years, using reinforcement learning(RL) methods
058
      for option hedging has become a research hotspot. RL leverages large amounts of data and con-
      tinuously interacts with the environment, allowing for the adjustment of hedging strategies based
059
      on environmental feedback, which resolves the issue that traditional option hedging models cannot
060
      fully reflect real market conditions(Hambly et al., 2023).
061
062   In the design of option hedging based on RL, the underlying asset price, the holding of the underlying
063   asset, etc. are typically considered as the states, while the trading volume or change in the holding
064
      of the underlying asset is treated as the action. The profit and loss(P&L) or changes in cash flow
      during the option hedging process are considered as the reward. The basic framework is illustrated
065
      in Figure 1. In this process, the setting of state variables, the estimation of the value function, and
066
      the collection of training data are three main issues.

      In terms of state variables, previous research has primarily focused on the current market information
082   (such as the price of the underlying asset, the holding of the underlying asset, and the time to
083   expiration when hedging). This means that the agent decides the optimal holding for hedging based

086   on the state at s2 , independent of the state at s1 , and so on. However, in actual hedging, considering
087   historical market information is also important. This means that at time t2 , the hedging decision
088   should take into account not only the market information at t2 but also before t2 , and so forth.

103   In terms of value function, previous research has primarily focused on the expected value of hedging
104   costs (as shown in the right of Figure 2). While this approach of focusing solely on the expected
105   value of hedging costs provides an average view of hedging costs under a given strategy, it fails to
106   reflect the overall distribution of hedging costs. In practice, the distribution of hedging costs is cru-
107   cial for evaluating the effectiveness of a hedging strategy. Therefore, ignoring the cost distribution
      when evaluating hedging strategies may increase the risk of strategy execution.

108
      Regarding data, RL methods necessitates extensive data. in practice, due to factors such as dif-
109   ferent issuance times and durations of options, the available option data is insufficient to meet this
110   requirement. As a result, research often uses finance parametric methods to generate data. However,
111   existing studies typically use a single method to generate data for training. Such approach can lead
112   to a classic problem in financial engineering: the model’s performance will primarily reflect accu-
113   racy on this type of simulated data, while performance may degrade when using other data(Lillicrap
114   et al., 2019).
115   To solve the issues in option hedging based on RL mentioned above, we propose a new distributional
116   RL method based on historical information for option hedging. The main contributions includes:
117
118
      (1)Incorporating Historical Information into State Variables: Addressing the issue where past
      research only considers current market information as the state, we include historical information
119
      into the state. We introduce GRU and leverage its memory function to extract historical information.
120
      The extracted historical information is then combined with the current information extracted by fully
121   connected layer and fed into subsequent network layers. This enables the agent to consider both
122   current and historical information when learning the option hedging strategy.
123
124
      (2)Estimating the Distribution of Hedging Costs: Tackling the problem where past research
      mainly focuses on the expected value of hedging costs, we employ a set of quantiles to estimate
125
      the hedging costs in the value network instead of estimating the expected value. In other words, we
126
      treat the output of the value network as a distribution and use quantile regression combined with the
127   Huber loss function to fit quantiles. This approach aims to estimate the distribution of hedging costs.
128
129   (3)Generating Simulated Data with Multiple Methods: To address the issue of using a single
130
      method to generate simulated data in past research, we combine multiple option pricing methods to
      generate simulated option data for training. By doing so, the agent is exposed to a richer variety of
131
      samples during the learning process, enhancing its adaptability to different market conditions.

134   2   R ELATED W ORK
135
136   Halperin (2020) was the first to apply RL to address dynamic option hedging. He proposed QLBS
137   model based on Q-Learning(Watkins, 1989), which provides an effective method for option pricing
138   and hedging without the constraint of continuous time. However, this approach is only effective in
139   finite state and action space and still assumes frictionless markets. To avoid the curse of dimension-
140   ality, Kolm & Ritter (2019) used SARSA(Sutton & Barto, 2018) to consider nonlinear trading costs
141   in continuous state space, making agent effective for hedging in environments that more closely
142
      resemble real markets. With the advancement of RL, Du et al. (2020) applied more advanced algo-
      rithms such as DQN(Mnih et al., 2013) and PPO(Schulman et al., 2017) for discrete option hedging,
143
      also addressing the issue of integer stocks and incorporating strike prices as additional state vari-
144
      ables. Furthermore, in their model training, the chosen strike prices were no longer fixed but rather
145   within a given range. This allowed the agent to train on a range of strike prices without retraining
146   for each specific strike price within that range. Although discretizing actions simplifies training pro-
147   cess, it also introduces significant errors. To address it, Cao et al. (2020) employed DDPG(Lillicrap
148   et al., 2019), which allows for continuous state and action spaces for option hedging, providing more
149   precise action outputs. They still used the current information as state variables. Additionally, they
150   used two value networks to estimate the expected value and its square of the hedging cost to calcu-
151   late the utility function. Their experiments under P&L and cash-flow showed the former was more
152   effective than the latter. In response to same problem, Mikkilä (2020) used TD3, and add moneyness
153
      and implied volatility to state variables. Addtionally, they introduced sim-to-real method(training on
      simulated data and testing on real data). Similarly, Giurca & Borovkova (2021) also used sim-to-real
154
      in DQN and DDPG. Both of them found that RL also outperformed traditional methods in actual
155
      data. Due to the uncertainty in financial markets, Zheng et al. (2023) incorporated uncertainty into
156   DDPG to develop more robust hedging strategies. Their results showed that accounting for uncer-
157   tainty provided better solutions compared to Cao et al. (2020). Similarly, to further align with real
158   market, Neagu et al. (2024) integrated market shocks into the state space. Their findings indicated
159   that incorporating market shocks into the state space helps better adapt to the market environment.
160   Previous research on option hedging relied directly or indirectly used parametric models to generate
161   data for training, to address this issue, Mikkilä & Kanniainen (2023) directly trained the model using
      actual data. Their results indicate that training DRL models directly on real data performs better.


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Contrary to them, Cannelli et al. (2023) solved this problem by using the more efficient CMAB algo-
163   rithm, but the data was still generated by GBM and BS models. In practice, effective option hedging
164   requires more than just current market conditions, historical market dynamics are crucial as well.
165   However, existing methods using RL often rely solely on current information, ignoring historical
166   data. Furthermore, when evaluating hedging strategies, it’s important to consider both the expected
167   hedging costs and the associated risks, but current methods tend to focus on the expected value of
168   costs while overlooking their distributional characteristics. Additionally, most research uses the BS
169   model for simulating option data, which lacks diversity and may result in strategies that perform
170   poorly on other types of data. Therefore, it is important to develop RL methods for option hedging
171
      that incorporate historical information, cost distribution, and data diversity.

361   4.1   DATA
362
      In the actual market, although there are options with the same strike price and expiration date, they
363
      cannot be considered identical due to factors such as the time of issuance and different remaining
364   periods. This results in a relatively limited amount of data for options with specific strike prices
365   and expiration dates in practice. However, in RL, the process by which the agent learns an option
366   hedging strategy requires a large amount of data. Therefore, we are considering using financial
367   models to simulate a substantial amount of underlying asset data and option data.
368
      For the underlying data, we use geometric Brownian motion to simulate its price. For option data,
369
      if only one method is used, the option hedging strategy learned by the agent may exhibit superior
370   performance for the data generated by that method, but performance may decline for data generated
371   by other methods. Therefore, we use three methods to simulate option prices: the BS model(Black
372   & Scholes, 1973), the BI model(Cox et al., 1979), and the Heston model(Heston, 2015). The data
373   generated by these three methods are then combined and provided to the agent for learning.

463   in option hedging. Moreover, when using RL methods for option hedging, generating option-related
464   simulation data using various methods can enhance the agent’s ability to adapt to market diversity.

      5   C ONCLUSION
530
531
      This paper proposes a new distributional reinforcement learning method based on historical infor-
      mation for option hedging. First, the historical state is included in the state variable, and the GRU
532
      is used to extract historical information, which is combined with the current information extracted
533
      from the full connection layer and then provided to the subsequent network layer for learning, to en-
534   sure that the agent can consider both current and historical market information when learning option
535   hedging; Secondly, we set the output of the value network as a set of quantiles, and use Quantile
536   Huber Loss function to fit the distribution of its evaluation, to evaluate the advantages and disadvan-
537   tages of the strategy from the distribution rather than the expected value; Then, in order to make the
538   data sources of agents diverse in the process of learning strategies, we use BS model, BI model and
539   Heston model to simulate a large number of option data. Finally, the experimental results show that
      this method can significantly reduce hedging costs.
