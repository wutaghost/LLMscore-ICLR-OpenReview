009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Tree ensemble methods provide promising predictions with models difficult to
012            interpret. Recent introduction of Shapley values for individualized feature contri-
013            butions, accompanied with several fast computing algorithms for predicted values,
014            shows intriguing results. However, individualizing coefficients of determination,
015            aka R2 , for each feature is challenged by the underlying quadratic losses, although
016            these coefficients allow us to comparatively assess single feature’s contribution to
017            tree ensembles. Here we propose an efficient algorithm, Q-SHAP, that reduces the
018            computational complexity to polynomial time when calculating Shapley values
019
               related to quadratic losses. Our extensive simulation studies demonstrate that
               this approach not only enhances computational efficiency but also improves the
020
               estimation accuracy of feature-specific coefficients of determination.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Models built with tree ensembles are powerful but often complicated, making it challenging to
027
      understand the influence of inputs. Feature importance plays a critical role in demystifying these
      models and enhancing their interpretability by assigning each input feature a score. This is crucial in
028
      domains like healthcare and biomedicine, where trust and interpretation of the model are essential
029
      (Stiglic et al., 2020; Bussmann et al., 2021). Common feature importance measures like gain can be
030   inconsistent (Lundberg and Lee, 2017b) while permutation importance lacks theoretical foundations
031   (Ishwaran, 2007).
032
033
      Shapley values, derived from cooperative game theory and introduced by Shapley (1953), offer a
      robust method for the fair distribution of payoffs generated by a coalition of players. This can be
034
      analogously applied to assess the contribution of each feature in a machine learning model. It ensures
035
      that each feature’s contribution is assessed by considering all possible combinations of features,
036   thereby providing a comprehensive understanding of feature impacts. Recent applications of Shapley
037   values have focused on local interpretation (Lundberg and Lee, 2017a;b; Chau et al., 2022), where
038   they are employed to examine the influence of individual features on specific predictions. Nonetheless,
039   there are numerous scenarios where global importance is preferred, such as analysis of the role of a
040   feature across the entire dataset (Molnar, 2020; Covert et al., 2020).
041
      Among the works that compute Shapley values in a global context, a popular approach is to use
042   model variance decomposition. Lipovetsky and Conklin (2001) decomposed R2 in linear regression,
043   offering consistent interpretations even in the presence of multicollinearity. Owen and Prieur (2017)
044   also conducted a conceptual analysis of Shapley values for the variance. However, computation
045   remains a significant challenge, as the calculation of Shapley values grows exponentially with the
046   number of features. To address this issue, several Monte Carlo-based methods have been proposed to
047   effectively reduce the computational burden (Song et al., 2016; Covert et al., 2020; Williamson and
048   Feng, 2020).
049   Although Monte Carlo-based, model-agnostic methods are more efficient than brute-force approaches,
050   they are still computationally intensive, especially when dealing with high-dimensional data that
051   requires extensive feature permutation sampling to ensure consistency (Lundberg and Lee, 2017a;
052   Lundberg et al., 2020). This challenge has prompted the development of methods that leverage
053   the specific structures of tree-based models. However, much of the focus has been on explaining
      individual predictions, as seen with TreeSHAP (Lundberg and Lee, 2017b), FastTreeSHAP (Yang,


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      2021), and LinearTreeSHAP (Bifet et al., 2022). Bénard et al. (2022) considered population-level
055   importance using R2 , specifically tailored for random forests (Breiman, 2001).
056
057   To the best of our knowledge, there is no available method to calculate Shapley values of quadratic
058
      losses by leveraging structures of decision trees for fast computation. In this paper, we propose
      Q-SHAP, which can decompose quadratic terms of predicted values of a decision tree into each
059
      feature’s attribute in polynomial time. It leads to fast computation of feature-specific R2 for a decision
060
      tree. We also extend our approach to Gradient Boosted Decision Trees.
061
062   The rest of the paper is structured as follows. In Section 2, we provide a brief overview of Shapley
063   values of R2 . In Section 3, we present our proposed algorithm Q-SHAP to calculate Shapley values of
064
      R2 in polynomial time for single trees, and then extend the approach for tree ensembles in Section 4.
      We justify the efficacy and efficiency of the algorithm using extensive simulations in Section 5
065
      and real data analysis in high dimension in Section 6. We conclude the paper with a discussion in
066
      Section 7.

      3     T HE A LGORITHM Q-SHAP FOR S INGLE T REES
147
148   Unlike most regression problems that can yield infinitely many predictions across the diverse input
149
      space X, decision trees restrict predictions to a finite set, specifically to the values at each leaf
      node. This nature revitalizes hope in previously unattainable solutions to Shapley values in decision
150
      tree-based models. The main idea of our algorithm lies in the fact that Shapley values with various
151
      targets such as predictions and various loss functions, are essentially weighted functions of the leaf
152   predictions. While Lundberg et al. (2020) suggests that explaining the loss function for a “path-
153   dependent” algorithm is challenging, we provide an exact solution to decomposing quadratic losses
154   using our Q-SHAP. Q-SHAP works for tree ensembles. For simplicity, we here illustrate it with a
155   single decision tree.

293   roots of unity because of their numerical stability and fast operations in matrix multiplications. Due
294   to the potential issue of ill condition, especially at large degrees, our calculation avoids inversion of

464   We illustrate the utility of Q-SHAP by applying it to predicting Gleason score, a grading prognosis of
465
      men with prostate cancer, with gene expressions. The dataset was obtained from The Cancer Genome
      Atlas Program (TCGA) (Weinstein et al., 2013), including 551 samples and 17,261 features. The
466
      Gleason score was retrieved through TCGAbiolinks (Colaprico et al., 2016) and further adjusted
467
      for age and race as potential confounding factors. The gene expression data was downloaded from
468   UCSC Xena (Goldman et al., 2020) and preprocessed using SIGNET (Jiang et al., 2023).
469
470   We first constructed the tree ensemble using XGBoost with tuning parameters optimized via 5-fold
471
      cross-validation and random research in a parameter space specified with the number of trees in
      {50, 100, 500, 1000, 1500, 2000, 2500, 3000}, maximum depth in {1, 2, · · · , 6}, and learning rate in
472
      {0.01, 0.05, 0.1}. As both SAGE and SPVIM cannot manage this large number of features, we only
473
      applied Q-SHAP to the tree ensemble built on 17,261 features, which took 11 minutes to compute
474   and reported the sum of all feature-specific R2 at 99.98% which is equivalent to the model R2 . The
475   15 highest feature-specific R2 values are reported in Figure 6.
476
477
      To allow the application of both SAGE and SPVIM, we selected the top 100 features based on the
      result of Q-SHAP, and rebuilt the tree ensemble with the selected 100 features using XGBoost. The
478
      rebuilt tree ensemble reported a total of R2 at 0.93. Calculating the 100 feature-specific R2 values
479
      took about 10 seconds of Q-SHAP, 16 minutes of SAGE, and 67 hours of SPVIM. As shown in
480   Figures 7-9, SPVIM tends to overstate the feature-specific R2 although all of its feature-specific
481   R2 only sums up to 0.72, much lower than 0.93 reported by Q-SHAP. On the other hand, SAGE
482   tends to underestimate the feature-specific R2 and all its feature-specific R2 also sums up to only
483   0.73, but its top 15 features match well with those by Q-SHAP. We also applied the three methods to
484   two additional datasets, and the results are summarized in Appendix F. In summary, the real data
485   analysis shows consistent results with the simulation study, confirming that Q-SHAP is superior in
      both computational time and the accuracy of feature-specific R2 .

      7          C ONCLUSION
516
517   The coefficient of determination, aka R2 , measures the proportion of the total variation explained by
518
      available features. Its additive decomposition, following Shapley (1953), provides an ideal evaluation
      of each feature’s attribute to explain the total variation. However, the calculation of corresponding
519
      Shapley values is an NP-hard problem, and is further complicated by the complexities involved
520
      in building tree ensembles. Recently, several methods (Lundberg and Lee, 2017b; Yang, 2021;
521   Bifet et al., 2022) have been developed to leverage the structure of tree-based models and provide
522   computationally efficient algorithms to decompose the predicted values. However, decomposing
523   R2 demands the decomposition of a quadratic loss reduction by multiple trees. We have shown
524   in Section 4 that we can attribute the total loss reduction by the tree ensemble to each single tree,
525   and the tree-specific loss reductions are subject to further decomposition to each feature. However,
526   decomposing a quadratic loss of a single tree needs work with the squared terms of predicted values,
527   invalidating previously developed methods for predicted values. Thus we developed the Q-SHAP
528   algorithm to calculate Shapley values of squared terms of predicted values in polynomial time. The
529
      algorithm works not only for R2 but also for general quadratic losses. Ultimately, it may provide a
      framework for more general loss functions via approximation.

       E    C OMPLEXITY OF THE A LGORITHM
1064
1065
1066
       Here we assume that the dataset includes n samples as well as p features, and a total of T trees are
1067
       constructed with the maximum tree depth D and maximum tree leaves L. We denote S the number
1068   of permutations taken in SAGE with S = 1020 by default. Then, when the trees are constructed
1069   by XGBoost, the complexity of SAGE is O(T DSpn) (Covert et al., 2020), and the complexity of
1070   SPVIM is O(T Dpn2 log n) (Williamson and Feng, 2020). Instead, the complexity of Q-SHAP is
1071   O(T L2 D2 n) which doesn’t rely on the number of features p.
1072
       Let’s first consider the complexity of Q-SHAP in Algorithm 1 for a single tree and one sample. As
1073
       shown in Figure 19, the two outer loops that iterate through the tree leaves, result in a complexity
1074   of O(L2 ). Within the inner loop, the computation involves the number of features induced by each
1075   pair of leaves, leading to O(D) operations. The evaluation of t[j] involves the computation of
1076   C(z) · P (z), which takes O(D) operations since the number of union features between two leaves is
1077   bounded by 2D. Combining these, the overall complexity for one tree and one sample is O(L2 D2 ).
1078   Thus, for the whole dataset, the complexity of Q-SHAP scales to O(nL2 D2 ) for a single tree. With
1079   the advancements introduced in Section 4, Q-SHAP has a total complexity of O(T nL2 D2 ) for the
       ensemble of T boosting trees.
