                                                  A BSTRACT
013
014
               Satellite images are increasingly valuable for modeling regional climate change.
015            Earth surface forecasting is one task that combines satellite imagery and meteoro-
016            logical data to understand how climate evolves over time. However, understand-
017            ing the complex relationship between meteorological variables and land surface
018            changes remains a challenge. Our paper introduces a pipeline that integrates prin-
019            ciples from perturbation-based techniques like LIME and global explainability
020            techniques methods like PDP, addressing the limitations of these techniques in
021            high-dimensional spatiotemporal models. This pipeline facilitates analyses such
022            as marginal sensitivity, correlation, and lag analysis, etc for complex land forecast-
023
               ing models. Using ConvLSTM for surface forecasting, we analyzed influence of
               variables like temperature, pressure, and precipitation on the NDVI of the surface
024
               predictions. Our study in EarthNet2021 Dataset (primarily consists of samples
025
               from the European Alps region, collected during the spring to fall seasons) re-
026            vealed that precipitation had the greatest impact, followed by temperature, while
027            pressure has little to no direct effect on NDVI. Additionally, interesting nonlinear
028            correlations between meteorological variables and NDVI have been uncovered.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      The Earth’s climate is undergoing a significant transformation, posing a substantial threat to human
      existence. Its detrimental effects on terrestrial surfaces, which sustain most life on our planet, are
034
      becoming increasingly evident. From the depletion of Arctic sea ice Shokr & Ye (2023) to the
035
      intensification of fire incidents Lampe et al. (2023), the repercussions of climate change manifest
036   across diverse and variable geographic regions. Studying the effects of meteorological variables like
037   temperature, precipitation, and pressure is central to climate change analysis.
038
039
      Over the past decade, there has been a notable increase in satellite sensors, leading to the availabil-
      ity of Earth observation data on an unprecedented scale. Initiatives like the Copernicus program
040
      Geudtner et al. (2014) offer high-resolution data with enhanced temporal coverage, enabling the
041
      generation of dense predictions and analyses that were previously unattainable. Land surface fore-
042   casting using spatiotemporal forecasting models plays a crucial role in predicting changes in surface
043   conditions over time, such as vegetation growth Beyer et al. (2023) van Oorschot et al. (2023), soil
044   moisture levels Chrysanthopoulos et al. (2023) Zhang et al. (2023), and land use patterns Witjes et al.
045   (2022) Wang et al. (2023). However, interpreting the outputs of these models and understanding the
046   factors driving their predictions pose significant challenges, particularly in the context of complex
047   spatiotemporal data and high-dimensional feature spaces.
048   Explainability has emerged as a critical requirement for ensuring the transparency, trustworthiness,
049   and reliability of machine learning models, including those used in land surface forecasting. Tradi-
050   tional explainability techniques, such as Local Interpretable Model-agnostic Explanations (LIME)
051   Ribeiro et al. (2016) and partial dependence plots (PDP) Friedman (2001) etc, have limitations
052   when applied to land surface forecasting models. These models often operate in high-dimensional
053   spatiotemporal feature spaces, making it challenging to isolate the effects of individual variables on
      model predictions.

054
      We introduce the Cluster-Segregate-Perturb (CSP) pipeline, a novel approach to explainability in
055   land surface forecasting models. CSP pipelines clustering, segregation, and perturbation to enable
056   comprehensive investigative analyses of model predictions. By clustering instances of meteorologi-
057   cal variables, we identify unique patterns within each meteorological variable. After clustering, the
058   data samples are segregated based on the earlier clusters. This segregation organizes the data into
059   segments we termed, weather segments, representing smaller, more homogeneous subsets of the
060   dataset based on distinct sets of meteorological conditions. Finally, by perturbing the meteorologi-
061   cal variables of data samples within each segment, the pipeline creates artificial samples representing
062   variations from the original data. These artificial samples make the analyses more robust.
063   In this paper, we present the development and evaluation of the CSP pipeline for investigative anal-
064   yses on land surface forecasting models. We demonstrate its effectiveness through empirical evalu-
065   ations in uncovering the relationships between meteorological variables and land surface evolution
066   via NDVI.

      2     C HALLENGES IN E XPLAINABILITY OF S PATIOTEMPORAL L AND S URFACE
069
070
            F ORECASTING M ODELS
071          • The primary challenge lies in managing the temporal dynamics of meteorological variables.
072            Unlike global explainability techniques like PDP, which use simple artificial samples to as-
073            sess the marginal effects of features, generating realistic high-dimensional spatiotemporal
074            data is complex due to the curse of dimensionality. Even if natural-looking spatiotemporal
075            samples are created, the reliability of a pre-trained model’s predictions can be compromised
076            if it has not been trained on data reflecting similar patterns. This issue is prevalent in com-
077            plex models with high-dimensional latent spaces, where approximating the distribution is
078            often sparse and difficult.
079
               To address these challenges, we applied perturbations to the original training data, keeping
               them within natural weather patterns. This approach generated realistic data points, allow-
080
               ing the model to make robust predictions without the need for creating artificial samples
081
               from scratch.
082
083
             • Another challenge is handling weather-based variations. As we know, within a season it-
               self, different temperature, pressure, and precipitation patterns will affect the land surface
084
               evolution differently. Therefore, any analysis without properly segregating these natural
085
               weather patterns might yield incorrect aggregated values.
086            To address this limitation, we divided the data into weather segments—smaller, more ho-
087            mogeneous subsets based on specific meteorological conditions. This approach allowed
088            us to better understand the model’s behavior across different weather scenarios, similar to
089            LIME, but operating at the segment level instead of the sample level.

      3.1    E XPLAINABLE AI
095
      Explainable AI (XAI) techniques have been developed to address the lack of interpretability in deep
096   learning models, including Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and LSTM models. These tech-
097   niques aim to provide insights into the decision-making process of these models. One approach is to
098   use saliency maps, feature attribution, and local interpretable model-agnostic explanations (LIME)
099   to enhance transparency and trustworthiness, another approach involves modifying the architecture
100   of CNNs to improve interoperability, such as in Habib et al. (2022) Habib et al. uses sinc-convolution
101   layers and explanation vectors to identify domain-specific insights and in De la Fuente et al. (2023)
102   authors proposed a modification of the LSTM architecture called HydroLSTM, which enhances the
103   interpretability of the model by representing internal system processes in a manner analogous to a
104
      hydrological reservoir.
105   Quantifying the global relationship between input features and model predictions in time-series
106   image forecasting presents a significant challenge and remains an active area of research and the
107   attention given to model explainability in time-series applications has not been as significant as in
      the fields of computer vision or natural language processing Rojat et al. (2021). Huang et al. in


                                                         2

      Huang et al. (2022) suggest two techniques for explainability in the spatiotemporal predictive learn-
109   ing task (SPLT): first, the synthesis of multiple independent components to analyze how the features
110   contribute to the prediction; and second, a state decomposition and expansion technique to separate
111   intertwined signals in the spatiotemporal dynamical system helping in exploring the mechanisms
112   underlying motion formation. they concluded that a collaboration mechanism, namely, extending
113   the present and erasing the past (EPEP), explains the motion formation in SPLT. Duckham et al. in
114   Duckham et al. (2022) utilises the Simple Event Model and PROV-O ontologies to enable queries

116   the system. This capability is embedded in the NEXUS system, which combines multiple reasoning

      proposed an interpretable and modular framework for unsupervised and weakly-supervised proba-

      with computational geometric techniques. Pham et al. in Pham et al. (2023) proposed Temporally
120   Weighted Spatiotemporal Explainable Neural Network for Multivariate Time Series (TSEM) merges
121   RNN and CNN capabilities by using RNN hidden units to weigh the temporal axis of CNN feature
122   maps. It matches STAM’s accuracy and fulfils several interpretability standards, including causality,
123   fidelity, and spatiotemporality.

125   3.2   E XPLORING M ETEOROLOGICAL VARIABLES THROUGH NDVI A NALYSIS
126
127   Several studies have examined the sensitivity, correlation, lag, etc of meteorological variables such
128   as precipitation and temperature concerning NDVI. Li and Guo, in Li & Guo (2010), analyzed the
129
      response characteristics and sensitivity of NDVI to climatic factors in Tianjin, China. They found
      that NDVI increases with rising temperatures but gradually decreases with increasing precipitation.
130
      Moreover, the impact of temperature on NDVI was more pronounced in spring and autumn, while
131
      precipitation had a dominant effect in spring, autumn, and early summer. Shun et al. Pan et al.
132   (2019), concluded that NDVI exhibits a higher correlation with air temperature in high-altitude
133   alpine and plateau areas, whereas it correlates more strongly with precipitation in grassland and
134   desert grassland regions. Yujie et al. in Yang et al. (2019), concluded that precipitation was iden-
135   tified as the most significant factor affecting vegetation evolution, followed by temperature, land
136   cover change, population, elevation, and nightlight. Hao et al. Hao et al. (2012) studied the link
137   between climatic variables and NDVI in the upper stream of the Yellow River and a strong ‘corre-
138   lation between NDVI and precipitation for grassland and forest. Their results suggest that higher
139   precipitation levels lead to elevated NDVI values. Additionally, the monthly highest temperature
140
      and precipitation significantly affected NDVI. Similarly, Wang et al. in Wang et al. (2001) explored
      the spatial distribution and year-to-year changes in NDVI across the central Great Plains and found
141
      a clear link between NDVI patterns and average annual precipitation. A strong correlation was ob-
142
      served between NDVI and precipitation deviations during dry years, like 1989, following another
143   dry year. Conversely, a weak correlation was observed during wet years, such as 1993, one of the
144   wettest on record. These findings indicate higher correlation coefficients during or after dry periods.
145   In Feng et al. (2021), Jianming et al. . studied the time accumulation effect of meteorological vari-
146   ables on NDVI. They observed a positive correlation between NDVI and accumulated temperature,
147   accumulated precipitation, and effective accumulated precipitation.

      7   L IMITATIONS
493
494   While the CSP pipeline is simple, model-agnostic and inherently generic, the approach can have
495   some limitations.
496
497          • The quality of results in the CSP pipeline depends heavily on selecting the appropriate
498            clustering method. Additionally, the performance of these methods is highly sensitive to
499
               hyperparameter settings, such as the number of clusters or the choice of distance metric.
               Poorly tuned parameters can result in suboptimal clustering, which negatively impacts the
500
               overall analysis.
501
502
             • Limited research in the field of Spatiotemporal clustering. In Ansari et al. (2020) Ansari
               et al. categorised spatiotemporal clustering into six broad categories however the review fo-
503
               cuses more on individual spatiotemporal points, such as events, geo-referenced data items,
504
               time series, moving objects, and trajectories unlike which most land surface prediction
505            dataset contains sequences of satellite images and meteorological data. For clustering en-
506            tire spatiotemporal samples, the technique would need to be adjusted to emphasize the
507            clustering of these samples as a whole, rather than focusing solely on the characteristics of
508            individual points. Time complexity would be another crucial aspect to consider.
509          • Limited research in the field of perturbations for attaining meaningful transformations.
510            Most existing techniques, such as adversarial perturbation, Gaussian noise injection, etc
511            add random noise to enhance model’s robustness. In contrast, the CSP pipeline requires
512            perturbations for meaningful transformations tailored to specific analytical goals. For ex-
513            ample, given a real state cost prediction model using satellite imagery, one might ask how
514            the average cost of the scene changes when buildings become more compact or more green
515            space is added. while techniques like Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) may also be bene-
516
               ficial, they can be difficult to train.
517
518   8   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
519
520   This paper presented a pipeline to improve the explainability of complex land surface prediction
521   models like ConvLSTM. The proposed methodology enables various investigative analyses, enhanc-
522   ing our understanding of the relationship between meteorological variables and model predictions.
523   Our analysis revealed that NDVI exhibits the highest marginal sensitivity against precipitation, fol-
524
      lowed by temperature and pressure, with approximate ratios of 12:2:1. Moreover, we observed a
      nonlinear correlation between NDVI and meteorological variables, resembling a parabolic curve.
525
      Furthermore, as the average NDVI of the scene increases, the influence of precipitation and temper-
526
      ature on the curvature of the correlation curve diminishes. Additionally, it is concluded that pressure
527   has little to no direct effect on NDVI.
528
529   In the future, we aim to pursue further advanced studies. This involves exploring the transitional
530
      impacts of meteorological variables by using techniques like Perlin noise Perlin (1985) which can
      ensure smoother interpolations, alongside conducting lag analysis. Nonetheless, the challenges in
531
      the clustering process need to be researched more, It is imperative to cluster samples based on
532
      factors other than meteorological variables, such as crop type, elevation, building density, etc and
533   downsampling might not be the option every time, to address this obstacle, we intend to develop
534   a spatiotemporal deep clustering method, thus enhancing the methodology’s adaptability to handle
535   more diverse datasets.
