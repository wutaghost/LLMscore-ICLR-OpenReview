031                                                          A BSTRACT
032
033
034               Current text-to-video (T2V) models have made significant progress in generating
035
                  high-quality video. However, these models are limited when it comes to generating
                  dynamic video scenes where the description can vary dramatically from frame to
036
                  frame. Changing the colour, shape, position and state of objects in the scene is
037
                  a challenge that current video models cannot handle. In addition, the lack of an
038               inexpensive image-based conditioning mechanism limits their creative application.
039               To address these challenges and extend the applicability of T2V models, we pro-
040               pose two innovative approaches: State Guidance and Image Guidance. State
041               Guidance uses advanced guidance mechanisms to control motion dynamics and
042               scene transformation smoothness by navigating the diffusion process between a
043               state triplet ⟨initial state, transition state, final state⟩. This mechanism enables the
044               generation of dynamic video scenes (Dynamic Scene T2V) and allows to control
045               the speed and expressiveness of the scene transformation by introducing temporal
046
                  dynamics through a guidance weighting schedule over video frames. Image Guid-
                  ance enables Zero-Shot Image-to-Video generation (Zero-Shot I2V) by injecting
047
                  reference image noise predictions into the initial diffusion steps. Furthermore, the
048
                  combination of State Guidance and Image Guidance allows zero-shot transitions
049               between two input reference frames of a video (Zero-Shot II2V). Finally, we intro-
050               duce the novel Dynamic Scene Benchmark to evaluate the ability of the models to
051               generate dynamic video scenes. Extensive experiments show that State Guidance
052               and Image Guidance successfully address the aforementioned challenges and
053               significantly improve the generation capabilities of existing T2V architectures.

      1      I NTRODUCTION
055
056
      Text-to-Video (T2V) generation is a rapidly growing area of computer graphics that aims to generate
057
      photorealistic videos from input text prompt. These generated videos have tremendous potential to
058
      revolutionize video content creation, from personalized short videos to CGI effects and the movie
059   industry.
060
061   Despite the rapid advancements in T2V models, significant room for improvement remains. Current
062
      T2V generation techniques are primarily limited to synthesizing simple scenes and often lack visual
      details and dynamic motion Zeng et al. (2023); Qing et al. (2023); Yuan et al. (2024). These models
063
      particularly struggle with videos that require distinctly different textual descriptions for the first and
064
      last frames, especially in dynamic scenes (see Figure 2). We identify two main reasons for this
065   limitation. First, pre-trained T2V models are rarely trained extensively on dynamic scenes due to
066   their scarcity in training datasets Bain et al. (2021); Chen et al. (2024b). Second, major part of T2V
067   models use the text conditioning mechanism inherited from T2I models Singer et al. (2022); Ho et al.
068   (2022); Blattmann et al. (2023b) that conditions each frame on a uniform text prompt intended to
069   describe the entire video sequence. As a result, frames lack variability and uniqueness. Moreover,
070   standard T2V models do not typically support image conditioning, limiting their general applicability.
071   Implementing image conditioning often requires developing a separate, resource-intensive model
072   without offering a universal solution Xing et al. (2023); Blattmann et al. (2023a).

098   To tackle the stated above problems, we propose two novel approaches - State Guidance and Image
099
      Guidance aimed to extend possibilities of stantard T2V models in a training-free manner. Both
      methods are built upon a modified diffusion sampling process via the guidance mechanism. State
100
      Guidance provides an alternative view on the T2V model text conditioning. It defines a video scene
101
      as a trajectory that has a start point - the initial state, an end point - the final state, and a trajectory of
102   motion - the transition state. As a result, a video scene is described by a state triplet ⟨initial state,
103   transition state, final state⟩. During each diffusion step, State Guidance makes step in direction
104   of each state simultaneously with different strengths for each frame. Scheduling State Guidance
105   strengths across video frame dimension allows to control video scene dynamic so that first frame
106   corresponds to initial state, last frame corresponds to final state, and intermediate frames smoothly
107   transition between them. The proposed inference scheme enables T2V models to generate dynamic
      scenes (see Figure 1A and Figure 2).

      Image Guidance is a method that enables the injection of image conditions into a pre-trained T2V
109   model without the need for retraining. This process is accomplished by integrating the denoising
110   trajectory with the conditional image in the diffusion trajectory. Image Guidance facilitates T2V
111   models to operate in a zero-shot Image-to-Video (I2V) regime (see Figure 1B). Furthermore, the com-
112   bination of State Guidance and Image Guidance allows for zero-shot video generation conditioned
113   on the start frame, end frame, and text prompt (Zero-Shot II2V, see Figure 1C).
114
      To evaluate the ability of T2V models to generate dynamic video scenes, we introduce a novel
115   Dynamic Scene Benchmark, consisting of 106 textual descriptions for various dynamic scenes.
116   Extensive experiments show that State Guidance significantly improves the generation capabilities
117   of existing T2V architectures, enhancing text understanding and motion quality without a notable
118   decline in temporal consistency. Additionally, we compare our Zero-Shot I2V and II2V regimes
119   with training-based approaches, demonstrating that pre-trained T2V models augmented with State
120   Guidance and Image Guidance can achieve comparable results.
121   Contributions: 1) We introduce State Guidance, a novel, training-free framework for T2V diffusion
122   model inference that enables dynamic video scene generation (see Figure 1A and Figure 2); 2) We
123   propose Image Guidance, a sampling technique that allows T2V models to condition on images
124   without the need for retraining, facilitating Zero-Shot I2V generation (refer to Figure 1B); 3) We
125   present a zero-shot image-to-video (Zero-Shot II2V) pipeline built upon a pre-trained T2V model,
126   leveraging the combined strengths of State Guidance and Image Guidance methods (refer to
127   Figure 1C) 4) We present the Dynamic Scene Benchmark, the first benchmark in the literature
128   specifically designed to evaluate the ability of T2V models to generate dynamic video scenes.

      2    R ELATED WORK
132
133   Text-to-Video generation. Recent breakthroughs in T2I generation using diffusion models have
134   significantly advanced T2V generation. Major T2V models extend T2I architectures by leveraging
135
      pre-trained weights and adding temporal layers for frame consistency Blattmann et al. (2023b); Guo
      et al. (2023); Girdhar et al. (2023); Qing et al. (2023); Zeng et al. (2023). They typically integrate
136
      temporal convolutional and attention layers into the 2D UNet of a Stable Diffusion model Rombach
137
      et al. (2022), generating in the latent space of a pre-trained VAE Esser et al. (2021); Rombach et al.
138   (2022). While this approach enhances training efficiency and reduces costs, it restricts scene variation
139   by conditioning each frame on a single prompt. Consequently, standard T2V models struggle to
140   produce video scenes with significant frame-by-frame variability. Our work introduces an innovative
141   sampling mechanism for T2V models, enabling dynamic scene generation in pre-trained models
142   without the need for retraining.
143
      Image-to-Video generation. A natural way to enhance the capabilities and improve the controllability
144   of the T2V model is through the incorporation of image conditioning. This involves extending the
145   architecture and training for this new task Blattmann et al. (2023a); Girdhar et al. (2023); Xing
146   et al. (2023); Zeng et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2023). For example, I2VGen-XL Zhang et al.
147   (2023) and DynamiCrafter Xing et al. (2023) add cross-attention layers for input image conditioning.
148   EmuVideo Girdhar et al. (2023) and PixelDance Zeng et al. (2023) modify the 3D U-Net by integrating
149   first-frame latent features into the input noise. Stable Video Diffusion Blattmann et al. (2023a)
150   replaces text embeddings with CLIP image embeddings and combines a noisy first frame with the 3D
151   U-Net input. In contrast to prior works, our sampling method allows a pre-trained T2V diffusion
152
      model to perform zero-shot I2V generation without additional optimization or fine-tuning.
153   Image-Image-to-Video generation. Transient video generation from two images (II2V generation)
154   is a newly explored task in video diffusion models. PixelDance Zeng et al. (2023) trains a model to
155   generate videos using the first and last frames with textual instructions. SIENE Chen et al. (2023)
156   employs a random mask model for text-guided scene transitions, while DiffMorpher Zhang et al.
157   (2024a) uses LoRA parameter interpolation for smooth semantic shifts. TVG Zhang et al. (2024b)
158
      builds its II2V pipeline on the pre-trained I2V model DynamiCrafter Xing et al. (2023) model using
      Gaussian process regression. In contrast, we demonstrate the feasibility of a zero-shot II2V model on
159
      a T2V framework without architectural changes or fine-tuning.
160
161   Text-to-Video benchmarks. A conventional T2V evaluation approach assesses the quality of
      generated frames using FVD Unterthiner et al. (2019) and IS Salimans et al. (2016), while measuring


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      text similarity with CLIPSIM Radford et al. (2021). However, recent studies indicate that these
163   metrics have a weak correlation with human ratings Girdhar et al. (2023). To address this, several
164   papers propose advanced benchmarks to evaluate generation quality Liu et al. (2024b;a); Huang
165   et al. (2023); Wu et al. (2024). Notably, EvalCrafter Liu et al. (2024a) evaluates videos across
166   four key parameters: visual quality, text-video alignment, motion quality, and temporal consistency.
167   VBench Huang et al. (2023) evaluates using 16 parameters linked to specific prompts, while FETV Liu
168   et al. (2024b) introduces automatic metrics like UMT Score and FVD-UMT, correlating better with
169   user ratings. Despite these advancements, existing benchmarks primarily focus on low-dynamic
170   video scenes, where the description of a single frame applies to the entire video. To address this
171
      limitation, we propose a new benchmark called the Dynamic Scenes Benchmark, which emphasizes
      videos featuring substantial scene progression from frame to frame.
172
173   Diffusion guidance. An important feature of Diffusion Models is their ability to customize outputs
174   without the need for retraining. Diffusion Guidance is a technique that modifies the backward
175   diffusion trajectory by adjusting the outputs of the denoising model. Classifier guidance Dhariwal
176   & Nichol (2021) facilitates class-conditional generation from an unconditional model by utilizing
177
      gradients from a pre-trained classifier during sampling. Classifier-Free Guidance Ho & Salimans
      (2021) allows for a balance between sample quality and diversity by combining class-conditional
178
      and unconditional estimations and controlling the weight of their mixture. Moreover, MUSE Chang
179
      et al. (2023) and MDTv2 Gao et al. (2023) introduce a dynamic guidance scale that changes over
180   the course of the sampling process, resulting in samples with greater diversity in the early steps and
181   higher fidelity in the later stages. In this work, we propose a guidance schedule across the frame
182   dimension of the generated video to manipulate the dynamics of the video effectively and mixing
183   backward diffusion trajectory with denoising direction to inject image conditioning in the T2V model.

204   In this paper, we identify a key limitation in modern T2V models: their inability to generate videos
205   with dynamically changing scenes. Specifically, these models struggle to produce video scenes,
206   where the description of the first frame z 1 and the last frame z F differ significantly. For example,

208

211   this limitation to two main factors: (1) Training Data: Current T2V models are trained on datasets
212   predominantly composed of static video scenes Bain et al. (2021); Chen et al. (2024b). (2) Model
213   Architecture: Many contemporary T2V models Singer et al. (2022); Ho et al. (2022); Blattmann
214   et al. (2023b) rely heavily on spatial cross-attention between text and latent features for text-guided
215   generation. This method imposes a strong prior, resulting in all frames of the generated video sharing
      the same description.

      5     C ONCLUSION
524
525
      In this paper, we introduced two novel sampling methods for T2V diffusion models: State Guidance
526
      and Image Guidance. These methods enhance the capabilities of pre-trained T2V models without
527
      requiring additional training or architectural modifications. State Guidance enables T2V models
528   to generate dynamic video scenes, overcoming the limitations imposed by their text conditioning
529   mechanisms. The efficiency of the proposed solution has been measured on the proposed first in
530   the literature Dynamic Scenes Benchmark. Meanwhile, Image Guidance incorporates image
531   conditioning into pre-trained T2V models, allowing them to generate content in a Zero-Shot I2V
532   mode. The combination of State Guidance and Image Guidance facilitates the generation of
533   zero-shot transition videos based on two reference images and a text prompt, namely Zero-Shot II2V.
534   While our approach has yielded significant results, there is substantial potential for further research.
535   First, we believe the text conditioning mechanism currently employed in most T2V models has
536   critical shortcomings and should be replaced with more modern architectural techniques. Second,
537   the framework introduced in State Guidance can be combined with trainable adapters for state
538   conditioning, which may enhance output video quality and controllability. Finally, the proposed
539   zero-shot II2V and zero-shot I2V schemes can be integrated with existing training-based methods to
      further improve final video quality.

      6   E THICS & R EPRODUCIBILITY
541
542
      The use of T2V foundation models raises several ethical concerns. These models have the potential
543   for misuse, such as generating misleading or counterfeit content, which could have harmful societal
544   impacts. Our work relies heavily on two models, VideoCrafter2 Chen et al. (2024a) and LaVie Wang
545   et al. (2023), making it vulnerable to these risks. Furthermore, the video datasets used to train these
546   models may contain inappropriate content or biases that the models could inadvertently perpetuate,
547   resulting in the generation of inappropriate material. In addition, our Custom I2V Benchmark scoring
548   is based on qualitative results from prior work, which could also be misused. To address these
549   concerns and promote reproducibility, we will release our source code and benchmarks under a
550   license that encourages ethical and legal use. Additional information about implementation details,
551
      metrics can be found in the Experiments section and in the Appendix.

      A.1    L IMITATIONS

      While State Guidance and Image Guidance enhance the pre-trained T2V models by introducing
761   new features and capabilities without the need for retraining, their generation quality is ultimately
762   constrained by the original T2V model. This limitation is illustrated in Figure 6. In the first example,
763   we attempt to generate a video with camera control. However, due to the inherent limitations of
764   standard T2V models in this area Hu et al. (2024), State Guidance inference simply inherits this issue:
765   instead of producing a video with a rotating camera, it results in a video featuring a rotating horse.
766   The second and third examples highlight challenges that the original model struggles to address,
767   such as "the sunflower turning into an astronaut" and "the lorry transforming into a robot." Although
768   State Guidance generates coherent and temporally consistent videos, it often fails to achieve the
769
      transformations exactly as requested. We attribute this to a possible lack of relevant transformations
      in the original training samples of the T2V model.

      Another significant limitation is that State Guidance and its combination with Image Guidance
      requires more model inferences during sampling. While classifier-free guidance demands only two
795
      model inferences per diffusion step — one conditional and one unconditional — State Guidance for
796
      dynamic video scene generation and combination of State Guidance and Image Guidance for Zero-
797   Shot II2V pipeline require four: three for each state and one unconditional for classifier-free guidance.
798   This increases the inference time and resource consumption. Lastly, while State Guidance and Image
799   Guidance add new features and capabilities to the pre-trained T2V models, they also introduce
800   additional hyperparameters, such as the guidance schedule and guidance interval, complicating the
801   use of T2V models.

897   Manual triplet generation. We describe the process of manually selecting prompts for our Dynamic
898   Scene Benchmark that describe dynamic changes in video scenes. The primary goal is to capture
899   evolving actions or transitions, such as objects changing properties (e.g., flowers blooming, ice
900   melting, or color changing) and changes in position (e.g., a person standing up or a bird flying away).
901   Prompts fall into two categories: those with an active main object that undergoes a clear evolution
902   while the background remains relatively static, and those where the background itself changes without
903
      a main object. The key criterion for selection is that the changes must be gradual, allowing for
      intermediate states, as opposed to instantaneous transitions that would not provide a smooth evolution
904

971   frames in the generated video becomes abrupt, causing most frames to closely resemble either the

1021
1022   The goal of our work is to tackle problem of prompt condition limitations in current video generation
1023   methods. State Guidance updates inference scheme of open source video generation models and
1024   pushes up the quality of their generated samples. Thus, any existing biases in these models, as long as
1025   potential harmful samples are explicitly inherited. Our method enhances quality of video generation,
       exhibiting a positive influence on video applications.
