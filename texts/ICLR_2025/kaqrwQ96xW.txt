011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            As text-to-image diffusion models become advanced enough for commercial ap-
014            plications, there is also increasing concern about their potential for malicious
015
               and harmful use. Model unlearning has been proposed to mitigate the concerns
               by removing undesired and potentially harmful information from the pre-trained
016
               model. So far, the success of unlearning is mainly measured by whether the
017
               unlearned model can generate a target concept while maintaining image quality.
018            However, unlearning is typically tested under limited scenarios, and the side effects
019            of unlearning have barely been studied in the current literature. In this work, we
020            thoroughly analyze unlearning under various scenarios with five key aspects. Our
021            investigation reveals that every method has side effects or limitations, especially in
022            more complex and realistic situations. By releasing our comprehensive evaluation
023            framework with the source codes and artifacts, we hope to inspire further research
024            in this area, leading to more reliable and effective unlearning methods.
025

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Text-to-image diffusion models have achieved remarkable success in various real-world applications
029
      based on the huge number of text-to-image pairs used for training (Ramesh et al., 2022; Dhariwal &
030   Nichol, 2021; Nichol et al., 2021; Podell et al., 2023). Since these pairs are often sourced from the
031   Internet, they may include violent, harmful, or unethical content (Schuhmann et al., 2022). Previous
032   studies show that large-scale text-to-image models can generate malicious images (Rando et al., 2022;
033   Ma et al., 2024a; Kim et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024b). Most models use a safety filter to block the
034   generation of undesirable content (Saharia et al., 2022; Rombach et al., 2022; Ramesh et al., 2022).
035   However, the safety filters can only work with a predefined set of malicious patterns and may be
036   vulnerable to generated images with unseen patterns.
037   As a practical solution, text-to-image unlearning methods have been proposed (Gandikota et al., 2023;
038   2024; Kumari et al., 2023; Heng & Soh, 2024; Fan et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2024). These methods
039   aim to modify pre-trained models to prevent the generation of images containing specific target
040   concepts. While promising, the evaluation of previous methods has been limited to verifying the
041   absence of target concepts and assessing the visual quality of generated images. This narrow focus
042   often overlooks other critical factors, such as unintended side effects or performance degradation on
043   unrelated concepts. Without a comprehensive evaluation framework, it remains challenging to assess
044
      and compare the ability of unlearning methods, leaving important questions about their effectiveness
      and limitations unanswered.
045
046   To address these limitations, we propose Holistic Unlearning Benchmark (HUB) that systematically
047   assesses unlearning methods across five key aspects, as summarized in Table 1: effectiveness on
048   target concepts, faithfulness of images, compliance with prompts, robustness on side effects, and
049   consistency in downstream applications. By examining each of these dimensions, HUB provides an
050
      in-depth assessment from multiple perspectives, highlighting both their strengths and weaknesses.
051   In this paper, we evaluate six state-of-the-art methods, including ESD (Gandikota et al., 2023),
052   UCE (Gandikota et al., 2024), AC (Kumari et al., 2023), SA (Heng & Soh, 2024), SalUn (Fan et al.,
053   2023), and Receler (Huang et al., 2024). Our empirical experiments reveal that no method works
      well in all aspects of evaluations, underscoring the need for more holistic unlearning approaches. By

081   2    R ELATED WORK
082
083
084   There is a growing body of research on unlearning techniques for pre-trained text-to-image models,
085   aiming to mitigate the generalization of inappropriate images. ESD (Gandikota et al., 2023) propose a
086   fine-tuning method that inversely guides the model against generating a specified target concept text.
087
      AC and SalUn (Kumari et al., 2023; Fan et al., 2023) proposes a fine-tuning method that can map the
      target concept to alternative concepts. UCE (Gandikota et al., 2024) fine-tunes cross-attention layers
088
      for unlearning. SA (Heng & Soh, 2024) introduces an unlearning based on continual learning, and
089
      Receler (Huang et al., 2024) uses an adapter and a masking scheme.
090
091   Several methods have been proposed to evaluate the robustness of text-to-image diffusion models
092   against malicious uses. Most of these approaches focus on optimizing soft prompts to generate
093
      undesired concepts (Pham et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2024a; Tsai et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024b; Chin
      et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024a; Rando et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2024b), or directly finding prompts
094
      that lead to unwanted content generation (Kim et al., 2024). Although these methods assess model
095
      vulnerabilities, they have two main limitations: 1) they only provide a prompt engineering view
096   of model robustness, and 2) they require access to the model weights, which may not always be
097   possible. In contrast, our proposed benchmark offers a more comprehensive evaluation from multiple
098   perspectives without requiring access to the model weights.
099
      Furthermore, some benchmarks have been introduced to evaluate unlearned models (Ma et al.,
100
      2024b; Zhang et al., 2024a; Schramowski et al., 2023). For example, Schramowski et al. (2023)
101
      introduce the I2P dataset to assess the ability of a model to avoid generating inappropriate content that
102   could be offensive, insulting, or anxiety-inducing. Zhang et al. (2024a) introduce a stylized image
103   dataset to evaluate models that have undergone style unlearning. Similarly, Ma et al. (2024b) offer a
104   copyright dataset to measure how effectively an unlearned model protects copyrighted material by
105   not reproducing protected content. While previous benchmarks have primarily focused on providing
106   datasets to evaluate models, our benchmark goes beyond this by addressing unintended changes
107   in model behavior and potential problems that can occur when unlearned models are applied in
      real-world applications.

138
139
      Concept unlearning. In this work, we focus on concept unlearning, where the concept is defined
140
      as a single English word or phrase. We refer to the concept to be unlearned as the target concept. Fol-
141
      lowing previous works (Gandikota et al., 2023; 2024; Fan et al., 2023), we select four target concepts
142   from the Imagenette dataset (Howard, 2019): church, parachute, gas pump, and English springer
143   covering diverse and exhaustive scenarios. These objects also vary in conceptual complexity—from
144   broad categories to specific entities—allowing us to analyze unlearning performance across different
145   levels of specificity.
146
      Baseline. We use Stable Diffusion 1.4 (Rombach et al., 2022) as our baseline model and apply six
147
      unlearning methods: AC (Kumari et al., 2023), SA (Heng & Soh, 2024), SalUn (Fan et al., 2023),
148   UCE (Gandikota et al., 2024), ESD (Gandikota et al., 2023), and Receler (Huang et al., 2024). Based
149   on their approach to handling the target concept, these methods are categorized into 1) mapping-based
150   approaches, which replace the target concept with an alternative concept during unlearning (AC, SA,
151   SalUn), and 2) non-mapping-based approaches, which utilize techniques like gradient ascent on the
152   target concept (UCE, ESD, Receler). For AC and SA, we select bird as the alternative concept since it
153   has easily distinguishable and distinct features. Detailed explanations and configurations for each
154   method are provided in Appendix A and Appendix B, respectively.

      4.1      H OW ROBUST IS THE UNLEARNING UNDER DIVERSE AND COMPLEX PROMPTS ?
176
177   Previous studies have evaluated unlearning methods with images generated from prompts generated
178   via template “a photo of a {concept}”1 , where {concept} is replaced by a target (Gandikota et al.,
179   2023; 2024; Heng & Soh, 2024; Fan et al., 2023). However, in real-world scenarios, users often
180   generate images with detailed and complex prompts that may generate the target concept indirectly.
181   Therefore, even if the unlearning performs well with straightforward prompts, it may fail when faced
182   with more diverse and realistic prompts.
183   To address this, we curate 100 prompts for each concept by generating diverse prompts from GPT-4o,
184   incorporating non-English instructions. Given an initial prompt set, we manually inspect prompts to
185   curate the final 100 prompts, denoted as ‘diverse prompts’ in experiments. While prior studies have
186   utilized LLM-based paraphrasing (Kumari et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2024), our approach goes further by
187   creating more sophisticated and complex prompts. The detailed GPT-4o instruction, curation process,
188   and examples of curated prompts are provided in Appendix C.2.
189   We generate ten images per diverse prompt with different random seeds, resulting in 1,000 generated
190   images for each target concept. We also generate 1,000 images with simple prompts. We measure
191   the proportion of images containing the target concept using GPT-4o, which has demonstrated high
192   accuracy in identifying visual objects (Wu et al., 2023; 2024). The instruction to classify the target
193   concept is provided in Appendix C.1.
194   Table 2 shows the proportion of the generated images with the four target concepts across six unlearn-
195   ing methods. Among the methods, Receler and SalUn demonstrate the most robust performance,
196   maintaining low target concept generation regardless of prompt diversity. In contrast, methods such
197   as AC and SA reveal significant differences between simple and diverse prompts, indicating weaker
198   generalization to more complex and realistic prompts.
199
      We observe that the specificity of the target concept is strongly related to the success of unlearning in
200   terms of target proportion. Broad concepts like church or parachute tend to reappear with diverse
201   prompts, making them harder to erase. In contrast, more specific concepts such as English springer,
202   which lack synonyms or related terms, do not consistently appear with simple and diverse prompts.
203   We also present the qualitative results in Fig. 1, showing that the target concept is generated from
204   diverse prompts. More samples can be found in Appendix C.2.

309
310   We extend our analysis to examine how unlearning affects the 1) generation of related concepts, such
311
      as the generation of a cross in a church-erased model, and 2) the underlying population distribution
      estimated by the generative model, e.g., the distribution of samples without any text condition.

314   5.1      W HAT IS THE INFLUENCE OF UNLEARNING ON RELATED CONCEPTS ?
315
      Existing unlearning methods evaluate whether unrelated concepts remain unaffected after the target
316
      concept is erased, such as generating gas pump images from a church-erased model (Gandikota et al.,
317
      2023; 2024; Fan et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2024). However, this evaluation often overlooks the impact
318   on semantically or visually similar concepts. Unlearning a target concept can lead to unintended
319   consequences on related concepts, a phenomenon we refer to as the over-erasing effect. This occurs
320   due to shared feature representations inherent in such models (Radford et al., 2021). For instance,
321   removing the concept church may also degrade the generation of target-related concepts like cross,
322   altar, or bible, as they share latent features with church. Addressing this issue is essential, as it limits
323   the capacity of models to generate accurate and diverse content after unlearning, posing a significant
      challenge for future research.

361   5.2    H OW DOES UNLEARNING CHANGE THE UNDERLYING ESTIMATED DISTRIBUTION ?
362
363   The over-erasing effect raises a natural question about the changes in the estimated density of the
364   generative models, as the primary goal of a generative model is to estimate the true data distribution
365   from the training set. This change could lead to a shift in the overall distribution, affecting its ability
366   to generate images. To answer this, we investigate how unlearning changes the estimated density of
367   the generative models.
368   Experimental settings. We use a diffusion model trained on the MNIST dataset rather than natural
369   images since the effects of unlearning are well pronounced with a limited set of classes. We adopt the
370   joint training method from classifier-free guidance (Ho & Salimans, 2022), enabling conditional and
371   unconditional generation. For each unlearning method, we train ten models, each unlearning one of
372   the classes from zero to nine. We exclude UCE and Receler from this experiment since they rely on
373   cross-attention layers, which are absent in the DDPM architecture. Detailed training procedures for
374
      each unlearning method are provided in Appendix B.2.

376   the class distributions of images generated unconditionally, as it is impossible to measure the density
377   from a model directly. We 1) unconditionally sample 10,000 images from each model, 2) classify
      them, and 3) compute the KL divergence of non-target class distributions between original and

474   6.2      T O WHAT EXTENT CAN THE UNLEARNED MODEL RESTORE A TARGET CONCEPT FROM A
475            NOISY INPUT ?
476
477   The results on the downstream tasks raise interesting questions: How robust are unlearning methods
478   in preventing the generation of the target concept under visual conditions? What level of visual
479   context is required to restore the target concept from an unlearned model? To further investigate the
480   robustness of unlearning methods, we utilize the denoising process of the diffusion models.
481   Choi et al. (2022) have been shown that even when a considerable level of noise is added to the image,
482   diffusion models can still restore a reference image without requiring a holistic context. However,
483   if the model is successfully unlearned, the model should no longer be able to recover the target
484   concept. Through the following experiments, we verify whether the unlearned model restores the
485   target concept from the noisy version of the image with the target concept. Different levels of noise
      can represent the different levels of model guidance.

      7     C ONCLUSION & L IMITATIONS
525
526
      Our study demonstrates that current unlearning techniques for text-to-image diffusion models are
527
      imperfect. While they can partially mitigate the generation of harmful or unwanted content, their
528   application in real-world scenarios remains limited due to issues with robustness, image quality, and
529   unintended side effects. As text-to-image diffusion models continue to evolve, future research needs
530   to focus on addressing these limitations. This includes improving the generalization of unlearning
531   methods to complex prompts, minimizing the trade-off between performance and image quality, and
532   developing techniques to prevent the over-erasing effect. By releasing our comprehensive evaluation
533   framework with the source codes, we hope to inspire further research in this area, leading to more
534   reliable and effective unlearning methods.
535   Limitations. Our benchmark currently focuses on unlearning object-based concepts due to the
536   difficulty of evaluating whether generated images contain specific styles or elements that could raise
537   copyright issues. Legal concerns make this task more complicated. If these challenges are resolved,
538   we believe our benchmark could be expanded to include a wider range of concepts. Also, we do not
539   perform NSFW experiments (Schramowski et al., 2023) to protect the mental health of the researchers,
      but we believe our findings can be generalized to the NSFW content.
