011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Although deep reinforcement learning (DRL) deals with sequential decision mak-
014            ing problems, temporal information representation is absent from state-of-the-art
015            actor-critic algorithms. The reliance on a single observation vector, representing
016            information from only one time step, combined with densely connected neural net-
017            works, causes instability and oscillations in action smoothness. Therefore many
018
               applied DRL robotics control methods employ various reward shaping, low-pass
               filter and traditional controller-based methods to mitigate this effect. However,
019
               the interactions of these different parts hinders the performance of the original
020
               goal for the RL algorithm. In this paper we present a reinforcement learning al-
021            gorithm extended with past action-state representation learning (PASRL), which
022            allows for the end-to-end training of RL-based control methods without the need
023            for common heuristics. PASRL is evaluated on the MuJoCo benchmark, show-
024            ing smoother actions that preserve exploration, eliminate the need for extensive
025            hyperparameter tuning, and provide a simple and efficient solution for enhancing
026            action smoothness.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Even though Reinforcement Learning (RL) Sutton & Barto (2018) is a powerful tool to deal with
032   physical control problems, it exhibits a well-known instability regarding the smoothness of its pre-
033   dicted control actions Song et al. (2023)Mysore et al. (2021a). Oscillating, jerky control signals
034   can degrade control performance and potentially damage the system Ibarz et al. (2021)Kim et al.
035   (2022). This issue could be attributed to the reliance on a single observation vector, representing
036   information only at time step t and densely connected nature of the deployed neural network con-
037   troller. Assuming that the state is a fully observable Markov Decision Process (MDP), instability
038   could represent divergence in training, drops in performance across episodes, performance oscilla-
039
      tions inside episodes and the actions taken by the agent could differ greatly from one time step to
      another. Furthermore, observation vectors that contain only the current time step’s sensory record-
040
      ings can lead to instabilities. This occurs when the agent lacks access to the complete observation
041
      space, transforming the underlying problem formulation into a Partially Observable Markov Deci-
042   sion Process (POMDP) Kaelbling et al. (1998). POMDPs could be induced by anomalies such as
043   flickering, noise or data transmission loss in sensors during real-world applications. Or by the agent
044   not having access to accurate information in its observation vector.
045
      Stability issues in MDP formulated RL problems have been tackled by multiple methods and their
046
      mixtures (Figure. 1). Most commonly methods incorporate a motor behavior reward part that en-
047
      courages improved action smoothness and the use of smaller action values into the desired reward
048   function Liu et al. (2024). Others include past sensory readings and agent outputs into their obser-
049   vation vector, use the frame stacking of previous sensory observations Mnih et al. (2015), or employ
050   state estimation models such as Kalman filters Kalman (1960), to better estimate the actual underly-
051   ing state based on the sensory information received from the environment. Applied RL controllers
052   commonly use low-pass filters to filter out large oscillations in the RL based control commands out-
053   put and make use of traditional control algorithms such as PD or PID controllers Kaufmann et al.
      (2023)Luo et al. (2024)Reddy et al. (2018)Han et al. (2024)Jin et al. (2022).

086   Densely connected layers have been the staple of most MDP formulated state-of-the-art algorithm
087   Fujimoto et al. (2018)Haarnoja et al. (2018)Kuznetsov et al. (2020)Fujimoto et al. (2024). However,
088   since these neural network structures contain no memory cells, they are prone to produce vastly
089   different actions for concurrent time steps.
090   POMDP formulated reinforcement learning algorithms have been shown to mitigate these problems
091   Dulac-Arnold et al. (2021) by either incorporating memory by stacking previous observations to-
092   gether ,Mnih et al. (2013) thus being able to turn partially observable MDPs to fully observable ones
093   Hausknecht & Stone (2015) and mitigating the effect inaccurate state recordings could pose on the
094   agent’s observation vector. Furthermore, incorporating recurrent architectures within the agent en-
095   ables the use of hidden states for memory integration. Additionally, it has been shown that recurrent
096   architectures are effective even without frame-stacked observations Hausknecht & Stone (2015);
097   Meng et al. (2021).
098   Recurrent neural network structure based agents have long been utilized in the Arcade Learning
099   Environment (ALE) Bellemare et al. (2013). This environment offers interfaces with wide range
100   of Atari 2600 games and has been a popular benchmark ever since. Most recurrent network-based
101   agents rely on distributed training to avoid ”representational drift,” where stored hidden states gen-
102   erated by older network parameters differ significantly from those produced by the network at the
103   current training step Kapturowski et al. (2018), Badia et al. (2020), Kapturowski et al. (2022), Es-
104
      peholt et al. (2018)Horgan et al. (2018).
105   Improving the action smoothness generated by reinforcement learning agents has been explored via
106   two main research lines. The modification of the RL training algorithms Shen et al. (2020), Mysore
107   et al. (2021a), Chen et al. (2021), Yu et al. (2021), Kobayashi (2022), Zou et al. (2022) and by
      modifications of the policy network Takase et al. (2022), Song et al. (2023), Wang et al. (2024).

108
      However, to the best of our knowledge no research has been conducted on recurrent reinforcement
109   learning agents effect on action smoothness comparing against the training of reinforcement learning
110   agents in concurrency with commonly used action smoothness reward, low-pass filter and traditional
111   control heuristics. The training of this segmented system of RL controllers, low-pass filters and
112   traditional control algorithms pose an issue since they are not optimized concurrently and rely on the
113   correct guessing of various control parameters and cutoff frequencies. Furthermore the incorporation
114   of an action smoothness term causes the agent to maximize the balance between achieving the main
115   objective and minimizing abrupt changes in actions, instead of solely maximizing the primary goal.
116   It is also important to note that taking smooth actions is not always optimal. Overly smoothed actions
117   can restrict the agent’s exploration, potentially limiting its performance and preventing it from fully
118   exploring the state-action space needed to achieve an optimal policy. Moreover, some environments
119   demand rapid action responses where swift or highly reactive control strategies are ideal. Hence, an
120   effective algorithm should balance responsiveness to accommodate rapid changes while minimizing
121   unnecessary oscillations in action selection.
122   In this paper we propose a non-distributed recurrent reinforcement learning agent, with learned
123   hidden states. Our approach can be thought of as an extension to TD7 Fujimoto et al. (2024),
124   which also learns decouples state and state-action embeddings. We augment this already existing
125   pipeline by creating time-dependent embeddings. The proposed RL agent could be trained end-to-
126   end without the commonly employed heuristics present in applied reinforcement learning methods.
127   We evaluate this algorithm’s performance in two metrics: the control methods achieved reward in
128   the main task of the environment and the action smoothness of the created control strategy. Our
129
      findings show that recurrent reinforcement learning agents achieve comparable task performance to
      mixed traditional and RL-based controllers, while generating substantially smoother actions without
130
      relying on heuristics or compromising exploration.

133   2   BACKGROUND
134
135
      Reinforcement learning formulates problems as a Markov Decision Process Bellman (1957)Sutton
136
      & Barto (2018). An MDP can be described as a tuple of 5 (S, A, R, p, γ), containing S the state
137
      space, A action space, R reward function, p dynamics model and discount factor γ. In RL the
138   objective is to find an optimal policy πθ : S → A, that maps
                                                              P∞ state s ∈ S to an action a ∈ A, in a
139   way that maximizes the discounted accumulative reward t=1 γ t−1 · rt , with parameters θ.
140
141
      Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) are widely applied in reinforcement learning (RL) to address
      tasks involving temporal dependencies, where decisions depend not only on the current observation
142
      but also on past experiences. By maintaining a hidden state that evolves over time, RNNs enable RL
143
      agents to incorporate historical information, making them particularly effective in partially observ-
144   able environments, such as Partially Observable Markov Decision Processes (POMDPs). The most
145   commonly used RNN variants in RL are Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks Hochreiter
146   & Schmidhuber (1997) and Gated Recurrent Units (GRUs) Cho (2014), both of which mitigate the
147   vanishing gradient problem and improve performance in tasks that require memory and sequential
148   decision-making.
149
      State-Action Learning Embeddings (SALE) Fujimoto et al. (2024) are designed to improve RL
150   algorithms by effectively capturing observation space structure and transition dynamics. It employs
151   two encoders: f transforms the state s into an embedding zs and g combines zs with action a to
152   create a state-action embedding zsa . SALE serves as the principle component for TD7 Fujimoto
153   et al. (2024), which is an improved version of TD3 Fujimoto et al. (2018).
154

156   3   ACTION SMOOTHNESS ISSUE OF CURRENT STATE - OF - THE ART METHODS
157
158   Although recurrent neural networks (RNNs) are widely used in POMDP tasks, the broader research
159   community has not fully adopted them into MDP tasks. Instead, many deep reinforcement learning
160   (DRL) algorithms are typically used off-the-shelf relying on densely connected neural networks
161   that lack memory of previous inputs or outputs. This is problematic, as reinforcement learning is
      fundamentally aimed at addressing sequential decision-making challenges.

197   4     M ETHODOLOGY
198
199   In this section we introduce our past action-state information learning method, as well as perform
200   in-depth empirical evaluations for the design choices when using past information augmentation.
201   PASRL is built on TD7 with additional recurrent encoder structure, with prioritized recent expe-
202   rience replay to alleviate recurrent state staleness Kapturowski et al. (2018) and use pink noise
203   Eberhard et al. (2023) for added exploration benefits with more correlated noise.
204
205   4.1   PAST ACTION -S TATE REPRESENTATION LEARNING
206
      The aim of past action-state representation learning is to learn time dependent embeddings (ztsa , zts ),
207
      which is able to capture the time dependent change of the observation space and environment char-
208
      acteristics. PASRL augments the encoder pair (f, g) present in TD7, with a recurrent bottleneck
209   hidden layer, with learnable hidden states. In PASRL f (s̃, ht ) encodes information from state s̃ and
210   the hidden state of the state encoder ht into time dependent state embedding zts and g(z s , ht , ã)
211   encodes state s̃ and action ã into a time dependent state action embedding ztsa .
212
213   4.2   R EPLAY BUFFER MODIFICATIONS
214
215   In order to train our algorithm with hidden states of the recurrent layers present, we modify our
      replay buffer to store transition tuples as well as the current ht and next hidden states nht at a given


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      time step (s̃, ã, s̃′ , r, ht , nht ). We also enhance the state and action vectors by defining them as
217   fixed-length sequences of history (hl = 10), referred to as s̃ and ã. These sequences are updated by
218   appending the current time step observation s, and action a at the end for s̃ and ã and removing the
219   oldest information. These observation and action vectors are initialized by zeroes and stored without
220   crossing episode boundaries.
221
      To alleviate recurrent state staleness and representation drift we employ a prioritizing recent experi-
222
      ence replay sampling method Wang & Ross (2019) to prioritize the probability of sampling transition
223   tuples created by more up-to-date network parameters.
224
225   Therefore in a given update phase we make K mini-batch updates. Suppose N is the size of the
226
      replay buffer, then for the kth update, where 1 ≤ k ≤ K, we perform uniform sampling from the
      most recently stored ck data points, which is defined by

234
235   4.3   R ECURRENT LAYER MODIFICATION
236
237   For the recurrent layer type in our algorithm we selected GRU layers, since compared to LSTMs
238   they have less parameters per unit, therefore we can increase the bottleneck size with less parameters.
239   Furthermore, GRU layer based methods typically achieve the best performance in POMDP based
240   benchmarks Morad et al. (2023).
241   To make the comparisons fair and to keep the encoder’s parameter size from growing substantially
242   due to more parameters found in a GRU unit, compared to DNN units, we have chosen to reduce
243   the number of layers our encoders utilize, to ensure the encoder is capable of creating meaningful
244   embeddings to the actor and critic networks. Therefore not hindering the overall training process by
245   outputting not trained embeddings.
246   To ensure that the encoders utilize all the recurrent bottleneck layer’s neurons, we used a dropout
247   Srivastava et al. (2014) of 20%.

277   4.5   E FFECTS OF REPRESENTATIONAL DRIFT
278
279   For the training of recurrent reinforcement learning models two methods are commonly described
280   Hausknecht & Stone (2015). The first replays entire episode trajectories, while the second utilizes
281
      the common sampling paradigm for training. Although these methods follow different chains of
      thought, they overall lead to the same performance, therefore in PASRL, we utilize the common
282
      sampling paradigm found in TD7.
283
284   As for the use of the hidden states values we can also divide them into two categories.
285
286         1. Zeroing out the hidden states of sampled transition tuples
287
            2. Storing the hidden states of the transition tuples.
288
289
      The first approach appeals in its simplicity for implementation, however limits the networks tempo-
290   ral information modeling capability. While the second suffers from an effect called representational
291   drift, where the stored hidden states generated by a sufficiently old network parameters causes dis-
292   crepancy, since the updated network’s parameter generated hidden states do not align with the stored
293   ones.

      6   C ONCLUSION
440
441
      The action smoothness of state-of-the-art RL algorithms is often addressed by adding smoothness
442   reward terms, low-pass filters, or traditional control methods. However, these approaches can hinder
443   performance. For this reason we introduce PASRL, a method to learn time-dependent state-action
444   embeddings to create smoother action controls.
445
      This paper highlights the issue of action smoothness found in RL algorithms using densely con-
446
      nected networks, and show how this could be overcome without changing the mathematical models
447
      behind the algorithm’s training. We also incorporate various advances in exploration and optimizers.
448
449   PASRL is able to generate smooth actions without requiring manually designed reward parts or
450   additional controllers, such as low-pass filters or PD controllers. This reduces the complexity of
451
      parameter tuning in applied RL cases. As a general-purpose technique, PASRL offers an alternative
      for reinforcement learning tasks where the smoothness of the controller is amongst key priorities. We
452
      found that PASRL is even able to match the performance of commonly employed RL and traditional

741   A.3.3    L OW- PASS - FILTER
742
743   Low-pass filters in reinforcement learning controllers aim to smooth out high-frequency fluctuations
744   in the actions generated by the policy. To implement a low-pass filter in our reinforcement learning
745   (RL) controller, two key parameters must be defined: the sampling frequency fs and the cutoff
746
      frequency ωc . The sampling frequency determines the time step, calculated as ∆t = 1/fs , and
      is typically provided by the reinforcement learning environment. The cutoff frequency defines the
747
      filter’s response characteristics. In our implementation, we selected a cutoff frequency of 20Hz to
748
      effectively balance responsiveness and smoothness of the policy.
749
750   In our low-pass-filter implementation we utilize a first-order Infinite Impulse Response (IIR) filter,
751   to minimize the delay in the control policy, which can be described by:
752
753                                      yt = b0 · at + b1 · at−1 + a1 · yt−1                              (4)
754
755   Where the coefficients in Equation 4 b0 and b1 control the contribution of the current and previous
      inputs, respectively, while a1 determines how much of the previous output affects the current output.


                                                           14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      The variable at represents the current input action, which is the new action generated by the rein-
757   forcement learning policy. The term at-1 denotes the previous action, allowing the filter to take into
758   account the action taken in the prior time step. Finally, yt-1 represents the previous filtered action
759   output, which serves as feedback to the current output. At the end the final filtered action output yt
760   is used as the input for the environment.
761
762   A.3.4    PD C ONTROLLER
763
764   A Proportional-Derivative (PD) controller is a widely used traditional control paradigm in various
765   robotic applications used to enhance the performance of action outputs. The PD controller combines
766   two components: the proportional P term, which provides an immediate response to the current
767
      error, and the derivative D term, which predicts future error based on its rate of change.
768   A PD controller can be described by the following formula:
769
770
                                                                    de(t)
771                                      u(t) = Kp · e(t) + Kd ·                                          (5)
                                                                     dt
772
773   In which equation: u(t) is the control output, e(t) is the error signal (in our case the policy action),
774   Kp is the proportional gain parameter, and Kd is the derivative gain.
775
776
      In the context of reinforcement learning, the PD controller is employed to smooth the action outputs
      of the policy. The proportional component reacts directly to the error in the action, adjusting the
777
      control output to reduce this error. The derivative component, on the other hand, aims to mitigate
778
      oscillations in the agent’s output actions. In our implementation we have utilized a PD controller
779   with proportional gain Kp = 1.0 and derivative gain Kd = 0.05 to provide a rapid response to the
780   given actions generated by the policy.

907   The action smoothness results were computed using the second-order derivative of each action out-
908   put, taking the absolute value, and then averaging the smoothness values across evaluation episodes.
909   These averaged values were further averaged across all seeds to obtain the final smoothness measure.
