009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Time-series analysis is critical in many industries such as healthcare, finance,
012
               transportation, and energy sectors. The practical application of time-series anal-
013
               ysis often involves analyzing time-series data alongside contextual information
014            in the form of natural language for informed decision making. However, current
015            time-series models are limited in their ability to perform reasoning that involves
016            both time-series data and textual information. In this work we address this gap
017            by introducing Chat-TS, a large language model (LLM) designed specifically for
018            reasoning over time-series and textual data. Unlike traditional time-series mod-
019            els Chat-TS integrates time-series tokens into the LLM vocabulary, enhancing its
020            reasoning ability over both text and time-series modalities without compromising
021            its core natural language capabilities, enabling practical analysis and reasoning
022
               across time-series data. To support the development and validation of Chat-TS
               we contribute three new datasets: the TS Instruct Training Dataset which pairs
023
               diverse time-series data with relevant text instructions and responses for instruc-
024
               tion tuning, the TS Instruct question and answer (QA) benchmark, a set of nearly
025            4000 multiple-choice questions designed to evaluate multi-modal reasoning and
026            the TS Instruct Qualitative Benchmark which provides a smaller subset of QA,
027            math and decision making questions for LLM evaluation. We have designed a
028            training strategy that preserves the inherent reasoning capabilities of the LLM
029            while augmenting it with time-series reasoning capabilities. Evaluation results
030            show that Chat-TS achieves state-of-the-art performance in multi-modal reason-
031            ing tasks, maintaining strong natural language proficiency while advancing time-
032            series reasoning. All models, datasets, and code will be made publicly available
033
               [Github URL].

036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038
039
      Time-series data is a valuable form of information, widely used in sectors such as healthcare (Morid
      et al., 2023), business (Zhang et al., 2024a), and industry (Kashpruk et al., 2023). Time-series
040
      analysis, particularly for forecasting, imputation, anomaly detection, and classification, has been
041
      extensively studied over several decades (Makridakis et al., 1977; Dempster et al., 2020; Jiang et al.,
042   2024). These pure time-series analysis tasks often make predictions and draw conclusions from
043   time-series data alone. However, the practical application of time-series data often necessitates
044   drawing conclusions and making decisions based on real-world knowledge alongside the time-series
045   data itself. For example, an investment banker may consider both stock prices and relevant company
046   reports, while a doctor may analyze a patient’s ECG readings in combination with their medical
047   history and clinical notes.
048   There has been extensive research into the application of large language models (LLM’s), for exam-
049   ple as financial trading agents (Wang et al., 2023; 2024a; Li et al., 2023) and medical analysts (Nazi
050   & Peng, 2024; Thirunavukarasu et al., 2023). As part of their analysis these models process domain
051   specific time-series. In these cases the time-series is converted from numerical representations to text
052   format to be processed by the LLMs. Despite the promising applications of LLMs to handle real
053   world multi-domain challenges, the training and evaluation of LLM reasoning abilities, specifically
      for time-series data, remains significantly under-explored.

054
      In other domains such as computer vision, multi-modal reasoning via LLMs is a quickly growing
055   research area with important works such as LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023), MM1 (McKinzie et al., 2024),
056   Open-Flamingo (Awadalla et al., 2023) among others (Kondratyuk et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024b)
057   enhancing LLMs for multi-modal reasoning. Multi-modal capacity has even been adopted by many
058   of the most popular commercial models such as GPT4o (OpenAI et al., 2024), and Gemini (Team
059   et al., 2024a).
060
      Unlike the multi-modal models under development in other domains, current time-series analysis
061   methods are typically limited in their ability to integrate other modalities, such as text, for holistic
062   decision-making. This gap highlights the need for models that can reason across both time-series
063   data and related textual information. In this work, we propose a multi-modal training process which
064   integrates time-series reasoning capabilities into existing LLMs. To demonstrate its effectiveness,
065   we also compare the results to the current state-of-the-art text encoding method introduced by Gru-
066   ver et al. (2024).
067   Developing an LLM with time-series(TS) reasoning capabilities presents several key challenges:
068   (1) Data Scarcity: There is a lack of existing training data for constructing models that combine
069   time-series data with reasoning. Multi-modal datasets that integrate both text and time-series are
070   currently limited. (2) Benchmarking: Similarly, there are no large-scale benchmarks available to
071   evaluate multi-modal reasoning and decision-making for time-series data. (3) Preserving LLM Ca-
072   pabilities: Current time-series approaches that rely on LLMs as sources of pre-trained parameters
073   do not preserve natural language knowledge and reasoning capabilities inherent in LLMs. Many
074   methods re-train the input and output layers to directly generate or classify time-series data, which
075
      often strip away these important reasoning skills.
076   To address these challenges, our paper makes the following critical contributions:
077
078          • We propose a novel approach to leveraging multi-modal LLMs for time-series analysis,
079            with a specific emphasis on maintaining the LLM’s reasoning capabilities and using its
080
               vast knowledge reservoir to enhance time-series data reasoning.
081          • We contribute three different datasets for training and evaluation of LLMs for time-series
082            reasoning tasks. The TS Instruct Training Dataset, a diverse multi-modal dataset that in-
083            tegrates time-series data with relevant textual information, addressing the critical scarcity of
084            such datasets. The TS Instruct QA Benchmark, a dataset of nearly 4000 multiple-choice
085
               questions with ground truth answers, designed to evaluate multi-modal reasoning capa-
               bilities in time-series analysis. The TS Instruct Quantitative Evaluation Benchmark,
086
               a quantitative benchmark of approximately 200 samples for assessing more nuanced rea-
087
               soning, including QA, math, and decision-making tasks, to provide a more comprehensive
088            evaluation of model performance beyond simple accuracy metrics.
089
090
             • Finally, we design a new family of Chat-TS Models that explicitly preserve the general
               knowledge and reasoning abilities of LLMs within ±2% of their original capacity on chal-
091
               lenging LLM benchmarks, and improve reasoning capabilities by on average ∼15% on our
092
               new TS Instruct QA benchmark compared to existing methods.
093
094   To ensure replicability and facilitate future research all datasets, models, and code for dataset gen-
095   eration, pre-processing, training, and testing are made publicly available at [link].

100
      creasingly leveraging deep learning techniques for tasks such as forecasting, imputation, anomaly
101

104   2023; Chang et al., 2024). Some models, such as Time-LLM (Jin et al., 2024), TEMPO (Cao et al.,

106   to harness LLMs for time-series analysis. However, these works focus primarily on forecasting and
107   modify the model architecture, removing the text generation capabilities, and preventing their use as
      general-purpose LLMs for time-series reasoning and analysis.

127   as it does not interfere with the text-based structure of the model. This encoding strategy serves
128   as our state-of-the-art baseline. Another example is InstructTime (Cheng et al., 2024), which fine-
129   tunes LLMs for time-series classification by predicting text labels. While effective for classification,
130   this approach is less suitable for general time-series reasoning due to the task-specific nature of the
131   fine-tuning process.
132   A more recent work by Merrill et al. (2024) evaluates LLMs specifically for time-series reasoning,
133   using LLMTime text encoding as a baseline, similar to our approach. This study employs synthetic
134   time-series data generated by AI, testing various model configurations. While it underscores the im-
135   portance of adapting LLMs for time-series reasoning, it does not provide methodologies or training
136   datasets to improve model performance. In contrast, our work addresses these limitations by intro-
137   ducing a comprehensive training strategy and novel datasets that enable LLMs to reason effectively
138   across time-series data without compromising their general language capabilities.
139   Table 1 summarizes the capabilities of existing models that integrate LLMs with time-series anal-
140   ysis. Most existing approaches, such as GPT4TS, LLM4TS, and TEMPO, use LLMs as their core
141   but fail to support comprehensive multi-modal reasoning, including text input, text generation, and
142   time-series reasoning. For example, models like InstructTime and LLMTime support text input and
143   generation, but they do not explore the models time-series reasoning capabilities. Our proposed
144   Chat-TS model is the first to combine all these elements, enabling both robust NLP and time-series
145   reasoning, thus filling a critical gap in the current literature.
146
147   Time-Series Evaluation There are many large-scale, diverse and publicaly available datasets for
148   training and evaluation models on time-series tasks, such as Monash Time-Series Forecasting Repos-
149
      itory (Godahewa et al., 2021), the Time-Series Classification Archive (Middlehurst et al., 2024) and

150
      time-series data with limited accompanying textual descriptions, making them unsuitable for train-
151
      ing multi-modal LLMs that require both time-series and text data for reasoning. To bridge this
152   gap, we augment the LOTSA dataset by adding detailed text descriptions for each time-series. This
153   data can then be used in conjunction with AI to generate high quality synthetic datasets for training
154   multi-modal LLM’s for time-series reasoning.

156   Discrete Tokenization Previous work in time-series (Cheng et al., 2024), computer vision (Yu
157   et al., 2024), and audio domains (Défossez et al., 2022; Borsos et al., 2023) typically rely on vector

159   factor of the input data for performing multi-modality tokenization. While these methods work
160   well for high-dimensional data, most time-series data in the literature have sequence lengths below
161   1000 points (Zhou et al., 2021; Max-Planck-Institut fuer Biogeochemie; Makridakis et al., 2020;

172   3.1      P ROBLEM D EFINITION
173
174   Multi-modal time-series reasoning involves a model’s ability to make informed decisions and answer
175   relevant questions based upon time-series data and other non-numerical data such as text.
176
      The model design objective is to expand the vocabulary of a pre-trained LLM to include time-series
177   tokens, training autoregressively alongside text. Let VL denote the original textual vocabulary and
178   VT the set of time-series tokens, forming an extended vocabulary V = VL ∪ VT . The LLM, param-
179   eterized by θ, is trained to predict the next token in a sequence y = {y1 , y2 , . . . , yT −1 }, where each
180   yt ∈ V, using the autoregressive model fθ : V ∗ → V. The training process maximizes the likelihood
                                                                          PT −1
181   of the next token via standard cross-entropy (CE) loss: L(θ) = − t=1 log pθ (yt+1 |y1 , y2 , . . . , yt ),
182   where pθ is the predicted probability of the next token. Since V contains joint representations for
183   text and time-series the training process is reduced to standard LLM training.
184
      The central challenge in developing a high-quality instruction model capable of reasoning with time-
185   series data is balancing the specialized time-series reasoning while maintaining the general natural
186   language understanding (NLU) capabilities of large language models. Our datasets include both
187   Etiological and Question and Answering based time-series reasoning as defined in Merrill et al.
188   (2024). Many existing time-series approaches, such as Zhou et al. (2023), Cao et al. (2024), and
189   Chang et al. (2024), tend to narrow the model’s focus by introducing task-specific heads, which
190   are trained over the natural language head and prevent the model from performing multi-modal
191   time-series reasoning tasks. In contrast, our approach seamlessly integrates TS data into the LLM’s
192   vocabulary, preserving its powerful NLU capabilities without sacrificing TS reasoning.

      We train our model using both text and time-series data, aiming to allow the model to reason over
217   time-series data while preserving its original language modeling capabilities. Unlike other multi-
218   modal models, which require connectors to modify representations for LLM integration (McKinzie
219   et al., 2024), our approach directly adds time-series tokens to the LLM’s vocabulary. This eliminates
220   the need for intermediary connecting components between the time-series and text representation
221   spaces, ensuring a more seamless integration. We then explore two initialization methods for the
222   new embeddings: (1) mean initialization, where embeddings are initialized as the average of existing
223   text tokens, and (2) pre-training, where we unfreeze only the embedding and final linear layer for
224   time-series data.
225   We fine-tune our model with instruction and answer pairs using the multi-modal TS-Instruct dataset
226   alongside a subset of the Open-Orca (Lian et al., 2023) text-only instruction tuning dataset. This
227   dual-dataset strategy helps preserve the LLM’s strong language modeling capabilities while also
228   augmenting its ability to understand and generate time-series data. The balanced training approach
229   ensures that the model remains proficient at text tasks while gaining expertise in time-series reason-
230   ing.

232   3.3   D ISCRETE T OKENIZERS
233
      Tokenizing time-series data presents unique challenges, as it involves converting continuous numer-
234
      ical values into discrete tokens that can be processed by an LLM. Our approach balances recon-
235
      struction accuracy with token scalability, ensuring that the tokenized time-series can be efficiently
236   represented without compromising data integrity.
237
238   There are several key considerations when designing a time-series tokenizer:
239
            1. Reconstruction accuracy: The tokenizer must encode and decode time-series data with
240
               minimal error.
241
242         2. Compression: It should allow for efficient compression to process larger time-series se-
243
               quences.
244         3. Scalability: The tokenizer should support varying vocabulary sizes, improving quality as
245            the number of tokens increases.
246
      Our discrete tokenizer functions as follows: Let X ∈ RL×M represent a multivariate time-series,
247
      where L is the sequence length and M is the number of channels. We quantize the time-series by
248
      dividing the range [−s, s] into K − 1 bins, mapping each value x to its corresponding bin index i,
249   which forms a sequence of discrete tokens Z. A special token Tc = K is added to mark the end
250   of each channel, and the token sequence T is then flattened and offset to align with the vocabulary
251   size of the text tokenizer. Decoding reverses this process, reconstructing the time-series by mapping
252   each index back to the bin centers. The special token is used as a channel separator to reconstruct
253   the original 2D time-series from the 1D sequence of tokens.
254
      We tested four tokenizers, including models using simple linear layers for encoding/decoding with
255   vector quantization (VQ) (van den Oord et al., 2018) and lookup-free quantization (LFQ) (Yu et al.,
256   2024), and a TCN-based (van den Oord et al., 2016) tokenizer using residual vector quantization
257   (RVQ) (Chen et al., 2010) as implemented in InstructTime (Cheng et al., 2024). We compare these
258   approaches to our simple discrete tokenizer that requires no training and provides near-perfect re-
259   construction of time-series data.
260
261   Evaluation To evaluate the performance of each tokenizer, we measured reconstruction quality us-
262   ing varying compression ratios and codebook sizes on the Azure VM Traces 2017 dataset (Hadary
263   et al., 2020). Figure 2 presents the results, showing that our discrete tokenizer significantly outper-
264   forms the others in terms of validation error and improves predictably as the codebook size increases.
265   The results clearly show that the discrete tokenizer far outperforms the other tokenizers in terms of
266   validation error and predictably improves when scaling the number of codebook tokens. The main
267   downside is that the discrete tokenizer does not compress the time-series. While this is a technical
268   limitation, the input and output capabilities of LLM’s is rapidly growing. We therefore feel this is
269   a reasonable limitation to work with. Importantly our tokenizer doesn’t require any training and
      therefore does not suffer from out of distribution problems that may arise from trained tokenizers.

      3.4                     DATASET C REATION
289
290
291   Our training data is categorized into three types: time-series data for pretraining, natural language
292
      data, and a new joint time-series and natural language dataset. Full details regarding each data type
      can be found in Appendix B.
293
294   Time-Series Data: We leverage the LOTSA (Woo et al., 2024) dataset archive, which spans diverse
295   domains and ensures variation in sequence length and channel dimensions. Time-series samples
296   are generated using a sliding window approach with configurable window size, stride, and channel
297   selection, optimizing for diversity and maximum sample size.
298   Natural Language Data: To maintain and augment performance on natural language tasks, we use
299   100,000 samples from the Open-Orca (Lian et al., 2023) dataset, which provides diverse high quality
300   natural language instruction tuning data.
301
      Joint Time-Series and Natural Language Data: As there is limited multi-modaility time-series
302   data available, we synthesize conversations between a user and an assistant using GPT4o-mini (Ope-
303   nAI et al., 2024). These dialogues incorporate time-series data represented as images alongside
304   metadata (length, channels) to guide the model. Conversations are designed to focus on general
305   reasoning, classification, decision-making, and mathematical analysis. Time-series data for reason-
306   ing tasks are sourced from LOTSA (Woo et al., 2024), while classification labels come from the
307   Time-Series Classification Archive (Middlehurst et al., 2024).

310   3.5                     T RAINING
311
312   We test two methods for integrating time-series tokens: mean initialization, and pre-training with
313   time-series data. Mean initialization sets new time-series embeddings based on the average of text
314   tokens in the model vocabulary, while pre-training unfreezes a small portion of the model (embed-
315   ding layer and final linear layer) to allow these new embeddings to specialize. Freezing most of the
316   model preserves the LLM’s existing capabilities while allowing the new embeddings to learn from
317   time-series data. Next, each model is trained either on our TS Instruct dataset and/or the Open Orca
318   text dataset.
319   In our experiments we use the 8 billion parameter LLama 3.1 model (Dubey et al., 2024) which
320   provides a reasonable balance between performance and compute. Our pretraining stage consists of
321   115,000 time-series samples for 1 epoch using an initial learning rate of 2e − 3. We then instruction
322   tune the model on our dataset comprising roughly 18,000 multi-modal samples and 100,000 text
323   samples with a learning rate of 2e − 5 for 1 epoch. In both cases we use cosine-scheduling for the
      learning rate. For more information refer to Appendix C.

      4     R ESULTS AND A NALYSIS
325
326   We evaluate our model using the TS-Instruct QA Benchmark, a dataset designed to address the
327   lack of benchmarks for multi-modal models capable of time-series reasoning. The benchmark evalu-
328   ates models through multiple-choice question-and-answer (QA) tasks, where each question includes
329   a description of the time-series data, four answer options, and a detailed explanation of the correct
330   answer. This allows us to assess both answer accuracy and reasoning capabilities. The dataset was
331   generated using GPT-4o-mini, which, combined with visual representations and time-series meta-
332   data is used to generate the text components of the multiple-choice questions. The full system
333   prompt is detailed in Appendix D.
334   To establish a controlled baseline for model evaluation and evaluate the quality of the generated
335   testing data, we curated a high-quality subset of the benchmark, the TS-Instruct QA Gold dataset.
336   This dataset consists of 100 random samples from the TS-Instruct QA Benchmark. We evaluated
337   the accuracy of these samples for their correctness and found that 92% of the samples were of high
338   quality. These samples were then reviewed and rewritten to ensure clarity, diversity, and accuracy.
339   The Gold dataset serves as a reliable benchmark for time-series reasoning under optimal conditions,
340
      providing a fair basis for comparing models.
341   The evaluation methodology comprises four components: (1) Baseline Comparison on the Gold
342   dataset to benchmark reasoning capabilities under controlled conditions; (2) Natural Language
343   Performance, evaluating the impact of time-series instruction tuning on the model’s general lan-
344   guage understanding and reasoning abilities; (3) evaluation on the Full TS-Instruct QA Bench-
345   mark (3741 samples) to assess the model architecture and training data composition; and (4) Quan-
346
      titative Analysis of Generated Explanations, assessing the quality of responses using metrics such
      as helpfulness, relevance, accuracy, and level of detail.

381   synthetic time-series data and noted that the visual baselines did not perform better than the models
382   which use text encoding based strategies (Gruver et al., 2024). This is a necessary trade-off in
383   building high quality time-series datasets that use real-world time-series.

      4.3   L IMITATIONS
506
507   While our Chat-TS models demonstrate strong time-series reasoning capabilities, several limitations
508
      remain. Accurate time-series generation has yet to be achieved, as our models struggle to reliably
      forecast time-series data. Addressing this limitation could enable broader applications such as fi-
509
      nancial or weather forecasting.
510
511   Additionally, reliance on normalization for tokenization alters original data, potentially distorting
512   real values and complicating numerical tasks requiring precision. For instance, while trends like
513   slope may be correctly computed, real-world values are often inaccurate due to normalized input. A
514
      more sophisticated approach to preserve raw values without normalization is necessary to overcome
      this challenge (see Appendix F.2).
515
516   Time-series classification is another area requiring improvement. Despite including classification
517   tasks during training, the models exhibit weak zero-shot performance, often guessing or repeating
518   class predictions. This indicates limited generalization to unseen classification tasks. Enhancing
519   model robustness through few-shot learning examples, longer context windows, more diverse la-
520
      beled data, and increased task complexity in training could address this gap.
521
522
523   5     C ONCLUSION
524
525
      Our work represents a significant advancement in developing LLMs capable of both natural lan-
526
      guage understanding and time-series reasoning, opening up new applications in areas like health-
527
      care—where analyzing patient data alongside clinical reports is crucial—or financial markets, where
528   joint analysis of stock trends and news reports can support better decision-making.
529
530   To support this effort, we provide several key contributions: the TS Instruct dataset for instruction-
531
      tuning time-series models, the TS Instruct QA benchmark for evaluating time-series reasoning,
      and a detailed quantitative analysis that assesses the quality of model responses, beyond simple
532
      accuracy. We establish a strong state-of-the-art baseline by leveraging recent time-series method-
533
      ologies and demonstrate that our models outperform existing benchmarks in each category, all while
534   preserving the language capabilities of the underlying LLM. This work lays the foundation for fu-
535   ture advancements in multi-modal reasoning, combining both text and time-series modalities in a
536   unified model. It is our hope that the contributions we have made in terms of models, training data
537   and benchmarks help democratize the advancement of multi-modal LLM’s for time-series reasoning
538   tasks. Given that the research of time-series reasoning via LLMs is in its infancy we are excited to
539   see how others can build upon this work to tackle some of the limitations we have discovered in the
      current methodologies.

843
844   Our training data is organized into three categories: pure time-series data used in pretraining, pure
845   natural language data, and joint time-series and natural language data.
846
847   B.1     T IME -S ERIES DATA
848
849
      While there are many collections of time-series data across various domains, we utilize the
      LOTSA (Woo et al., 2024) dataset archive, which is a large compilation spanning multiple domains.
850
      Since this dataset is used to pre-train the embeddings and language modeling head for time-series
851
      data, our goal is to ensure diversity not only in modalities but also in the physical characteristics
852   of the data, such as sequence length and channel dimensions. The dataset generation process is
853   described below:
854
                                                                    Ti ×C
855
      Given a time-series dataset {Xi }N  i=1 , where each Xi ∈ R           represents a time-series with Ti
      timesteps and C channels, we generate time-series samples {Sij }nj=1    i

856
857   proach. A fixed window size W and stride D are applied to extract subsequences Sij ∈ Rwij ×cij ,
858
      where wij is the length of the j-th segment and cij is the number of channels selected from C. These
      parameters ultimately control the number of samples parsed from each dataset.
859
      The start index for each subsequence is tij = jD for j = 0, 1, . . . , Ti −W

860                                                                             D    , and the segment length
861   wij is determined as wij = min(W, rand(m, min(M, Ti − tij ))), where m and M represent the
862   minimum and maximum allowable segment lengths. The dimensionality cij is selected randomly
863   such that 1 ≤ cij ≤ C, and the subsequence Sij is extracted from the original sequence. If the total
      number of elements |Sij | = wij × cij exceeds a specified maximum, cij is reduced iteratively. This

867
      B.2     NATURAL L ANGUAGE DATA
868
869   For the natural language data, we use the Open-Orca (Lian et al., 2023) dataset, which has been
870   successfully used to train high-quality instruction models and contains a large number of samples.
871   This data is combined with the time-series instruction tuning data to regularize training and preserve
872   the model’s performance on natural language tasks. To reduce overall training time, we use 100,000
873   samples from this dataset during full-parameter instruction tuning of the model.
874
875   B.3     T IME -S ERIES AND NATURAL L ANGUAGE DATA
876
      Currently, there are no joint datasets combining both time-series and natural language data. To train
877
      our multi-modal model, we generate synthetic conversations between a user and an assistant using
878
      GPT4o-mini (OpenAI et al., 2024), incorporating both modalities.
879
880   The process begins by sampling n time-series from a dataset D = {x1 , x2 , . . . , xN }, where n =
881   min(len(D), Ns ) samples are selected, Ns being the maximum samples per dataset. For each time-
882
      series xi , the corresponding target data yi ∈ RT ×C , where T is the length and C the number of
      channels, is also extracted. A random number of channels, C ′ ∈ [1, C], and a time-series length T
883
      (proportional to the full length, constrained by Lmax ) are selected.
884
885   The series lengths are computed as:
886                          T = max (1, min (⌊Lmax · U (αmin , αmax )⌋ , Lmax )) ,
887
888
      where U (a, b) is a uniform distribution, and αmin and αmax are the minimum and maximum per-
      centages of the sequence used.
889
890   To create conversations, we use a system prompt, an image of the time-series, and pass the length
891   and number of channels of the time-series. The system prompt provides explicit instructions for
892   generating the conversation, and the time-series dimensions (length, channels) are also provided to
893   ensure the model uses them during the conversation. Since GPT4o-mini cannot analyze the time-
894
      series directly, we attach an image to guide the generated conversation based on the trends and
      patterns within the time-series.
895
896   We design four types of conversations: (1) General Reasoning: These focus on descriptive dis-
897   cussions of time series data, highlighting key aspects and trends; (2) Classification: This involves
898   categorizing the time series based on user-provided labels; (3) Decision-Making: The model eval-
899   uates hypothetical scenarios posed by the user, drawing conclusions from the time series data; and
900
      (4) Mathematical Reasoning: This covers in-depth mathematical analysis of time series. Full de-
      tails and examples can be found in Appendix B.3, D and F. General reasoning, decision-making and
901
      mathematical reasoning time-series are from the LOTSA dataset compilation (Woo et al., 2024),
902
      the classification samples and labels are from the Time-Series Classification Archive (Middlehurst
903   et al., 2024).

       E.1     V ISION M ODELS AND M ULTI -M ODAL E VALUATION
1397
       A key contribution of our work is the creation of a multi-modal instruction-tuning and evaluation
1398   dataset for time-series reasoning, where the time-series data is derived from real-world datasets.
1399   This use of real-world time-series ensures that the generated instructions and tasks are grounded in
1400   practical, meaningful data. To achieve this, we rely on vision-based models to link the time-series
1401   data to corresponding textual instructions. However, this reliance on vision models comes with the
1402   trade-off that their performance on our benchmark is likely inflated, as they are naturally aligned
1403   with the dataset generation process. Another important factor is that the multi-modal adapters of
       these models benefit from the large volumes of multi-modal training data available, which means

       Recent work by (Merrill et al., 2024) provides additional context. It evaluates methods for time-

       series reasoning on a fully synthetic dataset and demonstrates that vision-based models do not
