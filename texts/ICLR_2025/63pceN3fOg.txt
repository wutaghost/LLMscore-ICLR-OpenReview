                                                   A BSTRACT
013
014
015
               What can an agent learn in a stochastic Multi-Armed Bandit (MAB) problem from
               a dataset that contains just a single sample for each arm? Surprisingly, in this work,
016
               we demonstrate that even in such a data-starved setting it may still be possible
017
               to find a policy competitive with the optimal one. This paves the way to reliable
018            decision-making in settings where critical decisions must be made by relying only
019            on a handful of samples. Our analysis reveals that stochastic policies can be
020            substantially better than deterministic ones for offline decision-making. Focusing
021            on offline multi-armed bandits, we design an algorithm called Trust Region of
022            Uncertainty for Stochastic policy enhancemenT (TRUST) which is quite different
023            from the predominant value-based lower confidence bound approach. Its design
024            is enabled by localization laws, critical radii, and relative pessimism. We prove
025            that its sample complexity is comparable to that of LCB on minimax problems
026
               while being substantially lower on problems with very few samples. Finally, we
               consider an application to offline reinforcement learning in the special case where
027
               the logging policies are known.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      In several important problems, critical decisions must be made with just very few samples of pre-
      collected experience. For example, collecting samples in robotic manipulation may be slow and costly,
034
      and the ability to learn from very few interactions is highly desirable (Hester & Stone, 2013; Liu et al.,
035
      2021). Likewise, in clinical trials and in personalized medical decisions, reliable decisions must be
036   made by relying on very small datasets (Liu et al., 2017). Sample efficiency is also key in personalized
037   education (Bassen et al., 2020; Ruan et al., 2023). However, to achieve good performance, the state-
038   of-the-art algorithms may require millions of samples (Fu et al., 2020). These empirical findings
039   seem to be supported by the existing theories: the sample complexity bounds, even minimax optimal
040   ones, can be large in practice due to the large constants and the warmup factors (Ménard et al., 2021;
041   Li et al., 2022; Azar et al., 2017; Zanette et al., 2019).
042   In this work, we study whether it is possible to make reliable decisions with only a few samples.
043   We focus on an offline Multi-Armed Bandit (MAB) problem, which is a foundation model for
044   decision-making (Lattimore & Szepesvári, 2020). In online MAB, an agent repeatedly chooses an
045   arm from a set of arms, each providing a stochastic reward. Offline MAB is a variant where the agent
046   cannot interact with the environment to gather new information and instead, it must make decisions
047   based on a pre-collected dataset without playing additional exploratory actions, aiming at identifying
048   the arm with the highest expected reward (Audibert et al., 2010; Garivier & Kaufmann, 2016; Russo,
049   2016; Ameko et al., 2020).
050   The standard approach to the problem is the Lower Confidence Bound (LCB) algorithm (Rashidinejad
051   et al., 2021), a pessimistic variant of UCB (Auer et al., 2002) that involves selecting the arm with the
052   highest lower bound on its performance. LCB encodes a principle called pessimism under uncertainty,
053   which is the foundation principle for most algorithms for offline bandits and reinforcement learning
      (RL) (Jin et al., 2020; Zanette et al., 2020; Xie et al., 2021; Yin & Wang, 2021; Kumar et al.,


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      2020; Kostrikov et al., 2021). Unfortunately, the available methods that implement the principle of
055   pessimism under uncertainty can fail in a data-starved regime because they rely on confidence intervals
056   that are too loose when just a few samples are available. For example, even on a simple MAB instance
057   with ten thousand arms, the best-known (Rashidinejad et al., 2021) performance bound for the LCB
058   algorithm requires 24 samples per arm in order to provide meaningful guarantees, see Section 2.
059   In more complex situations, such as in the sequential setting with function approximation, such a
060   problem can become more severe due to the higher metric entropy of the function approximation
061   class and the compounding of errors through time steps.
062   These considerations suggest that there is a “barrier of entry” to decision-making, both theoretically
063   and practically: one needs to have a substantial number of samples in order to make reliable decisions
064   even for settings as simple as offline MAB where the guarantees are tighter. Given the above technical
065   reasons, and the lack of good algorithms and guarantees for data-starved decision problems, it is
066   unclear whether it is even possible to find good decision rules with just a handful of samples.
067   In this paper, we make a substantial contribution towards lowering such barriers of entry. We discover
068   that a carefully-designed algorithm tied to an advanced statistical analysis can substantially improve
069   the sample complexity, both theoretically and practically, and enable reliable decision-making with
070   just a handful of samples. More precisely, we focus on the offline MAB setting where we show that
071   even if the dataset contains just a single sample in every arm, it may still be possible to compete with
072   the optimal policy. This is remarkable, because with just one sample per arm—for example from
073   a Bernoulli distribution—it is impossible to estimate the expected payoff of any of the arms! Our
074   discovery is enabled by several key insights:
075
             • We search over stochastic policies, which can yield better performance for offline-decision
076
               making;
077
078          • We use a localized notion of metric entropy to carefully control the size of the stochastic
079
               policy class that we search over;
080          • We implement a concept called relative pessimism to obtain sharper guarantees.
081
082   These considerations lead us to design a trust region policy optimization algorithm called Trust Region
083   of Uncertainty for Stochastic policy enhancemenT (TRUST), one that offers superior theoretical as
084
      well as empirical performance compared to LCB in a data-scarce situation.
085   Moreover, we apply the algorithm to selected reinforcement learning problems from (Fu et al., 2020)
086   in the special case where information about the logging policies is available. We do so by a simple
087   reduction from reinforcement learning to bandits, by mapping policies and returns in the former to
088   actions and rewards in the latter, thereby disregarding the sequential aspect of the problem. Although
089   we rely on the information of the logging policies being available, the empirical study shows that our
090
      algorithm compares well with a strong deep reinforcement learning baseline (i.e, CQL from (Kumar
      et al., 2020)), without being sensitive to partial observability, sparse rewards, and hyper-parameters.

193   In this section, we introduce our algorithm, called Trust Region of Uncertainty for Stochastic policy
194   enhancemenT (TRUST). At a high level, the algorithm is a policy optimization algorithm based on a
195   trust region centered around a reference policy. The size of the trust region determines the degree of
196   pessimism, and its optimal problem-dependent size can be determined by analyzing the supremum of
197
      a problem-dependent empirical process. In the sequel, we describe 1) the decision variables, 2) the
      trust region optimization program, and 3) some techniques for its practical implementation.

395   Augmentation with LCB. Compared to classical LCB, Algorithm 1 considers a much larger searching
396   space, which encompasses not only the vertices of the simplex but the inner points as well. This
397
      enlargement of searching space shows great advantage, but this also comes with the price of larger
      uncertainty, especially when the width ε is large. In LCB, one considers the uncertainty by upper
398
      bound the noise at each vertex uniformly, while in our case, the uniform upper bound for a sub-region
399
      of the shifted simplex must be considered. When ε is large, the trust region method will induce
400   larger uncertainty and tend to select a more stochastic policy than LCB and hence, can achieve worse
401   performance. Moreover, when each arm has sufficiently many data samples to roughly estimate its
402   mean return to reasonable accuracy, LCB works well because it chooses the arm with a tight lower
403   bound. However the current results for LCB do not cover the important case where only few samples
404   (e.g., less than 24 as described in Section 2) are available. Encouragingly our work shows strong
405   results in such settings. To determine the most effective final policy, one can always combine TRUST
406   (Algorithm 1) with LCB and select the better one between them based on the lower bound induced by
407   two algorithms. By comparing the lower bounds of LCB and TRUST, the value of the finally output
408
      policy is guaranteed to outperform the lower bound for either LCB or TRUST with high probability.
      We defer the detailed algorithm and its theoretical guarantees to Appendix G.
