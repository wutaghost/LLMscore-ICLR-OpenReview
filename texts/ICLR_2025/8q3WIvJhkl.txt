009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Training diffusion models is always a computation-intensive task. In this paper,
012
               we introduce a novel speed-up method for diffusion model training, called SpeeD,
013
               which is based on a closer look at time steps. Our key findings are: i) Time
014            steps can be empirically divided into acceleration, deceleration, and convergence
015            areas based on the process increment. ii) These time steps are imbalanced, with
016            many concentrated in the convergence area. iii) The concentrated steps provide
017            limited benefits for diffusion training. To address this, we design an asymmetric
018            sampling strategy that reduces the frequency of steps from the convergence area
019            while increasing the sampling probability for steps from other areas. Additionally,
020            we propose a weighting strategy to emphasize the importance of time steps with
021            rapid-change process increments. As a plug-and-play and architecture-agnostic
022
               approach, SpeeD consistently achieves 3× acceleration across various diffusion
               architectures, datasets, and tasks. Notably, due to its simple design, our approach
023
               significantly reduces the cost of diffusion model training with minimal overhead.
024
               Our research enables more researchers to train diffusion models at a lower cost.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Training diffusion models is not usually affordable for many researchers, especially for ones in
031   academia. For example, DALL·E 2 (OpenAI, 2023) needs 40K A100 GPU days and Sora (OpenAI,
032   2024) at least necessitates 126K H100 GPU days. Therefore, accelerating the training for diffusion
033   models has become urgent for broader generative AI and other computer vision applications.
034
      Recently, some acceleration methods for diffusion training focus on time steps, primarily using
035
      re-weighting and re-sampling 1) Re-weighting on the time steps based on heuristic rules. P2 (Choi
036   et al., 2022) and Min-SNR (Hang et al., 2023) use monotonous and single-peak weighting strategies
037   according to sign-to-noise ratios (SNR) in different time steps. 2) Re-sampling the time steps. Log-
038   Normal (Karras et al., 2022) assigns high sampling probabilities for the middle time steps of the
039   diffusion process. CLTS (Xu et al., 2024) proposes a curriculum learning based time step schedule,
040   gradually tuning the sampling probability from uniform to Gaussian by interpolation for acceleration
041   as shown in Fig. 1b.
042   To investigate the essence of the above accelerations, we take a closer look at the time steps. The
043   diffusion models essentially learn and estimate the process increment noted as δt := xt+1 − xt at time
044   step t at training and inference phase. As illustrated in the left of Fig. 1a, we visualize the changes
045   of mean and variance of process increment through the time steps. The time steps are categorized
046   into three areas: acceleration, deceleration, and convergence, based on observations and theoretical
047   analyses (Sec. 2.2) that both the changes of mean and variance initially accelerate, subsequently
048   decelerate, and ultimately converge in a narrow interval.
049   One can easily find that the proportions of the three areas are imbalanced: there are a large number of
050   time steps at convergence area, and the others are small. Another finding is that process increments at
051   the convergence area are almost identical noise, e.g., in DDPM, the distribution are nearly N (0, 2I),
052   where I is the unit matrix. To further explore the characteristics of these three areas, we visualize the
053   training loss curve in the right of Fig. 1a. The loss values from the convergence area are much lower
      than those from the other areas, which indicates estimating the identical noise is easy.

192
               • Pay more attention to the process increment’s rapid-change area which is hard to learn and
193
                 the corresponding time steps are fewer than the other areas.

241   2.4    OVERVIEW OF S PEE D
242
243   Based on the above observations and analyses, we propose SpeeD , a novel approach for achieving
244   lossless training acceleration tailored for diffusion models. As illustrated in Fig. 2, SpeeD sup-
245
      presses the trivial time steps from convergence area, and weight the rapid-change intervals between
      acceleration and deceleration areas. Correspondingly, two main modules, asymmetric sampling
246
      and change-aware weighting, are proposed. Asymmetric sampling uses a two-step step function to
247
      respectively suppress and increase the sampling probability corresponding to trivial and beneficial
248   time steps. Change-aware weighting is based on the change rate of process increment ∂t Ψ(t).

465
466   We perform extensive ablation studies to illustrate the characteristics of SpeeD . The experiments
467
      in ablation studies are conducted on the FFHQ dataset and U-Net model by default. We ablate
      our designed components: asymmetric sampling (abbreviated as asymmetric) and change-aware
468
      weighting (abbreviated as CAW), suppression intensity k in asymmetric sampling defined in Eqn. 3
469
      and symmetry ceiling λ for weighting in Sec. 2.6.
470
471
472
      Evaluating the components of SpeeD. Our approach includes two strategies: asymmetric sampling
      and change-aware weighting. We note these two strategies using ‘asymmetric’ and ‘CAW’. We ablate
473
      each component in SpeeD. As illustrated in Tab. 6a, combining our proposed strategies achieves the
474
      best results. Using weighting and sampling strategies alone improves the baseline by 0.6 and 1.5
475   FID scores, respectively, indicating that filtering most samples from the convergence area is more
476   beneficial for training.
477
478
      Evaluating of suppression intensity k. To prioritize the training of critical time steps, asymmetric
479
      sampling focus on the time steps out of the convergence area. A higher probability is given for these
480   time steps which is k times than the time steps from the convergence area. A larger suppression
481   intensity k means a larger gap in training between the different areas of time steps. We evaluate
482   different suppression intensity k from 1 to 25 and report the FID score In Tab. 6b. We observe that
483   k = 5 achieves the best performance. A huge suppression intensity decrease FID scores seriously,
484   which means that it hurts the diversity a lot to modeling data. This means that the samples in the
485   convergence area, although very close to pure noise, still contains some useful information. Extreme
      complete discard of these samples results in a degradation of the acceleration performance.


                                                                                 9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Evaluating of symmetry ceiling λ. The symmetry ceiling λ is a hyper-parameter that regulates the
487   curvature of the weighting function. λ is set in the interval [0.5, 1]. The midpoint of the re-scaled
488   weight interval is fixed at 0.5. The symmetry ceiling λ is the right boundary of the interval and the
489   left boundary is 1-λ. A higher λ results in a larger weight interval and a more obvious distinction in
490   weights between different time steps. In Tab. 6c, settings λ ≤ 0.8 obtain higher performance on FID
491   scores than the baseline, which indicates SpeeD is relatively robust on symmetry ceiling λ. Further
492   increase λ leads to performance degradation. This means weighting should be in moderation.

494   4   R ELATED W ORK
495
496   We discuss the related works about Diffusion Models and its Training Acceleration. The most related
497   works are as follows, and more about cross-modality and video generation are in Appendix C.
498
499   Diffusion models Diffusion models have emerged as the dominant approach in generative tasks (Sa-
500   haria et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023b; Wang et al., 2024a; Igashov et al., 2024), which outperform
501   other generative methods including GANs (Zhu et al., 2017; Isola et al., 2017; Brock et al., 2018),
502   VAEs (Kingma & Welling, 2013), flow-based models (Dinh et al., 2014). These methods (Ho et al.,
503   2020; Song et al., 2020; Karras et al., 2022) are based on non-equilibrium thermodynamics (Jarzynski,
504   1997; Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015), where the generative process is modeled as a reverse diffusion
505   process that gradually constructs the sample from a noise distribution (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015).
506
      Previous works focused on enhancing diffusion models’ generation quality and alignment with users
      in visual generation. To generate high-resolution images, Latent Diffusion Models (LDMs) (Saharia
507
      et al., 2022; Rombach et al., 2022) perform diffusion process in latent space instead of pixel space,
508
      which employ VAEs to be encoder and decoder for latent representations.
509
510   Acceleration in diffusion models To reduce the computational costs, previous works accelerate the
511   diffusion models in training and inference. For training acceleration, the earliest works (Choi et al.,
512   2022; Hang et al., 2023) assign different weights to each time step on Mean Square Error (MSE)
513   loss to improve the learning efficiency. The other methods in training acceleration are proposed,
514   e.g., network architecture (Ryu & Ye, 2022; Wang et al., 2024b) and diffusion algorithm (Karras
515   et al., 2022; Wu et al., 2023). Masking modeling (Gao et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2023) are recently
516   proposed to train diffusion models. Works (Go et al., 2023; Park et al., 2024b;a; Kim et al., 2024)
517   provide observations for explanation from the perspective of multi-tasks learning. SpeeD is of
518
      good compatibility with these methods, e.g., (Yu et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2024a;b). In the field of
      sampling acceleration, a number of works tackle the slow inference speed of diffusion models by
519
      using fewer reverse steps while maintaining sample quality, including DDIM (Song et al., 2020),
520
      Analytic-DPM (Bao et al., 2022), and DPM-Solver (Lu et al., 2022).
521
522
      Conditional generation. To better control the generation process with various conditions, e.g.,
523   image style, text prompt and stroke, Classifier-Free Guidance (CFG) proposes a guidance strategy
524   with diffusion models that balance the sample quality and prompt alignment. ControlNet (Zhang et al.,
525   2023b) reuses the large-scale pre-trained layers of source models to build a deep and strong encoder
526   to learn specific conditions. Recently benefiting from diffusion models in the image generation field,
527   video generation (Ma et al., 2024; Lab & etc., 2024) is getting trendy.
528
529
      5   C ONCLUSION
530
531
      We present SpeeD , a novel approach for accelerating diffusion training by closely examining time
532   steps. The core insights from this examination are: 1) suppressing the sampling probabilities of
533   time steps that offer limited benefits to diffusion training (i.e., those with extremely small losses),
534   and 2) emphasizing the importance of time steps with rapidly changing process increments. SpeeD
535   demonstrates strong robustness across various architectures and datasets, achieving significant accel-
536   eration on multiple diffusion-based image generation tasks. Additionally, SpeeD is easily compatible
537   with other diffusion acceleration methods, highlighting its wide applicability. We provide extensive
538   theoretical analysis in this paper, aiming to support future research in both academia and industry.

818   A.5     D ETAIL OF MDT + S PEE D E XPERIMENT
819
820   MDT utilizes an asymmetric diffusion transformer architecture, which is composed of three main
821   components: an encoder, a side interpolater, and a decoder. During training, a subset of the latent
822   embedding patches is randomly masked using Gaussian noise with a masking ratio. Then, the
823
      remaining latent embedding, along with the full latent embedding is input into the diffusion model.
824   Following the official implementation of MDT, We utilize DiT-S/2 and MDT-S/2 as our base archi-
825   tecture, whose total block number both is 12 and the number of decoder layers in MDT is 2. We
826   employ the AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017) optimizer with constant learning rate 1e-4 using
827   256 batch size without weight decay on class-conditional ImageNet with an image resolution of 2562 .
828   We perform training on the class-conditional ImageNet dataset with images of resolution 256x256.
829
      The diffusion models are trained for a total of 1000K iterations, utilizing a mask ratio of 0.3.
830
831   A.6     D ETAIL OF FDM + S PEE D E XPERIMENT
832
      FDM add the momentum to the forward diffusion process with a scale that control the weight of
833
      momentum for faster convergence to the target distribution. Following official implementation, we
834
      train diffusion models of EDM and FDM. We retrain these official network architecture which is
835   U-Net with positional time embedding with dropout rate 0.13 in training. We adopt Adam optimizer
836   with learning rate 1e-3 and batch size 512 to train each model by a total of 200 million images of
837   322 CIFAR-10 dataset. During training, we adopt a learning rate ramp-up duration of 10 Mimgs and
838   set the EMA half-life as 0.5 Mimgs. For evaluation, EMA models generate 50K images using EDM
839   sampler based on Heun’s 2nd order method (Süli & Mayers, 2003).
840
841   A.7     T EXT- TO -I MAGE E XPERIMENT D ETAIL
842
843   In text to image task, diffusion models synthesize images with textual prompts. For understanding
844   textual prompts, text-to-image models need semantic text encoders to encode language text tokens
845   into text embedding. We incorporate a pre-trained CLIP language encoder, which processes text with
846
      a maximum token length of 77. DiT-XL/2 is employed as our base diffusion architecture. We employ
      AdamW optimizer with a constant learning rate 1e-4 without weight decay. We train text-to-image
847
      diffusion models for 400K training iterations on MS-COCO training dataset and evaluate the FID and
848
      CLIP score on MS-COCO validation dataset. To enhance the quality of conditional image synthesis,
849   we implement classifier-free guidance with 1.5 scale factor.

       Text to image generation with diffusion models Text-to-image generation has emerged as a hotly

1051   et al., 2024). Convert textual descriptions into corresponding visual content, models not only learn to
1052   synthesize image content but also ensuring alignment with the accompanying textual descriptions.
1053   To better align images with textual prompt guidance, previous work has primarily focused on
1054   enhancements in several schemes including strengthening the capacity of text encoder (Raffel et al.,
1055   2020; Radford et al., 2021) improving the condition plugin module in diffusion model (Zhang et al.,
1056   2023b), improving data quality (Betker et al., 2023).
1057   Video generation with diffusion models. As diffusion models achieve tremendous success in image
1058   generation, video generation has also experienced significant breakthroughs, marking the field’s
1059   evolution and growth. Inspired by image diffusion, pioneering works such as RVD Yang et al. (2022)
1060   and VDM Ho et al. (2022b) explore video generation using diffusion methods. Utilizing temporal
1061   attention and latent modeling mechanisms, video diffusion has advanced in terms of generation
1062   quality, controllability, and efficiency Ho et al. (2022a); Singer et al. (2022); Zhou et al. (2022); He
1063   et al. (2022); Zhang et al. (2023a); Guo et al. (2023); Wu et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2023). Notably,
1064   Stable Video Diffusion Blattmann et al. (2023) and Sora Brooks et al. (2024) achieve some of the
1065
       most appealing results in the field.
1066   Other diffusion acceleration works To achieve better results with fewer NFE steps, Consistency
1067   Models Song et al. (2023) and Consistency Trajectory Models Kim et al. (2023) employ consistency
1068   loss and novel training methods. Rectified Flow Liu et al. (2022), followed by Instaflow Liu et al.

1070   improved prediction targets, together with the reflow operation.
