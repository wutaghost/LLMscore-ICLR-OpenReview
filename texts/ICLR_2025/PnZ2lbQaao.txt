009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            In cross-domain recommendation systems, addressing cold-start items remains a
012            significant challenge. Previous methods typically focus on maximizing perfor-
013            mance using cross-domain knowledge, often treating the knowledge transfer pro-
014            cess as a black box. However, the recent development of domain indexing intro-
015            duces a new approach to better address such challenges. We have developed an ad-
016            versarial Bayesian framework, Domain Indexing Collaborative Filtering (DICF),
017            that infers domain indices during cross-domain recommendation. This frame-
018
               work not only significantly improves the recommendation performance but also
               provides interpretability for cross-domain knowledge transfer. This is verified by
019
               our empirical results on both synthetic and real-world datasets.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
023
024
      In recommender systems, prior user-item interactions are crucial for facilitating accurate recom-
025   mendations. However, when an item has not previously interacted with any users – known as a
026   “cold-start” item – it becomes challenging to provide high-quality recommendations. This issue is
027   common in cross-domain recommendation, where we train the recommender system on user-item
028   interactions from a source domain but, during inference, encounter items from other domains that
029   the system has never seen before. For example, a recommender system is trained on users and items
030   from the United States, but during inference, it needs to handle items from the United Kingdom.
031   Extensive efforts have been made to address this problem. For instance, Jiang et al. (2016) pro-
032   posed a semi-supervised transfer learning approach that uses overlapped-user-based similarities to
033   regularize matrix factorization results. Zhu et al. (2019) introduced special embedding layers to
034   create unique embeddings for users and items in each domain, while the embeddings of overlapping
035   users are a combination of embeddings from different domains. Wang et al. (2015) incorporated
036   additional item and user content information to generate more robust latent representations across
037   domains. While these methods leverage cross-domain information to enhance performance, they
038   often treat the transfer process as a “black box,” limiting our understanding and hindering further
039
      model improvements. This issue is critical because it can obscure the model’s decision-making
      process, making it difficult to trust and audit.
040
041   Recent advances in Domain Adaptation, particularly in domain indexing (Wang et al., 2020; Liu
042   et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023; 2022), offer a new perspective on this problem. A domain index
043   – either a scalar or a vector that encodes domain semantics – has been shown to improve model
044   generalization while providing insights into the transfer process.
045   Inspired by this idea, we propose an adversarial Bayesian framework, dubbed Domain Indexing
046   Collaborative Filtering (DICF), which infers domain indices during cross-domain recommendation.
047   The core idea is to aggregate and distill domain-specific features, which then serve as the domain
048   index. This domain index, combined with adversarial learning, enables the model to learn generaliz-
049   able features, explore domain relationships, and highlight spurious information during cross-domain
050   recommendation, thereby improving our understanding of the process.
051   Example 1. Examples to Illustrate Domain Indices for Recommendation and Interpretability]
052   Consider the tissue products from Japan, Germany, and Spain, which, despite some similarities,
053   vary significantly in descriptions due to language differences. If a recommendation model is trained

090   2.1   P ROBLEM S ETTING AND N OTATION
091
      In this paper, we address recommendation problems involving a total number of N users and M
092
      items, with the items divided into K domains, and each domain k containing Mk items. Each item
093
      is characterized by content features represented as an M × J matrix x, while the user content is
094   given by an N × J matrix u. The user-item interactions are represented by an N × M binary matrix
095   R, where a value of “1” indicates that the user likes the item, while “0” indicates either a lack of
096   preference or no interaction with the item. Our model is trained on interactions (ratings) between
097   users and items across Ks source domains, aiming to predict interactions for items in Kt target
098   domains for all users, where Ks + Kt = K. Notably, all items in the target domains are cold-start
099   items, meaning that they are not present in the training set.
100
101   2.2   M ODEL I NTUITION
102
103   In this subsection, we explain what we mean by the “domain index” in the context of cross-market
104
      recommendation, discuss how it facilitates model interpretation and performance, and describe how
      to derive such a domain index.
105
106   In product recommendations, auxiliary data often improves outcomes. For instance, each product
107   typically includes product descriptions and tags – which we refer to as item contexts. Our goal is to
      learn item representations that generalize effectively from these contexts. Thus, we must eliminate


                                                       2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      non-generalizable (spurious) features during feature embedding. For example, the same item may
109   have contexts in different languages. While product information like brand and price is crucial, the
110   language used should not affect recommendations. Therefore, we separate features into useful and
111   spurious ones, using only the useful for prediction. Spurious features are domain-specific and cannot
112   generalize across domains; thus, they uniquely identify each domain, making them a good source
113   for the domain index.
114
      The domain index, aggregated from these spurious features, captures relationships among domains.
115   For instance, we might find that the domain index for U.S. products is closer to that of Mexico
116   than to that of Japan, indicating how the model transfers across different markets. Moreover, since
117   the domain indices embed these domain relationships, we include them as additional inputs to the
118   model, further enhancing performance, as verified in our experiments.
119
      To derive the domain index in an unsupervised manner, we leverage two properties of spurious
120
      features: (1) they do not generalize across domains, and (2) they do not facilitate recommendation.
121   Accordingly, we perform this decomposition using adversarial learning. We infer two types of
122   features: the domain index and domain-invariant features. We enforce independence between the
123   domain index and domain-invariant features using a discriminator. We then use only the domain-
124   invariant features for subsequent rating prediction. This ensures that prediction-related information
125   is retained in domain-invariant features, while domain-specific information is captured in the domain
126   index, as proved by Xu et al. (2023). Importantly, our domain index differs from general domain-
127   dependent features: it is a domain-level variable shared by all data points in the same domain, not
128   an instance-level variable. This enables the domain index to uniquely represent each domain.
129
130   2.3   P ROBABILISTIC G RAPHICAL M ODEL OF DICF
131
      Based on the intuition above, we propose a hierarchical Bayesian deep learning model, Domain
132
      Indexing Collaborative Filtering (DICF), to achieve this goal. It follows the generative process
133
      illustrated in Fig. 1 (left).
134
135   Generative Process of DICF. We assume the generative process below for DICF:
136
137
        • For each domain k, a domain index β k is generated from a prior distribution pθ (β|α).
138     • Using β k , we generate a domain-specific feature γ for each item within domain k from
139       pθ (γ|β k ).
140     • γ is then used to generate the item content features x from pθ (x|γ), where x represents the
141       item’s observable attributes.
142     • The item latent vector v is generated from distribution pθ (v|β k , γ, x) .
143
        • Finally, the predicted rating R for each user-item pair is computed using the user vector u and
144
          the item latent vector v with distribution pθ (R|u, v). Note that the user vector is generated
145
          from the user context using pretrained encoders and is treated as an observed variable.
146
147   Inference Process of DICF. In the inference process, we aim to estimate the latent variables γ, β k ,
148   and v from the observed data, as illustrated in Fig. 1 (right). The steps are as follows:
149
150     • Given the observed item content features x, we infer the local domain-specific feature γ using
151       qϕ (γ|x).
152     • We aggregate the inferred γ values for all items within domain k to estimate the domain indices
153       β k with distribution qϕ (β k |γ), capturing domain-level patterns.
154     • We infer the item embedding vectors v based on the estimated β k , the content features x, and
155       the local item indices γ with distribution qϕ (v|β k , γ, x).

372   We compare our proposed method with both the state of arts methods from both domain adaptation

      4                               R ELATED W ORKS
473
474
475   Domain Adaptation. Domain adaptation has been extensively studied (Pan & Yang, 2009; Pan
476   et al., 2010; Ganin et al., 2016; Long et al., 2018; Saito et al., 2018; Sankaranarayanan et al., 2018;
477
      Zhang et al., 2019b; Peng et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2019; Dai et al., 2019; Nguyen-Meidine et al.,
      2021; Zou et al., 2018; Kumar et al., 2020; Prabhu et al., 2021; Farahani et al., 2021; Mancini et al.,
478
      2019; Tasar et al., 2020; Jin et al., 2022) to leverage prior knowledge to improve the performance
479
      of models in new environments. Various approaches have been developed for domain adaptation,
480   with adversarial learning (Ganin et al., 2016; Ben-David et al., 2010; Tzeng et al., 2017; Zhang
481   et al., 2019b; Kuroki et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2019; Dai et al., 2019) emerging as one of the most
482   effective due to its high performance. Typically, adversarial learning focuses on learning domain-
483   invariant features that generalizes across domains. Recently, several studies (Wang et al., 2020; Xu
484   et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023) have introduced domain indices alongside adversarial
485   learning to further improve model generalization and interpretability. Building on this intuition, our
      work applies these ideas to the field of cross-domain recommendation.


                                                                                                                       9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Cross-Domain Recommendation. Cross-domain recommendation (Khan et al., 2017; Zhu et al.,
487   2021a; Zang et al., 2022) focuses on utilizing information from different domains to address issues
488   like cold-start and data sparsity. Scenarios typically differ based on whether there is overlap in users
489   or items across domains. Our research specifically targets scenarios where there is no overlap in
490   items and only partial overlap in users. Common approaches to these challenges include Collective
491   Matrix Factorization (Jiang et al., 2016; Rafailidis & Crestani, 2017; Yang et al., 2017; Zhang et al.,
492   2018; Zhu & Chen, 2022), Representation Combination for Overlapping Users (Perera & Zimmer-
493   mann, 2017; Zhu et al., 2019; 2020; 2021b), and Embedding Mapping (Man et al., 2017; Wang
494   et al., 2018; Fu et al., 2019; Li & Tuzhilin, 2021; Nahta et al., 2025). However, most of the works
495
      do not address our zero-shot problem setting, which lacks user-item interactions in the target item
      domains. Some methods require initial interactions (Zhang et al., 2019a; Zhu & Chen, 2022; Nahta
496
      et al., 2025), while others require additional contexts such as knowledge graphs (Bi et al., 2020;
497
      Lu et al., 2024) for making recommendations. In addition, no prior work has explicitly extracted
498   domain indices using adversarial learning to enhance both the performance and interpretability of
499   the model.

      5   C ONCLUSIONS
503
      In this paper, we addressed the challenge of cold-start items in cross-domain recommendation sys-
504
      tems by introducing the Domain Indexing Collaborative Filtering (DICF) framework. This adver-
505
      sarial Bayesian approach infers domain indices during the recommendation process, significantly
506   improving performance and providing interpretability for cross-domain knowledge transfer. Future
507   research could extend our framework to accommodate dynamic domains where user preferences and
508   domain characteristics evolve over time. We believe that our DICF framework opens new avenues
509   for both improving recommendation systems and advancing the interpretability of cross-domain
510   knowledge transfer.
