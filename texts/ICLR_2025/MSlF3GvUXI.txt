                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               The emergence of large language models (LLMs) has revolutionized natural lan-
010
               guage processing, but their development and deployment face significant chal-
011            lenges in computational resources and environmental sustainability. Traditional
012            self-supervised learning (SSL) paradigms requiring extensive computational in-
013            frastructure and exhibiting slow convergence rates, leading to increased energy
014            consumption and longer training durations. While existing model fine-tuning
015            techniques such as Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) are resource-intensive and fail
016            to facilitate swift knowledge updates when integrating a mount of new data in
017            model version iteration. To mitigate these challenges, we introduce Structured-
018            initialization learning (S AIL), a novel method for accelerating the training of neural
019
               network models by leveraging knowledge from (publicly available) pre-trained
               models. Our approach comprises two key components: (1) a parameter transforma-
020
               tion technique that adjusts the dimensions of pre-trained model parameters to match
021
               the target architecture, and (2) a proximal parameter integration and retraining
022            strategy that efficiently combines transformed parameters to initialize new models.
023            We formalize the concept of Proximal Parameter and provide theoretical guarantees
024            for its convergence advantages. Our approach achieves substantial reductions in
025            training time and computational resources while maintaining or improving model
026            performance on downstream tasks. These results indicate that S AIL provides a
027            promising direction for the more efficient and accessible development of the deep
028            learning community. Our code will be made publicly available.

      The emergence of Large Language Models (LLMs) such as GPT-3 (Brown et al., 2020), GPT-4 (Ope-
      nAI et al., 2023), PaLM (Chowdhery et al., 2023), and Gemini (Team et al., 2023) has ushered in a
034
      new era of natural language processing. These models have demonstrated unprecedented capabilities
035
      across a wide range of sequence tasks, showcasing remarkable advancements in representation
036   learning. However, their success is accompanied by significant challenges. The development
037   and deployment of LLMs require enormous computational resources, raising serious concerns
038   about environmental sustainability and accessibility (Strubell et al., 2020). Moreover, while recent
039   advancements have introduced efficient methods for data augmentation (Zhou et al., 2024) and
040   synthesis of higher-quality data (Kaddour et al., 2023) to streamline the dataset, the pre-training
041   process for these models remains prohibitively expensive and time-consuming (Sun et al., 2017).
042   A similar challenge constrains the advancement of self-supervised learning in both language
043   and vision domains. Specifically, self-supervised learning for large models effectively leverages
044   unlabeled data for representation learning (Liu, 2019; He et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2020), but it faces
045   significant challenges in convergence speed and efficiency (Liu & Zhao, 2021; Wang et al., 2021).
046   This paradigm renders the learning and fine-tuning process particularly resource-intensive (Faiz et al.,
047   2023; Gao et al., 2020). As diverse new training data are curated, including high-quality synthetic
048   data (Fan et al., 2024), the computational demands continue to escalate.
049   While techniques like LoRA and QLoRA (Hu et al., 2021; Dettmers et al., 2024; SONG et al.,
050   2024) enable efficient fine-tuning of pre-trained models on domain-specific data, and model editing
051   techniques (Meng et al., 2022) allow for rapid knowledge modification, these methods still demand
052   substantial resources. Multiple modifications and edits can lead to model collapse (Wu & Papyan,
053   2024; Gu et al., 2024). Moreover, as indicated by Zhu & Li (2023), post-training fine-tuning struggles
      to rectify erroneous knowledge learned during the training phase, potentially perpetuating hallucina-

097   To investigate our new learning paradigm claim in Proposition 1 , we introduce S AIL, a novel method
098   that leverages freely available pre-trained models to accelerate training. Our approach improves
099   efficiency in the initial training stages by effectively utilizing the parameters of pre-trained models
100   directly, thus establishing a rapid pathway for representation model training (see Figure 1 ).
101   The core of our method involves inheriting and integrating knowledge directly from pre-trained model
102   parameters, creating a shortcut in the learning process. This approach allows the initial model ϕ
103   to effectively reach a “Proximal Parameter” θ P that is closer to optimal than randomly initialized
104   parameters, thereby significantly accelerating the learning process. As formalized in Definition 1 ,
105   our method first aligns the parameter dimensions from various pre-trained models into a unified
106   format. Subsequently, we execute a weighted parameter averaging that accounts for the effective
107   knowledge embedded in the parameters of each model, thereby enhancing both knowledge transfer

126   2    R ELATED W ORK
127
128   Our work on S AIL builds upon and extends several areas of research in efficient training techniques,
129   particularly for LLMs. We discuss three distinct relevant areas: 1) techniques for efficient training;
130   2) methods for transforming and reusing deep models; and 3) model merging methods to combine
131
      different models.
132
133
      Efficient training techniques for representation learning. A critical aspect of efficient training
      involves effective model initialization, which can significantly influence convergence speed and
134
      overall training efficiency. Techniques such as Xavier Initialization (Glorot & Bengio, 2010) and
135
      Kaiming Initialization (He et al., 2015) have been foundational in ensuring stable gradients and
136   accelerating convergence during training.
137
138   Beyond initialization, dynamic architecture approaches achieve efficiency by dynamically activating
139
      or deactivating network components during training, employing strategies such as layer stacking
      (Gong et al., 2019), layer dropping (Zhang & He, 2020), and the use of sparse attention mechanisms
140
      (Child et al., 2019). Batch selection techniques enhance learning efficiency by prioritizing the most
141
      informative training examples, utilizing methods like selective backprop (Jiang et al., 2019), RHO loss
142   (Mindermann et al., 2022), and curriculum learning (Bengio et al., 2009). Furthermore, innovative
143   optimizers such as Lion (Wang et al., 2023a), Sophia (Liu et al., 2023), and AdaFactor (Shazeer &
144   Stern, 2018) provide alternatives to traditional optimizers like Adam(W), promoting more efficient
145   convergence. Techniques like mixed-precision training (Micikevicius et al., 2017) and gradient
146   checkpointing (Chen et al., 2016) further mitigate computational demands by reducing memory
147   consumption, thereby enabling the training of larger models on limited hardware resources.
148   Unlike these methods, our S AIL directly leverages the parameters of multiple pre-trained models to
149   create a well-informed starting point, potentially reducing the need for complex training optimizations.
150   While these existing techniques could be combined with our approach for further efficiency gains.
151
152   Model reuse and expansion. Approaches in this category focus on leveraging pre-existing knowl-
153   edge to initialize or expand models. Model reuse methods enable the adaptation of pre-trained models
154   for new tasks or larger architectures without retraining from scratch. Notable examples include
155   Samragh et al. (2024), who explore scalable model reuse strategies, and Wang et al. (2023b), who
156   investigate data-driven approaches for model adaptation.
157   Model expansion techniques aim to scale smaller models to initialize larger ones, ensuring that the
158   expanded models retain the learned representations. Classic methods like Net2Net (Chen et al.,
159   2015) provide a foundation for expanding neural networks by transferring knowledge from smaller to
160   larger architectures. More recent advancements, such as Learning to Grow (Wang et al., 2023a) and
161   MorphNet (Gordon et al., 2018), focus on dynamically increasing model capacity during training,
      thereby enhancing scalability and performance.

162
      Progressive learning methods gradually increase model capacity during training, which can lead
163   to more efficient learning and better generalization. Works by Li et al. (2022); Pan et al. (2024)
164   introduce automated strategies for progressive model scaling.
165
166
      Knowledge transfer techniques utilize distillation to transfer knowledge from smaller to larger models
      or between models of similar sizes, enhancing performance and training efficiency. Methods such
167
      as Knowledge Inheritance (Qin et al., 2021) and Born-Again Networks (Furlanello et al., 2018)
168
      exemplify effective strategies for transferring learned representations.
169
170   Our research focuses on the novel capability to incorporate parameters from multiple pre-trained
171   models with diverse architectures. This approach generates a sophisticated initialization for the target
172
      model, surpassing conventional methods that are limited to single-model adaptation or expansion.
173
174
      Model merging. This area focuses on combining multiple models to create a single, more powerful
      model. Simple approaches like Model Soup (Wortsman et al., 2022) apply straightforward weight
175
      averaging to merge models, thus combining their diverse learned representations. Advancements
176
      such as Checkpoint Merging (Liu et al., 2024) introduce Bayesian optimization to effectively select
177   and weight various checkpoints, resulting in a more robust and high-performing merged model.
178   Additionally, techniques like cross-model integration via MindMerger (Huang et al., 2024) enable
179   the fusion of models with varying specializations, enhancing the overall capabilities of the merged
180   system. Dynamic expert merging methods, including DELLA-Merging (Tej Deep et al., 2024),
181   integrate specialized expert models dynamically, allowing the merged model to adapt to a variety of
182   tasks during inference. Adaptive weighting approaches such as AdaMerging (Yang et al., 2023) and
183   MetaGPT (Zhou et al., 2024) leverage dynamic weighting schemes and meta-learning to fine-tune
184   the merging process, ensuring optimal integration of constituent models’ strengths. Furthermore,
185
      task-oriented merging strategies like Task Arithmetic (Ilharco et al., 2022), Language and Task
      Arithmetic (Chronopoulou et al., 2023), and Task Arithmetic in Tangent Space (Ortiz-Jimenez et al.,
186
      2024) focus on blending models trained on different tasks, thereby creating versatile LLMs adept at
187
      multiple applications.

      4     S TRUCTURED -I NITIALIZATION L EARNING
279
280   In this section, we introduce S AIL, a novel approach that accelerates the training of large language
281   models by directly leveraging the parameters of pre-trained models. Traditional methods, such as
282   knowledge distillation, focus on aligning model outputs or hidden states, often neglecting the rich
283   information embedded in the model parameters themselves. We posit that the parameters of a model
284   encapsulate compressed knowledge acquired during training, and different models may provide
285   diverse perspectives even when trained on similar data. By directly utilizing these parameters, we
286   aim to create an effective starting point for training new models, leading to faster convergence and
287   improved performance.
288   Our methodology comprises two main components: (1) Parameter Transformation, where we adjust
289   the dimensions of pre-trained model parameters to match the target model architecture, and (2)
290   Proximal Parameter Integration and Retraining, where we integrate the transformed parameters to
291   initialize the new model and continue training on new data.
292

401   To evaluate the robustness and effectiveness of our S AIL method across different data distributions
402   and scenarios, we conducted a series of experiments focusing on data distribution effects, overlap
403   impacts, and cross-dataset generalization.
404
405   Dataset Partitioning and Distributional Analysis We partitioned our dataset into three distinct
406   subsets (D1 , D2 , and Dt ) based on feature segmentation using mean token values. This partitioning
407   strategy, inspired by the theoretical foundations of our method, allows us to simulate diverse data
408
      distributions commonly encountered in real-world scenarios. We computed the mean token value for
      blocks of data and used the 33rd and 66th percentiles as thresholds, a choice motivated by our aim
409
      to create balanced yet distinct subsets. Samples with mean token values below the lower threshold
410
      were assigned to D1 , those between the thresholds to D2 , and those above the upper threshold to
411   Dt , resulting in three datasets with distinct distributions that serve as an ideal testbed for our S AIL
412   method.
413
414

      separated clusters, empirically confirming the effectiveness of our theoretically-motivated feature-
415
      based splitting approach.

419   using our S AIL method and evaluate the convergence speed of the merged model on the new data
420
      partition Dt , thereby testing the practical implications of our theoretical framework. As shown

421
      minimum loss of 4.9782 achieved by our S AIL method. This substantial gap not only demonstrates the
422
      effectiveness of our approach in providing a more favorable initialization point in the loss landscape

508      To demonstrate the versatility and effectiveness of our proposed S AIL method beyond natural
509      language processing, we extend our experiments to the computer vision domain. Specifically, we
510      apply S AIL to convolutional neural network architectures, focusing on ResNet variants trained on
511      image classification tasks. This section details the experimental setup, and results of applying S AIL
512      to different ResNet models under both self-supervised and supervised learning paradigms.
513      We conduct experiments using three configurations of ResNet architectures: ResNet-18 (He et al.,
514      2016), and ResNet-34. All models are trained on three datasets: CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009),
515      CIFAR-100 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009), and Tiny-ImageNet (Le & Yang, 2015).
516
         Figure 3a illustrates the training performance of S AIL compared to baseline methods for ResNet-
517     18 Modified across three datasets. Under both BYOL (self-supervised) and SupCE (supervised)
518     paradigms, models initialized with S AIL demonstrate faster convergence and higher final accu-
519     racy compared to standard initialization and baseline transformation methods. This indicates that
520     S AIL effectively leverages pre-trained parameters to provide a beneficial starting point for training,
521     consistently across different datasets of varying complexity.
522
         To assess the adaptability of our method to architectural variations, we applied S AIL to ResNet-18
523      Modified ( Figure 3a ), Standard ResNet-18 ( Figure 3b ) and ResNet-34 Modified ( Figure 3c ) on the
524      CIFAR-10 dataset. In all cases, S AIL-initialized models outperform baselines in terms of convergence
525      speed and final performance. The improvements are particularly pronounced under the SupCE
526      paradigm, suggesting that supervised fine-tuning benefits significantly from our informed parameter
527      initialization approach.

532       5                 C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
533
534      In this paper, we have introduced S AIL, a novel approach to accelerate the training of deep neural
535
         networks by leveraging the information from pre-trained counterparts. Our method comprises two
         primary components: (1) a parameter transformation technique that aligns the dimensions of pre-
536
         trained model parameters with the target architecture, and (2) a proximal parameter integration and
537
         retraining strategy that efficiently merges these transformed parameters to initialize new models.
538      Our approach significantly reduces training time and computational resources while maintaining or
539      enhancing model performance on downstream tasks.

       G    A DDITIONAL R ELATED W ORK
1810
1811   Knowledge distillation (KD) (Hinton et al., 2015) is a widely used technique for transferring knowl-
1812   edge from a larger teacher model to a smaller student model by training the student to mimic the
1813   teacher’s output logits or representations. The primary focus of KD is on transferring knowledge
1814
       through the output space, aiming for model compression and efficiency without significant loss in
       performance.
1815
1816   Various extensions of KD have been proposed to improve efficiency and performance. Self-
1817   distillation (Zhang et al., 2019) involves training a model using its own outputs as soft targets,
1818   while mutual learning (Zhang et al., 2018) involves co-training multiple models to learn from each
1819   other. In the context of large-scale models, KD has been applied to compress transformer-based
1820
       architectures (Sanh et al., 2019; Jiao et al., 2019) and to improve model generalization (Yuan et al.,
       2020).
1821
1822   Recent advances in KD have explored more sophisticated approaches. Cross-modal knowledge
1823   distillation (Gou et al., 2021) enables knowledge transfer between models operating on different
1824   modalities. Contrastive knowledge distillation (Tian et al., 2020) leverages contrastive learning to
1825   capture fine-grained structural knowledge. Additionally, adaptive knowledge distillation (Song et al.,
1826
       2022) dynamically adjusts the distillation process based on the learning status of the student model.
1827   While KD focuses on output-space knowledge transfer, our proposed method, S AIL, operates at the
1828   parameter level. S AIL directly transforms and integrates parameters from multiple pre-trained models
1829   to initialize a new model, leveraging the collective knowledge embedded in their parameters. This
1830   approach differs from KD in that it does not require training a student model to mimic a teacher’s
1831   outputs; instead, it constructs a proximal parameter initialization that accelerates convergence during
1832
       training.
1833   Moreover, S AIL can be considered complementary to knowledge distillation. After applying S AIL to
1834   initialize the target model, KD can be employed as a subsequent optimization step to fine-tune or
1835   align the model to specific tasks. This combination could enhance both training efficiency and model
       performance by leveraging both parameter-space and output-space knowledge transfer.

2298
2299          • θ1 : Trained on D1 .

2301
2302   Using the SAIL method, we transform and merge the parameters of θ1 and θ2 to form the proximal
2303   parameter θP , which serves as the initialization for training the target model on the full dataset D⋆ .
2304   We compare the performance of models initialized with θP against models with random initialization
2305   and models trained on D⋆ from scratch. All models are trained using the same neural network
2306   architecture: a multi-layer perceptron (MLP) with one hidden layer of 50 neurons and ReLU
2307   activation functions. The learning rate is set to 5 × 10−3 , and models are trained using the Adam
2308   optimizer for 300 epochs.
2309
       We evaluate the models based on three metrics: training loss, accuracy, and gradient norm. The
2310   gradient norm provides insight into the stability and efficiency of the optimization process.
