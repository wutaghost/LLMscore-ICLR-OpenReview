009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The ability to generate novel enzymes that catalyze specific target molecules is
012            a critical advancement in biomaterial synthesis and chemical production. How-
013            ever, a significant challenge arises when no recorded enzymes exist for the target
014            molecule, making it a zero-shot generation problem. This absence of known en-
015            zymes complicates the training of generative models tailored to the target substrate.
016
               To address this, we propose a retrieval-augmented generation method that leverages
               existing enzyme-substrate data to overcome the lack of direct examples. Since
017
               there is no recorded catalytic performance between the enzymes and the new target
018
               molecule, the challenge shifts to identifying enzymes that helpful for generation.
019            Our approach tackles this by retrieving enzymes whose substrates exhibit struc-
020            tural similarities to the target molecule, thereby exploiting functional similarities
021            reflected in the enzymes’ catalytic capability. This leads to the next challenge: how
022            to utilize the retrieved enzymes to generate a novel enzyme capable of catalyzing
023            the target molecule, given that none of the retrieved enzymes directly catalyze
024            it. To solve this, we employ a conditioned discrete diffusion model that takes the
025            aligned retrieved enzymes to generate a new enzyme. We train the generator with
026            guidance from an enzyme-substrate relationship classifier to make it output the
027
               optimal protein sequence distribution for different target molecules. We evaluate
               our model on enzyme design tasks involving a diverse set of real-world substrates,
028
               and our results including catalytic rate predictions, foldability assessments, and
029
               docking position analyses, demonstrate that our model outperforms existing pro-
030            tein generation methods for substrate-specified enzyme generation. Additionally,
031            we formally define the zero-shot substrate-specified enzyme generation task and
032            contribute a comprehensive dataset with evaluation methods.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Substrate-specified enzyme generation aims to design new proteins that catalyze reactions to spe-
038   cific new molecules and benefits a wide array of scientific fields, including biomaterials synthesis
039
      and chemical production innovation (Meghwanshi et al., 2020; Robinson, 2015; Jegannathan &
      Nielsen, 2013; Paraschiv et al., 2022; Nam et al., 2024). Taking the artificial compound of 1,2,3-
040
      trichloropropane (TCP) as an example, it is extensively utilized as a chemical intermediate and solvent
041
      despite its toxicity and resistance to biodegradation (ATSDR, 2021; Cheremisinoff & Rosenfeld,
042   2011), which leads to persistent groundwater contaminant. Researchers are actively engaged in
043   discovering or engineering enzymes capable of biodegrading TCP (Bogale et al., 2020; Samin &
044   Janssen, 2012). Since there is no existing natural enzymes for TCP, the synthesis paradigm only relies
045   on the expertise of replicating molecular structure of other natural heme-proteins (Zambrano et al.,
046   2022) and lacks the efficiency to discover novel and effective enzymes for the specific substrate.
047
      The recent emergence of deep learning based protein generation shows great potential for enzyme
048   design due to their unprecedented accuracy in structure and function prediction. A portion of these
049   methods falls under the category of unconditional generation, such as ProGen2 (Nijkamp et al., 2023)
050   and ProtGPT2 (Ferruz et al., 2022), possessing the capability to generate protein sequences that fold
051   into stable and functional structures and resemble real proteins, without relying on the predefined
052   substrate. The other subset of these methods is characterized by conditional generation, consisting
053   of ligand-conditioned sequence design and structure generation. The ligand-conditioned sequence
      design models (Gruver et al., 2023; Martinkus et al., 2023) are proposed to synthesize therapeutic


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      antibodies treating well to the antigen ligands. On the other hand, the ligand-conditioned structure
055   generation methods, like LigandMPNN (Dauparas et al., 2023) and RFdiffusionAA (Krishna et al.),
056   generate proteins structurally docking to a given target. By ensuring spatial compatibility, these
057   methods generate effective proteins associated with enhanced biological function and stability in
058   complex cellular environments.
059
      While the unconditional approaches fail to match requirements, existing work of conditional gen-
060
      eration cannot be repurposed directly to generate desired enzymes that catalyze specific substrates
061   represented as small molecules. Particularly, the ligand condition of these models is amino acid
062   sequences of antigens but our substrates are small molecules. The enzyme substrates exhibit a vast
063   chemical space with high structural diversity, including variations in functional groups, stereochem-
064   istry, and electronic properties, which make it challenging to learn the interactions with enzymes.
065   In addition, the catalytic capability of an enzyme is not solely determined by how it structurally
066   interacts with the substrate molecule, so these models are not yet capable of synthesizing functional
067   enzymes. The label-conditioned generative method, i.e. ZymCTRL (Munsamy et al., 2022), takes an
068   Enzyme Commission (EC) number and outputs a corresponding enzyme sequence. It requires prior
069
      knowledge about the expected enzyme’s EC, which relays human expertise heavily.
070   In this study, we formally define the task of zero-shot substrate-specified enzyme generation and
071   identify two primary challenges associated with it. The first challenge is the complete absence of
072   positive samples. For instance, without any effective enzymes for TCP as training data, it is difficult to
073   train or fine-tune a model to generate enzymes that catalyze TCP. A potential solution to this challenge
074   is the Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG). Specifically, RAG-based methods sample protein
075
      sequences as prompts and subsequently instruct models to generate sequences that are structurally
      and/or functionally similar (Ma et al., 2023; Alamdari et al., 2023; Lewis et al., 2020). However,
076
      the problem of retrieving proteins without relying on an exemplar enzyme needs to be addressed, as
077
      the only input is the target substrate. The second challenge is the generation of proteins that diverge
078   from training data. The generated TCP enzyme must differ from recorded enzymes, as none in the
079   record can effectively catalyze TCP molecules. This divergence requirement extends to enzymes
080   for other new substrates. Since the mainstream training methods focus on recovering recorded data,
081   a new approach is required—one that trains models to generate enzymes that are both divergent
082   from existing records and capable of catalyzing different target molecules. Furthermore, there is
083   currently no comprehensive evaluation framework for zero-shot enzyme generations. While Johnson
084   et al. (2024) and Song et al. (2024) introduced certain metrics for computationally designed enzymes,
085   there is a lack of refined datasets for zero-shot settings and multiple-perspective evaluations, as the
086
      substrate-specified enzyme generation task has not yet been fully formulated.
087   To address these two challenges, we propose Substrate-specified enzyme generator (SENZ). Our
088   main contributions are as follows:
089
             • We formally define the task of substrate-specified enzyme generation and present a curated
090
               dataset. This dataset consists of the substrate-enzyme pairs that are extracted from the
091
               known enzymes. We further partition it into training and test subsets without overlap in
092            terms of proteins and small molecules to secure the zero-shot setting.
093
             • We propose a substrate-indexed retrieval method to search the functionally-similar enzymes
094
               as prompting signals. The key merit is the enzymes associated with the structurally close
095            substrates exhibit similar catalyzing properties. Considering a query substrate, we compare
096            the structural closeness with other stored molecules and retrieve the pairwise enzyme data
097            of top-ranking molecules. This approach is distinct from traditional protein retrieval since
098            it retrieves based on substrate similarity instead of protein similarity, as traditional protein
099            retrieval does.
100          • We employ a discrete diffusion model to generate new enzymes based on the retrieved
101            ones and utilize a substrate-enzyme catalyzing classifier as guidance for the generative
102            process. The classifier transforms the complicated catalytic relationship into a continuous
103            and differentiable function for optimizing the generator. With different substrates, it guides
104            the generation toward different directions distinct from the whole record data distribution.
105          • Experimental results in designing enzymes for particular substrates demonstrate that our
106            model can generate novel enzymes of superior quality. Compared with rule-, unconditioned-,
107            sequence-, and structure-based methods, our framework generates proteins showing high
               enzymatic capability and high foldability.

110   We define the substrate-specified enzyme generation task by specifying the model’s input and output,
111   along with the training and testing data and evaluation methods.

      Problem definition. The task involves generating a protein that serves as the enzyme for the target
      molecule. Let m denote the Simplified Molecular-Input Line-Entry System (SMILES) (Weininger,
114
      1988) string representation of the molecule and let x denote the protein sequence. We have x =
115
      (a1 , a2 , a3 , ..., al ) ∈ Al where ai is an amino acid and A is the vocabulary of amino acids together
116   with related tokens including gap ("-"). Henceforth, the amino acid a can be represented as a one-hot
117   vector, and we do not differentiate between the protein sequence and the sequence of one-hot vectors,
118   which means x ∈ Al is a matrix with shape l × |A|. Let P denote the domain of all protein sequences
119   and let M denote the domain of all molecular SMILES strings. The function G : M → P means the
120   task of substrate-specified enzyme generation, which can be defined as x = G(m; θ) where θ is the
121   set of G’s parameters. If G is a machine-learning model, the training process is given by:
122                                     θ ∗ = arg min L(G(mD ; θ), xD , mD ).                              (1)
                                             θ

      and L is the loss function. The input can include various types of data: Enzyme Commission (EC)
125

      Cm or an existing enzyme Cx . The generative function can be extended as below:

130   Data construction. For this task, we construct a dataset of substrate-enzyme pairwise relationships
131   extracted from public raw data, as illustrated in Fig. 1(a). Each record in raw data comprises the
132   SMILES representations of a chemical reaction with a specific enzyme. To identify the specific
133   substrate in each chemical reaction, we select the least common reactant among all reactants in the
134   database, treating it as the specific substrate for the enzymes involved in that reaction. This approach
135
      is grounded in the established observation of substrate specificity (Jackson et al., 2010). Consequently,
      we define the "substrate-enzyme" relation (m, x) as protein x being the enzyme of molecule m, and
136
      the training dataset D can be defined as follows:
137
                                    D = {(m, x)}, x is the enzyme of m.                                     (3)
138
139   The substrate-enzyme pair (m, x) is the element of the dataset as in Eq. (3).
140   Zero-shot data split. All substrate-enzyme pairs (m, x) are split into D for training, Dvalid for
141   validation and Dtest for testing. To avoid of data leakage, two rules are designed for any two (m1 , x1 )
142   and (m2 , x2 ) in different subsets: 1. Molecules from different subsets should not be the same, i.e.
143   m1 ̸= m2 ; 2. Any two protein sequences from different subsets, i.e., x1 and x2 , should not have
144   an overlap of more than 30% (with an identity exceeding 30%). The split forms a zero-shot setting.
145   Take the target molecule TCP as an example. TCP is in Dtest and the model G is generating enzyme
146
      for TCP. G has never trained with TCP because TCP is not in D. G has never seen proteins similar
      to TCP’s ground truth enzymes because all of them are only in Dtest , and all proteins in D have at
147
      least 70% different from them. Therefore generating enzyme for TCP and any molecules in Dtest is
148
      zero-shot.
149
150   Evaluation. Regardless of the input data, models should be evaluated using consistent metrics. An
151   evaluation model feval scores the generated protein x as follows:
152                                            y = feval (x, m),                                        (4)
153   where m is optional. If the evaluation focuses solely on the generated protein, m is not required.
154   Given different functions of feval , the ideal training process should be framed as a multi-objective
155   optimization problem. However, in the substrate-specified enzyme generation task, we prioritize
156
      catalytic capability above all and thus focus primarily on the corresponding feval .

192   Since there are no existing enzymes for a target substrate in the zero-shot generation setting, it is
193

432
      foldability. Fig. 2(a) of kcat shows that the increase in retrieved sequences improves the performance
433   in terms of catalyzing. In Fig. 2(b) of pLDDT, the foldability decreases with the increase of retrieved
434   enzymes. The reason is that structure prediction examines the full sequence pattern with existing
435   proteins. The retrieved proteins do not resemble each other in full sequence, making the derived
436   generated sequence less similar to existing proteins. In fact, several short periods (enzymatic
437   active site) in the retrieved sequences dominate the proteins’ catalytic capability, which is different
438   from foldability’s requirement on the full sequence. Therefore, there’s a trade-off between the
439   enzyme’s folding stability and catalytic capability. In fact, the trade-off has been reported in other
440   literature (Vanella et al., 2024), which is the same case in our generated sequences. With more
441
      retrieved sequences, our model gives up sequence foldability for better catalytic performance.

520   5    C ONCLUSION
521
522   In this paper, we have formally defined the task of zero-shot substrate-specified enzyme generation,
523   wherein models are provided solely with a new target molecule and are required to output a protein
524   sequence possessing catalytic capabilities specific to that molecule. To address this task, we introduce
525   the Substrate-specified enzyme generator (SENZ), an RAG method. SENZ utilizes a single molecule
526   as a query to retrieve enzymes based on their substrate similarity to the target, thereby enabling the
527   retrieval of known proteins from new molecules. This retrieval strategy capitalizes on the functional
528   similarity of enzymes as indicated by their substrates. To generate enzymes from the retrieved
529
      sequences, we employ multiple sequence alignment (MSA) on them and introduce a diffusion model
      generator guided by an enzyme-substrate classifier. This classifier guides the generated protein
530
      distribution for different substrates, serving as the objective for the generator during training. In
531
      experiments involving the generation of enzymes for real-world target molecules, evaluation functions
532   assessed turnover rate and foldability together with other properties, demonstrating the superiority of
533   our model in enzyme generation.

732   A.1.1    M ORE D ETAILS ABOUT BASELINES
733
734   ProGen2 (Nijkamp et al., 2023) and ProtGPT2 (Ferruz et al., 2022): We utilized the pre-trained
735   weights for both models to generate sequences with a maximum length of 1024. These models serve
736   as benchmarks for the capability of protein language models to generate sequences without specific
737   functional guidance.
738   ZymCTRL (Munsamy et al., 2022): This model employs pre-trained weights and uses the Enzyme
739   Commission (EC) number as a prompt for the autoregressive generation process. It is worth noting
740   that the EC number provides more detailed information about enzymatic function compared to the
741   substrate alone, offering this baseline an advantage in generating enzyme sequences for the given
742   tasks.
743
      NOS (Gruver et al., 2023): We trained NOS following the methodology of its original paper. The
744   original NOS framework uses a discriminator to score the binding affinity between an antibody and
745   an antigen (two protein sequences). We replaced the original discriminator with our enzyme-substrate
746   probability scoring model in our adaptation. Furthermore, we replaced the target protein sequence
747   input with the target substrate molecule. During inference, the NOS generator is updated iteratively
748   for 10 steps using the test set input before sampling, following a discrete diffusion model for sequence
749   generation, as described in the original paper. These adjustments allow NOS to generate enzymes in
750   our setting while preserving its original generative framework.
751   LigandMPNN (Dauparas et al., 2023): This reverse folding model generates a protein sequence
752   based on a protein-ligand complex structure. To adapt it for our task, we randomly generated protein
753   sequences (length: 1024) and predicted their structures using ESMFold [1]. Using RDKit, we
754   generated the structure of the target substrate, and NeuralPLexer [2] was employed to dock the
755   substrate with the predicted protein structure, creating a complex structure. The resulting complex
      was then input into LigandMPNN for sequence redesign.

774   A.2     R ELATED W ORK
775
776   Unconditional protein generation. Some research focuses on generating proteins that resemble
777
      natural ones. Within this scope, the protein language model-based sequence-only approaches include
      ProGen2 (Nijkamp et al., 2023), ProtGPT2 (Ferruz et al., 2022), and ESM-2 (Lin et al., 2023). These
778
      models are trained to predict masked amino acids in natural protein sequences, thus learning to
779
      generate proteins that mimic natural ones. The discrete diffusion models approach, aimed at this
780   target, includes EvoDiff (Alamdari et al., 2023), which performs corruption and reconstruction on
781   multiple sequence alignments (MSA). Generative adversarial networks (GAN) approaches, such as
782   ProteinGAN (Repecka et al., 2021), use a discriminator to guide the generated protein to resemble
783   natural ones, enabling the generation of natural-like enzymes when a template is provided. Structure-
784   based methods include ProteinMPNN (Dauparas et al., 2022), which seeks to generate a protein
785   sequence likely to fold into a given structure. These methods do not target external generation
786   objectives or rely heavily on human-selected input templates to achieve specific functions.
787   Conditioned protein generation. Some researchers use non-protein data to guide protein generation.
788   ZymCTRL (Munsamy et al., 2022) uses an Enzyme Commission (EC) number as a prompt to generate
789   enzymes categorized in the corresponding EC. Progen (Madani et al., 2023) takes natural language
790   protein labels to output corresponding protein sequence. LigandMPNN (Dauparas et al., 2023) and
791   RFdiffusionAA (Krishna et al.) can recover a protein sequence and structure based on a binding
792   molecule, which is derived from their prediction ability on the ligand-protein complex.
793   Protein guided protein generation. Some research aims to generate new proteins that bind to a
794   given protein. The sequence approach includes NOS (Gruver et al., 2023), which merges antibody
795   and antigen in one sequence and uses the diffusion method to train a transformer, while some
796   property prediction models can be used in sampling to make the generated protein tend to have certain
797   properties. The structure approach includes AbDiffuser (Martinkus et al., 2023), which uses a SE(3)
798   equivariant neural network to model residue-to-residue relations. The generation target and output
799   protein are both in the same protein modality.
800   Enzyme evaluation. Enzyme evaluation models can help with enzyme design. ProSmith (Kroll
801   et al., 2023) predicts protein-small molecule interactions. UniKP (Yu et al., 2023) predicts the
802   kcat and Km value of enzyme and substrate. NeuralPLexer (Qiao et al., 2024) and AlphaFold
803   3 (Abramson et al., 2024) can predict the protein-ligand complex structures. Johnson et al. (2024)
804   proposes comprehensive methods for evaluating neural network-generated enzymes but does not
805   include metrics related to catalytic activity.
806   Retrieval method. Some research develops retrieval methods to help with generation or predic-
807   tion. RetMol (Wang et al., 2023) retrieves molecules based on similarity and desired properties
808   to refine molecules. MSA transformer (Rao et al., 2021) and AlphaFold 2 (Jumper et al., 2021)
809   uses evolutionary-based MSA to enhance structure prediction accuracy. They retrieve proteins with
      proteins by sequence similarity only.
