011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Calibration is crucial in deep learning applications, especially in fields like health-
014            care and autonomous driving, where accurate confidence estimates are vital for
015            decision-making. However, deep neural networks often suffer from miscalibra-
016
               tion, with reliability diagrams and Expected Calibration Error (ECE) being the
               only standard perspective for evaluating calibration performance. In this paper,
017
               we introduce the concept of consistency as an alternative perspective on model
018
               calibration, inspired by uncertainty estimation literature in large language models
019            (LLMs). We highlight its advantages over the traditional reliability-based view.
020            Building on this concept, we propose a post-hoc calibration method called Con-
021            sistency Calibration (CC), which adjusts confidence based on the model’s consis-
022            tency across perturbed inputs. CC is particularly effective in locally uncertainty
023            estimation, as it requires no additional data samples or label information, instead
024            generating input perturbations directly from the source data. Moreover, we show
025            that performing perturbations at the logit level significantly improves computa-
026            tional efficiency. We validate the effectiveness of CC through extensive compar-
027
               isons with various post-hoc and training-time calibration methods, demonstrating
               state-of-the-art performance on standard datasets such as CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100,
028
               and ImageNet, as well as on long-tailed datasets like ImageNet-LT. Code is avail-
029
               able at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/Consistency-Calibration-E248.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Calibration is essential in many deep learning applications where accurate confidence estimates
035
      are as important as the predictions themselves. In fields like healthcare Chen et al. (2018) and
036   autonomous driving Feng et al. (2019), decisions often rely not only on the model’s output but also
037   on how confident the model is in its predictions. A well-calibrated model should reflect the ground
038   truth uncertainty. In healthcare, for instance, a model that accurately reflects uncertainty can help
039   doctors trust the system’s confidence when diagnosing critical conditions.
040
      However, current deep learning models are often found to be miscalibrated (Guo et al., 2017). To
041
      evaluate calibration performance, Naeini et al. (2015) introduced ECE, which has become the gold
042   standard, based on the reliability diagram (DeGroot & Fienberg, 1983). Although several improved
043   metrics have since been proposed, such as AdaptiveECE (AdaECE) (Nixon et al., 2019) and Class-
044   wiseECE (CECE) (Kull et al., 2019), they all adopt the same fundamental perspective on calibration:
045   if a model assigns 80% confidence to its predictions, then, ideally, 80% of those predictions should
046   be correct. We refer to this classical approach as the reliability view, which seeks to align predicted
047   confidence levels with actual model accuracy.
048   The concept of consistency has gained increasing importance in black-box uncertainty estimation,
049   particularly in recent developments in large language models (LLMs) (Wang et al., 2022; Tam et al.,
050   2022; Xiong et al., 2023b; Geng et al., 2023). If an LLM is confident in its answer, it should provide
051   consistent responses to similar questions. For instance, if an LLM confidently answers the question
052   “What is the answer to 5 + 3?” with “8”, it should also consistently provide “8” for the similar
053   question “What is the result of five plus three?” In this paper, we extend this concept of consistency
      to model calibration, proposing a new perspective of calibration called consistency.

054
      Specifically, in a classification task, if a model is confident in its prediction, it should consistently
055   provide the same output across multiple perturbed versions of the input. Consistency measures how
056   often a model’s prediction remains unchanged when the input is perturbed within a small neigh-
057   borhood. A high consistency score implies that the model’s predictions are stable and confident.
058   In this view, a perfectly calibrated model should have its predicted confidence levels align with the
059   consistency observed across these perturbed inputs.
060
      In the following sections, we discuss the differences between calibration from the perspectives of
061   reliability and consistency in Sections 2.1 and 2.2. Section 2.3 highlights the advantages of the con-
062   sistency approach over the reliability view through a toy example. In Section 2.4, CC is introduced,
063   which involves perturbing the logits. We provide empirical evidence to explain its effectiveness in
064   Section 2.6. Finally, in Section 2.5, we demonstrate that consistency can serve as a reliable method
065   for local uncertainty estimation.
066
      Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
067
068          • We introduce a novel perspective on calibration based on consistency and highlight its
069            advantages over traditional reliability view represented by ECE.
070
             • We propose an easy-to-implement and computationally efficient post-hoc calibration
071
               method called Consistency Calibration, which replaces the original confidence score with
072
               a consistency measure calculated from perturbed logits using data neighbors.
073
074          • CC serves as a reliable and effective method for local uncertainty estimation, as it does not
075            require additional data samples or label information. Instead, it generates data neighbor-
076
               hoods based on the source data.
077          • We conduct comparisons with multiple post-hoc and training-time calibration methods,
078            demonstrating state-of-the-art performance on standard datasets, including CIFAR-10,
079            CIFAR-100, and ImageNet, as well as in long-tailed scenarios like ImageNet-LT.

125   2.2   C ALIBRATION IN THE VIEW OF C ONSISTENCY
126
127   We offer an alternative perspective on calibration by examining it through the concept of consistency.
128   In a real-world scenario, an individual confident in their answer tends to maintain that answer, even
129   when faced with external doubts or minor alterations to the question. On the other hand, someone
130   who is uncertain might change their response when presented with slightly misleading information
131
      or variations in the question. We define this adherence to the original answer as consistency.
132   Recent advances in LLMs, particularly black-box models utilize factual consistency to enhance
133   performance (Wang et al., 2022; Tam et al., 2022; Xiong et al., 2023b; Geng et al., 2023). These
134   studies frame the consistency of a model’s responses as an indicator of its uncertainty. In the context
135   of classification tasks, calibration can also be described in terms of consistency. Specifically, for
136   classification models, we can formalize this relationship as follows:
137   Proposition 1. If a model is confident in its prediction, it should consistently output the same pre-
138   diction when the input is slightly perturbed. The consistency c of a sample x is defined as

      2.6   W HY CONSISTENCY CALIBRATION WORKS ?
333
      Perturbing images results in straightforward and intuitive image neighborhoods, but the effectiveness
334   of perturbations at the logit level requires further explanation. To understand why logit perturbations
335   work, we examined the differences between highly confident correct predictions and overconfident
336   incorrect ones. These represent well-calibrated and poorly calibrated samples, respectively. During
337   logit disturbance, the label with second-largest logit most likely to become the prediction label. To
338   investigate this, we plotted box plots for both the maximum and second-largest logits for correct and
339   incorrect predictions, as shown in Figure 4a.
340   For CIFAR-10 test samples, we selected predictions with confidence higher than 99%. We refer to
341   the maximum logit of correct predictions as “Corr. Max” and that of incorrect predictions as “Incorr.
342   Max.” Similarly, “Corr. 2nd” represents the second-largest logit of correct predictions, while “Incorr.
343   2nd” refers to the second-largest logit of incorrect predictions. As shown in Figure 4a, the maximum
344   logit for correct predictions is significantly higher than for incorrect predictions. Additionally, the
345   second-largest logit in correct predictions is much lower than that in incorrect predictions. This
346   indicates that the gap between the maximum and second-largest logits is much larger for correct
347   predictions than for incorrect ones. Despite large difference, due to softmax saturation, the model
348
      assigns abnormally high confidence (greater than 99%) to both correct and incorrect predictions,
      leading to overconfident miscalibration.
349
350   Interestingly, we can leverage this difference in the logit gaps between correct and incorrect predic-
351   tions. Perturbations can easily alter the predictions of overconfident, miscalibrated samples, while
352   having minimal effect on well-calibrated, correct predictions. This different response to perturba-
353   tions explains why consistency calibration is effective at the logit level. We observed similar patterns
354
      in experiments with CIFAR-100 and ImageNet, as shown in Figure 4b and Figure 4c.

      Models We evaluate our approach across various neural network architectures, including ResNet-

422   using various CNNs and transformers.
423
424
      Calibration on Standard Datasets CC consistently outperforms these methods across CIFAR-
425
      10, CIFAR-100, and ImageNet-1K, significantly reducing calibration error. The most notable im-
426   provement is seen in CIFAR-100, where CC excels while GC, despite its strong performance on
427   other datasets, struggles. This highlights CC’s robustness across datasets with varying complexities.
428   CNNs, which often suffer from overconfidence, are generally well-calibrated with TS-based meth-
429   ods. However, transformers see limited calibration improvements from TS-based methods, with CC
430   outperforming them by a large margin. On larger datasets like ImageNet-1K, CC maintains its ad-
431   vantage. Although GC slightly outperforms CC on ViT-B/32, it is computationally expensive due to
      the additional grouping process, whereas CC balances both efficiency and effectiveness.

      Calibration on Long-Tail Datasets On long-tail datasets like ImageNet-LT, TS-based models
433   struggle to provide effective calibration, especially for transformers. For example, on ViT-B/32,
434   TS-based methods fail to calibrate effectively, as they apply uniform adjustments across the dataset,
435   smoothing or sharpening probabilities globally. In contrast, CC and GC perform well on long-
436   tail datasets, particularly with transformers. GC excels due to its multicalibration (Hébert-Johnson
437   et al., 2018), offering sample-wise adjustments, though it comes at a high computational cost. By
438   leveraging local uncertainty estimation through input perturbations, CC better captures uncertainties

533
534   Consistency offers an alternative perspective on calibration by focusing on prediction stability under
535   perturbations as an indicator of confidence. CC has proven highly effective in reducing calibration
536   errors across various datasets. However, CC has limitations, such as the need for tuning perturbation
537   strength and noise type, and its current focus on classification tasks, with its application to regres-
538   sion remaining unexplored. Future work can aim to develop a new, more universal consistency-
539   based metric to complement existing metrics like ECE. This would provide a more comprehensive
      evaluation to calibration, ultimately leading to more reliable deep learning models.
