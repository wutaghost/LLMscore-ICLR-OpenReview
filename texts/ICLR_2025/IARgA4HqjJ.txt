009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            We extend score-based generative modeling by incorporating Lie group actions
012            on the data manifold into the denoising diffusion process. Our approach yields
013            a Langevin dynamics whose infinitesimal transformations decompose as a di-
014            rect sum of Lie algebra representations, enabling generative processes that align
015            with the underlying symmetry properties of the data. Unlike equivariant models,
016
               which restrict the space of learnable functions by quotienting out group orbits, our
               method incorporates both global and local symmetries and can model any target
017
               distribution. Standard score-matching, which minimizes the Fisher divergence,
018
               emerges as a special case of our framework when the Lie group is the translation
019            group in Euclidean space. We prove that our generalized generative processes
020            arise as solutions to a new class of reverse-time stochastic differential equations
021            (SDEs), introduced here for the first time. We validate our approach through
022            experiments on diverse data types, demonstrating its effectiveness in real-world
023            applications such as SO(3)-guided molecular conformer generation and modeling
024            ligand-specific global SE(3) transformations for molecular docking. We show that
025            an appropriate choice of Lie group enhances learning efficiency by reducing the
026            effective dimensionality of the trajectory space and enables the modeling of tran-
027
               sitions between complex data distributions, lifting the requirement of a Gaussian
               prior. Additionally, we demonstrate the universality of our approach by deriving
028
               how it extends to flow matching techniques.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Deep probabilistic generative modeling amounts to creating data from a known tractable prior distri-
034   bution. Score-based models (Hyvärinen & Dayan, 2005; Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Ho et al., 2020;
035   Huang et al., 2021) achieve this by learning to reverse a corruption process of the data. The sampling
036   process is realized by a Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) using Langevin dynamics (Song et al.,
037   2021; 2020b), producing a probabilistic trajectory {xt }t=T,...,0 in the data space X guided by the
038   score function s(x) = ∇x log p(x): for each infinitesimal step xt+1 7→ xt − s(xt ) + noise, the score
039
      induces a small translation in X (Figure 1a), and the noise prevents the dynamics from collapsing
      into local minima (Welling & Teh, 2011; Neal, 2010). The observation that translations are just one
040
      of many transformations of Euclidean space leads to the central question of the present work: given
041
      a group G acting on X, can we construct a score-based dynamics whose steps correspond to the
042   infinitesimal transformation of G?
043
044   We will prove that the answer is positive when G is a Lie group, as these are suitable to describe
045
      local continuous transformations. Specifically, we construct a Langevin dynamics that decomposes
      as a direct sum of representations of the Lie algebra g of G, where each step is driven by tangent
046
      vectors associated with elements of g (Figure 1)c. This construction employs the formalism of Gen-
047
      eralized Score Matching (GSM) (Lyu, 2009; Lin et al., 2016), whose aim is to estimate a probability
048   density p(x) = exp(−U (x))/Z through its generalized score function L log p(x), where L is a
049   suitable linear operator. As Figure 1b suggest, a generalized score enables the dynamics to follow
050   curved paths, a crucial feature for our scope since group orbits typically have nonzero curvature.
051   In this work we will systematically develop the connection between the transformation-generating
052   Lie group G and the linear operator L. We will also show that such generalized dynamics admits a
053   stochastic differential equations (SDE) interpretation: the G-induced generative process satisfies a
      continuous-(reverse-) time SDE involving the generalized score function.

      At this point, the attentive reader might wonder whether all we are achieving is to merely introduce
072   multiple ways, according to the choice of G, to achieve the same goal of generating samples from
073   a target distribution. So, what’s the real benefit? This lies in the ability to tailor G to the specific
074   transformation properties of the data and the learning task: the score components can be designed to
075   align with the true degrees of freedom of the data, enhancing interpretability, sampling steerability,
076   and enabling dimensionality reduction by leveraging the data’s inherent symmetries.
077
      Figure 1d illustrates this in a simple but instructive case. Consider X = R2 and let the true score be
078   of the form s(x) = f (r, θ)b r. This is neither invariant nor equivariant since f depends on both r, θ,
079   thus it cannot be learned by an invariant/equivariant network. A standard score function neglects the
080   problem’s symmetry and is required to inefficiently (and likely inaccurately) learn the correct point-
081   dependent linear combination of both Cartesian components of the score. A SO(2) generalized score
082   function, however, directly learns the radial component f (r, θ), effectively reducing the problem to
083   a 1-dimensional formulation, as the angular component may be omitted from the leaning procedure.
084   In short, we propose an exact SDE-based diffusion framework that enables Lie group-guided curved
085   dynamics while operating entirely in Euclidean space, thus combining the advantages of curved
086   dynamics with the theoretical and practical effectiveness of Euclidean diffusion. Our framework in
087   unconstrained, unlike equivariant models, but still leverages the information of the group action on
088   the data space. Ours is the first methods we are aware of that realizes simulation-free training of Lie
089   group-like diffusion models, and the first result of denoising score-matching result for general
090   non-Abelian groups (unlike De Bortoli et al. (2022) and Huang et al. (2022)).
091   More specifically, we summarize the main contributions of this manuscript as follows:
092   Generalized score matching via Lie algebras: We develop the mathematical foundations for ex-
093   tending score-based generative modeling to incorporate Lie group-induced dynamics. We spell out
094   the conditions for a suitable G (valid for any differentiable manifold X) and demonstrate that the
095   dynamics decomposes as a linear combination of Lie algebra representations. We also show that
096   standard score-matching is recovered as a specific case of our framework, corresponding to the
097   group G = T (n) of translations on X = Rn .
098   Exact solution of a novel class of SDEs: We introduce a new class of solvable SDEs that govern
099   symmetry-aware processes in Euclidean space, significantly expanding the range of processes that
100   can be addressed using diffusion modeling techniques. Additionally, we show that our approach
101   extends naturally to flow matching (see Appendix D).
102   Dimensionality reduction, bridging non-trivial distributions and trajectory disentanglement:
103   Through extensive experiments* , we demonstrate that: (1) our approach can estimate, regardless of
104   the choice of G, any probability density (Sections 5(2,3,4d distributions) and 5)(QM9); (2) by appro-
105   priately selecting G to align with the data structure, the learning process is significantly simplified,
106   effectively reducing its dimensionality (Section 5(MNIST)) (3) our framework enables solutions to
107   processes that are challenging or unfeasible with standard score matching, such as bridging between
      complex data-driven distributions (Section 5(MNIST) and 5(CrossDocked));

122   We start this section by setting up notation and review the connection between vector fields and Lie
123   algebra actions on manifolds. A Lie group G is a group that is also a finite-dimensional differentiable
124   manifold, such that the group operations of multiplication · : G × G → G and inversion are C ∞ -
125   functions† . A Lie algebra g is a vector space equipped with an operation, the Lie bracket, [, ] :
126   g × g → g, satisfying the Jacobi identity. Every Lie group gives rise to a Lie algebra as its tangent
127   space at the identity, g = Te G, and the Lie bracket is the commutator of tangent vectors, [A, B] =
128
      AB − BA. In this work, we are interested in how Lie groups and Lie algebras act on spaces. Given
      a manifold X, a (left) group action of G on X is an associative map ρX : G × X → X such
129
      that ρX (e) = IdX . Fundamental concepts associated with a group action are the ones of orbits and
130
      stabilizers. The orbit of x ∈ X is the set of elements in X which can be reached from x through
131   the action of G, i.e., G · x = {ρX (g)(x), g ∈ G}. The stabilizer subgroup of G with respect to x
132   is the set of group elements that fix x, Gx = {g ∈ G|ρX (g)(x) = x}. The action of a Lie algebra
133   on X, A : g → Vect(X) is a Lie algebra homomorphism and maps elements of g to vector fields on
134   X such that the map g × X → T X, (A, x) 7→ A(A)(x) is smooth. Given A ∈ g and a group action
135   ρX , the flow on X induced by ρX is given by ξA : X × R → X, (x, τ ) → ρX (exp(τ A)) (x),
136   where the map exp : g → G is defined by exp(A) = γA (1), where γA : R → G is the unique
137   one-parameter subgroup of G whose tangent vector at the identity is A. The infinitesimal action of
138   g on X is defined as the differential of the map ρX , that is

      2.2   S UFFICIENT CONDITIONS FOR L IE GROUP - INDUCED GENERALIZED SCORE MATCHING
201
      We now address the properties our setup (X, G, g Π) must satisfy to meet the sufficient conditions
202   for score-matching and Langevin dynamics. We note that these result hold for any differentiable
203   manifold X. Proofs for these results can be found in Appendix B.
204
205   Condition 1: Completeness of Π. We start by establishing an algebraic-geometric condition for
206   Π’s completeness:
207
      Proposition 2.1. The linear operator induced by Π is complete if Π is the local frame of a vector
208   bundle E over X whose rank is n ≥ dim X almost everywhere. If rank E = n everywhere, then
209   E = T X, the tangent bundle of X.
210
211   The following result specifies which Lie groups yield operators Π satisfying the above proposition:
212   Proposition 2.2. The operator Π induced by g is complete if and only if the subspace U ⊆ X such
213   that dim GGx < n for x ∈ U , where n = dim X, has measure zero in X.

222   Condition 2: Homogeneity of X. While the completeness of the operators is necessary for esti-
223   mating the target density, it is not sufficient to ensure that the Langevin dynamics (5) will behave ap-
224   propriately, as the following example illustrates. Let X = R, and G = R∗+ , the multiplicative group
225   of non-zero positive real numbers. The orbits under the action ρX (a, x) = ax are O+ = (0, ∞),
226   O− = (−∞, 0), and O0 = {0}. If the dynamics begins within O+ , it will be never be able to reach
227   values in O− , as G-transformations cannot move the system outside its initial orbit. We therefore
228   ask that each pair of points of X is connected through the G action. This amounts to require that
229   X is homogeneous for G, that is, ∀x, y ∈ X there exists a g ∈ G such that ρX (g)x = y. We
230
      note that this condition solely ensures the generation outcome is independent of the initial sampling
      condition. Beyond this, the formalism remains fully applicable in the non-homogeneous case.
231
232

233
      frame of vector fields, [LA , LB ]f (x) = 0 ∀A, B and ∀f ∈ C ∞ (X). In this case, the coordinates
234
      τi ’s are orthogonal, and their flows commute, meaning the orbits parametrized by τi correspond

241   exclude non-Abelian groups; even if A1,2 ∈ g do not commute in the Lie algebra, their flows on X

      Representation theory applied to neural networks has been studied both theoretically (Esteves, 2020;
      Chughtai et al., 2023; Puny et al., 2021; Smidt, 2021) and applied to a variety of groups, architecture
401
      and data type: CNNs (Cohen & Welling, 2016; Romero et al., 2020; Liao & Liu, 2023; Finzi et al.,
402
      2020; Weiler & Cesa, 2019; Weiler et al., 2018), Graph Neural Networks (Satorras et al., 2021),
403   Transformers, (Geiger & Smidt, 2022; Romero & Cordonnier, 2020; Hutchinson et al., 2021), point
404   clouds (Thomas et al., 2018), chemistry (Schütt et al., 2021; Le et al., 2022a). On the topic of dis-
405   entanglement of group action and symmetry learning, Pfau et al. (2020) factorize a Lie group from
406   the orbits in data space, while Winter et al. (2022) learn through an autoencoder architecture in-
407   variant and equivariant representations of any group acting on the data. Fumero et al. (2021) learns
408   disentangled representations solely from data pairs. Dehmamy et al. (2021) propose an architec-
409   ture based on Lie algebras that can automatically discover symmetries from data. Xu et al. (2022)
410   predict molecular conformations from molecular graphs in an roto-translation invariant fashion with
411
      equivariant Markov kernels.
412   Partially related to our study is the field of diffusion on Riemannian manifolds. De Bortoli et al.
413   (2022) propose diffusion in a product space – a condition which is not a necessary in our framework –
414   defined by the flow coordinates in the respective Riemannian sub-manifolds. When the Riemannian
415   manifold is a Lie group, their method yields dynamics similar to ours, as illustrated in an example
416   in Section 3.1. In fact, our formalism could be integrated with their approach to create a unified
417
      framework for diffusion processes on the broader class of Riemannian manifolds admitting a Lie
      group action. These techniques has been applied in a variety of use cases (Corso et al., 2023; Ketata
418
      et al., 2023; Yim et al., 2023) for protein docking, ligand and protein generation. The works Zhu
419
      et al. (2024); Kong & Tao (2024) leverage trivialized momentum to perform diffusion on the Lie
420   algebra (isomorphic to Rn ) instead of the Lie group, thereby eliminating curvature terms, although
421   their approach is to date only feasible for Abelian groups. An interesting connection with our work
422   is the work of Kim et al. (2022): the authors propose a bijection to map a non-linear problem to a
423   linear one, to approximate a bridge between two non-trivial distributions. Our case can be seen as a
424   bijection between the (curved) Lie group manifold and the (flat) Euclidean data space.
425
      In the context of interpreting the latent space of diffusion models, Park et al. (2023) explores the
426   local structure of the latent space (trajectory) of diffusion models using Riemannian geometry. Sim-
427   ilarly, Haas et al. (2024) propose a method to uncover semantically meaningful directions in the
428   semantic latent space (h-space) (Wang et al., 2023) of denoising diffusion models (DDMs) by PCA.
429   Wang et al. (2023) propose a method to learn disentangled and interpretable latent representations
430   of diffusion models in an unsupervised way. We note that the aforementioned works aim to extract
431   meaningful latent factors in traditional DDMs, often restricting to human-interpretable semantic
      features and focusing on image generation.

527   6     C ONCLUSIONS AND O UTLOOK
528
529   We presented a framework for generative modeling that respects the action of any Lie group G and
530   derived its mathematical foundation. Our framework generates a curved Lie group diffusion dy-
531
      namics in flat Euclidean space, thus without the need to transform the data and of performing
      group projections. Specifically, we introduced a new class of exactly-solvable SDEs that guide the
532
      corruption and generation processes. Traditional diffusion score-matching emerges as a special case
533
      when the group G is set to the translation group in Euclidean space. Thus, our framework does not
534   merely complement existing methods, but expands the space of exactly solvable diffusion processes.
535   Our framework is particularly relevant given recent findings (Abramson et al., 2024) showing that
536   unconstrained models outperform equivariant ones: with our framework there is no need of a trade-
537   off, as we retain the expressivity of unconstrained models with the benefits of group inductive bias.
538   Moreover, our techniques descend quite straightforwardly to flow matching (Lipman et al., 2022)
539   through the Diffusion Mixture Representation Theorem (Peluchetti, 2023; Brigo, 2008). We spell
      out the connection in appendix D and we plan to expand on this in future work.

1326
1327   In the context of generative chemistry, particularly for modeling interactions within protein pockets,
1328   our methods could be employed to decouple the intrinsic generation of ligands from the global trans-
1329   formations required to fit the ligand into the pocket. This approach can also be extended beyond 3D
1330
       coordinates, for instance, by working with higher-order representations, such as modeling electron
       density (Rackers et al., 2023).
1331
1332   Moreover, for more complex problems, it is feasible that an optimal generation process can be
1333   achieved by combining different choices of G along the trajectory. In the context of ligand gener-
1334   ation, we propose a time-dependent group action Gt = tT (3N ) + (1 − t)(SO(3) × R+ )N : at
1335   the beginning of the diffusion process, when the point cloud is still far from forming a recognizable
1336
       conformer, we can leverage the properties of a true Gaussian prior. As the point cloud is gradu-
       ally optimized to “resemble a molecule”, we progressively transition to a generalized score-guided
1337
       process. This shift allows us to fine-tune chemically relevant properties, such as bonds and torsion
1338
       angles, ensuring that the intermediate and final conformers are chemically valid and accurate. This
1339   will be the focus of our forthcoming work.
