009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Knowledge distillation (KD) has proven highly effective for compressing large
012            models and enhancing the performance of smaller ones. However, its effective-
013            ness diminishes in cross-modal scenarios, such as vision-to-language distillation,
014            where inconsistencies in representation across modalities lead to difficult knowl-
015            edge transfer. To address this challenge, we propose frequency-decoupled cross-
016            modal knowledge distillation, a method designed to decouple and balance knowl-
017            edge transfer across modalities by leveraging frequency-domain features. We ob-
018            serve that low-frequency features tend to capture modality-agnostic, generalizable
019
               information, while high-frequency features are more modality-specific. Accord-
               ingly, we apply distinct losses to these features: enforcing strong alignment in
020
               the low-frequency domain and introducing relaxed alignment for high-frequency
021
               features. Additionally, we propose a scale consistency loss to address distribu-
022            tional shifts between modalities, and employ a shared classifier to unify feature
023            spaces. Extensive experiments across multiple benchmark datasets show that our
024            method substantially outperforms traditional KD and state-of-the-art cross-modal
025            KD approaches.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Knowledge Distillation (KD) has emerged as a fundamental technique for model compression and
031   performance improvement. The core concept of KD involves utilizing a large and high-capacity
032   teacher model to mentor a smaller yet more efficient student model. Through this process, the
033   student model learns to approximate the behavior of the teacher model, often achieving comparable
034   or even superior performance despite its reduced complexity.
035
      Despite the substantial success of traditional distillation methods in unimodal settings, such as im-
036   age or text tasks, many real-world applications inherently involve multimodal data, including vision,
037   language, and audio. In these cross-modal scenarios, effectively transferring knowledge among
038   modalities presents unique challenges. As a result, researchers have increasingly turned their atten-
039   tion to devise cross-modal knowledge distillation (CMKD) framework to enhance the performance
040   of a student model in one modality by leveraging the knowledge of a teacher model in a different
041   modality.
042   While preliminary progress has been made in cross-modal distillation, existing methods (Gupta
043   et al., 2016; Thoker & Gall, 2019; Afouras et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2023; Jin et al., 2023) often suffer
044   from several limitations: they are typically restricted to specific scenarios or primarily focus on dis-
045   tillation from a stronger modality to a weaker one. Recently, C2KD (Huo et al., 2024) introduced a
046   cross-modal distillation technique based on logits to reduce the gap between modalities. However,
047   this method overlooks the distillation of challenging samples with misaligned soft labels, which are
048   crucial for effective cross-modal knowledge transfer. Furthermore, C2KD exclusively emphasizes
049   logit-level distillation, while intermediate features, which encapsulate richer modal details and se-
050
      mantic information in cross-modal settings, play a more pivotal role in facilitating complementary
      and effective knowledge transfer. This motivates us to explore cross-modal feature distillation, ad-
051
      dressing the challenges posed by discrepant feature representations and misaligned feature spaces.
052
053   In this paper, we thoroughly analyze the feature representations across different modalities and re-
      veal that these features contain both modality-specific and modality-generic information. Inspired

069   gate the frequency representations of multimodal features, and show that features decomposed into

076
077   Based on these insights, we propose a new approach of decomposing features into low-frequency
078   and high-frequency components for distillation, applying distinct loss functions to each of them,
079
      respectively. For low-frequency features, we employ the traditional mean squared error (MSE) loss
      to ensure “strong consistency” such that modality-generic information from the teacher model can be
080
      better captured. Meanwhile, since high-frequency features contain modality-specific knowledge and
081
      exhibit greater variation, making the full alignment less suited, we introduce the logarithmic mean
082   squared error (logMSE) loss to maintain “weak consistency”. Furthermore, given that distribution
083   differences are critical for effective knowledge transfer (Pan & Yang, 2009; Li et al., 2019; Sun &
084   Saenko, 2016), we propose a scale consistency loss by the alignment of different modalities through
085   feature standardization, to address the significant discrepancies between modalities. This allows
086   the model to focus on intrinsic discriminative features and reduces the impact of scale variations.
087   We also introduce a shared classifier to align feature spaces further to ensure consistent decision
088   boundaries across modalities, thereby enhancing the effectiveness of cross-modal distillation.
089   To summarize, our contributions are three-fold:
090
091                      • By analyzing features across different modalities, we found that low-frequency features ex-
092                        hibit stronger correlations than high-frequency features. Based on this discovery, we intro-
093                        duce a novel cross-modal feature distillation framework. Our method decouples modality-
094                        specific and modality-generic information from a frequency perspective, allowing for more
095
                           effective knowledge transfer by processing each component independently.
096                      • We propose various modality-specific and modality-generic improvements for feature dis-
097                        tillation, which enhance focus on valuable discriminative features and improve the align-
098                        ment of feature representations across different modalities.
099                      • We perform extensive experiments on diverse datasets, covering various modalities and
100                        tasks and employing different network architectures to demonstrate the effectiveness of our
101                        approach.

105   2.1                G ENERIC K NOWLEDGE D ISTILLATION
106
107   Traditional knowledge distillation methods fall into two main categories: logit-based distillation
      and feature-based distillation. Logit-based distillation, first introduced by (Hinton et al., 2015),


                                                                                2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      transfers knowledge by minimizing the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence between the teacher and
109   student model outputs, helping the student learn inter-class relationships. DKD (Zhao et al., 2022)
110   refines this process by separating target and non-target class distillation, allowing better learning
111   of category-specific information. DML (Zhang et al., 2018) enhances transfer by having two mod-
112   els train each other as teachers, while DIST (Huang et al., 2022) uses correlation loss to improve
113   logit distillation by capturing inter-class and intra-class relationships. Feature-based distillation uses
114   intermediate features from the teacher model to supervise the student, aiding in better data represen-
115   tation. FitNet (Romero et al., 2014) was the first to have the student mimic the teacher’s intermediate
116   features. Review (Chen et al., 2021) introduced a mechanism that allows the student to learn teacher
117
      features layer by layer. Relational Knowledge Distillation (RKD) (Park et al., 2019) focuses on
      transferring relationships between samples, while PKD (Cao et al., 2022) preserves relational in-
118
      formation using Pearson correlation. Attention-based Feature Distillation (AFD) (Ji et al., 2021)
119
      utilizes attention mechanisms to dynamically match features, selectively transferring important in-
120   formation. OFD (Heo et al., 2019) employs partial L2 loss, ignoring unhelpful features and focusing
121   on beneficial ones. Generic Knowledge Distillation performs well in single-modal tasks, but due to
122   differences in modality representation, it underperforms in cross-modal scenarios.
123
124   2.2   C ROSS - MODAL K NOWLEDGE D ISTILLATION
125
126   In cross-modal knowledge distillation (CMKD) research, various studies have explored effective
127   methods for transferring knowledge across modalities. (Gupta et al., 2016) proposed an early CMKD
128   framework that transferred labeled supervision from RGB images to depth and optical flow, enhanc-
129   ing the performance of these unlabeled modalities. For action recognition, studies such as (Thoker &
130
      Gall, 2019; Dai et al., 2021; Lee et al., 2023) leveraged RGB or optical flow to design CMKD frame-
      works that improved action detection accuracy. In medical image segmentation, (Wang et al., 2023)
131
      addressed missing modalities by selecting the most contributive one for cross-modal distillation in
132
      multi-modal learning. CMKD has also been applied to tasks like camera-radar object detection
133   and visual place recognition, as seen in works like (Zhao et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024). These
134   works are limited to specific scenarios or focus on distillation for individual modalities. (Xue et al.,
135   2022) introduced the Modality Focusing Hypothesis (MFH), offering the first theoretical analysis
136   of CMKD’s effectiveness, highlighting modality-generic decisive features as crucial for knowledge
137   transfer. More recently, (Huo et al., 2024) identified modality imbalance and soft label misalign-
138   ment as major challenges for CMKD, and introduced the C2KD framework, which significantly
139   improved performance through bidirectional distillation and dynamic selection. These works fall
140   short in addressing the inconsistencies in specific modality information and fail to fully leverage
141
      modality-generic features for effective cross-modal transfer. Our work overcomes these limitations
      by introducing a frequency-domain feature decoupling approach.

144   3     H OW C ROSS -M ODAL F EATURES D IFFER ?
145
146   The major difference between conventional KD in single modality and cross-modal knowledge dis-
147   tillation (CMKD) is that, CMKD is designed to distill the knowledge from another different modal-
148   ity. This difference poses a significant challenge since the teacher and student are trained with data
149   in different modalities, and therefore have more distinct feature representations. Therefore, to de-
150
      sign our cross-modal feature distillation method, it is necessary to first analyze the difference of
      cross-modal features. In this section, we present two of our major findings: (1) modality-generic
151
      and modality-specific features act differently in frequency domain; (2) The features of different
152
      modalities exhibit significant differences in scale.
153
154
      3.1   D ECOUPLING M ODALITY- SPECIFIC AND M ODALITY- GENERIC F EATURES
155
156   Features from a modality can generally be divided into modality-specific and modality-generic com-
157   ponents (Ngiam et al., 2011). The modality-generic component shares the same semantic efficacy
158   across different modalities. While the modality-specific component contains unique and intrinsic
159   information in a certain modality that can hardly be transferred to other modalities. Therefore, to
160   improve knowledge transfer in CMKD, separating these two types of features is crucial. One effec-
161   tive approach to achieving this separation is by analyzing features in the frequency domain (Bruna
      & Mallat, 2013). By decomposing features into high-frequency and low-frequency components,


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      we can further decouple the modality-specific and modality-generic aspects of the features. Low-
163   frequency components typically capture the broader, structural patterns that are more likely to be
164   modality-generic and transferable across domains. In contrast, high-frequency components tend to
165   represent finer, modality-specific details that may not generalize as well across modalities.
166
      To demonstrate the above hypotheses, we leverage Centered Kernel Alignment (CKA) (Kornblith
167
      et al., 2019) to measure the similarities between different modalities in the decoupled low-frequency
168
      and high-frequency features. The calculation method for the CKA Score is in Appendix B. We
169   decoupled the original features of audio and vision models trained on CREMA-D (Cao et al., 2014)
170   and AVE (Tian et al., 2018) datasets, and calculated their CKA scores between audio and vision
171   modalities. As shown in Figure 1, despite all scores are small due to modality and model differences,
172   the low-frequency obtains the highest scores, while the high-frequency are more dissimilar than
173   the original features. This indicates that, more modality-generic information is represented in low
174   frequency, and modality-specific information is more likely to be in high frequency.
175   As a result, for a better cross-modal distillation, it is beneficial to decouple the features into low-
176   frequency and high-frequency features, and treat them respectively based on their unique character-
177   istics. We will formally introduce our strategy in Section 4.
178
179   3.2   S CALE D IFFERENCES ACROSS M ODALITIES
180
181   In transfer learning, distribution differences play a crucial role in determining whether knowledge
182   can be effectively transferred. If there are significant differences between the distributions of the
183   source and target domains, the model may fail to capture useful information during the transfer
184   process, leading to a substantial decrease in the effectiveness of the transfer.
185   In cross-modal knowledge distillation, we found that directly using MSE loss based on the raw
186   features resulted in a significant performance drop in the distillation model compared to the single-
187   modal model, as shown in Table 1.For example, the accuracy of the model distilled using raw fea-
188   tures on the CREMA-D dataset decreased by 1.5% and 2.5% on the audio and visual modalities,
189   respectively, compared to the uni-modal model. We hypothesize that this sharp decline in perfor-
190   mance is likely due to the distribution differences between features from different modalities. We
191   calculated the mean and standard deviation of audio and visual features in the CREMA-D and AVE
192
      datasets, as shown in Figure 2. The results show that there are significant differences in the mean and
      standard deviation between different modalities. For example, on the CREMA-D dataset, the mean
193
      and standard deviation of the audio modality features are higher than those of the visual modality by
194
      0.21 and 0.08, respectively.
195
196   When MSE is used to force the alignment of the student model’s features with those of the teacher
197   model, the student’s features may shift towards the mean of the teacher model’s features. However,
198
      this may conflict with the optimal mean expected in the student’s modality, leading to suboptimal
      performance in the student model.
199
200   Therefore, we should not directly use MSE loss to learn features from different modalities. Instead,
201   we should design a loss function that respects the inherent scale differences between modalities to
202   achieve more effective knowledge transfer.
203

      As previously discussed, we found that modality-specific and modality-generic information can be
207   effectively decoupled through frequency domain analysis, and there are significant differences in
208   the feature distributions across different modalities. In this section, we will formally introduce our
209   method to improve CMKD: (1) We decouple the features into low-frequency and high-frequency
210   components and apply different loss functions for distillation accordingly. (2) We ensure the features
211   from different modalities are consistent in scale and feature space.

532
      In this paper, we investigated the non-negligible challenges faced cross-modal knowledge distil-
533
      lation, particularly focusing on the discrepancies between modality-specific and modality-generic
534   information, and the differences in feature distributions across modalities. Based on the experimen-
535   tal observation that low-frequency features exhibit stronger correlations than high-frequency fea-
536   tures, we proposed a novel distillation framework – It decouples these types of information through
537   frequency-based feature analysis and introduces a differentiated distillation strategy for different
538   frequency components. Additionally, we addressed feature distribution discrepancies by incorpo-
539   rating a scale consistency loss and using a shared classifier for feature space alignment. Extensive
      experiments have demonstrated the effectiveness of our approach.

      Centered Kernel Alignment (CKA) is a metric used to measure the similarity between two sets of
767
      feature representations, often from different layers or models. It helps in understanding how neural
768   networks encode data.
769
770   CKA compares two sets of features by aligning their Gram matrices, which capture pairwise simi-
771
      larities between samples. It has the following properties:
772
             • Invariant to orthogonal transformations: Features can be compared even if they are in
773            different spaces.
774
             • Scale invariant: The score is unaffected by differences in feature magnitudes.
775
776   Given two sets of feature representations X ∈ Rn×p and Y ∈ Rn×q , where n is the number of
777   samples, the steps are as follows:
778
779         1. Gram matrix:
780                                          KX = XX⊤ ,        KY = YY⊤
781
            2. Centering the Gram matrices:
782
                                               1     1       1
783                                  K˜X = KX − 1KX − KX 1 + 2 1KX 1
784                                            n     n      n
785         3. CKA Score:
786                                                            Tr(K˜X K˜Y )
787                                   CKA(X, Y) = q
788                                                        Tr(K˜X K˜X )Tr(K˜Y K˜Y )
789
790   The CKA score ranges from 0 to 1. A score close to 1 indicates high similarity between the two sets
791
      of features, while a score near 0 indicates low similarity.
