                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011
012            Diffusion models achieve high-quality sample generation at the cost of a lengthy
013            multistep inference procedure. To overcome this, diffusion distillation techniques
014            produce student generators capable of matching or surpassing the teacher in a
015            single step. However, the student model’s inference speed is limited by the size
016            of the teacher architecture, preventing real-time generation for computationally
017
               heavy applications. In this work, we introduce Multi-Student Distillation (MSD),
               a framework to distill a conditional teacher diffusion model into multiple single-
018
               step generators. Each student generator is responsible for a subset of the con-
019
               ditioning data, thereby obtaining higher generation quality for the same capacity.
020            MSD trains multiple distilled students allowing smaller sizes and, therefore, faster
021            inference. Also, MSD offers a lightweight quality boost over single-student dis-
022            tillation with the same architecture. We demonstrate MSD is effective by training
023            multiple same-sized or smaller students on single-step distillation using distribu-
024            tion matching and adversarial distillation techniques. With smaller students, MSD
025            gets competitive results with faster inference for single-step generation. Using 4
026            same-sized students, MSD sets a new state-of-the-art for one-step image genera-
027            tion: FID 1.20 on ImageNet-64×64 and 8.20 on zero-shot COCO2014.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032
033   Diffusion models are the dominant generative model in image, audio, video, 3D assets, protein de-
034   sign, and more (Ho et al., 2020; Kong et al., 2022; Blattmann et al., 2023; Anand and Achim, 2022;
035   Nichol et al., 2022). They allow different conditioning inputs – such as class labels, text, or images
036
      – and achieve high-quality generated outputs. However, their inference process typically requires
      hundreds of model evaluations – with an often slow and bulky network – for a single sample. This
037
      procedure costs millions of dollars per day (Valyaeva, 2024; Google, 2024). It also prohibits appli-
038
      cations requiring rapid synthesis, such as augmented reality. Real-time, low-cost, and high-quality
039   generation will have huge financial and operational impacts while enabling new usage paradigms.
040
041   There has been a flurry of work on diffusion-distillation techniques to address the slow sampling
042   of diffusion models (Luhman and Luhman, 2021; Song et al., 2023; Yin et al., 2024a). Inspired
043   by knowledge distillation (Hinton et al., 2015), these methods use the trained diffusion model as a
044   teacher and optimize a student model to match its generated output in as few as a single step. How-
045   ever, most diffusion distillation methods use the same student and teacher architecture. This prevents
046
      real-time generation for applications with bulky networks, such as video synthesis (Blattmann et al.,
      2023). While reducing model size can reduce inference time, it typically yields worse generation
047
      quality, thus presenting a speed-to-quality tradeoff dilemma with existing distillation methods.
048
049   We tackle this dilemma with our method, Multi-Student Distillation (MSD), that introduces multiple
050   single-step student generators distilled from the pretrained teacher. Each student is responsible for
051   a subset of conditioning inputs. We determine which student to use during inference and perform
052   a single-model evaluation to generate a high-quality sample. This way, MSD enjoys the benefit of
053   a mixture of experts (Jordan and Jacobs, 1994): it increases the model capacity without incurring
      more inference cost, thereby effectively pushing the limit of the speed-quality tradeoff.

      When distilling into the same-sized students, MSD has the flexibility of being conceptually appli-
055   cable to any distillation method for a performance boost. In addition, MSD allows distilling into
056   multiple smaller students for reduced single-step generation time. Using smaller students prevents
057   one from initializing a student from teacher weights, posing an additional technical challenge. We
058   solve this challenge by adding a relatively lightweight score-matching pretraining stage before dis-
059   tillation (Sec. 4.3), and demonstrating its necessity and efficiency via extensive experiments.
060
061   We validate our approaches by applying MSD to distill the teacher into 4 same-sized students using
062
      distribution matching and adversarial distillation procedures (Yin et al., 2024a;b). The resulting stu-
      dents collectively outperform single-student counterparts, setting new state-of-the-art FID scores of
063
      1.20 on one-step ImageNet-64×64 generation (Tab. 1) and 8.20 on one-step zero-shot COCO2014
064
      generation (Tab. 2). We also distill the same teacher into 4 smaller students, which achieve a com-
065   petitive FID of 2.88 on ImageNet (Tab. 1), with 42% less parameters per student.
066
067   We summarize our contributions below, which include:
068
069          • A new framework, MSD, that upgrades existing single-step diffusion distillation methods
070            (Sec. 4.1) by increasing the effective model capacity without changing inference latency.
071          • Demonstrating the effectiveness of MSD by training multiple same sized students using
072            SOTA distillation techniques (Yin et al., 2024a;b) in Sec. 4.2, resulting in new record FID
073            scores in ImageNet-64×64 (Sec. 5.2) and zero-shot text-to-image generation (Sec. 5.3).
074
             • A successful scheme to distill multiple smaller single-step students from the teacher model,
075
               achieving comparable generation quality with reduced inference time.

078   2    R ELATED W ORK
079
080
      Diffusion Sampling Acceleration. While a line of work aims to accelerate diffusion models via
081
      fast numerical solvers for the PF-ODE (Lu et al., 2022a;b; Zheng et al., 2024; Karras et al., 2022;
082   Liu et al., 2022), they usually still require more than 10 steps. Training-based methods that usually
083   follow the knowledge distillation pipeline can achieve low-step or even one-step generation. Luh-
084   man and Luhman (2021) first used the diffusion model to generate a noise and image pair dataset
085   that is then used to train a single-step generator. DSNO (Zheng et al., 2023) precomputes the denois-
086   ing trajectory and uses neural operators to estimate the whole PF-ODE path. Progressive distillation
087   (Salimans and Ho, 2022; Meng et al., 2023) iteratively halves the number of sampling steps re-
088   quired without needing an offline dataset. Rectified Flow (Liu et al., 2023a) and follow-up works
089   (Liu et al., 2023b; Yan et al., 2024) straighten the denoising trajectories to allow sampling in fewer
090
      steps. Another approach uses self-consistent properties of denoising trajectories to inject additional
      regularization for distillation (Gu et al., 2023; Berthelot et al., 2023; Song et al., 2023; Song and
091
      Dhariwal, 2024; Luo et al., 2023; Ren et al., 2024; Kim et al., 2024).
092
093   The methods above require the student to follow the teacher’s trajectories. Instead, a recent line of
094   works aims to only match the distribution of the student and teacher output via variational score
095   distillation (Yin et al., 2024a;b; Salimans et al., 2024; Xie et al., 2024a; Luo et al., 2024; Zhou et al.,
096   2024a; Nguyen and Tran, 2024). The adversarial loss (Goodfellow et al., 2014), often combined
097   with the above techniques, has been used to enhance the distillation performance further (Xiao et al.,
098   2022; Zheng and Yang, 2024; Sauer et al., 2023a; 2024; Wang et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024; Lin et al.,
099   2024; Kim et al., 2024). Although MSD is conceptually compatible and offers a performance boost
100
      to all of these distillation methods, in this work, we demonstrate two specific techniques: distribution
      matching (Yin et al., 2024a) and adversarial distillation (Yin et al., 2024b).
101
102   Mixture of experts training and distillation. Mixture of Experts (MoE), first proposed in Jordan
103   and Jacobs (1994), has found success in training very large-scale neural networks (Shazeer et al.,
104   2017; Lepikhin et al., 2021; Fedus et al., 2022; Lewis et al., 2021; Borde et al., 2024). Distilling a
105   teacher model into multiple students was explored by Hinton et al. (2015), and after that, has been
106   further developed for supervised learning (Chen et al., 2020; Ni and Hu, 2023; Chang et al., 2022)
107   and language modeling (Xie et al., 2024b; Kudugunta et al., 2021; Zuo et al., 2022). Although sev-
      eral works (Hoang et al., 2018; Park et al., 2018; Ahmetoğlu and Alpaydın, 2021) have proposed


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      MoE training schemes for generative adversarial networks, they train the MoE from scratch. This re-
109   quires carefully tuning the multi-expert adversarial losses. eDiff-I (Balaji et al., 2022) uses different
110   experts in different denoising timesteps for a multi-step diffusion model. A recent work (Zhou et al.,
111   2024b) proposes to distill a pretrained diffusion model into an MoE for policy learning, which shares
112   similar motivations with our work. However, to the best of our knowledge, MSD is the first method
113   to distill multi-step teacher diffusion models into multiple one-step students for image generation.
114
115   Efficient architectures for diffusion models. In addition to reducing steps, an orthogonal approach
116
      aims to accelerate diffusion models with more efficient architectures. A series of works (Bao et al.,
      2022; Peebles and Xie, 2023; Hoogeboom et al., 2023) introduces vision transformers to diffusion
117
      blocks and trains the diffusion model with new architectures from scratch. Another line of work
118
      selectively removes or modifies certain components of a pretrained diffusion model and then either
119   finetunes (Kim et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024) or re-trains (Zhao et al., 2023) the
120   lightweight diffusion model, from which step-distillation can be further applied (Li et al., 2024;
121   Zhao et al., 2023). Our approach is orthogonal to these works in two regards: 1) In our method,
122   each student only handles a subset of data, providing a gain in relative capacity. 2) Instead of
123   obtaining a full diffusion model, our method employs a lightweight pretraining stage to obtain a
124   good initialization for single-step distillation. Combining MSD with more efficient architectures is
125   a promising future direction.

193   3.3   E NHANCING DISTILLATION QUALITY WITH ADVERSARIAL LOSS
194
195   The adversarial loss, originally proposed by Goodfellow et al. (2014), has shown a remarkable capa-
196   bility in diffusion distillation to enhance sharpness and realism in generated images, thus improving
197   generation quality. Specifically, DMD2 (Yin et al., 2024b) proposes adding a minimal discriminator
198   head to the bottleneck layer of the “fake” denoising model µfake , which is naturally compatible with
199   DMD’s alternating training scheme and the TTUR. Moreover, they showed that one should first train
200
      the model without GAN to convergence, then add the GAN loss and continue training. This yields
      better terminal performance than training with the GAN loss from the beginning. We use adversarial
201
      distribution matching (ADM) to refer to distribution matching with added adversarial loss.

250   The partition of Y into {Yk }K k=1 determines the input condition groups for which each student is
251   responsible. As a starting point, we make the following three simplifications for choosing a partition:
252
253   • Disjointness: This prevents potential redundant training and redundant usage of model capacity.
254
      • Equal size: Since students have the same architecture, the partitions {Yk }K
                                                                                   k=1 should be of equal
255
        size that require similar model capacity.
256
257   • Clustering: Conditions within each partition should be more semantically similar than those in
258
        other partitions, so networks require less capacity to achieve a set quality on their partition.
259
260   The first two conditions can be easily satisfied in practice, while the third is not straightforward. For
261   a class-conditional generation, partitioning by semantically similar and equal-sized classes serves
262
      a straightforward strategy, though extending it to text-conditional generation is nontrivial. Another
      promising strategy uses pretrained embedding layers such as the CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) embed-
263
      ding layer or the teacher embedding layer. One could find embeddings of the input conditions and
264
      then perform clustering on those embeddings, which are fixed-length numerical vectors containing
265   implicit semantic information. We ablate partition strategies in Sec. 5.4.
266
267   The data filtering function F determines the training subset data Dk from Yk . For example, a vanilla
268   filtering strategy could set F (D, Yk ) = Dk := DYk , where DYk denotes the subset of the training
269   dataset D that contains the desired condition Yk . Empirically, we found that this filtering works in
      most cases, although sometimes a different approach is justified, as demonstrated in Sec. 4.2.

295

296
      tions only on the desired partition for the KL loss but use the whole paired dataset for the regression
297   loss. This special filtering is based on the observation that the size of Dpaired critically affects the
298   terminal performance of DMD distillation: using fewer pairs causes mode collapse, whereas using
299   more pairs challenge the model capacity. Naı̈vely filtering paired datasets by partition reduces the
300   paired dataset size for each student and leads to worse performance, as in our ablation in App. B.2.
301   Instead of generating more paired data to mitigate this imbalance, we simply reuse the original
302   paired dataset for the regression loss. This is remarkably effective, which we hypothesize is because
303   paired data from other input conditions provides effective gradient updates to the shared weights in
304   the network.

      that our best students have comparable generation quality as the teacher.

411   the prepended teacher score matching (TSM) stage from Sec. 4.3. This achieved a 42% reduction
412   in model size and a 7% reduction in latency, with a slight degradation in FID score, offering a
413   flexible framework to increase generation speed by reducing student size, and increasing generation

513   6     D ISCUSSION
514
515   6.1   L IMITATIONS
516
517   MSD is the first work to explore diffusion distillation with multiple students, and it admits a few
518   limitations that call for future work. 1) Further explorations could offer more insights into optimal
519   design choices for a target quality and latency on various datasets, such as the number of students,
520   input condition size for each student, and other hyperparameters. This is especially beneficial if
521   the training budget is limited. 2) We apply simple partitioning for both class- and text-conditions
522   and assign them disjointly to different students. Although our empirical study shows that simple
523   alternatives do not offer obvious advantages, more sophisticated routing mechanisms may help.
524   3) We use simple channel reduction when designing smaller students to demonstrate feasibility.
525
      This results in a significantly smaller latency reduction than sample size reduction. Exploring other
      designs of smaller students will likely increase their quality and throughput. 4) We train different
526
      students separately, but we expect that carefully designed weight-sharing, loss-sharing, or other
527
      interaction schemes can further enhance training efficiency. 5) We hypothesize that MSD can be
528   applied to other diffusion distillation methods and other modalities for similar benefits, but leave
529   this for future work.
530
531   6.2   C ONCLUSION
532
533
      This work presented Multi-Student Distillation, a simple yet efficient method to increase the ef-
534   fective model capacity for single-step diffusion distillation. We applied MSD to the distribution
535   matching and adversarial distillation methods. We demonstrated their superior performance over
536   single-student counterparts in both class-conditional generation and text-to-image generation. Par-
537   ticularly, MSD with DMD2’s the two-stage training achieves state-of-the-art FID scores. Moreover,
538   we successfully distilled smaller students from scratch, demonstrating MSD’s potential in further
539   reducing the generation latency with multiple smaller student distillations. We envision building on
      MSD to enable generation in real-time, enabling many new use cases.

1021   Here we discuss some MSD deployment options for practitioners.

1023   A naive option for deployment is to use increased GPU memory to host all models simultaneously.

1025   user request. In settings with less GPU memory than all students’ sum memory requirement, we
       must swap student models on and off GPUs. This incurs extra latency, however, in the few-GPU

1054

1057   cost, so we pay little for it.
1058
1059   If the data has been partitioned uniformly according to user demand then the incoming requests are
1060   distributed uniformly among the student nodes. Therefore, we achieve equal throughput compared
1061   to the teacher without more overall model storage. However, finding such a partition is challenging,
1062
       and user demand may change over time. This leaves finding the optimal allocation of resources to
       the student nodes an open problem. In practice, we expect that a reduced student model size would
1063
       lead to an overall reduction in storage requirements compared to the teacher alone.
