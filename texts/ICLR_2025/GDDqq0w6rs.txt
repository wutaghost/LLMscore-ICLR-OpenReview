011                                                     A BSTRACT
012
013                The application of deep learning for biology, including foundation models, has
014
                   increased significantly in recent years. Some models are text-based, while others
                   are trained on the underlying biological data, especially omics data of various
015
                   modalities. Consistently comparing the performance of deep learning models for
016
                   biology has proven challenging due to the diversity of training data and downstream
017                tasks. Here, we utilize the fact that many models operate on the level of genes and
018                propose a unifying benchmark by defining hundreds of tasks based on ground-truth
019                gene properties collected from professionally curated bioinformatics databases.
020                We collect properties of five types: (1) genomic properties, including predicting
021                which genes can be methylated or which are dose-dependent; (2) regulatory func-
022                tions, evaluating how the genes participate in cellular regulatory processes; (3)
023                localization, including identification of differential expression in different tissues
024                or sub-cellular localization; (4) biological processes, including predicting gene in-
025
                   volvement in pathways or disease prognostics; and (5) protein properties, including
                   prediction of functional domains or post-translational modifications. These proper-
026
                   ties are used to define binary, multi-label and multi-class classification tasks. To
027
                   create an architecture-agnostic benchmark we extract gene representation vectors
028                from each model, including single-cell RNA-seq (scRNA) foundation models, large
029                language models, protein language models, DNA foundation models, and classical
030                baselines, and use them to train simple predictive models on the tasks. Depending
031                on the model, we utilize the model’s token-level embeddings of gene symbols or
032                transform the gene symbol to an input appropriate for the model, i.e. a description
033                of the gene for text models, the gene sequence for DNA models or amino acid
034                sequences for the protein models. Using these embeddings on the benchmark tasks,
035                we create a detailed assessment of the relative performance of the different models.
036
                   In general, we find that text-based models and protein language models outperform
                   the expression-based models on tasks related to genomic properties and regulatory
037
                   functions, while expression-based models tend to outperform the others on local-
038
                   ization tasks. We also observe performance for the classical bag-of-words baseline
039                that is similar to the large language models for many tasks. By enabling broad
040                systematic evaluation of diverse deep learning models in biology, this benchmark
041                can help direct future research in artificial intelligence toward improved biological
042                understanding and accelerated therapeutic discoveries. The code and benchmark
043                data can be extended to more models and tasks and is available at GitHub.

      modalities (Thieme et al., 2023). These models can identify functional sections of the genome, novel
077
      cell types, disease states, and more. Many of these efforts aim to identify the genes responsible
078
      for the various biological processes to identify future means to maintain these processes or restore
079   their function. The proliferation of models and the potential for significant impact on human health
080   gives rise to the need for robust evaluation and benchmarking. For text models, a number of
081   biological/medical benchmarks have been published such as MedQA (Jin et al., 2021), and been
082   widely adopted (Open Medical-LLM Leaderboard). Recent research provided a comparison of
083   foundation models in specific modalities such as single cell models (Liu et al., 2023). However, no
084   benchmarks have been proposed that compare foundation models across modalities or that compare
085   text models against models trained on biological data directly. Part of the difficulty is that the
086   downstream tasks that are used to compare scRNA FMs such as cell-type annotation, batch correction,
087
      and perturbation prediction (Ding et al., 2024) are very different from the benchmarking tasks used to
      evaluate NLP models, such as question-answering or sentence completion. The need for a benchmark
088
      that can work with text models and foundation models is particularly important in light of recent works
089
      such as Cell2Sentence (Levine et al., 2023), GenePT (Chen & Zou, 2023) and scInterpreter (Li et al.,
090   2024), which have shown that text-models can be repurposed to work directly with transcriptomic
091   data.
092
093
      Here we propose to use gene embeddings to create a new benchmark that enables comparison of
      biological foundation models across modalities and against text models. Gene embeddings are an in-
094
      herent component of expression-based foundation models built on Transformer architectures (Vaswani
095
      et al., 2017), parallel to word embeddings in text models. They can also be produced using the gene
096   symbol or gene description with a language model supporting text embedding. Smaller models such
097   as gene2vec (Du et al., 2019) or even bag-of-words models on textual descriptions of the gene can
098   also produce gene embeddings. As with text embedding benchmarks, it is assumed that the models
099   producing better gene embeddings are learning the ground truth more faithfully (Muennighoff et al.,
100   2022).
101
      To evaluate the gene embeddings, we compile a wide range of ground truth biological knowledge
102   about genes including their genomic properties, regulatory functions, localization, their involvement
103   in biological processes, and their protein properties (Table 1). We connect the gene embeddings
104   to the relevant tasks, and evaluate their performance as illustrated in Figure 1. Though each task
105   captures only a small part of the biology involving the gene, collectively they offer a multi-faceted,
106   panoramic view of the gene. Superior performance on this collection of tasks thus implies that the
107   model’s learned embeddings are more inherently meaningful and thus useful for diverse downstream
      tasks even without seeing labeled data for these tasks.

      We apply this benchmark to evaluate several families of models. These include text-based models,
109   where the embeddings utilize large language models to encode a textual description of the genes,
110   scRNA foundation models trained on multi-omics data, models that are based on protein or DNA
111   sequence, and classical ML methods to act as a baseline comparison on text and gene expression
112   data. Our analysis shows that text models outperform the other model families for most gene-related
113   tasks, even when the information is not explicitly in the text. This result underscores the need and the
114   potential to continue and improve knowledge integration into gene embeddings, thus improving gene
115   target identification and all downstream tasks.
116   The benchmark platform is available (under an Apache 2.0 license) at GitHub, and includes scripts
117   for task data downloads, as well as examples and documentation for using the benchmark on new
118   models.

121   2     B ENCHMARK TASKS
122
123   Following decades of work in bioinformatics and related fields, large amounts of structured data
124   regarding genes have been compiled through projects such as Reactome (Milacic et al., 2023), Human
125
      Protein Atlas (Uhlen et al., 2010; Human Protein Atlas), OpenTargets (Ochoa et al., 2022) and HUGO
      Gene Nomenclature Committee at the University of Cambridge (Seal et al., 2022). This enables us
126
      to compile a wide variety of validated properties to use to test the quality of gene embeddings. Our
127
      benchmarking package allows defining the tasks in general terms, which allows simple addition of
128   new tasks with multiple identifier types (see S6.1. Notably, the benchmarking package is not limited
129   to gene-tasks and can be easily extended to other modalities.

      2.1   TASKS D ESCRIPTION
132
133
      We compiled 312 gene properties, which we used to define evaluation tasks. Most of the tasks are
134   based on single gene properties, while some are based on gene-pairs or links between genes and
135   diseases.
136
137
      For simplicity, we sort the properties into the following five families:
138
      Genomic properties This family of tasks evaluates the ability to predict properties inherent to the
139
            gene sequence, including predicting which genes can be methylated, and which genes are
140         dose-dependent (their expression depends on the number of copies in the genome) There is
141         a total of 7 tasks in this family. See Table S1 for a full description.
142
143   Regulatory functions This family of tasks evaluates how the genes interact with other genes through
144
              the cellular regulatory processes and consists of a total of 6 tasks. These include predicting
             which genes are transcription factors, the number of connections in the gene-regulatory
145
              network, etc. See Table S3 for a full description.
146
147   Localization This family includes tasks for identifying differential expression and activity in different
148           tissues or sub-cellular localization. That includes predicting protein levels found in blood,
149           correctly assigning genes to expression clusters derived from various tissue samples, sub-
150
              cellular localization, etc. There are a total of 30 tasks in this family. See Table S4 for a full
              description.
151
152   Biological processes This family evaluates the biological functionality of the gene by evaluating
153           tasks such as involvement in pathways, being prognostic of survival, and being associated
154           with a disease. This family consists of 29 tasks, representing the most diverse set of questions.
155           See Table S5 for a full description.
156   protein properties This family focuses on properties of the protein product of the gene, including
157           its functional domains, post-translational modifications,and its ligands.
158
159   These properties cover many of the biological roles that genes play, providing an indication of how
160   well a given pre-trained model has captured various aspects of gene representation, allowing for
161   differentiation between various types of models and training data. Users can use the performance on
      different task families to select pre-trained models for their use-case.

178   2.2   TASK O RIGIN
179
180   To benchmark the pre-trained models, we aimed to collect properties that are as diverse as possible,
181   capturing the many roles that genes and the proteins they code play in biology. For reliability and
182
      reproducibility we have opted for gene properties that are manually validated by hand and freely
      available, see Section S6.1.6 for data availability details.
183
184
      Reactome The pathways tasks were curated by taking the full list of genes from the Human Genome
185          Nomenclature Committee (HGNC) downloadable files (including protein-coding gene, non-
186          coding RNA, pseudogene and other tables) (Seal et al., 2022) and labeling each symbol by
187          its inclusion in a top-level pathway S6 from Reactome (Milacic et al., 2023).
188
189
      Human Protein Atlas Protein atlas (Human Protein Atlas) tasks were created by compiling the
            protein atlas file v23 (Protein Atlas Data v23). We selected columns that contained features
190
            regarding gene properties, sorting them to binary, multiclass, multi-label, or regression tasks.
191
            We removed rows with missing data or genes that had no symbol name.
192
193   Open Targets The gene-disease association (Ochoa et al., 2022) task was compiled by downloading
194          the overall association score of genes and diseases from the open targets platform (we used
195          the direct file interface using the files published on 2023-09-21 version 23).
196   Uniprot Three protein properties tasks were created using data from the Universal Protein Knowl-
197          edgebase (UniProt) by assigning binary labels to gene symbols if a given keyword value is
198          present for any of the protein products of that gene symbol. The keyword categories used
199          were Domain, Ligand and Post-transcriptional modification (Consortium, 2022).
200
      Publications Since several papers have used gene properties to evaluate pretrained models, we
201
              included those tasks in our benchmark (Chen & Zou, 2023; Fang et al., 2024; Lambert et al.,
202
              2018). There are 9 tasks derived directly from publications.
203
204   These tasks cover a wide variety of gene roles and provide a well-grounded assessment of gene
205   representation quality. Because research in this domain evolves quickly, we have provided the ability
206   to extend our benchmark with more tasks, at GitHub.

      2.3   TASK D EFINITION
209
210   Independent of the task family, each task evaluates a specific outcome type: a) a binary , or b) a
211   multi-label assignment, or c) a multiclass, or d) a regression task. We used gene properties to define
212   tasks only if at least 1% of the covered entities had the label. Binary sub-tasks were derived from
213   multi-label tasks by selecting specific labels. For all tasks, we used the gene symbol as an identifier;
214   ensemble stable IDs were converted into symbols using MyGeneInfo (Wu et al., 2012). To simplify
215   comparisons between models we limited the scope of each task to the gene symbols shared by all
      encoding models.

      2.4   TASK E VALUATION
234
235   In contrast to text embedding benchmarks such as MTEB (Muennighoff et al., 2022) where the
236   quality of the model is assessed by its ability to generate similar embeddings for known similar
237   texts, evaluating the biological properties of genes embeddings requires a slightly different approach.
238   Because the genes have many biological properties, we cannot assess the model quality by similarity
239   alone. For this reason, we have primarily adopted classification metrics: the embeddings are provided
240   as inputs to a simple logistic or linear regression model to predict the ground truth properties, and
241   evaluated with 5-fold cross-validation. The benchmark can also be defined using non-linear models,
242   which could detect information in the representation vectors more successfully than a linear model,
243
      we discuss this and assess the differences in Section 5.
244   This setup enables us to evaluate whether the correct information is encoded in the vector without
245   making a-priori assumptions about the underlying information properties of the embedding space.

261
      For text embedding models, we create an embedding for a gene by extracting the standard sym-
      bol, full name, and description of the gene from the NCBI Entrez Gene database (Maglott et al.,
262
      2010). This information is packed into a textual description that is given to the model as a
263
      prompt in the format "Gene symbol <symbol> full name <full name> with the
264   summary <summary description>", and this prompt is embedded using a sentence em-
265   bedding model. As a result, the benchmark that we have defined works seamlessly with any
266   model supported by sentence_transformers (Reimers & Gurevych, 2019). For this as-
267   sessment, we selected the top performing models from the leading embedding benchmark, the
268   MTEB leaderboard (MTEB Leaderboard) and from the sentence transformers leaderboard (Sen-

291   3.2   G ENE EXPRESSION AND TRANSFORMER - BASED MODELS
292
293   Inspired by the success of transformer-based LLMs in NLP, these models aim to learn biology as
294   a “language” over scRNA-seq readings, fitting an embedding to each gene as if it were a ’word’
295   in an NLP model, and the transformer based architectures integrate the gene expressions into cell-
296
      level embeddings. We made use of a recent survey and benchmark to highlight the three best
      performing open scRNA foundation models (Liu et al., 2023). The gene embeddings were extracted
297
      from the publicly available model weights. Gene names were taken from the supplementary model
298
      configuration files.
299
300
      CellPLM A transformer-based foundation model for single-cell biology with over 80M parame-
301          ters (Wen et al., 2023). Trained on scRNA-seq and spatially resolved transcriptomic (SRT),
302          adding tissue level information. Trained using MLM variant, on 9 million scRNA-seq cells
303          and 2 million SRT cells. Embedding extracted from the embedder.feat_enc.emb
304          layer in the model downloaded (Wen et al.), with the gene names from the matching
305          configuration file.
306
307
      Geneformer A transformer based foundation model for single cell biology with 10.3M parameters.
             (Theodoris et al., 2023a). This model represents the scRNA expression using a list of genes
308
             ranked by their normalized expression levels. This is intended to make the order significant,
309
             and allows the use of context-aware attention mechanisms similar to these that work well in
310          NLP. The model is trained on about 30M scRNA-seq readings. Embedding extracted from
311          the embeddings.word_embeddings layer from (Theodoris et al., 2023b)
312
313   ScGPT A generative foundation model for single-cell transcriptomics utilizing a self-attention, with
314         53M parameters (Cui et al., 2024). Pretrained using masked language model (MLM) training.
315         Explicitly encoded genes, expression levels and conditions, concatenated to represent each
316
            gene in context. Training is performed using a masked language modeling variant, where
            masking is done with attention masking to accommodate for the non-sequential nature of
317
            the data. Embedding extracted from (Cui et al., b) following the instructions in (Cui et al.,
318
            a), steps 1 and 2. We used two variants, blood (designated ScGPT-B) trained on 10.3 million
319         blood and bone marrow cells and the human model (designated ScGPT-H) trained on 33
320         million normal human cells.
321
322   Gene2vec A 200 dimensional concept embedding of the human genes (Du et al., 2019), based on
323          the concept of Word2Vec (Mikolov et al., 2013) and learned from co-expression patterns,

      4     R ESULTS
362
363
      We report our gene-benchmarks on eleven models, evaluated on all tasks. The benchmark results
      demonstrate that the various models exhibit different performance patterns on different tasks. When
364
      grouping the performance measures by family tasks and averaging across tasks we find that the four
365
      text-based models exhibit better performance for the genomic properties and regulatory function
366   families, while the scRNA-based models performed better at the localization and biological process
367   tasks (Figure 2). These trends are consistent when using other evaluation metrics such as F1 (see
368   Figure S1). The calculation time for the benchmark varies depending on the model size. For the
369   largest model, with 7B parameters and embedding size 4096 (Table 3), creating the gene embeddings
370   took approximately 40 minutes on a single NVIDIA A100 80GB. The calculation time for fitting the
371   predictive models is highly dependent on the embedding size, with the smaller embeddings requiring
372   less than an hour to calculate all the benchmarks and the largest requiring 15+ hours on a 48-core
373   Xeon E5.

376   the MTEB-L model exhibits comparable performance to the smaller MTEB-S and MPNet models.

409
      that a more expressive model could perform better by training a multilayer perceptron model (MLP)

411   is closely correlated to logistic regression, as seen in Figure S12. For this reason, we prefer the linear
412   models which are not sensitive to hyperparameter selections and thus enable robust comparisons
413   across many thousands of combinations of models and tasks. Nevertheless, the benchmark package
414   code at GitHubsupports the use of any scikit-learn model.
415
      One notable result is that text models outperform the scRNA models in most disease involvement tasks
416   except in the Pathology tasks, chromosome, and N1 Network, indicating that there are exceptions to
417   the general rules of model performance we outlined. Similarly, expression-based models outperform
418   in cell-type localization tasks, but under-perform in sub-cellular localization tasks. This is in line
419   with our expectations, given the close relation between cell-type, tissue-type and single cell RNA
420   expression levels.

423   5                  S UMMARY AND D ISCUSSION
424
425   We present a gene-centric benchmark that includes hundreds of tasks, sorted into functional families.
426   We designed this benchmark to evaluate the gene embeddings provided by pretrained models applied
427   to biology, thus suggesting a common ground for evaluating the potential of the models to provide
428
      useful, and potentially novel insights on gene involvement in biological and medical questions.
429   We applied the benchmark to a representative set of models trained on text, gene-expression data,
430   protein sequences and DNA sequences and compared their performance. We observed that each family
431   of models exhibits superiority for a different set of tasks, hinting that combining the knowledge
      that comes from multiple modalities may provide additional benefits. It should be pointed out,


                                                                                                                  8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      however, that our analysis is not intended to be a comprehensive comparison of all text models and
433   all gene-expression models and that our results may not be generalizable to all such models.
434
435   The embeddings that we chose to use for the text models were based on a detailed description of
436
      the genes, rather than using the gene symbol, which could have evaluated whether the LLM has
      sufficient useful information on genes from its general training on text. However, while virtually
437
      all coding genes have literature in PubMed, the coverage is highly skewed, with the most popular
438
      10% of genes accounting for over 60% of the publication (Lee et al., 2019), suggesting that language
439   model pretraining may be skewed towards the more widely studied genes. Instead, in our evaluation
440   of text models, we included a bag-of-words model that can serve as a baseline of sorts, allowing us to
441   examine the ability of LLMs to generalize and contextualize. We find that in most tasks the LLMs
442   provide a modest improvement in performance, indicating that additional work may be needed to
443   create LLMs that are useful for basic biology.
444
      We point out that most of the models we evaluated here were not designed to provide useful gene
445   embeddings. Some were trained to perform textual tasks, while others were trained to predict
446   properties at the whole-cell level. However, utilizing pretrained gene embeddings from a foundation
447   model to improve performance of a more specialized model has become an active research area
448   with numerous promising results produced in the last year. For example, scFoundation (Hao et al.,
449   2023) showed improvement on gene perturbation prediction by injecting their gene embeddings
450   into GEARS (Roohani et al., 2023), SATURN (Rosen et al., 2024) has used ESM (Lin et al., 2023)
451   protein embeddings to enable cross-species cell label propagation and GNN based models such as
452   Otter-Knowledge (Lam et al., 2023) and BioBridge (Wang et al., 2023) have proposed comprehensive
453
      biomedical models built on embeddings across multiple domains. Given the interest and promise of
      this technology, our benchmark can help guide researchers toward more successful application of
454
      deep learning to biology.

457   5.1   L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
458
459
      We gathered the benchmark tasks from actively maintained professionally curated sources and we
      have relied on their quality control processes. For many reasons, the entire genome is not studied
460
      evenly (Lee et al., 2019) and when genes are studied, the full diversity of human ancestry is not
461
      evenly reflected (Fairley et al., 2019). As biological research improves in performance and fairness,
462   we look forward to updating our benchmark tasks accordingly. We used only open source models
463   with released weights excluding models that did not (Zrimec et al., 2022). Though we have explored
464   the benchmark tasks using gene embeddings, the tasks could be utilized in other ways, such as by
465   defining fine-tuning objectives for deep learning models or even as the basis for question answering
466   in text models. Such a strategy, while not applicable for all models, may uncover predictive power
467   that is specific to each model.

      S6.1     T HE G ENE -B ENCHMARK PACKAGE
705
706   This freely available package was developed to facilitate easy access to the tasks and efficient use
707   of them for benchmarking. It includes three main modules as well as notebooks and scripts that
708   demonstrate the package’s usability. The main flow of task evaluation using the package is described
709   in Figure 1. Below we review the main modules used in the package, which is available at GitHub.
710
711   S6.1.1    TASKS
712
713
      This module contains two main parts. The first is the means to load the task definition according to
      task name in a generic format into a designated, easy-to-use object. The class allows easy access
714
      to the entity identifiers (usually gene symbols) and their outcomes. They will usually be a single
715
      columned data frame, but if the task includes multiple genes per instance (for example, gene-to-gene
716   interaction), it will include multiple entities in a column structure. In the multi-label case, the output
717   is also a multi-columned data frame. The second part is a pipeline class that manages the process
718   from a task name to description (in the case of text-based models) to encoding, training a simple
719   prediction model in a cross-validation fashion, and creating a report. Adding additional tasks is
720   designed to be simple, all it requires is saving the task descriptions in a specific format.
721
722   S6.1.2    D ESCRIPTOR
723
724
      The module manages the transition from an entity identifier into a text description. For gene
      symbols, we retrieve the description fields from NCBI using the MyGene.Info services and construct
725
      a description sentence. We allow predefined descriptions by creating a descriptor that loads the
726
      descriptions from a CSV file. This feature enabled us to download the disease description from
727   open targets without needing to integrate with their service and facilitate easy introduction of new
728   descriptions. We are also able to construct multi identifier types descriptors, thus enabling the creation
729   of a descriptor that can describe tasks with different identifiers, such as in gene-disease association.
730
731   S6.1.3    E NCODER
732
733   This module manages the encoding of either the entity identifier or its textual summary. We enable
734   encoding using any HuggingFace sentence transformer supporting module. In addition, we enable
735
      encoding using a pre-computed encoder by loading the encodings from a precomputed CSV file.
      This enables us to pre-compute the encoding from scRNA-based models. In addition, we enable
736
      the creation of a multi-entity type encoder that enables encoding each type of entity differently. For
737
      example, in the case of Gene-Disease association, we can encode the genes using pre-computed
738   encoding and the disease using a sentence transform encoder.
739
740
      S6.1.4    BASE MODELS
741
742   The package supports any scikit-learn model. For the manuscript, we explored linear and logistic
743   regression with the default scikit-learn parameters, and an MLP with three hidden layers of size 100
744   and 500 max iterations.
745
746   S6.1.5    S CRIPTS AND N OTEBOOKS
747
      To efficiently create benchmarks the package includes a command line interface. Enabling benchmark-
748
      ing multiple models (described in YAML format) on multiple tasks (supplied in the command line or
749   in YAML) and output a single report in CSV format. An additional script is supplied that can extract
750   the embedding of the given identifiers list. The package also includes a notebook demonstrating how
751   the package can be used and how to create figures, as displayed in this manuscript.
752
