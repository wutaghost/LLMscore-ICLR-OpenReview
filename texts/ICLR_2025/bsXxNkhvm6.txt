009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Machine learning has been widely applied to stock movement prediction. How-
012            ever, research in this field is often hindered by the lack of high-quality benchmark
013            datasets and comprehensive evaluation methods. To address these challenges, we
014            introduce BenchStock, a benchmark that includes standardized datasets from the
015            two largest stock markets (the U.S. and China) along with an evaluation method
016            designed to facilitate a thorough examination of machine learning stock predic-
017            tion methods. This benchmark covers a range of models, from traditional machine
018
               learning techniques to the latest deep learning approaches. Using BenchStock, we
               conducted large-scale experiments predicting individual stock returns over three
019
               decades in both markets to assess both short-term and long-term performance.
020
               To evaluate the impact of these predictions in actual market conditions, we con-
021            structed a portfolio based on the predictions and used a backtesting program to
022            simulate its performance. The experiments revealed several key findings that have
023            not been reported: 1) Most methods outperformed the S&P 500 in the U.S. market
024            but experienced significant losses in the Chinese market. 2) Prediction accuracy
025            of a method was not correlated with its portfolio return. 3) Advanced deep learn-
026            ing methods did not outperform traditional approaches. 4) The performance of
027            the models was highly dependent on the testing period. These findings highlight
028            the complexity of stock prediction and call for more in-depth machine learning
029
               research in this field.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
      Among various time-series forecasting tasks, stock return prediction is a trendy research topic due to
034
      its substantial real-world implications. With the success of machine learning in domains such as nat-
035
      uaral language processing (NLP) and computer vision (CV), researchers have become increasingly
036   interested in applying these techniques to stock prediction. Methods based on most popular machine
037   learning networks, including Recurrent Neural networks (RNNs) (Deng et al., 2009; Du et al., 2021),
038   Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) (Sawhney et al., 2021) and Transformers (Wang et al., 2022), have
039   been used to improve the accuracy on stock return prediction. While all these methods are claimed
040   to achieve good results, it is hard to identify real progress from them due to following challenges.
041
      The first challenge arises from diverse datasets used in different studies, making it difficult to com-
042   pare results between them. In contrast to computer vision field, in which standardized benchmarks
043   such as ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009) and CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky & Hinton, 2009) provide a com-
044   mon ground for method evaluation, various market indices from different countries were tested in
045   stock prediction research and no identical dataset was used for comparison. For instance, DA-RNN
046   (Deng et al., 2009) used NASDAQ 100 from the U.S. market, while FactorVAE (Duan et al., 2022)
047   used China’s A-share market. This lack of benchmark datasets makes it challenging to directly
048   compare the effectiveness of different methods.
049   The second challenge arises from the lack of data standardization in current research. Many stud-
050   ies lack a standardized approach for preparing the data. While model structures and experimental
051   results are often discussed in detail, data preprocessing is usually mentioned only briefly. Given
052   the notoriously low signal-to-noise ratio in financial data, this lack of transparency further under-
053   mines the reliability of the research. Data preprocessing for stock data is particularly complex due
      to events like stock splits, which can significantly impact outcomes. Additionally, researchers with


                                                        1

054
      deep learning expertise but limited financial domain knowledge often rely on free sources for stock
055   data. For instance, several studies (Sawhney et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022; Xia et al., 2024) used
056   U.S. market datasets derived from Google Finance. These datasets not only fail to account for key
057   financial events like stock splits and dividends but also lack the adjustment factors necessary for
058   proper handling. By examining the Google Finance datasets used in these studies, we discovered
059   instances of abnormal price changes exceeding 100% in a single day due to stock splits. Such
060   anomalies can create returns and losses that do not exist in reality and significantly affect the result.
061   Consequently, the absence of a rigorous standardization process raises serious concerns about the
062   validity and reliability of findings in previous studies.
063   The third challenge comes from the absence of a unified evaluation method. The diverse datasets
064   used in existing studies often cover different short periods, leading to inconsistent and potentially
065   misleading results. AdaRNN (Du et al., 2021) analyzed data from 2017 to 2019, while DA-RNN
066   (Deng et al., 2009) was solely tested on 2016. Given the variability in model performance across
067   different periods, short-term analysis can skew the understanding of a model’s effectiveness in stock
068   prediction. This problem is further aggravated by using different evaluation metrics. While some
069
      works used error-based metrics such as MSE (Deng et al., 2009), others employed metrics like
      information coefficient (IC), information ratio of IC (ICIR), RankIC, and RankICIR (Du et al., 2021;
070
      Duan et al., 2022). Although these metrics are related to prediction accuracy, they do not directly
071
      reflect a model’s ability in generating returns. STHAN-SR, ALSP-TF and CI-STHPAN (Sawhney
072   et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022; Xia et al., 2024) are among the few works that evaluated their
073   methods using realized returns and Sharpe ratios of the formed portfolios. However, these studies
074   calculated returns by averaging the returns of the top five stocks without accounting for real-world
075   trading factors such as transaction fees. Implementing such a strategy in a market like China, where
076   the trading cost is 0.4% per trade (0.15% for buy orders and 0.25% for sell orders), implies that a
077   portfolio’s value could decrease by more than 60% in a year due to transaction fees. Consequently,
078   the portfolio must constantly generate annual returns exceeding 60% to avoid incurring a loss, which
079   is an almost impossible target in any stock market. Thus, a systematic and consistent evaluation
080
      method is essential to accurately and pratically gauge the strengths and weaknesses of these methods.
081   In this paper, we introduce a benchmark BenchStock for the stock prediction task in the machine
082   learning domain, aiming to provide a convenient tool for future research. We systematically eval-
083   uated 23 existing methods, including models from traditional machine learning to the most recent
084   advancement in deep learning, using the same datasets and a comprehensive evaluation framework.
085   We created two datasets from reputable sources, CRSP (Center for Research in Securities) and
086
      CSMAR (China Stock Market and Accounting Research Database), representing price and volume
      features of the U.S. and the Chinese stock markets, respectively, with thorough preprocessing. These
087
      methods were evaluated extensively by simulating real-world trading scenarios within a backtesting
088
      program from Microsoft’s Qlib (Yang et al., 2020) over three decades. The performances of these
089   models were quantified with prevalently used metrics from finance industry, including annual re-
090   turn (AR), Sharpe ratio (SR), information ratio (IR) and maximum drawdown (MDD). This study
091   ensures a robust and realistic assessment of each model’s efficacy in stock prediction.
092
      The experiments revealed several noteworthy findings that had not been previously reported. First,
093
      while most methods performed well in the U.S. market, this success did not extend to the Chinese
094
      market. Although nearly all methods outperformed the S&P 500 benchmark in the U.S., most
095   failed to generate positive annual returns in the Chinese market, underscoring the importance of
096   evaluating models across multiple markets. Second, prediction accuracy was not strongly correlated
097   with portfolio returns. The correlation between predicted and actual returns from the methods was
098   too low to reliably indicate portfolio performance. Third, the advanced deep learning methods did
099   not show better performance compared to traditional methods. More recently proposed methods
100   based on graph neural network (GNN) and Variational autoencoder (VAE) did not demonstrate better
101   performance. Finally, method performance was strongly influenced by the testing period. The top-
102   performing methods varied depending on the timeframe used for evaluation, suggesting that previous
103
      studies, which typically test models over just 2-3 years (Deng et al., 2009; Du et al., 2021; Duan
      et al., 2022; Sawhney et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022; Xia et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024), are too limited
104
      to definitively determine a model’s superiority. Future research should aim for more comprehensive
105
      evaluations that assess both long-term and short-term performance.
106

      2     BACKGROUND
122
123   2.1   S TOCK P REDICTION BACKGROUND
124
      Problem Formulation Stock prediction is commonly approached as a regression problem. Let us
125
      denote the set of stocks in the market as S = {s1 , s2 , . . . , sN }, where each stock is associated with
126
      observable features X = {x1 , x2 , . . . , xN } and xi ∈ Rd . The objective is to learn a model f that
127   forecasts future returns Y ∈ RN , as formulated in the equation:
128
129                                                 Y = f (X).                                              (1)
130
131   Related Methods In asset pricing field, research mainly focuses on features X, commonly re-
132   ferred to as factors in finance, in the equation. Linear regression is prevalently used as the default
133   model for testing the effectiveness of these factors. The representation of features xi has evolved
134   across different models during the last few decades, which shifted from several factors in the early
135   works (Sharpe, 1964; Fama & French, 1992) to hundreds of factors in more recent works (Cochrane,
136   2011; Mclean & Pontiff, 2016; Hou et al., 2018; Harvey et al., 2015). However, as more and more
137
      factors were being used, linear method started to struggle in learning patterns from high-dimensional
      data due to issues like collinearity. Luckily, with the emergence of artificial intelligence, researchers
138
      have begun to address these challenges using machine learning methods. A recent work constructed
139
      a dataset with 94 firm-level characteristics plus eight macroeconomic variables and applied multi-
140   ple machine learning models to compare with linear method (Gu et al., 2020). The results showed
141   that machine learning methods with ability to modeling non-linear relationship are generally more
142   accurate.
143
      Different from asset pricing studies (Sharpe, 1964; Fama & French, 1992; 2015), stock forecast
144
      research in machine learning domain emphasizes methodologies rather than the specific features
145
      used in the forecasting equations. Most of these works framed the task as a time-series forecasting
146   problem and restricted their feature sets to trading price and volume. A comprehensive introduction
147   to various machine learning methods for stock prediction is detailed in Sec. 3.3.
148
149
      Related Platforms For strict evaluation of stock prediction methods, a systematic pipeline of
150   data processing, model training and backtesting is required. Despite the limited number of open-
151   sourced platforms dedicated to stock prediction, there are a few noteworthy examples. FinRL-
152   Meta (Liu et al., 2022) has compiled various datasets and established an environment for financial
153   reinforcement learning tasks, setting a benchmark in RL-based methods. FinGPT (Wang et al.,
154   2023) provides a platform for aggregating stock-related news and predicting stock movements using
155   Large Language Models (LLMs).
156   Among various platforms, Qlib (Yang et al., 2020), developed by Microsoft, is an AI-oriented open-
157   source quantitative investment tool that provides comprehensive functionalities for testing stock
158   return prediction algorithms. It includes data processing, machine learning model prediction, and
159   backtesting modules essential for real-world quantitative trading. However, there are several issues
160   with Qlib that prevented us from using the platform, which we discussed in Appendix A.1. Conse-
161   quently, we created our own datasets and framework for prediction methods, and only used Qlib’s
      backtest testing module for evaluation.

183   3.1    DATASETS
184
185   Our benchmark features two price-volume-based datasets, each representing one of the world’s
186   largest stock markets, the United States and China, with daily frequency data. The data were fetched
187   from authoritative data sources CRSP and CSMAR respectively. For each market, the daily open
188   price, high price, low price, close price, trade volume and trade amount were used for prediction.
189   We focused on these two markets due to their significant differences in institutional backgrounds,
190   which highlights the potential limitations of models when applied across different markets. The
191   Chinese financial market is predominantly driven by retail investors, whereas the U.S. market is
192   dominated by institutional investors. The Chinese market is also subject to more stringent trading
193   regulations than the relatively liberal U.S. market. For example, the short trading has been strictly
194   regulated in China for individual stocks, which does not exist in the U.S. market.
195   Unlike existing studies that typically focused on a single market (Gu et al., 2020; Deng et al., 2009;
196   Du et al., 2021; Duan et al., 2022), our model comparison evaluated performance across different
197   markets. Previous works typically divided individual markets into multiple datasets based on stock
198   exchanges (Veličković et al., 2018; Sawhney et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022). Our approach consoli-
199   dates each market into a single dataset for a more comprehensive market overview. The U.S. market
200   dataset includes securities from NYSE, NASDAQ, and AMEX, spanning from 1989 to 2023, while
201
      the China A-share market dataset encompasses stocks from both the Shanghai and Shenzhen Stock
      Exchanges, covering the period from 1990 to 2023. To make fair comparisons with methods based
202
      on graph neural network (GNN) in our benchmark (Sawhney et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022; Xia
203
      et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024), which require consistent stock components throughout the dataset, we
204   have also generated an auxiliary dataset containing only stocks that persisted throughout 2000-2023
205   and maintained a consistent number of trading days in each market. For clarity, we call the datasets
206   covering all stocks “full datasets” (US-Full & CN-Full) and the auxiliary datasets containing con-
207   sistent stocks “consistent datasets” (US-Con & CN-Con). The details of the datasets can be found
208   in Appendix A.2.
209
210   3.2    P REPROCESSING
211
212   The data preprocessing of our benchmark include two main procedures: data cleaning and feature
213   extraction. Specifically, we built a customized pipeline with strict procedures for cleaning data from
214   each source, which includes removing noisy data, handling missing data and backward adjusting.
215   The feature extraction procedure processed price and volume features into the same scale with dif-
      ferencing and normalization. The detail of preprocessing can be found in Appendix A.3.

      3.3   S TOCK P REDICTION M ETHODS
217
218   Our benchmark covers a variety of methods, spanning from traditional machine learning models
219   to the latest deep-learning models in AI fields. For the convenience of discussion, we group these
220   methods into five categories as follows:
221
222
             • Traditional Machine Learning Methods: Linear Regression (LR), Gradient Boost (GBRT),
               Random Forest (RF), and Multilayer Perceptron (MLP) (Gu et al., 2020).
223
224          • RNN-Based Methods: LSTM (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997), DA-RNN (Deng et al.,
225            2009), AdaRNN (Du et al., 2021).
226          • Transformer-Based Methods: Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017), LogSparse (Li et al.,
227            2019), Reformer (Kitaev et al., 2020), Informer (Zhou et al., 2021), Autoformer (Wu et al.,
228            2021), Fedformer (Zhou et al., 2022), Crossformer (Zhang & Yan, 2023).
229          • GNN-based Methods: (GAT) (Veličković et al., 2018), STHAN-SR (Sawhney et al., 2021),
230            ALSP-TF (Wang et al., 2022), CI-STHPAN (Xia et al., 2024), MASTER (Li et al., 2024).
231          • Other Methods: NLinear, DLinear (Zeng et al., 2023), FactorVAE (Duan et al., 2022),
232            Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2024).
233
234   We treat the task as a time-series prediction problem, focusing exclusively on methods that forecast
235
      stock returns using price and volume factors. The information of methods in details are summarized
      in Appendix A.4.
236
237
      Acknowledging the diversity of methods in this domain, we have reproduced models that do not have
238   publicly released codes or are not designed for stock prediction. These reproductions, integrated into
239   our PyTorch framework, were based on descriptions in the original papers. To support the research
240   community, we commit to continuously updating our benchmark with novel, high-quality methods
241   as they emerge. This ongoing effort aims to facilitate easy access to cutting-edge techniques in stock
242   market analysis.
243
244   3.4   E VALUATION M ETHOD
245
246   We evaluated stock prediction results using a backtesting program from Microsoft’s Qlib, which
247   is designed to simulate real-world trading scenarios. Unlike traditional error-based metrics like
248
      MSE, Qlib assesses forecasts by forming portfolios and applying return-related financial metrics
      to gauge performance considering the purpose of model prediction is for portfolio management.
249
      For measuring the performance of the portfolio, we used financial metrics including Annual Return
250
      (AR), Sharpe Ratio (SR), Information Ratio (IR) and Maximum Drawdown (MDD). The explanation
251   of metrics in detail is included in Appendix A.5. Higher values are preferable for all the metrics.
252   For benchmark, we used S&P 500 and SSE Composite (SSEC) in the U.S. and the Chinese market
253   respectively. The reason we used SSEC instead of normally selected CSI 300 is because CSI 300
254   started from 2005 and could not cover the whole sample period of our dataset.
255
      To ensure a more comprehensive evaluation that closely reflects reality, we constructed portfolios
256
      based on daily forecasts and updated using a top-k strategy. We started with an initial capital of 100
257   million, maintaining 50 stocks and replacing k = 10 at the end of each trading day. Besides, we
258   extended the evaluation period from the shorter spans common in prior research to three decades,
259   providing insights into both long-term and short-term model performance. The implementation
260   detail of our evaluation can be found in Appendix A.6.

396   factors, with market volatility being a significant one. While the U.S. market consistently reached
397   new highs, the Chinese market fluctuated severely and remained stagnant for over a decade. Conse-

401

403

404   the necessity of testing models in various markets, as success in one does not guarantee success in
405   another. It also highlights the necessity of testing stock prediction methods in real-world scenarios,
406   as a method that does not account for trading costs may report positive returns while actually result-
407   ing in a loss. As a result, future research in the Chinese market should focus on strategies to reduce
408   trading costs in addition to improving prediction accuracy.

410   The prediction accuracy was not correlated with portfolio returns. One might expect that bet-
411   ter predictions of stock prices would lead to higher returns. To evaluate the accuracy of predictions
412   for stocks selected into portfolios, we measured it using the information coefficient (IC), which
413   ranges from -1 to 1 and represents the correlation between predicted and actual returns. The results,
414
      shown in Figure 2 and Appendix A.9, indicate that prediction accuracy did not strongly correlate
      with the mean return of selected stocks, particularly in the Chinese market. Although methods with
415
      higher prediction accuracy generally yielded higher mean returns, some methods with ICs close to
416
      0 also achieved similar mean returns. Specifically, while methods like GBRT, RF, LSTM, and most
417   Transformer-based models demonstrated better predictive ability, this did not translate to superior
418   returns compared to methods with lower ICs. Surprisingly, the IC in the Chinese market was actually
419   better than in the U.S. market, especially on consistent datasets.
420
      The predictability of stock data is quite low due to its inherently low signal-to-noise ratio. Most
421
      ICs are typically below 0.1, which is insufficient to guarantee higher returns when a method’s IC
422
      is only slightly better than others. With such low predictability, portfolio performance largely de-
423   pended on market conditions. In a stable market like the U.S., methods benefited from continuous
424   market growth. However, in the more volatile Chinese market—where 12 of the past 23 years ex-
425   perienced annual losses—methods suffered from market fluctuations. This explains why methods
426   performed worse in the Chinese market despite demonstrating better prediction accuracy. Conse-
427   quently, improvements in current model prediction accuracy were not significant enough to ensure
428   better portfolio returns.

513   on the most recent 4 years since COVID-19, Fedformer even generated negative return and only
514   Crossformer kept a relatively high annual return around 30% in the U.S. market. Similarly, in the

516   periods. Such differences are reflection of the market performance and macro economic condition,
517   as most economies struggled during the COVID-19 era. After all, evaluation in both long-term
518   and short-term can better examine a model’s stability under different market conditions. Previously
519   methods (Deng et al., 2009; Du et al., 2021; Duan et al., 2022; Veličković et al., 2018; Sawhney et al.,
520   2021; Wang et al., 2022; Li et al., 2024) tested only for 2-3 years could lead to biased conclusions.
521
      As a result, we believe the future research should all test both long-term and short-term performances
      of methods for a more comprehensive evaluation.

524   5    D ISCUSSION
525
526   In this paper, we introduce a benchmark for evaluating machine learning methods in forecasting
527   stock movements. We collected and strictly preprocessed two datasets from the U.S. and Chinese
528   stock markets, implemented 23 methods, and evaluated them through a unified backtesting program.
529   The evaluation spanned three decades, offering insights into both long-term and short-term perfor-
530
      mance for comprehensive assessments. We analyzed the results and revealed findings that could
      hopefully provide insights for future stock prediction research.
531
532   Despite evaluating stock prediction methods in a comprehensive way, our study has several limita-
533   tions. First, the reproduction of the benchmark methods using PyTorch was based on our understand-
534   ing of the original works. There may be differences in implementation details that led to varying
535   results. Second, this work purely focused on prediction methods using basic price and volume data.
536
      In the future, we plan to address these issues by improving datasets with additional features, and po-
      tentially incorporating Large Language Models (LLMs) for more accurate and reliable stock market
537
      forecasting.

651   A.1   P ROBLEM WITH Q LIB
652
653   Despite perfectly fitting the functional requirements for benchmarking stock return prediction meth-
654   ods, there are several issues within the platform. First, Qlib’s built-in data collection script down-
655
      loads data from Yahoo Finance, whose records exclude delisted stocks’ information. This leads to a
      problem known as survivorship bias (Brown et al., 1992) in finance, which means that performance
656
      would become better if an truncated stock pool is used. Therefore, we replaced Qlib’s data source to
657
      better align with actual market scenarios. Additionally, Qlib’s normal training methods are limited
658   to the traditional machine learning approach, where the training, validation, and test sets are fixed.
659   This approach is not ideal for financial data due to the frequent changes in stock market’s statistical
660   distribution. Using a model trained on stock data from 2000 to 2008 to predict stock returns from
661   2010 to 2020 is evidently not an reasonable strategy. Although Qlib has added a rolling training
662   mode that allows users to train with changing datasets, this function is still relatively restricted. The
663   initial training date in rolling mode is fixed, which means the training set grows larger as it pro-
664   gresses to test more recent data. This becomes an issue when testing over long-term data, as a test
665   set with 30 years of data means the training set would eventually include all 30 years of data. Thirty
666
      years of training data in DataFrame format would take over 300 gigabytes of memory and include
      many stocks that no longer exist in the market. The model would then likely fit delisted stocks and
667
      overfit historical data. In this work, we chose to use a rolling training mode that only included the
668
      most recent five years of data in our training and validation sets and discarded outdated data. With
669   this consideration, we decided to create our own datasets and prediction methods under this training
670   framework and incorporate only Qlib’s backtesting module for evaluation.

741   A.3     DATA P REPROCESSING
742
743   A.3.1    DATA C LEANING
744
745   Data cleaning is especially important for stock data since different data sources have various formats
746   and include lots of missing data. Even the most authoritative data sources have errors, so we built
747
      a customized pipeline with strict procedures for handling data from each source. First, we removed
      data that could bring extra noise in training, including duplicated data, data on IPO date of each stock
748
      and data of stocks being suspended. This assures all samples in dataset are valid trading records.
749
      Second, missing data were handled differently in various cases. Data with too little information
750   were removed. For instance, if one sample had no volume-related data or missed all price-related
751   data, we filtered them to avoid creating extremely inaccurate information. For samples with few
752   price features missed, we filled them with other price features from the same sample. Third, the
753   whole dataset was backward adjusted to eliminate effects from events like stock split and dividends.
754   This procedure is highly important as any unprocessed stock split would fully change the returns
755   from the stock. In the end, features from different data sources are converted into same format for
      training under the unified framework.

756
      A.3.2    F EATURE E XTRACTION
757
758   For features including open, high, low, and close prices, trade volume, and trade amount, two steps
759   were implemented to process them into same scale. First, raw price and volume data were trans-
760   formed into percentage change. Specifically, high, low, close prices’ were processed into percentage
761   change from open price, and open price was processed into difference from previous day’s close
762
      price. For trade volume and amount, the first difference of the time-series were calculated. Close
      price’s first difference was also included in addition to difference from open price. After the first
763
      step, all features were normalized with two steps: centering and scaling. During centering step, the
764
      median was subtracted from each feature, shifting the median of the feature to zero. This step en-
765   sures that the central tendency of the feature is not influenced by outliers. Following centering, the
766   feature values were scaled by dividing the interquartile range (IQR), which is the difference between
767   the 75th percentile and the 25th percentile of the feature. This scaling step normalizes the spread
768   of the feature values while being robust to outliers, as the IQR is a measure of statistical dispersion
769   that is not affected by extreme values. After all the procedures, the features were processed into a
770   uniform scale.

772   A.4     M ETHODS
773
774   The methods included in BenchStock can be summarized as the following types:
775
776
      I. Traditional Machine Learning Methods Traditional machine learning methods are those pre-
      viously used in empirical asset pricing fields, including Linear Regression (LR), Gradient Boost
777
      (GBRT), Random Forest (RF), and Multilayer Perceptron (MLP) (Gu et al., 2020). For decades,
778
      linear regression has been the default method in traditional asset pricing field. Researchers used
779   linear regression to explore the effectiveness of predictors in explaining stock returns. However,
780   as more and more predictors being tested, it became too demanding for linear regression to handle
781   such high-dimensional data. The inability to simulate non-linear relationships and address collinear-
782   ity prevents it from being the ideal method for asset pricing in the big data era. As a result, machine
783   learning methods are applied to avoid the restrictions of linear models. Experiments showed that
784   GBRT, RF and MLP demonstrated better performances compared to linear regression when using
785   94 firm-level predictors (Gu et al., 2020). We used these traditional methods as the baselines and
786   examined whether the recent advanced AI models would outperform them.
787
788
      II. RNN-Based Models Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) is renowned for its ability to handle
      sequential data, which makes it an ideal candidate for predicting stock returns. We included LSTM
789
      (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997), the most widely applied variation of the vanilla RNN method,
790
      as our baseline. In addition, we adopted several variation of RNN methods designed for stock return
791   prediction. A typical method is DA-RNN (Deng et al., 2009), which adds attention mechanism to
792   RNN network. Another RNN-based method is AdaRNN (Du et al., 2021), which incorporates adap-
793   tive learning into stock prediction. Due to the time-varying conditional distribution of stock data,
794   the distribution of test data could be significantly different from the training dataset. To mitigate the
795   overfitting issue caused by a significant distribution shift between training and testing data, AdaRNN
796   applies techniques that divide training sets into different groups, enabling better generalization on
797   unseen data.
798
799   III. Transformer-Based Methods Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017) has revolutionized machine
800   learning, significantly impacting both natural language processing and computer vision. This promi-
801
      nence extends to long-term time-series forecasting, with transformer-based methods becoming in-
      creasingly prevalent. Since stock return prediction is widely regarded as a time-series forecasting
802
      problem, we included a series of transformer-based models to test the most popular methods’ effects
803
      over this task. The vanilla Transformer (Vaswani et al., 2017) was included as a baseline. Pytorch
804   implementation of LogSparse (Li et al., 2019), Reformer (Kitaev et al., 2020), and Informer (Zhou
805   et al., 2021), which improve structure of the self-attention mechanism from vanilla transformer to
806   enhance effciency and accruacy, were adopted for comparison. These methods apply sparse version
807   of self-attention mechanism to improve the ability to discover long-range dependencies. Addition-
808   ally, we explored innovative approaches such as Autoformer (Wu et al., 2021), which incorporates
809   traditional time-series seasonal-trend decomposition techniques and replaced self-attention with au-
      tocorrelation to analyze sequence lags. Fedformer (Zhou et al., 2022), which adopts Autoformer’s


                                                         15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      decomposition strategy and combines Fourier analysis, was also integrated. Finally, Crossformer
811   (Zhang & Yan, 2023) was incorporated for its novel cross-dimensional feature attention, illuminat-
812   ing previously unexplored interdependencies.
813
814   IV. GNN-Based Methods Graph neural network (GNN) offers a novel approach to stock market
815   analysis by incorporating information from other stocks into individual stocks based on relationships
816   between them. These methods typically construct a market graph, identifying related stocks as
817   neighbors and leveraging their combined features for enhanced stock return predictions. We adopted
818   the Graph Attention Networks (GAT) (Veličković et al., 2018) as the foundational model for this type
819   of approach. We also included recent graph-based methods: STHAN-SR (Sawhney et al., 2021) and
820   ALSP-TF (Wang et al., 2022). STHAN-SR uses text data and industry classifications to map the
821
      stock market graph, implementing an LSTM + Attention model for prediction. Meanwhile, ALSP-
      TF introduces a data-driven approach, using Dynamic Time Warping (DTW) (Jeong et al., 2011) to
822
      establish stock relationships based solely on their features, overcoming data availability limitations
823
      in traditional methods. CI-STHPAN (Xia et al., 2024) combines both STHAN-SR and ALSP-TF’s
824   approaches of constructing graph and adds patching technique in attention mechanism. MASTER
825   (Li et al., 2024) further explores the data-driven approach by using an attention mechanism to form
826   a graph and incorporate market information for prediction.
827
828   IV. Other Methods Alongside the four major types of methods mentioned above, we included
829   other methods that do not fit into these categories but offer intriguing approaches to stock prediction.
830   We included two variations of linear method (Zeng et al., 2023) that challenged the effectiveness
831   of transformers. The study used two simple linear methods DLinear and NLinear and tests over
832   time-series forecast datasets, which achieve better outcome compared to previous transformer-based
833   methods. This led to the conclusion that transformers are not effective for time series forecasting.
834
      As a result, we included these two methods to check the validity of such claim under stock return
      prediction task. FactorVAE (Duan et al., 2022) applies VAE to identify effective latent factors from
835
      highly noised features extracted by GRU (Chung et al., 2014) network, adding a new approach
836
      in learn effective factors in stock data. Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2024) is the latest method that uses
837   structured state space models (SSMs) to improve Transformers’ computational efficiency. It avoids
838   using attention mechanism but manages to perform even better than Transformer-based state-of-art
839   methods in multiple tasks. We included Mamba for testing the effects of latest methods on stock
840   data.

      A.5   E VALUATION M ETRICS
919
920   We evaluated stock prediction results using a backtesting program from Microsoft’s Qlib, which
921   is designed to simulate real-world trading scenarios. Unlike traditional error-based metrics like
922   MSE, Qlib assesses forecasts by forming portfolios and applying return-related financial metrics to
923   gauge performance considering the purpose of model prediction is for portfolio management. The
924   following finance metrics are used for measuring portfolio performance:
925
             • Annual Return (AR): This is the geometric average of the annual returns realized from the
926

       A.6   E VALUATION M ETHOD
973
974    To ensure a more comprehensive evaluation that closely reflects reality, we tested the methods as fol-
975    lows. First, portfolios were constructed based on daily forecasts and updated using a top-k strategy.
976    We started with an initial capital of 100 million, maintaining 50 stocks and replacing k = 10 at the
977    end of each trading day. This approach differs from previous studies, which typically involved buy-
978    ing and selling 5 stocks daily and calculating the average return from those stocks (Sawhney et al.,
979    2021; Wang et al., 2022). Our strategy accounted for trading costs and stability, as frequent trading
980
       can lead to high commission fees and increased volatility. Second, Qlib considered stock suspen-
       sions based on our dataset and adhered to regulations specific to each market to enhance realism.
981
       Third, we extended the evaluation period from the shorter spans common in prior research to three
982
       decades, providing insights into both long-term and short-term model performance. It is important
983    to note that stock prediction results can be more random compared to tasks with higher signal-to-
984    noise ratios; thus, short-term performance does not guarantee long-term effectiveness. Therefore,
985    we evaluated both long-term and short-term performance comprehensively. For the U.S. market, we
986    analyzed data from 1994 to 2023, while for the Chinese market, the analysis covered 1996 to 2023,
987    given that the market primarily began in 1991. For consistent datasets in both markets, methods
988    were assessed from 2005 to 2023.
