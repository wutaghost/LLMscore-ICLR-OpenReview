011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013             Multimodal large language models are an exciting emerging class of language mod-
014             els (LMs) that have merged classic LM capabilities with those of image processing
015             systems. However, the ways that these capabilities combine are not always intuitive
016
                and warrant direct investigation. One understudied capability in MLLMs is visual
                spatial planning—the ability to comprehend the spatial arrangements of objects
017
                and devise action plans to achieve desired outcomes in visual scenes. In our study,
018
                we introduce VSP, a benchmark that 1) evaluates the spatial planning capability
019             in these models in general, and 2) breaks down the visual planning task into finer-
020             grained sub-tasks, including perception and reasoning, and measure the capabilities
021             of models in these sub-tasks. Our evaluation shows that both open-source and
022             private MLLMs fail to generate effective plans for even simple spatial planning
023             tasks. Evaluations on the fine-grained analytical tasks further reveal fundamental
024             deficiencies in the models’ visual perception and bottlenecks in reasoning abilities,
025             explaining their worse performance in the general spatial planning tasks. Our work
026             illuminates future directions for improving MLLMs’ abilities in spatial planning.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   The rapid advancement of large language models has driven considerable growth in their capabilities
032   to produce fluent text in many domains, generating outputs exhibiting potential “reasoning” and
033
      “understanding” abilities. Touvron et al. (2023); Bi et al. (2024); Jiang et al. (2024); Brown et al. (2020).
      Recently, multimodal large language models (MLLMs) have advanced on LLMs through additional
034
      training on native image inputs, to achieve impressive performance generating text describing and
035
      relating to input images Achiam et al. (2023); Liu et al. (2024); Team et al. (2023); Awadalla et al.
036   (2023); Alayrac et al. (2022), with applications in image captioning, visual question answering,
037   visual reasoning, and others Ying et al. (2024); Yang et al. (2024); Shao et al. (2023); Zheng et al.
038   (2023). The swift evolution of MLLMs has enabled them to tackle increasingly sophisticated tasks
039   that require multiple emerging abilities in complex scenarios. However, as model capabilities and
040   deployment needs advance, the challenges in usefully evaluating them grow in kind.
041
      Planning is a fundamental capability in intelligent systems that is particularly contested in LMs
042   Valmeekam et al. (2023), and is understudied in MLLMs. Visual spatial planning refers to the task of
043   comprehending the spatial arrangement of objects in a scene and designing action plans to achieve a
044   desired outcome. For example, the classical maze problem can be considered a visual planning task,
045   where an agent is given an input image describing the maze environment and is asked to produce
046   a viable path to navigate the player from the starting position to the goal. This task requires two
047   capabilities: image perception, which enables the agent to understand the objects, environment and
048   spatial relations present in the image, and reasoning, which enables the agent to perform strategic
049   decision-making.
050   Visual spatial planning is an important capability in many potential applications for MLLMs, such as
051   navigating in complex environments with autonomous driving Tian et al. (2024); Ma et al. (2023) or
052   manipulating objects with robotic hands Chang et al. (2023); Hu et al. (2023). However, although
053   there have been increasingly more benchmarks to evaluate the vision processing capabilities of
      MLLMs, few current benchmarks systematically evaluate their capability of performing visual spatial

      2.2   S PATIAL AND VISUAL PLANNING IN LM S
134
135   Many general planning tasks in LMs involve understanding visual environments and comprehending
136
      spatial information. In robotics and embodied agent studies, LMs play a crucial role in grounding
      visual entities with references in open-domain instructions and formulating plans based on spatial
137
      constraints. Consequently, they are increasingly used in physically grounded scenarios such as object
138
      rearrangement Chang et al. (2023); Hu et al. (2023), cooking Joublin et al. (2023); Sakib and Sun
139   (2023), and navigation Hazra et al. (2024); Ahn et al. (2022). LMs are also used in AIGC to propose
140   spatial arrangements of entities following instructions Feng et al. (2024). While realistic planning
141   tasks align with real needs, their complexity and expansive action spaces limit the analysis of LMs’
142   detailed planning capabilities. Therefore, research also focuses on LMs’ planning in simulated
143   environments and games. For example, mystery blocksworld is a dynamically generated set of
144   blocksworld tasks to test generalization in LMs Valmeekam et al. (2023). Additionally, many text
145   games have been introduced to test LMs’ abilities in spatial understanding and imagination Shridhar
146   et al. (2020); Wu et al. (2023); Yang et al. (2023); Aghzal et al. (2023). However, most of these studies
147
      transform visual information into text inputs, thus not directly measuring LMs’ visual abilities.

      3.1                 OVERVIEW OF THE B ENCHMARK
180
181   In this benchmark, our objectives are two-fold: ∂ quantify the visual spatial planning capabilities of
182   current MLLMs; and ∑ uncover current capability bottlenecks that limit the effectiveness of MLLMs
183   in visual spatial planning tasks. The first objective requires broader task designs. Specifically, the
184   tasks should range from classical planning tasks Brockman et al. (2016); Valmeekam et al. (2022) to
185   ones in those more dynamic and realistic environments. On top of that, the second objective requires
186   deeper task designs. In particular, performing spatial planning in visual environments requires a
187   series of cohesive steps. For example, to generate an accurate path to navigate a player to a goal, an
188
      agent needs to be able to correctly view and understand the visual map, reason to find which actions
      are safe or dangerous, and come up with a detailed plan to achieve the goal. Each of these steps could
189
      be challenging for a developing MLLM, and understanding which of these subtasks challenge them
190
      most will drive future improvement.
191
192   To this end, we propose the Visual Spatial Planning (VSP) benchmark, with the objective of measuring
193   and diagnosing the capabilities of MLLMs in producing accurate spatial plans in visual environments.
194
      The VSP benchmark consists of four scenarios: ∂ the simulated Maze Navigation scenario, whose
      main task is to move a game character through a maze; ∑ the photo-realistic Blocks World scenario,
195
      whose main task is to move blocks from a starting configuration to a goal configuration; ∏ the
196
      dynamic Collision scenario, whose main task is to determine if there is a danger of collision between
197   two objects in the environment, and π the realistic Google Map scenario, whose main task is to find
198   a path in real streets of New York City. In addition to the main task, VSP introduces four sub-tasks in
199   first two scenarios that focus on the individual capabilities needed for the main task:
200
      • T1. Single Object Perception – Determine the characteristics of a single object;
201
202   • T2. Spatial Relation Perception – Determine the relative positions of two objects;
203   • T3. Environment Perception – Find textual descriptions that describe the visual environment;
204   • T4. Spatial Reasoning – Determine the consequence of a series of actions or moves.
205
      The sub-task details are designed specific to both scenarios. Furthermore, to demonstrate the model’s
206
      performance under different levels of environmental complexity, we establish progressive difficulty
207   settings for various tasks, which are measured by parameters such as map size, minimum required
208   number of actions, etc. The detailed task statistics are provided in appendix A. In what follows, we
209   introduce each scenario in detail, as well as the data curation and the task creation processes.
210

252   3.3        T HE B LOCKS W ORLD S CENARIO
253
254   The Blocks World is a widely-adopted planning problem Valmeekam et al. (2022); Hao et al. (2023);
255   Gokhale et al. (2019). As depicted in Figure 3 left, in this scenario, the agent is given images
256   containing sets of blocks in unique colors. These blocks are stacked vertically, forming multiple
257   stacks on the table. The agent is asked to turn the blocks from initial state to target state through
258   a series of moving actions. For each action, the agent can only move the top block of any stack,
259   providing it is moved to either the table or the top of another stack.
260   Similarly, the main spatial planning task and the four sub-tasks are defined as follows:
261
262
      • Main Task (Spatial Planning) – Form a moving plan to achieve the target state of block arrangement;
263
      • T1 (Single Object Perception) – Determine the color of the block at a specific position;
264
      • T2 (Spatial Relation Perception) – Determine the spatial relation between two blocks;
265
      • T3 (Environment Perception) – Find the text representation that fits the visual environment;
266
      • T4 (Spatial Reasoning) – Determine the consequence of a given moving plan.
267   An example of input image and questions is demonstrated in Figure 3. Similar to the Maze Navigation
268   scenario, each task is equipped with progressive adjusted difficulty. Specifically, in Main Task and
269   T4, the difficulties are measured by the number of actions involved, ranging from 1 to 7, which
      quantifies the complexity of the action plan. On the other hand, for tasks T1-T3, which focus on

275   3.4   T HE C OLLISION S CENARIO AND G OOGLE M AP S CENARIO
276
      On top of the previous two scenarios, we further experiment in a more dynamic (the collision
277
      scenario) and realistic (the Google map scenario) settings to explore the capabilities of models in
278
      more challenging cases. The input of these two scenarios are shown in the right panels of Figure 1.

281   • Collision Scenario In this scenario, the input map is similar to Maze Navigation scenario. However,
282
      in this case, the player is moving in an environment where a car is also present and moving. Given
      the moving information (speed, direction) of the player and the car, the model needs to determine the
283
      time the player and the car reaches the goal, and then determine if there is a collision danger here.
284
285   • Google Map Scenario In this scenario, the input image is a real Google map depicting the streets
286   and avenues in New York City. The starting location and the goal are randomly chosen crossroads
287   in the map and are marked on the image. The goal of the model is to find a path from the starting
288
      location to the goal. The model needs to output a path described by directions (north, east, west,
      south) and the number of blocks to traverse (e.g., head north for 2 blocks, then head east for 3 blocks).

291   3.5   B ENCHMARK C REATION P ROCESS
292
      In Figure 4, we demonstrate the general process for benchmark creation. We use the Blocks World
293
      and Maze Navigation scenarios as examples.
294
295   First, in the left panel of Figure 4, we prepare the input images used for each task and scenario. In
296   the Maze Navigation scenario, we generate input maps using the OpenAI Gym package Brockman
297   et al. (2016), with modifications to ensure that the positions of the player, the goal, and the holes are
298
      all randomly generated. In the Blocks World scenario, we sample pairs of images from the BIRD
      dataset Gokhale et al. (2019), ensuring there is at least one viable plan to move the blocks from the
299
      initial state to the target state. The images are prepared conditional on different levels of difficulty.
300
301   Second, as shown in the center of Figure 4, we formulate input prompts for each task. The prompt
302   consists of interleaved text and images to provide sufficient information. For example, for Maze
303   Navigation, we include images to show the appearance of elements in the map and provide example
304
      maps to better illustrate how the models should interpret the map. We invite native speakers to refine
      the prompts so that they accurately describe the task requirements. The prompts are in Appendix A.
305
306   Finally, in the right panel of Figure 4, we evaluate the performance of MLLMs under each task. It
307   is worth noting that the answer for each task is often not unique. For example, in the Blocks World
308   scenario, there can be many ways to move the blocks to reach the target state. As such, we develop
309   scripts to automatically evaluate the answers for each task.
310   In addition to the steps above, some tasks require extra steps to construct meaningful questions,
311   candidates, and answers. For example, in T4 of the Blocks World scenario, the input actions must
312   cover various valid/invalid movements. The detailed steps we followed to create each task set are
313   provided in Appendix A. We release all images, texts, and scripts to facilitate replication and scaling.

      MLLMs face significant difficulties with spatial planning in dynamic and realistic environments.
401
      Based on the experimental results, most models perform worse when tested in dynamic (Collision
402
      scenario) and realistic (Google Map scenario) settings. We identify two major reasons for this:
403   First, the tasks in the Collision scenario are complex and typically require multiple capabilities. For
404   example, to assess collision danger, the model must locate both the player and the car on the map,
405   calculate the time needed for the player to reach the goal, and determine if the car will hit the player
406   during that time. This poses significant challenges for the models. Second, the inputs in the Google
407   Map scenario are intricate and contain a series of irrelevant symbols, making it difficult for the model
408   to accurately interpret the map. The models’ performance in these environments suggests that current
409   models are not yet equipped to handle spatial reasoning in such complex environments. Consequently,
410   we focus on the Maze Navigation and Blocks World scenarios in the following experiments to diagnose
411
      the models’ hidden weaknesses in VSP tasks.
412   Quick performance decay as difficulty increases. We observe a significant drop in the success
413   rates of MLLMs as task difficulty escalates. For example, GPT-Vision may achieve a success rate of
414   over 50% on 3x3 size maps, but this plummets to just 10% on 8x8 maps. Analyzing the impact of
415   increased difficulty, we identify two major challenges for the models: First, increasing size of the
416   map in Maze Navigation scenario could make it difficult for the model to accurately perceive the
417
      positions of elements within the map. Second, the increase in both map size and the number of steps
      required for moving blocks heightens the challenge for the model to reason deeply through the entire
418
      path and devise a complete, viable solution. In the following experiments, we focus on these two
419
      factors and provide in-depth analysis with subsequent tasks.
420
421   Challenges in open-source models. Finally, we note that open-source models often face challenges
422   when evaluating on these tasks. We identify two main factors. ¨ Context length: Open-source
423
      models typically have significantly shorter context windows compared to private models. Besides,
      image embeddings can occupy many tokens. Thus, these models may not have enough capacity to
424
      understand the complete inputs. For example, LLAVA - V 1.6- VICUNA -7 B is trained with a maximum
425
      context window of 2048 tokens, while each image consumes 576 tokens. Consequently, when fed
426   with multiple images and relatively long texts in our tasks, the total token length may surpass training,
427   resulting in poor performance. ≠ Multiple image input: Our tasks require the model to understand
428   multiple images interleaved with text inputs, whereas many open-source models are only trained
429   with single-image inputs, with the image positioned at the start of the input. To further explore
430   their potential in our tasks, we assess their performance after training on our inputs in Section 4.6.
431   Meanwhile, we suggest that future open-source models could consider increasing their context length
      and reducing restrictions on input formats to address complex and realistic tasks effectively.

      4.4   T HE E FFECTS OF V ISUAL I NPUT P ERCEPTION AND R EASONING
466
467   Previous analysis shows that even current state-of-the-art models have clear deficiencies in various
468   aspects of visual spatial planning. In this study, we focus on disentangling the effects of perception
469   and reasoning by exploring the performance gain assuming the model had perfect perception.
470
      The key strategy here is to create a scenario where the model has acquired all necessary information
471
      that would typically be obtained through visual perception. To this end, for every input image, we
472   produce the corresponding textual inputs and replace those images, as shown in Figure 5. For the
473   Maze Navigation scenario, we use either pure text descriptions or tables to depict the image. For
474   the Blocks World scenario, we use pure text descriptions. We do not use tables for the Blocks World
475   scenario because the number of blocks in each horizontal stack is usually unequal, making it difficult
476   to form a complete table. Appendix B includes complete examples with pure text or table input.
477
      The results are shown in Figure 6. We observe a clear performance improvement when using textual
478   input across every task. This suggests image perception presents significant challenges for MLLMs,
479   and poor perception ability is a key factor in the inferior performance observed in previous tasks.
480   Meanwhile, we observe that even with textual input, Gemini still cannot achieve decent performance
481   on tasks that require reasoning. This indicates deficiencies in its reasoning capabilities as well.

      5     C ONCLUSION
526
527   We present VSP, a benchmark measuring and diagnosing the visual spatial planning capabilities
528
      in MLLMs. VSP quantifies the model’s performance through a series of carefully designed tasks,
      with main tasks focusing on general spatial planning abilities and sub-tasks focusing on individual
529
      capabilities needed for the main task. Experiments show that both private and open-source models
530
      fail to generate effective plans for even simple spatial planning tasks, and further analyses expose
531   their bottlenecks in spatial perception and reasoning abilities. Our work illuminates future directions
532   for improving MLLMs’ abilities in spatial planning.
