009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent research suggests that the failure of Vision-Language Models (VLMs) in
012            visual reasoning could be attributed to the CLIP image encoder ambiguously en-
013            coding distinct images into embeddings with high cosine similarity, namely er-
014            roneous agreements. In this paper, we show that they are not the sole issue,
015            as multimodal large language models (MLLMs) may extract distinct information
016            even from image embeddings with high cosine similarities. On Subset A of the
017            What’sUp benchmark, where the Left/Right image pairs are embedded by CLIP
018            with average cosine similarity greater than 0.99, CLIP’s performance is near ran-
019
               dom guess. In contrast, LLaVA-1.5-7B, which uses the same image encoder as
               CLIP, achieves nearly 100% accuracy. This discrepancy is also observed between
020
               LLaVA-1.5-7B and CLIP-like models on similar benchmarks. To investigate this
021
               performance gap, we conduct controlled experiments to test the effect of varying
022            evaluation methods, training data, and language processing choices. We find that
023            the CLIP image embeddings contain more extractable information than previously
024            suggested, but it is likely obscured by the inadequate vision-language alignment
025            of the CLIP’s paradigm. Motivated by this observation, we reconsider the LLaVA-
026            1.5 model on the MMVP benchmark, for which prior work showed that it could
027            not distinguish image pairs with high cosine similarity. We observe a performance
028            gain brought about by an alternative decoding algorithm, which attends more to
029            visual input. Further, we show that the accuracy significantly increases if the
030
               model can take both images as input to emphasize their nuanced differences. Both
               findings indicate that LLaVA-1.5 did not utilize extracted visual information suffi-
031
               ciently. In conclusion, our findings suggest that while improving image encoders
032
               could benefit VLMs, there is room to enhance the models with a fixed image en-
033            coder through better strategies for extracting and utilizing visual information.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038
039
      Despite the rapid development and success of Vision-Language Models (VLMs), recent work
      pointed out that state-of-the-art VLMs (Radford et al., 2021; Zhai et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024;
040
      Google, 2023a;b; OpenAI, 2023) still struggle with some simple visual reasoning tasks (Li et al.,
041
      2023d; Liu et al., 2023b; Tong et al., 2024c; Rahmanzadehgervi et al., 2024), where they were asked
042   to answer questions about the images, such as recognizing shapes and describing the object relation-
043   ships. These are basic tasks that VLMs should be able to solve before we deploy them to real-world
044   scenarios like home robots responding to spoken or written commands.
045
      Recent work argued that the pretrained CLIP image encoder (Radford et al., 2021), which serves as
046
      the “eyes” of many VLMs, could be the cause and cure for such visual shortcomings (Tong et al.,
047
      2024c). In these VLMs, any input image is first encoded by the CLIP image encoder and then
048   used to calculate image-text similarity or as the input for a generative language model. Therefore,
049   any deficiency of the CLIP image encoder propagates into the VLMs. The deficiency found by the
050   authors is named erroneous agreements: Visually different images could be ambiguously encoded
051   with high cosine similarity in the embedding space. They claimed this suggested information loss
052   and caused VLMs’ failure in relevant visual reasoning tasks, such as the MMVP benchmark (Tong
053   et al., 2024c). This benchmark consists of selected, semantically distinct image pairs erroneously
      agreeing in the CLIP image embedding space, and CLIP-based VLMs failed to answer questions

      regarding the visual semantic difference better than random chance. This criterion is adopted in
055   Taghipour et al. (2024) and similarly used in Tong et al. (2024b).
056
057   In this work, we provide evidence that VLMs face challenges beyond erroneous agreements. The
058
      query-relevant visual information might still be present in the image embeddings despite the high
      cosine similarity, but a better strategy is required to pull it out. For instance, in the What’sUp
059
      benchmark (Kamath et al., 2023a) with paired, tightly controlled image pairs for evaluating VLM’s
060
      spatial reasoning ability, the average cosine similarity of image pairs on three out of four subsets
061   is greater than 0.95 in CLIP image embedding space, reaching the similarity threshold in Tong
062   et al. (2024c). While CLIP’s accuracy in distinguishing these images is nearly random (about 50%),
063   LLaVA-1.5-7B (Liu et al., 2024) with the pretrained, frozen image encoder of CLIP-ViT-L/14-336px
064   still achieves beyond 80% in binary classification accuracies on all four subsets in What’sUp. Sim-
065   ilarly, on the COCO-spatial and GQA-spatial benchmark used in Kamath et al. (2023a), LLaVA-
066   1.5-7B surpasses CLIP-like models (including SigLIP (Zhai et al., 2023)) by a large margin. On the
067   more challenging MMVP and MMVP-VLM benchmark (Tong et al., 2024c), though its absolute
068   performance is poor, LLaVA-1.5 still outperforms CLIP-like models, showcasing a stronger ability
069
      to extract information from given image embeddings.
070   What causes their discrepancy in extracting the given visual information? First, we unify the eval-
071   uation methods of CLIP and LLaVA and observe that the performance gap still exists. Then we
072   decompose their difference into three parts: training data, language processing choice, and model
073   paradigm (training and inference pipeline). Through ablation studies, we find that CLIP’s failure is
074   likely caused by the inadequate visual-language alignment of CLIP’s paradigm. This also implies
075
      that the visual information extraction module in LLaVA-1.5, consisting of the two-layer MLP con-
      nector and the language model, adopts an inherently different mechanism from CLIP’s paradigm.
076
077   The above results emphasize the importance of effective visual information extraction and highlight
078   LLaVA-1.5’s extracting ability. However, its poor performance on the MMVP benchmark remains a
079   mystery. We look into its failure and provide insight into future directions in the discussion section.
080   To help LLaVA-1.5 keep the visual information during decoding, we try an alternative decoding
081
      algorithm, Multi-Modal Mutual-Information Decoding (M3ID) (Favero et al., 2024), leading to per-
      formance gain (+6%). We further find that visual nuances are often extracted and aligned with the
082
      correct semantics by LLaVA-1.5 rather than being discarded after visual encoding, but they did not
083
      induce enough difference in outputs. To explore the amount of such visual formation, we reevaluate
084   LLaVA-1.5 with relaxed constraints, which allows for comparing the slight difference induced in the
085   outputs of two images. In this setting, its accuracy is significantly above random chance (+23.3%),
086   while the result in the original one-image setting is just around random chance (+0.3%), suggest-
087   ing insufficient visual information utilization in the original evaluation. In conclusion, despite the
088   erroneous agreements in the CLIP embedding space, visual nuances might still be extracted with
089   improved strategies. This underscores the potential to enhance model performance by employing
090   better extraction and utilization techniques with the same pretrained image encoder.

096   Benchmarking VLMs’ visual reasoning ability. Vision reasoning tasks underline VLMs’ visual
097   perception ability. Many recent challenging benchmarks on visual reasoning focus on assessing spe-
098   cific abilities of current VLMs like compositionality (Winoground (Thrush et al., 2022), ARO (Yuk-
099
      sekgonul et al., 2023), SugarCrepe (Hsieh et al., 2024)), hallucination (POPE (Li et al., 2023d),
      HallusionBench (Liu et al., 2023a), and VHILT (Rawte et al., 2024)), distinguishing image pairs
100
      (MMVP (Tong et al., 2024c)), spatial understanding of VLMs (What’sUp (Kamath et al., 2023a)
101
      and Embspatial-bench (Du et al., 2024)), and core visual perception abilities (BLINK (Fu et al.,
102   2024) for various aspects like visual correspondence and BlindTestbasic (Rahmanzadehgervi et al.,
103   2024) for recognizing basic patterns). State-of-the-art VLMs often fail unexpectedly on simple test
104   cases, performing significantly worse than human accuracy or even random guess. For our discus-
105   sion on erroneous agreements, we mainly consider the MMVP and What’sUp benchmark since their
106   image pairs exhibit this property in the CLIP embedding space. Nevertheless, findings on these
107   benchmarks reveal the relationship between vision encoders and VLMs, supporting the broader goal
      of enhancing VLMs for general visual reasoning.

127   Exploring causes of the visual shortcomings of VLMs. Researchers are actively exploring the
128   root causes of VLMs’ failures on benchmarks above, mainly from the model perspective. (1) Vision
129   modality. Tong et al. (2024c) argued that the flawed CLIP image encoder adopted by many VLM
130
      architectures could lead to downstream failure because of erroneous agreements. (Chandhok et al.,
      2024) agreed that the image encoder is responsible for the information loss in spatial reasoning tasks
131
      since CLIP’s performance is quite low. However, we find evidence that erroneous agreements do not
132
      necessarily lead to VLM’s failure and that LLaVA is stronger at extracting information from simi-
133   lar visual embeddings than CLIP. (2) Language understanding. In CLIP, Kamath et al. (2023b);
134   Tong et al. (2024b) found that its text encoder could also lose relevant information during encoding.
135   In multimodal LLMs (MLLMs), the language model might not timely terminate answer genera-
136   tion (Yue et al., 2024), put false priority on the input text and format (Stan et al., 2024), or neglect
137   information like negation (Quantmeyer et al., 2024). Qiao et al. (2024) decoupled the perception
138   stage and reasoning stage of VLMs and found that they are often limited by reasoning ability. In
139   this paper, we perform an ablation study on the text encoder and find that CLIP-like models still fail
140   when equipped with a stronger text encoder. (3) Vision-language alignment. Others discussed the
141
      importance of modality alignment, such as visual grounding (Rajabi & Kosecka, 2023). From the
      language model side, Ye et al. (2024) pointed out that the MLLMs might utilize multimodal spu-
142
      rious correlation in the training data due to the coarse-grained training objectives. Similarly, Yang
143
      et al. (2024a) found that the model wrongly raised the probability of deceptive candidates. From the
144   image encoder side, Yang et al. (2024b) proposed the cross-modal Alignment and Correspondence
145   score of visual representations, which is linearly correlated to model performance. We abstractly
146   view the components of VLMs other than the vision part as visual information extraction and uti-
147   lization module and demonstrate their different abilities. (4) Other factors. Apart from the model,
148   others looked into the problem with training data (Udandarao et al., 2024) or downstream tasks, such
149   as the hardness of the visual query (Zhang et al., 2024).
150   Improving the visual reasoning ability of VLMs. Following the observations about VLM’s limi-
151   tation, researchers mainly focused on improving the model structure with better image encoders and
152   vision-languageconnectors (Luo et al., 2024a; Zeng et al., 2021; Yao et al., 2024; Kar et al., 2024;
153   Jiang et al., 2023; Zong et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024; Tong et al., 2024a; Meng et al., 2024) or using
154   different training objectives with additional loss terms (Zhang et al., 2023; Zeng et al., 2024). From
155   the data-centric perspective, previous paper tried adding relevant instruction tuning data (Ranasinghe
156   et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024), using long caption in pretraining (Zheng et al., 2024), hard negative
157   mining (Yuksekgonul et al., 2023; Paiss et al., 2023) or using synthesized images (Chatterjee et al.,
158
      2024; Jiao et al., 2024).
159   Post-training techniques are also explored to improve the performance of off-the-shelf VLMs. Some
160   leveraged feedback from other models (Wang et al., 2024a; Luo et al., 2024b; Deng et al., 2024),
161   various text or visual prompting methods (Wan et al., 2024; Lei et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024), or
      multi-turn reflection (Huang et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024; Wu & Xie, 2023; Kim et al., 2024b).

189   3     E RRONEOUS AGREEMENTS
190
191   We begin by introducing the task setup and the concept of erroneous agreements. Using a toy ex-
192   ample, we demonstrate that information might still be extracted through alternative methods despite
193
      erroneous agreements. This is further validated through LLaVA-1.5’s good performance on the
      What’sUp benchmark (Kamath et al., 2023a), showing that erroneous agreements are not the sole
194
      issue as it is possible for LLaVA-1.5 to extract distinct visual information from highly similar em-
195
      beddings. We also notice a significant performance gap between LLaVA-1.5 and CLIP on What’sUp
196   and across several other benchmarks.
197
198
      3.1   TASK S ETUP
199
200   This paper focuses on the setup in which VLMs are asked to choose from several captions based
201   on a given image. For MLLMs, the image is accompanied by a question. Here, we use What’sUp
202   benchmark Kamath et al. (2023a), which was proposed for evaluating VLM’s spatial reasoning
203   ability. Every test case includes four captions (e.g., “A dog left of/right of/on/under a table”) and four
204   corresponding images photographed with minimal change except for the object spatial relationship.
205   For the convenience of calculating cosine similarity and comparing it to model performance, we
206
      split each test case into two pairs: In the previous example, one pair consists of “A dog left of a
      table” and “A dog right of a table” together with the ground truth images, and the other pair is the
207
      remaining captions and images. This way, we get four subsets of the original benchmark.
208
209   For CLIP-like models, we calculate the matching score between images and texts. For CLIP with
210   image encoder fv and text encoder ft , this is the cosine similarity between its image embeddings
211   fv (v) and text embeddings ft (t), denoted as
212                                                             fv (v)⊤ ft (t)
213                                   SC (fv (v), ft (t)) =                                                 (1)
                                                              ||fv (v)||||ft (t)||
214
215   As evaluation metrics, pair accuracy (Tong et al., 2024c; Kamath et al., 2023a) requires correct
      matching for both images, while the accuracy for two images independently is called individual

      accuracy. An example from the What’sUp benchmark, together with the evaluation of CLIP, is
217   shown in Figure 2.

219   The concept of erroneous agreements stems from this evaluation setup for CLIP. Specifically, if
220
      erroneous agreement happens for two different images v1 and v2 , then
221                                          SC (fv (v1 ), fv (v2 )) > τ                                   (2)
222   where τ is a chosen threshold near 1 (e.g., τ = 0.95 is used in Tong et al. (2024c)). Intuitively, when
223   the cosine similarity is high, the two image embeddings point in nearly the same direction, and they
224   will be close in Euclidean distance after l2 -normalization. From the view of captions, they will result
225   in highly similar image-text matching scores with any caption. From the image side, the margin is
226   small, so adding a slight noise in either embedding could reverse the preference over the captions,
227
      which might result from a small perturbation in either v1 or v2 . Hence, the difference between v1
      and v2 cannot be stably extracted by the CLIP model, and the relevant information seems lost. If
228
      this suggests the CLIP image encoder is “blind,” this will also undermine VLMs that use it as “eyes.”
229
230   This intuition is supported by the results on the MMVP benchmark designed to include image pairs
231   with a cosine similarity greater than 0.95 for CLIP embeddings but less than 0.6 for DINOv2 embed-
232   dings, along with the MMVP-VLM benchmark for CLIP-like models (Tong et al., 2024c). LLaVA-
233
      1.5 and CLIP perform close to random chance on these two benchmarks, respectively. The authors
      also demonstrated a correlation between CLIP model accuracy and LLaVA-1.5’s accuracy on differ-
234
      ent visual patterns.

      3.2   D O ERRONEOUS AGREEMENTS MEAN BLINDNESS ?
237
238   We note that cosine similarity does not depict all aspects of vector pairs. One criticism of it as the
239   similarity metric is that it only captures the linear relationship of vectors. As an example, consider
240   the following image embeddings
241
                                  fv (v1 ) = [10, 11, 12]⊤ , fv (v2 ) = [12, 11, 10]⊤
242
243
      While SC (fv (v1 ), fv (v2 )) > 0.989, Spearman’s rank correlation coefficient can tell their sharp
      difference: ρ = −1, showing that their order information is fully opposed. Therefore, the difference
244
      in visual inputs might still be extracted through other means when erroneous agreements occur.
245
246   We show that this scenario happens in experiments. In many VLMs using CLIP image encoder as
247   their “eyes,” the output score is nonlinear, different from CLIP-like models. For instance, in LLaVA-
248   1.5 (Liu et al., 2024), the CLIP image embeddings first pass through a two-layer MLP and are then
249
      used as input tokens for the transformer, Vicuna-1.5, which yields the token probability determining
      the model response. We evaluate LLaVA-1.5-7B using the pretrained weights and design the ques-
250
      tion format. (An illustration and an example are in Figure 1, and more details are in Appendix A.1.)
251
      The results are shown in Table 1. Despite the high cosine similarity, LLaVA-1.5-7B’s individual
252   accuracy and pair accuracy are both quite high, showing that it can extract and align query-relevant
253   information from image embeddings and produce the correct answer. In other words, erroneous
254   agreements do not contribute to the failure of VLMs on their own.
255
      Apart from this two-way evaluation, in Table 2, we report the results of the original evaluation,
256
      which is a four-way classification for each image. Besides, we include the results on COCO-spatial
257
      and GQA-spatial used in Kamath et al. (2023a) also for evaluating VLM’s spatial reasoning ability.
258   These benchmarks are in the format of an image paired with two captions differing only by a prepo-
259   sition. On all these benchmarks, LLaVA-1.5-7B wins CLIP-like models by a large margin, even
260   compared with the best model XVLM-COCO (Zeng et al., 2021) reported in the paper. We also find
261   that this performance gap relative to CLIP generalizes to some other MLLMs with different scales
262   and language models in Appendix B.5.
263

296   4     I NVESTIGATE THE P ERFORMANCE G AP
297
298   The above performance gap might be caused by various factors: evaluation methods, training data,
299
      language processing choice, and model paradigm. Firstly, we used cosine-similarity-based evalua-
      tion for CLIP-like models and model-response-based evaluation for LLaVA-1.5. Apart from this,
300
      CLIP-like models and LLaVA-1.5 were trained on different data, adopted different language pro-
301
      cessing techniques, and were in different VLM paradigms.
302
303   In this section, we design and conduct ablation studies to determine whether these factors contribute
304   to the failure of CLIP-like models. The ablation of evaluation methods is conducted first to determine
305
      whether there is really a performance gap. Then, we control the training data and text encoder of
      CLIP-like models to see if they cause the failure (See the illustration in Figure 3).

      4.1   U NIFIED E VALUATION
309
      One might first question the different evaluations performed on the CLIP-like models and LLaVA-
310   1.5. For the former, the evaluation is numeric-value based, while the latter is judged by its out-
311   put response as a conversation agent, following the practice in previous work (Tong et al., 2024c).
312   Hence, we test LLaVA-1.5 again using standard Multiple-Choice (MC) evaluation, where we rank
313   the perplexity of options (“A” and “B”) calculated based on the probability of output tokens. This is
314   similar to the CLIP-like models’ evaluation, where the image-text matching scores are ranked.
315
      In Table 3, we observe that MC evaluation yields similar results on the MMVP benchmark and even
316   better results on MMVP-VLM. Thus, this does not count for their performance discrepancy. The
317   increase in results on MMVP-VLM is possibly related to the hallucination of MLLMs, where they
318   tend to follow their language prior during long answer generation and might gradually “forget” the
319   visual input.

410   4.3   L ANGUAGE M ODEL C HOICES
411
      Previous research suggested that the CLIP text encoder is “blind” too (Tong et al., 2024b; Kamath
412
      et al., 2023b; Yuksekgonul et al., 2023)– It struggles with capturing changed word orders, negation,
413
      and spatial or numerical details. On the other hand, LLaVA-1.5-7B employs a pretrained large
414   language model (LLM), Vicuna-1.5-7B, which is supposed to be better than the CLIP text encoder
415   at language reasoning, and the commonsense knowledge it learned during language modeling should
416   benefit VLMs.
417

418
      further experiments on finetuning CLIP-like models using both the LLaVA-1.5 training data and

422   encoding texts. We replace the original CLIP text encoder with this pretrained model and add a
423   two-layer MLP connector on top of it to align its output dimension with the CLIP image encoder.
424   Since this text encoder is well-trained, we freeze it during finetuning. To make a fair comparison, we
425   lock the image encoder again and use the same connector design as the one used in LLaVA-1.5-7B
426   with only differing widths.

453   strategies matter.

      5     D ISCUSSION
456
457
      Although we find that visual information extraction methods matter a lot, and LLaVA-1.5 has a
458
      stronger extraction ability on highly similar embeddings, its poor performance on the MMVP bench-
459
      mark remains unexplained (Tong et al., 2024c). In this section, we reconsider its failure and provide
460   insight into future improvements based on two findings in MLLMs: They might not attend enough
461   to the visual input, and the visual information is often aligned correctly but probably did not induce
462   enough differences in the output token probability.
463
464
      5.1   A LTERNATIVE D ECODING FOR LL AVA
465
466   Inspired by the findings in Section 4.1 that MLLMs might “forget” the visual input gradually, one
467   possible improvement is to “remind” MLLMs of them, magnifying the effect of visual input on lan-
468   guage models. Multi-Modal Mutual-Information Decoding (M3ID) was designed for this purpose
469   on MLLMs like LLaVA (Favero et al., 2024). For token in each decoding step t, M3ID computes the
470   output probability with the image and without any input image, denoted as lc and lu , respectively.
471   The latter corresponds to the language prior. Then a correction term (lc − lu ) is added to lc with
472   weight 1−exp(−λt)
                exp(−λt) if the model is not highly confident with the next token (maxk (lc )k < log α).
473   This correction prevents the VLM from omitting the visual input and relying on the language prior.
474
      We test this decoding strategy on the MMVP benchmark in the standard setting. In Table 6, this
475   method achieves the most gain (+6%) relative to the baseline LLaVA-1.5-7b. We note that this
476   surpasses some methods that modified the vision part, such as Libra (30.0 with a decoupled and
477   more complex vision system) (Xu et al., 2024) and is on par with I-MoF (31.3 with interleaved
478   CLIP and DINO features) (Tong et al., 2024c). This result suggests that LLaVA-1.5 did not attend
479   to the visual input enough and thus might miss the key information for answering the query. A
480   similar finding was described through the interpretability perspective in Stan et al. (2024).
481

499   difference in model output, similar to the MC evaluation, we calculate the model perplexity of two
500   possible options. MC only uses the letters “A” and “B” when computing perplexity, but we use the
501   full option for perplexity computation, e.g., “(a) Open” and “(b) Closed” in the questions provided by
502   the original benchmark. Denote the perplexity of two options (normalized by the number of tokens)
503   given two images to be ppli1c1 , ppli1c2 , ppli2c1 , ppli2c2 , respectively. We consider the model to be
504   correct for this test case if they satisfy

      In other words, the model considers (image 1, caption 1) with (image 2, caption 2) more possible
508   matches than (image 2, caption 1) with (image1, caption 2). This way, we “force” the model to
509   output differently for two images in a pair, and thus, the random chance is 50%. Through this
510   comparison, we amplify the semantics induced by visual nuances. We also apply this evaluation on
511   CLIP, replacing perplexity with cosine similarity. In Table 7, the new performance is significantly
512   higher than random chance as the baseline (+23.3%), compared with the pair accuracy under the
513   original evaluation (+0.3%). This means more visual information can be extracted from the image
514   embedding and aligned with the correct semantics than the original results suggested.
515   The possible reason why they failed to be extracted in the original setting is that the language model
516   did not fully utilize the image-induced semantics. Consequently, they failed to affect the output
517   probability enough to produce the correct answer. Influenced by language prior and spurious corre-
518   lation with irrelevant text tokens (Ye et al., 2024), it will probably output common answers or even
519   hallucinations. Hence, reaching this “upper bound” in the original evaluation requires the VLM to
520   utilize extracted visual nuances properly and balance it with language prior during generation.
521
522   6   C ONCLUSION
523
524   Our study questions the use of erroneous agreements to reflect CLIP image encoders’ information
525   loss or blindness and finds that they are not the sole cause of VLM failures. We show that the
526   amount of extracted visual information largely depends on the extraction strategy, which varies
527   widely across VLMs. LLaVA-1.5, with a stronger extraction ability, outperforms CLIP-like models
528   on our benchmarks. Our controlled experiments suggest that the key factor in their performance
529   discrepancy might lie in their paradigms. We believe the information loss of the image encoder
530
      should be defined when conditioning on the VLM paradigm and possibly the downstream task.
531   Our results suggest there is still room to enhance VLMs with a fixed, pretrained image encoder.
532   While balancing the visual grounding ability and image-text correspondence (e.g., combining pop-
533   ular visual representation learning models in various styles) could reach the best trade-off on the
534   curve for heterogeneous benchmarks, developing advanced methods for VLMs to extract and utilize
535   given visual information might shift the curve upwards.
536   Limitation. We view the VLMs abstractly and do not look into fine-grained details on how the
537   visual information is extracted and leads to the model’s output. We leave its dissection for future
538   research. For ablation studies, we do not train CLIP or SigLIP models from scratch or use larger
539   batch sizes due to the limitation in computing resources, so the conclusion on the effects of different
      factors is restricted.

847
848   The What’sUp benchmark (Kamath et al., 2023a) contains 820 images of pairs of household ob-
849   jects, 408 in Subset A and 412 in Subset B. We corrected the mislabeled images in the GitHub
850   Issues and reevaluated the pretrained VLMs. For CLIP, SigLIP, and XVLM’s evaluation, we use
851   the official code provided by the What’sUp benchmark’s authors in https://github.com/
852   amitakamath/whatsup_vlms.
853   For LLaVA-1.5, the questions used for evaluation are listed in Table 8. Then the question is concate-
854   nated with the fixed prompt template (“USER: <image> \n(question) ASSISTANT:”). Consider-
855   ing the position bias in LLMs (Wang et al., 2024b), we exchange the position of two prepositions in
856   the question with 50% probability on COCO-spatial and GQA-spatial benchmarks for fair results.
857   On the What’sUp benchmark, the orders are always the same for two images. Then, we evaluate the
858   outputs by keyword matching since we observe that the output is quite structured.
859   The reason why we use different commands after the main question (e.g., “Answer left or right”,
860   “Choose from the two options”, and “Give a short answer”) is that we found the LLaVA-1.5 model
861   sensitive to such command. We tried “Answer on or under” for the On/Under subset in What’sUp
862   Subset A, and the model accuracy is quite low. This is one of its limitations that deserves future
863   research. However, we aim to show that LLaVA-1.5 can extract such information, so we use the best
      prompt to showcase its ability.

878
879   The MMVP benchmark contains 150 pairs of similar images, and the MMVP-VLM benchmark has

885
886   In the standard setting, we evaluate the correctness of the model response by human evaluation.
887   Although the accuracy given by the GPT-4 evaluation was close to that of a human evaluation on
888   average, we noticed that it is unreliable since it gave several wrong judgments. So, we evaluate the
889   correctness of the answers manually to avoid models getting higher accuracy by cheating GPT-4. In
890
      the Multiple-Choice setting, we calculate and rank the perplexity of “A</s>” and “B</s>” given
      by the model.

893   Closed” for the example in Figure 4). We also add the EOS (“</s>”) to the end of these options
894   and normalize the perplexity by their number of tokens.

952
953   Observing the similar failure of the data-informed attempt, previous work concluded that even with
954
      relevant, high-quality data and hard negatives, denser supervision is likely required to let the model

955
      supervision at the bounding-box level. However, LLaVA does not incorporate downstream task-
956
      related inductive bias or denser supervision to achieve high accuracy, yet it beats XVLM finetuned
957   on COCO (Lin et al., 2014) on the What’sUp benchmark.

960
      //github.com/zengyan-97/X-VLM), but no improvement is observed in the results (the
      last two model rows in Table 11). The image encoder is locked during finetuning. We use both
961
      contrastive learning loss and image-text matching loss. The evaluation is performed through the
962
      image-text matching score. We finetune the XVLM-16M model for 5 epochs with a learning rate of
963   1e − 5 and a weight decay rate of 0.01. We use 10% steps of warmup and AdamW optimizer with
964   a lambda learning rate schedule. The batch size is 128, and we train the model on 4 gpus.
