009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Post-training quantization (PTQ) without access to real data is enabling efficient
012            model optimization and deployment in scenarios where privacy or proprietary
013            constraints restrict the use of original datasets. Traditional data free quantiza-
014            tion methods rely on Batch Normalization (BN) statistics from the trained full-
015            precision model to generate calibration dataset for quantization. However, this re-
016
               liance on BN statistics limits their applicability to deep neural networks (DNNs)
               without BN layer such as AlexNet. In this paper, we propose a calibration dataset
017
               generation algorithm that is agnostic to BN statistics, leveraging just the back-
018
               propagation to create synthetic images for PTQ. We also demonstrate that it is
019            not necessary to include samples from every target category in the calibration
020            dataset to get the representative activation ranges for quantization. Extensive ex-
021            periments with both large and lightweight models on large-scale image classifica-
022            tion tasks demonstrate that our method consistently improves quantization perfor-
023            mance across various DNN architectures, especially in low-bit settings. Notably,
024            in 4-bit quantization, we achieve an improvement of 3.42% in top-1 accuracy for
025            the ResNet18 model and 3.14% for the InceptionV3 model compared to the state-
026            of-the-art (SOTA) DSG method. Importantly, we use very few synthetic samples
027
               for quantization compared to other methods.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   DNNs have achieved remarkable success across a wide range of applications, driven by advance-
032   ments in computational power, large-scale datasets, and innovative algorithms (LeCun et al., 2015).
033   These networks have revolutionized the field of computer vision. They power image classifica-
034   tion (Krizhevsky et al., 2012), object detection (Girshick et al., 2014), and semantic segmenta-
035   tion (Long et al., 2015) systems with unprecedented accuracy. With these domains, DNNs have
036   made significant strides in robotics, enhancing the ability of robots to perceive and interact with
037   their environments autonomously (Mnih et al., 2015). In autonomous driving, deep learning mod-
038   els play a crucial role in enabling vehicles to understand and navigate complex road conditions
039
      safely (Bojarski et al., 2016). The medical field has also seen transformative impacts, with DNNs
      aiding in disease diagnosis, medical imaging analysis, and personalized treatment planning (Esteva
040
      et al., 2017). However, deploying these neural networks on resource-constrained devices remains
041
      a considerable challenge due to their substantial memory requirements and intensive computational
042   demands (Howard, 2017). The advancement of hardware capable of low-precision computations
043   has made quantization a favored method for addressing these challenges (Jacob et al., 2018). Quan-
044   tizing the floating-point values of weights and/or activations in a neural network to integers can
045   significantly reduce the model size without altering the architecture.
046
      Quantization methods are generally categorized into two types: quantization-aware training (QAT)
047
      and PTQ. QAT incorporates quantization into the training process, allowing the model to learn and
048   adapt to the quantized weights and activations. This approach typically results in higher accuracy
049   compared to PTQ, as the model is optimized to perform well under the constraints of quantiza-
050   tion (Choi et al., 2018). While the performance degradation is minimal with QAT, the process can be
051   computationally intensive and time-consuming (Nahshan et al., 2021). PTQ involves applying quan-
052   tization techniques to a pre-trained model without training process (Cai et al., 2020; Qin et al., 2023).
053   One of the challenges in quantization is determining the range of activation values in DNN. Many
      quantization methods are designed for data-driven scenarios, requiring access to either training or


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      validation data, or relying on a small set of unlabeled calibration data to accurately represent the
055   activation values (Banner et al., 2019). However, real data may not always be available due to some
056   constraints, particularly with proprietary data. To mitigate this, data-free quantization techniques
057   have been developed to allow for the quantization without needing access to real data (Cai et al.,
058   2020). Among existing data-free quantization methods, generative approaches create calibration
059   data by aligning the distribution of synthetic data with the BN statistics of the trained full-precision
060   model (Xu et al., 2020). Generative approaches can greatly reduce the accuracy gap between quan-
061   tized models that use synthetic data and those that use real data (Qin et al., 2023; Cai et al., 2020).
062   However, relying on BN statistics limits the applicability of these methods to neural networks which
063
      don’t have BN layer such as AlexNet (Krizhevsky et al., 2012). In our work, we demonstrate that it
      is not necessary to depend on the BN statistics of the full-precision model to generate a calibration
064
      dataset for achieving optimal performance from the quantized model. Additionally, the common
065
      practice when curating a calibration dataset is to include at least one image from each target class
066   to calibrate the image classification model (Zhang et al., 2023). We show that optimal performance
067   can be achieved without having a sample from each category and that fewer samples can suffice.
068
069
      In this paper, we primarily explore the method of generating synthetic calibration dataset for PTQ
      of DNN models. We demonstrate that the BN statistics of the original floating-point model are not
070
      necessary to create effective synthetic data. The main contributions of this paper are:
071
072          • We propose a method to generate synthetic data using the trained full-precision model
073            agnostic to BN statistics, making our approach applicable to any model architecture.
074
             • We experimentally demonstrate that it is not necessary to include a sample from each target
075            category in the calibration dataset; selecting only a few target classes is sufficient to create
076            an effective calibration dataset which in turn reduces PTQ time.
077
             • Through extensive PTQ comparisons on several standard network architectures such as
078
               ResNet18/20/50 (He et al., 2016), SqueezeNext (Gholami et al., 2018), InceptionV3
079
               (Szegedy et al., 2016), and lightweight architectures like ShuffleNet (Zhang et al., 2018),
080            and MobileNetV2/V3 (Sandler et al., 2018; Howard et al., 2019), we show that our
081            method significantly outperforms existing generative quantization methods with calibra-
082            tion. Specifically, in 4-bit precision setting on the ResNet18 and InceptionV3 models, our
083            method improves the top-1 accuracy by over 3.42% and 3.14% compared to the SOTA
084            DSG method.

      2     R ELATED W ORK
087
088
      In this section, we first review and organize existing research on quantization into two primary
089   methodologies: QAT and PTQ. Additionally, we categorize these methods based on whether they
090   require any form of training or validation data for the quantization process.
091
092   2.1   Q UANTIZATION AWARE T RAINING
093
094   QAT is an advanced technique in which the quantization process is integrated into the training phase
095   of the neural network. (Jacob et al., 2018) integrate low-precision computations typically used dur-
096   ing inference into the forward pass of training, while maintaining standard backpropagation with
097   floating-point weights and biases to allow precise adjustments. This approach allows the model
098   to learn and adapt to the quantization noise, resulting in higher accuracy for the quantized model
099
      compared to PTQ. (Courbariaux et al., 2016) introduce the concept of binarized neural networks
      (BNNs), where both weights and activations are constrained to +1 or -1. They replace most arith-
100
      metic operations with bit-wise operations to reduce memory size. (Krishnamoorthi, 2018) evaluated
101
      various quantization methods and bit-widths, revealing that even with per-channel quantization,
102   lightweight networks do not reach baseline accuracy with INT8 PTQ and require QAT. Using an
103   annealing learning rate schedule and a very small final learning rate, (McKinstry et al., 2018) show
104   that many networks can be fine-tuned for just one epoch after quantizing to INT8 and still reach
105   baseline accuracy. (Zhou et al., 2016) introduce DoReFa-Net, a framework for training neural net-
106   works with low bit-width weights, activations, and gradients. They also propose QAT techniques to
107   ensure effective learning with low precision. (Rastegari et al., 2016) presented XNOR-Net, a QAT-
      trained binary CNN architecture, achieving impressive accuracy on ImageNet and demonstrating


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      the feasibility of highly efficient binary networks for real-world tasks. PACT (Choi et al., 2018)
109   dynamically adjusts the clipping values to minimize quantization error, leading to higher accuracy,
110   especially for low-bit width quantization.
111
112
      QAT maintains quantized model accuracy close to the full precision model while significantly re-
      ducing the model size and computational complexity. However, it requires more computational
113
      resources and time due to additional quantization operations, making it more complex and resource-
114
      intensive than standard training (Nahshan et al., 2021). Additionally, QAT implementation is chal-
115   lenging, needing modifications to the training pipeline.
116
117
      2.2   P OST T RAINING Q UANTIZATION
118
119   PTQ is a widely used technique aimed at reducing the memory and computational requirements of
120   neural networks without the need for retraining. Unlike QAT, which incorporates quantization into
121   the training process, PTQ is applied after the model has been fully trained using a small calibration
122   dataset created from the original training or validation dataset. To quantize activations in DNNs, it
123   is essential to determine the activation ranges. (Banner et al., 2019) address this by analytically find-
124   ing the activation clipping ranges. They demonstrate that the activations typically follow bell-curve
125   statistics, fitting either Laplace or Gaussian distributions, and they formulate the clipping process as
126   an optimization problem. (Zhao et al., 2019) introduce Outlier Channel Splitting (OCS), a technique
127
      designed to handle outliers in the distribution of weights and activations that can negatively impact
      quantization. OCS works by identifying a small number of channels containing outliers, duplicating
128
      them, and then halving the values in those channels, which moves the affected outliers toward the
129
      center of the distribution. (Li et al., 2021) introduce a novel method called Block Reconstruction
130   Quantization (BRECQ), which focuses on optimizing the quantization of neural network weights in
131   a block-wise manner. BRECQ achieves this through local optimization and layer-wise error correc-
132   tion, significantly reducing quantization-induced errors without requiring retraining. (Nagel et al.,
133   2020) present a novel adaptive rounding technique, AdaRound, that dynamically determines whether
134   to round weights and activations up or down during quantization. This optimization-based approach
135   minimizes the quantization error and preserves model accuracy without requiring retraining. Unlike
136   AdaRound, which confines quantized weights to be within ±1 of their rounded values, AdaQuant
137   (Hubara et al., 2021) grants more freedom. It achieves this by independently optimizing each layer’s
138
      weights and quantization parameters, using the calibration set to minimize the mean-squared-error
      between the original and quantized layer outputs. (Cai et al., 2020) introduces a zero-shot quanti-
139
      zation approach that eliminates the need for real calibration data by generating synthetic data. This
140
      synthetic data is used to calibrate the quantization parameters, allowing for effective quantization
141   without access to real data. (Qin et al., 2023) addresses the homogenization of synthetic data for
142   quantization and improves quantized model performance.
143
144
      2.3   DATA - DEPENDENT Q UANTIZATION
145
146   Data-dependent quantizations are the techniques that leverage the characteristics and distribution of
147   the training data to enhance the efficiency and accuracy of quantized neural networks. Different fixed
148   precision data-dependent QAT schemes have been proposed in the literature. (Gupta et al., 2015)
149   use 16 bits word length for weights, biases, and other intermediate variables during training with
150   stochastic rounding. (Wang et al., 2019) mine channel-wise interaction to learn interacted bit count
151   to prevent performance degradation in binary neural networks. Unlike traditional methods (Zhuang
152   et al., 2021) first optimized the network with quantized weights and then with quantized activations.
153
      To address the increased training time and computational costs of data-driven QAT methods, PTQ
      methods using real data have been proposed. (Banner et al., 2018) introduce Analytical Clipping for
154
      Integer Quantization (ACIQ) to optimize activation clipping, a per-channel bit allocation policy to
155
      minimize mean-squared-error, and a bias-correction method to address inherent biases in quantized
156   weights. This technique enhances the accuracy and efficiency of 4-bit quantized convolutional neural
157   networks. A low-bit precision linear quantization framework is proposed by (Choukroun et al.,
158   2019) in which the optimal solution to the quantization problem is found via mean-squared-error
159   optimization with fixed precision constraints. (Zhao et al., 2019) sampled activation distribution
160   using 512 training images to determine quantization grid points, then use this grid during testing.
161   Generally, when quantizing the DNNs, assigning each floating-point weight to its nearest fixed-
      point value is the common approach but (Nagel et al., 2020) show that this isn’t the best approach.

162
      In AdaRound they use a better weight-rounding mechanism for PTQ that adapts to the data and the
163   task loss. Even though these methods provide good quantization techniques, they still need access
164   to training or validation data.

168   2.4   DATA - INDEPENDENT Q UANTIZATION
169
170   Due to privacy or proprietary constraints, access to training or validation datasets is not always
171   possible. Consequently, several works have been proposed to perform quantization using synthetic
172   data to determine activation ranges. DFQ by (Nagel et al., 2019) leverages the scale-equivariance
173   property of activation functions to equalize weight ranges within the network and also corrects
174   quantization-induced biases in the error. DFQ works fine on 8-bit quantization, but the accuracy
175
      drops significantly for lower bits. (Xu et al., 2020) use a generator to generate the synthetic data and
      fine-tune the quantized model. Unlike other generative data-free quantization approaches, (Qian
176
      et al., 2023) determine if the knowledge carried out by generated samples is informative or not to
177
      the quantized model. They generate samples with adaptive ability to boost the quantized model
178   performance. (Choi et al., 2020) propose an adversarial knowledge distillation framework that min-
179   imizes the possible loss for a worst case via adversarial learning by constraining a generator in the
180   adversarial learning framework. The Inception scheme is proposed by (Haroush et al., 2020) to gen-
181   erate the data; they proposed knowledge distillation-based methods to improve accuracy. Methods
182   like (Haroush et al., 2020; Cai et al., 2020; Qin et al., 2023) purely use BN statistics of the floating
183   point model to create synthetic data. (Qin et al., 2023) show that the synthetic data generated by
184   constraining BN statistics suffers serious homogenization at both the distribution level and sample
185   level and improves it by slacking distribution alignment and layerwise sample enhancement. While
186
      the aforementioned methods employ various techniques to generate synthetic data for quantization
      and enhance model accuracy, they typically require fine-tuning or rely on BN statistics from the
187
      full-precision model, which we demonstrate is not necessary for achieving good performance.
188

191   3     M OTIVATION
192
193
194   BN is a commonly utilized technique during the training of neural networks to stabilize and ac-
195   celerate the convergence process by normalizing activations within each layer. However, when it
196   comes to generating synthetic data for PTQ, the primary objective shifts to synthesizing inputs that
197   produce a wide and representative range of activations within the network. This objective can be
198   effectively met through direct optimization techniques such as backpropagation, which iteratively
199   refines synthetic inputs using the model’s gradients. By doing so, we can ensure that these synthetic
200   inputs accurately represent the target classes without the need for BN statistics. Furthermore, Qin
201
      et al. (2023) demonstrate that samples generated solely from BN statistics exhibit minimal statistical
      variation compared to real-world samples, which are inherently diverse. To address this, we intro-
202
      duce Gaussian noise during the optimization process to enhance the variability and diversity of the
203
      generated synthetic data.
204
205   Additionally, while generating representative datasets for quantization, the prevailing practice has
206   been to select at least one sample from each class to ensure comprehensive coverage of the activation
207
      space (Zhang et al., 2023). This approach is based on the assumption that including every class
      is necessary to capture the full range of activations required for effective quantization. However,
208
      this method can be computationally intensive and time-consuming, especially for large datasets
209
      with numerous classes. (Banner et al., 2018) show that the statistical distributions of weights and
210   activations follow a bell curve, indicating that large values occur very rarely compared to small
211   values. Motivated by this for a more efficient process, we investigate whether a smaller subset of
212   classes could provide an effective activation range. By focusing on a reduced number of target
213   classes, our goal was to streamline the calibration data generation process while maintaining the
214   efficacy of quantization. This exploration was driven by the hypothesis that a subset of classes could
215   still encompass the diverse activation patterns needed for accurate model quantization as described
      in subsection 5.4.

239   The algorithm 1 for generating synthetic images begins by randomly selecting M unique target
240   classes from a total of N available classes, ensuring diversity in the synthetic dataset. A loss func-
241   tion, L, is defined to measure the discrepancy between the model’s prediction and the target label.
242   For each selected target class, the process initiates by creating a synthetic image, x, with values
243   drawn from a normal distribution, N (0, 1). The target label, y, is set to the current class label c.
244
      Inspired by the Fast Gradient Sign Method (FGSM) introduced by (Goodfellow et al., 2014), the
      algorithm then enters an iterative loop for a specified number of iterations, T . In each iteration, the
245
      synthetic image x is updated by performing gradient descent, where the gradient of the loss function
246
      with respect to x is subtracted, scaled by a learning rate α as shown in equation 1. This step is anal-
247   ogous to the FGSM approach, which perturbs the input in the direction of the gradient to maximize
248   the loss. To introduce variability in the samples, Gaussian noise, N (0, 0.1), is added to the image.
249   The algorithm checks if the model’s prediction for x matches the target label, and if the loss falls
250   below a predefined threshold, ϵ. If both conditions are satisfied, the loop breaks early, indicating that
251   a satisfactory synthetic image has been generated. This process is repeated for each of the M target
252   classes, and the resulting synthetic images are stored in an array X. Finally, the array of synthetic
253   images is returned as the output.

463   6   C ONCLUSION
464
465   In this paper, we have introduced a method for post-training data-free quantization that generates
466   synthetic data using the trained full-precision model independent of BN statistics. This makes our
467   approach versatile and applicable to any model architecture. Our experimental results demonstrate
468   that it is not necessary to include samples from each target category in the calibration dataset; select-
469   ing only a few target classes is sufficient to create an effective calibration dataset. Our method con-
470   sistently outperforms existing generative data-free quantization methods with calibration, as demon-
471
      strated through extensive comparisons across various standard network architectures. These include
      ResNet18/50, SqueezeNext, InceptionV3, as well as lightweight architectures like ShuffleNet and
472
      MobileNetV2/V3. Notably, our approach shows significant improvements in 4-bit precision set-
473
      tings. For instance, on the ResNet18 model, our method increases the top-1 accuracy by over 3.42%
474   compared to SOTA DSG method. These findings underscore the effectiveness and generalizabil-
475   ity of our approach, highlighting its potential to achieve high accuracy with lower bit-widths and
476   fewer calibration samples. This makes it a promising solution for efficient model deployment in
477   resource-constrained environments.
