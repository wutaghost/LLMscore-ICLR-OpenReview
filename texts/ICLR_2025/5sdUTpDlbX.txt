009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011
                 While electroencephalogram (EEG) based brain-computer interface (BCI) has
012              been widely used for medical diagnosis, health care, and device control, the
013              safety of EEG BCI has long been neglected. In this paper, we propose Professor
014              X, an invisible and robust “mind-controller” that can arbitrarily manipulate the
015              outputs of EEG BCI through backdoor attack, to alert the EEG community of the
016              potential hazard. However, existing EEG attacks mainly focus on single-target
017              class attacks, and they either require engaging the training stage of the target BCI,
018              or fail to maintain high stealthiness. Addressing these limitations, Professor X
019              exploits a three-stage clean label poisoning attack: 1) selecting one trigger for
020
                 each class; 2) learning optimal injecting EEG electrodes and frequencies strategy
                 with reinforcement learning for each trigger; 3) generating poisoned samples
021
                 by injecting the corresponding trigger’s frequencies into poisoned data for each
022
                 class by linearly interpolating the spectral amplitude of both data according to
023              previously learned strategies. Experiments on datasets of three common EEG tasks
024              demonstrate the effectiveness and robustness of Professor X, which also easily
025              bypasses existing backdoor defenses. Code will be released soon.

028   1       I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Electroencephalogram (EEG) is a neuroimaging technology to record of the spontaneous electrical
031   activity of the brain. EEG-based brain-computer interface (BCI) has been widely used in medical
032   diagnosis (Ahmad et al., 2022), healthcare (Jafari et al., 2023), and device control (Lorach et al., 2023;
033   Altaheri et al., 2023). While most EEG community researchers devote themselves to advancing the
034   performance of EEG BCI, the safety of EEG BCI has long been neglected. Inspired by Professor X1 ,
035
      a superhuman with the ability to control other’s minds, we wonder whether a malicious adversary can
      arbitrarily manipulate the outputs of EEG BCI like him. It will be severely dangerous if so. Backdoor
036
      attack (BA), where an adversary injects a backdoor into a model to control its outputs for inference
037
      samples with a particular trigger, offers a feasible approach (Doan et al., 2022).
038
039   However, designing an effect and stealthy BA for EEG modality is not trivial for three difficulties,
040   resulting in three questions. D1: Low signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) and heterogeneity in EEG format
041
      (i.e., the montage and sampling rate of EEG recordings) are major obstacles. Q1: How to develop a
      generalizable BA for various EEG tasks (usually have different EEG formats)? D2: Previous studies
042
      demonstrated for different EEG tasks, different critical EEG electrodes and frequencies strongly
043
      related to the performance of EEG BCI (Parvez & Paul, 2014; Jana & Mukherjee, 2021; Baig et al.,
044   2020; Herman et al., 2008), indicating that the trigger-injection strategy (i.e., which electrodes and
045   frequencies to inject triggers) inevitably affects the performance of BA. Q2: How to find the optimal
046   strategy for different EEG tasks? D3: Certain classes of EEG have specific morphology that can
047   easily be identified by human experts, e.g., in epilepsy detection, the EEG during the ictal phase
048   contains more spike/sharp waves than those during the normal state phase (Blume et al., 1984). Q3:
049   How to maintain the consistency of the label and the morphology?
050   The first BA for EEG modality is demonstrated in Fig 1 (a), where the narrow period pulse (NPP)
051   signals are added as the trigger for single-target class attacks (Meng et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023b).
052   To generate invisible triggers, the adversarial loss is applied to learn a spatial filter as the trigger

106   2023; Yuan et al., 2023). BadNets (Gu et al., 2019) is the first BA, where the adversary maliciously
107   control models to misclassify the input images contain suspicious patches to a target class. Other
      non-stealthy attacks include blended (Chen et al., 2017) and sinusoidal strips based (Barni et al.,

      2019). To achieve higher stealthiness, some data poisoning BA were developed, including shifting
109   color spaces (Jiang et al., 2023a), warping (Nguyen & Tran, 2020b), regularization (Li et al., 2020)
110   and frequency-based (Zeng et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022; Hammoud & Ghanem, 2021; Hou et al.,
111   2023; Feng et al., 2022; Gao et al., 2024). Other stealthy attacks (Nguyen & Tran, 2020a; Doan et al.,

141   2.3    BACKDOOR D EFENSES
142
143   To cope with the security problems of backdoor attacks, several categories of defensive methods have
144
      been developed. Neural Cleanse (Wang et al., 2019) is a trigger reconstruction based methods. If
      the reconstructed trigger pattern is significantly small, the model is identified as a backdoor model.
145
      Assuming the trigger is still effective when a triggered sample is combining with a clean sample,
146
      STRIP (Gao et al., 2019) detects the backdoor model by feeding the combined samples into the
147   model to see if the predictions are still with low entropy. Spectral Signature (Tran et al., 2018) detects
148   the backdoor model based on the latent representations. Fine-Pruning (Liu et al., 2018a) erases the
149   backdoor by pruning the model.
150
      Besides the above defenses designed for backdoor attacks, there are some common EEG pre-
151
      processing methods, such as bandstop filtering and down-sampling, should be considered when
152
      designing a practical robust backdoor attack for EEG BCI in the real-world scene.

292   4.2   BASELINES
293
294   Non-stealthy Baselines. As mentioned in previous sections, to the best of our knowledge, Professor
295   X is the first work that studies multi-trigger and multi-target class (MT) backdoor in EEG BCI. For
296   comparison, we design several baseline approaches which can be divided into two main groups:
297
      non-stealthy and stealthy. Non-stealthy attacks contains PatchMT and PulseMT. For a benign EEG
      segment x ∈ RE×T . PatchMT is a multi-trigger and MT extension of BadNets (Gu et al., 2019)
298
      where we fill the first βT timepoints of a EEG segments with a constant number, e.g., {0.1, 0.3, 0.5}
299
      for three-class task. PulseMT is a multi-trigger and MT extension of NPP-based backdoor attacks
300   (Meng et al., 2023) where we use NPP signals with different amplitudes, e.g., {-0.8, -0.3, 0.3, 0.8}
301   for different target classes.
302
303
      Stealthy Baselines. Previous works generate stealthy poisioned samples by controlling the training
      stage and can only attack single target class (Meng et al., 2024; Ding et al., 2022; Jiang et al.,
304
      2023c). As they control the training of target model, it is unfair to directly compare their methods
305
      with Professor X. There is no stealthy MT BA for EEG. Thus, we design two MT stealthy attacks
306   baselines: CompMT and AdverMT. CompMT generates poisoned samples for different target
307   classes by compressing the amplitude of EEG with different ratios, e.g., {-0.1, 0, 0.1} for three-class
308   task. AdverseMT is a multi-trigger and MT extension of adversarial filtering based attacks (Meng
309   et al., 2024), where we using a local model trained only on Sp to generate different spatial filters W∗i
310   for different target classes, then we apply these spatial filters to generate poisoned samples. More
311   details are written in Appendix F.

      We follow the poisoning attack setting as the previous works (Meng et al., 2023) and consider three
      widely-used EEG BCIs for classifier f : EEGNet (Lawhern et al., 2018), DeepCNN (Schirrmeister
316

317
      each EEG dataset D is divided into three parts: training set Dtrain , poisoning set Dp , and test set
318   Dtest . Specifically, for a dataset contains n subjects, we select one subject’s data as Dp one by one,
319   and the remaining n − 1 subjects to perform leave-one-subject-out (LOSO) cross-validation, i.e., one
320   of the subjects as Dtest , and the remaining n − 2 subjects as Dtrain (one of the subjects in Dtrain is
321   chosen to be validation set). In summary, for a dataset contains n subjects, there are n(n − 1) runs to
322   validate each EEG BCI backdoor attack method. A poisoned subset Sp of M (M < N ) examples
323   is generated based on Dp . Then Sp is combined with Dtrain to acquire S = {Sp , Dtrain }. The
      poisoning ratio is defined as : ρ = M/N .

530      6                    C ONCLUSION
531
        In this paper, we proposed Professor X, a novel EEG backdoor for manipulating EEG BCI, where
532
        the adversary can arbitrarily control the output for any input samples. To the best of our knowledge,
533
        Professor X is the first method that considers which EEG electrodes and frequencies to be injected
534     for different EEG tasks and formats. We specially design the reward function in RL to enhance
535     the robustness and stealthiness. Experimental results showcase the effectiveness, robustness, and
536     generalizability of Professor X. This work alerts the EEG community of the potential danger of the
537     vulnerability of EEG BCI against BA and calls for defensive studies for EEG modality. It is worth
538     noting that Professor X can also be applied for protecting intellectual properties of EEG datasets and
539     BCI models, offering a concealed and harmless approach to add authors’ watermark (backdoor can
        be regarded as watermark), indicating the real-world application of Professor X.

      B     L IMITATIONS
811
812
      Our Professor X is a backdoor attack in the frequency domain, which requires to transform the EEG
813
      signals into frequency domain through fast Fourier transform (FFT) and return to temporal domain
814   through inverse FFT (iFFT). The operation of FFT and iFFT in the trigger injection function are a
815   little more time-consuming compared to other backdoor attack directly in the temporal domain, like
816   PatchMT (Gu et al., 2019) and PulseMT (Meng et al., 2023). Future effort will be devoted into the
817   faster implementation of FFT and iFFT, for example, taking the advantage of modern GPUs.
818
      It is a little more time-consuming for the reinforcement learning to acquire the optimal strategies for
819
      each trigger. However, we can obtain a general injecting strategy for each EEG BCI tasks, which can
820   achieve a relatively good performance without reinforcement learning, as we can see from Table 4
821   that random injection strategy has an acceptable performance.
822
823
824   C     B ROADER I MPACTS
825
826   With the rapid development of techniques, EEG BCIs gain a wide range of applications from health
827   care to human-computer interaction. Some companies like Neuralink adopt the EEG BCI to assist
828   paralytic patients helping themselves in daily lives. However, if the EEG BCI is backdoor attacked
829   by Professor X, which allows the attacker to arbitrarily control BCI’s outputs, the BCI users may
830
      fall into tremendous fatal troubles. For instance, one paralytic patient controls his/her wheelchair by
      EEG BCI, the attacker can manipulate the wheelchair to run down a steep staircase. For an epileptic
831
      patient, the attacker can let all the output be Normal State, even when the patient is experiencing
832
      an epileptic seizure. This paper reveals the severe danger faced by EEG BCIs, demonstrating the
833   possibility that someone can maliciously manipulate the outputs of EEG BCIs with arbitrary target
834   class.
835
836
      Professor X can also be used for positive purposes, like protecting intellectual property of EEG
      dataset and EEG models with watermarking. As our Professor X has a very small impact of the clean
837
      accuracy, and the poisoning approach is clean label poisoning, Professor X is a fantastic method for
838
      watermarking EEG dataset and models.
839
840   For a company that provides EEG dataset, it can select different EEG triggers for different customs
841   to generate poisoned data and inject into the dataset provided to customs who buy the dataset. As a
842
      result, the company have the information of which trigger is corresponding to which customs, e.g.,
      trigger x is in the dataset provided to custom X, trigger y is in the dataset provided to custom Y. If an
843
      EEG model from a company which didn’t buy dataset is detected having this watermark (backdoor)
844
      with trigger x, the company knows that the custom X leaked the dataset. Similarly, if an EEG model
845   is detected having this watermark (backdoor) with trigger y, the company knows that the custom Y
846   leaked the dataset.

849   D     D ISCUSSION OF D EFENSIVE S TUDY AGAINST P ROFESSOR X
850
851   Thanks to the reviewer JuBu, uEuL and kEdv in the ICLR conference, who asked many questions
852   regarding the defensive study against Professor X. These insightful concerns deepen our understanding
853   of our attack and how to guard backdoor attack in EEG BCIs. Thus, we add a new section here to
854   discuss our humble opinion on the defensive study against Professor X, which we hope will benefit
855
      the future research.
856   Since backdoor attack is primarily studied in the image processing field, the defensive research is also
857   conducted for protecting image model. However, EEG modality, a kind of multi-variate time series, is
858   far different from image modality. These difference may inherently cause failure of existing backdoor
859   defensive methods. Next, we would like to discuss the limitation of these defensive methods.
860
861   D.1    N EURAL C LEANSE
862
863   Neural Cleanse (NC) (Wang et al., 2019) aims to reconstruct the trigger pattern in the backdoor
      model. It is conducted based on the following assumption:


                                                        16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
            1) The trigger pattern is the same for different input, which is called input-agnostic.
865
866
            2) The backdoor model learns a shortcut for the trigger pattern.
867         3) The trigger pattern is relatively small compared to the whole input.
868
869
      NC first initialize a random noise and a random noise as the trigger pattern, then optimize the noise
      and mask to make the backdoor model outputs the target label for a input injected with the trigger,
870
      and let the mask as small as possible. At last, NC calculate a anomaly index according to the size
871
      of the mask. The smaller the mask, the higher the anomaly index. Empirically, the anomly index
872   threshold is set to 2. NC works well on detecting BA likes BadNets (Gu et al., 2019) and Trojan
873   Backdoor (Liu et al., 2018b), which are basically consistent with the above assumptions.
874
875
      However, the trigger patterns for EEG BCI are always not small, like NPP signals and our attack (the
      trigger can be seen in Fig. 8, the red residual is the trigger). These trigger patterns are wide and cover
876
      all time points of EEG signals. Thus, NC is not effective in detecting our attack. It can be seen from
877
      Fig. 9, 10 and 11 that the reconstructed trigger patterns of clean model and backdoor model are quite
878   similar. And the mask reconstructed for both model are all very wide and extend to most channels
879   and time points. In short, NC fails to detect our attack.
880
881   D.2    STRIP
882
883   STRIP (Gao et al., 2019), which perturbs the input EEG and calculates the entropy of the predictions
884   of these perturbed EEG data. STRIP detects the backdoor based on thees assumptions:
885
886         1) backdoor trigger is input-agnostic;
887         2) backdoor trigger is strong and effective when performing input perturbation;
888         3) the backdoor models’ outputs (softmax) of poisoned data has very low entropy.
889
890   STRIP has several strengths:
891
892         1) Insensitive to trigger-size: STRIP is effective no matter the trigger is big or small.
893         2) Plug and Play: STRIP is plug and play, and compatible in any models. We only need the
894            inputs and outpus of the backdoor models (treated as a black box as we don’t need any
895            intermediate outputs), then calculate the entropy of the outputs.
896         3) Backdoor model architecture-agnositc: STRIP only needs the inputs and outputs of
897            the backdoor model, so it is an architecture-agnositc method and is generalize to many
898            real-world application senarios.
899
900   However, STRIP also has some weaknesses. Any trigger that may affect the above findings may
901
      cause STRIP’s detection failure:
902
            1) the trigger is input-specific;
903
904
            2) the trigger is not that strong, it fails when performing input perturbation;;
905         3) the trigger won’t cause the backdoor model to predict with very low entropy.
906
907
      So why STRIP fails in detecting Professor X? Firstly, our trigger is injected in the frequency
      domain, leading to the input-specific pattern in the temporal domain, causing assumption 1 to be
908
      invalid. Moreover, the input perturbation in the temporal domain may damages the frequency
909
      information, causing our trigger disapper, leading to the assumption 2 to be invalid. Lastly, as
910   EEG is a nonstationary modality, the outputs of EEG models are always with high entropy, making
911   assumption 3 to be invalid. Thus, STRIP is not effective in detecting Professor X attack.
912
913   D.3    S PECTRAL S IGNATURE
914
915   Spectral Signature (Tran et al., 2018) detects the backdoor samples by statistical analysis of clean data
916   and backdoor data in the latent space, which first find the top-right singular vector of the covariance
917   matrix of the latent vectors of a small subset of clean samples, then each sample is calculated a
      correlation score to this singular vector. It detects whether a sample is backdoor sample by the


                                                         17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      correlation score, the difference the correlation score, the higher the possibility of being a backdoor
919   sample. Spectral Signature aims to purify the datasets, it can remove all the possible backdoor sample.
920   However, any clean sample can also be possibly removed by Spectral Signature.
921
922
      The reason of the failure of Spectral Signature on EEG BCI might be that EEG signals are nonstation-
      ary, so the latent space of EEG model contains a lots of noises. These noises causes the similarity
923
      between backdoor samples and clean samples.
924
925
926   D.4    F INE -P RUNING
927
928   Fine-Pruning (Liu et al., 2018a) assumes that the defender has a validation dataset Dvalid in which
929
      all data are clean. The defender feeds these clean data into the backdoor models, and recrods the
      average activation of each neuron. Afterwards, the defender iteratively prunes neurons from the DNN
930
      in increasing order of average activations. Thus, the low-activated neurons are those the average
931
      activation is low when feeding in clean data.
932
933   However, Fine-Pruning can inadvertently remove important features that are crucial for classification.
934   Because the average activation is obtained from the small subset Dvalid , so the low-activated neurons
935
      determined by Dvalid may be high-activated neurons when feeding another clean validation dataset
        ′                                                                         ′
      Dvalid . That is, the important neurons for classifying clean sample x ∈ Dvalid   may be low-activated
936
      neurons for all samples in Dvalid , resulting in the pruning of these important neurons.
937
938   As we discussed above, Fine-Pruning requires that the defender has a validation dataset As we
939   discussed above, Fine-Pruning requires that the defender has a validation dataset. The performance
940   of Fine-Pruning relies heavily on the quality of the validation dataset, since the low-activated neurons
941
      are determined by the validation dataset.
942   So in the future, building a large, diverse, high quality, and absolutely clean validation dataset is
943   the key for improving the Fine-Pruning’s performance. The most important part is the diversity,
944   which not only means the diversity of EEG tasks, but also means the diversity of EEG formats. Thus,
945   improving the defenses against backdoor attacks is not an easy task and needs joint efforts of the
946   medical and academic communities.

      In this section, we introduce the three datasets used in our experiments, and explain the preprocessing.
957
      We elaborately selected these three datasets because of three reasons: 1) They cover three different
958
      EEG tasks that are important and common in EEG BCI field; 2) The EEG formats of these datasets
959   vary significantly; 3) The EEG tasks are all multi class classification tasks, that is, the number

      E.1   E MOTION R ECOGNITION (ER)

965
      The SJTU Emotion EEG Dataset (SEED) was incoporated as the representative dataset of emotion
966   recogniton tasks (Zheng & Lu, 2015). It consists of EEG recordings from 15 subjects watching
967   15 emotional video clips with three repeated session each on different days. Each video clip is
968   supposed to evoke one of the three target emotions: positive, neutral, and negative. The EEG signals
969   were acquired by the 62-channel electrode cap at a sampling rate of 1000 Hz. We performed below
970   preprocessing procedures for the 62-channel EEG signals: 1) Down-sampling from 1000 Hz to 200
971   Hz, 2) Band-pass filtering at 0.3-50 Hz, 3) Segmenting EEG signals into 1-second (200 timepoints),
      obtaining 3394 EEG segments in each session for each subject.

977    movements (i.e., right hand, left hand, feet, and tongue) for four seconds. Each session consists of a

       the montages (i.e., the number and the places of electrodes) of EEG signals vary significantly among
987
       different subjects’ recordings, we select to use only the EEG recordings with the same 23 channels
988
       (see Appendix A) and discard other channels or the recordings don’t have all these 23 channels. Due
989    to the purpose is to test whether the backdoor attack works on the ED task, not to study the epilepsy
990    EEG classification, we segment part of the CHB-MIT dataset to form a four-class ED dataset (i.e.,
991    the preictal, ictal, postictal, and interictal phases). Specifically, for a ictal phase EEG recording
992    of ti seconds from [si , ei ] timepoints, we segment the [si − ti , ei ] EEG as the preictal phase, the
993    [ei , ei + ti ] EEG as the postictal phase, and another ti seconds EEG recordings as the interictal phase
994    which satisfying there is no ictal phase within half an hour before or after. Then we segment the
995    23-channel EEG signals into 1-second segments, consequently, there are 41336 segments left in total
996    from all subjects, 10334 for each phase. As the imbalanced amount of data across different subjects,

       we separate these 41336 segments into 10 groups and treat the ten groups as 10 subjects.

1007   F.2     D ETAILS OF BASELINE M ETHODS
1008
1009   In our Professor X backdoor attacks, for an EEG segment xi ∈ RE×T , we modify the βF frequency-
1010   points and γE electrodes of a EEG segments with a constant number.
1011   There are four baseline methods in our study for multi-target backdoor attacks, two of them are
1012   non-stealthy attacks (PatchMT and PulseMT) and two are stealthy attacks (CompressMT and
1013   AdverseMT). In order to achieve a fair comparison, we modify only first γE electrodes for all
1014   baseline attack methods. For the non-stealthy attacks, which are all on the temporal domains, we
1015   modify βT timepoints of EEG signals. For the stealthy attacks, there is no constraint of the numbers
1016   of the modify timepoints as these attacks achieve stealthiness in another way.
1017   For each baseline method, we try our best to find out the best performance, as demonstrated below.
1018   We promise that we did not maliciously lower the performances of the baseline methods.
1019
1020
       F.2.1    PATCH MT
1021
1022   PatchMT is a multi-trigger and MT extension of BadNets (Gu et al., 2019) where we fill the first βT
1023   timepoints and γE electrodes of a EEG segments with a constant number. Specifically, for an EEG
1024   segment xi ∈ RE×T , we set the first γE electrodes and the first βT timepoints of the EEG segment
1025   to a constant number. We normalize the EEG segment xi ∈ RE×T to let xi ’s mean is 0 and std is
       1. Then set the first γE electrodes and the first βT timepoints of xi to a different constant number
