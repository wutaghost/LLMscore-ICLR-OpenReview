009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            A fundamental aspect of diffusion models is the variance schedule, which governs
012            the evolution of variance throughout the diffusion process. Despite numerous
013            studies exploring variance schedules, little effort has been made to understand
014            the variance distributions implied by sampling from these schedules and how it
015            benefits both training and data generation. We introduce a novel perspective on
016            score-based diffusion models, bridging the gap between the variance schedule
017            and its underlying variance distribution. Specifically, we propose the notion of
018
               sampling variance according to a probabilistic rationale, which induces a density.
               Our approach views the inverse of the variance schedule as a cumulative distri-
019
               bution function (CDF) and its first derivative as a probability density function
020
               (PDF) of the variance distribution. This formulation not only offers a unified
021            view of variance schedules but also allows for the direct engineering of a variance
022            schedule from the probabilistic rationale of its inverse function. Additionally, our
023            framework is not limited to CDFs with closed-form inverse solutions, enabling
024            the exploration of variance schedules that are unattainable through conventional
025            methods. We present the tools required to obtain a diverse array of novel
026            variance schedules tailored to specific rationales, such as separability metrics
027            or prior beliefs. These schedules may exhibit varied dynamics, ranging from
028            rapid convergence towards zero to prolonged periods in high-variance regions.
029
               Through comprehensive empirical evaluation, we demonstrate the efficacy of
               enhancing the performance of diffusion models with schedules distinct from
030
               those encountered during training. We provide a principled and unified approach
031
               to variance schedules in diffusion models, revealing the relationship between
032            variance schedules and their underlying probabilistic rationales, which yields
033            notable improvements in image generation performance, as measured by FID.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
039
040
      Diffusion models have emerged as powerful tools in the realms of image, audio, and video gener-
041
      ation, facilitating remarkable progress in capturing complex data distributions. The general frame-
042   work of diffusion models was first introduced in Sohl-Dickstein et al. (2015) and later re-popularized
043   by Ho et al. (2020), both using a discrete time Markov chain to transform the data distribution to
044   noise. Song et al. (2020) relaxed the framework to operate in continuous time by rephrasing the
045   distribution transforming process as a Markov process following a stochastic differential equation
046   (SDE). This approach allows the deployment of a variety of SDE and ordinary differential equation
047   (ODE) solvers for the reverse process, leading to significant performance gains (Karras et al., 2022).
048   An essential component of diffusion models is the definition of a variance schedule, which describes
049   the evolution of the variance in the underlying stochastic process over time. Despite numerous stud-
050
      ies on different variance schedules, little effort has been made to characterize the distribution of
      variance and justify specific schedules beyond empirical performance observations.
051
052   To foster a better understanding of the variance schedule in score-based diffusion models, we inter-
053   pret the inverse function of the variance schedule as a cumulative distribution function (CDF), with
      an associated probability density function (PDF) induced by a probabilistic rationale that weights


                                                        1

054
      the importance of variances throughout the diffusion process. Our rephrased framework has several
055   theoretical and practical contributions:
056
057
      Introducing rationale: We propose the notion of a rationale that enables designing variance
058   schedules from a distribution of variances, ranging from rapid convergence to zero variance to pro-
059   longed periods in high-variance regions, or smooth transition from high to low variance.
060
061   Inverse engineering diffusion: We show that a driftless forward process is fully determined by the
062   choice of a rationale enabling us to “inverse engineer” the diffusion process by selecting a rationale
063   and constructing the variance schedule from the inverse function of the respective CDF.
064
065   Choosing any probability density: We demonstrate that a variance schedule can be defined for
066   any rationale and its corresponding probability density. Even if the resulting CDF lacks a closed-
067   form solution, the generalized inverse of the CDF can be used to derive the variance schedule.
068
069   Simplifying design choices: We show that loss weighting simplifies to choosing a different ra-
070   tionale for training than for sampling. Furthermore, we explore the effect of switching variance
071   schedules with different rationales after training.
072
      Our experimental results confirm the effectiveness of our approach, demonstrating that variance
073   schedules derived from a rationale, can lead to improved image quality. We highlight one rationale
074   in particular, which would have been unattainable through conventional methods.

132   3.1               A UNIFIED PERSPECTIVE
133
      In order to better understand the characteristics of sampling variance proportional to uniformly dis-
134
      tributed time-steps in a variance schedule, we firstly introduce the notion of a rationale.
135
136   Definition 3.1 Given an interval I = (σinf , σsup ) ⊂ R, we call the positive
                                                                                  R function r : I → R>0
137   a rationale on I if r is integrable with a finite normalizing constant Zr := I r(τ )dτ < ∞.
138
139   The rationale r(σ(t)) scores the benefit of sampling a particular value σ(t) at any point in time t of
140   the forward diffusion process. By definition, any such rationale can be normalized to obtain a PDF
141
      on the interval I. The idea is to add a meaning (i.e., rationale) to oversampling certain variances
      and then sample proportionally. For all rationales, a variance schedule exists such that sampling
142
      from the rationale is equivalent to sampling from the respective variance schedule with uniformly
143
      distributed inputs, as will be explored in the following. Such rationales can incorporate metrics or
144   prior knowledge on the diffusion process, allowing for variance schedules in training and inference
145   that follow a well-understood PDF.
146
147

      schedule σ(t) on the interval t ∈ [0, 1] (Song et al., 2020; Karras et al., 2022). The inverse func-
148
      tion of the variance schedule can then be interpreted as a CDF. Applying the Smirnov transform,
149
      sampling from the variance schedule with uniformly distributed inputs is equivalent to sampling
150   proportionally to the PDF of the respective CDF. The connection between the PDF and the respec-
151   tive variance schedule will be explained in the following.

231   3.3             C HOOSING A RATIONALE
232
      As shown in section 3.2, driftless forward diffusion processes are defined by a rationale. Choosing
233
      a good rationale is crucial to obtaining a diffusion process that benefits training or sampling. In this
234
      work, we condition the score function sθ directly on the standard deviation σr (t) at time t, rather than
235   t itself, such that models can be used with different variance schedules in sampling. In the following,

237   schedule that is based on a separability metric. The impact of all rationales on the density p0t of
238   the forward diffusion process is visualized in fig. 2, we observe different dynamics, ranging from
239   rapid convergence towards zero variance to prolonged periods in high-variance regions. Additional
240   rationales that are not discussed further can be found in appendix C.

353   4                        E XPERIMENTS
354
355   We investigated the effects of different rationales on CIFAR-10 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009) and
356   ImageNet-32, a version of ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009) that has been downsampled to 32 × 32
357   images. We explore differences in performance regarding the Frechét-Inception Distance (FID)
358   (Heusel et al., 2017) and the Inception Score (IS) (Salimans et  al., 2016) for the variance schedules
      of respective rationales: VE, squared L2-norm, and N 0, 52 . We chose N 0, 52 , as a represen-

359
      tative of variance schedules with gaussian rationale through an ablation study (see appendix D.1 for
360
      more details).
361
362   Not only do we explore the effect of training with specific variance schedules, but also the effects of
363   sampling with variance schedules that are distinct from the variance schedule used during training.
364   We can achieve this by conditioning the diffusion model on the standard deviation σr (t) at time t
365
      of the forward diffusion process, rather than t itself. This way, we do not only evaluate the effect of
      variance schedules on the convergence during training, but also the effect on the reverse diffusion
366
      process during sampling. While it may appear counter-intuitive to use a different variance schedule
367
      during sampling, we note that loss weighting effectively induces a distinct variance schedule dur-
368   ing training (see section 3.4) and is commonly featured in the standard framework of score-based
369   diffusion models (Song et al., 2020; Karras et al., 2022). Furthermore, we will show that switching
370   the variance schedule of the diffusion process during sampling can lead to better results. The ex-
371   periments aim to disentangle what type of rationale (or variance schedule) should be followed when
372   training a diffusion model and when sampling from it.

      5   R ELATED WORK
487
488
      Modeling of diffusion processes The foundational research conducted by Song et al. (2020)
489
      presents a cohesive framework for modeling the transformation of distributions through stochas-
490   tic processes in continuous time, incorporating an exact reverse-time model. Subsequent extensive
491   investigations have explored (Karras et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023) and expanded upon (Jing et al.,
492   2022; Kim et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2022; Lou & Ermon, 2023; Song et al., 2023; Yoon et al., 2023;
493   Bartosh et al., 2024) the continuous-time perspective on generative models using SDEs.
494
495   Advancements in score-based diffusion models While (Karras et al., 2022) offers a compre-
496   hensive analysis of the components within the score-based diffusion framework, it does not delve
497   into the underlying density of variance induced by variance schedules. Unlike Song et al. (2020),
498   which conditions the score model during training on the time step via a time embedding, Karras
499   et al. (2022) and Song et al. (2023) directly utilize the marginal variance of the diffusion process for
500   conditioning. However, they do not provide further characterization of the marginal variance distri-
501
      bution in terms of a probability density function and the resulting rationale. In Raya & Ambrogioni
      (2024), the authors explore the effects of sampling at lower variances for the variance preserving
502
      (VP) variance schedule with drift, motivated by spontaneous symmetry breaking.
503
504
505   6   C ONCLUSION
506
507   In this work we introduce a novel perspective on the score-based diffusion framework, which al-
508   lows to reason about the rationale and effect of sampling variance during the training and inference
509   of score-based diffusion models. Our work highlights the nuanced impact of variance schedules
510   on training and sampling processes for generative models applied to CIFAR-10 and ImageNet-32.
511   By evaluating models with different training and sampling configurations, we observed distinct ad-
512   vantages for each approach. Notably, the squared L2-norm variance schedule demonstrated faster
513
      convergence during training but a propensity for overfitting on CIFAR-10. In contrast, the VE vari-
      ance schedule exhibited slower yet smoother convergence on both datasets.
514
      Remarkably, the best FID scores were achieved when sampling according to the N 0, 52 ratio-

515
516   nale, which did not yielded neither fast nor stable convergence during training. This underscores
517   the importance of viewing training and sampling as separate entities. Our findings challenge the
518   intuitive approach of viewing variance schedules to serve the same purpose for both training and
519
      sampling, suggesting that different discretization processes may yield better outcomes depending
      on the specific objectives and solvers.
520
521   Futhernmore we proved that loss weighting induces a different variance schedule during training and
522   that using a variance schedule during sampling that is distinct to that, which was used during training
523   can be beneficial. Using the perspective of rationales, we can not only disentangle the implied
524   variance schedule when training with loss weighting, but also better understand the distribution of
525
      variance for any given variance schedule via its inverse function.
