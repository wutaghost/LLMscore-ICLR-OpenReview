009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Graphs and hypergraphs provide powerful abstractions for modeling interactions
012            among a set of entities of interest and have been attracting a growing interest in
013            the literature thanks to many successful applications in several fields. In particular,
014            they are rapidly expanding in domains such as chemistry and biology, especially in
015            the areas of drug discovery and molecule generation. One of the areas witnessing
016            the fasted growth is the chemical reactions field, where chemical reactions can
017            be naturally encoded as directed hyperedges of a hypergraph. In this paper, we
018            address the chemical reaction classification problem by introducing the notion of a
019
               Directed Line Graph (DLG) associated with a given directed hypergraph. On top
               of it, we build the Directed Line Graph Network (DLGNet), the first spectral-based
020
               Graph Neural Network (GNN) expressly designed to operate on a hypergraph
021
               via its DLG transformation. The foundation of DLGNet is a novel Hermitian
022            matrix, the Directed Line Graph Laplacian L     ⃗ N , which compactly encodes the
023            directionality of the interactions taking place within the directed hyperedges of
024            the hypergraph thanks to the DLG representation. L      ⃗ N enjoys many desirable
025            properties, including admitting an eigenvalue decomposition and being positive
026            semidefinite, which make it well-suited for being adopted within a spectral-based
027            GNN. Through extensive experiments on chemical reaction datasets, we show that
028            DLGNet significantly outperforms the existing approaches, achieving on a collec-
029            tion of real-world datasets an average relative-percentage-difference improvement
030            of 3.27%, with a maximum improvement of 5.28%.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   In recent years, ground-breaking research in the graph-learning literature has been prompted by
036   seminal works on GNNs such as Scarselli et al. (2009); Micheli (2009); Li et al. (2016); Kipf &Welling
037   (2017); Veličković et al. (2018). However, representing data solely through graphs, either undirected
038   or directed, can be limiting in many real-world applications where more complex relationships exist.
039
      In such cases, generalizations of graphs known as hypergraphs, which allow for higher-order (group)
      relationships among the vertices, have emerged as powerful alternatives. Hypergraphs extend the
040
      traditional concept of a graph by allowing hyperedges to connect an arbitrary number of nodes,
041
      thereby capturing both pairwise (dyadic) and group-wise (polyadic) interactions (Schaub et al., 2021).
042   This has naturally led to a new stream of research devoted to the investigation of Hypergraph Neural
043   Networks (HNNs) (Feng et al., 2019; Chien et al., 2021; Huang &Yang, 2021; Wang et al., 2023a;b).
044
045
      Among many successful applications, graph and hypergraph representations have recently been
      applied in chemistry and biology to address various tasks such as drug discovery (Bongini et al.,
046
      2021), molecule generation (Hoogeboom et al., 2022), and protein interaction modeling (Jha et al.,
047
      2022). Several graph-based representations have also been developed and employed for the study
048   of chemical reactions, which has applications in areas such as reaction engineering, retrosynthetic
049   pathway design, and reaction feasibility evaluations. In particular, retrosynthetic modeling, where a
050   synthetic route is designed starting from the desired product and analyzed backward, benefits greatly
051   from accurate reaction type identification. This capability enables the elimination of unfeasible
052   pathways, thereby streamlining the discovery of efficient routes for chemical production. This is
053   particularly important in industries such as pharmaceutical and material sciences, where optimizing
      synthetic routes can lead to significant cost savings and innovation. A similar situation holds, in

      reaction feasibility analysis, where predicting the likelihood of a reaction’s success based on the
055   molecular inputs is essential for designing scalable and efficient processes.
056
057   One of the most relevant techniques to model reactions is the directed graph (Fialkowski et al., 2005),
058
      where molecules are represented as nodes and the chemical reactions are represented as directed edges
      from reactants to products. Despite its popularity, such a directed graph model suffers from some key
059
      limitations. In particular, modeling each reaction as a collection of individual directed edges between
060
      each reactant-product pair fails to fully capture the complexity of multi-reactant or multi-product
061   reactions, which are key to many important applications (Restrepo, 2022; Garcia-Chung et al., 2023).
062   To mitigate this issue, Chang (2024) proposed a hypergraph representation in which molecules are
063   nodes and each reaction is captured by a hyperedge. However, this model lacks a mechanism to
064   represent the directionality of a reaction, thus failing to capture the reactant/product relationship
065   within it. As a further attempt, Restrepo (2023) introduced a directed hypergraph representation
066   which is able to model both the chemical reactions structure and their directionality, where directed
067   hyperedges model the directional interactions between reagents (heads) and products (tails), better
068   capturing the full complexity of chemical reactions. Let us note that this literature only focuses on
069
      modeling reaction structures without considering any form of hypergraph learning methods. We set
      ourselves out to developing one in this paper.
070
071   In contrast to prior studies that address node classification or link prediction tasks (Dong et al., 2020;
072   Wang et al., 2023b; Zhao et al., 2024), in this work we tackle the reaction classification problem (i.e.,
073   the problem of predicting the reaction type of a given set of reactants and products) as a hyperedge
074   classification task. This task can be reframed as a node classification task by using the concept of the
075
      line graph.

154   Let L be a generic Laplacian matrix of a given 2-uniform hypergraph H. We assume that L admits an
155   eigenvalue decomposition L = U ΛU ∗ , where U ∈ Cn×n represents (in its columns) the eigenvectors,
156   U ∗ is its conjugate transpose, and Λ ∈ Rn×n is the diagonal matrix containing the eigenvalues.
157   Let x ∈ Cn be a graph signal, i.e., a complex-valued function x : V → Cn of the vertices of H.
158   We define x̂ = F(x) = U ∗ x as the graph Fourier transform of x and F −1 (x̂) = U x̂ its inverse
159   transform. The convolution y ⊛ x between x and another graph signal y ∈ Cn , acting as a filter, in
160   the vertex space is defined in the frequency space as y ⊛ x = U diag(U ∗ y)U ∗ x. Letting Ŷ := U ĜU ∗
161   with Ĝ := diag(U ∗ y), we can write y ⊛ x as the linear operator Ŷ x. See Shuman et al. (2013) for
      more details.

      In the context of GNNs, explicitly learning y as a non-parametric filter presents two significant
163   limitations. Firstly, computing the eigenvalue decomposition of L can be computationally too
164   expensive (Kipf &Welling, 2017). Secondly, explicitly learning y requires a number of parameters
165   proportional to the input size, which becomes inefficient for high-dimensional tasks (Defferrard et al.,
166   2016). To address these issues, the GNN literature commonly employs filters where the graph Fourier
167   transform is approximated as a degree-K polynomial of Λ, with K kept small for computational
168   efficiency. For further details, we refer the reader to Kipf &Welling (2017); Defferrard et al. (2016);

323   input and output channels, while the last layer has c input and c′ output channels (c′ is also the number
      of input channel to the linear layers). Let d be number of output channel of the last linear layer (where

      d is the number of classes to be predicted). With ℓ convolutional layers and S linear layers, DLGNet’s
325   complexity is O(mnc0 ) + O(ℓ(m2 c + mc2 ) + mc + (S − 1)(mc′2 ) + mc′ d + md). Assuming
326   O(c) = O(c′ ) = O(d) = c̄, we have a complexity of O(ℓ(m2 c̄) + (ℓ + S)(mc̄2 )). This shows that

      5.2   BASELINES
371
      We categorize our baseline models into two main groups: Hypergraph Neural Networks that handle
372   undirected hypergraphs and those designed specifically for directed hypergraphs. Similar to Graph
373   Neural Networks (GNNs), HNNs can be classified into spectral-based and spatial-based approaches.
374   Spatial-based HNNs treat the convolution operator as a localized aggregation function (Dong et al.,
375   2020). On the other hand, spectral-based HNNs define the convolution operator (based on the graph
376   Fourier transform theory) as a function of the eigenvalue decomposition of the Laplacian matrix
377   associated with the hypergraph Feng et al. (2019). Differently, the spectral-based one defines the
      convolution operator (a rigorous one in this case) as a function of the eigenvalue decomposition of the


                                                          7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      Laplacian matrix associated with the hypergraph Feng et al. (2019). In the spectral-based category,
379   methods such as HGNN (Feng et al., 2019), HCHA in Dong et al. (2020), HGNN+ (Gao et al., 2022)
380   are analogous to GNNs applied on clique expansions of hypergraphs. For spatial-based methods,
381   HNNs such as HNHN (Dong et al., 2020), UniGCNII (Huang &Yang, 2021), HyperDN (Tudisco
382   et al., 2021), LEGCN Yang et al. (2022), as well as set-based models (Chien et al., 2021), AllDeepSets
383   and AllSetTransformer, incorporate hyperedge features and employ a message-passing framework,
384   which can be interpreted as GNNs applied to the star expansion graph. Additionally, ED-HNN (Wang
385   et al., 2023a) leverages gradient diffusion processes to generalize across a broad class of hypergraph
386   neural networks, while PhenomNN (Wang et al., 2023b) introduces a framework based on hypergraph-
387
      regularized energy functions. Finally, in the context of directed HNNs, two state-of-the-art models
      are considered: DHM (Zhao et al., 2024) and DHRL (Ma et al., 2024). The first one, DHM, encodes
388
      high-order information in directed hypergraphs and captures the directional information of directed
389
      hyperedges through an attention mechanism and a directed hypergraph momentum encoder. The
390   second method, DHRL, approximates the Laplacian of the directed hypergraph and formulates the
391   convolution operation on this directed hypergraph structure.
392
393
      Differently from these baselines, as shown in Section 3, DLGNet is a spectral-based GCN that
      leverages the Directed Line Graph Laplacian and convolves on the directed line graph derived from
394
      the directed hypergraph.

516   6   C ONCLUSIONS
517
518   We introduced the Directed Line Graph Network (DLGNet), the first spectral GNN specifically de-
519   signed to operate on directed line graphs associated with directed hypergraphs by directly convolving
520   hyperedge features. DLGNet leverages a novel complex-valued Laplacian matrix, the Directed Line
521   Graph Laplacian, which is a Hermitian matrix encoding the interactions among the hyperedges of a
522   hypergraph using complex numbers. This formulation allows for the natural representation of both
523
      directed and undirected relationships between the hyperedges, capturing rich structural information.
      Our proposed DLGNet network utilizes this new Laplacian matrix to perform spectral convolutions
524
      on the line graph featuring both undirected and directed edges. Via the Directed Line Graph repres-
525
      entation, our proposed model enables the seamless integration of the directionality present in the
526   hypergraph at hand, which is crucial for accurately modeling various real-world phenomena involving
527   asymmetric high-order interactions.
528
529
      We evaluated our approach on the chemical reaction classification problem using three real-world
      datasets. In these experiments, we demonstrated the superiority of DLGNet, which achieved an
530
      average relative percentage difference improvement of 3.27% over the second-best method across the
531
      three datasets. This highlights the importance of directly convolving the hypergraph features on the
532   directed line graph, instead of doing so in the undirecetd/directed hypergraph. Though an ablation
533   study, we demonstrated the relevance of encoding directional information via the directed line graph
534   associated with a directed hypergraph as opposed to ignoring it. We also provided a qualitative
535   analysis DLGNet’s results in light of the underlying chemical reaction classification task.
536
      In light of the promising results we obtained and as a future perspective, we would like to address
537
      more complex and challenging tasks, such as retrosynthetic planning and reaction discovery, which
538   require sophisticated analysis and deeper insights into the underlying chemical processes.
