009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated immense utility across vari-
012            ous industries. However, as LLMs advance, the risk of harmful outputs increases
013            due to incorrect or malicious instruction prompts. While current methods effec-
014            tively address jailbreak risks, they share common limitations: 1) Judging harm-
015            ful responses from the prefill-level lacks utilization of the model’s decoding out-
016            puts, leading to relatively lower effectiveness and robustness. 2) Rejecting po-
017            tentially harmful responses based on a single evaluation can significantly impair
018
               the model’s helpfulness. This paper examines the LLMs’ capability to recognize
               harmful outputs, revealing and quantifying their proficiency in assessing the dan-
019
               ger of previous tokens. Motivated by pilot experiment results, we design a robust
020
               defense mechanism at the decoding level. Our novel decoder-oriented, step-by-
021            step defense architecture corrects the outputs of harmful queries directly rather
022            than rejecting them outright. We introduce speculative decoding to enhance us-
023            ability and facilitate deployment to boost secure decoding speed. Extensive ex-
024            periments demonstrate that our approach improves model security without com-
025            promising reasoning speed. Notably, our method leverages the model’s ability to
026            discern hazardous information, maintaining its helpfulness compared to existing
027            methods.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   In recent years, significant progress has been made in developing large language models (LLMs).
033
      Meanwhile, the safety of LLMs has attracted significant attention from the research community and
      industry (Weidinger et al., 2021; Achiam et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023b). One of the primary safety
034
      concerns is jailbreaking, where malicious actors or errant inputs prompt LLMs to produce harmful
035
      or inappropriate content, effectively bypassing ethical guidelines. Many attempts have been made
036   to address these risks. For example, Meta has implemented several strategies in both pre-training
037   and fine-tuning phases to improve the safety of their Llama-series models (Touvron et al., 2023;
038   Dubey et al., 2024). Despite these efforts, some studies have reported that focusing too narrowly on
039   safety may diminish the models’ general capability (Bai et al., 2022; Huang et al., 2024). Therefore,
040   enhancing LLMs’ safety without compromising their utility has become a critical area of research.
041
      Recent defense strategies against jailbreaks can be roughly categorized into two groups (as shown in
042   Figure 1). The first group is prefill-level defense (Wu et al., 2023a; Phute et al., 2023; Zheng et al.,
043   2024). It enhances the models’ protective capabilities by integrating additional security measures
044   into the initial prompts (prefills) or refining their representation. However, this approach primarily
045   depends on user inputs to detect harmful responses, making it susceptible to rapidly advancing jail-
046   breaking techniques. Moreover, this reliance can lead to inaccuracies in interpreting user intentions,
047   thereby reducing the overall utility of the LLMs. Another group of methods is response-level de-
048   fenses (Phute et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024). It involves using safety filters that assess the potential
049   harmfulness of model-generated responses. This method focuses on the output of the models, po-
050
      tentially offering improved performance by directly addressing the content generated. However, this
      strategy typically involves a single evaluation point, which may result in false positives that could
051
      diminish the model’s utility by restricting benign responses.
052
053   In practice, jailbreak instructions can bypass the prefill-level defenses and achieve their purposes
      in the model’s response Wei et al. (2024). Therefore, assessing jailbreak behavior in LLMs should

      focus on decoding dimensions, including the context of both the prefill and the model’s response.
078   We aim to directly address and rectify jailbreak behavior by focusing on the decoding level. (Zheng
079   et al., 2024) has demonstrated models’ ability to distinguish between harmful and benign prefill.
080   This raises the question: Can LLMs extend this discriminative capability to their own decoding?
081   To investigate this hypothesis, we conduct a series of preliminary experiments to explore the model’s
082   ability to discern its own decoding. Specifically, we evaluate five open-source LLMs and visualize
083   the hidden state of the decoding on a token-by-token basis. We observe that LLMs cannot distinguish
084   harmful tokens from benign tokens in one step, but it can achieve identification through multi-step
085   judgment at the decoding, especially for harmful prefill.
086   Based on pilot results, we introduce a novel decoder-oriented defense, termed RDS, defencing by
087   step-by-step evaluation during inference. Informed by the discriminative capability of LLMs on
088   decoding, RDS utilizes a trainable classifier to assess the harmfulness of candidate tokens during
089   sampling and adjust their logits accordingly. Subsequently, RDS reorders the candidate tokens and
090   prioritizes the token with lower harmfulness at each step to ensure a safe response iteratively. The
091   step-by-step safe generation provides a root defense on LLM’s decoding (encompassing the context
092
      of both prefill and response) perspective and multi-step evaluation. Furthermore, speculative decod-
      ing is incorporated into RDS for hidden state prediction to enhance the generation speed, potentially
093
      achieving a more fundamental and efficient defense mechanism.
094
095   We evaluate RDS on five LLMs and a series of harmful and benign query benchmarks. Experimental
096   results demonstrate that RDS outperforms existing approaches in terms of both security and help-
097   fulness, reducing compliance with harmful queries from 14.4% to 2.4% on Xstest (Röttger et al.,
098
      2023)) (without safety prompt) and increasing token generation speed by 2.12× ∼ 3.09× com-
      pared to other baselines. We hope this method offers a new perspective to security defense, i.e.,
099
      assessing the security of a problem from the decoding level, thereby achieving a root defense effect.

      2.1   LLM S ’ D ISCRIMINATIVE C APABILITY OF D ECODING
109
110   The prefill stage for LLMs typically includes a user query, often accompanied by prefixed or suffixed
111   elements such as system prompts. Previous study (Zheng et al., 2024) has demonstrated that LLMs
112   can discriminate between different types of prefill and use this ability to enhance safety mechanisms.
113   However, relying solely on prefill analysis for security evaluations presents significant limitations:
114   1) Jailbreaking behaviors often manifest in the model’s output, and focusing solely on prefill may
115   overlook these behaviors, compromising overall robustness; 2) Evaluation based purely on prefill
116
      places excessive dependence on the model’s initial discriminative capacity, and a single-stage eval-
      uation may lead to rejecting outputs prematurely, reducing the model’s utility.
117
118   To address these limitations, we explore whether LLMs can discriminate harmful from benign con-
119   tent during decoding, which encompasses both the prefill and the model’s generated outputs. If
120   LLMs can reliably evaluate the safety of their own outputs in real time, they can offer a more
121   comprehensive and proactive approach to security. Decoding-based defenses leverage the dynamic
122
      nature of model outputs, allowing for a more fundamental and continuous risk assessment. Follow-
      ing DRO (Zheng et al., 2024), we use the hidden states of the harmful and benign queries at the top
123
      layer of the model for classifier training. Details of the classifier’s training objective is provided as

      follows.

      Motivated by validating the capability to recognize responses, we propose RDS to ensure the safety
219
      of LLMs at the decoder level. The architecture of RDS is illustrated in Figure 3. We design a
220   step-by-step defense mechanism that directly corrects the harmful token into a safe token when

222   token generation. Benefitting from step-by-step safe generation and speculative decoding, RDS
223   achieves root security without compromising helpfulness and speed.

      3.3   S PECULATIVE DECODING
284
285   RDS leverages the discriminative ability of decoding for defense by computing the harmful score of
286   candidate tokens based on their hidden states. It concatenates decoding at step t − 1 with candidate
287   tokens to obtain the hidden state at step t resembling speculative decoding processes (such as EA-
288   GLE (Li et al., 2024)) that predict hidden states from decoding and tokens. To increase decoding
289   speed, RDS extends EAGLE’s resampling to accelerate generation.
290   Unlike traditional LLMs that compute hidden state through autoregressive decoding with multiple
291   Transformers blocks, RDS utilizes EAGLE Head to predict the hidden state hi at step ti , thereby
292   accelerating the inference process. This prediction is based on the candidate token and the hidden
293   state of decoding at step ti−1 . The hidden state in Equation (7) can be expressed as:

305   3.4   H IGHLIGHTS
306
307   As a decoder-oriented defense, the advantages of RDS are summarized as follows:
308   First, RDS demonstrates a root defense by leveraging the discriminative capabilities in LLMs’ de-
309   coding level. It fully utilizes the model’s understanding of context by evaluating the harmfulness
310   from both input and output dimensions. Guided by a classifier with fewer parameters, RDS iden-
311   tifies harmful tokens during the early inference stage and corrects them to safe tokens, thereby
312   reducing harmfulness in the output. Subsequent experimental results indicate that RDS can enhance
313   the model’s defensive capability without additional training for the LLMs.
314   Secondly, RDS adopts a step-by-step correction strategy by incrementally adjusting the token log-
315   its during the sampling process and progressively correcting harmful labels. Instead of relying on
316   single-point evaluations, RDS improves the safety of LLMs through multi-step evaluations, thereby
317   providing stronger assistance capabilities and a lower false alarm rate for user queries. Furthermore,
318   experiments demonstrate that RDS is more helpful than other methods on various safety bench-
319   marks, further indicating the transferability of RDS.
320   Finally, to enhance the reasoning speed of RDS and facilitate its practical implementation, we incor-
321   porate a speculative decoding structure into the resampling process. It leverages the advantages of
322   the step-by-step mechanism to accelerate the generation process. Experimental results demonstrate
323   that the token generation speed of RDS is approximately 2.12× ∼ 3.09× faster than that of the
      baselines. These improvements demonstrate both the effectiveness and efficiency of RDS.

      We evaluate the efficiency of baselines and RDS on the Custom (benign). We select one response

455   The inference speed of prefill-level defenses nearly matches that of the original LLMs. At the same

      for each LLM, which demands exceptionally high computational resources. Conversely, RDS em-

477   5.1   E XISTING D EFENSES
478
479   Existing safety defenses can be divided into input-based defenses and output-based defenses.
480   Prefill-level defenses induce LLMs to reject harmful questions by optimizing the input, such as
481   adding a safety system prompt or filtering the input. For instance, IAPrompt (Zhang et al., 2024b)
482   delves into the intent of input before decoding. Perplexity filtering (Alon & Kamfonas, 2023) pro-
483   poses to detect the adversarial suffixes as the signal of harmful input before generating a response.
484   Modifying the input on the encoder for defense can also be modified for attack (Zhao et al., 2024).
485   Modifying input for attacks is a deficient consumption and efficient behavior. At present, multiple
      methods have successfully carried out jailbreak attacks from user input, such as GCG (Zou et al.,

      5.2   S PECULATIVE D ECODING
519
520   Traditionally, token generation is performed step-by-step, where the model generates one token for
521   each step by autoregressive decoding. The generated token concatenated to the input serves as
522   the new input for the next step (Chen et al., 2023a). This approach is straightforward but can be
523   computationally expensive and slow, particularly when generating long text (Kim et al., 2023).
524   Speculative Decoding is an optimization technique used in LLMs to accelerate the process of token
525   generation (Leviathan et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023b). By the Draft-then-Verify paradigm, specu-
526   lative decoding generates multiple tokens at each step (Xia et al., 2024). For example, (Zhang et al.,
527   2024a) proposes to use the same serious but more minor LLM as the draft model without additional
528   training. Not all models have a smaller draft model; self-draft becomes a new paradigm instead
529   of using a separate draft model. For instance, Medusa (Cai et al., 2024) incorporates feedforward
530   neural heads atop the decoder to predict tokens in different positions in parallel.
531
532   6     C ONCLUSIONS
533
534   Our study delves into and confirms the discriminative capacity of LLMs at the decoder level.
535   Through preliminary validation, we indicate that LLMs consistently can discern the harmfulness
536   of output tokens at multiple steps. Motivated by these findings, we propose a Root Defense Strategy
537   originating from the decoding level, namely RDS. The incremental safe token generation process
538   enforces security measures. Furthermore, speculative decoding is introduced in RDS to enhance
539   usability and facilitate deployment. Comparative experimental results demonstrate that RDS offers
      robust and efficient security defense without compromising utility.

      A    ETHICS STATEMENT
703
704
      Our research (including papers, code, and data) may have potential adverse effects, as jailbreaks
705   could exploit our methods to target commercial APIs, creating harmful content or privacy violations.
706   Despite the risk of misuse, we consider the current work justifiable. As LLMs perform outstand-
707   ingly, researchers are presently concentrating on enhancing their helpfulness. Hence, investigating
708   and mitigating security vulnerabilities is crucial, as these models could be vulnerable to black-box
709   attacks when the exact specifics of the target model are undisclosed. The technology we have in-
710   troduced can effectively thwart such misuse and empower the Red Team to deploy efficiently ahead
711   of time, progressively achieving secure generation starting at the decoder level. In conclusion, our
712   study underscores the significance of security defense starting at the decoder level.
