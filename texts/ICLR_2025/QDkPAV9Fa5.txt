011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Knowledge distillation has been proven effective for compressing transformer ar-
014            chitectures by transferring knowledge from teacher to student models. Logits-
015            based methods of knowledge distillation cannot fully capture the intermediate
016            representations and features within the teacher model, which may result in the
017            student model not fully learning all the knowledge from the teacher model. Thus,
018            previous work focuses on transferring knowledge through intermediate features
019
               or attention maps. However, leveraging multi-head attention maps in transformers
               for knowledge distillation presents challenges due to head misalignment and sub-
020
               optimal feature alignment, often requiring projectors to align features or special
021
               modifications to the model architecture. To address above limitations, we pro-
022            pose the Squeezing-Heads Distillation (SHD) method. This method reduces the
023            number of attention maps to any desired number through linear approximation,
024            without requiring additional projectors or parameters. This facilitates better align-
025            ment and knowledge transfer between models with different numbers of heads,
026            enhancing both flexibility and efficiency. Experimental results demonstrate sig-
027            nificant improvements in both language and vision generative models, validating
028            the effectiveness of our method.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      In recent years, generative large models have experienced rapid growth, significantly impacting
034
      both NLP (e.g., GPT series[Brown (2020), Achiam et al. (2023)], LLaMA series[Touvron et al.
035
      (2023), Dubey et al. (2024)]) and computer vision domains (e.g., text-to-image generation[Esser
036   et al. (2024)], text-to-video generation[Blattmann et al. (2023)]). Despite their impressive capabili-
037   ties, these models typically involve a massive number of parameters, posing substantial challenges
038   for practical online applications.
039
      As the core of transformer models, the multi-head attention mechanism allows each head to attend
040
      to different parts of the input sequence, enabling the model to capture diverse and complex relation-
041
      ships between tokens. However, research such as Voita et al. (2019) has shown that only a small
042   subset of attention heads significantly contributes to performance, suggesting redundancy among
043   heads. By pruning the redundant heads, models can maintain their performance while reducing
044   complexity. Similarly, Michel et al. (2019) demonstrates that most attention heads can be removed
045   during testing without substantial performance degradation. Both studies and our observation3.1
046   suggest redundancy among multiple heads.
047
      While pruning is a powerful compression technique, knowledge distillation offers another approach
048   to model compression and performance improvement. Traditional knowledge distillation methods,
049   particularly those developed for CNNs before the transformer era, focus on transferring knowledge
050   through logits and intermediate feature maps. Techniques like DeiT[Touvron et al. (2021)] use a
051   distillation token, and Patient-KD[Sun et al. (2019)] transfers intermediate features. However, trans-
052   formers emphasize attention mechanisms, prompting research into distilling attention maps directly.
053   TinyBERT[Jiao et al. (2019b)] and MobileBERT[Sun et al. (2020)] both explore the distillation of
      attention maps but require special model designs of matching head numbers between teacher and


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      student models, which is alignment barriers in knowledge distillation for transformers. See a more
055   detailed description in our observation3.2.
056
057   We aim to design a practical method for generating multi-head attention map supervision from the
058
      teacher model during training, matching the student’s head number to facilitate fine-grained knowl-
      edge transfer. This approach addresses head number mismatch, coarse-grained attention transfer,
059
      and the need for additional projectors. By introducing a non-standard attention matrix, we can
060
      achieve lossless feature representation with fewer heads. However, as stated in observation3.3, the
061   unconstrained matrix lacks necessary attention knowledge for distillation.
062
063   In response to these challenges, we propose the Squeezing Multi-Heads Distillation method. This
064
      approach compresses multiple attention maps into a single attention map through efficient linear
      approximation, achieving fine-grained knowledge transfer between teacher and student models with
065
      different numbers of heads. The Squeezing Multi-Heads Distillation method offers several advan-
066
      tages. Flexibility: Unlike existing approaches that require matching attention head counts, our
067   method allows for models with varying numbers of heads, broadening its applicability across differ-
068   ent architectures. Fine-grained Attention Knowledge: it goes beyond simply transferring attention
069   features F ∈ RN ×D from the multi-head attention output, such as the Gram matrix F T F , which
070   provides a coarse approximation akin to single-head attention distillation. Efficiency: By com-
071   pressing multiple attention maps into a single map using linear approximation, our method reduces
072   computational overhead during distillation, improving overall efficiency compared with using tradi-
073   tional projectors with extra parameters.
074   The main contributions of this paper are as follows:
075
076          • We analyzed the behavior of multi-head compression using both unconstrained and con-
077            strained attention matrix approximations during training, and proposed an efficient Squeez-
078            ing Multi-Heads Distillation(SHD) method based on linear approximation to address re-
079            dundancy and alignment challenges in multi-head attention distillation.
080          • Our method provides a flexible and efficient solution for fine-grained knowledge transfer in
081            knowledge distillation, which can be seamlessly integrated into existing distillation frame-
082            works.
083          • We demonstrated the effectiveness of our method through comprehensive experiments on
084            both language and vision generative tasks across diverse settings.

      2.1   E FFICIENT M ULTI - HEAD ATTENTION
089
090   The transformer architecture has revolutionized the field of natural language processing and com-
091   puter vision, enabling the development of powerful models. One of the key components of the
092   transformer is the attention mechanism, which allows the model to focus on relevant parts of the
093   input sequence when making predictions. While inferencing these layers is often slow, due to the
094   memory-bandwidth cost of repeatedly loading the large ”keys” and ”values” tensors. MQA[Shazeer
095   (2019)] uses a single key-value head, drastically speeds up decoder inference, while GQA[Ainslie
096   et al. (2023)] introduce grouped-query attention to avoid quality degradation of MQA. Research
097
      such as [Voita et al. (2019)] and [Michel et al. (2019)] conduct a detailed analysis of the functional
      roles of individual heads within the transformer’s multi-head attention mechanism. Specifically,
098
      they assess whether certain heads are underperforming or redundant, and explore the feasibility of
099
      directly discarding these less contributive heads.
100

      2.2   K NOWLEDGE D ISTILLATION OF T RANSFORMER
102
103   Knowledge distillationHinton (2015) aims to train student networks by compressing or transferring
104   knowledge from teacher model to student model. There are two common methods in this field,
105   logits-based methods [Cho & Hariharan (2019), Furlanello et al. (2018), Mirzadeh et al. (2020),
106   Zhang et al. (2018), Zhao et al. (2022)] which convey knowledge on the logits level and hint-based
107   methods [Heo et al. (2019), Huang & Wang (2017), Kim et al. (2018), Park et al. (2019), Peng
      et al. (2019)] which convey knowledge through intermediate features. As an example of using both


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      above methods in knowledge distillation of transformer, DistillBERT(Sanh et al. (2019)) initializes
109   the student with teacher’s partial parameters, and minimized the soft target probabilities and co-
110   sine similarity of hidden states between the teacher and the student. Through Alishahi et al. (2019)
111   find that the attention weights learned by BERT can capture substantial linguistic knowledge, Tiny-
112   BERT(Jiao et al. (2019a)) propose the attention based distillation to encourage that the linguistic
113   knowledge can be transferred from teacher to student. MobileBERT(Sun et al. (2020)) train a spe-
114   cially designed inverted-bottleneck and bottleneck structures to keep their layer number and hidden
115   size the same for the teacher and the student, transferring knowledge through feature maps and self-
116   attention maps. MINILM(Wang et al. (2020)) introduce the scaled dot-product between values in
117
      the self-attention module as the new deep self-attention knowledge, in addition to the attention dis-
      tributions. However, the above work require that the number of attention heads must be same for the
118
      teacher and the student, which is not in line with reality.

123   3.1   O BSERVATION 1: H EAD R EDUNDANCY IN TRANSFORMERS
124
125   Multi-head attention is a very common technique that improves the performance of attention mech-
126   anisms in transformer models, leading to significant improvements. However, we observed that
127   different heads often capture similar or redundant attention pattern, as illustrated in Fig.1.
128   The most common patterns of attention maps are diagonal and vertical lines, indicating the impor-
129   tance of adjacent tokens or key elements. This phenomenon is prevalent in well-trained generative
130   models, especially the large ones with many heads. This suggests that the number of heads is redun-
131   dant to some degree.

150   3.2   O BSERVATION 2: A LIGNMENT BARRIERS IN K NOWLEDGE D ISTILLATION FOR
151         T RANSFORMERS
152
153   In recent literature, we have identified two major alignment barriers in knowledge distillation for
154   transformers.
155
      First, the significant dimensional gap between large and small transformer models makes feature
156   alignment less effective. Knowledge distillation in generative models faces challenges due to differ-
157   ences in hidden dimensions; large models typically have higher-dimensional hidden layers, making
158   feature alignment with smaller models difficult. For example, GPT-3[Brown (2020)]’s model di-
159   mension is 12,288, which is almost 20 times larger than that of GPT-3[Brown (2020)] Small. Some
160   methods introduce projection layers to align features[ Sun et al. (2020)], but these methods add
161   extra parameters and do not focus on the attention module. Consequently, most feature alignment
      knowledge distillation methods appear ineffective for generative models.


                                                       3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Second, transformer models often differ in the number of attention heads, leading to head alignment
163   problems. Previous methods like MobileBERT[ Sun et al. (2020)] employed specialized model
164   designs to circumvent the head alignment issue, but this required both teacher and student models
165   to be specifically engineered, potentially reducing performance. To our knowledge, we are the first
166   to directly address the head alignment problem of attention maps in knowledge distillation.
167

172   DiT models have d = 64 per head but are trained on images with 64 × 64 = 4096 tokens. This also
173   occurs in most generative models.
174   Reviewing how the attention map of a single head is computed:

      4     S QUEEZING H EADS : A B ETTER S UPERVISION B EYOND J UST L OGITS
197
198
      Traditional knowledge distillation algorithms primarily focus on feature distillation. However, the
      gap in hidden dimensions between teacher and student transformer models makes feature alignment
199
      challenging. Attention maps provide a better supervision signal since they are independent of hidden
200
      dimensions. Yet, teachers and students often have different numbers of heads due to computational
201   constraints in model design. Our method addresses this issue by improving knowledge transfer. We
202   propose a technique that compresses diverse multi-head attention into a single attention map while
203   preserving key information, which can then be used as supervision for knowledge distillation. Ini-
204   tially, we introduce lossless unconstrained attention compression and optimal constrained attention
205   compression based on teacher features, but these methods are computationally inefficient for every
206   training iteration. We then present our “squeezing heads” method, which compresses attention maps
207   by efficient linear approximation and achieves strong results in practice.

517   6   C ONCLUSION
518
519   In this work, we introduce the Squeezing-Heads Distillation (SHD) method, a novel approach to
520   knowledge distillation that addresses the challenges posed by head misalignment and redundancy
521   in multi-head attention mechanisms of transformer models. Our method effectively compresses
522   multiple attention maps into a single map through a linear search process, enabling better alignment
523   and knowledge transfer between models with different numbers of heads. This approach not only
524
      enhances the flexibility and efficiency of the distillation process but also improves the performance
      of student models across various generative tasks.
525
526   We validate the effectiveness of SHD through comprehensive experiments on both image generation
527   and language pretraining tasks. Our results demonstrate significant improvements in key metrics
528   such as FID, IS, and accuracy, outperforming traditional knowledge distillation methods and other
529   representation distillation techniques. The proposed method also proves to be robust across different
530
      model sizes and architectures, showing its general applicability.
531   Furthermore, our ablation studies confirm that SHD provides a more stable and efficient improve-
532   ment compared to hard selecting or constant merging of heads. The introduction of attention temper-
533   ature further enhances the distillation process by softening the output probabilities, leading to better
534   performance in student models. Notably, SHD achieves these improvements without introducing
535   additional training parameters or significantly impacting training speed.
536   In conclusion, the Squeezing-Heads Distillation method offers a practical and effective solution for
537   optimizing knowledge distillation in transformers, enabling the deployment of smaller, more effi-
538   cient models without compromising on performance. This work paves the way for further research
539   into flexible and efficient distillation techniques that can adapt to the diverse architectures of modern
      large-scale models.
