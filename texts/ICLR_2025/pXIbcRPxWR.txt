                                                 A BSTRACT
008
009
               Large Language Models (LLMs) have revolutionized natural language process-
010
               ing and hold immense potential for advancing Artificial Intelligence. However,
011
               the core architecture of most mainstream LLMs—the Transformer—has inherent
012            limitations in computational depth, rendering them theoretically incapable of solv-
013            ing many reasoning tasks that demand increasingly deep computations. Chain of
014            Thought (CoT) prompting has emerged as a technique to address these architec-
015            tural limitations, as evidenced by several theoretical studies. It offers a promis-
016            ing approach to solving complex reasoning tasks that were previously beyond the
017            capabilities of these models. Despite its successes, CoT and its variants (such
018            as Tree of Thought, Graph of Thought, etc.) rely on a “one-prompt-for-all” ap-
019            proach, using a single prompt structure (e.g., “think step by step”) for a wide
020
               range of tasks—from counting and sorting to solving mathematical and algorith-
               mic problems. This approach poses significant challenges for models to generate
021
               the correct reasoning steps, as the model must navigate through a vast prompt tem-
022
               plate space to find the appropriate template for each task. In this work, we build
023            upon previous theoretical analyses of CoT to demonstrate how the one-prompt-
024            for-all approach can negatively affect the computability of LLMs. We partition
025            the solution process into two spaces: the prompt space and the answer space. Our
026            findings show that task-specific supervision is essential for navigating the prompt
027            space accurately and achieving optimal performance. Through experiments with
028            state-of-the-art LLMs, we reveal a gap in reasoning performance when supervi-
029            sion is applied versus when it is not. Our goal is to provide deeper insights into
030            the mechanisms underlying CoT, offering guidance for the effective design of CoT
031
               variants. Additionally, we underscore the limitations of traditional “unsupervised”
               prompting methods, arguing that users of CoT cannot simply “sit back” and rely
032
               entirely on the model. Instead, we advocate for task-specific “supervised” CoT,
033
               enriched with human knowledge, to enable more effective reasoning in LLMs.

036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   The advent of large language models (LLMs) (Achiam et al., 2023) has ushered in a new era for nat-
039   ural language processing and artificial intelligence (Kojima et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2023). These
040   models exhibit remarkable capabilities across various domains (Thirunavukarasu et al., 2023; Wei
041   et al., 2022; Valmeekam et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023), achieving near-human performance in tasks
042   such as knowledge retrieval and articulation (Chang et al., 2024). However, concerns have been
043   raised regarding their reasoning abilities (Valmeekam et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2024). These tasks
044   range from fundamental operations like counting, sorting, and multiplication (Dziri et al., 2024), to
045
      more complex challenges such as mathematical problem-solving, algorithm design, and coding (Xu
      et al., 2022; Thirunavukarasu et al., 2023). Previous research has explored several factors contribut-
046
      ing to these reasoning deficiencies, including training optimizations (Thorburn & Kruger, 2022),
047
      tokenization methods (Singh & Strouse, 2024), and dataset choices (Ye et al., 2024). Among these,
048   the architecture of the model plays a pivotal role in determining its reasoning capabilities (Raghu
049   et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2024; Delétang et al., 2022). The backbone architecture of most main-
050   stream LLMs—the Transformer (with finite precision) (Vaswani, 2017)—has intrinsic limitations
051   related to computational depth (Li et al., 2024). Specifically, the attention mechanism within Trans-
052   formers can perform only a fixed number of sequential computational steps (Li et al., 2024; Zhang
053   et al., 2024; Sanford et al., 2024; Dehghani et al., 2018), leading to constant-depth modeling (Li
      et al., 2024). As a result, when relying solely on the Transformer’s internal reasoning, the model’s

      The emergence of Chain of Thought (CoT) (Wei et al., 2022) has transformed the computational lim-
079   itations imposed by architectural design. Specifically, it overcomes the constant depth limitation of
080   a model’s internal (latent space) reasoning by extending the reasoning process into the “text” space
081   through prompting (Li et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024; Feng et al., 2024). As demonstrated by both
082   theoretical (Li et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024; Feng et al., 2024) and empirical research (Li et al.,
083   2024; Zhang et al., 2024), CoT effectively enhances the reasoning depth of Transformer-based mod-
084   els (with finite precision), achieving “Turing Completeness” under ideal conditions (Li et al., 2024;
085   Zhang et al., 2024). While the theoretical analysis of CoT focuses on the model’s upper bound com-
086   putational power, which may not always align with real-world performance, a deep understanding of
087
      the CoT mechanism—particularly how it breaks architectural constraints—is crucial for designing
      better prompts that maximize the computational potential of real-world LLMs. This understanding
088
      also forms the basis for our analysis of “supervised” CoT and our prompt search space theories.
089
      Therefore, our work first revisits the underlying mechanisms of CoT from a computational perspec-
090   tive, integrating insights from prior research (Li et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024; Feng et al., 2024)
091   to demystify them in a clear, yet comprehensive manner, enriched by our unique perspective.
092
093
      Although theoretical analysis has proven the existence of solutions for (almost) any problem using
      CoT, based on computability and Turing Completeness theory, the actual discovery of those solu-
094
      tions can be much more challenging. This is akin to how a Turing machine can model solutions
095
      for any problem (Boolos et al., 2002) but finding the exact Turing machine for a specific NP prob-
096   lem could be difficult. These challenges arise from two main factors for LLMs with CoT. First, the
097   model must develop the correct “step-by-step” template, which essentially embodies the algorithm
098   used for solving the problem (Figure 1.a-b). For instance, the “steps” for solving a graph search
099   problem using depth-first search (DFS) differ from those of a breadth-first search (BFS) algorithm.
100   Second, even after the template (algorithm) is established, finding the solution might require ex-
101   tensive reasoning and exploration to achieve the optimal outcomes. For example, using the BFS
102   template to locate a target node in a tree involves traversing multiple paths in the search space that
103   can be computationally expensive and error-prone.
104   The vanilla design of CoT is “unsupervised”, meaning that the model generates its step template
105   without task-specific supervision from humans. Specifically, when prompted to “think step by step”,
106   LLMs autonomously generate a step template (algorithm) it needs to follow—for instance, generat-
107   ing previously visited paths at each step—and then proceeding to search for answers based on this
      self-generated template (Figure 1.a). Clearly, this naive CoT approach can lead to poor performance,

      Variants of Chain of Thought, such as Tree-of-Thought (Yao et al., 2024) and Graph-of-

      than the prompt space, and remain unsupervised. These “X-of-thought” approaches still rely on a

131   more effectively. For instance, Graph-of-Thought encourages the model to frequently revisit previ-

134   generated by the model and can be poorly suited to the problem (Figure 1.a), especially when task-
135   specific supervision (guidance) is lacking.
136   In this work, we thoroughly investigate the distinction between prompt space and answer space in
137   the CoT process. Building on insights from previous theoretical analyses of CoT (Li et al., 2024),
138   we explore why “supervision” is necessary and how it can be provided to guide the model in finding
139   the optimal steps. We conduct extensive experiments on structured reasoning tasks, demonstrating
140   that task-specific “supervised” CoT is crucial for achieving optimal solutions and highlighting the
141   performance gap when supervision is used versus when it is not. Our work is the first of its kind
142   to focus on prompt space exploration and offers valuable insights into understanding and designing
143
      effective prompt techniques for reasoning tasks.

      2.1   L IMITATIONS OF T RANSFORMER A RCHITECTURE
154
      Transformers, unlike recurrent networks, are not designed to perform reasoning over an arbitrary
155
      number of sequential steps (depth) internally. Specifically, in a Transformer model, the hidden state
156   ht-1 at time step t-1 is not reused when calculating ht (Figure 2.b), as it would be in recurrent
157   networks like RNN (Figure 2.a). Instead, the hidden state h is passed forward only through the
158   layers of the Transformer (Dehghani et al., 2018) (Figure 1.c), not through time, which means
159   that the number of sequential steps is fixed and limited for any given Transformer architecture (Li
160   et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024; Elbayad et al., 2019). In contrast, Recurrent Neural Networks
161   (RNNs) (Grossberg, 2013) allow the hidden state h to be passed through time steps via recurrent
      connections (Figure 2.a), enabling sequential computation over h through an arbitrary number of


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      input tokens. This capability allows RNNs to perform deeper reasoning over h, which is essential
163   for solving complex tasks (Zhang et al., 2024).
164
165   The hidden state h plays a crucial role in reasoning, as it stores both reasoning memory and in-
166
      termediate reasoning results (Zhang et al., 2024). The ability to sequentially compute and update
      h over time allows a model to build reasoning depth, which is necessary for addressing complex
167
      problems. This depth advantage provided by recurrent connections cannot be replicated by autore-
168
      gressive models. Autoregressive models, instead of passing the hidden state ht forward, pass the
169   generated token yt . However, y cannot replace the role of h for the following reasons: y is a discrete
170   value extracted from h and only contains partial information (Figure 2.b), making it insufficient for
171   continued reasoning in many tasks. y exists outside the latent space where h operates (Figure 2.b),
172   meaning it cannot be used for computation in the same way that h can (Zhang et al., 2024).
173
174
175   2.2   NATURE OF R EASONING
176
177   Reasoning inherently requires sequential depth. For tasks with input of length n, reasoning is typi-
178   cally performed step by step to arrive at the final result. Examples include counting (incrementing a
179
      counter iteratively), playing chess (updating the board state iteratively), and searching (marking vis-
      ited nodes iteratively). To solve a given task, there is a theoretical lower bound on the required depth
180
      of computation (Sanford et al., 2024). Since models like Transformers can only perform a constant
181
      number of sequential reasoning steps over the hidden state h, they are unable to solve reasoning
182   tasks where the depth requirement increases with the length of the input.
183
184   Consider chess as an example. For a sequence of chess moves, xn = (x1 , x2 , . . . , xn ), to validate
185
      the n-th move, the n-th board state hn must be calculated. This requires n sequential computations,
      as the n-th board state depends not only on the sequence of moves x but also on the previous
186
      board state hn-1 . While a neural network could memorize the mapping from xn to the correct
187
      h (Arpit et al., 2017), bypassing the need for sequential computation, memorization is much more
188   resource-intensive than reasoning. This is because memorization would require storing all possible
189   permutations of xn and their corresponding resulted board states, an exponential challenge that
190   eventually demands infinite memory to store instances of arbitrary length.
191
      Thus, in the example of simulating a chess game, the model’s internal representation h, which
192
      encodes the board state, must be sequentially computed n times to simulate the game. Transformers,
193
      which lack the infinite precision needed for memorization, cannot perform such tasks, as their hidden
194   states h are computed a fixed number of times, regardless of the input length.
195

197   2.3   C OT + AUTOREGRESSIVE = R ECURRENT
198
199   As previous studies have shown Li et al. (2024); Zhang et al. (2024); Feng et al. (2024), Chain of
200   Thought (CoT) effectively bridges the gap between autoregressive Liang et al. (2022); Liu et al.
201   (2022) models and recurrent structures Zhang et al. (2024) within large language models (LLMs).
202   Instead of merely outputting tokens to answer questions, CoT also generates intermediate steps
203   which are not part of the answers. These intermediate steps, represented as a sequence of natural
204
      language tokens (o1 , o2 , . . . , ok ), act as a discretization of the latent information hn (Figure 2.c).
      Given that natural language is a powerful medium for encoding nearly any type of information, h
205
      is effectively transformed into a token sequence o, which is then converted back into a vector h
206
      via the embedding layer. In this way, computational information is preserved through a process of
207                                                                       discritization              vectorization
208
      discretization followed by vectorization, represented as: ht =======⇒ (o1 , o2 , · · · , ok ) =======⇒
209   ht+1 (Figure 2.c). This approach, effectively achieve the same effect as ht ⇒ ht+1 in the RNN-like
210   recurrent network, allowing h to be recurrently updated by the network.
211   In the earlier chess example, the LLM generates intermediate reasoning steps as natural language
212   strings during the CoT process. Specifically, it produces a sequence of tokens (e.g., in English) to
213   describe the board state hk after the first k moves, detailing the positions of pieces such as the bishop
214   and the king. In the subsequent computation, the LLM reads this board description up to move k and
215   uses it to calculate the k+1-th board state, thereby avoiding the need to re-compute the reasoning
      from scratch—something Transformers cannot do internally due to their non-recurrent architecture.

      In conclusion, LLMs with CoT effectively extend the reasoning process from the model’s internal
217   latent space H to a natural language-based token space O. Thanks to the powerful encoding ability
218   of natural language, intermediate reasoning steps are encoded and stored in text form, which the
219   model can reuse in subsequent computations. This approach significantly increases the model’s rea-
220   soning depth to T (n), where T (n) is the number of CoT steps performed. Under ideal theoretical
221   conditions—such as infinite CoT steps and perfect information conversion between latent and text
222   space—LLMs with CoT can achieve Turing completeness, theoretically solving any problem, in-
223   cluding those beyond symbolic tasks (e.g. recognizing regular languages). This theoretical analysis
224   provides strong guidance for designing effective “supervised” CoT approaches, which we introduce
225
      in subsequent sections.

      While theory suggests CoT-augmented LLMs can solve any problem Li et al. (2024), finding solu-
231
      tions in practice is much harder. CoT is limited by a finite number of steps, and the conversion from
232   latent states h to token sequences o is imperfect. Consequently, only partial information is extracted
233   at each step, making it crucial to identify the right data to continue the correct computation. We
234   decompose the CoT reasoning into two components: template search within the prompt space and
235   answer search within the answer space. We show how effective navigation of the prompt space can
236   simplify answer space complexity and reveal limitations of unsupervised “X-of-thought” methods.
237
238
239   3.1   P ROMPT S PACE
240
241   The latent vector h contains rich intermediate information when processing a task, including coun-
242   ters, sums, flags for binary indicators, and more. When LLMs are prompted to “think step by step”
243   along with the task instance, they generate a step template, specifying which information from h
244   to extract and discretize into tokens (o1 , o2 , . . . , ok ). Ideally, as k→∞—meaning the length of
245   the CoT is arbitrarily long—all vectorized information in h can be fully textualized, achieving true
246
      recurrence through autoregression. However, with limited k, only partial information is discretized.
247   If we define the amount of information stored in h as m bits, and each CoT step extracts up to s bits
248   of information into o, each unique step template specifies a way to extract s bits from the full m-bit
                                                                                    m!
249   space. Thus, the total number of potential step templates is C(m, s) = s!(m−s)!     , which estimates
250   the number of ways information can be extracted via CoT at each step. Each template defines an
251   extraction of unique s bits of information.
252   For example, in the chess simulation case, h encodes details such as the <current board
253   layout>, <the next player>, <board status>, <number of pieces taken
254   by each player> and so on. When given the instruction to “think step by step”, the model
255   decides which information to extract based on the step template it generates. Extracting the wrong
256   information might hinder reasoning in subsequent steps as recurrence can not be effectively per-
257   formed on the needed information.
258   The prompt search complexity C(m, s) depends on both m, the total information in h, and s, the
259   amount of information each CoT step can extract. If a model is sufficiently trained, the total amount
260   of encoded information in h is proportional to the dimension size of h (Allen-Zhu & Li, 2023), d,
261   denoted by m ∝ d. In this context, m represents the size of the search space, while s correlates with
262   the length of CoT tokens o, as longer CoT steps tend to extract more information from h. Thus,
263   s serves as the search step size. In practice, step template search is not entirely random. Models
264   often find relevant templates using heuristics, which significantly reduces the search complexity of
265   C(m, s). However, identifying the optimal template remains challenging, and using an incorrect
266
      template can severely degrade performance, as demonstrated in our experiments.
267   In conclusion, the step (prompt) template defines how information is extracted and used recurrently
268   in the CoT process. Finding the correct template is equivalent to discovering the algorithm for
269   solving a given task, determining what information is needed at each step and how it should be used
      to compute the next state.

314   3.3   C OT AS AN U NSUPERVISED TASK S OLVER
315
316
      CoT operates in an unsupervised manner for any given task, relying on a single universal prompt,
317
      Think Step by Step, and leaving it to the model to generate its own step template p ∈ P for
318   extracting information at each step. Since humans do not supervise step completion, the generation
319   of steps—i.e., determining which information to extract from h and compute recurrently—comes
320   primarily from the model’s heuristics. For example, in counting tasks, LLMs use learned heuristics
321   to extract a Counter value from h and perform recurrent updates. However, these unsupervised,
322   heuristic-driven templates are often unreliable, as the model lacks the knowledge to identify key
323   components for computation, as demonstrated in previous work Valmeekam et al. (2022) and our
      experiments.

      4     E XPERIMENTS
359
360
      In this section, we conduct experiments to demonstrate the importance of supervision in the CoT
      process. Specifically, we design scenarios where the correct step template is provided through su-
361
      pervision, and compare them to cases where incorrect steps are simulated by the model. Our results
362
      show significant performance degradation when the step templates are incorrectly derived, high-
363   lighting the need for human supervision to ensure reliable task performance with LLMs.
364
365   The objective of our experiments is not to evaluate the reasoning performance of different LLMs, but
366
      to emphasize the critical role that “supervision” plays in CoT. Comparing the abilities of various
      models is beyond the scope of this work.

370
      Although we used chess simulation as an example of reasoning with CoT due to its resemblance to
371

482   To explain this further, when a step template is incorrectly specified (e.g., outputting the sum up to

      5     S UPERVISED C OT: U SERS ’ P ERSPECTIVE
507
508   5.1   H OW TO S UPERVISE ?
509
510
      As we’ve demonstrated, providing correct supervision is crucial for helping the model achieve ac-
      curate results. A natural question arises: how can effective supervision be derived? The key to good
511
      supervision lies in understanding CoT’s underlying mechanism, which essentially involves relaying
512
      information through the text space. For tasks requiring multiple steps, users need to identify what
513   each step is and what key information should be extracted at each step. While this might seem
514   straightforward in the basic reasoning tasks used in our experiments, it becomes more complex for
515   challenging tasks, where correctly identifying the information requires careful task analysis. There-
516   fore, human knowledge is critical for enhancing the model’s computational abilities and can directly
517   influence task success. However, this supervision adds substantial workload, as each task demands
518   a unique understanding of its computational structure.
519
520   5.2   W HEN TO S UPERVISE ?
521
522   As we’ve observed, using an incorrect step template—whether model-derived or human-
523   injected—can result in significant performance degradation. Based on this, it’s important to avoid
524   providing supervision unless you are reasonably confident that the steps will not hinder the reasoning
525   process. In cases of uncertainty, it may be better to rely on the model’s own heuristics.

      6     C ONCLUSIONS
529
530   Our work offers a unique perspective on the mechanics of Chain of Thought (CoT) prompting and
531
      its role in enhancing model reasoning. Through theoretical analysis and practical insights, we show
      how CoT transforms latent information into text space, enabling iterative and resumable reasoning
532
      steps that expand a model’s computational depth. We further connect the model’s problem-solving
533
      capabilities with the complexities of finding solutions. Our analysis of prompt space and answer
534   space underscores the importance of identifying the correct step template to simplify navigation—an
535   often overlooked aspect in prompt-related research. The success of CoT hinges not only on gener-
536   ating steps but on extracting the right information at each stage. Our experiments demonstrate that
537   incorrect step templates can severely impact reasoning, reinforcing the importance of supervision.
538   Even small errors in template selection can lead to significant failures. Our findings combine the-
539   oretical analysis and experimental evidence, offering valuable insights into CoT’s limitations and
      potential for improving reasoning tasks in large language models.

      A.1   E XPERIMENTAL D ESIGN
641
      Our experimental setup carefully addresses potential pitfalls that could influence the model’s per-
642   formance, specifically focusing on tokenization and context length. Tokenization issues can sig-
643   nificantly affect how models handle specific tasks, often leading to failures not tied to the model’s
644   reasoning ability. To counter this, we reformatted task instances to eliminate tokenization biases.
645   Moreover, LLMs often struggle with retrieving information from long contexts, leading to hallu-
646   cinations or forgotten data during extended reasoning processes. This tends to degrade accuracy,
647   as models fail to maintain accurate references to the initial task elements throughout longer se-
      quences. While these challenges are important in real-world applications of LLMs, they are outside

                                                       12
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


648
      the scope of our investigation, which prioritizes analyzing the effect of using different step template.
649   To maintain a controlled environment, we restrict task lengths from 20 to 35 elements. This thresh-
650   old was determined from preliminary analysis, where longer task sequences often introduced issues
651   not related to reasoning but to the model’s internal optimization process. When the task sequence
652   exceeds 35 steps, models can divide output over multiple contexts, which distorts accurate informa-
653   tion retrieval. By maintaining a manageable length, we isolate and evaluate the differences between
654   reasoning with and without CoT, avoiding disruptions caused by excessive context length. For each
655   task, we generate 50 instances using a pre-written script and the results are examined by humans.
