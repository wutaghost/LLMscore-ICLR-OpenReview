009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            In this paper, we aim to model 3D scene geometry, appearance, and physical infor-
012            mation just from dynamic multi-view videos in the absence of any human labels.
013            By leveraging physics-informed losses as soft constraints or integrating simple
014            physics models into neural networks, existing works often fail to learn complex
015            motion physics, or doing so requires additional labels such as object types or
016            masks. In this paper, we propose a new framework named GVFi to model the
017
               motion physics of complex dynamic 3D scenes. The key novelty of our approach
               is that, by formulating each 3D point as a rigid particle with size and orienta-
018
               tion in space, we choose to directly learn a translation rotation dynamics system
019
               for each particle, explicitly estimating a complete set of physical parameters to
020            govern the particle’s motion over time. Extensive experiments on three exist-
021            ing dynamic datasets and two newly created challenging synthetic and real-world
022            datasets demonstrate the extraordinary performance of our method over baselines
023            in the task of future frame extrapolation. A nice property of our framework is that
024            multiple objects or parts can be easily segmented just by clustering the learned
025            physical parameters. Our datasets and code will be released at https://github.com/.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Regarding our daily dynamic 3D scenes such as falling balls, rotating fans, and folding chairs, pre-
      cisely modeling their geometry, appearance, and physical properties, and further predicting their
031
      future states are crucial for emerging applications in robotics, mixed reality, and embodied AI. With
032
      the advancement of recent 3D representations such as NeRF (Mildenhall et al., 2020) and 3DGS
033   (Kerbl et al., 2023), a plethora of works (Pumarola et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024)
034   have been proposed to model various dynamic 3D scenes, achieving excellent performance in inter-
035   polating novel views within the observed time. However, they often fail to extrapolate future frames,
036   fundamentally because they cannot learn the underlying physics priors of complex 3D scenes.
037
      To learn physics priors, existing methods mainly consist of two categories: 1) physics-informed
038
      neural network (PINN) based methods (Raissi et al., 2019) which integrate the governing partial
039
      differential equations (PDEs) into loss functions to drive neural networks to learn physically plau-
040
      sible dynamic 3D scenes such as floating smoke (Chu et al., 2022) and simple moving objects (Li
041
      et al., 2023b). Although demonstrating promising results in modeling 3D geometry and physics
042   such as velocity and viscosity, these methods usually need boundary constraints such as accurate
043   object/foreground masks which may not always be available in practice. In addition, adding PINN
044   losses is not a free lunch, but significantly sacrificing the efficiency in training and accuracy at
045   boundary regions. 2) Physics model based methods (Jonathan et al., 2020; Zhong et al., 2024; Whit-
046   ney et al., 2024) which encode various physics systems into neural networks to model elastic objects,
047   fluids, etc.. Thanks to the explicit physics priors, these methods obtain impressive results in physical
048   properties learning and simulation. Nevertheless, they are often limited to specific types of objects,
049   materials, or motions due to the lack of generality of encoded physics priors, thus being unable to
050
      predict future motions of complex dynamic 3D objects and scenes.
051   In this paper, we aim to introduce a new framework to model dynamic 3D scenes just from multi-
052   view RGB videos, without needing any additional human labels such as object types or masks,
053   ultimately being able to predict future frames viewing from arbitrary angles. Among various phys-
      ical properties of a dynamic 3D scene, following the recent work NVFi (Li et al., 2023a), we also

      choose to learn a velocity field as it directly governs 3D scene movement. However, to accurately
062
      learn the physical velocity from RGB videos is extremely challenging, essentially due to the lack of
063   sufficient physics constraints from raw color pixels. This problem is even harder when multiple ob-
064   jects or parts are undergoing rather different motion patterns. For example, regarding two adjacent
065   objects moving in opposite directions in 3D space, the velocity of neighboring 3D surface points at
066   the intersection region tends to have particularly distinct patterns. This means that the latent rep-
067   resentation of per-point dynamics in 3D space could be discrete in nature. Therefore, it is more
068   desirable to model per-point dynamics independently, thus every point having its unique motion.
069   For generality, we regard each 3D point in space as a rigid particle with its size and orientation. If
070   its size is zero, the rigid particle degenerates to a point.
071   With this insight, for each rigid particle in space, we propose to learn an independent dynamics
072   system that includes a complete set of physical parameters to govern its motion over time. According
073   to the laws of classical mechanics, for a specific rigid particle traversing 3D space over time, its
074   motion can always be regarded as a rotational movement about a rotation center which has its own
075   translation. Given this general foundation, we choose to learn a translation rotation dynamics system
076   for each rigid particle, allowing its future motion to be derived accordingly. Alongside learning the
077
      core dynamics, we must also model the geometry and appearance of 3D scenes. In this paper,
      we naturally choose the recent 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) (Kerbl et al., 2023) as our scene
078
      representation, thanks to its unprecedented fidelity in reconstruction and its particle (a Gaussian
079
      kernel) based representation in nature, which shares the basic concept of our defined rigid particle.
080
081   As illustrated in Figure 1, our framework consists of two major components: 1) a 3D scene repre-
082   sentation module to learn dynamic scene geometry and appearance at a canonical timestamp, which
083
      is implemented by a vanilla 3DGS (Kerbl et al., 2023), though other variants can be adopted as well;
      2) a translation rotation dynamics system module to learn a full set of physical parameters for
084
      each input rigid particle, which is just realized by multilayer perceptrons (MLPs). Based on these
085
      system parameters, the rigid particle’s velocity is then derived according to the laws of classical
086   mechanics, without needing additional physics priors such as PINN (Raissi et al., 2019) in training.
087
088   The key to our framework is the second module which simply regards each 3D Gaussian kernel as
089
      a rigid particle and takes it as input into MLPs. Nevertheless, we empirically find that it is hard to
      optimize this module due to the inaccuracy and instability of Gaussian kernels regressed at early
090
      training epochs. To tackle this issue, we simply train an auxiliary deformation field in parallel with
091
      our second module using an existing work such as (Yang et al., 2024) and (Wu et al., 2024).
092
093   Different from current works for modeling dynamic scenes, including NeRF-based methods, e.g.,
094   D-NeRF(Pumarola et al., 2021)/TiNeuVox(Fang et al., 2022)/HexPlane(Cao & Johnson, 2023), and
095   3DGS-based methods such as DefGS (Yang et al., 2024), 4DGS (Wu et al., 2024), and E-D3DGS
096   (Bae et al., 2024), our core novelty is the introduced translation rotation dynamics system together
097   with its effective optimization strategy, which allows us to truly learn physical parameters, ultimately
098   achieving future frame extrapolation. By comparison, all those existing methods fail to do so, though
099   they perform well for past frame interpolation, as extensively verified in Tables 1&2.
100
      Our method, named GVFi, leverages 3D Gaussians to model scene geometry and appearance, while
101   learning velocity fields via estimating translation rotation dynamics systems. Our contributions are:
102
103   • We introduce a new framework to model motion physics of complex dynamic 3D scenes, without
104
        needing prior knowledge of object shapes, types, or masks.
105   • We propose to learn a translation rotation dynamics system for each 3D rigid particle, thus allow-
106
        ing the velocity field to be derived without needing additional physics constraints in training.
107   • We demonstrate superior results in future frame extrapolation on three existing datasets, and two
        newly collected synthetic and real-world datasets with extremely challenging dynamics.

      2   R ELATED W ORKS
109
110
      3D Shape Representations: Static 3D objects and scenes are traditionally represented by voxels,
111
      point clouds, meshes, etc., but they usually have limited representation capabilities due to the nature
112   of discretization. Recently, implicit representations have been developed in the literature, includ-
113   ing occupancy fields (OF) (Mescheder et al., 2019; Chen & Zhang, 2019), un/signed distance fields
114   (U/SDF) (Park et al., 2019; Chibane et al., 2020), and radiance fields (NeRF) (Mildenhall et al.,
115   2020). Although demonstrating excellent performance in novel view synthesis and shape recon-
116   struction, they are time-consuming to render 2D images or extract 3D shapes due to the integration
117   of their continuous coordinate-based representations. To tackle this issue, the very recent 3D Gaus-
118   sian Splatting (Kerbl et al., 2023) turns to represent a 3D shape as a set of explicit Gaussian ker-
119   nels with various properties, achieving real-time rendering speed. In our framework, we adopt this
120
      particle-based representation, as it is amenable to our particle-based physics learning framework.
121   Dynamic 3D Reconstruction: Recent advances in dynamic 3D reconstruction primarily follow the
122   development of static 3D techniques such as SDF, NeRF, and Gaussian Splatting. To model the
123   temporal relationship, existing works (Tretschk et al., 2021; Li et al., 2021; Barron et al., 2021;
124   Gao et al., 2021; Tian et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Cao & Johnson, 2023; Fridovich-Keil et al.,
125   2023; Cai et al., 2022; Fang et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022; Park et al., 2021; You & Hou, 2023; Du
126
      et al., 2021; Park et al., 2023; Xian et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2024b) usually add
      the time dimension into static 3D representations to learn a motion or deformation field for rigid or
127
      deformable objects and scenes. Despite achieving excellent performance in novel view synthesis,
128
      especially when integrating 3DGS as the backbone (Wu et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024; Li et al.,
129   2024; Lei et al., 2024; Lin et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024), these works can only interpolate 2D views
130   within the observed time, instead of predicting physically meaningful future frames. Basically, this
131   is because the commonly learned motion or deformation field does not encode physics priors in
132   nature, but just fits the correlation between pixels. In this paper, the key difference between these
133   works and us is that we separately learn translation rotation dynamics systems for 3D rigid particles,
134   thus enabling us to estimate physically meaningful future frames, whereas they cannot.
135
      3D Physics Learning: To learn various physical properties for 3D objects and scenes, the recent
136
      physics-informed neural networks (PINN) (Raissi et al., 2019; Mishra & Molinaro, 2023; Raissi
137
      et al., 2020; Hao et al., 2023; Baieri et al., 2023; Chalapathi et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024; Zhao
138   et al., 2024) are widely applied to convert PDEs into loss functions as soft constraints, driving neural
139   networks to learn physically meaningful targets. However, it is often inefficient to train PINNs due
140   to the large amount of data samples needed to regularize, and the soft constraints are usually not
141   sufficient to obtain satisfactory results. In this paper, we do not rely on such inefficient PINN losses
142   to incorporate physics priors to train neural networks. Another line of works (Qiao et al., 2022; Deng
143   et al., 2023; Xue et al., 2023; Franz et al., 2023; Whitney et al., 2024) integrate explicit physics
144   systems such as springs, graphs, etc., into the learning process to model elastic objects (Zhong et al.,
145   2024; Zhang et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024a), fluids (Jonathan et al., 2020; Lienen et al., 2024), etc.,
146   achieving impressive results in physics learning and simulation. In this paper, we also opt to learn
147   physics systems. However, the core difference is that we learn a translation rotation dynamics system
148   which is applicable to common deformation and transformation dynamics, whereas existing works
149   often learn a spring or fluid system only applicable to elastic objects or fluids.

      3   GVF I
152
153
      Our framework mainly comprises two modules together with an auxiliary deformation field to model
154
      3D geometry, appearance, and physics. Given dynamic multi-view RGB videos with known camera
155
      poses and intrinsics, the 3D scene representation module aims to learn a set of 3D Gaussian kernels
156   to represent the 3D scene geometry and appearance in a canonical space. The auxiliary deformation
157   field is designed to predict the translation and distortion of each Gaussian kernel given the current
158   training time t. For these two components, we simply follow the design of existing works (Kerbl
159   et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024) briefly elaborated in Section 3.1. Notably, the deformation field alone
160   cannot extrapolate frames beyond the training time. Our core module of the translation rotation
161   dynamics system aims to learn a set of physical parameters for each 3D rigid particle, governing its
      motion dynamics over time, which is detailed in Section 3.2.

198   3.2   T RANSLATION ROTATION DYNAMICS S YSTEM
199
200   This module aims to learn physical parameters that govern the motion of 3D scenes. However, the
201   dynamics of an entire space are extremely complex. Here, we simplify this problem and formulate it
202
      into just learning per rigid particle dynamics, where we treat each (canonical or deformed) Gaussian
      kernel as a rigid particle with size and orientation. According to the laws of classical mechanics, for
203
      a specific rigid particle P ∈ R3 , its motion in a 3D world coordinate system can be regarded as a
204
      rotational movement about a rotation center which has its own translation. To this end, we aim to
205   learn the following two groups of physical parameters for each 3D rigid particle P :
206
207
      • Group #1 - Rotation Center Parameters including: 1) the center’s position Pc ∈ R3 , 2) the center’s
        velocity vc ∈ R3 , and 3) acceleration ac ∈ R3 in the world coordinate system.
208
209
      • Group #2 - Rigid Particle Rotational Parameters including: 1) the rigid particle’s rotation vector
        wp ∈ R3 with regard to its center Pc , and 2) the rigid particle’s angular acceleration ϵp .
210
211   As illustrated in Figure 2, our translation rotation dynamics system module, denoted as ftrd , takes
212   a rigid particle P as input, directly predicting the physical parameters of its rotation center and its
213
      own rotational information. Then, this rigid particle will be naturally driven by its learned physical
214   parameters, forming its motion dynamics, as illustrated by the trajectory in Figure 2. This module
215
      is implemented by simple MLPs:

235   Theoretically, our above updating scheme can be naturally extended to higher orders or reduced to
236   lower orders with regard to future time t. Intuitively, a higher order relationship from time 0 to t is
237   expected to capture extremely complex dynamics such as a rolling ball suddenly breaking up into
238   pieces due to unknown explosives inside, whereas a much lower order relationship tends to only
239   capture static or constant speed scenes, thus being oversimplified. In this paper, we opt to the above
240   second-order scheme to update dynamics parameters from time 0 to t for two primary reasons:
241   • In many applications such as robot manipulation, the need for future prediction usually involves
242
        a relatively short interval, i.e., |t − 0| is rather small, e.g., in milliseconds. In this case, a second-
243
        order relationship is usually sufficient to achieve decent approximations. In addition, a simple
244
        sliding window based approach can be applied to continuously and incrementally predict future
245
        frames given the newest visual observations from sensors.
246
247
      • In our daily life, the majority of common physical movements such as rolling balls or moving cars
248
        can be generally described by a second-order relationship. In fact, both Newton’s First and Second
249
        Law of Motion can be captured. Notably, since the whole 3D scene comprises a large number of
250
        rigid particles, each particle has up to second-order dynamics, i.e., with a constant acceleration be-
251     tween 0 ∼ t. Therefore, the compounded dynamics for the entire 3D scene can be rather complex,
252     including various deformations and transformations in our daily lives.
253   Nevertheless, it is still interesting yet non-trivial to learn much higher-order relationships and we
254   leave it for future exploration. More implementation details of this module are in Appendix A.3.

      Datasets: Our method is designed to learn meaningful physical information of 3D dynamic scenes,
318   aiming at accurately predicting future motions, instead of just fitting observed video frames. In this
319   regard, the closest work to us is the recent NVFi (Li et al., 2023a). Following NVFi, we primar-
320   ily evaluate our method on its three dynamic datasets: 1) Dynamic Object dataset. It consists of
321   6 dynamic objects. Each object displays a unique motion pattern belonging to either rigid or de-
322   formable movement. 2) Dynamic Indoor Scene dataset. It has 4 complex indoor scenes. Each
323   scene has multiple objects undergoing different rigid body motions. 3) NVIDIA Dynamic Scene
      dataset (Yoon et al., 2020). It consists of two real-world dynamic 3D scenes.

      Upon a closer look at the above three datasets, we find that their dynamics captured are relatively
325   simple. In our daily life, the majority of objects and scenes consist of multiple parts undergoing
326   radically different motions over time, showing extremely challenging physical patterns to learn.
327   To further evaluate the effectiveness of our design, we collect a new synthetic dataset, named 4)
328   Dynamic Multipart dataset, and a new real-world dataset by 20 GoPros, named 5) GoPro dataset.
329
      Our new synthetic dataset comprises 4 objects. Each has 2 to 5 distinct motion patterns on different
330
      object parts. Following (Li et al., 2023a), for each object, we collect RGBs at 15 different viewing
331
      angles over 1 (virtual) second after normalization, where each viewing angle has 60 frames captured.
332   We reserve the first 46 frames at randomly picked 12 viewing angles as the training split, i.e., 552
333   frames, while leaving the 46 frames at the remaining 3 viewing angles for testing interpolation
334   ability, i.e., 138 frames for novel view synthesis within the training time period, and keeping the last
335   14 frames at all 15 viewing angles for evaluating future frame extrapolation, i.e., 210 frames.
336
      Our new real-world dataset captures 4 dynamic scenes with 20 GoPro cameras. For each dynamic
337
      scene, we select 89 frames from each view, and resize images to be a resolution of 960 × 540. We
338
      reserve the first 67 frames at 17 picked viewing angles as the training split, i.e., 1139 frames, while
339
      leaving the 67 frames at the remaining 3 viewing angles for evaluating novel view interpolation
340
      within the training time period, i.e., 201 frames. We keep the last 22 frames at all 20 viewing angles

      for evaluating future frame extrapolation, i.e., 440 frames in total. More details are in Appendix A.6.
343   Baselines: We select the following baselines: 1) NVFi (Li et al., 2023a): This is the closest work
344   to us, but differs from us in two folds. First, NVFi relies on PINN losses to learn physics priors, but
345   we directly learn physical parameters. Second, NVFi adopts NeRF as a backbone, being short in 3D
346   scene geometry and appearance modeling, but our method is amenable to and adopts the powerful
347   3DGS in nature. 2) T-NeRF (Pumarola et al., 2021). 3) D-NeRF (Pumarola et al., 2021). 4) NSFF
348
      (Li et al., 2021). 5) TiNeuVox (Fang et al., 2022). The latter four methods are based on NeRF and
      designed for novel view interpolation. Therefore they are expected to be rather weak for future frame
349
      extrapolation. For a fair and extensive comparison, we also include the following two baselines. 6)
350
      DefGS (Yang et al., 2024), 7) 4DGS (Wu et al., 2024), and 8) E-D3DGS (Bae et al., 2024). These
351   very recent deformable 3D Gaussians methods are particularly strong to model dynamic 3D scenes
352   for novel view synthesis using 3DGS as a backbone. 9) DefGSnvf i . We build this baseline by

443   Our framework mainly comprises 3DGS as the backbone and our core translation rotation dynamics
444   system module, together with an auxiliary deformation field. To verify different choices of our
445
      method, we conduct the following three groups of ablation experiments.

447   consecutive frames in the training set as δt, we compare three choices of the time difference ∆t in
448   training stage: {δt, 2δt, 3δt}. We choose ∆t = 2δt in our main experiments.
449   (2) Removing the auxiliary deformation field fdef o : In particular, we feed the Gaussian particles
450   at time t′ = 0 directly into ftrd , and use the output physical parameters to directly move particles to
451   a target future timestamp t. Note that, here ∆t is meaningless.
452
      (3) Learning time-dependent physical parameters at t′ : Instead of using Equation 4 to derive
453
      physical parameters at time t′ , we directly learn them by a 6-layer MLPs as: ftrd′ (P , t′ ). Theoreti-
454
      cally, such a complex function can learn higher order relationships to approximate arbitrary motions
455
      than our second-order Equation 4. Nevertheless, it would be more challenging to learn and unable
456
      to guarantee physical parameters of the same motion pattern to be consistent over time.

      5     C ONCLUSION
476
477
      In this paper, we have demonstrated that complex motion dynamics can be explicitly learned just
      from multi-view RGB videos without needing additional human labels such as object types and
478
      masks. This is achieved by a new generic framework that simultaneously models 3D scene geome-
479
      try, appearance and physics by extending the appealing 3D Gaussian Splatting technique. In contrast
480   to existing works which usually rely on PINN losses as soft constraints to learn physics priors, we
481   instead directly learn a complete set of physical parameters to govern the motion pattern of each 3D
482   rigid particle in space via our core translation rotation dynamics system module. Extensive experi-
483   ments on three public dynamic datasets and a newly created dynamic multipart dataset have shown
484   the extraordinary performance of our method in the challenging task of future frame extrapolation
485   over all baselines. In addition, the learned physical parameters can be directly used to segment
      objects or parts according to the similarity of parameters.

724   A.1   P RELIMINARY FOR VANILLA 3DGS
725
726   3D Gaussian Splatting (Kerbl et al., 2023) represents a 3D scene by a set of colored 3D Gaussian
727
      kernels. Specifically, each Gaussian kernel is parameterized by a 3D position P ∈ R3 , an orienta-
      tion represented by a quaternion r, and a scaling s. By transforming the orientation r and scaling
728
      s into the rotation matrix R and scaling matrix S, a 3D covariance matrix Σ can be composed as
729
      Σ = RSST RT . Then the Gaussian kernel can be evaluated at any location x ∈ R3 in the 3D space:
730
                                                          1        T   −1
731                                      G(x) = e− 2 (x−P ) Σ               (x−P )
                                                                                     .                 (9)
732
733
      Besides, each Gaussian kernel has an opacity σ indicating its influence in rendering, and a color c
      computed from spherical harmonics (SH) for view-dependent appearance.
734
735   The rendering of Gaussian kernels on the image consists of two steps. Firstly, The Gaussian kernels
736   are projected onto the image plane, following the differentiable rasterization pipeline proposed in
737   (Zwicker et al., 2001). The 3D position P and covariance matrix Σ of each Gaussian kernel are
738   projected into 2D position P ′ = JW P and covariance matrix Σ′ = JW ΣW T J T respectively,
739
      where J denotes the Jacobian of the approximated projective transformation and W denotes the
      transformation from the world to camera coordinates. Secondly, the color of a pixel µ on the image
740
      can be rendered by α-blending as follows:
741
742                                           X                           i−1
                                                                          Y
743                                C(µ) =            Ti αi ci ,    Ti =         (1 − αj ),           (10)
744                                              i                        j=1
745   where αi is obtained by evaluating the projection of the Gaussian kernel Gi on the pixel µ, i.e.,
746              1     ′ T ′−1       ′
      αi = σi e− 2 (µ−P ) Σ (µ−P ) . By adjusting the parameters of Gaussian kernels mentioned above
747
      and adaptively controlling the Gaussian density, a high-fidelity representation of a 3D scene can be
748   obtained from multi-view images. We refer readers to (Kerbl et al., 2023) for more details.

      A.2   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS OF AUXILIARY D EFORMATION F IELD
751
752   We leverage an existing deformation field introduced in (Yang et al., 2024) as our auxiliary defor-
753   mation field. In particular, the 3D position x0 of each canonical Gaussian kernel and the current
754   timestamp t are fed into an MLP-based deformation network, denoted as fdef o . The implementa-
755   tion of this MLP is directly adapted from (Yang et al., 2024), i.e., an MLP with 8 layers in total
      and 256 hidden sizes for each layer, plus a ResNet layer at layer 4. At the input layer, an 8-degree


                                                              14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      positional embedding is applied onto the 3D position x0 and a 5-degree positional embedding onto
757   time t.
758
759   Mathematically, the deformation field fdef o (x, t) : R4 → R10 is defined as
760
761                                       (δx, δr, δs) = fdef o (x0 , t),                             (11)
762
763   where δx represents the translation of the center of Gaussian kernel, δr represents the rotation for
764   the pose of Gaussian kernel in quaternion representation, and δs is the difference of Gaussian sizes.
765   Note a Gaussian size vector is parametrized by z = log(s), so the difference can be defined as
766
      δs = exp(δz).
767   After applying the deformation field onto Gaussian kernels, we can deform the Gaussian kernels
768   from canonical time to time t as:
769
770
771                                     xt = x0 + δx                                                  (12)
772                                     rt = δr ◦ r0                                                  (13)
773                                     st = exp(z0 + δz) = s0 ⊙ δs,                                  (14)
774
775   where ◦ is quaternion multiplication and ⊙ is element-wise multiplication.
