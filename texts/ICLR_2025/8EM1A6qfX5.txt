009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated remarkable potential in various
012            tasks, however, there remains a significant lack of open-source models and data
013            for specific domains. Previous work has primarily focused on manually specifying
014            resources and collecting high-quality data for specific domains, which is extremely
015            time-consuming and labor-intensive. To address this limitation, we introduce large
016            models into the data collection pipeline to guide the generation of domain-specific
017            information and retrieve relevant data from Common Crawl (CC), a large public
018            corpus. We refer to this approach as Retrieve-from-CC. It not only collects
019            data related to domain-specific knowledge but also mines the data containing
020
               potential reasoning procedures from the public corpus. By applying this method,
               we have collected a knowledge domain-related dataset named Retrieve-Pile, which
021
               covers four main domains, including the sciences, humanities, and other categories.
022
               Through the analysis of Retrieve-Pile, Retrieve-from-CC can effectively retrieve
023            relevant data from the covered knowledge domains and significantly improve the
024            performance in tests of mathematical and knowledge-related reasoning abilities.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Large language models (LLMs) are becoming the new trend not only in natural language processing
031   but also in the entire AI community, pioneered by OpenAI ChatGPT and GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023).
032   While commercial LLMs are close-sourced, open-source models such as LLaMA (Touvron et al.,
033
      2023a) and Mistral (Jiang et al., 2023) are widely studied by the community since they serve as
      general base models for building LLM applications. Based on these base models, domain-specific
034
      models, show great potential in specific domains, such as medicine (Yang et al., 2022; Gao et al.,
035
      2023), finance (Wu et al., 2023; Zhang & Yang, 2023), science (Taylor et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2023),
036   and law (Nguyen, 2023; Cui et al., 2023). These domain-specific enhanced models are based on
037   specific human-collected dataset (Azerbayev et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023c).
038
039
      However, crafting domain-specific data is very costly. As depicted in Figure 1a, traditional data
      collection methods involve the selection of relevant resources by domain experts, followed by data
040
      collection and processing by engineers. On the one hand, such endeavors are highly labor-intensive,
041
      requiring several months of collaboration between multiple domain experts and engineers for corpus
042   collection. On the other hand, some specific domain-related data distribution may be highly scattered,
043   which poses many challenges for large-scale domain-specific data collection. Therefore, in this
044   paper, we introduce an automatic strategy to retrieve data from public corpora for specific domain
045   knowledge, which we call Retrieve-from-CC.
046
      In Retrieve-from-CC, we initially collected seed information in some specific domains, such as
047
      keywords, frequently asked questions, and textbooks, to serve as inputs for the Query Expanding
048   stage. Leveraging the great generalization capability of LLMs, we can effortlessly expand the initial
049   seed information and extend it to an amount of domain-relevant queries. Inspiration from Wang et al.
050   (2023b) and (Xu et al., 2024), we encompassed two stages of expansion, namely Question Extension
051   and Thought Generation, which respectively extend the queries in terms of breadth and depth, for
052   retrieving the domain-related data with a broader scope and deeper thought. Subsequently, based on
053   the queries, we retrieved relevant documents from public corpora, and after performing operations
      such as duplicate data removal and filtering, we formed the final training dataset.

091   2.1   L ARGE LANGUAGE MODEL FOR KNOWLEDGE - BASED REASONING
092
093   In recent years, significant progress has been made in the field of Natural Language Processing
094   (NLP), driven by the emergence of large language models (OpenAI, 2023; InternLM-Team, 2023; Bai
095   et al., 2023; Sun et al., 2023). Particularly, in the domain of academic and professional examinations,
096
      Some language models such as ChatGPT and GPT-4 (OpenAI, 2023) have demonstrated remarkable
      success in solving complex tasks, achieving human-like performance through the utilization of the
097
      capability of reasoning (Wei et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023a). However, open-source LLMs lag in
098
      performance (Like Llama (Touvron et al., 2023a), Mistral (Jiang et al., 2023) etc.), possibly due to a
099   lack of data.

107   PaLM (Chowdhery et al., 2023) and observed that augmenting the model with more mathematical
      data significantly enhanced its proficiency in mathematical reasoning. Similarly, numerous works

      3.1   OVERVIEW
156
157   To collect domain-specific data at a lower cost, we propose a retrieval-based method for data collection,
158   which utilizes LLMs to expand keywords into a variety of domain-related queries. These queries are
159   then used to retrieve relevant data from public corpora, the process we refer to as Retrieve-from-CC.
160   An overview of Retrieve-from-CC is illustrated in Figure 2. This framework consists of two main
161   stages: Query Expanding and Data Retrieval. The input to the entire pipeline consists of seed
      keywords related to the target domain. These seed keywords are expanded into a broader set of


                                                          3
162
      domain-relevant queries using LLMs during the Query Expanding stage. In the Data Retrieval stage,
163   the generated queries is used as queries to retrieve relevant data from public corpora.
164
165   As shown in Figure 2, during the Query Expanding stage, LLMs generate questions and answers
166
      centered around the provided keywords. Both the generated questions and the corresponding answers
      serve as domain-relevant queries that will be used in the Data Retrieval phase. During the Data
167
      Retrieval phase, we employ the BM25 algorithm to retrieve documents relevant to the queries, and
168
      then we obtain the final data.
169
170
      3.2   Q UERY E XPANDING
171
172   To efficiently and comprehensively retrieve high-quality data relevant to the given seed information,
173   we employed the Query Expanding, inspired by Wang et al. (2023b). This phase consists of two steps
174   aimed at broadening the scope of the final query. First, we utilize LLMs to generate questions related
175   to the given keywords or other seed information, we refer it as ’Question Extension.’ Subsequently,
176   using these questions, we prompt the LLMs to provide answers and generate reasoning strategies for
177   solving the questions, thereby retrieving thought-related data from public corpora.
178
179   Question Extension To expand the scope of our queries, we utilize LLMs to generate questions
180
      relevant to the provided seed words. By leveraging the generalization capabilities of LLMs, we can
      easily generate a series of questions related to the seed information, thereby expanding the conceptual
181
      boundaries of the target domain. Figure 2 illustrates an example of ’Question Extension’. Given
182
      the seed word ’biology,’ LLMs generate related questions, such as those concerning ’genetics’ and
183   ’meiosis’. This process evolves limited seed information into a more comprehensive representation
184   that encompasses a range of related concepts. Consequently, this approach significantly enhances the
185   breadth of the query set, ensuring more comprehensive coverage of various aspects within the target
186   domain. All generated questions are saved in the seed information pool for future iterations and also
187   serve as queries for data retrieval.
188
189   Thought Generation In addition to expanding the range of queries through Question Extension,
190   we also notice that the answer and the reasoning path are important for ensuring quality. For the
191   generated questions in ’Question Extension’, we employ LLMs to generate answers and the reasoning
192   processes required to obtain them. This enables us to acquire detailed and insightful responses. This
193
      approach supports a more thorough exploration of concepts related to seed information and facilitates
      the generation of cognitive processes essential for answering questions. These reasoning processes
194
      are also used as queries for data retrieval. While we found that some of these thought data may
195
      contain errors or grammatical issues, we still use them as queries to retrieve accurate data from public
196   corpora.
197
198   Post processing After generation (both in ’Question Extension’ and ’Thought Generation’), the
199   generated data is stored in a seed information pool for the next iteration and also in a query pool for
200   data retrieval. Both processes involve the same post-processing methods: cleaning and deduplication.
201   The cleaning step removes incomplete language data to eliminate the impact of non-natural language.
202   For the deduplication stage, Minhash-LSH (Broder, 1997) was employed to remove duplicate data.
203
204   3.3   DATA R ETRIEVAL
205
206   Based on the query expanding stage, we get extensive and in-depth queries. During the data retrieval
207
      stage, utilizing the enriched queries, we employ the BM25 (Robertson & Walker, 1994) algorithm to
      retrieve data from general public corpora. BM25 is a widely adopted relevance calculation method
208
      commonly used by search engines. It calculates the relevance score between the given query and
209
      target documents by weighting and summing the matching degree of keywords in the query with
210   the target documents. Efficiency is the reason why we use BM25 to calculate the relevance. When
211   dealing with billion data, performing relevance calculations for each query against every document
212   becomes exceedingly challenging, while BM25 rapidly retrieves documents relevant to the target
213   query. Compared with Dense Retriever (Karpukhin et al., 2020), it may incur a potential loss in
214   retrieval accuracy, but the latter comes with an unbearable high computational cost. Exploring the
215   potential impact of retriever selection on the quality of collected data might be a valuable direction
      for future research.

485                   Data contamination Data contamination analysis is equally important in the study of pre-training
                      corpora. Many research (Chowdhery et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023b; Jiang et al., 2024) have


                                                                                                                          9
486
      defined data contamination as the n-gram overlap between the pre-training corpus and the test set.
487   For instance, PaLM Chowdhery et al. (2023) defines contamination as a 70% 8-gram overlap, while
488   Llama2 Touvron et al. (2023b) considers it contamination if more than 10 tokens overlap between the
489   training and test sets. Inspired by previous works, we utilized the Overlapy codebase to calculate the
490   8-gram overlap between Retrieve-Pile and the test set. Table 6 shows the proportion of documents
491   with 8-gram overlaps between Retrieve-Pile and downstream tasks. It is important to note that this
492   metric calculates direct overlaps, potentially leading to false positives, as overlaps may occur between
493   semantically different contents. As shown in table 6, the overlap across most downstream tasks is
494   relatively minimal, generally below 0.1%. For example, gsm8k and BBH exhibit an overlap rate
495
      of 0%, while MMLU, containing a significant amount of conceptual content, shows only about a
      1.5% overlap. One potential explanation is that MMLU incorporates substantial knowledge-related
496
      questions, particularly general knowledge, which may be reflected by similar descriptions in the pre-
497
      training corpus. Additionally, domain-specific knowledge typically consists of specialized terms and
498   standardized expressions, which are prone to repetition. Overall, the low n-gram overlap of Retrieve-
499   Pile across downstream tasks suggests that contamination in Retrieve-Pile may not be significant.
500   This also highlights the dataset’s broad adaptability in handling diverse tasks.

      5     L IMITATION AND H ALLUCINATION A NALYSIS
504
505
      5.1   L IMITATION
506
      In this paper, we propose a collection method for domain-specific data, Retrieve-from-CC, which
507   extends domain-relevant queries through LLMs generated for data retrieval. Additionally, we
508   demonstrate through empirical evaluation on further training of LLAMA and Mistral that data
509   collected using this method significantly improves the ability of LLM in some specific domains.
510   However, this method still has the following potential limitations:
511
512   Data Quality The quality of data collected by Retrieve-from-CC depends largely on the quality
513   of data in public corpora. In this work, we utilized the Common Crawl corpus as our public corpus,
514   extracting and processing the CC dump up to April 2023. Despite leveraging some methods (Wenzek
515   et al., 2020) for processing high-quality web data, we found that the corpus still contains an amount
516   of low-quality and erroneously extracted content. Thus, improving the data quality of public corpora
517   is also a direction for future research.
518
519   5.2   H ALLUCINATION A NALYSIS
520
521
      The output of LLMs generally exhibits significant hallucination issues. Previous work has shown
      that LLMs are prone to hallucinations, and using their output directly in training without filtering
522
      can exacerbate the model’s hallucinations for certain issues. However, in the data collection pipeline
523
      of Retrieve-from-CC, the model is only used to generate queries and is not directly applied in
524   training. Therefore, even if the synthesized queries from the LLMs contain incorrect information,
525   the information retrieved from the corpus based on these incorrect queries is correct. Hallucinatory
526   queries do not lead to the retrieval of incorrect information.
527

529
      6     C ONCLUSION
530
531
      In this study, we propose an efficient method Retrieve-from-CC, for the automated collection of
      specialized domain data. Leveraging seed data from some specific domains, we employ a language
532
      model for query expanding. By optimizing the breadth and depth of queries, we expand the query to
533
      retrieve data relevant to the specified domain. Ultimately, we collected and released an open dataset
534   comprising approximately 735GB of Retrieve-Pile, equivalent to approximately 188 billion tokens,
535   in the fields of mathematics and knowledge. Experimental results demonstrate that the adoption
536   of Retrieve-Pile significantly enhances the model’s performance in some reasoning tasks, such as
537   math word problems and professional examinations. Our objective is not only to establish a research
538   foundation for community studies in mathematical and knowledge-related reasoning but also to
539   provide an efficient and cost-effective method for collecting high-quality data, thereby facilitating the
      accumulation of more high-quality data.

      In this study, we train all models with bf16 mixed precision and only data parallel. We employed the
      AMSP (Chen et al., 2023), shard optimizer state across 8 cards to reduce communication overhead.
818
      Simultaneously, data parallelism was employed for training. To enhance throughput and reduce
819
      memory consumption, we introduced the Flash attention 2 (Dao, 2024) module. All models underwent
820   training for 50,000 steps, with a global batch size of 4 million tokens per step, totaling 200 billion
821   training tokens.
822
823
      During the initial 2,000 steps of training, the learning rate was warmed up to the maximum, and then,
      at the end of training, it was decayed according to cosine decay to the specified minimum learning
824
      rate. Specifically, for Llama2-QoC, the maximum learning rate during training was set to 2e-5, and
825
      the minimum learning rate was set to 2e-6. For Mistral-QoC, the maximum learning rate during
826   training was 5e-6, descending to 2e-7 at the end of training. In our experiment, Llama2-QoC and
827   Mistral-QoC achieved a training throughput of approximately 4000 tokens per GPU per second(TGS).
828   Despite the relatively higher training efficiency of Llama2-QoC, it still utilized 14,000 GPU hours.

922   D     C OLLECTION IMPLEMENT DETAILS
923
924   In the implement section, we primarily discuss three components, the public corpora (D.1), retrieval
925
      engine (D.2), and the post-processing settings (D.3).
926
927   D.1    P UBLIC C ORPORA
928
      With the development of large language models, public corpora have become increasingly rich,
929
      including the Pile, RedPajama, and Common Crawl. Common Crawl is an open-source web crawler
930   project containing all publicly available web pages from 2013 to the present. In theory, it encompasses
931   a significant portion of the information present on the web. Due to hardware cost constraints, we
932   utilized WARC format data from the years 2016 to 2023 to build our retrieval database. We performed
933   extraction, filtering, and cleaning procedures on the data obtained from Common Crawl to ensure
934   the quality of the retrieval dataset. The final retrieval database comprises several billion records,
935   occupying a total of 50TB of disk space.
936
937   D.2    R ETRIEVAL E NGINE
938
939
      Retrieving data from billions of documents is highly challenging, and we need to calculate the
      relevant score between queries and each document in the retrieved database. To improve storage
940
      and retrieval efficiency, we built the retrieval engine based on Elasticsearch (ES). Elasticsearch
941
      is an open-source distributed search engine that employs distributed storage and inverted indexes,
942   achieving data retrieval highly efficient. We selected the BM25 algorithmic as the relevance scorer
943   because of its efficiency. For each query, we recall the top 1000 most relevant documents. Leveraging
944   Elasticsearch’s efficient storage and indexing algorithms, we can complete a query retrieval within
945   100ms.
946
947   D.3    P OST P ROCESSING
948
949   To enhance the quality of Retrieve-Pile, inspired by Wenzek et al. (2020), we conducted data quality
950   filtering and deduplication in the post-processing stage.
951   In the data quality filtering phase, we manually selected some high-quality data as positive examples
952   and randomly selected low-quality data from Common Crawl, such as poorly structured data and
953   advertising data, as negative examples to train our scoring model. High-quality data mainly includes
954   papers, books, and high-quality forum data. We chose BERT-base-uncased as the backbone to train
955   the model and tested it on a subset of data to ensure its high usability. Finally, we scored the retrieved
956   data and filtered out data with quality scores below 0.8.
957   In the deduplication phase, we employed the Minhash-LSH method. For hyperparameter selection, we
958   computed the similarity scores based on 13 grams and set the similarity threshold to 0.8. Additionally,
959   we set num perm to 128 to balance computation efficiency and deduplication performance.
