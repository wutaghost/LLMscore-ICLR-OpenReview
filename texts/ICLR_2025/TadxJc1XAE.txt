011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            In this paper, we introduce a novel dataset for monitoring the activities of faculty
014            members in academic office environments. Advances in computer vision have en-
015
               abled the automation of workplace monitoring, particularly in educational institu-
               tions, where tracking faculty activities presents significant challenges and ethical
016
               considerations. Traditional methods of manual supervision are labor-intensive and
017
               prone to human error, underscoring the potential of automated video analysis as a
018            more efficient solution. While substantial progress has been made in Human Ac-
019            tivity Recognition (HAR) across various domains, research specifically focused
020            on monitoring faculty activities in office settings is limited. Most existing studies
021            concentrate on classroom and student monitoring, revealing a critical gap in fac-
022            ulty surveillance. This paper seeks to address that gap by introducing TeacherAc-
023            tivityNet, a novel video dataset designed to recognize teachers’ activities in aca-
024            demic offices, encompassing nine distinct action classes. We tweak the YOLOv8n
025            architecture to propose our model, Teacher Activity Net (YOLOTAN), which is
026
               then fine-tuned using our dataset, achieving an average precision of 74.9%, signif-
               icantly outperforming benchmark models. A comparative analysis of our dataset
027
               and methods against existing solutions highlights the potential of TeacherActiv-
028
               ityNet to improve automated faculty monitoring systems. The dataset, trained
029            models, and accompanying code are available at https://tinyurl.com/4ub94phh

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   The improvement in different computer vision models has opened new frontiers in the automation
035   of various tasks, including the monitoring and surveillance of workplaces. In educational institu-
036   tions, monitoring teacher activities such as ensuring safety, improving operational efficiency, and
037   evaluating performance can be complex and time-consuming. Moreover, typical manual supervi-
038   sion methods not only demand significant human resources but are also prone to human errors and
039
      inconsistencies. To address these challenges, automated faculty monitoring systems using video
      analysis can be a promising solution.
040
041   In recent years, a considerable amount of work has been done in the field of Human Activity Recog-
042   nition in various contexts such as sports (Host & Ivašić-Kos, 2022)(Xiao et al., 2023), pose estima-
043   tion (Atikuzzaman et al., 2020), crime scene detection (J & Thinakaran, 2023), healthcare (Gupta
044   et al., 2022), etc. While solving the problem of human activity recognition from videos, the re-
045
      searchers primarily focus on a few aspects - analyzing the applicability of Machine Learning (ML)
      and Deep Learning (DL) methods in different HAR tasks, creating new datasets for domain-specific
046
      HAR-related tasks, and proposing novel solutions for diverse HAR problems. Educational organi-
047
      zations have been the center of experimentation to introduce automation using various AI methods
048   to streamline processes (Ben Williamson & Potter, 2023). Dimitriadou & Lanitis (2023) have crit-
049   ically analyzed the use of AI and emerging technologies in the classroom and recommended that
050   the computer vision-based surveillance system can ensure the safety and security of the classroom
051   alongside tracking the students’ participation and attendance. Additionally, a smart surveillance sys-
052   tem can help teachers in plagiarism detection and student supervision in an online setting (Saini &
053   Goel, 2019). These systems can also help in identifying abnormal activities on the school premises
      through CCTV footage analysis (Liu et al., 2023).

071   2   R ELATED W ORK
072
073   Human Action Recognition (HAR) is a critical research area with applications across various do-
074   mains such as security or surveillance, sports, education, and more. Many researches were done
075
      throughout the years to improve the prediction and recognition task of a variety of activities.
076   Yuganthini et al. (2021) proposed a wireless method named the Zigbee technique to track employee
077   activity using computer vision. The dataset used in this process consists of videos collected from
078   CCTV cameras in the workplace. In order to find the efficiency of their system, they compared
079   their measured time with the actual in and out time collected from bio-metric entry. However, the
080   system is only supposed to function for a specific region. Another study of the same year, Sikder &
081
      Nahid (2021) introduced a dataset, the KU-HAR for heterogeneous HAR. The dataset was created
      from videos featuring 90 participants performing 18 different actions. Using a Random Forest (RF)
082
      classifier, they achieved a precision of 90%.
083
084   Later on, many studies were done focusing on the learning or teaching environments of different
085   educational institutions. Rashmi et al. (2021) detected the student actions were performed in com-
086   puter laboratories. They collected 688 image frames from CCTV cameras installed in the labs and
087
      gathered 54,862 samples from these frames for five different action classes. YOLOv3 was used as a
      method to detect the actions. In the same year Zhao et al. (2021) predicted teacher-student behavior
088
      by analyzing various student actions in the classroom during teaching. Some very recent studies also
089
      reflected to contribute in the same track. Wang et al. (2024) evaluated teaching quality in real-time
090   by predicting the students’ “head-up rate” using YOLOv5. This approach generated more effective
091   results in education quality assessment than traditional survey questionnaires. In order to boost the
092   process and have more reliable outputs, different models are also being proposed in recent studies.
093   Dey et al. (2024) proposed the AdaptSepCX Attention Network model to detect student actions in
094   online education. Their model achieved a high validation accuracy of 92.73%. Moreover, Pabba &
095   Kumar (2024) proposed a vision-based student engagement model, focusing on seven action classes
096   based on students’ facial expressions. They used the Multi-task Cascaded Convolutional Networks
097   (MTCNN) method for facial recognition.
098   Additionally, the activity recognition process was introduced to detect anomalies in fitness activities.
099   Yang et al. (2023) used Pose-Based Branch (PBB) and RGB-Based Branch (RBB) features sepa-
100   rately with CNN, ResNet152, and 3D-CNN models to compare. MPOSE-2019, Body Movements-
101   Based Dataset (BMbD), Multi-target Body Movements-Based Dataset (M-BMbD), and the Joint
102   Body Movements and Object Position-Based Dataset (JBMOPbD) were used in this study. The
103   pose-based method outperformed the RGB-based approach. As a final result, the Pose-Based Branch
104
      (PBB) outperformed the other one as a feature consideration.
105   Moreover, some other activities related to security and the prediction of anomalies or suspicious
106   events are introduced in this research area. Singh et al. (2020) proposed CNN and RNN-based
107   models with Inception V3 to predict crime scenes in the UCF crime dataset which contains 1,800
      videos. In this study, it was assured that considering a larger dataset and using augmentation played

      3.1     DATASET
163
164   One of our major contributions is the creation of a dataset that includes a wide range of activities
165   for the task of teacher monitoring. To accomplish this, we collected videos of 19 participants for
166   9 action classes. The action classes are Arriving, Counselling, Eating, Idle, Leaving, Sleeping,
167   Talking, Using Phone and Working. The participants were asked to sign an informed consent form
168   before making the videos and an appropriate opt-out policy was followed in every step of the data
169   collection process. As per the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) Union (2016), data
170
      privacy and security are prioritized. For the diversification of the dataset, the videos were taken
      from 8 different rooms. The videos were recorded using an iPhone 11 Pro Max, with varying
171
      camera distances based on room size and diverse angles capturing multiple participants’ actions.
172
      The cameraman’s average height was 5’6”. The dataset includes 19 participants (3 female), mostly
173   aged 20–22, with some around 30. All actions were controlled and guided.
174
175
      3.1.1     C OLLECTION
176
177   The dataset creation process took place primarily in two stages - training and validation, and testing.
178   In the first stage, we took a sample of 12 from the participants and they were asked to perform one
179   action at a time. Nine videos for each of the action classes per participant were recorded. After
180   collecting the training videos, we took a sample of size 3 from the remaining 7 participants and
181   asked them to do the same. These video sets of three participants across the nine classes are used as
182
      the validation set in our experiments.
183   In the second stage, we made 10 videos of the remaining 4 participants who were not involved in the
184   previous stage of the data collection. As we are interested in measuring how accurate our models
185   are in real-time, unlike the previous stage where different actions were done in different videos, we
186   asked the participants to perform the actions continuously for a specific period. To critically test the
187   model performance in real-time, we made most of the videos in such rooms that were not present
188
      in the earlier phase of data collection. In Table 1, the descriptions of the action classes including
      duration and number of instances after annotation are provided.
189

      3.1.2     A NNOTATION
192   After collecting the dataset, we generate the image frames from the training and validation videos
193   using Roboflow1 through manual annotation. Three annotators performed manual labeling, with
194   a verifier ensuring the accuracy of all annotations. From each frame, a bounding box is drawn to
195   annotate the object with an action class. Extra caution is exercised while annotating the images
196   that look the same but fall into different action classes. For example, in “Leaving” and “Arriving”
197   action classes, there is a moment in the videos when the participants leaving the seat and taking the
198   seat, look almost identical. Another case is while performing “Idle” action, the participants closed
199   their eyes which potentially conflicts with “Sleeping” action. Taking these ambiguous scenarios
200
      into consideration, such frames are discarded from the dataset. After annotating, the numbers of
      instances in the training, validation, and test sets are 6498, 1337, and 808, respectively.

      3.2   YOLO T EACHER ACTIVITY N ET (YOLOTAN)
255
256
      YOLOv8 is considered state-of-the-art for object detection in real-time. This version of YOLOv8
257
      has more speed and accuracy than the previous versions. A few key components of YOLOv8
258   are anchor-free detection, multi-scale predictions, and decoupled head architecture. We adopt the
259   YOLOv8n as the base model for our detection task. In YOLOv8n there are three main parts in the
260   architecture - Backbone, Neck, and Head.
261
      In our proposed model, we modify the backbone of the YOLOv8n architecture. In the backbone,
262
      there are five feature pyramids (P) and four stages with each having a c2f (Cross-Stage Partial Net-
263
      work with 2 Convolutions and Fusion) module and a convolution module. We modify the last layer
264   of the convolution module with a residual connection. The input of the convolution module of the
265   last layer adds to the output of the same layer. This process is done for every convolution module.
266   The modified convolution module is shown in Figure 4. In Figure4, the internal mechanism of the
267   modified convolution module with residual connection is presented. It adds a residual connection
268   when the input and output channels of the convolution layer are the same with a stride of (1,1). This
269   can help mitigate the vanishing gradient problem and potentially improve the learning of deeper
      features. Residual connections offer a direct pathway for gradients to propagate backward, helping

      We present TeacherActivityNet, a novel dataset for monitoring teachers’ activities, consisting of

      videos meticulously recorded in academic office environments. Our proposed model YOLOTAN
533
      demonstrates a substantial improvement in average precision over the base model. One of the major
534   limitations of our dataset is that the videos were captured using smartphones to simulate CCTV
535   recordings. Utilizing actual CCTV footage in future work could potentially enhance the model’s
