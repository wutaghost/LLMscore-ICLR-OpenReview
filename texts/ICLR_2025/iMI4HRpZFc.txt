                                                   Abstract
009
010
011             Target-directed agents utilize self-generated targets, to guide their behaviors for
012             better generalization. These agents are prone to blindly chasing problematic tar-
013
                gets, resulting in worse generalization and safety catastrophes. We show that
                these behaviors can be results of delusions, stemming from improper designs
014
                around training: the agent may naturally come to hold false beliefs about certain
015
                targets. We identify delusions via intuitive examples in controlled environments,
016             and investigate their causes and mitigations. With the insights, we demonstrate
017             how we can make agents address delusions preemptively and autonomously. We
018             validate empirically the effectiveness of the proposed strategies in correcting
019             delusional behaviors and improving out-of-distribution generalization.

023   1     Introduction
024
025
026   An important use of Reinforcement Learning (RL) is to learn skills generalizable for application,
027
      after training from limited training tasks. Despite numerous efforts to bridge the “generalization
      gap” between performance during training and evaluation, generalization abilities of RL agents
028
      remain largely unsatisfactory. Recent works attributed this to existing agents’ lack of reasoning
029
      abilities to face Out-Of-Distribution (OOD) changes (Di Langosco et al., 2022).
030
031   Thus, embracing the concept of intentions, a certain type of decision-time planning agents are
032   developed, which make use of their more adaptive planning outcomes, which we call targets, to
033
      direct its behaviors to generalize better in novel situations (Alver & Precup, 2022). For instance,
      these targets can help decompose a complicated task into small and familiar steps. In this paper,
034
      we use targets to denote (sub-)goals produced by the agents themselves during decision-time plan-
035
      ning. These “target-directed” agents make use of generators to propose candidate targets, as well
036   as optional estimators to evaluate the favorability of the candidates, to select a target to commit to.
037
038   While target-directed agents were supposed to be able to generalize well OOD, they are often ob-
039
      served to be blindly chasing unreachable or unsafe targets (Bengio et al., 2024). This paper focuses
      on the investigations of these behaviors, which existing literature has largely neglected. We intro-
040
      duce the readers to a new perspective to show that these agents are likely designed to be delusional.
041
      Unlike hallucinations, delusions are obviously wrong beliefs that an agent holds, and reflect an in-
042   ability to reject false beliefs (Kiran & Chaudhury, 2009). These in the RL context correspond to false
043   beliefs that are natural results of an agent’s improper learning process, which limit the agents’ sit-
044   uational understanding and lead to worse generalization or even safety catastrophes. Delusional
045   behaviors stem from the incoordination of the generator and the estimator: problematic targets
046   should not be generated, and even if they were, they should not be favored (Corlett, 2019).
047
      This paper discusses some necessary conditions for agents to address delusions autonomously and
048   preemptively: 1) incorporation of an estimator, enabling the rejection of problematic targets; 2)
049   adequate learning rules with which false beliefs about targets can be counteracted and 3) proper
050   training data that can counteract delusions. With clear diagnoses in the controlled environments,
051   we identify types of delusions, and investigate proper designs of the training processes. We then
052   applied our ideas to hindsight relabeling, a fundamental form of target-directed RL training, and
053   validated experimentally that our strategies led to lower delusion-related errors and reduction in
      delusional behaviors, resulting in significant improvements in OOD generalization performance.

      3.1     Generator’s Role in Delusional Behaviors: Proposing Bad Targets
130
131   Most learned generators are subjected to unwanted generalization, thus inevitably generate prob-
132   lematic targets (unreachable or unsafe), a behavior characterized as hallucination (Jafferjee et al.,
133   2020). While hallucination can be reduced to a degree, they are generally unavoidable, especially
134   in OOD scenarios. Despite that not all delusions are caused by problematic targets, generator
135   hallucinations pose a major safety risk (Liu et al., 2022; Bengio et al., 2024).
136   Generators are implemented in various ways, thus it is ineffective to enumerate the detailed causes
137   of problematic target generation. We skip these discussions and directly focus on a categorization
138   of problematic targets, based on an often neglected perspective - the temporal relationship inside
139   the source-target pairs. This leads to the following two disjoint types of problematic targets:
140
141   3.1.1     Type G.1 - Nonexistent
142
143   The first type of problematic targets includes those that do not correspond to valid states in the
144   task MDP. They can be classified into: 1) Invalid: semantically invalid targets for the task, such
145   as a target SSM observation generated without the agent’s location; 2) Impossible: semantically
146
      valid but unreachable targets, e.g. an SSM target cell surrounded by lava traps, (such as in Fig. 2 a).
      G.1 targets only cause delusional behavior if the estimator produces matching E.1 delusions, to be
147
      discussed later. Imperfectly generated targets corresponding to valid states may not be problematic,
148
      as estimators may filter out imperfections, possibly using a state encoder.
149
150
      3.1.2     Type G.2 - Temporarily Unreachable
151
152   The second type includes targets that correspond to valid states in the task MDP, but cannot be
153   fulfilled from the current state. Unlike G.1, these G.2 targets are only temporarily inappropriate and
154   could be perfectly good targets if the agent were in a different state. For the time-dependency, G.2
155   can be easily overlooked. There are some notable subtypes, such as: 1) Irreversible: targets once
156   reachable but are now blocked due to a past transition. For instance, after acquiring the sword
157   in SSM, the agent transitions from class ⟨0, 0⟩ to ⟨1, 0⟩, sealing off access to ⟨0, 0⟩ or ⟨0, 1⟩; 2)
158
      Segregated: targets that can only be reached from specific initial states that are not available to
      the agent. For example, an agent spawned in class ⟨1, 0⟩ cannot reach states in ⟨0, 0⟩ or ⟨0, 1⟩.
159
160   G.2 targets are often overlooked in literature, since hallucinations are mostly discussed in contexts
161   without temporal progression, and also they do not exist in all MDPs. G.2 can appear more fre-
      quently if we do not avoid training the generators with them. For example, a conditional target

      generator which learns from “episode” will be more likely to produce G.2 targets (compared to
163   “future”), such as the one in Fig. 2 b). This applies to training beyond HER as well.

165   Problematic targets such as G.1 and G.2 can cause delusional behaviors of chasing unreachable

      targets, if they are favored during decision-time.

168   3.2     Estimator’s Role in Delusional Behaviors: Bad Evaluation
169
170   An estimator evaluates the favorability of proposed targets, based on some relationship between
171   the current states (sources) and the candidates (targets), which often involve assessing the imple-
172
      mentability of targets, represented as, e.g., distances, etc. Acting as a firewall, the estimator of a
      target-directed agent can enable the selection among the candidates, enabling the potential to filter
173
      out hallucinated targets. Agents without estimators, e.g. Director (Hafner et al., 2022), must accept
174
      proposed targets unconditionally, and thus are at significant risk of chasing problematic targets.
175
176   Incorporating an estimator only delegates the responsibility of avoiding delusional behavior from
177   the generator to the estimator, as the estimators’ own delusional beliefs about targets can directly
178
      cause delusional behaviors. A lack of either of the following two necessary aspects results in blind
      spots of knowledge about targets, i.e. delusions regarding both delusional or non-delusional targets.
179
180   Update Rules: we expect estimators to learn to reject problematic, undesirable or unsafe targets,
181   by evaluating them with low favorability. However, such expectation may not be achieved, if the
182   estimators’ update rules do not have the characteristic that (continuously) punishes unachieved /
183   unsafe targets, when these targets are sampled during training for the estimator to learn from.
184   Training Data Even with proper update rules, delusions can still arise from the lack of exposure
185   to data counteracting the estimators’ false beliefs. This is often caused by improper experience
186   organization mechanisms, responsible for transforming the experienced interactions with envi-
187   ronments into training data, as shown in Fig. 1. Despite these mechanisms like HER can lead to
188   more diversity in training data, they are often accompanied by delusions: 1) Certain relabeling
189   strategies naturally cause delusions. For instance, “future” only relabels with future observations,
190
      thus only exposes a learner to future reachable targets, leaving the estimator to guess when a “past”
      target is proposed at decision-time; 2) Trajectory-level relabeling can also be problematic. Short
191
      trajectories, common in many training procedures, only cover limited portions of the state space
192
      and prevent estimators from learning about distant targets, leaving estimators to guess when a dis-
193   tant target is generated at decision-time. Short trajectories can be a product of experimental design
194   (initial state distributions, Maximum Episode Lengths, MELs (Erraqabi et al., 2021)) or innate envi-
195   ronmental characteristics (density of terminal states). It is worth noting that delusions caused by
196   improper training data (organizing mechanisms) also extend to cases like few-shot generalization.
197
      With these necessary conditions, we identify types of estimator delusions based on the targets they
198
      evaluate. We use the identifiers E.0, E.1, and E.2 for their resulting delusional behaviors.
199
200
201
      3.2.1    Type E.0 - Misevaluating Non-Delusional Targets
202
      The standalone type of delusions describes false estimations about non-delusional targets. E.0 delu-
203   sions can lead to undesirable or delusional targets being favored and thus can hurt generalization.
204
205
      3.2.2    Type E.1 - Misevaluating G.1 Targets
206
207   This type of delusion appears when an estimator misevaluates the favorability of a G.1 target. If
208   effective learning rules are present, this can still be caused by the lack of necessary training data
209   (e.g., relabeled transitions with G.1 targets in buffer). In Fig. 2 a), an example is visualized. Note that
210   the resulting E.1 behaviors, i.e. those chasing selected G.1 targets, can be potentially catastrophic
211   if the G.1 targets are beyond safety constraints.
212
213   3.2.3    Type E.2 - Misevaluating G.2 Targets
214
215   Similar to E.1, in Fig. 2 b), an example of delusional behavior is visualized, a result of a combination
      of G.2 (temporarily unreachable) and E.2. E.2 behaviors can hurt the agents’ generalization abilities.

      4     Addressing Delusions in Target-Directed Agents
217
218
      Having identified delusions, taking advantage of the shared reliance on source-target pairs, we
219
      can develop strategies applicable to general target-directed agents coming from various training
220
      procedures. Then, we provide examples on how to materialize such strategies in agents trained
221   with HER, because of its popularity, its direct association with source-target pairs and the fact that
222   one can trivially transform HER-based training into losses established over two sampled transitions
223   from ordinary experience replays.
224
      Without the loss of generality, assume we are dealing with a hypothetical target-directed agent
225
      with a dual-component architecture as in Fig. 1, learning both components exclusively from
226
      hindsight-relabeled transitions. The generator proposes targets corresponding to potentially dis-
227   tant states, and the estimator learns the favorability of targets by (continuously) punishing the
228   unachieved or unsafe targets sampled during training. Note that even for methods whose genera-
229   tors are not trained with HER, our estimator-focused strategies still apply.
230
      As discussed earlier, to address delusions, we need effective update rules and training data. By
231
      assuming this framework, we skip discussing update rules, as they depend on the specific designs
232
      of the chosen target-directed agents. Our emphasis on the training data shines light on the fact
233   that most existing RL agents only learn from experienced data, while addressing delusions requires
234   learning from targets that can never be experienced. This corresponds to a discrepancy between
235   (most existing) target-directed agents’ behaviors and training: at decision-time, targets outside
236   experience can be proposed; while during training, only experienced targets are learned from.
237
238
      4.1     Assistive Strategies for Addressing Delusions
239
240   We first introduce two ideas to improve training data distributions, which can then be material-
241   ized as two hindsight relabeling strategies for HER. These ideas seek to expand the support of
242   the training data distribution, to include those source-target combinations that the agent could
243   never experience, i.e. those involving G.1 and G.2 targets. Since our proposed ideas do not rely on
244   additional assumptions, they should be expected to be applicable generally. Additionally, with con-
245   vergent learning rules (provided by the target-directed framework that these strategies are applied
246   to), the strategies should also lead to the correct estimation of G.1 and G.2 targets.
247   Later, we compare the ideas behind all HER strategies, including the existing and the newly pro-
248   posed, and study how the two groups can be combined to accommodate the learning needs.
249
250   4.1.1    “generate”: Let Estimators Learn About Candidates (to be generated)
251
252   The first strategy, named “generate”, is to let the estimator learn about targets that could be proposed
253   at decision time, s.t. it could figure out preemptively that problematic targets are not favorable.
254   Zhao et al. (2024) identified delusional behaviors resulted from E.1 delusions, in the language of
255   this paper, and proposed to train the estimator additionally with candidate targets proposed by the
256   generator. With HER, we can transform this auxiliary loss into a Just-In-Time (JIT) HER strategy
257   that, as a transition is sampled for training, relabels it with a proposed target by the generator. We
258   can expect “generate” to be effective for training estimators, as the estimators will get exposed to
259   all kinds of problematic targets that the generator could offer: When a generator learns to generate
260   mostly viable targets, “generate” will be primarily effective at dealing with the E.0 and E.1 (G.1s
261   are unlikely to be eradicated). “generate” requires the use of the generator, thus it incurs additional
262
      computational burden, depending on the complexity of target generation processes.
263   Adapting “generate” beyond HER should be straightforward: whenever a target is involved during
264   estimator learning, we may replace it with generated targets. “generate” comes at a cost of extra
265   computation, to which attention may be required in real-world applications with speed demands.
266
267   4.1.2    “pertask”: Let Estimators Learn About Experienced Targets Outside the Episode
268
269   The second strategy, namely “pertask”, is to expose the estimator to all targets experienced before, s.t.
      it could figure out that some previously achieved targets are unreachable from the current state.

270
      We materialize “pertask” into an assistive atomic strategy that relabels transitions with observa-
271   tions from the same training task, sampled across the entire memory. Importantly, “pertask” brings
272   exposure to the estimator to learn against E.2 delusions and to the long-distance source-target pairs
273   against E.0 caused by short trajectories. Take SSM as an intuitive example, a current state in situ-
274   ation ⟨1, 0⟩ in the current episode can now be paired with a target in ⟨0, 1⟩ in another episode, s.t.
275   the estimator learns such G.2 target is unreachable, as shown in Fig. 4 (in Appendix).
276
      It is worth noting that “pertask” also biases the training data distribution, making the agent spread
277   out its efforts into learning the source-target pairs potentially far away from each other. Despite
278   increasing training data diversity, long-distance pairs are less likely to contribute to better decision-
279   making compared to the shorter-distance in-episode ones offered by “episode”, as shown later.
280
      Adapting “pertask” beyond HER requires algorithmic designs recording all past observations.
281
282   Both strategies help the estimator figure out the features shared by problematic targets, s.t. OOD
283   delusions can also be identified. Note that “pertask” cannot be used to address E.1 delusions. How-
284   ever, “generate” can be used for E.2 if the generator generates the respective problematic targets.

320   4.3   Hybrid Strategies - A 2-Slotted Approach
321
322   While an estimator needs the exposure to problematic targets to counteract delusions, a generator
323   benefits from learning to generate only useful targets, and should avoid exposure to problematic
      targets. Thus, generators and estimators often have conflicting needs during training.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      Each source of training data has its own biases. Atomic hindsight relabeling strategies in HER,
325   as shown in Tab. 1, can simultaneously help one component while hindering another. Instead of
326   trying to tradeoff the needs of both components with a single source of training data, we propose a
327   hybrid (2-slotted) approach, allowing the generator and estimator to receive data tailored to their
328   needs, through two independent relabeling processes. This combination of 2 slots and mixtures
329   can produce various hybrid strategies, as demonstrated in Sec. 5.4.
330
      Separating training data for the generator and estimator based on their needs is straightforward
331   in training procedures beyond HER.
332

336   5     Experiments
337
338
      To investigate the effectiveness of our strategies against delusions and how they help target-
339   directed agents achieve better generalization, we implement a combination of environments with
340   clearly defined delusional cases, as well as methods whose estimators can be easily analyzed. This
341   leads to our 4 sets of experiments, coming from a combination of 2 environments (one dominantly
342   haunted by G.1, and another by G.2) and 2 target-directed frameworks. Due to page limit, 3 out of
343   4 sets of experiments are presented in the Appendix. We summarize those at the end of this section.
344
345
346   5.1   Environments & Settings
347
348   For environments, we favor tasks where the delusions are present, dangerous and intuitive to
349   inspect, for which the introduced SSM offers us clear advantage. Introduction and the results on
350   another environment are presented in the Appendix.
351   For each training seed, we sample and preserve 50 training tasks of size 12 × 12 and difficulty
352   δ = 0.4. All agents are trained for 1.5 × 106 interactions by randomly selecting one of the 50
353   frozen tasks for each training episode. To speed up training, we make the initial state distributions
354   span all the non-terminal states in each training task. This change increases risks of E.2, due to the
355   presence of dense episode-terminating lava traps and relatively short MELs (128 for SSM).
356
357
358   5.2   Evaluative Criteria
359
360   We use the following criteria to investigate the changes in agent’s estimations and behaviors:
361   Estimation Error - E.0, E.1, E.2 & Non-Delusional: At each evaluation point, we solve the
362   ground truth distances between states and inspect the agents’ estimation errors. The errors are
363   split based on the source-target pairs, into those involving problematic targets, i.e., E.1 and E.2,
364   and those involving only valid targets, which includes the case of E.0. The estimation errors show
365   the internal degree of delusions about targets within the estimators.
366   Delusional Behavior Frequencies: We also monitor the frequency of a problematic target (G.1
367   or G.2) being chosen by the agents, as the result of their delusions in decision-time planning. The
368   frequencies demonstrate how often delusional targets are being favored because of delusions.
369
      Improvement on OOD Generalization Performance: We analyze the changes in agents’ OOD
370
      generalization performance, due to the strategies introduced to handle delusions. The evaluation
371
      tasks are sampled from a gradient of OOD difficulties - 0.25, 0.35, 0.45 and 0.55. For aggregated
372   OOD performance, such as in Fig. 3 g), we sample 20 tasks from each of the 4 OOD difficulties,
373   and combine the performance across all 80 episodes, which have a mean difficulty matching the
374   training tasks. By comparing the resulted performances of atomic strategies with those of the
375   newly proposed hybrids, we can also have an intuitive grasp of the “delusion gap”, which is the
376   amount of performance degradation caused by delusions. To maximize difficulty, the initial state is
377   fixed in each novel evaluation task instance: the agents are not only spawned to be at the furthest
      side of the monster, but also in semantic class ⟨0, 0⟩, i.e., without the sword nor the shield in hand.

      5.3   Methods
379
380   We seek to demonstrate the generality of our strategies by applying them onto different methods
381   with very different behaviors. Thus, we adapted the following target-directed methods:
382
      Skipper: generates candidate target states that, together with the current state, form a directed
383
      graph at decision-time. The states act as vertices and the edges are pairwise estimations of cumu-
384   lative rewards and discounts (interchangeable with distances), under its evolving policy. A target
385   state is chosen after applying value iteration (VI), i.e. the favorability of targets are defined by the
386   Q values of the planned paths (Zhao et al., 2024).
387
      LEAP (in Appendix): Instead of VI, LEAP uses an evolutionary algorithm to evolve sequences of
388
      subgoals leading to the task goal, with distances as fitness. The immediate subgoal of the elitest
389
      sequence is chosen to be the target, and used to condition a lower-level policy. LEAP is prone to
390   delusions, since one problematic subgoal can destroy a whole sequence (Nasiriany et al., 2019).
391
392   For both agents, the generators and estimators are trained with HER, allowing easy switching of re-
393
      labeling strategies for comparison. Roughly, both agents employ update rules that can reject wrong
      estimations with data exposure (more discussions in Appendix). Targets are acquired by encoding
394
      observations imagined by the generators, where G.1 & G.2 propositions are clearly identified.
395

      5.4   Hindsight Relabeling Strategies
397
398   We focus on the following baselines for fairer comparison (excluding bad generator performance):
399
400    • “F-E”: “future” for generator, “episode” for estimator;
401    • “F-P”: “future” for generator, “pertask” for estimator;
402
       • “F-G”: “future” for generator, with 100% chance using “generate” JIT on estimator;
403
404   Additionally, the following hybrid strategies are proposed and compared:
405
406    * “F-(E+G)”: A hybrid strategy mostly for E.1. “future” for generator, “episode” for estimator with
407      50% chance using “generate” JIT, resulting in a half-half mixture of “episode” & “generate”;
408    * “F-(E+P)”: mostly for E.2. “future” for generator, half “episode” & half “pertask” for estimator;
409    * “F-(E+P+G)”: for both E.1 & E.2 behaviors. “future” for generator, mixture of 2/3 “episode”
410      and 1/3 “pertask” for estimator, with 1/4 chance using “generate” JIT, resulting in a mixture
411      of 50% “episode”, 25% “pertask” and 25% “generate” for estimator;

      6   Related Works
487
488
      Target-Directed Agents. Dual-component frameworks are explicitly investigated with temporal
489   abstraction for purposes such as path planning (Nasiriany et al., 2019), OOD generalization (Zhao
490   et al., 2024) and task decomposition (Nair & Finn, 2020; Zadem et al., 2024). Davchev et al. (2021)
491   employs the similar framework to assist exploration, without using HER for training.
492
      Hindsight Relabeling is crucial for enhancing sample efficiency in goal-directed RL (Andrychow-
493
      icz et al., 2017), as it enables learning from failed experiences (Dai et al., 2021). From the perspective
494
      of source-target pairs, improvements were proposed, including divergence maximization (Zhang
495   & Stadie, 2022), addressing distributional change (Bai et al., 2023), multistep relabeling strategies
496   (Yang et al., 2021b), prioritizing rarely-seen achieved transitions (Kuang et al., 2020), etc.
497
498
      HER’s success was centered on its performance gains via sample efficiency (in non-delusional
      cases), around which most follow-up works revolved as well. In reality, performance deficiencies of
499
      target-directed frameworks can be resulted from multiple factors, one of which is delusions. Shams
500
      & Fevens (2022) studied the performance of atomic strategies from the view of sample efficiency,
501   without looking into the failure modes. Deshpande et al. (2018) detailed experimental techniques
502   in sparse reward settings using “future”. In (Yang et al., 2021a), a mixture strategy similar to “gen-
503   erate” improved non-delusional estimations, though the impact on delusions was not explored,
504   possibly because of the lack of an appropriate environment. Nasiriany et al. (2019) used a mixture
505   of up to 3 atomic strategies for producing effective training data in a single-task setting, while the
506   contributions of the mixture to addressing delusions were not investigated. It is widely known
507   that performance of existing HER-trained agents are often limited by their exclusive reliance on
508   “future” or “episode” (He et al., 2020), whose delusions this paper intends to address.
509   Delusions in RL can stem from other improper designs. Lu et al. (2018) identified delusions caused
510   by the limitations of function approximators for greedy policies in model-free RL; Often accompa-
511   nying hallucinations, delusions can arise in non-target-directed frameworks as well. In an offline
512   planning framework such as Dyna, hallucinations can irreversibly destabilize value estimations
513   (Jafferjee et al., 2020). Di Langosco et al. (2022) classified goal misgeneralization, a class of delu-
514   sional behaviors with which an agent competently pursues an undesired target leading in novel
515
      test situations. Zhao et al. (2024) gave first examples of delusional behaviors caused by improper
      hindsight relabeling. The perspective of delusions can often be overlooked because of the lack of
516
      estimators in target-directed methods, as discussions regarding hallucinations get magnified.
517
518
519   7   Empirical Guidelines
520
521   The following steps can be applied to target-directed methods (applicable also beyond HER):
522
       1. Incorporate an estimator to enable rejection of unwanted targets;
523
524    2. Use proper update rules and try to maximize the diversity of the training data (against E.0);
525    3. Inspect the candidate targets if possible, as their low quality may indicate higher E.1 risks,
526       which can be mitigated with “generate”;
527    4. Analyze the state structure of the target tasks and identify temporary unreachabilities. These
528       E.2 risks may indicate the effectiveness of “pertask”.
529
530
      8   Conclusion & Future Work
531
532
      We investigated the causes of delusional behaviors, a shared failure mode of target-directed agents.
533
      When applying our mitigation strategies on HER, the resulting hybrid hindsight relabeling strate-
534   gies flexibly satisfy the needs of the generator and the estimator, granting the agents the ability to
535   address delusions autonomously and preemptively avoid delusional behaviors.
536
537
      It is likely that we did not exhaustively identify all potential types of delusions in target-directed
      agents. We wish to continue the investigation into unidentified causes of failure modes and design
538
      more useful skill-learning agents.

886   The third set of experiments, similar to the previous two, is a comparative study for LEAP vari-
887   ants’ performance on SSM. LEAP is different from Skipper, as its decision-time planning process
888   constructs a singular sequence of subgoals leading to the task goal. Due to a lack of backup sub-
889   goals, even if one among them is problematic, the whole resulting plan would be delusional, mak-
890   ing LEAP much more prone to failures compared to Skipper, where candidate targets can still be
891   reused if deviation from the original plan occurred.
892   SSM has a relatively large state space that requires more intermediate subgoals for LEAP’s plans.
893   However, an increment of the number of subgoals also dramatically increases the frequencies of
894   delusional plans, damaging the agents’ performance. Because of this, our experimental results of
895   LEAP on SSM with size 12 × 12 became difficult to analyze because of the rampant failures. We

1017   “pertask” takes the advantage for the fact that training is done on limited number of fixed task
1018
       instances. We give each task a unique task identifier. At relabeling time, “pertask” samples ob-
       servations among all the transitions marked with the same identifier as the current training task

1022   B.5.2           Discussions
1023
1024   “generate” not only creates targets with G.1 targets, but also generate valid targets that should
1025   resemble the distribution it was trained on. Thus, it is not clear if mixing in data augmented by
       “generate” would result in lower sample efficiency in the estimation cases involving valid targets.


                                                                                      19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
       Take SSM as an example, “generate” seemed to have detrimental effect to non-delusional cases
1027   when applied to Skipper, while it greatly boosted accuracies for LEAP overall.
1028
1029   In some experiments, “pertask” demonstrated clear effectiveness in addressing E.1 as well, despite
1030
       that it was not designed to. This is likely because of some generalization effects of the estimator,
       which were trained with additional data that boosted data diversity.
1031
1032   In some environments, we expect that “pertask” could also be used (for mixtures of the generator)
1033   to learn to generate longer-distance targets from the current states if the generator has trouble
1034   doing so with “future”, with the accompanied risks of lower efficiency and G.2 hallucinations.

1036   C       Implementation Details for Experiments
1037
1038   C.1      Skipper
1039
1040   Our adaptation of Skipper over the original implementation1 in Zhao et al. (2024) is minimal. We
1041   have additionally added two simple vertex pruning procedures before the vertex pruning based
1042   on k-medoids. These two procedures include: 1) prune vertices that are duplicated, and 2) prune
1043
       vertices that cannot be reached from the current state with the estimated connectivity.
1044   We implemented a version of generator that can reliably handle both RDS and SSM with the same
1045   architecture. Please consult models.py in the submitted source code for its detailed architecture.
1046
       For SSM instances, since the state spaces are 4-times bigger than those of RDS, we ask that Skipper
1047   generate twice the number of candidates (both before and after pruning) for the proxy problems.
1048
1049   All other architectures and hyperparameters are identical to the original implementation.
1050   For better adaptability during evaluation and faster training, Skipper variants in this paper keeps
1051   the constructed proxy problem for the whole episode during training and replanning only triggers
1052   a re-selection, while during evaluation, the proxy problems are always erased and re-constructed.
1053
       The quality of our adaptation of the original implementation can be assured by the fact the “F-E”
1054   variant’s performance matches the original on RDS.
1055
1056
       C.2      LEAP
1057
1058   C.2.1      Adaptation for Discrete Action Spaces
1059
1060
       LEAP’s training involves two pretraining stages, that are, generator pretraining and distance esti-
       mator training.
1061
1062   We improved upon the adopted discrete-action space compatible implementation of LEAP (Nasiri-
1063   any et al., 2019) from Zhao et al. (2024). We gave LEAP additional flexibility to use fewer subgoals
1064   along the way to the task goal if necessary. Also, we improved upon the Cross-Entropy Method
1065   (CEM), such that elite sequences would be kept intact in the next population during the optimiza-
1066
       tion process. We increased the base population size of each generation to 512 and lengthened the
       number of iterations to 10.
1067
1068   For RDS 12 × 12 and SSM 8 × 8, at most 3 subgoals are used in each planned path. We find that
1069   employing more subgoals greatly increases the burden of CEM and lower the quality of the evolved
1070   subgoal sequences, leading to bad performance that cannot be effectively analyzed.
1071   We used the same generator architecture and hyperparameters as in Skipper. All other architec-
1072   tures and hyperparameters remain unchanged.
1073
       Similarly for LEAP, for better adaptability during evaluation, the planned sequences of subgoals
1074
       are always reconstructed whenever planning is triggered. While in training, the sequence is reused
1075
       and only a subgoal selection is conducted.
1076
1077   The quality of our adaptation of the original implementation can be assured by the fact the “F-E”
1078   variant’s performance matches the original on RDS.
