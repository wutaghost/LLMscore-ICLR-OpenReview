011                                                     A BSTRACT
012
013                Instruction-based image editing methods allow user-friendly instruction to en-
014
                   hance controllability via natural command. However, without a user-provided
                   mask, existing methods could not identify and edit specific objects if multiple
015
                   similar instances exist, such as “add the man on the right a hat”. Furthermore,
016
                   the iterative nature of the editing process may inherently involve ambiguous ref-
017                erences from users, such as ‘change it to blue’, posing challenges in identifying
018                the target without a contextual understanding. Multimodal large language models
019                (MLLMs) offer impressive cross-modal comprehension and co-reference resolu-
020                tion capabilities. In this work, we present ReferPix2Pix, which leverages MLLMs
021                to interpret editing instructions and provide regions of interest (RoI) for precise
022                editing. Such pixel-grounded guidance from MLLMs enhances comprehension of
023                referring expressions and resolves ambiguous references that facilitate localized
024                editing of editing models. Additionally, we developed CoReferEdit benchmark to
025
                   evaluate editing capabilities across iterative editing phases with multimodal co-
                   references. Our comprehensive experiments show that our approach significantly
026
                   enhances editing capability in referring and co-referential editing tasks. Our code
027
                   and data will be made publicly available1 .

030   1       I NTRODUCTION
031
032   As the need for visual content continues to grow across industries like photography, advertising, and
033   social media, the role of image editing in improving and modifying images has become more crucial.
034   Using natural language, an intuitive and adaptable tool, simplifies the guidance of the image editing
035   process. Consequently, text-guided image editing has become increasingly favored, surpassing the
036   popularity of other methods (Ling et al., 2021; Shi et al., 2022; Meng et al., 2021) that need users to
037
      specify editing regions.
038   Early text-based editing methods (Nam et al., 2018; El-Nouby et al., 2019; Meng et al., 2021;
039   Hertz et al., 2022) relied on description-based captions, where the editing command outlines the
040   desired image’s attributes. This approach is not user-friendly, as it requires individuals to provide
041   an extensive description of the target image rather than a straightforward editing instruction. In-
042   stPix2Pix (Brooks et al., 2023) is the first to collect a large-scale instruction-based editing dataset
043
      with input-goal-instruction triplet, where the instruction is generated by GPT-3, and the target image
      is synthesized from Prompt-to-Prompt (Hertz et al., 2022). MagicBrush (Zhang et al., 2024) intro-
044
      duces instruction-based interactive editing in the multi-round scenario and provides the edit mask
045
      annotations.
046
047   However, existing approaches have two notable limitations. First, they perform in benchmarks that
048   contain images with a predominant single instance, which doesn’t align with real-world scenarios
049
      where images often contain multiple instances. For instance, a user may want to specify an edit for
      one particular item, like ”change the shirt of the right man to blue”. This requires the editing model
050
      to understand referring expressions. Unfortunately, current instruction-guided, mask-free methods
051
      fall short of accurately grounding these referring phrases, leading to incorrect edits that affect all
052   instances in the image, not just the intended subject, as shown in fig. 3.

085   Moreover, the iterative nature of image editing introduces challenges with ambiguous co-references.
086   For instance, after an initial instruction like ”change the shirt of the right man to blue”, a subse-
087   quent command such as “add him a hat” can be unclear without a proper contextual understanding
088   or memory of previous interaction. Although existing benchmark MagicBrush (Zhang et al., 2024)
089   introduces multi-round editing with commands like “have him a cowboy hat” or “wear it a neck-
090   lace”, however, there is only one dominant instance within the source image, thus not consider the
091   scenario of ambiguous references in the editing conversation. Due to the absence of datasets with
092
      multimodal coreferences and the limitation of model design, current approaches struggle to resolve
      ambiguous references in multi-turn editing, as shown in fig. 4.
093
094   In this work, we harness the outstanding multi-modal compression capabilities of MLLMs to
095   identify referring expressions and disambiguate references during editing sessions. Our approach
096   leverages MLLM to direct a latent diffusion-based editing model, enabling precise localization of
097   the target object without requiring explicit masks, as MLLM generates the intermediate editing
098
      mask. To tackle the data scarcity in referring edits, we adeptly modify the original ReferCOCO
      dataset (Kazemzadeh et al., 2014) for the referring editing task. In the first stage, the MLLM is
099
      trained to process interleaved source images and editing instructions. Its output is then mapped to the
100
      SAM-based model (Kirillov et al., 2023) to generate pixel-grounded guidance. In the second stage,
101   we align the frozen MLLM and a diffusion-based editing model, where the MLLM’s pixel-grounded
102   guidance is used as conditional input of the editing model, ensuring referring/co-references editing.
103
104
      Furthermore, to assess the model’s ability in multi-modal co-reference resolution, we estab-
      lished a test set CoReferEdit by utilizing ReferCOCO (Kazemzadeh et al., 2014) annotations and
105
      GPT4V (OpenAI, 2023) generation, incorporating referring expressions in initial editing rounds and
106
      ambiguous references in the follow-up editing turns.

123   Text-based Image Editing. Description-based image editing: Text-based editing models (Nam
124
      et al., 2018; El-Nouby et al., 2019) via GAN are limited to unrealistic synthesis. Diffusion
      models (Ho et al., 2020; Ramesh et al., 2022; Meng et al., 2021; Hertz et al., 2022), by con-
125
      trolling cross-modal attention maps between global description and latent pixels, achieve more
126
      semantically aligned manipulation. Local image editing enables detailed adjustments by filling
127   in specified areas provided by users (Nichol et al., 2021; Couairon et al., 2022; Avrahami et al.,
128   2022; Wang et al., 2023; Bar-Tal et al., 2022). Instruction-guided image editing: Different from
129   description-based editing, instruction-guided editing (El-Nouby et al., 2019; Fu et al., 2020; Zhang
130   et al., 2021) allows users to modify images by providing textual instructions, eliminating the
131   need for detailed descriptions or region selection. InstPix2Pix (Brooks et al., 2023) constructs a
132   large-scale instruction-based editing dataset by collecting synthetic texts from GPT-3 that finetuned
133   on human-annotated instructions, and target images by (Hertz et al., 2022), enables image editing
134   by following instructions. HIVE (Zhang et al., 2023b) utilizes training triplets and human ranking
135
      results to provide stronger supervision signals for better model training. MagicBrush (Zhang et al.,
      2024) introduces instruction-based interactive editing in the multi-round scenario. MGIE learns a
136
      projection from MLLMs to an editing model (Brooks et al., 2023) for instructional editing tasks.
137
      In this work, we advance the interactive editing task with referring expressions and co-reference
138   resolution to facilitate more natural conversational editing in the real world.

      localize a target object in an image described by a referring expression phrased in natural language.
142

      sion segmentation, comprehension, and visual grounding. In this work, we introduce referring
144   expressions to the image editing task, where the model is required to localize the edit object given

148

149

151   to the story visualization setting. In this work, we investigate co-reference resolution within the
152   context of interactive image editing tasks. It requires the model to identify and precisely modify
153   the targeted object when users provide ambiguous references throughout multiple rounds of editing
154   sessions.

204   MLLMs excel in vision-language tasks, such as image captioning (Li et al., 2023b) and ground-
205   ing (Chen et al., 2023; Rasheed et al., 2023). MGIE is the first to use MLLMs to offer semantic
206   guidance by mapping the hidden states of eight additional tokens onto a latent diffusion text condi-
207   tioning space for image editing. However, such semantic-level guidance struggles to provide visual
208   details for referring phrases within the editing instructions. In addition, the scarcity of multi-turn
209   editing data with co-references hampers its performance on editing with co-references resolution.
210   To overcome the limitations of previous methods, we develop a two-stage pipeline for advanced
211   editing tasks. In the first stage, the MLLM is trained to take images and editing instructions as in-
212   put and produce pixel-level guidance. To circumvent data constraints, we innovatively repurpose a
213   richly annotated image comprehension dataset for the referring editing task. In the second stage, we
214   align a latent diffusion-based editing model with the first-stage MLLM, thereby enhancing its abil-
215   ity to comprehend referring expressions and resolve ambiguous co-references in multi-turn editing
      scenarios.

      3.1     G ENERATING P IXEL -G ROUNDED G UIDANCE VIA MLLM S
217
218   In the first stage, we leverage MLLMs to comprehend interleaved images and textual instructions,
219   thereby facilitating pixel-level grounding that enhances precise editing guidance. More specifically,
220   the input image ximg is encoded by an image encoder CLIP ViT-H/14 (Radford et al., 2021), and its
221   visual representation Ix is projected to the input space of LLM L denoted as fv2t (Ix ). To facilitate
222   fine-grained pixel-level object grounding, similar to (Rasheed et al., 2023), we utilize a pretrained
223   SAM (Kirillov et al., 2023) encoder as a segmentation image encoder ESAM and a SAM-based
224
      segmentation decoder DSAM . We add a new token [SEG] to the LLM vocabulary, and the last
      hidden state h[SEG] of [SEG] token is projected to the segmentation decoder’s input space, denoted
225
      as ft2m (h[SEG] ). Therefore, the MLLM is trained to predict the ground truth text, represented as
226
      yt = L(fv2t (Ix ), xt ). Its last hidden state of [SEG], as well as the encoded feature by SAM
227   encoder ESAM (ximg ) are taken as input to the decoder DSAM to produce segmentation mask M ,
228   defined as follows:
229
230
231                                   M = DSAM (ft2m (h[SEG] ), ESAM (ximg ))                            (1)
232
233
      The predicted output yt and the segmentation mask M are used for calculating the caption loss
      Lcaption and the mask loss Lmask respectively. The whole pipeline of the first stage is demonstrated
234
      in fig. 2 with gray arrows.

237
238   For datasets with input-goal-instruction triplet, along with the editing masks, e.g., Mag-
239   icBrush (Zhang et al., 2024), we can directly use it as our instruction finetuning data. The input
240   prompt is defined as follows:
241
      User: The <image> provides an overview of the picture. Given this editing instruction: {edit
242
      instruction}. Please segment the edited region in this image.
243
244   Assistant: Sure, it is [SEG].
245   where the <image> token is replaced by 256 tokens generated by the image encoder E. The MLLM
246   learns to produce “Sure, it is [SEG]”, and the last hidden state of the [SEG] is then passed through
247   the segmentation decoder D to produce segmentation mask as mentioned above.
248
      Editing with Referring Expressions. However, there is no large-scale editing dataset with editing
249
      mask annotations. Therefore, we have devised a strategy to effectively leverage annotated data from
250   other tasks, repurposing them for editing instruction training. Specifically, we turn our attention to
251   ReferCOCO (Kazemzadeh et al., 2014), a dataset originally curated for referring expression com-
252   prehension, which features multiple object instances within each image. Each of these instances
253   is annotated with both referring expressions and corresponding segmentation masks By harness-
254   ing this richly annotated data, we can ingeniously adapt ReferCOCO (Kazemzadeh et al., 2014)
255   for our edit instruction training needs through the automated generation of a comprehensive set of
256   edit instructions derived from the dataset’s existing annotations. The modified version is denoted
257   as ReferCOCOedit . Below is an example in the automatically generated list of editing templates:
258
      “replace {class name} with {new class}”, where {class name} represents the referring expression
      corresponding to an instance within a ReferCOCO image, while {new class} is obtained by ran-
259
      domly sampling from the set of COCO object classes. Please refer to the supplementary to find the
260
      full list of automatically generated edit templates.
261
262   This design enables us to adeptly harness the referring expressions within ReferCOCO to generate
263   edit instructions with referring phrases and utilize the corresponding segmentation masks as editing
264
      guides. This eliminates the need for manual annotation, editing of masks, or the generation of
      target images for training, thereby effectively circumventing the constraints posed by the scarcity of
265
      instruction-based datasets with mask annotations.

291   xt . We build on top of latent diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022) that learns to generate data samples
292   through a sequence of denoising in the latent space of a pretrained variational autoencoder with en-
293   coder EV AE and decoder DV AE . More specifically, as shown in fig. 2, the MLLM will take as input

      The second-stage end-to-end training design that uses the mask predicted by MLLMs as the con-
317

318   training, is anchored in a critical insight: the inherent discrepancy between the ground-truth mask
319   and the predicted mask. Utilizing the ground truth mask as the sole training input could lead to
320   a scenario where, during inference, the editing model might indiscriminately modify every region
321   indicated by the MLLM’s mask, resulting in suboptimal editing outcomes, especially if the guidance
322   from MLLM lacks precision. To mitigate this risk and enhance the model’s performance, we mix up
323   the ground truth masks and predicted masks by MLLM as the conditional input for latent diffusion,
      thereby ensuring greater flexibility and robustness in its editing capabilities.

      Although MagicBrush (Zhang et al., 2024) includes references in the multi-round editing session,
      such as “Have him a cowboy hat” or “Wear it a necklace”. However, there is only one object
328
      instance in the image, thereby it does not consider the scenario of multiple instances in one image,
329
      where the model is required to identify the target edit object across different instances given an
330   ambiguous reference. Hence, we design an automatic pipeline to collect a test set to evaluate the
331   model’s editing ability in a multi-round co-reference resolution setting.
332
333
      Specifically, we consider ReferCOCO (Kazemzadeh et al., 2014) images since they contain multiple
      instances in an image and the corresponding referring expressions. Then we fed randomly sampled
334
      image, the edit object with the referring expression, such as “the man on the right” and original
335
      caption to gpt-4-vision-preview (OpenAI, 2023). In the first round, GPT4V is used to
336   generate an edit instruction regarding the input edit object, a global caption that modifies the original
337   caption based on the generated edit instructions, and a local caption that focuses on describing the
338   edit object only. In the follow-up turns, GPT4V is prompted to generate edit instructions regarding
339   the same object but uses ambiguous references for the edit object and generates a new global/local
340   caption. fig. 10 demonstrates an example in our collected CoReferEdit data, and fig. 9 shows the
341   distribution of edit object class and edit instruction in the collected set. All the edit sessions include
342   3 rounds of editing, and after manual quality control, there are 403 editing sessions and 1196 edit
343   turns in the collected test set.

      5.1    DATASETS AND E VALUATION METRICS
349
      MagicBrush. MagicBrush (Zhang et al., 2024) features multiple rounds of editing within its ses-
350
      sions, with 8,807 editing turns for training, 528 for dev, and 1,053 for test set. We adhere to the
351
      original train/dev/test splits for training and evaluating models. The results are in the context of
352   multi-round editing, where the images edited in the final turn are evaluated using L1/L2 distance,
353   CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) image-image similarity (CLIP-I), CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) image-text
354   similarity (CLIP-T), and the DINO (Zhang et al., 2022) score, consistent with MagicBrush (Zhang
355   et al., 2024). We report the final turn results to evaluate the editing capability in multi-round editing.
356
      GQA-Inpaint. GQA-Inpaint (Yildirim et al., 2023) was built on top of the GQA Dataset (Hudson &
357
      Manning, 2019) which includes multiple instances and referring expressions for the images. GQA-
358   Inpaint leverages the annotations in GQA and designed editing instructions containing referring
359   expressions such as “remove the woman at the right of the boat”, where the edit object is selected
360   from the scene graphs of GQA. All the comparison methods report zero-shot performance using
361   L1/L2 distance, CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) image similarity (CLIP-I), and DINO (Zhang et al.,
362   2022) score on this dataset. We utilize this dataset to assess the editing capability with referring
363   expressions.
364   CoReferEdit. We mentioned the collection pipeline and data distribution details in section 4. The
365   test set contains 403 edit sessions and 1196 edit turns. We facilitate the evaluation of the collected
366   dataset by calculating the global or local CLIP text-image similarity for the final turn, or all turns on
367   average. For local caption, the edited image is cropped based on the bounding box of the edit object
368   to calculate the local image-text similarity. We utilize this dataset to assess the editing capability
369   with ambiguous references in multi-round editing.

      6      C ONCLUSION
528
529
530   In conclusion, we first discussed the limitations of existing instruction-based image editing methods
531
      that struggle with identifying and modifying specific objects in the presence of multiple instances
      without user-provided masks. The challenge is further compounded during iterative editing pro-
532
      cesses, where vague references like ’change it to blue’ require a contextual understanding to ac-
533
      curately identify the target. We introduce ReferPix2Pix, which utilizes the MLLM’s multimodal
534   reasoning comprehension and co-reference resolution capabilities for advanced editing tasks. This
535   enables the interpretation of editing instructions and the provision of precise RoI for image editing,
536   thereby significantly improving the ability to understand referring expressions and resolve ambigu-
537   ous references in iterative editing turns. Furthermore, we established CoReferEdit for evaluating the
538   performance of editing models in handling co-referential editing tasks. Our comprehensive experi-
539   ments show that our approach significantly enhances editing capability in referring and co-referential
      editing tasks.

951   E        I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS .
952
953   In the first stage, we use MagicBrush (Zhang et al., 2024), ReferCOCOedit and ReferCOCOcoref edit as
954   the training data. The MLLM is trained with captioning loss, Mask BCELoss, and Mask DICELoss.
955   The training batch size is 16 and uses AdamW optimizer with learning rate 1e − 4 for 4 epochs. We
956
      use MagicBrush (Zhang et al., 2024) and modified ReferCOCO (Kazemzadeh et al., 2014) for the
      first stage of training. In the second stage, the first stage model is kept frozen, and we only train
957
      the Unet of the latent diffusion. The input channel of the first convolution layer is set to 12. The
958
      training is conducted with a batch size of 64 and a learning rate of 1e − 4 over 4k steps. We use
959   MagicBrush (Zhang et al., 2024) and InstPix2Pix (Brooks et al., 2023) as the training data in the
960   second stage. αI and αT are set to be 1.5 and 7.5 respectively. All experiments are conducted in
961   PyTorch on 2 80G A100 GPUs.
