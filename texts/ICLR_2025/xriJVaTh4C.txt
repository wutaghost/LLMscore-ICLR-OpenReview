008                                                A BSTRACT
009
010            Training neural networks with high certified accuracy against adversarial examples
011            remains an open challenge despite significant efforts. While certification methods
012            can effectively leverage tight convex relaxations for bound computation, in training,
013            these methods, perhaps surprisingly, can perform worse than looser relaxations.
014            Prior work hypothesized that this phenomenon is caused by the discontinuity,
015            non-smoothness, and perturbation sensitivity of the loss surface induced by tighter
016            relaxations. In this work, we theoretically show that Gaussian Loss Smoothing
017
               (GLS) can alleviate these issues. We confirm this empirically by instantiating GLS
               with two variants: a zeroth-order optimization algorithm, called PGPE, which
018
               allows training with non-differentiable relaxations, and a first-order optimization
019
               algorithm, called RGS, which requires gradients of the relaxation but is much more
020            efficient than PGPE. Extensive experiments show that when combined with tight
021            relaxations, these methods surpass state-of-the-art methods when training on the
022            same network architecture for many settings. Our results clearly demonstrate the
023            promise of Gaussian Loss Smoothing for training certifiably robust neural networks
024            and pave a path towards leveraging tighter relaxations for certified training.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   The increased deployment of deep learning systems in mission-critical applications has made their
029   provable trustworthiness and robustness against adversarial examples (Biggio et al., 2013; Szegedy
030   et al., 2014) an important topic. As state-of-the-art neural network certification has converged to
031   similar approaches (Zhang et al., 2022; Ferrari et al., 2022), increasingly reducing the verification
032   gap, the focus in the field is now shifting to specialized training methods that yield networks with
033   high certified robustness while minimizing the loss of standard accuracy (Müller et al., 2023; Mao
034
      et al., 2023a; De Palma et al., 2024).
035
      Certified Training State-of-the-art (SOTA) certified training methods aim to optimize the network’s
036
      worst-case loss over an input region defined by an adversarial specification. However, as computing
037
      the exact worst-case loss is NP-complete (Katz et al., 2017), they typically utilize convex relaxations
038   to compute over-approximations (Gowal et al., 2018; Singh et al., 2018; 2019). Surprisingly, training
039   methods based on the least precise relaxations (IBP) empirically yield the best performance (Shi
040   et al., 2021), while tighter relaxations perform progressively worse (left, Figure 1). Jovanović et al.
041   (2022) and (Lee et al., 2021) investigated this surprising phenomenon which they call the “Paradox
042   of Certified Training”, both theoretically and empirically, and found that tighter relaxations induce
043   harder optimization problems. Specifically, they identify the continuity, smoothness, and sensitivity of
044   the loss surface induced by a relaxation as key factors for the success of certified training, beyond its
045   tightness. Indeed, all state-of-the-art methods are based on the imprecise but continuous, smooth, and
046
      insensitive IBP bounds (Müller et al., 2023; Mao et al., 2023a; De Palma et al., 2024). However, while
      these IBP-based methods improve robustness, they induce severe regularization, significantly limiting
047
      the effective capacity and thus standard accuracy (Mao et al., 2023b). This raises the following
048
      fundamental question:
049
050
         Can we enable certified training with tight convex relaxations by addressing the discontinuity,
      non-smoothness, and perturbation sensitivity, thus obtaining a better robustness-accuracy trade-off?
051
052

093   2     T RAINING FOR C ERTIFIED ROBUSTNESS
094
095   Below, we first introduce the setting of adversarial robustness before providing a background on
096   (training for) certified robustness.
097
098   2.1   A DVERSARIAL ROBUSTNESS
099
      We consider a neural network fθ (x) : X → Rn , parameterized by the weights θ, that assigns
100
      a score to each class i ∈ Y given an input x ∈ X . This induces the classifier F : X → Y as
101   F (x) := arg maxi fθ (x)i . We call F locally robust for an input x ∈ X if it predicts the same class
102   y ∈ Y for all inputs in an ϵ-neighborhood Bpϵ (x) := {x′ ∈ X | ∥x − x′ ∥p ≤ ϵ}. To prove that a
103   classifier is locally robust, we thus have to show that F (x′ ) = F (x) = y, ∀x′ ∈ Bpϵ (x).
104
105   Adversarial Attacks and Empirical Robustness Disproving local robustness for a given input x
106   is done by finding an adversarial example x′ ∈ Bpϵ (x) such that F (x′ ) ̸= F (x). The procedure of
107   searching for adversarial examples is called adversarial attack. The most common attack methods
      (Goodfellow et al., 2015; Madry et al., 2018) use first-order gradient information to maximize the


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      loss function associated with x′ . When such a method fails to find an adversarial example, we say
109   that the network is empirically robust for the given input x and perturbation radius ϵ.
110
111   Robustness Guarantees Local robustness is equivalent to the log-probability of the target class y
112   being greater than that of all other classes for all relevant inputs, i.e., minx′ ∈B,i̸=y f (x′ )y − f (x′ )i >
113   0. As solving this neural network verification problem exactly is generally NP-complete (Katz et al.,
114   2017), state-of-the-art neural network verifiers relax it to an efficiently solvable convex optimization
115   problem (Brix et al., 2023). To this end, the non-linear activation functions are replaced with convex
116
      relaxations in their input-output space, allowing linear bounds of the following form on their output
      f (x) to be computed:
117
                                         Al x + bl ≤ fθ (x) ≤ Au x + bu ,                                          (1)
118
119
      for some input region Bpϵ (x). These bounds can in turn be bounded concretely by ly =
120
      minx∈B Ali x + bli ∈ R and uy analogously. Hence, we have ly ≤ f (x) ≤ uy .
121   To obtain (certifiably) robust neural networks, specialized training methods are required. For
122   a data distribution (x, t) ∼ D, standard training optimizes the network parametrization θ to
123   minimizePthe expected cross-entropy
                                               loss θstd = arg minθ ED [LCE (fθ (x), t)] with LCE (y, t) =
124   ln 1 + i̸=t exp(yi − yt ) . To train for robustness, we, instead, aim to minimize the expected
125   worst-case loss for a  given robustness specification,
                                                             leading to a min-max optimization problem:
126   θrob = arg minθ ED maxx′ ∈Bϵ (x) LCE (fθ (x′ ), t) . As computing the worst-case loss by solving
127   the inner maximization problem is generally intractable, it is commonly under- or over-approximated,
128   yielding adversarial and certified training, respectively.
129
      Adversarial Training optimizes a lower bound on the inner optimization objective. To this end,
130   it first computes concrete examples x′ ∈ B ϵ (x) that approximately maximize the loss term LCE
131   and then optimizes the network parameters θ for these examples. While networks trained this way
132   typically exhibit good empirical robustness, they remain hard to formally certify and are sometimes
133   vulnerable to stronger attacks (Tramèr et al., 2020; Croce & Hein, 2020).
134
135   Certified Training typically optimizes an upper bound on the inner maximization objective. To
136   this end, the robust cross-entropy loss LCE,rob (B ϵ (x), t) = LCE (y ∆ , t) is computed from an upper
137   bound y ∆ on the logit differences y ∆ := y − yt obtained via convex relaxations as described above
138   and then plugged into the standard cross-entropy loss. As this can induce strong over-regularization
139   if the used convex relaxations are imprecise and thereby severely reduce the standard accuracy of
140   the resulting models, current state-of-the-art certified training methods combine these bounds with
141
      adversarial training (De Palma et al., 2022; Müller et al., 2023; Mao et al., 2023a; De Palma et al.,
      2024). In the following, we introduce the convex relaxations popular for neural networks.

      2.3   T HE PARADOX OF C ERTIFIED T RAINING
202
      When training networks for robustness with convex relaxations, higher robustness is achieved by
203
      sacrificing standard accuracy. Usually, more precise relaxations induce less overapproximation
204   and thus less regularization, potentially leading to better standard and certified accuracy. However,
205   empirically the least precise relaxation, IBP, dominates the more precise methods, e.g., D EEP P OLY,
206   with respect to both certified and standard accuracy (see the left-hand side of Figure 1). This is all the
207   more surprising given that state-of-the-art certified training methods introduce artificial unsoundness
208   into these IBP bounds to improve tightness at the cost of soundness to reduce regularisation and
209   improve performance (Müller et al., 2023; Mao et al., 2023a; De Palma et al., 2024).
210   Jovanović et al. (2022) and Lee et al. (2021) explained this paradox, by showing that these more
211   precise relaxations induce loss landscapes suffering from discontinuities, non-smoothness, and
212   perturbation sensitivity (a proxy for difficulty to optimize with gradients), making it extraordinarily
213   challenging for gradient-based optimization methods to find good optima. Thus the key challenge of
214   certified training is to design a robust loss that combines tight bounds with a continuous, smooth,
215   and insensitive loss landscape. In §3, we discuss these challenges in more detail and show how to
      overcome them.

237   The second key factor, non-smoothness, is originally defined as the variation of loss values along the

      is particularly important for gradient-based optimization methods, as this controls the theoretical
242
      convergence of such methods.
243
244   The third key factor, sensitivity, can be interpreted as the difficulty to optimize with gradients.
245   Jovanović et al. (2022) show that D EEP P OLY is more sensitive than IBP, thus gradient-based optimiza-

249

250   If a function has a non-positive deviation from convexity, it is convex, thus gradient-based methods
251   can find global optimum. Since this directly measures non-convexity, intuitively, a function with
252   smaller deviation from convexity is easier to optimize with gradient-based methods. We remark that
253   sensitivity as defined in Jovanović et al. (2022) is different to the deviation from convexity, but the
254   two are closely related in that both indicate how difficult it is to optimize a function with gradients.

256   3.2   G AUSSIAN L OSS S MOOTHING FOR C ERTIFIED T RAINING
257
      We now discuss how Gaussian Loss Smoothing can address these challenges. The central result in
258
      this section is formalized in Theorem 3.1 (proof in App. B.1):
259
260   Theorem 3.1. Let the parameter θ ∈ Rd . Let the nonnegative loss function L(θ) : Rd → R
261
      have bounded growth, that is, L(θ) exp(−∥θ∥2−δ ) ≤ M for some δ < 2 and M > 0. Then, the
      loss smoothed by an isotropic Gaussian N (0, σ 2 I), defined as Lσ (θ) := Eϵ∼N (0,σ2 I) L(θ + ϵ), is
262
      infinitely differentiable. In addition, the deviation from convexity of the smoothed loss never exceed
263
      that of the original loss, that is, D(Lσ ) ≤ D(L); equality holds iff L is an affine function. Further,
264   assuming θ is in a compact set throughout optimization, Lσ is also Lipschitz continuous.
265
266   Theorem 3.1 shows several desired qualities of GLS. First, it shows that GLS can turn any discontinu-
267   ous loss function into a continuous one that is differentiable everywhere, as visualized in Figure 3a.
268   Second, GLS can make the loss surface Lipschitz continuous if we optimize in a compact set, thus
269   ensuring that the loss surface is smooth. Third, GLS can help to overcome the sensitivity issue since
      it provably reduces the deviation from convexity as long as the loss function is not affine. As we show

331   neuro-evolution algorithms. In this context, it is among the best-performing methods for supervised
332   learning (Lange et al., 2023). This property also allows us to apply it for training with tighter, but
333   non-differentiable bounding methods, such as α-CROWN (Xu et al., 2020).
334
335   3.4   R ANDOMIZED G RADIENT S MOOTHING (RGS)
336
337   While the Loss Smoothing induced by the sampling procedure of PGPE leads to a provably continuous
338   and infinitely differentiable loss surfaces, it can be costly to compute. To reduce the training costs,
339   we propose to approximate GLS by RGS (Duchi et al., 2012). RGS approximates the gradient of the
340
      smoothed loss by sampling points θ + ϵi and then averaging the gradients at these perturbed points:
341                                                    1X
                                        ∇θ L̂σ (θ) ∝         ∇θ Lσ (θ + ϵi ).                            (5)
342                                                    n i
343
344   While RGS which approximates in first-order does not provably recover in expectation the gradient
345   of the smoothed loss when the original function is discontinuous (see App. B.3), Duchi et al. (2012)
346   have shown its empirical effectiveness, even with a tiny sample size (n = 2). Therefore, we apply
347
      this alternative to study the performance of GLS in larger networks, as RGS requires much fewer
      samples than PGPE and thus scales better. Further, contrary to before, σ is now a hyperparameter
348
      that needs to be tuned rather than learned. A comparison of training costs is included in App. E.6,
349
      where RGS is shown to be up to 40 times faster than PGPE.

352
353   We now extensively evaluate the effect of GLS via PGPE and RGS on the training characteristics of
354   different relaxation methods. First, we show in §4.1 that PGPE enables training with tight relaxations,
355   even when the relaxation is not differentiable. Second, we demonstrate in §4.2 that RGS scales GLS
356   training to deeper networks, surpassing the performance of the SOTA methods on the same network
357   architecture in many settings. The impact of different hyperparameters on the performance of the
358
      proposed methods is studied in App. C, and a comparison of PGPE and RGS is provided in App. D.3.
      Overall, our results show that GLS can enable certified training with tight relaxations.

518   5    D ISCUSSION
519   This work shows the promise of Gaussian Loss Smoothing (GLS) to enable certified training with tight
520   relaxations. PGPE and RGS, our proposed methods implementing GLS, achieve strong performance
521   empirically. However, there are several limitations and challenges that need to be addressed in
522   future work. First, GLS provably mitigates the discontinuity, non-smoothness, and perturbation
523   sensitivity issues identified, but it is unknown whether these are all the factors contributing to the
524   paradox of certified training. Future work should investigate other potential factors and how they
525   can be addressed. Second, while our methods achieve strong performance, they are computationally
526   expensive. Future work should focus on more computationally efficient smoothing approaches.
527
      Finally, we present a first step towards training with tight relaxations, but our methods could be
      further optimized, similar to how IBP-based methods have been optimized over the years. Overall,
528
      our work opens up a new direction for certified training using tight relaxations, and we hope it will
529
      inspire future work in this area.
530
531   6    C ONCLUSION
532
      This work shows that the three issues contributing to the paradox of certified training identified by
533
      prior works, namely discontinuity, non-smoothness, and perturbation sensitivity, can be mitigated
534   by Gaussian Loss Smoothing (GLS), based on sound theoretical analyses. We instantiate GLS with
535   two methods: Policy Gradients with Parameter-based Exploration (PGPE) and Randomized Gradient
536   Smoothing (RGS). Empirically, we demonstrate that both improve training with tight relaxations,
537   presenting a solid step towards overcoming the paradox. Further, we show that both methods have
538   unique advantages: PGPE allows training with non-differentiable relaxations, while RGS scales
539   better. Our results confirm the importance of loss continuity, smoothness, and insensitivity in certified
      training, and pave the way for future work to leverage tighter relaxations for certified training.

840   B.3   P ROPERTIES OF R ANDOMIZED G RADIENT S MOOTHING
841
842   Discontinuity Considering again the quantized function as defined in App. B.2, we observe that the
843   derivative of the original function is zero almost everywhere, so the smoothed gradient estimated by
844   RGS will also be zero. This means that RGS is incapable of finding the minimum of the discontinuous
845
      functions in general. However, in practice we rarely work with quantized loss functions we used for
      the analysis; instead, we can model the discontinuous loss function as h(x) = f (x) + g(x), where
846
      f (x) is discontinuous like the quantized function and g(x) is continuous. In this case, the derivative
847
      of h is equal to the derivative of g almost everywhere, and thus the RGS algorithm will converge to
848   the same locations when optimizing h as when optimizing g. If the minima of g and h are sufficiently
849   aligned, we can expect RGS to find a good minimum of h.
850
851   Higher Dimensions In higher dimensions, however, the behavior of RGS becomes unpredictable,
852   as not every discontinuous function h can be decomposed into a continuous function g and a quantized
853   function f (e.g. h(x1 , x2 ) = x1 · sign (x2 ) consists of two plane sections separated by a discontinuity
854   along the x1 -axis). In this case, the equivalent loss landscape that the RGS algorithm is optimizing is
855   strongly dependent on the optimization path and the starting point and therefore cannot be defined.

1004   perturbation settings, but more work is needed to surpass the performance of SOTA methods.
1005
1006   Discussion on the regularization induced by IBP for large preturbations Note that L1 regu-
1007   larization has been sufficiently tuned by Mao et al. (2024) for MTL-IBP, thus only increasing L1
1008   regularization strength cannot achieve the kind of regularization needed for certifiability. Therefore,
1009   we speculate that large ϵ leads to much more unstable neurons, leading to exponential growth of
1010   certification difficulty. Thus, for large ϵ, strong (and maybe unnecessary to robustness) regularization
1011   is required to further reduce certification difficulty. Note that the effects of this IBP regularization are
1012   more complex than just limiting the magnitude of the weights, as described by Mao et al. (2023b).

       D.4   C OMPARISON OF GLS AND S HARPNESS -AWARE M INIMIZATION (SAM)
1044
       The gradient computation by perturbing the network weights used in PGPE and RGS has some
1045
       similarities with the Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM, Foret et al. (2020)) algorithm. However,
1046   the SAM algotithm is fundamentally different to GLS. This is because GLS takes the expectation
1047   of neighborhood loss rather than the worst case loss; in fact, SAM is closer to adversarial training
1048   with FGSM (Goodfellow et al., 2015) rather than GLS. In particular, SAM does not resolve the
1049   discontinuity problem, while GLS provably solves it (Theorem 3.1). To see this, consider the threshold
1050   function I(x > 0) and an initial x0 = 0.1. Any single-point gradient based methods (including
1051   SAM) will only get zero gradient, and thus cannot optimize it. Therefore, while it is likely that GLS
1052   has the benefit of reduced sharpness as well, GLS enjoys fundamentally different benefits to SAM.
1053   To confirm this empirically, we apply SAM to IBP and D EEP P OLY training. Specifically, we
1054   update the parameters with gradients computed based on the adversarially perturbed network w′ =
1055   w + ρ × ∇w L/∥∇w L∥2 . We train with IBP and D EEP P OLY on MNIST ϵ = 0.1 with the same CNN3
1056   architecture used in the paper. The results are shown in Table 8, all networks certified with MN-BaB.
