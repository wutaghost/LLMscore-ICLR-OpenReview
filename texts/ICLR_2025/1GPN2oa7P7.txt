011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            High-quality annotations are essential for object detection models, but ensuring
014            label accuracy — especially for bounding boxes — remains both challenging and
015
               costly. This paper introduces ClipGrader, a novel approach that leverages vision-
               language models to automatically assess the accuracy of bounding box annota-
016
               tions. By adapting CLIP (Contrastive Language-Image Pre-training) to evaluate
017
               both class label correctness and spatial precision of bounding box, ClipGrader of-
018            fers an effective solution for grading object detection labels. Tested on modified
019            object detection datasets with artificially disturbed bounding boxes, ClipGrader
020            achieves 91% accuracy on COCO with a 1.8% false positive rate. Moreover, it
021            maintains 87% accuracy with a 2.1% false positive rate when trained on just 10%
022            of the COCO data. ClipGrader also scales effectively to larger datasets such as
023            LVIS, achieving 79% accuracy across 1,203 classes. Our experiments demonstrate
024            ClipGrader’s ability to identify errors in existing COCO annotations, highlighting
025            its potential for dataset refinement. When integrated into a semi-supervised object
026
               detection (SSOD) model, ClipGrader readily improves the pseudo label quality,
               helping achieve higher mAP (mean Average Precision) throughout the training
027
               process. ClipGrader thus provides a scalable AI-assisted tool for enhancing an-
028
               notation quality control and verifying annotations in large-scale object detection
029            datasets.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   The performance of machine learning models is heavily dependent on the quality of training data. In
035   object detection, a fundamental task in computer vision, the accuracy of annotations which encom-
036   passes both the correctness of the class label and spatial precision of the bounding box is crucial.
037   However, curating high-quality object detection datasets is a significant challenge due to the time-
038   consuming and expensive nature of manual annotation processes, not to mention the inevitability
039
      of errors creeping in (Kuznetsova et al., 2018; Vondrick et al., 2013). Existing approaches to data
      collection and annotation, such as crowd sourcing, web scraping, or AI-generated labels, often intro-
040
      duce noise and inconsistencies, potentially compromising model performance (Papadopoulos et al.,
041
      2016; Northcutt et al., 2021; Zare & Yazdi, 2022). With the increasing complexity and scale of
042   datasets, traditional methods of quality control such as manual reviews or simple heuristics struggle
043   to meet the demand. Even widely-used datasets such as COCO (Lin et al., 2014) are subject to label
044   errors, as revealed in prior research (Wang et al., 2022; Chachuła et al., 2023). This highlights the
045   need for automated systems that can efficiently identify and flag noisy labels for review.
046
      In recent years, foundation models have demonstrated remarkable capabilities across various do-
047
      mains (Bommasani et al., 2022). One such model, CLIP (Contrastive Language-Image Pre-training)
048   (Radford et al., 2021), has shown impressive zero-shot performance on a wide range of visual classi-
049   fication tasks. CLIP’s ability to understand both visual and textual information makes it a promising
050   candidate to assess the quality of object detection labels. We hypothesize that the task of grading
051   label quality is inherently simpler than the task of object detection itself. This allows our grader to
052   achieve high performance with relatively less data and computational resources compared to train-
053   ing an accurate object detection model. This principle is similar to that observed in reward models
      (Ouyang et al., 2022) of Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF).

054
      However, CLIP’s original architecture was designed for associating images with textual descrip-
055   tions. Hence it excels in many classification tasks, but does not inherently understand the concept of
056   markers (bounding boxes) added to an image or their quality. Our key innovation lies in developing a
057   training strategy that successfully fine-tunes CLIP to understand what constitutes a “good” bounding
058   box and make accurate judgments about label quality. This involves teaching the model to assess not
059   only the correctness of the class label but also the bounding box’s location and tightness. Notably,
060   our results demonstrates that CLIP can learn and understand the concept of “markers” on images,
061   potentially opening new avenues for visual reasoning tasks (Wu et al., 2017). The key contributions
062   of this paper are as follows:
063
             • We introduce ClipGrader, a novel framework that leverages vision-language models to
064
               automatically assess both class label correctness and bounding box spatial accuracy for
065            object detection annotations.
066
             • We demonstrate that our training strategy enables CLIP to understand and evaluate bound-
067
               ing box “markers” on images — potentially introducing a new capability for vision-
068            language models and expanding their applications.
069
             • Our results highlight ClipGrader’s high accuracy, robustness and data efficiency across var-
070
               ious setups. It maintains strong performance even with very limited labeled data, demon-
071
               strating its effectiveness in providing effective quality control for object detection data cu-
072            ration and improving semi-supervised object detection (SSOD) models (Wang et al., 2023)
073            through pseudo label screening.
074
075   Interestingly, during our error analysis, we observed cases where ClipGrader’s inference results
076   turned out to be more accurate than the ground truth annotations in COCO (see Appendix B), despite
077   ClipGrader being trained with COCO dataset. This phenomenon could be attributed to human errors
078   and inconsistencies in labeling or ambiguities in class definitions (Ma et al., 2022) and underscores
079
      the potential of our approach to contribute to the refinement and improvement of even widely-used
      benchmarks in the field.

082   2   R ELATED W ORK
083
084   CLIP and its adaptations for related vision tasks CLIP (Contrastive Language-Image Pre-
085   training) (Radford et al., 2021) has emerged as a powerful vision-language model, demonstrating
086   impressive zero-shot classification capabilities across a wide range of visual tasks. Its architecture,
087
      combining a vision encoder and a text encoder trained on a large-scale dataset of image-text pairs,
      allows it to understand and relate visual and textual information effectively. Several works have
088
      attempted to adapt CLIP for object detection and segmentation tasks. Notable examples include
089
      RegionClip(Zhong et al., 2021), CLIPSeg (Lüddecke & Ecker, 2022) and GLIP (Li et al., 2022).
090   However, these works focus on direct visual tasks like segmentation or detection, whereas our ap-
091   proach is the first to leverage CLIP as a grader to assess the quality of bounding box annotations.
092   By using CLIP’s multimodal understanding, we evaluate both class label correctness and spatial ac-
093   curacy, introducing a novel application that shifts CLIP’s role from a detector to a quality assurance
094   tool for object detection datasets.
095
      Label quality assessment Label quality assessment has been an ongoing challenge in machine
096   learning, particularly for large-scale datasets. Previous works have explored various approaches,
097   including confidence learning (Northcutt et al., 2022; Chachuła et al., 2023) and learning with noisy
098   labels (Song et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022). Our work contributes to this field by introducing a novel
099   foundation model-based approach specifically tailored for object detection tasks, focusing on both
100   class label correctness and bounding box quality. Also, ClipGrader itself is not a object detection
101   model. It could be trained more data efficiently, such that it would be made available early in the
102   data curation process with only very few seed label data available.
103   Semi-Supervised Learning in Object Detection Semi-supervised learning approaches, such as
104   ConsistentTeacher (Wang et al., 2023), have shown great promise by generating pseudo-labels from
105   large amounts of unlabeled data. These methods (Xu et al., 2021; Sohn et al., 2020) typically rely on
106   internal confidence measures or consistency between pseudo-labels to evaluate these self-generated
107   pseudo-labels, but they may struggle to accurately assess the quality of the AI-generated annotations.
      ClipGrader complements these approaches by providing an external, language-grounded assessment

140   3     M ETHODOLOGY
141
142   Our goal is to transform CLIP into a grading model that evaluates both the accuracy of an anno-
143   tation’s object label and the quality of its corresponding bounding box. Below, we detail the task
144   formulation, data preparation, model modification, and training strategy.
145
146
      3.1   TASK F ORMULATION
147
148
      We formalize the grading problem as an image-text matching task using CLIP’s contrastive learning
149   framework. However, assessing the quality of bounding box annotations presents several unique
150   challenges that extend beyond typical classification tasks:
151
152
      Context Dependency: The quality of a bounding box is inherently context-dependent. For example,
      a partially visible object in a bounding box due to occlusion or image framing (e.g. the truck in the
153
      2nd example of Figure 1) does not necessarily indicate a poor-quality bounding box. The model
154
      must account for these contextual nuances.
155
156   Spatial Reasoning: The task demands sophisticated spatial reasoning capabilities. The model needs
157   to understand complex spatial relationships, including different viewpoints, occlusions, and potential
158
      interactions between objects and/or the background. This requirement significantly surpasses the
      spatial understanding typically needed for standard image classification tasks.
159
160   Multi-modal Integration: The task involves integrating both visual and semantic (textual) infor-
161   mation. The model must align visual features of the bounding box region with the associated text
      prompts to determine if the bounding box accurately captures the target object in the text prompt.


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      To address these challenges, we propose to evaluate each annotation independently within each im-
163   age, ensuring that the input image to CLIP contains only a single bounding box, represented in
164   magenta to reduce ambiguity, as this color is seldom present in the original images. This configura-
165   tion allows the model to concentrate on assessing the accuracy and quality of individual bounding
166   box annotations. Furthermore, we utilize an image preprocessor that crops a region surrounding the
167   annotation (see examples in Figure 1), enabling the model to focus specifically on the semantics
168   of the annotation. Text prompts are constructed to describe both the target object and the quality
169   of the bounding box - either “good” or “bad”, i.e. for an object detection dataset, we generate
170   corresponding textual prompts for each of the object classes:
171   • Good bounding box: “The magenta bounding box is a good bounding box of [object class].”
172
      • Bad bounding box: “The magenta bounding box is a bad bounding box of [object class].”
173
174   • Background box: “The magenta bounding box is a good bounding box of background.”
175
      Each original object class maps to two text prompts, one for high quality bounding box and one for
176   low quality bounding box. This results in a contrastive learning problem with 2n + 1 prompts for
177   n object classes, where the additional category corresponds to a background class where no target
178   object is in the box.
179
      Let T = {t1 , t2 , . . . , t2n+1 } represent the set of text prompts for a dataset. The training goal is
180
      to maximize the alignment between the visual encoding fv (I) of an image crop I, and the text
181
      encoding ft (ti ) of all correct text prompts in a batch, vice versa for text embedding. In CLIP’s
182   original contrastive learning framework, the ground truth for each batch is an identity matrix, where
183   each image has a one-to-one correspondence with a single text prompt. In contrast, our task allows
184   for multiple correct matches within a batch, similar to (Khosla et al., 2021), as the same (object
185   class, bounding box quality) combination will appear multiple times in a batch. This is formalized
186   as minimizing the two cross-entropy losses Limage and Ltext :

199
      To allow for multiple correct matches, we also modify the way the ground truth (GT) matrix is
200   encoded. For each image in a batch, a ground truth vector is created where all correct text prompts
201   in the batch are given a value of 1, and 0 otherwise. Then each GT vector is normalized by its sum
202   to ensure that the total probability mass sums to 1 and then they are stacked together to form the
203   GT matrix of a batch. This modified GT matrix is no longer diagonal as in CLIP, but it is still a
204   symmetric matrix. Using this modified ground truth, we compute the cross-entropy losses defined
205   above for both image-to-text and text-to-image matches with the final loss being the average of
206   these two. The image loss encourages the model to align image embeddings with all correct text
207   embeddings and the text loss encourages the model to align text embeddings with all correct image
208
      embeddings, allowing for aligning the model to our grading task.
209   By framing the problem in this manner, we leverage CLIP’s ability to process and align visual and
210   textual information. Simultaneously, we guide the model to learn the nuanced characteristics that
211   distinguish between high-quality and suboptimal bounding boxes across object classes in the dataset.

213   3.2   T RAINING DATA P REPARATION
214
215   We use the COCO and LVIS (Large Vocabulary Instance Segmentation) (Gupta et al., 2019) datasets
      for training and evaluation, both of which provide diverse object categories and bounding box an-


                                                         4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      notations. To create a robust dataset for anotation grading, we augment each ground truth bounding
217   box to generate good, bad, and background examples:
218
219   • Good bounding boxes: The GT annotations provided by the dataset serve as examples of good
220
      bounding boxes; however, they are not without imperfections. The quality of a “good” bounding
      box can vary and may include some level of noise, which deep learning models generally tolerate
221
      and overcome. As a side effect, the noise gives the model a sense of intra-class variability which
222
      can be beneficial for robust training (Vahdat, 2017).
223
224   • Bad bounding boxes: These are generated by randomly modifying GT bounding boxes where
225   the position and size deviates from the ground truth with IoU between 0.5 and 0.8, hence the object
226
      class label is correct but the bounding box is not snug. We empirically selected this IoU range to
      ensure that these randomly disturbed bounding boxes are sufficiently challenging for the model to
227
      learn.
228
229   • Background bounding boxes: These are randomly generated bounding boxes that do not have
230   major overlap (i.e. IoU ≤ 20%) with any labelled objects in the same image. This is the garbage
231   class - lack of clear target object in the box.
232   These augmentation steps are crucial for creating a balanced and informative dataset using only
233   standard object detection annotations. It provides the model with a range of examples from accurate
234   bounding boxes to subtly incorrect ones to irrelevant ones. This procedure is used to generate both
235   training and evaluation data using standard COCO and LVIS training and validation splits.
236   After generating these bounding box variants, a filtering step is applied where we remove objects
237   that have bounding boxes with both height and width smaller than 20 pixels. This selective filtering
238   still retains objects that may be thin or elongated (e.g., a knife or a pole), but removes tiny bounding
239   boxes. This decision is based on several reasons: the difficulty in generating meaningful “bad”
240   examples for extremely small objects, and the often low-quality of image patches for very small
241   objects in COCO and LVIS. This filtering process removes approximately 7% of the COCO dataset,
242   which we deemed an acceptable trade-off between data preservation and training signal quality.
243   Our image preprocessing pipeline diverges from the original approach used in the CLIP image pre-
244   processor. To encode the spatial relationships of bounding boxes, as illustrated in Figure 1, we draw
245   magenta boxes (3 pixels thick) directly on the image allowing the model to perceive the bounding
246   boxes as integral components of the image. Instead of the center crop used by CLIP, we dynami-
247   cally crop the image in a square around the bounding box, with the length of the side set to 1.2 to
248   1.5 times the bounding box size, ensuring enough context is included for spatial reasoning. If the
249   bounding box is near the edge of the image, the crop is adjusted to maximize the inclusion of original
250
      image content. Additionally, the center of the crop is randomized to prevent positional biases - the
      bounding box is not always in the center. Hence this image preprocessing pipeline maintains spatial
251
      relationships and context while adapting the bounding-box-added input to CLIP’s architecture.

255   3.3   M ODEL A RCHITECTURE AND T RAINING S ETUP
256
257
      We use the largest CLIP model (ViT-L/14@336px) provided by OpenAI as our base architecture.
258   In our experiments, we found that model size significantly impacts performance, with the largest
259   CLIP model yielding significantly better results. To enhance generalization, we add dropout layers
260   (dropout rate 0.25) in both the vision and text encoders. Flash Attention 2(Dao, 2024) is used which
261   speeds up the computation and reduces memory bandwidth requirements of attention operations.
262   Other model architecture and parameters remain the same as original CLIP so as to take advantage
263   of the pretrained model. Our primary focus is on developing an effective learning strategy for this
264   new task, as we believe the core CLIP architecture is sufficiently powerful when coupled with the
265   right training strategy.
266   The dataloader makes a best effort to sample one bad, one background and one good bounding box
267   from each image to ensure class balance. To achieve larger batch sizes, our training pipeline employs
268   mixed precision training, gradient checkpoint and gradient accumulation. The larger batch size
269   helps to provide a diverse set of negative examples in each batch which is important for contrastive
      learning. The effective batch size is roughly 2500, distributed across 6 Nvidia A100 GPUs.


                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      For optimization, Adam optimizer (Kingma, 2014) and cosine annealing scheduler (Loshchilov &
271   Hutter, 2017) are used. We set the initial learning rate to 5e-5, which we found to provide a good
272   balance between learning speed and stability. The β1 and β2 parameters of Adam are set to 0.9
273   and 0.98 respectively, following common practice in CLIP training. Since dropout is added for
274   regularization, no weight decay is used.

276   3.4   L EARNING S TRATEGY
277
278   To understand the relative importance of the different training techniques in our task, we conducted a
279   series of experiments including prompt engineering, changes in model size, and different fine-tuning
280   strategies i.e. full model fine-tuning versus fine-tuning only the vision encoder or text encoder.
281   Our results showed that fine-tuning only the vision encoder (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020) yielded per-
282   formance nearly identical to full model fine-tuning, while fine-tuning only the text encoder led to
283   significantly worse performance. To further investigate the role of the text domain in our model, we
284   conducted additional experiments with prompt engineering. We designed more elaborate prompt
285   mixes that included several detailed descriptions of what constitutes a good or bad bounding box.
286   For example, instead of simply stating “a good bounding box of [object]”, we used prompts like
287
      “a precise bounding box that tightly encloses the entire [object]” or “an inaccurate bounding box
      that is too large for [object]”. Surprisingly, these more descriptive prompts did not result in faster
288
      training or better accuracy compared to our simpler prompt structure. This suggests that the bulk of
289
      the learning happens in the visual domain as shown in Section 4.2.
290
291   These findings offer valuable insights into the nature of the bounding box quality assessment task.
292   They suggest that future research in this area might benefit from a greater focus on enhancing vi-
293
      sual processing capabilities. Despite that, we believe that the combination of visual and textual
      information in CLIP is also valuable which we discuss further in Section 4.3.
294
295   Overall, our modeling approach offers several key innovations and advantages. By modifying the
296   CLIP model and training strategy, we created a model tailored to the nuances of bounding box
297   quality assessment. The result is a flexible and scalable solution that can adapt to the diverse needs
298   of real-world applications, providing a powerful tool to assess and improve the quality of object
299
      detection labels.

301   4     E XPERIMENTS AND M AIN R ESULTS
302
303   Evaluation Datasets We conducted a comprehensive set of experiments to evaluate the performance
304   and characteristics of ClipGrader. This section details our experimental setup, metrics, and find-
305   ings. Our experiments use two widely used object detection datasets: COCO and LVIS. The COCO
306   dataset contains 80 object categories, while LVIS features a much larger set of 1203 categories. For
307
      both datasets, we utilized their provided training and validation splits for our experiments. Dur-
      ing training, each epoch took approximately 2-3 hours to complete. We ran our experiments for a
308
      maximum of 15 epochs, though in many cases, convergence was achieved earlier.
309
310   Evaluation Metrics While overall classification accuracy serves as a general indicator of model
311   performance, our evaluation also looked at metrics more directly related to our goal of assessing and
312   filtering labels. Specifically, we calculated: (1) Mean recall of good labels: This metric (higher is
313
      better) measures the ability to correctly retain high-quality labels and lower the workload of human
      review. (2) Mean false acceptance (false positives) of bad labels: This metric (lower is better) helps
314
      us gauge low-quality label contamination, particularly for cases where the object class is correct but
315
      the bounding box is not snug. These metrics align with common types of labeling errors and with
316   our objective of efficiently screening low-quality labels for human review. They are first computed
317   per-class, then the mean across all classes is reported, minimizing the impact of class imbalance.

          To further explore the generalization capabilities of ClipGrader, we applied the model trained on
398       100% of COCO directly to grade bounding boxes of unseen classes in the LVIS dataset. This chal-
399       lenging task required the model to assess bounding boxes for over 1100 unseen classes during train-
400       ing (we removed the common classes between COCO and LVIS), relying solely on the pretraining
401       knowledge of CLIP and the concept of bounding boxes learned during our fine-tuning process.
402
          In this evaluation, the ClipGrader model, trained on the COCO dataset, demonstrates a constrained
403
          yet significant ability to assess bounding box quality across over 1100 entirely unseen object classes
404       - it achieved an accuracy of approximately 11%. As can be expected, many unseen class samples are
405       classified as background which makes sense since they are indeed “background” during its training
406       on COCO. While this accuracy is considerably lower than the model’s performance on seen classes,
407       it should be noted that it significantly outperforms random guessing in over 1100 categories. More-
408       over, ClipGrader’s zero shot capability is weaker than CLIP’s capability for classification, most
409       likely due to the limitation of fine tuning dataset and the newly added ’background’ category.
410       The relatively low accuracy suggests that this zero-shot capability is not yet sufficient for practical
411       applications. Some amount of labeled bounding box data for new classes of interest is still necessary
412       for the model to achieve high-quality assessment and seperate them from the ’background’ class.
413       However, this result provides evidence that our fine-tuning process teaches the model to learn a
414       generalizable concept of bounding box quality, which lays the foundation for data efficient fine
415       tuning or few-shot learning for new classes.

      4.4          C ASE S TUDY IN S EMI - SUPERVISED O BJECT D ETECTION : CLIP-T EACHER
465
466
      To showcase a simple practical utility of ClipGrader, we integrate it into a semi-supervised object
467
      detection (SSOD) framework, referred to as “CLIP-Teacher”. Pseudo-labeling is essential in SSOD,
468   where bounding boxes generated by a teacher model serve as pseudo-ground truth (pseudo-GT)
469   for training the student model. However, without effective quality control, poor pseudo-labels can
470   degrade overall model performance. By incorporating ClipGrader to screen and retain only high-
471   quality pseudo-labels, CLIP-Teacher improves the effectiveness of the SSOD process.
472
      We implemented CLIP-Teacher by integrating ClipGrader into the Consistent-Teacher SSOD frame-
473
      work. We compare our method against the original strategy used in Consistent-Teacher on partially
474   labeled CoCo datasets. Two setups are tested (5% and 10% percent of the COCO training dataset)
475   in our comparison. The only difference between CLIP-Teacher and Consistent-Teacher is the use of
476   ClipGrader to filter the pseudo-labels generated by the teacher model. Both models were trained on
477   the same partially labeled dataset (5% or 10% of COCO dataset) and use the same warm up check-
478   point(12k iterations) to ensure a fair comparison. Importantly, ClipGrader was also trained using
479   the same 5% or 10% of COCO labeled data to avoid data leakage. While ClipGrader is not trained
480   with any image data augmentation, examples in Fig. 6 (Appendix A) shows it is quite robust to the
481   strong data augmentation employed by Consistent Teacher. Please refer to Appendix A for more
482
      implementation details.
483   Figure 5 shows the comparison of mAP (mean Average Precision) performance between the two
484   approaches across training iterations. The results demonstrate that CLIP-Teacher consistently out-
485   performs Consistent-Teacher after warm up, particularly by filtering out low-quality pseudo-labels
      that the baseline model does not eliminate. This results in faster convergence and improved model

      accuracy throughout the training process. The performance metrics in Table 2 further confirm the
487   advantage of CLIP-Teacher, with an improvement in AP50, AP75, and AP50:95 across the board.
488   These results highlight ClipGrader’s capability to serve as a robust quality control mechanism, en-
489   suring that only higher quality pseudo-labels are used in training, ultimately leading to more accurate
490   object detection models in semi-supervised settings.

493   5   D ISCUSSION AND F UTURE W ORK
494
495   An important direction for future work is the application of ClipGrader to improve existing datasets
496   through error analysis and correction. Our examination of mispredictions on the COCO test set
497
      reveals that in many cases where ClipGrader disagrees with ground truth labels, the discrepancy
      is due to label ambiguity or errors in the original annotations. Figures 7 and 8 (in Appendix B)
498
      illustrate several such examples where ClipGrader’s judgments appear more accurate or nuanced
499
      than the original COCO annotations. This finding suggests that ClipGrader has learned a robust
500   concept of bounding box quality that can generalize beyond the errors present in COCO. By flagging
501   ambiguous or potentially incorrect annotations for human review, we could create a feedback loop
502   to continually improve dataset quality. This process could be particularly valuable for large-scale
503   datasets like COCO, where comprehensive manual review is impractical.
504
      Another future work could focus on developing efficient workflows that integrate ClipGrader into
505
      the dataset curation process, allowing for targeted human intervention to resolve ambiguities and
506   correct errors identified by the model. This could involve creating interfaces that present flagged
507   annotations to human annotators along with ClipGrader’s predictions, facilitating quick decision-
508   making and corrections. In the early stages of data collection and annotation, ClipGrader can be
509   bootstrapped with a small amount of seed data to guide the rest of the curation process. Addition-
510   ally, studying the types of errors and ambiguities frequently identified by ClipGrader could provide
511   insights into common pitfalls in annotation processes, informing the development of improved an-
512   notation guidelines and quality control measures for future data collection efforts.
513   Several avenues for future research could further enhance our approach. Extending the method
514   to grade multiple bounding boxes in a single image simultaneously would bring higher efficiency.
515   It’s worth noting that while ClipGrader can assess existing annotations, it is not trained to find
516   missing annotations in an image, presenting an area for potential future work. While we focused on
517   bounding box quality for object detection, similar approaches could be developed for other computer
518   vision tasks, such as segmentation. Furthermore, our methodology could be applied to larger, more
519   advanced vision-language models, e.g. Florence-2(Xiao et al., 2024), which could lead to even
520
      stronger results given its increased capacity and better understanding of visual/textual relationships.
521
522   6   C ONCLUSION
523
524
      In this paper, we introduced a novel CLIP-based approach for assessing bounding box quality in
525   object detection datasets. Our method demonstrates high effectiveness and efficiency across diverse
526   object classes and dataset complexities. It maintains strong performance even with limited data,
527   achieving 87% accuracy using only 10% of COCO. This data efficiency promises to automate and
528   improve annotation quality control from initial collection to ongoing dataset refinement.
529
      ClipGrader’s generalization capabilities also extend to complex datasets like LVIS, highlighting its
530
      adaptability. Our experiments reveal the primacy of visual processing for this task, informing future
531   model designs. This work represents an advancement in AI assisted data curation and verification for
532   object detection, contributing to enhanced reliability and performance of object detection systems
533   across various requirements.

678   A    M ORE DETAILS ON C LIP -T EACHER
679
680   CLIP-Teacher is a simple extension of Consistent-Teacher, with an additional pseudo-label screen-
681   ing step. After the teacher model generates pseudo-labels (object class and bounding box) for the
682   unlabeled images in a batch, CLIP-Teacher uses ClipGrader to filter out low-quality pseudo-labels.
683
      In CLIP-Teacher, each pseudo-label, along with the corresponding image, is first passed to the image
684   preprocessor described in Sec. 3.1. The image crop, containing the bounding box and surrounding
685   region, is then evaluated by ClipGrader for quality. A bounding box is considered high-quality if the
686   predicted class label matches the pseudo-label and the probability score for the corresponding text
687   prompt of “good” bounding box exceeds a predefined threshold.
688
      In this pipeline, ClipGrader acts as an additional quality control mechanism, ensuring that only high-
689
      quality pseudo-labels are selected for student model training, while low-quality ones are discarded.
690   Examples of ClipGrader’s assessments are shown in Figure 6. Notably, even though ClipGrader is
691   not trained with image augmentations, it remains robust to the strong augmentations, e.g. Random-
692   Resize, RandErase, (Xu et al., 2021) used in Consistent-Teacher pipeline.
693
      To ensure consistency with prior art, we used the default settings of Consistent-Teacher (Wang et al.,
694
      2023), using RetinaNet (Lin et al., 2020) as the object detector based on MMdetection framework
695
      (Chen et al., 2019). For this experiment, we tried two setups, using 5% or 10% CoCo training
696   data respectively. Both Consistent-Teacher and Clip-Teacher models were trained on the same 5%
697   or 10% of COCO dataset and use the same warm up checkpoint (12k iterations) to ensure a fair
698   comparison. Importantly, ClipGrader used by CLIP-Teacher is also trained using the corresponding
699   5% or 10% of COCO data to avoid data leakage. Both CLIP-Teacher and Consistent-Teacher are
700   optimized using SGD, utilizing a constant learning rate of 0.01, a momentum of 0.9, and a weight
701   decay of 0.0001. Both CLIP-Teacher and Consistent-Teacher are trained for 52,000 iterations across
      4 Nvidia A100 GPUs. During training, Consistent-Teacher generates an average of approximately 4

      pseudo-labels per image, while CLIP-Teacher generates an average of 3.25 pseudo-labels per image,
703   filtering out approximately 19% of the pseudo-labels in our current implementation.
