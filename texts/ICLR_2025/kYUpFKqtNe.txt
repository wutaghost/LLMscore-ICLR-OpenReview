011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Multi-Source Domain Adaptation (MSDA) aims to adaptively apply knowledge
014            from multiple source pre-trained models to an unlabeled target domain. Cur-
015
               rent MSDA methods typically require extensive parameter tuning for each source
               model, which becomes computationally expensive, especially when dealing with
016
               numerous source domains or larger source models. With the recent advance-
017
               ments of Vision-Language Models (VLMs) as natural source models, the chal-
018            lenges of cross-domain tasks based on multi-source domains have evolved: 1)
019            VLMs rapidly adapt to downstream tasks through prompt tuning, yet learnable
020            prompt tokens are prone to overfitting due to limited training samples; 2) Rapidly
021            leveraging knowledge from multiple source domains and encouraging the learn-
022            ing of invariant representations across these domains is a central issue; 3) The
023            presence of visual and textual domain gaps, as well as cross-modal misalignment,
024            can significantly impact model performance. In this paper, we propose a fine-
025            tuning framework that integrates prompts with multimodal Low-Rank Adaptation
026
               (LoRA). This framework employs learnable prompt features as shared character-
               istics across different domains and utilizes multimodal LoRA matrices to repre-
027
               sent domain-specific features for individual fine-tuning of VLMs across multiple
028
               source domains. Furthermore, it encourages interaction between fine-tuning pa-
029            rameters from different domains and modalities to enhance consistency. We com-
030            bine all source domain-specific LoRA modules into an integrated module using
031            a set of coefficients and adapt this integrated module to learn on the target do-
032            main. Extensive experiments demonstrate that our approach achieves significant
033            improvements on standard image classification benchmark datasets, highlighting
034            its effectiveness in multi-source domain adaptation tasks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      With the rapid advancement of deep learning technologies, Multi-source Unsupervised Domain
      Adaptation (MUDA) has emerged as a significant research direction in the field of computer vi-
040
      sion. The goal of MUDA is to transfer knowledge from multiple labeled source domains to an
041
      unlabeled target domain, thereby enhancing the model’s adaptability capabilities on the target do-
042   main (Zhu et al., 2019a). Unlike single-source unsupervised domain adaptation, where all training
043   samples come from a single domain, MUDA aligns more closely with real-world application sce-
044   narios where labeled images originate from multiple distinct domains. In the era of large models,
045   existing MUDA methods that rely on comprehensive parameter tuning for each source model not
046   only increase computational costs but also limit their feasibility in large-scale (Li et al., 2024).
047
      In recent years, Vision-Language Models (VLMs) have achieved significant progress in multimodal
048   learning tasks, particularly in tasks that involve understanding and generating information that com-
049   bines visual and linguistic data. VLMs have propelled the development of cross-modal learning
050   by fusing image and text information. However, as VLMs are increasingly applied, the challenges
051   associated with MUDA continue to evolve. Firstly, VLMs can rapidly adapt to various downstream
052   tasks through prompt tuning, with many researchers proposing fine-tuning methods based on this ap-
053   proach. (Zhou et al., 2022a;b; Ge et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024)Yet, the reliance on
      learnable prompt tokens can lead to overfitting when training samples are limited, severely impact-


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      ing the model’s adaptability and generalization capabilities (Li et al., 2023). Efficiently harnessing
055   knowledge from multiple source domains to foster learning of cross-domain invariant representa-
056   tions remains a central research issue. Effective cross-domain representations can reduce biases
057   between different source domains and improve model performance on the target domain. Moreover,
058   the differences between visual and textual modalities and cross-modal misalignments significantly
059   affect the overall model performance (khattak et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2024a). Specifically, visual
060   and linguistic information exhibit substantial differences in expression forms, semantic associations,
061   and data distributions, making models prone to information loss and comprehension barriers during
062   cross-modal learning. Such modality misalignments not only affect the model’s feature extraction
063
      capabilities but also the accuracy of final classification or regression tasks.
064   To address these challenges and resolve the aforementioned issues, this paper introduces a novel
065   fine-tuning framework that integrates prompt tuning with multimodal Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA)
066   technology. Specifically, our approach constructs shared features across different domains through
067   learnable prompt token features, which not only enhances the efficiency of knowledge transfer be-
068   tween source domains but also strengthens the model’s adaptability to the target domain. Con-
069
      currently, by employing multimodal LoRA matrices, we can perform personalized fine-tuning of
      domain-specific features, enabling VLMs to seamlessly transition between feature spaces of differ-
070
      ent source domains. Furthermore, we introduce a new mechanism to encourage interaction between
071
      fine-tuning parameters from different domains and modalities. This interaction mechanism allows
072   the model to learn more consistent and robust representations, thereby exhibiting stronger robust-
073   ness and adaptability when handling complex multi-domain tasks. Ultimately, by combining an
074   optimized set of coefficients, we integrate all available LoRA modules into an ensemble module for
075   adaptive learning on the target domain. In summary, our main contributions are:
076
      1)We propose a novel fine-tuning framework that integrates prompt tuning with multimodal Low-
077
      Rank Adaptation (LoRA) for adapting VLMs to MUDA task.
078   2)We introduce learnable, class-specific prompts to represent domain-invariant features and employ
079   separate multimodal LoRA adapters for each domain to effectively capture domain-specific charac-
080   teristics.
081   3)Furthermore, we incorporate interactions between cross-domain and cross-modal fine-tuning pa-
082   rameters to enhance consistency and accuracy during the learning process.

087   2.1   M ULTI -S OURCE U NSUPERVISED D OMAIN A DAPTATION (MUDA)
088
      The advent of MUDA has been prompted by the limitations of single-source unsupervised domain
089
      adaptation, which struggles to capture the diversity and complexity of real-world application sce-
090
      narios. MUDA is inherently more challenging yet aligns more closely with practical needs, offering
091   greater value. MUDA originated from the pioneering work of Yang et al. Yang et al. (2007), and
092   has since evolved with a foundation of theoretical underpinnings (Ben-David et al., 2010; Mansour
093   et al., 2008) and a multitude of practical applications (Duan et al., 2012; Xu et al., 2018a). Over
094   the years, a variety of methods have been explored for MUDA tasks. Some of these methods aim to
095   minimize the discrepancies between multiple source domains and the target domain, striving to align
096   their distributions and mitigate the impact of domain shift, thereby reducing the target domain gen-
097   eralization error. For instance, MFSAN (Zhu et al., 2019a) employs Maximum Mean Discrepancy
098   (MMD) to align distributions, while M3SDA (Peng et al., 2019a) calculates the moment distances
099
      between domains and designs a moment matching network to align feature distributions. Li et al.
      Li et al. (2018) utilize Wasserstein distance to diminish domain disparities. Mansour et al. Mansour
100
      et al. (2008) propose that the ideal target hypothesis can be represented as a weighted combination
101
      of the distributions of source hypotheses. Consequently, some methods aggregate predictions from
102   different source domains to derive the final prediction for the target domain. MFSAN (Zhu et al.,
103   2019a), after training the model with a series of regularization terms, averages the predictions from
104   each source domain. M3SDA (Peng et al., 2019a) weights its predictions based on the accuracy de-
105   rived from each source domain, while DCTN (Xu et al., 2018a) trains a domain discriminator, using
106   its output as the basis for weighting across multiple source domains. Wu et al. Wu et al. (2020) cal-
107   culate the conditional Wasserstein distance from different source domains to the target domain and
      use these results for weighting. Beyond the aforementioned approaches, other methods have been


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      devised to address MUDA challenges. MOST (Nguyen et al., 2021a) leverages optimal transport
109   theory and employs imitation learning between student and teacher classifiers to enhance MUDA
110   performance. STEM (Nguyen et al., 2021b) incorporates adversarial learning concepts, mapping
111   multiple source teacher networks and a target domain student network to the same feature space,
112   enabling the student classifier to effectively mimic the teachers for classification predictions. PFSA
113   (Fu et al., 2021a) selectively aligns features from multiple source domains that are compatible with
114   the target domain by minimizing the loss of intra-class sample clustering and maximizing inter-class
115   distance differences. MPA (Chen et al., 2024) employs CLIP as the backbone and designs prompts
116   that encompass both domain-invariant and domain-specific features, training such prompts for each
117
      source and target domain pair. It uses a contrastive loss to train and bridge the gap between source
      and target domains.

      2.2   V ISION L ANGUAGE M ODELS (VLM S )
121   Vision-Language Models (VLMs) integrate natural images with linguistic supervision, diverging
122   from traditional vision models that rely on discrete labels for category prediction. Instead, VLMs
123   harness supervision from natural language, thereby leveraging the semantics encapsulated in text
124   to a greater extent. A quintessential example is the CLIP model (Radford et al., 2021b), which
125   employs a contrastive learning approach in the feature representation space. It aggregates positive
126   pairs—related images and texts—while separating negative pairs—unrelated instances—to establish
127   correspondences between text and images. This approach fosters a deeper understanding of the
128   complex interplay between text and images. The multimodal representations of vision and language
129
      also render their applications more flexible and diverse.Recently, VLMs like CLIP (Radford et al.,
      2021b), ALIGN (Jia et al., 2021), FILIP (Yao et al., 2022), LiT (Zhai et al., 2022), and Florence
130
      (Yuan et al., 2021) have been utilized as foundational models for a broad range of downstream tasks.
131
      These models are typically trained on web-scale datasets; for instance, CLIP and ALIGN are trained
132   on 400 million and 1 billion image-text pairs, respectively, to develop their multimodal networks.
133   Although these pre-trained VLMs learn generalized representations, effectively adapting them to
134   downstream tasks remains a challenging issue.There has been significant work on devising methods
135   to adapt VLMs to downstream tasks such as few-shot image recognition (Kim et al., 2022; Zhang
136   et al., 2021), image segmentation (Ding et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022; Lüddecke & Ecker, 2022; Rao
137   et al., 2022), and object detection (Feng et al., 2022; Maaz et al., 2022; Bangalath et al., 2022; Zhou
138   et al., 2022c). Given VLMs’ robust capability to integrate semantic information, they are particularly
139   well-suited for unsupervised domain adaptation. In this work, we also employ the CLIP model as
140
      our foundational model and design methods to better adapt it to MUDA tasks, thereby enhancing
      performance.

      2.3   F INE -T UNING M ETHODS BASED ON P RE -T RAINED M ODELS
144
      Pre-trained models have garnered extensive knowledge and generalized representations, yet effi-
145   ciently adapting them to downstream tasks remains a formidable challenge. The most straightfor-
146   ward adaptation approach is full fine-tuning in the downstream task, but this becomes increasingly
147   expensive and even infeasible as model parameter counts grow. Consequently, researchers have
148   delved into more efficient fine-tuning techniques. Prompt tuning stands out as an efficient param-
149   eter tuning method that guides the model to achieve desired outputs by designing learnable input
150   queries with minimal parameters while keeping the original model frozen. CoOp (Zhou et al.,
151   2022a) pioneered the introduction of soft prompts in VLMs, demonstrating that appropriate textual
152   prompts could enhance image recognition performance. Building on CoOp, CoCoOp (Zhou et al.,
153
      2022b) further addressed overfitting issues by learning a lightweight neural network to dynamically
      generate prompts for each input image. DAPrompt (Ge et al., 2022) embedded domain-specific
154
      knowledge into prompts to facilitate domain alignment. VPT (Jia et al., 2022) successfully ap-
155
      plied prompts to the image encoder, yielding promising results. DoPrompt (Zheng et al., 2022)
156   leveraged prompt learning to embed source domain knowledge into domain prompts for predicting
157   target domain outcomes. MaPLe (khattak et al., 2023) introduced the novel concept of multimodal
158   prompt learning, simultaneously learning prompts on both visual and textual branches to improve
159   representation consistency. PDA (Bai et al., 2024) also employed multimodal prompts and designed
160   a dual-branch prompt tuning paradigm, achieving impressive results. Adapter tuning is another pop-
161   ular efficient parameter tuning method that inserts additional trainable parameters into pre-trained
      models to adapt to downstream tasks. A variety of adapters such as Clip-Adapter (Gao et al., 2021),


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Tip-Adapter (Zhang et al., 2021), VL-Adapter (Yi-Lin Sung, 2022), SVL-Adapter (Pantazis et al.,
163   2022), crossmodal-adapter (Jiang et al., 2022), MV-Adapter (Valipour et al., 2023), and MMA
164   (Yang et al., 2024a) have been integrated into backbone networks to accomplish a wide array of
165   downstream tasks. Some adapters, like Clip-Adapter (Gao et al., 2021) and Tip-Adapter (Zhang
166   et al., 2021), are added to a single modality branch, while some adapters, such as crossmodal-adapter
167   (Jiang et al., 2022) and MMA (Yang et al., 2024a), facilitate cross-modal interactions.

      3.2.1    C ATEGORY-S PECIFIC P ROMPTING
249
250   Designing learnable prompts to replace handcrafted ones allows for more nuanced and flexible fine-
251   tuning of the model, leading to promising results. MPA[Multi-prompt alignment for multi-source
252   unsupervised domain adaptation](Chen et al., 2024) method trains distinct prompts for different do-
253   mains to accomplish MUDA tasks and has achieved certain effects. However, Li et al. Li et al. (2023)
254   argue that learnable prompts trained on limited samples are prone to overfitting, which may under-
255   mine the adaptability and generalization capabilities of the pre-trained model. Therefore, training
256
      domain-specific prompts for each source domain may not be an optimal choice. Moreover, consid-
      ering that in MUDA tasks, all source domains and the target domain share the same label space, i.e.,
257
      identical classes, it is beneficial to use data from all source domains to jointly train a set of learnable
258
      class-specific prompts. This approach not only leverages precise and flexible prompts for model
259   fine-tuning but also reduces the risk of overfitting due to limited training samples. Specifically, we
260                                                     b
      introduce bb learnable tokens P i ∈ R512 i=1 into the language branch of CLIP and merge them
261
      with class labels, with the prompt format as follows:

268   bedding corresponding to the last token is projected into the common V-L latent embedding space.

395   Home (Venkateswara et al., 2017): A medium-scale dataset consisting of 15,588 images spanning

397   and Product images (R). We employed all domain combinations to formulate four MUDA tasks:
398
      C,P,R→A;A,P,R→C; A,C,R→P; A,C,P→R. (3) DomainNet (Peng et al., 2019a): A large-scale

      domains: Clipart, Infograph, Painting, Quickdraw, Real, and Sketch. Using all domain combina-

      tions, we established six MUDA tasks→Clp;→Inf;→Pnt;→Qdr;→Rel;→Skt.
401   Implementation Details.: We employed CLIP as our base model, utilizing the Vision Transformer
402   (ViT-B/16) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021a)for the image encoder. We loaded the pre-trained weights,
403   which were kept frozen throughout the experimental process. The prompts were trained for 25
404   epochs using a mini-batch Stochastic Gradient Descent (SGD) optimizer with a batch size of 32.
405   The initial learning rate was set to 0.003, and we applied a cosine annealing schedule for learn-
406   ing rate decay (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2016). Low-rank matrices were applied exclusively to the
407   query, key, and value matrices with a rank r=2. We regularized the input to the LoRA modules
408   through a dropout layer with a probability p=0.25 (Feng et al., 2022). Regarding hyperparameters,
409
      the length of the class-specific prompt tokens was set to 16. The pseudo-labeling threshold was
      set to 0.5 to ensure the generation of reliable labels. Compared Methods and Evaluation Met-
410
      ric.:To validate the effectiveness of our model, we have selected the following MUDA models as

      our multi-source baselines:DAN(Long et al., 2015), D-CORAL(Sun & Saenko, 2016),DCTN(Xu

489   Multimodal LoRA Matrix Selection. Initially, we explored the incorporation of LoRA matrices
490
      into individual modalities of the CLIP model by adding them to the visual encoder and textual en-
      coder separately to assess performance. Subsequently, we introduced LoRA matrices to both the
491
      visual and textual encoders, with the matrices operating independently without interaction. We then
492
      tested the performance under this configuration. Finally, we integrated multimodal LoRA matrices
493   and facilitated interaction between them through a shared projection layer, further testing the perfor-
494   mance. By comparing the model performance across these various scenarios, it was evident that the
495   use of multimodal LoRA matrices with a shared projection layer for interaction yielded the best re-
496   sults. This finding validates the effectiveness and necessity of our design approach. Additionally, we
497   investigated the impact of adding multimodal LoRA matrices at different transformer layers within
498   the encoders and found that incorporating them at higher layers of the encoders resulted in better
499   performance.
500   Hyperparameter Selection.We conducted a thorough examination of the hyperparameters for the
501   pseudo-labeling threshold τlabel and the length of the prompt tokens b. Specifically, we explored
502   τlabel in the set 0.4,0.5,0.6,0.7,0.8 and b in the set 8,12,16,20. As labellabel increases, the quality
503   of the pseudo-labels improves, but fewer images are input into the model, which can harm overall
504   performance. Conversely, the general trend for the prompt token length is that longer prompts yield
505   better performance. Therefore, we selected τlabel = 0.5 and b=16 to strike a balance between
506   performance and efficiency. When applying the LoRA matrices, a higher rank corresponds to a
507
      larger number of fine-tuning parameters, which may lead to overfitting when the training data is
      limited. Hence, we opted for LoRA matrices with a rank r=2.

511
512   This paper presents a novel framework leveraging Vision-Language Models (VLMs) for Multi-
513   Source Unsupervised Domain Adaptation (MUDA). We introduce a fine-tuning approach that in-
514
      tegrates category-specific prompting with multimodal Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) to enhance
      model adaptability across domains. Extensive experiments on benchmark datasets demonstrate our
515
      method’s effectiveness in improving classification accuracy, showcasing significant advances over
516
      existing MUDA techniques. Our work provides a promising direction for adapting VLMs to diverse
517   real-world applications.
