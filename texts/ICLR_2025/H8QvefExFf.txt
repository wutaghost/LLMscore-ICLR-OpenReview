031                                             A BSTRACT
032
033           Text-to-audio (T2A) generation has achieved remarkable progress in generating a
034           variety of audio outputs from language prompts. However, current state-of-the-
035           art T2A models still struggle to satisfy human preferences for prompt-following
036           and acoustic quality when generating complex multi-event audio. To improve
037           the performance of the model in these high-level applications, we propose to en-
038           hance the basic capabilities of the model with AI feedback learning. First, we
039           introduce fine-grained AI audio scoring pipelines to: 1) verify whether each event
040           in the text prompt is present in the audio (Event Occurrence Score), 2) detect
041
              deviations in event sequences from the language description (Event Sequence
              Score), and 3) assess the overall acoustic and harmonic quality of the generated
042
              audio (Acoustic Harmonic Quality). We evaluate these three automatic scor-
043
              ing pipelines and find that they correlate significantly better with human pref-
044           erences than other evaluation metrics. This highlights their value as both feed-
045           back signals and evaluation metrics. Utilizing our robust scoring pipelines, we
046           construct a large audio preference dataset, T2A-FeedBack, which contains 41k
047           prompts and 249k audios, each accompanied by detailed scores. Moreover, we
048           introduce T2A-EpicBench, a benchmark that focuses on long captions, multi-
049           events, and story-telling scenarios, aiming to evaluate the advanced capabilities
050           of T2A models. Finally, we demonstrate how T2A-FeedBack can enhance cur-
051           rent state-of-the-art audio model. With simple preference tuning, the audio gen-
052
              eration model exhibits significant improvements in both simple (AudioCaps test
              set) and complex (T2A-EpicBench) scenarios. The project page is available at
053
              https://T2Afeedback.github.io

      1   I NTRODUCTION
055
056
      Recent Text-to-Audio (T2A) generation models (Huang et al., 2023b;a; Liu et al., 2023a; 2024;
057
      Ghosal et al., 2023; Majumder et al., 2024; Vyas et al., 2023) have made drastic performance im-
058
      provements. By trained on massive audio-text data (Gemmeke et al., 2017; Fonseca et al., 2021;
059   Chen et al., 2020; Kim et al., 2019), these generative models learn to generate diverse sounds with a
060   given language prompt. For audio generation, generating harmonious multi-event audio or describ-
061   ing a story with audio has important applications in music (Agostinelli et al., 2023), advertising,
062   video-audio generation (Luo et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024), etc. However, as shown in Figure. 1,
063   existing audio generation models are struggling to generate harmonious and high-quality audio from
064   narrative and complex descriptions, which limits the potential for high-level applications.
065   The failure of the generated results is often demonstrated in three aspects: 1) cannot fully include all
066   the events described, 2) cannot accurately follow the order of all the events described, and 3) cannot
067   organize all the events harmoniously. Therefore, the model performance in multi-event scenarios is
068   determined by its capabilities in these three fundamental aspects.
069
      To improve the model’s performance across more advanced applications, we focus on strengthening
070
      the audio generation model’s fundamental abilities. Inspired by feedback learning in large language
071   models (Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022; Touvron et al., 2023), we propose creating an au-
072   dio preference dataset centered on three abilities necessary for generating harmonic and complex
073   audio: 1) Event Occurrence Prompt-Following, 2) Event Sequence Prompt-Following, and 3)
074   Acoustic&Harmonic Quality. Based on the preference information, we can refine the model’s core
075   abilities, resulting in better results in both simple and challenging scenarios.
076
      However, due to the scarcity of audio data and the challenges of annotating the scale of user prefer-
077   ences, it is difficult to collect massive audio preference datasets that only rely on human annotators.
078   To fill this void, we explore using AI feedback (Cui et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2024;
079   Burns et al., 2023) in text-to-audio generation, utilizing AI models to rank audios instead of re-
080   lying on human annotators. Compared to manual annotation, automating the data collection and
081   annotation process reduces the cost of obtaining audio preference data and enhances scalability.
082
      Specifically, we develop three AI scoring pipelines to evaluate the generated audio in a fine-grained
083   and holistic manner, corresponding to three core capabilities:
084
085          • Event Occurrence Score: To specifically check whether each event occurs in, we calculate
086            the audio-text semantic matching score for each described event separately. A lower score
087            suggests that the corresponding event might be absent from the audio.
088
089
             • Event Sequence Score: To verify the correctness of event order, we analyze the start and end
               times of each event and compare them with the event order outlined in the text prompt. A
090
               higher score implies a greater similarity between the event sequences in caption and audio.
091
092          • Acoustics&Harmonic Quality: Drawing inspiration from aesthetic scoring methods used
093            in image quality scoring, we manually annotate acoustic and harmonic quality for audio
094            samples. These data are then used to train an automatic acoustic&harmonic predictor.
095
096   We confirm that our three scoring functions show a stronger correlation with human evaluations
097   compared to existing automatic audio evaluation methods (Wu et al., 2023b; Xie et al., 2024). Con-
098
      sequently, in addition to their application in ranking preference data, these scoring functions can be
      used as evaluation metrics that more effectively capture human preferences across different aspects.
099
100   Leveraging these advanced AI scoring pipelines, we establish a comprehensive data collection and
101   annotation framework. As a result, we construct T2A-Feedback, a large audio preference dataset
102   comprising 41,627 captions and 249,762 generated audios, each annotated with detailed scores.
103   Furthermore, to evaluate the higher-level capabilities of text-to-audio models in multi-event scenar-
104   ios, we introduce a more challenging benchmark, T2A-EpicBench, which features longer, more
105   imaginative, and story-telling captions for audio generation. We enhance the advanced text-to-audio
106   diffusion model, Make-an-Audio 2 (Huang et al., 2023a), with T2A-Feedback. Our results show
107   that using T2A-Feedback not only effectively improves the basic capabilities of the model in simple
      AudioCaps benchmark, but also emergently improves the performance in complex T2A-EpicBench.

      2.1   T EXT- TO -AUDIO G ENERATION
111
112
      Text-to-audio generation is an emerging field that aims to convert textual descriptions into corre-
113   sponding audio outputs. Existing text-to-audio generation methods can be divided into two cat-
114   egories: Diffusion-based and Language model-based. Diffusion-based techniques have gained
115   prominence for generating high-quality, realistic audio by modeling the process of denoising.
116   These methods, like Make-an-Audio (Huang et al., 2023b;a), AudioLDM (Liu et al., 2023a; 2024),
117   Tango (Ghosal et al., 2023; Majumder et al., 2024), start with random noise and iteratively refine
118   it to produce coherent audio over a series of denoising steps. On the other hand, Language model-
119   based methods (Borsos et al., 2023; Agostinelli et al., 2023; Cideron et al., 2024) tokenize audios as
120   acoustic discrete tokens, and predict the tokens within an auto-regressive model conditioned on text
121
      inputs.
122   The above models acquire the ability to generate diverse audio by training on large-scale audio-text
123   datasets. However, current datasets like AudioSet (Gemmeke et al., 2017), AudioCaps (Kim et al.,
124   2019), and FSD50k (Fonseca et al., 2021) only provide tag-level annotations or short captions. As a
125   result, when processing long, detailed language prompts, existing models often produce low-quality,
126   noisy outputs and struggle to accurately follow the text. Due to the difficulty of annotating detailed
127
      audio captions, scaling rich and accurate audio descriptions remains a challenge. In this work, we
      focus on enhancing the model’s basic abilities in event occurrence, sequence, and harmony, thereby
128
      improving its performance in both simple scenarios and advanced applications.
129
130
131   2.2   P ERFERENCE T UNING WITH H UMAN &AI F EEDBACK
132
133   Tuning generative models according to human preferences has emerged as a standard practice for
134
      improving the quality of outputs. By tuning with feedback information on different aspects, the
      model can be improved and aligned with human preferences in corresponding aspects. Traditionally,
135
      this preference data used for tuning relied heavily on human evaluators who rank multiple generated
136
      results, assessing their quality based on various criteria such as relevance, coherence, and aesthetic
137   value (Bai et al., 2022; Touvron et al., 2023; Ouyang et al., 2022; Kirstain et al., 2023; Liang et al.,
138   2024; Wu et al., 2023a; Cideron et al., 2024).
139
140
      While effective, manual human annotation is costly and time-consuming, which greatly hampers the
      scalability of preference tuning across more diverse generative tasks. To address the difficulty, more
141
      recent developments have focused on leveraging pre-trained AI models to automate the process of
142
      scoring generated content (Cui et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2024; Burns et al., 2023).
143   Such an AI feedback approach has achieved impressive improvements in large language models.
144
145   Recently, some studies have attempted preference fine-tuning in text-to-audio generation models.
146
      One recent paper related to our work, Tango2 (Majumder et al., 2024), utilizes contrastive language-
      audio pre-training (CLAP) (Wu et al., 2023b) to rank audio generated by the Tango model. However,
147
      CLAP can only evaluate the global alignment between audio and text but falls short in assessing the
148
      fine-grained details, like detailed event occurrence, sequence, and harmony. In this paper, we con-
149   struct more robust AI audio scoring pipelines with fine-grained recognition ability. Our method
150   shows a much stronger correlation with human preference and the constructed dataset brings signif-
151   icant improvement to the current text-to-audio generation model.
152
153
      2.3   T EXT- TO -AUDIO E VALUATION M ETRIC
154
155
      Existing evaluation metrics for audio generation, such as FAD and IS, assess audio distributions
156   but cannot evaluate the quality of individual samples. Additionally, many studies rely on similarity
157   scores from the CLAP model to assess global audio-text semantic alignment. PicoAudio (Xie et al.,
158   2024) uses a text-to-audio grounding model (Xu et al., 2024) to detect audio segments based on
159   language prompts. However, there remains a lack of fine-grained evaluation methods for assessing
160   detailed event occurrence, sequencing, and acoustic quality. Our research fills this gap by creating
161   robust audio AI scoring pipelines, that show a strong correlation with humans, and significantly
      surpass alternative methods.

      3     T2A-F EEDBACK
182
183
      In this section, we first dive into the three AI audio scoring pipelines: (i) Event Occurrence Prompt-
184   following, in Section. 3.1; (ii) Event Sequence Prompt-following, in Section. 3.2; (iii) Acoustic
185   Quality, in Section. 3.3. We then describe the specific data generation and sorting method for the
186   T2A-Feedback dataset in Section. 3.4.
187
188   3.1   E VENTS O CCURRENCE P ROMPT- FOLLOWING
189
190   Generating audio that accurately reflects the events described in a given prompt is the fundamental
191   requirement of prompt-following. However, when multiple events are included in the text descrip-
192   tion, current text-to-audio generation models often struggle to generate each event precisely. To
193
      improve the generation model’s event occurrence prompt-following ability, we first build an AI
      pipeline to determine the occurrence of events in audio.
194
195   Previous methods primarily utilize contrastive language-audio pre-training (CLAP) (Wu et al.,
196   2023b) over the audios and language descriptions to assess their semantic relevance. However,
197   in multi-event scenarios, the sentence-level matching score struggles to identify event-level mis-
198   alignment, and can not pinpoint which specific events are present and which are not, as shown in
199
      Figure. 5. To accurately identify misaligned events, we propose to measure the audio-text semantic
      alignment at the event-level. To this end, we first separate the language description and audio into
200
      basic events, as shown in the “Event Separation” part of Figure. 2. Specifically, we utilize a large
201
      language model (LLM) (Jiang et al., 2023) to decompose descriptions into event captions accord-
202   ing to the described order. Meanwhile, we employ an advanced audio separation model (Liu et al.,
203   2023b) to segment the audio into event-level sub-audios based on these event captions. By calcu-
204   lating the similarity between each event-level description and its corresponding sub-audio in CLAP
205   space, we can gain clearer insights into the specific aligned and misaligned events.
206
      To encourage the models to comprehensively generate all described events, for each audio-text pair,
207
      we select the lowest value among all event-level audio-text matching scores as the Event Occur-
208   rence Score. For audios generated from the same caption, a higher score indicates that the audio is
209   more likely to contain all the described events.
210
211   3.2   E VENTS S EQUENCE P ROMPT- FOLLOWING
212
213   In addition to generating all events, whether these events occur in the temporal order described
214   in the caption is also a crucial aspect of prompt-following. Some recent work attempts to detect
215   the sequence of events in audio. Tango2 (Majumder et al., 2024) computes the CLAP matching
      score between the temporal description and corresponding audios, but we find the sentence-level

      CLAP score is not sensitive to the temporal description in captions, as demonstrated in Figure. 5
217   and Table. 2. On the other hand, PicoAudio (Xie et al., 2024) employs audio grounding model (Xu
218   et al., 2024) to detect audio segments. However, due to the limitation of the training scale, the
219   generalization performance of the audio grounding model is limited.
220
      To robustly analyze audio event sequences, we propose a new pipeline for event sequence analysis.
221
      Similar to event occurrence, we first use the LLM and audio separation model to extract event-level
222
      descriptions and their corresponding sub-audios. For each separated audio track, we determine the
223   event’s start and end times based on volume levels. Specifically, we normalize the volume to a range
224   of [0,1], and the period where the normalized volume exceeds a certain threshold is identified as the
225   event’s duration.
226
      In multi-event scenarios, there are multiple complex temporal relationships. To comprehensively
227
      assess the temporal alignment between the language prompt and the generated audio, and to specif-
228
      ically identify which temporal relationships are accurate and which are misaligned, we employ
229   Kendall’s τ coefficient. This widely used non-parametric statistic measures rank correlation be-
230   tween two variables. Considering n events and their n(n − 1) event pairs, we use LLM to analyze
231   the relationships between each event pair in the language description and extract the event sequence
232   in the audio based on the starting time of each event. The Events Sequence Score (e.g., Kendall’s
233   τ coefficient between event sequences in language and audio) is calculated as:

      3.3    ACOUSTIC &H ARMONIC Q UALITY
244
245   In addition to generating all events accurately following the language prompt, organically integrating
246   different events to create a pleasant-sounding effect is also one of the basic capabilities. However,
247   current audio generation models often produce low-quality and noisy results.
248
      To alleviate this challenge, we first develop an audio acoustic&harmonic quality predictor. Inspired
249
      by the image aesthetic predictor in Schuhmann et al. (2022), we first manually score audio samples
250   on a scale from 1 to 4 according to their quality. Two annotators independently score the audio
251   samples according to the same criteria, and samples with consistent scores are accepted as training
252   data. The detailed scoring criteria are as follows:
253
254         Annotators need to score the auditory quality of audio from the following four perspectives:
255         Acoustic Quality: Does the generated audio sound realistic and pleasant?
256         Harmony: Do different sound elements integrate well, forming a cohesive auditory scene?
257         Background Noise: Is there noise that disrupts the clarity and naturalness of the audio?
258         Dynamic Range: Are the different audio elements within their reasonable volume range?
259         The specific standards for each score are as follows:
260          Score Standard
261                   Poor audio quality; sounds unrealistic with disjointed elements,
262            1
                      severe background noise interference, and extremely limited dynamic range.
263
                      Normal audio quality; some events are natural, but overall harmony is lacking.
264            2
                      Background noise affects clarity, and dynamic range is limited.
265
266                   Good audio quality; most events are realistic with harmonious integration.
               3
267                   Background noise is minimally disruptive, and dynamic range is reasonable.
268                   Excellent audio quality; all events are very realistic with perfect integration,
               4
269                   well-managed background noise, and wide dynamic range.

      3.4   P REFERENCE DATA G ENERATION
290
291   To generate diverse and comprehensive audio, we first augment the text prompts used for audio
292
      generation. We begin with the captions from the training set of the large-scale audio-text dataset,
      AudioCaps. By employing an LLM, we decompose these captions into fundamental event descrip-
293
      tions and calculate their semantic similarity within the CLAP space to filter out non-overlapping,
294
      basic event descriptions. Then, we prompt the LLM with randomly selected events to create varied
295   and natural multi-event audio descriptions, with explicit temporal ordering. Finally, we combine the
296   enhanced 3,769 captions with the 37,858 captions from the training set of AudioCaps, serving as the
297   prompt source for audio generation.
298
      As highlighted in Cui et al. (2023), diversity is crucial for preference datasets. To mitigate the
299
      potential bias of using a single audio generation model and to enhance the generalization of the gen-
300
      erated data, we employ three advanced audio generation models: Make-an-Audio2, AudioLDM2,
301   and Tango2. Each model generates 2 audio per caption, resulting in a total of 6 audio files for each
302   caption. In summary, we produce 249,762 audios from 41,627 descriptions. For audios generated
303   from the same captions, we combine three rankings of each score to derive the overall ranking.
304
      The histogram plots of the scores on all the generated audios are shown in Figure. 3. The distribution
305
      of Event Occurrence Scores and Acoustic&Harmonic Quality is similar to a Gaussian distribution.
306
      Since most descriptions contain one or two sequential events, Event Sequence Scores are concen-
307   trated between -1 and 1. As noted in Liang et al. (2024), this discriminative score distribution ensures
308   a balanced ratio of negative to positive samples, enabling effective preference tuning.

      4     T2A-E PIC B ENCH
313
314   Current text-to-audio generation models are mainly evaluated and compared on the AudioCaps test
315   set. However, the captions in AudioCaps are generally short and simple, averaging 10.3 words
316   per sentence. Specifically, 17% of the captions feature only a single event, and 44% contains two
317   events. This is not enough to assess the model’s capabilities in more advanced applications involving
318   detailed, multi-event, and narrative-style audio generation.
319   To fill this gap, we propose T2A-EpicBench, consisting of 100 detailed, multi-event, and story-
320   telling captions. Each caption averages 54.8 words and 4.2 events, with 86% containing four events
321   and the remainder featuring five or more. Initially, we manually write 10 detailed captions, then
322   used them as in-context examples to prompt LLM for generating the remaining captions. All 100
323   captions are manually reviewed for accuracy. Several examples from T2A-EpicBench are included
      in the Appendix.

      5.2     A NALYSIS OF P REFERENCE T UNING
425
426   To demonstrate the effect of T2A-Feedback dataset in improving audio generation model, we fine-
427   tuning the advanced text-to-audio model, Make-an-Audio 2 (Huang et al., 2023a), with two pref-
428   erence training methods: Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Wallace et al., 2024) and Reward

      5.2.1    Q UANTITATIVE R ESULTS ON AUDIO C APS
462
      The classical automated metrics (FAD, KL, IS, and CLAP), as well as our three new scores (EOS,
463
      ESS, and AHQ) are employed to quantitatively evaluate and compare different model variants.
464
465   The quantitative results are provided in Table. 4. FAD, KL, and IS assess audio fidelity by evaluating
466   the distribution of the generated audio. For these metrics, both the preference dataset and training
467
      methods result in similar overall improvements. CLAP is commonly used to measure the semantic
      alignment between the input prompt and the generated audio. While both Audio-Alpaca and T2A-
468
      Feedback improve the CLAP score, T2A-Feedback yields greater gains.
469
470   Moreover, as analyzed in Section. 5.1.1, the proposed EOS and ESS are more accurate than CLAP
471   in judging event occurrence and event sequence, and AHQ shows a strong correlation to human
472   preference in acoustic and harmony. We calculate the three scores for different model variants to
473
      evaluate audio generation results more accurately and comprehensively. The significantly better
      results across these three metrics demonstrate that T2A-Feedback yields far greater improvements
474
      compared to Audio-Alpaca, and the DPO method outperforms RAFT in our setting.

529
530
531
      In this paper, we build AI scoring pipelines to evaluate three fundamental capabilities of audio
      generation: Event Occurrence Prompt-following, Event Sequence Prompt-following, and Acous-
532
      tics&Harmonic Quality. Using these automatic evaluation metrics, which are highly correlated with
533
      human preferences, we build a large-scale audio preference dataset, T2A-Feedback. Experimen-
534   tally, we extensively demonstrate the accuracy and robustness of our AI scoring pipelines. The three
535   scores demonstrate a strong correlation to human preferences, which highlights its potential to bet-
536   ter evaluate text-to-audio generation models. To assess the model’s ability in complex multi-event
537   scenarios, we propose a new challenging benchmark, T2A-EpicBench, which requires models to
538   generate detailed and narrative audios. Using our T2A-Feedback to tune the audio generation model
539   effectively improves its capabilities in the three core aspects and achieves better performance in both
      simple (AudioCaps) and complex (T2A-EpicBench) scenarios.
