009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Large language models (LLMs) have made significant advancements in address-
012            ing diverse natural language processing (NLP) tasks. However, their performance
013            is often limited by inherent comprehension of problems. To address this limita-
014            tion, we propose Exchange-of-Perspective (EoP), a novel framework designed to
015            exchange perspectives across different definitions of problem, so that it can break
016
               the fixed mindset from any particular formulation of the question. We conducted
               extensive and comprehensive experiments on 8 benchmarks. The results show
017
               that EoP can significantly improve performance. For instance, compared to the
018
               non-commutative baseline PHP, with GPT-3.5-Turbo and EoP, we observe a 3.6%
019            improvement on AQuA (60.6% → 64.2%), while GPT-4-powered EoP demon-
020            strates a 7.7% enhancement on Math (53.9% → 61.6%) and a 3.5% improvement
021            on OlympiadBench Maths (43.5% → 47.0%) when using Qwen-2.5-72b.

      offering a solution and then trying to revise it repeatedly. One effective method of enhancing com-
055   prehension is to view the question from different perspectives, we observe a phenomenon that when
056   humans answer questions, different definitions of the same question can lead to varied responses.
057   This indicates that diverse phrasing of question has potential to yield multiple perspectives. When
058   the responses are consistent, the likelihood of the response being correct also increases.
059
      Building on this insight, we introduce the Exchange-of-Perspective (EoP) framework, as shown
060
      in Figure 1. Unlike CoT and PHP (Zheng et al., 2023), EoP redefines the original question first,
061   and then dynamically incorporates external perspectives by iteratively exchanging answers for the
062   same question presented with different definitions. Figure 2 illustrates EoP further. It executes as
063   outlined below: (1) For a given question, we redefine it into an augmented question with LLM,
064   subsequently forming two branches: the original branch and the augmented branch. (2) Instruct the
065   LLM to generate initial answers for both branches. (3) Swap the answers and combine them with
066   the question from the other branch using the phrase ”Hint: The answer is near to”, which follows
067   (Zheng et al., 2023), to derive follow-up answers. (4) Continue the process in step (3) until meeting
068   termination condition.

275
      Why EoP can get significant performance improvement? Table 4 showcases the performance
276   metrics for each branch. The Org Branch (with original question) yields 83.3% for CoT Prompt and
277   83.1% for Complex CoT Prompt, while the Aug Branch (with rephrased questions) results in 82.1%
278   and 81.7% respectively. These results indicate that the rephrased questions perform even worse
279   than the original questions. However, when integrating both branches, EoP framework outperforms
280   individual branches with scores of 84.9% for CoT Prompt and 85.3% for Complex CoT Prompt.
281   So the performance gain of EoP is not from rephrasing the question, while it comes from two main
282   factors: (1) Error Correction, where insights from one branch can rectify misinterpretations from
283   another, thereby improving problem analysis accuracy, and (2) Complementary Information, where
284
      merging branches provides more extensive and holistic insights to problem-solving. So EoP can
      effectively mitigating the potential inaccuracies introduced by rephrased question.

417
418   Chain-of-Thought prompting Since CoT underscores the value of multi-step logical pathways
419   in deriving conclusive answers, a series of enhancement strategies has been proposed. One note-
420   worthy strategy is the careful selection of demonstrations. For instance, Lu et al. (2023) introduced
421   a framework known as PromptPG, which employs policy gradients to choose demonstrations. Re-
422   search conducted by Wang et al. (2024) focused on training dense retrievers to select high-quality
423
      demonstrations. For complex tasks, a two-stage method is recommended, involving the breakdown
      of tasks into manageable sub-tasks that are solved sequentially before integration (Zhou et al., 2023;
424
      Khot et al., 2023). Additionally, approaches such as Program-of-Thought (PoT; Chen et al., 2023b),
425
      Program-Aided Language models (PAL; Gao et al., 2023b) propose to generate intermediate rea-
426   soning programs and employ external interpreters for execution. Moreover, equipping LLMs with
427   external tools, such as scratch pads for intermediate computations, search engines for information
428   retrieval, QA systems for clarifying inquiries, and calculators for performing mathematical opera-
429   tions, can further enhance task performance (Nye et al., 2021; Shuster et al., 2022; Schick et al.,
430   2023). However, these methods rely on a single reasoning pathway, if the initial reasoning steps
431   exhibit inaccuracies or inherent biases, these defects may propagate through the reasoning process,
      culminating in erroneous conclusions (Bai et al., 2022; Lyu et al., 2023).

450
451   Self-Correction Self-correction represents a method that allows LLMs to refine their outputs
452   based on feedback from prior responses. Two main branches exist for self-correction: fine-tuning
453   and prompting. In the field of fine-tuning, McAleese et al. (2024) advocate for developing a critic
454
      model that assesses the responses of LLMs, which is subsequently employed to improve their re-
      sponses. Conversely, research by An et al. (2023) and Kumar et al. (2024) recommends directly fine-
455
      tuning the LLM rather than training an additional critic model. However, this fine-tuning method
456
      demands considerable effort and resources. In the field of prompting, research conducted by (Kim
457   et al., 2023) and (Madaan et al., 2023) utilizes the LLM’s self-reflection and past errors to enhance
458   reasoning. Zheng et al. (2023) developed the PHP method, which employs hints from earlier re-
459   sponses to guide models toward more accurate outcomes. Nonetheless, recent studies (Huang et al.,
460   2024; Qu et al., 2024) illustrate that even top-tier models frequently struggle with self-correcting
461   reasoning mistakes and may experience performance declines without external feedback. This ex-
462   ternal feedback may originate from humans, other models (Wang et al., 2023b; Paul et al., 2024),
463   or external tools and knowledge sources (Gou et al., 2024a; Chen et al., 2024; Gao et al., 2023a).
464   However, in practice, high-quality external feedback is often unavailable. This study proposes a sim-
465
      ple yet effective external feedback approach that incorporates different perspectives by exchanging
      answers to the same question framed with varied definitions.
466

468   5                                                          C ONCLUSION
469
470
      We have introduced the EoP framework, a novel and impactful approach aimed at enhancing the
471   reasoning capabilities of LLMs. By integrating external perspectives from various definitions of the
472   question, EoP overcomes the limitations of current chain-of-thought and self-correction methods,
473   which rely heavily on the model’s internal perspective. This method highlights the significance of
474   comprehensively grasping the question prior to developing a solution, and sharing diverse perspec-
475   tives can cultivate more resilient and nuanced reasoning. Our experimental results across a variety
476   of complex reasoning tasks demonstrate that the EoP framework significantly outperforms existing
477   benchmarks, highlighting its potential to improve the problem-solving skills of LLMs. In conclu-
478   sion, our work emphasizes the importance of integrating external perspectives obtained from diverse
479
      problem definitions, setting a new direction for the development of more reliable and accurate lan-
      guage models.
