011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Sequential recommendation (SR) aims to suggest items users are most likely to
014            engage with next based on their past interactions. However, in practice, SR systems
015
               often face the out-of-distribution (OOD) problem due to dynamic environmental
               factors (e.g., seasonal changes), leading to significant performance degradation in
016
               the testing phase. Some methods incorporate distributionally robust optimization
017
               (DRO) into SR to alleviate OOD, but the sparsity of SR data challenges this. Other
018            approaches use random data augmentations to explore the OOD, potentially dis-
019            torting important information, as user behavior is personalized rather than random.
020            Additionally, they often overlook users’ varying sensitivity to distribution shifts
021            during the exploration, which is crucial for capturing the evolution of user prefer-
022            ences in OOD contexts. In this work, inspired by information bottleneck theory
023            (IB), we propose the Conditional Distribution Information Bottleneck (CDIB), a
024            novel objective that creates diverse OOD distributions while preserving minimal
025            sufficient information regarding the origin distribution conditioned on the user.
026
               Building on this, we introduce a framework with a learnable, personalized data
               augmentation method using a mask-then-generate paradigm to craft diverse and
027
               reliable OOD distributions optimized with CDIB. Experiments on four real-world
028
               datasets show our model consistently outperforms baselines. The code is available
029            at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/CDIB-51C8.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Nowadays, recommendation systems are important in addressing information overload across various
035   applications, such as e-commerce, online retail platforms, and so on (Cen et al., 2020; Guy et al.,
036   2010). SR is one of the crucial topics focusing on capturing users’ dynamic interest to recommend
037   content that aligns with it more accurately (Hidasi et al., 2016; Kang & McAuley, 2018).
038   Nevertheless, most methods assume that the popularity distribution during training and testing is
039   independent and identically distributed, an unrealistic assumption in most cases (Zheng et al., 2021;
040   Zhang et al., 2023). In SR, popularity distribution can shift due to time-sensitive environmental
041   factors, leading to changes in user preferences (e.g., the World Cup boosting soccer jersey sales
042   or seasonal changes increasing T-shirt sales in summer and sweater sales in winter), which causes
043   performance degradation of the model during the testing phases.
044   Furthermore, we observe that different users have varying sensitivity to distribution shifts, leading
045   to different impacts from OOD scenarios. As shown in Figure 1, for blockbuster users who engage
046   with trending content, the model can adjust and continue providing relevant recommendations as
047   trends shift (①→③) due to its inherent bias toward popular items (Zhang et al., 2021). However, for
048   niche users who follow mainstream items less, despite the model capturing their preferences during
049   training, it often defaults to providing popular items when environmental factors change, likely due
050
      to unfamiliar behavior patterns, misaligning with niche users’ true preferences (②→④).
051   To alleviate the OOD problem in SR, various models have been developed, employing techniques
052   such as reweighting (Wang et al., 2022b), causal inference (Wang et al., 2023b; He et al., 2022),
053   distributionally robust optimization (Yang et al., 2023b; Wen et al., 2022), and contrastive learning
      (CL) (Liu et al., 2021; Xie et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2023a; Qiu et al., 2022).

073   However, existing methods have two main issues: (i) Methods like DRO in SR (Yang et al., 2023b)
074   optimize the model for the worst-case distribution within a family of distributions around the training
075   data, ensuring robustness to unknown distributions. However, the feasible distribution family for
076
      DRO is inherently limited by the sparse nature of recommendation data (Wang et al., 2024). (ii) Data
      augmentation models (e.g., CL methods (Qiu et al., 2022; Xie et al., 2022)) can expand the training
077
      distribution but often rely on unguided or hand-crafted augmentations, risking the loss of important
078
      interaction data while retaining noisy or irrelevant information for augmentation, misleading user
079   preference modeling. Additionally, they overlook users’ varying sensitivity to distribution shifts
080   mentioned above, which is essential for capturing the evolution of user preferences in OOD contexts.
081
082
      To this end, inspired by IB theory (Gondek & Hofmann, 2003; Tishby & Zaslavsky, 2015; Alemi
      et al., 2016; Lee et al., 2023; Choi & Lee, 2023), we propose the Conditional Distribution Information
083
      Bottleneck (CDIB) that generates diverse distributions while preserving minimal sufficient information
084
      from the original distribution conditioned on the user. It aims to introduce more interaction patterns
085   influenced by other time-sensitive environmental factors at training, enhancing performance on
086   unknown distributions. Specifically, CDIB diversifies the generated data by minimizing the mutual
087   information between original distribution D and generated distribution D̃ given the user features,
088   while retaining personalized critical information by maximizing the mutual information between
089   generated D̃ and the target items reflecting users’ preferences. To control the information flow
090   from D to D̃, we propose a mask-then-generate mechanism. First, we introduce a Learnable Mask
091   that adaptively identifies stable and sensitive items (e.g., comic book and soccer jersey during the
092   World Cup) in the interaction sequence based on their semantic relatedness. The sensitive items are
093   then perturbed and reconstructed using the proposed Distribution Generator, built on the latent
094   diffusion model (Ho et al., 2020; Rombach et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023a). This process constrains
095   sensitive information transmission while enriching interactions by reconstructing unseen sensitive
096   items. Moreover, we provide theoretical analyses to justify the rationality of the generated D̃.
097   To summarize, this work makes the following contributions: (i) We introduce CDIB, which guides the
098   generation of diverse and reliable distributions with personalized information, and conduct theoretical
099   analyses to prove its rationality. (ii) We propose a mask-then-generate mechanism to constrain
100   sensitive information transmission and enrich sensitive item interactions for better performance in
101   OOD scenarios. (iii) Extensive experiments demonstrate the effectiveness and robustness of CDIB.

128   2.2   I NFORMATION B OTTLENECK P RINCIPLE
129
      The information bottleneck principle (Tishby & Zaslavsky, 2015; Alemi et al., 2016) is an approach
130
      based on information theory designed to balance the trade-off between compressing a random
131   variable and preserving its minimum sufficient information about the target variable. It aims to find a
132   compact representation that retains as much information about the target as possible and discards the
133   target-irrelevant information.
134
135   Definition 2.1 (IB) Given input variable X, target Y, and bottleneck variable Z, respectively, the
136   IB aims to compress X to Z, while keeping the information relevant for Y:
137
                                              min I(X; Z) − βI(Y; Z)                                           (2)

139                    P                  p(u,v)
140
      where I(U; V) = u,v p(u, v) log p(u)p(v)   is the mutual information between U and V, and β ∈ R
141   is a Lagrange multiplier balancing the two mutual information terms.

145   In this section, we first formally propose the DIB principle in Section 3.1, a straightforward application

150   3.1   D ISTRIBUTION I NFORMATION B OTTLENECK P RINCIPLE
151
152   In this section, we introduce the DIB, anchored in the IB. This principle facilitates the generation
153   of new distributions, represented as D̃ = (X̃s , X̃e ), derived from the training distribution Dtr =
154   (Xs , Xe ). With the DIB, while the D̃ preserves the stable elements within the Dtr , it introduces a
155   spectrum of sensitive elements, thereby enhancing the diversity of the data.
156

158   D̃, respectively, we define DIB as follows:
159

      3.2     C ONDITIONAL D ISTRIBUTION I NFORMATION B OTTLENECK P RINCIPLE
168
169   Although DIB can generate a diverse and promising distribution D̃, the generation process of the
170   distributions is still not fully reliable due to the lack of constraints. Specifically, there is no certainty
171   that the distributions obtained from the minimization of DIB will represent those encountered in
172   the testing stage. It’s because X̃e can be generated in any direction that diverges from Xe as
173   long as it can minimize the I(Dtr , D̃). Furthermore, the straightforward application of DIB fails
174   to account for users’ sensitivity to OOD. More concretely, some users are readily influenced by
175   environmental factors like popular trends. In contrast, others display less susceptibility to such
176   influences. Consequently, the generation of X̃e should be personalized, suggesting that the generation
177   of D̃ should be more controlled. To this end, we introduce the CDIB, aiming to guide the personalized
178   generation of D̃ and steer it, to a certain extent, towards aligning with the testing distribution:
179
180   Definition 3.2 (CDIB) Given original training distribution Dtr , target Y, generated distribution D̃,
181   and user embeddings Γ respectively. The formulation of CDIB is as follows:
182                                      min I(Dtr ; D̃ | Γ) − βI(Y; D̃ | Γ)                                   (4)

451   remove key modules to implement three variants of CDIB: (i) “w/o LM”: CDIB without the learnable
452
      mask, setting Mu = 1. (ii) “w/o IB”: CDIB without optimization with CDIB, using contrastive
      learning with InfoNCE loss instead. (iii) “w/o DG”: CDIB without latent diffusion. Gaussian noise is
453
      added to Hu to get H̃u (i.e., H̃u = N (0, I) ⊙ Mu + Hu ⊙ (1 − Mu ). From the results (Table 1),
454
      we observe that: (i) Removing the learnable mask significantly degrades performance, underscoring
455
      its essential role in identifying elements to be disturbed during data augmentation. Without this

      Sequential Recommendation is designed to predict the next item a user is likely to prefer based on
      their interaction history. Traditional methods have leveraged Markov chains to capture first-order
478
      item-to-item correlations through transition matrices (Rendle et al., 2010; He & McAuley, 2016).
479
      With the development of deep learning, which excels at modeling complex sequential patterns, various
480   deep recommendation models have been developed. For instance, GRU4Rec (Hidasi et al., 2016)
481   employs Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU) units to model the temporal dynamics of interaction sequences.
482   SASRec (Kang & McAuley, 2018) and BERT4Rec (Sun et al., 2019) enhance computational efficiency
483   in lengthy sequences by incorporating self-attention mechanisms. More recently, inspired by selective
484   state space models (Gu & Dao, 2024), Mamba4Rec (Liu et al., 2024a) has been introduced, utilizing
485   the mamba framework to recommend items efficiently. Despite their capabilities, these models
      often suffer performance declines when OOD occurs. To address this, CDIB introduces a user

      Distributionally Robust Sequential Recommendation has recently attracted significant research
514
      interest, which aims to train a model that performs well not only at the training stage but also at the
515
      testing stage. Methods DRO (Schnabel et al., 2010; Bottou et al., 2013; Wang et al., 2022b; Yang et al.,
516   2023b; Wen et al., 2022) optimize the model for the worst-case distribution to improve the robustness.
517   For example, DROS (Yang et al., 2023b) unifies the DRO and sequential recommendation paradigms
518   to enhance model robustness against distribution shifts. Causal inference methods capture real causal
519   relationships but assume the causal graph is static (Wang et al., 2023b; He et al., 2022; Yang et al.,
520   2020; Wang et al., 2022a), these methods face challenges with sparse data. While contrastive learning
521   approaches seek to enrich the training data distribution through data augmentation (Liu et al., 2021;
522   Xie et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2023a; Qiu et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2023), but hardly rely on the
523   hand-crafted data augmentation strategies. To fill the gap, we introduce the CDIB principle, using the
524
      user features to guide the exploration of the other distribution.
525   Information Bottleneck with Conditional Information is also widely utilized. The CIB (Gondek
526   & Hofmann, 2003) theory has been applied in CGIB (Lee et al., 2023) to identify key structures in
527   molecules that predict interaction behaviors between graph pairs, focusing on important subgraphs.
528   Additionally, TimeCIB (Choi & Lee, 2023) extends the CIB to impute time series data, preserving
529   vital temporal information. To the best of our knowledge, CDIB marks the first use of CIB to guide
530
      the distribution generation process. The detailed introduction of related works is in Appendix E.4.
531
532   6    C ONCLUSION
533
534   In this work, to address the limitations of existing methods that struggle with sparse data or depend on
535   hand-crafted augmentations, we introduce CDIB, an innovative principle that guides the generation
536   of diverse and reliable distributions based on user features. Theoretical analyses demonstrate the
537   rationality of this approach. Building on CDIB, we propose a framework that employs a learnable
538   method to generate distributions for OOD exploration, guided by a conditional generation term
539   and a conditional regularization term. Extensive experiments on four public datasets confirm the
      effectiveness and robustness of our model.

978    balancing the diversity and reliability of generated distribution, we also visualized the generation

980    concluded that at lower β values (i.e., 1e0, 1e1), feature collapse occurs in the generated items. This
981    collapse may happen because a small β shifts the model’s focus towards generating low similarity
982    distributions (minimizing I(Dtr ; D̃|Γ), thereby neglecting the intrinsic features of the original dataset.
983    Such generated distributions can introduce unnecessary noise to the model, complicating its learning
984    process. Hence, the model performs poorly when β is small (see Section 4.2). As β increases, the
985    distribution generated by latent diffusion gradually aligns more closely with the original distribution
986    and retains a clustering effect of popular items to some extent, while the overall distribution becomes
987
       more uniform, aiding the model’s learning process (Yu et al., 2022). When β reaches 1e4, there is no
       distinguishable difference between the generated and original distributions. At this level, the model
988
       prioritizes preserving the original data characteristics as much as possible (maximizing I(Y; D̃|Γ),
989
       which limits the exploration of out-of-distribution scenarios and decreases overall effectiveness.

1165

1166

1170   than L empirically, the overall time complexity of CDIB is O((|I| + |U|)d), which is affordable.

1281   E.4   M ORE R ELATED W ORKS
1282
1283   Sequential Recommendation is designed to predict the next item a user is likely to prefer based on
1284   their interaction history. Traditional methods have leveraged Markov chains to capture first-order
1285   item-to-item correlations through transition matrices (Rendle et al., 2010; He & McAuley, 2016).
1286   With the development of deep learning, which excels at modeling complex sequential patterns, various
1287
       deep recommendation models have been developed. For instance, GRU4Rec (Hidasi et al., 2016)
       employs Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU) units to model the temporal dynamics of interaction sequences.
1288
       Caser (Tang & Wang, 2018) uses a time convolutional neural network (TCN) to account for both long-
1289
       term and short-term user interests in personalized recommendations. SASRec (Kang & McAuley,
1290   2018) and BERT4Rec (Sun et al., 2019) enhance computational efficiency in lengthy sequences by
1291   incorporating self-attention mechanisms. More recently, inspired by selective state space models (Gu
1292   & Dao, 2024), Mamba4Rec (Liu et al., 2024a) has been introduced, utilizing the mamba framework
1293   to recommend items efficiently. Despite their capabilities, these models often suffer performance
1294   declines when OOD occurs. To address this, CDIB introduces a user feature-guided generation
1295   approach that proactively explores OOD scenarios during the training phase, enhancing the model’s
       generalization capabilities.


                                                        24
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1296
       Distributionally Robust Sequential Recommendation has recently attracted significant research
1297   interest, which aims to train a model that performs well not only at the training stage but also at the
1298   testing stage. Methods like reweighting and DRO (Schnabel et al., 2010; Bottou et al., 2013; Wang
1299   et al., 2022b; Yang et al., 2023b; Wen et al., 2022) presume that the test dataset’s distribution can
1300   be inferred from prior knowledge. For example, IPS (Schnabel et al., 2010) re-weight each instance
1301   with the inverse propensity score, which implicitly assumes the testing distribution is uniform (Zhang
1302   et al., 2023). DROS (Yang et al., 2023b) unifies the DRO and sequential recommendation paradigms
1303   to enhance model robustness against distribution shifts but faces challenges with sparse data. Causal
1304   inference methods capture real causal relationships but assume the causal graph is static (Wang et al.,
1305
       2023b; He et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2020; Wang et al., 2022a), while contrastive learning approaches
       seek to enrich the training data distribution through data augmentation (Liu et al., 2021; Xie et al.,
1306
       2022; Yang et al., 2023a; Qiu et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2023), but hardly rely on the data augmentation
1307
       strategies. What’s more, most of the existing models ignore the user’s sensitivity during the process
1308   of distribution shift. To fill the gap, we introduce the CDIB principle, using the user features to guide
1309   the exploration of the other distribution.
1310
1311
       Information Bottleneck with Conditional Information has been increasingly utilized in recent
       research. Various studies have adopted the information bottleneck (IB) principle by incorporating
1312
       conditional, aiming to extract information that aligns with specific objectives. The conditional
1313
       information bottleneck (CIB) theory (Gondek & Hofmann, 2003) has been applied in methods
1314   such as CGIB (Lee et al., 2023) to identify crucial molecular structures that predict interactions
1315   between graph pairs, with a focus on significant subgraphs. TimeCIB (Choi & Lee, 2023) extends
1316   the CIB to time series data imputation, ensuring the preservation of essential temporal information.
1317   Drawing inspiration from these precedents, CDIB employs CIB to steer the generation of distributions,
1318   enhancing the model’s robustness. To the best of our knowledge, CDIB is the first application of CIB
1319   to guide the distribution generation process.
1320   Diffusion-based Augmentation Models Earlier approaches like Diff4Rec (Wu et al., 2023) and
1321   DiffuASR (Liu et al., 2023) followed a three-step process: training the diffusion model, generating
1322   new data with the diffusion model, and then training the recommendation model on new data, which
1323   can lead to a disconnect between the generation and downstream tasks due to the discrete nature of
1324   these stages, preventing the flow of gradient information. Our model, however, employs an end-to-end
1325   training approach, which maintains the alignment between the generation and downstream tasks.
1326   What’s more, in the SR scenario, interaction data is very sensitive (Ye et al., 2023), and there is a risk
1327   of losing significant information during the data augmentation phase, which may compromise the
1328
       quality of the generated data, a concern overlooked in previous methods. Our model addresses this
       by utilizing a learnable mask mechanism to safeguard critical interactions adaptively and is guided by
1329
       IB theory in the generation process.

1332   F    M ECHANISM OF THE D IFFUSION M ODEL
1333
1334   Diffusion Models have shown impressive generative performance across several domains, including
1335
       computer vision (Ho et al., 2020; Rombach et al., 2022), natural language processing (Austin et al.,
       2021), and time series (Rasul et al., 2021; Shen & Kwok, 2023; Liu et al., 2024b). Generally, diffusion
1336
       models consist of two pivotal phases: the forward process and the reverse process. In the forward
1337
       process, Gaussian noise is incrementally introduced into the initial data sample x0 ∼ q(x0 ), creating
1338   a sequence x1:T through T steps in a Markov chain, which can be formulated as follows:
1339                                                       p
1340                                 q(xt |xt−1 ) = N (xt ; 1 − βt xt−1 , βt I),                          (36)
1341   where N indicates the Gaussian distribution and βt ∈ (0, 1) specifies the scale of noise introduced
1342   at each step t. Through the reparameterization trick and principle that the sum√of two independent
                                                                                                   √
1343   Gaussian noises is also Gaussian, xt can be directly derived from x0 as xt = ᾱt x0 + 1 − ᾱt ϵt ,
                                                         QT
1344   with ϵt ∼ N (0, I) as the added noise, and ᾱt = t′ =1 (1 − βt′ ) signifying the cumulative product of
1345   noise scaling factors. The reverse process, conversely, aims to iteratively remove the noise from xt to
1346
