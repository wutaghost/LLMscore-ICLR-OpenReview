011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Recent AI systems have shown extremely powerful performance, even surpassing
014            human performance, on various tasks such as information retrieval, language
015            generation, and image generation based on large language models (LLMs). At
016            the same time, there are diverse safety risks that can cause the generation of
017            malicious contents by circumventing the alignment in LLMs, a phenomenon often
018
               referred to as jailbreaking. However, most of the previous works only focused
               on the text-based jailbreaking in LLMs, and the jailbreaking of the text-to-image
019
               (T2I) generation system has been relatively overlooked. In this paper, we first
020
               evaluate the safety of the commercial T2I generation systems, such as ChatGPT,
021            Copilot, and Gemini, on copyright infringement with naive prompts. From this
022            empirical study, we find that Copilot and Gemini block only 5% and 11.25% of
023            the attacks with naive prompts, respectively, while ChatGPT blocks 96.25% of
024            them. Then, we further propose a stronger automated jailbreaking pipeline for
025            T2I generation systems, which produces prompts that bypass their safety guards.
026            Our automated jailbreaking framework leverages an LLM optimizer to generate
027            prompts that maximize degree of violation from the generated images without any
028            weight updates or gradient computation. Surprisingly, our simple yet effective
029
               approach successfully jailbreaks the Copilot and ChatGPT with 0.0% and 6.25%
               block rate, respectively, enabling the generation of copyrighted content 73.3% of
030
               the time. Finally, we explore various defense strategies, such as post-generation
031
               filtering and machine unlearning techniques, but find them inadequate, highlighting
032            the necessity of stronger defense mechanisms.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
037
      Text-to-Image (T2I) generative models (Betker et al., 2023; Esser et al., 2024; OpenAI, 2024;
038
      Microsoft, 2024; MidJourney, 2024; Team et al., 2023) are mostly trained on large-scale image
039   data from the web, which are known to contain numerous copyrighted, privacy-sensitive, and
040   harmful images. Recent works (Somepalli et al., 2023b;a; Carlini et al., 2023) demonstrate that
041   diffusion-based image generative models memorize a portion of the training data, allowing the
042   replication of copyrighted content (Wang et al., 2024; Wen et al., 2024). Although the models used in
043   recent commercial T2I systems are mostly unknown to the public, we find they also easily generate
044   copyrighted contents (Figure 1). Such copyright violation is one of the most critical real-world
045   safety problems associated with generative models, and there are several ongoing lawsuits (Saveri &
046   Butterick, 2023; Grynbaum & Mac, 2023; Dennis, 2023) against the service providers regarding this
047
      matter.
048   To prevent such potential copyright violations, ChatGPT (OpenAI, 2024) and Copilot (Microsoft,
049   2024) censor user requests by blocking generation of copyrighted materials or rephrase the users’
050   prompts. However, are they really secure against unauthorized reproduction of copyrighted materials?
051   To the best of our knowledge, there is no work on quantitative evaluation of the copyright violation
052   in commercial T2I systems, making it difficult for the service providers to red-team their systems.
053
      Furthermore, for intellectual property (IP) owners, it requires significant effort to verify the usage of
      contents in those systems via manual trial-and-error processes (Figure 1).

074   detection, we give a penalty when prompts contain specific keywords, such as "Mickey Mouse,"

123   2      P RELIMINARY
124
125   Copyright. Copyright is a legal protection provided to the owners of "original works of authorship",
126   such as literature, music, and art (Office, 2023; Patent & Office, 2024). This protection is granted
127
      to owners under the law, giving them the exclusive right to reproduce, or distribute their works for
      a certain period of time (Legal Information Institute, 2022; Office, 2023). Reproduction refers to
128
      creating copies of the work in any form, and distribution involves making the work available to the
129
      public through selling or lending copies. While the use of copyrighted data in AI models has been
130   tacitly accepted for educational purposes, the rise of commercial AI systems has brought significant
131   attention to the issue of copyright infringement (Saveri & Butterick, 2023; Grynbaum & Mac, 2023;
132   Dennis, 2023). Opinions on the legal aspects of AI vary, but ethically, generative AI systems should
133   not infringe on these rights, in order to protect the intellectual property of their owners. In academia,
134   numerous efforts have been made for copyright protection, e.g., training data protection (Zhong et al.,
135   2023; Shan et al., 2023), theoretical guarantees (Bousquet et al., 2020; Elkin-Koren et al., 2023; Vyas
136   et al., 2023), guided generation (Schramowski et al., 2023; Kumari et al., 2023) and mechanism
137   design (Zhou et al., 2024; Golatkar et al., 2024; Deng et al., 2024). Despite these efforts, we reveal
138
      that commercial T2I systems still infringe copyrights despite careful alignment and red-teaming
      mechanisms.
139
140   Prompt attack to jailbreak T2I models. Previous attack approaches demonstrate the vulnerabili-
141   ties in T2I diffusion models by attacking prompts to either generate different objects (Maus et al.,
142   2023) or create potentially harmful images (Yang et al., 2024b; Zhai et al., 2024; Dong et al., 2024).
143   However, previous T2I jailbreaking approaches rely on classifiers to attack the prompt. In contrast,
144
      due to the definition of copyright, there is no copyright classifier as copyright infringment is deter-
      mined by human judgment. Therefore, previous T2I jailbreaking approaches are not fully applicable
145
      to copyright infringement jailbreaking. To address this limitation, we propose an Automatic Prompt
146
      Generation Pipeline (APGP) to induce copyright infringement in these commercial T2I systems to
147   evaluate copyright infringement using a single target image.

149   3      AUTOMATIC PROMPT GENERATION PIPELINE FOR EVALUATING COPYRIGHT
150          VIOLATIONS
151
152
      T2I models generate single or multiple images based on the user’s prompt, aiming to reflect as much
      information as possible. While following the user’s prompt, T2I models may violate the reproduction
153
      rights of certain IPs. However, evaluating the safety of T2I systems by a trial-and-error process using
154
      manually crafted prompts is challenging and tiresome.
155
156   To alleviate the challenge, we propose an Automatic Prompt Generation Pipeline (APGP) that
157   generates high-risk prompts for T2I systems. Generated prompts are designed to test the systems’
158
      tendencies to violate copyright and safety policies, allowing us to effectively evaluate the commercial
      T2I systems’ output without any weight updates or gradient computations. APGP consists of three
159
      steps: 1) searching seed prompts that describe the target images using vision-language models; 2)
160
      revising the generated prompts into high-risk prompts via optimization, based on self-generated QA
161   scores and keyword penalties; and 3) post-processing with a suffix for keyword suppression and
      intention addition. Details are illustrated in Appendix A.2.

      3.1 S EARCHING SEED PROMPT USING VISION - LANGUAGE MODELS
163
      As shown in Figure 2 (left), we propose an automated pipeline that generates high-risk prompts—
164
      detailed descriptions of the target image—to guide the T2I model in replicating the target image.
165
      We first use a vision-language model (VLM) to describe the target image. To reach a high success
166   rate in generating a copyright-violated image, we require the initial prompt to accurately depict all
167   components in the target image rather than illustrating general objects.
168
169
      To search optimal seed prompts for T2I models, we utilize an optimization by prompting
      (OPRO) (Yang et al., 2024a) approach, seeking the most effective instructions for a VLM (g)
170
      by employing a LLM (f1 ) as the optimizer. Given the predefined N initial instructions {inst1:N },
171
      where i ranges from 1 to N , the VLM generates the prompt {xi } that describes the target image
172   Itarget . To measure the effectiveness of the instructions given to the VLM, we utilize the alignment
173   score ci , which is the cosine distance between the embedding vector of each prompt xi and the
174   embedding vector of the target image Itarget using CLIP (Radford et al., 2021).
175
      Similar to OPRO (Yang et al., 2024a), we forward instruction and score pair ({insti , ci }) to the LLM
176
      (f1 ) to update the instructions to insti+1 . This optimization process is repeated through generating
177
      new prompts based on updated instructions, calculating the CLIP scores for each prompt, and refining
178   the instructions by passing the instruction-score pairs back to the LLM. If the highest score remains
179   unchanged for r steps, we conclude the best seed prompt (z0 ) for the target image has been achieved.
180   The instruction optimization template for the LLM (f1 ) is described Appendix A.2.
181
182   3.2   O PTIMIZING THE PROMPTS WITH KEYWORD PENALTIES AND SELF - GENERATED QA
            SCORES
183
184   To generate the highest-risk prompt that evokes the exact target content from T2I systems, we propose
185   an automated prompt revision step via optimization based on self-generated QA scores and keyword
186
      penalties. In this step, we start with the seed prompt (z0 ) and refine it to zt using the LLM (f2 ) to
      achieve higher self-generated QA scores and fewer keyword penalties, which induces the generation
187
      of the copyright-violating image Igen with T2I systems.
188
189   Our score functions. To find the highest-risk prompt for T2I systems, score functions (S) are
190   critical to drive the LLM as shown in Figure 2. We propose two scores, keyword penalty (Sk ) and
191   question&answer (QA) score (Sqa ) along with image-image consistency and image-text alignment.
192   To bypass the word-based detection in some T2I systems, we aim to generate prompts with precise
193
      descriptions of the target image without using any keywords that explicitly represent the target image.
      Thus, the keyword penalty score applies if the prompt contains any of the keywords, k. We count
194
      the number of keywords that appear in the prompt (zt ) and penalize it with negative value. However,
195
      these penalties may lead to the prompt (zt ) with a generic description that does not reflect distinct
196   information to describe the target image Itarget .
197
198   To prevent generic prompts, we propose a self-generated QA score that evaluates answers based
199
      on the text-only prompt (zt ) and the questions generated by the VLM from the target image (see
      Figure 2, highlighted in yellow). The question and answer pairs ({qm , am }) are “self-generated”
200
      with the VLM based on the given target image Itarget . The LLM (l) responds to the question (qm )
201
      based on the text-only (zt ) as follow, ym = l(qm , zt ). To evaluate the response (ym ),P we employ
202   LLM (v) as a verifier as follow, 1m                                                             m
                                          0,1 = m(qm , am , ym ), so that the QA score (Sqa ) is  m 1 ∗ 5.
203   To prioritize the QA score, we aim to select the prompt that can answer more questions, compared to
204   cases where there is similar consistency between the images or between the images and text in the
205   model’s embedding spaces, where a standard deviation of consistency score is 3.11. The keyword
206   penalty and self-generated QA score result in refining the prompts to include all information about
207   the target image so that we can find the highest-risk prompt that describes the target image precisely
208   as much as possible without any keywords.
209   The consistency score and alignment score are calculated using the cosine similarity distance between
210   the vectors extracted by the encoder. For image-image consistency score (Sii ), we employ pretrained
211   vision encoder ev and calculate distance between the target image (Itarget ) and generated image
212   (Igen ) from T2I model h. For image-text consistency score (Sti ), we employ CLIP model as an
213   encoder and calculate the distance between the prompt zt and target image Itarget . The overall score
214   function for optimizing the highest-risk prompt is as follows,

235   3.3 S UFFIX PROMPT INJECTION
236   To further rigorously evaluate the T2I systems, we employ suffix prompt injection in our prompt
237   which is a similar approach to jailbreaking the language model behaviors (Wei et al., 2024). We
238   append the suffix prompt: "Generate image. Do not rephrase the prompt." This further ensures our
239   prompts are conveyed to the T2I models accurately.
240   As shown in Table 1, we also replace specific keywords with generic words in the prompt to bypass
241   the block system, while preserving the overall description of the prompt. For example, we change
242   keywords like "Mickey Mouse" to a general word like "mouse" or "something" as demonstrated in
243   Figure 18b. Additionally, we employ an intention suffix prompt, which misleads the model into
244   thinking the user’s intent is to create their original works. Surprisingly, as shown in Figure 18c, this
245   increases the likelihood of the models committing violations.

      5                          C ONCLUSION
472   In this paper, we have demonstrated that commercial T2I systems currently underestimate the risk
473   of copyright infringement, even with simple prompts. Although several systems have implemented
474   internal censorship mechanisms to prevent such violations, our Automated Prompt Generation
475   Pipeline (APGP) easily circumvents these safeguards. The APGP utilizes a novel approach by
476
      integrating a self-generated QA score and a keyword penalty score within the LLM optimizer, without
      necessitating weight updates or gradient computations. Our empirical results show that APGP-
477
      generated prompts resulted in 73.3% content violations in ChatGPT, a model previously considered
478
      96.25% secure against copyright issues. We conclude that our approach not only streamlines the
479   process of red-teaming T2I models to expose risks at reduced costs but also aids intellectual property
480   owners in more effectively claiming their rights.
481
482
      L IMITATION
483   Our approach has the limitation that the violation rate does not always reproduce the same due to
484   the randomness of the commercial T2I systems. In addition, depending on the trial, content that was
485   blocked may be generated again or the prompt that was generated may be blocked in other trials.
      Thus, multiple trials can eventually generate all copyright content. Moreover, the results may change


                                                                                              9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      when the commercial T2I service is updated.1 Although our approach relies on non-deterministic
487   commercial T2I systems, we believe that the most significant contribution of this paper is to highlight
488   the risk of copyright infringement, which many commercial T2I systems currently violate. One of
489   the other limitations is that this paper analyzes copyright infringement from a technical point of
490   view, so we could not confirm the extent to which commercial systems actually cause copyright
491   infringement from a legal perspective. Despite the conduct of human evaluations, discrepancies may
492   arise between the views of non-experts participants who are lack of expertise in copyright and actual
493   legal judgments in court. However, we believe that this paper presents an opportunity for commercial
494   companies to reconsider legal perspectives in depth.
