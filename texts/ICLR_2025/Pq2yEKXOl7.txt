011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Foundation models have made incredible strides in achieving zero-shot or few-
014            shot generalization, leveraging prompt engineering to mimic the problem-solving
015
               approach of human intelligence. However, when it comes to some foundation
               models like Segment Anything, there is still a challenge in performing well under
016
               real-world shift. One of the real-world shift is the distribution shift, the out-of-
017
               distribution data, such as camouflaged and medical images. Another is inconsis-
018            tent prompting strategies during fine-tuning and testing, leading to decreased per-
019            formance. We draw inspiration from human intelligence, particularly the process
020            by which individuals decompose scenes into components in unfamiliar environ-
021            ments to determine the positions or boundaries of each component. To this end,
022            we introduce SlotSAM, a method that reconstructs features from the encoder in
023            a self-supervised manner to create object-centric representations. These represen-
024            tations are then integrated into the foundation model, bolstering its object-level
025            perceptual capabilities while reducing the impact of distribution-related variables.
026
               The beauty of SlotSAM lies in its simplicity and adaptability to various tasks,
               making it a versatile solution that significantly enhances the generalization abili-
027
               ties of foundation models. Through limited parameter fine-tuning in a bootstrap
028
               manner, our approach paves the way for improved generalization in novel envi-
029            ronments.

058
      The poor performance of current foundation models in unknown environments can be attributed to
      two types of real-world shifts. The first is distribution shift (Koh et al., 2021; Taori et al., 2020),
059
      which occurs when the data used for training (source domain) has a different distribution from the
060
      data encountered in downstream tasks during the actual application (target domain). The second
061   is prompt shift (Abdul Samadh et al., 2024; Zhou, 2018), where downstream tasks provide only
062   coarse weak supervision instead of the fine-grained labels available in the source domain.
063
064
      To address these challenges, we draw inspiration from the perceptual pipeline of humans, partic-
      ularly the process by which individuals decompose scenes into components in unfamiliar environ-
065
      ments to determine the positions or boundaries of each component. We aim to simulate human-like
066
      intelligence (Burns et al., 2023) by abstracting the real world at the object level and injecting this
067   capability into any foundation model. Object-centric learning (Locatello et al., 2020) operates based
068   on causal mechanisms that align with the physical world. By leveraging its combinatorial reason-
069   ing properties in scene comprehension, object-centric learning reduces reliance on domain-specific
070   variables and enables more robust handling of out-of-distribution data. However, applying Slot-
071   Attention (Locatello et al., 2020), the core technology of object-centric learning, to unsupervised
072   RGB pixel reconstruction in foundation models lacks meaningfulness for three reasons: (1) The
073   optimization objective of reconstructing the image itself lacks sufficient information for discerning
074   real-world objects, potentially leading to degradation, as shown in Figure 6. (2) The training of
075
      foundation models typically involves large-sized images, resulting in unacceptable resource over-
      head associated with Slot-Attention. (3) The injection of object-centric representations compatible
076
      with foundation models and the enhancement of their object perception capabilities warrant careful
077
      consideration.
078
079   Considering the aforementioned factors, our objective is to redefine the reconstruction target of Slot-
080   Attention as high-level features with stronger inductive biases. Since the encoder of the foundation
081
      model effectively extracts high-level semantics for each object within the image, it offers a uniform
      representation of the high-dimensional nature of the real world without being biased by pixel color
082
      reconstruction. After the acquisition of high-quality object-centric representations, they could be
083
      seamlessly integrated with existing tokens in most foundation models and can be considered as ob-
084   ject tokens. During the forward process, object tokens can leverage the attention mechanism among
085   tokens to access global image context, geometric region, semantic information, and mask regions.
086   This significantly enhances the foundation model’s object perception capabilities with minimal fine-
087   tuning parameters. As the entire process is unsupervised and reinforces the generalizability of the
088   foundation model, relying on its exceptional feature representation, we define it as bootstrapping.
089   Our contributions can be summarized as follows:
090
091          • We propose SlotSAM to obtain high-quality representations from foundation model and
092
               innovatively project them as object tokens that can be seamlessly integrated.
093          • We utilize object tokens and attention mechanisms to access the global context, geometry,
094            and semantics of the image, injecting object-centric perceptual ability into the foundation
095            model with only minimal parameter adjustments.
096          • In real-world scenarios, including distribution shift and prompt shift, SlotSAM signifi-
097            cantly enhances segmentation accuracy across various downstream tasks, facilitating the
098            safe deployment of the foundation model in the open world.

      4.1     S ETUP
233
234
      Benchmarks. We conduct evaluations under real-world shifts (distribution and prompt shift).
235
236   Distribution shift: The training dataset for SAM from the source domain is SAIB, primarily collected
237   from natural environments. We select four types of real-world downstream task datasets as the target
238
      domain. Among them, the distribution shift is small for the natural image datasets VOC (Ke et al.,
      2024) and COCO (Ke et al., 2024), and the robotic image dataset OCID (Ke et al., 2024). In contrast,
239
      the distribution shift is significant for the medical dataset ISIC (Ke et al., 2024), PolyP (Ke et al.,
240
      2024), and the camouflaged object image dataset CAMO (Ke et al., 2024). The division of the
241   training and test sets follows previous work (Das et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024),
242   with fine-tuning performed on the training set and the test set used for performance evaluation.
243
244
      Prompt shift: Fine-tuning is weakly supervised, meaning that the available labels are incomplete,
      which is different from the fine mask labels used in the training of the foundation model and is
245
      considered as prompt shift. Consistent with existing work, we obtain the minimal bounding box
246
      that completely encompasses the instance segmentation mask, which serves as the box prompt. The
247   point prompt is generated by randomly selecting five positive points within the ground-truth instance
248   segmentation mask and five negative points outside it. A coarse segmentation mask is simulated by
249   fitting a polygon around the ground-truth mask, where the number of vertices is determined as P/20,
250   with P representing the mask’s perimeter. The minimum number of vertices required is three.
251
      Evaluation Protocols. We present the mean Intersection over Union (mIoU) as the primary evalua-
252
      tion metric. For each input prompt, the mIoU is calculated by comparing the ground-truth segmen-
253   tation mask with the predicted mask. The final mIoU is obtained by averaging the IoU values across
254   all instances.
255
256
      Implementation Details. We utilize the ViT-B (Alexey, 2020) as the image encoder of SAM. The
      standard prompt encoderis employed in SlotSAM. Throughout all experiments, the LoRA module
257
      of the image encoder and MLP are fine-tuned using the Adam optimizer. We configure a batch size
258
      of 4 on four NVIDIA RTX4090 GPUs and set the learning rate to 0.0001 along with a weight decay
259   of 0.0001. The rank of the LoRA module is 4. The number of epochs for fine-tuning with Lbase is
260   consistent with previous work (Zhang et al., 2024), with 400 epochs used for slots acquisition, and
261   20 epochs for slots injection. We set the number of object tokens No and the number of slots K to
262   1 and 8 respectively.

505
506   5.2   O BJECT-C ENTRIC L EARNING
507
508
      Object-Centric Learning is revolutionizing the field of computer vision with its ability to perceive
509
      and understand scenes in terms of discrete objects. Slot-Attention (Locatello et al., 2020) intro-
510
      duces the concept of “slots” that can bind to any object in the input through a competitive attention
511   process. It laid the foundation for subsequent innovations in object-centric representation learn-
512   ing. SlotLifter (Liu et al., 2024) addresses the challenge of 3D scene understanding, it aggregates
513   multi-view features for decoding via slot-guided feature lifting, significantly advancing in scene de-
514   composition and novel-view synthesis. An advancement (Jia et al., 2022) proposes a model that
515   utilizes learnable queries to initialize Slot-Attention learning, demonstrating potential for concept
516   binding and generalization. Some works (Elsayed et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2023) focus on object
517   tracking, introducing an index merge module and a memory module to address the challenges of
518   object fragmentation and temporal consistency. An interesting approach (Wang et al., 2024) offers a
519
      novel perspective, using cyclic walks between parts of an object to enhance object-centric learning,
      providing a new way to handle object fragments and their reassembly. EAGLE (Kim et al., 2024)
520
      introduces a method for unsupervised semantic segmentation that learns object-centric representa-
521
      tions without manual annotations, significantly reducing the reliance on annotated data. SlotSAM
522   differs from existing research that solely investigates whether slots can effectively represent objects.
523   We examine how foundation models perceive and interpret visual data, employing cross-distribution
524   invariant object representations to enhance model generalization performance and facilitate secure
525   deployment in open-world environments.

531
      In this work, we introduce SlotSAM, a novel approach to enhance the generalization of segmen-
532
      tation foundation models under distribution and prompt shifts. Drawing inspiration from human
533
      object-centric perception, SlotSAM employs self-supervised learning to extract and integrate high-
534   quality object-centric representations, bolstering the model’s ability to perceive and segment objects
535   in varied environments. Our method’s effectiveness is underscored by its superior performance over
536   existing state-of-the-art methods across diverse downstream tasks. The simplicity and versatility of
537   SlotSAM make it readily integrated into various foundation models, marking a significant stride in
538   enhancing their generalization capabilities. SlotSAM exemplifies the potential of human-inspired
539   learning to advance machine perception, marking an important step toward developing models ca-
      pable of safely and effectively navigating open-world scenarios with limited supervision.
