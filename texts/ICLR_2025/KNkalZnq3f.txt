011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Natural language processing evaluation has made significant progress, largely
014            driven by the proliferation of powerful large language models (LLMs). New eval-
015            uation benchmarks are of increasing priority as the reasoning capabilities of LLMs
016
               are expanding at a rapid pace. In particular, while multi-document (MD) reasoning
               is an area of extreme relevance given LLM capabilities in handling longer-context
017
               inputs, few benchmarks exist to rigorously examine model behavior in this setting.
018
               Moreover, the multi-document setting is historically challenging for benchmark
019            creation due to the expensive cost of annotating long inputs.
020            In this work, we introduce MDBench, a new dataset for evaluating LLMs on the
021            task of multi-document reasoning. Notably, MDBench is created through a novel
022            synthetic generation process, allowing us to controllably and efficiently gener-
023            ate challenging document sets and the corresponding question-answer (QA) ex-
024            amples. Our novel technique operates on condensed structured seed knowledge,
025            modifying it through LLM-assisted edits to induce MD-specific reasoning chal-
026            lenges. We then convert this structured knowledge into a natural text surface form,
027
               generating a document set and corresponding QA example. We analyze the be-
               havior of popular LLMs and prompting techniques, finding that MDBench poses
028
               significant challenges for all methods, even with relatively short document sets.
029
               We also see our knowledge-guided generation technique (1) allows us to read-
030            ily perform targeted analysis of MD-specific reasoning capabilities and (2) can be
031            adapted quickly to account for new challenges and future modeling improvements.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   The rapid advancements in natural language processing (NLP) have been largely driven by the devel-
037   opment and deployment of large language models (LLMs). These models have showcased remark-
038   able improvements in various tasks, including understanding, generating, and reasoning over text.
039   However, despite these advancements, evaluation frameworks for NLP systems have struggled to
040   keep pace (Chang et al., 2024), notably for tasks involving reasoning over multiple documents (Mavi
041   et al., 2024).
042   Multi-document (MD) reasoning involves synthesizing and inferring information across multiple
043   diverse texts (Caciularu et al., 2021), posing unique challenges not addressed by traditional single-
044   document benchmarks. While LLMs are increasingly capable of handling longer-context multi-
045   document inputs, there is a scarcity of benchmarks that rigorously examine the specific reasoning
046   characteristics that are prominent in this setting. In addition, many existing benchmarks consist of
047   static, hand-crafted datasets, which are labor-intensive to produce. These datasets are often suscepti-
048   ble to data contamination (Xu et al., 2024) over time, e.g., LLMs are exposed to public benchmarks
049   during training. This can compromise the integrity of the evaluation.
050   In this work, we address these limitations with MDBench, a benchmark using a novel generation
051   technique for multi-document reasoning evaluation. Our benchmark is generated through a syn-
052   thetic process that leverages structured knowledge as seed information. This process uses a strong
053   LLM (GPT-4o) to augment structured knowledge by injecting complexities that require advanced
      reasoning skills, then generates text documents from the augmented knowledge.

054
      Our benchmark generation pipeline begins with a structured knowledge source serving as the seed
055   information. Each knowledge entry (i.e., row of the table) encapsulates distinct knowledge that
056   forms the basis of a document in the generated set. We follow a three-step augmentation process to
057   source knowledge, augment knowledge, and generate document sets with multi-document reasoning
058   challenges:
059
060         1. Source Seed Knowledge: We collect tabular data where each row contains information
061            that will contribute to a generated document.
062         2. Augment Knowledge: Using a powerful LLM, we edit the structured knowledge to inject
063            challenging reasoning dependencies and enrich the context for document creation. By
064            treating rows as proxies for documents, we model cross-document dependencies through
065            cross-row knowledge interactions. In this step, we also generate question-answer pairs that
066            utilize the introduced reasoning dependencies.
067         3. Generate Natural Text: We map the augmented knowledge into natural text by generating
068            a corresponding multi-document set from the augmented table. This process allows us
069            to systematically inject critical reasoning challenges while producing examples that are
070            realistic and fluent.
071
072   We produce a substantial number of multi-document QA examples using this pipeline (300 human-
073
      validated, and 700 more automatically-validated for quality) and evaluate the performance of models
      from several prominent LLM families including GPT, Claude, Gemini, and Llama. We find that:
074
075
             • MDBench poses a strong challenge, even for state-of-the-art methods, with the best ones
076            achieving ∼59% performance on this MD reasoning task.
077
078
             • Frontier models such as GPT-4o and Claude Sonnet significantly outperform smaller LLMs
               across different prompting methods. This highlights the importance of model capacity and
079
               sophistication in handling complex multi-document reasoning tasks.
080
081          • When comparing performance on document reasoning versus tabular reasoning (i.e., struc-
082
               tured format pre-document generation), we find that strong models are mostly performant
               in both settings. However, smaller models struggle more in the long-form document set-
083
               ting. This suggests that multi-document reasoning is influenced by both the fundamental
084
               reasoning complexity, and also from the nuances of the surface form.
085
086          • Prompting techniques such as Chain-of-Thought (Wei et al., 2022) can improve perfor-
087
               mance across strong models. However, they are insufficient to significantly enhance the
               performance of weaker models like Llama3-7B and GPT-3.5. This indicates that while
088
               prompting strategies can aid reasoning, underlying model capabilities remain a limiting
089
               factor for this task, which makes MDBench suitable for future, advanced model evaluation.

092   2   R ELATED W ORK
093
094   Evaluating the capabilities of LLMs is a critical aspect of NLP research. As LLMs continue to
095   improve rapidly, existing evaluation frameworks often lag behind, particularly in assessing complex
096
      reasoning abilities such as multi-document (MD) reasoning. As LLMs rapidly increase in reasoning
      capacity, there is a pressing need to develop evaluation methods that can capture these higher-order
097
      reasoning skills.
098
099
      Multi-Document Reasoning MD reasoning involves synthesizing and inferring information
100
      across multiple texts. Existing work in this area includes datasets targeting specific phenomena such
101
      as temporal reasoning (Xiong et al., 2024; Wan, 2007), summarization (Xiao et al., 2021; Peper et al.,
102   2023; Lior et al., 2024), multi-hop question answering (Yang et al., 2018; Qi et al., 2021; Trivedi
103   et al., 2022) and ambiguous entity resolution (Lee et al., 2024). Notably, many of these MD datasets
104   are publicly-sourced and often reliant on significant human effort to curate For example, Zhu et al.
105   (2024) introduce FanOutQA, a recent multi-hop, multi-document question answering dataset, which
106   targeted decomposable QA examples sourced from public Wikipedia knowledge and relied on thou-
107   sands of manual annotations. Our work seeks to use knowledge-controlled generation to offer a
      scalable alternative for producing nuanced and unseen multi-document reasoning examples.

                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Tabular Reasoning with LLMs LLMs have demonstrated strong performance in tasks involving
109   structured knowledge, such as tabular data or knowledge bases (Lu et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023a).
110   Recent studies have observed success in applying LLMs to table reasoning, manipulation, and aug-
111   mentation (Lu et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023a). While there are limitations in LLM pre-training which
112   can lead to formatting sensitivities and limitations with handling large tables, Nahid & Rafiei (2024)
113   find improved performance by decomposing the tabular knowledge into a digestible size. Similarly,
114   leveraging tabular knowledge within reasoning chains allows for compact and effective represen-
115   tation of complex problems, as explored in the Chain-of-Tables framework (Wang et al., 2024).
116   These insights highlight the potential of using condensed knowledge as a foundation for generating
117
      challenging reasoning tasks.
118
119   LLM-Supported Synthetic Benchmark Creation To address the need for more dynamic eval-
120   uation datasets, LLM-powered synthetic benchmark creation has gained significant traction (Long
121
      et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024; Li et al., 2023b), particularly as there is growing concern of bench-
      mark data contamination Xu et al. (2024) Some work has been done in the multi-document setting,
122
      although automation is largely used for extending existing annotated multi-document benchmarks
123
      to more complex tasks (Schnitzler et al., 2024). While not directly modeling multi-document tasks,
124   Sprague et al. (2023) explore synthetic generation in the related multi-step reasoning setting, using a
125   neurosymbolic generation algorithm which maps synthetic structure into natural text examples. Our
126   method seeks to build off related work in synthetic generation to address efficient multi-document
127   benchmark creation.

      3     MDB ENCH G ENERATION P IPELINE
131
132
      In this section, we motivate and overview the generation process, and provide details on the compo-
      nents and steps taken to produce the MDBench evaluation benchmark.
133
134
135   3.1   B ENCHMARK G ENERATION G OALS
136          • Contain Novel and Unseen Text: We aim to produce examples that are not merely scraped
137            from public datasets but rather contain newly-generated content. This ensures that models
138            are tested on scenarios they have not encountered during training, avoiding overfitting to
139            pre-existing benchmarks.
140          • Contains Cross-Document Knowledge Dependencies: A key focus is to produce exam-
141            ples that require reasoning across multiple documents. We design our benchmark to have
142            intentional cross-document dependencies, making them particularly challenging, testing
143            multi-document reasoning capabilities.
144
             • Grounded in Real-World Scenarios: Even though the examples are synthetically gen-
145
               erated, they should ideally remain grounded in real-world concepts and situations. This
146            ensures that the reasoning challenges presented are realistic and relevant to practical NLP
147            applications.
148
             • Counterfactual Alterations: To further mitigate data contamination and leakage risks
149
               from public sources, we incorporate slight counterfactual or fictional twists on real-world
150
               scenarios. This allows for a fresh take on familiar domains while maintaining the integrity
151            of the benchmark.
152
153
             • Scalability and Control: Our approach is designed to offer control during benchmark
               generation. We allow one to specify seed information such as domain and behavior types,
154
               and can control the complexity and nature of the reasoning tasks present in the benchmark.
155

187   Step 1: Obtaining Seed Knowledge We start with the intuition that compressed structured knowl-
188   edge provides an effective foundation for multi-document reasoning. Several valid sources of this
189   exist, such as knowledge bases, tabular information, or even by performing information extraction
190   to consolidate data from existing documents and text corpora. For the MDBench benchmark, we uti-
191   lize the TabFact (Chen et al., 2020) dataset, which comprises 16,000 tables sourced from Wikipedia.
192
      Our motivation for exploring this dataset is threefold: (1) TabFact tables provide a reliable and cu-
      rated source of seed knowledge (2) the data spans a wide range of domains, including news, sports,
193
      media, and technology, and (3) has an emphasis on human-readability both in scale and content.
194
      This structured knowledge serves as the starting point for our knowledge augmentation process,
195   which significantly transforms the raw data into more challenging and complex reasoning tasks. We
196   heuristically filter the dataset to select tables that are rich in content yet manageable in size, choosing
197   those with 5 to 15 rows and 3 to 8 columns.
198
199
      Step 2: Knowledge Augmentation An important component of our technique is the knowledge
200   augmentation step. This step modifies information, applying operations that inject complex knowl-
201   edge dependencies and reasoning challenges. Figure 1 overviews our pipeline, while full detailed
202   examples of the knowledge augmentation prompts are provided in Appendix A.
203
204             • Multi-document Reasoning Demonstrations Prior to altering the existing information we
205               first demonstrate relevant skills for multi-document reasoning. Each skill is demonstrated
206               in both ‘simple’ and ‘challenging’ forms. The demonstrations include examples, along
207               with explanations and rationales for solving them. For the purpose of this benchmark,
208               we define and emphasize five reasoning components which are particularly relevant in the
209               multi-document setting. For each skill we demonstrate both a simple and more complex
210
                  example, each highlighting the relevant reasoning. We describe these skills in Table 2.
211             • Knowledge Augmentation Demonstrations In addition to demonstrating relevant reason-
212               ing skills, we next provide knowledge edit demonstrations. These demonstrations illustrate
213               plans for how simple tables can be enhanced to form nuanced QA examples. Each demon-
214               stration consists of an initial table, a series of edits, and a resultant augmented table and
215               QA annotation. When performing knowledge augmentation, we provide one demonstration
                  from a small set of high-quality curated examples.

252   Step 3: Document Set Generation Once the tabular knowledge has been augmented, we map
253   this information into natural language text; each row in the table is used to generate a document,
254   with the augmented knowledge ensuring that reasoning across documents (rows) is required to solve
255   the accompanying QA task. We independently generate each document, the generation prompt
256   parameterized by the following components: (1) the augmented table and title, (2) the column names
257
      and (3) a specific row of content within the table indicated for generation. Iterating this process over
      all n rows in the table, we generate an n-document set. This approach of knowledge-grounded
258
      generation ensures the generated document set maintains logical coherence while presenting unique
259
      cross-document reasoning challenges.

303
      To assess the challenges of MDBench, we test the performance of many popular LLMs in com-
304
      bination with conventional prompting setups. Concretely, we test open-source LLMs with Meta’s
305
      Llama-3 (Dubey et al., 2024), using the 8B-Instruct and 70B-Instruct variants. For API-based pro-
306   prietary models, we use models from the popular Anthropic Claude, OpenAI GPT, and Google
307   Gemini model familes, which represent the state-of-the-art in LLM performance. For Claude, we
308   use Claude-3-Opus-20240229 and Claude-3.5-Sonnet-202406201 . For GPT we use GPT-3.5-turbo-

310   0514 (Team et al., 2024).
311
      We explore both zero-shot and one-shot QA prompting scenarios, noting that when prompting in the
312   one-shot case we use a single representative demonstration across models for consistency. We use a
313   conventional question-answering prompt, and also further instruct the models to ‘think step by step’
314   to additionally produce Chain-of-Thought (CoT) rationales. Examples of these prompt formats are
315   provided in Appendix B. To evaluate on the QA task, we use GPT-4o as a reference-based scorer,
316   first parsing the final answer from each output, then comparing the similarity of the predicted answer
317   with the ground-truth answer (conditioned on the original question). We calculate both an exact
318   match score as well as an accuracy score, where the scorer can assign partial correctness credit on a
319   1-10 scale.

434   In this work, we present MDBench, a novel benchmark designed to evaluate large language models
435   on multi-document reasoning tasks. By leveraging structured seed knowledge and augmenting it
436   with nuanced reasoning dependencies, MDBench enables the systematic development of challeng-
437   ing, multi-document QA examples and addresses key challenges in traditional benchmark creation,
438   including issues related to data contamination and the difficulty of efficiently generating diverse
439   reasoning examples. Our work introduces a new method for probing complex cross-document rea-
440   soning, paving the way for more rigorous evaluation of models’ abilities to handle real-world, multi-
441   source information, and advancing the development of LLMs capable of deeper, contextually aware
