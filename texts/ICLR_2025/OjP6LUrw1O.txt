011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Modern Large Language Models (LLMs) are composed of matrices with billions
014            of elements, making their storage and processing quite demanding in terms of
015
               computational resources and memory usage. Being significantly large, such ma-
               trices can often be expressed in low-rank format with potential to relax resource
016
               requirements. Unlike prior works which focus on developing novel matrix de-
017
               composition algorithms, in this work we first study the emergence of low-rank
018            structures across matrices within different layers of LLMs and establish a con-
019            sequential relationship between the gradient dynamics and emerging low-rank
020            expressiveness of matrices. Our findings reveal that different layers exhibit varying
021            levels of converged low-rank structure, necessitating a non-uniform rank reduction
022            across them to minimize performance drop due to compression. In view of that,
023            we present Weight Low-Rank Projection (WeLore) that unifies weight compres-
024            sion and memory-efficient fine-tuning as ONE, in a data-agnostic and one-shot
025            way. WeLore capitalizes the heavy-tail distribution of singular values to identify
026
               a suitable rank reduction ratio for matrices within LLMs. Going beyond only
               as a compression technique, WeLore categorizes weight matrices into Low-rank
027
               Components (LRCs) and Non-Low-rank Components (N-LRCs) based on their
028
               ability to express themselves as low-rank. Our gradient perspective and extensive
029            experiments illustrate that LRCs tend to have better finetuning capabilities and can
030            closely mimic (sometimes outperform) the training loss trajectory and performance
031            of full-finetuning with notable memory and compute footprint reduction. For
032            example, finetuning a 50% compressed LLaMa-2 7B model using only a fraction
033            of parameters in LRCs (WeLore) can outperform its full finetuning with ∼3×
034            better throughput and ∼0.6× GPU requirement.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
038
039
      In the modern era of deep learning, observing low-rank structures across gigantic matrices is common.
      Over the decades, low-rank structures have been notably useful and ubiquitous across numerous
040
      applications, such as image and data compression (Lingala et al., 2011; Arif et al., 2019; Yu et al.,
041
      2014), deep neural network compression (Hsu et al., 2022; Kaushal et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023;
042   Jaiswal et al., 2023a; Wang et al., 2023), and recently for fine-tuning large language models (LLMs)
043   (Hu et al., 2021; Dettmers et al., 2024; Meng et al., 2024; Biderman et al., 2024; Lialin et al., 2023).
044   The storage efficiency and fine-tuning memory footprints associated with the large matrices of LLMs
045   are currently prohibitive to unlocking the full potential of lightweight domain-specific applications
046   around them. For example, regular 16-bit fine-tuning of a LLaMA-65B parameter model requires
047   more than 780 GB of GPU memory (Dettmers et al., 2024), and the VRAM consumption for training
048   GPT-3 175B reaches 1.2TB (Meng et al., 2024).
049   In recent efforts to address storage demands and computational complexity linked to the large matrices
050   of LLMs, several works have been exploring the low-rank characteristics associated with weights and
051   gradients (Zhao et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2021; Hsu et al., 2022; Kaushal et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023;
052   Wang et al., 2023; Meng et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024). One primary limitation of the existing works
053   is an under-explored assumption of the uniform existence of low-rank structures across the pre-trained
      weights, with a main focus on developing matrix factorization techniques for LLM compression.

066   Recently, (Sharma et al., 2023) interestingly found that it is often possible to significantly improve
067   the performance of LLMs by selectively removing higher-order components of their weight matrices.
068
      In this work, we first explore how the low-rank structure emerges and differs across weight matrices
069
      corresponding to different Attention and MLP layers within transformer blocks of LLMs. Motivated
070   by the findings of Galore (Zhao et al., 2024), which establish that gradients during the pretraining of
071   LLMs become low-rank, our work makes an effort to understand how the gradient behavior changes
072   over time during LLM pretraining and attempts to establish a consequential relationship between the
073   emergence of low-rank weight subspace and gradient subspace.
074
      Weight Low-Rank Subspace through the Lens of Gradient Behaviour: Recently, GaLore (Zhao
075
      et al., 2024) theoretically argues that the gradient matrix becomes low-rank during training but does
076   not establish how the gradient behavior accumulates in the weight space. Moreover, it provides
077   no distinct consideration on training dynamics of different layers (e.g., attention, MLP) across
078   transformers blocks in LLMs. To this end, we first carefully investigated the gradient behavior of
079   all weight matrices during back-propagation starting with random initialization (usually full-rank)
080   during full pretraining. We found that gradient matrices of some layers (e.g., majority of middle
081   MLP matrices) saturate significantly within a short span of training iterations. On the other hand,
082   gradients for some weight matrices (e.g., attention matrices from the first and last transformer blocks)
083   continuously carry rich error signals from training data and develop low-rank gradient subspace
084
      throughout the training. We conjecture that as a consequence of the cumulative accumulation
      of gradients within a low-rank gradient subspace, the corresponding weight matrices exhibit the
085
      emergence of high-quality stable low-rank subspace. Our study found that different layers within
086
      an LLM pose varying levels of converged low-rank structure, which should be accounted for during
087   low-rank decomposition.
088
089   This new gradient perspective into nonuniform weight ranks unfolds several interesting dimensions:
090
                               • Weight matrices corresponding to different layers across transformer blocks can be broadly
091
                                 categorized as: 1 Low-rank Components (LRCs) that exhibit high-quality low-rank struc-
092                              ture (can be estimated by heavy-tail in sorted singular values obtained with SVD) and their
093                              gradients can carry rich error signals from data; 2 Non-Low-rank Components (N-LRCs)
094                              with non-converged low-rank structure (missing heavy-tail in singular values distribution)
095                              and cannot be low-rank factorized without introducing noticeable reconstruction error.
096                            • It provides us a unique opportunity to unify weight compression and memory-efficient fine-
097                              tuning (MEFT) as ONE: (a) compression angle: LRCs with stabilized low-rank weight
098                              structure can be factorized by SVD to significantly high compression ratio; and (b) MEFT
099                              angle: when fine-tuning, we back-propagate only over LRCs in their low-rank decomposed
100                              format to make the most effective gradient progress while leaving N-LRCs frozen.

      in LRCs and N-LRCs. More specifically, to achieve a target rank reduction ratio, we exploit the
104
      heavy tail property of normalized singular values of weight matrices factorized using SVD1 . LRCs

155   The continuous growth in scale of LLMs is making the computational and memory costs of inference
156   and finetuning them notably prohibitive. Finetuning LLMs has recently been very successful in
157   boosting their capabilities to follow instructions, adopting response-generating style, and limiting
158
      undesirable behaviors like hallucination, generating toxic contents, etc. To enable the democratization
      of these abilities with consumer-grade GPUs, enormous efforts are directed toward LLM compression
159
160   performance (Table 2). However, we intentionally focus on simple SVD at weight space to overcome the
161   high sensitivity of activation-based SVD on calibration datasets along with facilitating ease in system-level
      implementation (Chavan et al., 2023).

171   structures within gigantic matrices in LLMs. In addition, they fail to explore their emergence and

224   LLMs are omnipresent and recently the race of scaling them have attributed to gigantic computational
225   and memory footprints. Among numerous efforts towards democratization for consumer-grade
226   GPUs, low-rank decomposition of pretrained weights as a product of two smaller dense matrices
227
      receives special attention because it can leverage the highly optimized floating-point dense matrix
      multiplication kernels unlike sparsity and quantization which require specialized kernels to be written,
228
      often different for each hardware backend in order to enable speedup. Recently, several works (Hsu
229
      et al., 2022; Kaushal et al., 2023; Yuan et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023; Saha et al., 2023; Wang et al.,
230   2024) have explored matrix factorization of LLMs’ pretrained weights. We found that these works
231   primarily focus on improving SVD using more informative signals like activation, fisher information
232   and applying it unilaterally (same rank reduction ratio) across all the weights. As discussed in previous
233   section, low-rank emergence varies significantly across candidate weights in a pretrained checkpoint.
234   To this end, we pose an under-explored question: How can we carefully curate a layer-adaptive rank
235   reduction ratios for all layers in the pretrained checkpoint?

294   4                           M EMORY-E FFICIENT L OW-R ANK A MICABLE F INETUNING
295
296   Parameter-Efficient finetuning techniques (PEFT) which enable LLMs to perform a new task with
297   minimal updates has received enormous attention to their ability to allow fine-tuned by only updating
298   a small number parameters. Unlike LoRA and its varients which finetune a small added fraction of
299   parameters to original pretrained weight checkpoints not relevant to original pretraining optimization,
300
      WeLore provides an alternative approach by capitalizing the gradient perspective to select a small
      fraction of weights from the pretrained model which can undergo fine-tuning. As discussed above,
301
      LRCs exhibits low-rank structure with rich gradient dynamics while N-LRCs can’t be well-expressed
302

358   Network Architectures: For understanding gradient dynamics and its consequent on the weight
359   space during pretraining, we adopt the LLaMa-130M architecture following (Lialin et al., 2023; Zhao
360   et al., 2024). For our continual and downstream finetuning experiments, we adopted the pretrained
361   checkpoint of LLaMa-2 7B, LLaMa-2 13B and Mistral-7B6 from HuggingFace.
362   Low Rank Compression: For low-rank compression using WeLore for LLaMa-2 7B and 13B
363   models, we used torch.svd(Wl ) to decompose a layer l’s weight matrix Wlm×n = Am×r B r×n
364   where r is decided by the heavy tail distribution of the singular values of W as described in Section
365   3. If W belongs to LRCs, it will be replaced with a composition of two linear layers with low-rank
366   matrices A & B to improve the computational efficiency. For baselines, we compared with commonly
367   used uniform rank reduction (Hsu et al., 2022; Kaushal et al., 2023) and adopted recently proposed
368
      outlier-weighed non-uniform ratio (OWL) (Yin et al., 2023). We additionally augmented activation-
      guided SVD techniques (Yuan et al., 2023) with WeLore’s adaptive layer-wise rank reduction ratio to

      understand how it can benefit them.

      compressed LLaMa-2 7B and 13B models at different compression ratios using C4 dataset. The
      C4 dataset is a massive collection of Common Crawl’s web crawl corpus, meticulously filtered
373
      and cleaned to ensure high-quality language modeling and training. For downstream task finetun-
374
      ing of compressed models, we consider a good mixture of tasks from commonsense reasoning

      6                     C ONCLUSION
531
      We study the emergence of non-uniform low-rank structures across different layers of transformer
532
      blocks from gradient behavior perspective. We present WeLore, an adaptive layer-wise low-rank
533
      compression strategy for low-rank decomposition which can achieve high compression ratio with
534   minimal drop in performance. The unique proposition of WeLore lies in categorizing weight matrices
535   of pretrained models into two borad categories - LRCs and N-LRCs based on their ability to express
536   themselves as low-rank. We conducted extensive experiments to validate that LRCs pose better
537   trainability than N-LRCs. Given limited compute & memory budget, WeLore recommends finetuning
538   LRCs while keeping N-LRCs frozen with back-propagation for maximal gain (sometimes better than
539   full-finetuning). The primary limitation of our work remains limited exploration for only the LLaMa
      family of models and unexplored benefits of WeLore for training LLMs from scratch.

      A.1    BACKGROUND W ORK
705
706   Memory-Efficient Finetuning: Memory-efficient fine-tuning of LLMs aims to address the signifi-
707   cant costs associated with their fine-tuning. This field encompasses several notable techniques. For
708   instance, Prompt Learning Methods optimize input tokens or embedding while keeping the model’s
709   remaining parameters static Hambardzumyan et al. (2021); Zhong et al. (2021). Layer-freezing tech-
710   niques enhance training efficiency by selectively freezing certain layers Liu et al. (2021); Brock et al.
711
      (2017); Li et al. (2024). Additionally, Adapter Methods introduce a small, update-focused auxiliary
      module into the model’s architecture, significantly reducing the number of trainable parameters, as
712
      introduced by Houlsby et al. (2019); Diao et al. (2022). Among them, one noteworthy technique
713
      is Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021) and its successors (Renduchintala et al., 2023;
714   Sheng et al., 2023; Xia et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023; Hayou et al., 2024; Hao et al., 2024; Liu et al.,
715   2024), which introduces a low-rank weight adapter for each layer to reduce the memory footprint by
716   only optimizing the adapter. These low-rank adapters can then be seamlessly merged back into the
717   original model.
718
      Unlike LoRA which performs proxy optimization over additional parameters while keeping the
719
      original parameters frozen, WeLore backed by an understanding of gradient dynamics suggests
720   finetuning the original parameters of LRCs in represented in low-rank to mimic full-finetuning.
721   Recently, (Biderman et al., 2024) found that full finetuning is more accurate and sample-efficient
722   than LoRA across several task categories and WeLore can be an effective alternative to achieve the
723   benefits of full-finetuning within a limited compute and memory budget.
724
      Low Rank Compression: Large Language Models (LLMs) have succeeded remarkably across
725
      various natural language processing tasks. However, the massive scale of these models poses
726   significant challenges in terms of storage efficiency and computational complexity. Among several
727   techniques of LLM compression (e.g.,pruning, quantization, etc.), low-rank decomposition which
728   retains only the top-k components in the low-rank space have special privilege to leverage the existing
729   highly efficient kernels over floating point matrices. (Hsu et al., 2022) developed a data-aware
730   modification of SVD that incorporates approximate second-order gradient information. Similarly,
731   (Yuan et al., 2023) proposed a data-aware decomposition method that minimizes activation error. One
732   primary drawback of these reductions is that they uniformly reduce rank across all weight matrices.
733   In contrast, our work experimentally validates existence of non-uniform low-rank expressiveness
734
      across different layers and should be accounted for during low-rank compression. Recently, (Zhao
      et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023) found that dynamic rank selection during pretraining can achieve
735
      comparable prediction performance as full-rank counterpart.
