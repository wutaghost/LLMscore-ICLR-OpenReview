009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               Reinforcement Learning with Human Feedback (RLHF) has gained significant at-
012
               tention for aligning AI behavior with human preferences. Self-play style RLHF
013
               has shown strong advantages, as highlighted by many studies. However, cur-
014            rent self-play style RLHF approaches face several limitations, including the lack
015            of provable sample efficiency, absence of active exploration, and limited diver-
016            sity in training data. To address these challenges, we propose a novel RLHF
017            framework that balances exploration and exploitation while providing theoreti-
018            cal guarantees. We introduce Two-Agent Nash Policy Optimization (TANPO) as
019            an equivalent and easy-to-implement two-agent algorithm building on this frame-
020            work. In TANPO, the two players are trained using different loss functions to
021            ensure more diverse and informative data collection. We also propose Single-
022
               Agent Diversity-driven Optimization (SADPO), a single-agent approximation of
               TANPO, supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical evidence. Our the-
023
               oretical analysis shows that our theoretical algorithm framework enjoys sublin-
024
               ear regret under general function approximation and mild structural conditions,
025            with a detailed analysis provided for the linear case. Empirically, we implement
026            TANPO and SADPO using Zephyr-7B-SFT as our base model, outperforming
027            several baselines across multiple evaluation benchmarks, such as AlpacaEval 2.0,
028            MT-Bench and various standard academic benchmarks. Our experiments also
029            show that TANPO improves performance on AlpacaEval 2.0 over extended train-
030            ing epochs, demonstrating its ability to consistently improve and reduce overfit-
031            ting.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036
037   Large Language Models (LLMs) have shown significant proficiency in understanding and generat-
038   ing natural language. Reinforcement Learning with Human Feedback (RLHF) is key to improving
039
      LLMs by directly integrating human feedback into their training (Christiano et al., 2017; Ziegler
      et al., 2019). This process typically involves training the model using reinforcement learning tech-
040
      niques to maximize the reward from human-labeled data (Ouyang et al., 2022). Many RLHF algo-
041
      rithms treat this setup as a contextual bandit problem, where the prompt corresponds to the state, the
042   generated response represents the action, and the feedback received acts as the reward.
043
044   Another approach, in addition to modeling RLHF as a contextual bandit problem, is to utilize self-
045
      play methods. Self-play is a technique in which one or more agents learn by competing against
      themselves, allowing them to iteratively improve their strategies by evaluating and adapting to their
046
      own responses. This approach has proven to be a powerful method in various fields, including
047
      traditional reinforcement learning such as AlphaGo Zero (Silver et al., 2017) and generative models
048   such as GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2020). In the context of RLHF, self-play algorithms typically
049   involve one or multiple agents generating multiple responses for each prompt. These responses
050   compete against each other through feedback provided either by human evaluators or AI annotators,
051   which is then used to train the agents. Recently, a line of works (Rosset et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024;
052   Ye et al., 2024; Munos et al., 2023) have proposed a variety of self-play style RLHF algorithms. The
053   goal of these algorithms is to find the Nash equilibrium strategy. These works have demonstrated
      the effectiveness of self-play algorithms in improving LLM performance.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      Despite recent advances, there are several limitations in current self-play style RLHF approaches.
055   First, there lacks theoretical guarantee on how these practical self-play algorithms can approximate
056   Nash equilibrium, or there is a significant gap between theoretically guaranteed algorithms and
057   practical implementations. Second, most existing algorithms lack active online exploration, which
058   limits their ability to efficiently gather informative data during the learning process. Theoretically,
059   active exploration can provide formal guarantees for learning efficiency (Xiong et al., 2023; Ye et al.,
060   2024). Practically, it enhances model performance by ensuring that the training data remains diverse
061   and novel, leading to better generalization and improved model performance (Zhang et al., 2024;
062   Xie et al., 2024).
063   Therefore, a key research problem is: Can we design an easy-to-implement and provably efficient
064   self-play RLHF algorithm that approximates Nash equilibrium with active exploration?
065
      In this paper, we propose a new self-play RLHF algorithm. To effectively balance the trade-off
066
      between exploration and exploitation, the max-player aims to maximize the summation of (i) the
067   expected Nash equilibrium value function and (ii) the negative estimation loss of that reward func-
068   tion. Similarly, the min-player seeks to maximize the summation of (i) the expected best response
069   value function based on the max-playerтАЩs strategy and (ii) the negative estimation loss of that reward
070   function. We provide theoretical guarantee for this framework, showing that it achieves a sublinear
071   regret under mild structural conditions.
072
      We demonstrate that, under certain conditions, our algorithm framework is equivalent to an easy-
073   to-implement algorithm. In this practical implementation, the max-player optimizes an MLE loss,
074   while the min-player optimizes an MLE loss with a simple exploration bonus. The min-playerтАЩs
075   inclination towards exploration leads to more diverse and novel outputs, whereas the max-playerтАЩs
076   responses tend to align more closely with the reference policy. This dynamic contrast enables both
077   players to engage with a broader range of information, ultimately improving their overall perfor-
078   mance. Supported by both theoretical analysis and empirical evidence, we propose a single-agent
079   algorithm that mimics the behavior of the two-agent algorithm.
080   Contributions. The main contributions of our work are as follows.
081
082         1. We introduce a theoretical two-player RLHF framework that effectively balances exploita-
083            tion and exploration while providing a theoretical guarantee. Building on this framework,
084            we propose a practical and easy-to-implement two-agent algorithm TANPO, where both
085            players have simple and practical objectives. Additionally, we propose a single-agent algo-
086            rithm SADPO that approximates the two-agent algorithm.
087         2. We prove that our theoretical algorithm achieves sublinear regret under general function ap-
088            proximation and mild structural conditions. We specify this result to a linear case and then
089            provide a detailed regret analysis, showing our theoretical algorithm achieves a sublinear
090            regret.
091         3. We implement our algorithms, along with several baselines, using Zephyr-7B-SFT (Tun-
092            stall et al., 2023) as the base model and the UltraFeedback dataset for prompts. Our al-
093            gorithm outperforms several baseline methods across multiple evaluation benchmarks, in-
094            cluding AlpacaEval 2.0, MT-Bench, PairRM win rate and various academic benchmarks.
095            Additionally, we demonstrate that our algorithm continues to improve performance during
096            a second epoch on the same dataset, highlighting its ability to achieve consistent gains and
097            mitigate overfitting.

290   It is observed that although the min-player in TANPO benefits from an exploration bonus term, the
291
      max-playerтАЩs objective in TANPO remains identical to DPO objective. This raises a question: How
      does TANPO offer improvements over DPO?
292
293   The key lies in the fact that TANPO generates more diverse training data. While the max-player does
294   not have an explicit exploration bonus, it can still benefit from the increased diversity in the training
295   data. In the original two-agent setup, both the max-player and min-player are trained on the same
296   dataset but pursue different optimization objectives. The max-player focuses solely on minimizing
297
      the MLE loss function, aiming to maximize reward while closely approximate the reference policy
      ╧Аref . In contrast, the min-player incorporates an additional exploration bonus term E [log ┬╡(a|x)]
298
      into its objective, encouraging it to sample from less likely regions of the action distribution. As a
299
      result, the response pairs (a1 , a2 ) exhibit greater diversity and contrast.

      7   C ONCLUSION
493
      In this work, we introduce Two-Agent Nash Policy Optimization (TANPO), a two-agent algorithm
494
      that balances exploration and exploitation. Additionally, we present Single-Agent Diversity-driven
495   Optimization (SADPO) as a simplified approximation of TANPO, supported by theoretical and em-
496   pirical results. Our theoretical analysis shows sublinear regret under general conditions, while em-
497   pirical evaluations demonstrate that TANPO and SADPO outperform baseline methods across multi-
498   ple benchmarks, highlighting their effectiveness in improving performance and reducing overfitting.
499   We hope our work can provide insights for future research into designing provable efficient and
500   practical self-play RLHF methods.

      A.1    T HEORETICAL STUDIES ABOUT RLHF
712
713
714
      Theoretical research on RLHF largely originates from foundational work in dueling bandits and
      dueling reinforcement learning (Xu et al., 2020; Novoseller et al., 2020; Pacchiano et al., 2021; Wu
715
      & Sun, 2023; Zhan et al., 2023). Recently, there has been growing interest in exploring RLHF theory
716
      across diverse settings. Many previous works only consider tabular settings and linear settings (Du
717   et al., 2024; Das et al., 2024; Xiong et al., 2023), which do not fully capture the complexities of real-
718   world scenarios. Beyond the tabular and linear settings, some recent research has explored RLHF
719   theory under general function approximation (Chen et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023;
720   Liu et al., 2024b).
721
      Among these researches, most approaches use a contextual bandit framework. Close to our ap-
722
      proach, some studies give algorithms and theoretical guarantees under two-agent zero-sum game
723   setting (Wang et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2024). Ye et al. (2024) considered a reverse-KL regularized
724   minimax game between two LLMs and gave a sample-efficient algorithm, and Wang et al. (2023)
725   gave guarantees for RLHF algorithm under general arbitrary preferences settings. However, their
726   algorithms contain theoretical confidence bounds that are hard to compute, thus are unable to be
727   implemented in experiments. Liu et al. (2024a) proposed a two-agent algorithm framework which
728   approximates Nash equilibrium with active exploration and proved the algorithm has a sublinear
729   guarantee. Different from ours, their work only focused on MDP and Markov game settings.
730
731
732   A.2    E MPIRICAL ALGORITHMS FOR RLHF
733
734   RLHF has gained significant traction in the deployment of large-scale models, such as ChatGPT
735   (Achiam et al., 2023), Gemini (Team et al., 2023) and Llama (Touvron et al., 2023), where it has been
736   employed to align model behavior with human preferences effectively. A widely used algorithm in
737   this context is Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) (Schulman et al., 2017), which iteratively up-
738   dates the model feedback from a learned reward model. However, while PPO has demonstrated
739   success in many practical applications, it also has notable limitations, such as high sample com-
740   plexity and sensitivity to hyperparameters (Engstrom et al., 2020), which can affect its stability and
741
      performance. To address these shortcomings, recent work has proposed alternative approaches like
      Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2024), which directly optimizes the model
742
      based on human preferences. In addition to the initial formulation of the DPO algorithm, researchers
743
      have developed a wide range of variants (Liu et al., 2023; Azar et al., 2024; Ethayarajh et al., 2024;
744   Hong et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024b), each tailored to specific optimization problems or designed to
745   enhance certain aspects of the algorithmтАЩs performance.
746
747
      Unlike offline RLHF, which is constrained by a static dataset, online RLHF (Ouyang et al., 2022;
      Guo et al., 2024; Bai et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2023; Gulcehre et al., 2023; Xiong et al., 2023; Calan-
748
      driello et al., 2024; Pang et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024; Ji et al., 2024) continually
749
      generates better and more diverse learning data as the model learns and adapts over time, allowing
750   the model to refine its understanding through real-time human feedback. In online RLHF, explo-
751   ration plays a critical role by allowing the model to avoid being trapped in local minima, contin-
752   uously encouraging it to seek new actions and learn from a more diverse set of responses. Zhang
753   et al. (2024); Xie et al. (2024); Cen et al. (2024) propose algorithms that add DPO with explo-
754   ration bonuses similar to ours. Liu et al. (2024b) also utilize a similar confidence bound but aim to
755   avoid overoptimization in offline RLHF. Unlike our work, these works only focus on single-agent
      contextual bandit settings.

       E.3     I MPLEMENTATION OF BASELINES
1466
       In this subsection, we discuss the details of how we implemented the baselines.
1467
1468            тАв Online DPO. We implement the online DPO by ourselves. Aside from the loss function,
1469              all other training setups are exactly the same as in our algorithms. ItтАЩs important to note
1470              that the practical algorithm introduced by Rosset et al. (2024) is essentially equivalent to
1471              iterative DPO.
1472            тАв Hybrid GSHF. We implement Hybrid GSHF (Xiong et al., 2023) ourselves, where two
1473              responses for each prompt are generated by the reference policy and the current policy. All
1474              other training hyperparameters are kept identical to those in our algorithms. Since Hybrid
