009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Complex multi-step reasoning tasks, such as solving mathematical problems or
012            generating code, remain a significant hurdle for even the most advanced large
013            language models (LLMs). Verifying LLM outputs with an Outcome Reward Model
014            (ORM) is a standard inference-time technique aimed at enhancing the reasoning
015            performance of LLMs. However, this still proves insufficient for reasoning tasks
016
               with a lengthy or multi-hop reasoning chain, where the intermediate outcomes
               are neither properly rewarded nor penalized. Process supervision addresses this
017
               limitation by assigning intermediate rewards during the reasoning process. To
018
               date, the methods used to collect process supervision data have relied on either
019            human annotation or per-step Monte Carlo estimation, both prohibitively expensive
020            to scale, thus hindering the broad application of this technique. In response to
021            this challenge, we propose a novel divide-and-conquer style Monte Carlo Tree
022            Search (MCTS) algorithm named OmegaPRM for the efficient collection of high-
023            quality process supervision data. This algorithm swiftly identifies the first error
024            in the Chain of Thought (CoT) with binary search and balances the positive and
025            negative examples, thereby ensuring both efficiency and quality. As a result, we
026            are able to collect over 1.5 million process supervision annotations to train Process
027
               Reward Models (PRMs). This fully automated process supervision alongside the
               weighted self-consistency algorithm is able to enhance LLMs’ math reasoning
028
               performances. We improved the success rates of the instruction-tuned Gemini Pro
029
               model from 51% to 69.4% on MATH500 and from 86.4% to 93.6% on GSM8K.
030            Similarly, we boosted the success rates of Gemma2 27B from 42.3% to 58.2%
031            on MATH500 and from 74.0% to 92.2% on GSM8K. The entire process operates
032            without any human intervention or supervision, making our method both financially
033            and computationally cost-effective compared to existing methods.

036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Despite the impressive advancements achieved by scaling Large Language Models (LLMs) on
039
      established benchmarks (Wei et al., 2022a), cultivating more sophisticated reasoning capabilities,
      particularly in domains like mathematical problem-solving and code generation, remains an active
040
      research area. Chain-of-thought (CoT) generation is crucial for these reasoning tasks, as it decomposes
041
      complex problems into intermediate steps, mirroring human reasoning processes. Prompting LLMs
042   with CoT examples (Wei et al., 2022b) and fine-tuning them on question-CoT solution pairs (Perez
043   et al., 2021; Ouyang et al., 2022) have proven effective, with the latter demonstrating superior
044   performance. Furthermore, the advent of Reinforcement Learning with Human Feedback (RLHF;
045   Ouyang et al., 2022) has enabled the alignment of LLM behaviors with human preferences through
046   reward models, significantly enhancing model capabilities.
047
      Beyond prompting and further training, developing effective decoding strategies is another crucial
048   avenue for improvement. Self-consistency decoding (Wang et al., 2023) leverages multiple reasoning
049   paths to arrive at a voted answer. Incorporating a verifier, such as an off-the-shelf LLM (Huang
050   et al., 2022; Luo et al., 2023), can further guide LLMs in reasoning tasks by providing a feedback
051   loop to verify final answers, identify errors, and suggest corrections. However, the gain of such
052   approaches remains limited for complex multi-step reasoning problems. Reward models offer a
053   promising alternative to verifiers, enabling the reranking of candidate outcomes based on reward
      signals to ensure higher accuracy. Two primary types of reward models have emerged: Outcome

      2     R ELATED W ORK
114
      Improving mathematical reasoning ability of LLMs. Mathematical reasoning poses significant
115
      challenges for LLMs, and it is one of the key tasks for evaluating the reasoning ability of LLMs.
116
      With a huge amount of math problems in pretraining datasets, the pretrained LLMs (OpenAI, 2023;
117   Gemini Team et al., 2024; Touvron et al., 2023) are able to solve simple problems, yet struggle with
118   more complicated reasoning. To overcome that, the chain-of-thought (Wei et al., 2022b; Fu et al.,
119   2023) type prompting algorithms were proposed. These techniques were effective in improving the
120   performance of LLMs on reasoning tasks without modifying the model parameters. The performance
121   was further improved by supervised fine-tuning (SFT; Cobbe et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2024; Yu et al.,
122   2023) with high quality question-response pairs with full CoT reasoning steps.
123
124   Application of reward models in mathematical reasoning of LLMs. To further improve the
125   LLM’s math reasoning performance, verifiers can help to rank and select the best answer when
126
      multiple rollouts are available. Several works (Huang et al., 2022; Luo et al., 2023) have shown that
      using LLM as verifier is not suitable for math reasoning. For trained verifiers, two types of reward
127
      models are commonly used: Outcome Reward Model (ORM) and Process Reward Model (PRM).
128
      Both have shown performance boost on math reasoning over self-consistency (Cobbe et al., 2021;
129   Uesato et al., 2022; Lightman et al., 2023), yet evidence has shown that PRM outperforms ORM
130   (Lightman et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024a). Generating high quality process supervision data is the
131   key for training PRM, besides expensive human annotation (Lightman et al., 2023), Math-Shepherd
132   (Wang et al., 2024a) and MiPS (Wang et al., 2024b) explored Monte Carlo estimation to automate the
133   data collection process with human involvement, and both observed large performance gain. Our
134   work shared the essence with MiPS and Math-Shepherd, but we explore further in collecting the
135   process data using MCTS.
136
137   Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS). MCTS (Świechowski et al., 2021) has been widely adopted
138   in reinforcement learning (RL). AlphaGo (Silver et al., 2016) and AlphaGo Zero (Silver et al., 2017)
139   were able to achieve great performance with MCTS and deep reinforcement learning. For LLMs,
140
      there are planning algorithms that fall in the category of tree search, such as Tree-of-Thought (Yao
      et al., 2023) and Reasoning-via-Planing (Hao et al., 2023). Recently, utilizing tree-like decoding to
141
      find the best output during the inference-time has become a hot topic to explore as well, multiple
142
      works (Feng et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024; Tian et al., 2024; Feng et al., 2024;
143   Kang et al., 2024) have observed improvements in reasoning tasks.

      3.1   P ROCESS S UPERVISION
149
      Process supervision is a concept proposed to differentiate from outcome supervision. The reward
150   models trained with these objectives are termed Process Reward Models (PRMs) and Outcome
151   Reward Models (ORMs), respectively. In the ORM framework, given a query q (e.g., a mathematical
152   problem) and its corresponding response x (e.g., a model-generated solution), an ORM is trained
153   to predict the correctness of the final answer within the response. Formally, an ORM takes q and x
154   and outputs the probability p = ORM(q, x) that the final answer in the response is correct. With a
155   training set of question-answer pairs available, an ORM can be trained by sampling outputs from a
156   policy model (e.g., a pretrained or fine-tuned LLM) using the questions and obtaining the correctness
157   labels by comparing these outputs with the golden answers.
158   In contrast, a PRM is trained to predict the correctness of each intermediate step xt in the solution.
159   Formally, pt = PRM([q, x1:t−1 ], xt ), where x1:i = [x1 , . . . , xi ] represents the first i steps in the
160   solution. This provides more precise and fine-grained feedback than ORMs, as it identifies the exact
161   location of errors. Process supervision has also been shown to mitigate incorrect reasoning in the
      domain of mathematical problem solving. Despite these advantages, obtaining the intermediate signal


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      for each step’s correctness to train such a PRM is non-trivial. Previous work (Lightman et al., 2023)
163   has relied on hiring domain experts to manually annotate the labels, which is and difficult to scale.

      3.2   P ROCESS A NNOTATION WITH M ONTE C ARLO M ETHOD
166
167   In two closely related works, Math-Shepherd (Wang et al., 2024a) and MiPS (Wang et al., 2024b),
168   the authors propose an automatic annotation approach to obtain process supervision signals using the
169   Monte Carlo method. Specifically, a “completer” policy is established that can take a question q and
170   a prefix solution comprising the first t steps x1:t and output the completion — often referred to as a
171   “rollout” in reinforcement learning — of the subsequent steps until the final answer is reached. As
172   shown in Fig. 2(a), for any step of a solution, the completer policy can be used to randomly sample
173
      k rollouts from that step. The final answers of these rollouts are compared to the golden answer,
      providing k labels of answer correctness corresponding to the k rollouts. Subsequently, the ratio of
174
      correct rollouts to total rollouts from the t-th step, as represented in Eq. (1), estimates the “correctness
175
      level” of the prefix steps up to t. Regardless of false positives, x1:t should be considered correct as
176   long as any of the rollouts is correct in the logical reasoning scenario.

181   Taking a step forward, a straightforward strategy to annotate the correctness of intermediate steps
182   in a solution is to perform rollouts for every step from the beginning to the end, as done in both
183   Math-Shepherd and MiPS. However, this brute-force approach requires a large number of policy
184   calls. To optimize annotation efficiency, we propose a binary-search-based Monte Carlo estimation.
185
      Monte Carlo estimation using binary search. As suggested by Lightman et al. (2023), supervising
186   up to the first incorrect step in a solution is sufficient to train a PRM. Therefore, our objective is
187   locating the first error in an efficient way. We achieve this by repeatedly dividing the solution and
188   performing rollouts. Assuming no false positives or negatives, we start with a solution with potential
189   errors and split it at the midpoint m. We then perform rollouts for s1:m with two possible outcomes:
190   (1) cm > 0, indicating that the first half of the solution is correct, as at least one correct answer can
191   be rolled out from m-th step, and thus the error is in the second half; (2) cm = 0, indicating the
192   error is very likely in the first half, as none of the rollouts from m-th step is correct. This process
193   narrows down the error location to either the first or second half of the solution. As shown in Fig. 2(b),
194
      by repeating this process on the erroneous half iteratively until the partial solution is sufficiently
      small (i.e., short enough to be considered as a single step), we can locate the first error with a time
195
      complexity of O(k log M ) rather than O(kM ) in the brute-force setting, where M is the total number
196
      of steps in the original solution.

      3.3   M ONTE C ARLO T REE S EARCH
199
200   Although binary search improves the efficiency of locating the first error in a solution, we are still not
201   fully utilizing policy calls as rollouts are simply discarded after stepwise Monte Carlo estimation. In
202   practice, it is necessary to collect multiple PRM training examples (a.k.a., triplets of question, partial
203   solution and correctness label) for a question (Lightman et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024a). Instead
204   of starting from scratch each time, we can store all rollouts during the process and conduct binary
205   searches from any of these rollouts whenever we need to collect a new example. This approach
206   allows for triplets with the same solution prefix but different completions and error locations. Such
207
      reasoning structures can be represented as a tree, as described in previous work like Tree of Thought
      (Yao et al., 2023).
208
209   Formally, consider a state-action tree representing detailed reasoning paths for a question, where
210   a state s contains the question and all preceding reasoning steps, and an action a is a potential
211   subsequent step from a specific state. The root state is the question without any reasoning steps:
212   rroot = q. The policy can be directly modeled by a language model as π(a|s) = LM(a|s), and the
213
      state transition function is simply the concatenation of the preceding steps and the action step, i.e.,
      s0 = Concatenate(s, a).

252   Collecting PRM training examples for a question can now be formulated as constructing such a
253   state-action tree. This reminds us the classic Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) algorithm, which
254   has been successful in many deep reinforcement learning applications (Silver et al., 2016; 2017).
255   However, there are some key differences when using a language model as the policy. First, MCTS
256   typically handles an environment with a finite action space, such as the game of Go, which has fewer
257
      than 361 possible actions per state (Silver et al., 2017). In contrast, an LM policy has an infinite action
      space, as it can generate an unlimited number of distinct actions (sequences of tokens) given a prompt.
258
      In practice, we use temperature sampling to generate a fix number of k completions for a prompt,
259
      treating the group of k actions as an approximate action space. Second, an LM policy can sample
260   a full rollout until the termination state (i.e., reaching the final answer) without too much overhead
261   than generating a single step, enabling the possibility of binary search. Consequently, we propose an
262   adaptation of the MCTS algorithm named OmegaPRM, primarily based on the one introduced in
263   AlphaGo (Silver et al., 2016), but with modifications to better accommodate the scenario of PRM
264   training data collection. We describe the algorithm details as below.
265

268   sequence of consecutive steps from the node s. The nodes also store a set of statistics,

      4.4             A LGORITHM E FFICIENCY
478
479
      As described in Section §3.2 and §3.3, we utilize binary search and Monte Carlo Tree Search to
480   improve the efficiency of OmegaPRM process supervision data collection by effectively identifying
481   the first incorrect step and reusing rollouts in Monte Carlo estimation. To quantitatively measure
482   the efficiency of OmegaPRM, we collected process supervision data using both brute-force-style
483   method (Wang et al., 2024a;b) and OmegaPRM with the same computational budget. As a result, we
484   were able to generate 200K data points using the brute-force algorithm compared to 15 million data
485   points with OmegaPRM, demonstrating a 75-times efficiency improvement. In practice, we randomly
      down-sampled OmegaPRM data to 1.5 million for PRM training.

      5   L IMITATIONS
487
488
      There are some limitations with our paper, which we reserve for future work:
489
490
      Automatic process annotation is noisy. Our method for automatic process supervision annotation
491   introduces noise in the form of false positives and negatives, but experiments indicate that it can
492   still effectively train a PRM. The PRM trained on our dataset performs better than one trained on
493   the human-annotated PRM800K dataset. The precise impact of noise on PRM performance remains
494   uncertain. For future research, a comprehensive comparison of human and automated annotations
495   should be conducted. One other idea is to integrate human and automated annotations, which could
496   result in more robust and efficient process supervision.
497
498   Human supervision is still necessary. Unlike the work presented in AlphaGo Zero (Silver et al.,
499   2017), our method requires the question and golden answer pair. The question is necessary for LLM
500   to start the MCTS and the golden answer is inevitable for the LLM to compare its rollouts with
501
      and determine the correctness of the current step. This will limit the method to the tasks with such
      question and golden answer pairs. Therefore, we need to adapt the current method further to make it
502
      suitable for open-ended tasks.
503
504
505   6   C ONCLUSION
506
507   In conclusion, we introduce OmegaPRM, a divide-and-conquer Monte Carlo Tree Search algorithm,
508   designed to automate the process supervision data collection for LLMs. By efficiently pinpointing
509
      the first error in the Chain-of-Thought and balancing data quality, OmegaPRM addresses the short-
      comings of existing methods. Our automated approach enables the collection of over 1.5 million
510
      process supervision annotations, which are used to train a PRM. Leveraging this automated process
511
      supervision with the weighted self-consistency algorithm, we improve LLM mathematical reasoning
512   performance, achieving a 69.4% success rate on the MATH benchmark — a 18.4% absolute increase
513   over the base model which amounts to a relative improvement of 36%. Additionally, our method
514   significantly reduces data collection costs compared to human annotation and brute force Monte-Carlo
515   sampling. These findings highlight OmegaPRM’s potential to enhance LLM capabilities in complex
516   multi-step reasoning tasks.

      A    Q UESTION F ILTERING
814
815
      During the evaluation of partial solution correctness using MC estimation, false negative noise may
      be introduced when a question is too hard for the model, thus no correct rollout can be found even
816
      with correct partial solution. Or false positive noise may be introduced when a question is too easy,
817
      that model can conclude in correct answer given partial solution with wrong step. It is not possible
818   to exclude such noise completely, but we can reduce the chance by filtering out questions that are
819   either too hard or too easy for the model. Specifically, we ran a k = 32 rollouts for each question in
820   the 12K training data, and filter out the questions that with no correct answer (too hard) or no wrong
821   answer (too easy) in the 32 rollouts.

      B    PAIRWISE L OSS F ORMULA
824
825
      When training with pairwise labels, the Bradley-Terry model (people typically use this objective to
826   train reward models in RLHF) generally accepts two probability scalars summing up to 1. When we
827   select the two actions as a pair, there are two cases. The first case is that one sample with a zero MC
828   value, and the other sample with a positive MC value. The second case is that both samples are with
829   positive MC values. The first case is straight-forward, and a normalization step is required for the
830   second case.
831
      Assume the two MC values are p and q, and they follow the Bernoulli distribution: P (X = 1) = p
832   and P (Y = 1) = q. We need to calculate the probability that action X is preferred over action Y and
833   vice versa.
834
835
836             P (X > Y ) = P (X = 1, Y = 0) = p(1 − q),
837             P (X < Y ) = P (X = 0, Y = 1) = (1 − p)q,                                                (5)
838             P (X = Y ) = P (X = 0, Y = 0) + P (X = 1, Y = 1) = (1 − p)(1 − q) + pq.
839
840   For the tied situation, each action has half the chance of being preferred. Thus,
841
842
843            P (action X is preferred) = P (X > Y ) + 1/2 ∗ P (X = Y ) = 1/2 ∗ (1 + p − q),
844
                                                                                                         (6)
               P (action Y is preferred) = P (X < Y ) + 1/2 ∗ P (X = Y ) = 1/2 ∗ (1 + q − p).
845
846   Now the MC values are normalized and we can train with the pairwise loss.
