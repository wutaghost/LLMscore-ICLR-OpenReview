                                                   A BSTRACT
013
014
               Representation learning on text-attributed graphs (TAGs) has attracted signifi-
015
               cant interest due to its wide-ranging real-world applications, particularly through
016
               Graph Neural Networks (GNNs). Traditional GNN methods focus on encoding
017            the structural information of graphs, often using shallow text embeddings for node
018            or edge attributes. This limits the model to understand the rich semantic informa-
019            tion in the data and its reasoning ability for complex downstream tasks, while
020            also lacking interpretability. With the rise of large language models (LLMs), an
021            increasing number of studies are combining them with GNNs for graph represen-
022            tation learning and downstream tasks. While these approaches effectively leverage
023            the rich semantic information in TAGs datasets, their main drawback is that they
024            are only partially interpretable, which limits their application in critical fields. In
025
               this paper, we propose a verbalized graph representation learning (VGRL) method
               which is fully interpretable. In contrast to traditional graph machine learning mod-
026
               els, which are usually optimized within a continuous parameter space, VGRL con-
027
               strains this parameter space to be text description which ensures complete inter-
028            pretability throughout the entire process, making it easier for users to understand
029            and trust the decisions of the model. We conduct several studies to empirically
030            evaluate the effectiveness of VGRL and we believe this method can serve as a
031            stepping stone in graph representation learning. The source code of our model is
032            available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/VGRL-7E1E

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Many real-world graphs incorporate textual data, forming what are known as Text-Attributed Graphs
038   (TAGs) (Yang et al., 2021). In TAGs, nodes represent textual entitities such as papers, while edges
039
      denote relationships between them, such as citations or co-authorships. For instance, the Cora
      dataset can be modeled as a TAG, where each node represents a research paper, and the node at-
040
      tributes include features such as the paper’s title, abstract, and keywords. By integrating textual
041
      attributes with graph topology, TAGs facilitate more effective representation learning, making them
042   valuable for tasks like document classification, clustering (Wang et al., 2023), citation analysis, and
043   recommendation systems (Zhu et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023a). This combination of textual and
044   relational data offers deeper insights, especially when both content and connections are essential to
045   the analysis.
046
      Although traditional Graph Neural Network (GNN) models, such as Graph Convolutional Network
047
      (GCN) (Kipf & Welling, 2016) and Graph Attention Network (GAT) (Veličković et al., 2017),
048   have achieved significant performance improvements across multiple tasks, they generally suffer
049   from a lack of interpretability. As these models largely rely on complex network architectures
050   and implicit feature learning processes, understanding their internal decision mechanisms and how
051   specific features influence task outcomes becomes challenging, thereby limiting their transparency
052   and trustworthiness in practical applications. To address this issue, researchers have proposed sev-
053   eral interpretable GNN models. These interpretable methods can generally be divided into input
      interpretability, training process interpretability, and decision-making process interpretability. For

084
085   In recent years, with the breakthroughs of large language models (LLMs) in the field of natural
086
      language processing, researchers have gradually begun to integrate them with GNNs to enhance
      model performance and capabilities. For instance, LLMs can act as predictors (Tang et al., 2024),
087
      generating more accurate predictions by analyzing node features and structural information for the
088
      TAGs. Also, TAPE (He et al., 2023) prompts a powerful LLM to explain its predictions and serve
089   explanations as supplementary text attributes for the downstream LMs and GNN models. Due to
090   the powerful text inference capabilities of LLMs, they are capable of processing TAGs, reasoning
091   about the node classification prediction process of TAGs, and generating explanations in text that
092   is comprehensible to humans. Therefore, we consider the use of LLMs to achieve comprehensive
093   interpretability. However, using LLMs to handle graph tasks and provide interpretability is not easy.
094   Specifically, there are currently two main approaches to applying LLMs in the field of graph: one
095   is to pre-train or fine-tune LLMs to adapt to various graph downstream tasks. But due to the vast
096   number of parameters typically found in LLMs, the cost of fine-tuning LLMs is quite high and the
097
      training time is long. The second is to directly freeze the LLMs for inference but this method does
      not yield good results. For example, we directly froze the predictor LLMs for node classification
098
      prediction in subsequent experiments, and the prediction accuracy was generally not high, as shown
099
      in Table 3.
100
101   In summary, we face two major challenges to achieve comprehensive interpretability with LLMs:
102   Challenge 1: How can we ensure that a model is interpretable in terms of input, training process,
103   and decision-making simultaneously?
104
      Challenge 2: How can we optimize the performance of LLMs without fine-tuning the model pa-
105
      rameters to reduce costs?
106
107   To address these challenges, we propose the Verbalized Graph Representation Learning (VGRL)
      method. For Challenge 1, VGRL utilizes a verbalized approach to create intuitive connections

      between input features and predictions and VGRL generates textual explanations at each iteration
109   stage, helping researchers and practitioners better grasp the training dynamics of the model. Also,
110   VGRL provides natural language descriptions for the model’s predictions, clearly explaining the ra-

124          • We seek to reduce the high GPU overhead associated with pre-training or fine-tuning in cur-

      3     R ELATED W ORK
150
151
      In this section, we review the existing literature related to integrating Large Language Models
152
      (LLMs) and Graph Neural Networks (GNNs). Prior work has focused on several key areas, in-
153   cluding traditional methods for trusted GNNs, the role of LLMs in graph-based tasks, and recent
154   advances in optimization frameworks utilizing LLMs. We explore these approaches to highlight
155   their contributions and limitations, establishing the foundation for our proposed Verbalized Graph
156   Representation Learning (VGRL) framework.
157
158   3.1   G RAPH AND LLM S
159
160   Traditional approaches to trusted GNNs. There are currently two main approaches: post-hoc
161   explanation methods and self-interpretable models . The former tries to interpret the output of the
      model by adding a model-independent interpreter, for example (Ying et al., 2019; Vu & Thai, 2020;


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Zhang et al., 2023b). However, this can lead to incomplete explanatory information in the output, or
163   even generate explanatory information that is incorrect in the opinion of humans. The latter tries to
164   solve this problem by constructing models that themselves have interpretable principles, for example
165   (Dai & Wang, 2021; Zhang et al., 2022a). However, these interpretable principles are based on their
166   inductive bias, and only experts in the relevant fields can accurately judge whether such inductive
167   bias is reasonable or not.
168
      LLM in Graph. Existing methods are mainly categorized into three types: (1) LLM as Enhancer
169   which mainly enhances the performance of GNNs by adding LLM-generated information, for ex-
170   ample (He et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024; Ni et al., 2024); (2) LLM as Predictor which mainly
171   performs a downstream task by directly inputting the graph structure into the LLM, for example
172   (Tang et al., 2024; Qin et al., 2023); (3) LLM as Alignment which mainly enhances the performance
173   by aligning embedding spaces of GNNs and LLMs, for example (Yang et al., 2021; Mavromatis
174   et al., 2023). Among them, there is explanation-based LLM-as-Enhancer approach (He et al., 2023),
175   which achieves better performance by letting LLM generate natural language explanation informa-
176   tion of graph structures and then embedding it into GNNs for downstream tasks. However, after the
177
      embedding from natural language to graph structure is not directly visible as a black box to humans,
      and can only be proven effective indirectly through the performance of downstream tasks.
178
179
180
181
182   3.2   LLM S O PTIMIZATION
183
184
185   LLMs for planning and optimization. Large language models (LLMs) have been successfully
186   applied to planning tasks for embodied agents (Song et al., 2023; Xie et al., 2023; Li et al., 2022;
187   Liang et al., 2023), enabling them to follow natural language instructions and complete complex
188   tasks. More recently, LLMs have also been utilized to tackle optimization problems by generating
189
      new solutions from prompts that incorporate previously generated solutions and their associated loss
      values. While these LLM-based optimization (Xiao et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024) methods bear
190
      some resemblance to our approach, as we also use LLMs to address optimization challenges, a key
191
      limitation of existing work is that it has not yet been explored in the graph domain. To address
192   this gap, we propose an extension of this framework to the graph domain, introducing Verbalized
193   Graph Representation Learning (VGRL), which applies LLMs to graph neural networks (GNNs)
194   and opens new possibilities for solving graph-based optimization problems through natural language
195   interactions.
196
      Prompt engineering and optimization. Numerous prompting techniques (Wei et al., 2022; Zhang
197
      et al., 2022b; Zhou et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022; Yao et al., 2024; 2023; Weston & Sukhbaatar,
198   2023) have been developed to enhance the reasoning capabilities of LLMs. To minimize the manual
199   effort required in designing effective prompts, various automatic prompt optimization approaches
200   (Zhang et al., 2022b; Zhou et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2024; Pryzant et al., 2023; Wen et al., 2024;
201   Deng et al., 2022; Li et al., 2024b; Ma et al., 2024; Sordoni et al., 2023) have been introduced.
202   However, traditional prompt optimization methods primarily focus on refining the text prompt with-
203   out changing its underlying semantic meaning. In contrast, our VGRL framework goes beyond mere
204   prompt adjustments by directly updating the parameters of the language-based model through the
205   integration or modification of prior information. This not only improves optimization but also en-
206
      sures that the learner model remains fully interpretable in its predictions, offering a more robust and
      transparent solution for graph-based learning tasks.
207
208   LLMs for multi-agent systems. Given their strong instruction-following capabilities, LLMs can
209   assume various roles within multi-agent systems (Qian et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023; Hong et al.,
210   2023; Li et al., 2023). For instance, explore multi-agent collaboration systems designed to solve
211   complex tasks such as software development. In the VGRL framework, this concept is extended to
212
      a two-agent system, where one LLM functions as the learner and the other as the optimizer.
213   Our approach sidesteps the problem of modeling black boxes by having the LLM generate human-
214   readable information as promt of another LLM making it perform the downstream task. This can be
215   viewed as a “guidance-feedback-redirection” process between models, which, after many iterations,
      returns the optimal guidance solution for a given task, which is directly human-readable.

235
236   In this paper, we present the Verbalized Graph Representation Learning (VGRL) framework, a pi-
237   oneering approach that integrates large language models (LLMs) with graph-based tasks while en-
238   suring full interpretability throughout the process. Our methodology encompasses four innovative
239   components, each designed to enhance both the performance and the transparency of LLMs in han-
240   dling graph data.
241
242   4.1               I NCORPORATING G RAPH S TRUCTURE INTO LLM P REDICTIONS
243
244   Although Large Language Models (LLMs) can achieve competitive zero-shot performance on spe-
245   cific datasets without considering graph structures, their performance often lags behind Graph Neu-
246
      ral Networks (GNNs) on benchmark datasets such as CORA, CITESEER, and OGBN-ARXIV. This
      gap underscores the importance of graph structural information in tasks like node classification, in-
247
      dicating the need to explore how incorporating graph structures into prompts could enhance LLM
248
      performance.
249
250   Given that LLMs (e.g., ChatGPT) are not natively designed to process adjacency matrices or perform
251   graph-based computations, it is impractical to directly integrate graph operations into LLMs. Thus,
252
      an alternative approach is to verbalize graph information as text that LLMs can process effectively.
      This transformation allows LLMs to interpret node relationships and structural dependencies in
253
      natural language format. In (Chen et al., 2024), various methods are evaluated to represent node
254
      connections textually, aiming to enhance LLM reasoning capabilities for graph-based tasks.
255
256   One effective method is the ‘ego-graph’ approach, which focuses on the local subgraph surrounding
257   a target node. By constraining the LLM’s focus to a limited number of nodes, this method reduces
258
      complexity while preserving key local graph structure. To simulate the neighborhood aggregation
      process typical in GNNs, the input prompt incorporates a summary of attributes from neighboring
259
      nodes. Thus, important information from the graph is conveyed to the LLM without altering its
260
      reasoning mechanisms. This process can be formalized as:

285   4.2   V ERBALIZING M ODEL PARAMETERS FOR I NTERPRETABILITY
286
287   Traditional machine learning models, such as neural networks, rely on numerical parameters,
288   θ = {θ1 , θ2 , . . . , θt }, which are often difficult to interpret. These parameters are typically repre-
289
      sented as abstract numerical values, making it complex and non-intuitive to understand or explain
      the internal workings of the model. In contrast, the Verbalized Graph Representation Learning
290
      (VGRL) framework leverages large language models (LLMs) to express model parameters through
291
      natural language, providing full interpretability.
292
293   In VGRL, the model parameters θt are defined by a text prompt, which consists of human-readable
294   natural language tokens, θt ∈ Θlanguage , where Θlanguage is the set of all interpretable text sequences.
295
      This approach contrasts with traditional models where parameters are abstract numbers, which are
      hard to interpret directly. The VGRL framework unifies both data and model parameters into a
296
      natural language-based format that is inherently understandable.
297
298   The key features of this framework include:
299
300          • Discrete Parameters: The natural language used to express parameters θ is inherently dis-
301
               crete. This is in contrast to the continuous parameter representations in traditional models,
               enhancing the intuitiveness of parameter interpretation.
302
303          • Sequential Structure: The parameters exhibit a sequential structure, as θ =
304
               {θ1 , θ2 , . . . , θt }, reflecting the temporal or contextual relationships between parameters.
               This sequential nature aids in capturing and understanding the dynamics between parame-
305
               ters.
306
307          • Human Interpretability: Since the parameters θt are verbalized in natural language, they
308
               are inherently comprehensible to humans. This allows the model’s reasoning process and
               learning mechanisms to be more transparent, facilitating interpretability and easier analysis.
309
310   An advantage of using natural language for model parameters is that it enables the integration of
311   prior knowledge and inductive biases directly into the model. As the model updates its parameters
312   θt , the changes are fully interpretable, providing clear insights into what the model is learning. For
313   example, changes in θt can be directly mapped to natural language descriptions, offering an intuitive
314   understanding of the model’s learning process.
315
      Our empirical evidence demonstrates that text-based parameters often correspond to recognizable
316   patterns in the data, further reinforcing the interpretability and transparency of the VGRL approach.
317   This natural language parameterization not only enhances the intuitiveness of model but also im-
318   proves its application, offering clearer insights into model tuning and interpretation in real-world
319   scenarios.

321   4.3   L EVERAGING LLM S FOR N ODE C LASSIFICATION
322
323   Our approach centers on utilizing LLMs as interpretable predictors by querying them in an ‘open-
      ended’ manner. Unlike existing methods that primarily rely on message passing mechanisms, our


                                                          6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      method employs a label feature matching mechanism. We match based on the inherent characteris-
325   tics of the nodes themselves and the information from their neighbors. This label feature matching
326   mechanism places a stronger emphasis on the intrinsic attributes of node, as it aligns with the insights
327   provided in the prompt.
328
      The core of this method is represented by the following equation:
329
330
331                                              ŷvi = fp (Zvki , θt−1 )                                   (2)
332
333   Here, ŷvi denotes the predicted label for node vi , and Zvki represents the enhanced node representa-
334   tion incorporating vi ’s k-hop neighbors. θt−1 refers to the LLM’s parameters at the previous step,
335   enabling the model to leverage its prior knowledge and reasoning capabilities to generate the pre-
336   diction. The function fp serves as the predictor that utilizes the enhanced representation and model
337   parameters to produce the label output. This formulation emphasizes the LLM’s role as a predictor,
338
      focusing on generating interpretable outputs.
339   For each node vi ∈ V, a prompt is crafted that includes not only the node’s features, such as the
340   paper title and abstract, but also relevant graph structure information. Specifically, the attributes
341   of neighboring nodes up to the k-hop neighborhood are embedded in the prompt, as encapsulated
342   in Zvki . This enables the LLM to better understand the node’s context and surroundings within the
343   graph, leading to more informed and accurate predictions.
344
345   4.4   LLM AS AN OPTIMIZER WITH INTERPRETABLE OPTIMIZATION PROCESS
346
347
      For the predictor LLM, we provide textual descriptions of node categories, which serve as model
      parameter θ, and the model determines which category the input node vi belongs to based on the
348
      given descriptions. The quality of node category descriptions θ directly affects the performance of
349
      LLM predictions; hence, obtaining suitable node category descriptions is very important. Addition-
350   ally, for better explainability, VGRL imposes a strong constraint on θ, ensuring that the updated θ
351   still belong to natural language sequences that humans can understand.
352
353
      Under these conditions, it is not advisable to use classical machine learning optimization methods
      such as gradient descent to optimize θ. Inspired by Xiao et al. (2024), the optimizer LLM can output
354
      natural language that satisfies the constraints, so we only need to ask the LLM to play the role
355
      of an optimizer, then optimized category descriptions are also in natural language understandable
356   by humans. Therefore, we directly use another LLM to optimize θ. Given a mini-batch B, the
357   optimization process is as follows:
358
359                                 θevt i = gopt (Zvki , yvi , ŷvi , θt−1 , Ψ), vi ∈ B                    (3)
360
361   where yvi is the true label of vi , θevt i represents the intermediate parameter values for node vi during
362   the t-th iteration, and Ψ denotes the parameter of the optimizer LLM, which is a text prompt. Specif-
363   ically, we optimize the intermediate parameter value θevt i of each node vi in B, and then summarize
364   the intermediate parameter values of these nodes through a summary LLM (Section 4.5) to obtain a
365
      new round of parameter θt . The overall framework for optimizer optimization and the text prompt
      template are given in Figure 2. The parameter Ψ of the optimizer LLM is actually a text prompt
366
      provided by humans and is not updated. The text prompt linguistically specifies the optimization
367
      loss function, guiding the optimizer LLM to optimize θ. The LLM-parameterized optimizer allows
368   users to interact with it directly, which not only helps to trace model failures but also permits the
369   incorporation of prior knowledge to enhance optimization. In addition, we also guide the LLM
370   to output explanations of the optimization process, demonstrating the explainability of the VGRL
371   optimization process.
372
373   4.5   S UMMARY LLM
374
375   The role of the Summary LLM is to aggregate and summarize the updated intermediate parameters
376   from the optimizer LLM, generated during the previous minibatch, to obtain updated θ. Specifically,
377   given a set of updated parameters from the last minibatch B, the Summary LLM consolidates these
      updates into a new set of parameters, θt . This process can be formalized as:


                                                             7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
379

                                           θt = fs {θevt i | vi ∈ B}                                                  (4)
380
381
382   Here, θevt i represents the intermediate parameter values for node vi during the t-th iteration, and B
383   denotes the set of nodes in the current minibatch. The function fs operates by combining these
384
      parameter updates to produce a cohesive set of parameters, θt , which reflects the overall learning
      progress across the minibatch. This aggregation ensures that key information from each node’s
385
      updated parameters is captured while maintaining coherence in the overall optimization process.

      4.6   C HAIN - OF -T HOUGHT P ROMPTING
389
      Inspired by (Wei et al., 2022), we introduce the zero-shot and one-shot Chain-of-Thought (CoT)
390   methods in prompt. For the zero-shot method, we encourage the LLM to perform step-by-step text
391   generation by restricting and guiding the LLM to make the generated explanatory information as
392   structured and precise as possible, in order to achieve a better final result generation based on the
393   self-generated information. Although zero-shot VGRL is already fully interpretable, we still want to
394   customize the interpretation in specific domains to ensure that the interpretation information is more
395   in line with the norms of the human mind and thus enhance the model’s performance. Therefore, we
396   introduce the one-shot method by manually constructing a sample of the CoT, so that the model can
397   generate the interpretation information and the final output based on the sample. The motivation for
398
      the one-shot approach is that we believe that the content generated by the LLM based on a sample
      that conforms to the logic of the human mind will better contribute to the completion of the final
399
      task.

517   6           THEORETICAL ANALYSIS
518
519
520
      In this section, our goal is to demonstrate that the category descriptions generated by LLM can
      provide useful information for predicting label categories. Specifically, if the obtained category
521
      descriptions can faithfully represent the information of each category, then they are useful. At the
522
      same time, the LLM is non-redundant, as it can provide information that X cannot provide. Let θ
523   be the textual category descriptions generated by LLM; Hl are the embeddings of category from the
524   LLM; X are the input of graph structure embeddings, y is the target and H(·|·) is the conditional
525   entropy. The specific proof process can be found in Appendix A.
526
527   Theorem. Given the following conditions: 1) Fidelity: θ can faithfully represent the information of
528
      Hl such that H(Hl |θ) = ϵ, with ϵ > 0; 2)Non-redundancy: Hl contains information not present in
                                            ′      ′

529   X, that is, H(y|X, Hl ) = H(y|X) − ϵ , with ϵ > ϵ. Then it follows that H(y|X, θ) < H(y|X).

533
534   This paper introduces Verbalized Graph Representation Learning (VGRL), a novel approach to
535   text-attributed graph learning that ensures full interpretability by representing learned parameters
536   as textual descriptions instead of continuous vectors. This method enhances transparency and user
537   understanding of the decision-making process, fostering greater trust in the model’s outputs. While
538   the current application is limited to foundational graph learning paradigms, VGRL shows promise
539   for broader use in more complex models, offering potential advancements in explainable AI and
      graph-based learning systems.

      In this section, our goal is to demonstrate that the category descriptions generated by LLM can
      provide useful information for predicting label categories. We formulate our theorem as follows:
708
709
710   Theorem. Given the following conditions:
711
      1) Fidelity: θ can faithfully represent the information of Hl such that
712                                           H(Hl |θ) = ϵ, ϵ > 0;                                       (5)
713
714
      2)Non-redundancy: Hl contains information not present in X, that is
                                                                   ′   ′
715                                    H(y|X, Hl ) = H(y|X) − ϵ , ϵ > ϵ;                                 (6)
716
717   Then we can obtain:
718                                          H(y|X, θ) < H(y|X).                                         (7)
719
      where θ be the textual category descriptions generated by LLM ; Hl are the embeddings of category
720
      from the LLM ; X are the input of graph structure embeddings, y is the target and H(·|·) is the
721   conditional entropy.
722
723   Proof. We aim to demonstrate that H(y|X, θ) < H(y|X), the process is following:
724   Start with:
725                                            H(y|X, θ)                                                 (8)
726
      We decompose the original expression Equation 8 into two parts based on the properties of entropy:
727
728                              H(y|X, θ) = H(y|X, Hl , θ) + I(y; Hl |X, θ)                             (9)
729
      Based on the definition of mutual information, we can obtain:
730
731                              I(y; Hl |X, θ) = H(Hl |X, θ) − H(Hl |X, θ, y)                          (10)
732
      Due to the non-negativity of conditional entropy, we have:
733
734                                       I(y; Hl |X, θ) ≤ H(Hl |X, θ)                                  (11)
735
      By substituting Equation 11 into Equation 9, we further obtain:
736
737                               H(y|X, θ) ≤ H(y|X, Hl , θ) + H(Hl |X, θ)                              (12)
738
      When conditional variables decrease, the conditional entropy increases; so we have:
739
740                                  H(y|X, θ) ≤ H(y|X, Hl ) + H(Hl |θ)                                 (13)
741
      Applying the two aforementioned conditions and substituting Equations 5 and 6 into Equation 12,
742   we can obtain:                                          ′
743                            H(y|X, θ) ≤ H(y|X) + ϵ − ϵ < H(y|X)                               (14)
744
      The conclusion is thus proven.
