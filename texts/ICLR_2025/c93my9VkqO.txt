009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Relational databases, organized into tables connected by primary-foreign key re-
012            lationships, are widely used in industry. Companies leverage this data to build
013            highly accurate, feature-engineered tabular models—often using boosted decision
014            trees—to predict key metrics such as customer transactions and product revenues.
015            However, these models need frequent retraining as new data is introduced, which
016
               is both expensive and time-consuming. Despite this, by being the result of ex-
               tensive engineering effort, they remain difficult to outperform using generalist
017
               methods, like Temporal Graph Neural Networks (TGNNs) trained over the same
018
               relational data. Rather than attempting to replace tabular models with general-
019            ist approaches, we propose to combine the strengths of tabular models and static
020            Graph Neural Networks (GNNs). GNNs offer better speed and scalability than
021            TGNNs, and, as we argue, the primary strength of graph representation learning
022            for these tasks does not lie in modeling temporal dynamics—something highly-
023            engineered tabular models excel at—but in capturing complex relationships within
024            the database, which are hard to featurize. Our approach integrates all predictive
025            embeddings of all tabular models developed for various tasks into a single static
026            GNN framework. Experimental results on the RelBench benchmark show that
027
               our approach achieves a performance improvement of up to 33% and an inference
               speedup of up to 1050x, making it highly suitable for real-time inference.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Relational databases are extensively used in industry due to their flexibility, extensibility, and
033   speed (Wheeler et al., 2000; Kremer, 2006; Johnson et al., 2016). The information, organized into ta-
034   bles, not only record entities, their features, and their relations (via primary and foreign key relation-
035   ships), but also records events, such as transactions, with their associated timestamps. The tabular
036   format simplifies data maintenance and enhances data accessibility and retrieval through query lan-
037   guages like SQL (Codd, 1970; Chamberlin & Boyce, 1974). Due to the presence of both timestepped
038   events and relations, tasks over relational databases tend to be both temporal and relational, such
039
      as forecasting future product sales and predicting future customer purchases and churn (Robinson
      et al., 2024). For decades, companies have been building in-house predictive models over rela-
040
      tional databases by creating meticulously engineered relational and temporal features (Dong & Liu,
041
      2018; Ganguli & Thakur, 2020) that flatten the complex temporal-relational data into a single table,
042   which is then used as input for tabular models like XGBoost and LightGBM (Ke et al., 2017; Chen
043   & Guestrin, 2016). However, incorporating new data sources or addressing new tasks with these
044   tabular models is both costly and time-consuming (Heaton, 2016).
045
      Generalist models such as Temporal Graph Neural Networks (TGNNs) (Rossi et al., 2020; Dileo
046
      et al., 2023; Cini et al., 2023; Longa et al., 2023) promise to offer a more cost-and-time effective
047
      alternative to tabular models (Fey et al., 2024), and have shown some early success (Robinson et al.,
048   2024). These generalist approaches are applicable as relational databases can be naturally repre-
049   sented as temporal, heterogeneous graphs, where each row in a table corresponds to an attributed
050   node, and the edges are defined by primary-foreign key relationships. However, generalist models
051   often struggle to outperform tabular models, which benefit from years of extensive engineering and
052   domain-specific optimizations and inside knowledge. Therefore, several important questions arise:
053   (1) Can we integrate existing tabular models with generalist approaches (GNNs) to leverage their
      complementary strengths (speed, accuracy, and flexibility)? (2) Could such a hybrid framework

      Contributions. Our work proposes a hybrid tabular-generalist modeling framework for predictive
072   tasks on relational databases, which addresses the challenges of using existing tabular models while
073   effectively dealing with temporal scalability in large relational databases. Specifically, the knowl-
074   edge extracted from each existing tabular model is distilled into separate multi-layer perceptrons

079   & Ribeiro (2022), which demonstrates that if a separate model captures the temporal dynamics of

081

082
      efficiency but, as shown in experiments on the RelBench benchmark (Robinson et al., 2024), allows
083   this lightweight model to outperform both standard tabular models and computationally expensive

088

089   machine learning pipelines.

145   2.2   ML TASKS IN R ELATIONAL DATABASES
146
147   Many real-world machine learning tasks on relational databases consist on predicting the future
148   state of specific entities. For instance, on rel-hm, one of the key tasks is forecasting the total
149   sales of an article for the upcoming week. This is relevant to H&M as it enables effective inventory
150   management, optimizes stock replenishment, and helps in crafting targeted marketing strategies to
151   maximize revenue and reduce the risk of article shortages or overstock situations. Predictive tasks
152   often involve entities from dimension tables and require the specification of a seed time, which
153
      is defined as “the present” in the prediction task. Consider the task of predicting the churn (no
      transactions) for a customer (v). Given a seed time t in days (“the present”), we would like to
154
      predict the churn of v over the “next week”, i.e., in the interval [t, t + 7] in the database. Classical
155
      machine learning methods, such as gradient-boosted decision trees (GBDT), dominate relational
156   database modeling due to their superior performance on these tabular data tasks (Gorishniy et al.,
157   2021; Shwartz-Ziv & Armon, 2022). When paired with manually-designed feature engineering,
158   tabular methods like LightGBM or XGBoost represent the industry’s go-to methods for constructing
159   predictive models on relational databases (Heaton, 2016). Unfortunately, improving these tabular
160   models is both costly and time-consuming, particularly when incorporating new data sources or
161   addressing new tasks. In recent years, the intrinsic structure of relational databases has attracted
      the use of generalist models such as TGNNs. We formalize next how a relational database can be

233     knowledge from tabular methods, and 2) construct a much lighter graph by utilizing the time-

344   4    R ELATED W ORK
345
346   Relational databases are integral to a wide-range of applications, from e-commerce plat-
347   forms (Agrawal et al., 2001) and social media networks (Almabdy, 2018) to banking sys-
348   tems (Aditya et al., 2002) and healthcare services (Park et al., 2014). Tree-based methods, especially
349   XGBoost, remain the preferred methods for learning on these relational data (Fey et al., 2024). In-
350   deed, although efforts to design deep learning architectures for tabular data has shown promising
351   results (Huang et al., 2020; Arik & Pfister, 2021; Gorishniy et al., 2021; 2022; Chen et al., 2023), no
352   deep-learning model has yet been demonstrated to clearly outperform tree-based methods on tabular
353   data (Shwartz-Ziv & Armon, 2022; McElfresh et al., 2024).
354   Among the deep learning models proposed for relational data, graph neural networks (GNNs)
355   have also gained attention, with models specifically designed to handle relations among
356   nodes (Schlichtkrull et al., 2018; Cvitkovic, 2020; Zahradnı́k et al., 2023; Ferrini et al., 2024). Re-
357   cently, (Fey et al., 2024; Robinson et al., 2024) proposed a general end-to-end learnable framework
358   for solving tasks on relational data that incorporates a temporal dimension. This proposed approach
359   bridges the gap between relational GNNs and temporal GNNs (Kapoor et al., 2020; Gao et al., 2021;
360
      Sankar et al., 2020; Cui et al., 2019; Zhao et al., 2019; Lv et al., 2020; Pareja et al., 2020; Jin et al.,
      2020; Manessi et al., 2020; Rossi et al., 2020; Gao & Ribeiro, 2022; Heeg & Scholtes, 2023; Longa
361
      et al., 2023; von Pichowski et al., 2024; Marisca et al., 2024; Beddar-Wiesing et al., 2024), introduc-
362
      ing R DL which serves as both our starting point and main competitor in the development of more
363   effective and light-way GNN-based solutions for relational databases.
364
365   Other works have explored the combination of GNNs with boosting methods (Ivanov &
366
      Prokhorenkova, 2021; Sun et al.; Shi et al., 2021; Zheng et al., 2021; Tang et al., 2024; Deng et al.,
      2021).However, they focus on improving standard GNNs for graph datasets that are not derived
367
      from relational databases, and therefore lack temporal and heterogeneous components. Moreover,
368
      while these methods often aim to replace trees with GNNs within a boosting setup, we instead in-
369   corporate pretrained tree-based models as a dedicated component for modeling temporality. This is
370   complemented by a static GNN, which captures the structural relationships within the data.
371

      not only maintains competitive accuracy but outperforms the best baselines in many scenarios, all

      5.1   DATASETS
388
389
      For these experiments we consider four datasets from the RelBench benchmark (Robinson et al.,
390   2024). The rel-hm relational database contains customer purchase histories and product metadata
391   from the brand’s online shopping network. The rel-f1 database provides comprehensive data on
392   Formula 1 racing since 1950, including information about drivers, constructors, circuits, and race
393   results. The rel-event database is derived from the Hangtime app and includes anonymized
394   user actions, event metadata, and social relations data. The rel-stack dataset is sourced from
395   the stats-exchange site, comprising user activity, posts, comments, and voting histories. Finally,
396   rel-amazon stores information about product, users and reviews from Amazon platform. For
397   each dataset, we considered several tasks, which include both regression and classification tasks
398
      aimed at making future predictions at the entity level of the tables.
399
400   5.2   TRE E LGNN C ONFIGURATION AND BASELINES
401
402
      TRE E LGNN is configured using two models: (a) the pretrained feature-engineered L IGHT GBM
      model from RelBench (Robinson et al., 2024); and (b) a static GNN (HGS AGE) that is a static
403
      HeteroGraphSAGE (Hamilton et al., 2017), identical to the (static) GNN used at each timestep in
404
      the R DL model (Robinson et al., 2024).
405
406   We evaluate TRE E LGNN against two baselines which are the strongest-performing methods in
407   Robinson et al. (2024): the pretrained feature-engineered L IGHT GBM model from RelBench and
408
      the R DL model. Since TRE E LGNN uses the additional features derived through distillation, to
      ensure a fair comparison, we also create two versions of R DL that incorporate these additional
409
      features. First, we added the pointwise predictions of L IGHT GBM as extra features on the nodes
410
      (R DL w.P.). Second, we introduced the embeddings obtained from the distillation process, as done
411   with TRE E LGNN (R DL w.D.). The architectural details of the different models are provided in
412   Appendix B, the specifics of the distillation process can be found in Appendix C, while the details
413   about the batch size used for calculating runtime can be found in Appendix F.4.

481   Comparing training and inference times. TRE E LGNN achieves a substantial reduction in
482   training times compared to R DL across all datasets and tasks, with speedups ranging from 100x
483   to 2800x (see Figure 4 (b) and Figure 5 (b)). This dramatic speedup is due primarily to two factors:
484   (i) the effect of the number of timestamps of the tasks, which does not affect TRE E LGNN and
485   significantly affects R DL (see Figure 2) and (ii) the much smaller number of model parameters in
      TRE E LGNN (see Appendix B). Unlike R DL, where the training graph G(V≤t ) is constructed using


                                                                           9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      entities and relations of all the time up to t, TRE E LGNN’s graph G(Vt ) only uses the information
487   at the timestamp t before the inference, so that the size of its graph is independent of the number of
488   snapshots.

491

492
      Figure 4 (c) and Figure 5 (c)). These speedups largely compensate for the occasional limited drop
493   in classification performance (Table 2). Indeed, the largest decrease in classification performance is
494   2.1% in the user-badge task of the rel-stack dataset, where TRE E LGNN has a 95-fold increase in in-
495   ference speed over R DL. This substantial improvement makes TRE E LGNN particularly well-suited
496   for relational database ML applications, that demand low-latency and low-computational overhead.
497   As a result, TRE E LGNN is not only preferred for scenarios requiring rapid response times but is
498   also highly practical for deployment in industrial settings where pretrained tabular models already
499   exist and computational resources and inference latency are critical constraints.

527   6    C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this work, we introduced the TRE E LGNN framework, a novel relational deep learning method
530
      that integrates tabular models and graph neural networks in a time-then-graph framework. Our re-
      sults on the RelBench benchmark demonstrate that leveraging the strengths of both tabular models
531
      and static GNNs can significantly improve predictive accuracy and efficiency compared to using
532
      either approach in isolation. Specifically, by embedding predictive features from existing tabular
533   models into a unified static GNN framework, we achieve substantial performance gains across mul-
534   tiple tasks at a fraction of the computational cost of current R DL approaches.
535
536
      The TRE E LGNN framework simplifies the integration of new data into predictive workflows using
      a static GNN, enabling real-time inference and enhancing its practicality for industrial applications.
537
      Additionally, this work demonstrates the potential of graph representation learning to complement
538
      traditional tabular models, paving the way for future research on hybrid architectures that effectively
539   combine feature-engineered and graph-based representations for complex relational data.

761

763   teractions with friends. The dataset contains anonymized data on user actions, event metadata, and
764   demographic information, as well as users’ social connections, allowing for an analysis of how these
765   relations influence behavior. No personally identifiable information is included in the dataset. The
766   entity predictive tasks on this database are:
767
             • user-attendance: Predict how many events each user will respond ”yes” or ”maybe” to
768
               within the next seven days.
769
770          • user-repeat: Determine whether a user will attend another event (by responding ”yes” or
771
               ”maybe”) within the next seven days, given they have attended an event in the last 14 days.
772          • user-ignore: Predict whether a user will ignore more than two event invitations within the
773            next seven days.

776
      1950 to the present. It includes comprehensive information on key stakeholders, such as drivers,

777

778
      qualifying positions, sprints, and pit stops. The entity predictive tasks on this database are:
779

781            the upcoming two months.
782          • driver-dnf: Predict whether a driver will fail to complete a race (DNF - Did Not Finish)
