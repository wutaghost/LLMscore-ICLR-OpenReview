                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               In generative modeling, tokenization simplifies complex data into compact, struc-
               tured representations, creating a more efficient, learnable space. For high-
011
               dimensional visual data, it reduces redundancy and emphasizes key features for
012
               high-quality generation. Current visual tokenization methods rely on a traditional
013            autoencoder framework, where the encoder compresses data into latent representa-
014            tions, and the decoder reconstructs the original input. In this work, we offer a new
015            perspective by proposing denoising as decoding, shifting from single-step recon-
016            struction to iterative refinement. Specifically, we replace the decoder with a diffu-
017            sion process that iteratively refines noise to recover the original image, guided by
018            the latents provided by the encoder. We evaluate our approach by assessing both
019            reconstruction (rFID) and generation quality (FID), comparing it to state-of-the-
020            art autoencoding approach. We hope this work offers new insights into integrating
021
               iterative generation and autoencoding for improved compression and generation.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Generative modeling aims to capture the underlying distribution of training data, enabling realistic
027
      sample generation during inference. A key preprocessing step is tokenization, which converts raw
      data into discrete tokens or continuous latent representations. In vision tasks, continuous latents are
028
      typically produced by an encoder, whereas discrete tokens are commonly derived from embeddings
029
      in language tasks. These compact representations allow models to efficiently learn complex patterns,
030   enhancing the quality of generated outputs.
031
032   Two dominant paradigms in modern generative modeling are autoregression (Radford et al., 2018)
033
      and diffusion (Ho et al., 2020). Tokenization is an essential in both: discrete tokens direct step-
      by-step conditional generation in autoregressive models, while continuous latents streamline the
034
      denoising process in diffusion models. Empirical results across language (Achiam et al., 2023;
035
      Anil et al., 2023; Dubey et al., 2024) and vision (Baldridge et al., 2024; Esser et al., 2024; Brooks
036   et al., 2024) tasks show that tokenization—whether discrete or continuous—improves generative
037   performance. We focus on tokenization for latent diffusion models, which excel at generating high-
038   dimensional visual data.
039
      Given its central role in both paradigms, understanding how tokenization works is essential. In
040
      language processing, tokenization is relatively straightforward, involving segmenting text into dis-
041
      crete units such as words, subwords, or characters (Sennrich et al., 2015; Kudo & Richardson,
042   2018; Kudo, 2018). However, tokenization in visual domains poses greater challenges due to the
043   continuous, high-dimensional, and redundant nature. Instead of direct segmentation, compact rep-
044   resentations are typically learned using an autoencoding (Hinton & Salakhutdinov, 2006). Despite
045   rapid advancements in visual generation techniques, the design of tokenizers has received relatively
046   little attention. This is evident in the minimal evolution of tokenizers used in state-of-the-art models,
047   which have remained largely unchanged since their initial introduction (Van Den Oord et al., 2017).
048   In this paper, we address this gap by revisiting the widely adopted visual autoencoding formula-
049   tion (Esser et al., 2021), aiming to achieve higher compression rates and improved reconstruction
050   quality, thereby enhancing generation quality of downstream generative models. Our key idea is to
051   rethink the traditional autoencoding pipeline, which typically involves an encoder that compresses
052   the input into a latent representation, followed by a decoder that reconstructs the original data in a
053   single step. In our approach, we replace the deterministic decoder with a diffusion process. Here,
      the encoder still compresses the input into a latent representation, but instead of a one-step recon-

054
      struction, the diffusion model iteratively denoises the data to recover the original. This reframing
055   turns the reconstruction phase into a step-by-step refinement, where the diffusion model, guided by
056   the latent representation, progressively restores the original data.
057
058
      To implement our approach effectively, several key design factors must be carefully considered.
      First, the architectural design must ensure effective conditioning of the diffusion decoder on the
059
      latent representations provided by the encoder. Second, the objectives for training the diffu-
060
      sion decoder should also explore potential synergies with traditional autoencoding losses, such as
061   LPIPS (Zhang et al., 2018) and GAN (Esser et al., 2021). Finally, diffusion-specific design choices
062   are crucial, including: (1) the model parameterization, which defines the prediction target for the
063   diffusion decoder; (2) the noise schedule, which dictates the optimization trajectory; and (3) the dis-
064   tribution of time steps during training and testing, which balances noise levels during learning and
065   generation. Our study systematically explores all these components under controlled experiments.
066
      In summary, our contributions are as follows: (1) introducing a novel approach that fully leverages
067   the capabilities of diffusion decoders for more practical diffusion-based autoencoding, achieving
068   strong rFID, high sampling efficiency (within 1 to 3 steps), and robust resolution generalization; (2)
069   presenting key design choices to optimize performance; and (3) conducting extensive controlled ex-
070   periments that demonstrate our method achieves high-quality reconstruction and generation results,
071   outperforming leading visual auto-encoding paradigms.

      2    BACKGROUND
074
075   We start by briefly reviewing the basic concepts required to understand the proposed method. A
076   more detailed summary of related work is deferred to Appendix A.
077
078
      Visual tokenization. To achieve efficient and scalable high-resolution image synthesis, common
      generative models, including autoregressive models (Razavi et al., 2019; Esser et al., 2021; Chang
079
      et al., 2022) and diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022), are typically trained in a low-resolution
080
      latent space by first downsampling the input image using a tokenizer. The tokenizer is generally
081   implemented as a convolutional autoencoder consisting of an encoder, E, and a decoder, G. Specif-
082   ically, the encoder, E, compresses an input image x ∈ RH×W ×3 into a set of latent codes (i.e.,
083   tokens), E(x) = z ∈ RH/f ×W/f ×nz , where f is the downsampling factor and nz is the latent
084   channel dimensions. The decoder, G, then reconstructs the input from z, such that G(z) = x.
085
      Training an autoencoder primarily involves several losses: reconstruction loss Lrec , perceptual loss
086
      (LPIPS) LLPIPS , and adversarial loss Ladv . The reconstruction loss minimizes pixel differences (i.e.,
087
      typically measured by the `1 or `2 distance) between x and G(z). The LPIPS loss (Zhang et al.,
088   2018) enforces high-level structural similarities between inputs and reconstructions by minimizing
089   differences in their intermediate features extracted from a pre-trained VGG network (Simonyan &
090   Zisserman, 2015). The adversarial loss (Esser et al., 2021) introduces a discriminator, D, which
091   encourages more photorealistic outputs by distinguishing between real images, D(x), and recon-
092   structions, D(G(z)). The final training objective is a weighted combination of these losses:
093                                LVAE = Lrec + λLPIPS · LLPIPS + λadv · Ladv ,                            (1)
094
095
      where the λ values are weighting coefficients. In this paper, we consider the autoencoder optimized
      by Eq. 1 as our main competing baseline (Esser et al., 2021), as it has become a standard tokenizer
096
      training scheme widely adopted in state-of-the-art image and video generative models (Chang et al.,
097
      2022; Rombach et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2022; 2023; Kondratyuk et al., 2024; Esser et al., 2024).

147   3     M ETHOD
148
149   We introduce  -VAE, with an overview provided in Figure 1. The core idea is to replace single-step,
150   deterministic decoding with an iterative, stochastic denoising process. By reframing autoencoding as
151   a conditional denoising problem, we anticipate two key improvements: (1) more effective generation
152   of latent representations, allowing the downstream latent diffusion model to learn more efficiently,
153   and (2) enhanced decoding quality due to the iterative and stochastic nature of the diffusion process.
154   We systematically explore the design space of model architecture, objectives, and diffusion training
155   configurations, including noise and time scheduling. While this work primarily focuses on gener-
156   ating continuous latents for latent diffusion models, the concept of iterative decoding could also be
157   extended to discrete tokens, which we leave for future exploration.

247
      Noise scheduling. In diffusion models, noise scheduling involves progressively adding noise to
248   the data over time by defining specific functions for αt and σt in Eq. 2. This process is crucial
249                                                       α2
      as it determines the signal-to-noise ratio, λt = σ2t , which directly influences training dynamics.
250                                                        t

251   Noise scheduling can also be adjusted by scaling the intermediate states xt with a constant factor
252
      γ ∈ (0, 1], which shifts the signal-to-noise ratio downward. This makes training more challenging
      over time while preserving the shape of the trajectory (Chen, 2023).
253
254   In this work, we define αt and σt according to rectified flow formulation, while also scaling xt by
255   γ, with the value chosen empirically. However, when γ 6= 1, the variance of xt changes, which can
256   degrade performance (Karras et al., 2022). To address this, we normalize the denoising input xt by
257   its variance after scaling, ensuring it preserves unit variance over time (Chen, 2023).
258   Time scheduling. Another important aspect in diffusion models is time scheduling for both training
259   and sampling, controlled by π(t) during training and ∆t during sampling, as outlined in Eq. 3 and
260   Eq. 4. A common choice for π(t) is the uniform distribution U(0, T ), which applies equal weight
261   to each time step during training. Similarly, uniform time steps ∆t = T1 are typically used for
262   sampling. However, to improve model performance on more challenging time steps and focus on
263   noisy regimes during sampling, the time scheduling strategy should be adjusted accordingly.
264   In this work, we sample t from a logit-normal distribution (Atchison & Shen, 1980), which empha-
265   sizes intermediate timesteps (Esser et al., 2024). During sampling, we apply a reversed logarithm
266   mapping function ρlog , defined as:

      4     E XPERIMENTS
271
272
      We evaluate the effectiveness of  -VAE on image reconstruction and generation tasks using the Im-
273   ageNet (Deng et al., 2009). The VAE formulation by Esser et al. (2021) serves as a strong baseline
274   due to its widespread use in modern image generative models (Rombach et al., 2022; Peebles & Xie,
275   2023; Esser et al., 2024). We perform controlled experiments to compare reconstruction and gener-
276   ation quality by varying model scale, latent dimension, downsampling rates, and input resolution.
277
      Model configurations. We use the encoder and discriminator architectures from VQGAN (Esser
278
      et al., 2021) and keep consistent across all models. The decoder design follows BigGAN (Brock
279   et al., 2019) for VAE and from ADM (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021) for  -VAE. Additionally, we
280   experiment with the DiT architecture (Peebles & Xie, 2023) for  -VAE. To evaluate model scaling,
281   we test five decoder variants: base (B), medium (M), large (L), extra-large (XL), and huge (H), by
282   adjusting width and depth accordingly. Further model specifications are provided in Appendix B.1.
283
      We experiment with two encoder configurations: (1) a light-weight version with 6M parameters, a
284
      downsampling rate of 16, and 8 latent channels; (2) a standard version based on Stable Diffusion
285   with 34M parameters, a downsampling rate of 8, and 4 latent channels. Configuration (1) is in-
286   tentionally designed as a more challenging setup and serves as the primary focus of analysis in the
287   paper. For this configuration, we further explore downsampling rates of 4, 8, and 32, as well as latent
288   dimensions of 4, 16, and 32 channels. Both VAE and  -VAE are trained to reconstruct 128×128 im-
289   ages under these controlled conditions. Additionally, we validate our method in the standard setup
290   of Configuration (2) (detailed in Appendix C.2), where we compare it against state-of-the-art VAEs.
291   Evaluation. We evaluate the autoencoder on both reconstruction and generation quality using
292   Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) (Heusel et al., 2017) as the primary metric, computed on 10,000
293   validation images. For reconstruction quality (rFID), FID is computed at both training and higher
294   resolutions to assess generalization across resolutions. For generation quality (FID), we generate la-
295   tents from the trained autoencoders and use them to train the DiT-XL/2 latent generative model (Pee-
296   bles & Xie, 2023). This latent model remains fixed across all generation experiments, meaning im-
297   proved autoencoder latents directly enhance generation quality. We also report Inception Score (IS)
298   (Salimans et al., 2016) and Precision/Recall (Kynkäänniemi et al., 2019) as secondary metrics.

      struction quality (rFID) and sampling efficiency (NFE). The results are summarized in Table 3.
401
402   Baseline. Our evaluation begins with a baseline model: an autoencoder with a diffusion decoder,
403   trained solely using the score matching objective. This baseline follows the vanilla diffusion setup
404   from Ho et al. (2020), including their UNet architecture, parameterization, and training configura-
405   tions, while extending to a conditional form as described in Eq. 8. Building on this baseline, we
406
      progressively introduce updates and evaluate the impact of our proposed method.
407   Impact of proposals. In (a), transitioning from standard diffusion to rectified flow (Liu et al.,
408   2023) straightens the optimization path, resulting in significant gains in rFID scores and NFE. In
409   (b), adopting a logit-normal time step distribution optimizes rectified flow training (Esser et al.,
410   2024), further improving both rFID scores and NFE. In (c), updates to the UNet architecture (Nichol
411   & Dhariwal, 2021) contribute to enhanced rFID scores. In (d), LPIPS loss is applied to match
412
      reconstructions x̂t0 with real images x0 . In (e), adversarial trajectory matching loss aligns (x̂t0 , xt )
      with (x0 , xt ), the target transition in rectified flow. Both objectives improve model understanding
413
      of the underlying optimization trajectory, significantly enhancing rFID scores and NFE.
414
415   Up to this point, with the full implementation of Eq. 1, we can compare our proposal with the
416   VAE (B) model, which achieves an rFID score of 11.15. Our model, with a score of 8.24, already
417   surpasses this baseline. We further improve performance by optimizing noise and time scheduling
418
      within our framework, as described next.
419   In (f), scaling xt reduces the signal-to-noise ratio (Chen, 2023), presenting challenges for more ef-
420   fective learning during training. Figure 3 (middle) demonstrates that a scaling factor of 0.6 produces
421   the best results. Finally, in (g), reversed logarithmic time step spacing during inference allows for
422   denser evaluations in noisier regions. Figure 3 (right) demonstrates that this method provides more
423   stable sampling in the lower NFE regime compared to the original uniform spacing.

      Iterative and stochastic decoding. A key question within the rate-distortion-perception trade-off
459
      is whether the iterative, stochastic nature of diffusion decoding offers advantages over traditional
460   single-step, deterministic methods (Kingma, 2013). The strengths of diffusion (Ho et al., 2020) lie
461   in its iterative process, which progressively refines the latent space for more accurate reconstructions,
462   while stochasticity allows for capturing complex variations within the distribution. Although itera-
463   tive methods may appear less efficient, our formulation is optimized to achieve optimal results in just
464   three steps and also supports single-step decoding, ensuring decoding efficiency remains practical
465   (see Figure 3 (left)). While stochasticity might suggest the risk of “hallucination” in reconstructions,
466   the outputs remain faithful to the underlying distribution by design, producing perceptually plausible
467   results. This advantage is particularly evident under extreme compression scenarios (see Figure 4),
468
      with the degree of stochasticity adapting based on compression levels (see Figure 5).
469   Scalability. As discussed in Section 4.1, our diffusion-based decoding method maintains the reso-
470   lution generalizability typically found in standard autoencoders. This feature is highly practical: the
471   autoencoder is trained on lower-resolution images, while the subsequent latent generative model is
472   trained on latents derived from higher-resolution inputs. However, we acknowledge that memory
473   overhead and throughput become concerns with our UNet-based diffusion decoder, especially for
474
      high-resolution inputs. This challenge becomes more pronounced as models, datasets, or resolu-
      tions scale up. A promising future direction is patch-based diffusion (Ding et al., 2024; Wang et al.,
475
      2024b), which partitions the input into smaller, independently processed patches. This approach has
476
      the potential to reduce memory usage and enable faster parallel decoding.

479   6   C ONCLUSION
480
481   We present  -VAE, an effective visual tokenization framework that introduces a diffusion decoder
482   into standard autoencoders, turning single-step decoding into a multi-step probabilistic process. By
483   exploring key design choices in modeling, objectives, and diffusion training, we demonstrate signif-
484   icant performance improvements. Our approach outperforms traditional visual autoencoders in both
485   reconstruction and generation quality, particularly in high-compression scenarios. We hope our con-
      cept of iterative generation during decoding inspires further advancements in visual autoencoding.

      A    R ELATED WORK
811
812
813   Image tokenization. Image tokenization is crucial for effective generative modeling, transform-
814
      ing images into compact, structured representations. A common approach employs an autoen-
      coder framework (Hinton & Salakhutdinov, 2006), where the encoder compresses images into low-
815
      dimensional latent representations, and the decoder reconstructs the original input. These latent
816
      representations can be either discrete commonly used in autoregressive models (Van den Oord et al.,
817   2016; Van Den Oord et al., 2017; Chen et al., 2020; Chang et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2023; Kondratyuk
818   et al., 2024), or continuous, as found in diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Dhariwal & Nichol,
819   2021; Rombach et al., 2022; Peebles & Xie, 2023; Gupta et al., 2023; Brooks et al., 2024). The
820   foundational form of visual autoencoding today originates from Van Den Oord et al. (2017). While
821   advancements have been made in modeling (Yu et al., 2022; 2024b), objectives (Zhang et al., 2018;
822   Karras et al., 2019; Esser et al., 2021), and quantization methods (Yu et al., 2024a; Zhao et al., 2024),
823   the core encoding-and-decoding scheme remains largely the same.
824   In this work, we propose a new perspective by replacing the traditional decoder with a diffusion
825   process. Specifically, our new formulation retains the encoder but introduces a conditional diffusion
826   decoder. Within this framework, we systematically study various design choices, resulting in a
827   significantly enhanced autoencoding setup.
828
      Additionally, we refer to the recent work MAR (Li et al., 2024), which leverages diffusion to model
829   per-token distribution in autoregressive frameworks. In contrast, our approach models the overall
830   input distribution in autoencoders using diffusion. This difference leads to distinct applications of
831   diffusion during generation. For instance, MAR generates samples autoregressively, decoding each
832   token iteratively using diffusion, token by token. In our method, we first sample all tokens from the
833   downstream generative model and then decode them iteratively using diffusion as a whole.
834
      Image compression. Our work shares similarities with recent image compression approaches that
835   leverage diffusion models. For example, Hoogeboom et al. (2023a); Birodkar et al. (2024) use dif-
836   fusion to refine autoencoder residuals, enhancing high-frequency details. Yang & Mandt (2024)
837   employs a diffusion decoder conditioned on quantized discrete codes and omits the GAN loss.
838   However, these methods primarily focus on the traditional rate-distortion tradeoff, balancing rate
839   (compactness) and distortion (input fidelity) (Shannon et al., 1959), with the goal of storing and
840   transmitting data efficiently without significant loss of information.
841   In this work, we emphasize perception (distribution fidelity) alongside the rate-distortion tradeoff,
842   ensuring that reconstructions more closely align with the overall data distribution (Heusel et al.,
843   2017; Zhang et al., 2018; Blau & Michaeli, 2019), thereby enhancing the decoded results from the
844   sampled latents of downstream generative models. We achieve this by directly integrating the diffu-
845   sion process into the decoder, unlike Hoogeboom et al. (2023a); Birodkar et al. (2024). Moreover,
846   unlike Yang & Mandt (2024), we do not impose strict rate-distortion regularization in the latent
847   space and allow the GAN loss to synergize with our approach.
848   Diffusion decoder. Several studies (Preechakul et al., 2022; Shi et al., 2022; Pernias et al., 2024;
849   Nguyen & Tran, 2024; Sauer et al., 2024; Luo et al., 2023) have explored diffusion decoders con-
850   ditioned on compressed latents of the input, which are relevant to our work. We outline the key
851   differences between these works and  -VAE: First, prior works have not fully leveraged the synergy
852   between diffusion decoders and standard VAE training objectives. In this work, we enhance state-of-
853   the-art VAE objectives by replacing the reconstruction loss with a score matching loss and adapting
854   LPIPS and GAN losses to ensure compatibility with diffusion decoders. These changes yield signif-
855
      icant improvements in autoencoding performance, as evidenced by lower rFID scores and faster in-
      ference. Second, we are the first to investigate various parameterizations (e.g., epsilon and velocity)
856
      and demonstrate that modern velocity parameterization, coupled with optimized train and test-time
857
      noise scheduling, provides substantial benefits. These enhancements improve both reconstruction
858   performance and sampling efficiency. Third, previous diffusion-based decoders (Preechakul et al.,
859   2022; Shi et al., 2022; Pernias et al., 2024), which often rely on ad-hoc techniques like distillation
860   or consistency regularization to speed up inference (Nguyen & Tran, 2024; Sauer et al., 2024; Luo
861   et al., 2023), our approach achieves fast decoding (1 to 3 steps) without such techniques. This is
862   made possible by integrating our proposed objectives and parameterizations. Last but not least,
863    -VAE exhibits strong resolution generalization capabilities, a key property of standard VAEs. In
      contrast, models like DiffusionAE (Preechakul et al., 2022) and DiVAE (Shi et al., 2022) either lack


                                                         16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      this ability or are inherently limited. For example, DiVAE’s bottleneck add/concat design restricts
865   its capacity to generalize across resolutions.
866
867   Another closely related work, SWYCC (Birodkar et al., 2024), also explores joint learning of con-
868
      tinuous encoders and decoders using a diffusion model. However, SWYCC differs fundamentally
      from our approach: it replaces the GAN loss with a diffusion-based loss, while we focus on identify-
869
      ing optimal synergies between traditional autoencoding losses (including GAN loss) and diffusion-
870
      based decoding. Our goal is to identify an optimal strategy for combining these elements, rather
871   than simply substituting one for another.
872
873   Image generation. Recent advances in image generation span a wide range of approaches, including
874
      VAEs (Kingma, 2013), GANs (Goodfellow et al., 2014), autoregressive models (Chen et al., 2020)
      and diffusion models (Song et al., 2021; Ho et al., 2020). Among these, diffusion models have
875
      emerged as the leading approach for generating high-dimensional data such as images (Saharia
876
      et al., 2022a; Baldridge et al., 2024; Esser et al., 2024) and videos (Brooks et al., 2024; Gupta et al.,
877   2023), where the gradual refinement of global structure is crucial. The current focus in diffusion-
878   based generative models lies in advancing architectures (Rombach et al., 2022; Peebles & Xie, 2023;
879   Hoogeboom et al., 2023b), parameterizations (Karras et al., 2022; Kingma & Gao, 2024; Ma et al.,
880   2024; Esser et al., 2024), or better training dynamics (Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021; Chen, 2023; Chen
881   et al., 2023). However, tokenization, an essential component in modern diffusion models, often
882   receives less attention.
883   In this work, we focus on providing compact continuous latents without applying quantization dur-
884   ing autoencoder training (Rombach et al., 2022), as they have been shown to be effective in state-
885   of-the-art latent diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022; Saharia et al., 2022a; Peebles & Xie, 2023;
886   Esser et al., 2024; Baldridge et al., 2024). We compare our autoencoding performance against the
887   baseline approach (Esser et al., 2021) using the DiT framework (Peebles & Xie, 2023) as the down-
888   stream generative model.

954   Conditioning. In addition to injecting conditioning via channel-wise concatenation, we explore
955   providing conditioning to the diffusion model by adaptive group normalization (AdaGN) (Nichol
956   & Dhariwal, 2021; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021). To achieve this, we resize the conditioning (i.e.,
957
      encoded latents) via bilinear sampling to the desired resolution of each stage in the U-Net model, and
      incorporates it into each residual block after a group normalization operation (Wu & He, 2018). This
958
      is similar to adaptive instance norm (Karras et al., 2019) and FiLM (Perez et al., 2018). We report the
959
      results in Table 5 (top), where we find that channel-wise concatenation and AdaGN obtain similar
960   reconstruction quality in terms of rFID. Because of the additional computational cost required by
961   AdaGN, we thus apply channel-wise concatenation in our model by default.
962
963
      Trajectory matching. The proposed denoising trajectory matching objective matches the start-to-
      end trajectory xt → x0 by default. One alternative choice is to directly matching the distribution
964
      of x̂t0 and x0 without coupling on xt . However, we find this formulation leads to unstable training
965
      and could not produce reasonable results. Here, we present the results when matching the trajectory
966   of xt → xt−∆t , which is commonly used in previous work (Xiao et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024a).
967   Specifically, for each timestep t during training, we randomly sample a step ∆t from (0, t). Then,
968   we construct the real trajectory by computing xt−∆t via Eq. 5 and concatenating it with xt , while
969   the fake trajectory is obtained in a similar way but using Eq. 10 instead. Table 5 (bottom) shows the
970   comparison. We observe that matching trajectory xt → x0 yields better performance than matching
971   trajectory xt → xt−∆t , confirming the effectiveness of the proposed objective which is designed
      for the rectified flow formulation.
