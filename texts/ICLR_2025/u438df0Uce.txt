011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Spiking neural network (SNN) has attracted great attention due to its great en-
014            ergy efficiency on neuromorphic hardware. By transferring the parameters of
015            pretrained artificial neural network (ANN) and utilizing the ANN quantization,
016
               recent works of ANN-SNN conversion can produce SNNs with close-to-ANN
               accuracy and low inference latency (known as the number of time-steps). Never-
017
               theless, existing works fail at providing theoretic equivalence between Quantized-
018
               ANN (QANN) and its converted SNN, while the SNN accuracy at small time-step
019            (i.e. Pareto-frontier) can be further improved. To solve the problems, this paper
020            proposes a novel conversion framework called SpikeZIP. The SpikeZIP utilizes the
021            ANN-Quantized ANN(QANN)-SNN two-step conversion to obtain SNN which
022            improves the Pareto frontier of accuracy versus inference time-steps. SpikeZIP
023            integrates two novel algorithms: 1) a paths-ensemble training algorithm that con-
024            siders the SNN temporal information when fine-tuning QANN; 2) a mathemati-
025            cally equivalent conversion algorithm between the whole QANN and SNN. In the
026            experiment, SpikeZIP can achieve 73.92% accuracy on ImageNet with VGG-16
027
               within 9 time-steps and 74.21% accuracy on ImageNet with ResNet-34 within 11
               time-steps which are better than SOTA works. Spiking Neural Networks, Quanti-

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Spiking neural network (SNN) Maass (1997) is a type of biologically plausible neural network in-
      spired by brains of living organisms. Unlike modern artificial neural networks (ANNs) LeCun et al.
034
      (2015) use continuous activation value to propagate information between neurons synchronously,
035
      SNNs utilize discrete events or “spikes” for asynchronous neuron-to-neuron communication and
036   processing Merolla et al. (2014); Davies et al. (2018). Such event-driven characteristic in SNN is
037   considered as one of the key factors to achieve remarkable energy efficiency in human brain (∼20W)
038   Roy et al. (2019). Given the astonishing growing pace of computing power demands of ANN models
039   (doubled per 2 months since 2020 Mehonic & Kenyon (2022)), evolving ANNs to energy-efficient
040   SNNs is in urgent demand for cost-effective inference.
041
      Currently, methods to train SNN come in twofold: learning- and conversion-based Roy et al. (2019).
042   Both methods attempt to obtain SNNs with high accuracy and less time-step. Previous learning-
043   based works leverage variants of spike-timing-dependent plasticity Diehl & Cook (2015) or gradient
044   descent algorithms Wu et al. (2018; 2019); Neftci et al. (2019); Kim & Panda (2021); Zenke &
045   Vogels (2021) to update the synaptic weights of SNN. Unfortunately, although learning methods
046   train SNN at small time-step (e.g., 4 time-step), due to the inaccurate gradient approximation Neftci
047   et al. (2019) for the non-differential SNN neuron, e.g., integrate and fire (IF) neuron, an accuracy
048   gap persists between SNN and its ANN counterpart Fang et al. (2021a).
049   Rather than directly training an SNN, the conversion-based methods transfer the parameters of the
050   pre-trained ANN into its SNN counterpart that yields close-to-ANN accuracy. As the accuracy
051   versus time-step curve shown in fig. 1, the SOTA works (Offset Hao et al. (2023), QCFS Bu et al.
052   (2023), etc.) reduce the inference time-step to 16. These works replace the ReLU function with
053   its quantized version (Q-ReLU), and use a quantization-aware training algorithm to compress the
      quantization level of activation to reduce the inference time-step. However, the low quantization

093            the-art works (74.14% at 16 time-steps by Offset Hao et al. (2023)).

168
169   3.1   C ONVERSION F LOW IN S PIKE ZIP
170
171   SpikeZIP takes four steps in total to convert an ANN to its SNN counterpart. It firstly converts
172   original ANN to SNN-friendly QANN through (1) SNN-friendly morphing and (2) paths-ensemble
173
      training. Then, SpikeZIP utilizes the (3) operator fusion and the (4) neuron replacement to QANN
      to obtain a high-performance SNN.
174
175
176   1) SNN Friendly Morphing. Since the original ANN may include operators and topology that are
177   unfriendly to SNN, we modify those operators with the SNN-friendly counterparts. In detail, we al-
178
      ter the max-pooling to the average-pooling Rueckauer et al. (2017), as it is easier for equivalent SNN
      implementation. Moreover, we conduct residual connection re-routing (specified in section 3.3) for
179
      ResNet, to optimize the latency. Finally, the ReLU function is replaced by Q-ReLU function to
180
      convert ANN to QANN, for successive training.
181
182
      2) Paths-Ensemble Training (PET) is proposed and utilized to train the morphed ANN (e.g.
183
      QANN) from the last step. PET (specified in section 3.4) is a variant of LSQ-based quantiza-
184
      tion aware training algorithm, which is designed to simultaneously improve the accuracy of trained
185   QANN that quantized with varying quantization levels on the fly.
186
187
      3) Operator Fusion & 4) Neuron Replacing. In operator fusion, SpikeZIP fuses the batch nor-
188
      malization layers of trained QANN into their front (e.g., convolution or linear) layers as in Chen
189
      et al. (2018). In the successive neuron replacing, we update all the Q-ReLUs to ST-BIF neurons, and
190   adjust the configurations of ST-BIF neurons (e.g., Vt=0 , Vthr , Smax and etc., as discussed in proof 3.1)
191   accordingly.
192
193
      3.2   I NPUT AND B IAS E NCODING
194
195   We design a unique analog encoding method for input and bias1 , which is also the prerequisite of
196   QANN-SNN equivalence in section 3.5. Unliking traditional analog encoding that converts the raw
197   image X0 to Vint = X0 · ∆v, ∀t and fires for each time-step, we choose the evenly-release strategy
198
      which sets Vin
                   t = X0 · ∆v/n, t ∈ {1, 2, ..., Toff }. Hereby, input stops applying at Toff = n, where n
199   is the quantization level of QANN. Moreover, the evenly-release strategy is adopted for the bias in
200   convolution/linear layers as well.

      SpikeZIP constructs a framework for obtaining a high performance SNN, supported by comprehen-
534   sive experiments and rigorous proof in this work. We anticipate the SpikeZIP as a foot-stone for
535   future investigations of brain-inspired SNN, which bridges and inherits the existing research of deep
