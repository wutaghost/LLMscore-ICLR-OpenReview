                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Audio-Visual Speech Enhancement (AVSE) aims to improve speech quality in
012            noisy environments by utilizing synchronized audio and visual cues. In real-world
013            scenarios, noise is often non-stationary, interfering with speech signals at varying
014            intensities over time. Despite these fluctuations, humans can discern and under-
015            stand masked spoken words as if they were clear. This capability stems from
016            the auditory system’s ability to perceptually reconstruct interrupted speech us-
017            ing visual cues and semantic context in noisy environments, a process known as
018            phonemic restoration. Inspired by this phenomenon, we propose Cross-Modal
019
               Contextual Modeling (CM2 ), integrating contextual information across differ-
               ent modalities and levels to enhance speech quality. Specifically, we target two
020
               types of contextual information: semantic-level context and signal-level context.
021
               Semantic-level context enables the model to infer missing or corrupted content
022            by leveraging semantic consistency across segments. Signal-level context further
023            explores coherence within the signals developed from the semantic consistency.
024            Additionally, we particularly highlight the role of visual appearance in modeling
025            the frequency-domain characteristics of speech, aiming to further refine and enrich
026            the expression of these contexts. Guided by this understanding, we introduce a Se-
027            mantic Context Module (SeCM) at the very beginning of our framework to capture
028            the initial semantic contextual information from both audio and visual modalities.
029            Next, we propose a Signal Context Module (SiCM) to obtain signal-level con-
030
               textual information from both raw noisy audio signal and the previously acquired
               audio-visual semantic-level context. Building on this rich contextual information,
031
               we finally introduce a Cross-Context Fusion Module (CCFM) to facilitate fine-
032
               grained context fusion across different modalities and types of contexts for further
033            speech enhancement process. Comprehensive evaluations across various datasets
034            demonstrate that our method significantly outperforms current state-of-the-art ap-
035            proaches, particularly in low signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) environments.
036
037   1   I NTRODUCTION
038
039   Speech Enhancement (SE) aims to improve the intelligibility and quality of speech by eliminating
040   background noise. This task has numerous applications in challenging acoustic environments. Tra-
041   ditional audio-only speech enhancement methods (AOSE) exclusively utilizes auditory signals, and
042   struggles in scenarios with very low SNR (Defossez et al., 2020; Thakker et al., 2022a; Cao et al.,
043   2022). Insights from cognitive psychology, particularly phenomena like the “Cocktail Party Effect”
044
      (Cherry, 1953) and the “McGurk Effect” (McGurk & MacDonald, 1976), highlight the importance
      of incorporating visual cues into speech enhancement. These phenomena have led to the flourish-
045
      ing development of Audio-Visual Speech Enhancement (AVSE), which integrates both auditory and
046
      visual modalities (Pan et al., 2021; Yang et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024).
047
048   In real-world scenarios, background noises, including traffic, natural, and indoor noises, are pre-
049   dominantly non-stationary. This variability in noise distribution causes substantial interference in
050
      certain speech segments, while others are comparatively less affected.
051   Research in neuroscience, psychology, and phonetics offers crucial insights into addressing this is-
052   sue. The human auditory system has the capability to reconstruct speech segments obscured by
053   noise through semantic and signal contexts, a process termed phonemic restoration (Sunami et al.,
      2013; Sivonen et al., 2006). Inspired by this phenomenon, we propose that two types of contextual

081   Building on these insights, we propose Cross-Modal Contextual Modeling (CM2 ), integrating con-
082
      textual information across different modalities and levels to enhance speech quality. Our contribu-
      tions could be summarized in three folds:
083
084   1. Given the challenge of non-stationary noise in speech enhancement, and drawing inspiration from
085      the operational principles of the human auditory system, we have introduced two complementary
086
         forms of contextual information to support AVSE: semantic and signal context. By extracting
         and integrating these types of context across different modalities and domains, our approach
087
         significantly enhances the quality of speech.
088
089   2. We highlight and have experimentally validated the critical role of visual information along the
090      audio frequency domain. This discovery holds potential to inspire future research across the
091
         audio-visual community.
092   3. Comprehensive evaluations on four composite datasets clearly show the advantages of our work.
093      Especially, our approach consistently outperforms the current state-of-the-art (SOTA) methods
094      across a wide range of SNR levels. Notably, at a signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) of -15 dB, our
095      method achieves substantial relative improvements, enhancing the Signal-to-Distortion Ratio
096
         (SDR) by 63.6%, the Perceptual Evaluation of Speech Quality (PESQ) by 58.1%, and the Short-
         Time Objective Intelligibility (STOI) by 20.3%, relative to existing benchmarks. Significant
097
         enhancements continue even at an SNR of 0 dB, improving by 24.5% in SDR, 44.4% in PESQ,
098
         and 5.6% in STOI.

      2      R ELATED W ORK
102   Audio-Only Speech Enhancement: AOSE has long been the predominant method for speech en-
103   hancement and remains a critical foundation in the field of speech signal processing. Initially, AOSE
104   relies on statistical priors of noise data, employing techniques such as spectral subtraction (Boll,
105   1979), Wiener filtering (Lim & Oppenheim, 1978), and the minimum mean square error method
106   Ephraim & Malah (1984). Subsequently, AOSE transitions to data-driven deep learning approaches,
107   surmounting the limitations of traditional methods in handling non-white noise. AOSE methods
      are broadly categorized into two types: Time domain (T-domain) (Fu et al., 2018; Pandey & Wang,


                                                                                             2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      2019; Defossez et al., 2020; Thakker et al., 2022b) and Time-Frequency domain (TF-domain) meth-
109   ods (Lu et al., 2013; Kolbæk et al., 2017; Fu et al., 2019; Cao et al., 2022). While T-domain meth-
110   ods typically estimate the audio waveform directly, TF-domain approaches can directly estimate the
111   spectrum (Fu et al., 2017; Strake et al., 2020) or compute the spectrum by predicting a mask (Wang
112   & Wang, 2013; Williamson et al., 2015; Fu et al., 2019; Cao et al., 2022). Our work is also based on
113   the TF-domain mask approach, but we distinguish from AOSE by using supplementary visual cues.
114
      Audio-Visual Speech Enhancement: Inspired by these researches in cognitive psychology (Cherry,
115   1953; McGurk & MacDonald, 1976), methods that introduce visual information for speech enhance-
116   ment have emerged (Fisher III et al., 2000; Smaragdis & Casey, 2003; Parekh et al., 2017). Numer-
117   ous AVSE approaches (Gabbay et al., 2018b; Hou et al., 2018; Afouras et al., 2019; Michelsanti
118   et al., 2019; Pan et al., 2021; Gao & Grauman, 2021; Wang et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2024) have made
119   efforts in enhancing modal fusion to maximize the benefits of both modalities. Iuzzolino & Koishida
120   (2020a) introduced cross-modal squeeze-excitation mechanism for audio-visual fusion, which out-
121   performs single channel-wise cancatenated fusion strategy. Wang et al. (2023) facilitated robust
122   dynamic fusion of audio and visual modalities by assessing the dynamic reliability of each modal-
123
      ity. Li et al. (2024a;b) implemented top-down attention for audio-visual fusion at multi temporal
      scales, mimicking the audio-visual pathways in the brain. Given the inherent synchronization of
124
      audio and visual modalities along the temporal dimension, most existing approaches focus solely
125
      on the fusion in the temporal domain and overlook the potential correlations between the frequency
126   dimensions of the visual and audio modalities. In contrast, our work highlight the role of visual
127   features in enhance the frequency dimension of audio.
128
129
      Phonemic Restoration: Phonemic restoration is a phenomenon identified in cognitive psychology
      and neuroscience where listeners can perceptually “fill in” missing sounds in a speech signal, using
130
      contextual cues from the surrounding auditory and linguistic information (Warren, 1970; Samuel,
131
      1981; Bashford et al., 1992; Riecke et al., 2008; Shahin et al., 2009; Powers & Hevey, 2016). Be-
132   yond linguistic contexts, visual cues are also proved to take part in facilitating the brain’s phonemic
133   restoration(Abbott & Shahin, 2018). This phenomenon has been extensively studied to understand
134   both its neural underpinnings and its applications in improving communication aids for the hearing
135   impaired (Shahin et al., 2009; Powers & Hevey, 2016). The cognitive mechanisms behind phonemic
136   restoration is linked to top-down processing, where the brain utilizes contextual information and lin-
137   guistic knowledge to reconstruct missing speech sounds (Samuel, 1981). This is particularly evident
138   in conditions where speech is interrupted with silent intervals or noise bursts (Warren, 1970). These
139   phenomena have inspired us to investigate the role of contexts in speech enhancement.

      3.1      S EMANTIC C ONTEXT M ODULE
188
189   The Semantic Context Module (SeCM) is designed to extract semantic contexts from the audio-
190   visual input , laying the foundation for subsequently enhancing target speech through semantic-level
191   continuity. Inspired by the success of speech recognition models (Martinez et al., 2020) in extracting
192   features closely linked to semantic information, we introduce three manners to construct our SeCM.
193   SeCMV (Visual Speech Recognition-based Module): In this manner, we introduce a simple learn-
194   able visual module similar to VSR models to obtain the initial semantic contexts. Specifically, we
195   adopt the common structure cascaded by a 3D convolutional layer, a ResNet18 backbone, and a
196   four-layer Temporal Convolutional Network (TCN), which will be learned from scratch for the tar-
197   get AVSE task with the input of grayscale facial frames V ∈ RH×W ×Tv .
198   SeCMP V (Pre-trained Visual Branch): In the second manner, we take the pre-trained large-scale
199   Audio-Visual speech representation models to obtain rich semantic contextual information. Specif-
200   ically, models like AVHuBERT (Shi et al., 2022a), VATLM (Zhu et al., 2024), or Auto-AVSR (Ma
201   et al., 2023) can all serve as our SeCM. Given that these models were originally pre-trained to obtain
202   the shared semantic information between paired clean audio and video data, the corrupted noisy au-
203   dio input in our task would lead to a discrepancy with the synchronized video data in these models.
204   Therefore, we take only the visual modality as input to these models to obtain developed semantic
205   information. The SeCMP V remains frozen during our training phase with the input of gray-scale
206
      frames of the mouth Region of Interest (ROI) following Shi et al. (2022a); Zhu et al. (2024).
207   SeCMP AV (Pre-trained Audio-Visual Module): For the third option, we introduce models that
208   were pre-trained with noisy speech and paired videos, enabling them to more effectively handle
209   noisy audio inputs and efficiently extract common semantic information from both noisy audio
210   speech and video. Specifically, we adopt the robust AVHuBERT (Shi et al., 2022b) here to ob-
211   tain robust semantic information. SeCMP AV shares the same visual input as SeCMP V , with the
212
      addition of noisy speech waveforms A also provided as input.
213   In summary, each variant of the SeCMs output the semantic context information at different degrees,
214   collectively denoted as E ∈ RCe ×Te , where Ce and Te denote channel and temporal dimensions
215   respectively. The semantic context is subsequently used for signal context modeling and cross-
      context fusion in the later stages.

234   3.2     AUDIO E NCODER
235   The audio encoder aims to map the noisy spectral input X ′ into a high-dimensional feature space,
236   enabling subsequent context extraction and modeling. The encoder consists of 4 convolution blocks.
237   The first convolution block comprises a convolution layer, an instance normalization (Ulyanov,
238   2016) and a PReLU activation (He et al., 2015), being used to extend the three input features to
239   an intermediate feature map with C channels. The middle two convolution blocks each include one
240   convolution layer, a BatchNorm, and a residual connection. The final convolution block mirrors the
241   structure of the first block and is tasked with reducing the frequency dimension to Fx′ to decrease
                                                                                    ′
242   complexity. It then outputs the high-dimensional audio feature P ∈ RC×Tx ×Fx .

299                                       Ete = ⟨Et [...,:C//2], Pt [...,C//2:]⟩,                        (6)

302
      Attention-based Fusion Block (AFBlock): After the CSBlock, the output contain both high-level
303
      semantic information and fine-grained sequential information from the raw noisy speech. Building
304   on this fusion, we introduce SiCM G to extract the enhanced signal contexts Itp and Ite from Ptc
305   and Etc , respectively. Then, a linear layer followed by a SiLU activation layer is applied on Ptc to
                                                 ′
306   generate the attention map Mt ∈ RBFx ×Tx ×C . Given that Ptc and Etc exhibit symmetrical struc-
307   tures, the selection of either feature for generating the attention maps does not influence subsequent
308   outcomes. In this instance, we choose to generate Mt from the features Ptc . Subsequently, Mt is
309   element-wise multiplied with IP and IE and fused through addition. Finally, a 2D convolution P is
310   applied to the attention result, forming the fused cross-modal signal contexts G. The whole process
311   can be summarized as:                                                 ′
312                            Mt = SiLU(Linear(Ptc )), Mt ∈ RBFx ×Tx ×C ,                               (9)

319                                         Ot = Et + Gt + G(Pt ).                                    (12)

328   Our Time-Frequency Block (TFBlock) includes two sequentially connected SiCM, each dedicated
329   to extracting signal context from the time domain and frequency domain of the enhanced feature

      3.6   D ECODERS
336
337   Similar to prior methods in time-frequency domain speech enhancement (Cao et al., 2022), we utilize
338   two decoders to independently predict the Ideal Ratio Mask (IRM) of the magnitude spectrum, as
339
      well as the real and imaginary components of the complex spectrum. These two decoders share the
      same architectural framework as the encoder, with the exception of two minor adjustments. Firstly,
340
      the downsampling operation applied to the frequency dimension in the encoder has been replaced
341
      by an upsampling operation, aimed at restoring the frequency dimension of the spectrum. Secondly,
342   the magnitude decoder outputs with a single channel, while the complex decoder features two output
343   channels, aligning with their respective target outputs.
344
345
      Specifically, the magnitude decoder generates the Ideal Ratio Mask (IRM) of the spectrum, which
      is subsequently applied via point-wise multiplication to the input magnitude spectrum. Then, the
346
      complex decoder directly predicts the real and imaginary components (Ŝr′ , Ŝi′ ) of the complex spec-
347
      trum. Following Yu et al. (2022); Li et al. (2022), the final complex spectrogram is obtained by the
348
      combination of the estimated magnitude Ŝm and the noisy phase Xp :
349
350                             Ŝr = Ŝm cos(Xp ) + Ŝr′ Ŝi = Ŝm sin(Xp ) + Ŝi′                   (16)
351   Finally, an inverse short-time Fourier transform (ISTFT) is applied to get the enhanced speech x̂.
352

355   spectrogram Lm and complex spectrogram Lri :
356                                  Lm = MSE(Xm , X̂m ),                                               (17)
357
                                      Lri = MSE(Xr , X̂r ) + MSE(Xi , X̂i ).                          (18)

      For the adversarial training, the generator loss Lgan and discriminator loss Ld can be expressed as
359
360                   Lgan = MSE(Dis(Sm , Ŝm ), 1),                                                    (19)
361                      Ld = MSE(Dis(Sm , Sm ), 1) + MSE(Dis(Sm , Ŝm ), P ESQgt ),               (20)
362   where Dis(·, ·) refers the discrminator and P ESQgt refers to the normalized PESQ score. The final
363   optimization loss are employed as follows:
364
                                        LG = αLm + βLri + γLgan ,                                   (21)

      where α, β, and γ are the weights of the corresponding losses, chosen to achieve equal importance.

520   5 C ONCLUSION
521   In this study, we introduce a novel framework, Cross-Modal Contextual Modeling (CM2 ), inspired
522   by the phonemic restoration phenomenon observed in the human auditory system. This approach
523   enhances AVSE by leveraging two types of contextual information: semantic and signal contexts.
524   Additionally, we incorporate the visual information into the frequency domain, a critical aspect
525   often overlooked in previous research. Our method consistently outperforms existing techniques
526
      across all metrics with a wide SNR ranges. Through systematic ablation studies, we have validated
      the effectiveness of our proposed semantic contexts, signal contexts, and the integration of visual
527
      frequency dimensions.

      The initial learning rate for our model is established at 0.001, with a batch size of 48. Training is

      B    E VALUATION M ETRICS
819
820
      We employ three popular metrics for evaluation, including the the Signal-to-Distortion Ratio (SDR)
      (Raffel et al., 2014), Short-Time Objective Intelligibility (STOI) (Taal et al., 2011), and the Percep-
821
      tual Evaluation of Speech Quality (PESQ) (Rix et al., 2001). SDR is utilized to evaluate the quality
822
      of speech by measuring the ratio of signal power to distortion between the enhanced version and the
823   original clean speech signals. STOI quantifies the intelligibility of a signal on a scale from 0 to 1,
824   with higher values indicating better intelligibility. PESQ assesses the overall perceptual quality of
825   the output signal on a scale from 0.5 to 4.5, where higher values denote superior quality.

      C    DATASET D ETAILS
829
830   We conducted our AVSE experiments on LRS3+DNS4, GRID+CHiME3, TCD-TIMIT+NTCD-
831
      TIMIT, and MEAD+DEMAND datasets. In each dataset group, the first dataset comprises clean
      audio-visual data, while the second dataset consists of noise samples collected from a variety of
832
      settings. For all visual inputs, SeCMV processes a 112 × 112 grayscale image of the face following
833
      Wang et al. (2023). Meanwhile, SeCMP V and SeCMP AV receive an 88 × 88 grayscale image of the
834   mouth Region of Interest (ROI), in accordance with the configurations of Shi et al. (2022a); Ma et al.
835   (2023); Zhu et al. (2024). In the training phase, the 112x112 facial images and the 88x88 mouth
836   ROI images are randomly cropped from larger 128x128 facial images and 96x96 mouth images,
837   respectively. In the testing phase, a center cropping technique is employed.
838
      LRS3 + DNS4: LRS3 contains 438 hours of talking videos from TED and TEDX clips downloaded
839
      from YouTube. We evaluate our method on the pretrain subset which contains 407 hours of video.
840   We partitioned this subset into training, validation, and testing sets with a ratio of 8:1:1. We follow
841   Defossez et al. (2020) to obtain the noise signal from the noise subset of the DNS4 dataset. The
842   subset contains approximately 181 hours of noise audio collected from a wide variety of events.
843   During training and evaluation, we utilized these samples as background noise to add noise to the
844   clean speech and construct synthetic noisy audio inputs.
845
      GRID + CHiME3: GRID consists of 33 speakers. For our experiments, we follow the general
846   setting Balasubramanian et al. (2023) to designate speakers s2 and s22 as the validation set, speakers
847   s1 and s12 as the unseen unheard test set, and the remaining 29 speakers as the training set. We
848   sample noise from CHiME to corrupt the clean speech. The noise in CHiME is categorized into 4
849   types: Cafe, Street, Bus, and Pedestrian. The CHiME dataset is divided into training, validation and
850   testing sets with an 8:1:1 ratio.
851
      TCD-TIMIT + NTCD-TIMIT: TCD-TIMIT consists of AV speech data from 56 English speakers
852   with an Irish accent. Each utterance is approximately 5 seconds long and sampled at 16kHz. As
853   recommended in Harte & Gillen (2015), we split the dataset into training, validation, and testing
854   sets, with 39 speakers for training, 8 for validation, and 9 for testing. The noisy speech input is
855   derived from the NTCD-TIMIT dataset. This dataset is created by adding six different types of
856   noise to the original speech data from the TCD-TIMIT corpus. The noise types include Living
857   Room, White, Cafe, Car, Bable, and Street, and each noise type is associated with a specific SNR.
858   Similar to the approach in Golmakani et al. (2023), we selected 5 utterances per noise level and
859   noise type for each test speaker to create a test set of 1350 utterances.
860   MEAD + DEMAND: The MEAD dataset consists of recordings from 46 participants, who uttered
861   sentences expressing eight different emotions at three intensity levels under seven camera view-
862   points. This dataset is extensively employed in research across various fields, including affective
863   computing, human-computer interaction, and robust AVSE. Following Kang et al. (2022), choose
      videos that captured frontal views and the highest level (level 3) of emotion intensity for experiment.
