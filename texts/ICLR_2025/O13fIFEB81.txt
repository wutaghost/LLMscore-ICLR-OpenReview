009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Diffusion models are known for the supreme capability to generate realistic im-
012            ages. However, ethical concerns, such as copyright protection and generation of
013            inappropriate content, pose significant challenges for the practical deployment of
014            diffusion models. Recent work has proposed a flurry of watermarking techniques
015            that inject visually noteless patterns into generated images, offering a promising
016            solution to these issues. While effective, the essential elements for watermark-
017            ing and the interconnections among various methods are still chaos. In this pa-
018            per, we dissect the design principles of state-of-the-art watermarking techniques
019
               and introduce a unified framework. We identify a set of dimensions that explain
               the manipulation enforced by watermarking methods, including the distribution
020
               of individual elements, the specification of watermark regions within each chan-
021
               nel, and the choice of channels for watermark embedding. Moreover, under this
022            framework we instantiate a new watermarking method to minimize impacts on
023            the model performance from a distributional perspective. Through the empirical
024            studies on regular text-to-image applications and the first systematic attempt on
025            watermarking image-to-image diffusion models, we thoroughly verify the effec-
026            tiveness of our proposed framework through comprehensive evaluations. On all
027            the diffusion models, including Stable Diffusion, our approach induced from the
028            proposed framework not only preserves image quality but also outperforms exist-
029            ing methods in robustness against a range of attacks.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   The rise of diffusion models has significantly impacted image generation, enabling the creation of
035   diverse and high-quality images across various styles. However, the widespread use of these mod-
036   els also introduces critical ethical challenges, particularly concerning copyright protection and the
037   generation of inappropriate or misleading content. In this regard, watermarking generated images
038
      offers a promising approach to tracing image origins and mitigating potential misuse.
039   A core challenge in image watermarking is the trade-off between the robustness of the watermark
040   and the quality of the generated images. Traditional watermarking techniques (Al-Haj, 2007; Navas
041   et al., 2008) primarily rely on post-processing methods to embed subtle modifications into the im-
042   age’s frequency representation, making them imperceptible to human users. Though effective, these
043   approaches suffer from reduced image quality and insufficient robustness against common attacks
044
      such as compression and cropping, to name a few.
045   To improve the trade-off, Zhang et al. (2019); Zhu et al. (2018); Hayes & Danezis (2017) pro-
046   posed end-to-end deep learning methods to construct watermarks with powerful learning capabil-
047   ities, while such methods are black-box and require additional training. Specifically for diffusion
048   models, Wen et al. (2023); Yang et al. (2024) developed latent-representation-based watermarks,
049   which manipulate latent representations to match specific patterns.
050   Despite the empirical successes of image watermarking, the key components thereof are poorly un-
051   derstood, and the connections between watermarking methods remain unclear. In this paper, we
052   carefully analyze the state-of-the-art watermarking methods, and introduce a unified framework that
053   identifies and connects the underlying design principles. This framework reveals three critical design
      dimensions, including ❶ the distribution of individual elements, ❷ the specification of watermark


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      regions within each channel, and ❸ the choice of channels for embedding. Under the unified frame-
055   work, we integrate the key designs from both image and language watermarking; we propose a novel,
056   training-free watermarking approach that applies directly to diffusion models without altering the
057   training process. Notably, our method embeds watermarks directly into the latent space of diffusion
058   models (rather than the frequency domain), avoiding extraneous operations and errors during detec-
059   tion. Extensive experiments are conducted; in addition to the conventional focus on text-to-image
060   generation, we extend the application scope of our proposed image watermarking to the scenarios
061   involving image-to-image diffusion model. Our method turns out both preserving visual quality of
062   generated images and providing robustness against a wide range of adversarial attacks.
063   In summary, our paper contributes the following:
064   • We present a unified framework that identifies and connects the key design principles underlying
065     state-of-the-art watermarking methods.
066   • Under the unified framework, we propose a novel, training-free watermarking method, and theo-
067     retical analysis showing preservation of the latent representation distribution is accompanied.
068   • We extend watermarking techniques beyond the traditional focus on text-to-image generation sce-
069     nario, and give the first systematic approach to watermarking image-to-image diffusion models.
070     Our proposed method exhibits robustness against a variety of adversarial attacks and high visual
071     quality, validating the efficacy of our method as well as the unified framework.

      In this section, we comprehensively review the related works on watermarking images and recent
076   extensions to diffusion models.
077
078
      Watermarking Image. Digital watermarking (Van Schyndel et al., 1994) embeds traceable iden-
079   tification information in carrier data for copyright protection and content authentication. Traditional
080   image watermarking techniques, often applied in post-processing, focus on frequency domain meth-
081   ods (Cox et al., 2007; Al-Haj, 2007; Hamidi et al., 2018; Kundur & Hatzinakos, 1997; Lee et al.,
082   2007; Navas et al., 2008) to enhance robustness. For instance, DwtDctSvd (Cox et al., 2007) com-
083   bines Discrete Wavelet Transform, Discrete Cosine Transform, and Singular Value Decomposition
084   for watermark embedding. More recently, deep learning-based approaches (Zhu et al., 2018; Tancik
085   et al., 2020; Fernandez et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2019; Hayes & Danezis, 2017) like RivaGAN
086   (Zhang et al., 2019) leverage neural networks to improve watermarking, employing adversarial net-
087
      works for both embedding and extraction. Despite advancements, post-hoc watermarking often in-
      troduces visible noise and is vulnerable to attacks like cropping and compression (Fernandez et al.,
088
      2023), as it is applied to the final image, making the watermark prone to distortion or removal.
089
090   Watermarking Diffusion Models. With the rise of generative models, particularly diffusion mod-
091   els, watermarking AI-generated content or the models themselves has gained importance. Yu et al.
092   (2021) and Zhao et al. (2023b) proposed embedding watermarks into training datasets, so models
093
      inherently generate watermarked content. However, this is impractical for large-scale diffusion mod-
      els trained on vast datasets. To overcome this, researchers have embedded watermarks during the
094
      generation process. For example, Fernandez et al. (2023) and Feng et al. (2024) fine-tuned model
095
      weights to modify latent representations, with Stable Signature (Fernandez et al., 2023) fine-tuning
096   LDM decoders to embed hidden watermarks in generated images.
097
098   Other approaches, like Tree-ring watermark (Wen et al., 2023), modify the initial noise in the sam-
099
      pling process, requiring deterministic samplers like DDIM (Song et al., 2020) for watermark extrac-
      tion through inversion. Similarly, Yang et al. (2024) adjusts the initial noise naturally, improving
100
      image quality and robustness against attacks. Inspired by these works, we examine critical design
101
      dimensions for embedding watermarks in the latent space, offering insights into diffusion water-
102   marking techniques

112   3.1   R ECAP OF THE D IFFUSION P ROCESS
113
114   To map a regular image x ∈ RH×W ×3 to the latent space, Latent Diffusion Models (LDMs) formally
115
      behave as an autoencoder, using an encoder E to obtain the representation z0 as z0 = E(x) ∈
      Rh×w×c ; conversely, a decoder D reconstructs the image x from the latent space as x = D(z0 ). In
116
      generating images, (dropping the encoder E and) directly feeding a random signal z0 to the decoder
117
      D of a pre-trained LDM can provably return an image following the pre-training distribution.
118
119   Despite the autoencoder framework, the magic of LDMs comes from its diffusion-like process to
120   obtain z0 . Specifically, an initial latent representation zT ∈ Rh×w×c is first sampled from a standard
121
      Gaussian distribution N (0, I); subsequently, iterative denoising methods like DDIM (Song et al.,
      2020) are used to transform zT into z0 , and the decoder then generates the image: x = D(z0 ).
122
123   Beyond standard LDMs, inversion techniques enable moving in the opposite direction—from a
124   generated image back to the initial noise state. It is based on the assumption that the Ordinary
125   Differential Equation (ODE) process can be reversed in the limit of small steps. Dhariwal & Nichol
126   (2021) gave an inverse process based on DDIM (named DDIM inversion), which reconstructs the
127
      initial noise tensor zT given an image x. The inversion can be expressed as:

      3.2   I MAGE WATERMARKING : A CRYPTOLOGICAL PERSPECTIVE
143
144   In general, image watermarking strategy is designed for a comprehensive scenario involving three
145   key players: John, the thief Emma, and two types of users, David and Sarah. John is responsible
146   for training the model, deploying it on a platform, and providing an API for users in the case of not
147   open-sourcing the code or model weights. Emma, instead of using John’s services, steals images
148   generated by his model and falsely claims copyright ownership as if she is the artist. Meanwhile,
149   David and Sarah, as community users, utilize the API to generate and share images. David adheres
150   to community guidelines, while Sarah engages in the creation of deep fakes and infringing content.
151   To evade detection and traceability, Sarah applies various data augmentation techniques to alter illicit
152   images. To prevent this misuse, John embeds a watermark into each generated image. Extracting
153   this watermark not only provides proof of John’s rightful ownership but also confirms that the image
154   is artificially generated, distinguishing it from natural images.

      4.1   A C OMPREHENSIVE F RAMEWORK FOR L ATENT R EPRESENTATION WATERMARKING
163
164   To embed a robust and stealthy watermark within diffusion models, it is crucial to preserve the
165   distribution of the initial latent representation zT ∈ Rh×w×c (which is intricately linked with im-
166   age semantics), enhancing detectability against various attacks. To this end, various watermarking
167   methods are raised.
168   We attempt to categorize watermarking methods for latent representations along three dimensions,
169   which serves as our method framework: ❶ the distribution of individual elements, ❷ the speci-
170   fication of watermark regions within each channel, and ❸ the choice of channels for watermark
171   embedding. The three dimensions constitute a comprehensive image watermarking framework, and
172   each of them all plays a pivotal role in the effectiveness of the watermarking method, impacting
173   both the quality of the generated images and the resilience of the watermark. As a prologue, we
174   decompose existing image watermarking methods under our proposed framework in the following.
175
176   Distribution of Individual Elements. The first dimension concerns the domain used and how
177   the values are set for each element. Previous work such as Tree-ring (Wen et al., 2023) set the
178   value to a constant in the Fourier frequency domain within each circle, creating a ring-like pattern.
179   Ring-ID (Ci et al., 2024) advances this approach by imprinting the tree-ring pattern only in the
180
      real part of the Fourier transform and leaving the imaginary part blank, satisfying the odd function
      constraint and improving imperceptibility. Post-hoc processing methods like DwtDctSVD (Cox
181
      et al., 2007) embed the watermark by modifying wavelet coefficients; they alter the singular values of
182
      the Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT) coefficients in the Discrete Wavelet Transform (DWT) domain
183   to encode the watermark. Gaussian-shading (Yang et al., 2024) imprints the watermark in the spatial
184   domain by selecting the value of each element from a sub-distribution of the original, maintaining
185   the overall distribution while embedding the watermark. Notably, learning-based methods, such
186   as Stable Signature (Fernandez et al., 2023) and AquaLoRA (Feng et al., 2024), tuned the model
187   weights to learn the values to inject, allowing the model to embed the watermark seamlessly during
188   the generation process.
189
190   Specification of Watermark Regions. The second dimension addresses the selection of water-
191   mark regions within each channel of size h × w. Tree-ring (Wen et al., 2023) and Ring-ID (Ci et al.,
192   2024) introduce a watermark as a ring shape consisting of several concentric circles in the frequency
193   domain, targeting specific frequency components to achieve a balance between robustness and invis-
194
      ibility. DwtDctSVD (Cox et al., 2007) embeds the watermark into pixel blocks within the combined
      DWT and DCT frequency domains. Similarly, Gaussian-shading (Yang et al., 2024) defines blocks
195
      as the regions to inject the watermark. Learning-based methods typically inject the watermark into
196
      all locations within the channel, allowing the model to determine the optimal embedding regions
197   during training.
198
199
      Choice of Channels for Watermark Embedding. The third dimension pertains to the choice of
200   channels for watermark embedding among the c channels. Tree-ring (Wen et al., 2023) and post-hoc
201   processing methods like DwtDctSVD (Cox et al., 2007) added the watermarks into specific chan-
202   nels based on empirical observations, often selecting channels that are less sensitive to perceptual
203   changes to maintain image quality. In contrast, Gaussian-shading (Yang et al., 2024), Ring-ID (Ci
204   et al., 2024), and learning-based methods injected the watermark into all channels, leveraging the
205   entire latent space for embedding and potentially increasing robustness against attacks.
206

212

252   4.3   R EGION S PECIFICATION
253
254   Compared to LLM watermarking, image watermarking needs to specify the regions (the collec-
255   tions of elements) to be separately watermarked; watermarks that span the entire initial input can
256   be overly sensitive to various transformations, making detection vulnerable to noise, distortions, or
257   local perturbations. In this regard, previous works primarily define watermark embedding regions
258   as ring-shaped (Wen et al., 2023), block-shaped (Cox et al., 2007), or learnable values distributed
259
      across all locations (Zhang et al., 2019). Specifically, Cox et al. (2007); Yang et al. (2024) introduced
      block-based structures, which enhance robustness by localizing the watermark to discrete regions,
260
      demonstrating resilience against noise addition, particularly global noise. On the other hand, ring-
261
      shaped regions offer robustness against geometric transformations such as rotation. In this section,
262   we will illustrate how to specify the regions for the watermark, to further improve the distribution-
263   preserving generalized red/green list method proposed in Section 4.2; the corresponding watermark
264   detection processes are deferred to the “Detection aggregation along channels” paragraph in Sec-
265   tion 4.4.
266
267   “Random Gaussian” Watermarking with Redundant and Dispersed Watermarks. Our first
268   idea is to introduce redundancy to attain resistance to attacks. Inspired by Vision Transformers
269   (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020), we split the initial input into patches, each carrying an identical watermark
      matrix W of the same shape as the patch; every element of W is a watermark value m denoting

      the “green” domain. This redundancy strengthens robustness, as information from multiple patches
271   can be aggregated during detection; even if some patches are compromised, ensemble methods help
272   reconstruct the watermark, ensuring reliable detection.

      interval of 2. We divide the images into patches, with each patch containing 64 elements. Consid-
404
      ering the common user practice of sharing generated images without retaining the original prompts,
405
      we perform inversions using an empty prompt and a guidance scale of 1. Inversions are executed
406   with 50 steps using the DDIM method (Song et al., 2020).
407
408   We further evaluate the effectiveness of our watermarking approach in image-to-image editing tasks
409
      using pre-trained image-conditioned diffusion models. Specifically, we leverage the instruct-pix2pix
      model (Brooks et al., 2023), a high-performance model fine-tuned from Stable Diffusion on paired
410
      image datasets. For these editing tasks, we apply DDIM inversion, again using an empty prompt and
411
      an empty original image with a guidance scale of 1. Both inference and inversion are performed with
412   100 steps to maintain consistency with the original setup, while all other hyperparameters remain
413   consistent with Stable Diffusion.
414
415
      For evaluating generation quality, we adopt the Frechet Inception Distance (FID) (Heusel et al.,
      2017) and CLIP-Score (Radford et al., 2021). FID is calculated on the COCO2017 validation set,
416
      which contains 5,000 images, to assess image quality. Additionally, prompts from Stable-Diffusion
417
      (Rombach et al., 2022) and images from Instructpix2pix (Brooks et al., 2023) are used to generate
418   images. For the Stable Diffusion model, we calculate the CLIP score between the generated images
419   and the text prompts, while for the instruct-pix2pix model, we evaluate the CLIP score between the
420   generated images and the ground truth descriptions.
421
      In the detection scenario, we compute the true positive rate (TPR) corresponding to a fixed false
422
      positive rate (1% FPR). In the traceability scenario, we assess identification accuracy across var-
423
      ious watermark patterns. For AUC and TPR@1%FPR, we generate 1,000 watermarked and 1,000
424   unwatermarked images in each run. All reported metrics are averaged across three runs, each using
425   different random seeds in line with this protocol.

      In this paper, we introduce a unified framework to dissect watermarking approaches for diffusion
534   models along three distinct dimensions. By further adapting the principle of LLM watermarking,
535   we innovatively instantiate a more effective hybrid model under this framework, maintaining high
536   fidelity, ensuring robust watermarking against a variety of attacks, and attaining minimal computa-

538   to-image applications, which outperforms existing state-of-the-art image watermarking methods.

      the exceptional performance further evidence a significant advancement in digital watermarking.

      B.3   I LLUSTRATION OF THE S TATISTICAL T EST
865
866   In this work, our primary focus is on evaluating the actual performance of the watermarking method.
867   However, a statistical analysis can also be derived. Let m ∈ {0, 1}k represent a k-bit (independent)
868   watermark embedded in the model. We extract the message m′ from an image x and compare it
869   with m. As outlined in previous works, the detection test is based on the number of matching bits,
870   A(m, m′ ). Specifically, if
871                                A(m, m′ ) ≥ τ     where τ ∈ {0, . . . , k},
872
873   then the image is flagged. This approach provides a level of robustness against imperfections in the
874   watermarking process.
875   Formally, we test the statistical hypothesis H1 : ”image x was generated by the watermarked model”
876   against the null hypothesis H0 : ”image x was not generated by the watermarked model.” Under
877   H0 (i.e., for non-watermarked images), we assume that the bits m′1 , . . . , m′k are independent and
878   identically distributed (i.i.d.) Bernoulli random variables with a parameter of 0.5. Consequently,
879   A(m, m′ ) follows a binomial distribution with parameters (k, 0.5). This assumption has been ex-
880   perimentally validated.
881   The theoretical FPR is defined as the probability that A(m, m′ ) exceeds the threshold τ . It is calcu-
882   lated using the CDF of the binomial distribution. A closed-form expression can be derived using the
883   regularized incomplete beta function Ix (α; β):
