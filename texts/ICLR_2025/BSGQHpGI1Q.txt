011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Regularization is essential in neural network training to prevent overfitting and
014            improve generalization. In this paper, we propose a novel regularization tech-
015            nique that leverages decomposable distribution and central limit theory assump-
016            tions by exploiting the properties of characteristic functions. We first define Prob-
017
               ability Function Informed Neural Networks as a class of universal function ap-
               proximators capable of embedding the knowledge of some probabilistic rules
018
               constructed over a given dataset into the learning process (a similar concept to
019
               Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs), if the reader is familiar with those).
020            We then enforce a regularization framework over this network, aiming to impose
021            structural constraints on the network’s weights to promote greater generalizability
022            in the given probabilistic setting. Rather than replacing traditional regularization
023            methods such as L2 or dropout, our approach is intended to supplement this and
024            other similar classes of neural network architectures by providing instead a con-
025            textual delta of generalization. We demonstrate that integrating this method into
026            such architectures helps improve performance on benchmark supervised classi-
027            fication datasets, by preserving essential distributional properties to mitigate the
028
               risk of overfitting. This characteristic function-based regularization offers a new
               perspective for enhancing distribution-aware learning in machine learning models.

      1   A N I NTRODUCTION AND ( RATHER INFORMAL ) D EFINITION O F
033       P ROBABILITY F UNCTION I NFORMED N EURAL N ETWORKS
034
035   Let’s define Probability Function Informed Neural Networks (PFINNs) as a class of universal func-
036   tion approximators designed to integrate probabilistic knowledge into the learning process and more
037   specifically into the learning architecture. Refomulating concepts from Physics-Informed Neural
038
      Networks (PINNs), which leverage physical laws to guide the training of neural networks (Cuomo
      et al., 2022), we can say that PFINNs focus on embedding probabilistic rules into a networks con-
039
      struction from the practitioner’s understanding of a given dataset.
040
041   At their core, PFINNs aim to enhance the learning of complex relationships and mappings by util-
042   ising the underlying probability distributions that characterize the data. This allows the model to
043   incorporate essential statistical information, which can lead to some level of interpretability. By
044
      explicitly encoding probabilistic principles, such as conditional dependencies or marginal distribu-
      tions, into the neural network architecture, PFINNs should be able better navigate the search space
045
      of the data they are trained on to give more reasonable results as a functional approximator.
046
047   In simpler terms, PFINNs can be thought of as neural networks that not only learn from data but also
048   respect some probabilistic structures the practitioner would like to embed into the network that they
049   believe governs that data. This makes them particularly powerful for applications where uncertainty
050
      plays a significant role, allowing practitioners to build models that are not only predictive but also
      statistically sound for their context.
051
052   To explore the existence of PFINNs as (neuro)symbolic AI and as hybrid systems would require
053   significantly more than 9 pages in a manuscript. We believe instead, it is best to begin with a
      simple PFINN architecture example derived from the lens of generalising the MNIST dataset and

      3   M OTIVATION FOR THE R EGULARISATION
169
      The first question to address is whether these N questions (in the case of MNIST N = 10) are
170
      sufficient enough for generate a function that can capture a perfect solution space from the training
171
      to generalise over unseen data later. Maybe other potential questions may merit consideration?
172   Given that we can reformulate these new questions into binary (yes/no) formats, they could serve
173   to expand the function we derive from the PFINN to get a better picture of the true distribution and
174   solution space.
175
      Treating each question as statistically independent, defined informally such that the response to
176
      one question does not influence the probabilities associated with another. For instance, if we pose
177
      questions such as ”Is this 10?”, ”Is this a goose?”, ”Is it currently raining?”, ”Was this instance
178   run on Alan Turing’s computer?”, ”Was this instance run on Ada Lovelace’s Analytical Engine?”,
179   or ”Was this run on Claude Shannon’s GPAC?”, we can generate an infinite series of inquiries.
180   The sheer volume of potential questions of course poses a practical impossibility for storage but
181   nevertheless, we can leverage the conceptual framework inherent in this infinite set of questions
182   (through the Central Limit Theorem (CLT), more specifically in our case Lyapunov’s CLT) , to be
183   able to relax the function we find through the PFINN to ”generalize” better, granted our process
184   respects any required relevant assumptions.
185   Essentially for this, we consider the concept of decomposable distributions. The Bernoulli distri-
186   butions we have currently are indecomposable so maybe finding and exploiting a relationship to an
187   infinitely decomposable function like the normal distribution may yield some interesting results. It is
188   through discovering this relationship, via us toying around with the linear combinations of Bernoulli
189   variables which we have, that yields us the regularisation property; namely through exploiting their
190   convergence to a normal distribution if we assume the existence of the previously introduced infinite
191   question space.

318   5    N UMERICAL C ONSIDERATIONS
319
320   The characteristic function is generally a considered a ”pure mathematical tool” whereby it’s con-
321   tinuous nature presents significant challenges when implemented in discrete computational environ-
322   ments. Modern computers rely on finite precision arithmetic, which inherently restricts the exact
323   representation of continuous functions, including characteristic functions. This means that we have
      to formulate discretizations based on some assumptions to integrate the characteristic function into


                                                                 6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      a practical regularization algorithm for machine learning, enabling it to operate and execute within
325   finite time.
326
327   Specifically, we will have to restrict it’s domain to a ”good enough” range since t ∈ R and the
328
      associated infinite nature of the reals. In other words, abstractly the problem is then as follows (with
      the help of some informal proof sketches for the sake of brevity due to page limit and for the reader’s
329
      sanity) :
330
331   Proposition 2. The set of real numbers R is uncountably infinite.
332   Informal Proof Sketch 1. This can be shown using Cantor’s famous Diagonal Argument. Assume
333   for contradiction that R is countable. Then we can list all real numbers in the interval [0, 1] as
334   r1 , r2 , r3 , . . .. We can then construct a new real number r by taking the diagonal of this list and
335   changing each digit, ensuring that r differs from each rn at the n-th digit. Therefore, r cannot be
336
      in our original list, contradicting the assumption that we had listed all real numbers. Thus, R is
      uncountably infinite. (Cantor, 1932)
337
338   Proposition 3. The set of real numbers R is complete.
339   Informal Proof Sketch 2. The completeness of R can be demonstrated using Dedekind’s cuts. A
340   Dedekind cut partitions the rational numbers into two non-empty sets A and B, where all elements
341   of A are less than all elements of B. For any non-empty set of rationals that is bounded above, there
342   exists a least upper bound (supremum) in R. This property ensures that every Cauchy sequence of
343   real numbers converges to a real number, establishing the completeness of R. (Dedekind, 2012)
344   Proposition 4. The set of computable real numbers is countably infinite.
345   Informal Proof Sketch 3. The set of computable real numbers can be described as those numbers
346   for which there exists a finite algorithm (Turing machine) that can produce their digits. Since the
347   set of all finite algorithms is countable, it follows that the set of computable real numbers is also
348   countable.(Bournez, 2024)(Weihrauch, 2012)
349   Proposition 5. The set of computable real numbers is not complete.
350
      Informal Proof Sketch 4. To see this, consider the sequence of computable numbers defined
351
      by rn = n1 , which converges to 0. Although 0 is a limit point of the sequence, it is not com-
352   putable because there is no finite algorithm that can output the exact value of 0. This demon-
353   strates that there exist Cauchy sequences of computable real numbers that do not converge to a com-
354   putable limit, thereby showing that the set of computable real numbers is not complete. (Bournez,
355   2024)(Weihrauch, 2012)
356
357   It becomes evident that propositions 2, 3, 4, and 5 present significant challenges in computing the
358   desired function ϕ(t) especially on a Discrete Dynamical System like the modern computer we use.
359
      To address this, we have adopted a strategy of restricting to a finite domain of t ∈ [−2π, 2π] where
      we discretize this interval into n = 1000 finite segments, which can be easily accomplished using
360
      a linear space function such as numpy.linspace or similar methods on modern programming
361
      languages.
362
363   The rationale for selecting the interval [−2π, 2π] is motivated by the analysis of the figures C.1 and
364   C.3 in the appendix of the characteristic function for the standard normal distribution, as well as
365
      the set of convergence graphs for the ℵ-modelled linear combinations of Bernoulli random variables
      observed in C.5. The region of primary interest lies within this interval, and while any variations
366
      outside this interval may be potentially significant under certain circumstances, we can effectively
367
      treat them as an acceptable level of statistical noise, we are willing to quantified by some ϵ. This
368   allows us to disregard this noise in the context of testing viability, though it may come at the expense
369   of some regularization ”performance”.
370
371
      There is no universally ”correct” range or sample size (n); however, for the purposes of our experi-
      mentation, we consider this choice to be sufficient.

      As a quick aside we would like to caution the reader regarding empirical tests such as the one
438
      presented above. This is due to the myriad of factors that can influence overall performance; the
439   results are also highly dataset-dependent. For instance, variations in the gradient starting point or
440   learning rate can yield significantly different empirical outcomes for the same problem. There is
441   no guaranteed method to achieve a perfect readout, as this would necessitate exploring an infinite
442   search space, which is computationally impossible.
443
      A natural question would be why was K-fold cross-validation not employed for evaluation. To
444
      address this, K-fold cross-validation while effective for approaching more optimal hyperparame-
445   ters, truly involves exploration of an infinite search space for the ”fairest” representation of the best
446   metrics for a given method. Although it seemingly provides more robust performance estimates
447   compared to a vanilla implementation, our focus in this study is on presenting a context-driven alter-
448   native method rooted in probability theory rather than maximizing some raw performance metrics
449   delta.
450
      Given this approach, we believe that demonstrating reasonable performance with a random seed
451   using simple constructions and standard default values suffices to validate our method. Furthermore,
452   the relevant code is provided, allowing others to reproduce our results and adapt the methods to their
453   own contexts, as detailed later in the reproducibility statement.
454
      In essence, using different seeds for any given parameter can tell different stories, and even aggre-
455
      gating results over multiple seeds may lead to varied outcomes. The key takeaway is that while this
456
      approach may serve as a viable alternative for a specific dataset and problem, it might not always
457   be the optimal choice. The purpose of such empirical tests is to demonstrate that they can be a
458   ‘good enough’ and ‘reliable’ option when necessary. Ultimately, it is the practitioner’s responsi-
459   bility to evaluate how well this approach aligns with their specific problem and to discern whether
460   employing a particular tool makes sense in their context.
