009                                                                              A BSTRACT
010
011                  Fine-tuning large language models (LLMs) is computationally expensive and
012                  memory-intensive due to their vast number of parameters. To mitigate these chal-
013                  lenges, Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) methods and model quantization
014                  techniques have been developed. Recent works have combined PEFT with quan-
015                  tization, proposing methods to adjust quantized model parameters before fine-
016
                     tuning to reduce quantization errors. However, we observe that such adjustments
                     can lead to suboptimal performance, as they may introduce discrepancies between
017
                     the quantized and original models. Additionally, the inherent fragility of quan-
018
                     tized models makes them sensitive to increased training complexity, potentially
019                  degrading performance. To address these issues, we introduce QR-Adaptor,
020                  a general fine-tuning framework that jointly optimizes quantization bit-widths
021                  and LoRA ranks for each layer in a gradient-free manner. Our method directly
022                  uses actual performance and memory usage as optimization objectives, bypassing
023                  network errors introduced by quantization. Through a three-stage optimization
024                  process—initialization based on task-specific layer importance, global exploration
025                  using a Pareto ranking genetic algorithm, and local refinement with Bayesian
026                  optimization—QR-Adaptor efficiently identifies optimal configurations. Exper-
027
                     imental results demonstrate that QR-Adaptor yields fine-tuned low-bit quantized
                     models that outperform their 16-bit counterparts while maintaining similar mem-
028
                     ory usage to 4-bit models. For instance, on the MMLU benchmark, our method
029
                     achieves a 3.3% accuracy improvement over methods like LoftQ and LQ-LoRA.

      1      I NTRODUCTION
046
      Large Language Models (LLMs) have achieved unprecedented success across various natural lan-
047
      guage processing tasks (Makridakis et al., 2023; Raiaan et al., 2024; Chang et al., 2024), demon-
048   strating exceptional capabilities in both language understanding and generation. However, adapting
049   these models to specific downstream tasks remains challenging due to significant computational and
050   memory constraints (Wan et al.). To address these issues, Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT)
051   methods, such as Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2022), have emerged, introducing low-
052   rank matrices to approximate updates to pre-trained weights, thereby enabling efficient fine-tuning.
053   Meanwhile, model quantization techniques (Gong et al., 2014; Gupta et al., 2015) reduce weight
      precision to decrease computational costs, enhancing training and inference efficiency.


                                                                                            1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      Recent advancements, such as QLoRA (Dettmers et al., 2023), integrate PEFT with quantiza-
055   tion techniques to improve fine-tuning efficiency and achieve higher-performing quantized models.
056   LoftQ (Li et al., 2023) and LQ-LoRA (Guo et al., 2024) propose minimizing the Frobenius norm
057   ∥W − Q − AB∥F by adjusting the parameters of quantized weight matrix Q, low-rank matrices
058   A and B, using this initialization to reduce the error between quantized models and full-precision
059   models. However, this initialization only fits a small portion of the error, and the resulting model
060   Q′ + A′ B′ no longer equals the original pre-trained model weight matrix W or the quantized model
061   weight matrix Q (i.e., Q′ + A′ B′ ̸= W and ̸= Q). Fine-tuning this model does not necessarily
062   lead to better performance, and in some cases, it may even perform worse than directly using the
063
      quantized model. In addition, another way to save resources is to reduce the number of parame-
      ters in the low-rank matrix. For example, AdaLoRA (Zhang et al., 2023b) dynamically prunes the
064
      rank of the low-rank matrix during fine-tuning based on its importance score. This dynamic prun-
065
      ing process introduces additional complexity and may lead to unexpected performance issues, as
066   continuously adjusting the rank forces the model to adapt to a constantly changing parameter space.
067   Such dynamic adjustments during fine-tuning are not well-suited for quantized models. In quantized
068   models, the errors introduced by quantization already degrade the model’s robustness (Gong et al.,
069   2024), significantly weakening its ability to capture meaningful features.
070
      We validate these two hypotheses in Section 2: Q′ + A′ B′ ̸= Q before fine-tuning may lead to
071
      performance degradation, and changing the trainable parameters during fine-tuning may also cause
072   performance issues. Based on these observations, we propose two strict constraints for fine-tuning
073   quantized models: first, before fine-tuning, Q′ + A′ B′ = Q, ensuring consistency at the starting
074   point of fine-tuning; second, keeping the number of trainable parameters unchanged during fine-
075   tuning to ensure that the quantized model effectively captures essential features.
076
      Under these two constraints, and beyond approaches like QLoRA(Dettmers et al., 2024), achieving
077
      efficient fine-tuning of quantized models necessitates a strategic allocation of limited computational
078   and memory resources to maximize performance. Specifically, we propose assigning different quan-
079   tization bit-widths and LoRA ranks to various layers of the model, not solely based on their impor-
080   tance to the downstream task but also considering each layer’s adaptability and expressiveness after
081   quantization. By allocating higher precision (i.e., larger bit-widths) and larger LoRA ranks to lay-
082   ers that require more capacity to adapt to the task—thereby granting them additional computational
083   resources—and assigning lower precision and smaller ranks to layers that maintain sufficient ex-
084   pressiveness even under quantization, we enhance the model’s performance where it is most needed
085   without excessively increasing memory usage.
086   Furthermore, to avoid introducing additional approximation errors, we employ actual task perfor-
087   mance and memory consumption as indicators to guide the allocation process. However, deter-
088   mining the optimal assignment of bit-widths and ranks across layers results in a vast combinatorial
089   solution space, and real-world evaluations are computationally intensive and time-consuming. Tra-
090   ditional methods, such as exhaustive enumeration or linear programming, become impractical in this
091   context due to their high computational cost.
092   To tackle these challenges, we reformulate the problem as a gradient-free optimization task and
093   introduce a three-stage optimization framework, QR-Adaptor, which efficiently navigates the solu-
094   tion space through initialization, interpolation, and extrapolation (see Figure 1). Our approach com-
095   prises: Task-Informed Initialization, where we derive initial layer configurations based on each
096   layer’s adaptability and contribution to the task; Global Exploration with Pareto Ranking Ge-
097   netic Algorithm (PRGA), inspired by NSGA-II (Deb et al., 2002), to effectively explore the broad
098
      configuration space and identify optimal trade-offs between performance and memory usage; and
      Local Refinement with Bayesian Optimization, where we employ customized weighted objective
099
      functions to refine configurations, constructing surrogate models that approximate the performance
100
      landscape and selecting optimal fine-tuned configurations. To accelerate the search process, we uti-
101   lize a subset of the dataset for fine-tuning during optimization. Experimental results demonstrate
102   that the low-precision models fine-tuned with QR-Adaptor outperform the 16-bit fine-tuned models,
103   while maintaining memory usage comparable to that of 4-bit quantized models during fine-tuning
104   and without requiring any structural adjustments, thereby showcasing its generalizability and effec-
105   tiveness.

224   3.2   QR-A DAPTOR F RAMEWORK
225
226   Our approach differs from previous methods relying on fixed or hierarchical dynamic single config-
227
      urations. We jointly explore the configuration space of quantization bits and LoRA ranks, creating
      a comprehensive search space that encompasses all potential optimal configurations. The main
228
      challenges in implementing this gradient-free optimization process are (a) The high-dimensional,
229
      discrete nature of the configuration space. (b) The computational cost of evaluating performance.
230   To address these challenges, we propose QR-Adaptor, a method that effectively finds the relative
231   optimal solution in three stages.
232
233   Task Information Based Initialization Our optimization process begins with a task-oriented as-
234   sessment of the relative importance of each layer in the model. This approach is based on the tacit
235   understanding that different layers contribute unequally to the model’s overall performance for spe-
236   cific tasks. Unlike previous methods that relied on gradient norms to quantify layer importance—an
237   approach that fails to accurately represent a layer’s contribution during inference—we employ a
238   task-specific method based on information entropy during the inference process. We define the
239
      importance of a layer l for a given task as:
240                                         I(l) = H(Y ) − H(Y |Xl )                                       (6)
241
242
      In the above, H(Y ) is the entropy of the model’s output for the task, and H(Y |Xl ) is the conditional
      entropy of the output given the intermediate representation at layer l. This measure quantifies how
243
      much information each layer contributes to the final output, providing a more accurate representation
244
      of layer importance in the context of the specific task. This task-oriented approach allows us to
245   strategically allocate higher bit widths and ranks to layers that are critical for the given task, rather
246   than relying on generic importance metrics that may not reflect true inference contributions.

288   Before introducing the PRGA flow, we first introduce some key foundational concepts. In a multi-
289   objective minimization problem with n objective components fi (x), i = 1, . . . , n, the Pareto Domi-
290   nance Relationship is defined between any two decision variables Xa and Xb . We say that Xa domi-
291   nates Xb if for all i ∈ {1, 2, . . . , n}, fi (Xa ) ≤ fi (Xb ), and there exists at least one i ∈ {1, 2, . . . , n}
292   such that fi (Xa ) < fi (Xb ). A Non-dominated Solution is a decision variable that is not dominated
293
      by any other decision variable in the set. The concept of Pareto Rank is used to categorize solu-
      tions within a set. Non-dominated solutions are assigned a Pareto rank of 1. After removing these
294
      rank 1 solutions from the set, the remaining non-dominated solutions are assigned a Pareto rank of 2.
295
      This process continues iteratively, assigning increasing ranks to subsequent layers of non-dominated
296   solutions until all solutions in the set have been ranked.

506             5                      R ELATED W ORK
507
508            LLM Quantization. The field of LLM quantization has witnessed substantial progress, driven by
509            the need for efficient model deployment. Recent research has introduced several innovative ap-
510            proaches. Frantar et al. (2023) have developed GPTQ, which achieves 4-bit precision with layer-
511
               wise quantization. Lin et al. (2023) have proposed AWQ, which improves accuracy for heavily
               quantized models. Yao et al. (2022) have introduced ZeroQuant, which preserves zero-shot capa-
512
               bilities at lower bit widths. Dettmers et al. (2022) have presented LLM.int8(), which enables 8-bit
513
               quantization for consumer hardware. Kim et al. (2023) have combined quantization with pruning
514            and knowledge distillation in SqueezeLLM. Guan et al. (2024) have optimized the balance between
515            compression and performance through mixed-precision quantization with APTQ. These develop-
516            ments significantly enhance the efficiency and accessibility of large language models.
517
               Parameter Efficient Fine-Tuning. PEFT techniques have become crucial for enhancing LLMs
518
               without increasing inference overhead. Recent innovations have expanded the field. Dettmers et al.
519
               (2023) have introduced QLoRA, which combines 4-bit quantization with low-rank adapters. Li
520            et al. (2023) have presented LoftQ, which alternates between quantization and low-rank approxima-
521            tion steps. Berman & Peherstorfer (2024) have introduced CoLoRA for accelerating the prediction
522            of solution fields under new parameters. AdaLoRA (Zhang et al., 2023a) proposes adaptive budget
523            allocation for low-rank updates, while LQ-LoRA (Guo et al., 2023) combines low-rank decomposi-
524            tion with quantization for efficient fine-tuning under memory constraints. Additionally, Zhou et al.
525            (2024) have introduced RankAdaptor, which is a hierarchical dynamic low-rank adaptation method
526            for structural pruned LLMs. These advancements demonstrate the evolving landscape of PEFT
527            techniques, offering innovative solutions for efficient LLM fine-tuning across diverse applications.

529             6                      C ONCLUSION
530
531            We have identified the issues arising in the current fine-tuning of quantized models and have estab-
532            lished two constraints accordingly. Under these constraints, the performance of fine-tuning quan-
533            tized models will at least not be worse than before fine-tuning. To achieve higher performance in
534            low-bit models while saving memory during fine-tuning, we propose QR-Adaptor, a general and ef-
535            ficient fine-tuning framework. It enables low-bit models to outperform fine-tuned models at the orig-
536            inal precision. Based on our experimental results, we found that altering the bit-width of each layer
537
               and adjusting the allocation of trainable parameters can lead to significant shifts in performance,
               and this trend is largely predictable by the algorithm. In theory, our framework is also applicable to
538
               high-precision models, but this paper primarily focuses on fine-tuning under quantization.

      To ensure the reproducibility of our results, we provide comprehensive documentation on the steps
543   required to replicate our experiments. Our code is available in scripts such as optuna main-v3.py,
544   post training mixed quant.py, and run optuna.py, which handle hyperparameter optimiza-
545   tion, mixed-precision quantization, and evaluation. For data preparation, we utilize the Alpaca
546   Cleaned Dataset from yahma/alpaca-cleaned, which is automatically downloaded and processed
547   using the datasets library. Our environment setup requires an NVIDIA GPU with CUDA sup-
548   port, preferably with at least 20 GB of memory for the LLaMA 2 model, as well as Python 3.8+
549   and dependencies like PyTorch, Transformers, Optuna, BitsAndBytes, PEFT, and other libraries,
550   which can be installed via the requirements.txt file. The model we fine-tune is the LLaMA 2
551
      architecture (NousResearch/Llama-2-7b-hf), using a mixed-precision quantization approach via
      bitsandbytes and Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) with the peft library. The training is conducted
552
      using a mixed-precision setup where the model’s dtype is set to torch.bfloat16 to optimize mem-
553
      ory usage and computation efficiency. Our hyperparameter optimization framework leverages Op-
554   tuna to maximize model accuracy while minimizing memory usage, tuning parameters like quan-
555   tization bits (4 or 8 bits) and LoRA ranks (2 to 16). To replicate our training process, researchers
556   can execute the provided scripts using the specified command-line arguments, which configure the
557   model, output directories, number of trials, and evaluation tasks. Model checkpoints and Optuna re-
558   sults are saved at regular intervals. The training is conducted using the Hugging Face Trainer, con-
559   figured with parameters including a batch size of 4, gradient accumulation steps of 16, warmup steps
560   of 100, and a learning rate of 1e-4, with evaluation and model saving steps set to every 200 steps.
561   Evaluation is conducted using the lm eval library, where metrics such as accuracy are recorded
562
      and saved in JSON format. All hyperparameter settings and model configurations are logged in the
      output directory, along with training progress and memory usage. Random seeds are set to ensure
563
      deterministic behavior. By following these steps, including hardware and software specifications,

      and running the scripts with the provided configurations, researchers can reproduce our experiments

995    D     M ORE I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
996
997    In optimizing the pruned LLaMA-7B model, a carefully designed hyperparameter configuration has
998    been implemented to strike a balance between model performance and computational efficiency.
999    The model is fine-tuned using a learning rate of 3 × 10−4 , with a batch size of 128, divided into
1000
       micro-batches of 4 to effectively manage memory limitations. Input sequences are capped at 256
       tokens, and a dropout rate of 0.05 is applied to the LoRA layers, specifically targeting the query,
1001
       key, value, and output projections, as well as the gate, down, and up projections. Layer-specific
1002
       quantization is applied at both 4-bit and 8-bit levels, optimizing memory usage while maintaining
1003   computational accuracy. The training is performed using the paged AdamW optimizer with 32-bit
1004   precision, ensuring both stability and efficiency. These settings have been rigorously tested and
1005   refined through the Optuna framework to achieve an optimal balance between model performance
1006   and resource efficiency.
