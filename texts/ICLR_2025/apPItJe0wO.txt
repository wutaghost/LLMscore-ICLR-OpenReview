009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            In many critical machine learning (ML) applications it is essential for a model to in-
012            dicate when it is uncertain about a prediction. While large language models (LLMs)
013            can reach and even surpass human-level accuracy on a variety of benchmarks, their
014            overconfidence in incorrect responses is still a well-documented failure mode. Tra-
015            ditional methods for ML uncertainty quantification can be difficult to directly adapt
016
               to LLMs due to the computational cost of implementation and closed-source nature
               of many models. A variety of black-box methods have recently been proposed,
017
               but these often rely on heuristics such as self-verbalized confidence. We instead
018
               propose a framework for measuring an LLM’s uncertainty with respect to the
019            distribution of generated explanations for an answer. While utilizing explanations
020            is not a new idea in and of itself, by interpreting each possible model+explanation
021            pair as a test-time classifier we can calculate a posterior answer distribution over
022            the most likely of these classifiers. We demonstrate how a specific instance of
023            this framework using explanation entailment as our classifier likelihood improves
024            confidence score metrics (in particular AURC and AUROC) over baselines across
025            five different datasets. We believe these results indicate that our framework is a
026            promising way of quantifying uncertainty in LLMs.
027
028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Large language models (LLMs) are known to at times confidently provide wrong answers, which can
031   greatly mislead non-expert users of the model (Xiong et al., 2023; Chang et al., 2023). In the some
032   cases an LLM may even ‘hallucinate’ facts all together (Xiao & Wang, 2021; Zhang et al., 2023).
033   Although scaling generally improves factual accuracy, past work has shown that even the largest
034   models can give incorrect answers to certain types of questions (Lin et al., 2021).
035
      To prevent these misleading scenarios, one intuitive approach is to have the model also report its
036   confidence (or uncertainty) in the accuracy of its own response. This task, known as uncertainty
037   quantification, has a vast associated literature (Abdar et al., 2021; Gawlikowski et al., 2023). In its
038   most naive form, this can entail taking the softmax of prediction logits to calculate a ‘distribution’
039   over answers. However in most cases there is no guarantee that this metric should correspond to the
040   actual probability of correctness on a new datum. Empirically this mismatch has been demonstrated
041   for LLM token logits (Kuhn et al., 2023; Achiam et al., 2023).
042   One might instead hope that by probing the model (e.g. through its weights or activations) one
043   could infer a measure of confidence that somehow aligns with our expectations. However, full
044   access to a large language model is often infeasible due to a combination of proprietary restrictions
045   and computational expense. Recently a range of ‘black-box’ approaches have been proposed that
046   avoid the need for access to internal model information (Kadavath et al., 2022; Xiong et al., 2023;
047   Shrivastava et al., 2023). These approaches typically rely on custom prompting strategies to elicit
048   self-verbalized (linguistic) confidence or generate multiple variations of a response (consistency).
049   While empirically promising, these methods are heuristic and still return overconfident responses in
050
      many cases.
051   We reason that a central issue with existing uncertainty quantification methods for LLMs stems from
052   the underlying inductive assumption that test and training data are sampled from the same distribution.
053   Unfortunately, this is often not true in practice, meaning any uncertainty quantification strategy that is
      well-calibrated on one dataset is not guaranteed to be calibrated on new test data. However, an LLM


                                                          1
054
      offers a unique opportunity to adjust its decision boundary at test-time, i.e. transductively (Vapnik &
055   Kotz, 2010). It does this via intermediate text (explanations) generated after observing the question.
056   While inserting random text would likely lead to a high-entropy decision distribution, adding relevant
057   facts or logical step-by-step reasoning serves to ‘stabilize’ the sampled answers around an isolated
058   minimum. Indeed, prompts inducing chain of thought (CoT) reasoning have already shown to
059   improve model accuracy in this manner (Wei et al., 2022) and reduce entropy (see Appendix B.4).
060   However, more recent work has suggested that even CoT explanations can be biased and may not
061   correspond with the correct answer (Turpin et al., 2024). Therefore, to properly determine an LLMs
062   uncertainty for new questions, one must determine which explanations are ‘stable’, both in the sense
063
      of reducing entropy towards a single answer and maintaining consistency with observed evidence.
064   In this work we propose a method for generating confidence scores from the distribution of LLM-
065   generated explanations for an answer. This method, which we call stable explanations confidence,
066   can be thought of as computing the posterior predictive distribution by marginalization over likely test-
067   time classifiers. We illustrate the usefulness of these scores on two common uncertainty quantification
068   tasks: calibration, in which we measure how close confidence is to empirical accuracy, and selective
069
      uncertainty, in which we determine how well the scores can discriminate between correct and
      incorrect predictions. We compare to other recently proposed methods across five datasets of different
070
      scope and complexity (CommonsenseQA, TruthfulQA, MedQA, MMLU Professional Law, MMLU
071
      Conceptual Physics) using two popular LLMs (GPT-3.5 (Brown et al., 2020) and GPT4 (Achiam et al.,
072   2023)). We find that our method on average outperforms baselines on the selective uncertainty task
073   (measured via AUROC and AURC), particularly for more complex question-answering problems.

      In this section we first summarize the uncertainty quantification problem in machine learning. We
      then highlight key challenges in the natural language generation setting and the ‘confidence gap’ of
079
      existing LLM models. Lastly we discuss exisiting approaches for LLM uncertainty quantification
080
      and methods for their evaluation.

082
      2.1   U NCERTAINTY Q UANTIFICATION IN M ACHINE L EARNING
083
084   Defining and reasoning about uncertainty has been a long-standing problem in different disciplines
085   including philosophy, statistics, and economics. Many formal representations with unique properties
086   have been proposed, (e.g. Dempster-Shafer belief functions, ranking functions, etc. (Halpern, 2017)),
087   but in the machine learning setting uncertainty quantification typically relies on the standard language
088   of probability measures. For a classification task we can think of the sequential training data-label
089   pairs D := {(xi , yi )}N
                             i=1 as the model’s source of knowledge about the world. Given some test datum
090   xN +1 , we would like the model to both make a prediction ŷN +1 and provide a ‘useful’ confidence
091
      score rN +1 ∈ [0, 1]. Useful confidence scores should allow models to express their belief in the
      accuracy of a prediction, and is called well-calibrated if on average predictions with confidence r
092
      are correct close to 100r% of the time. If the classification task also specifies cases for which it is
093
      better to return no prediction than a wrong one, we can imagine creating some selection rule using
094   confidence scores to determine whether to trust the classifier’s prediction. We will formalize these
095   two related goals later when discussing evaluation metrics in Section 4.1.
096
097
      Uncertainty quantification methods differ from one another based on their assumptions about where
      uncertainty is coming from. Sources of uncertainty are traditionally categorized into two broad
098
      classes: epistemic uncertainty arising from the agent’s incomplete knowledge of the world, and
099
      aleatoric uncertainty inherent to the data generating process (e.g. the flip of a coin). In reality,
100   definitions vary among the machine learning community (Baan et al., 2023) and most methods do
101   not fit neatly into either category. In this work we discuss a few of most common methods based on
102   the underlying assumptions placed on the test data. We make this distinction because without this
103   fundamental assumption it is impossible to know anything about the test distribution from training
104   data. Note that for a full discussion and taxonomy of the numerous uncertainty quantification methods
105   in machine learning we refer to a suvery paper such as (Abdar et al., 2021; Gawlikowski et al., 2023).
106
107   Related Training and Test Worlds. Most uncertainty quantification methods rely on the fundamen-
      tal assumption that the test data comes from the same distribution as the training set. Under this type


                                                         2
108
      of assumption Bayesian approaches such as Bayesian Neural Networks (BNNs) are popular. BNNs
109   measure epistemic uncertainty through a posterior on the learned weights, which can be reduced as
110   more data is recieved (Neal, 2012; Jospin et al., 2022). Another popular method is that of conformal
111   prediction, which introduces a somewhat dual notion of the conformal set. Under a slightly weaker
112   exchangibility assumption (i.e. that the joint distribution remains the same under permutations of
113   the training and test data), the conformal set of predictions is guaranteed to contain the true label
114   with error probability less than some ϵ (Shafer & Vovk, 2008). Weaker predictive models result in
115   larger conformal sets, and so set size can be taken as an indicator for higher model uncertainty. Other
116   methods include looking at the robustness of predictions under semantic-preserving transformations
117
      of the input, as mentioned in (Gawlikowski et al., 2023).
118
119
120   Different Training and Test Worlds. Small and large differences between training and test
121   distributions are typically denoted as distribution shift and out-of-distribution respectively (Yang
122   et al., 2021). In this setting methods like prior networks attempt to capture the specific notion of
123   this distributional uncertainty through and additional prior over predictive distributions and training
124   explicitly on a loss objective (Malinin & Gales, 2018).

      2.2   U NCERTAINTY Q UANTIFICATION IN LLM S
129
130   Recently much attention has been devoted to measuring uncertainty specifically in LLMs (Geng et al.,
131   2023; Huang et al., 2023). Since LLMs are generative models, uncertainty may be measured with
132   respect to an infinite set of text sequences as opposed to a fixed number of classification labels (Baan
133   et al., 2023). Many works, however, use multiple choice question answering tasks to evaluate LLMs
134   using standard classification methodologies (Wang et al., 2022; Kadavath et al., 2022), and we will
135
      follow a similar approach in this work. Issues with using token logits directly to compute confidence
      are well known. Recent works (Achiam et al., 2023; Kadavath et al., 2022; Steyvers et al., 2024)
136
      show that larger models are typically better calibrated on multiple choice datasets than smaller ones,
137
      but are still sensitive to question reformulations as well as typical RLHF training strategies. Another
138   recent work (Yin et al., 2023) notes that language models fail to identify unanswerable questions at a
139   higher rate than humans.
140
141
      At a high level, existing techniques for LLM confidence elicitation can be classified as either white-
      box, requiring access to internal model weights and token probabilities, or black-box, using only
142
      samples from the model (Geng et al., 2023). We choose to summarize inference time interventions
143
      below, as training time interventions are often computationally expensive and require strict inductive
144   assumptions.
145
146   White-box Methods. Access to the last activation layer of the LLM (token logits) admits calculating
147
      token and token sequence probabilities via the softmax function. One can incorporate text sequence
      probabilities to implement conformal prediction (Kumar et al., 2023) methods, or adjust them based
148
      on semantic importance of individual tokens to improve calibration (Duan et al., 2023). Surrogate
149
      models can also serve as an effective substitute if access the original model is restricted-access
150   (Shrivastava et al., 2023). Internal activations can also be observed to determine if certain feature
151   directions are more or less truthful (Azaria & Mitchell, 2023; Burns et al., 2022).
152
153
      Black-box Methods. Black-box confidence typically uses one or both of the following approaches:
      Sample+aggregate methods involve analyzing the distributions of multiple responses sampled from
154
      the model (Xiong et al., 2023). Responses can be generated in a variety of ways, such as using
155
      chain-of-thought prompting (Wang et al., 2022), asking for multiple answers in a single response
156   (Tian et al., 2023), or perturbing the question in-between samples (Li et al., 2024). Confidence can
157   be found by observing the frequency with which answers occur, or by averaging over other metrics
158   (Chen & Mueller, 2023). Self-evaluation methods use customized prompts in order for the model to
159   generate its own confidence estimates in natural language (Kadavath et al., 2022). These methods can
160   also be augmented with chain-of-thought or other more complex reasoning steps (Dhuliawala et al.,
161   2023). Much effort has been put into analyzing how changes in prompt (e.g. by including few-shot
      examples) affects these confidences (Zhou et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2024).

      Given a question, we would like to assign a confidence value to an answer based on how plausible its
165   associated explanations are. Intuitively, humans are confident in an answer when likely explanations
166   exist for it and no other answers have reasonable explanations. However, the space of explanations
167   (variable-length token sequences) is infinite and hard to work with directly. To overcome this, we
168   will first approximate this distribution by sampling a set of explanations from the LLM conditioned
169   on the question, and then reweight based on their logical consistency with the question description.
170   Afterwards we can compute the degree to which explanations support each answer. We can view
171   these two steps as estimating the posterior likelihood of the explanation given the question, and the
172   conditional answer distribution of the test-time model parameterized by this explanation. These two
173
      components will allow us to compute a posterior predictive distribution in a Bayesian fashion. We
      formalize each step in the following subsections, and summarize the complete method in Algorithm 1.

254   4.1   S ETUP
255
256   Evaluation Method. How do we know whether a proposed confidence metric is useful or not?
257   In line with previous works (Kadavath et al., 2022; Xiong et al., 2023; Shrivastava et al., 2023;
258   Tian et al., 2023) there are typically two tasks that uncertainty metrics are evaluated on. The first is
259   confidence calibration, where the goal is to produce confidence scores approximating the empirical
260   probability that the model answers the question correctly. Expected calibration error (ECE) (Naeini
261
      et al., 2015; Nixon et al., 2019) attempts to estimate this using differences between the average
      confidence and accuracy for a group of similarly scored answers, however ECE can be misleading
262
      (see Section 5). We still include this metric in our reports for ease of comparison with previous work.
263
      The second related task is typically called selective uncertainty (also known as failure prediction).
264   Here the goal is to create a binary classifier using confidence scores that predict when the model
265   should return ‘I don’t know’ instead of its original prediction. A variety of classifier metrics can be
266   used, depending on how one chooses to penalize false positive (overconfident) and false negative
267   (underconfident) predictions. In this work we use two of the most common metrics: area under the
268   reciever-operator curve (AUROC) (Hendrycks & Gimpel, 2016), and area under the risk-coverage
269   curve (AURC)(Ding et al., 2020). Uninformative (i.e. completely random) confidence scores will
      have a worst-case AUROC of 0.5 and an worst-case AURC equal to average model accuracy. The

453   5    D ISCUSSION
454
455   In this study, we propose a framework for eliciting confidences from large language models (LLMs)
456   by estimating the distribution of semantically likely explanations, which can be thought of as a set of
457   conditional classifiers. We compare our method with five other common confidence metrics across
458   five benchmark datasets and find that our method on average improves the ability to predict incorrect
459
      answers (selective uncertainty), particularly for GPT-4-Turbo and for more complex questions such as
      MedQA. We believe that these results encourage thinking about uncertainty with respect to test-time
460
      model parameters and data, as opposed to empirical calibration with previously seen data.
461
462
      Alternate Perspectives. While the most straightforward description of our stable explanation
463
      method is via a Bayesian predictive posterior, there are interesting connections to be made with
464   transductive inference, stability analysis, and asymptotically to Solomonoff induction. We highlight
465   the transductive connection here, and include additional perspectives in Appendix C. Transductive
466   learning optimizes a classifier at inference-time based on a combination of training and test data,
467   typically by fine-tuning some classifier parameter based on an explicit loss objective (Dhillon et al.,
468   2020; Vapnik; Joachims et al., 1999). In the LLM setting one can view finetuning an explanation
469   before providing an answer as a way of doing partial transductive inference. While obviously one
470   cannot at inference time compute the full loss over all training and test data, using a logical consistency
471   measure like entailment probability may effectively be approximating this training loss, as it prevents
472
      overfitting to the test datum.
473
474
      Calibration With regards to performance of calibration (ECE) task not being at the state-of-the-art,
      we stress that calibration metrics rely on the inductive hypothesis that training, test, and calibration
475
      data are all drawn from the same distribution, which is nether verifiable nor falsifiable at test-time.
476
      Therefore, ECE metrics conflate uncertainty about the answer, which is the confidence measure
477   we wish to quantify, with uncertainty about the validity of the inductive hypothesis, that cannot be
478   quantified. Additionally previous work such as (Ding et al., 2020) have demonstrated bias in the
479   metric depending on accuracy and binning strategy. For this reason we indicate the ECE metric in red
480   in the tables, but include the results nonetheless for transparency and ease of comparison.
481
482   Limitations and Future Work A notable exception to the observed trend of improved selective
483   uncertainty occurs when making stable confidence predictions on simpler questions (e.g. average
484   question lengths of CSQA and MMLU Conceptual Physics are less than half of others). We hy-
485   pothesize that when questions resemble classical inductive classification tasks, the advantage of
      our test-time computation is less evident. Additionally, our analysis is limited in scope to multiple


                                                                        9
486
      choice datasets, leaving open-ended responses to future work. While entailment probability does
487   help discount some logically incorrect explanations (Figure 1), there are still instances where it
488   fails to properly distinguish. We test some alternatives to explanation faithfulness in Appendix B.5,
489   but further exploration is needed. Efficiently sampling high quality explanations remains an open
490   question as well. Our method adjusts the given explanation distribution based on plausibility, but
491   better explanations may still exist that are not sampled by the LLM. One possible solution could
492   involve using our entailment probability measure as a way to accept or reject incoming samples,
493   increasing complexity but ensuring higher quality.

       Transductive learning selects a classifier at inference-time based on a combination of training and test
1156
       data (Dhillon et al., 2020; Vapnik; Joachims et al., 1999). Typically transductive learning involves
1157   fine-tuning some classifier parameter w based on an explicit loss objective. However, we claim that
1158   using an LLM to generate a sequence of text before an answer (i.e. an explanation) is an alternate
1159   way of doing transductive reasoning. First, note that answering a question after an explanation, such
1160   as in chain-of-thought prompting (Wei et al., 2022), effectively changes the decision boundary of the
1161   LLM classifier at inference time. Second, consider that when an LLM generates an explanation, it
1162   produces concepts related to those in the question. These additional concepts can be thought of as
1163   forcing the LLM at inference time to pay more attention to the decision boundary in the area around
1164   the test datum. In-context learning literature, which examines LLM performance after manually

1211   Another recent line of work has been analyzing LLMs through the lens of stochastic dynamical
1212   models (Soatto et al., 2023). Through the perspective of stability analysis one could interpret our

1216   on this explanation (a′ ← ϕ(q, e)). Intuitively this system mirrors how a human expert may think
1217   about a question by considering alternative conclusions one could draw given beliefs about the world.

1219
