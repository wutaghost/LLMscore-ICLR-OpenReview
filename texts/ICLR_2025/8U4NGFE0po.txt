009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent advances explore prompt tuning for large language models (LLMs) and
012            develop automatic optimization frameworks to obtain suitable prompts with re-
013            spect to desired output quality metrics. Although existing approaches can handle
014            conventional tasks such as fixed-solution question answering, defining the met-
015            ric becomes complicated when the output quality cannot be easily assessed by
016            comparisons with standard golden samples, especially for those natural language
017            applications that multiple outputs are equally valid. Consequently, optimizing the
018
               prompts effectively and efficiently without a clear metric becomes a critical chal-
               lenge. To address this issue, we present PLHF, a few-shot prompt optimization
019
               framework inspired by the well-known RLHF technique. Different from näive
020
               strategies involving human experts, PLHF employs a specific evaluator module
021            acting as the metric to estimate the output quality. PLHF requires only a single
022            round of human feedback to complete the entire prompt optimization process. Em-
023            pirical results on both public and industrial datasets show that PLHF significantly
024            outperforms existing output scoring strategies for LLM prompt optimizations.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   General-purpose large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated substantial capabilities across
030   various fields in recent years. However, solving complex tasks with LLMs often requires appropriate
031   customizations on LLMs to fit the task requirements. While fine-tuning pre-trained LLMs is a
032   common approach, it may be infeasible when there is limited training data, restricted computational
033
      resource, or when working with a black-box LLM. Alternatively, previous studies (Wang et al., 2022;
      Shin et al., 2020) have shown that the potential of LLMs can also be fully leveraged with suitable
034
      prompts. Recent literature develops automatic few-shot prompt optimization for LLM usages, such
035
      as DSPy (Khattab et al., 2024) and TextGrad (Yuksekgonul et al., 2024). To determine an effective
036   prompt for the LLM, existing methods often employ gradient descent or other algorithms (Yang
037   et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2023; Guo et al., 2023) to optimize the performance with respect to desired
038   metrics (i.e., definition of output quality). Overall, the key to success heavily relies on the output
039   quality evaluations which shall precisely reveal the model performance to the optimizer.
040
      Although such output quality metrics are often well-defined for the tasks which can be modeled as
041
      the traditional discriminative tasks (e.g., classifications and regressions) where the performance can
042   be directly evaluated given ground-truths, scoring the outputs often becomes non-trivial for most
043   generation-type of tasks. An example comes from the essay writing task where the LLM needs
044   to output an essay given specific requirements. To perform prompt optimizations, the evaluator
045   needs to score an essay written by an LLM. This could be extremely challenging without human’s
046   involvement. Another typical scenario is the dialogue systems (e.g., chat-bots), as automatically
047   rating the outputs is difficult. In this case, the output quality could be affected by numerous factors
048   such as the context, the circumstances of the environment, as well as specific user preferences.
049   Therefore, a generalized evaluation metric is difficult to formulate.
050   The absence of a precise metric would hinder the effectiveness of the prompt optimization process
051   for a generative task. Most prompt optimization systems adopt two types of mechanisms to score the
052   generated outputs. The first is employing simple evaluators, such as exact matching or soft matching
053   (based on certain similarity measurement) to compare the generated outputs with the observed sam-
      ples. The second common strategy is to utilize existing LLMs for the output scoring. For instance,

086   several studies (Wang et al., 2023; Fu et al., 2024) leverage advanced OpenAI GPT models (Achiam
087   et al., 2023) as an external judge to score the generated outputs. Nevertheless, such scorers suffer
088   from a critical drawback as generic pre-trained LLMs might not have enough contextual or back-
089   ground knowledge to behave as accurate scoring functions. (See Figure 1 for the examples of failure
090   cases.) Ultimately, the purpose of prompt optimization is to tackle complex tasks that the original
091   LLM, when provided with a simple prompt, fails to handle effectively. Consequently, employing
092   such a sub-optimal LLM as the scoring mechanism is likely to diminish the effectiveness of the
093
      prompt optimization framework. As a result, for applications related to response generation, it is
      highly demanded to involve real human experts to evaluate the results generated by LLMs, yet such
094
      scheme suffers from a frequently occurring issue — constrained budget to employ human experts.
095
096   For most of the automatic prompt optimization frameworks (Khattab et al., 2024; Yuksekgonul et al.,
097   2024; Pryzant et al., 2023; Deng et al., 2022; Wen et al., 2024), multiple iterations of optimizations
098   are performed, with the quality estimated based on the given metric. With querying human experts
099
      acting as the metric, once the prompt is updated with any modifications, we have to ask human ex-
      perts for their judgement again for each of the training inputs, which might cause serious efficiency
100
      bottleneck. To address the aforementioned issues, we present PLHF (which stands for Prompt
101
      Learning with Human Feedback), a few-shot prompt optimization framework. Inspired by the fa-
102   mous Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) technique (Ouyang et al., 2022; Li
103   et al., 2023), PLHF introduces a particular evaluator module E which requires human scoring no
104   greater than linear (with respect to the number of training samples) times during the optimization
105   process. To leverage human feedback in few shots, we consider utilizing a prompt-optimized LLM
106   as E to evaluate the output of the main responder R. The overall framework is depicted in Fig-
107   ure 2. Specifically speaking, first, we employ human experts to provide judgements as scores on a
      set of training samples D, containing pairs of the inputs and sample text-outputs. Then, we perform


                                                                                 2
108
      prompt optimization on another LLM to mimic the human experts’ preference pattern based on D.
109   Since the prompting task on the evaluator module is relatively typical (e.g., binary classifications
110   or regressions), we can leverage any existing automatic few-shot prompt optimization frameworks
111   (e.g., DSPy (Khattab et al., 2024)) with trivial metrics (e.g., Accuracy or Mean Absolute Error) to
112   obtain the evaluator module. Finally, we can perform prompt optimizations for both E and R to
113   establish the entire framework.
114
      To verify the actual effectiveness of PLHF, experiments are conducted not only on multiple bench-
115   mark datasets but also on a real-world industrial data collected from an online customer support
116   chat-bot product of an AI company. The experimental results shown in Section 4 demonstrate that
117   PLHF can boost the output quality of existing automatic few-shot prompt optimization frameworks
118   with our duo-module design. Moreover, to verify the effectiveness of each module, we also pro-
119   vide an analysis towards the relations in performance curves between the number of training sam-
120   ples and the output scores for each subtask. The source codes for the experiments are available at
121   https://(keeping secret for the double-blind paper review).
122   In summary, our contributions are as follows:
123
124         • We study automatic few-shot prompt optimization for LLMs with limited number of human
125           feedback calls, which is a more reasonable and feasible setting in real-world applications,
126           especially for those have specific user preferences or multiple acceptable outputs.
127         • We introduce PLHF, a novel prompt optimization framework that does not directly rely on
128           well-defined metrics for the text output. Instead, we design an evaluator module to provide
129           an automatic mechanism that evaluates text outputs for LLM program prompting.
130         • We conduct extensive experiments on publicly accessible benchmark datasets as well as a
131           test on industrial data to validate the effectiveness of PLHF. The results show that PLHF
132           has superiority in terms of output quality, compared with the approaches employing string
133           matching or adopting the state-of-the-art LLM (GPT-4o) as the evaluator.

      Recent research has investigated various strategies to obtain suitable prompts for LLMs. Earlier
138   studies have introduced techniques of automating the search process for data samples (Gao et al.,
139   2021), learning prompts through gradient-based searching methods (Shin et al., 2020; Wen et al.,
140   2024; Pryzant et al., 2023), refining prompts using evolutionary algorithms (Guo et al., 2023; Fer-
141   nando et al., 2023) and utilizing other LLMs for prompt generation (Yang et al., 2024; Zhou et al.,
142   2023). Several studies have also attempted to optimize prompts using reinforcement learning, ex-
143   ploring prompt editing at different granular levels such as word-level (Deng et al., 2022), phrase-
144   level (Zhang et al., 2023), and within text-to-image generation tasks (Hao et al., 2024).
145   As LLMs are increasingly applied in real-world scenarios, in-context learning (McCann et al., 2018;
146   Radford et al., 2018; Brown et al., 2020) is becoming an emerging trend for effective LLM program-
147   ming. Instruction tuning (Ouyang et al., 2022) further enhances this process by enabling complex
148   behaviors through the use of structured prompts (Press et al., 2023; Yao et al., 2023; Khot et al.,
149   2023; Madaan et al., 2024).
150   For automatic few-shot prompt optimization, Khattab et al. (2024) introduced DSPy, a state-of-
151   the-art prompt optimization framework, which considers LLM usages in a programmatic fashion.
152   DSPy parameterizes each module to learn the data pattern and the desired behaviors by iteratively
153   bootstrapping useful demonstrations. On the other hand, Yuksekgonul et al. (2024) inspired by LLM
154   fine-tuning procedures and proposed TextGrad, a framework refining the prompt with the back-
155   propagation algorithm. Instead of deriving numeral-valued gradients, TextGrad regards LLMs’ text
156   feedback as the ‘gradient’ in texts.
157   All these techniques rely on well-defined metrics to set their objectives. While advanced general-
158   purpose LLMs like GPT-4 can be adopted for text-output evaluations (Zimbres, 2024; Zheng et al.,
159   2023; Tan et al., 2024), they may lack the contextual or background knowledge needed for accurate
160   evaluation in specific tasks. Hence, involving human feedback becomes inevitable for the prompt
161   optimizations in such tasks. To address the issue, Lin et al. (2024) inspired by dueling bandits and
      designed a strategy to choose pairs of prompts to query for human feedback during the prompt

201   3       PLHF: P ROMPT L EARNING FROM F EW- SHOT H UMAN F EEDBACK
202
203   As shown in Figure 2, our entire framework, PLHF, is designed to perform prompt optimizations
204   for typical language model program usages — output a proper response based on the given input.
205
      The whole process is guided by the principal intuition of taking advantages from few-shot in-context
      learning Brown et al. (2020) to capture the contextual patterns from limited number of labeled sam-
206
      ples. Since there is no explicit metric available, a scoring function is needed for existing prompt
207
      optimization frameworks. Hence, we introduce an evaluator module E, acting as the scoring func-
208   tion for the main responder module R. The two modules correspond to two respective subtasks.
209
210

213   ponent is based on a base LLM, denoted as MR , which generates outputs based on the input query.
214   LLM MR is starting from pairing with an initial prompt PR describing the roles of input and out-
215   put, as well as the relationship between input and output (i.e., how the input determines the output).

      3.2   E VALUATOR TASK
255
256
      The evaluator task for E is an auxiliary task designed to verify and score the output generated by
257
      the responder R. Similar to the responder R, the evaluator E is also built with a base LLM, denoted
258   as ME . The evaluator task considers training samples in the triplet form of input, output, and the
259   corresponding score (e.g., verdict, rating) labeled by human experts. Note that, different from the
260   training samples for R, this time we consider all training data samples (regardless of the positivity)
261   as the references for E. To provide a nuanced verdict, we also optimize the prompt PE for ME
262   with a trivial metric L (such as the conventional Accuracy or Mean Absolute Error) to evaluate the
263   quality of the response. The metric L is specifically defined as the loss to estimate the difference
264   between the predicted scores and the actual labeled scores in D. Overall speaking, The evaluator
265   module E leverages LLM ME with the finalized prompt PE′ to provide a score rating toward any
266
      responses for the original AI task.
267   With the evaluator E, PLHF ensures that the outputs from the responder R are not only technically
268   accurate but also contextually appropriate for the task. The feedback loop between the evaluator E
269   and the responder R helps refine the overall model performance, as the scores determined by E are
      used to inform future responses generated by R.

      3.3     I NTEGRATION AND F EEDBACK L OOPS
271
272   With the integration of responder R and evaluator E, the entire system operates in a feedback-loop
273   structure, as described in pseudo codes stated in Algorithm 1. At the beginning, we initialize prompts
274   as PR′ := PR and PE′ := PE for modules R and E, respectively. In each iteration of PLHF, first,
275   training data samples D are used to optimize the evaluator E (i.e., to update PE′ ). Then, we optimize
276   the responder R (i.e., update PR′ ) regarding the evaluator E with prompt PE′ as the metric. After an
277   iteration of prompt optimizations for PR′ and PE′ , we obtain a version of optimized prompts for the
278   responder R and the evaluator E. Figure 3 provides an example of a toy generative AI problem to
279
      demonstrate the optimized prompts PR′ and PE′ .
280   For batch tests, the whole optimization process is ended by Line 16 in Algorithm 1 and the finalized
281   prompt PR are used for the LLM MR to generate the outputs for upcoming test inputs/queries. On
282   the other hand, if the scenario has incrementing data samples, starting from Line 17 in Algorithm 1,
283   we can augment the training sample set D with the user feedback on new samples, then repeat the
284   optimization processes for E and R.
285   Overall, the proposed PLHF framework is capable of performing prompt optimizations for LLMs
286   even when occurring challenges of (a) no available well-defined metrics to evaluate the LLM output
287   quality for the specific task, (b) limited number (few-shot) of labeled samples for LLM prompting,
288   and (c) multiple valid outputs for a single input.

      4.1.1    S CHEMA G UIDED D IALOGUE (SGD) DATASET
304
305   The Schema Guided Dialogue (SGD) dataset Sun et al. (2021) is a large-scale dataset designed for
306   task-oriented dialogue systems. It comprises dialogues collected in English, specifically designed
307   to encompass a wide range of dialogue scenarios, schema-based actions, and services. The dataset
308   contains 1,000 dialogues, contributing to a total of 13,833 utterances. Each user utterance in the
309   dataset is labeled with a satisfaction score on a 5-point Likert scale (Likert, 1932). Rating 1 indicates
310   the lowest level of satisfaction, while rating 5 denotes the highest satisfaction. The distribution of
311   satisfaction ratings {1, 2, 3, 4, 5} is {120, 769, 11151, 1494, 50}. These human-assigned satisfaction
312
      scores are valuable for assessing chat-bot responses with respect to user satisfaction.

314   4.1.2    AUTOMATED E SSAY S CORING (AES) DATASETS
315

316
      (ASAP). The dataset, named as AES-ASAP, consists of eight essay sets varying in length, ranging
317
      from an average of 150 to 550 words per response. The responses were written by students in grades
318   seven through ten, and all essays were hand-graded by human experts. Each essay was double-
319   scored, with a resolved score provided to harmonize the differences between raters. We use the
320   training set (with the scores in domain 1) of the first set of essays in our experiments. The actual
321   text of the student’s response is included. We consider the average score as aggregated result from
322   these raters. The scores are distributed from 1 to 30. In our experiments, we discard the essays with
323   transcription errors (marked as “illegible” or containing placeholder text such as “???”) from the
      training data.

417   4.3   E VALUATIONS
418
419   For the model output, we employ multiple human experts as the judges to provide professional
420
      scores with respect to the score scales of the original data. However, for the public datasets, the
      original people who labeled the data are unavailable to give their judgement for our new generated
421
      outputs for the data inputs. In our experiments, we introduce a concept of pseudo-human judge to
422
      execute output evaluations. Specifically, we use GPT-4o with prompt optimizations via DSPy as
423   the pseudo-human judge. Since we consider GPT-3.5 for the base LLMs in all the methods for
424   experiments, the pseudo-human judge (i.e., GPT-4o with DSPy PO based on training samples) is a
425   more powerful model that can provide fair evaluations toward output quality.

471   new highs with greater number of samples. Last but not least, as expected, the standard deviations

      5     C ONCLUSION
475
476
477
      In this paper, we focused on prompt optimizations for LLMs with a limited amount of human feed-
      back — a more practical and achievable approach for real-world applications. To address the chal-
478
      lenges of no well-defined metrics and the scarce human resources, we introduced PLHF, a few-shot
479
      prompt learning with an evaluator module design to automatically score the outputs generated by
480   LLMs. We performed extensive experiments with public datasets and a real industrial dataset to
481   verify the effectiveness of PLHF. The experimental results demonstrated that PLHF outperforms
482   existing methods across from simple string matching functions to even the latest publicly available
483   LLMs as output evaluators in terms of the output quality. Overall, our proposed framework is practi-
484   cally effective especially for the scenarios when directly applying pre-trained general-purpose LLMs
485   are not the best option. Our future work involves enhancing and deploying the proposed framework
      across diverse applications, particularly for tasks that utilize multi-modal data.
