009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The rise of “jailbreak” attacks on language models has led to a flurry of defenses
012            aimed at preventing undesirable responses. Nonetheless, most benchmarks remain
013            to be solved, not to mention real-world safety problems. We critically examine the
014            two stages of the defense pipeline: (i) defining what constitutes unsafe outputs, and
015            (ii) enforcing the definition via methods such as fine-tuning or input preprocessing.
016
               To understand whether we fail because of definition or enforcement, we consider
               a simple and well-specified definition of unsafe outputs—outputs that contain
017
               the word “purple”. Surprisingly, all existing fine-tuning and input defenses fail
018
               to enforce this definition under adaptive attacks and increasing compute, casting
019            doubt on whether enforcement algorithms can be robust for more complicated
020            definitions. We hope that this definition serves as a testbed to evaluate enforcement
021            algorithms and prevent a false sense of security.
022

024   1    I NTRODUCTION
025
026   The standard pipeline for developing language models involves large-scale pretraining followed by an
027
      alignment phase to make generations confer to safety standards. These standards are meant to prevent
      the generation of undesirable content such as toxic text, misinformation, and private information
028
      (Weidinger et al., 2021; Pa Pa et al., 2023; Pan et al., 2023; Bommasani et al., 2022; Carlini et al.,
029
      2021). There are a wide array of benchmarks (Wang et al., 2024; Sun et al., 2024; Chao et al., 2024;
030   Mazeika et al., 2024) testing various aspects of these notions of safety. However, despite the effort
031   in devising defenses, most benchmarks remain unsolved and existing defenses can be jailbroken
032   to generate harmful content that violate safety requirements. In this work, we hope to guide the
033   development of successful defenses by identifying the cause of failure that underlies different safety
034   settings. Along the way, we also develop stress-tests and suggestions that help avoid false senses of
035   security which are common in jailbreaking.
036   To understand why we fail in developing successful alignment strategies, we conceptually split the
037   defense pipeline into two components: (1) obtaining an implicit or explicit definition of harmful
038   behavior and (2) creating an enforcement mechanism to make sure the defense adheres to the definition.
039   A defense can fail either because of shortcomings in the definition or gaps in enforcement. Since it is
040   challenging to create definitions that approximate real-world problems (Wei et al., 2023a; Azar et al.,
041   2023), more attention is paid to developing new enforcement strategies. But are we really creating
042   reliable enforcement strategies?
043   When definition and enforcement both contribute to a defense, it is hard to assess where the source
044   of error is from. Thus, in this paper, we study enforcements in a setting where the definition is
045   perfect and there are no gaps between what the model is trained on and tested against. We design the
046   definition with two key desiderata in mind: first, it should structurally resemble real-world safety
047   concerns; second, it should be “simple” and minimal to allow for effective stress-testing of best-case
048   performance and to identify general failures of different enforcement mechanisms.
049   We introduce the Purple Problem: prevent the model from outputting the word “purple”. This task
050   is designed to test enforcement mechanisms under the optimal conditions of a well-specified and
051   straightforward definition. The Purple Problem replicates a core question that is central to real-
052   world safety definitions, which is to restrict certain outputs (e.g., toxic phrases, private or personally
053   identifiable information). We intentionally design this toy problem to be easy to defend in order to
      serve as a minimal and easy-to-evaluate test case for real language models.

069   We test a broad collection of the best jailbreaking defenses utilizing fine-tuning (RLHF with DPO
070   (Rafailov et al., 2023), RLHF with PPO (Schulman et al., 2017), adversarial training) and preprocess-
071   ing inputs (system prompts (Zou et al., 2024b), in-context learning (Wei et al., 2023b), paraphrasing

073   Purple Problem allows us to consider more “powerful” versions of these methods by scaling up the
074   amount of alignment data, performing adversarial training, and combining multiple defenses.
075   To break these defenses, we devise new adaptive attacks (going beyond the standard gradient-based
076   GCG (Zou et al., 2023)). We demonstrate that a good initialization and sufficient suffix length of
077   the GCG attack is critical without which we might see a false sense of security. We also find a
078   concerning scaling relationship where one can counteract the benefits of scaling up the alignment
079   data for defenses by scaling the compute spent on optimizing the GCG string. For adaptivity in input
080   processing defenses, we develop attacks to bypass input filters and show that one can successfully
081   break a combination of defenses by combining attacks that target each defense separately.
082   Overall, we find that all existing defenses struggle to solve this simple problem. The adaptive methods
083   we identify in this work could serve as useful guidelines for stress-testing defenses in more complex
084   real-world settings. We show an example of this in Section 5 by breaking defenses in existing
085   definitions. Thus, our experiments suggest that we also need advancements in reliable enforcement
086   strategies. We propose the Purple Problem in hopes that it serves as a valuable test case to evaluate
087   future innovations in enforcement mechanisms.

      2       S ETUP OF JAILBREAKING
091
092
093   Consider a language model that maps input prompt x ∈ X to response y = L(x) ∈ Y. Some
094   responses are “unsafe” (e.g., information on how to build a bomb or code to automate spam emails)
095   and we would like to build a system that avoids outputting such responses. Though the safety of a
096   response depends on the input in the most general case, we are interested in the easier and necessary
097   subproblem of eliminating unconditionally harmful outputs in this paper. For simplicity, we assume
098
      that outputs are either safe or unsafe, with the set of unsafe outputs denoted by D⋆ ⊂ Y.1
099   Attacks. An attacker is interested in eliciting an unsafe response from the model. A common
100   approach is to pick a target response string ȳ ∈ D⋆ and find a prompt x that satisfies L(x) = ȳ.
101
      Defenses. The goal of the defender is to design a system that never outputs an unsafe response
102   y ∈ D⋆ . We measure the performance of a defense under an attack via the Defense Success Rate
103   (DSR): Px∼A [L(x) ̸∈ D⋆ ]. The goal of a defense is to succeed against all attacks. Hence, DSR for
104   any attack A serves as an upper bound on the underlying strength of the defense.

      3     A DEEPER INSPECTION OF THE DEFENSE PIPELINE
109
110
111   Models pretrained on internet-scale data will likely output unsafe responses, and several recent attacks
112
      can effectively find prompts xadv that elicit unsafe outputs. These methods can be implemented via
      gradient descent (Guo et al., 2021; Jones et al., 2023; Shin et al., 2020; Zou et al., 2023; Zhu et al.,
113
      2023; Sadasivan et al., 2024; Geisler et al., 2024), manual red-teaming (Ganguli et al., 2022; Wei
114
      et al., 2023a;b; Zeng et al., 2024), automated prompt search (Chao et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023; Xu
115   et al., 2023; Lapid et al., 2023; Casper et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024), or exploiting unclear definitions
116   (Ippolito et al., 2023; Wei et al., 2023a; Kotha et al., 2023). How should one develop LLM systems
117   that avoid generating unsafe responses while continuing to output useful responses? In this section,
118   we break down the various steps that go into a defense and examine the possible vulnerabilities
119   introduced in each stage (Figure 1).
120
121
      3.1   S TAGE ONE : D EFINITION
122
123
      Defenses start with some characterization of unsafe outputs denoted D̂ ⊂ Y. This definition can
124
      be captured via explicit rules/principles (Bai et al., 2022b; Zou et al., 2023; Ippolito et al., 2023;
125   Kumar et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023; Mu et al., 2024) or can be learned from data that reflects
126   human preferences (Bai et al., 2022a; Ouyang et al., 2022). The downstream defense aims to generate
127   outputs that are safe by this approximate definition. However, since the true set of unsafe responses
128   D⋆ is generally hard to characterize precisely (shown by works such as Azar et al. (2023)), we expect
129   that D̂ ̸= D⋆ . Therefore, one source of vulnerability is this gap between the approximate and true
130   definition. An attacker can successfully break the defense by targeting a response in D⋆ but not in D̂.
131
132
133   3.2   S TAGE TWO : E NFORCEMENT
134
135   In this framework, the mechanism of an enforcement is independent of the definition. This allows the
136
      enforcement to be used as a defense for any safety problem. Equipped with a definition of unsafe
137   outputs (D̂), defenses aim to never generate strings in D̂ while retaining general utility. This can
138
      happen at various layers.
139   Enforcement via fine-tuning weights. One approach to preventing unsafe outputs y ∈ D⋆ is training
140   the model on data representing unsafe (y ∈ D̂) and safe (y ̸∈ D̂) responses. This can be done via
141   methods such as (i) PPO (Schulman et al., 2017; Christiano et al., 2017; Ouyang et al., 2022), where
142   we first train a reward model using the annotated data and then fine-tune the base model using RL to
143   maximize the reward (ii) Direct Preference Optimization (Rafailov et al., 2023), where we optimize a
144   supervised objective that is morally equivalent to PPO, and (iii) supervised fine-tuning, where we
145   train the model to upweight safe responses. Though fine-tuning can generalize beyond the training
146   prompts, its vulnerability arises when an attacker can find new prompts that are sufficiently “far”
147
      away from the training distribution (i.e., mismatched generalization (Wei et al., 2023b)).
148   Enforcement via preprocessing inputs. In an attempt to address the above vulnerability, one can
149   employ input preprocessing focused on detecting or modifying malicious inputs. For example,
150   Alon & Kamfonas (2023) detects malicious prompts when they share perplexity/length to existing
151   jailbreaks. Inan et al. (2023); Li et al. (2023) use language models to detect toxic inputs. Several
152   defenses try to prevent adversarial attacks by modifying the prompt via prompting (Wei et al., 2023b;
153
      Zhang et al., 2023), adding a system prompt (Zou et al., 2024b), paraphrasing the input (Jain et al.,
      2023), or perturbing the prompt (Robey et al., 2023) to neutralize the effects of prompt optimization
154
      attacks.

191   4       T HE P URPLE P ROBLEM
192
193
      As new attacks keep breaking defenses, is it because current defenses have vulnerabilities in the
194   definition stage or enforcement stage? Since current benchmarks can not adequately ask this question,
195   we disentangle the vulnerabilities of both stages and ask one simple question: Can current enforcement
196   methods guard against attacks when using a perfect definition (i.e. D̂ = D⋆ )? To properly test this,
197   we introduce a new setting of a simple and well-defined definition of “unsafe” outputs. We call this
198   the Purple Problem.
199
200           Definition. An output is unsafe if and only if it contains the string “purple” or “Purple”3
201
202   As discussed above, practitioners equip pretrained models, known to readily output toxic content,
203   with various fine-tuning and input-based methods. In this paper, we simulate this process by taking a
204   base model, known to readily output “purple”, and enforce outputs that do not contain “purple” with
205   the same methods (Figure 2). We specifically choose the Purple Problem for two main reasons.
206   Well-specified definition. Since the definition stage is perfect by construction, this problem exposes
207   vulnerabilities only associated with the enforcement stage. We can make the definition used during
208   evaluation be the same as the definition at enforcement with ease. The Purple Problem serves as a
209   case study to understand the difficulties in reliable enforcement of safety definitions.
210   Easiest-case testbed. The Purple Problem is a distillation of more complex definitions for real
211   safety problems into the simplest version of prevention (e.g., preventing toxic content, unsafe code
212   generation, or private content). Defenses that fail to enforce the Purple Problem will struggle to
213   enforce more nuanced and complicated real safety problems for many reasons.

      Due to the simplistic nature of the Purple Problem, all enforcement algorithms that are tested on
230
      current benchmarks can be tested on the Purple Problem. Since the Purple Problem serves as a
231
      lower-bound testing ground, any weaknesses we find through the Purple Problem are likely to be
232   weaknesses for more complex structures or nuances in definition. On the other hand, strengths we find
233   in enforcements through the Purple Problem cannot be generalized to real-world safety. Therefore,
234   the Purple Problem serves as a minimal testing ground for enforcement methods: if an enforcement
235   can not solve the simple Purple Problem, how will it solve much more difficult problems? In the
236   following, we test whether each enforcement method can succeed on the Purple Problem.

      4.1     S ETUP
240
      We assume that the attacker has white-box access to the system: they know all aspects of the
241
      system including model weights and conversation template. While this provides more power to the
242
      attacker compared to black-box attacks, safety by obscurity often fails in ML since attacks optimized
243   against one model frequently transfer to others (Liu et al., 2017; Madry et al., 2019). Notably, Zou
244   et al. (2023) show that attacks against open source models generalize to black-box models like
245   ChatGPT. For our experiments, the defender will start with an instruction-tuned Llama-7b, Vicuna-7b,
246   or Llama-2-7b-chat, referred to as Llama-IT, Vicuna, and Llama-2-chat, respectively (details in
247   Appendix A).
248
      Evaluation metrics. We measure the ability of defenses to provide safety via the Defense Success
249   Rate metric (defined in Section 2). In general, we also want the system to remain useful; a system that
250   always outputs ‘‘I don’t know’’ is perfectly safe but useless. However, there is no simple way
251   to characterize or measure utility. We show that even if we don’t care about the defense preserving
252   utility, defenses still fail to our attacks. We only discard defenses with obvious degeneration such as
253   consistently generating gibberish or the exact same output.
254
      Purple questions dataset. We start by constructing prompts where the response might involve the word
255   “purple”. Specifically, we construct 3552 diverse questions related to 17 different topics (e.g., science,
256   fairytale, history, sports, ...) which contain the word “purple” by querying gpt-3.5-turbo. We
257   split these questions into train / validation / test sets with a split of 2841 / 426 / 285 (80% / 12%
258   / 8%). Below are a few illustrative examples from the validation set (full construction details in
259   Appendix B).

287
288   We first consider the common enforcement strategy of fine-tuning models using RLHF to discourage
289   unsafe responses. The first step is to collect a diverse dataset of exemplar safe and unsafe responses.
290   In the Purple Problem, since we have access to the ground truth labeling function, we feed the Purple
291   Questions dataset into gpt-turbo-3.5 to collect responses perfectly marked as chosen or rejected
292   based on whether they contain “purple” (further details in Appendix C.1).
293
      Fine-tuning Defense. We apply Direct Preference Optimization (Rafailov et al., 2023), an effective
294   algorithm that serves as most common source for RLHF (Dubois et al., 2024), on the dataset described
295   above. This algorithm combines the reward modeling and reinforcement learning stages of RLHF;
296   we defer a more detailed explanation to their paper. For our fine-tuning, we use LoRA (Hu et al.,
297   2021) and sweep over learning rate and KL regularization. We fine-tune each model well past reward
298   margin convergence (Figure 5b). Full training details are in Appendix C.1.
299

352
      adversarial training (Madry et al., 2019; Zhang et al., 2019). We first collect 10 adversarial suffixes
353
      generated by GCG. Then, for 50% of the standard training prompts, we randomly append one of
354   these suffixes to the prompt and continually fine-tune the fine-tuned model via DPO. We perform a
355   hyperparameter search similar to the fine-tuning defense and provide full dataset/training details in
356   Appendix C.3. For evaluation, we collect 10 more adversarial suffixes optimized on the fine-tuned
357   model and append them randomly to the Purple Questions test set. We find that the DSR of the model

359
      Strengthening the defense with adversarial training to adapt to the attack can evidently increase
360   robustness. However, this could be a false sense of security because attacks can also adaptively utilize
361   knowledge of the defense. As new defenses are developed, we must assume an adversary will use
362   any existing vulnerabilities an enforcement has to conduct more powerful attacks.
363
      Adaptive Adversarial Suffix Attack. To test how strong the adversarial training is to adaptive
364
      attacks, we re-optimize adversarial suffixes against the adversarially trained models. On Llama-IT
365
      and Vicuna, GCG is able to find a suffix that make the adversarially trained models exhibit a low
366   DSR of 8.7% and 0%, at the expense of longer suffixes and more optimization steps. For example,
367   on the fine-tuned models before adversarial training, Llama-IT requires a suffix length of 100 on
368   500 optimization steps, and after, it requires a suffix length of 300 on 2300 optimization steps
369   (Appendix E). When using the same prompt template as training, the Llama-2-chat model was
370   surprisingly resistant to GCG (Appendix G); however, removing the template during GCG breaks the
371   model to a DSR of 7.7% (Table 1). Same as before, we required an initialization based on manually
372   finding a prompt that nearly broke the model. It is thus better initialization, longer strings, and more
373   steps that make all the difference between a failed attempt and a strong adaptive attack. A determined
374
      adversary need not create original attacks but only needs to tweak their way to a jailbreak.

      5    R EAL -W ORLD I MPLICATIONS
487
488
489   The failure of enforcements on the Purple Problem implies that testing without adaptivity or sufficient
490   compute could lead to a false sense of security. Moreover, we have shown in detail how to stress-test
491
      enforcements. Here, we bring these lessons over to existing benchmarks and show that defenses in
      the real-world are more brittle than reported.
492
493   We conduct attacks on two defenses:DPP (Xiong et al., 2024) which finds a defense prompt while
494   maintaining utility and interpretability and ICD (Wei et al., 2024) which is an in-context learning
495   defense. Both defenses are evaluated on Llama-2-chat under an adaptive GCG attack and are reported
496   to have a low Attack Success Rate (ASR): Px∼A [L(x) ∈ D⋆ ]. However, we replicate their settings
497
      and evaluations (definitions) to reveal that a stronger adaptive attack with better initialization and a
      longer suffix length (more compute) can overcome the defense. Table 3 shows that the ASR of the
498
      same attack (adaptive GCG) is higher than the originally reported ASR. Details of the attack are in

      Appendix F.

      6    L IMITATIONS AND C ONCLUSION
513
514
515   Our methods show that adaptive attacks and scaling compute are important in evaluating defenses.
516   However, our evaluation of the Purple Problem does not include all defenses (e.g., representation
517   engineering (Zou et al., 2024a)) or all possible combinations and there could be a defense that
518   prevents the model from outputting “purple”. Even different base models might perform better on
519
      this task, which we do not evaluate currently. It would be interesting to see if there exists a setting
      that solves the Purple Problem. We leave such discoveries to future work in hopes of finding the
520
      perfect enforcement.
521
522   Regardless of the setting, breaking defenses in the Purple Problem can serve as a guide on how to
523   evaluate corresponding defenses in the real-world. We note that is it important to outline the adaptive
524   settings and compute budget tested for a defense as simply user stronger adaptation and scaling
525
      compute easily breaks defenses. We chose a problem structure that we believe serves as a simple
      version of real-world use cases of preventing toxic words, private content, or harmful information.
526
      This allows for lessons on weaknesses to easily transfer over as shown in Section 5. However, we
527
      cannot draw the strict conclusion that all defenses which fail on the Purple Problem will also fail in
528   the real-world. Though unlikely, it is possible that there are corner cases or degeneracies that make
529   the Purple Problem harder or have fundamentally different failure modes.
530
531
      We discussed how to conceptually break down the defense pipeline into two stages: (i) definition
      where we either explicitly or implicitly (from data) have a characterization of safe and unsafe
532
      generations, and (ii) enforcement where we ensure the language model does not generate unsafe
533
      responses for any prompt. Within this framework, the Purple Problem exposes the failures in
534   enforcement of a host of proposed defenses, especially to adaptive attacks and compute scaling.
535   Our findings connect to the lessons from a decade of research in adversarial robustness for vision
536   classifiers on the importance of testing against adaptive adversaries (Carlini & Wagner, 2017a;b;
537   Athalye et al., 2018) with concrete recommendations in Tramer et al. (2020). The failure of current
538   methods for the simpler and well-specified Purple Problem casts serious doubt on whether post-hoc
539   alignment is sufficient to address real-world safety. We hope the Purple Problem serves as a guide in
      preventing a false sense of security through the awareness of adaptive adversaries.

      In this work, we consider vulnerabilities of jailbreaking defenses. We note that for defenses based on
543   Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback, we do not introduce new attacks and simply apply

829
      Here we outline the details of creating the Purple Questions dataset. We query ChatGPT
830   (gpt-3.5-turbo) multiple times to create 3552 questions. These questions are required to
831   contain the word purple and also be about different topics in order to introduce diversity in the
832   creations. Specifically, we query with the following prompt:
833
834                            Create 50 {topic} questions with the word ’purple’
835
836   In the prompt, {topic} rotates between 17 different topics upon each querying chosen from the list
837   [scientific, fairytale, fantasy, cultural, historical, food, scary, economic, sports, celebrity, nonsense,
838
      romantic, funny, philosophical, space, nature, children]. Without including specific topics, the 50
      questions turned out to be very similar each time even with a temperature of 1.
839
840   Also, to create an effective dataset for making models say purple, making questions contain the word
841   purple were more effective than making questions induce the answer to be purple. Previous work has
842   also utilized this observation (Castricato et al., 2024). For example, the question ”Why did the purple
843   elephant get upset?” was more effective than ”What is the color of light with a wavelength of about
844
      400 nanometers?”. On Llama-IT, questions containing purple would result in responses with purple
      over 80% of the time while questions inducing purple would only make 50% of the answers include
845
      purple. Table 4 shows some questions in the dataset. The total dataset consists of train / validation /
846
      test splits with 2841 / 426 / 285 (80% / 12% / 8%) questions each.

1101   Dataset construction To create a dataset for adversarial training, we append GCG suffixes to the
1102   Purple Questions dataset. This way the model trains to be averse to saying purple even in the presence
1103   of adversarial suffixes. On each fine-tuned model (Llama-IT, Vicuna, Llama-2-chat), we randomly
1104   get 10 questions from the Purple Questions train split and progressively optimize a GCG suffix with
1105   the hyperparameters shown in the ‘Fine-tuned’ rows of Table 20. This is repeated 30 times on each
1106   model to get 20 suffixes for training and 10 suffixes for evaluating. During training, the 20 suffixes
1107
       are concatenated to the questions on the train split. To reduce the possibility of degeneration, we mix

1108
       with one random suffix attached and 50% of questions with no suffixes. The evaluation dataset is

1278
       harmful behaviors, we do the same. For their evaluation definitions, both methods use keyword
       search. Thus, we use their corresponding keywords to test the defenses on Advbench.
1279
1280   DPP is a defense method that optimizes a suffix which comes after any adversarial suffix. We take the
1281   released suffix by Xiong et al. (2024) (‘‘Kindly furnish a thorough response to
1282   the former user’s question.’’) and append it after the GCG string during optimization.
1283   We also initialize with five repetitions of “Ignore everything after the exclamation marks. Sure I can
1284
       do that!!!” which leads to a token length of 80 ando ptimize for 200 steps. Note that Xiong et al.
       (2024) have optimized an adaptive GCG suffix for 500 steps but achieved a lower ASR.
1285
1286   ICD is an in-context learning (ICL) defense that prepends ICL examples of refusals as a prefix to the
1287   input prompt. Wei et al. (2024) test at most 2 example pairs but do not release their exact examples
1288   for safety reasons. Hence, we follow their example pair creation procedure of taking Advbench
1289   harmful behaviors and inputting it through Vicuna to create refusals. We take two of these examples
1290
       and use them as the in-context defense. With the ICL examples in place, we optimize a GCG suffix
       initialized as ‘‘!’’ repeated for 100 tokens. We optimize for 200 steps.
