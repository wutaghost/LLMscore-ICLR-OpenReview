               In Natural Language Processing (NLP) and Computer Vision (CV) as well as myr-

               iad other domains, pre-trained models have achieved significant breakthroughs.
013            However, their advancements in the sphere of general Time Series Analysis (TSA)
014            has been comparatively limited. The principal challenge lies in the dearth of ex-
015            tensive training data that is endemic to the field of TSA. This scarcity hampers
016            the direct application of such pre-training models to time series data, resulting
017            in unsatisfactory performance. Despite numerous attempts to adapt NLP or CV
018            models, which have been pre-training on billions of tokens, to TSA to address
019            this challenge, these pre-training models are not directly suitable for time series
020            data. In this work, we introduce a new general Pre-Training Encoder specifically
021
               designed for Time Series analysis, called PTE4TS. It is designed to be universal
               for any type of time series data, and is easy to adapt to various downstream tasks
022
               such as classification, anomaly detection, and forecasting. First, we revisited the
023
               masking methods in time series and found that patch masking, which was widely
024            adopted previously, is not necessary. Therefore, we developed an improved mask-
025            ing model tailored to the characteristics of time series. Additionally, to address
026            the issue of the Low-Rank structure in conventional bidirectional attention mech-
027            anisms, which may diminish the model’s expressiveness, we have developed a
028            straightforward yet efficacious hybrid attention encoder. The combination of this
029            encoder with our masking methods can improve the representation ability of the
030            model. Finally, PTE4TS achieved state-of-the-art performance on several real-
031            world datasets, further validating the potential of Large Model for general time
032
               series analysis. We hope that PTE4TS will not only open new perspectives in the
               field of TSA, enhancing feature representation and inferencing capabilities across
033
               various domains, but also lay the foundation for a general artificial intelligence
034
               that is capable of understanding and processing common time series data.

038   1    I NTRODUCTION
039
040   The disciplines of Natural Language Processing (NLP) Radford et al. (2018); Devlin et al. (2018)
041   and Computer Vision (CV) He et al. (2022); Feichtenhofer et al. (2022) have witnessed a surge in
042   innovation thanks to the development of Large pre-training Models, propelling both fields into an era
043   of breakthroughs Zhao et al. (2023); Awais et al. (2023). However, the impact of these large models
044   within the specialized realm of Time Series Analysis (TSA) has been notably subdued. A significant
045
      factor contributing to this disparity is the deficit of large-scale, versatile training datasets tailored to
      TSA, which compromises the efficacy of these sophisticated models when applied to the distinctive
046
      nature of time series data Zhou et al. (2023). The scientific community has made concerted efforts to
047
      transpose the success of pre-training NLP and CV models, models that have been honed on extensive
048   corpora of billions of tokens, to the context of TSA Liu et al. (2023a); Yu et al. (2023); Gruver et al.
049   (2023). Nevertheless, the challenge remains formidable. The underlying architecture and training
050   of these models are not inherently compatible with the unique characteristics of time series data.
051   Therefore, while NLP and CV benefit from transformative advances owing to these large models, the
052   quest continues to either cultivate TSA-specific models or ingeniously modify existing pre-training
053   ones to resonate with the complex patterns and dynamics inherent in time series analysisLiu et al.
      (2024).

      Time series data is data collected or recorded at multiple points in time, and it is often utilized to
055   observe or predict phenomena that change over time. This type of data is widely applied across
056   various fields such as electricity Zhou et al. (2021), weather Wu et al. (2021) , transportation Yin &
057   Shang (2016), disease prevalence Liu et al. (2018), and more. Time series data can vary significantly
058   in dimensions, length, and sampling frequency depending on the field of application, presenting
059   considerable challenges for data integration and cross-disciplinary research. It can be difficult to
060   align these diverse datasets for training within a single model. Furthermore, since Time series data
061   typically requires long-term accumulation, some specialized areas may experience a shortage of
062   data, insufficient for the needs of training complex models Godahewa et al. (2021). The mask task
063
      technique can help mitigate issues related to data scarcity. The fundamental concept behind this
      technique is to randomly mask portions of the data within the training set, compelling the model to
064
      learn in the absence of some information, thereby enhancing the model’s adaptability to unseen data
065
      Devlin et al. (2018); He et al. (2022). This approach simulates a more diverse data environment by
066   presenting slightly different inputs in each iteration of training. Randomly masking the training data
067   creates a scenario where the input is likely to be different each time, and a high masking ratio can
068   heighten this probability.

104   tuning using Large Language Model (LLMs). Specifically, our contributions can be summarized into
105   three aspects:
106

      2.1   M ASKING M ETHODS
127
128   In our exploration of the TSA masking models, we’ve focused on the PatchTST method . In the mask
129   model of time series analysis, most methods opt to borrow from the BERT Devlin et al. (2018) in
130   NLP and the MAE He et al. (2022) in CV. These approaches are predominantly based on the patch-
131   based Transformer model. They do not significantly alter the Transformer; instead, they simply add
132   a patch method or, in some cases, replace the original decoder with a single layer of MLP Nie et al.
133   (2022); Tang & Zhang (2022). Patch can compress data and reduce the dimensionality of input data,
134
      to a certain extent lowering redundant features. Moreover, patch have a smoothing effect that can
      mitigate the impact of outliers and help filter fluctuations and random noise, retaining only more
135
      stable and representative information.
136
137   However, as illustrated in Figure 1a, we’ve identified certain limitations in their application. While
138   patch-level masking does create a more challenging task for the model by increasing the difficulty
139   of reconstructing the masked data, which can help the model learn local features of the data, this
140
      technique can also lead the model to overemphasize recurrent patterns ( Figure 1b) and neglecting
      continuous changes between patches, resulting in difficulty in learning the “bigger picture”, such
141
      as trends. In Figure 1b, we have selected three visualization cases to more clearly demonstrate
142
      how patch-level masking leads the model to overly focus on repeated patterns. The reconstruction
143   results in the figure clearly show that the model’s output consists almost entirely of repeated patterns,
144   indicating that the model has not learned detailed local information. On the other hand, tasks with
145   a higher point-level mask ratio are not only capable of constructing a similar level of difficulty but
146   also ensure that the model remains sensitive to minor variations. This enables the model to better
147   learn the original semantics without overlooking the point-level features. Furthermore, due to its
148   increased uncertainty, point-level masking provides a unique learning environment for the model,
149   emphasizing a deeper understanding of the data’s intrinsic structure, going beyond simple pattern
150   recognition.

211   3   PTE4TS
212
213   In multivariate time series analysis, it is common to be given historical observations XN =
214   {x1 , · · · , xL } ∈ RL from L time steps, composed of N univariate time series X. Clearly, multivari-
215   ate time series tasks can also be decomposed into univariate time series tasks. Under this condition,
      we are only concerned with the feature within each univariate series, while ignoring the interactions


                                                                                                        4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      across different variables. One of the main reasons for this is that in real-world scenarios, it is possi-
217   ble that the time points do not contain exactly the same timestamps Liu et al. (2023b), and enforcing
218   cross-variable alignment in the model can easily lead to the learning of chaotic systems.
219
220
      We propose the PTE4TS as shown in Figure 2, which utilizes a Transformer Vaswani et al. (2017)
      architecture that can be viewed as an Encoder-only structure, but with some simple modifications.
221
      It additionally includes an input process, embedding, and projection. Similar to the previous Mask
222
      Model, we do not use redundant Encoder-Decoder for representation learning. The task of gener-
223   ating complete series can essentially be handed over to a linear layer, which has also been demon-
224   strated in previous work, a simple linear layer is sufficient for many tasks Zeng et al. (2023); Chen
225   et al. (2023).
226
      Our PTE4TS focuses on representation learning and adaptive association. Each time series, driven
227
      by potentially complex processes, is first patched to describe the nature of local features. They are
228
      then subjected to mutual interactions via attention, and processed into patch representations by a
229   feedforward network. After multiple layers of the Encoder-Block, the original input is gradually
230   reconstructed.

      3.1   I NPUT P ROCESS AND E MBEDDING
233
234   To better focus on point-level attention and create a more challenging masking task, we need to
235   redesign previous masking methods to suit the characteristics of time series data. First, we apply
236   instance normalization to the input data to facilitate knowledge transfer. Next, we partition the time
237   series data into patches. In this stage, we select patches to be masked at the patch level but instead of
238   simply initializing these parts randomly, we assign the mean value of the patch or randomly shuffle
239   the order within the patch. We hope this type of masking will enable the model to learn point-level
240
      sequential information within the series, as well as the relationship between the original data and its
      mean. For the remaining data that still needs to be masked, we perform point-level masking, with
241
      the selected points being randomly initialized.
242
243   Time series data is typically recorded at successive time points and then input into the model. How-
244   ever, in PatchTST, adjacent and continuous time points are treated as a patch before being input
245   into the model. For point masking, each time point is considered an individual entity to be masked,
246
      preserving the trend of the time series. In contrast, patch masking treats adjacent and continuous
      time points as an entire patch to be masked. This means that primarily consecutive time points are
247
      masked, resulting in the remaining data lacking a continuous trend.
248
249   Our specific averaging operation targets the part that needs to be masked by setting this portion of
250   the data to the mean of the remaining visible data. Random shuffling is also targeted at the part that
251   needs to be masked, shuffling this portion within the patch. This approach helps the model learn the
252
      relationships between data points.
253   The input process is responsible for processing time series data into the form required for pre-
254   training. Subsequently, an embedding projects the data onto the dimensions needed by the pre-
255   training model. We have chosen the patch embedding method, noting that the patch size in patch
256   embedding and the patch size in the patch mask can differ. Patch embedding, by aggregating infor-
257   mation from adjacent time points, forms a patch-based token that can better extract local semantic
258
      information, significantly increasing the temporal scope of the input data and reducing informational
      redundancy in the Transformer model.

      3.3   M ASKED L AYER N ORM
294
295   Layer normalization (LN) Ba et al. (2016) is a normalization technique commonly used in deep
296   neural networks to mitigate the issue of internal covariate shift during the training process. In typical
297   LN, the module computes the mean and variance of all the features within each sample of the input
298   to that layer. However, for the training data of PTE4TS, in order to make the sequences uniform in
299   length, we apply padding to supplement the data from shorter sequences, which may result in the
300   effective data length being much shorter than the length of the input data. These padding values do
301   not represent effective information in reality, and including these padding parts when calculating the
302
      mean and variance could mistakenly incorporate irrelevant noise into the model’s learning process,
      leading to decreased performance and slower convergence. To address this issue, we have designed
303
      a variant called Masked LayerNorm. In this version, we use a mask to identify the padding parts
304
      within the input data. Masked LayerNorm first calculates the mean µ and variance σ 2 across all the

361
      univariate time series for T timesteps ahead, based on the known values X. For long-term forecasts,

362
      module with a simple linear layer, which has been proven to perform well in time series forecasting.

      Subsequently, we maintained all other components unchanged and proceeded with fine-tuning on

519   5      C ONCLUSION
520
521
      The PTE4TS model proposed in this paper marks an innovative breakthrough in the field of time
522
      series analysis. By adopting a novel pre-training strategy, PTE4TS surpasses traditional methods,
523   becoming the first general time series analysis model pre-trained from scratch, effectively solving
524   key issues in processing time series data. Our model not only brings theoretical innovation but also
525   confirms its superior performance through a series of experiments in practical applications, demon-
526   strating the effectiveness and vast application potential of our approach. The core innovation of
527   the PTE4TS model lies in combining point-level and patch-level mask pre-training methods, which
528   are more conducive to learning local features and overall patterns in time series data. Moreover, to
529   overcome the limitations of Low-Rank in bidirectional attention and the shortcomings of unidirec-
530   tional attention in mask models, we designed an efficient hybrid attention encoder that optimizes
531
      the model’s expressive capabilities when dealing with masking issues. In various mainstream time
      series analysis tasks, including time series classification, long-term forecasting, data imputation, and
532
      anomaly detection, the PTE4TS model exhibits outstanding performance, on par with or surpassing
533
      existing state-of-the-art technology. Extensive experimental results demonstrate the versatility and
534   efficiency of the PTE4TS model, further solidifying the leading position of our proposed method.
535   In summary, the PTE4TS model provides a new perspective and a powerful analytical tool for the
536   field of time series analysis. We expect it to promote in-depth research in related areas and lay a
537   solid foundation for building a more general and efficient AI framework for time series analysis. In
538   the future, we will continue to explore and optimize Large Models in the time series, extending their
539   application to a broader range of time series tasks, thereby advancing the development of the entire
      field.

      A.1   L ARGE M ODEL IN T IME S ERIES
759
760   Large Models have advantages in capturing long-term dependencies, handling high-dimensional
761   data, and combating noise in the field of time series Zhao et al. (2023); Awais et al. (2023). They can
762   be applied to a variety of specific scenarios including sleep analysis, weather prediction, and traffic
763   flow forecasting. With the continuous development and innovation of technology, the potential
764   applications of Large Models will be further unleashed, bringing more possibilities and value to
765
      time series analysis.
766   PromptCast Xue & Salim (2023) introduces a new paradigm for time series forecasting that lever-
767   ages language models for prediction. In this paradigm, numerical inputs and outputs are transformed
768   into prompts, and the forecasting task is constructed in a sentence-to-sentence manner. This allows
769   for the direct application of language models to make predictions. The Frozen Pretrained Trans-
770   former (FPT) Zhou et al. (2023) improves time series tasks by utilizing Transformer Blocks from
771
      a pretrained language model, specifically GPT, for time series analysis. Additionally, the authors
      have discovered that the self-attention modules exhibit similarity to Principal Component Analy-
772
      sis (PCA) Wold et al. (1987) from both theoretical and empirical perspectives. This observation
773
      is instrumental in explaining how Transformers overcome disparities between domains, and it is a
774   critical step toward understanding the universality of Transformers. TIME-LLM Jin et al. (2023a)
775   achieves time series forecasting by repurposing LLMs. This method leverages the powerful pattern
776   recognition and reasoning capabilities of LLMs on complex sequence data, aligning time series data
777   with natural language to achieve accurate time series forecasting. LLM4TS Chang et al. (2023)
778   enhances time series forecasting by leveraging pretrained LLMs. By combining methods of time
779   series patching and time encoding, it improves the capability of LLMs to process time series data.
780   TEST Sun et al. (2023) activates the capability of LLMs to process time series data by designing
781   a time series embedding method suitable for LLMs. It first tokenizes the time series, then encodes
782
      the series through instance-level, feature-level, and text-prototype aligned contrastive learning meth-
      ods. Next, it creates prompts to facilitate the LLM’s understanding of the embedded information,
783
      ultimately enabling the execution of time series tasks. TEMPO Cao et al. (2023) introduces a gen-
784
      erative pre-training Transformer model for representation learning and forecasting of time series.
785   TEMPO decomposes time series into three components: trend, seasonality, and residuals, using lo-
786   cally weighted scatterplot smoothing. Each component is then mapped to a hidden space to construct
787   the time series input embeddings for the generative pre-training Transformer. TEMPO uses a pool
788   of prompts to guide the model’s forecasting task, encoding temporal knowledge through the reuse
789   of a set of learnable continuous vector representations. Most of these methods focus primarily on
790   time series forecasting, with little or no attention to other applications within the time series domain.
791   Although FPT is proposed to improve time series analysis tasks, it explores the application of LLMs
792   in time series.
793
794   A.2   P RE -T RAINING M ODELS IN T IME S ERIES
795
      In addition to employing LLMs for time series analysis, the development of pre-training models and
796
      associated infrastructure based on time series data also holds significant potential and promise for
797
      application Jin et al. (2023b).
798
799   Voice2Series Yang et al. (2021) framework leverages the representational learning capabilities of
800   pre-training voice processing models, adeptly converting voice data into univariate time signals for
801
      classification. CLUDA Ozyurt et al. (2022) stands as an innovative unsupervised domain adaptation
      framework for time series, employing custom and nearest-neighbor contrastive learning. Through
802
      contrastive learning, CLUDA aims to create a representational space where samples with semantic
803
      similarities are drawn closer together, while those with lesser affinity are distanced from each other,
804   thereby facilitating the learning of contextual representations for multivariate time series across
805   domains. STEP Shao et al. (2022) comprises a pre-training model and a spatio-temporal graph neu-
806   ral network (STGNN), where the pre-training model is focused on effectively capturing temporal
807   patterns from long-term historical time series, generating segment-level representations to provide
808   contextual information for short-term time series inputs to the STGNN, and enhancing the modeling
809   of correlations among time series. MTSMAE Tang & Zhang (2022) is a self-supervised pretraining
      method developed from the foundational Masked Autoencoder (MAE) concept, specifically tai-


                                                         15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      lored for multivariate time-series forecasting. The concept behind MTSMAE is straightforward: it
811   segments the multivariate time series data into patches, then masks random patches from the in-
812   put, and subsequently attempts to reconstruct the missing patches.The Patch embedding proposed
813   by MTSMAE is instrumental in reducing memory usage, as it allows the model to handle longer
814   sequences more efficiently. SimMTM Dong et al. (2023) extends the application of pre-training
815   masked models to time series by uncovering the local structure of the manifolds. This model re-
816   constructs masked time points through weighted aggregation of multiple neighbors outside the man-
817   ifold, thereby improving the quality of sequence reconstruction. PatchTST Nie et al. (2022) is a
818   Transformer-based model specifically designed for long-term time series forecasting. The model
819
      incorporates a patch mechanism to extract local semantic information, enabling each univariate
      sequence within the channel-independent architecture to learn its own attention map for accurate
820
      predictions. TSMixer Ekambaram et al. (2023) is a lightweight MLP-Mixer model tailored for mul-
821
      tivariate time series forecasting. The model integrates two online reconciliation heads that fine-tune
822   and enhance predictions by exploiting the hierarchical patch aggregation characteristics within the
823   time series and the inter-channel correlations.

888   B.2     P RE - TRAINING
889
890   Our PTE4TS pre-training framework is illustrated as shown in Figure 2, where we conduct pre-
891   training across UTSD and performing data augmentation, specifically by downsampling the original
892   training data to simulate different sampling frequencies.
893
      We developed a 6-layer Encoder Transformer with attention layers(each providing a 256-
894   dimensional representation of the data it processes and 16 heads). The architecture included
895   position-wise feed-forward networks with an expansive 2048-dimensional inner state, allowing for
896   rich internal representations to be formed during the learning process. Each iteration of our training
897   provides the model with mini-batches composed of 1024 randomly selected small batch data, with
898   each sequence capable of accepting up to 1440 input tokens. Optimization was carried out using the
899   Adam scheme Kingma & Ba (2014), with peak learning rates capping at 0.0001. Diverging from
900   the sine-based position embedding proposed in the foundational literature, we implemented learned
901   positional embedding, solidifying their contributions to the model’s understanding of sequence by
902
      locking them during fine-tuning stages. By freezing these embedding in downstream tasks, we
      ensured their gradients remained untouched, preserving their intrinsic values for consistent perfor-
903
      mance.
904
905   In the pre-training task, we did not set a fixed random number seed and conducted experiments on
906   four NVIDIA V100 16GB GPUs. We selected an exceptionally high mask ratio of 75%. Due to the
907   absence of a fixed random seed, this can largely guarantee that the data inputted into the model in
908
      each round of the pre-training phase is different.

      In vanilla Attention, the positional relationship amongst elements is not directly accounted for, re-
958
      sulting in the architecture’s inability to effectively capture the critical information of element se-
959
      quence within the input series. This issue is somewhat alleviated through the use of positional em-
960   bdedding. However, such sequential information is vital for the analysis of time series data, which
961   often exhibits a pronounced sequential dependency; that is, the occurrence of one event might be
962   directly influenced by a preceding event. The employment of lower or upper triangular masks can
963   serve as a more effective approach to handling positional embdedding information. Essentially,
964   the triangular mask undermines the permutation invariance characteristic of the Transformer model,
965   introducing a definitive left-to-right sequence order. Specifically, in the presence of a triangular
966   mask, subsequent tokens in the sequence can only attend to preceding tokens, not to following ones,
967   thereby inherently incorporating positional information.
968   Furthermore, the utilization of a full-rank weight matrix implies that the network can capture a
969   broader array of input data features, making full use of model parameters to provide a richer rep-
970   resentational capacity. In the absence of additional restrictive conditions, a full-rank matrix may
971   exhibit a strong expressive ability, as it can represent a wider range of transformations. Within the
      context of attention models, expressive capability generally refers to the model’s capacity to capture


                                                                                                                       18
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       and utilize information, as well as its ability to model complex relationships. Although the property
973    of full-rank does not inherently guarantee high expressive capability, it bestows upon the model the
974    potential for such capacity.
