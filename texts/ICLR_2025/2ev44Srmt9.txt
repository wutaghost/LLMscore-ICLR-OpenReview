009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Shuffling-type gradient methods are favored in practice for their simplicity and
012            rapid empirical performance. Despite extensive development of convergence
013            guarantees under various assumptions in recent years, most require the Lipschitz
014            smoothness condition, which is often not met in common machine learning models.
015            We highlight this issue with specific counterexamples. To address this gap, we
016            revisit the convergence rates of shuffling-type gradient methods without assuming
017            Lipschitz smoothness. Using our stepsize strategy, the shuffling-type gradient
018
               algorithm not only converges under weaker assumptions but also match the current
               best-known convergence rates, thereby broadening its applicability. We prove
019
               the convergence rates for nonconvex, strongly convex, and non-strongly convex
020
               cases, each under both random reshuffling and arbitrary shuffling schemes, under a
021            general bounded variance condition. Numerical experiments further validate the
022            performance of our shuffling-type gradient algorithm, underscoring its practical
023            efficacy.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Gradient-based optimization has always been a critical area due to its extensive practical applications
029   in machine learning, including reinforcement learning (Sutton and Barto, 2018), hyperparameter
030   optimization (Feurer and Hutter, 2019), and large language models (Radford et al., 2018). While
031   numerous gradient-based algorithms have been developed for convex functions (Nemirovskij and
032   Yudin, 1983; Nesterov, 2013; d’Aspremont et al., 2021), research on nonconvex functions has become
033   particularly active in recent years, driven by advances in deep learning. Notably, with unbiased
034
      stochastic gradients and bounded variance, SGD achieves an optimal complexity of O(ϵ−4 ) (Ghadimi
      and Lan, 2013), which matches the lower bound established by Arjevani et al. (2023).
035
036   In practice, however, random shuffling-type methods have demonstrated superiority over SGD.
037   These methods are not only easier and faster to implement but also show faster convergence rates,
038   as evidenced by experiments cited in Bottou (2009; 2012). Theoretical studies on shuffling-type
039   methods have been conducted in various settings in recent years, presenting unique challenges due
040
      to the lack of independence between most neighboring steps. While much of this research assumes
      strong convexity (Gürbüzbalaban et al., 2021; HaoChen and Sra, 2019; Safran and Shamir, 2020),
041
      studies such as Nguyen et al. (2021); Koloskova et al. (2023); Mishchenko et al. (2020) have also
042
      explored applications in nonconvex scenarios.
043
044   Although theoretical analysis has been conducted in many settings of shuffling-type gradient algo-
045   rithms, most of these works require Lipschitz smoothness assumption, which requires restrictive
046
      quadratic lower and upper bounds and thus cannot cover many popular machine learning mod-
      els such as language model (Zhang et al., 2019), phase retrieval (Chen et al., 2023), distributionally
047
      robust optimization (Chen et al., 2023), etc. We will demonstrate counterexamples in more detail in
048
      Section 2. To fill this gap, in this paper, we aim at analyzing the convergence rate of shuffling-type
049   gradient algorithm under relaxed mild smoothness assumptions for both convex and nonconvex cases.
050
051   We consider the following finite sum minimization problem:

079   2.1   S HUFFLING -T YPE G RADIENT A LGORITHM
080
081   In practice, the random shuffling method has demonstrated its superiority over SGD, as shown in
082   Bottou (2009) and Bottou (2012). Specifically, Bottou (2009) shows that shuffling-type methods
083   achieve a convergence rate of approximately O(1/T 2 ), where T is the iteration count. Beyond
084
      shuffling-type stochastic gradient methods, variants such as SVRG have been applied in various
      scenarios, including decentralized optimization, as discussed in Shamir (2016) and De and Goldstein
085
      (2016).
086
087   The analysis of shuffling-type methods has a long history. For convex cases, Gürbüzbalaban et al.
088   (2021) demonstrated that when the objective function is a sum of quadratics or smooth functions with
089   a Lipschitz Hessian, and with a diminishing stepsize, the average of the last update in each epoch of
090
      RGA converges strictly faster than SGD with probability one. Additionally, they showed that when the
      number of epochs T is sufficiently large, the Reshuffling Gradient Algorithm (RGA) asymptotically
091
      converges at a rate of O(1/T 2 ). Similarly, Nguyen et al. (2021) established a convergence rate of
092
      O(1/T 2 ) for strongly convex and globally L-smooth functions. Furthermore, with uniform sampling
093   and a bounded variance assumption or convexity on each component function, they showed that the
094   convergence rate can be improved to O(1/nT 2 ).
095
096
      In contrast, there is not much research on nonconvex cases. For example, Nguyen et al. (2021)
097
      demonstrated   a convergence rate of O(T −2/3 ); Koloskova et al. (2023) proved a convergence rate of

      2.3   R ELAXATION OF L IPSCHITZ S MOOTHNESS
135
      Because of the existence of these counterexamples, people have recently been investigating about
136   smoothness assumptions that are more general than the traditional Lipschitz smoothness. In Zhang
137   et al. (2019), (L0 , L1 )-smoothness was proposed as the first relaxed smoothness notion motivated by
138   language modeling. It is defined as below:
139
      Definition 2.1. ((L0 , L1 )-smoothness) A real-valued differentiable function f is (L0 , L1 )-smooth if
140
      there exist constants L0 , L1 > 0 such that
141
142                                      ∥∇2 f (w)∥ ≤ L0 + L1 ∥∇f (w)∥.
143
144   Lipschitz smoothness can be viewed as a special case of (L0 , L1 ) smoothness when L1 = 0. Under
145   (L0 , L1 )-smoothness assumption, various convergence algorithms have been developed including
146   clipped or normalized GD/SGD (Zhang et al., 2019), momentum accelerated clipped GD/SGD
147   (Zhang et al., 2020), ADAM (Wang et al., 2022) and variance-reduced clipping (Reisizadeh et al.,
148   2023) with optimal sample complexity on stochastic non-convex optimization.
149   Other relaxed smoothness assumptions include asymmetric generalized smoothness motivated by
150   distributionally robust optimization (Jin et al., 2021) and its extension to α-symmetric generalized
151   smoothness (Chen et al., 2023) and ℓ-smoothness (Li et al., 2023a). In this paper, we use the definition
152   of ℓ-smoothness as below:
153   Definition 2.2. (ℓ-smoothness) A real-valued differentiable function f is ℓ-smooth if there exists
154   some non-decreasing continuous function ℓ : [0, +∞) → (0, +∞) such that for any w ∈ dom(f )
                                      C                                                     C
155   and constant C > 0, B(w, ℓ(∥∇f (w)∥+C) ) ⊆ dom(f ); and for any w1 , w2 ∈ B(w, ℓ(∥∇f (w)∥+C)  ),
156
157                         ∥∇f (w1 ) − ∇f (w2 )∥ ≤ ℓ(∥∇f (w)∥ + C) · ∥w1 − w2 ∥.
158
159   For nonconvex optimization problems, ℓ function is required to be sub-quadratic. (L0 , L1 )-
160   smoothness can be regarded as a special case of ℓ-smoothness. It is straightforward to verify that
161   both phase retrieval and DRO have ℓ-smooth loss functions. Notice that ℓ-smoothness degenerates to
      traditional Lipschitz smoothness if ℓ is a constant function.

      3     A LGORITHM
163
164
      As demonstrated in our counterexamples, the Lipschitz smoothness assumption does not always hold
165
      in problem (P). In such non-Lipschitz scenarios, gradients can change drastically, posing a significant
166   challenge for these algorithms. To address this issue, we propose a new stepsize strategy, detailed
167   in Algorithm 1 and section 4, to improve performance under these challenging conditions. This
168   strategy aims to choose the stepsize to accommodate the variance and instability in gradients, thereby
169   enhancing the robustness of the optimization process.
170
      In this algorithm, we start with an initial point w̃0 . During each iteration t ∈ [T ], either all the samples
171
      are shuffled, or we keep the order of the samples as in the last epoch. This reshuffling introduces
172   variance in the order of samples, which can help mitigate issues related to gradient instability. For
173                                                                                      (t)
      each step j ∈ [n], we use the gradient from a single sample with number πj to update the weights
174                       (t)
175   w. The notation πj is used to denote the j-th element of the permutation π (t) for j ∈ [n]. Each
176
      outer loop through the data is counted as an epoch, and our convergence analysis focuses on the
      performance after the completion of each full epoch.
177
178   There are multiple strategies to determine π (t) :
179
180          • If π (t) is a fixed permutation of [n], Algorithm 1 functions as an incremental gradient method.
181            This method maintains a consistent order of samples, which can simplify the analysis and
182            implementation.
183          • If π (t) is shuffled only once in the first iteration and then used in every subsequent iteration,
184            Algorithm 1 operates as a shuffle-once algorithm. This strategy introduces randomness at the
185            beginning but maintains a fixed order thereafter, providing a balance between randomness
186            and stability.
187          • If π (t) is regenerated in every single iteration, Algorithm 1 becomes a random reshuffling
188            algorithm. This approach maximizes the randomness in the sample order, potentially offering
189            the most robustness against the erratic behavior of non-Lipschitz gradients by constantly
190            changing the sample order.
191

365   4.2     P ROOF S KETCH AND T ECHNICAL N OVELTY
366
367   Broadly speaking, our approach involves two main goals: first, demonstrating that Lipschitz smooth-
368
      ness is maintained with high probability along the training trajectory {w̃t }, and second, showing
      that, conditioned on Lipschitz smoothness, the summation of gradient norms is bounded with high
369
      probability.
370
371   For the first goal, in Lemma A.4, we prove by induction that when starting an iteration with a bounded
372   gradient, the entire training trajectory during this iteration will have bounded gradients. Consequently,
373   we only need to verify the Lipschitz smoothness condition at the start of each iteration. However, at
374
      this point, the two goals become intertwined. We need Lipschitz smoothness to bound the gradient
      differences, but we also need the gradient norm bounds to establish Lipschitz smoothness. Our
375
      solution is to address both issues simultaneously.
376
377   Assuming that, before a stopping time τ , Lipschitz smoothness holds, we bound the gradient norm up
      to that time in Lemma A.5. However, this process is nontrivial. Since we are examining behavior


                                                                 7
378
      before a stopping time, every expectation is now conditioned on t < τ , rendering all previous
379   estimations for shuffling gradient algorithms inapplicable. This presents a contradiction: we want
380   to condition on t < τ when applying Lipschitz smoothness, but we do not want this condition
381   when estimating other quantities. In Lemma A.5, we find a method to separately handle these two
382   requirements, allowing us to achieve both goals simultaneously.
383
384   4.3    L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE WORKS
385
386   Although we have proved upper bounds for the complexity of shuffling gradient algorithms, there are
387   certain limitations in our work that we leave for future research:
388
             • First, as is common with many optimization algorithms, it is challenging to verify that the
389            bounds presented are indeed the lower bounds. Future work could explore improving these
390            results, for instance, by reducing the dependency on δ to a logarithmic factor, or by proving
391            that the current bounds are, in fact, tight lower bounds.
392          • Second, although we showed results for arbitrary shuffling schemes, there are better results
393            for single shuffling under Lipschitz smoothness, for example Ahn et al. (2020) proved
394            O( nT1 2 ) convergence rate for strongly convex objectives. It is interesting to see whether we
395            can achieve the same convergence rate with ℓ smoothness as well.
396          • Lastly, shuffling gradient methods have been integrated with variance reduction techniques
397            (Malinovsky et al., 2023). Exploring the performance of these algorithms under relaxed
398            smoothness assumptions is another promising direction for future work.

      6     C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this paper, we have advanced the understanding of shuffling-type gradient algorithms under non-
533   uniform smoothness assumptions and improved these algorithms with specific learning rates. We
534   provided counterexamples to illustrate the existence and divergence of non-Lipschitz functions for
535   (P). Our results show that these algorithms converge efficiently under a general bounded variance
536   assumption. Additionally, we established robust convergence rates for both strongly convex and
537   non-strongly convex cases, demonstrating the versatility and effectiveness of our approach. These
538   convergence results outperform SGD also when Lipschitz smoothness is violated, which is demon-
539   strated by numerical experiments. Future research can build on these findings to explore further
      generalizations and applications in various optimization contexts.
