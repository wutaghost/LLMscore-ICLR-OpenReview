011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Exemplar-free Class Incremental Learning requires the learning agent to incre-
014            mentally acquire new class information and maintain past knowledge without
015            having access to samples from previous tasks. Despite the significant performance
016            achieved by the subspace ensemble of a mixture of experts (MoE) with Gaussian
017            prototypical networks, a critical gap still exists. As the downstream tasks arrive,
018
               the subspace representation of old classes gets updated, resulting in a prototype
               drift and leading to forgetting. To address the forgetting problem, we propose
019
               ProCEED to dynamically realign previous classes’ representation in the latest
020
               subspace to adjust the drifted class prototypes and preserve their decision bound-
021            aries. Specifically, we compute the inter-subspace angular drifts of the prototype
022            of previous incremental stages with the current one, holding the local semantic
023            relationship between the incremental subspaces. The angular drift is then used to
024            adjust old tasks’ prototypes into the subspace of incremental tasks. Furthermore,
025            the model inherits combined knowledge from MoE, supporting plasticity without
026            extra computational burden. Consequently, ProCEED significantly balances the
027            stability-plasticity dilemma over incoming incremental tasks, allowing the model to
028            learn continually. The experimental evaluations on challenging benchmark datasets
029
               demonstrate dominant accuracy for ProCEED compared to the state-of-the-art
               class-incremental learning methods.

033   1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Intelligent learning machines should imitate human learning ability to accumulate knowledge while
036   adapting to dynamically changing environments without the availability of previous information.
037   However, when a traditional learning model is designed to learn a sequence of tasks from streaming
038   datasets, the previously learned parameters are overwritten by the current task, and the model suffers
039
      from Catastrophic Forgetting McCloskey & Cohen (1989); French (1999); Kirkpatrick et al. (2017);
      Li & Hoiem (2017); Lopez-Paz & Ranzato (2017); Schwarz et al. (2018); Zenke et al. (2017). To
040
      address this issue of forgetting, in recent years, the deep learning community has shifted its attention
041
      towards Class Incremental Learning (CIL), where the primary goal is to learn to classify all previously
042   seen classes from sequences of tasks Zhu et al. (2022). The forgetting problem can be naively resolved
043   by rehearsing representative samples from previous tasks. However, rehearsing samples increases the
044   computational cost linearly with tasks and raises data privacy questions due to the requirement of
045   continuous access to sensitive data, especially in the medical sector and national security Goswami
046   et al. (2024).
047
      Exemplar-free class-incremental Learning (EFCIL), a sub-field of CIL, is a challenging learning
048   paradigm that seeks to mitigate forgetting without storing samples from previous tasks. In the
049   literature, researchers tend to use 50% of the training samples upfront to learn a strong feature
050   extractor and freeze it after the first task (known as warm-start learning) Zhu et al. (2022); Hou et al.
051   (2019b); Petit et al. (2023); Goswami et al. (2024); Ma et al. (2023). Recently, prompt-based methods
052   have been used in CIL McDonnell et al. (2024); Zhou et al. (2024a), employing linear discriminant
053   analysis Panos et al. (2023) or a simple nearest class mean (NCM) classifier Janson et al. (2022).
      These methods use a transformer pre-trained model (PTM) on large-scale datasets like ImageNet-21k

      Ridnik et al. (2021) and focus on classifier-incremental learning. However, the model possesses
069   extreme rigidity and requires high computational cost due to using a task-specific expert.

071   A category of EFCIL focuses on learning task-specific subspace/prompts and prototypes using either
072
      a linear incremental classifier as a classification head or a prototypical network and shows promising
      results in mitigating forgetting Snell et al. (2017); Cha et al. (2021); Wang et al. (2022b). Expert
073
      Gate learns task-wise subspace growing with experts to alleviate forgetting, which is limited by
074
      computational cost Aljundi et al. (2017). Wang et al. reduced the memory constraint by training a
075   fixed number of experts to generate an ensemble of feature representations but with a high penalty,
076   limiting the plasticity Wang et al. (2022a). Rypeść. et al. uses a fixed number of subspaces to learn
077   the Gaussian prototype representation for all the classes and uses an optimal expert to be fine-tuned
078   for the remaining tasks based on statistical measures Rypeść et al. (2023). The major drawbacks with
079   the current prototypes-based approaches are 1) the growing computational cost with task-specific
080   experts and 2) overwriting the respective subspace of the fine-tuned expert by new class prototypes
081   once the new tasks arrive. Figure 1 shows how the task subspace changes incrementally when the
082   model is trained with current methods in the literature. Since the prototype represents the statistical
083
      representation of features, a drift in the features results in a severe drift in the prototype, making the
      model vulnerable to forgetting the previous knowledge.
084
085   In this paper, we address these drawbacks by developing a deep mixture of experts (MoE) and
086   leveraging their subspaces to enhance the plasticity of the learning model without task interference
087   during inference. Being inspired by Zhou et al. (2024b), the proposed approach realigns the local
088   semantic relationship between class features in old subspaces into the latest subspace to improve the
089
      model’s generalization ability. A single expert network from MoE is selected to be fine-tuned on the
      current task while simultaneously inheriting knowledge from the rest of the experts without these
090
      experts explicitly participating in the optimization. We employ a prototype-based ensemble of deep
091
      Gaussian classifiers during inference, helping the model estimate the non-linear decision boundary
092   and significantly increasing the model’s plasticity. The main contributions are summarized as:
093
094          • Introduce a novel Prototype Consolidation and Ensemble-based Exemplar-Free Deep In-
095            cremental Learning (ProCEED) that leverages the statistics of deep features subspace to
096            realign the representation of old tasks into the latest subspace. ProCEED prevents feature
097
               drifts and significantly abridges the stability gap without extra optimization overhead.
098          • Propose a knowledge distillation technique where a single expert (learning model) inherits
099            knowledge from previous tasks learned by MoE without optimizing the entire ensemble of
100            experts on the current task, leading to efficient improvement of the model’s plasticity.
101          • Leverage the subspace ensemble of MoE during inference, which demonstrates superior
102            task-agnostic accuracy on challenging benchmark datasets with an equal class distribution
103            across tasks.

105   2    R ELATED W ORKS
106
107   Class Incremental Learning (CIL). CIL approaches can be broadly divided into three categories:
      rehearsal, regularization, and expansion-based methods. Rehearsal-based methods use representative


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      samples from previous tasks during training on downstream tasks and apply knowledge distillation to
109   preserve previous knowledge while learning new tasks Hou et al. (2019a); Wu et al. (2019); Wang
110   et al. (2022d); Douillard et al. (2020); Kang et al. (2022). Some of the rehearsal methods mitigated
111   forgetting by controlling the feature adjustment Kirkpatrick et al. (2017); Smith et al. (2021); Toldo &
112   Ozay (2022). Despite the improvements in solving the forgetting issue, rehearsal methods are limited
113   due to the growing computational cost and raise concerns about data privacy.
114
      Exemplar-Free Class Incremental Learning. Despite being challenging, learning the sequential
115   tasks without storing samples from previous stages makes a learning agent more pragmatic Li &
116   Hoiem (2016); Yu et al. (2020); Smith et al. (2021); Zhu et al. (2021b); Zhou et al. (2021); Petit et al.
117   (2023). Zhu et al. (2021c); Smith et al. (2021) combined the regularization with prototype rehearsal
118   to enhance the model’s plasticity. Prototypes represent the feature space statistics (features mean and
119   standard deviation of respective classes) used to reminiscence the decision boundaries of the previous
120   stages without the explicit need for exemplars. Zhou et al. (2021; 2022b) use prototype augmentation
121   and self-supervision optimization to learn the transferable features for future tasks. Ye & Bors (2020);
122   Cong et al. (2020) trained a generator to rehearse previous knowledge as exemplars. However, due
123
      to the need for high-quality generated data, this approach also suffered from forgetting. Learning
      task-specific prompts with a large pre-trained network as a feature extractor has also received a lot of
124
      attention in the literature Wang et al. (2022c;b); McDonnell et al. (2024); Zhou et al. (2024a)
125
126   Dynamic and Ensemble-based Learning involves neural modifications, including expanding,
127   trimming, or freezing components to suit different incremental tasks Rusu et al. (2016); Yoon et al.
128   (2018); Hung et al. (2019); Li et al. (2019); Nie et al. (2023); Ramesh & Chaudhari (2022). For
129
      example, Aljundi et al. (2017) used a dedicated network for each task, while van de Ven et al. (2020)
      trained separate generative networks for incremental learning stages. The latest CL methods in
130
      the literature used a pre-trained ViT as a features extractor and a prototypical network Snell et al.
131
      (2017) as a classifier head either by adopting cosine similarity Zhou et al. (2024b) or using random
132   projections McDonnell et al. (2024). Regardless of the massive improvement in accuracy, these
133   approaches require high computational costs and task identity during the inference, which makes
134   these algorithms hardly practical Class-Incremental Learning.
135
      Gaussian Models in CL. Rehearsal-free CIL methods are vulnerable to recency bias towards the
136
      classes of recent tasks due to the cross-entropy loss during optimization Wu et al. (2019); Masana et al.
137
      (2022a). Rebuffi et al. (2017); Yu et al. (2020) mitigated this issue by employing the nearest class
138   mean (NCM) classifier with stored class centroids. Rao et al. (2019) modeled the incoming classes
139   with the Gaussian mixture model (GMM). Goswami et al. (2024) adopted the prototypical-based
140   Bayes classifier and inferred the classes using the Mahalanobis distance. These methods require 50%
141   of the samples upfront and require task identity during the inference, which seems unreliable in many
142   practical applications, e.g., medical imaging. To address such issues, Rypeść et al. (2023) incorporated
143   a mixture of experts with multivariate Gaussian distributions to learn the Gaussian prototype of input
144   samples. However, due to the rigid feature-distillation-based regularization, the model focused on
145   maintaining stability over plasticity and required a large number of experts. Furthermore, there is
146
      heavy semantic drift and recency bias in the subspace of fine-tuned experts, eventually leading to the
      problem of forgetting.

524   5    C ONCLUSIONS
525
      The practicality of real-world learning agents is fulfilled by the ability of their inherent models to learn
526   incrementally. This paper proposes a Prototype Consolidation and subspace Ensemble Exemplar-free
527   Deep class-incremental learning (ProCEED) with a CNN-based mixture of experts (MoE). The
528   proposed ProCEED prevents a drift in the representation of previous tasks by consolidating the
529   previous knowledge with the current task knowledge. The drift compensation is achieved by mapping
530   the local semantic relationship between features of previous tasks and the current one, eventually
531   significantly reducing the forgetting. The model also leverages adaptive knowledge distillation as a
532   regularizer that seeks to inherit the learned features representation from MoE without causing extra
533   computational overhead and much fewer parameters. ProCEED demonstrates superior improvement
534
      in accuracy compared to state-of-the-art CIL methods when empirically validated on challenging
      benchmark datasets in cold-start, task-agnostic, and exemplar-free settings.
535
536   Limitations: The limitations and future directions of the proposed method are summarized as follows:
537   (1) Estimating the feature covariance drift is still an open question and is a potential future extension,
538   which would better estimate the exact drift in the representations. (2) Approximating the prototype of
539   the old task associated with experts, specifically, completing the element above the diagonal elements
      in Equation 9, could significantly enhance the model plasticity.
