010                                               A BSTRACT
011
012            Existing multi-objective multi-armed bandit (MO-MAB) approaches mainly focus
013            on achieving Pareto optimality. However, a Pareto optimal arm that receives a
014
               high score from one user may lead to a low score from another, since in real-world
               scenarios, users often have diverse preferences across different objectives. Instead,
015
               these preferences should inform customized learning, a factor usually neglected
016
               in prior research. To address this need, we study a preference-aware MO-MAB
017            framework in the presence of explicit user preferences, where each user’s overall-
018            reward is modeled as the inner product of user preference and arm reward. This
019            new framework shifts the focus from merely achieving Pareto optimality to further
020            optimizing within the Pareto front under preference-centric customization. To the
021            best of our knowledge, this is the first theoretical exploration of customized MO-
022            MAB optimization based on explicit user preferences. This framework introduces
023            new and unique challenges for algorithm design for customized optimization. To
024            address these challenges, we incorporate preference estimation and preference-
025
               aware optimization as key mechanisms for preference adaptation, and develop new
               analytical techniques to rigorously account for the impact of preference estimation
026
               errors on overall performance. Under this framework, we consider three preference
027
               structures inspired by practical applications, with tailored algorithms that are
028            proven to achieve near-optimal regret, and show good numerical performance.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
      Multi-objective multi-armed bandit (MO-MAB) problem is an important extension of the multi-
033   armed bandits (MAB) (Drugan & Nowe, 2013). In MO-MAB problems each arm is associated
034   with a D-dimensional reward vector. In this environment, objectives could conflict, leading to arms
035   that are optimal in one dimension, but suboptimal in others. A natural solution is utilizing Pareto
036   ordering to compare arms based on their rewards (Drugan & Nowe, 2013). Specifically, for any arm
037   i ∈ [K], if its expected reward µi is non-dominated by that of any other arms, arm i is deemed to be
038   Pareto optimal. The set containing all Pareto optimal arms is denoted as Pareto front O∗ . Formally,
039   O∗ = {i | µj ̸≻ µi , ∀j ∈ [K] \ i}, where u ≻ v holds if and only if u(d) > v(d), ∀d ∈ [D]. The
040   performance is then evaluated by Pareto regret, which measures the cumulative minimum distance
041
      between the learner’s obtained rewards and rewards of arms within O∗ (Drugan & Nowe, 2013).
      However, simply obtaining a solution that has good Pareto regret does not take into account the fact
042
      that individual users would like to pick the choice that matches their specific needs. As the example
043
      depicted in Fig. 1, given multiple Pareto optimal restaurants, one user may give a higher preference
044   to quality, while another user may give a higher preference to affordibility. This means that user
045   preferences need to be accounted for in the MO-MAB problem set up in order to choose the right
046   solution on the Pareto front O∗ . This is the focus of this paper.
047
      Although numerous MO-MAB studies have been conducted, most of them achieve Pareto optimality
048   via an arm selection policy that is uniform across all users, which we refer to as a global policy.
049   Specifically, one representative line of research focuses on efficiently estimating the entire Pareto
050   front O∗ , and the action in each round is randomly chosen on the estimated Pareto front (Drugan &
051   Nowe, 2013; Turgay et al., 2018; Lu et al., 2019; Drugan, 2018; Balef & Maghsudi, 2023). Another
052   line of research transforms the D-dimensional reward into a scalar using a scalarization function,
053   which targets a specific Pareto optimal arm solution without the costly estimation of entire Pareto
      front Drugan & Nowe (2013); Busa-Fekete et al. (2017); Mehrotra et al. (2020); Xu & Klabjan (2023).

      However, simply achieving Pareto optimality using a global policy may not yield favorable outcomes,
076   since, as mentioned earlier, users often have diverse preferences across different objectives. Consider
077   the following scenario depicted in Fig. 1(a), where two users with distinct preferences interact with a
078   conversational recommender to find a nearby restaurant for dinner. The upper section lists restaurant
079   options, each associated with multi-dimensional rewards (e.g., price, taste, service), while the lower
080   section shows the dialogues and users’ reward ratings for the recommendations. Clearly, restaurants
081   A, B, and C are Pareto optimal, as none of their rewards are dominated by others. Previous research
082   using a global policy would either randomly recommend a restaurant from A, B, or C, or select one
083   based on a fixed global criterion to achieve Pareto optimality. However, while recommending a
084
      restaurant like B might lead to positive feedback from user-1, it is likely to result in a low reward
      rating from user-2, who prefers an economical meal, since restaurant B is expensive. In contrast,
085
      Fig. 1(b) illustrates that when the system accurately captures user preferences (e.g., user-1 prefers a
086
      tasty meal, while user-2 prefers a cheap meal), it can select options more likely to receive positive
087   reward ratings from both users. Therefore, we argue that optimizing MO-MAB should be customized
088   based on the user preferences rather than solely aiming for Pareto optimality with a global policy.
089
      While interactive user modeling and customized optimization cross multiple objectives presents
090
      promising experimental results in some areas including recommendation (Xie et al., 2021), ranking
091   (Wanigasekara et al., 2019), and more (Reymond et al., 2024), there are no theoretical studies on
092   MO-MAB customization under explicit user preferences. Particularly, two open problems remain: (1)
093   how to develop provably efficient algorithms for customized optimization under different preference
094   structure (e.g., unknonwn preference, non-stationary preference, corrupted preference)? (2) how does
095   the additional user preferences impact the overall performance?
096   To fill this gap, we introduce a formulation of MO-MAB problem, where each user is associated
097   with a D-dimensional preference vector, referred to as a preference for short, with each element
098   representing the user’s preference for the corresponding objective. Formally, in each round t, user
099   incurs a stochastic preference ct ∈ RD . The player selects an arm at and observes a stochastic reward
100   r at ,t ∈ RD . We define the scalar overall-reward as the inner product of arm reward r at ,t and user
101   preference ct . The learner’s goal is to maximize the overall-reward accrued over a given time horizon.
102   For performance evaluation, we define the regret metric as the cumulative expected gap related to the
103
      overall-reward. We term this problem as Preference-Aware MO-MAB (PAMO-MAB).
104   Our contributions are summarized as follows.
105   • New theoretical results. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first work that explicitly showcases
106     the fundamental impact of user preferences in the regret optimization of MO-MAB problems. Moti-
107     vated by real applications, we consider the PAMO-MAB problem under three practical preference
        structures: known (possibly dynamic) preferences, unknown (possibly dynamic) preferences with

      • New preference-aware algorithm design. We derive a lower bound to highlight the fundamental
112     reason why existing algorithms based on the global policies are no longer feasible for the PAMO-

114     structures. In contrast to other MO-MAB methods, our algorithms involve two novel designs:
115     (D1) Preference estimation mechanism and (D2) Preference-aware optimization, which allows us
116     to effectively capture the user preferences and optimize the overall outcome under the estimated
117     preferences for customization. Note that the designs of (D1) and (D2) are not trivial generalizations
118     of existing MO-MAB methods because the preference structure and the reward structure are
119     different. In addition to reward estimation, the preference estimation also introduces uncertainty,
120
        which further affects the arm selection and reward estimation, making it necessary to carefully
        design the estimation approach and new objective term for optimization.
121
122   • New analytical ideas. Our regret analysis involves novel ideas for solving the new difficulties
123
        due to the design of (D1) and (D2). (a) The regret is influenced by the joint estimation error of
        both preference and reward, which significantly increases the difficulty of regret analysis. To
124
        address this, we introduce a tunable parameter ϵ to decompose the suboptimal actions into two
125
        disjoint sets based on whether the corresponding preference estimation is sufficiently accurate or
126     not. This enables the regret that is caused by reward estimation error to be independently analyzed
127     on such two sets. (b) When the preference estimation is accurate under parameter ϵ, the error can
128     be analyzed based on the reward estimation. Moreover, when the preference estimation is not
129     sufficiently accurate, since the idea (a) does not explicitly decouple the effect of the joint error
130     in preference and reward estimations, the effect of the set of suboptimal actions is still unclear.
131     To address this, we transfer this set to a uniform imprecise estimation set, such that a tractable
132     formulation can be constructed based on the distance bound.

134   2    R ELATED W ORK
135
136   Multi-Objective Multi-Armed Bandits. MO-MAB extends scalar rewards in the standard MAB
137   problem to multi-dimensional vectors. The Pareto-UCB work (Drugan & Nowe, 2013) introduced
138
      the MO-MAB framework and Pareto regret as a metric, achieving O(log T ) Pareto regret using
      the UCB technique. Other techniques, including Knowledge Gradient (Yahyaa et al., 2014) and
139
      Thompson Sampling (Yahyaa & Manderick, 2015), have subsequently been adapted for MO-MAB.
140
      Additionally, researchers have extended the contextual setup to MO-MAB, where the action reward
141   for each objective is modeled as a function of the input context and action (Turgay et al., 2018; Lu
142   et al., 2019). These studies aim to efficiently approximate the entire Pareto front O∗ , and employ a
143   random arm selection policy on the estimated Pareto front to achieve Pareto optimality. However,
144   computing the full Pareto front is computationally expensive, leading to another line of work where
145   multi-dimensional rewards are scalarized. This approach converts the multi-dimensional reward into
146   a scalar value through a scalarization function, targeting a specific Pareto optimal solution without
147   approximating the entire Pareto front. The scalarization function can either be randomly initialized
148   (chosen) (Drugan & Nowe, 2013; Xu & Klabjan, 2023), or optimized based on a fixed metric, such
149
      as the Generalized Gini Index score (Busa-Fekete et al., 2017; Mehrotra et al., 2020). Nonetheless,
      existing studies primarily achieve Pareto optimality through a global policy for arm selection across
150
      all users. As discussed in Section 1, merely achieving Pareto optimality with a global policy may
151
      not yield favorable outcomes, as users have diverse preferences on different objectives. Therefore,
152   customized MO-MAB optimization under user preferences is essential, which is the goal of our work.
153
      Preference-based MO-MAB optimization. Recent studies have explored MO-MAB optimization
154
      using lexicographic order (Ehrgott, 2005) to reflect user preferences. In lexicographic order, objectives
155
      are prioritized hierarchically, where the first objective takes absolute precedence over the second, and
156   so on. Hüyük & Tekin (2021) first introduced lexicographic order to MO-MAB, and Cheng et al.
157   (2024) extended it to mixed Pareto-lexicographic environments. However, lexicographic order may
158   not adequately capture a user’s overall satisfaction in real-world applications, where preferences often
159   involve trade-offs rather than strict prioritization. For example, a user may prefer a $10 meal with
160   good taste over a $9.5 meal with poor taste, even though cost is a priority. Our work proposes a more
161   general framework that incorporates a weighted order based on the user’s explicit preference space.
      Notably, the lexicographic order becomes a special case of our proposed PAMO-MAB framework.

      4    A L OWER B OUND
201   In the following, we develop a lower bound (Proposition 1) on the defined regret for PAMO-MAB.
202   Such a lower bound will quantify how difficult it is to control regret without preference-adaptive
203   policies under PAMO-MAB. Firstly, we present a definition characterizing a class of MO-MAB
204   algorithms of which the sequential decision-making is independent of the preference information.
205   Definition 1 (Preference-Free Algorithm). Let ct = {c1 , c2 , ..., ct } ∈ RD×t and ct =
206   {c1 , c2 , ..., ct } ∈ RD×t be the preference sequence and the sequence of corresponding mean vectors
207   up to t episodes. Let πtA be the policy of algorithm A at time t for selecting arm at in a PAMO-MAB
208   problem. Then A is defined as a preference-free algorithm if its policy πtA is independent of ct and
209
      ct , i.e., PπtA (at = i|ct , ct ) = PπtA (at = i) for all arms i ∈ [K] and all episodes t ∈ (0, T ].
210   To our knowledge, most existing algorithms in theoretical MO-MAB studies (Drugan & Nowe,
211   2013; Busa-Fekete et al., 2017; Xu & Klabjan, 2023; Hüyük & Tekin, 2021; Cheng et al., 2024) fall
212   within the class of preference-free algorithms, which employ a global policy for arm selection, while
213   neglecting users’ preferences—an essential feature commonly observed in practical applications.
214   Proposition 1. Assume an MO-MAB environment contains multiple objective-conflicting arms, i.e.,
215   |O∗ | ≥ 2, where O∗ is the Pareto Optimal front. Then, for any preference-free algorithm, there exists
      a subset of preference such that the regret R(T ) = Ω(T ).

      Proposition 1 shows that for the PAMO-MAB problem with |O∗ | ≥ 2, sub-linear regret is no longer
217   achievable for preference-free algorithms. The reason is that for any arm i ∈ O∗ that is optimal in
218   one preference subset C + , there exists another preference subset C − where arm i becomes suboptimal.
219   However, preference-free algorithms cannot adapt their policies to different sets of preferences, and
220   thus fail to consistently perform optimally across the entire preference space C. Please see Appendix
221   B for the detailed proof of Proposition 1. We therefore ask the following question: Can we design
222   preference-adaptive algorithms that achieve sub-linear regret for PAMO-MAB? The answer is
223   yes. In the following, we conduct a comprehensive analysis of PAMO-MAB under three structures,
224   considering both prior-known and unknown preference environments. We demonstrate that through
225
      preference adaptation, the algorithms can achieve sub-linear regret.

655   A.1.1 C OMPARISON WITH BASELINES
656   In this section, we verify the capability of PRUCB and PRUCB-SPM to model user preference ct and
657   optimize the overall reward in a stationary preference environment. We compare these two algorithms
658   in terms of regret defined in Eq 2 with the following multi-objective bandits algorithms.
659   • S-UCB (Drugan & Nowe, 2013): the scalarized UCB algorithm, which scalarizes the multi-
660     dimensional reward by assigning weights to each objective and then employs the single objective
661     UCB algorithm Auer et al. (2002). Throughout the experiments, we assign each objective with
662     equal weight.
663   • S-MOSS: the scalarized UCB algorithm, which follows the similar way with S-UCB by scalarizing
664     the multi-dimensional reward into a single one, but uses MOSS (Audibert & Bubeck, 2009) policy
665     for arm selection.
666   • Pareto-UCB (Drugan & Nowe, 2013): the Pareto-based algorithm, which compares different arms
667     by the upper confidence bounds of their expected multi-dimensional reward by Pareto order and
668     pulls an arm uniformly from the approximate Pareto front.
669   • Pareto-TS (Yahyaa & Manderick, 2015): the Pareto-based algorithm, which makes use of the
670     Thompson sampling technique to estimate the expected reward for every arm and selects an arm
671     uniformly at random from the estimated Pareto front.
672
673   Experimental settings. For evaluation, we use a synthetic dataset. Specifically, we consider the
674   MO-MAB with K arms, each arm i ∈ [K] associated with a D-dimensional reward, where the reward
675
      of each objective d follows a Bernoulli distribution with a randomized mean µi (d) ∈ [0, 1]. For user
      preference, we consider two settings including predefined preference and randomized preference.
676
      For predefined preference-aware structure, we define the mean preference c as c(d) = 2.0 if d =
677
      j; 0.5 otherwise, where j ∈ [D] is randomly selected. The practical implication of this structure is
678   that it represents a common scenario in which the user exhibits a markedly higher preference for one
679   particular objective while showing little interest in others. For randomized preference, the values of
680   mean preference c are randomly defined within [0, 5]. For both setups, the instantaneous preference is
681   generated under Gaussian distributions with corresponding means and variance of 0.5. To guarantee
682   the non-negative preference, we clip the generated instantaneous preference within [0, 2c].
683
      Implementations. For the implementations of the algorithms, we reveal the true expected preference
684   for PRUCB before arm pulling in each episode, while for PRUCB-SPM, we use the estimated
685   preference instead. Following the previous studies (Auer et al., 2002; Audibert et al., 2007), we set
686   α = 1. The time horizon is set to T = 5000 rounds, and we repeat 10 trials for each set of evaluation
687   due to the randomness from both environment and algorithms.
688
      Results. We report the averaged regret performance of the algorithms under stationary preference
689
      distributions in Fig. 6. It is evident that our algorithms significantly outperform other competitors in
690   all experiments. This is expected since the competing algorithms are designed for Pareto-optimality
691   identification and do not utilize the preference structure of users considered in this paper, which our
692   algorithm explicitly exploits. Additionally, from the zoom-in window, we observe that PRUCB-SPM
693   exhibits only a very slight performance degradation compared to PRUCB, which knows the preference

840   multi-objective rewards (ratings) of arms (recommendation candidates) are obtained from a diverse

843   ability to model arm reward and user preference, thus enabling the customized optimization of users’
844   overall ratings. In Figure 10(b), we present an intuitive example of the protocol in the context of
845   real-world hotel recommendations. Specifically, the blocks represent different cities (e.g., NYC,

847   each time step, a customer travels to a city, stays in a hotel recommended by the system, and leaves
848
      feedback (both objective and overall ratings) after her or his stay. In the next episode, the customer
      travels to a different city and encounters a new set of hotel options. The hotel recommender system
849
      needs to learn the multi-objective rewards of all hotel candidates from various customers and model

      each customer’s preference based on their multi-objective and overall feedback. This enables the

1584   Remark D.1 (Tightness of attack tolerance). Theorem 4 shows a tight attack tolerance threshold
1585   for PRUCB-SPM against stochastic preference attack. Note that there exists a minimax lower bound
1586
       for the attack tolerance: if ηi − |z T ∆i | ≤ 0, then for any policy π, inf π supC×R R(T ) = Ω(T ),

1587
       a∗ , making arm i appear optimal over a∗ . Our algorithm presents a slightly relaxed threshold
1588
       Bi = ηi /(1 + 1/D) − ∥z∥2 ∥∆i ∥2 . Here, ηi /(1 + 1/D) acts as a lower confidence bound for the
1589   overall-reward gap ηi due to preference estimation error. By Cauchy–Schwarz inequality, ∥z∥2 ∥∆i ∥2
1590   is an upper bound for |z T ∆i |. This implies that the attack tolerance Bi of PRUCB-SPM matches the
1591   attack tolerance in minimax lower bound up to a constant factor of 1/(1 + 1/D).
