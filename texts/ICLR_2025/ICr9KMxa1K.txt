008                                                  A BSTRACT
009
010             This paper focuses on detecting complex-shaped action tubes in videos. Existing
011             methods assume that actor’s position changes slightly in short video clips. These
012             methods either oversimplify the shape of action tubes by representing them as
013
                cuboids or conjecture that action tubes can be summarized into a set of learnable
                positional patterns. However, these solutions may produce an action tube losing the
014
                corresponding actor when the actor trajectory becomes complex. This is because
015
                these methods rely solely on position information to determine action tubes, lacking
016             the ability to trace the same actor when their movement patterns are intricate. To
017             address this issue, we propose Actor-related Tubelet (ART), which incorporates
018             actor-specific information when generating action tubes. Regardless of the com-
019             plexity of an actor’s trajectory, ART ensures that an action tube consistently tracks
020             the same actor, relying on actor-specific cues rather than solely on positional infor-
021             mation. To evaluate the effectiveness of ART in handling complex-shaped action
022             tubes, we introduce a dedicated metric that quantifies tube shape complexity. We
023             conduct experiments on three commonly used tube detection datasets: MultiSports,
024
                UCF101-24 and JHMDB51-21. ART presents remarkable improvements on all the
                datasets.

067   tubes, whereas challenging cases emerge in more recent dataset (Li et al., 2021). These challenges

074   The above analysis shows that it is crucial for an action detector to cope with complex-shaped action
075   tubes. Unfortunately, this property is scarce from existing frameworks. Broadly, current frameworks
076
      can be categorized into cuboid-based (Singh et al., 2017; Hou et al., 2017; Kalogeiton et al., 2017; Li
      et al., 2020a) and query-based (Zhao et al., 2022; Gritsenko et al., 2024) approaches. Specifically,
077
      cuboid-based methods rely on pre-designed cuboid anchors or duplicate a bounding box along the
078
      time dimension to form a regular-shaped tube. The query-based methods TubeR (Zhao et al., 2022)
079   and STAR (Gritsenko et al., 2024) assumes any action tube can be characterized into a set of learnable
080   spatio-temporal positional queries. All of them are established on a position-related assumption that
081   an actor’s position changes very slightly in short video clips. Therefore, these methods are mainly
082   crafted to detect the aforementioned easy action tubes and tend to fail in detecting tubes with complex
083   shapes like in Fig 1. The root cause is that these methods determine action tubes solely based on
084   position information without considering actor information. An illustration is shown in Fig. 2. When
085   two actors are intertwined, the trajectories of the action tubes become complex. Existing methods
086   adhere to positions and still predict regular-shaped tubes even covering wrong actors (Fig. 2(a)). The
087
      orange tubelet begins with the man but ends with the woman, while the green tubelet starts with
      the woman and ends with the man. This position-related tubelet is unable to effectively distinguish
088
      between different actors. To address this limitation, we propose the Actor-related Tubelet (ART),
089
      which incorporates actor-specific information to generate accurate action tubes. As shown in Fig. 2(b),
090   ART consistently adheres to the same actor over time.
091
092   As discussed above, when the actor’s trajectory becomes complicated, the position-related assumption
093
      underlying existing methods are prone to failure. Intuitively, regardless of how intricate the actor’s
      trajectory becomes, once the tube of an actor is determined, it is much easier to determine the
094
      evolution of actions. A natural way to achieve this is by applying a person tracker to construct actor
095
      tubelets. However, this approach demands extra effort and data for training or fine-tuning the tracker
096   to adapt to the specific domain. Instead, we adopt a query-based detector and leverage its attention
097   mechanism to formulate tubelet queries that automatically track target actors, eliminating the need
098   for an additional linker or tracker. Specifically, we allow the model to briefly analyze the video
099   beforehand, providing it with knowledge of which actors are present. With this information, we
100   incorporate this prior knowledge into the tubelet queries, referred to as actor tubelet queries. These
101   queries enable the model to effectively determine the presence of the target actor in each frame of the
102   video clip, irrespective of changes in the actor’s position.
103   Our model named Actor-related Tubelet (ART), comprises an Actor Decoder responsible for localizing
104   actors within keyframes, and an Action Decoder that generates the final action tubelets. A Tubelet
105   Query Generator is the bridge to connect the two decoders by dynamically constructing actor tubelet
106   queries. Besides, to evaluate ART for complex action tubes, we propose a metric to measure tube
107   complexity and divide datasets into different subsets with different complexity scores. ART shows
      big gains for most complicated tubes. In summary, our contributions are:

117   2    R ELATED W ORK

119
      Traditional action detection. Spatio-temporal human action detection in video has garnered sustained
      attention, e.g (Cao et al., 2010; Tran & Yuan, 2012; Weinzaepfel et al., 2015; Peng & Schmid, 2016;
120
      Girdhar et al., 2018; Sun et al., 2018; Pan et al., 2021). With the advancements in deep neural networks,
121
      significant improvements have been made in the field of action detection in video. Some early
122   works (Peng & Schmid, 2016; Saha et al., 2016; Singh et al., 2017) applied 2D convolutional networks
123   to detect actions per-frame, drawing inspiration from object detection. These methods require linking
124   frame-wise predictions to form action tubes. To effectively leverage temporal information, action
125   detection at the tubelet level (Li et al., 2018; Song et al., 2019; Yang et al., 2019; Li et al., 2020a) has
126   gained significant popularity since it was introduced by Jain et al (Jain et al., 2014). Hou et al(Hou
127   et al., 2017) and Kalogeiton et al (Kalogeiton et al., 2017) employed faster-RCNN/SSD detector with
128   3D cuboid anchors to generate action tubelets. Subsequently, Yang et al (Yang et al., 2019) proposed
129   to progressively refine 3D cuboid anchors across time. Li et al (Li et al., 2020a) detected tubelet
130
      instances by relying on center position hypotheses instead of cuboid anchors. In order to detect
      large motions, Singh et al (Singh et al., 2022) employed an offline person tracker to generate actor
131
      tubes and pooled features based on these tubes. The key distinction between ART and the method
132
      is that ART is an end-to-end, transformer-based system that automatically generates actor tubelets,
133   eliminating the need for any external tracking systems.
134
135   Recently, 3D convolutional networks are widely used for video understanding due to its superior
136
      ability to capture temporal information. Gu et al (Gu et al., 2018) integrated a 3D convolutional
      network into a Faster R-CNN detector to enhance the understanding of action categories by learning
137
      spatio-temporal features. Derived from this regime, two-stage methods aimed at improving action
138
      recognition by using offline person detectors to localize actors were introduced. Feichtenhofer et
139   al (Feichtenhofer et al., 2019) designed a slowfast network for this purpose. Tang et al (Tang et al.,
140   2020) and Pan et al (Pan et al., 2021) explicitly models relations between actors and objects, which
141   favors for action understanding. Singh et al (Singh et al., 2022) and Faure et al (Faure et al., 2022)
142   employed ensemble models, incorporating either a tracker or pose estimation, respectively. Beyond
143   two-stage methods, Chen et al (Chen et al., 2021) proposed an end-to-end single model capable of
144   jointly training actor localization and action classification. These mentioned methods detected actions
145   per-frame, whereas ART is designed for detecting action tubelets at video level.
146   Transformer-based video understanding. Girdhar et al(Girdhar et al., 2019) proposed a video
147   action transformer network to improve action recognition by aggregating features from the spatio-
148   temporal context around actors. Fan et al (Fan et al., 2021) and Li et al (Li et al., 2022) proposed
149   to learn multiscale feature hierarchies with transformer models for video recognition. Additionally,
150   MeMViT (Wu et al., 2022) processed long videos in an online manner by maintaining prior memory
151   to capture long-term context. Recently, a hierarchical Vision Transformer without the bells-and-
152   whistles (Ryali et al., 2023) presents superior performance on multiple vision tasks, including video
153
      recognition. In the realm of action detection, Zhao et al (Zhao et al., 2022) proposed TubeR for
      detecting action tubelets in video clips with 3D convolutional backbone and a transformer encoder-
154
      decoder. Subsequently, STAR (Gritsenko et al., 2024) presented a purely-transformer based model.
155
      Although ART and TubeR/STAR are all query-based detector, ART exhibits fundamental differences.
156   Specifically, TubeR and STAR learned action tubelet queries from randomly initialized positions,
157   which poses limitations when dealing with complex action tubes, as discussed earlier. In contrast,
158   ART constructs tubelet queries that focus on target actors and remain unaffected by actor positioning,
159   allowing ART to effectively manage more intricate action tubes. Other query-based action detection
160   methods including STMixer (Wu et al., 2023) and EVAD(Chen et al., 2023) generated action boxes
161   only at the frame level. ART, however, is designed for the more challenging task of video-level tube
      detection.

176   3.1     S HAPE C OMPLEXITY OF T UBE
177
      We first introduce a metric designed to quantify the complexity of action tube shapes, enabling us to
178
      assess the effectiveness of our method in handling complex scenarios. Designing such a metric is
179   challenging, as it must account for various factors, including camera motion, actor shape deformation,
180   and fast motion. Additionally, interactions between tubes further complicate detection. To address
181   this, we utilize intra-tube IoU (Intersection over Union) to measure the complexity within a tube and
182   inter-tube IoU to assess interactions between tubes within a video.
183
      Given a video containing M tubes T1 , T2 , ..., TM , a tube Tj = {Bj1 , Bj2 , ..., Bjl }, j ∈ 1, 2, ..., M , Bji
184
      (i ∈ 1, 2, ..., l) is a bounding box at time i. The intra-tube IoU for a tube Tm is the average of IoUs
185   for box pairs in the tube:
186
187                                                                   \label {equ:intra} \vspace {-2mm} \text {Intra\_IoU\_m} = \sum _{i=1}^{l-1} \text {IoU}(B^i_m, B^{i+1}_m) / (l-1)                                  (1)
188
189   The lower the Intra IoU m, the higher the shape complexity of the tube. Thus, we define the inner
190   complexity of the tube Tm as Intra C m:
191
192                                       \label {equ:intra_C} \text {Intra\_C\_m} = 1 - \text {Intra\_IoU\_m} \vspace {-3mm}  (2)
193   To measure the complexity of Tm due to its interaction with other tubes in the video, we first calculate
194   the inter-tube IoU for Tm and each other tube Tj in the video:
195                            \label {equ:inter} \text {Inter\_IoU\_mj} = \text {TIoU}(T_m, T_j), {j\in {1,2,..., M} \& j\neq m} \vspace {-2mm}  (3)
196
      TIoU means the tube IoU (Zhao et al., 2022). Tubes that overlap with Tm increase its complexity.
197
      To quantify the effect of these overlapping tubes on Tm , we normalize all TIoUs between the tubes
198
      overlapping with Tm and define the normalized value as an interaction coefficient (Icoe) between Tm
199   and an overlapping tube Tj :
200
201                                           \label {equ:Ice} \text {Icoe\_mj} = \text {Inter\_IoU\_mj} / \sum _{j} \text {Inter\_IoU\_mj}, {j\in {1,2,..., M} \& j\neq m} \vspace {-3mm}                               (4)
202
203   The interaction complexity of Tm is defined as the weighted sum of inner complexity of the tubes
204   overlapping with Tm :
205
                                                                             \label {equ:inter_C} \text {Inter\_C\_m} = \sum _{j\neq m} (\text {Icoe\_mj} * \text {Intra\_C\_j}) \vspace {-3mm}                          (5)
206
207
      The final complexity of tube Tm is the sum of inner complexity and interaction complexity:
208
209                                                                                     \label {equ:Cm} \text {C\_m} = \text {Intra\_C\_m} + \text {Inter\_C\_m}                                                         (6)
210   With the defined tube complexity score, we are able to analyze action tube complexity distribution on
211   the datasets to support our motivation. Fig. 3 (a) shows the cumulative density function of intra-tube
212   IoU is plotted for the training sets of MultiSports, UCF and JHMDB. Notably, 30% of the tubes in
213   MultiSports exhibit an IoU of 0.0, indicating significant variation in the bounding boxes within these
214   tubes. JHMDB, which consists of simple actions characterized by short tubes and samll motion, has
215   only 22% of tubes with an IoU lower than 0.5, compared to 50% in UCF and 73% in MultiSports.
      Fig. 3 (b) shows the cumulative density function of IoU for pairs of tubes (inter-tube) within a video.

247   3.3.1    P RELIMINARY
248
249   A query-based action detection framework learns a set of spatio-temporal positional queries
250   Q={Q1 , ..., Qn , ..., QN } ∈ RN ×T ×C to model action tubelet patterns. Here N is the number
251   of queries, T the temporal duration of the tubelet and C the feature dimension. Each query
252
      Qi = {Qi1 , . . . , Qij , . . . , QiT } contains T spatial positional queries (i.e. Qij ∈ RC ) correspond-
      ing to T frames, respectively. These queries are intended to represent an action tubelet across T
253
      frames. Given that the average number of spatial positional queries are K per frame, the learning
254
      complexity of the tubelet query grows exponentially to the power of K w.r.t the temporal duration
255   of video clip T . To alleviate such an issue, TubeR (Zhao et al., 2022) assumes actor’s position
256   changes very slowly over time, thus significantly reducing the complexity (from O(K T ) to O(KT )).
257   However, this simplification constrains TubeR’s ability to effectively learn complex action tubelets,
258   as discussed in the Introduction.
259
      Our work targets complex action tubes. We build actor tubelet query, each of which is supposed to
260
      trace a specific actor along time in the input video clip. It applies an Actor Decoder to detect actors
261   on the keyframe (i.e the center frame of the input video clip). Subsequently, we generate actor tubelet
262   queries that leverage the distinctive features of each actor.

                        3.3.4 D ISCUSSION
                 318    There are three key distinctions between ART and TubeR. 1) Impact: ART learns actor-related
                 319    tubelets for complex-shaped action tubes; TubeR, constrained by the position assumption, learns
                 320    position-related tubelets and struggles to detect complex-shaped tubes. 2) Computation: ART learns
                 321    a set of spatial embeddings Qs ∈ RN ×C and temporal embeddings Qt ∈ RT ×C . Again, consider
                 322    that the average number of actor queries are K per frame, the learning complexity is only O(K + T ),
                 323    which   offers a significant reduction compared O(KT ) in TubeR. 3) Structure: ART is more concise
                           whereas TubeR requires short-term context module and the memory bank (Wu et al., 2019).

      3.4                      ACTION D ECODER
325
326   Similar to Actor Decoder, Action Decoder consists of m transformer-decoder blocks, each of which
327   contains a self-attention layer (SA) and a cross-attention layer (CA). It decodes action tubelet features
328   Faction ∈ RN ×T ×C through the encoded video features F and actor tubelet queries Qa . To capture
329   temporal information, the temporal compensation features Tc ∈ RN ×T ×C is added to Qa in each
330   decoder layer. At last, two FC layers are respectively utilized for regressing bounding boxes on
331   each frame in the tubelet and recognizing actions for each tubelet. We get action tubelet boxes
332
      Bt ∈ RN ×T ×4 and action probabilities St ∈ RN ×L . L is the number of action categories.

347   Datasets. Our experiments are conducted on three video datasets with tube-level annotations.
348   JHMDB51-21 (Jhuang et al., 2013) consists of 21 action categories presented in 928 trimmed
349   videos. We report the average results over all three splits. Although action tubes on JHMDB exhibit
350   less variety, it remains a widely-used benchmark for tube-level action detection. UCF101-24 (Soomro
351   et al., 2012) features 24 sport-related classes distributed across 3,207 untrimmed videos. It contains
352   20% high complex action tubes as analyzed in Fig 3. We use the revised annotations following
353   (Singh et al., 2017) and report performance on “split-1”. MultiSports (Li et al., 2021) is a large-scale
354   multi-person dataset for sports actions. It is built on 4 sports classes, collects 3200 video clips, and
355
      annotates 37701 action tube instances with 902k bounding boxes. It has well-defined tube-level anno-
      tations. And most of the action tubes are complicated. Thus, it is a suitable datasets for validating our
356
      method. MultiSports contains 66 fine-grained classes. Following the official evaluation protocol (Li
357
      et al., 2021), we only do evaluation on 60 classes.
358
359   Evaluation criteria. ART is specifically designed for action tube detection. Thus, we primarily
360   report video-mAPs. Frame-mAPs are not reported, as they do not directly reflect the effectiveness of
361
      ART. For those interested in frame-mAPs on AVA, please refer to the supplementary material.
362   Implementation details. We apply a Transformer backbone Hiera (Ryali et al., 2023) with ViT (Doso-
363   vitskiy et al., 2021) in Encoder for a pure transformer system. For fair comparisons to existing methods,
364   we also conduct experiments with ConvNets backbones, including I3D (Carreira & Zisserman, 2017)
365   with VGG (Simonyan & Zisserman, 2015), CSN (Tran et al., 2019) with ResNets (He et al., 2016),
366   which further verify the universality of our design. All backbones are pre-trained on Kinetics-400 (Kay
367
      et al., 2017). We set the number of query N = 10 on UCF and JHMDB and N = 20 on MultiSports.
      Video clip length is compatible with the used video backbone if not specified. During training, we
368
      use the bipartite matching (Georgiev & Lió, 2020) based on the Hungarian algorithm (Kuhn, 1955)
369
      between predictions and the ground truth. For data augmentation, each video is resized to 256 pixels
370   and randomly cropped 224 on the short edge, if not specified. We use the AdamW (Loshchilov &
371   Hutter, 2017) optimizer with an initial learning rate 5e − 6 for the backbone and 1e − 4 for others.
372   We decrease the learning rate 10 when the validation loss saturates. The weight decay is set to 1e − 8.

      5     C ONCLUSION
534
535   We propose Actor-related Tubelets (ART) as an end-to-end solution for complex action tube detection.
536   Unlike existing methods that rely on positional assumptions, ART integrates actor-specific information
537   to generate action tubes, enabling the consistent tracking of the same actor over time. ART not
538   only effectively handles large shifts in actor position but also reduces the complexity of learning.
539   Furthermore, ART demonstrates significant improvements in action detection across multiple datasets,
      particularly on the complex dataset MultiSports.
