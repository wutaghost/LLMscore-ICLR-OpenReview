009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            We show that deep neural networks achieve dimension-independent rates of con-
012            vergence for learning structured densities such as those arising in image, audio,
013            video, and text applications. More precisely, we show that neural networks with a
014            simple L2 -minimizing loss achieve a rate of n‚ąí1/(4+r) in nonparametric density
015            estimation when the underlying density is Markov to a graph whose maximum
016            clique size is at most r, and we show that in the aforementioned applications, this
017            size is typically constant, i.e., r = O(1). We then show that the optimal rate in L1
018            is n‚ąí1/(2+r) which, compared to the standard nonparametric rate of n‚ąí1/(2+d) ,
019            shows that the effective dimension of such problems is the size of the largest clique
020            in the Markov random field. These rates are independent of the data‚Äôs ambient
021
               dimension, making them applicable to realistic models of image, sound, video,
               and text data. Our results provide a novel justification for deep learning‚Äôs ability
022
               to circumvent the curse of dimensionality, demonstrating dimension-independent
023
               convergence rates in these contexts.

026   1    I NTRODUCTION
027
028   Deep learning has emerged as a remarkably effective technique for numerous statistical problems
029   that were historically extremely challenging, especially in high-dimensional settings. In the realm of
030   deep generative models, which can be framed as density estimation, deep methods have showcased
031   the ability to learn density functions with thousands or millions of dimensions using merely a few
032   million data points (Oussidi & Elhassouny, 2018; Ho et al., 2020; Cao et al., 2024). This stands in
033   stark contrast to standard density estimation theory, which would demand astronomical sample sizes
034   due to the curse of dimensionality. The manifold hypothesis is perhaps the most widely accepted
035   explanation for deep learning‚Äôs ability to circumvent this curse (Bengio et al., 2013; Brahma et al.,
036
      2016). This hypothesis posits that, despite a distribution‚Äôs ambient space being high-dimensional,
      the mass of the density is heavily concentrated around a lower-dimensional subset of that space,
037
      such as an embedded manifold. As we will argue later, for complex data types of significant inter-
038
      est‚ÄĒimages, video, sound, and text‚ÄĒthis assumption is intimately linked to spatio-temporal local-
039   ity. For instance, covariates1 that are nearby spatio-temporally, e.g., neighboring pixels, tend to be
040   strongly dependent, suggesting they lie near a lower-dimensional subspace.
041
042
      This paper investigates the benefits of leveraging the converse structure: The independence of
      spatio-temporally distant covariates. Covariates that are spatio-temporally distant often exhibit near-
043
      independence, particularly when conditioned on intervening covariates. Consider a sound recording:
044
      two one-second segments separated by a minute might share common elements, such as the same
045   speaker. However, given the intervening minute of audio, these segments become effectively inde-
046   pendent. The minute-long interval contains sufficient information to render the separated segments
047   mutually uninformative. This principle of conditional independence extends to various data types,
048   including images, where pixels far apart tend to be independent when conditioned on the surrounding
049   region. This sort of dependence structure is naturally described by a Markov random field (MRF).
050
      In this work, we show that, for a very general class of densities that are Markov to an undirected
051   graph (a.k.a. MRF), density estimation can be achieved with a neural network and a simple L2

      2.2   M ANIFOLD H YPOTHESIS
112
113   The manifold hypothesis posits that many high-dimensional real-world distributions concentrate
114   around lower-dimensional spaces, such as submanifolds of the ambient space. This assumption un-
115   derpins, either explicitly or implicitly, numerous machine learning methods. For instance, principal
116   component analysis assumes that a distribution is concentrated around an affine subspace, while
117   sparsity assumptions can be formulated as a union of manifolds, where the subsets of the union are
118   axis-aligned subspaces.
119   The success of deep learning methods in handling high-dimensional data, such as images, videos,
120   and audio, is frequently attributed to the manifold hypothesis. Experimental validation of the mani-
121   fold hypothesis has been conducted for image datasets. In Pope et al. (2021), the authors determined
122   that the intrinsic dimension of the ImageNet dataset lies between 25 and 40 dimensions, significantly
123   lower than its ambient dimension. This hypothesis is closely linked to correlation and dependency
124   between covariates. In images, for example, adjacent pixels xi and xj typically have similar values,
125   causing the dataset to concentrate towards the linear subspace xi = xj , which is a submanifold.
126   Figure 3(a) illustrates this concept, showing the values of pixels (8, 8) and (8, 9) for 100 randomly
127
      selected images from the grayscaled CIFAR-10 dataset, where a strong concentration along the di-
      agonal is evident.
128
129   Further supporting this hypothesis, Carlsson et al. (2008) discovered that the set of 3 √ó 3 pixel
130   patches from natural images concentrates around a 2-dimensional manifold. Theoretically, distri-
131   butions concentrating around lower-dimensional subsets of the ambient space have been shown to
132   yield improved estimation properties. For instance, Weed & Bach (2019) demonstrated that while a
133   dataset typically converges at rate n‚ąí1/d to the true distribution in Wasserstein distance, when the
                                                                                                   ‚Ä≤
134   dataset exhibits a lower d‚Ä≤ -dimensional structure, it converges at the faster rate of n‚ąí1/d . Similar
135
      results illustrating the manifold hypothesis and its benefits can be found in Pelletier (2005); Ozakin
      & Gray (2009); Jiang (2017); Schmidt-Hieber (2019); Nakada & Imaizumi (2020); Berenfeld et al.
136
      (2022); Jiao et al. (2023); Tang & Yang (2024).
137
138   While the manifold hypothesis explains local dependencies, it‚Äôs worth considering scenarios that
139   deviate from this model. For example, the manifold hypothesis cannot be satisfied when covariates
140   are independent. For example, if two covariates x ‚ąľ px and y ‚ąľ py are independent, their joint den-
141
      sity px,y (x, y) = px (x)py (y) fills a rectangle in their product space. As one may expect, pixels that
      are distant from one another tend to become more independent. This phenomenon is illustrated in
142
      Figure 3(d), which plots the grayscale values of pixels (8, 8) and (14, 28), showing a more dispersed
143
      pattern. This observation naturally leads to modeling the space of images as a Markov random field,
144   where local dependencies are captured while allowing for independence between distant pixels.

152

      While standard path and grid MRF models may suffice for correcting extremely local or high-
      frequency noise in sequential or spatial data, they fall far short of capturing the true distribution
232
      of complex data types. Consider, for example, audio data consisting of 21-second clips where the
233
      middle second is missing and needs to be predicted. According to the path MRF model, this missing
234   second would depend solely on the audio samples directly preceding and following it. Consequently,
235   under a Markov chain (i.e. path MRF) model, the remaining 20 seconds of audio (less two samples)
236   would be deemed completely uninformative for predicting the middle second, given these two adja-
237   cent samples.
238
      This simplistic model fails to capture the richer, longer-range dependencies present in real-world
239
      audio data. In practice, the content of the missing second is likely influenced by a broader context
240   than just its immediate neighbors. For instance, the rhythm or theme established in the preceding
241   few seconds, or the anticipation of what follows immediately after, could be crucial for predict-
242   ing the missing segment. This moderately broader context is entirely discarded by the basic path
243   MRF. Similarly, for image data, the standard grid MRF model suggests that a region of an image
244   depends only on its immediate bordering pixels. However, realistic images often exhibit patterns
245   and structures that span multiple pixels in various directions. For example, the edge of an object
246   or a gradient in lighting might extend across several pixels, creating dependencies that the basic
247   grid model fails to capture. Figure 4 illustrates this concept concretely, demonstrating the effects
248
      of different MRF models on image inpainting tasks and highlighting the implications of varying
      levels of contextual information. These limitations motivate the need for more sophisticated MRF
249
      models where segments or regions are more extensively connected, allowing for the incorporation
250
      of relevant contextual information without necessarily spanning the entire dataset.
251
252   To model sequential and spatial data more realistically, we propose using the ‚Äúpower graph‚ÄĚ of the
253   path and grid models. For a graph G, the power graph G t with t ‚ąą N is defined as the graph where an
254
      edge exists between every pair of vertices within t steps of each other in G, with G 1 = G. Figures 5
      and 6 illustrate this concept using path graphs and grid graphs, respectively. This construction causes
255
      contiguous sections of sequences and patches of grids to become fully connected, as demonstrated
256
      in Figure 5.
257
258   Applying this power graph concept to a grid graph assumes that local patches of images are highly
259   dependent, making no assumptions about conditional independence within a patch. It also implies
260
      that distant regions of an image become independent as the distance between them increases, and
      that these regions are independent when conditioned on a sufficiently wide separating region of
261
      pixels.

518
      Neural density estimation has been the subject of intense study over the past few decades, dating at
      least back to Magdon-Ismail & Atiya (1998). There has recently been interest in designing structured
519
      neural density estimators that exploit graphical structure (Germain et al., 2015; Johnson et al., 2016;
520
      Khemakhem et al., 2021; Wehenkel & Louppe, 2021; Chen et al., 2024). In this work, we have
521   presented a novel perspective on the success of neural networks in density estimation problems. Our
522   approach, based on Markov Random Field (MRF) structures, offers an alternative explanation to the
523   widely accepted manifold hypothesis for why deep learning methods can circumvent the curse of
524   dimensionality, and aligns with these recent developments on structured density estimation.
525
      We have demonstrated that leveraging MRF assumptions can achieve dimension-independent con-
526
      vergence rates for density estimation, with the effective dimensionality determined by the largest
527   clique in the MRF graph rather than the ambient data dimension. This potentially explains the
528   efficacy of neural networks in domains like image and sequential data processing.
529
530
      Our MRF-based approach complements, rather than replaces, the manifold hypothesis. We envision
      a combination of local manifold-like structures and global MRF-like independence properties at
531
      play in real-world scenarios, with the manifold hypothesis explaining local features and our MRF
532
      approach capturing broader independence structures.
533
534   This work opens avenues for future research, including investigating the interplay between local
535   manifold structures and global MRF properties, and developing practical algorithms exploiting these
536
      structures within deep learning frameworks.
537   In conclusion, our work provides a novel theoretical framework for understanding neural networks
538   in high-dimensional spaces, offering an alternative to the manifold hypothesis and potentially stim-
539   ulating new directions in both the theory and practice of machine learning for high-dimensional
      tasks.
