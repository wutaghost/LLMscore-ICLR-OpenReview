                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
               The Tsetlin Automaton (TA) is a foundational single-state reinforcement learning
010
               model, but its fixed depth parameter (N) poses a significant limitation for navigat-
011            ing the exploration and exploitation dilemma. Despite remarkable advancements,
012            existing TA models lack adaptability in real-world scenarios where dynamic depth
013            adjustments are essential. In this paper, we introduce the Adaptive Depth Tsetlin
014            Automaton (ADTA), a novel solution addressing this challenge. ADTA integrates
015            TA with a reinforcement agent capable of dynamically modifying N. We ana-
016            lyze ADTA using Lyapunov stability theorem and Markov chain analysis within a
017            dual-environment framework: the outer environment, where TA operates to max-
018            imize rewards, and the inner environment, where a reinforcement learning agent
019
               evaluates TA’s performance based on N. Through actions like ’Grow,’ ’Shrink,’
               and ’Stop,’ the inner agent configures N dynamically. Unlike conventional TA
020
               configurations with fixed N, our approach demonstrates improved reward maxi-
021
               mization and regret minimization. Furthermore, we present numerical simulations
022            that corroborate our theoretical results.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   The Tsetlin Automaton (TA) (Tsetlin, 1961; Narendra & Thathachar, 2012) is an innovative con-
028   cept in a single-state reinforcement learning (Sutton & Barto, 2018; Çalışır & Pehlivanoğlu, 2019;
029   Zhang et al., 2022), designed to capture the intricate nature of human decision-making and calcula-
030   tion Narendra & Thathachar (2012). Inspired by principles from psychology, it emulates human-like
031   learning strategies within computational frameworks. Notably, the TA represents a pioneering so-
032   lution to the well-known multi-armed bandit problem (Yuan et al., 2022; Amani & Thrampoulidis,
033
      2021; Ramponi et al., 2021) and serves as the first learning algorithm in the Learning Automaton
      (LA) (Granmo, 2018; Abeyrathna et al., 2020; Belaid et al., 2023) family.
034
035   The TA (Narendra & Thathachar, 2012; Granmo, 2018) is characterized by its state-machine archi-
036   tecture, featuring a grid-like configuration of K actions and a fixed depth parameter N for each action
037   (number of nodes in each action), illustrated in Figure 1. Within the TA, each node corresponds to
038   a specific action and is identified by a unique pair. This design allows the automaton to transition
039
      between nodes based on rewards from its environment seamlessly. When receiving a reward, the
      chosen action is reinforced by transitioning from higher-numbered nodes to lower-numbered nodes
040
      within the action’s depth, thereby increasing its likelihood of selection. Conversely, in case of a
041
      penalty, the transition occurs from lower-numbered nodes to higher-numbered ones within the ac-
042   tion. If the highest-numbered node is reached and a penalty is incurred, the next action is chosen
043   according to a clockwise policy for the subsequent iteration. For example, φ(3,2) denotes the first
044   node of action 3. Upon receiving a reward, the TA transitions to φ(3,1) to increase the likelihood of
045   selecting action 3 twice. Conversely, a penalty leads the TA to transition to φ(4,2) , indicating that
046   selecting action 3 was incorrect and its selection chances are exhausted. This structured framework
047   enables the TA to effectively capture and represent the nuances of the learning process within a fixed
048   architecture.
049   TAs are typically employed in online learning decision-making scenarios, making it impractical to
050   adjust their depth using offline data (Narendra & Thathachar, 2012; Granmo, 2018). Therefore, an
051   online mechanism is essential for adapting the TA’s depth based on specific problem requirements,
052   allowing for incremental, decremental, or unchanged adjustments. This mechanism will help the
053   TA to explore and exploit the environment efficiently. Lower depth values may result in more ex-
      ploration and frequent action switching. Conversely, higher depth values may lead to challenges in

085   Moreover, from the perspective of Automated Machine Learning (AutoML) (He et al., 2021; Feurer
086   et al., 2015; Kübler et al., 2022), the TA lacks internal mechanisms or tools to discern whether
087   the selected depth is suitable or requires adjustment based on environmental conditions (Narendra

089

090
091   In this paper, we introduce the Adaptive Depth Tsetlin Automaton (ADTA), a novel approach that
092   intelligently learns the appropriate depth for the TA. By employing a central reinforcement learning
093   agent, the ADTA dynamically adjusts the depth of all actions within the automaton. This self-
094
      adaptive, flexible methodology enables the TA to strike an ideal balance between exploration and
      exploitation, significantly improving its performance and adaptability in a wide range of complex
095
      environments.

119   TA Model. The TA consists of K actions denoted as a1 , a2 , . . . , aK , and KN nodes represented by
120   φ(1,1) , φ(1,2) , . . . , φ(1,N) , . . . , φ(K,N) . Each node is defined by an ordered pair (i, j), where 1 ≤ i ≤ K
121   indicates the action number, and 1 ≤ j ≤ N denotes the node number. When the TA is in node
122   φ(i, j) , it performs action A(n) = ai in the nth iteration. The environment will respond to the TA
123   with r, where r ∈ R = {0, 1}. In the event of an unfavorable response (i.e., R(n) = r = 0), the state
124   transitions occur as follows:
125
                                                         φ(i, j) → φ(i, j+1) (1 ≤ j ≤ N − 1)
126                                                                                                                    (1)
                                                         φ(i, j) → φ(i+1, j) ( j = N)
127

      Similarly, in the case of a favorable response (i.e., R(n) = r = 1), the state transitions are determined
      by:
130
131
                                            φ(i, j) → φ(i, j−1) (2 ≤ j ≤ N)
                                                                                                             (2)
132                                         φ(i,1) → φ(i,1)     ( j = 1)

      The selection of the next action in this automaton follows a clockwise pattern (φ(i, j) → φ(i+1, j) ).
134
135   Goal. In scenarios where the TA requires a change in action due to receiving a penalty from the
136   environment (φ(i, j) → φ(i+1, j) ), the current depth parameter N may not be appropriately configured.
137   Thus, the main objective becomes either to increase (N ← N + 1) for exploitation, decrease (N ←
138   N − 1) for exploration, or maintain the depth unchanged in such situations.

140   1.3    R ELATED W ORKS
141
      Tsetlin Automaton. Drawing inspiration from Sutton and Barto’s influential book (Sutton & Barto,
142
      2018) on reinforcement learning, the exploration of learning automaton (LA) has played a pivotal
143
      role in shaping modern research, particularly in the domain of trial-and-error learning. Among the
144   variants of LA, the TA (Granmo, 2018; Granmo et al., 2019) stands out as a prominent model that
145   offers valuable insights into human decision-making processes and cognitive mechanisms. Notably,
146   its recent integration with Neural Networks (Sharma et al., 2023; Seraj et al., 2022; Abeyrathna et al.,
147   2021b; Bhattarai et al., 2022; Darshana Abeyrathna et al., 2020; Phoulady et al., 2019; Abeyrathna
148   et al., 2021a; 2020; Glimsdal & Granmo, 2021; Bhattarai et al., 2023) has gained remarkable at-
149   tention from researchers. Furthermore, over its renowned half-century existence, it has demon-
150   strated its versatility and applicability across a wide spectrum of fields (Narendra & Thathachar,
151   2012) including: decentralized control (Tung & Kleinrock, 1996), searching on the line (Oommen,
152
      1997), equi-partitioning (Oommen & Ma, 1988), streaming sampling for social activity networks
      (Ghavipour & Meybodi, 2018), faulty dichotomous search (Yazidi & Oommen, 2018), learning in
153
      deceptive environments (Zhang et al., 2016), and routing in telecommunication networks (Oommen
154
      et al., 2007).
155
156   Despite the TA’s effectiveness in uncertain and stochastic environments where problems require
157   quick trial-and-error solutions, it faces two fundamental challenges affecting its performance across
158
      various applications (Narendra & Thathachar, 2012): (1) The clockwise policy for selecting the next
      action; (2) The fixed depth value.
159

161   the clockwise policy of choosing the next action in TA by integrating it with an RL agent. This
      method is called the Hybrid Learning Automaton or HLA. In this method, an RL agent will learn

      4   D ISCUSSION
497
498   We consider the depth parameter, N, in the TA to develop a new learning agent, ADTA, capable of
499
      adapting to a wide range of environments, including both stationary and non-stationary scenarios.
      The ADTA can be analyzed from multiple perspectives, as outlined below.
500
501   Regarding the number of iterations, the ADTA inherently requires more iterations compared to
502   standard TA due to its adaptive mechanism designed to balance exploration and exploitation. Unlike
503   TA, which operates with a fixed depth and may continue doing so even when inappropriate, the
504   ADTA dynamically adjusts its depth, reducing the risk of suboptimal performance.
505   As demonstrated in our experiments, ADTA’s performance is particularly prominent at lower depths.
506   An inappropriate depth setting leads to excessive exploration, causing the learning agent to receive
507   fewer rewards. However, ADTA’s integrated reinforcement learning mechanism effectively tunes
508   the depth, allowing the agent to converge on the appropriate depth value. At higher depths, the per-
509   formance gap between ADTA and other learning automaton agents narrows, as the system naturally
510   approaches a near-optimal depth configuration.
511   Our approach leverages a large number of actions to showcase the scalability of ADTA, setting it
512   apart from traditional learning agents within the learning automaton family. This highlights ADTA’s
513   ability to adapt to more complex and dynamic environments effectively.
514
      The inclusion of a reinforcement learning agent, such as VASLA or multi-armed bandits, introduces
515   minimal structural changes to the TA, requiring only the addition of a vector to track the probabilities
516   for ’Grow’, ’Stop’, and ’Shrink’ actions.
517
518
      The model is highly flexible in terms of RL agent selection. Multiple RL agents, including multi-
      armed bandits and Q-learning, can be utilized as depth controllers. However, employing these agents
519
      may introduce additional parameters, such as λ1 and λ2 in VASLA or the ε parameter in the ε-greedy
520
      multi-armed bandit, adding a layer of complexity to the learning process.
521
522
523   5   C ONCLUSION
524
525   In this paper, we introduce the ADTA as a solution to the explore-exploit dilemma inherent in tradi-
526   tional TA approaches. This dilemma arises from the challenge of selecting an optimal depth for the
527   TA. The ADTA addresses this issue by autonomously adjusting its depth in unknown environments
528   through integration with an RL agent. Leveraging Lyapunov stability theorem and Markov chain
529   processes, we investigate ADTA’s learning capabilities. Our comprehensive evaluations consistently
530   demonstrate ADTA’s superiority over traditional TA methods and state-of-the-art techniques like
531   HLA. Interesting future research directions include providing stronger proof for ADTA’s effective-
532   ness in complex scenarios, exploring the integration of ADTA with HLA to jointly address depth
533
      and action selection policies, and investigating asymmetric depth adjustments for the ADTA.

806   actions. In contrast, model-free RL involves agents learning directly from interactions with the
807   environment, refining actions through trial-and-error. The choice between these approaches depends
808   on task characteristics, with model-based methods suited for environments where a reliable model is
809   available, and model-free methods excelling in complex and uncertain environments. In this paper,
      we chose learning automaton from the model-free category.

      C.1      VARIABLE S TRUCTURE L EARNING AUTOMATON (VSLA)
851
852   Variable structure learning automaton (Narendra & Thathachar, 2012; Yazidi et al., 2020; Safara
853   et al., 2020; Ghanavati et al., 2020) can be defined mathematically by a quintuple < A, R, P, λ1 , λ2 >,
854   where A = {a1 , a2 , · · · , aK } denotes the finite action set from which the automaton can select the
855   intended action, R denotes the reward of the environment (R ∈ [0, 1]), P = {p1 , p2 , · · · , pK } denotes
856   the action probability vector, such that pi is the probability of choosing the ai action (1 ≤ i ≤ K),
857   λ1 , and λ2 indicate the reward and penalty parameters that determine the amount of increase and
858   decrease of the action probabilities at epoch n.
859   λ1 and λ2 can have different values. Based on these values, the updating rule of the probability
860   vector can be categorized as follows:
861
862            • LR−P : This updating scheme, which is called ”linear reward-penalty,” comes from the
863              equality of the reward and penalty parameters (λ1 = λ2 ). When both are the same, the
                 probability vector of the learning automaton increases or decreases at a monotonic rate.


                                                                                        16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
             • LR−εP : This updating scheme, which is called ”linear reward-ε penalty,” leads to a much
865            greater value of the reward parameter in relation to the penalty parameter (λ1 >> λ2 ).
866
867          • LR−I : When there is no penalty in an updating scheme (0 < λ1 < 1, λ2 = 0), this updating
868
               scheme is called ”linear reward-Inaction.” The probability vector of the learning automaton
               will not change upon receiving an unfavorable response from the environment.
869
870          • LP−I : If the conducted probability vector in the learning automaton doesn’t change by
871            receiving the favorable action, this updating scheme is called ”linear penalty-Inaction”
872            (λ1 = 0, 0 < λ2 < 1).
873          • Pure Chance: An updating scheme in which there is no penalty and reward parameter
874            (λ1 = λ2 = 0) is called ”Pure Chance.” In this updating scheme, the probability vector of
875            the automaton will not change in any conditions.

1226   In this section, we present additional synthetic experiments on more complex environments known
1227
       as non-stationary environments. In these environments, where the penalty probability ci changes

1228
       penalties Narendra & Thathachar (2012). To succeed in such conditions, learning automaton must
1229

1232   State-dependent. In these cases, the learning automaton operates within a finite set of environments,
1233   denoted as E1 , E2 , ..., ED Narendra & Thathachar (2012). To maintain consistency with other sec-

1235   learning agent.

1483
1484   Addressing overfitting in deep neural networks, especially in large architectures, presents a
1485   formidable challenge. The dropout technique (Srivastava et al., 2014), introduced to mitigate this is-
1486   sue, involves randomly omitting neurons and connections during network training to curb excessive
1487
       co-adaptation. It employs random unit dropping with a fixed retention probability, typically within
       the [0.5, 1] range. This fixed probability lacks adaptability and requires extensive experimentation to
1488
       determine suitable values for various network configurations. (Gholami et al., 2023) pioneered the
1489
       application of LA to this problem, incorporating a three-action HLA, with ’increase,’ ’decrease,’ and
1490   ’stop’ actions to adjust dropout probabilities. The HLA avoids exceeding preset bounds. Thinned
1491   networks are sampled by the HLA during training, and employed in forward and back-propagation
1492   within mini-batches. A single HLA manages dropout probability, adjusted per mini-batch. Gradi-
1493   ents are averaged within mini-batches, and LA reinforcement signals depend on thinned network
1494   loss values.

1520   I      A PPLICATION : B LOCKCHAIN S ECURITY
1521
1522   The Bitcoin network is inherently dynamic, making it challenging to arrive at deterministic de-
1523   cisions. Therefore, a probabilistic decision-making mechanism is essential for critical decision-
1524   making in this environment. Given the vast state space, it is more efficient to employ a single-state
1525   decision-maker. As a result, we leverage ADTA to design a novel defense mechanism, named Nik
1526   Nikhalat-Jahromi et al. (2024; 2023), aimed at countering the selfish mining attack Eyal (2015);
1527   Eyal & Sirer (2018) in Bitcoin Nakamoto (2008); Wang et al. (2019); Babaioff et al. (2012).
1528   We begin with a brief introduction to relevant concepts such as Bitcoin mining and selfish mining.
1529   Following this, we detail the experiments conducted to evaluate the automaton’s performance in this
1530   complex setting. Additionally, the developed simulator is available on GitHub1 .
1531
1532   I.1     C ONCEPTS
1533
1534   Bitcoin Nakamoto (2008), introduced by Satoshi Nakamoto in 2009, is a decentralized cryptocur-
1535   rency that has gained significant attention due to its decentralized nature Wang et al. (2019).
1536   Transactions in the Bitcoin network are recorded in blocks, and creating a new block requires solving
1537   a cryptographic puzzle, which comes with a dedicated reward. Participants who contribute resources
1538   to solve these puzzles are known as miners Nakamoto (2008); Eyal & Sirer (2018); Nikhalat-Jahromi
1539   et al. (2024; 2023); Wang et al. (2019).
1540
       The mining process incentivizes the safety of Bitcoin by rewarding miners based on their shared
1541   resources, ensuring the network’s decentralization Wang et al. (2019).
1542
1543   However, maintaining Bitcoin’s decentralization is a challenging task, as attacks like selfish mining
1544
       Eyal & Sirer (2018) threaten its fundamental properties. Selfish miners keep newly discovered
       blocks private and reveal them selectively to maximize their rewards.
1545
1546   When selfish miners reveal their withheld blocks, a fork occurs in the blockchain. In such situations,
1547   the honest branch of the fork, resulting from valid work, is discarded, and consensus is reached on
1548   the selfish branch Wang et al. (2021).
1549   Our proposed automaton presents a novel approach to address this issue in Bitcoin. Our goal is to
1550   simplify the problem by making decisions among the forked branches within each distributed miner.
1551   This approach aims to overcome the challenges posed by selfish mining and ensure the integrity of
1552   the Bitcoin network.
1553
1554   I.2     P ROPOSED D EFENSE
1555
1556   In this section, we introduce our novel defense mechanism that leverages the power of learning
1557   automaton to address the challenge of selfish mining in Bitcoin. The learning automaton serves as a
1558
       decision-maker at each node, assisting in the selection of a branch from the forked blockchain, even
       in the presence of selfishly mined branches. To make informed decisions, predefined criteria based
1559
       on branch characteristics are employed:
1560
1561             • Branch Length (L L): It represents the number of blocks in a specific branch of the fork.
1562
                 • Branch Weight (W W ): Calculated by comparing the blocks of a branch with the same height
1563
                   in other branches. The branch with the most recent creation time is incremented by one at
1564
                   each iteration.

       To facilitate branch selection, the following parameters are taken into consideration:
1567
1568          • Fail-Safe Parameter (δδ ): This parameter helps miners choose a branch based on L or W .
1569            If the length of a branch in the fork exceeds the others by a threshold of δ , that branch is
1570            chosen. Otherwise, the branch with the highest weight, as determined by W , is selected.
1571          • Decision-Making Time (ττ ): This refers to the duration a miner takes to check for existing
1572            forks and make a decision. If a fork is detected, the miner considers the ∆ parameter for
1573            branch selection.
1574          • Time Window Parameter (θθ ): It configures the next value of ∆ using the learning au-
1575            tomaton. Each θ consists of multiple τ intervals.
1576
1577   The decision-making algorithm for branch selection involves the following steps:
1578
1579
             1. Calculation of L for each branch.
1580         2. Calculation of W for each branch.
1581         3. Sorting the branches in descending order based on length. If the difference between the
1582            length of the longest and second-longest branch is greater than δ , the longest branch is
1583            chosen. Otherwise, the branch with the highest weight is selected.
1584         4. When τ reaches its end, the learning automaton determines the next value of δ . Typically, δ
1585            oscillates between a minimum value (δmin ) and a maximum value (δmax ). The learning au-
1586            tomaton has three options: 1) ”Grow” to increase δ by one, 2) ”Stop” to keep δ unchanged,
1587            and 3) ”Shrink” to decrease ∆ by one.
1588         5. When θ reaches its end, the learning automaton receives feedback from the environment.
1589            We have designed a virtual environment to provide information about the learning automa-
1590            ton’s decision. The reward (R) is computed by dividing the number of decisions made
1591            based on W by the total number of decisions, which includes decisions based on length and
1592            weight. The following equation demonstrates the R parameter of the learning automaton.
