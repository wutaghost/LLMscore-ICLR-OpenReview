009                                                         A BSTRACT
010
011            Aligning large language models (LLMs) with human values is a vital task for LLM practitioners.
012            Current alignment techniques have several limitations: (1) requiring a large amount of annotated
013            data; (2) demanding heavy human involvement; (3) lacking a systematic mechanism to continuously
014            improve. In this work, we study aligning LLMs to a new domain with limited samples (e.g. < 100).
015            We propose an algorithm that can self-align LLMs iteratively without active human involvement.
016            Unlike existing works, our algorithm relies on neither human-crafted instructions nor labeled rewards,
017            significantly reducing human involvement. In addition, our algorithm can self-improve the alignment
018            continuously. The key idea is to first retrieve high-quality samples related to the target domain and
019
               use them as In-context Learning examples to generate more samples. Then we use the self-generated
               samples to finetune the LLM iteratively. We show that our method can unlock the LLMs’ self-
020
               generalization ability to perform alignment with near-zero human supervision. We test our algorithm
021
               on three benchmarks in safety, truthfulness, and instruction-following, and show good performance
022            in alignment, domain adaptability, and scalability.

025   1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      The technique to make Large language models (LLMs) follow human instructions and generate safe outputs is
028   alignment (Ouyang et al., 2022). Currently, it is the key to generating sophisticated text and tackling a variety of
029   language-based tasks (Brown et al., 2020; Bubeck et al., 2023; OpenAI, 2023; Liu et al., 2023). The mainstream
030   alignment approaches include SFT (Wei et al., 2021) and RLHF (Ouyang et al., 2022). However, both SFT and RLHF
031   are heavily data-dependent. The lack of high-quality data significantly blocks the democratization of usable and safe
032   LLMs. In this work, we explore scenarios with limited examples from the target alignment domain such as safety,
033   truthfulness, and helpfulness. A few prior works propose to solve this problem with self-alignment (Wang et al., 2022;
034   Sun et al., 2023c), i.e. making the LLMs align themselves with samples generated by themselves. The common
035   assumption is the pretrained LLMs have already learned a good amount of hidden knowledge related to the aligned
036
      behaviors and we just need to “elicit” it with samples generated by LLMs themselves rather than using direct human
      instructions.
037
038   However, the current self-alignment techniques are not truly free of human instructions. They still involve some form
039   of hand-crafted instructions or principles to enhance the quality of the model-generated responses. It leads to two
040   limitations: (1) Crafting effective human instruction is complex. For example, Sun et al. (2023c) needs to manually
041   design 16 generic principles and multiple specific principles for different tasks. It requires substantial domain knowledge
042
      and risks erring at a higher level compared to a more bottom-up data-driven approach. More importantly in practice,
      designing and refining human instructions requires considerable labor, which contradicts the scenario of limited samples
043
      where human resources are lacking. Additionally, adapting these instructions for new alignment domains often requires
044
      new guidelines, which motivates a more automatable approach. (2) Current self-alignment can work only on large
045   models. Existing works often require models with a significantly large number of parameters, e.g. Wang et al. (2022)
046   use 175B GPT-3 and Sun et al. (2023c) use LLaMA-65B. And often the approach would be less effective for smaller
047   models like LLaMA-7B since they are less capable of following instructions and understanding the contents (Li et al.,
048   2023c).
049
      A different line of approach is to use an external reward model to filter LLM-generated answers (Gulcehre et al., 2023),
050
      as opposed to applying supervising principles when generating answers. However, in scenarios where the target domain
051   for alignment has limited samples, developing high-quality reward models is challenging and often still requires a

082     Is it possible to self-align LLMs to a target domain with only a few examples and without any human-designed

085   To this end, we propose an alignment algorithm ISARA (Iterative Self-Alignment with Retrieval-Augmented in-context
086   learning), illustrated in Figure 1. ISARA diverges from traditional methods by leveraging retrieval-augmented in-context
087   learning (ICL) to generate high-quality answers using contextually relevant, retrieved examples. The key idea is to first
088
      retrieve relevant and high-quality prompt-output pairs related to the target domain and use them as In-context Learning
      examples to generate more relevant samples belonging to the target domain. Then we use the self-generated samples to
089
      finetune the LLM iteratively.
090
091   Another key design of ISARA is its iterative mechanism, containing multiple training cycles. Each training cycle
092   leverages the most recent LLM to generate a dataset of more refined quality. This is logical given we end up with a
093   more aligned model after the alignment, which can generate more high-quality data that in return can be used to further
094
      align LLMs until we reach the limit imposed by the LLM capacity and data quality. ISARA can work on small models
      because we rely on retrieved examples rather than human instructions. Thanks to this design, the model only needs
095
      to imitate the style of the examples and does not need to understand the abstract concept of safety, truthfulness, or
096
      helpfulness from human-crafted principles, which would require a stronger ability that is only shown in large models.
097   We find empirically that our framework can be adaptable to models as small as 350M and can be applied across various
098   domains without the need for redesigning principles or retraining reward models. We compare our approach with other
099   self-alignment methods in Table 1.
100
      We conduct comprehensive experiments across three key alignment benchmarks: safety, truthfulness, and instruction-
101
      following. We find the iterative training scheme enhances alignment performance over time. We also show we
102
      consistently outperform the SFT models in the conventional alignment pipeline. In terms of balancing between
103   harmlessness and helpfulness, we notably improve harmlessness rates without compromising helpfulness. Furthermore,

105   its adaptability and effectiveness.

108   2    R ELATED W ORK
109
110   Our work contributes to the growing body of work focused on training or fine-tuning LLMs with self-generated
111
      datasets. Existing studies in this area, such as those by Wang et al. (2022); Sun et al. (2023c), often rely on human
      instructions, which we argue counteracts the objective of self-alignment to reduce human intervention. Notably, those
112
      two prominent self-alignment frameworks necessitate LLMs with at least 65B parameters, as smaller models struggle
113
      with following complex human instructions using in-context learning (ICL). Our framework, however, diverges from
114   this trend by eliminating the requirement for human-crafted principles or demonstrations, thus significantly reducing
115   human involvement and catering to LLMs that are less proficient in instruction-following.
116
117
      A recent development in this field is the ReST framework (Gulcehre et al., 2023), which introduces an iterative
      self-alignment model that utilizes a learned reward system to filter out low-quality QA pairs from generated datasets,
118
      thereby avoiding the complexities of elaborate principles. In contrast, our approach employs retrieval-augmented ICL to
119
      enhance the generation quality. Our framework is not just iterative but also operates independently of both handcrafted
120   principles and learned reward models, marking a unique advancement in self-alignment methodologies.
121
122   Self-alignment is intrinsically linked to self-supervised learning, typically involving prompt-only datasets. For instance,
123
      LMSI (Huang et al., 2022) leverages Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting (Wei et al., 2022) to generate high-quality
      responses for unlabeled datasets. Similarly, SALMON (Sun et al., 2023b) creates a principle-adhering reward model
124
      from a synthetic dataset and further enhances the LLM through reinforcement learning. Moreover, QA datasets can
125
      be derived from existing text corpora, like web corpora, prompting LLMs to generate questions from the inherent
126   knowledge. Methods in this sphere include backtranslation (Köksal et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023a), self-chat (Xu et al.,
127   2023), and self-QA (Zhang & Yang, 2023). However, these techniques depend on handcrafted principles to improve the
128   dataset quality and typically involve a single training iteration. We detail these comparisons in Table 1.

183   4     M ETHODOLOGY
184
185   Our methodology only requires a few question-answer examples, denoted by D0 . The proposed framework consists of
186   multiple iterations, each encompassing both dataset generation and fine-tuning phases. The entire framework is detailed
187   in Algorithm 1.
188
189   4.1    DATA G ENERATION
190
      In the k-th iteration, our goal is to prompt the LLM to generate a dataset of N new question-answer (QA) samples. This
191
      process begins by sampling C QA pairs from all preceding datasets D0 , . . . , Dk−1 , ensuring each dataset contributes at
192
      least one example to enhance diversity. We sample QA pairs from all preceding datasets and use those as contexts to
193   prompt the LLM to generate one new question at a time (line 5-6 of Algorithm 1).
194
195   Next, we adopt retrieval-augmented in-context learning to annotate the newly generated question with a corresponding
196
      aligned answer (line 8-10). Specifically, we utilize k-nearest-neighbors (kNN) to identify similar questions from prior
      datasets based on sentence embeddings, using external embedding models like text-embedding-ada-002. Both
197
      questions and answers from these pairs are used as contexts in answer generation. Upon generating a set of C new
198
      QA pairs, we apply simple filtering criteria to remove low quality samples, such as excluding pairs where the question
199   already exists in previous datasets (line 13).
200
201   Since our context for the LLM is always a combination of examples without any human-designed principles, we do not
202
      require LLM to have the ability to follow human instructions and also reduce human efforts to a new minimum. The
      prompts are shown in Appendix A.2 and A.3 for question and answer generation, respectively.
203
204
205
      4.2    F INETUNING
206
      After we create dataset Dk , we perform supervised fine-tuning (SFT) (line 15). This SFT incorporates both the newly
207   generated dataset Dk and the initial dataset D0 . The design is to ensure the alignment training data to be as the most


                                                                         4
208
      high-quality as we can get. Since the initial dataset D0 is manually annotated or selected by human, it should have high
209   quality. In addition, as the LLM aligns iteratively, the latest LLM should be the most aligned, and therefore the data
210   generated by it should have the best quality among all self-generated samples, except the initial samples. We employ a
211   coefficient γ ∈ (0, ∞) to regulate the proportion of data used from each dataset during the fine-tuning process. We use
212   the cross-entropy loss defined in (3.1) as our SFT loss function.
213
214   4.3   I TERATIVE E NHANCEMENT
215
216   We find empirically that retrieval-augmented alignment can iteratively enhance the performance of the finetuned model.
217   Therefore, we repeat the data generation and finetuning phases iteratively until a threshold is reached — specifically,
218   when less than α ∈ [0, 1] of newly generated samples remain post-filtering (line 17-19). We call this ratio the stopping
219   threshold. This threshold indicates the model’s peak capability in producing high-quality new QA pairs based on
220   the current data. If the threshold is not provided by users, we can still stop the iterative training process by setting a
221   maximum number of iterations. In Algorithm 1, we set both of those stopping criteria and output the finetuned model in
222
      the latest iteration before stopping.

224   5     E XPERIMENTS
225
226   5.1   S ETUP
227
228   Our empirical experiments are designed to evaluate the efficacy of our method across three distinct metrics: safety,
229   truthfulness, and instruction-following. These metrics correspond to three benchmarks: B EAVERTAILS, T RUTHFUL QA,
230   and A LPACA -E VAL. We set the context example count, C, to 8 for B EAVERTAILS and T RUTHFUL QA, and 6 for
231   A LPACA -E VAL. Those numbers are much smaller than the number of samples required for performing supervised
232   fine-tuning. During both question and answer generation phases, each dataset contributes one question-answer pair,
233
      with the exception of the initial dataset D0 , which is of assured quality. Consequently, we sample C − k examples from
      D0 in the k-th iteration. In all experimental settings, we fix the coefficient γ at 1 and the stopping threshold α at 0.3.
234
      We also set the maximum number of iterations, K, to ⌈C/2⌉ to ensure that at least half of the examples in the context
235
      are sourced from the initial dataset.
236
237   Additionally, our filtering rule at line 13 of Algorithm 1 removes a sample if it meets any of the following criteria: (1)
238   The ROUGE-L score of a generated question, compared to those in the context, is 0.7 or higher. (2) The question is a
239
      duplicate, having appeared previously in either the initial or a generated dataset. (3) The answer is merely a repetition
      of the question. (4) Either the question or the answer contains fewer than 5 words.

      We focus on safety alignment using ISARA and seek to address the following key questions: (1) How does ISARA’s

245
      (3) Can ISARA generalize across different domains in alignment tasks? (4) Does iterative training yield better results
246
      than a single training session? (5) Does ISARA maintain utility (helpfulness) while enhancing harmlessness? (6) What
247   level of data scaling efficiency does ISARA achieve?

443
444
445
      We propose ISARA (Iterative Self-Alignment with Retrieval-Augmented ICL), a framework designed to self-align
      Large Language Models. Our primary objective is to eliminate the reliance on human instructions, a common limitation
446
      in prior works (Wang et al., 2022; Sun et al., 2023c). ISARA integrates retrieval-augmented ICL to generate high-quality
447
      samples, enhancing the model’s self-alignment capabilities. Another key feature of ISARA is its iterative training
448   framework, developed upon the insight that retrieval-augmented ICL can further elevate the performance of already
449   finetuned models. In each iteration, the framework utilizes the latest model iteration to produce a dataset of progressively
450   higher quality.
451
      Our comprehensive experiments across safety, truthfulness, and instruction-following alignment benchmarks have
452
      demonstrated ISARA’s superiority in terms of alignment performance, domain adaptability, and scalability. These
453
      findings underscore ISARA’s potential to significantly advance the field of LLM alignment, offering a pathway towards
454   more autonomous, efficient, and adaptable LLMs. In the future, we will compare with more baseline methods to address
455   this limitation.
