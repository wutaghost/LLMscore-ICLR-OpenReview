011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            The evolutionary processes of complex systems contain critical information about
014            their functional characteristics. The generation time of edges can reveal the his-
015            torical evolution of various networked complex systems, such as protein-protein
016
               interaction networks, ecosystems, and social networks. Recovering these evolu-
               tionary processes holds significant scientific value, such as aiding in the inter-
017
               pretation of the evolution of protein-protein interaction networks. However, the
018
               scarcity of temporally labeled network data poses challenges for predicting edge
019            generation times under current network structures, leading to issues of insufficient
020            data and significant differences between training and prediction networks. To ad-
021            dress this, we introduce a diffusion model that learns the generative mechanisms
022            of networks, producing sufficient augmented network data to effectively mitigate
023            issues of limited and incomplete data. Experimental results demonstrate a 13.7%
024            improvement in prediction accuracy using our approach. Moreover, the model can
025            uniformly predict edge generation times across different types of networks, elim-
026            inating the need to retrain the model for each specific network, thus significantly
027
               enhancing generalization capability and efficiency.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   The core challenge in reconstructing the evolution of complex networks is inferring the order in
033
      which edges are formed in dynamic graphs, thus reproducing the network’s evolutionary process
      (Boccaletti et al. (2006); Liao et al. (2017)). This task is intrinsically tied to the dependency between
034
      network structure and edge formation sequence, as accurately inferring this order can reveal the un-
035
      derlying dynamic mechanisms of the network, as illustrated in Figure 1. Successfully reconstructing
036   network evolution has significant applications across various domains, such as social network anal-
037   ysis, traffic system optimization, and biological network research (Barabâsi et al. (2002); Wang
038   et al. (2023); Seferbekova et al. (2023)). These fields require a deep understanding of how networks
039   evolve over time to predict future trends and implement effective interventions or optimizations.
040
      However, reconstructing complex network evolution is a typical small-sample problem (Margeloiu
041
      et al. (2023)). The real-world mechanisms driving network evolution are often highly complex and
042   unclear, making theoretical reconstruction exceedingly difficult. Machine learning-based algorithms
043   offer promising solutions to this problem (Banerjee et al. (2023)). For instance, methods like those
044   in Nature Communications 2024 proposed by Wang et al. (2024) employ graph embedding and
045   machine learning to automatically learn the spatiotemporal structures of networks. Nonetheless,
046   these algorithms face significant challenges due to data scarcity. The dynamic evolution of real
047   networks is rarely fully observable, making it difficult to acquire high-quality labeled data. As a
048   result, models typically rely on a limited number of samples for training, which not only leads to
049   overfitting but also severely hampers their generalization and transferability, resulting in suboptimal
050
      performance across different networks and scenarios.
051   To address these challenges, we propose a graph generation-based sample augmentation method
052   aimed at enhancing the reconstruction of network evolution. By generating dynamic graph samples
053   that adhere to the actual evolutionary patterns of real networks, this method supplements the origi-
      nal small training dataset, significantly improving the model’s reconstruction capabilities. However,

087   2     P RELIMINARIES
088
089   In the study of complex networks, the generation time of edges is a critical factor influencing net-
090   work evolution. Our prediction task aims to learn the relationship between network structure and
091   edge generation time, given a temporal network. Specifically, we focus on how to infer the genera-
092   tion order of edges in the absence of time labels.
093
      Our ultimate goal is to predict the generation time of each edge for a pure network structure. To
094
      simplify the task, we specify it as follows: for any two edges ei and ej in the network, we need to
095   predict their generation order, determining which edge is generated first and which is generated later.
096   By inferring the order of all edges, we can construct the overall generation sequence of edges in the
097   network, thereby gaining a comprehensive understanding of the network’s evolutionary process.
098
099   2.1   M ATHEMATICAL D EFINITION
100
101   1. Temporal Network Representation: Let the temporal network G consist of a set of nodes V
102   and a set of edges E. Each edge ek ∈ E represents a connection between a pair of nodes (uk , vk )
103   and has an associated generation time tk .
104   2. Edge Generation Order: For any two edges ei = (ui , vi ) and ej = (uj , vj ), we define the
105   generation order relation R(ei , ej ) as follows:
106
107                                                         1, if ti < tj
                                            R(ei , ej ) =
                                                            0, if ti ≥ tj

      Here, R(ei , ej ) = 1 indicates that edge ei is generated before edge ej , while R(ei , ej ) = 0 indicates
109   that the generation time of edge ei is later than or equal to that of edge ej .
110
111   3. Edge Generation Time Prediction: Our task can be expressed as a binary relation prediction
112
      problem, where the objective is to predict the edge generation order R(ei , ej ) by learning the struc-
      tural features of the network S(G):
113
114                                         R̂(ei , ej ) = f (S(G), ei , ej )
115
116   In this context, R̂(ei , ej ) is the predicted generation order, and the function f represents the model
117   used to infer the generation order based on network structural features.
118
      4. Construction of Edge Generation Sequence: Once the generation order for all edges is pre-
119   dicted, we can obtain the overall edge generation sequence T , expressed as:
120
121                                             T = {ek1 , ek2 , . . . , ekm }
122
      Here, k1 , k2 , . . . , km are the indices of edges arranged according to the predicted generation order.
123
124   Through the above description and mathematical definitions, our task clearly demonstrates how to
125   infer edge generation times based solely on structural information in the absence of time labels, thus
126
      providing robust support for the evolutionary prediction of complex networks.

187   3.2    DATA AUGMENTATION FOR E DGE T IME P REDICTION M ODEL
188
189   As shown in Figure 2, we sample networks with temporal labels, varying in type and size, to train the
190   diffusion model. The diffusion model generates networks resembling the target temporal networks,
191   augmenting the original training data and improving edge generation time prediction accuracy. Two
192
      distinct augmentation frameworks address the limitations of existing methods.
193   1. Mitigating Transfer Learning Challenges: Existing models often struggle with transfer learning
194   due to structural and temporal discrepancies between training and target networks. To resolve this,
195   we employ the diffusion model to generate temporal networks that closely resemble the target net-
196   work. The model is trained exclusively on these generated networks, ensuring alignment between
197   training and target data, which eliminates transfer learning issues and enhances predictive perfor-
198
      mance.
199   2. Enhancing Robustness and Fitting Ability: A single network often provides insufficient training
200   data. When multiple networks are combined, structural inconsistencies can degrade performance.
201   To overcome this, we generate additional temporal networks similar to the training network using
202   the diffusion model. By combining the original and generated networks, we enrich the training set,
203   improving the model’s robustness and fitting capability across similar network structures.
204

      The construction of the training dataset for our diffusion model is a crucial step in enhancing the
207
      edge order prediction task. Our approach begins with a labeled temporal network, which serves as

211   work samples from the original labeled temporal network. Specifically, we start by sampling a subset
212
      of edges, beginning with 50% of the total edges and progressively increasing to include all edges in
      the network. This approach allows us to explore various network densities and their impacts on the
213
      learning process.
214
215   Creation of Diverse Samples: For each sampling iteration, we construct a new training network by
      retaining the sampled edges and discarding the remaining edges. This results in 100 distinct training

      5     RELATED WORK
433
434
435   5.1   T EMPORAL N ETWORKS IN C OMPLEX N ETWORKS
436
437   Recent advancements in temporal networks have significantly enriched complex network analysis.
438   Early models such as Erdős-Rényi and Barabási-Albert (Karoński & Ruciński (1997); Watts & Stro-
439
      gatz (1998)) mainly addressed static network properties, offering limited applicability to dynamic
      systems. Holme & Saramäki (2012) provided foundational concepts like temporal paths, establish-
440
      ing a basis for subsequent research. Karsai et al. (2012) highlighted bursty interaction patterns in
441
      real-world networks, emphasizing temporal heterogeneity. Masuda et al. (2017) extended random
442   walk theory to temporal networks, crucial for understanding diffusion processes. Mucha et al. (2010)
443   introduced methods for detecting communities in dynamic structures, while Millidge et al. (2024)
444   integrated predictive models for forecasting interactions. Rossetti & Cazabet (2018) reviewed evolv-
445   ing community detection approaches, showcasing the need to address both temporal and topological
446   dynamics in analysis.
447
448
449   5.2   DYNAMIC G RAPHS AND T EMPORAL N ETWORK G ENERATION
450
451   Recent developments in temporal network generation have significantly advanced the field, focusing
452
      on capturing both the structural and temporal dynamics of evolving networks. Zhou et al. (2020)
      improved temporal modeling by transforming temporal interactions into static graphs, while Zeno
453
      et al. (2021) utilized motif-based approaches for generating time-dependent structures, though it
454
      assumed fixed rates and lacked adaptability to real-world temporal fluctuations. Gupta et al. (2022)
455   introduced TIGGER leveraging temporal point processes, combining both inductive and transduc-
456   tive learning to handle large-scale, complex networks with superior efficiency and accuracy. This
457   progression highlights the growing ability of temporal models to replicate the nuanced evolution of
458   real-world dynamic systems.
459
460
461   5.3   DATA AUGMENTATION VIA G RAPH D IFFUSION M ODELS
462
463   Employing generative methods such as generative adversarial networks (GANs) to learn a distribu-
464   tion based on a small sample of data and subsequently conduct sampling to obtain a large quantity
465   of training samples is a commonly adopted method (Zhang et al., 2022; Abdollahzadeh et al., 2023;
466
      Zhang et al., 2018; Fu et al., 2024). Diffusion models have recently shown significant advantages in
      performance, and have done well on graph generation tasks (Vignac et al., 2022; Niu et al., 2020; Jo
467
      et al., 2022), making them suitable for solving the problem of small-sample learning in reconstruct-
468
      ing network evolutions. Specifically, Niu et al. (2020) first propose to introduce the score-based
469   diffusion model to solve the graph generation problem. The Gaussian noise is adopted to construct
470   the diffusion process, leading to poor sparsity generated graphs. Jo et al. (2022) explore jointly
471   modeling the nodes and edges with semantic features. Vignac et al. (2022) use multinomial noise
472   to make the diffusion process operating in discrete space, which greatly improves the performance.
473   Therefore, we can see that using graph diffusion models to generate sufficient samples to enhance
474   edge order prediction performance is promising.
475

477   6     C ONCLUSION
478
479
      Our work focuses on predicting edge generation times from given network structures, which aids
480   in understanding network evolution and forecasting future developmental trends. We leverage dif-
481   fusion models to generate additional temporal networks, addressing challenges related to limited
482   sample sizes that often lead to overfitting and poor generalization. By augmenting the training data,
483   we significantly enhance the model’s fitting capability. Furthermore, by generating augmented data
484   for the target network, we mitigate transferability issues, resulting in an improvement of 13%. Im-
485   portantly, our approach allows for the training of a unified model capable of generating augmented
      data across all network types, maximizing flexibility and applicability in diverse contexts.

595

      In the supplementary materials, we provide further details on the three network models: BA model,
597   PSO model, and Fitness model.
598
599         • Barabási–Albert (BA) model: The BA model generates networks through preferential
600           attachment. Starting with a small fully connected graph, each new node added to the net-
601           work connects to an existing node with probability p ∝ k, where k is the degree (number
602           of connections) of the existing node. This model reflects the power-law degree distribution
603           found in many natural networks, where a few nodes dominate with many connections. The
604           construction of the BA model used in this work is as follows:
605             1. Generate an ER random network with the number of nodes N0 = 10 and connection
606                probability q = 0.5 as the initial network of the BA model.
607             2. At every time step, a single new node is introduced to the network.
608             3. The new node connects to n existing nodes in the network. The existing nodes are
609                selected by the rule of preferential attachment, in which the nodes are selected with
610                the probability proportional to their degree. Mathematically, the probability Pa that a
611                new node added at time step t connects to an existing node a is given by:
612
                                                             ka
613                                                    Pa = PNt           ,
614                                                              b=1 kb
615                where ka is the degree of node a, Nt is the number of the existing nodes at time step
616                t, and kb is the degree of node b.
617             4. Iterate steps 2 and 3 until all nodes and edges are added.
618         • Popularity-similarity-optimization (PSO) model: The PSO model is designed to capture
619           both popularity and similarity between nodes. Nodes are embedded in hyperbolic space,
620           where each new node introduced at time step t is assigned a radial coordinate r = ln t and
621           a random angular coordinate. The connection between the new node and existing nodes
622           is determined by minimizing the product of the birth time s of the existing nodes and the
623           angular distance θst between the new and existing nodes, such that nodes with the smallest
624           sθst values are selected to connect. The radial distance corresponds to node popularity,
625           while the angular distance represents their similarity. The parameters used in this work are
626
              set as m = 5, L = 5, γ = 2.1, T = 0.4, and ζ = 1. This model effectively simulates social
              networks where both popularity and shared interests influence connections.
627
628         • Fitness model: In the Fitness model, each node is characterized by a fitness value ηi , re-
629           flecting its intrinsic ability to attract connections. The probability of a new node connecting
630
              to an existing node is given by:
631
                                                           ηa ka
632                                                 Pa = PNt
633                                                       b=1 ηb kb
634           where ηa and ηb represent the fitness of nodes a and b, respectively, and ka is the degree of
635           node a at time t. The fitness values are drawn from a power-law distribution, P (η) = η −3.5 ,
636           emphasizing the heterogeneity in node attractiveness. This model is commonly used to
637           simulate networks where both competitiveness and popularity influence connections, such
638           as academic citation or business networks.
