009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Despite significant advancements, Large Language Models (LLMs) exhibit blind
012            spots that impair their ability to retrieve and process relevant contextual data effec-
013            tively. We demonstrate that LLM performance in graph tasks with complexities
014            beyond the “needle-in-a-haystack” scenario—where solving the problem requires
015            cross-referencing and reasoning across multiple subproblems jointly—is influ-
016            enced by the proximity of relevant information within the context, a phenomenon
017            we term “lost-in-distance”. We examine two fundamental graph tasks: identify-
018
               ing common connections between two nodes and assessing similarity among three
               nodes, and show that the model’s performance in these tasks significantly depends
019
               on the relative positioning of common edges. We evaluate three publicly available
020
               LLMs—Llama-3-8B, Llama-3-70B, and GPT-4—using various graph encoding
021            techniques that represent graph structures for LLM input. We propose a formu-
022            lation for the lost-in-distance phenomenon and demonstrate that lost-in-distance
023            and lost-in-the middle phenomenas occur independently. Results indicate that
024            model accuracy can decline by up to 6x as the distance between node connections
025            increases, independent of graph encoding and model size.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Large Language Models (LLMs) have attained an unprecedented level of generality by leverag-
031   ing scale and attention-based architectures (Kaplan et al., 2020; Vaswani, 2017). These models
032   exhibit remarkable, often superhuman, capabilities across a diverse range of tasks, including lan-
033
      guage translation, reading comprehension, and question answering (Costa-jussà et al., 2022; Sanh
      et al., 2021). Additionally, LLMs are increasingly serving as essential and flexible building blocks
034
      for various user-facing machine learning and artificial intelligence applications beyond traditional
035
      language processing domains, such as recommendation systems (Geng et al., 2022), graph-related
036   tasks (Wang et al., 2024), knowledge bases (AlKhamissi et al., 2022; Petroni et al., 2019), and more.
037   These applications highlight the versatility of LLMs but also expose new challenges in handling
038   domain-specific data encoded as textual input.
039
      Particularly, by leveraging the extensive common knowledge and powerful semantic comprehension
040
      abilities of LLMs, recent research has aimed to apply them to tasks related to graph structures (Wang
041
      et al., 2024). LLMs are increasingly being adopted for a variety of tasks that involve graph structures,
042   such as planning in robotics (Andreas, 2022), knowledge extraction using knowledge graphs (Shen
043   et al., 2020; Saxena et al., 2020), and multi-hop question answering (Creswell et al., 2022; Fang
044   et al., 2019). For instance, they have been used to guide agents through structured graph-based
045   environments (Huang et al., 2022). Building upon these applications, recent works by Sanford
046   et al. (2024), Perozzi et al. (2024), and Agarwal et al. (2020) have demonstrated that graph tasks
047   can be encoded into textual formats that allow pre-trained LLMs to solve them as out-of-domain
048   tasks. This innovative approach effectively transforms graph problems into a language that LLMs
049   can understand and process.
050   While LLMs are being expanded in many applications, they suffer from certain blind spots that
051   significantly affect their performance. In particular, how these models process information in their
052   context and retrieve relevant data to solve the task at hand remains an active area of research (Kad-
053   dour et al., 2023). Understanding these limitations is crucial for extending context length (Gemini
      et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023) and improving in-context learning (Zhou et al.,


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      2022; Wei et al., 2023; An et al., 2024). Recent works have shown that the performance of LLMs
055   depends on the location of information in their context. Primarily, Liu et al. (2023) introduces the
056   “lost-in-the-middle” phenomenon, where information placed in the middle of a prompt is less effec-
057   tively utilized by the model compared to information at the beginning or end, resulting in significant
058   performance degradation when the position of relevant information in the context changes.
059
      Unlike these previous works which mainly focus on shortcomings of LLMs in NLP tasks, e.g. lost-
060
      in-the-middle, we focus on the deficiency of these models in tasks beyond natural language process-
061   ing, specifically solving fundamental graph problems. This area is heavily under-explored and has
062   wide practical applications (Perozzi et al., 2024; Colon-Hernandez et al., 2021; Xie et al., 2023).
063   Since these tasks require understanding graph structure and relationship between objects, they pro-
064   vide us with great insights into model’s blind spots. Through our analysis, we provide insight that
065   LLMs not only have blind spots regarding where information exists in the context, but their per-
066   formance in solving complex tasks also depends on the relative position of information within the
067   context.
068   Particularly, we look into Common Connection and Similarity tasks, which are the main algorithms
069   used as the backbone of many applications such as molecular design (Tan et al., 2023; Xia et al.,
070   2023), social network analysis (Gao et al., 2024), and recommendation systems (Li et al., 2023).
071   For example, these tasks are the main algorithms in “user-user” and “user-item” recommendations
072   in large industry recommendation products (Xie et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2015; Wu et al., 2022).
073   These tasks not only require understanding of subgraph structures but also demand integration of
074   information and reasoning across subgraphs. We demonstrate that strong, publicly available LLMs
075
      universally degrade in performance as when relevant pieces of information are distant from each
      other. Our analysis shows that this effect is present even when one controls for the effects absolute
076
      position of the relevant information in the context. To summarize
077
078          • For tasks that require cross-subgraph information lookup, such as identifying common con-
079            nections or measuring similarity, model performance not only degrades due to the “lost-
080            in-the-middle” effect based on the absolute positions but is also affected by the relative
081            distance between pieces of information in the context—a phenomenon we term “lost-in-
082            distance”. The further apart the information is, the more the model’s performance deterio-
083            rates.
084          • We demonstrate these shortcomings across different graph encoding algorithms and various
085            publicly available LLMs such as Llama-3-8B, Llama-3-70B (Dubey et al., 2024) and GPT-
086            4 (Achiam et al., 2023) indicating a universal limitation in current architectures.
087
088   Our findings suggest that current LLMs have inherent limitations in processing contextual informa-
089
      tion that is not sequentially localized or is widely dispersed within the input. This has significant
      implications for their application in domains that require complex reasoning over structured data,
090
      such as graph analysis.

      1.1   N OTATIONS AND D EFINITIONS
094
      We define a graph G = (V, E), where V = {v1 , v2 , . . . , vn } and E represent the sets of nodes and
095   edges, respectively. If nodes vi and vj are directly connected, we denote the edge between them
096   as eij ∈ E. The neighbors of node vi are defined as N (vi ) = {vk ∈ V | eik ∈ E}. A subgraph
097   associated with node vi is defined as Gvi = ({vi } ∪ N (vi ), Evi ), where Evi = {eij | eij ∈ E, vj ∈
098   N (vi )}.
099
      We define the distance between a common node v within two subgraphs Gu and Gz as the number of
100
      tokens separating the two occurrences of node v in the context (i.e., the textual representation of the
101   subgraphs). The overall distance between relevant information for common connections between
102   the two subgraphs is defined as the median of all such distances computed for each common node.
103   Throughout the paper, we use p to indicate position and d to indicate distance.
104
      We use accuracy, as defined below, to measure the performance of an LLM model in solving a given

      task:

116   2.1   G RAPH E NCODING FOR LLM
117
      Representing graph-structured data as text is an important step in enabling LLMs to understand
118
      graph structures and provide accurate answers to questions. Encoding graphs as text involves repre-
119
      senting both nodes and edges. Different graph encodings can lead to varying performance of LLMs
120   in graph reasoning tasks (Agarwal et al., 2020; Fatemi et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024a). In this
121   work, we encode nodes as integers, where each node is represented by a unique integer, such that
122   vi ∈ {0, 1, . . . , n − 1}.
123
      We experiment with three encoding functions from Fatemi et al. (2024) to encode edges in the graph,
124
      investigating whether patterns are consistently observed across different encoding functions. More
125
      specifically, we consider the following edge encoding functions:
126
127          • Incident: Given a source node vi , the edge information for node vi is encoded as an
128            adjacency list in natural language. For example, “node vi is connected to nodes vj , vk ”.
129
             • Adjacency: Given a source node vi and a target node vj , the edge is encoded as (vi , vj ).
130
131          • Expert: Given a source node vi and a target node vj , the edge is encoded as vi → vj .
132
133
      Since the graph tasks considered in this paper only require access to the subgraph and the subgraph
      structure, we encode only the edge information for the nodes of interest. This is a common practice
134
      where a subgraph is extracted from a database before being processed by a compute engine (Shao
135
      et al., 2013). Figure 1 shows an example about only including a subgraph with three encoding
136   functions in the prompt. In this example, node 0 and node 1 are nodes of interest so we only encode
137   their subgraph in the prompt.

      2.3    G RAPH TASKS
163
164   We aim to analyze the performance of LLMs in three fundamental graph problems which require
165   models to have thorough understanding of the input graph structure.
166
167         1. Edge Existence: Given two nodes vi and vj sampled from a graph G, node vi and node vj
168            are directly connected if eij ∈ E. The edge existence task is to ask LLMs whether node vi
169
               and node vj are directly connected.
170         2. Common Connection: Given two nodes vi and vj sampled from a graph G, the common
171            connection between two nodes are N (vi ) ∩ N (vj ). For this task, we ask LLMs to find the
172            number of common connections between node vi and node vj , denoted as |N (vi )∩N (vj )|.
173         3. Similarity: Given three nodes vi , vj and vk sampled from a graph G, we let vj be the source
174            node and vi and vk be the target nodes. The task for LLMs is to compare the number of
175            common connections |N (vi ) ∩ N (vj )| and |N (vj ) ∩ N (vk )|.
176
      Note that these tasks are roughly ordered in terms of general complexity. For example, solving
177
      the edge existence only depends on the model being able to retrieve the edge information from the
178
      representation. One step further, in finding the number of common connections, models needs to
179   first identify the set of shared connections between two nodes and then calculate the size of that set.
180   Finally, the similarity task is more complex than the common connection task, as it requires LLMs
181   to consider three nodes and identify two sets of common connections and then compare their sizes.
182   As a result, these tasks are a good representative set to evaluate LLMs since they require LLMs to
183   both retrieve and reason about the graph information. Furthermore, these tasks are also essential and
184   the building blocks for solving practical problems in applications such as recommendation systems
185   (Ying et al., 2018), protein folding (Strokach et al., 2020), bad actor detection (Papegnies et al.,
186   2017) or any other task that requires graph understanding.
187
188   3     L OST- IN - THE - MIDDLE FOR E DGE E XISTENCE
189
190   The edge existence task is analogous to the needle-in-a-haystack problem (Ivgi et al., 2023) and the
191   document question-answering task (Liu et al., 2023), as it requires the LLM to retrieve the answer
192   from the prompt without performing any computation. Building upon prior work in the literature
193   by Liu et al. (2023), this study demonstrates the impact of the position of relevant information on
194   the performance of LLMs. Specifically, it is shown that the accuracy in the edge existence task
195   decreases when the information about the edge in question is placed in the middle of the prompt.
196   The prompt structure is constructed using the following procedure, which enables controlling the
197   location of information within the prompt:
198
199         1. Randomly sample two nodes from a graph along with their corresponding connections.
200         2. Randomly select nine additional nodes as noise nodes and incorporate their textual sub-
201            graph encodings into the prompt. This step is necessary to examine the impact of the
202            position of relevant information.
203         3. Group the subgraph structures of the two nodes of interest and position them at the begin-
204            ning, middle, or end of the input context.
205
            4. Query the model to determine whether an edge exists between the two nodes of interest.
206
207
      An example of a prompt with different positions for the two nodes of interest is illustrated in Fig-
208   ure 2.

261   4     L OST- IN -D ISTANCE
262
263   Tasks such as the edge existence require LLMs to perform needle-in-a-haystack retrieval, which, as
264   previously shown, suffers from the lost-in-the-middle phenomenon in long contexts. However, in
265   many tasks, the model not only needs to look up relevant information in the context but also requires
266
      to perform cross-referencing between retrieved information. For example, tasks like the common
      connection require the model to retrieve connections that jointly appear in both subgraphs.
267
268   We hypothesize that for tasks requiring cross-referenced retrieval, the model’s performance is also
269   impacted by the distance between relevant pieces of information, a phenomenon we term lost-in-
      distance. Specifically, for these tasks, the model’s performance is influenced by two compounding


                                                                            5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      phenomena: lost-in-the-middle when retrieving relevant information and lost-in-distance when per-
271   forming a join between retrieved information.
272
273   To explore this, we define G(p) as the model’s performance when the relevant information is at
274
      position p. Similarly, we define F (p1 , p2 ) as the model’s performance when the relevant information
      is at positions (p1 , p2 ). The value of F (p1 , p2 ) is estimated based on the accuracy of model in
275
      a complex task that requires cross-referencing. We hypothesize that F and G have the following
276
      relationship:
277
278
279                                    F (p1 , p2 ) = γ G(p1 ) G(p2 ) H(d),                               (2)
280
281   where d = |p2 − p1 | represents the distance between relevant information in the prompt and H(d)
282   represents the effect of lost-in-distance.
283   In the experimental section, by studying LLM performance on common connection and similarity
284   tasks, we first demonstrate that lost-in-the-middle alone cannot explain the model’s performance
285   degradation in solving tasks that require joint reasoning across multiple subgraphs, and that it is af-
286   fected by another factor, lost-in-distance. Then, by leveraging the experimental results, we measure
287   the goodness of fit for Equation 2 in Section 6.

      5     E XPERIMENTATION
290
291
      In our initial experiments, we focused on the common connection task. This task requires the model
292   to determine the number of common connections between two nodes by joining information across
293   two subgraphs. Our results demonstrate that the models’ performance degrades as the distance
294   between the relevant pieces of information in the two subgraphs increases. Specifically, when the
295   information about each node’s connections is placed further apart in the context, the models struggle
296   to effectively retrieve and integrate this information to compute the correct number of common
297   connections.
298   We then investigated how the lost-in-distance impacts tasks that require multiple cross-referencing
299   steps, such as the similarity task. In the similarity task, the model needs to first identify the common
300   connections between each of the two nodes and a reference node, and then compare these sets to
301   determine the degree of similarity. Our findings reveal that performance degradation is even more
302   pronounced in this case, as the task requires the model to perform multiple join operations over
303   dispersed pieces of information within the context.

359   5.2.1    L OST- IN -D ISTANCE IN C OMMON C ONNECTION TASK
360
361   The results presented in Figure 5 illustrate the impact of varying the positions of common edges
362   within each subgraph (following the methodology outlined in the previous section) on the model’s
363
      performance in the common connection task. Unlike the edge existence task, the model’s perfor-
      mance is influenced not only by the lost-in-the-middle phenomenon but also by the relative distance
364
      between common connections.
365
366   With the position of relevant information fixed in one subgraph, we observe that the model’s perfor-
367   mance degrades when the other subgraph is positioned closer to the middle of the prompt, influenced
368   by the lost-in-the-middle phenomenon. For example, in adjacency encodings (Figure 5, middle plot),
369
      when the first node’s common connection is at position 0 (the beginning of the prompt), the model’s
      performance deteriorates from 40% to 20% as the second node’s common connection shifts from
370
      position 5 (the end) to position 3 (the middle). However, in contrast to the lost-in-the-middle phe-
371
      nomenon, Figure 5 demonstrates that across all three graph encodings, the model achieves optimal
372   performance when relevant information is centrally located, with minimal distance between compo-
373   nents at positions (2, 3). This illustrates the effect of lost-in-distance. Furthermore, when the first
374   node’s common connection is at position 2, the model’s accuracy drops by up to 50% as the second
375   node’s common connection shifts from position 3 (the middle of the prompt) to position 5 (the end
376   of the prompt), thereby increasing the distance between relevant information. These observations
377   confirm that the lost-in-distance phenomenon and the lost-in-the-middle effect have independent,
      compounding effects on model performance, as hypothesized in Equation 2.

401
402    Solving the similarity between three nodes vi , vj , and vk requires the model to perform two common
403
       connection tasks, |N (vi ) ∩ N (vj )| and |N (vj ) ∩ N (vk )|, and subsequently compare the results. As
       a result, the model needs to execute two cross-referencing operations between the subgraphs: one
404
       between Gvi and Gvj , and the other between Gvj and Gvk . Therefore, as we will demonstrate in this
405
       section, solving the similarity task inherently suffers from the lost-in-distance phenomenon.
406
407    To measure the effect of the lost-in-distance phenomenon, we select three nodes—vi , vj , and
408    vk —from a given graph and randomly shuffle the edges within each node’s subgraph. We desig-
409
       nate vj as the source node for the similarity task, vi as the first target node, and vk as the second
       target node. In all scenarios, to mitigate the influence of the lost-in-the-middle effect and highlight
410
       the effect of lost-in-distance, the textual encoding of the source node vj ’s subgraph (Gvj ) is posi-
411
       tioned at the center of the prompt, while the subgraphs of the other two nodes are placed one before
412    and one after it.
413
414    We quantify the lost-in-distance effect by calculating the median distance, measured in terms of
415
       tokens, between the common connections of the two subgraphs, specifically |N (vi ) ∩ N (vj )| and
       |N (vj ) ∩ N (vk )|. The distance distribution is illustrated in Figure 6 for three different graph encod-
416
       ings. We utilize the thresholds presented in Table 1 to categorize the distances into small, medium,
417
       and large groups. Furthermore, in order to make sure more uniform coverage, we employ rejection
418    sampling to ensure that each distance group contains one hundred samples with balanced responses.
