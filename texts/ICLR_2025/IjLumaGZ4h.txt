                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011
               Stereo depth estimation using event cameras is a promising approach for real-
012            time vision tasks, offering low-latency, high-speed data capture. However, exist-
013            ing methods often suffer from high computational overhead, limiting their real-
014            time applicability. To address these challenges, we introduce ROSE (Reduced
015            Overhead Stereo Event and Intensity) a Real-Time framework for efficient depth
016            estimation from events and intensity images. Current approaches rely on dense
017            networks that fail to scale with increasing data complexity, constraining both ac-
018            curacy and speed. In contrast, ROSE incorporates lightweight event represen-
019            tation networks and optimizes the stereo matching process to reduce model size
020
               and computational load without compromising accuracy. We replace conventional
               network components with efficient spatio-temporal representations and stream-
021
               line adaptive aggregation modules, reducing computational complexity by 1000×
022
               compared to previous methods. Furthermore, we adapt event grouping strategies
023            to better align with intensity images, improving the quality of depth estimation un-
024            der various lighting and motion conditions. Extensive experiments on the DSEC
025            and MVSEC benchmarks demonstrate that ROSE achieves real-time performance,
026            boosting frame rates to 32.2 FPS on DSEC and 66.9 FPS on MVSEC while main-
027            taining competitive depth accuracy. This marks a significant improvement over
028            prior work in terms of speed and scalability, making ROSE a viable solution for
029            real-time stereo depth estimation in resource-constrained environments. Our code
030            and models will be released to support further advancements in the field.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Stereo depth estimation has long been a key challenge in computer vision, traditionally addressed
035
      through epipolar geometry and pixel matching across intensity image frames (RGB or grayscale)
036   (Mayer et al., 2016; Laga et al., 2020; Smolyanskiy et al., 2018; Poggi et al., 2022) have gained
037   prominence, with state-of-the-art (SoTA) approaches involving feature extraction, cost aggregation,
038   depth estimation, and refinement (Zhang et al., 2019; Xu & Zhang, 2020; Xu et al., 2023). Despite
039   these advancements (Laga et al., 2020), key challenges remain: (i) depth inaccuracies due to lighting
040   conditions (Jeon et al., 2016; Sharma & Cheong, 2018; Heo et al., 2013), (ii) motion blur from
041   conventional camera limitations (Xu & Jia, 2010), and (iii) high computational costs for processing
042   high-resolution intensity images.
043   Recent research has explored integrating event cameras to address these challenges (Gallego et al.,
044   2020; Zheng et al., 2023; de Queiroz Mendes et al., 2021; Jia et al., 2022; Xu & Zhang, 2020; Rancon
045   et al., 2022). While intensity cameras capture detailed scene information, they suffer from motion
046   blur, latency, and poor performance under challenging textures and lighting conditions (Mueggler
047   et al., 2014; Lagorce et al., 2015; Gallego et al., 2018). In contrast, event cameras (neuromor-
048   phic cameras) (Lichtsteiner et al., 2006; 2008) capture pixel-level changes asynchronously, offering
049   advantages like low latency and a wide dynamic range. These qualities make event cameras less sus-
050
      ceptible to motion blur and lighting distortions. However, their sparse, asynchronous data outputs
      pose considerable challenges for integration with intensity-based depth estimation methods.
051
052   Event cameras generate asynchronous, sparse event streams represented by pixel coordinates (x, y),
053   polarity, and timestamps. Due to the vast number of events, stacking event data is a common method
      for dense depth estimation (Mostafavi I. et al., 2020; 2021; Nam et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022).

095   To address these limitations, we propose ROSE (Reduced Overhead Stereo Event and Intensity
096   Network). It includes a novel event representation model in addition to further network elements
097   designed to achieve SoTA depth estimation with real-time performance. The core of ROSE is a
098   spiking neuron-based event representation learning that is robust to light artifacts and motion blur,
099   enabling it to outperform prior methods in both speed and efficiency.
100   Compared to SE-CFF (Nam et al., 2022), ROSE runs at over 3× faster at 32.2 FPS while requiring
101   64% fewer parameters. On MVSEC (Zhu et al., 2018), ROSE achieves 66.9 FPS, far surpassing the
102   8.3 FPS of SMC-Net (Cho & Yoon, 2022b) with 96% fewer parameters than EIT (Ahmed et al.,
103   2021), making it a highly efficient and desirable solution for real-time applications.

121   2.1     E VENT C AMERAS IN S TEREO D EPTH E STIMATION
122
      As introduced in Section 1, event cameras stream asynchronously in tuples relative to pixel locations,
123
      diverging from the conventional concept of a ‘frame’. This asynchronous streaming is challenging
124   in general for independent representation and also for alignment with frames from other modalities.
125   To tackle the aforementioned challenges, prior works attempt to align event data with conventional
126   deep learning methodologies, by processing it either in a stacked format (Wang et al., 2019) or
127   as discrete event volumes (Zhu et al., 2019). When combined with image data, these approaches
128   have demonstrated improved performance over the uni-modal image-only setting (Mostafavi et al.,
129   2021b; Nam et al., 2022; Cho & Yoon, 2022a;b; Gehrig et al., 2021a).
130   EI-Stereo (Mostafavi et al., 2021b) uses a Recycling Network that aims to reconstruct details of RGB
131   images and event stacks. SCSNet (Cho & Yoon, 2022a) uses cascaded Neighbor Cross Similarity
132   Feature modules to align event and image data. SE-CFF (Nam et al., 2022) proposes a Concentration
133   Network that learns to stack 10 event frames, where a frame is obtained by stacking millions of
134   events. However, a critical aspect often overlooked by prior methods is the effective utilization
135   of temporal information inherent to event data. Hence, unlike prior works, our work focuses on
136   learning representations that preserve the spatial and temporal information present in the event data.
137
138   2.2     I MPORTANCE OF E VENT-S TREAM S PLITTING
139
140   When learning event representations, we split the event data stream by a time interval of size “δt”
141
      for easier computations. This is because the event stream contains millions of data points which
      makes processing the individual events difficult, leading to out-of-memory errors. Selecting an
142
      optimal value of δt is crucial as it heavily governs the quality of the input event data. As presented
143
      in Figure 1, grouping excessive events (large δt), will lead to motion blur and not including enough
144   events (small δt), increases the risk of missing details and preventing effective learning. Chosing
145   the correct δt is challenging as the optimal value can be different for each dataset as it depends on
146   the properties of the event stream and the scene being captured.
147
      There have been efforts to circumvent picking an optimal value for δt. For instance, SE-CFF (Nam
148
      et al., 2022) propose “Concentration Network” that arbitrarily splits the event stream into 10 different
149
      groups of various δt values, irrespective of the dataset. While they achieve SoTA results on DSEC,
150   such arbitrary splitting leads to groups of non-uniform event densities, thus leading to some contain-
151   ing too many events and some containing too few events. On the other hand, EI-Stereo (Mostafavi
152   et al., 2021b) propose a “Recycling Network” (based on e2sri (Mostafavi I. et al., 2020; 2021)) that
153   uses 3 event groupings obtained by splitting the stream by very small δt time-intervals. But, these
154   groupings contain too little event information, thus requiring multiple cycle iterations in the Recy-
155   cling Network, leading to excessive computations at low FPS rates. The effects of performing such
156   arbitrary splitting can directly be observed in their learned event representations and predicted depth
157   maps, where they contain many depth inaccuracies due to motion blur and light artifacts. Samples
158
      are portrayed in Figure 4 and Figure 5 for reference.
159   We hypothesize that splitting the event stream by arbitrary δt values makes it harder to learn good
160   representations, worsening motion blur. Adding to that, such arbitrary values lead to vastly different
161   results depending on the dataset, since each dataset has highly different event streams. Hence, these
      challenges necessitate a systematic way of selecting an optimal event grouping strategy.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Building on top of these observations, we perform an in-depth analysis where the aim is to use a
163   fixed value for δt to group the event stream per dataset. Through our analysis, we find and propose an
164   optimal way to select δt for the most desirable stereo depth performances, outlined in Section 3.1.
165   We follow this up by proposing techniques that can considerably improve the efficiency and FPS
166   rates while achieving SoTA performances on the popular DSEC dataset (Gehrig et al., 2021b).

175   two properties of the event groupings: (i) standard deviations σ, and (ii) event densities ρ.
176   We first split the DSEC event stream by δt, where δt ∈ {2ms, 10ms, 25ms, 50ms}, and obtain
177   multiple different event groupings. This allows us to calculate each grouping’s σ value. We also

      3.2   L EARNING S PATIO -T EMPORAL E VENT R EPRESENTATIONS E FFICIENTLY
203
204   Convolutional layers use receptive fields which act as an inductive bias that helps in learning mean-
205   ingful patterns present in the neighboring pixels (Krizhevsky et al., 2012). The extent to which
206   neighboring pixels are utilized is dependent on the kernel size, and increasing this size leads to an
207   increase in parameters. SE-CFF’s Concentration Net (Nam et al., 2022) is computationally expen-
208   sive because it implements multiple convolutional layers of varying kernel sizes, making it expensive
209   and inefficient. EI-Stereo’s Recycling Network (Mostafavi et al., 2021b) suffers from this as well,
210   since the inputs and outputs and re-iterated multiple times, thus requiring even more computations.
211   To address this problem, we propose utilizing just two convolutional layers, each with a kernel of
212   size 1 × 1. To facilitate better temporal learning, we use the Leaky Integrate-and-Fire (LIF) spiking
213   neurons (Hunsberger & Eliasmith, 2015). Spiking neurons have shown great promise (Cordone
214   et al., 2021), as they naturally handle asynchronous data streams (Rathi et al., 2023). By using an
215   LIF neuron model. we can effectively capture the dynamics of asynchronous event data by modeling
      the membrane potential changes.

298   3.4       P RESERVING S PATIAL I NFORMATION WITH S KIP C ONNECTIONS

      Until now, we have emphasized the importance of learning the spatio-temporal information present
      in the event data. When both Intensity and Event data (i.e. multimodal) are used for the stereo depth
301
      estimation, there is enough spatio-temporal information to facilitate effective learning. On the other
302
      hand, using just unimodal Event data is more challenging due to the lack of spatial information,
303   which leads to inferior performance metrics.
304
305   To address this disparity in performance, we propose a simple yet effective solution to preserve
306
      the spatial information. We introduce skip connections between the event data and the event rep-
      resentation network’s output. This straightforward solution drastically improves results, leading
307
      to near-SoTA results with much higher FPS than prior works on DSEC in the event-only setting, as
308
      shown in Table 7. The results when skip connections are not used are reported in Appendix Table 14.

526   5     C ONCLUSION
527
528   We present ROSE and a novel spiking neuron-based event representation method for stereo depth
529   estimation of event and intensity cameras in real time. We leverage the low-latency nature of event
530   cameras and efficiently process large volumes of event data at high FPS. Our results show that ROSE
531   maintains competitive performance across various time intervals δt, confirming the importance of
532   preserving temporal information for real-time applications. Additionally, ROSE mitigates common
533
      issues associated with event stacking, learning robust event representations with fewer artifacts.
534
535   5.1      L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
536
537   While ROSE effectively captures temporal information in event data, as discussed in Section 4.3.1,
538   balancing the sparsity of events within T frames remains a key factor. Future work may explore
539   approaches to process events individually. However, the current limitations of computational tech-
      nology make real-time processing of each event at the camera’s temporal resolution a challenge.

705   A.1   D ETAILS ABOUT S TEREO M ATCHING N ETWORK
706
707
      The Feature Extractor extracts the features of the input and produces a list of layers. Each lay-
      ers’ height and width dimensions become progressively smaller while the channel dimension grows
708
      larger, resulting in a pyramid-like structure for the output.
709
710   The Cost Volume module constructs a cost volume pyramid by feature correlation and convolving
711   over the features.
712   The Adaptive Aggregation Network is based on Deformable Convolutional Layers (DC layers).
713   Their advantageous adaptive nature Dai et al. (2017) lead to better results. DC layers also help
714   overcome the implicit dependence on the neighbourhood pixels which is present in traditional con-
715   volutional layers.
716
      The Disparity Estimation Pyramid is the disparity estimates for each of the layers in the convolved
717   cost volume pyramid.
718
719   The Stereo Disparity Refinement Module is the final part of the stereo-matching network consists
720
      of upsampling layers that refine the lower-resolution disparity maps. For DSEC, there are two such
      layers, whereas there is just one for MVSEC. Post-refinement, the new layers are appended back to
721
      the original pyramid to create a K-layered disparity pyramid.

727   to reduce conflicts between them (Gadzicki et al., 2020). As such, there are 2 intuitive approaches
728   to combining them: (i) Input-level (i.e., early-fusion), and (ii) Feature-level (i.e., late-fusion). The
729   former refers to the concatenation of event representations with the images prior to feeding them to
730   the feature extractor module (FE). The latter refers to extracting features of each modality using its
731   own individual FE and subsequently concatenating them together. The resulting combined features
732
      are then utilized for dense depth prediction.

      A.3      D ETAILS ON THE O BJECTIVE F UNCTION .
757
758   The ground truth, y ∈ RH×W , is the disparity map between the left and right images, captured by
759   LiDAR (Gehrig et al., 2021b; Zhu et al., 2018). Given the multi modal inputs to our stereo matching
760   network, X L and X R , we obtain the K-layered pyramid output, f .
761
      Let n ∈ {1, 2, ..., K} be the indices of f ’s layers, where K is the last layer. Then, fn is the estimated
762   disparity of the nth layer. Prior to calculating the loss, fn is bi-linearly interpolated to be the same
763   size as y, and hence, fn ∈ RH×W .
764
765
      Therefore, the objective is to minimize the weighted sum of the Smooth L1 losses at each layer of f
      w.r.t. to the ground truth, y:
766                                                K
                                                  X
767                                          min     wn · L (y, fn )                                  (1)
768                                                               n=1
769
      where wn is the nth layer’s assigned weight. The Smooth L1 loss is preferred because it is less
770   sensitive to outliers and noise compared to L2 loss Barron (2019).

796   By focusing on the temporal information in the low-latency event data, RoSE is able to extract
797   meaningful representations. As a result, ROSE achieves SoTA results with real-time capabilities in

808   catenate the frames along with the learned representation, forming a (T + 1)-channel output. This
809   would be essentially similar to concatenated skip connections (Huang et al., 2016). We compare

      to as in StereoDRNet Chabra et al. (2019). Lastly, note that we perform experiments only until
