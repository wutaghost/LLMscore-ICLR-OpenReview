009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large language models (LLMs) can be improved by aligning with human pref-
012            erences through fine-tuning—the so-called reinforcement learning from human
013            feedback (RLHF). However, the cost of fine-tuning an LLM is prohibitive for many
014            users. Due to their ability to bypass LLM fine-tuning, prediction-time tokenwise
015            reward-guided text generation (RGTG) methods have recently been proposed. They
016            use a reward model trained on full sequences to score partial sequences during
017            decoding in a bid to steer the generation towards sequences with high rewards.
018
               However, these methods have so far been only heuristically motivated and poorly
               analyzed. In this work, we show that reward models trained on full sequences are
019
               not compatible with scoring partial sequences. To alleviate this issue, we propose
020
               to train a Bradley-Terry reward model on partial sequences explicitly, and autore-
021            gressively sample from the implied tokenwise policy during decoding time. We
022            study the properties of this reward model and the resulting policy: We show that
023            this policy is proportional to the ratio of two distinct RLHF policies. Our simple
024            approach outperforms previous RGTG methods and performs similarly to strong
025            offline baselines without large-scale LLM finetuning.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Large language models (LLMs) provide a modern foundation for most, if not all, text generation
031   tasks (Radford et al., 2019; Brown et al., 2020; Touvron et al., 2023a;b). In practice, significant
032   improvements in the quality of text generation are achieved by aligning LLMs to human preferences
033
      (Stiennon et al., 2020b; Ouyang et al., 2022). This is typically performed via reinforcement learning
      from human feedback (RLHF), which involves two steps: i) learning a reward model from preference
034
      data and ii) fine-tuning an LLM to maximize expected rewards by reinforcement learning (Ziegler
035
      et al., 2019b). Usually, this is done via a reinforcement learning algorithm such as proximal policy
036   optimization (PPO, Schulman et al., 2017). Nevertheless, recently, Rafailov et al. (2023) showed that
037   the reward modeling step (i) can be bypassed by directly fine-tuning an LLM with preference data,
038   resulting in a method called direct preference optimization (DPO). While this simplifies RLHF, the
039   fine-tuning step (ii) remains prohibitively costly for most users since it requires high-performance
040   computational resources with large GPUs.
041
      In order to alleviate the computational issue above, Khanov et al. (2024); Deng & Raffel (2023)
042   explored tokenwise reward-guided text generation (RGTG) techniques that avoid any fine-tuning of
043   the LLM. More precisely, the LLM remains frozen (i.e., not finetuned), and the reward model is used
044   at the decoding time to adjust the softmax scores of the tokens directly. Unlike DPO, this line of work
045   retains the reward modeling step, but training reward models are typically a much cheaper endeavor
046   than finetuning text-generation LLMs since smaller models can be utilized for reward modeling.
047   Furthermore, reward models are modular: they can easily be composed and reused without any cost
048   to guide text generation in conjunction with any base LLM. In contrast, RLHF via DPO requires
049   fine-tuning of every LLM that we wish to improve based on human preference data.
050   While RGTG is an interesting alternative to the standard offline RLHF, it is often based on heuristics
051   and still poorly analyzed. For instance, ARGS (Khanov et al., 2024) proposed to simply use a
052   reward model trained on full sequences to score each partial sequence during autoregressive decoding.
053   Meanwhile, Deng & Raffel (2023) used a custom tokenwise loss to distill a reward model trained
      on full sequences. Thus, it is unclear if these approaches can give rise to a sound tokenwise text

054
      generation policy. Controlled decoding (CD; Mudgal et al., 2024), on the other hand, uses rollouts
055   from the base model along with a reward model trained on full sequences to distill the partial reward.
056
057   In this work, we analyze this common RGTG approach. First, we show that the usage of full-sequence
058
      reward models for scoring partial sequences in a tokenwise policy is pathological. To alleviate this,
      we propose to explicitly train a Bradley-Terry reward model on partial sequences. We prove that
059
      this text generation policy is a ratio of two different RLHF policies trained on sequences of different
060
      lengths. Ideally, the policy would be derived from a single RLHF policy, but as we shall also show in
061   Section 3, the exact computation of such a policy is intractable. By deriving the policy from a ratio of
062   distinct RLHF policies, we obtain a tractable sampling procedure. This is akin to the argument of
063   Zhao et al. (2024) where they use a similar ratio but for deriving a sequential Monte Carlo method.
064
      We empirically validate our analysis on three different text generation datasets on two recent LLMs.
065
      Evidence shows that our RGTG approach achieves better performance compared to ARGS and CD,
066
      matching the performance of the more expensive, offline PPO and DPO baselines. In summary:
067
068     (i) We analyze the recent practice of using full-sequence reward models for guiding the LLM
069         decoding process. In particular, we show a deficiency in this approach.
070    (ii) We thus propose to explicitly train a Bradley-Terry reward model on partial sequences and
071         sample from the induced per-token policy induced by it during the decoding time.
072   (iii) We show that this reward model induces a ratio of two distinct RLHF policies over sequences
073         with different lengths. This is a tradeoff that one must make to make tokenwise RGTG free of
074
            the pathology and yet still tractable.
075   (iv) Extensive experiments with recent LLMs and various text generation tasks validate our insights.

085   LLMs generally consist of probabilistic models that can generate a response y given a prompt x.
086   More specifically, the generation of y is done token-by-token by sampling the next token from a

131

132

133

134

137   softmax distribution of the scores that has a similar form to the RLHF policy in (3):

144

307   Language model alignment Simple fine-tuning and instruction tuning (Wei et al., 2021) are ways
308   to align LLMs to labeled data. Recently, RLHF methods (Christiano et al., 2017; Ziegler et al.,
309
      2019a; Lee et al., 2021; Nakano et al., 2021; Snell et al., 2022) have provided a direct method to
      align LLMs to human preferences. The PPO algorithm has specially been popular and has shown
310
      promising results for a range of tasks (Askell et al., 2021; Bai et al., 2022; Ouyang et al., 2022).
311
      However, training RL models is compute intensive and researchers have turned their attention to
312   supervised fine-tuning methods that can learn directly from preference data. Liu et al. (2023a) turns
313   the preference data into prompts with which they fine-tune the LLM. Dong et al. (2023) uses the
314   reward model to filter the training set to better fine-tune the model. DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023;
315   2024) models the LLM itself as a Bradley-Terry model and optimizes the RLHF objective without
316   any need for RL. TDPO (Zeng et al., 2024) incorporates token-level KL divergence into the DPO
317   objective to improve content diversity. These methods, however, still fine-tune the base LLM, which

366   train an explicit reward model but instead introduce a ranking function which can rank the next token

369   5     E XPERIMENTS
370
371   We evaluate our proposed approach, which we call Partial Alignment as Reward-Guided Sampling
372   (PARGS)—in contrast to ARGS which considers full sequences and greedy decoding instead of
373   sampling—on two language generation tasks: summarization and dialogue generation.
374
375   5.1    S ETUP
376
377   Summarization task We use the Reddit TL;DR dataset (Völske et al., 2017), where, the context
      x is a post on the Reddit forum and y is the summary of the post. We use the human preference

378
      dataset from Stiennon et al. (2020a) to train the reward model and the relevant baselines. Our base
379   summarization model is GPT2-large, fine-tuned on the TL;DR training set. We use a pretrained
380   reward model based on the DeBerta-v3-large architecture and train it with partial sequences for an
381   additional epoch. Our baselines include top-k sampling (Fan et al., 2018), Best-of-N generation,
382   which involves sampling N sequences from reference LLM (N = 10 for all our experiments) and
383   returning the best one according to the reward model, RLHF models based on PPO (Schulman et al.,
384   2017) and DPO (Rafailov et al., 2023), the reward-base decoding method ARGS (Khanov et al.,
385   2024) and controlled decoding (CD; Mudgal et al., 2024). We use CD-Fudge as the baseline in all our
386   CD experiments, noting that its performance is similar to CD-Q (see Table 4 in Mudgal et al. (2024)).
387   Dialogue task Next, we evaluate our model on single-turn dialogue using the Anthropic Helpful
388   and Harmless (HH; Bai et al., 2022) dataset. The goal is to generate a helpful and harmless response
389   to a general purpose query. Each sample provides a prompt x and two responses y with a label
390   indicating the preferred response. We use Llama-2-7b as the base model and DeBerta-v3 as the
391   reward model which is about 20× smaller. All details are in Appendix B.
392   Fine-grained text generation task We also evaluate our model on text generation using the
393   UltraFeedback dataset (Ganqu Cui et al., 2024). We use Zephyr-7B as the base LLM and Phi-1.5 (1.3
394   billion parameters) as the reward model.
395
      Machine translation task We perform additional experiments on machine-translation on the
396
      IWSLT-2017 dataset (Cettolo et al., 2017). We used the post-edit dataset from Kreutzer et al. (2020)
397   on the IWSLT-2017 English-German dataset to provide token-wise reward signals. We use Gemma-2b
398   as both the base model and the reward model. The evaluation is based on the standard BLUE score.
399
400
      Evaluation Following Khanov et al. (2024), we compare all methods based on average reward on
      the test samples as measured by the reward model. We use a different full-sequence reward model
401
      and not the partial-sequence reward model (that we trained for our algorithm) to evaluate the models.
402
      Since evaluating language generation, especially unconditionally, is nuanced and human evaluation
403   is very expensive, we use GPT-4-based evaluation, which has been shown to align with human
404   assessment (Zheng et al., 2023; Rafailov et al., 2023). Following Chiang et al. (2023) we construct
405   prompts for the two tasks and ask GPT-4 to score and rank response pairs. We randomly shuffle the
406   order of the responses to mitigate position bias (Zheng et al., 2023). Finally, we use the Rouge-L
407   score (Lin, 2004) and the BLEU score to evaluate the dialogue and translation tasks, respectively.

      6   C ONCLUSION
487
488
      We have discussed the pitfalls in tokenwise, decoding-time reward-guided text generation (RGTG)
489   with reward models trained on full sequences. These pitfalls can lead to inadequate reward during
490   the autoregressive decoding process and may lead to subpar performance. To alleviate this issue, we
491   proposed to train reward models on partial sequences and then sample from the implied per-token text
492   generation policy during decoding. We proved that this policy is a ratio of two distinct reinforcement
493   learning from human feedback (RLHF) policies. This means that this policy is not equivalent to
494   the standard offline RLHF methods. However, we have also shown that it is intractable to obtain
495   a tokenwise policy that is equivalent to a single RLHF policy. Training a partial-sequence reward
496   model can thus be seen as a tradeoff between avoiding the pitfall of using full-sequence reward model
497
      in RGTG and tractability. Our experiment results validated our approach: it performs better than a
      recent RGTG methods, ARGS, that leverages full-sequence reward models and CD.

      Theorem 1. A reward model r trained to minimize the Bradley-Terry loss (1) on full sequences
705   y1:|y| may assign arbitrary rewards to partial sequences y1:i (where i < |y|). More precisely,
706   r(y1:i |x) = vx,y1:i where vx,y1:i ∈ R can be any value.
707
708   Proof. Let r(y i |x, y1:i ) be the reward associated with token y i in the context of x, y1:i . Then
709   token-level and (partial) sequence-level rewards are related by the following identity:
710                                         Pi
711                            r(y1:i |x) = j=1 r(y j |x, y1:j−1 )    for all x, y, i                   (8)
712
      Optimizing a reward model with full-sequence preference data yields specific values for r(y1:|y| |x).
713   Since partial sequence rewards are not directly optimized, it is not clear what values they may
714   converge to. The above system of linear equations can be used to infer partial sequence rewards from
715   full sequence rewards. However the system is underdetermined since there are more variables than
716   equations: there is one equation for every combination of x, y, and i, while there is one variable per
717   combination of x, y, and i on the left-hand side of each equation and many more variables on the
718   right-hand side. Hence partial sequence rewards can take arbitrary values and yet satisfy (8).
