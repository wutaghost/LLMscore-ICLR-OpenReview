011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Probabilistic forecasting models for joint distributions of targets in irregular time
014            series are a heavily under-researched area in machine learning with, to the best of
015            our knowledge, only three models researched so far: GPR, the Gaussian Process
016
               Regression model (Dürichen et al., 2015), TACTiS, the Transformer-Attentional
               Copulas for Time Series (Drouin et al., 2022; Ashok et al., 2024) and ProFITi
017
               (Yalavarthi et al., 2024b), a multivariate normalizing flow model based on invertible
018
               attention layers. While ProFITi, thanks to using multivariate normalizing flows,
019            is the more expressive model with a better predictive performance, we will show
020            that it suffers from marginalization inconsistency: it does not guarantee that the
021            marginal distributions of a subset of variables in its predictive distributions coincide
022            with the directly predicted distributions of these variables. Also, TACTiS does not
023            provide any guarantees for marginalization consistency.
024            We develop a novel probabilistic irregular time series forecasting model, Marginal-
025            ization Consistent Mixtures of Separable Flows (moses), that mixes several nor-
026            malizing flows with (i) Gaussian Processes with full covariance matrix as source
027            distributions and (ii) a separable invertible transformation, aiming to combine
028            the expressivity of normalizing flows with the marginalization consistency of
029
               Gaussians. In experiments on four different datasets we show that moses outper-
               form other state-of-the-art marginalization consistent models, perform on par with
030
               ProFITi, but different from ProFITi, guarantees marginalization consistency.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      In many real-world domains ranging from health to astronomy time-variant data is measured in an
036   irregular fashion: different channels are measured at different times and usually not on a regular grid.
037   Besides mere point estimates, i.e., just the expected target values for some channels at some future
038   time points, one is usually interested in a probabilistic/distributional forecast of a distribution of
039   target values. A full predicted distribution of target values provides way more information about
040   the targets. For example, when bioengineers are growing bacteria in tanks they are interested in
041   predicting the oxygen levels. The expected value is already interesting, but if additionally we can
042   predict more fine-grained the whole distribution of possible oxygen levels this allows them now to
043   quantify the risk that the oxygen level falls below some critical threshold and all bacteria die; the
044
      expected value alone is not sufficient to do so. Consequently, in recent years several models have
      been developed for probabilistic forecasting of irregular time series (De Brouwer et al., 2019; Deng
045
      et al., 2020; Biloš et al., 2021; Schirmer et al., 2022).
046
047   However, all these models are limited to forecasting the distribution of the value of a single channel
048   at a single time. But decision makers often require information about several variables and their
049   interaction, that is, about the predicted joint distribution of several channels at several times. In our
050
      initial example, the bioengineers will be interested not just in the forecasts of the oxygen levels and
      the biomass, but also in their interaction: a risk for either low biomass or low oxygen levels might be
051
      tolerable, but a risk that both occur at the same time will require their intervention.
052
053   Probabilistic forecasting models for joint distributions of targets in irregular time series are a heavily
      under-researched area in machine learning with, to the best of our knowledge, only three models

      researched so far: GPR, the Gaussian Process Regression model (Dürichen et al., 2015) uses a
055   multivariate normal distribution as predictive distribution, TACTiS, the Transformer-Attentional
056   Copulas for Time Series (Drouin et al., 2022; Ashok et al., 2024) uses a copula with normalizing
057   flows for the marginal distributions, and ProFITi (Yalavarthi et al., 2024b) uses a multivariate
058   normalizing flow model based on invertible attention layers. ProFITi, thanks to using normalizing
059   flows, is able to learn way more expressive predictive distributions, for example, it could express
060   multi-modal predictive distributions, while GPR being limited to multivariate Gaussians cannot do
061   that. Consequently, it outperforms GPR by a wide margin in experiments.
062   Models for probabilistic forecasting of joint target distributions in irregular time series have to be able
063   to express distributions over a varying number of variables, as by the nature of irregular time series,
064   the number of observations that fall into a given span of time might vary. However, the capability
065   to provide predictive distributions for a varying number of observations/variables introduces a new
066   issue: to get the predictive joint distribution for values of channels at some time points, we can query
067   the model multiple ways: (i) we can just ask for the predictive distribution of those variables directly
068   or (ii) we can ask for the joint predictive distribution of all variables, and then marginalize out the
069
      variables that were not queried. Using a consistent model we expect to get the same answer either
      way — we call this property marginalization consistency. An important special case is that the
070
      univariate marginals of the models’ joint predictive distributions agree with the univariate predictive
071
      distributions it provides when directly queried.
072
073   We show that the currently best performing probabilistic forecasting model for joint target distributions
074   in irregular time series, ProFITi, neither provides any guarantees to be marginalization consistent nor
075
      empirically happens to be marginalization consistent on the standard datasets used in experiments.
      Its main competitor, TACTiS, does not provide guarantees for marginalization consistency either. In
076
      contrast, while GPR is marginalization consistent, it shows worse predictive performance.
077
078   From this starting point we will construct a novel model that combines the ideas of Gaussian
079   Processes and normalizing flows in a way completely different from ProFITi and GPR, to achieve
080   both, guaranteed marginalization consistency and high predictive accuracy (see Figure 4).
081   Overall our contributions as follows:
082
083           1. We propose a measure for the degree of marginalization consistency of models for joint
084              distributions with varying size, the Wasserstein Distance between the (possibly numerically)
085              marginalized predicted joint distribution of several variables and the directly predicted
086
                 marginal distribution (Section 6);
087           2. We show that the currently best performing model for probabilistic irregular time series
088              forecasting, ProFITi, does not provide any guarantees for marginalization consistency, and in
089              experiments we show furthermore that it actually suffers from marginalization inconsistency
090
                 (Section 7);
091           3. We develop a novel probabilistic irregular time series forecasting model, Marginalization

101              of Separable Flows outperform other state-of-the-art marginalization consistent models,

      We make use of the triplet representation of an irregular time series for probabilistic forecasting (Horn
111
      et al., 2020). Here, an irregularly sampled time series X is a sequence of N -many triplets:
112
113                  X ∶= ((tnOBS , cnOBS , vnOBS ))n=1∶N ∈ Seq(X )       X = R × {1, . . . , C} × R           (1)
114
115   where tnOBS ∈ R is the observation time point, and vnOBS ∈ R is the observed value in channel
116   cnOBS ∈ {1, . . . , C}. A time series query Q is a sequence of K-many pairs:
117
                           Q ∶= ((tkQRY , ckQRY ))k=1∶K ∈ Seq(Q)          Q = R × {1, . . . , C}               (2)
118
119   where tkQRY ∈ R is the future time point and ckQRY ∈ {1, . . . , C} is the queried channel. A forecasting
120   answer y is a sequence of scalars: y = (y1 , . . . , yK ), where yk is the forecasted value in channel ckQRY
121   at time tkQRY . Here, Seq(X ) denotes the space of finite sequences over X .
122
123   For forecasting, all the query time points are after the observations: mink=1∶K tkQRY > maxn=1∶N tnOBS .
124   The task of probabilistic irregular time series forecasting is to find a model p̂ that can predict the joint
125
      multivariate distribution p̂(y ∣ Q, X) of the answers y, given the query points Q and observed series
      X. Both the context N = ∣X∣ and the query length K = ∣Q∣ are allowed to be dynamic (R1).
126
127   As the time stamp and channel-ID are included in each sample, the order of the samples does not
128   matter, and hence any model prediction should be independent of it (R2). Moreover, for a subquery
129   Q′ of Q, there are two different ways to predict the joint distribution: either by marginalizing the
130   predicted distribution of the complete query Q, or by predicting the joint distribution of the subquery
131
      Q′ directly. The two predictions should be equivalent (R3).
132
133   Requirements. A marginalization consistent probabilistic irregularly sampled time series forecast-
134
      ing model must satisfy the following requirements:
135
      R1 Joint Multivariate Prediction.
136      The model p̂ can predict the joint distribution across multiple time steps of a multivariate time
137      series for arbitrary sizes of both the query K = ∣Q∣ and context N = ∣X∣.

178   3    R ELATED W ORK
179
180   There have been multiple works that deal with point forecasting of irregular time series (Ansari
181   et al., 2023; Che et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2024; Yalavarthi et al., 2024a). In this work we deal with
182   probabilistic forecasting of irregular time series. Models such as NeuralFlows (Biloš et al., 2021),
183   GRU-ODE (De Brouwer et al., 2019), and CRU (Schirmer et al., 2022) predict only the marginal
184   distribution for a single time stamp. Additionally, interpolation models like HetVAE (Shukla & Marlin,
185   2022) and Tripletformer (Yalavarthi et al., 2023) can also be applied for probabilistic forecasting.
186   However, they also produce only marginal distributions. All the above models assume underlying
187
      distribution is Gaussian which is not the case for lots of real-world datasets. On the other hand,
      Gaussian Process Regression (Dürichen et al., 2015), TACTiS/TACTiS-2, the Transformer-Attentional
188
      Copulas for Time Series (Drouin et al., 2022; Ashok et al., 2024) and ProFITi (Yalavarthi et al.,
189
      2024b) can predict proper joint distributions. TACTiS is a copula model and ProFITi a conditional
190   normalizing flow model, both can predict any arbitrary distribution. But as both use self-attention on
191   the queries in their encoders, their parametrization of their copula and flow, respectively, depends on
192   all queries in a complex way, and thus they cannot provide any marginalization guarantees.
193
      There have been works on models for tractable and consistent marginals for fixed number of variables
194
      such as tabular data. Probabilistic Circuits (Choi et al., 2020) create a sum-prod network on the
195
      marginal distributions in such a way that marginals are tractable and consistent. Later, univariate
196   normalizing flows were added to the leaf nodes of the circuit for better expressivity by Sidheekh
197   et al. (2023). However, it is not trivial to extend such circuits to deal with sequential data of variable
198   size. Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) (Duda & Hart, 1974) are often used only for unconditional
199   density estimation, but can be extended to conditional density estimation. They can provide tractable
200   and consistent marginal distributions. However, GMMs are not expressive enough and often require a
201   very large number of components to approximate even simple distributions, as shown in Figure 1.
202   Note that normalizing flow models such as (Dinh et al., 2017; Papamakarios et al., 2017; 2021)
203   neither provide tractable marginals nor are applicable to varying number of variables.
204   Existing works have explored mixtures of normalizing flows for fixed-length sequences. For ex-
205   ample, Pires & Figueiredo (2020) and Ciobanu (2021) used flows with affine coupling or masked
206   autoregressive transformations for density estimation, while Postels et al. (2021) applied them to
207   reconstruction tasks. However, these models cannot handle dynamic sequence lengths, and their
208   marginals are intractable.

227   Gaussian. To capture more complex distributions, more expressive models are required. Normalizing
228   flows are a popular choice for this task (Rezende & Mohamed, 2015; Papamakarios et al., 2021).

      Existing approaches to normalizing flows use very simple source distributions, typically a multivariate
237
      standard normal pZ (z) ∶= N (z ∣ 0, I), and model interactions between variables by means of the
238   transformation (Rezende & Mohamed, 2015; Papamakarios et al., 2021). Current approaches for
239   conditional normalizing flows for a variadic number of variables followed the same approach and
240   tackled the problem by engineering expressive transformations between vectors of same size, for any
241   size (Liu et al., 2019; Biloš & Günnemann, 2021; Yalavarthi et al., 2024b). For example, ProFITi
242   uses an invertible attention mechanism.
243
      All these models in general will not have a guarantee for marginalization consistency. To the best of
244   our knowledge, there is no simple condition on the transform that would provide such a guarantee.
245
246   We therefore propose a drastic change, reversing the standard approach for normalizing flows: to
247
      combine (i) simple, separable transforms with (ii) a richer source distribution, namely a Gaussian
      Process with full covariance matrix. This way interactions between variables cannot be represented by
248
      the transformation anymore, but they can be represented by the covariance of the source distribution.
249

466   Evaluation metric for multivariate distribution. We use normalized joint negative log-likelihood
467   (njNLL) (Yalavarthi et al., 2024b) as the evaluation metric (15). njNLL generalizes the joint neg-

469   probabilistic univariate forecasting, cannot be applied for multivariate distributions. Also, sampling
470   based metrics for multivariate distributions like Energy Score not only suffers from the curse of
471   dimensionality but also cannot evaluate the forecasts properly (Marcotte et al., 2023). On the other
472
      hand, another metric CRPS-sum (Rasul et al., 2021) was shown to provide misleading indication of
      the performance (Koochali et al., 2022). In their study CRPS-sum cannot distinguish the predictions
473
      of a state-of-the-art model from random noise. Hence, we evaluate using njNLL in our experiments.

513   C ONCLUSIONS
514
515   In this work, we propose, moses: marginalization consistent mixture of separable flows, a novel
516   model for probabilistic forecasting of irregular time series. We showed how to parametrize various
517   components of moses such that it is decomposable and marginalization consistent. Our experimental
518   results on 4 real-world irregularly sampled time series datasets show that moses not only performs
519   similar to state-of-the-art ProFITi model but unlike ProFITi moses is marginalization consistent.
