009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Equivalent Representations (ERs) of code are textual representations that preserve
012            the same semantics as the code itself, e.g., natural language comments and pseu-
013            docode. ERs play a critical role in software development and maintenance. How-
014            ever, how to automatically generate ERs of code remains an open challenge. In
015            this paper, we propose a self-reflection approach to generating ERs of code. It
016            enables two Large Language Models (LLMs) to work mutually and produce an
017            ER through a reflection process. Depending on whether constraints on ERs are
018
               applied, our approach generates ERs in both open and constrained settings. We
               conduct a empirical study to generate ERs in two settings and obtain eight find-
019
               ings. ❶ Generating ERs in the open setting. In the open setting, we allow LLMs
020
               to represent code without any constraints, analyzing the resulting ERs and uncov-
021            ering five key findings. These findings shed light on how LLMs comprehend
022            syntactic structures, APIs, and numerical computations in code. ❷ Generating
023            ERs in the constrained setting. In the constrained setting, we impose constraints
024            on ERs, such as natural language comments, pseudocode, and flowcharts. This
025            allows our approach to address a range of software engineering tasks. Based on
026            our experiments, we have three findings demonstrating that our approach can ef-
027            fectively generate ERs that adhere to specific constraints, thus supporting various
028            software engineering tasks. ❸ Future directions. We also discuss potential fu-
029
               ture research directions, such as deriving intermediate languages for code gener-
               ation, exploring LLM-friendly requirement descriptions, and further supporting
030
               software engineering tasks. We believe that this paper will spark discussions in
031
               research communities and inspire many follow-up studies. The source code and
032            data are available in (Anonymous, 2024).

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   Given a code snippet, its Equivalent Representations (ERs) are textual representations that pre-
038   serve the same semantics as the code. In this paper, ERs include natural language comments,
039   pseudocode, flowcharts, and more. For instance, natural language comments and pseudocode aid
040   developers in efficiently understanding the code and reducing communication barriers (Geng et al.,
041   2024; Li et al., 2021), while flowcharts visualize the control flow and data flow, facilitating code
042   optimization. These ERs play a crucial role in the software development and maintenance process.
043   Despite their significance, the automatic generation of ERs remains an open challenge.
044   In this paper, we propose a self-reflection approach to generating ERs of code. Our approach is built
045   upon a dual framework consisting of two Large Language Models (LLMs). Given an input code,
046   two LLMs work mutually and produce an ER through a reflection process. More implementation
047   details of our approach are in Section 2. Depending on whether adding constraints on ERs, our
048   approach generates ERs in both open and constrained settings. In the open setting, the form of
049   the ERs is unrestricted, allowing the LLMs to freely use their preferred representations of code. In
050
      the constrained setting, we impose specific formats on the ERs (e.g., natural language). Using this
      approach, we conduct a large-scale empirical study to generate ERs under two settings, leading to
051
      eight key findings.
052
053   Generating ERs in the open setting. In this setting, LLMs are free to generate ERs without any
      constraints. As noted, language is the tool of thought (Mercer, 2000). We believe that the way

054
      LLMs represent code reflects how they comprehend it. Therefore, we analyze the generated ERs
055   and summarize four key findings: ❶ LLMs generate diverse forms of ERs in the open setting,
056   including structured ERs (e.g., dictionaries and tables), natural language ERs (e.g., comments and
057   pseudocode), and symbolic ERs (e.g., arithmetic expressions). ❷ From the structured ERs, we find
058   that LLMs treat the code as a structured sequence instead of plain text. LLMs even can produce
059   a plausible syntax tree. ❸ Based on the natural language ERs, we discover that LLMs can reason
060   about the functionality of APIs based on the code text, generating natural language text to explain
061   the APIs. ❹ Through the symbolic ERs, we find that LLMs use mathematical symbols to represent
062   the numerical calculations in code, converting the code into arithmetic expressions. ❺ For different
063
      types of code, LLMs flexibly generate different types of ERs. For example, LLMs often generate
      SQL-style ERs when the code involves searching for elements in a list. Details of these findings and
064
      real cases are in Section 3.1. These findings offer insights into how LLMs understand various types
065
      of code, which in turn helps us explore how to better formulate requirements that align with the way
066   LLMs perceive and generate different types of code.
067
068   Generating ERs in the constrained setting. By applying constraints to ERs, our approach can
069
      support a wide range of software engineering tasks. In our experiments, we impose specific con-
      straints on ERs, including natural language comments, pseudocode, and flowcharts. Based on the
070
      experimental results, we obtain three findings. ❻ Our approach effectively generates ERs tailored
071
      to software engineering tasks and significantly reduces hallucinations produced by LLMs. ❼ The
072   generated comments provide clear explanations of the code’s purpose and implementation, help-
073   ing developers quickly grasp the code’s functionality. ❽ The generated pseudocode and flowcharts
074   clearly illustrate the control flow and data flow within the code, aiding developers in optimizing the
075   code. Details of these findings and real cases are in Section 3.2. Looking ahead, researchers can
076   utilize our approach to address various software engineering tasks by applying different constraints.
077
      Finally, we discuss the future directions of our work.
078
079   Deriving an Intermediate Language for Code Generation. Directly generating source code from
080   natural language requirements is a challenging task. An effective enhancement strategy is to intro-
081
      duce an intermediate language as a bridge, guiding LLMs to first generate the intermediate language
      and then the final code (Li et al., 2024; Paul et al., 2024). Our proposed approach can be used to
082
      automatically derive an intermediate language. Specifically, we use the approach to obtain an ER
083
      that lies between natural language and code, with constraints to ensure it adheres to specific syntax
084   rules. For example, we can generate a table that follows a specific pattern. LLMs can first generate
085   this table to clearly represent the entities and relationships in the requirements, and then proceed to
086   generate the code.
087
      Exploring LLM-friendly Requirement Descriptions. Currently, natural language text is primarily
088
      used to describe requirements in code generation. However, our findings suggest that in an open
089
      setting, LLMs use various forms to represent code. This leads us to consider whether we can express
090   requirements from the perspective of LLMs to facilitate the generation of different types of code.
091   For instance, when a requirement involves extensive numerical calculations, we can directly use
092   mathematical expressions instead of natural language text to describe the requirement. We refer to
093   this approach as “LLM-friendly requirement descriptions.” We believe that such descriptions can
094   unlock LLMs’ reasoning capabilities and improve their accuracy in code generation.
095
      Supporting Software Engineering Tasks. There are many software engineering tasks to generate
096   code-related artifacts, such as code comments and pseudocode. Previous work has primarily fo-
097   cused on specific tasks (Li et al., 2021; Geng et al., 2024). We provide a general and unsupervised
098   approach. On one hand, by adding different constraints, our approach can handle different tasks. On
099   the other hand, it does not require labeled data, making it applicable to tasks where data is scarce.

      2.1   A N OVERVIEW OF OUR APPROACH
123
124
      Figure 1 shows an overview of our approach. It employs a dual framework consisting of two
125   LLMs, i.e., MCR and MRC . Given an input code c, it produces an ER through multiple trials. In
126   each trial, MCR generates a plausible ER based on the code, and MRC generates a new code snippet
127   ĉ based on the ER. The similarity between c and ĉ serves as an indicator of how much of the original
128   code’s semantics is preserved in the ER. This similarity acts as a supervisory signal to optimize the
129   approach. Besides, our approach allows for external constraints on ERs, which are incorporated into
130   the supervisory signal to guide the process further.
131   Because LLMs typically are large-scale or black-box, it is heavy and hard to train them further. To
132   address this problem, we follow the Reflexion framework (Shinn et al., 2023) and design a self-
133   reflection algorithm to optimize our approach. Specifically, we convert the numerical supervisory
134   signals (i.e., the similarity between c and ĉ, external constraints) into natural language feedback.
135   The feedback acts as a ‘semantic’ signal by providing a concrete direction to improve representa-
136   tions. In subsequent trials, the ERs and feedback from previous trials are incorporated as additional
137   context for MCR , allowing it to learn from prior mistakes and generate more accurate representa-
138   tions. This mirrors the way humans reflect on past failures to improve their future performance.
139
140   2.2   D UAL F RAMEWORK
141
142   As shown in Figure 1, our approach employs a dual framework consisting of two LLMs, i.e., MCR
143   and MRC . Next, we provide a detailed description of two LLMs.
144
      MCR is built upon an LLM capable of following human instructions. Given an input code, we
145
      instruct it to transform the code into an ER. The instruction for MCR is shown in Figure 3. If
146   constraints are imposed on the ERs, these constraints are explicitly described within the instruction.
147   As stated in Section 2.1, we expect our approach to learn lessons from past trials. To achieve
148   this goal, we integrate a memory module with MCR . The memory stores the outputs of the dual
149   framework in past trials. The memory is added as additional context for MCR in the next trial,
150   helping it generate better ERs.
151
      MRC is also an LLM that can follow human instruction. We instruct it to generate a new code
152   snippet based on the ER produced by MCR . The instruction for MRC is shown in Figure 4.

187   ❶ Semantic-equivalent Score. A semantic-equivalent representation can be seamlessly transformed
188   into the original code. Thus, we measure the similarity between the original code c and reconstructed
189   code ĉ. This similarity reflects how much of the original code’s semantics is preserved in the ER.
190
      How to compute the code similarity remains an open question. Our motivation is that the code
191   not only can be tokenized into a token sequence but also be parsed as a syntax tree. The simi-
192   larity between code snippets may show in different modalities. As an initial work, we propose a
193   hybrid similarity metric, which combines text similarity Simtext and syntax similarity Simsyntax .
194   Simtext measures the rate of overlapping n-grams between two code snippets. Simsyntax ignores
195   text surface and computes the rate of overlapping syntax trees between two code snippets. The
196   computations of two similarity metrics are shown in Section A.2.
197   Finally, we compute the semantic-equivalent score as a weighted sum of Simtext and Simsyntax :
198
199
                         Scoresemantic = a ∗ Simtext (co , cr ) + b ∗ Simsyntax (co , cr )               (1)
200
201
      where a and b are hyper-parameters. We empirically set them to 0.5 and 0.5.
202
203   ❷ Constraint Score. When additional constraints are imposed on ERs, we further measure whether
204   the ER satisfies the constraints. Since real-world constraints can vary widely, we formally define the
205   constraint score as follows:
206
207                            Scoreconstraint = F (representation; constraints)                         (2)
208
209   where F (·) is a function returning a numerical score, which indicates the probability that a repre-
210   sentation satisfies the constraints.
211   Generating Natural Language Feedback. We proceed to generate natural language feedback

      2.4     T HE S ELF -R EFLECTION A LGORITHM
217
218   Our self-reflection approach can be formalized as an iterative optimization process, outlined in Al-
219   gorithm 1. Given an input code, our approach enters a loop of trials (Lines 4-18 in Algorithm 1).
220   In each trial, an ER is generated (Line 5), followed by the production of natural language feedback
221   (Lines 7 and 8). These outputs are stored in memory and used as additional context in the subsequent
222   trial (Line 9). The loop terminates when either the ER scores exceed a threshold, or the maximum
223   number of trials is reached. Finally, the ER with the highest evaluation score is returned.

403     Finding ❷: From the structured ERs, we find that LLMs treat the code as a structured sequence
404     instead of plain text. LLMs even can produce a plausible syntax tree.
405
406   LLMs generate many structured ERs (55.04%) in the open setting. In the dictionary and table, LLMs
407   parse the code into different components and reason about their types. In the XML, LLMs even
408   produce a plausible syntax tree. These cases show that LLMs view the code as a structured sequence
409   instead of plain text. Thus, we suspect that LLMs can understand the grammatical structures of code.
410   However, existing LLMs do not explicitly model grammatical structures during training. Therefore,
411   how LLMs learn the structures remains unclear.
412
413     Finding ❸: Based on the natural language ERs, we discover that LLMs can reason about the
414     functionality of APIs based on code text and generate natural language text describing the APIs.
415
416
      For example, in the example of pseudocode, LLMs reason about the API’s purpose (e.g., get
      current process ID) from its names (e.g., os.getpid). This process is akin to the pro-
417
      cess of reasoning the meaning of a phrase from its abbreviation. Similar phenomena are found in
418
      previous studies (Zhang et al., 2022). They found that LLMs can reason about the functionality of
419   code based on a corrupted version.
420
421     Finding ❹: Through the mathematical ERs, we find that LLMs use mathematical notations to
422     represent numerical calculations in code and can convert the code into an arithmetic expression.
423

426   understanding code. Mathematical expressions serve to unify various numerical operations within
427   the code, facilitating LLMs’ comprehension of underlying data flows.

527   Reflexion. Technically, our self-reflection approach is inspired by the Reflexion framework (Shinn
528   et al., 2023). We follow it and leverage natural language feedback to ‘optimize’ LLMs instead of
529   updating parameters. In contrast to Reflexion, our approach focuses on generating ERs of code and
530   introduces new effective designs (e.g., semantic-equivalent scores and external constraints). Based
531   on the approach, we further conduct a large-scale study and summarize eight valuable findings.
532   5     C ONCLUSION
533
534   This paper explores generating Equivalent Representations (ERs) of code by proposing a self-
535   reflection approach. Based on whether to add constraints on ERs, our approach can generate ERs
536   in open and constrained settings. Then, we explore generating ERs in both settings and summarize
537   nine valuable findings. In the open setting, our findings reveal how LLMs understand the different
538   elements in code, e.g., grammatical rules and APIs. In the constrained setting, we find that our
539   approach can effectively support various software engineering tasks. Finally, we discuss the future
      directions of this work.
