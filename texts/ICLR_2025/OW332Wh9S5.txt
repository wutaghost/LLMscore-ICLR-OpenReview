009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Spoken language models (SLMs) have gained increasing attention with advance-
012            ments in text-based, decoder-only language models. SLMs process text and
013            speech, enabling simultaneous speech understanding and generation. This paper
014            presents Double-Codebook Speaker-invariant Clustering (DC-Spin), which aims
015            to improve speech tokenization by bridging audio signals and SLM tokens. DC-
016            Spin extracts speaker-invariant tokens rich in phonetic information and resilient
017            to input variations, enhancing zero-shot SLM tasks and speech resynthesis. We
018
               propose a chunk-wise approach to enable streamable DC-Spin without retrain-
               ing and degradation. Comparisons of tokenization methods (self-supervised and
019
               neural audio codecs), model scalability, and downstream task proxies show that
020
               tokens easily modeled by an n-gram LM or aligned with phonemes offer strong
021            performance, providing insights for designing speech tokenizers for SLMs.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Spoken language models (SLMs) and related applications have gained more interest with the ad-
027   vancements of large language models (LLM) and audio tokenization techniques (Wu et al., 2024).
028   These speech LMs resemble causal LMs in natural language processing, but SLMs take speech
029   and, optionally, text as input and generate speech or text. Hence, these LMs can perform tasks like
030   speech continuation (Lakhotia et al., 2021), automatic speech recognition (ASR) (Rubenstein et al.,
031   2023; Maiti et al., 2024), text-to-speech synthesis (TTS) (Wang et al., 2023), and the more compli-
032   cated spoken language understanding (SLU) problems (Gong et al., 2023; Chu et al., 2023; Nguyen
033
      et al., 2024). SLM has two main research directions: 1) LM architecture and training and 2) speech
      tokenization techniques, the latter of which is the focus of this paper.
034
035   Since directly taking raw audio waveform as input to an SLM is infeasible, tokenizing speech into
036   text-like discrete units has become an essential component of recent SLMs. We define four key
037   qualifications for a good speech tokenizer inspired by prior studies. First, the tokens should contain
038   strong phonetic or semantic information so that the SLM can use the content of speech to perform
039
      ASR and SLU (Lakhotia et al., 2021). Second, the tokens should retain acoustic details for being
      resynthesized into speech for generative tasks like TTS and speech-to-speech translation (Lee et al.,
040
      2022; Zhang et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023). Third, the tokenizer should be robust to perturbations
041
      like additive noise, reverberation, and speaker change because the perturbations are irrelevant to
042   how an SLM understands human speech and language (Gat et al., 2023; Messica & Adi, 2024).
043   Fourth, the tokenizer should be lightweight and fast, supporting real-time interaction between users
044   and SLMs. Hence, this paper tries to answer the following question: how to build and evaluate a
045   good speech tokenizer for spoken language models that satisfies these key qualifications?
046
      We simplify the setup of this paper by training a unit-based speech LM (uLM) (Lakhotia et al., 2021)
047
      and a Hifi-GAN unit-to-speech synthesizer (Kong et al., 2020; Polyak et al., 2021). This setup is
048   commonly used in SLM studies and applications (Maiti et al., 2024; Messica & Adi, 2024; Hassid
049   et al., 2024), which is an ideal proxy for more advanced SLMs. uLMs are decoder-only transformer
050   LMs (Vaswani et al., 2017) and trained with the next-token prediction objective on speech tokens.
051   uLMs can perform zero-shot tasks by estimating the probability of utterances, including detecting
052   real spoken words and determining correct syntactic structures (Nguyen et al., 2020), and can be
053   fine-tuned for ASR. Moreover, we train Hifi-GANs to convert tokens to audio and quantify the
      intelligibility of the resynthesized speech to simulate speech generation with SLMs. With uLM and

      resynthesis, we can examine speech tokenizers on the first two required qualities. Next, we follow
055   Gat et al. (2023) to quantify the robustness by comparing the extracted tokens between clean and
056   perturbed speech. Finally, we measure the inference speed of offline and streaming tokenization.
057
058
      After defining the goals and evaluation pipelines, we propose Double-Codebook Spin (DC-Spin)
      by extending speaker-invariant clustering (Spin) with an auxiliary codebook to extract better speech
059
      units, where Spin is a self-supervised fine-tuning method for capturing phonetic units via online
060
      clustering and speaker-invariant swapped prediction (Chang et al., 2023). To further boost robust-
061   ness and token quality, we propose pre-training the Hidden-unit BERT (HuBERT) self-supervised
062   speech encoder with Spin codeword units as a better initialization for DC-Spin (Hsu et al., 2021),
063   denoted as SpinHuBERT. The contributions of this paper are listed as follows:
064
                1. The proposed speech tokenizer produces high-quality speaker-invariant speech tokens,
065
                   achieving state-of-the-art spoken language modeling and speech resynthesis compared to
066
                   open-source tokenizers on multiple benchmarks with limited resources.
067             2. We propose a simple chunk-wise method to repurpose offline speech tokenizers into
068                streaming mode with a negligible performance drop.
069             3. We analyze multiple proxy tasks to understand the relation between speech tokenizer and
070                SLM performance. We find that phoneme and character-normalized mutual information
071                and the proposed n-gram predictability are good proxies for downstream tasks.

076   2       R ELATED W ORK

078
      Spoken Language Models (SLM) SLMs or speech language models usually refer to decoder-only
079
      LMs that input or output speech and sometimes text. The two main approaches to integrating speech
080
      into LMs are adaptor and token-based.1 Because of the recent advancements in LLMs, researchers
081   connect speech encoders and text-based LMs through adaptors (Chu et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2024;
082   Tang et al., 2024), allowing speech understanding and ASR but requiring a more sophisticated design
083   for speech generation (Dubey et al., 2024). In contrast, a more common approach, which is the main
084   focus of this paper, is to tokenize speech to serve as both input and output of SLMs (Lakhotia et al.,
085   2021; Hassid et al., 2024; Maiti et al., 2024). Under this setup, SLMs treat audio waveforms as text-
086   like tokens, allowing SLMs to process speech and text jointly (Nguyen et al., 2024) and to generate
087   speech by synthesizing tokens into audio (Polyak et al., 2021).
088   In Lakhotia et al. (2021), SLMs are trained with unlabeled speech tokens to discover the spoken
089   content, which can be evaluated with zero-shot tasks in Nguyen et al. (2020). This concept allows
090   an SLM to be fine-tuned with paired speech-text data for ASR, TTS, and SLU (Maiti et al., 2024).
091   Advanced techniques like interleaving speech and text tokens (Nguyen et al., 2024), initializing with
092   text-based LMs (Hassid et al., 2024), and integrating multiple token types (Borsos et al., 2023) are
093   developed to improve performance. Because SLMs simultaneously understand and generate speech,
094   and the speech tokens are the only media between the models and audio signals, speech tokenizer
095
      design has become a crucial part of SLM research.
096   Self-supervised Learning (SSL) SSL is introduced to leverage large unlabeled audio datasets to
097   pre-train speech encoders, mitigating the need for extensive human labeling (Mohamed et al., 2022).
098   SSL models are trained to predict pseudo labels given a partial speech utterance. Pseudo targets
099   could be Mel spectrograms (Chung et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2021), vector-quantized features (Baevski
100   et al., 2020; Hsu et al., 2021; Chiu et al., 2022), or an exponential average of the model itself (Baevski
101
      et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023). Pre-trained SSL models offer good initialization for speech processing
      tasks (Yang et al., 2024). Moreover, evidence has shown that speech SSL models excel at extracting
102
      phonetic representations (Pasad et al., 2021; Chang et al., 2023; Choi et al., 2024), so quantizing SSL
103
      hidden layer embeddings with K-means clustering is widely adopted to tokenize speech (Lakhotia
104   et al., 2021; Hassid et al., 2024; Maiti et al., 2024). Gat et al. (2023) and Messica & Adi (2024)
105   further fine-tune SSL encoders for robust speech tokenizers.

      Neural Audio Codec Neural network-based codecs compress audio into compact units and recon-
109   struct high-fidelity signals from the units (Zeghidour et al., 2021; Défossez et al., 2023; Wu et al.,
110   2023). These models resemble autoencoders and comprise an encoder, a quantization module, and
111   a decoder. A commonly used technique for the quantization module is residual vector quantiza-
112   tion (RVQ) (Zeghidour et al., 2021). RVQ has multiple codebooks, each quantizing the residual
113   features computed from the previous codebook, making the first few codebooks preserve more criti-
114   cal information for reconstructing audio waveforms. Zhang et al. (2024) proposes SpeechTokenizer
115   by enforcing the first codebook to capture phonetic units by distilling knowledge from a pre-trained
116   SSL teacher, but the teacher bounds the performance. One of the benefits of neural codecs is that
117
      the model itself has an audio resynthesis module, i.e., the decoder. Still, SSL-based tokenizers can
      resynthesize speech with a separate vocoder.
118
119   Besides the open-source tokenizers, closed models like USM (Rubenstein et al., 2023) are claimed
120   to be powerful for SLMs, but these tokenizers are difficult to reproduce or compare because the
121   details remain unrevealed. In contrast, this paper aims to offer insights into designing tokenizers
122   and shares all details for future studies. Additionally, some works categorize speech tokens into
123
      semantic and acoustic tokens for understanding and generative tasks, respectively (Zhang et al.,
      2024; Borsos et al., 2023). However, we will demonstrate that a single type of speech token is
124
      sufficient to perform well on both tasks.

129   3.1   BACKGROUND
130
      Speaker-invariant Clustering (Spin) Spin is a self-supervised fine-tuning approach inspired by
131
      Caron et al. (2020) and captures speaker-invariant content in speech signals through online cluster-
132   ing and swapped prediction (Chang et al., 2023). During training, each utterance is perturbed to
133   sound like a different speaker but with the same content by randomly scaling the F0 and formant
134   frequencies. Both utterances are fed to a pre-trained SSL encoder, and the frame-level output of each
135   utterance is transformed into a sequence of probability distributions with a learnable codebook. The
136   distributions are smoothed to enforce full codebook usage and serve as the learning target. Finally,
137   the model performs swapped prediction by minimizing the cross-entropy loss between the original
138   codeword distribution and the smoothed targets from the perturbed output and vice versa.
139   Spin efficiently improves SSL encoders in content-related problems like ASR and phoneme recog-
140   nition (PR). Robust-Spin (R-Spin) extends Spin for robust speech recognition but requires more
141   complicated training stages and implementation (Chang & Glass, 2024). Although Chang &
142   Glass (2024) have shown that discrete units produced by Spin codebooks are closely aligned with
143   phonemes and characters, the applications of these tokens remain undiscovered.
144   Hidden-unit BERT (HuBERT) HuBERT is an SSL pre-training method for speech representation
145   learning (Hsu et al., 2021). Like BERT in NLP (Devlin et al., 2019), HuBERT is pre-trained with
146   a mask prediction objective for multiple iterations with pseudo labels derived by K-means clustered
147   continuous audio representations. First, the labels are K-means cluster IDs of Mel-frequency cep-
148   stral coefficients (MFCCs). Then, the second iteration model predicts K-means clusters from the
149   first model’s hidden embeddings. Besides serving as pre-training labels, K-means units are useful
150   in SLM and speech-to-speech translation (Lakhotia et al., 2021; Lee et al., 2022). This paper adopts
151   HuBERT as the initialization of the proposed speech tokenizers (Section 3.2) and further improves
152
      HuBERT by introducing better learning targets (Section 3.3).

      3.2   S PIN AS S PEECH T OKENIZER
155
      This section proposes tokenizing speech with Spin codebook along with methods to improve the
156   quality of Spin discrete units. Because Spin codebooks capture phonetic information and have
157   a unique speaker-invariant property, the tokens extracted from Spin satisfy the first qualification
158   in Section 1. These properties are especially useful for speech generation because the vocoder
159   can condition on different speakers, allowing more flexible speech synthesis. Compared with K-
160   means, Spin’s codebook is optimized with gradient descent, proven highly scalable (LeCun et al.,
161   2002). In contrast, K-means clustering requires extracting and storing hidden features, leading to
      high memory consumption and special implementation when scaling (Zanon Boito et al., 2024).

303   4.2     Z ERO - SHOT S POKEN L ANGUAGE M ODELING
304
305   This section discusses the impact of tokenizers on SLM by adopting the following tasks.
306   T SC We use the “Topic” Spoken StoryCloze to evaluate an SLM’s ability to capture continuation
307
      coherence and fine-grained textual nuances (Hassid et al., 2024). Each sample comprises two simi-
      lar spoken stories with different endings. The SLM must find the utterance with a consistent ending.
308
      sWUGGY We adopt the sWUGGY spot-the-word task from ZeroSpeech (Nguyen et al., 2020).5
309
      Each sample has two spoken words with similar pronunciations, with one of the words absent from
310   the English vocabulary. The “all” subset combines the “in-vocab” subset and out-of-vocabulary
311   words that do not appear in the LibriSpeech training set.
312   sBLIMP The sBLIMP acceptability metric is also adopted from ZeroSpeech. Each sample com-
313   prises two similar utterances, but one is ungrammatical. The above tasks require an SLM to compute
314   a pseudo probability for each audio recording in a sample and compare the probabilities to determine
315   which is more likely to be the correct answer. The results are reported in accuracy.
316   Table 2 shows the results of unsupervised speech tokenization techniques based on HuBERT Base
317   and LibriSpeech for a fair comparison. DC-Spin demonstrates superior performance compared with
318   previous methods. We observe consistent improvement of DC-Spin over Spin across different unit
319   sizes, but the gap is narrowed when the codebook size is 500. Among all tasks, DC-Spin improves
320   sWUGGY most significantly because this problem is closely related to how well speech tokens
321   represent pronunciation, which is directly related to phonetic information. The results strongly
322   indicate the effectiveness of DC-Spin.

      5     C ONCLUSION
524
525
      This paper studies building and evaluating effective and robust speech tokenizers for spoken lan-
526   guage modeling and speech resynthesis. We propose SpinHuBERT and DC-Spin, which demon-
527   strate strong capabilities on several tasks compared with open-source speech tokenizers. Our meth-
528   ods satisfy the four qualifications for an ideal tokenizer: captures phonetic information, preserves
529   acoustic details for resynthesis, is robust to perturbations, and fast inference. Furthermore, we found
530   n-gram predictability, PNMI, and CNMI metrics highly correlate with downstream performance,
531   making these tasks ideal proxies. The findings and proxy tasks offer guidelines for future tokenizer
532   and spoken language model development.
533   Limitations and Future Works This paper focuses on the effectiveness of speech tokenizers, so
534   the evaluation tasks are on a smaller scale. Although the proposed tokenizers achieve state-of-the-art
535   zero-shot metrics with small SLMs, it is worth investigating their gains on multimodal LLMs. Our
536   models are trained and evaluated on English speech, so extending to multilingual and general audio
537   is left for future studies. TTS and speech-to-speech translation are also potential applications.

1009   in fairseq. The LM is trained on 8 NVIDIA 32GB V100 GPUs with a gradient accumulation of 8
1010   steps, a maximum of 8192 tokens per GPU, and 3072 tokens per utterance. Utterances with lengths
1011   exceeding 3072 tokens are split into shorter sequences. The learning rate linearly increases to 5e-4
1012   in the first 4k steps and decays as in Vaswani et al. (2017). We choose the checkpoint with the lowest
1013
       validation perplexity for zero-shot evaluation and ASR fine-tuning.
1014   ASR Fine-tuning Similar to SLM pre-training, ASR fine-tuning has the same training setup except
1015   for the data preparation. We extract tokens and concatenate transcription from the LibriSpeech 960h
1016   training corpus. To construct the ASR data, a special token <|asr|> is inserted after each tok-
1017   enized utterance, followed by the corresponding character-based transcription. E.g., an utterance for
1018   training would look like 69 10 ... 11 482 <|asr|> Z E U S | S E E S | ...,
1019
       where “|” denotes whitespace. The LM input embedding is extended by randomly initializing em-

1020
       resources are the same as SLM pre-training. The learning rate linearly increases to 2e-4 in the first
1021
       2k steps and decays as in Vaswani et al. (2017). After training, we decode with a beam size of 5

       B.3   S UPERVISED DC-S PIN F INE - TUNING
1159
1160   Method Inspired by the speech encoders in multimodal LLM studies (Gemini Team, 2023; Dubey
1161   et al., 2024), we include supervised fine-tuning to boost tokenizer quality. Different from Sec-
1162   tion 3.2, the SFT here is applied during DC-Spin fine-tuning so that the tokenizer is jointly opti-
1163   mized with several objectives, i.e., multitask learning. We consider three types of SFT: Mel spectro-
1164   gram reconstruction (Mel), CTC-based character recognition (CTC-ASR), and CTC-based phoneme
1165   recognition (CTC-PR). Note that Mel reconstruction is unsupervised, but we consider these tasks to-
1166
       gether as SFT for a simpler presentation. For Mel reconstruction, we adopt the speaker encoder and
       the decoder proposed in Chou & Lee (2019). The speaker encoder transforms the Mel spectrogram
1167
       of an utterance to a single speaker embedding. The decoder then reconstructs the Mel spectrogram
1168
       by taking the speech encoder’s output embedding and fuses the speaker embedding with adaptive
1169   instance normalization (Huang & Belongie, 2017). Because the input for Spin training consists of
1170   pairs of utterances with the same content spoken by different speakers, we reconstruct each utterance
1171   into two Mel spectrograms using the original and the perturbed speaker embeddings, as illustrated
1172   in Figure 5. This training objective is expected to help disentangle speaker and content representa-
1173   tions. We use a standard L1 loss for this task. For CTC-ASR and CTC-PR, a linear prediction is
1174   added to project hidden representations to logits over all possible output textual tokens. Thus, the
1175   loss function of the supervised DC-Spin is
1176              LDC-Spin-SFT = LDC-Spin + λMel LMel + λCTC-ASR LCTC-ASR + λCTC-PR LCTC-PR ,
1177
       where λMel , λCTC-ASR , and λCTC-PR are hyperparameters. With the SFT tasks, the learned codebooks
1178
       are expected to align better with the underlying textual and phonetic distribution.
1179
1180   Setup The training setup is almost identical to DC-Spin as discussed in Appendix A.4, but the peak
1181   learning rate here is 2e-5. In the experiments, we always let
1182                                       λMel + λCTC-ASR + λCTC-PR = 5
1183
1184
       and assign the same value for each loss. E.g., when Mel reconstruction and PR are applied, we
       have λMel = λCTC-PR = 2.5 and λCTC-ASR = 0. We compute 80-bin Mel spectrograms with torchau-
1185
       dio (Yang et al., 2022).

1530
1531   This section evaluates the SSL models in this paper on the Speech Processing Universal Performance
1532   Benchmark (SUPERB) (Yang et al., 2021; Tsai et al., 2022). SUPERB is a benchmark that assesses
1533   the usefulness of hidden representations of pre-trained speech SSL encoders by applying these rep-
1534   resentations to a wide range of speech processing tasks. During downstream task training, a speech
1535   encoder is frozen, and hidden layer features are extracted. A learnable weighted-sum mechanism
1536   then aggregates each frame across all layers to a sequence. The aggregated features are then fed to
1537   a lightweight prediction head optimized with a supervised objective like CTC. We encourage the
1538
       readers to refer to the original SUPERB papers for a more complete explanation of the tasks and
       evaluation metrics. We follow the implementation in the S3PRL library.18 The results are shown in
1539
       Table 21.
1540
1541   Comparing the HuBERT it3 models, we found increasing the number of layers improves almost
1542   all tasks (6 vs. 12 vs. 18 layers), showing the effect of model size scaling in downstream tasks.
1543   When comparing 50Hz HuBERT it3 and SpinHuBERT models, the 25Hz models are usually better
1544
       at content-related tasks like PR, ASR, keyword spotting (KS), and speech translation (ST), which
       might be a result of the shortened sequence of features. The ASR results of HuBERT it3 and Spin-
1545
       HuBERT are worse than some prior methods, which is consistent with the findings in the previously
1546
       discussed ASR experiments. Similar to the results in Chang et al. (2023), the SpinHuBERT models
1547   trained with Spin units offer high-quality representations and improve content-related tasks like PR.
1548   Although trained with speaker-invariant targets, SpinHuBERT performs similarly to prior methods
1549   on speaker-related tasks because the speaker information is preserved at the bottom layers, which
1550   will be discussed in the next paragraph.
1551
       We visualize the weighted-sum mechanism of the SUPERB downstream models in Figure 7 to un-
1552
       derstand the importance of each hidden layer for different downstream tasks. Following Chang et al.
1553   (2022), we normalize the weights by scaling with a factor of the averaged L2 norm of each layer’s
1554   hidden representations. We observed that top layers are more important for content and semantic-
1555   related tasks since the weights have higher values (layers eight to twelve), consistent with prior
1556   studies (Chang & Glass, 2024; Yang et al., 2024). Moreover, emotion recognition (ER) relies on
1557   the top layers as well (Figure 7g), indicating that classifying speech emotion depends on the content
1558   representations. In contrast to the previous tasks, speaker-related problems use the bottom layers,
1559   implying that speaker information is stored at those layers (Figures 7h, 7i, and 7j). Comparing Hu-
1560   BERT and SpinHuBERT models, the proposed SpinHuBERT models encode speaker information at
1561
       lower layers (Figures 7h and 7i), which is caused by the speaker-invariant pseudo labels for SSL pre-
       training, forcing the models to drop speaker information at early layers. Hence, the results verify the
1562
       findings in the previous paragraph. Overall, this section comprehensively discusses the effectiveness
1563
       of SpinHuBERT’s downstream applications and analyzes the importance of each layer.

1658

1660

1661   models trained with three iterations. SpinHuBERT achieves the lowest ABX error rate at the last

       G.1     M ETHOD

       This section describes the chunk-wise streaming token extraction approach. As depicted in Fig-
1733
       ure 9, the tokenizer extracts units after the speaker completed speaking Tchunk seconds of speech.
1734   Then, after another Tshift seconds, the tokenizer repeats the same operation with an increased con-
1735   text length (Tchunk + Tshift seconds). Assuming the tokenizer produces Lchunk and Lshift tokens given
1736   Tchunk and Tshift seconds of speech, respectively. Let Loverlap = (Lchunk − Lshift ) / 2. The last Loverlap
1737   extracted tokens are neglected in each chunk because the lack of future frames degrades token qual-
1738   ity. Under this setup, the tokens extracted in the first few chunks might be less accurate than the
1739   offline extracted tokens but will gradually improve after an expanded context.
1740   For instance, in Figure 9, Lchunk = 7 and Lshift = 3, making Loverlap = 2. The first chunk extracts
1741   seven tokens and neglects the last two, and the first five tokens are fed into the SLM. When shifted
1742   by Tshift seconds, the token sequence length increased by three. We then neglect the last two tokens
1743   and take the three tokens before them as the SLM input.
1744
       We implement the chunk-wise streaming tokenization by repeatedly increasing the audio samples
1745
       fed into the tokenizer. Each time, the tokenizer (e.g., HuBERT) takes a longer input, and we select
1746   tokens according to the methods in the previous paragraph. We then concatenate all selected tokens
1747   into one sequence, with approximately the same amount as in the offline extracted sequence. Note
1748   that more advanced model architectures and streaming strategies can be implemented to improve
1749   streaming performance. For example, reusing encoded hidden states and attention maps from the
1750   previous chunks might increase inference efficiency. Nevertheless, we only consider this repurpos-
1751   ing approach to achieving streaming tokenization to demonstrate that the proposed tokenizers are
1752   streamable without retraining.
