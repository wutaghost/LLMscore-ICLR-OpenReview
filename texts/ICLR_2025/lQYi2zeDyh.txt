009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Supervised learning for causal discovery from observational data often achieves
012            competitive performance despite seemingly avoiding the explicit assumptions that
013            traditional methods require for identifiability. In this work, we analyze CSIvA (Ke
014            et al., 2023b) on bivariate causal models, a transformer architecture for amortized
015            inference promising to train on synthetic data and transfer to real ones. First, we
016
               bridge the gap with identifiability theory, showing that the training distribution
               implicitly defines a prior on the causal model of the test observations: consistent
017
               with classical approaches, good performance is achieved when we have a good
018
               prior on the test data, and the underlying model is identifiable. Second, we find
019            that CSIvA can not generalize to classes of causal models unseen during training:
020            to overcome this limitation, we show that learning on datasets generated from
021            different types of causal models, unambiguously identifiable in isolation, improves
022            the test generalization. We analyze this empirical evidence with theory, illustrating
023            that the ambiguous cases resulting from the mixture of identifiable causal models
024            are unlikely to occur. Overall, we find that amortized causal discovery still adheres
025            to identifiability theory, violating the previous hypothesis from Lopez-Paz et al.
026            (2015) that supervised learning methods could overcome its restrictions.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Causal discovery aims to uncover the underlying causal relationships between variables of a system
031   from pure observations, which is crucial for answering interventional and counterfactual queries
032   when experimentation is impractical or unfeasible (Peters et al., 2017; Pearl, 2009; Spirtes, 2010).
033   Unfortunately, causal discovery is inherently ill-posed (Glymour et al., 2019): unique identification
034   of causal directions requires restrictive assumptions on the class of structural causal models (SCMs)
035   that generated the data (Shimizu et al., 2006; Hoyer et al., 2008; Zhang & Hyvärinen, 2009). These
036   theoretical limitations often render existing methods inapplicable, as the underlying assumptions are
037
      usually untestable or difficult to verify in practice (Montagna et al., 2023a).
038   Recently, supervised learning algorithms trained on synthetic data have been proposed to overcome
039   the need for specific hypotheses, which restrains the application of classical causal discovery methods
040   to real-world problems (Ke et al., 2023b; Lopez-Paz et al., 2015; Li et al., 2020; Lippe et al., 2022;
041   Lorch et al., 2022). Seminal work from Lopez-Paz et al. (2015) argues that this learning-based
042   approach to causal discovery would allow dealing with complex data-generating processes and would
043
      greatly reduce the need for explicitly crafting identifiability conditions a-priori: despite this ambitious
      goal, the output of these methods is generally considered unreliable, as no theoretical guarantee is
044
      provided. A pair of non-identifiable structural causal models can be associated with different causal
045
      directed acyclic graphs (DAGs) G =    ̸ G̃, while entailing the same joint distribution p on the system’s
046
      variables. It is thus unclear how a learning algorithm presented with observational data generated from
047
      p would be able to overcome these theoretical limits and correctly identify a unique causal structure.
048   However, the available empirical evidence seems not to reflect impossibility results, as these methods
049   yield surprising generalization abilities on several synthetic benchmarks. Our work aims to bridge this
050   gap by studying the performance of a transformer architecture for causal discovery through the lens
051   of the theory of identifiability from observational data. Specifically, we analyze the CSIvA (Causal
052   Structure Induction via Attention) model for causal discovery (Ke et al., 2023b), focusing on bivariate
053   graphs, as they offer a controlled yet non-trivial setting for the investigation. As our starting point, we
      provide closed-form examples that identify the limitations of CSIvA in recovering causal structures

054
      of linear non-Gaussian and nonlinear additive noise models, which are notably identifiable, and
055   demonstrate the expected failures through empirical evidence. These findings suggest that the class of
056   structural causal models that can be identified by CSIvA is inherently dependent on the specific class
057   of SCMs observed during training. Thus, the need for restrictive hypotheses on the data-generating
058   process is intrinsic to causal discovery, both in the traditional and modern learning-based approaches:
059   assumptions on the test distribution either are posited when selecting the algorithm (traditional
060   methods) or in the choice of the training data (learning-based methods). To address this limitation,
061   we theoretically and empirically analyze when training CSIvA on datasets generated by multiple
062   identifiable SCMs with different structural assumptions improves its generalization at test time. Our
063
      experimental findings are based on the analysis of ∼1M test runs. In summary:
064
             • We show that the class of structural causal models that CSIvA can identify is defined by the
065            class of SCMs observed through samples during the training. We reinforce the notion that
066            identifiability in causal discovery inherently requires assumptions, which must be encoded
067            in the training data in the case of learning algorithms for amortized inference: this violates a
068            previous hypothesis in Lopez-Paz et al. (2015), which suggests that these methods could
069            exceed the boundaries of identifiability.
070          • We empirically show that CSIvA is expected to fail to generalize on datasets generated by
071            structural causal models characterized by mechanism types or noise terms distributions un-
072            seen during training. While this appears as a significant limit of amortized causal discovery,
073            systematic analysis has been disregarded by previous work in the literature.
074          • To mitigate this limitation, we study the benefits of CSIvA training on mixtures of causal
075            models. We analyze when algorithms learned on multiple models are expected to identify
076            broad classes of SCMs (unlike many classical methods). Empirically, we show that training
077            on samples generated by multiple identifiable causal models with different assumptions on
078            mechanisms and noise distribution results in significantly improved generalization abilities.

      2    R ELATED WORKS
081
082
      In this paper, we study amortized inference of bivariate causal graphs, i.e. supervised optimization
083
      of an inference model to directly predict a causal structure from newly provided data. In particular,
084   this is the first work that draws a connection between identifiability theory and amortized inference of
085   causal DAGs. Dai et al. (2023) studies supervised learning of the graph skeleton, limiting its analysis
086   to the role of identifiability of unshielded triplets. Several algorithms have instead been proposed.
087
088   Algorithms for amortized inference closely related to CSIvA. In the context of purely obser-
089   vational data, Lopez-Paz et al. (2015) defines a classification problem mapping the kernel mean
090   embedding of the data distribution to a causal graph, while Li et al. (2020) relies on equivariant
091   neural network architectures. More recently, Lippe et al. (2022) and Lorch et al. (2022) proposed
092   learning on interventional data, in addition to observations (in the same spirit as CSIvA). Despite
093   different algorithmic implementations, the target object of estimation of most of these methods is the
094
      distribution over the space of all possible graphs, conditional on the input dataset (similarly, the ENCO
      algorithm in Lippe et al. (2022) models the conditional distribution of individual edges). This justifies
095
      our choice of restricting our study to the CSIvA architecture (despite this being a clear limitation),
096
      as in the infinite observational sample limit, these methods approximate the same distribution.
097
098
      Other learning-based algorithms for causal discovery. Out of the scope of this work, there
099   are methods that necessarily require interventional data (Brouillard et al., 2020; Ke et al., 2023a;
100   Scherrer et al., 2022), and learning-based algorithms unsuitable for amortized inference (Lachapelle
101   et al., 2020; Ng et al., 2020; Zheng et al., 2018; Zhang et al., 2022; Bello et al., 2022).
102
103   Differences with Lopez-Paz et al. (2015). Before moving forward, we remark on the main
104   differences between our paper and Lopez-Paz et al. (2015), as both works concentrate on supervised
105   learning for the inference of bivariate causal graphs. Lopez-Paz et al. (2015) frames causal discovery
106   as a classification problem, where the goal is estimating and mapping the kernel mean embeddings
107   of the distribution of the observed data to the correct causal order (assuming a causal relation is
      in place). Building on the theory of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces, they provide finite sample

      learning rates. In particular, their study assumes observations generated by identifiable causal models.
109   In contrast, we aim to (i) empirically investigate what conditions enable identifiability in amortized
110   causal discovery (ii) theoretically and empirically investigate how to exploit the known identifiability
111   results to train algorithms with improved test generalization.

125   3.1   C AUSAL DISCOVERY FROM OBSERVATIONAL DATA
126
127   Causal discovery from observational data is the inference of the causal graph G from a dataset
128   of i.i.d. observations of the random vector X. In general, without restrictive assumptions on the
129
      mechanisms and the noise distributions, the direction of edges in the graph G is not identifiable, i.e.
      it can not be found from the population density pX . In particular, it is possible to identify only a
130
      Markov equivalence class, which is the set of graphs encoding the same conditional independencies
131
      as the density pX . To clarify with an example, consider the causal graph X1 → X2 associated
132   with a structural causal model inducing a density pX1 ,X2 . If the model is not identifiable, there
133   exists an SCM with causal graph X2 → X1 that entails the same joint density pX1 ,X2 . The set
134   {X1 → X2 , X2 → X1 } is the Markov equivalence class of the graph X1 → X2 , i.e. the set of all
135   graphs with X1 , X2 mutually dependent. Clearly, in this setting, even the exact knowledge of pX1 ,X2
136   cannot inform us about the correct causal direction.
137   Definition 1 (Identifiable causal model). Consider a structural causal model with underlying graph G
138   and pX joint density of the causal variables. We say that the model is identifiable from observational
139   data if the density pX can not be entailed by a structural causal model with graph G̃ ̸= G.

157   3.2   M OTIVATION AND PROBLEM DEFINITION
158
159   Causal discovery from observational data relies on specific assumptions, which can be challenging
160   to verify in practice (Montagna et al., 2023a). To address this, recent methods leverage supervised
161   learning for the amortized inference of causal graphs (or simply amortized causal discovery), i.e.
      optimization of an inference model to directly predict a causal structure from newly provided data


                                                             3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      (Lopez-Paz et al., 2015; Li et al., 2020; Lippe et al., 2022; Lorch et al., 2022; Ke et al., 2023a;
163   Löwe et al., 2020). While these approaches also aim to reduce reliance on explicit identifiability
164   assumptions, they often lack a clear connection to the existing causal discovery theory, making their
165   outputs generally unreliable. We illustrate this limitation through an example.
166
      Example 1. We consider the CSIvA transformer architecture proposed by Ke et al. (2023b), which
167   can learn a map from observational data to a causal graph. The authors of the paper show that, in the in-
168   finite sample regime, the CSIvA architecture exactly approximates the conditional distribution p(·|D)
169   over the space of possible graphs, given a dataset D. Identifiability theory in causal discovery tells us
170   that if the class of structural causal models that generated the observations is sufficiently constrained,
171   then there is only one graph that can fit the data within that class. For example, consider the case
172   of a dataset that is known to be generated by a nonlinear additive noise model, and let p(·|D, ANM)
173   be the conditional distribution that incorporates this prior knowledge on the SCM: then p(·|D, ANM)
174   concentrates all the mass on a single point G ∗ , the true graph underlying the D observations. Instead,
175
      in the absence of restrictions on the structural causal model, all the graphs in a Markov equivalence
      class are equally likely to be the correct solution given the data. Hence, p(·|D), the distribution
176
      learned by CSIvA, assigns equal probability to each graph in the Markov equivalence class of G ∗ .
177
178   Our arguments of Example 1 are valid for all learning methods that approximate the conditional
179   distribution over the space of graphs given the input data (Ke et al., 2023b; Lopez-Paz et al., 2015;
180   Li et al., 2020; Lippe et al., 2022; Lorch et al., 2022), and suggest that these algorithms are at most
181   informative about the equivalence class of the causal graph underlying the observations. However,
182   the available empirical evidence does not seem to highlight these limitations, as in practice these
183   methods can infer the true causal DAG on several synthetic benchmarks. Thus, further investigation
184
      is necessary if we want to rely on their output in any meaningful sense. In this work, we analyze these
      "black-box" approaches through the lens of established theory of causal discovery from observational
185
      data (causal inference often lacks experimental data, which we do not consider). We study in
186
      detail the CSIvA architecture (Ke et al., 2023b) (see Appendix A), a variation of the transformer
187   neural network (Vaswani et al., 2017) for the supervised learning of algorithms for amortized causal
188   discovery. This model is optimized via maximum likelihood estimation, i.e. finding Θ that minimizes
189   −EG,D [ln p̂(G|D; Θ)], where p̂(G|D; Θ) is the conditional distribution of a graph G given a dataset
190   D parametrized by Θ. We limit the analysis to CSIvA as it is a simple yet competitive end-to-end
191   approach to learning causal models. While this is clearly a limitation of the paper, our theoretical
192   and empirical conclusions exemplify both the role of theoretical identifiability in modern approaches
193   and the new opportunities they provide. Additionally, it fits well within a line of works arguing that
194   specifically transformers can learn causal concepts Jin et al. (2024); Zhang et al. (2024); Scetbon et al.
195
      (2024) and can be explicitly trained to identify different assumptions in context (Gupta et al., 2023).

197   4     E XPERIMENTAL RESULTS THROUGH THE LENS OF THEORY
198
199   In this section, we present a comprehensive analysis of bivariate causal discovery with transformers
200   and its relation to the theoretical boundaries of causal discovery from observational data. We show
201   that suitable assumptions must be encoded in the training distribution to ensure the identifiability of
202   the test data, and we additionally study the effectiveness of training on mixtures of causal models to
203   overcome these limitations, improving generalization abilities. In Appendix C we discuss how our
204
      findings can be extended to the case of multivariate causal models.
205
206
      4.1   E XPERIMENTAL DESIGN
207
      We concentrate our research on causal models of two variables, causally related according to one
208   of the two graphs X → Y , Y → X. Bivariate models are the simplest non-trivial setting with a
209   well-known theory of causality inference (Hoyer et al., 2008; Zhang & Hyvärinen, 2009; Peters et al.,
210   2014), but also amenable to manipulation. This allows for comprehensive training and analysis of
211   diverse SCMs and facilitates a clear interpretation of the results.
212
213   Datasets. Unless otherwise specified, in our experiments we train CSIvA on a sample of 15000
214   synthetically generated datasets, consisting of 1500 i.i.d. observations. Each dataset is generated
215   according to a single class of SCMs, defined by the mechanism type and the noise terms distribution.
      The coefficients of the linear mechanisms are sampled in the range [−3, −0.5] ∪ [0.5, 3], removing


                                                          4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      small coefficients to avoid close-to-unfaithful effects (Uhler et al., 2012). Nonlinear mechanisms
217   are parametrized according to a neural network with random weights, a strategy commonly adopted
218   in the literature of causal discovery (see Appendix B.2; alternatively, we provide experiments on
219   data generated simulating nonlinear mechanisms by sampling from a Gaussian process, as described
220   in Appendix D.7). The post-nonlinearity of the PNL model consists of a simple map z 7→ z 3 . Noise
221   terms are sampled from common distributions and a randomly generated density that we call mlp,
222   previously adopted in Montagna et al. (2023a), defined by a standard Gaussian transformed by a
223   multilayer perceptron (MLP) (Appendix B.2). We name these datasets mechanism-noise to refer
224   to their underlying causal model. For example, data sampled from a nonlinear ANM with Gaussian
225
      noise are named nonlinear-gaussian. More details on the synthetic data generation schema are found
      in Appendix B.2. All data are standardized by their empirical variance to remove opportunities to
226
      learn shortcuts (Geirhos et al., 2020; Reisach et al., 2021; Montagna et al., 2023b).
227
228
      Metric and random baseline. As our metric we use the structural Hamming distance (SHD),
229
      which is the number of edge removals, insertions or flips required to transform the predicted graph to
230   the ground-truth. In the context of bivariate causal graphs with a single edge, this is simply an error
231   count, so correct inference corresponds to SHD = 0, and an incorrect prediction gives SHD = 1.
232   Additionally, we define a reference random baseline, which assigns a causal direction according
233   to a fair coin, achieving SHD = 0.5 in expectation. Each architecture we analyze in the experiments
234   is trained 3 times, with different parameter initialization and training samples: the SHD presented
235   in the plots is the average of each of the 3 models on 1500 distinct test datasets of 1500 points each,
236   and the error bars are 95% confidence intervals.
237   We detail the training hyperparameters in Appendix B.1. Next, we analyze our experimental results,
238   starting by investigating how well CSIvA generalizes on distributions unseen during training.

240   4.2    WARM UP : IS CSI VA CAPABLE OF IN AND OUT- OF - DISTRIBUTION GENERALIZATION ?
241
242   In-distribution generalization. First, we investigate the generalization of CSIvA on datasets
243   sampled from the structural casual model that generates the train distribution, with mechanisms and
244   noise distributions fixed between training and testing. We call this in-distribution generalization.
245
      The main goal of these experiments is to validate that the performance of our CSIvA implementation
      is non-trivial. As a benchmark, we present the accuracy of two state-of-the-art approaches from the
246
      literature on causal discovery: we consider the DirectLiNGAM and NoGAM algorithms (Shimizu
247
      et al., 2011; Montagna et al., 2023c), respectively designed for the inference on LiNGAM and
248   nonlinear ANM generated data1 . The results of Figure 1 show that CSIvA can properly generalize
249   to unseen samples from the training distribution: the majority of the trained models present SHD
250   close to zero and comparable to the relative benchmark algorithm.
251
252   Out-of-distribution generalization. In practice, we generally do not know the SCM defining the
253   test distribution, so we are interested in CSIvA’s ability to generalize to data sampled from a class
254   of causal models that is unobserved during training. We call this out-of-distribution generalization
255   (OOD). We study OOD generalization to different noise terms, analyzing the network performance
256   on datasets generated from causal models where the mechanisms are fixed with respect to the
257
      training, while the noise distribution varies (e.g., given linear-mlp training samples, testing occurs
      on linear-uniform data). Orthogonally to these experiments, we empirically validate CSIvA’s OOD
258

314   Implications. CSIvA generalizes well to test data generated by the same class of SCMs used
315
      for training, in line with the findings in Ke et al. (2023b), which validates our implementation and

316

317
      From a practical perspective, this is a relevant finding, given that existing works on amortized causal
318   discovery lacks systematic experiments on the OOD setting. While training on a wider class of
319   SCMs might overcome this limitation, it requires caution. The identifiability of causal graphs indeed
320   results from the interplay between the data-generating mechanisms and noise distribution. However,
321   as we argue in our Example 1, the class of causal models that a supervised learning algorithm can
322   identify is generally not clear. In what follows, we investigate this point and its implications for
323   CSIvA, showing that the identifiability of the test samples can be ensured by imposing suitable

      4.3   H OW DOES CSI VA RELATE TO IDENTIFIABILITY THEORY FOR CAUSAL GRAPHS ?
325
326   The CSIvA algorithm does not make structural assumptions about the causal model underlying
327   the input data. This implies that the output of this method is unclear: as CSIvA should target
328   the conditional distribution p(·|D) over the space of graphs, in the absence of restrictions on
329   the functional mechanisms and the distribution of the noise terms, the causal graph X → Y is
330   indistinguishable from Y → X, as they are both equally likely to underlie the joint density pX,Y
331   generating the data. As we discuss in Example 1, the graphical output of the trained architecture
332
      could at most identify the equivalence class of the true causal graph. Yet, our experiments of Section
      4.2 show that CSIvA is capable of good in-distribution generalization, often inferring the correct
333
      DAG at test time. We explain this seeming contradiction with the following hypothesis, which
334
      motivates the analysis in the remainder of this section.
335
336
      Hypothesis 1. The class of structural causal models that can be identified by CSIvA is defined by the
      class of structural causal models underlying the generation of the training data.
337
338   To support and clarify our statement, we present the following example, adapted from Hoyer et al.
339   (2008).
340
      Example 2. Consider the causal model Y = f (X)+N, where f (X) = −X and pX , pN are Gumbel
341
      densities pX (x) = exp(−x − exp(−x)) and pN (n) = exp(−n − exp(−n)). This model satisfies
342   the assumptions of the LiNGAM, so it is identifiable, in the sense that a backward linear model with
343   the same distribution does not exist. However, in this special case, we can build a backward nonlinear
344   additive noise model X = g(Y ) + Ñ with independent noise terms: taking pY (y) = exp(−y −
345   2 log(1 + exp(−y))) to be the density of a logistic distribution, pÑ (ñ) = exp(−2ñ − exp(−ñ)) and
346   g(y) = log(1+exp(−y)); we see that pX,Y can factorize according to two opposite causal directions,
347   as pX,Y (x, y) = pN (y −f (x))pX (x) = pÑ (x−g(y))pY (y). Given a dataset D of observations from
348   the forward linear model, causal discovery methods like DirectLiNGAM (Shimizu et al., 2011) can
349   provably identify the correct causal direction X → Y , assuming that sufficient samples are provided.
350   Instead, the behavior of CSIvA seems hard to predict: given that the network approximates the
351
      conditional distribution p(·|D) over the possible graphs, for D with arbitrary many samples we have
      p(X → Y |D) = p(Y → X|D) = 0.5. On the other hand, given the prior knowledge that the data-
352
      generating SCM is a linear non-gaussian additive noise model, we have p(X → Y |D, LiNGAM) = 1,
353
      because the LiNGAM is identifiable. In this sense, the class of structural causal models that CSIvA
354   correctly infers appears to be determined by the structural causal models underlying the generation
355   of the training data. Under our Hypothesis 1, training CSIvA exclusively on LiNGAM-generated
356   data is equivalent to learning the distribution p(·|D, LiNGAM), such that the network should be able
357   to identify the forward linear model, whereas it could only infer the equivalence class of the causal
358   graph if its training datasets include observations from a nonlinear additive noise model.
359
360   The empirical results of Figure 3a show that CSIvA behaves according to our hypothesis: when
361
      training exclusively occurs on datasets {Di,→ }i generated by the forward linear-gumbel model of
      Example 2, the network can identify the causal direction of test data generated according to the same
362
      SCM. Similarly, the transformer trained on datasets {Di,← }i from the backward nonlinear model
363
      of the example can generalize to test data coming from the same distribution. According to our claim,
364   instead, the network that is trained on the union of the training samples {Di,→ }i ∪ {Di,← }i from
365   the forward and backward models (50:50 ratio in Figure 3a) displays the same test SHD (around
366   0.5) as a random classifier assigning the causal direction with equal probability.
367
      Further, we investigate CSIvA’s relation with known identifiability theory by training and testing the
368
      architecture on data from a linear Gaussian model, which is well-known to be unidentifiable. Not
369
      surprisingly, the results of Figure 3b show that none of the algorithms that we learn can infer the
370   causal order of linear Gaussian models with test SHD any better than a random baseline.
371
372   Implications. Our experiments show that CSIvA learns algorithms that closely follow identifiability
373   theory for causal discovery. In particular, while the method itself does not require explicit assumptions
374   on the data-generating process, the chosen training data ultimately determines the class of causal
375   models identifiable during inference. Notably, previous work has argued that supervised learning
376   approaches in causal discovery would help with "dealing with complex data-generating processes
377   and greatly reduce the need of explicitly crafting identifiability conditions a-priori", Lopez-Paz et al.
      (2015). In the case of CSIvA, this expectation does not appear to be fulfilled, as the assumptions still

461   4.5                    C AN WE TRAIN CSI VA ON MULTIPLE CAUSAL MODELS FOR BETTER GENERALIZATION ?
462
463   In this section, we investigate the benefits of training over multiple causal models, i.e. on samples
464   generated by a combination of classes of identifiable SCMs characterized by different mechanisms
465   and noise terms distribution. Our motivation is as follows: given that our empirical evidence
466   shows that CSIvA is capable of in-distribution generalization, whereas dramatically degrades the
467
      performance when testing occurs out-of-distribution, it is thus desirable to increase the class of
      causal models represented in the training datasets. We separately study the effects of training over
468
      multiple mechanisms and multiple noise distributions and compare the testing performance against
469
      architectures trained on samples of a single SCM.
470
471
472
      Mixture of causal mechanisms. We consider four networks optimized by training of CSIvA on
      datasets generated from pairs (or triples) of distinct SCMs, with fixed mlp noise and which differ in
473
      terms of their mechanisms type: linear and nonlinear; nonlinear and post-nonlinear; linear and post-
474
      nonlinear; linear, nonlinear and post-nonlinear. The number of training datasets for each architecture is
475   fixed (15000) and equally split between the causal models with different mechanism types. The results
476   of Figure 4 show that the networks trained on mixtures of mechanisms all present significantly better
477   test SHD compared to CSIvA models trained on a single mechanism type. We find that learning on
478   multiple SCMs improves the SHD from ∼ 0.5 to ∼ 0.2 both on linear and nonlinear test data (Figures
479   4a and 4b), and even better accuracy is achieved on post-nonlinear samples, as shown in Figure 4c.
480
481   Mixture of noise distributions. Next, we analyze the test performance of three CSIvA networks
482   optimized on samples from structural causal models that have different distributions for their noise
483   terms, while keeping the mechanism types fixed. Figure 5 shows that training over different noises
484   (beta, gamma, gumbel, exponential, mlp, uniform) always results in a network that is agnostic with
485   respect to the noise distributions of the SCM generating the test samples, always achieving SHD < 0.1,
      with the exception of datasets with mlp error terms (0.2 average SHD on linear and nonlinear data).

      5    C ONCLUSION
520
521   In this work, we investigate the interplay between identifiability theory and supervised learning for
522
      amortized inference of causal graphs, using CSIvA as the ground of our study. Consistent with
      classical algorithms, we demonstrate that good performance can be achieved if (i) we have a good
523
      prior on the structural causal model generating the test data (ii) the setting is identifiable. In particular,
524
      prior knowledge of the test distribution is encoded in the training data in the form of constraints on
525   the structural causal model underlying their generation. With these results, we highlight the need
526   for identifiability theory in modern learning-based approaches to causality, while past works have
527   mostly disregarded this connection. Further, our findings provide the theoretical ground for training
528   on observations sampled from multiple classes of identifiable SCMs, a strategy that improves test
529   generalization to a broad class of causal models. Finally, we highlight an interesting new trade-
530   off regarding identifiability: traditional methods like LiNGAM, RESIT, and PNL require strong
531   restrictions on the structural mechanisms underlying the data generation (linear, nonlinear additive,
532   or post-nonlinear) while generally being agnostic relative to the noise terms distribution. Training
533
      on mixtures of causal models instead offers an alternative that is less reliant on assumptions on the
      mechanisms, while incorporating knowledge about all possible noise distributions in the training data
534
      is practically impossible to achieve. We leave it to future work to reproduce our analysis on a wider
535
      class of architectures, as well as extending our study to interventional data with more than two nodes.

      A.2    CSI VA ARCHITECTURE
784
785   In this section, we summarize the architecture of CSIvA, a transformer neural network that can learn
786   a map from data to causally interpreted graphs, under supervised training.
787
788   Transformer neural network. Transformers (Vaswani et al., 2017) are a popular neural network
789   architecture for modeling structured, sequential data data. They consist of an encoder, a stack of
790   layers that learns a representation of each element in the input sequence based on its relation with
791   all the other sequence’s elements, through the mechanism of self-attention, and a decoder, which
792   maps the learned representation to the target of interest. Note that data for causal discovery are not
793   sequential in their nature, which motivates the adaptations introduced by Ke et al. (2023b) in their
794
      CSIvA architecture.
795
      CSIvA embeddings. Each element xji of an input dataset is embedded into a vector of dimension-
796
797
      ality E. Half of this vector is allocated to embed the value xji itself, while the other half is allocated
      to embed the unique identity for the node Xi . We use a node-specific embedding because the values
798
      of each node may have very different interpretations and meanings. The node identity embedding
799
      is obtained using a standard 1D transformer positional embedding over node indices. The value
800
      embedding is obtained by passing xji , through a multi-layer perceptron (MLP).
801
802
      CSIvA alternating attention. Similarly to the transformer’s encoder, CSIvA stacks a number of
803
      identical layers, performing self-attention followed by a nonlinear mapping, most commonly an
804   MLP layer. The main difference relative to the standard encoder is in the implementation of the
805   self-attention layer: as transformers are in their nature suitable for the representation of sequences,
806   given an input sample of D elements, self-attention is usually run across all elements of the sequence.
807   However, data for causal discovery are tabular, rather than sequential: one option would be to unravel
808   the n × d matrix of the data, where n is the number of observations and d the number of variables,
809   into a vector of n · d elements, and let this be the input sequence of the encoder. CSIvA adopts a
      different strategy: the self-attention in each encoder layer consists of alternate passes over the attribute

1174   D.5     E XPERIMENTS WITH BIVARIATE INDEPENDENT GRAPHS
1175
1176   In the main manuscript, we consider training and testing of CSIvA on bivariate graphs with an edge:
1177   X → Y , Y → X. This can be phrased as a classification problem with two labels. We motivate our
1178   choice by noticing that, in the bivariate setting, identifiability is a property of connected graphs: the
1179
       empty graph with no edge defines a Markov equivalence class with one element, i.e. a singleton. This
       is known to be identifiable without explicit assumptions on the functional form of the mechanisms
1180
       or the noise term distributions in the causal model. The goal of this section is to show that, if we
1181
       include datasets generated according to an empty graph in the training procedure, CSIvA can learn to
1182   disambiguate between the three classes (the empty graph, X → Y , Y → X). To motivate our claim,
1183   we notice that classifying empty and connected graphs can be done by testing independence between
1184   the input variables: previous works phrase independence testing as a classification task and show that
1185   this can be learned via deep neural networks (Bellot & van der Schaar, 2019; Sen et al., 2017). The
1186   experiments of Fig. 11 sustain our claim. We consider three CSIvA architectures, each trained on
1187   independent pairs and one between linear, nonlinear, or post-nonlinear data. Our results show that the
       neural network can learn to disambiguate between the three classes in all scenarios.

       D.6                      M IXED TRAINING WITH UNLIMITED BUDGET
1189
1190   We present our experimental results on one further question, to help clarify the results in the main text
1191   of the paper. We aim to understand when to make tradeoffs between computational resources, and
1192   having models that have been trained on a wider variety of SCMs. We compare training on multiple
1193   SCMs to single-SCM training, when all models see the same amount of training data from each SCM
1194   type as a non-mixed model (i.e. a mixed network trains on 15, 000 linear datasets and 15, 000 PNL
1195   datasets, instead of 15, 000 divided between the two SCM types).
1196   In the main text of this paper, we compare neural networks trained on a mix of structural causal
1197   models (e.g. noise distributions, or mechanism types), to models trained on a single mechanism-noise
1198   combination, where all models have the same amount of training data, 15, 000 datasets. In mixed
1199   training, we split these evenly, so a "lin, nl" model is trained on 7, 500 datasets from linear SCMs,
1200   and 7, 500 from nonlinear SCMs. Our results in this framework are promising, and show that for
1201   many combinations of SCM types, we can train one model instead of two, and achieve good progress,
1202   while making a 50% savings on training costs. However, if our training budget is high/unlimited, we
1203   should also ask whether we can we achieve the same performance as a model trained on a single
1204
       SCM type. Fig. 12 shows good results in this direction - the models trained with the same number of
       datasets per SCM type as an unmixed model had similar (or even better, for PNL data) performance
1205
       as the un-mixed model trained on the same SCM type as the test data. These mixed models are
1206
       also significantly more useful than having 2 or 3 separate models per SCM type, as they have good
1207   across-the-board performance. However, if we used the same computational resources to train 3
1208   separate networks (one for each mechanism type) and wanted to use them for causal discovery on a
1209   dataset with unknown assumptions, we would be left with the rather difficult task of deciding which
1210   model to trust.
