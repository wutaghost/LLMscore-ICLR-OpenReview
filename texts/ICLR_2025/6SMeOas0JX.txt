009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Text-to-3D shape generation has shown great promise in generating novel 3D con-
012            tent based on given text prompts. However, existing generative methods mostly
013            focus on geometric or visual plausibility while ignoring functionality for the gen-
014            erated 3D shapes. This greatly hinders the practicality of generated 3D shapes in
015            real-world applications. In this work, we propose Fun3D, a physics compliance
016            functional text-to-3D shape generation method. By analyzing the solid mechan-
017            ics of generated 3D shapes, we reveal that the 3D shapes generated by existing
018
               text-to-3D generation methods are impractical for real-world applications as the
               generated 3D shapes do not conform to the laws of physics. To this end, we lever-
019
               age 3D diffusion models to provide 3D shape priors and design a data-driven dif-
020
               ferentiable physics layer to optimize 3D shape priors with solid mechanics. This
021            allows us to optimize geometry efficiently and learn physics information about 3D
022            shapes at the same time. Experimental results demonstrate that our method can
023            consider both geometric plausibility and functional requirement, further bridging
024            3D virtual modeling and physical worlds.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Text-to-3D shape generation can synthesize novel 3D content based on text prompts. It is critical for
030   a variety of applications, including personalized customization, film-making, robotics simulation,
031   gaming, and so on. Recently, text-to-3D shape generation Poole et al. (2022); Lin et al. (2023);
032   Tang et al. (2023) have achieved promising results with the development of generative models (e.g.,
033
      generative adversarial networks Goodfellow et al. (2020) , diffusion models Ho et al. (2020); Rom-
      bach et al. (2022)) and 3D representations (e.g., DeepSDF Park et al. (2019) and neural radiance
034
      fields Mildenhall et al. (2021)). Existing text-to-3D generation methods mostly focus on the visual
035
      realism of generated 3D shapes.
036
037   The remarkable performance of text-to-3D shape generation has not only led to significant advance-
038   ments in the computer vision domain but has also generated substantial interest in real-world appli-
039
      cations, such as the engineering design Guo et al. (2023); Rios et al. (2023). In contrast to computer
      vision, where the primary focus is on the visual aspects of 3D objects, real-world design prob-
040
      lems demand additional considerations for physical functionality in real-world environments. For
041
      instance, in the design of mechanical components, it is crucial to incorporate features such as cham-
042   fered corners to mitigate stress concentration and avoid vertical transitions to enhance component
043   longevity Sackfield et al. (2013). These requirements necessitate the integration of physical princi-
044   ples with visual aesthetics to ensure that the generated 3D objects are both practical and functional
045   in real-world applications. However, most current methods fail to incorporate physical information,
046   leading to 3D objects that are unfunctional for real-world environments. More seriously, due to
047   physical deficiencies, the generative model may even generate ill-defined or unrealistic 3D-shapes,
048   further obstructing the generative capability.
049   Real-world functionality requirements such as engineering designs commonly account for the in-
050   teraction between 3D geometry and the physical environment, with physical information being key
051   to describing this interaction. Given the importance of physics, this work emphasizes incorporating
052   physical information into the generative process. Specifically, we treat the object described by the
053   3D geometry as a solid and incorporate basic solid mechanics properties into our analysis. Thus,
      we conform the 3D geometry to linear elasticity Bertram & Glüge (2015) behavior. In Figure 1,

076
      In this paper, we propose Fun3D, a physics compliance text-to-3D shape generation method to
077
      achieve the functional generation. Specifically, our method contains two stages. In the first stage,
078
      we initialize a 3D shape based on 3D diffusion models and convert it into implicit geometry rep-
079   resentations, Signed Distance Function (SDF) Park et al. (2019). This allows our approach to be
080   flexibly combined with existing generative methods. In the second stage, we design a data-driven
081   differentiable physics layer to enable geometry and physics optimization at the same time. Our dif-
082   ferentiable physics layer is parameterized by neural networks and used to learn the solid mechanics
083   of generated 3D shapes on demand when optimizing geometries. To guarantee the precision of the
084   feedback physics information, we introduce a physics prior embedding which is achieved by lever-
085   aging the FEM results computed for the initial generated 3D shape to initialize the differentiable
086   physics layer. We introduce a relaxed geometry loss and a series of physics losses during training to
087
      guide the optimization. Moreover, we design an alternating training strategy to learn geometry and
      physics information collaboratively. In this way, our method can simultaneously optimize geometry
088
      and learn the physics of generated 3D shapes, considering both the geometric plausibility and the
089
      functionality requirement, i.e., “What you get is what you text, and what you get is immediately
090   functional”.
091
092   Overall, our contributions are summarized as follows:
093
      • We propose Fun3D, i.e., a physics compliance text-to-3D functional shape generation method, to
094
        incorporate the physical laws into generated 3D shapes. This approach bridges the gap between
095     3D virtual modeling and the physical world, ensuring that the generated shapes are both visually
096     appealing and physically functional.
097
      • We design a data-driven differentiable physical layer to learn physics cues during training effec-
098
        tively. Moreover, an alternating training strategy is introduced to stabilize the learning of physics.
099     Thus, our method can collaboratively optimize geometry and physics flexibly and reliably.
100
      • Experiments demonstrate that our method can improve geometric or visual preferences without
101
        requiring extra training data and manual interaction and endow generated 3D shapes with physical
102
        perception capabilities.

107   Text-to-3D Shape Generation Recently, text-to-3D shape generation has achieved significant suc-
      cess. On the one hand, some generation methods train their models on text-3D pairs and can directly

      Therefore, these methods do not require any 3D data. However, whether these generation methods
120   train their models on 3D data or text-to-image priors, there is no guarantee that they can perceive
121   physics information from large amounts of data. This work shows that our method can learn physics
122   properties for the generated 3D shapes during training.
123
      Physics-aware 3D Generation There exist several physics-aware 3D generation meth-
124
      ods Mezghanni et al. (2021; 2022); Liu et al. (2023b); Wang & He (2019); Xie et al. (2024); Zhong
125
      et al. (2024); Guo et al. (2024); Chen et al. (2024). These methods utilize either offline simulations
126   or online simulations to ensure the physical validity of generated shapes. Mezghanni et al. (2021)
127   pre-trains a surrogate network using offline simulations to predict stability to inform the generative
128   learning. Mezghanni et al. (2022); Liu et al. (2023b) design online simulation layers to provide
129   physical guidance for the generative learning. All these methods do not need to learn physics feed-
130   back during shape learning. Unlike them, our method collaboratively learns geometry and physics
131   during training.
132   Physics-Informed Machine Learning The simulation and modeling of physical systems have
133   long played a crucial role in various scientific and engineering domains Heermann & Heermann
134   (1990). Traditionally, the FEM has been instrumental in these tasks. However, recent advancements
135   in deep neural networks have prompted researchers to explore novel applications in these domains
136   Raissi (2018). Among these methods, PINNs have garnered significant attention Raissi et al. (2019).
137   PINNs stand out due to their ability to integrate governing equations of mechanics, often expressed
138   through PDEs, into the learning objectives of neural networks. This integration ensures the network
139   outputs align with the fundamental governing physics. Consequently, there has been a proliferation
140
      of studies on PINNs in recent years Deng et al. (2023); Chiu et al. (2022); Mao et al. (2020); Wang
      et al. (2023). While several methods have been proposed, the majority of research on PINNs primar-
141
      ily focuses on simulating physical systems. There is a limited number of discussions on optimization
142
      based on PINNs, such as physics-informed inverse design Lu et al. (2021), PDE constrained opti-
143   mization Hao et al. (2022), and PINN-assisted topology optimization Jeong et al. (2023); Yin et al.
144   (2023). However, most of these works concentrate on optimizing simple functions or 2D geometries.
145   None of them address the optimization of 3D geometries.

                          189          Geometry Representations. Our method consists of two key representations, geometry and
                          190          physics. In order to allow our geometry to support physics perception, the geometry representa-
                          191          tion should have the following properties.
                          192
                          193          • Differentiability of geometry: The geometry representation should be differentiable to allow for
                          194            end-to-end back-propagation during training.
                          195
                                       • Scalability of geometry: The geometry representation should be scalable to allow for fine-grained
                          196
                                         sampling in the entire 3D space.
                          197
                          198          • Discriminability of interior and exterior: For physics, the solid mechanics will work on geometry
                          199            surfaces and interior. Therefore, the geometry representation should make it easy to distinguish
                          200            between interior and exterior.
                          201
                          202          To this end, we employ an implicit SDF Park et al. (2019) encoded by neural networks to represent
                          203
                                       our geometry. It is naturally differentiable and can be evaluated at different resolutions. For any 3D
                          204          point x, its SDF value fˆS (x) represents the signed distance to the surfaces. That is, if the output SDF
                          205
                                       is a negative value, the corresponding point will be outside the object; otherwise, the corresponding
                                       point will be inside. Therefore, it is easy for point x to distinguish between interior and exterior with
                          206
                                       its SDF sign.

                          209          that the physics network can easily understand. Inspired by topology optimization Bendsoe &

      Geometry Initialization. To initialize our geometry representations, we use a 3D diffusion model
217   to generate an initial 3D shape M based on the text prompts T . In general, 3D diffusion models
218   will output meshes to represent 3D shapes because mesh representations provide a clear topological
219   structure, making them convenient for operations and analysis. To allow our method for the subse-
220   quent optimization with SDF, we convert the mesh M to an initial SDF finit by training a geometry
221   network Gθ parameterized by θ. This geometry network transfers the shape prior information from
222   3D diffusion models, thus we can focus on optimizing this geometry network in the next stage.

224   3.3   P HYSICS R EPRESENTATIONS AND G OVERNING E QUATIONS

226   Physics Quantities. To describe the solid mechanics of geometry, we consider three fundamental
227   physical quantities within the context of physics Bertram & Glüge (2015).
228
229   • Displacement: Representing the shift of any point within a geometric structure, displacement is
230     characterized by a 3D vector u(x) = (u1 (x), u2 (x), u3 (x)).
231   • Strain: Strain serves as a measure of deformation, depicting the relative displacement between
232     points within a geometric body. Typically, strain at a point is denoted by a second-order tensor
233     ϵ(x) = {(ϵij (x))|i, j = 1, 2, 3}.
234
      • Stress: Stress encapsulates the internal forces exerted by neighboring points within a continuous
235     geometric entity. The Cauchy stress tensor, often employed to describe stress, is denoted as
236     σ(x) = {(σij (x))|i, j = 1, 2, 3}.
237
238   Moreover, it is important to acknowledge that the applicability of physical principles is often contin-
239   gent upon specific external environmental factors, typically referred to as boundary conditions. In
240   this study, the boundary conditions for a given geometry are defined based on typical usage scenar-
241   ios associated with the object. For example, in the case of a table, an external force may be applied
242   to its top surface in alignment with practical functional requirements.
243   Governing Equations. Governing equations serve as a foundational set of equations, often com-
244   prising ordinary or partial differential equations, to articulate the behavior of a physical system. As
245   mentioned in Sec. 1, in this work, our focus lies primarily on the classical linear elastic system,
246   which aptly characterizes elastic bodies under conditions of minimal deformation. The following
247   equations delineate this system:

272   Physics Prior Embedding. To enhance physics compliance guidance in geometry optimization, a
273   high-precision simulator capable of providing accurate physical insights is essential. FEM stands
274   out as a classical and reliable approach, renowned for its accuracy in capturing physical phenom-
275   ena, particularly in simple systems such as linear elastic setups. However, FEM-based simulators
276   often lack differentiability, which presents challenges when integrating them into online geometry
277   optimization workflows. Physics-informed machine learning has recently emerged as a promising
278
      alternative to traditional physical simulations. The differentiability of neural network architectures
      enables seamless integration into online geometry optimization processes. Despite this advantage,
279
      physics-informed machine learning is still evolving, and current implementations face difficulties in
280
      achieving highly precise simulations for complex 3D geometries. To address this issue, we introduce
281   a technique called physics prior embedding. This approach leverages the inherent differentiability
282   of neural networks while enhancing the precision of physics predictions by incorporating FEM data
283   into the training process of the physics-informed layers. By combining the differentiability of neural
284   networks with the high-precision physical information provided by FEM, this technique facilitates
285   effective geometric optimization, ensuring accurate physics guidance throughout the optimization
286   process.

533
534   In this paper, we have proposed a physics compliance text-to-3D functional shape generation method
535   to endow generated 3D shapes with physics perception capability. Our work reveals that the 3D
536   shapes generated by existing text-to-3D generative methods do not conform to the laws of physics.
537   Therefore, current generated 3D shapes are impractical in many real-world applications. To address
538   this, we design a data-driven physics layer to learn physics information on demand when optimizing
539   geometries. This guides our generated 3D shapes to satisfy both visual preferences and physical
      functionality, opening up a new avenue for text-to-3D shape generation.

      A.4   I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
841
842
843   Our method is implemented by PyTorch Paszke et al. (2019) on two Teslia V100 GPUs with 32MiB
844   graphics memory. The 3D diffusion model used in our method to obtain the initial 3D mesh is
845   Diffusion-SDF Li et al. (2023). This model mainly generates two object types: chair and table. In
846   addition, we also validate our method using other 3D diffusion model, Shap-E Jun & Nichol (2023)
847   and Zero123 Liu et al. (2023a).
848   Our geometry network Gθ is modeled by an 8-layer multi-layer perceptron (MLP) with 256 hidden
849   units. It is initialized by the geometric initialization Atzmon & Lipman (2020). To transfer the shape
850   priors from 3D diffusion models to the geometry network, we follow DeepSDF Park et al. (2019)
851   to train our geometry network. The geometry network is trained for 50 epochs, with each epoch
852   comprising 200 training steps. A batch size of 16,384 SDF samples is employed, and the learning
853   rate is set to 5e-4.
854   Our physics network Uϕ is modeled by a 6-layer MLP with 125 hidden units. To train the physics
855   networks efficiently and effectively, a dense-sparse strategy is employed to partition computation
856   regions into “dense” regions where physics precision is crucial, and “sparse” regions where physics
857   precision can be relaxed (i.e.regions distant from the mesh). The dense-sparse ratio is set at 0.3, with
858   the dense regions being defined as regions where finit (x) < 0.10. The physics network is trained
859   for 10,000 epochs with a learning rate 5e-3.
860   The co-training process spans 1000 epochs, with a learning rate of 1e-5 set for both the physics
861   network and the geometry network. The weights corresponding to Lpde , Lbc , Lf em , Lcombine , Lgc ,
862   and Leikonal are set to 1, 10, 10, 20, 10, and 10, respectively. The t in Algorithm 1 is set to 10. In
863   terms of the FEM data, we provide the displacement and stress values for the tetrahedral with 7000
      nodes. All these data are obtained using Abaqus Barbero (2023).

      A.8   L IMITATION AND F UTURE W ORK
919
920   While our method can generate high-quality 3D shapes with the assistance of precise physics data,
921   there are still some limitations: 1) Efficiency. We use vanilla MLPs to parameterize our geometry
922   and physics representations. This compromises the efficiency of our approach. Inspired by Instant-
923   NGP Müller et al. (2022), we can leverage multi-resolution hash encoding to parameterize these two
924   representations to improve our efficiency. 2) Precision of Physics. The precision of our physics in-
925   formation depends in part on the number of sampling points, following the similar reason explained
926
      in Chiu et al. (2022). In the future, we may increase the number of sampling points to boost the
      precision of physics information using more efficient geometry and physics representations.
