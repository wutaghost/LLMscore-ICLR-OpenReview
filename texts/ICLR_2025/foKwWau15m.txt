                                                              A BSTRACT
033
                   Ultra-low bitrate image compression is a challenging and demanding topic. With
034
                   the development of Large Multimodal Models (LMMs), a Cross Modality Com-
035                pression (CMC) paradigm of Image-Text-Image has emerged. Compared with
036                traditional codecs, this semantic-level compression can reduce image data size
037                to 0.1% or even lower, which has strong potential applications. However, CMC
038                has certain defects in consistency with the original image and perceptual quality.
039                To inspire insights into such a problem, we introduce CMC-Bench, a benchmark
040                of the cooperative performance of Image-to-Text (I2T) and Text-to-Image (T2I)
041                models for image compression. This benchmark covers 18,000 and 40,000 images
042                respectively to verify 6 mainstream I2T and 12 T2I models, including 160,000
043
                   subjective preference scores annotated by human experts. At ultra-low bitrates, it
                   proves that the combination of some I2T and T2I models has surpassed the most
044
                   advanced visual signal codecs; meanwhile, it highlights where LMMs can be fur-
045
                   ther optimized toward the compression task. We encourage LMM developers to
046                participate in this test to promote the evolution of visual signal codec protocols.
047

049   1      I NTRODUCTION
050
051   Visual signal compression aims to minimize image data, playing a crucial role in delivering high-
052   quality image/video services with limited network resources and storage capacity. Since the MPEG-
053   1 (Le Gall, 1991) standard was introduced, compression rates for visual signals have doubled (Sko-
      dras et al., 2001; Wiegand et al., 2003; Sullivan et al., 2012; Bross et al., 2021) every decade. In


                                                                    1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      recent years, traditional image codecs have achieved a 500 times compression rate while ensuring
055   a decent visual experience for humans. However, traditional codecs are approaching the Shannon
056   limit of 1,000 times Compression Rate (CR) in the upcoming next-generation protocols. Fortunately,
057   the rapid development of Large Multimodal Models (LMMs) has opened up possibilities for such
058   Ultra-low-Bitrate (ULB) compression.
059
      Why use LMMs for compression? LMMs support the conversion between multiple modalities,
060
      where text consumes much less space than image modalities. By cascading Image-to-Text (I2T)
061   and Text-to-Image (T2I) models, images can be compressed and reconstructed from semantic infor-
062   mation. This Cross-Modality Compression (CMC) paradigm operates at the semantic level, which
063   outperforms traditional codecs at the pixel level. It enables easy attainment of ULB, and even the
064   Extreme-low Bitrate (ELB) for CR about 10,000 times. However, at such low bitrates, CMC presents
065   two significant issues that cannot be overlooked. (1) Consistency: The reconstruction process heav-
066   ily relies on intermediate textual information. Any omission by the I2T model (encoder) or mis-
067   understanding by the T2I model (decoder) can result in severe distortion. Unlike minor changes in
068   brightness and color, this can lead to a semantic-level inversion of the entire image. (2) Perception:
069
      Textual encoding provides a coarse representation of the image, necessitating the T2I model to add
      details. Insufficient details degrade perceptual quality, while excessive ones compromise faithful-
070
      ness to the original image. Unfortunately, as the bitrate decreases, the conflict between them (Blau
071
      & Michaeli, 2018; 2019) becomes more pronounced. As the consistency and perception failure
072   cases in Figure 1, these issues jointly limit the application of CMC.
073
074   For LMMs, there is a lack of effective evaluation criteria both in terms of consistency and percep-
075
      tual aspects. Although numerous benchmarks have recently emerged for LMMs, they are primarily
      designed to assess the performance of either I2T (Image-to-Text) or T2I (Text-to-Image) models
076
      working alone, such as captioning/visual question answering for I2T (Liu et al., 2024b; Li et al.,
077
      2023a), or generation quality/realism for T2I (Bakr et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2023). Consequently,
078   we introduce the first joint benchmark called CMC-Bench, aimed at testing the collaborative capa-
079   bilities of I2T and T2I models. Our contributions include:
080
081          • A large-scale dataset consists of 58,000 images using the CMC paradigm. 4,000 images
082            among them have 160,000 expert annotations, covering both consistency and perception
083            issues, paving the way for information loss modeling in the I2T and T2I processes.
084          • A comprehensive evaluation standards, consisting of four compression modes under differ-
085            ent requirements, along with the two dimensions mentioned above. We validate mainstream
086            models (including 6 I2T and 12 T2I) to explore optimal combinations.
087          • A throughout comparison with traditional codecs. We compared the benchmark winner
088            with existing image codecs, revealing the significant advantages of the CMC image com-
089            pression paradigm and some remaining drawbacks. We encourage LMM developers (both
090            I2T and T2I) to participate in CMC-Bench to further expand the application of CMC.

      2   R ELATED W ORKS
093
094
      Cross-Modality Compression. The earliest CMC method (Li et al., 2021) emerged in 2021, achiev-
095
      ing a compression ratio of almost 10,000 times through text modality. However, as a simple com-
096   bination of I2T and T2I models, their results often exhibit noticeable differences from the original
097   images. Subsequently, Text+Sketch (Lei et al., 2023) employed edge operators and ControlNet
098   (Zhang et al., 2023a) to refine CMC, but its consistency remained inferior to traditional codecs. The
099   most advanced CMC methods, like M-CMC and MISC (Gao et al., 2024b;a; Mao et al., 2024; Xue
100   et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024a;c), have surpassed advanced codecs like VVC (Bross et al., 2021) in
101   both consistency and perception, indicating the promising future of this paradigm. Nevertheless,
102   there is still room for improvement in these two aspects. All existing CMC methods are from one
103   specific I2T and one T2I model, and the models used are relatively outdated. Considering the rapid
104
      development of Generative-AI, how to combine the latest models towards a better CMC becomes an
      unrevealed question.
105

107   models. For I2T, they usually take a specific image sequence as input, compare the text output
      by LMM with the ground truth, and use the relevance of the two as a performance indicator. The

126   Benchmark for Image Compression. Given the significance of visual information compression,
127   several related competitions (Ballé et al., 2020; Gu et al., 2022) have been held in recent years.
128
      However, these competitions often limit their scope to Natural Scene Images (NSIs). Screen Con-
      tent Images (SCIs) (Ni et al., 2017; Min et al., 2017), which are prevalent on the internet, and the
129
      emerging AI-Generated Images (AIGIs) (Li et al., 2023c; Xu et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023c) have
130
      received some attention with new datasets, but no existing dataset comprehensively considers them
131   together. Moreover, the performance evaluation of compression algorithms can be challenging, of-
132   ten requiring subjective quality assessments from human viewers to train Image Quality Assessment
133   (IQA) (Li et al., 2023b; 2022; Zhang et al., 2024c;d; 2023f;d; Li et al., 2024f; Zhang et al., 2024b)
134   models, which provide objective metrics for compression algorithms. In the context of ULB image
135   compression, both the consistency between the distorted and reference images, as well as the inher-
136   ent appeal of the distorted image in human perception, need to be annotated. Existing IQA datasets
137   typically annotate only one aspect, while often in a coarse-grained manner through Single Stimulus
138   (SS) or Double Stimulus (DS) comparisons. In contrast, Mean Opinion Score (MOS) derived from
139
      multiple subjects offers a more detailed and objective evaluation as shown in Table 1.

180   3.2     C OMPRESSION M ODE
181
182   Drawing on previous work in CMC, we categorize CMC into four working modes, as shown in
183   Figure 3. Each type employs distinct configurations and is suitable for different scenarios:
184
      Text: The I2T model converts images to text and is directly restored by the T2I model. Due to its
185
      reliance on the text modality only, this approach achieves a CR of 10,000, ideal for ELB situations.
186
187   Pixel: Each 64 × 64 blocks from ground truth are merged and quantized into one pixel. Beyond
188   the Text mode, these pixels initialize the T2I process. The pixel representation is relatively compact,
189
      offering a CR of around 5,000, suitable for less rigorous ELB but higher demands on consistency.
190   Image: Traditional codecs are employed to compress the image, which serves as input for the T2I
191   model for enhancement. Unlike the previous two, it omits the time-consuming I2T process by
192   leaving the text input of the T2I model empty. This approach can achieve a CR of 1,000, suitable
193   for ULB bandwidth but with high real-time requirements.
194   Full: Extending the Image mode, the T2I is guided by text content, encompassing the full pipeline
195   of I2T, traditional codec, and T2I. It also has a CR of approximately 1,000, suitable for the most
196   demanding performance scenarios.

      3.4   H UMAN P REFERENCE A NNOTATION
244
245
      Referring from previous large-scale subjective annotation (Ballé et al., 2020) methods, we do not
      perform coarse-grained labeling on the entire dataset considering the limitation on annotator num-
246
      bers. Instead, we fine-grain the annotations on 4,000 images to ensure multiple ratings for each
247
      image. Note that, as the benchmark indicator should be adjusted on subjective data, we did not
248   directly select subsets from the 58,000 test images. Instead, we generated new images to prevent
249   prior exposure to the content being evaluated. Given the greater impact of T2I models on CMC
250   tasks than I2T models, we follow the T2I paradigm described in Section 3.3. The I2T model is fixed
251   as GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2023) and combined with 12 different T2I models, compressing 100 ground
252   truth into 4,000 distorted images. To ensure quality diversity, we randomly assigned strength from
253   0.2 to 0.9 rather than a fixed value. Each distorted image is paired with its corresponding ground
254   truth and shown to 20 experienced participants who provided ratings on consistency and perception
255   dimensions. Each image is then summarized into two overall scores from 0 to 5, combining all
256
      participants’ feedback. For a detailed description of the experimental setup and data process, please
      refer to the appendix.

529   5                        C ONCLUSION
530
531   We construct CMC-Bench, a benchmark for assessing the collaborative functioning of I2T and T2I
532   models in image compression. Anticipating the bitrate requirements for codecs in the next decade,
533   we proposed four collaboration modes among LMMs, along with two indicators of consistency
534   and perception. By employing 6 mainstream I2T and 12 T2I models, we collected 58,000 dis-
535   torted images through CMC with 160,000 human subjective annotations to train objective metrics
536   for comprehensive evaluation. Our assessment demonstrates that even without dedicated training for
537   compression tasks, combinations of several advanced I2T and T2I models have already surpassed
538
      traditional codecs in multiple aspects. However, there is still a long way to go before LMMs can
      directly become the future codecs paradigm. We sincerely hope that CMC-Bench will inspire future
539
      LMMs to perform better compression towards the evolution of visual signal codecs.

      A.1   L IMITATIONS AND S OCIAL I MPACT
865
866   Limitation 1: Although we have considered most of the mainstream I2T and T2I models in CMC-
867   Bench (till March 2024), the number of models is still insufficient to fully characterize the perfor-
868   mance of all current LMMs on CMC. Taking the open-source T2 model as an example, more than
869   20,000 models have been released on huggingface (till May 2024). Although we cannot run all
870   models, the capabilities of some relatively unpopular or more advanced LMMs in the future need to
871   be further updated on CMC-Bench.
872   Limitation 2: CMC-Bench is currently designed for the performance verification of image com-
873   pression, not video compression. Considering that the temporal information of videos is relatively
874   complex, the current LMMs are only applicable to image compression, which makes it difficult to
875   ensure consistency with the reference when generating videos. However, as LMMs gradually apply
876   to video compression in the future, CMC-Bench will also be evaluated at the video level.
877
      Social Impact: Through the CMC paradigm, the size of the image can be compressed by 1,000
878   times, and even 10,000 times in extreme cases. This will effectively promote image communication
879   between a large number of terminals under limited bandwidth, thereby realizing multi-device collab-
880   oration in the Internet of Things and semantic communication. Considering that traditional codecs
881   have encountered bottlenecks after three decades of development and the compression rate is grad-
882   ually approaching the Shannon limit, we believe that LMM will effectively achieve semantic-level
883   compression and thus become the future evolution direction of visual information codec protocol.
884   From an industry perspective, CMC is ready for the following two real-world scenarios. (1) Commu-
885   nication: In severe scenarios, channel resources are extremely limited, such as deep sea and space;
886   or there are too many devices, that is, hundreds of devices in IoT share a local area network. At
887   this time, traditional compression methods cannot adapt to such a low bit rate and can only com-
888   municate images through CMC; (2) Storage: According to statistics from mainstream social media,
889   10% of visual information contributes to 99% of views, and most images are ‘junk data’. For these
890   images that are rarely clicked but not suitable for deletion, their storage will consume considerable
891   resources. Therefore, they can be compressed in CMC format and decompressed when needed. With
892
      the advancement of LMM, models with lower complexity have emerged in recent years. We believe
      CMC can move from these two applications without latency requirements to real-time scenarios.

       each model, we choose the output length closest to this bitrate, that is, Q(20) for GPT-4o (OpenAI,

       ShareGPT (Chen et al., 2023b); and the default length for InstructBLIP (Dai et al., 2023).

1162   A.7          F URTHER E XPERIMENTAL A NALYSIS
1163
1164   A.7.1              T2I S TRUCTURE
1165
1166   The CMC-Bench mainly discussed the diffusion structure for T2I models instead of Auto-Regressive
1167
       (AR). This is not because the AR performance metrics are insufficient, but because generating pixels
       one by one leads to a higher complexity. We acknowledge that the AR model is more controllable
1168
       and may sometimes achieve better performance than the Diffusion model, but it requires a massive
1169
       amount of parameters for the T2I model and longer decoding times. Work Sun et al. (2024) shows
1170   AR generating a 384*384 image takes more than 6 seconds, which is enough time to generate several
1171   1024 resolution images with a Diffusion model whose size is less than 1B. Considering the real-time
1172   nature of visual communication, we believe that the Diffusion model is more suitable for CMC.
1173
       Among the Diffusion architectures, their performance requires a case-by-case discussion, including
1174
       the Restoration, SDXL, and SD architectures. The leaderboard in Figure A3 shows that on the ULB,
1175
       the order is Restoration > SD > SDXL, because Restoration fine-tunes based on the original image,
1176   SD uses the original image as the starting point to draw, and SDXL, compared to SD, has more
1177   freedom to make changes, which can lead to excessive modifications. On the ELB, the order is
1178   SDXL > SD, because without a reference image, Restoration is directly unavailable. Compared to
1179   SDXL’s aggressive strategy, which becomes an advantage, it can produce more details.
1180

1183   We considered some more advanced T2I models, including two I2T (Gemini, InternLM-
1184
       XComposer2) and two T2I (Clandestine, SUPIR). Their performance is shown in Figure A8. It can
       be seen that these models have not yet shaken the leading position of GPT4-o as I2T and DiffBIR
1185
       as T2I. We welcome more I2T/T2I developers to participate in the test.

1188
       The consistency and perception have always been a dilemma in the image compression task. For
1189   low-bitrate image compression (< 0.1 bpp) Blau & Michaeli (2018), the compression algorithm
1190   provides a rough encoding of the original image, necessitating the decoder to add details. Inad-
1191   equate detail leads to poor perceptual quality, while excessive detail results in inconsistency with
1192   the original image. As bitrates decrease further to ultra-low levels (< 0.024 bpp) Blau & Michaeli
1193   (2019), the conflict between these two objectives becomes even more intensified.
1194
       Therefore, ensuring both consistency and perceptual quality at such low bitrates remains a challenge.
1195   When using LMM for compression, adding how many details, is the decisive factor for such a trade-
1196   off. Thus, we believe the CMC-Bench enables LMM developers with comprehensive metrics that
1197   cater to both objectives.

1199   A.7.4    U SER STUDY
1200
1201   To verify the practicality of CMC in real-life scenarios, we conduct a subjective user study beyond
1202
       the objective indicators, to analyze the human preference for the compressed image. We established
       an environment with standard lighting, displaying the ground truth centrally, and two compressed
1203
       images on a monitor with a × 2,304 resolution. Viewers are required to select preferences between
1204
       two images compressed by different algorithms, at both consistency and perception levels. The
1205   experiment involved 5 graduate students (2 males and 3 females) as subjects. CMC, using the Full
1206   mode of GPT-4o+DiffBIR, is compared with four state-of-art compression metrics, namely CHENG
1207   Cheng et al. (2020), CDC Yang & Mandt (2023), HiFiC Mentzer et al. (2020), and PICS Lei et al.
1208   (2023). Same bitrate is set for all those metrics for a fair comparison. The validation results il-
1209   lustrated in Figure A9 demonstrate the superior performance of MISC across all evaluated criteria.
1210   Notably, CMC performs comparably to the PICS for consistency, and HiFiC for perception. Further-
1211   more, compared to NSIs, AIGIs compressed by MISC were more preferred by human evaluators.
