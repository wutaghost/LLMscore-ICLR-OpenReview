009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Deep neural networks (DNNs) are widely used today, but they are vulnerable to
012            adversarial attacks. To develop effective methods of defense, it is important to
013            understand the potential weak spots of DNNs. Often attacks are organized taking
014            into account the architecture of models (white-box approach) and based on gradient
015            methods, but for real-world DNNs this approach in most cases is impossible. At
016            the same time, several gradient-free optimization algorithms are used to attack
017            black-box models. However, classical methods are often ineffective in the multidi-
018
               mensional case. To organize black-box attacks for computer vision models, in this
               work, we propose the use of an optimizer based on the low-rank tensor train (TT)
019
               format, which has gained popularity in various practical multidimensional applica-
020
               tions in recent years. Combined with the attribution of the target image, which is
021            built by the auxiliary (white-box) model, the TT-based optimization method makes
022            it possible to organize an effective black-box attack by small perturbation of pixels
023            in the target image. The superiority of the proposed approach over three popular
024            baselines is demonstrated for seven modern DNNs on the ImageNet dataset.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   In recent years, extensive research has been carried out on the topic of adversarial attacks on DNNs,
030   including for classification problems in computer vision as described, for example, in reviews (Qiu
031   et al., 2019; Akhtar et al., 2021; Chakraborty et al., 2021). New successful adversarial attacks allow us
032   to better understand the functioning of DNNs and develop new effective methods of protection against
033
      real attacks in various practical applications. White-box attacks, which assume that the internal
      structure of the attacked DNN is known and the gradient of the target output can be calculated, are
034
      very successful in many cases. However, for real-world models, a black-box attack is much more
035
      relevant, in which information about the internal structure of the DNN is not used, and it is only
036   possible to make requests to the network and obtain the corresponding outputs.
037
038   In the case of black-box attacks, gradient-free optimization methods (Larson et al., 2019) are most
039
      often used to find the optimal perturbation of the target image, which leads to a change in the model’s
      prediction. These methods are based either on constructing an estimate of the gradient of the objective
040
      function followed by conventional gradient descent, or on various sampling heuristics, including
041
      genetic algorithms and evolutionary strategies. However, in the case of a substantially multidimen-
042   sional search space, these methods may not be effective enough, and it becomes attractive to use the
043   TT-format (Oseledets, 2011), which is a universal approach for manipulating multidimensional data
044   arrays (tensors).
045
      The TT-format provides a wide range of methods for constructing surrogate models and effectively
046
      performing linear algebra operations (solution of linear systems, integration, convolution, etc.).
047
      Recently it was shown in (Sozykin et al., 2022; Chertkov et al., 2022) that the TT-format can also be
048   successfully applied for optimization problems, and the quality of the solution in many cases turns
049   out to be higher than for methods based on alternative gradient-free approaches. Therefore, in this
050   work, we propose the use of a new TT-based optimization method PROTES (Batsheva et al., 2023) for
051   black-box attacks. To further speed up the computation procedure, we build an attribution map for the
052   target image by a popular Integrated Gradients method (Sundararajan et al., 2017) using an auxiliary
053   pre-trained DNN for which gradients are assumed to be available. In terms of an untargeted attack,
      we select a set of pixels that have the highest attribution value, and then only change them during the

138

139

140   TT-format has become widespread in various practical applications (Cichocki et al., 2017; Chertkov
141   & Oseledets, 2021; Khrulkov et al., 2023), including surrogate modeling, solving differential and
142   integral equations, acceleration and compression of neural networks, etc.

145
146   TT-format can be applied for black-box optimization problems. In the recent work (Sozykin et al.,
147   2022) the TTOpt algorithm was proposed and its superiority over many popular optimization ap-
148   proaches, including genetic algorithms and evolutionary strategies, was shown. A more powerful
149   algorithm PROTES was presented in later work (Batsheva et al., 2023). The applicability of these
150   methods to various problems was considered in (Nikitin et al., 2022; Chertkov et al., 2022; 2023b;
151   Morozov et al., 2023; Belokonev et al., 2023).

191   2.3   ATTRIBUTION OF N EURAL N ETWORKS
192
193   Attribution plays an important role in interpreting artificial intelligence models (Zhang et al., 2021;
194   Matveev et al., 2021). It allows for a given input image x ∈ Rd to construct an attribution map, that
195   is, the vector a ∈ Rd , such that a[i] is the degree of significance of the i-th pixel on the model score
196   f(x) ≡ F (x)[c] for the image class prediction c. In the context of adversarial attacks, attribution is of
197   interest because it allows us to select only a part dˆ of the pixels of the image based on their semantic
198   significance for the attack.
199   The Saliency Map method (Simonyan et al., 2013) is a basic approach to white-box model attribution
200   in which the derivative of the DNN output is computed for each pixel of the input image, i.e.,
201                                          ∂ f(x)

207   of linear transformations of the image x into a baseline x′ (a completely black image or a noise-

      2.4    B LACK - BOX A DVERSARIAL ATTACKS
217
218   Black-box attacks can be divided into query-based attacks and transfer-based attacks. White-box
219   auxiliary models and existing white-box attacks are often used for transfer-based attacks to generate
220   adversarial perturbations, which are then fed to the target DNN in a black-box setting. The effective-
221   ness of such attacks depends heavily on transferability across different models, and it seems unlikely
222   that a great deal of knowledge can be extracted from one auxiliary model. As the auxiliary model
223   is trained to be representative of an attacked DNN in terms of its classification rules, it becomes
224   computationally expensive and hardly feasible when attacking large models. To increase the transfer-
225
      ability of such attacks, various methods are proposed to escape from poor local optima of white-box
      optimizer (Dong et al., 2018) or increase the diversity of candidates with augmentation-based methods
226
      (Wang & He, 2021).
227
228   In the case of query-based attacks, only interaction with the target model is carried out to generate an
229   adversarial example, and various methods for approximate gradient construction followed by gradient
230   descent (Ilyas et al., 2018), or gradient-free heuristics (Su et al., 2019; Andriushchenko et al., 2020;
231
      Pomponi et al., 2022) can be used. The heuristic-based approach is usually more query-efficient since
      estimating the gradient (with finite differences or stochastic coordinate descent) requires additional
232
      queries to the DNN.
233
234   We classify our method TETRADAT as a query-based method with gradient-free heuristics, despite
235   the use of an auxiliary white-box model, since we need it only to identify the semantic features of the
236   image and its internal structure (provided that the semantic map is correct) has little impact on the
237
      quality of our results. Therefore, as the closest alternative approaches we consider Onepixel, Square,
      and Pixle methods.
238
239   The Onepixel attack algorithm (Su et al., 2019) applies the differential evolution (Storn & Price,
240   1997) to search the suitable pixels and only modify them to perturb target images. The vector norm
241   of perturbation when using this method is rather small, but attacks may be perceptible to people since
242   the changes in the image are very intense and irregular.
243   The Square attack algorithm (Andriushchenko et al., 2020) performs randomly selected squared
244   perturbations, i.e., it selects localized square-shaped updates of the target image at random positions
245   so that at each iteration the perturbation is situated approximately at the boundary of the feasible set.
246   This method turns out to be query efficient, but the distortion it introduces into the target image is in
247   many cases easily distinguishable visually.
248   The Pixle attack algorithm (Pomponi et al., 2022) generates adversarial examples by rearranging a
249   small number of pixels using random search, i.e., a patch of pixels is iteratively sampled from the
250   target image, and then some pixels within it are rearranged using a predefined heuristic function.
251   The specificity of image changes in this approach potentially makes it possible to obtain adversarial
252   examples that are practically visually indistinguishable from the original image, however, in some
253   cases this heuristic may not work effectively.

502   5   C ONCLUSIONS
503
504   In this work, we presented a new method TETRADAT for untargeted adversarial query-based black-
505   box attacks on computer vision models. Our approach is based on gradient-free optimization with the
506   low-rank tensor train format and additional dimensionality reduction by using the semantic attribution
507   map of the target image. TETRADAT with a constant set of hyperparameters demonstrates a high
508   percentage of successful attacks for various modern neural network models in comparison with
509   well-known alternative black-box methods, while image distortion in many cases turns out to be
510
      negligible. As part of the further work, we plan to extend our approach to targeted attacks and other
      relevant problems, including label-based and universal attacks.
