009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               Transformer-based Large Language Models (LLMs) have exhibited remarkable
012
               success in various natural language processing tasks primarily attributed to self-
013
               attention mechanism, which requires a token to consider all preceding tokens as
014            its context to compute the attention score. However, when the context length
015            L becomes very large (e.g., 32K), more redundant context information will be
016            included w.r.t. any tokens, making the self-attention suffer from two main limi-
017            tations: 1) The computational and memory complexity scales quadratically w.r.t.
018            L; 2) The presence of redundant context information may hamper the model to
019            capture dependencies among crucial tokens, which may degrade the representa-
020            tion performance. In this paper, we propose a plug-and-play Core Context Aware
021            (CCA) Attention for efficient long-range context modeling, which consists of two
022
               components: 1) Globality-pooling attention that divides input tokens into groups
               and then dynamically merges tokens within each group into one core token based
023
               on their significance; 2) Locality-preserved attention that incorporates neighbor-
024
               ing tokens into the attention calculation. The two complementary attentions will
025            then be fused to the final attention, maintaining comprehensive modeling ability
026            as the full self-attention. In this way, the core context information w.r.t. a given to-
027            ken will be automatically focused and strengthened, while the context information
028            in redundant groups will be diminished during the learning process. As a result,
029            the computational and memory complexity will be significantly reduced. More
030            importantly, the CCA-Attention can improve the long-context modeling ability
031            by diminishing the redundant context information. Extensive experimental re-
032            sults demonstrate that our CCA-Attention significantly outperforms state-of-the-
033
               art models in terms of computational efficiency and long-context modeling ability.

      Large language models (LLMs) (Brown et al., 2020; OpenAI, 2023; Touvron et al., 2023a) have
      demonstrated exceptional proficiency across various applications by effectively modeling extended
040
      contexts, particularly in tasks involving natural language understanding and generation (Ouyang
041
      et al., 2022; Chang et al., 2024). The remarkable success of LLMs is predominantly credited to the
042   self-attention mechanism (Vaswani et al., 2017), which requires each token in the input sequence to
043   calculate attention with all preceding tokens. Nonetheless, the computational and memory require-
044   ments of self-attention grow quadratically with the increase in sequence length, posing challenges
045   for long context understanding tasks (Liu et al., 2024; Shaham et al., 2023).
046
      Recent studies (Beltagy et al., 2020; Zaheer et al., 2020; Xiao et al., 2024b) have demonstrated that
047
      the majority of layers within autoregressive LLMs exhibit redundant tokens across various attention
048   heads and input tokens. This redundancy is visually exemplified in Figure 1, where we present a de-
049   tailed visualization of the attention weights within LLaMA2 (Touvron et al., 2023a). Our empirical
050   analysis reveals that a substantial proportion of attention weights are disproportionately assigned to
051   a limited number of tokens, often irrespective of their relevance to the language modeling task. This
052   disproportionate allocation suggests that the inherent redundancy within the attention mechanisms
053   may not necessarily hinder the model’s performance. This observation opens up the possibility of
      leveraging this redundancy to reduce the computational complexity of attention.

      Efficient Attention. Self-attention is a fundamental module in Transformer-based Large Language
111   Models (LLMs) (Brown et al., 2020; OpenAI, 2023; Touvron et al., 2023a). It captures the global
112   relationship between each token throughout the input sequence. However, the computational com-
113   plexity of self-attention increases quadratically with the sequence length, thereby limiting the appli-
114   cation of LLMs to long documents. Various works have sought to mitigate this complexity through
115   approaches such as sparse attention (Beltagy et al., 2020; Zaheer et al., 2020; Ding et al., 2023) and
116   linear attention approximations (Choromanski et al., 2020; Katharopoulos et al., 2020; Sun et al.,
117   2023). Specifically, Longformer (Beltagy et al., 2020) and BigBird (Zaheer et al., 2020) employ
118   sparse attention mechanisms to handle long sequences by utilizing strided attention patterns, where
119
      attention is only paid at fixed intervals. Linear Transformer (Katharopoulos et al., 2020) and Ret-
      Net (Sun et al., 2023) reformulates self-attention as a linear dot-product of kernel feature maps and
120
      leverages the associativity property of matrix products to achieve linear complexity.
121
122   Recently, LongLoRA (Chen et al., 2024) designs a shifted sparse attention mechanism that computes
123   attention among grouped input tokens. To facilitate communication between groups, this approach
124   shifts the group partition by half the group size. StreamingLLM (Xiao et al., 2024b) and LM-
125
      Infinite (Han et al., 2023) prioritize attention on the initial and final tokens, effectively disregarding
      the intermediate tokens. InfLLM (Xiao et al., 2024a) employs a sliding window attention mecha-
126
      nism and a block-level context memory to selectively attend to relevant context information, avoid-
127
      ing noise and reducing computational costs. MInference (Jiang et al., 2024) accelerates long-context
128   LLM inference by dynamically identifying and applying three distinct sparse attention patterns (A-
129   shape, Vertical-Slash, and Block-Sparse), using optimized GPU kernels for efficient computation.
130   However, these methods fail to ensure that each token has access to all preceding tokens, lead-
131   ing to inferior performance in tasks requiring comprehensive long-context understanding. Instead,
132   our CCA-Attention proposes a globality-pooling attention where each token can communicate with
133   previous tokens via number-reduced core tokens. Our approach provides better reachability than
134   abovementioned sparse attention and achieves superior performance.
135   Long-context Large Language Models (LLMs). LLMs are often pretrained with a relatively small
136   and pre-defined context length due to computational cost constraints, such as 4K for Llama-2 (Tou-
137   vron et al., 2023b). This limitation restricts the applicability of LLMs to tasks with long documents.
138   Recently, several attempts have been made to extend the context length of LLMs through continuous
139   training. Position Interpolation (Chen et al., 2023) addresses this by linearly down-scaling the input
140   position indices to fit within the original context window size, thereby extending the context length
141   of RoPE-based LLMs. Furthermore, YaRN (Peng et al., 2024) enhances performance by combining
142
      interpolation techniques with dynamic scaling. Beyond modifications to position embeddings, other
      efforts focus on designing more efficient attention mechanisms (Chen et al., 2024; Dao et al., 2022;
143
      Dao, 2024) for context window extension. Our method aligns with these efficient design efforts
144
      and is orthogonal to position embedding methods. During inference, our approach accelerates the
145   forward propagation processes, while position embedding modifications alone do not provide.

186   3.1       M OTIVATION AND OVERVIEW
187
188   In the full self-attention that is commonly used in decoder-only transformers (e.g., GPT (Brown
189   et al., 2020; OpenAI, 2023) and LLaMA series (Touvron et al., 2023a)), each token Xi in the input
190   sequence is able to attend to all preceding tokens X[1:i−1] and itself (as shown in Eqn. (1)). This
191   ensures that the full self-attention mechanism is able to effectively exploit information from any
192   position in the input sequence during autoregressive generation of new tokens. We define this capa-
193
      bility as reachability, which encapsulates the potential for information exchange between tokens.
194   Definition 1. (Reachability) We say the token j is reachable from the token i in the attention map
195   if and only if the attention weight from the token j to i is positive, i.e., aij > 0.
196
197   The full self-attention excels in reachability, where the attention weights aij for i ≥ j are posi-
198   tive. However, the computational complexity of the full self-attention grows in quadratical time as
199   the sequence length L increases, i.e., O(L2 ). This poses a significant challenge in handling long
200   sequences, affecting both processing time and memory footprint. Most existing methods (Beltagy
201
      et al., 2020; Ding et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024) mitigate the computational cost by applying pre-
      defined and fixed sparse attention patterns. Unfortunately, they often overlook the importance of
202
      maintaining reachability between tokens. This oversight may lead to inadequate information trans-
203
      fer between tokens, which hinders the performance of complex tasks involving long sequences.
204
205   In this paper, we seek to reduce the computational complexity associated with full self-attention
206   without sacrificing token reachability. To achieve this, we propose a Core Context Aware Attention
207
      (CCA-Attention), which employs globality-pooling and locality-preserved attention to capture both
      global and local dependencies within a long context. As shown in Figure 2, globality-pooling atten-
208
      tion operates by generating representative core tokens from segmented groups of the input sequence.
209
      It then computes attention using these reduced-number core tokens, thereby reducing the computa-
210   tional cost (see Section 3.2). However, the autoregressive nature inherently limits each token to
211   access core token within the same group (i.e., local context), failing to achieve token reachability.
212   To address this, locality-preserved attention is responsible for capturing the local information of the
213   neighborhood to ensure comprehensive coverage (see Section 3.3). Furthermore, we devise an adap-
214   tive fusion strategy to integrate the insights from both attentions (see Section 3.4). This strategy is
215   crucial as it retains the reachability of the full self-attention within our CCA-Attention framework.
      The pseudo-code for our proposed CCA-Attention is presented in Algorithm 1.

358   3.5   E FFICIENT C OMPUTATION WITH R EDUCED C OMPLEXITY AND K EY- VALUE C ACHE
359
360   Compared with full self-attention, our CCA-Attention offers significant advantages in terms of com-
361   putational complexity and key-value cache memory usage. These benefits substantially enhance the
362
      running speed and memory usage efficiency for our CCA-Attention (see results in Figure 4).
363   Acceleration through Reduced Computational Complexity. Our CCA-Attention exhibits vary-
364   ing computational complexities depending on the type of task. For tasks with fixed-length sequences
365   (such as multi-choice question answering), our CCA-Attention exhibits a linear computational com-
366   plexity of O(Lm + Ls), marking a significant enhancement over the full self-attention with a com-
367   plexity of O(L2 ). Here, we define the number of group m as a constant. Specifically, for globality-
368
      pooling attention, the query and key matrices encompass L and m tokens, respectively, resulting
      in a computational complexity of O(Lm). Regarding locality-preserved attention, each token only
369
      attends preceding s tokens. With L tokens in total, the complexity amounts to O(Ls).
370
371   For tasks with variable-length sequences (such as open-ended question answering), models generate
372   subsequent tokens in an autoregressive manner. In this case, we set the group size k as a constant,
373   ensuring that our CCA-Attention is able to leverage key-value caching during autoregressive token
374
      generation. Once one group has certain k tokens, the corresponding core token is also determined
      and can be cached. Thus, our CCA-Attention achieves a computational complexity of O(L2 /k +
375
      Ls). The complexity analysis follows a similar pattern to the tasks with fixed-length sequences.
376
377   Acceleration through Reduced Key-Value (KV) Cache. In attention-based transformers, the KV
      cache leverages the autoregressive nature to store and reuse key-value pairs (i.e., K and V in


                                                         7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      Eqn. (1)), thereby significantly boosting the efficiency of attention calculations. The size of the
379   KV cache scales linearly with the length of the input sequence, consuming a major part of the mem-
380   ory footprint during inference. The expanded KV cache not only consumes considerable memory
381   but also potentially hinders inference speed due to the increased demand for IO operations.
382
      Compared with full attention’s complexity of O(L), our proposed CCA-Attention achieves a storage
383
      complexity of O(L/k+s). For globality-pooling attention, we only retain the key and value matrices
384
      for core tokens, rather than for all original tokens. This reduces the memory requirement to O(L/k).
385   Besides, locality-preserved attention confines the attention of each token to the preceding s tokens.
386   Consequently, the storage complexity for this component is O(s). Experimentally, we have set k=16
387   and s=1024. The results show a significant reduction in storage complexity compared to traditional
388   full attention models (see results in Figure 4).

532      5                              C ONCLUSION
533
534     In this paper, we proposed a Core Context Aware Attention (CCA-Attention) for language modeling
535     with reduced computational overhead compared to full self-attention. Our CCA-Attention includes
536     two components: 1) Globality-pooling attention exploits the importance of input tokens to encap-
537     sulates core tokens and uses them for attention, capturing global coarse-grained information. 2)
538     Locality-preserved attention focuses on neighboring tokens to capture local fined-grained context,
539     serving as a complement for globality-pooling attention. Extensive experiments show the effective-
        ness of our CCA-Attention with promising performance and decreased computational cost.

      We quantitatively evaluate our models and other considered models using three metrics:
815
816   Exact Match Score (EM Score). This measures the model’s ability to find the key information
817   within a long context in a multi-document question-answering task. In this task, each test sample
818
      comprises a certain number of documents to reach the specified context length (20 for 4K, 48 for
      8K, 96 for 16K, 190 for 32K), followed by a question. We evaluate EM score metric (Liu et al.,
819
      2024) with the multi-document question-answering dataset in Lost in the Middle (Liu et al., 2024),
820
      which is collected from NaturalQuestions-Open and Wikipedia. We use the exact match score as the
821   evaluation metric, judging whether any of the correct answers appear in the predicted output.
822
823   Massive Multitask Language Understanding (MMLU). This metric evaluates the model’s profi-
824
      ciency across a diverse set of language-understanding tasks. It tests the model’s ability to apply its
      knowledge to a broad spectrum of topics and question types, reflecting its generalization capability
825
      in real-world scenarios. The MMLU metric (Hendrycks et al., 2021), which tests world knowl-
826
      edge and problem-solving abilities in zero-shot and few-shot settings, is evaluated using the MMLU
827   dataset (Hendrycks et al., 2021). This dataset spans 57 subjects across disciplines such as STEM,
828   humanities, and social sciences. We test the MMLU metric in a 5-shot setting.
829
830
      Perplexity (PPL). This metric quantifies how effectively a model can predict the context. It is
      calculated as the exponentiated average negative log-likelihood of a sequence, offering a statistical
831
      measure of model performance in language modeling tasks.

943   C.1   M ORE D ISCUSSIONS WITH MI NFERENCE
944
      MInference (Jiang et al., 2024) is an efficient attention method that dynamically identifies and ap-
945
      plies three distinct sparse attention patterns (A-shape, Vertical-Slash, and Block-Sparse), using op-
946   timized GPU kernels for efficient computation. Compared with MInference, the strengths and dif-
947   ferences of our proposed CCA-Attention are as follows.
948
949          • Stronger contextual reachability than MInference. MInference relies on an offline
950            search algorithm to determine static sparse attention patterns for each attention head. This
951            may fail to capture critical information in sequences where the positions of important to-
952            kens vary significantly across inputs. In contrast, our CCA-Attention employs a globality-
953            pooling strategy to derive core tokens based on token importance. This ensures that each
954            token maintains communication with all preceding tokens via the reduced set of core to-
955
               kens, providing stronger reachability for long-context modeling.
956          • Empirical comparisons with MInference. On the Longbench benchmark, our CCA-
957            Attention achieves better performance (22.12% vs. 22.08%). Moreover, given a 32K
958            context, our method delivers a 1.62× speedup in inference and reduces memory usage
959            for KV cache by 90.63% (1.5 GB vs. 16 GB) compared to MInference, highlighting its
960
               computational and storage efficiency.

1079   pooling retains only the token with the highest response, thereby discarding the semantic impor-

1092    performance.
1093    Effect of Group Size k. To investigate the effect of different group sizes k, we implement the
1094    proposed CCA-Attention with different k ∈ {4, 8, 16, 32, 64, 128}. In Table 9a, as k increases, the
1095    computational efficiency improves while the PPL increases. Upon closer examination, the smallest
1096    group size k captures the most comprehensive information, which translates to the highest compu-
1097    tational cost but also the optimal PPL. Conversely, an excessively large k leads to an overemphasis
1098    on globality-aware attention, compressing information to the point where crucial semantic nuances
1099    may be overlooked, thereby curtailing performance. To strike a balance between computational
1100    efficiency and model performance, we have selected k=16 as the default setting.

1103    {16, 64, 256, 512, 1024, 2048}. In Table 9b, an increase in w correlates with lower PPL and higher
1104    MMLU, but this is counterbalanced by a rise in computational cost. A larger s captures more contex-
1105    tual information with neighborhood tokens, but also increases computational demands. Conversely,
1106    a smaller s, indicative of a limited receptive field, constrains the exchange of information within
1107
        the locality-preserved attention, resulting in diminished performance. Striking a balance between
        computational efficiency and model efficacy, we opted for s=1024 in our experimental setup.
