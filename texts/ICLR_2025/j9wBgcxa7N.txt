009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011                Large Language Models’ (LLM) reasoning can be improved using test-time ag-
012                gregation strategies, i.e., generating multiple samples for each problem and ag-
013                gregating over them to find a better answer. While these improve performance,
014                they often reach a saturation point beyond which additional samples provide no
015                return. Refinement offers an alternative by using model-generated feedback to
016                improve answer quality. However, refinement introduces three key challenges: (1)
017                Excessive refinement: Uniformly refining all instances can cause over-correction
018
                   and reduce overall performance. (2) Inability to localize and address errors:
                   LLMs have a limited ability to self-correct and struggle to identify and correct their
019
                   own mistakes in a targeted way. (3) Insufficient refinement: Deciding how many
020
                   iterations of refinement are needed is non-trivial, and stopping too soon could leave
021                errors unaddressed. To tackle these issues, we propose MAG IC O R E, a framework
022                for Multi Agent Iteration for Coarse-to-fine Refinement. MAG IC O R E aims to
023                avoid excessive refinement by categorizing problems as easy or hard, solving easy
024                problems with coarse-grained aggregation, and solving hard ones with fine-grained
025                and iterative multi-agent refinement. To enable more granular error localization, we
026                incorporate external step-wise reward model (RM) scores. To ensure effective re-
027                finement, we employ a multi-agent loop with three agents: the Solver, the Reviewer
028                (which generates targeted feedback based on step-wise RM scores) and the Refiner
029
                   (which incorporates feedback and generates new solutions). To ensure sufficient
                   refinement, we re-evaluate updated solutions, iteratively initiating further rounds of
030
                   multi-agent refinement. We evaluate MAG IC O R E on Llama-3-8B and GPT-3.5 and
031
                   show its effectiveness across five math reasoning datasets, with consistent gains for
032                all datasets and models. Even one iteration of MAG IC O R E beats Self-Consistency
033                by 3.4%, Best-of-k by 3.2%, and Self-Refine by 4.0% while using less than 50%
034                of the samples. Unlike iterative refinement with baseline methods, MAG IC O R E
035                continues to improve with more iterations. Finally, our ablations highlight the
036                importance of MAG IC O R E’s use of RMs and multi-agent communication. 1

136   2     MAG IC O R E : A N A DAPTIVE C OARSE - TO -F INE R EFINEMENT F RAMEWORK
137
138
      MAG IC O R E is an adaptive framework that aims to improve both the performance and efficiency
139   of multi-step reasoning with LLMs by intelligently applying test-time aggregation and refinement.
140   MAG IC O R E consists of three intertwined components, resulting in a unified solution to the three
141   issues discussed in Section 1: Excessive refinement, the inability of LLMs to localize and address
142   their own errors, and insufficient refinement: (1) MAG IC O R E avoids excessive refinement by
143   categorizing problem difficulty as easy or hard, using coarse-grained aggregation for easy problems
144   and fine-grained, iterative multi-agent refinement for hard ones. (2) To improve error localization, we
145   incorporate step-wise scores from an external reward model, providing targeted feedback and thereby
146   helping the LLM to identify problematic steps. To enable effective refinement where the feedback is
147
      properly incorporated, we employ a multi-agent setup with three agents: the Solver, the Reviewer,
      which generates targeted feedback based on step-wise RM scores, and the Refiner, which incorporates
148
      feedback to generate new solutions. (3) To ensure sufficient refinement, the refined solutions are
149
      re-evaluated, iteratively initiating further rounds of multi-agent refinement if needed, and making the
150   communication between agents bidirectional. Below we introduce MAG IC O R E in detail.
151
152
153
      2.1    OVERALL MAG IC O R E F RAMEWORK
154
      Background. We incorporate three models: (1) an LLM with three roles: the Solver, the Reviewer,
155
      and the Refiner for generating initial solutions, targeted feedback, and refinement, respectively (more
156   details in Section 2.3), (2) an Outcome Reward Model (ORM; Cai et al., 2024) for generating global,
157   solution-level correctness score, and (3) a Process Reward Model (PRM; Wang et al., 2023) for
158   generating local step-by-step correctness scores. The ORM and PRM perform both independent
159   and joint tasks. The ORM’s global correctness score is crucial for deciding which refined samples
160   to retain during iterative refinement, and the PRM’s local correctness score is used for generating
161   targeted feedback. Both the ORM and PRM contribute to (1) assessing problem difficulty and (2)
      Weighted Self-Consistency (Li et al., 2023) for final answer selection (see Appendix B for details).

191   Overview. A high-level overview of MAG IC O R E is presented in Fig. 2. The process begins with the
192   Solver generating k reasoning chains for each problem, followed by the ORM and PRM providing
193   solution-level scores (as illustrated in the top-left of Fig. 2). Next, the input problem’s difficulty is
194   classified based on two criteria (top-right of Fig. 2): (1) the quality of the majority answer and (2) the
195   RMs’ answer confidence. Refinement is initiated only when the problem is deemed difficult, which
196   occurs when the majority answer receives a low average RM score and the answer distribution is flat
197
      – indicating no single answer is significantly better than the others (i.e., low confidence). For these
      hard samples requiring refinement, we employ a multi-agent setup with three agents: the Solver, the
198
      Reviewer, and the Refiner (bottom of Fig. 2). The Reviewer uses the k reasoning chains generated by
199
      the Solver, along with the step-wise scores from the PRM, to provide targeted feedback. The Refiner
200   then enhances the k solutions based on this feedback. This bidirectional communication between the
201   Reviewer and Refiner can continue for multiple iterations. At the end of each iteration, the ORM
202   assigns a global correctness score to each chain, determining which top k solutions to retain, with the
203   final answer being aggregated from these top k solutions.

      2.2      C LASSIFYING P ROBLEM D IFFICULTY AND A SSIGNING TO C OARSE OR F INE M ETHODS
206
207   We categorize each problem’s difficulty as easy or hard using the following conditions (cf. Fig. 2).
208   Condition 1: Is the Majority Answer of High Quality? The Solver generates k solutions for the
209   input question and we group them by their final answers. From the largest cluster of solutions, we
210   calculate the average RM score and normalize it by the average score across all solutions, denoted as
211   Savg . After normalization, if Savg ≥ 0, Condition 1 is true, meaning the majority answer is already
212   high-quality, and hence no refinement is needed. Otherwise, Condition 1 is false, suggesting even
213   the most frequent answer is poor, and refinement may help. We evaluate the quality of the majority
214   answer using both ORM and PRM separately but with the same procedure, i.e., if any of the RMs
215   gives the majority answer a low score, Condition 1 will be false (see Appendix C for more details).
      Condition 2: Is Reward Models’ Answer Confidence High? We check if the RMs are confident in


                                                                               4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      any single answer. Confidence is determined by how concentrated the answer distribution is (i.e., if
217   one cluster stands out). A concentrated distribution indicates high confidence, while a spread-out
218   distribution shows low confidence. The answer distribution is formed by (1) the frequency of each
219   unique answer and (2) the total RM score of each answer cluster. That is, we weigh each answer
220   cluster by its aggregated RM score, similar to the approach used in Weighted Self-Consistency (Li
221   et al., 2023). We calculate confidence (denoted as C) using the entropy of the answer clusters. If
222   C ≥ 0.5, Condition 2 is true, meaning RMs are confident in one answer. Otherwise, Condition 2 is
223   false, indicating uncertainty and the need for further refinement. Note that 0.5 is a natural threshold
224   requiring no tuning since we pass the entropy to a sigmoid function; further details can be found in
225
      Appendix C. Again, we evaluate both ORM and PRM’s answer confidence separately, each following
      the same procedure (i.e., if any of the RMs has low confidence, Condition 2 is false).
226
227
      Coarse-to-Fine Decision. If either of the conditions is met (the quality of the answer is high or the
      RMs are confident on an answer), an instance is delegated to the easy – or coarse-grained – method.
228
      Conversely, if both conditions are not satisfied, the instance is delegated to the hard – or fine-grained
229
      – method (as described in Section 2.3). For easy instances, we apply the coarse-grained method:
230   Weighted Self-Consistency (Li et al., 2023), using the sum of the solution-level scores generated by
231   both ORM and PRM3 . Therefore, the coarse-grained method does not involve refinement.

233   2.3      F INE -G RAINED M ULTI -AGENT I TERATIVE R EFINEMENT
234
235   Our multi-agent setup has three agents: (1) the Solver, which generates the initial solution (2) the
236   Reviewer, which takes PRM scores and a reasoning chain as input, and generates targeted feedback
237   that pinpoints the errors within the chain, and (3) the Refiner, which takes the feedback generated by
238   the Reviewer to refine the previous chain.
239   Solver generates k solutions. Recall that in Section 2.2, we assess problem difficulty using k
240   generated solutions. Even for easy instances where refinement is not needed, generating k solutions
241   and aggregating them improves the performance (Wang et al., 2022). For hard instances, refinement
242   is applied on top of the k solutions simultaneously. Thus, whether refinement is needed or not,
243   generating k solutions is necessary. Consequently, MAG IC O R E begins by generating k solutions to
244   a given problem. Implementationally, this is done when assessing problem difficulty (see top-left
245   of Fig. 2, where the solutions are generated and scored). When a problem is classified as easy , we
246
      aggregate the k solution without refinement. When a problem is classified as hard (i.e., would benefit
      from refinement and does not fall in the purview of “Issue 1”), we can directly re-use the k solutions
247
      already generated by the Solver.
248
249   Reviewer generates targeted feedback. As we pointed to in Fig. 1, even when we know refinement
250   is warranted, LLMs (like the Reviewer agent) may struggle to isolate and localize errors in their
251   own responses (Huang et al., 2024), which we highlight as “Issue 2” of refinement (Inability to
252
      Localize and Address Errors). Therefore, to assist the Reviewer in effectively localizing errors and
      generating useful feedback, we supply the Reviewer with external step-wise PRM scores for each step
253
      of the solution. We append these scores to the end of each step (see the bottom-left of Fig. 2, where
254
      scores are assigned), and pass the result to the Reviewer. The goal of the Reviewer is to incorporate
255   step-wise correctness scores from the PRM to generate actionable feedback. That is, it takes a chain
256   with the PRM scores as input, and is prompted to identify problematic steps that need refinement and
257   possible ways to fix them. We use a 1-shot prompt to guide the Reviewer how to generate targeted
258   feedback, and the prompt is shown in Appendix G.
259
      Refiner improves solutions based on targeted Reviewer feedback. The Refiner then uses the
260
      targeted feedback generated by the Reviewer to refine the reasoning chain that was generated by the
261   Solver. Inspired by the finding that LLM can resolve errors when explicitly pointed out (Tyen et al.,
262   2024), and given that the Reviewer already indicates which steps of the reasoning chain should be
263   refined, the Refiner agent can focus exclusively on how the step should be modified so as to resolve
264   or fix the error based on feedback from the Reviewer. Here we again use a 1-shot prompt to teach
265   the Refiner how to fix the error and improve a reasoning chain based on targeted feedback, and the
266   prompt for the Refiner is shown in Appendix G.
267   Iterating the refinement process for bidirectional communication. For some hard instances,
268   one round of refinement may be insufficient, as the Reviewer may have generated some irrelevant

271   Thus, the Reviewer and the Refiner need to collaborate with each other over the course of multiple
272   refinement iterations. This creates a bidirectional communication cycle between the Reviewer and
273   the Refiner, with outputs from the Refiner going back to the Reviewer in the next round. To prevent
274   excessive refinement, we re-evaluate the two conditions described in Section 2.2 in each iteration.
275   The refinement continues until (a) one of the conditions is met, or (b) a predefined maximum number
276   of iterations is reached. This process is illustrated in Fig. 2 (bottom panel) by the arrow going from
277   the Refiner output back to the Reviewer, which shows both conditions being re-assessed.
278   Final answer selection. The refinement process described above operates on all k chains simultane-
279   ously, producing k refined chains in each iteration. At the end of each iteration, we use the ORM
280   to assess whether the refined solution has improved based on its global correctness score. In other
281   words, by the end of each iteration, we have 2k reasoning chains – k initial and k refined – but
282   retain only the top k based on their global ORM scores. Here we choose to base the decision on the
283   ORM score because the targeted feedback is generated with PRM’s step-wise scores, so selecting the
284   solution via another scoring model avoids overfitting. Finally, the answer is selected using Weighted
285
      Self-Consistency over these retained top k chains, at the end of each iteration.
286   Key Improvements. By leveraging external RMs and an iterative multi-agent setup, our method
287   (1) avoids excessive refinements by only refining hard samples. (2) Enhances error localization
288   and resolution by using PRM’s step-wise scoring to offer targeted feedback, enabling effective
289   communication between the Reviewer and the Refiner. (3) Ensures thorough refinement by re-
290   evaluating refined solutions, and initiating additional rounds of refinement when necessary.

      Implementation Details. We develop MAG IC O R E with Llama3-8B-Instruct (AI@Meta,
295   2024) and GPT-3.5-Turbo (OpenAI, 2022) as the base LLMs. Based on their strong performance
296   on standard reward modeling evaluations (Lambert et al., 2024), we choose InternLM-7B (Cai et al.,
297   2024) as the ORM, and Math-Shepherd-7B (Wang et al., 2023) as the PRM for computing the RM
298   scores. By default, we sample k = 40 reasoning chains in each iteration for MAG IC O R E, and the
299   decoding temperature is set to 0.8. The maximum number of iterations is set to 3, with additional
300   analysis in Fig. 3 and Table 12. We compare against different categories of baselines, as follows.
301
      • Vanilla Prompting. The first baseline as a reference is zero-shot Chain-of-Thought (Wei et al.,
302     2022); note that this only generates one reasoning chain per question without aggregation.
303

      and are used in the solutions to all three problems (excessive refinement, inability to localize and

516   community is actively advancing the performance of reward models evidenced by a benchmark
517   for reward models (Lambert et al., 2024), MAG IC O R E is thus complementary to and enhanced by
518   progress in reward modeling, rather than constrained by it. While it is possible to train a custom
519   error-identification model, this approach is often data-dependent and prone to obsolescence. In
520   contrast, MAG IC O R E’s modular design overcomes this limitation by enabling the integration of new
521   state-of-the-art models as they become available. Moreover, our experiments in Table 6 indicate that
522   when trained reward models are unavailable, we can use sufficiently strong LLMs in place of trained
523   RMs. For example, we use GPT4o-mini as a reward model for commonsense and logical reasoning.

526   5   C ONCLUSION
527
528   Building on the observation that different problems require varying amounts of computation, we
529   introduced MAG IC O R E, a method that adaptively allocates more computational resources to more
530   challenging problems and selectively applies refinement where appropriate, i.e., on harder problems.
531
      MAG IC O R E addresses three key issues in refinement: excessive refinement on easy examples, the
      inability of LLMs to detect and correct errors in their reasoning, and insufficient refinement on hard
532
      instances. Our approach tackles these issues by employing both global and local external reward
533
      models to decide which samples to refine. We then incorporate local correctness scores to generate
534   targeted feedback and an iterative multi-agent communication framework to refine solutions for
535   hard problems. Our results across five math datasets and two models show that our coarse-to-fine
536   method consistently outperforms both coarse-grained aggregation and fine-grained refinement alone
537   at any given budget and even outperforms baselines using substantially more computation. In our
538   ablations, we demonstrate the importance of selective refinement, showing that performance generally
539   drops when refining all samples uniformly. We also highlight the role of iteration in our framework,
      showing increased performance across iterations even as baselines stagnate.

      E THICS S TATEMENT
541
542
      In this work, we propose an inference-time method, MAG IC O R E, which operates without the need for
543   additional training or fine-tuning. Consequently, the LLMs utilized by MAG IC O R E (e.g., Llama-3,
544   GPT-3.5) may still exhibit stereotypes, biases, and other negative traits inherent in their pre-training
545   data (Weidinger et al., 2021), over which we have no control. Therefore, the outputs produced by
546   MAG IC O R E carry the same potential for misuse as those from other test-time methods. Further
547   research is necessary to assess and mitigate these biases in LLMs.

760
761   Improving Reasoning by Aggregation. Self-Consistency (SC; Wang et al., 2022) generates k
762   reasoning chains and marginalizes over the chains to obtain answer clusters; the most frequent answer
763   is selected as the final prediction. While simple and effective, it generates k solutions for every sample,
764   as both past work and our work show that SC saturates when k increases (Chen et al., 2024b; Li et al.,
765   2024a). Several studies adaptively determine the number of samples (k) required for each instance to
766   address this (Aggarwal et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024b). However, the performance of such approaches
767   is upper-bounded by SC – they address the cost issue but do not enhance overall performance. To
768   surpass SC, Yin et al. (2024) propose using the LLM to evaluate answer clusters, taking into account
769
      both frequency and the LLM-evaluated quality of the answers. Instead, we propose using external
      RMs to decide between coarse-grained aggregation and fine-grained refinement, identify errors, and
770
      aid refinement; this allows us to improve over aggregation or refinement alone.
771
772   LLM-based Verification and Refinement. Past work mostly uses RMs for verification purposes (Li
773   et al., 2023; Khalifa et al., 2023; Cobbe et al., 2021; Lightman et al., 2023). Havrilla et al. (2024)
774   considers local correctness for refinement in a non-adaptive way and requires specific data curation
775
      with fine-tuning, while our work is adaptive and uses off-the-shelf global and local models. Another
      line of work has proposed using the LLM itself as a verifier, in place of an RM (Liu et al., 2024;
776
      Zhang et al., 2024; Aggarwal et al., 2023; Madaan et al., 2023). However, recent work shows the
777
      inability of LLMs to “self-verify” their own reasoning (Huang et al., 2024; Stechly et al., 2023;
778   Kamoi et al., 2024; Tyen et al., 2024; Kamoi et al., 2024). Hence, MAG IC O R E uses external global
779   and local reward models (Wang et al., 2023; Cai et al., 2024) for selective coarse-to-fine refinement.
780   Shridhar et al. (2024) trained specific models to decide when to refine and when to trust refined
781   solutions. This contrasts with our method, where the decision to refine is based on a coarse-to-fine
782   resource allocation method that differentiates easy from hard problems (for which we use global
783   and local reward models), and where refinement is done based on off-the-shelf models. We also
784   iteratively refine guided by global correctness to ensure sufficient refinement, whereas Shridhar et al.
785   (2024) refine only once. Wadhwa et al. (2024) propose a multi-agent detect-critique-refine pipeline
786
      for generation tasks. MAG IC O R E instead focuses on reasoning tasks and uses external RMs for
      selective coarse-to-fine refinement (whereas Wadhwa et al. (2024)’s detection uses the same metric
787
      as their evaluation, which is infeasible in reasoning where the metric – accuracy – requires access to
788
      the gold answer).
789
790   Multi-Agent Systems with LLMs. LLMs can be used in multi-agent systems, where the agents
791   interact, collaborate, and compete (Wang et al., 2024; Lu et al., 2024; Feng et al., 2024). Related to
792
      our work, one line of multi-agent research focuses on structured debates or discussions between LLM
      agents, where the interaction helps refine and improve previously generated solutions (Du et al., 2023;
793
      Liang et al., 2023; Yin et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024a). These studies show improvements over single-
794
      agent systems, but a major challenge in multi-agent systems is achieving a correct consensus among
795   LLMs; external feedback can help prevent this consensus from aligning with the agents’ internal and
796   possibly erroneous outputs. Therefore, MAG IC O R E’s multi-agent refinement incorporates external
797   RMs for more objective scoring, enabling the generation of targeted feedback for better refinement.
798
