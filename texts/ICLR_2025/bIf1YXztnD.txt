                                                    A BSTRACT
008
009
               Orientation estimation is a fundamental task in 3D shape analysis which consists
010
               of estimating a shape’s orientation axes: its side-, up-, and front-axes. Using
011            this data, one can rotate a shape into canonical orientation, where its orientation
012            axes are aligned with the coordinate axes. Developing an orientation algorithm
013            that reliably estimates complete orientations of general shapes remains an open
014            problem. We introduce a two-stage orientation pipeline that achieves state of the
015            art performance on up-axis estimation and further demonstrate its efficacy on full-
016            orientation estimation, where one seeks all three orientation axes. Unlike previous
017            work, we train and evaluate our method on all of Shapenet rather than a subset of
018            classes. We motivate our engineering contributions by theory describing funda-
019
               mental obstacles to orientation estimation for rotationally-symmetric shapes, and
               show how our method avoids these obstacles.

022   1    I NTRODUCTION
023
024   Orientation estimation is a fundamental task in 3D shape analysis which consists of estimating a
025   shape’s orientation axes: its side-, up-, and front-axes. Using this data, one can rotate a shape into
026   canonical orientation, in which the shape’s orientation axes are aligned with the coordinate axes.
027
      This task is especially important as a pre-processing step in 3D deep learning, where deep networks
      are typically trained on datasets of canonically-oriented shapes but applied to arbitrarily-oriented
028
      shapes at inference time. While data augmentation or equivariant and invariant architectures may
029
      improve a model’s robustness to input rotations, these techniques come at the cost of data efficiency
030   and model expressivity (Kuchnik & Smith, 2019; Kim et al., 2023). In contrast, orientation estima-
031   tion allows one to pre-process shapes at inference time so that their orientation matches a model’s
032   training data.
033
      Orientation estimation is a challenging task, and developing an orientation pipeline that reliably
034
      estimates complete orientations of general shapes remains an open problem. The naı̈ve deep learn-
035
      ing approach is to train a model with an L2 loss to directly predict a shape’s orientation from a
036   point cloud of surface samples. However, this strategy fails for shapes with rotational symmetries,
037   where the optimal solution to the L2 regression problem is the Euclidean mean (Moakher, 2002) of
038   a shape’s orientations over all of its symmetries. In contrast, works such as Poursaeed et al. (2020)
039   discretize the unit sphere into a set of fixed rotations and train a classifier to predict a probability dis-
040   tribution over these rotations, but find that this approach fails for any sufficiently dense discretization
041   of the unit sphere.
042   Our key insight is to divide orientation estimation into two tractable sub-problems. In the first stage
043   (the quotient orienter), we solve a continuous regression problem to recover a shape’s orientation
044   up to octahedral symmetries. In the second stage (the flipper), we solve a discrete classification
045   problem to predict one of 24 octahedral flips that returns the first-stage output to canonical orienta-
046   tion. Octahedral symmetries form a small set covering a substantial proportion of the symmetries
047   occurring in real-world shapes. Consequently, quotienting our first-stage regression problem by
048   octahedral symmetries prevents its predictions from collapsing to averages, while also keeping the
049   subsequent classification problem tractable.
050   Using this strategy, our method achieves state-of-the-art performance on the well-studied problem
051   of up-axis prediction, and additionally performs well on full-orientation prediction, which few prior
052   works have tackled. Unlike previous work, we train and evaluate our model on the entire Shapenet
053   dataset rather than a subset of classes. We further demonstrate its generalization capabilities on
      Objaverse, a large dataset of real-world 3D models of varying quality.

083   2   R ELATED WORK
084
085
      Classical methods. A simple method for orientation estimation is to compute a rotation that aligns
      a shape’s principal axes with the coordinate axes; Kaye & Ivrissimtzis (2015) propose a robust
086
      variant of this method for mesh alignment. However, Kazhdan et al. (2003) find that PCA-based
087
      orientation estimation is not robust to asymmetries. Jin et al. (2012); Wang et al. (2014) propose
088   unsupervised methods that leverage low-rank priors on axis-aligned 2D projections and third-order
089   tensors, respectively, constructed from input shapes. These priors are restrictive, and the resulting
090   orientation pipelines also fail on asymmetric shapes.
091
      Another set of classical methods observe that as many man-made objects are designed to stand
092
      on flat surfaces, their up axis is normal to a supporting base. Motivated by this observation, these
093
      methods attempt to identify a shape’s supporting base rather than directly infer their up axis. Fu et al.
094   (2008) generate a set of candidate bases, extract geometric features, and combine a random forest
095   and SVM to predict a natural base from the candidates. Lin & Tai (2012) simplify a shape’s convex
096   hull, cluster the resulting facets to obtain a set of candidate bases, and compute a hand-designed
097   score to select the best base. Both of these methods rely heavily on feature engineering and fail on
098   shapes that do not have natural supporting bases.
099
100   Deep learning-based methods. Motivated by the limitations of classical approaches, several
101   works use deep learning for orientation estimation. Liu et al. (2016) train two neural networks
102   on voxel representations of 3D shapes. A first-stage network assigns each shape to one of C classes.
103   Based on this prediction, the shape is routed to one of C second-stage networks that are indepen-
104
      dently trained to predict the up axis from voxel representations of shapes in their respective classes.
      This method is unable to handle shapes that lie outside the C classes on which the networks were
105
      trained.
106
107   Pang et al. (2022) draw inspiration from classical methods and train a segmentation network to
      predict points that belong to a shape’s supporting base. They fit a plane to the predicted base points


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      and output a normal vector to this plane as the predicted up axis. This method represents the current
109   state of the art for orientation estimation, but struggles to handle shapes without well-defined natural
110   bases and and only predicts a shape’s up axis. In contrast, our method succeeds on general shapes
111   and predicts a full rotation matrix that returns a shape to canonical orientation.
112
      Chen et al. (2021) use reinforcement learning to train a model to gradually rotate a shape into upright
113
      orientation. While this algorithm performs well, it is evaluated on few classes and is costly to train.
114
      Kim et al. (2020) adopt a similar perspective to Fu et al. (2008), but use ConvNets to extract features
115   for a random forest classifier that predicts a natural base. Poursaeed et al. (2020) use orientation
116   estimation as a pretext task to learn features for shape classification and keypoint prediction. They
117   also investigate a pure classification-based approach to orientation estimation that discretizes the 3D
118   rotation group into K rotations and predicts a distribution over these rotations for an arbitrarily-
119   rotated input shape. They find that its performance decays rapidly as K increases, reaching an
120   accuracy as low as 1.6% for K = 100 rotations.
121   We also highlight a related literature on canonical alignment. This literature includes works such as
122   Kim et al. (2023); Sajnani et al. (2022); Spezialetti et al. (2020); Zhou et al. (2022), which seek to
123   map arbitrarily-rotated shapes to a class-consistent pose, as well as Katzir et al. (2022); Sun et al.
124   (2021), which seek to learn pose-invariant representations of 3D shapes. These works only attempt
125   to learn a consistent orientation within each class, but this orientation is not consistent across classes
126   and is not generally aligned with the coordinate axes. In contrast, we tackle the more challenging
127   task of inferring a canonical orientation that is consistent across all objects.

131
      In this section, we motivate and describe our orientation pipeline. We first identify fundamental
132
      obstacles to orientation estimation and show that learning a shape’s orientation with the L2 loss
133
      fails when the shape is rotationally symmetric. Motivated by these observations, we introduce our
134   two-stage orientation pipeline consisting of a quotient orienter followed by a flipper. Our quotient
135   orienter model solves a regression problem to recover a shape’s orientation up to octahedral sym-
136   metries, which commonly occur in real-world shapes. The flipper then predicts one of 24 octahedral
137   flips that returns the first-stage output to canonical orientation. We finally use conformal prediction
138   to enable our flipper to output prediction sets whose size varies with the model’s uncertainty. This
139   allows end users to resolve ambiguities in a shape’s orientation by choosing from a small set of
140   plausible candidate orientations.

172   Theoretical challenges. An ideal orienter f should satisfy two desiderata: (1) It should accept
173   arbitrarily-oriented shapes RS as input and output their orientation ΩRS = RΩS , and (2) it should
174   succeed on most shapes S that occur in the wild. As many real-world shapes possess at least one
175   non-trivial rotational symmetry, an ideal orienter should therefore succeed on rotationally-symmetric
176   shapes. We begin by showing that no function can simultaneously satisfy these desiderata.

321   3.2   I MPLEMENTATION
322
323   Informed by our insights from Section 3.1, we now present our state-of-the-art method for orienta-
      tion estimation. Our pipeline consists of two components. Our first component, which we call the


                                                         6

324
      quotient orienter, is a neural network trained to solve Problem 3. We quotient the L2 objective by
325
      R̂ := O ⊆ SO(3), the octahedral group containing the 24 rotational symmetries of a cube. This
326
      is among the largest finite subgroups of SO(3) (only the cyclic group Cn for n ≥ 48 and dihedral
327   group Dn for n ≥ 4 can contain more subgroups), and it includes many rotational symmetries that
328   commonly occur in real-world shapes. However, our method is general, and one may implement it
329   with a different choice of R̂ by generating a set of rotation matrices representing the symmetries in
330   R̂ and retraining our model. This amounts to editing a few lines of code before retraining.
331
332   Our second component, which we call the flipper, is a neural network trained to solve the classifica-
333
      tion problem 5. We illustrate the output of each stage of this pipeline in Figure 5. As many shapes
      possess multiple plausible orientations, we use conformal prediction to enable our flipper to output
334
      adaptive prediction sets whose size varies with the flipper model’s uncertainty. We provide further
335
      implementation details below.
336
337
      Quotient orienter. We parametrize our quotient orienter by a DGCNN (Wang et al., 2019) op-
      erating on point clouds. To ensure that our predicted orientations lie in SO(3), we follow Brégier
338
      (2021) and map model outputs from R3×3 to SO(3) by solving the special orthogonal Procrustes
339
      problem. We train the quotient orienter on point clouds sampled from the surfaces of meshes in
340   Shapenet (Chang et al., 2015). As these meshes are pre-aligned to lie in canonical orientation, we
341   fix ΩS = I for all training shapes S. We provide full architecture and training details in Appendix
342   B.
343
      In our experiments, we observe that our quotient orienter yields accurate predictions for most input
344
      rotations R but fails for a small subset of rotations. To handle this, we follow Liu et al. (2016) and
345
      employ test-time augmentation to improve our model’s predictions. This consists of (1) randomly
346   rotating the inputs RS by K random rotations Rk ∼ U (SO(3)), k = 1, ..., K, (2) obtaining the quo-
347   tient orienter’s predictions fθ (Rk RS) for each shape, (3) returning these predictions to the original
348   input’s orientation by computing Rk⊤ fθ (Rk RS), and (4) outputting the prediction Rk⊤∗ fθ (Rk∗ RS)
349   with the smallest average quotient distance to the remaining predictions.
350   Flipper. We also parametrize our flipper by a DGCNN operating on point clouds. We train the
351   flipper on point clouds sampled from the surface of Shapenet meshes by optimizing Equation 5. We
352   draw rotations Q ∼ U (O) during training, and simulate inaccuracies in our quotient orienter’s pre-
353   dictions by further rotating the training shapes about a randomly drawn axis by an angle uniformly
354   drawn from [0, 10] degrees. We provide full architecture and training details in Appendix B.
355   We also employ test-time augmentation to improve our flipper model’s predictions. Similarly to the
356
      case with the quotient orienter, we (1) randomly flip the inputs by K random rotations Rk ∼ R̂ = O,
357   (2) obtain the flipper’s predictions for each shape, (3) return these predictions to the original input’s
358   orientation, and (4) output the plurality prediction.
359
      Adaptive prediction sets. Many real-world shapes have several plausible canonical orientations,
360
      even when they lack rotational symmetries. Furthermore, the flipper model may map nearly-
361   symmetric shapes with unique canonical orientations to a uniform distribution over their near-
362   symmetries due to factors such as insufficiently dense point clouds or the smoothness of the flipper
363   function.
364
      To mitigate this issue in pipelines with a human in the loop, we enable our flipper model to output
365
      adaptive prediction sets whose size varies with the flipper’s uncertainty (Romano et al., 2020). This
366
      method uses a small conformal calibration set drawn from the validation data to learn a tuning
367   parameter τ > 0 that controls the size of the prediction sets. Given the flipper model’s output
368
      probabilities pϕ (S) ∈ ∆|R̂|−1 for some shape S, one sorts pϕ (S) in descending order and adds
369
      elements of R̂ to the prediction set until their total mass in pϕ (S) reaches τ . Intuitively, these sets
370
      will be small when the flipper is confident in its prediction and assigns large mass to the highest-
371   probability classes. Conversely, the sets will be large when the flipper is uncertain and assigns
372   similar mass to most classes.

530
531
      This work introduces a state-of-the-art method for 3D orientation estimation. Whereas previous
      approaches can only infer upright orientations for limited classes of shapes, our method successfully
532
      recovers entire orientations for general shapes. We show that naı̈ve regression-based approaches for
533
      orientation estimation degenerate on rotationally-symmetric shapes, which are common in practice,
534   and develop a two-stage orientation pipeline that avoids these obstacles. Our pipeline first orients
535   an arbitrarily rotated input shape up to an octahedral symmetry, and then predicts the octahedral
536   symmetry that maps the first-stage output to the canonically-oriented shape. We anticipate that this
537   factorization of geometric learning problems will be broadly applicable throughout 3D deep learning
538   for tackling problems that are ill-posed due to the presence of symmetries. We also believe that our
539   results can be further improved by training our quotient orienter and flipper models on larger datasets
      of consistently-oriented shapes as they become available.

      B.3   A DAPTIVE PREDICTION SETS
778
      We implement adaptive prediction sets following the method in Angelopoulos et al. (2020) with
779   their regularization parameter λ set to 0. To calibrate our conformal flipper, we first draw a subset of
780   the validation set (the calibration set), apply a random octahedral flip Q ∼ U (O) to each calibration
781   shape, and then pass each flipped shape QS through the trained flipper to obtain class probabilities
782   pϕ (QS) ∈ ∆2 3. The calibration score for a shape S is the sum of the model’s class probabilities
783   p(QS)i ranked in descending order, up to and including the true class i∗ corresponding to the ground
784   truth flip Q. We fix a confidence level 1 − α and return the (1 − α)-th quantile τ of the calibration
785   scores for each shape in the calibration set. In general, smaller values of 1 − α lead to smaller values
786   of τ , which ultimately results in smaller prediction sets at inference time, whereas large values of
787
      1 − α lead to larger prediction sets at inference time but with stronger guarantees that these sets
      include the true flip.
788
789   At inference time, we first obtain the flipper model’s output probabilities pϕ (S) ∈ ∆|R̂|−1 for some
790   shape S, then sort pϕ (S) in descending order and add elements of R̂ to the prediction set until their
791   total mass in pϕ (S) reaches τ . Intuitively, these sets will be small when the flipper is confident in
792   its prediction and assigns large mass to the highest-probability classes. Conversely, the sets will be
793   large when the flipper is uncertain and assigns similar mass to most classes.
