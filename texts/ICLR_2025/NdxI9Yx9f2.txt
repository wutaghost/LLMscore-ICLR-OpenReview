009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Recent successes in self-supervised learning (SSL) model spatial co-occurrences
012            of visual features either by masking portions of an image or by aggressively crop-
013            ping it. Here, we propose a new way to model spatial co-occurrences by aligning
014            local representations (before pooling) with a global image representation. We
015            present CO-SSL, a family of instance discrimination methods and show that it
016            outperforms previous methods on several datasets, including ImageNet-1K where
017            it achieves 71.5% of Top-1 accuracy with 100 pre-training epochs. CO-SSL is also
018
               more robust to noise corruption, internal corruption, small adversarial attacks, and
               large training crop sizes. Our analysis further indicates that CO-SSL learns highly
019
               redundant local representations, which offers an explanation for its robustness.
020
               Overall, our work suggests that aligning local and global representations may be
021            a powerful principle of unsupervised category learning.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Recent self-supervised learning (SSL) approaches learn visual representations that perform well on
027   diverse downstream tasks, including object categorization. These methods include instance discrim-
028   ination (ID) (Chen et al., 2020a) and masked modeling (MM) (He et al., 2022; Bao et al., 2021)
029   approaches. While ID methods train representations to be invariant over different small crops of
030   augmented images, MM uses a partially masked image to predict the masked information. Existing
031   analyses suggest that the best configurations involve discarding most of the information from the
032   images (Tian et al., 2021; He et al., 2022; Assran et al., 2023). Thus, in both cases, the learning
033
      mechanism hinges on the same learning principle, i.e. modeling the co-occurrences of visual fea-
      tures in different parts of an image. For instance, if there is an elephant trunk, the method may
034
      learn that there is likely also an elephant tusk in the image, and vice versa. Intuitively, this makes
035
      category recognition (an elephant) less sensitive to the exact visual features (trunk or tusk) present
036   in an image.
037
038   Spatial statistical learning is also a fundamental aspect of biological vision. A classic study investi-
039
      gated the ability of humans to extract spatial co-occurrences among features (Fiser & Aslin, 2001).
      They used a set of 12 shapes, which, unknown to subjects, were organized into 6 pairs. During a
040
      familiarization phase, subjects were exposed to arrays of shapes where the members of a pair were
041
      always adjacent, without specific instructions. During a test phase, subjects had to name the more
042   familiar pair among a previously seen pair and a new pair. Subjects were able to identify the famil-
043   iar pair, confirming their ability to learn spatial regularities during familiarization. Follow-up works
044   showed that the extraction of spatial co-occurrences is automatic (Turk-Browne et al., 2005; Fiser &
045   Aslin, 2002) and allows the creation of perceptual “chunks” (Fiser & Aslin, 2005).
046
      Here, we introduce a new self-supervised learning model that constructs similar visual represen-
047
      tations for frequently co-occurring local visual features. Concretely, we propose applying an SSL
048   loss function between local representations (right before a final pooling stage) and the global image
049   representation at the final layer, resulting in a new family of SSL methods we call Co-Occurrence
050   SSL (CO-SSL). Intuitively, CO-SSL “pushes” a global image representation (elephant) to lie at the
051   center of and “attract” its co-occurring local representations (trunk, tusk, etc.). We derive two mem-
052   bers of this family, namely CO-BYOL and CO-DINO. To fully harness CO-SSL’s ability to relate
053   representations at different spatial scales, we introduce the RF-ResNet family of neural architec-
      tures, a variation of ResNets (He et al., 2016) that extracts an averaged bag of patch representations

054
      with small RFs after the last pooling layer. This enhances CO-SSL’s ability to infer the statistical
055   relationships between different portions of an image.
056
057   Our experiments show that CO-SSL achieves 71.5% accuracy with 100 pre-training epochs on
058
      ImageNet-1K. CO-SSL also outperforms its SSL counterparts on Tiny-ImageNet, ImageNet-100
      and ImageNet-1K (10% images) and is more robust to noise corruption, internal corruption, small
059
      adversarial attacks. Our analysis shows that CO-SSL extracts more redundant local representations,
060
      offering an explanation for its robustness. In sum, our contributions are the following:
061
062          • We propose CO-SSL, a new family of SSL methods that model co-occurrences among local
063            and global representations;
064          • we introduce a new CNN architecture, RF-ResNet, that extracts a bag of local patch repre-
065            sentations to harness CO-SSL’s ability to align local and global representations;
066
             • we identify which properties of CO-SSL make it better at category recognition and more
067
               robust to diverse corruptions and small adversarial attacks.
068
069   Overall, our work demonstrates the benefits of aligning local and global image representations for
070   seeing the whole in its parts.

      2   R ELATED WORKS
073
074
      Self-supervised ID learning. ID methods learn to align visual representations of different views of
075
      an image, each view passing through two different data-augmentation pipelines (Chen et al., 2020a;
076   Grill et al., 2020; Bardes et al., 2022a; He et al., 2020). Importantly, augmentations drastically alter
077   images without perturbing their semantic content, e.g. cropping, color jittering . . . ID methods split
078   into four approaches that mainly differ with respect to how they keep all representations different
079   from each other: 1- contrastive learning methods explicitly make an image representation dissimilar
080   from all other images’ representations (Chen et al., 2020a; He et al., 2020; Wang & Qi, 2022; Hu
081   et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023b; Dwibedi et al., 2021); 2- Distillation methods use asymmetric
082   neural network architectures to align two image representations (Grill et al., 2020; Chen & He,
083   2021; Gidaris et al., 2020; Caron et al., 2021); 3- Feature decorrelation methods reduce the feature
084
      redundancy within/across views’ representations (Zbontar et al., 2021; Bardes et al., 2022a; Wang
      et al., 2023a); 4- clustering-based methods apply one of the previously mentioned method, but on
085
      clusters of image representations (Caron et al., 2020; Pang et al., 2022; Amrani et al., 2022; Estepa
086
      et al., 2023). For more information, we refer to a recent review of ID (Giakoumoglou & Stathaki,
087   2024). Unlike us, these works do not focus on learning similar representations for co-occurring
088   visual features, beyond learning invariance to crops at each training iteration.
089
090   Patch representations. Learning patch representations in a neural network can significantly boost
091   the supervised performance of diverse neural architectures (Trockman & Kolter, 2023; Liu et al.,
092   2022a; Khan et al., 2022). But these works mostly focus on patch embeddings with tiny RFs (e.g.
093   0.5% of the image) and few non-linear layers, making difficult to extract semantic features. (Brendel
094   & Bethge, 2018) found that simply aggregating visually local category predictions constructed with
095   supervision works well; here, we are concerned about SSL. Recent works tend to argue that a rapid
096   increase of the RF size through layers leads to better performance (Ding et al., 2022; Liu et al.,
097
      2022c). Here, we take an opposite stand and show that CO-SSL with small RFs can outperform
      standard methods. Other works employ dense SSL to learn patch representations (Bardes et al.,
098
      2022b; Wang et al., 2021; Xie et al., 2021b; Yun et al., 2022; Ziegler & Asano, 2022), but have not
099
      demonstrated strong benefits for downstream category recognition

107   the sample efficiency of ID methods (Caron et al., 2020; 2021). A notable adaptation of VICReg
      (Bardes et al., 2022a) proposes an extreme form of multi-crop with tens of crops. Once aggregated,

144   3     M ETHOD
145
146
      CO-SSL aims to learn similar representations for visual features that co-occur in the same image.
147   Instead of leveraging co-occurrences between image crops, we apply the loss function of a given
148   SSL algorithm between local representations (before the final pooling layer) and the global repre-
149   sentation of an image (last layer of the visual backbone). To analyze the impact of the RF size of
150   local representations, we introduce a new family of convolutional architectures named Receptive
151   Field ResNet (RF-ResNet). Each RF-ResNet is configured by the maximum RF size of its local
152   representations, while keeping the same amount of layers and parameters.

154   3.1   CO-SSL: C O -O CCURRENCE S ELF -S UPERVISED L EARNING
155
156   CO-SSL in general. In principle, CO-SSL is adaptable to most SSL methods (SimCLR Chen
157   et al. (2020a), MoCoV3 Chen et al. (2021), BYOL Grill et al. (2020) . . . ). Relative to the original
158   SSL method, the CO-SSL variant extracts local representations (before the average pooling layer)
159   and individually forwards them to a new projection head. Then, CO-SSL computes the averaged
160   SSL loss function between individual local embeddings and the global embedding computed by
161
      the original method. To demonstrate the generality of CO-SSL, we implement CO-BYOL, CO-
      MoCoV3 and CO-DINO. Because of space constraints we only describe CO-BYOL in detail.

198   3.2    RF-R ES N ET FAMILY
199
200   To promote the learning of relationships among local image features, we want to avoid excessively
201
      large receptive field sizes prior to the final average pooling. Modern CNN architectures learn local
      representations with RF size beyond 400 × 400 pixels (Araujo et al., 2019). Given that the standard
202
      input images in SSL have a size of 224 × 224, each “local” representation may potentially be a
203
      representation of the whole image. Thus, we here propose a modification to the ResNet family to
204   compute local representations with RF smaller than the size of the image. For a fair comparison,
205   we further ensure that, for a given RF size, the number of layers and parameters remain the same.
206   Note that optimizing the neural network architecture is orthogonal to our contribution (Tan & Le,
207   2019; Sandler et al., 2018). The RF-ResNet family, shown in Figure 2, includes three modifications
208   compared to ResNets.
209

210   ResNet50). To achieve this, we severly limit convolution strides and kernel sizes (cf. Appendix B.1
211   for a formula to compute RF sizes, taken from Araujo et al. (2019)). We replace all 3×3 convolution
212   layers by 1 × 1 convolution layers (with stride 1), except the first 3 × 3 convolution in the first and
213   middle blocks in each of the four stacks of blocks. We also keep identical the very first convolution
214   of the ResNet. The stride of the first 3 × 3 convolution in each middle block is always set to
215   1. Depending on the desired RF size, we add hyperparameters that control whether we keep the
      MaxPool layer (m) and denote the strides of the 3 × 3 convolution in the second to fourth blocks (s,

      Second, local features on their own may be insufficient to build strong global representations. Be-
240
      tween the average pooling layer and the projection heads, we include an MLP with the same structure

      as one convolution block (BottleNeck or Basic depending on the ResNet), with a number of output

247

251   the network. In the following, we call “RF99-ResNet50” a RF-ResNet50 with hyperparameters that

      4.4    CO-SSL BUILDS SIMILAR LOCAL REPRESENTATIONS BY INFERRING STATISTICAL
458
             CO - OCCURRENCES .
459
460
      To better understand the impact of CO-BYOL on the learnt representations, we extract a random
461
      subset of 250,000 different image pairs from the ImageNet-1K validation set and compute the co-
462
      sine similarity between global representations of images and the average pair-wise cosine similarity
463   between local representations of images. First, we observe in Figure 3B) that local similarities
464   strongly correlate with global similarities for all methods and architectures, confirming that local
465   representations shape the structure of distances between global representations. Second, an impor-
466   tant difference between CO-BYOL and BYOL is the increased similarity between intra-image local
467   representations (vertical bars). This indicates that CO-BYOL extracts similar local representations
468   within an image, regardless of the neural architecture. This is likely due to the triangle inequality:
469   local representations of an image are all pushed towards the same global representation, and thus
470   pushed towards each other. We presume that this explains the robustness of CO-SSL, as a corruption
471
      must then alter several local representations instead of one to corrupt a global feature.
472   The high similarity between local representations may have two origins. First, it may be that CO-
473   BYOL infers co-occurrence statistics to construct a features representation that is invariant to fre-
474   quently co-occurring features representations. Second, CO-BYOL may shortcut the learning process
475   by learning the same local representations covering the whole image, but through different paths in
476   the visual backbone. The latter point is impossible for RF99-R50 due to the bounded receptive fields
477
      of local representations; this means that CO-BYOL indeed infers their co-occurrence statistics. To
      investigate the strategy of CO-BYOL (R50), we plot the saliency maps resulting from maximiz-
478
      ing all the units in local representations in Figure 4. This allows us to see the effective RFs of
479
      local representations for a given image (Luo et al., 2016). We observe that the saliency maps of
480   CO-BYOL (R50) cover distinct subparts of an image, although larger than CO-BYOL (RF99-R50).
481   Furthermore, our quantitative analysis in Appendix A.5 indicates that the number of “impactful”
482   pixels is only slightly higher for a ResNet50 than for RF99-ResNet50. Thus, CO-BYOL (R50)
483   probably employs a mixed strategy of shortcut and inference. This offers an explanation of why
484   CO-BYOL (RF99-50) performs slightly better than CO-BYOL (R50) in Table 1, 6 and 7. Overall,
485   CO-BYOL manages to construct (dis)similar local representations by inferring their frequency of
      co-occurrences.

      5     C ONCLUSION
509
510
      We proposed CO-SSL, a family of SSL approaches that make similar local representations (before
511
      final pooling layer) and global representations (before projection heads). We instantiated two models
512
      of this family, namely CO-BYOL and CO-DINO. We applied CO-SSL with standard CNNs and our
513   newly introduced RF-ResNet, a CNN family that bounds the size of the receptive field of local
514   representations. We tested CO-SSL on a series of datasets, including ImageNet-1K (100 epochs),
515   and found that CO-BYOL consistently outperforms the comparison baselines. We also discovered
516   that CO-BYOL is more robust than BYOL to noise corruptions, internal masking, small adversarial
517   attacks and large minimum crop ratios. Our analysis demonstrates that CO-SSL assigns similar local
518   representations to co-occurring visual features, which presumably rules the advantages of CO-SSL.
519   We found that a simple averaged bag of patch representations (20% of the image) learnt by CO-
520   BYOL reaches 71.2% of accuracy on ImageNet-1K (100 epochs), which is on par with CO-BYOL
521   (ResNet-50). Such a good performance seems counterintuitive, as it may critically prevent the in-
522   tegration of local features into global ones. However, recent findings highlight that standard CNNs
523   mostly extract similar patchworks of local features without taking into account their global arrange-
524   ment (Baker & Elder, 2022; Baker et al., 2018; Jarvers & Neumann, 2023). In humans, the ventral
525   stream may also employ this strategy (Jagadeesh & Gardner, 2022; Ayzenberg & Behrmann, 2022),
526   emphasizing the potential importance of this processing stage. Yet, we lacked computational re-
527
      sources to touch the boundaries of what can be learned with a bag of small patch representations.
      That would require even deeper ResNets and more training epochs.
528
529   Previous works highlighted the importance of different real-world co-occurrences to build semantic
530   visual representation, e.g. across modalities (Radford et al., 2021) or time (Aubret et al., 2023;
531   Parthasarathy et al., 2023). Here, we also showed the advantages of modelling spatial co-occurrences
532   at the level of the whole object and large object parts through local and global representations. In
533
      theory, one could exploit even lower-level visual co-occurrences (e.g. between neighboring pixels or
      low-level representations) for learning intermediate representations. Given our results, we speculate
534
      that this may make the representations even more robust to corruptions and adversarial attacks.

942   A.5   Q UANTITATIVE ANALYSIS OF THE RF
943
      In Section 4.4, we qualitatively observed that local representations of CO-BYOL (R50) focus on
944
      distinct subparts of an image. To investigate this question quantitatively, we compute the ERF
945   of BYOL and CO-BYOL following a procedure similar to Luo et al. (2016) on the ImageNet-1k
946   validation set: we backpropagate the gradient of local representations onto the image, normalize the
947   resulting saliency map with its maximum value and compute the square root of the number of pixels
948   with a gradient value superior to 1 − 95% = 5% and to 1 − 68% = 32% of the best gradient value.
949
      Figure 6 shows that a ResNet50 has a much larger number of pixels that slightly impact local rep-
950
      resentations (> 5%) compared to RF99-ResNet50. We also see that CO-BYOL tends to slightly
951   extend the ERF, compared to BYOL. In contrast, if we look at the number of “important” pixels
952   (> 32%) in Figure 6, we observe that the difference of ERF sizes between a ResNet50 and a RF99-
953   ResNet50 is relatively small. This suggests that a local representation is mostly shaped by very local
954   visual features.

956   A.6   “L AYER 4” IS THE BEST LAYER TO SAMPLE LOCAL REPRESENTATIONS
957
958   Previous work proposed to maximize a SSL loss between the final representation and intermediate
959   representations Jang et al. (2023); Bachman et al. (2019). Here, we replicate their analysis with our
960   RF99-ResNet, loss function, a crop size of 0.2 and a downsampling of 64 (because feature maps
961
      can be very large in intermediate representations). Following the ResNet nomenclature, we find that
      selecting CO-BYOL’s local representations in the “layer3” or “layer2” of RF99-ResNet leads to a
962
      respective decrease of accuracy of 0.4% and 2.1% in ImageNet-100. We deduce that the last layer
963
      is more appropriate for CO-SSL.
