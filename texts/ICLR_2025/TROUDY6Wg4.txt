                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011
               Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF) has emerged as a pivotal
012
               tool for aligning large language models (LLMs) with human preferences. Direct
013
               Preference Optimization (DPO), one of the most popular approaches, formulates
014            RLHF as a policy optimization problem without explicitly estimating the reward
015            function. It overcomes the stability and efficiency issues of two-step approaches,
016            which typically involve first estimating the reward function and then optimizing
017            the policy via proximal policy optimization (PPO). Since RLHF is essentially an
018            optimization problem, and it is well-known that momentum techniques can ac-
019            celerate optimization both theoretically and empirically, a natural question arises:
020            Can RLHF be accelerated by momentum? This paper answers this question in
021            the affirmative. In detail, we first show that the iterative preference optimization
022
               method can be viewed as a proximal point method. Based on this observation,
               we propose a general Accelerated Preference Optimization (APO) framework,
023
               which unifies many existing preference optimization algorithms and employs Nes-
024
               terovвАЩs momentum technique to speed up the alignment of LLMs. Theoretically,
025            we demonstrate that APO can achieve a faster convergence rate than the standard
026            iterative preference optimization methods, including DPO and SPPO. Empirically,
027            we show the superiority of APO over DPO, iterative DPO, and other strong base-
028            lines for RLHF on the AlpacaEval 2.0 benchmark.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032   Large Language Models (LLMs) have emerged as a pivotal technique in the era of artificial gen-
033   eral intelligence and recently demonstrated impressive capabilities in tasks such as text generation
034   (Bubeck et al., 2023; Anil et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023), coding (Chen et al., 2021; Austin et al.,
035   2021), and problem solving (Cobbe et al., 2021; Wei et al., 2022). A key element contributing to
036   these achievements is the alignment of LLMs with human preference data, utilizing reinforcement
037   learning from human feedback (RLHF) (Ziegler et al., 2019; Christiano et al., 2017; Ouyang et al.,
038   2022; Bai et al., 2022; Munos et al., 2023).
039   The standard RLHF method (Ouyang et al., 2022) involves three main steps: feedback collection, re-
040   ward modeling, and policy optimization. Specifically, the LLM receives human-generated prompts
041   and produces several possible responses. Subsequently, human preferences for these responses are
042
      collected and used to train a reward model that matches these preferences. Finally, the policy opti-
      mization process updates the large language model using optimization algorithms such as Proximal
043
      Policy Optimization (PPO) (Schulman et al., 2017) to produce responses with higher preference
044
      based on the trained reward model.
045
      Recently, Rafailov et al. (2023) introduced the Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) method based
046
      on the Bradley-Terry (BT) model (Bradley & Terry, 1952). This method skips the reward modeling
047
      process and replaces it with a reparameterized reward with respect to the policy, allowing for di-
048   rect optimization of the LLM. It simplifies the implementation, while maintaining a comparable or
049   even better performance than standard RLHF. Later, several works (Munos et al., 2023; Azar et al.,
050   2024; Wu et al., 2024; Rosset et al., 2024) considered the general preference model rather than the
051   BT model, and designed algorithms (Nash-MD, IPO, SPPO, and DNO) that more flexibly repre-
052   sent human preferences while maintaining a simple implementation. Nonetheless, both the standard
053   RLHF process and its variations rely on the policy optimization process. In fact, for general op-
      timization problems, it is well-known that momentum techniques (Polyak, 1964; Nesterov, 2013;

054
      Bubeck et al., 2015b) can accelerate the convergence of the optimization algorithm in both theory
055   and practice. Therefore, a natural question arises:
056
057                    Can reinforcement learning from human feedback be accelerated?
058   In this paper, we affirmatively answer this question. In details, following Xu et al. (2023); Yuan
059   et al. (2024b); Chen et al. (2024); Wu et al. (2024), we consider the iterative preference optimization
060   framework. In this framework, a series of models is constructed, with each model being improved
061   based on the previous one using preference optimization algorithm such as DPO and SPPO. We
062   demonstrate that the policy optimization problem under this framework resembles the proximal
063   point method (Bregman, 1967; Censor & Zenios, 1992; Kiwiel, 1997). Based on this observation,
064
      we adapt NesterovвАЩs acceleration method (Nesterov, 1983; 2008; 2013; Lin et al., 2018), and intro-
      duce a variant of the iterative preference optimization framework, named Accelerated Preference
065
      Optimization (APO). At the core of APO is an extrapolation step after each policy update. The
066
      contributions of our work are highlighted as follows:

468   Next, we discuss the effect of generation length. All iterative DPO training baselines, including
469   SnorkelвАЩs Mistral-PairRM-DPO and our DPO Iter3, exhibit a trend of increasing response
470   length. This is inevitable because all response pairs used for training are generated online and ranked
471   by PairRM, where longer sequences are more likely to be chosen as winners. This effect accumulates
472
      as the model trains on its own generation. We discuss this effect from the following perspectives:
      First, both humans and GPT-4 exhibit length bias, and our goal is not to prevent any length growth
473
      but to evaluate it from a perspective of fairness. Therefore, we primarily refer to the LC win rate 2 ,
474
      which reflects the predicted win rate when response lengths are at the same level as the baseline.
475   Second, we note that APO Iter2 achieves a significantly higher LC win rate and overall win
476   rate compared to SnorkelвАЩs iterative DPO implementation, while generating much shorter responses
477   (2636 characters compared to 2736). When compared to our DPO Iter3, APO Iter2 achieves
478   a comparable win rate, but also with shorter response lengths. This verifies a faster convergence rate
479   and enhanced performance of our methodology. Finally, this length growth is not inherent to our
480   method but is a result of the online training and PairRM ranking mechanism. It can be mitigated if
481   we query winner/loser pairs and preference choices from GPT-4 or human, with much higher cost.

533   6     C ONCLUSIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
534   In this work, we studied the iterative preference optimization framework for aligning large language
535   models (LLMs) with human preferences and showed that it resembles the proximal point method.
536   Based on this observation, we introduced a general framework, APO, incorporating NesterovвАЩs mo-
537   mentum technique. Theoretically, we show that our method achieves a faster convergence rate than
538   the standard iterative DPO and SPPO methods. Our experimental results demonstrate the superiority
539   of APO over iterative DPO on the AlpacaEval 2.0 benchmark and on the instruction-following tasks
      of MT-Bench, achieving both accelerated convergence rate, and better final performance.

      A     R ELATED W ORK
801   A.1   R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING FROM H UMAN F EEDBACK
802
      Learning from human feedback in reinforcement learning can be traced back to Knox & Stone
803
      (2009); Wirth et al. (2017) and was later popularized by Christiano et al. (2017), which incorporated
804   human preferences into deep reinforcement learning. Recently, RLHF has gained popularity in
805   natural language processing and has become a paradigmatic method for fine-tuning large language
806   models (LLMs) (Achiam et al., 2023; Touvron et al., 2023; OpenAI, 2023) to align with human
807   objectives. The standard process for alignment with human feedback (Ouyang et al., 2022) requires
808   both reward modeling and policy optimization. Recently, Rafailov et al. (2023) proposed the Direct
809   Preference Optimization method, which replaces the reward modeling process with a reparame-
      terized reward and directly performs policy optimization using preference data. Similarly, Kawin


                                                       15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      et al. (2023) proposed the Kahneman-Tversky Optimization method (KTO) with a reparameterized
811   reward, while considering different human-aware loss functions.
812
      More recently, iterative variants of policy optimization have garnered increasing attention. Xu
813
      et al. (2023) investigated the iterative preference optimization method and proposed the PAIRWISE
814   CRINGE algorithm, which iteratively generates a new preference dataset using the current model
815   and then updates the model with a combination of the original preference data and the newly la-
816   beled data. Additionally, they studied the iterative version of Direct Preference Optimization (DPO)
817   and demonstrated that it achieves better performance than standard PPO or DPO. Later, Yuan et al.
818   (2024b) studied Self-Rewarding Language Models, where the algorithm iteratively updates the ref-
819   erence policy in the DPO method and uses LLM-as-a-Judge prompting (Zheng et al., 2024b) to
820   provide its own rewards for the generated new preference data. Despite this work demonstrating the
821   superiority of iterative optimization process, there is a lack of theoretical foundations for these prac-
822
      tical frameworks. Recently, Xiong et al. (2023) proposed Gibbs Sampling from Human Feedback,
      offering theoretical analysis with the aid of linear function approximation and the incorporation of
823
      an uncertainty bonus. Compared with previous methods, the reference policy remains fixed, the
824
      newly trained model is only used to generate new preference data, and this trained model will not be
825   inherited in the subsequent iteration. On the contrary, our research focuses on studying the iterative
826   DPO model without incorporating an uncertainty bonus, and it continually updates the reference
827   policy throughout the process.
828   Most of the works motioned above rely on the assumption that the latent preference distribution
829   P (y1 вЙї y2 |x) follows the Bradley-Terry (BT) model (Bradley & Terry, 1952)), and there exists a
830   series of works focusing on general preference, where human preference may not strictly be transi-
831   tive. Under the general preference assumption, iterative optimization (Munos et al., 2023; Swamy
832   et al., 2024; Rosset et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2024) is also employed to find the Nash-equilibrium
833   policy. We leave the extension of our algorithm to general preference model in the future work.
834
835   A.2   ACCELERATED O PTIMIZATION WITH M OMENTUM
836
837   The idea of accelerating gradient methods has been extensively explored over the decades. One of
838   the earliest contributions to this field was the Polyak momentum (Polyak, 1964), which achieved
839   faster convergence by leveraging the history of previous iterates. However, this early approach
840   sometimes failed to converge even for strongly convex objectives (Lessard et al., 2016). This was
841
      further refined by NesterovвАЩs accelerated gradient (NAG) (Nesterov, 1983), with a guarantee for
      faster convergence rates for general smooth convex functions. Following these foundational works,
842
      other acceleration algorithms and analyses emerged for gradient descent and its proximal variants
843
      (Beck & Teboulle, 2009; Tseng, 2009; Taylor et al., 2017; Allen-Zhu & Orecchia, 2014; Bubeck
844   et al., 2015a; Diakonikolas & Orecchia, 2017; Cohen et al., 2020). In addition, there has been
845   another line of work in understanding NesterovвАЩs acceleration through continuous analysis and Lya-
846   punov function analysis (Su et al., 2016; Shi et al., 2019; Shi, 2021; Shi et al., 2022; Chen et al.,
847   2022). Momentum techniques have also proven effective in accelerating minimax game settings (Jin
848   et al., 2022; Kovalev et al., 2022; Thekumparampil et al., 2022; Li et al., 2023a; Yuan et al., 2024a).
849   However, the application of acceleration techniques to LLMs remains relatively unexplored.
850   The Proximal Point Method (PPM) (Moreau, 1965; Martinet, 1970; Rockafellar, 1976; GuћИler, 1992;
851   Bauschke & Combettes) is a fundamental methodology that achieves optimality by iteratively solv-
852   ing auxiliary problems in a regularized form. Generally, these auxiliary problems cannot be solved
853   directly, and different approximation methods yield various types of acceleration (He & Yuan, 2012;
854   Salzo et al., 2012). One of the connections between PPM and NAG is established in Ahn & Sra
855
      (2022), where the authors interpret the updates of NAG as different approximations of the prox-
      imal method. The Catalyst approach (Lin et al., 2015; 2018) represents another variant of PPM
856
      that achieves accelerated convergence through practical inexact schemes (Lin et al., 2015; Salzo
857
      et al., 2012). This method effectively integrates NesterovвАЩs acceleration with the proximal point
858   framework, making it applicable to a wide range of machine learning algorithms, including gradient
859   descent and block coordinate descent. It can be applied to variance reduction algorithms (Lin et al.,
860   2015; 2018), demonstrating its strong adaptability to various machine learning scenarios. Despite
861   the widespread adoption of momentum acceleration in both theoretical and practical applications,
862   we are the first to establish a connection between DPO and the proximal point method, linking it-
863   erative DPO with the Catalyst approach, and providing a comprehensive theoretical analysis of this
      interpretation.
