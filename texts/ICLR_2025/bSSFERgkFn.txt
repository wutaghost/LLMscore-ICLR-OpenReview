009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
012            In this paper, we introduce a novel counterfactual reasoning method using eBERT
013            embeddings to convert customers from an e-commerce company who frequently
014            add items to their cart but don’t proceed to checkout. We demonstrate that our
015            method i) outperforms existing techniques such as DiCE, GANs, and CFRL in
016
               key metrics such as coverage, while also maintaining a low latency; ii) balances
               high coverage and low latency by adjusting the number of nearest unlike neigh-
017
               bors, highlighting a trade-off between these competing goals; and iii) allows cus-
018
               tomization of mutable features, improving the practical applicability of our coun-
019            terfactual explanations.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026
027
      In various industries, counterfactual reasoning can be used to analyze ‘what-if’ scenarios to better
      understand customer behavior. For instance, customers often add items to their cart but do not
028
      proceed to checkout. Similarly, it is also important for loan companies Zhang et al. (2023); Grath
029
      et al. (2023), insurance providers Rye & Boyd (2022); Kumar & Ravi (Year), and fraud detection
030   Whitrow et al. (2009); Ngai et al. (2011) companies to explain to their customers why they were
031   denied loans or insurance. Understanding the reasons behind these behaviors and finding strategies
032   to convert these customers is important for improving conversion rates. In recent years, the demand
033   for explainable AI has grown, becoming essential across sectors to ensure transparency and trust
034   among customers Gohel et al. (2023); Verma et al. (2020); Adadi & Berrada (2018).
035
      Existing counterfactual reasoning methods like Nearest Instance Counterfactual Explanations
036   (NICE) Brughmans et al. (2022), Diverse Counterfactual Explanations (DiCE) Mothilal et al. (2019),
037   Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) Goodfellow et al. (2014), Counterfactual GANs (Coun-
038   teRGAN) Nemirovsky et al. (2021), and Counterfactuals with Reinforcement Learning (CFRL)
039   Samoilescu et al. (2021) often suffer from either high latency, lack of mutability (i.e., changing
040   only user-specified features), or suboptimal performance in terms of plausibility (i.e., how closely
041   the counterfactual resembles the real world data) and distance (i.e., distance between the original
042   instance and the counterfactual). Providing counterfactuals with actionable and customizable (i.e.,
043   mutable) features is essential for achieving business goals.
044   To address these challenges, we propose a novel counterfactual reasoning method that is built on
045   the foundations of NICE but adds support for mutability and uses contextual embedding techniques
046   to find the nearest neighbors from the opposite class. Our approach involves converting each data
047   row into text, considering both feature and value, and then generating embeddings using a BERT
048   Devlin et al. (2018) model fine-tuned on the company’s product titles (eBERT). Through BERT-
049   based embeddings, feature values are represented better semantically, making it an effective method
050
      of finding neighbors who are similar but have a different label, increasing plausibility.
051   Our new technique is particularly valuable in production systems because it supports mutability,
052   allowing business users to specify which features can be changed. As a result, the generated coun-
053   terfactuals are more actionable. Also, by using embeddings for similarity, the counterfactuals are
      highly plausible, providing the best plausibility and coverage compared to all the baseline methods.

079   2     DATA P REPROCESSING
080
081   Our company’s dataset consists of 200,000 shopping sessions to understand customer behavior. The
082   dataset includes a total of 47 features extracted primarily from User, Cart, and Listing tables. Of
083
      these features, 36 are categorical, such as product categories and purchase data, while the remaining
      11 are numerical variables, like shipping costs, item prices, and feedback scores. The task is a binary
084
      classification of shopping session outcomes, either 1 (i.e., success) if the customer successfully
085
      checked out or 0 (i.e., failure) otherwise.
086
087   To preprocess the data for embedding generation, we scaled the price and shipping fee features
088   between 0 and 100 and categorized them into four buckets. The price features were divided into
089
      four buckets: 0-20 (budget), 20-40 (affordable), 40-60 (premium), and 60-100 (luxury). Shipping
      fees were also categorized into four buckets: 0-25 (low), 25-50 (medium), 50-75 (high), and 75-100
090
      (very high).
091
092   For building the classification pipeline, we removed null values, scaled the data using a standard
093   scaler, and encoded categorical data using a binary encoder. We explored several encoding methods
094   for the categorical features, including label encoding, one-hot encoding, target encoding, and binary
095
      encoding. While one-hot encoding achieved slightly higher accuracy, it led to overfitting due to high
      cardinality, increasing the dataset to over 9,000 features and significantly increasing computation
096
      time. Binary encoding, although slightly less accurate, reduced dimensionality to 165 features,
097
      preventing overfitting and significantly reducing the computation time. Binary encoding was chosen
098   due to its ability to reduce dimensionality and computation time, while preventing overfitting.
099
100   The dataset was divided into a training set of 160,000 instances and a test set of 40,000 instances to
101
      evaluate model performance. This preprocessing step ensured that both categorical and numerical
      features were ready for embedding generation.

      4   BASELINE C OUNTERFACTUAL M ETHODS
127
128   Here, we describe four strong baseline counterfactual reasoning methods we compared against.
129
      Diverse Counterfactual Explanations (DiCE) DiCE Mothilal et al. (2019) primarily focuses on gen-
130
      erating feasible and diverse counterfactuals. It extends counterfactual explanations by incorporating
131   determinantal point processes (DPP) Kulesza et al. (2012), which is a probabilistic model used to
132   ensure diversity in the generated examples. This allows DiCE to provide a range of alternatives for
133   changing outcomes.
134
      DPP selects a subset of diverse examples by maximizing the determinant of a kernel matrix built
135
      from the examples. This diversity is balanced against proximity, which measures the closeness of
136
      counterfactuals to the original input. The method optimizes a loss function that combines y-loss (the
137   difference in prediction), proximity, and diversity, adjusted using hyperparameters λ1 and λ2 . The
138   counterfactuals are generated through gradient descent, which iteratively adjusts feature values to
139   meet the objective while respecting any real-world constraints on feature manipulation.
140
      While DiCE focuses on generating diverse counterfactuals, it suffers from lower coverage and higher
141
      reconstruction error. In contrast, our method achieves higher coverage and lower L1 distance, pro-
142
      viding more actionable and plausible counterfactuals, especially in e-commerce applications where
143   proximity is more critical than diversity.
144
145   Nearest Instance Counterfactual Explanations (NICE) NICE Brughmans et al. (2022) generates
146
      counterfactual explanations using a nearest unlike neighbor-based approach. The algorithm identi-
      fies the nearest neighbor with a different class label and changes one feature value at a time from the
147
      original instance to match that of the neighbor. This process generates hybrid instances, guided by a
148
      reward function that prioritizes sparsity, proximity, or plausibility, depending on the specific NICE
149   variant.
150
151   NICE allows for different objective functions depending on the type of optimization desired. This
152
      approach has been tested across various datasets and domains, demonstrating its flexibility and abil-
      ity to produce minimal yet effective feature changes.
153
154   Although NICE produces counterfactuals with minimal L1 distance, it lacks support for mutability,
155   limiting its practical applicability. Our method not only supports feature mutability, making it more
156   customizable for real-world business needs, but also provides faster counterfactual generation with
157   reduced latency.
158   Generative Adversarial Networks (Standard GAN & CounteRGAN) CounteRGAN Nemirovsky et al.
159   (2021) is an extension of Residual GAN (RGAN), designed to generate realistic and actionable
160   counterfactuals by applying small perturbations to existing data points rather than creating new
161   instances from scratch. The idea is to generate subtle modifications that can flip a model’s prediction,
      while ensuring that the changes are realistic and feasible.


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      In RGAN, the generator produces perturbations that modify input data, while the discriminator
163   attempts to distinguish between real and modified data. CounteRGAN adds a target classifier to
164   this process, which ensures that the generated counterfactuals not only resemble real data but also
165   result in the desired class change. This approach integrates the adversarial learning process with a
166   counterfactual search for more purposeful outcomes.
167
      Standard GAN and CounteRGAN generate counterfactuals with the lowest latency, but at the cost of
168
      higher reconstruction error and L1 distance, which reduces plausibility. Our method, while slightly
169   slower, strikes a balance by providing highly plausible counterfactuals with lower L1 distance and
170   full coverage, making it more effective in producing realistic and actionable outcomes.
171
172
      Counterfactuals using Reinforcement Learning (CFRL) CFRL Samoilescu et al. (2021) uses a rein-
      forcement learning framework for counterfactual generation, transforming the optimization process
173
      into a learnable task. It enables the generation of multiple counterfactuals in a single forward pass,
174
      relying solely on the feedback from the classifier’s predictions. This model-agnostic method allows
175   for feature-level constraints, ensuring real-world feasibility.
176
177   CFRL uses a critic to estimate rewards from the environment and an actor to output counterfactual
178
      latent representations. This method enables high flexibility, as feature-level constraints like im-
      mutability can be incorporated via conditioning vectors, ensuring that the generated counterfactuals
179
      are plausible and actionable.
180
181   CFRL supports mutable feature customization. However, it has the lowest coverage amongst all
182   the other methods and a higher L1 distance compared to our method. By offering a better balance
183   between feature mutability, coverage, and proximity, our method produces more actionable and
184
      realistic counterfactuals.
185   Other counterfactual generation methods such as CERT Sharma et al. (2020) and MACE Karimi
186   et al. (2020) were not considered due to their high latency Schleich et al. (2023), and GeCo Schleich
187   et al. (2023), although it offers many customization options for the counterfactual, suffers from very
188   low coverage Brughmans et al. (2022).

      Our novel embedding-based counterfactual reasoning method is designed to address the limitations
193
      of NICE by using embeddings to find the nearest unlike neighbors, and it supports mutability. Our
194   method performs better than all other methods supporting mutability, specifically DiCE and CFRL,

196   eBERT to generate embeddings that capture deep semantic relationships within our data, in order to
197   identify more plausible and actionable counterfactuals.

221   5.2   N EAREST U NLIKE N EIGHBORS G ENERATION
222
223   After generating embeddings using eBERT, the next step involves finding the nearest unlike neigh-
224   bors, which are used to find a counterfactual explanation. Instead of using the Heterogeneous
225
      Euclidean-Overlap Metric as in NICE, our method employs FAISS (Facebook AI Similarity Search)
      Facebook Engineering (2017) IndexFlatL2 to identify the k nearest unlike neighbors based on the
226
      L2 distance.
227
228   FAISS is a library optimized for fast similarity searches, particularly for high-dimensional vectors
229   such as embeddings. We use the IndexFlatL2 index type, which is a brute-force index that searches
230   the nearest neighbors using L2 distance. For example, in our case, an embedding vector representing
231
      an unsuccessful shopping session is queried against the index containing embeddings of successful
      sessions to find the k nearest neighbors with the “successful” class label.
232
233   The nearest unlike neighbors retrieved represent instances that lie on the opposite side of the de-
234   cision boundary. Each of these neighbors has different values for features such as PRICE, SH-
235   PNG COST, and PAYMNT TYPE, which makes them candidates for generating counterfactuals.
236   The overall process involves building the FAISS index, adding all dataset embeddings, associating
237   each embedding with a class label, and then retrieving the k nearest unlike neighbors using FAISS.

275   5.3   C OUNTERFACTUAL S EARCH
276
277   Once we identify the nearest unlike neighbors, the next step is to generate counterfactuals using a
278   greedy heuristic search method. In counterfactual search, we find data points that can be used to alter
279   a classifier’s decision, while making sure that they have a low distance from the original instance.
280   Using the neighbors identified in the previous section, the greedy heuristic search modifies fea-
281   tures one at a time. Referring to Figure 1, the original instance has a PRICE of expensive, a SH-
282   PNG COST of medium, and uses PAYMNT TYPE = CreditCard. We modify each of these fea-
283   tures individually to match one of the nearest unlike neighbors. For example, lowering the PRICE
284   from expensive to affordable or lowering the SHPNG COST from medium to low could flip the
285   prediction from class 0 to class 1.
286   As the features are adjusted, the heuristic search keeps track of how each modification affects the
287   classifier’s decision. The goal is to generate the closest counterfactual, i.e., the one that modifies the
288   fewest features while successfully flipping the class. In some cases, a combination of modifications,
289   like changing both PRICE and SHPNG COST, may be required.
290
      The Counterfactual Generation Algorithm 1 seeks to identify the closest counterfactual instance to
291   an original data point by iteratively modifying its features to achieve a desired target classification.
292   Initiated with the original instance (xorig ), the algorithm progresses through the feature space by
293   comparing against a set of nearest unlike neighbors (N). For each neighbor, the algorithm creates
294   Xmod , which is a set of new instances derived from xorig where each instance in Xmod is generated
295   by altering one specific mutable feature of xorig to match the corresponding feature in the neighbor.
296
      Each modified instance, denoted as x′ , is evaluated for its potential as a counterfactual: the mod-
297   ifications are checked to ensure they not only bring x′ closer to achieving the target classification
298   but also maintain minimal distance from xorig . This iterative process continues until a satisfactory
299   counterfactual that meets the target classification is found, or all possibilities are exhausted. This
300   method ensures that each proposed counterfactual is a minimal and interpretable adjustment to the
301   instance that alters the model’s decision.

      In this study, we introduced a novel counterfactual reasoning approach that uses embeddings gener-
463
      ated by an eBERT model to create more accurate and actionable counterfactuals. We extend NICE’s
464   basic concepts by adding mutability and advanced natural language embedding techniques. This
465   approach is especially effective in e-commerce settings where companies’ fine-tuned embedding
466   models capture domain-specific data relationships, thus increasing the plausibility of the counter-
467   factuals.
468
      Our experiments conducted on 200K shopping sessions show that our method outperforms existing
469
      counterfactual generation methods such as DiCE, GANs, and CFRL in terms of coverage, recon-
470   struction error, and L1 distance, while maintaining a lower latency. The final latency of just 0.49
471   seconds per counterfactual indicates that our method is suitable for real-time applications.

      Additionally, when testing the method with a limited set of mutable features, it consistently outper-
      formed DiCE and CFRL. The parameter k, representing the number of nearest unlike neighbors,
474
      proved to be an imortant hyperparameter influencing both coverage and latency. Adjusting k allows
475
      us to balance coverage against computational speed when searching for plausible counterfactuals,
476   highlighting the flexibility of our approach. In future work, we will add functionality to specify a
477   range of values for each feature, which will improve the customization of our method.
478
479
      The results demonstrate that our approach not only enhances plausibility but also provides the best
      coverage and outperforms other methods offering mutability, specifically DiCE and CFRL, across
480
      all evaluation metrics.
