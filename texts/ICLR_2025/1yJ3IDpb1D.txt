009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011              Accurately forecasting multiple future events within a given time horizon is cru-
012              cial for finance, retail, social networks, and healthcare applications. Event timing
013              and labels are typically modeled using Marked Temporal Point Processes (MTPP),
014              with evaluations often focused on next-event prediction quality. While some stud-
015              ies have extended evaluations to a fixed number of future events, we demonstrate
016              that this approach leads to inaccuracies in handling false positives and false neg-
017              atives. To address these issues, we propose a novel evaluation method inspired
018
                 by object detection techniques from computer vision. Specifically, we introduce
                 Temporal mean Average Precision (T-mAP), a temporal variant of mAP, which
019
                 overcomes the limitations of existing long-horizon evaluation metrics. Our ex-
020
                 tensive experiments demonstrate that models with strong next-event prediction
021              accuracy can yield poor long-horizon forecasts and vice versa, indicating that spe-
022              cialized methods are needed for each task. To support further research, we re-
023              lease HoTPP1 , the first benchmark designed explicitly for evaluating long-horizon
024              MTPP predictions. HoTPP includes large-scale datasets with up to 43 million
025              events and provides optimized procedures for both autoregressive and parallel in-
026              ference, paving the way for future advancements in the field.

029   1       I NTRODUCTION
030
031   The world is full of events. Internet activity, e-commerce transactions, retail operations, clinical vis-
032   its, and numerous other aspects of our lives generate vast amounts of data in the form of timestamps
033   and related information. In the era of AI, it is crucial to develop methods capable of handling these
034
      complex data streams. We refer to this type of data as Event Sequences (ESs). Event sequences
      differ fundamentally from other data types. Unlike tabular data (Wang & Sun, 2022), ESs include
035
      timestamps and possess an inherent order. In contrast to time series data (Lim & Zohren, 2021), ESs
036
      are characterized by irregular time intervals and additional data fields. These differences necessitate
037   the development of specialized models and evaluation practices.
038
039   Sequence modeling is the primary task in the Event Sequences (ESs) domain. In its simplest form,
040
      each event is defined by its type and occurrence time, a framework commonly known as Marked
      Temporal Point Processes (MTPP) (Rizoiu et al., 2017). Some studies extend MTPP to incorporate
041
      additional data fields and model complex dependencies between them (McDermott et al., 2024).
042
      However, the majority of MTPP approaches, as well as their evaluation pipelines, primarily focus
043   on predicting the next event based on historical data.
044
045   In practice, a common question arises: what events will occur, and when, within a specific time
046
      horizon? Forecasting multiple future events presents unique challenges that differ from traditional
      next-event prediction tasks. For instance, autoregressive event sequence prediction involves apply-
047
      ing the model to its own potentially erroneous predictions. However, the challenges of autoregressive
048
      prediction in the context of MTPP have not been thoroughly explored. Another difficulty lies in eval-
049   uation. Methods like Dynamic Time Warping (DTW) are typically unsuitable for event sequences
050   due to strict ordering constraints (Su & Hua, 2017). Some works have applied Optimal Transport
051   Distance (OTD), a variant of the Wasserstein distance, to compare sequences of predefined lengths.
052   Still, the limitations of this metric have not been previously considered.

068   In this work, we provide the first in-depth analysis of models and metrics for long-horizon event
069   forecasting, establishing a rigorous evaluation framework and a baseline for MTPP studies. Our key
070   contributions are as follows:
071
            1. We demonstrate that widely used evaluation methods for MTPPs often overlook critical
072
               aspects of model performance. We demonstrate that simple rule-based baselines can some-
073            times outperform popular deep learning methods when evaluated using OTD.
074
            2. We introduce Temporal mean Average Precision (T-mAP), a novel evaluation metric in-
075
               spired by best practices in computer vision. T-mAP evaluates variable length sequences
076
               within a specified time horizon, as illustrated in Figure 1. Unlike previous approaches,
077            T-mAP accurately accounts for false positives and false negatives while being invariant to
078            linear calibration. Additionally, we address a theoretical gap in computer vision by proving
079            the correctness of the T-mAP computation algorithm.
080
            3. Using our established methodology, we demonstrate that high next-event prediction accu-
081            racy does not necessarily translate into high-quality long-horizon forecasts; in many cases,
082            our experiments show the opposite. This highlights the necessity of developing specialized
083            models for the long-horizon prediction task.
084         4. We release HoTPP, a new open-source benchmark designed to facilitate long-horizon event
085            sequence prediction research. HoTPP brings together datasets and methods from various
086            domains, including financial transactions, social networks, healthcare, and recommender
087            systems, greatly expanding the diversity and scale of data compared to prior benchmarks.
088            Additionally, we offer an efficient inference algorithm necessary for large-scale evalua-
089            tions.

      2    R ELATED W ORK
093
      Marked Temporal Point Processes. A Marked Temporal Point Process (MTPP) is a stochastic
094
      process consisting of a sequence of pairs (t1 , l1 ), (t2 , l2 ), . . . , where t1 < t2 < . . . represent event
095
      times and li ∈ {1, . . . , L} denote event type labels (Rizoiu et al., 2017). Common approaches to
096   MTPP modeling focus on predicting the next event. A basic solution involves independently pre-
097   dicting the event time and type. A more advanced approach splits the original sequence into subse-
098   quences, one for each event type, and independently models each subsequence’s timing. Depending
099   on the time-step distribution, the process is called Poisson or Hawkes.
100
      Over the last decade, the focus has shifted toward increasing model flexibility by applying neural
101
      architectures. Several works have employed classical RNNs (Du et al., 2016; Xiao et al., 2017; Omi
102   et al., 2019) and transformers (Zuo et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2020), while others have proposed
103   architectures with continuous time (Mei & Eisner, 2017; Rubanova et al., 2019; Kuleshov et al.,
104   2024). In this work, we evaluate MTPP models with both discrete and continuous time architectures.
105   Unlike previous benchmarks, we also assess simple rule-based predictors and popular methods from
106   related fields, including GPT-like prediction models for event sequences (McDermott et al., 2024;
107   Padhi et al., 2021) and Next-K models from time series analysis (Lim & Zohren, 2021). For more
      details on MTPP modeling, refer to Appendix A.

      MTPP Evaluation. Previous benchmarks have primarily focused on medical data or traditional
136
      MTPP datasets. EventStream-GPT (McDermott et al., 2024) and TemporAI (Saveliev & van der
137   Schaar, 2023) consider only medical data and do not implement methods from the MTPP field,
138   despite their applicability. Early MTPP benchmarks, such as Tick (Bacry et al., 2017) and Py-
139   Hawkes2 , implement classical machine learning approaches but exclude modern neural networks.
140   While PoPPy (Xu, 2018) and EasyTPP (Xue et al., 2023) include neural methods, they do not con-
141   sider rule-based and Next-K approaches. Furthermore, PoPPy does not evaluate long-horizon pre-
142   dictions at all. EasyTPP addresses this limitation to some extent by reporting the OTD metric,
143   though it does not provide the corresponding evaluation code.
144   Previous work also highlighted the limitations of Dynamic Time Warping (DTW) for event sequence
145   evaluation (Su & Hua, 2017), showing that DTW’s strict ordering constraints are impractical and
146   should be avoided. This work proposed an alternative, the Order-preserving Wasserstein Distance
147   (OPW). In our study, we address the limitations of OTD, a variant of the Wasserstein Distance, and
148   propose a new metric called T-mAP. Unlike OPW, T-mAP operates on timestamps rather than event
149   indices and involves two hyperparameters, compared to three in OPW.

      3       L IMITATIONS OF THE N EXT-E VENT AND OTD M ETRICS
153
154   MTPPs are typically evaluated based on the accuracy of next-event predictions, with time and type
155   predictions assessed independently. The quality of type predictions is measured by the error rate,
156   while time prediction error is evaluated using either Mean Absolute Error (MAE) or Root Mean
157
      Squared Error (RMSE). However, these metrics do not account for the model’s ability to predict
      multiple future events. For example, in autoregressive models, the outputs are fed back as inputs
158
      for subsequent steps, which can lead to cumulative prediction errors. As a result, long-horizon
159
      evaluation metrics are necessary.

183   K predicted events will extend far beyond the horizon of the first K target events, leading to a

      ground truth events in the evaluation. Therefore, evaluating a dynamic number of events is essential
185
      to align with the ground truth’s time horizon properly.
186
187   MTPP evaluation metrics can be categorized into two groups: those considering event indices and
188   those assessing time and label prediction quality independently of actual positions. Next-event met-
189   rics rely on ordering, even when events share identical timestamps, making the order meaningless.
190
      While OTD itself is invariant to order, the extraction of sequences for comparison is influenced by
      event indices. For example, when OTD compares length prefixes K, the algorithm must place K
191
      correctly predicted events at the beginning of a sequence to minimize the OTD score. As a result,
192
      OTD, like next-event metrics, can depend on event ordering even when the order cannot be uniquely
193   determined. This dependency is non-trivial and difficult to measure or control accurately.
194
195   Another limitation of the OTD metric is its inability to evaluate the full predicted distribution of la-
196
      bels, as shown in Figure 2.2. OTD considers only the labels with the highest probability, ignoring the
      complete distribution. This makes it dependent on model calibration and limits the ability to assess
197
      performance across a broader range of event types, such as long-tail predictions. However, models
198
      typically predict probabilities for all classes, allowing for a more comprehensive assessment. There-
199   fore, we aim to develop an evaluation metric that accurately captures performance across common
200   and rare classes.

203   4   T EMPORAL M AP: P REDICTION AS D ETECTION
204
205   In this section, we introduce a novel metric, Temporal mAP (T-mAP), which analyses all errors
206   within a predefined time horizon, explicitly controls ordering, and is invariant to linear calibration.
207   T-mAP is inspired by object detection methods from computer vision (Everingham et al., 2010), as
208   illustrated in Fig. 1. Object detection aims to localize objects within an image and identify their
209   types. In event sequences, we tackle a similar problem but consider the time dimension instead of
210   horizontal and vertical axes. Unlike object detection, where objects have spatial size, each event
211   in an MTTP is a point without a duration. Therefore, we replace the intersection-over-union (IoU)
212
      similarity between bounding boxes with the absolute time difference.
213   T-mAP incorporates concepts from OTD but addresses its limitations, as outlined in the previous
214   section. Firstly, T-mAP evaluates label probabilities instead of relying on final predictions. Sec-
215   ondly, T-mAP restricts the prediction horizon rather than the number of events. These adjustments
      result in significant differences in formulation and computation, detailed below.

      4.3            T- M AP H YPERPARAMETERS
271
272   T-mAP has two hyperparameters: the maximum allowed time delta δ and evaluation horizon H. We
273   set δ twice the cost of the OTD because when the model predicts an incorrect label, OTD removes
274   the prediction and adds the ground truth event with the total cost equal to 2C. The horizon H must
275   be larger than δ to evaluate timestamp quality adequately. Therefore, depending on the dataset, we
276   select H to be approximately 3-7 times larger than δ, ensuring the horizon captures an average of
277   6-15 events. The empirical study of T-mAP hyperparameters is presented in Figure 4. As shown,
278
      the chosen δ parameter is positioned to the right of the initial slope of the parameter-quality curve,
      ensuring an optimal balance between time granularity and task difficulty.

295   5              H OTPP B ENCHMARK
296
297   The HoTPP benchmark integrates data preprocessing, training, and evaluation in a single toolbox.
298   Unlike previous benchmarks, HoTPP introduces the novel T-mAP metric for long-horizon predic-
299   tion. HoTPP differs from prior MTPP benchmarks by including simple rule-based baselines and
300   next-k models, simultaneously predicting multiple future events. The HoTPP benchmark is de-
301   signed to focus on simplicity, extensibility, evaluation stability, reproducibility, and computational
302
      efficiency.
303   Simplicity and Extensibility. The benchmark code is organized into a clear structure, separating
304   the core library, dataset-specific scripts, and configuration files. New methods can be integrated
305   at various levels, including the configuration file, model architecture, loss function, metric, and
306   training module. Core components are reusable through the Hydra configuration library (Yadan,
307   2019). For example, HoTPP can apply the HYPRO rescoring method to backbone models such
308
      as IFTPP, RMTPP, NHP, and ODE. Another example is our Next-K implementation, which can be
      applied to IFTTP or RMTPP.
309
310   Evaluation Stability. In the MTPP domain, many datasets contain only a few thousand sequences
311   for training and evaluation. For example, the StackOverflow test set includes 401 sequences,
312   Retweet contains 1956, and Amazon has 1851 sequences for testing. Previous long-horizon evalua-
313   tion pipelines (Xue et al., 2022; 2023) made predictions only at the end of each sequence, resulting
314
      in a limited number of predictions and reduced evaluation stability. To address this limitation, we
      evaluate long-horizon predictions at multiple intermediate points.
315
316   Reproducibility. The HoTPP benchmark ensures reproducibility at multiple levels. First, we use
317   the PytorchLightning library (Falcon & The PyTorch Lightning team, 2019) for training with a spec-
318   ified random seed and report multi-seed evaluation results. Second, data preprocessing is carefully
319   designed to ensure datasets are constructed reproducibly. Finally, we specify the environment in a
320
      Dockerfile.
321   Computational Efficiency. Some methods are particularly slow, especially during autoregressive
322   inference on large datasets. Straightforward evaluation can take several hours on a single Nvidia
323   V100 GPU. We optimized the training and inference pipelines in two ways to accelerate computa-
      tion. First, we implemented an efficient RNN that reuses computations during parallel autoregressive

      6     E XPERIMENTS
379
380
      We conduct experiments on five datasets of varying sizes and origins: the Transactions dataset (AI-
381   Academy, 2021), the MIMIC-IV medical dataset (Johnson et al., 2023), two social networks
382   datasets, Retweet (Zhao et al., 2015) and StackOverflow (Jure, 2014), and the Amazon reviews
383   dataset (Jianmo, 2018). Dataset statistics and evaluation parameters are provided in Appendix C.4.
384
      We evaluate representative modeling methods from different groups:
385
386         1. Rule-based baselines. MostPopular generates a constant output with the most popular
387            label from the prefix and the average time step. The Last N baseline outputs N previous
388            events with adjusted timestamps.
389
            2. Intensity-free approaches. We implement the IFTPP method, which combines mean abso-
390            lute error (MAE) of the time step prediction with cross-entropy categorical loss for labels
391            (Shchur et al., 2019; Padhi et al., 2021; McDermott et al., 2024).
392
            3. Intensity-based approaches. We implement RMTPP (Du et al., 2016) as an example of
393
               the TPP approach with a traditional RNN. We add NHP (Mei & Eisner, 2017), based on a
394            continuous time LSTM architecture. We also evaluate the AttNHP approach that utilizes a
395            continuous time transformer model (Yang et al., 2022) and ODE (Rubanova et al., 2019).
396
            4. Next-K approaches. We adapt IFTPP and RMTPP to predict multiple future events directly,
397
               implementing IFTPP-K and RMTPP-K, respectively. These approaches originate in time
398            series analysis and have not been previously applied in the MTPP domain.
399
            5. Reranking. We implement HYPRO (Xue et al., 2022), which generates multiple hypothe-
400
               ses and selects the best sequence using a contrastive approach.
401
402   Additional details on these methods are provided in Appendix A, with training specifics outlined in
403   Appendix D. Hyperparameters for both the methods and metrics are presented in Appendix E. The
404   main evaluation results are shown in Table 1. In the following sections, we will discuss these results
405   from various perspectives and offer additional analysis of the methods’ behavior.
406
407   6.1   M ETRICS C OMPARISON
408
409   The evaluation results in Table 1 show that the OTD metric can yield low values even for simple
410   rule-based baselines. For instance, the Last baseline achieves the lowest OTD values on the Retweet
411
      and Amazon datasets. In contrast, the T-mAP metric more clearly distinguishes between rule-based
      baselines and deep learning models. This difference arises from the ability to measure model confi-
412
      dence. OTD evaluates predicted labels alone, while T-mAP measures average precision by adjusting
413
      logits thresholds. Unlike rule-based baselines, deep models provide confidence scores, whereas
414   baselines output only labels, resulting in low T-mAP scores for simple baselines.
415
416   The HYPRO and NHP methods maximize T-mAP, while statistical baselines, IFTPP, and RMTPP
417
      achieve the lowest OTD values. The difference between the two metrics lies in balancing the im-
      portance of event order versus time prediction quality. OTD evaluates the first 5-10 events, and
418
      to minimize OTD, a model must correctly identify the events that constitute the beginning of the
419
      sequence. IFTPP and RMTPP jointly predict the next event’s time and label, leading to better or-
420   dering. In contrast, methods like NHP, AttNHP, and ODE predict time steps independently for each
421   event type. When the differences between timestamps are small, these methods can result in random
422   ordering, negatively affecting the OTD score. T-mAP, on the other hand, is less sensitive to event
423   order as long as the time predictions are accurate.
424
      The HYPRO method, designed to differentiate between ground truth and predicted sequences using
425
      a discriminator model, achieves high T-mAP scores. This highlights T-mAP’s ability to reward
426   models that generate realistic sequences. We also observed that methods with high T-mAP scores
427   often have low next-event prediction accuracy. For instance, on the Retweet, Amazon, and MIMIC-
428   IV datasets, high long-horizon performance is usually accompanied by low next-event prediction
429   scores. This is particularly true for the HYPRO method, whose loss function is heavily focused on
430   long-horizon quality. The contrast on the MIMIC-IV dataset can be attributed to the large number of
431   events with identical timestamps, making precise event ordering more difficult and impacting both
      next-event and OTD scores.

      6.2                     I NTENSITY-BASED VS I NTENSITY-F REE
433
434   Previous works mostly compared either intensity-based neural methods (Du et al., 2016; Xue et al.,
435   2022) or intensity-free approaches (Padhi et al., 2021; McDermott et al., 2024). The only exception
436   is IFTPP (Shchur et al., 2019), which did not compare with NHP and included only test set likelihood
437   as an evaluation metric. This raises the question: which approach is superior? We compare the
438   intensity-free IFTPP method with intensity-based RMTPP, NHP, and ODE approaches. Table 1
439   shows that the intensity-free IFTPP method excels in the next-event MAE prediction, as it optimizes
440   this metric. In other scenarios, the results vary between datasets. Therefore, we conclude that there
441   is no preferred solution, and both approaches warrant attention in future research.
442
443
      6.3                     AUTOREGRESSION VS D IRECT H ORIZON P REDICTION
444
445   We compared the IFTPP and RMTPP methods with their Next-K counterparts. Our results indicate
446   that autoregressive models generally perform better regarding next-event prediction quality, while
447   Next-K approaches improve T-mAP scores on all datasets except Transactions. The OTD-based
448   rankings vary across datasets, with no single approach consistently outperforming the others. De-
449   spite these findings, little effort has been made to adapt Next-K models to MTPPs, suggesting future
450   research in this area.
451   We also observed that the Next-K approaches exhibit some interesting properties compared to au-
452   toregression. For instance, the entropy of label distributions in autoregression models decreases
453   with increasing generation steps, as shown in Figure 5. The likely reason for this behavior is the
454   dependency of the model’s future output on its past errors. Conversely, Next-K models demonstrate
455   increasing entropy, which is expected as future events become harder to predict. This suggests that
456   Next-K models have the potential to predict better the distributions of future labels, a factor that
457   should be considered in future research. Appendix G provides a qualitative analysis of predictions
458   diversity.

474   6.4                     T HE M AXIMUM S EQUENCE L ENGTH
475
476   We observed that long-horizon prediction quality largely depends on the maximum number of al-
477   lowed predictions in autoregression and Next-K models. As shown in Fig. 6, the optimal number of
478   predicted events is usually less than the maximum number of events on the horizon. For example,
479   in the Transactions dataset, the optimal length ranges from 3 to 5, depending on the method. This
480   indicates that the quality of confidence estimation degrades for events further in the future, therefore
481   it is beneficial to limit the number of predictions manually.
482   Therefore, we conclude that greater attention should be given to probability calibration between
483   generation steps. This finding also highlights progress in long-horizon prediction tasks, particularly
484   in determining the maximum horizon that can be accurately modeled. T-mAP is the only metric ca-
485   pable of evaluating the optimal predicted sequence length, while the OTD metric remains unaffected
      by sequence length as it compares fixed-size prefixes.

      7              L IMITATIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
502
503   The proposed benchmark has several shortcomings. The training process for some methods is rela-
504   tively slow. For example, a continuous time LSTM from the NHP method lacks an effective open-
505
      source GPU implementation, which could be developed in the future. Autoregression can also be
      optimized using specialized GPU implementations, which would significantly impact HYPRO train-
506
      ing. Additionally, the list of implemented backbone architectures and losses can be extended, for
507
      example, by incorporating hybrid (Deshpande et al., 2021) and diffusion models (Zhou et al., 2023).
508
509   Our benchmark encourages future research to develop improved techniques for predicting future
510   events and establish simple baselines for better measurement of progress in the field. For example,
511
      Next-K models, which predict multiple future events without autoregression, show promising results
      in long-horizon prediction tasks. We suggest further exploration of these models. We also highlight
512
      the importance of label distribution estimation and emphasize the need to improve confidence esti-
513
      mation and calibration.
514
515   The T-mAP metric can be applied in domains beyond event sequences, like action recognition (Su
516   & Hua, 2017). T-mAP can potentially offer better timestamp evaluation in these domains than
517
      evaluating indices with OPW. T-mAP can also provide a more natural hyperparameter selection
      regarding the modeling horizon and maximum allowed time error. Our theoretical justification of
518
      T-mAP can potentially be adapted to the mAP estimation algorithms in computer vision.
519

521   8              C ONCLUSION
522
523   In this paper, we propose the HoTPP benchmark to assess the quality of long-horizon events fore-
524   casting. Our extensive evaluations using established datasets and various predictive models reveal
525   a critical insight: high accuracy in next-event prediction does not necessarily correlate with su-
526   perior performance in horizon prediction. This finding underscores the need for benchmarks like
527   HoTPP, emphasizing the importance of long-horizon accuracy and robustness. By shifting the fo-
528   cus from short-term to long-term predictive capabilities, HoTPP aims to drive the development of
529   more sophisticated and reliable event sequence prediction models. This, in turn, has the potential
530   to significantly enhance the practical applications of sequential event prediction in various domains,
531
      fostering innovation and improving decision-making processes across a wide range of industries.

645   modeled. The following equation gives the relationship between the PDF and the intensity function:

      The derivation is provided in (Rizoiu et al., 2017). Different TPPs are characterized by their inten-
649   sity function λ(ti ). In Poisson and non-homogeneous Poisson processes, the intensity function is
650   independent of previous events, meaning event occurrences depend solely on external factors. In
651   contrast, self-exciting processes are characterized by previous events increasing the intensity of fu-
652   ture events. A notable example of a self-exciting process is the Hawkes process, in which each event
653   linearly affects the future intensity:

662   where λ0 (ti ) ≥ 0 is the base intensity function, independent of previous events, and ϕ(x) ≥ 0 is the
663
      so-called memory kernel function. Given the intensity function λ(ti ), predictions are typically made
      by sampling or expectation estimation. Sampling is usually performed using the thinning algorithm,
664
      a specific rejection sampling approach. For details on the implementation of sampling, please refer
665
      to (Rizoiu et al., 2017).
666
667   Recent research has focused on modeling complex intensity functions. Various neural network ar-
668   chitectures have been adapted to address this problem. These approaches differ in the type of neural
669
      network used and the model of the intensity function. Neural architectures range from simple RNNs
      and Transformers to specially designed continuous-time models like NHP (Mei & Eisner, 2017)
670
      and Neural ODEs (Rubanova et al., 2019). The intensity function between events can be modeled
671
      as a sum of intensities from previous events, as in Hawkes processes, or by directly predicting the
672   inter-event intensity given the context embedding.
673
674   Intensity-free modeling. Some methods evaluate the next event time distribution without using
675
      intensity functions. For example, intensity-free (Shchur et al., 2019) represents the distribution as a
      mixture of Gaussians or through normalizing flows. Instead of predicting the distribution directly,
676
      other approaches solve a regression problem using MAE or RMSE loss. However, it can be shown
677
      that both MAE and RMSE losses are closely related to distribution prediction. Specifically, RMSE
678   is analogous to log-likelihood optimization with a Normal distribution, and MAE is similar to the
679   log-likelihood of a Laplace distribution (Bishop, 1994). In our experiments, we evaluated a model
680   trained with MAE loss as an example of an intensity-free method.
681
      Rescoring with HYPRO. HYPRO (Xue et al., 2022) is an extension applicable to any sequence
682
      prediction method capable of sampling. HYPRO takes a pretrained generative model and trains an
683
      additional scoring module to select the best sequence from a sample. It is trained with a contrastive
684   loss to distinguish between the generated sequence and the ground truth. HYPRO generates multiple
685   sequences with a background model during inference and selects the best one by maximizing the
686   estimated score. Although HYPRO is intended to improve quality compared to simple sampling, it is
687   unclear whether HYPRO outperforms expectation-based prediction. In our work, we apply HYPRO
688   to the outputs of the RMTPP model.
689
      Next-K models. While not commonly used in the MTPP field, Next-K approaches are popular
690   in time series modeling (Lim & Zohren, 2021). These methods predict multiple future events at
691   once, avoiding the need for autoregressive inference. The advantages of Next-K approaches include
692   fast inference and stability, as predictions do not depend on potential errors from previous steps,
693   unlike autoregression. The main limitation is a fixed prediction horizon, as the model cannot predict
694   sequences of arbitrary length. However, applying a modified autoregressive approach can potentially
695   address this limitation.
696   In our work, we implement a Next-K variant of the IFTPP model, which is straightforward. Given
697   K predictions and K ground truth events, we compute MAE and cross-entropy losses between cor-
698   responding pairs of events from both sequences. The Next-K variant of the RMTPP approach is
699   more complex, as it violates Hawkes assumptions: the i-th prediction doesn’t depend on predictions
700   1 . . . i − 1. Consequently, RMTPP-K, unlike RMTPP, cannot model dependencies between the pre-
701   dicted K events. Despite this, RMTPP-K still performs strongly, particularly on the StackOverflow
      dataset.

865
866   MTPP models are typically evaluated based on the accuracy of next-event predictions. Common
867   metrics include mean absolute error (MAE) or root mean square error (RMSE) for time shifts and

      Notes on MIMIC-IV preprocessing
919
920   MIMIC-IV is a publicly available electronic health record database that includes patient diagnoses,
921   lab measurements, procedures, and treatments. We leverage the data preprocessing pipeline from
922
      EventStreamGPT to construct intermediate representations in EFGPT format. This process yields
      three key entities: a subjects data frame containing time-independent patient records, an events data
923
      frame listing event types occurring to subjects at specific timestamps, and a measurements data
924
      frame with time-dependent measurement values linked to the events data frame.
925
926   We combine events and measurements to create sequences of events for each subject. The classi-
927   fication labels are generally divided into two categories: diagnoses, represented by ICD codes, and
928
      event types, such as admissions, procedures, and measurements. A key challenge is that relying
      solely on diagnoses results in sequences that are too short, while using only event types leads to
929
      highly repetitive sequences dominated by periodic events, such as treatment start and finish.
930
931   However, using both diagnoses and event types together introduces another issue: diagnoses are
932   sparsely distributed within a constant stream of repeated procedures, leading to imbalanced class
933   distributions and poor performance. To mitigate this, we filter the data by removing duplicate events
934
      between diagnoses, allowing us to retain useful treatment data while preserving class balance.
935   The final labels are created by converting ICD-9 and ICD-10 codes to ICD-10 chapters using Gen-
936   eral Equivalence Mapping (GEM) and adding event types as additional classes. This conversion is
937   necessary because the number of ICD codes is too large to use them directly as classes. Addition-
938   ally, we address the issue of multiple diagnoses occurring in a single event by sorting timestamps
939   for reproducibility.

       This section analyzes a toy dataset containing highly irregular event sequences. This dataset includes
1083
       a single label, which aims to predict timestamps. Most time intervals in the dataset are zero, with
1084   only 5% of intervals equal to one. We compare three simple baselines:
1085
1086                         • ZeroStep, which predicts events with timestamps identical to the last observed event (zero
1087                           intervals);
1088
                             • UnitStep, which predicts events with a unit time step (the largest time step in the dataset);
1089
1090                         • MeanStep predicts events using the average time step computed from historical data.
1091
1092
       Evaluation results are shown in Figure 9. The results indicate that the MAE and OTD metrics assign
       the lowest error to the ZeroStep baseline, which simply repeats the last event without accounting for
1093
       the dataset’s irregularity. In contrast, T-mAP identifies the MeanStep baseline as the most effective,
1094
       as it is the only method that analyzes historical data and incorporates timestamp statistics (mean
1095   interval) into its predictions.
1096
1097   These findings suggest that T-mAP is a more appropriate metric for assessing the ability of methods
1098
       to predict irregular events.
