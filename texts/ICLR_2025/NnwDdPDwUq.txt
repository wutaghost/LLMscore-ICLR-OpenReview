009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               In recent years, preference-based human feedback mechanisms have become in-
012
               tegral to improving model performance across a range of applications, including
013            conversational AI systems like ChatGPT. However, existing methodologies often
014            overlook critical factors such as model uncertainty and variability in feedback qual-
015            ity. To address these limitations, we propose an innovative entropy-based human
016            feedback framework designed for contextual bandits, which balances exploration
017            and exploitation by soliciting expert feedback when model entropy surpasses a pre-
018            defined threshold. Our method is model-agnostic and adaptable to any contextual
019            bandit agent employing stochastic policies. Through rigorous experimentation, we
020            demonstrate that our approach requires minimal human feedback to achieve signifi-
021
               cant performance gains, even with suboptimal feedback quality. Our work not only
               introduces a novel feedback solicitation strategy but also underscores the robustness
022
               of integrating human guidance into machine learning systems. Our code is publicly
023
               available: https://anonymous.4open.science/r/CBHF-33C5

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Contextual bandits (CB) have emerged as a powerful framework across various applications, in-
030   cluding recommendation systems (Li et al., 2010; Xu et al., 2020), healthcare (Yu et al., 2024),
031   and finance (Zhu et al., 2021), among others (Bouneffouf et al., 2020). CBs enable personalized
032   decision-making by learning from the contextual information in each instance. However, current
033   systems often rely heavily on implicit feedback signals, such as clicks, which are inherently biased
034   and incomplete, limiting their ability to fully capture true user preferences (Qi et al., 2018).
035
      To address these challenges, we explore the incorporation of explicit human feedback in a CB setting.
036   Human feedback has shown promise in reinforcement learning by integrating human guidance into
037   the learning process (Christiano et al., 2017; MacGlashan et al., 2017). Incorporating human feedback
038   enables models to generate more accurate and informative responses, improving performance in
039   applications such as conversational AI like ChatGPT (Ouyang et al., 2022; Achiam et al., 2023), and
040   robotics (Osa et al., 2018).
041
      Human feedback can generally be categorized into action-based feedback from human experts (Osa
042   et al., 2018; Li et al., 2023), and preference-based feedback (Christiano et al., 2017; Saha et al., 2023).
043   This work focuses on the latter. Preference-based feedback, where humans indicate their preference
044   between two options selected by the learner, has gained popularity due to its simplicity. However,
045   existing methods fail to address two critical issues: the varying quality of human feedback and the
046   uncertainty in the model’s decisions. These factors often result in inefficient learning and suboptimal
047   performance, especially in high-stakes or complex environments. In this work, we aim to answer the
048   key question: Can we propose a simple yet effective strategy to incorporate preference-based
049   human feedback in contextual bandits?
050   A key challenge in CB problems is balancing exploration and exploitation, which becomes more
051   complex with the addition of human feedback. The algorithm must balance this input to avoid over-
052   reliance while ensuring efficient learning. To address this, we propose a simple criterion for feedback
053   solicitation and introduce two methods for incorporating human feedback into CB, evaluating their
      performance.

054
      We present two feedback settings. In the action recommendation (AR) method, a human expert
055   provides recommended actions for a given context. In the reward manipulation (RM) method, the
056   expert assigns a reward penalty when the learner selects an action not recommended by the expert.
057   Feedback solicitation is based on model uncertainty, quantified by policy entropy, and human feedback
058   is requested when model entropy exceeds a certain threshold.
059
      These additions underscore the key finding of our study: even low-quality human feedback, when
060
      appropriately solicited, can lead to significant performance improvements.
061
062   Our contributions are threefold. First, we propose a framework to integrate human feedback into
063   CB across different environments and analyze the relative performance of two feedback strategies:
064
      action recommendation and reward penalty. Second, we identify limitations in current approaches
      and introduce an entropy-based criterion to enhance learning. This criterion not only improves
065
      performance but also deepens our understanding of how these methods support learning. Finally, we
066
      evaluate the impact of expert feedback quality on CB learner performance, showing how varying
067   levels of human recommendation accuracy affect cumulative rewards. Our findings advocate for
068   the inclusion of our methods in decision-making models and expand the understanding of human
069   feedback integration in reinforcement learning.

072   2   R ELATED WORKS
073
074   Contextual bandits Contextual bandits have diverse applications in recommendation systems (Li
075   et al., 2010; Xu et al., 2020), healthcare (Yu et al., 2024), finance (Zhu et al., 2021), and other
076   fields (Bouneffouf et al., 2020). CBs are a variant of the multi-armed bandit problem where each
077   round is influenced by a specific context, and rewards vary accordingly. This adaptability makes CBs
078   valuable for enhancing various machine learning methods, including supervised learning (Sui & Yu,
079   2020), unsupervised learning (Sublime & Lefebvre, 2018), active learning (Bouneffouf et al., 2014),
080
      and reinforcement learning (Intayoad et al., 2020).
081   To tackle CB challenges, several algorithms have been developed, such as LINUCB (Li et al., 2010),
082   Neural Bandit (Allesiardo et al., 2014), and Thompson sampling (Agrawal & Goyal, 2013). These
083   typically assume a linear dependency between the expected reward and its context. Despite these
084   advancements, CBs often rely on implicit feedback, like user clicks, leading to biased and incomplete
085   evaluations of user preferences (Qi et al., 2018). This reliance complicates accurately gauging user
086
      responses and tailoring the learning process.
087   Human feedback in the loop Recent advancements in human-in-the-loop methodologies have shown
088   significant successes in real-life applications, such as ChatGPT via reinforcement learning with
089   human feedback (RLHF) (MacGlashan et al., 2017), as well as in robotics (Argall et al., 2009) and
090   health informatics (Holzinger, 2016).
091   Preference-based feedback can be categorized into three groups: i) action-based prefer-
092   ences (Fürnkranz et al., 2012), where experts rank actions, ii) state preferences (Wirth & Fürnkranz,
093   2014), and iii) trajectory preferences Busa-Fekete et al. (2014); Novoseller et al. (2020). Action-
094   based feedback from humans is explored in (Mandel et al., 2017), where experts add actions to a
095   reinforcement learning agent to boost performance. Other forms of explicit human feedback include
096   reward shaping (Xiao et al., 2020; Bıyık et al., 2022; Ibarz et al., 2018; Arakawa et al., 2018). These
097   approaches however do not account for acquiring feedback based on the learner’s uncertainty or the
098
      impact of varying levels of feedback on performance.
099   Contextual bandits with human feedback Human-in-the-Loop Reinforcement Learning addresses
100   the bias problem of implicit feedback in contextual bandits. The exploration of learning in multi-
101   armed bandits with human feedback is discussed in (Tang & Ho, 2019), where a human expert
102   provides biased reports based on observed rewards. The learner’s goal is to select arms sequentially
103   using this biased feedback to maximize rewards, without direct access to the actual rewards.
104   Preference-based feedback in contextual and dueling bandit frameworks has been explored in previous
105   studies (Sekhari et al., 2023; Dudík et al., 2015; Saha, 2021; Wu et al., 2023). The learner presents
106   candidate actions and receives noisy preferences from a human expert, focusing on minimizing regret
107   and active queries. In contrast, we consider a setup where the learner receives direct feedback from
      human experts and show how the fraction of active queries varies with different sets of experts.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      Active learning in contexual bandits Active learning (Judah et al., 2014) enhances performance by
109   selectively querying the most informative data points for labeling, rather than passively receiving
110   labels for randomly or sequentially presented data. In the context of bandit algorithms, active learning
111   has been employed to optimize the exploration-exploitation trade-off by guiding the algorithm to
112   request feedback or labels when it is most uncertain about an action’s outcome (Taylor & Stone,
113   2009). For example, Bouneffouf et al. (2014) integrated active learning with Thompson sampling and
114   UCB algorithms in contextual bandits, resulting in improved sample efficiency.
115   In our work, we build on this idea by combining active learning techniques with human feedback,
116   utilizing an entropy-based mechanism to query feedback when necessary. By incorporating active
117   learning principles into our contextual bandit framework, we aim to more effectively balance ex-
118   ploration and exploitation, particularly in scenarios where human feedback is noisy or costly. This
119   approach not only improves sample efficiency but also helps mitigate the challenges posed by varying
120   feedback quality.
121   Other related areas Our work builds on several important research areas, including counterfactual
122   reasoning, imitation learning, preference optimization, and entropy-based active learning. We draw
123   inspiration from Tang and Wiens Tang & Wiens (2023), whose counterfactual-augmented importance
124   sampling informs our feedback framework, and extend DAGGER Ross et al. (2011) by dynamically
125   incorporating expert feedback instead of using fixed imitation. We also acknowledge parallels
126   with Active Preference Optimization (APO) Das et al. (2024), adapting trajectory-level preference
127   feedback to reward manipulation in more complex settings. Additionally, we connect with entropy-
128   driven methods like BALD Houlsby et al. (2011) and IDS Russo & Van Roy (2014), adapting their
129
      principles for contextual bandit problems to balance information gain and decision-making efficiency
      in sequential exploration. These connections highlight how our approach advances real-time feedback
130
      integration and decision optimization.

249   3.3   W HEN TO SEEK HUMAN FEEDBACK ?
250
251
      An important question that naturally arises when integrating human feedback into the contextual
      bandit algorithm is when the algorithm will actively seek out such feedback. In the contextual
252
      duelling bandit setup in (Di et al., 2024), the algorithm presents two options to the human and asks
253
      them to choose a preferred one based on a given context. In the case of model misspecification, where
254   the underlying reward function assumed by the algorithm does match the true rewards generated by
255   human preferences, the algorithm can actively query the human expert to obtain feedback on the
256   predicted reward or rankings (Yang et al., 2023). In our work, we take a different approach where
257   the learner seeks for expert feedback based on model uncertainty. The model computes the entropy
258   of the policy at each round t which quantifies the degree of unpredictability in the policy’s decision
259   making process using the following expression
260
261                                            X
262
                                   H(π) = −          π(at | st ) log(π(at | st )),                       (7)
                                                at
263
264   where H(π) denotes the entropy of policy π. The model then queries for human feedback when the
265   model entropy exceeds a predefined threshold λ. Appropriate choice of λ will depend on the problem
266   domain and are obtained using hyper parameter search. Our proposed entropy based approach for
267   querying the expert depends on the learner’s ability to compute an entropy for its policy. Thus for
268   certain models when model uncertainty is not available, we can still obtain two forms of human
269   feedback periodically, we also demonstrate the effect on model performance when these two types of
      human feedback are incorporated for different periods.

      3.4   Q UALITY OF EXPERTS
271
272   We consider the effect of learner’s performance based on different quality of expert feedback received.
273   We define the quality of feedback in this case as the accuracy of the expert in providing correct
274   recommendation. We first show how the performance of the contextual bandit learner measured
275   by the expected cumulative reward varies for different expert levels of accuracy. Let qt ∈ [0, 1] be
276   the probability of providing correct recommendation associated with a particular level of expert.
277   During training, the algorithm seeks expert feedback described in Section 3.2.1 and 3.2.2 when
278
      H(π) ≥ λ. For action recommendation via direct supervision, the expert provides the correct action
      with probability qt and provides a randomized action with probability 1 − qt . For reward manipulation
279
      feedback, the expert wrongly penalizes the learner with a probability of 1 − qt .

286   In this sub-section, we present the environment settings, baselines, and experimental results. We also
287   discuss the effect of entropy thresholds and expert accuracy on model performance.
288
      Algorithms and Environments Considered. We conduct experiments across a range of environments
289
      and contextual bandit agents. The agents fall into two categories: (i) classic contextual bandit
290
      algorithms and (ii) policy-based reinforcement learning (RL) algorithms with a discount factor of 0,
291   focusing on immediate rewards.
292
293   Classic Contextual Bandit Algorithms. For the classic contextual bandit setup, we employ three
294
      key algorithms: 1. LinearUCB (Li et al., 2010): An extension of the traditional Upper Confidence
      Bound (UCB) algorithm (Auer, 2002), where the expected reward for each action depends linearly on
295
      the context or features associated with that action. 2. Bootstrapped Thompson Sampling (Kaptein
296
      & Eckles, 2014): This method replaces the posterior distribution in standard Thompson Sampling
297   with a bootstrapped distribution, enhancing robustness by resampling historical data instead of
298   relying on a parametric model. 3. EE-NET (Ban et al., 2021): This approach utilizes two neural
299   networks—one for exploration and one for exploitation—to learn a reward function and adaptively
300   balance exploration with exploitation.
301
      Policy-Based Reinforcement Learning Algorithms. For policy-based RL, we evaluate four algo-
302
      rithms, with the discount factor set to 0 to prioritize immediate rewards: Proximal Policy Opti-
303   mization (PPO) (Schulman et al., 2017), PPO with Long Short-Term Memory (PPO-LSTM),
304   REINFORCE (Williams, 1992), Actor-Critic (Haarnoja et al., 2018).
305
306
      Baseline Comparison. We include the TAMER framework (Knox & Stone, 2009) as a baseline,
      which allows human trainers to provide real-time feedback to the agent, supplementing the predefined
307
      environmental reward signal. In our experiments, we simulate human feedback by revealing the true
308
      labels during training.
309
310   Expert Feedback Comparison. For all contextual bandit agents, we compare two types of expert
311   feedback as described in sections 3.2.1 and 3.2.2. Expert feedback is solicited only during the training
312
      phase, and each learner is evaluated after five independent runs, with the mean cumulative reward
      reported.
313

408   4.3                                I NCORPORATING ENTROPY BASED FEEDBACK ACHIEVES HIGHER PERFORMANCE
409                                      COMPARED TO BASELINES
410
411   We optimize the model performance across various expert levels and compare these results with
412   baseline models, including TAMER and EE-Net. Figure 3 presents the mean cumulative reward for
413   the optimized expert level (as obtained from Table 1), highlighting the significant performance gains
414   achieved by incorporating entropy-based feedback over the baselines.
415   Our analysis, conducted across all datasets, demonstrates that integrating entropy-based feed-
416   back—specifically Action Recommendation (AR) and Reward Modification (RM)—consistently
417   outperforms both TAMER and EE-Net. Moreover, we observe that the proportion of steps during
418   which the algorithm seeks human expert feedback varies across datasets. Importantly, the results
419   reveal two key findings:
420   Firstly, learners benefit substantially from entropy-based feedback compared to when no such
421   feedback is provided. This improvement underscores the effectiveness of entropy thresholds in
422   selectively involving human experts, thereby guiding the learning process. In fact, even with a modest
423   number of queries to the human expert (less than 30% of the total training steps), entropy-based
424   feedback drives superior performance over the baseline models. Secondly, the final performance of
425   the learners is not strictly dependent on the quality of the human feedback, as shown in Figure 2.
426   Interestingly, the performance of AR and RM varies between datasets. For example, on the Bibtex
427   dataset, AR performs worse compared to RM, while on the Delicious dataset, AR demonstrates the
428   best performance among the three. This difference arises due to how penalties affect exploration:
429   Bibtex, with fewer actions, benefits less from AR’s action-space limitation, whereas Delicious, with
430   many possible actions, sees AR accelerating convergence by narrowing down the action space early
431   in the learning process. As a result, AR’s advantage becomes more apparent in environments where
      an overwhelming number of actions could otherwise slow down the learner’s progress.

437   4.4                       E FFECT OF ENTROPY THRESHOLD AND EXPERT ACCURACY ON MODEL PERFORMANCE
438
439   Figure 4 presents bubble plots comparing model performance at different expert levels and entropy
440   threshold values for both AR and RM feedback types. The size and color of each bubble represent
441   the mean cumulative reward for the corresponding learner.
442   We begin by analyzing the results for AR feedback. Generally, we observe that at higher entropy
443   threshold values, the model’s performance remains relatively stable across different expert levels.
444   This behavior is expected, as higher entropy thresholds result in fewer queries to the human expert,
445   reducing the impact of expert quality on performance.
446
      However, at lower entropy thresholds, an interesting pattern emerges: increasing expert quality
447
      can actually lead to a decrease in model performance. This phenomenon relates to the exploration-
448   exploitation trade-off. At high expert levels, the expert consistently provides accurate recommenda-
449   tions, and since the model is designed to always accept these recommendations in the AR setting,
450   the result is pure exploitation. Conversely, at lower expert levels, where recommendations are more
451   random, the model is encouraged to explore a broader set of actions, which can ultimately yield
452   higher cumulative rewards.
453
      A similar pattern is observed with RM feedback. At higher entropy thresholds, the differences in
454   performance between varying expert levels are minimal, as fewer queries are made to the expert. At
455   lower entropy thresholds, however, we again see a decline in performance as expert quality increases.
456
457

      be found in Appendix B.

      4.5   O BSERVED DIFFERENCES BETWEEN FEEDBACK TYPES
487
488   Figure 3 illustrates how the two forms of feedback, AR and RM, interact differently with the
489   underlying algorithms and datasets. The choice of feedback type should therefore depend on the
490   specific application.
491
      Our results generally indicate that at higher expert levels, AR tends to be more effective than RM. This
492   is likely because AR directly influences the actions taken by the contextual bandit (CB), interfering
493   less with its reward-based learning process. At low expert levels, however, AR can become disruptive,
494   leading to poor exploration by prematurely narrowing the action space. In contrast, at high expert
495   levels, AR provides clearer guidance for the bandit’s exploration, optimizing action selection while
496   leaving the reward structure relatively intact.
497
      Ultimately, this suggests that AR is particularly advantageous when expert quality is high, as it can
498   effectively guide exploration without destabilizing the learning process.

      5     C ONCLUSION
502
      In conclusion, this work introduces an effective entropy-based framework for incorporating human
503
      feedback into contextual bandits. By utilizing model entropy to trigger feedback solicitation, we
504
      significantly reduce the reliance on continuous human intervention, thus making the system more
505   efficient and scalable. Our experiments show that even with low-quality human feedback, substantial
506   performance gains can be achieved, underscoring the potential of entropy-based feedback mechanisms
507   in various real-world applications. This framework enhances learning efficiency and provides new
508   insights into the dynamics of human-machine collaboration in reinforcement learning environments.
509   Future work may focus on refining feedback solicitation strategies and exploring their applicability in
510   broader AI contexts, ensuring even more adaptive and responsive learning systems.

1177   In systems that integrate human feedback, the cost of feedback solicitation plays a crucial role in
1178   determining the efficiency and practicality of the algorithm. Below, we provide a structured analysis
1179   of these costs and their effects.
1180
1181   H.1   C OST C OMPONENTS IN F EEDBACK S OLICITATION
1182
1183   Feedback solicitation costs can be broken into three primary components:
1184
1185
              • Human Effort Cost (Ch ): Time, cognitive load, or financial compensation required for a
                human expert to provide feedback.
1186
1187          • System Overhead (Cs ): Computational and communication overhead associated with
                querying, collecting, and processing feedback.

1188
               • Opportunity Cost (Co ): Delay or missed opportunities to explore other actions during
1189             feedback solicitation.

1191   The total cost per solicitation can be expressed as:
1192
1193
                                              Ctotal = Ch + Cs + Co .

1195   H.2     T RADE - OFF B ETWEEN F EEDBACK AND P ERFORMANCE
1196
       Feedback improves learning by reducing uncertainty in decision-making but comes at a cost. The
1197
       trade-off is evident in two opposing factors:
1198
1199           • Benefits: Incorporating feedback accelerates convergence, reduces regret, and improves
1200             cumulative rewards.
1201
               • Costs: Frequent feedback queries increase the total cost, potentially diminishing the system’s
1202             overall utility.
1203
1204   The cumulative rewards RT after T rounds with feedback solicitation frequency p can be modeled as:
1205
                                                     T
1206                                                 X
                                              RT =         rt − p · Ctotal ,
1207
                                                     t=1

1210   solicited.

1212   H.3     E FFECT OF F EEDBACK Q UALITY AND F REQUENCY
1213
       H.3.1    H IGH -Q UALITY F EEDBACK (qt → 1)
1214
1215           • Impact: High-quality feedback significantly reduces regret, as the system quickly learns
1216             optimal actions.
1217           • Cost Justification: Even with higher solicitation costs, the performance gains justify
1218             frequent feedback, especially in complex environments.
1219
1220   H.3.2    L OW-Q UALITY F EEDBACK (qt → 0)
1221
               • Impact: Low-quality feedback adds noise to the learning process, diminishing performance
1222
                 gains.
1223
1224           • Cost Justification: Frequent solicitation becomes inefficient, and selective feedback solici-
1225             tation based on entropy thresholds (λ) is preferred.
1226
1227   H.3.3    F REQUENCY OF F EEDBACK (p)
1228           • High p improves learning but incurs higher total costs, leading to diminishing returns as
1229             cumulative rewards plateau.
1230           • Low p reduces costs but risks slower convergence and higher regret.

       H.4     E NTROPY-BASED F EEDBACK S OLICITATION
1233
1234   An entropy-based mechanism optimizes feedback solicitation by querying only when the model’s
1235   uncertainty surpasses a predefined threshold (λ):
1236
1237           • High Entropy (H(π) > λ): Feedback is requested to resolve uncertainty, ensuring maxi-
1238             mum utility from the cost incurred.
1239           • Low Entropy (H(π) ≤ λ): Feedback is avoided as the model is confident in its decision.

1241   This selective querying strategy reduces the total feedback cost while maintaining performance by
       focusing resources where they have the highest impact.
