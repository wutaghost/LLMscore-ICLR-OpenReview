009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011            Zero-shot imitation learning algorithms hold the promise of reproducing unseen
012            behavior from as little as a single demonstration at test time. Existing practical
013            approaches view the expert demonstration as a sequence of goals, enabling imitation
014            with a high-level goal selector, and a low-level goal-conditioned policy. However,
015            this framework can suffer from myopic behavior: the agent’s immediate actions
016            towards achieving individual goals may undermine long-term objectives. We
017            introduce a novel method that mitigates this issue by directly optimizing the
018
               occupancy matching objective that is intrinsic to imitation learning. We propose to
               lift a goal-conditioned value function to a distance between occupancies, which are
019
               in turn approximated via a learned world model. The resulting method can learn
020
               from offline, suboptimal data, and is capable of non-myopic, zero-shot imitation,
021            as we demonstrate in complex, continuous benchmarks.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
039
040
      The emergence of zero/few-shot capabilities in language modeling (Brown et al., 2020; Wei et al.,
041
      2022; Kojima et al., 2022) has renewed interest in generalist agents across all fields in machine
042   learning. Typically, such agents are pretrained with minimal human supervision. At inference, they
043   are capable of generalization across diverse tasks, without further training, i.e. zero-shot. Such
044   capabilities have also been a long-standing goal in learning-based control (Duan et al., 2017).
045   Promising results have been achieved by leveraging the scaling and generalization properties of
046   supervised learning (Jang et al., 2022; Reed et al., 2022; O’Neill et al., 2023; Ghosh et al., 2024; Kim
047   et al., 2024), which however rely on large amounts of expert data, usually involving costly human
048   participation, e.g. teleoperation. A potential solution to this issue can be found in reinforcement
049   learning approaches, which enable learning from suboptimal data sources (Sutton & Barto, 2018).
050
      Existing methods within this framework ease the burden of learning general policies by limiting the
      task class to additive rewards (Laskin et al., 2021; Sancaktar et al., 2022; Frans et al., 2024) or single
051
      goals (Bagatella & Martius, 2023).
052
053   This work lifts the restriction of previous approaches, and proposes a method that can reproduce rich
      behaviors from offline, suboptimal data sources. In particular, we allow arbitrary tasks to be specified

054
      through a single demonstration at inference time, conforming to a zero-shot Imitation Learning (IL)
055   framework. From a practical standpoint, this demonstration may be partial (i.e., lack action labels) and
056   rough (e.g., only contain a small set of abstract key states to be reached). For example, when tasking a
057   robot arm with moving an object along a path, it is sufficient to provide the object’s position for a few
058   “checkpoints” without specifying the exact pose that the arm has when each checkpoint is reached.
059
      In principle, a specified goal sequence can be decomposed into multiple single-goal tasks that can be
060
      accomplished by goal-conditioned policies, as proposed by recent zero-shot IL approaches (Pathak
061   et al., 2018; Hao et al., 2023). However, we show that this decomposition is prone to myopic behavior.
062   Continuing the robotic manipulation example from above, let us consider a task described by two
063   sequential goals, each specifying a certain position that the object should reach. In this case an
064   optimal goal-conditioned policy would attempt to reach the first goal as fast as possible, and possibly
065   throw the object towards it. The agent would then relinquish control of the object, leaving it in a
066   suboptimal—or even unrecoverable—state. In this case, the agent would be unable to move the object
067   towards the second goal. This myopic behavior is a fundamental issue arising from goal abstraction,
068   as we formally argue in Section 3, and results in catastrophic failures in hard-to-control environments,
069
      as we demonstrate empirically in Section 5.
070   In this work we instead provide an holistic solution to zero-shot offline imitation learning by adopting
071   an occupancy matching formulation. We name our method ZILOT (Zero-shot Offline Imitation
072   Learning from Optimal Transport). We utilize Optimal Transport (OT) to lift the state-goal distance
073   inherent to GC-RL to a distance between the expert’s and the policy’s occupancies, where the latter is
074   approximated by querying a learned world model. Furthermore, we operationalize this distance as an
075
      objective in a standard fixed horizon MPC setting. Minimizing this distance leads to non-myopic
      behavior in zero-shot imitation. We verify our claims empirically by comparing our planner to
076
      previous zero-shot IL approaches across multiple robotic simulation environments, down-stream
077
      tasks, and offline datasets. Our code is available on our anonymous website1 .

122   In the field of machine learning, it is often of interest to match distributions, i.e. find some probability
123   measure µ that resembles some other probability measure ν. In recent years there has been an
124   increased interest in Optimal Transportation (OT) (Amos et al., 2023; Haldar et al., 2022; Bunne
125   et al., 2023; Pooladian et al., 2024). As illustrated in figure 2, OT does not only compare probability
126   measures in a point-wise fashion, like f -Divergences such as the Kullbach-Leibler Divergence (DKL ),
127   but also incorporates the geometry of the underlying space. This also makes OT robust to empirical
128
      approximation (sampling) of probability measures (Peyré & Cuturi (2019), p.129).
129   Formally, OT describes the coupling γ ∈ P(X × Y) of two measures µ ∈ P(X ), ν ∈ P(Y) with
130   minimal transportation cost w.r.t. some cost function c : X × Y → R. The primal Kantorovich form
131   is given as the optimization problem

173   3       G OAL ABSTRACTION AND MYOPIC PLANNING
174
175   The distribution matching objective at the core of IL problems is in general hard to optimize. For
176   this reason, most4 practical methods for zero-shot IL leverage a hierarchical decomposition into a
177   sequence of GC-RL problems (Pathak et al., 2018; Hao et al., 2023). We will first describe this
178   approach, and then show how it potentially introduces myopic behavior and suboptimality.
179
      In the pretraining phase, Pathak et al. (2018) propose to train a goal-conditioned policy πg : S × G →
180   A on reaching single goals and a goal-recognizer C : S × G → {0, 1} that detects whether a given
181   state achieves the given goal. Given an expert demonstration g1:M and an initial state s0 , imitating
182   the expert can then be sequentially decomposed into M goal-reaching problems, and solved with
183   a hierarchical agent consisting of two policies. On the lower level, πg chooses actions to reach the
184   current goal; on the higher level, C decides whether the current goal is achieved and πg should target
185   the next goal in the sequence.
186   We define the pre-image ϕ−1 (g) = {s ∈ S : ϕ(s) = g} as the set of all states that map to
187   a goal, and formalize the suboptimality of the above method under goal abstraction as follows.

219   a method for zero-shot offline imitation learning that directly optimizes the occupancy matching

271   that minimizes the OT costs between the empirical expert goal occupancy, and the induced empirical
272   policy state occupancy. This type of optimization problem fits naturally into the framework of planning
273   with zero-order optimizers and learned world models (Chua et al., 2018; Ha & Schmidhuber, 2018);
274   while these algorithms are traditionally used for additive costs, the flexibility of zero-order optimizers

276   application to our problem. The objective in equation 11 can thus be directly optimized with CEM

      Implementation The method presented relies solely on three learned components: a dynamics
315
      model P̂ , and the state-goal and goal-goal GC value functions V and W . All of them can be learned
316
      offline from the dataset Dβ . In practice, we found that several existing deep reinforcement learning
317
      frameworks can be easily adapted to learn these functions. We adopt TD-MPC2 (Hansen et al.,
318   2024), a state of the art model-based algorithm that has shown promising results in single- and
319   multitask online and offline RL. We note that planning takes place in the latent space constructed
320   by TD-MPC2’s encoders. We adapt the method to allow estimation of goal-conditioned value
321   functions, as described in appendix C. We follow prior work (Andrychowicz et al., 2017; Bagatella
322   & Martius, 2023; Tian et al., 2021) and sample goals from the future part of trajectories in Dβ in
323   order to synthesize rewards without supervision. We note that this goal-sampling method also does
      not require any knowledge of h.

      5     E XPERIMENTS

      This section constitutes an extensive empirical evaluation of ZILOT for zero-shot IL. We first describe
327   our experimental settings in terms of environment, baselines and metrics, and then present qualitative
328   and quantitative result, as well as an ablation study. We consider a selection of 30 tasks defined over
329   5 environments, as summarized below and described in detail in appendix A and C.
330
      fetch (Plappert et al., 2018) is a manipulation suite in which a robot arm either pushes (Push), or
331
      lifts (Pick&Place) a cube towards a goal. We adopt these two environments directly. To illustrate the
332
      failure cases of myopic planning, we also evaluate a variation of Push (i.e. Slide), in which the table
333   size exceeds the arm’s range, the table’s friction is reduced, and the arm is constrained to be always
334   touching the table. As a result, the agent cannot fully constrain the cube, e.g. by picking it up, or
335   pressing on it, and the environment strongly punishes careless manipulation. In all three environments,
336   tasks consist of moving the cube along trajectories shaped like the letters “S”, “L”, and “U”.
337
      halfcheetah (Wawrzyński, 2009) is a classic Mujoco environment where the agent controls a
338

      6    R ELATED W ORK
487
488   Zero-shot IL When a substantial amount of compute is allowed at inference time, several methods
489   have been proposed to leverage pretrained models to infer actions, and retrieve an imitator policy via
490   behavior cloning (Pan et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2023; Torabi et al., 2018). As already discussed in
491   section 3, most (truly) zero-shot methods cast the problem of imitating an expert demonstration as fol-
492   lowing the sequence of its observations (Pathak et al., 2018; Hao et al., 2023). Expert demonstrations
493   are then imitated by going from one goal to the next using a goal-conditioned policy. In contrast, our
494   work proposes a holistic approach to imitation, which considers all goals within the planning horizon.
495
496   Zero-Shot RL Vast amounts of effort have been dedicated to learning generalist agents without
497
      supervision, both on the theoretical (Touati & Ollivier, 2021; Touati et al., 2023) and practical side
      (Laskin et al., 2021; Mendonca et al., 2021). Among others, (Sancaktar et al., 2022; P. et al., 2021;
498
      Bagatella & Martius, 2023) learn a dynamics model through curious exploration and show how it
499
      can be leveraged to optimize additive objectives. More recently, Frans et al. (2024) use Functional
500   Reward Encodings to encode arbitrary additive reward functions in a latent that is used to condition
501   a policy. While these approaches are effective in a standard RL setting, they are not suitable to
502   solve instances of global RL problems (Santi et al., 2024) (i.e., distribution matching). One notable
503   exception is the forward-backward framework (Touati & Ollivier, 2021; Pirotta et al., 2024), which
504   we discuss in detail in appendix B.
505
506   Imitation Learning A range of recent work has been focused on training agents that imitate experts
507   from their trajectories by matching state, state-action, or state-next-state occupancies depending on
508   what is available. These methods either directly optimize various distribution matching objectives
509   (Liu et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2022) or recover a reward using Generative Adversarial Networks (GAN)
510
      (Ho & Ermon, 2016; Li et al., 2023) or in one instance OT (Luo et al., 2023). Another line of work
      has shown impressive real-world results by matching the action distributions (Shafiullah et al., 2022;
511
      Florence et al., 2021; Chi et al., 2023) directly. All these approaches do not operate in a zero-shot
512
      fashion, or need ad-hoc data collection.
513
514   OT in RL Various previous work has used Optimal Transport in RL as a reward signal. One
515   application is online fine-tuning where a policy’s rollouts are rewarded in proportion to how closely
516   they match expert trajectories or the rollouts of experts (Dadashi et al., 2021; Haldar et al., 2022). Luo
517   et al. (2023) instead use a similar trajectory matching strategy to recover reward labels for unlabelled
518   mixed-quality offline datasets. Most of the works mentioned above do not have any special metric or
519   cost-function they use for their OT problems. The most common choices are Cosine Similarities and
520   Euclidean distances for their general applicability.
521
522   7    D ISCUSSION
523
524   In this work, we point out a failure-mode of current zero-shot IL methods that cast imitating an
525   expert demonstration as following a sequence of goals with myopic GC-RL policies. We address this
526   issue by framing the problem as occupancy matching. By introducing discretizations and minimal
527   approximations, we derive an Optimal Transportation problem that can be directly optimized at
528   inference time using a learned dynamics model, goal-conditioned value functions, and zero-order
529
      optimizer. Our experimental results across various environments and tasks show that our approach
      outperforms state-of-the-art zero-shot IL methods, particularly in scenarios where non-myopic
530
      planning is crucial. We additionally validate our design choices through a series of ablations.
531
532
      Limitations Our method is limited in practice by relying on a learned world model and to a lesser
533
      extent also by limited compute. The inaccuracy and computational cost of predictions from learned
534   dynamics models increases with the prediction horizon. This forces the optimization of a fixed-
535   horizon objective, which reintroduces a slight degree of myopia, as the agent may fail to consider
536   goals beyond the planning horizon. However, we found the degree of myopia to be acceptable in
537   our experimental settings, and expect our framework to become more and more applicable as the
538   accuracy of learned world models improves. The fact that ZILOT is non-markovian, even when
539   expert demonstrations are markovian can be viewed as a further limitation as it requires that all past
      states of the current episode are stored during execution.

1003   B      F ORWARD -BACKWARD R EPRESENTATIONS AND I MITATION L EARNING
1004
1005   In a foundational paper in zero-shot, model-free RL, Pirotta et al. (2024) propose several different
1006   methods based on the forward-backward (FB) framework (Touati & Ollivier, 2021). FB trains two
1007   functions F and B, which recover a low-rank approximation of the successor measure, as well as a
1008   parameterized policy (πz )z∈Rd . These functions can be trained offline, without supervision, so that
1009   for each reward r, an optimal policy πzr can be recovered. This property gives rise to a range of
1010   reward-based and occupancy-matching based methods for zero-shot IL. In the following we will go
1011   over each method, and discuss how it differs from ZILOT in terms of objective. We will highlight
1012
       how several methods do not directly apply to our setting, which involves actionless and rough expert
       demonstrations. We will evaluate those that are suitable for our setting. We refer the reader to
1013
       section C.10 for implementation details of the baselines based on FB.
1014
1015
1016
       B.1        FB I MITATION L EARNING A PPROACHES
1017   Behavioral Cloning The first approach in Pirotta et al. (2024) is is based on a gradient descent on
1018   the latent z to find πz that maximizes the likelihood of a given expert dataset. Since this approach
1019   requires expert actions it does not apply in our case.
1020
1021   Reward-Based Imitation Learning Pirotta et al. (2024) derive two reward-based zero-shot IL
1022   methods maximizing the reward r(·) = ρE (·)/ρDβ (·) (ERFB ) (Ma et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2022)and
1023   its regularized counterpart r(·) = ρE (·)/(ρE (·) + ρDβ (·)) (RERFB ) (Reddy et al., 2020; Zolna et al.,
1024   2020). While ZILOT’s objective is based on a Wasserstein distance, these rewards are derived from
1025   regularized f -divergence objectives. These objectives are fortunately tractable, and can be minimized
       by solving an RL problem with additive rewards. In practice, this corresponds to assigning a scalar

1056   trained to approximate successor measures over abstract states. While the standard implementation
1057   of distribution-matching FB-IL cannot imitate rough demonstrations, we believe that an extension in
1058   this direction may be interesting for future work.

1062
       one of our baselines (Pi+Cls). However, their framework assumes that trajectories to imitate are not
1063   partial and, instead of using a classifier, the goal can slide by one step at each time-step. In any case,
1064   their approach remains myopic as per Proposition 1. Empirically, Pirotta et al. (2024) observe that
1065   this instantiation of FB-IL does not outperform an equivalent method relying on TD3+HER instead.
1066   As the latter method is very similar to our Pi+Cls baseline, we do not investigate this approach further
1067   in this work.

       C.1     ZILOT
1108
1109   The proposed method is motivated and explained in section 4. We now present additional details.
1110
1111   Sinkhorn First, we rescale the matrix C by Tmax and clamp it to the range [0, 1] before running
1112   Sinkhorns algorithm. The precise operation performed is
1113
                                                        C ← min (1, max(0, C/Tmax )) .                                                                                (14)
1114
1115   This is done so that the same entropy regularization ϵ can be used across all environments, and to
1116   ensure there are no outliers that hinder the convergence of the Sinkhorn algorithm. For the algorithm
1117
       itself, we use a custom implementation for batched OT computation, heavily inspired by Flamary
       et al. (2021) and Cuturi et al. (2022). We run our Sinkhorn algorithm for r = 500 iterations with a
1118
       regularization factor of ϵ = 0.02.
1119
1120
       Truncation When the agent gets close to the end of the expert trajectory, then we might have that
1121   tK < k + H, i.e. the horizon is larger than needed. We thus truncate the planning horizon to the
1122   estimated remaining number of steps (and at least 1), i.e. we set
1123
                                                      Hactual ← max (1, min(tK − k, H)) .                                                                             (15)
1124
1125
       Unbalanced OT As mentioned in the main text in section 5.3, we can use unbalanced OT (Liero
1126   et al., 2018; Séjourné et al., 2019) to address that fact that the uniform marginal for the goal
1127   occupancy approximation may not be feasible. Unbalanced OT replaces this hard constraint of
1128   T ⊤ · 1N = 1M into the term ξb KL(T ⊤ · 1N , 1M ) in the objective function. For our experiments
1129   we have chosen ξb = 1.

1131   C.2     TD-MPC2 M ODIFICATIONS
1132
1133   As TD-MPC2 (Hansen et al., 2024) is already a multi-task algorithm that is conditioned on a learned
       task embedding t from a task id i, we only have to switch out this conditioning to a goal latent zg

1137   see section 2.3). For training we adopt all TD-MPC2 hyperparameters directly (see table 9). As
1138   mentioned in the main text, we also train a small MLP to predict W that regresses on V .

       C.3   RUNTIME
1159
       ZILOT runs at 0.5 to 3Hz on an Nvidia GTX 2080ti GPU, depending on the size of H and the size of
1160   the OT problem. Given that the MPC+Cls method runs at around 12 to 35Hz with the same networks
1161   and on the same hardware, it is clear that most computation is spent on preparing the cost-matrix
1162   C and running the Sinkhorn solver. Several further steps could be taken to speed-up the Sinkhorn
1163   algorithm itself, including η-schedules and/or Anderson acceleration (Cuturi et al., 2022) as well
1164   as warm-starting it with potentials, e.g. from previous (optimizer) steps or from a trained network
1165   (Amos et al., 2023).
