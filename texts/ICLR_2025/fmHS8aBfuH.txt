010                                              A BSTRACT
011
012            In recent years, conversational large language models (LLMs) have shown tremen-
013            dous success in tasks such as casual conversation, question answering, and per-
014            sonalized dialogue, making significant advancements in domains like virtual as-
015
               sistance, social interaction, and online customer engagement. However, they often
               generate responses that are not aligned with human values (e.g., ethical standards,
016
               safety, or social norms), leading to potentially unsafe or inappropriate outputs.
017
               While several techniques have been proposed to address this problem, they come
018            with a cost, requiring computationally expensive training or dramatically increas-
019            ing the inference time. In this paper, we present DIESEL, a lightweight inference
020            guidance technique that can be seamlessly integrated into any autoregressive LLM
021            to semantically filter undesired concepts from the response. DIESEL can function
022            either as a standalone safeguard or as an additional layer of defense, enhancing
023            response safety by reranking the LLM’s proposed tokens based on their similar-
024            ity to predefined negative concepts in the latent space. This approach provides
025            an efficient and effective solution for maintaining alignment with human values.
026
               Our evaluation demonstrates DIESEL’s effectiveness on state-of-the-art conversa-
               tional models (e.g., Llama 3), even in challenging jailbreaking scenarios that test
027
               the limits of response safety. We further show that DIESEL can be generalized
028
               to use cases other than safety, providing a versatile solution for general-purpose
029            response filtering with minimal computational overhead.

      Given the limitations of existing techniques, methods that efficiently operate at inference time are

081

082

083   additional model training. DIESEL addresses the challenge of generating safer responses by rerank-
084   ing the tokens proposed by the original LLM according to their similarity to predefined negative
085   concepts, steering the generation process away from undesirable outcomes. Importantly, DIESEL
086   aims to maintain the flow of conversation by providing nuanced, “soft” responses rather than out-
087   right denying discussion, as shown in Figure 1. DIESEL consists of three steps: candidate selection,
088   semantic latent space similarity, and token reranking. By using a lightweight off-the-shelf sentence
089   embedding model, DIESEL effectively guides the decoding process towards safer outputs based
090   on simple textual descriptions of negative concepts. Utilizing textual descriptions allows DIESEL
091
      to flexibly filter out any undesirable concepts without requiring specialized expertise or additional
      training in case new concepts need to be added or existing ones removed.

119   2     R ELATED W ORK
120
121   In this section, we review recent studies on conversational safety in LLMs, focusing on alignment,
122   filtering approaches, and inference guidance Dong et al. (2024). A key differentiator among these
123   approaches is their integration point within the model’s lifecycle: whether they are applied during
124   training (i.e., ad-hoc) or at inference time (i.e., post-hoc).
125
126   2.1   S AFETY A LIGNMENT
127
128
      Alignment algorithms are crucial for ensuring that LLMs adhere to desired objectives, such as hu-
      man values and safety. The alignment process typically begins with supervised fine-tuning (SFT) on
129
      high-quality prompt-response datasets Rajpurkar et al. (2016). Then, RLHF Ouyang et al. (2022)
130
      is employed, leveraging human feedback and preferences to further enhance the model’s alignment.
131   Given the complexity of balancing multiple alignment objectives, Multi-Objective RLHF Zhou et al.
132   (2023) has been proposed to manage trade-offs between safety and other goals (e.g., helpfulness).
133   An alternative approach, known as reinforcement learning with AI feedback (RLAIF) Bai et al.
134   (2022), uses surrogate LLMs to generate training data, reducing the need for human annotation.
135   While RLHF-based methods have been shown to be highly effective, they have several drawbacks:
136   (a) resource-intensive - they require extensive additional training time (ad-hoc) and, in most cases,
137   human annotation, although RLAIF reduces this need by using AI-generated data; (b) lack of robust-
138   ness - models that rely solely on RLHF or RLAIF have been found to be vulnerable to adversarial
139
      attacks during inference Carlini et al. (2024). As opposed to these ad-hoc techniques, our proposed
      method employs a post-hoc approach. It can serve either as an additional layer of defense to enhance
140
      the safety of RLHF-trained models or as the primary safety mechanism.
141
142
      2.2   I NPUT /O UTPUT F ILTERS
143
144   Filtering mechanisms, applied either to the input prompt or the generated output, are typically inte-
145   grated during the inference phase of the model lifecycle (i.e., post-hoc). These mechanisms aim to
146   detect and mitigate harmful content and can be broadly categorized into rule-based and model-based
147   filters. Rule-based filters are designed to capture specific characteristics of harmful content. For in-
148   stance, the PPL (perplexity) filter Alon & Kamfonas (2023) eliminates inputs with excessively high
149   complexity. Jain et al. (2023) proposed paraphrasing and retokenization techniques to modify the ex-
150   pression of statements, and SmoothLLM Robey et al. (2023) employs character-level perturbations
151
      to counteract perturbation-sensitive techniques. Model-based filters use learning-based approaches
      to identify harmful content. Modern methods include LLM-based filters, where an LLM classifies
152
      the harmfulness of the given text, such as Perspective Google (2024), Moderation OpenAI (2024),
153
      and Llama Guard Inan et al. (2023). While filtering mechanisms are widely used and popular among
154   various LLM providers, they primarily focus on detection either in the input or the output. In con-
155   trast, our approach is integrated directly into the generation phase, emphasizing the production of
156   safer responses from the outset, rather than merely suppressing those that fail to meet safety criteria.
157
158   2.3   I NFERENCE G UIDANCE
159
160   Inference guidance is a technique used to enhance the safety of LLMs during the generation phase
161   without modifying the model’s parameters. One prominent method involves utilizing the sys-
      tem prompt to influence the model’s behavior. By carefully designing a prompt that emphasizes

182   system encourages the generation of safer outputs. Another approach is token selection adjustment,

230   3.2     DIESEL - DYNAMIC I NFERENCE -G UIDANCE VIA E VASION OF S EMANTIC E MBEDDINGS
231           IN LLM S
232
      DIESEL is a lightweight technique aimed at guiding the decoding process (i.e., next-word predic-
233
      tion) away from pre-defined negative concepts without requiring additional model fine-tuning. To
234
      achieve this, DIESEL reranks the potential tokens proposed by the language model to better align
235   with the desired goal. DIESEL operates in three steps: (a) candidate selection, (b) latent space
236   semantic similarity, and (c) token reranking. The full procedure is shown in Algorithm 2. In the
237   remainder of this section, we describe each step in detail.
238
239   3.2.1    S TEP 1: C ANDIDATE S ELECTION
240
241   For token selection, we use the first two steps of the Nucleus (Top-p) sampling algorithm, primarily
242   due to its low computational cost and its ability to reduce repetitive generation while maintaining a
243
      high level of text coherence Wiher et al. (2022). During inference, in the n-th step, a token sequence
      x1:n−1 is fed into the language model fθ1 , producing probability distribution P (xn |x1:n−1 ) over the
244
      vocabulary V . The candidate token selection (outlined in Algorithm 1) involves the following steps:

255     the next token, denoted as Vb . Here, b is a tunable parameter of DIESEL that controls the number

476   4.2.5   U SER S TUDY
477
      To verify DIESEL’s ability to generate safer responses compared to vanilla auto-regressive infer-
478
      ence from a human perspective, we conducted a user study with 20 human evaluators, equally split
479
      between male and female participants. For each prompt, participants were given two responses and
480   asked to rank each prompt on a scale of 1 to 5. A score of 1 indicates that “response 1” is sub-
481   stantially safer than “response 2” (vice versa for a score of 5), a score of 2 indicates that “response
482   1” is slightly safer than “response 2” (vice versa for a score of 4), and a score of 3 indicates that
483   both responses are equally safe/unsafe. To reduce bias, the responses of DIESEL and vanilla auto-
484   regressive inference were randomly shuffled for each participant and each prompt. The averaged
485   results show that for 80% of DIESEL’s responses are safer than those of a vanilla auto-regressive
      inference, 10% are equally safe, and the remaining 10% are slightly unsafer.

      4.2.6   B EYOND S AFETY
487
488   To demonstrate the generalizability of DIESEL beyond safety-focused tasks, we conducted an ex-
489   periment in the domain of movies. For this, we used the Wiki Movie Plots dataset Priya (2024),
490   which provides detailed movie summaries across various genres. In this experiment, we tasked the
491   model with summarizing plots of horror films while treating the genre “horror” as a negative con-
492   cept. Specifically, we focus on horror movies, where the objective was to evaluate whether DIESEL
493   could effectively reduce the presence of horror-related content in the generated summaries. Then,
494
      using the LLM judge, we compare the generated summaries with the originals from the dataset,
      asking the model to assess which summary exhibited more horror elements. The results indicated
495
      that 82% of the summaries generated with DIESEL contained fewer horror elements than their orig-
496
      inal counterparts, underscoring DIESEL’s ability to suppress undesired content in domains beyond
497   safety-related contexts.

500   5   L IMITATIONS
501
502   One limitation of DIESEL is that as the response length increases (during generation), the sentence
503   embedding model faces challenges in accurately assessing the similarity between the generated con-
504   tent and predefined negative concepts. However, we observed that once the general ”direction” of
505   the response is established, subsequent tokens tend to follow the same trajectory, minimizing the
506   impact of this limitation on the overall safety of the generated output. Future research could explore
507   embedding models that are more specialized in handling long sequences, or dynamic strategies that
508   adapt to the response length and complexity of generated responses. Another limitation of DIESEL
509   relates to the irrevocable nature of token selection during each iteration. Once a token is selected at
510
      the end of an iteration, it cannot be undone. In some instances, a token chosen at iteration i may not
      be flagged as unsafe in isolation but, when combined with a token selected in a subsequent itera-
511
      tion, may result in an unsafe sentence. While this issue could potentially be mitigated by employing
512
      a look-ahead mechanism, this approach would come at the cost of increased runtime. Given the
513   trade-off between computational efficiency and safety, we opted to maintain a lightweight approach
514   suitable for real-world applications.

517   6   C ONCLUSION
518
519   In this paper, we introduced DIESEL, a novel lightweight inference guidance technique designed to
520   enhance the safety of responses generated by large language models. We demonstrated that DIESEL
521   effectively mitigates harmful outputs while maintaining coherence and relevance. Moreover, our
522   evaluations against a competitive state-of-the-art inference guidance technique highlighted DIESEL
523   ’s practical advantages, including significantly lower runtime overhead, making it suitable for real-
524   world applications. We also evaluated DIESEL’s robustness against jailbreak attacks, showing that
525   it offers an additional layer of defense even in adversarial contexts. Importantly, our method’s re-
526
      liance on simple textual descriptions of negative concepts allows it to be flexible, easily updated,
      and usable without specialized expertise. Finally, through both automated metrics and human eval-
527
      uations, we verified DIESEL’s ability to produce safer responses without compromising the quality
528
      or truthfulness of benign outputs.

531   7   E THICAL I MPACT
532
533
      This paper aims to enhance the safety of large language models (LLMs) by introducing a novel
534   lightweight inference guidance technique. As LLMs find broader application in real-world scenar-
535   ios, ensuring their safety becomes increasingly crucial. Importantly, the development of DIESEL
536   does not involve crafting new jailbreak attacks but instead makes use of those that are already pub-
537   licly available. For illustration, we include examples of harmful model responses. We acknowl-
538   edge that the introduction of DIESEL may inspire the creation of new attack strategies aimed at
539   circumventing its defenses. We will release the associated code and demonstrations to aid future
      red-teaming efforts in preventing LLM misuse.
