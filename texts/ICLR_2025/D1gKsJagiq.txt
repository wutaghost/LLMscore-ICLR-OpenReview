009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Anomaly segmentation aims to identify pixels of objects not present during the
012            model’s training. Recent approaches address this task using mask-based archi-
013            tectures, but these methods have high training costs due to the large transformer
014            backbones involved. While vision adapters can help reduce training costs, they
015            are not specialized for this task, leading to inferior performance. In this work,
016            we propose Dual-Stream Adapters (DSA), a vision adapter tailored for anomaly
017            segmentation. DSA extracts both in-distribution and out-of-distribution features
018            via (i) an anomaly prior module that produces separate initial embeddings for the
019
               two streams; and (ii) a dual-stream feature refinement that implicitly guides the
               separation of in-distribution from out-of-distribution features. We train DSA us-
020
               ing a novel hyperbolic loss function that provides supervised guidance for differ-
021
               entiating in-distribution and out-of-distribution features. Experiments on various
022            benchmarks show that dual-stream adapters achieve the best results while reduc-
023            ing training parameters by 38% w.r.t. the previous state-of-the-art.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Anomaly segmentation of driving scenes is a task that aims at segmenting, with pixel-level granular-
029
      ity, objects in road scenes not belonging to any category from the training distribution (Ackermann
030
      et al., 2023; Blum et al., 2021; Gal & Ghahramani, 2016; Grcić et al., 2022). This is crucial because,
031   given an object category that is unseen during training (e.g., a cow), a standard semantic segmenta-
032   tion model would erroneously classify it as one of its known classes (e.g., car, road), being unable
033   to flag a potential danger. On the other hand, an anomaly segmentation model should segment such
034   unseen objects, identifying them as anomalies. Although this task requires making predictions at
035   the pixel level (anomalous pixel / not anomalous pixel), few recent methods have demonstrated that
036   it is advantageous to resort to mask-architectures to directly identify anomalies as whole masks (Rai
037   et al., 2023a;b; Ackermann et al., 2023; Nayal et al., 2023). However, the improvements achieved by
038   these architectures largely depend on training large visual transformer backbones (Liu et al., 2021;
039
      Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) with a significant number of training parameters and, consequently, a sub-
      stantial training cost (cf. Fig. 1). Thus, we aim to develop an anomaly segmentation method that is
040
      more efficient at training time (with fewer trainable parameters).
041
042   To achieve this goal, we take inspiration from vision transformer adapters (Chen et al., 2023),
043   which have been shown to be effective for dense prediction tasks, while also being efficient in
044   terms of training parameters. However, these adapters are meant for general computer vision tasks
045
      and are not specialized for anomaly segmentation, a task that not only requires dense predictions,
      but it also needs to model the concepts of in-distribution and out-of-distribution. Therefore, in
046
      this work, we present the Dual-Stream Adapter, a novel adapter tailored for anomaly segmenta-
047
      tion. Its architecture consists of two key components: i) an anomaly prior module that initially
048   learns in-distribution and out-of-distribution features through distinct feature-level encodings; ii) a
049   dual-stream feature refinement module that refines these features and combines them with the pow-
050   erful representations of the frozen ViT backbone. This architecture is complemented by a novel hy-
051   perbolic loss function that guides the learning process, using void labels as supervision, to increase
052   uncertainty for OOD pixels, while reducing uncertainty for ID pixels. We perform extensive experi-
053   ments on standard anomaly segmentation benchmarks (Fishyscapes (Blum et al., 2021), Segment Me
      If You Can (Chan et al., 2021a), Road Anomaly Lis et al. (2019)), showing that Dual-Stream Adapter

086   Anomaly Segmentation is synonymous with the problem of pixel-level anomaly detec-
087
      tion. Hendrycks & Gimpel (2017) first delved into this problem by leveraging techniques origi-
      nally developed for image-wide anomaly detection. Their solution, based on the softmax prediction
088
      probability, is still considered a standard baseline for anomaly segmentation. Since then, other tech-
089
      niques have been used to segment anomalies, such as deep ensembles (Fort et al., 2019), Bayesian
090   deep learning (Mukhoti & Gal, 2018; Gal & Ghahramani, 2016; Tu et al., 2024), and class-logits rea-
091   soning (Jung et al., 2021; Hendrycks et al., 2021). Another strategy is to rely on image re-synthesis,
092   by looking at the discrepancy of the generated data w.r.t the in-distribution training data (Lis et al.,
093   2019).
094
      While all the methods discussed so far are completely unsupervised, recent methods improve re-
095
      sults via outlier exposure (Bevandić et al., 2018; Di Biase et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023), i.e.,
096   providing supervision to the model by training on mixed images that contain instances of novel
097   categories (negative crops) pasted on them. These negative crops are generally sampled from the
098   MS-COCO (Lin et al., 2014) or ADE-20K (Zhou et al., 2017) datasets, which contain objects absent
099   in in-distribution datasets. Alternatively, when appropriate negative data may be unavailable, syn-
100   thetic negatives may be used effectively, as demonstrated by Grcić et al. (2021) which introduced a
101   normalizing flow-based method. Afterward, Gudovskiy et al. (2023) improved the prior approach
102   with an energy-based normalizing flow method. Concurrently, DaCUP Vojı́r & Matas (2023) pro-
103   posed novel embedding bottleneck and image-conditioned distance features to improve anomaly
104
      segmentation.
105   Current state-of-the-art for anomaly segmentation (Nayal et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023; Grcić et al.,
106   2023; Ackermann et al., 2023; Rai et al., 2023b;a) leverage mask-based architectures (Cheng et al.,
107   2021; 2022) that perform pixel-wise classification as a direct set prediction, outputting a set of binary
      masks with corresponding category labels. By using these architectures and by aggregating anomaly


                                                                                                          2

      scores on the predicted masks, rather than reasoning on individual pixels, RBA (Nayal et al., 2023),
109   EAM (Grcić et al., 2023), Mask2Anomaly (Rai et al., 2023b;a), and Maskomaly (Ackermann et al.,
110   2023) obtain significant improvements in anomaly segmentation w.r.t. previous solutions. However,
111   the state-of-the-art results rely on using large Transformer backbones, such as ViT (Dosovitskiy
112   et al., 2021) and Swin (Liu et al., 2021), which have a significant number of training parameters,
113   thereby incurring in a substantial training cost.
114
      To address this problem, we propose the Dual-Stream Adapter, an adapter tailored for anomaly seg-
115   mentation, which directly leverages a large ViT backbone, pre-trained on a large dataset to give
116   robust features without retraining. Our Dual-Stream Adapter retains the advantages of previous
117   mask-based anomaly segmentation methods, while being more training efficient. Moreover, at de-
118   ployment, this solution enables model reuse, since the same frozen pre-trained backbone can be
119   utilized for other downstream tasks, eliminating the need for multiple specialized versions.
120
121   Adapters. Learning universal representations that can be specialized to multiple tasks and do-
122   mains is quintessential in deep learning. Among the techniques developed to transfer learned repre-
123   sentations, adapters (Rebuffi et al., 2017; 2018) have emerged as an effective and parameter-efficient
124   solution. The idea behind adapters is to get compact parametrizations of models by partitioning
125   their parameters into two parts: a vector of fixed parameters that capture a universal representation,
126
      and a smaller vector of learnable parameters that are domain or task-specific. Adapters have re-
      cently gained a lot of traction in Natural Language Processing (NLP), where they have been used
127
      as a mechanism to adapt the large language models, such as BERT (Devlin et al., 2019), to many
128
      downstream tasks, while using a minimal number of trainable parameters (Houlsby et al., 2019).
129   In computer vision, adapters have been utilized for incremental and multi-task/multi-domain learn-
130   ing (Rebuffi et al., 2017; Rosenfeld & Tsotsos, 2018; Rebuffi et al., 2018). More recently, with the
131   success of vision-language models, a plethora of adapters (Zhang et al., 2022; Sung et al., 2022),
132   for the CLIP architecture (Radford et al., 2021), were introduced to address zero-shot/few-shot clas-
133   sification or vision-language tasks. Similarly to NLP, in computer vision the emergence of large
134   pre-trained models (Kirillov et al., 2023; Oquab et al., 2024) is making the utilization of adapters
135   quite compelling. In fact, fine-tuning these models for possibly many downstream tasks is inefficient
136   not only in terms of required training resources, but also in terms of deployed resources, as a de-
137
      vice that must perform multiple tasks would require multiple specialized copies of the same model.
      Moreover, it was also demonstrated that fine-tuning these large vision models could distort the pre-
138
      trained features (Kumar et al., 2022) and lead to poor generalization. This has inspired a few works
139
      to use a vanilla ViT architecture (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) and adapt it to various vision tasks (Li
140   et al., 2021; 2022). Along these lines, Chen et al. (2023) have introduced the ViT-Adapter, which
141   integrates vision-specific inductive biases into the plain ViT, reaching comparable performance to
142   recent vision-specific transformer variants on several tasks, but in a parameter-efficient way.
143
      Unlike the general-purpose ViT-Adapter (Chen et al., 2023), which is designed for multi-
144
      ple vision tasks, we introduce an adapter specifically for anomaly segmentation. Since the
145
      anomaly segmentation problem requires the classifier to identify ‘none-of-the-above’ scenarios, our
146   Dual-Stream Adapter is built to reinforce the separation between in-distribution features and com-
147   plementary features that represent unfamiliar or anomalous objects. To the best of our knowledge,
148   this is the first adapter explicitly tailored for anomaly segmentation.

172   Mask2Former (Cheng et al., 2022) is a universal architecture that approaches segmentation as a di-
173

174

175
      that generates high-resolution per-pixel embeddings from the encoder’s low-resolution features, and
176   (iii) a transformer-decoder that operates on image features to process object queries. For brevity, we
177   do not delve into a detailed description of these components. Interested readers can refer to Cheng
178   et al. (2022) for a comprehensive explanation of the Mask2Former architecture. Additionally, for
179   the sake of compactness and readability, we henceforth refer to the pixel decoder and transformer
180   decoder collectively as the decoder (see Fig. 1-left).

209   4     D UAL S TREAM A DAPTER
210
211   The Dual-Stream Adapter (DSA) architecture is based upon the ViT-Adapter structure (Fig. 2). It
212   incorporates an anomaly prior module to capture initial ID and OOD features and dual-stream fea-
213   ture refinement modules that refine these features through interaction with frozen ViT features. We
214   guide the separation of ID and OOD features by training them with a novel hyperbolic loss function.
215   In the following subsections, we discuss these technical novelties in detail. For clarity, we omit the
      dependency of the adapter modules from the parameters θa .

321   4.3                       U NCERTAINTY BASED H YPERBOLIC L OSS
322
323   Although the adapter architecture described so far is designed to generate two streams of features,
      we need a way to guide the learning process so that these two streams capture characteristics of the

324
      in-distribution and out-of-distribution pixels, respectively. To provide this guidance without relying
325   on extra data, we leverage the void/background class, considering those pixels as OOD whereas the
326   labeled objects are ID, and we design a loss in hyperbolic space.
327
328
      The hyperbolic space formulation was demonstrated to be effective in a plethora of visual
      tasks (Atigh et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2020) over Euclidean space. Moreover, it was shown that it en-
329
      ables the use of low-dimensional embeddings without compromising representation power (Nickel
330
      & Kiela, 2017), which is particularly well-suited for adapters. Most importantly, Atigh et al. (2022)
331   demonstrated that with hyperbolic embeddings in a Poincaré ball the pixel uncertainty can be ob-
332   tained by taking the L2 norm to the origin of the ball itself. We use this property to design a new
333   loss function termed as uncertainty-based hyperbolic loss (Lubhl ), that maximizes the uncertainty
334   for OOD pixels and minimizes uncertainty for ID pixels. In the following, we discuss Lubhl in
335   detail.

350   We add Lubhl to the total training loss (that is the same as Mask2Former (Cheng et al., 2022))
351   formulated as:
352
353                                              Ltotal = λubhl Lubhl + Lmask + λclass Lclass                                                        (10)
354
      Here, Lmask is the mask loss which is a combination of the binary cross entropy loss and dice loss,
355
      and Lclass is the classification loss weighted by λclass = 2. λubhl is the weight of Lubhl which is set
356
      to 0.1.

510   5.2    A BLATION S TUDY
511
      We conduct all ablation experiments on the DSA-Tiny architecture.
512
      Component-wise ablation: Table 5(a) presents the component-wise ablation of the novel parts
513   present in the dual-stream adapteron SMIYC RO-21. We find that removing the anomaly prior
514   and dual-stream feature refinement modules shows a significant performance drop in anomaly seg-
515   mentation. This is due to the reason that the network is not able to separate well ID and OOD
516   features in the absence of the aforementioned components. Finally, using Lubhl further reduces the
517   false positive rate and improves the AuPRC score.
518   Number of dual-stream feature refinement: In Tab. 5(b), we present the effect of the number
519   of dual-stream feature refinement on anomaly segmentation performance on SMIYC RO-21. As
520   observed from the table, setting the number of dual-stream feature refinement modules as 4 and 6
521
      gives the best AuPRC and FPR95 scores, respectively. We opt for 4 modules to have fewer trainable
      parameters.
522
      Curvature size: In Fig. 6(a), we discuss the anomaly segmentation performance with varying cur-
523
      vature size of the Poincaré ball. Results shown are the average performance taken on all the datasets.
524   We empirically find that setting the curvature to 0.01 yields overall better performance compared to
525   other values.
526   Loss comparison: Figure 6(b) shows the anomaly segmentation performance comparison when us-
527   ing our proposed loss Lubhl , and when replacing it with a binary cross-entropy loss or contrastive
528   loss. The results are the average performance given by all datasets. We find that Lubhl yields the best
529   performance among all the losses, confirming the advantage of using the hyperbolic representation
530   to separate the features.

532   6     C ONCLUSION
533
534   In this work, we introduce a novel adapter architecture for anomaly segmentation.
535   Our architecture incorporates two key architectural components: anomaly prior modules
536   and dual-stream feature refinement tailored to improve anomaly segmentation. The anomaly prior
537   module learns to extract the initial ID and OOD features that are refined and improved by passing
538   through a set of dual-stream feature refinement blocks. We introduce an uncertainty-based hyper-
539   bolic loss to explicitly learn the ID and OOD features. We hope that our dual-stream adapter will
      pave new ways for developing efficient anomaly segmentation methods based on vision adapters.

758   A.1   I MPLEMENTATION DETAILS
759
760   DSA-Tiny: The model architecture consists of a ViT-Tiny the backbone and a Mask2Former
761   decoder, which includes 9 mask attention layers. The ViT backbone is pre-trained on ImageNet-1K.
762   The number of dual-stream adapter blocks are kept 4. The FFN consists of two MLP layers
763   having a GELU activation layer between them. We used deformable attention architecture as a
764   cross-attention in our architecture. We used the AdamW optimizer with an initial learning rate
765
      of 12 × 10−5 , a batch size of 32, and a weight decay of 0.01. Layer-wise learning rate decay of
      0.95 is used during training. The DSA-Tiny model was trained for 46000 iterations on Cityscapes,
766
      utilizing a batch size of 32 and an image crop size of 512 × 512. The model contains a total of
767
      28.5M parameters, with 5.5M from the frozen ViT backbone. Of the remaining 23M trainable
768   parameters dual-stream adapter has 2.4M parameters.
769
770
771
      DSA-Large: The architecture of DSA-Large consists of a ViT-Large backbone with BEiT archi-
      tecture and a Mask2Former decoder, which includes two mask attention layers. The ViT backbone
772
      is pre-trained on ImageNet-22K dataset. The number of dual-stream adapter blocks is set to 4. We
773
      employed the AdamW optimizer with an initial learning rate of 2 × 10−5 and a weight decay of
774   0.05. During training, we applied layer-wise learning rate decay of 0.9. The DSA-Large model
775   was trained for 60, 000 iterations on Cityscapes, with a batch size of 8 and an image crop size of
776   768 × 768. The model has a total of 435.9M parameters, of which 303.3M are from the frozen
777   ViT backbone. Out of the remaining 133M trainable parameters, dual-stream adapter accounts for
778   27.7M parameters.
779   Outlier supervision is a widely used technique to improve the performance of anomaly seg-
780   mentation methods (Rai et al., 2023b; Nayal et al., 2023; Grcić et al., 2022) by training models
781   with synthetic anomaly images generated on the fly. In our approach, we create these synthetic
782
      images using AnomalyMix (Tian et al., 2022), which cut objects from the MS-COCO (Lin et al.,
      2014) dataset based on ground truth annotations and randomly pasting them onto Cityscapes
783
      images. This produces a binary mask, where the MS-COCO object area is labeled as 1 showing
784
      an anomalous region, and the Cityscapes background area is labeled as 0 for the non-anomalous
785   region. For training, we employ a mask contrastive loss (Rai et al., 2023b). During this phase,
786   only the mask decoder is trained, and the loss term Lubhl is not applied since DSA is frozen. We
787   train DSA-Tiny and DSA-Large for 2000 iterations, using batch sizes of 8 and 2, respectively.
788   The probability of selecting an outlier in each training batch is set to 0.2. All other hyperparam-
789   eters are kept consistent with previous settings. The margin for the mask contrastive loss is set to 1.0.
790
