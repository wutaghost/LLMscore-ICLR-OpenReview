                                                   A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               The challenges faced by neural networks on tabular data are well-documented and
               have hampered the progress of tabular foundation models. Techniques leveraging
011
               in-context learning (ICL) have shown promise here, allowing for dynamic adapta-
012
               tion to unseen data. ICL can provide predictions for entirely new datasets without
013            further training or hyperparameter tuning, therefore providing very fast inference
014            when encountering a novel task. However, scaling ICL for tabular data remains
015            an issue: approaches based on large language models cannot efficiently process
016            numeric tables, and tabular-specific techniques have not been able to effectively
017            harness the power of real data to improve performance and generalization. We
018            are able to overcome these challenges by training tabular-specific ICL-based ar-
019            chitectures on real data with self-supervised learning and retrieval, combining the
020            best of both worlds. Our resulting model – the Tabular Discriminative Pre-trained
021
               Transformer (TabDPT) – achieves state-of-the-art performance on the CC18 (clas-
               sification) and CTR23 (regression) benchmarks with no task-specific fine-tuning,
022
               demonstrating the adapatability and speed of ICL once the model is pre-trained.
023
               TabDPT also demonstrates strong scaling as both model size and amount of
024            available data increase, pointing towards future improvements simply through the
025            curation of larger tabular pre-training datasets and training larger models.

050   In-context learning (ICL) – referring to the phenomenon where a model generalizes to new tasks
051   using only in-context template examples with no additional fine-tuning – is one avenue showing
052   promise in building neural networks that can dynamically adapt to input data. ICL was first observed

      tables are not text, though, it is challenging to apply LLMs to tabular data. The cell-based, textual
055   tokenization in particular is highly inefficient and makes context size a major limitation (Fang et al.,
056   2024). This has hindered the adoption of LLM-based ICL techniques in practical tabular settings.
057   An alternative technique directly trained to perform ICL is the transformer-based TabPFN (Holl-
058   mann et al., 2023), designed specifically for tabular data. TabPFN is pre-trained exclusively on
059   synthetic data (Müller et al., 2022) and is able to more efficiently use its context by avoiding
060   cell-based tokenization: instead, the rows essentially act as tokens. While the performance of
061   TabPFN is impressive (McElfresh et al., 2023), especially since the lack of task-specific fine-tuning
062   greatly speeds up inference time, the lack of training on real data leaves something to be desired: we
063
      conjecture that the synthetic data generation procedure used in training is not sufficiently diverse,
      and improving it is a highly non-trivial task. Furthermore, it cannot natively perform regression,
064
      and struggles as dataset size increases, greatly limiting its potential as a TFM.
065
066   Given the adaptability and efficiency of ICL, we would like to scale it to both handle larger datasets
067   and benefit from real pre-training data. The former can be accomplished using retrieval-based
068   training for more efficient use of the context; Thomas et al. (2024) showed that this can improve
069
      performance when fine-tuning on specific tasks, and we demonstrate here that it can also be
      adapted to the pre-training phase. As for the latter, we can turn to self-supervised learning (SSL)
070
      techniques to augment the relatively low amount of quality pre-training tabular data that is publicly
071
      available. Specifically, we perform random column prediction to enhance the amount of training
072   data, analogously to what has been done in language (Devlin et al., 2019) and vision (He et al.,
073   2022). Combining a transformer-based ICL with retrieval-based SSL results in our method – the
074   Tabular Discriminative Pre-trained Transformer (TabDPT) – which demonstrates impressive
075   performance even on brand new tabular tasks. We summarize our contributions below:
076
077            1. We introduce TabDPT as a TFM that performs both classification and regression on unseen
078               datasets with no additional training or hyperparameter tuning, backed by transformer-
079
                  based ICL, retrieval-based self-supervised pre-training, and retrieval-based inference.
080            2. We comprehensively evaluate TabDPT on the OpenML-CC18 (Bischl et al., 2021) and
081               OpenML-CTR23 (Fischer et al., 2023) benchmarks, showing state-of-the-art performance
082
                  on unseen datasets even when compared with methods that train on that data with 30
                  rounds of per-dataset hyperparameter tuning. Our runtime is therefore also much lower
083
                  than these baselines once we have a pre-trained model.
084
085            3. As there is no single accepted benchmark in the tabular domain, we introduce the idea of
086
                  using duel-based ranking methods (Elo, 1967; Glickman, 2012) to evaluate the relative
                  performance of models even when pairwise comparison across all datasets is unavailable,
087
                  mimicking similar developments in LLMs (Chiang et al., 2024).
088
089            4. We show that the performance of TabDPT scales with both model size and amount of train-
090
                  ing data, with Figure 1 in particular demonstrating the power of pre-training with real data.
091            5. We will release all code, which includes the weights of the trained TabDPT,1 methods for
092               training TabDPT, a comprehensive evaluation suite, and a library for detecting leakage in
093               tabular datasets which confirms that we are not training on downstream data.2

      Self-Supervised Learning and Generalization in the Tabular Domain SSL has proven to be suc-
114
      cessful for text and images (Devlin et al., 2019; Dosovitskiy et al., 2021), but has not demonstrated
115   the same level of success on tabular data. Many tabular SSL methods cannot generalize beyond the
116   dataset they were pre-trained on (Huang et al., 2020; Yoon et al., 2020; Majmundar et al., 2022; Sui
117   et al., 2024). This raises the question of whether tabular SSL methods can benefit from cross-task
118   training. The answer to this appears increasingly likely to be in the affirmative, as it has been shown
119   very recently that even tree-based methods benefit from tuning their default hyperparameters across
120   tasks (Holzmüller et al., 2024); this same work, following (Rubachev et al., 2022), demonstrates
121   that basic MLPs can also be competitive in predictive tabular tasks when leveraging SSL. Con-
122   sequently, tabular SSL methods have begun to show generalization across tasks and competitive
123
      performance (Zhu et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2023). However, they still require task specific fine-tuning
      and hyper-parameter tuning, which can be time- and resource-intensive. The only other tabular
124
      SSL method we have seen that is able to generalize across tasks without task-specific fine-tuning
125
      is by Gardner et al. (2024). However, this 8 billion parameter model still only has a maximum
126   context size of 32 data points – as it is LLM-based – and its performance is not competitive. To our
127   knowledge, we are the first to demonstrate competitive performance and successful generalization
128   of tabular SSL across tasks without task-specific fine-tuning or hyperparameter tuning.

      3.1   T RANSFORMER E NCODER FOR I N -C ONTEXT L EARNING ON TABULAR DATA
137
      Our main goal in this work is to understand how to build tabular foundation models that will
138   scale with model size and amount of data. First, we focus on the architecture. We have found the
139   backbone of TabPFN (Hollmann et al., 2023) to be suitable: it is a non-autoregressive transformer
140   encoder wherein entire rows of incoming tabular data can attend to each other and thus play the role
141   of “tokens”. More precisely, for every input table with N rows and F features, we first standardize
142   the feature dimension to a fixed size Fmax , achieved by either padding with zeros or subsampling
143   features. The table is then embedded into a tensor of shape (N, d), where d represents the trans-
144   former’s hidden dimension, via a linear layer. We do not handle categorical or numerical variables
145   differently, with more details on that in Section 3.2. Subsequently, in the transformer layers, we treat
146
      the row dimension N as the sequence length so that individual instances can attend to each other.
147   A row-based tabular encoding contrasts with that of LLMs which require tokenization of each cell in
148   the input data, inflating the memory requirements by a factor of F ×⟨Ntok ⟩, where ⟨Ntok ⟩ is the aver-
149   age number of tokens per cell. Even with techniques such as sparse attention (Child et al., 2019) and
150   Byte Pair Encoding (Gage, 1994), the overhead remains significant, limiting the table size that can
151   be processed. A similar phenomenon is observed in the image domain when comparing pixel-based
152
      transformers (Chen et al., 2020) to ViTs (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) which use coherent numerical
      patches of images for embedding, resulting in more efficient models. Similarly, we argue that tables
153
      should be divided into structurally meaningful units, such as rows, for more efficient processing.
154
155   By reducing memory consumption, row-based encoding permits processing a large number of rows,
156   which in turn enables efficient ICL on new tables. In contrast, cell-based methods (Huang et al.,
157   2020; Gorishniy et al., 2024; van Breugel et al., 2024) are limited in the number of rows they can
158
      process at a time, rendering them incapable of processing full tables. In turn this prevents ICL and
      requires additional training or fine-tuning on new datasets, unlike our method.
159
160   The final point to discuss on architecture is our approach for training both classification and regres-
161   sion with the same backbone, which is also the biggest change from the architecture of Hollmann
      et al. (2023). For this, we attach two heads after the transformer layers, each consisting of two-layer

      3.3      T RAINING WITH E ND - TO - END R ETRIEVAL
199
200   One of the primary challenges in training in-context transformer-based models for tabular data
201   is the quadratic growth of compute and memory usage with the context length. This limitation
202   restricts the number of support examples that can be effectively utilized within the context window.
203   While language-based models (Gardner et al., 2024) or TabPFN (Hollmann et al., 2023; McElfresh
204   et al., 2023) can handle small datasets where the entire training set can fit within the context, their
205   scalability to larger, more complex datasets is limited. This raises the question of how to efficiently
206   select and use context when dealing with larger datasets.
207   Recently Thomas et al. (2024) and Xu et al. (2024) showed that using a dynamic context local to
208   each query point greatly improves the performance and scalability of TabPFN at inference. We
209   hypothesized that training our model end-to-end on local context would improve the downstream
210   performance even further as it results in a better alignment between training and testing objectives.
211   This objective is similar to works such as RETRO (Borgeaud et al., 2022) or TabR (Gorishniy et al.,
212   2024) which have shown improved results by using retrieval during training. However, performing
213   an exact kNN search for each point in the minibatch during training is expensive. The main cost is
214   not from the search itself, but rather the fact that now each query point has its own unique context,

247   3.4      I NFERENCE S TRATEGIES
248
249   Recall from Section 3.1 that our architecture needs a pre-defined maximum number of features Fmax
250   and classes Cmax . We discuss how to overcome these limitations with inference-time techniques.
251   Features When the number of features in a table exceeds Fmax , we reduce the dimensionality of
252   the table using Principal Component Analysis (PCA) to Fmax .
253
      Classes If a dataset contains C classes with C > Cmax , we cannot perform classification in a single
254
      forward pass. While we could do binary classification in a one-versus-all fashion, this would require
255   C forward passes which may drastically impact the inference speed of our algorithm; some datasets
256   have hundreds of classes. A much more computationally efficient idea    is to write  C in base Cmax
257   and predict each base-Cmax digits separately. This then requires only logCmax (C) forward passes,
258   which is very efficient. For instance, if we only train using Cmax = 10 classes and have to predict
259   on C ≤ 100, this requires at most two forward passes: one for each digit of the class to predict.

281   4.2    E VALUATION DATA
282
283   For our evaluation, we consider two public benchmarks: CC18 (Bischl et al., 2021) for classification
284   tasks and CTR23 (Fischer et al., 2023) for regression tasks.
285   CC18 is a curated suite of 72 datasets with classification targets originally sourced from OpenML.
286   These datasets each have between 500 and 100,000 instances, less than 5,000 features, and originate
287   from diverse domains such as finance, biology, games, banking, industrial applications, or natural
288   signals such as vision or sound. Datasets were selected according to curation criteria that included
289   avoiding synthetic data, requiring source information, and removing datasets where a simple algo-
290   rithm achieved 100% accuracy. CC18 is a widely used benchmark for evaluating tabular learning
291   (Bahri et al., 2022; Hollmann et al., 2023; McElfresh et al., 2023).
292   CTR23 is a benchmark suite of 35 datasets also curated from OpenML. It follows most of the
293   design choices of CC18 but contains regression rather than classification tasks. In particular, it
294   uses the same restrictions on number of samples and features as CC18, but replaces the accuracy
295   restriction with a requirement that a linear model must not achieve R2 = 1 on the selected datasets.

297   4.3    C ONTAMINATION A NALYSIS
298
299   To ensure that the datasets used for training did not contain any information about the evaluation
300   data, we extracted a range of metadata from each dataset and compared them across all pairs of
301
      training and evaluation datasets. This includes: i) dataset names, ii) hashes of dataset files, iii) num-
      bers of columns and rows, iv) target mean and variance, v) mean, variance, skew, and kurtosis of each
302
      feature, and vi) coefficients of a univariate linear fit between each feature and the target if available.
303
304   To allow for efficient pairwise comparisons between all features in all datasets, we use k-d
305   trees (Bentley, 1975) constructed for each dataset that contain the feature statistics. Any pairs of
306   datasets with unusual similarities detected were manually evaluated and removed from training if
307
      they were found to be related. Since this procedure is primarily based on automated checks, it can
      be used in the future to further scale our training data.

        5.2               S CALING L AWS FOR TABULAR DATA
410
411    To the best of our knowledge, this work presents the first analysis of scaling laws for tabular
412    foundation models. We observe how performance changes when systematically varying model size
413    by adjusting the number of layers and transformer dimensions, as well as the amount of training
414    data. Our models range from 33K to 78M parameters, trained on data subsets spanning from 52M
415    cells (104K rows) to 2B cells (32M rows). For PFN++, we limit the variation to model size, keeping
416    the prior-generating function fixed. Following Hoffmann et al. (2022), we adopt the joint power-law
417    model ℓ̂(P, D) = A/P α + B/Dβ + E, where ℓ̂ represents the estimated loss (or another target metric),
418    P denotes the number of parameters, and D the number of tokens, or in our case the number of cells
419    in the entire training set. Notably, although we use row-based encodings, not all rows affect the
420
       model (especially the encoder layer) equally, and thus cell count is a better measure for dataset size.
       We use the improved methodology by Besiroglu et al. (2024) to estimate the parameters A, B, α, β,
421
       and E. For the scaling exponents in particular, we find α = 0.42 and β = 0.39, which are within
422
       the expected range and are very close to each other, mirroring Hoffmann et al. (2022)’s observation.
423
424    In Figure 1, we illustrate the scaling behavior of our models along with the power-law fit. Since we
425    train on both classification and regression tasks, with roughly 50% of the samples in each category,
426
       the loss on the y-axis represents the average of the cross-entropy loss for classification and 1 − ρ
       for regression, where ρ is the correlation between the prediction and true target, equivalent to MSE
427
       for normalized vectors. Note that for visualization purposes we report, on a log-scale, the excess
428
       loss ℓ̂(P, D) − E (the estimated loss de-biased by E) instead of the raw loss. The empirical values
429
       (P, D, ℓ(P, D)) are reported alongside the power-law fit.
430
431    We also provide the empirical values for models using the TabPFN prior (Hollmann et al., 2023),
       shown in green. These models are not fit to the joint power-law model, as the data size D is


                                                                                                                     8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      not known a priori. However, we can estimate the number of rows or cells seen during training,
433   which totals to approximately 17B rows and 860B cells for all model sizes. As shown in Figure 1,
434   the quality of the data – whether real or synthetic – affects both the shape of the loss curve and
435   the terminal loss. We hypothesize that the synthetic data generated by TabPFN contains many of
436   the “easy” patterns present in real-world data, but not all. This is supported by smaller models
437   outperforming ones trained on real data of up to 2B cells, while larger models trained on TabPFN
438   data perform comparably to models trained on 300-600M real cells.
439   From Figure 1, we observe that as the models have more parameters, their performance becomes
440   more predictable. However, for larger models on smaller amounts of data, the loss is greater than
441   predicted (and in some cases unstable, see Appendix F), which could indicate signs of overfitting.
442   Additionally, for the joint classification and regression loss we observe a behaviour predictable
443   by power-law models, but neither classification nor regression alone is explained quite as well.
444   In Appendix F, we discuss the scaling analysis in more detail.
445   Our analysis suggests an important insight: although neural network-based methods have struggled
446   with tabular data for a long time, performance continues to improve as model size and data quantity
447   increase, much like text and image data.

450
451   In Figure 4a, we approximate the speed of each algorithm by computing the median time it takes
452   to perform a full training and evaluation – including HPO search – on datasets with size larger than
453   10,000 rows from CC18. From this, we compute the median time to process 1,000 rows, along
454   with the 25th and 75th percentiles. We also note, although it is not shown, that average time is
455   usually much higher than median for the baselines. We report the runtimes of TabDPT models with
456   different context sizes, indicated by the number labelling TabDPT points. It is also worth noting
457   that even the biggest TabDPT model with the largest context size is at least one order of magnitude
458
      (up to 4) faster than the baseline models. While the baseline models need to train on each dataset
      separately, TabDPT is much faster thanks to its ICL capability.
459
460   In addition, since TabDPT performs inference with a fixed batch and feature size, our speed per 1,000
461   rows is very consistent. We also add TabDPT (subsampling) which uses a shared random context to
462   classify all the test points – in the style of TabPFN – allowing for even faster inference at the cost of
463   performance. However, while tree-based and DL baselines offer faster inference after training, their
464
      efficiency depends on the scenario: TabDPT is advantageous for streaming data requiring frequent
      retraining, whereas traditional models are preferable for fixed data needing rapid inference.
465
466   In the pre-training phase, we have also observed that TabDPT achieves a higher performance than
467   PFN++ within the same number of epochs especially early on during training. In Figure 13, we can
468   see that TabDPT obtains lower test loss given a fixed compute budget, especially for larger models.
469   This clearly highlights the importance of real data compared to synthetic data.

512       6                           L IMITATIONS , C ONCLUSION , AND F UTURE W ORK
513
514
515       While our model achieves very competitive performance on two popular tabular benchmarks, it is
516       still subject to some important limitations that have not been addressed in this work.
517       i) As of now, the current model cannot use textual information. Note that it is still possible to
518       modify the architecture to use embeddings of feature names, but we did observe overfitting when we
519       attempted it. We believe training the model on more tables with more features overall could lead to
520       improvements but we leave this to future work. ii) We still suffer from the limitation on the number
521       of features, and the non-invariance to the class and feature ordering, inherited from the “attention
522       over rows” architecture of TabPFN (Hollmann et al., 2023). The ordering dependence is mitigated
523       through randomization during training and we proposed inference techniques to address the former
524       limitations, although a better outcome would be finding a performant architecture that inherently
525
          does not suffer from these limitations. iii) We have made some assumptions on the types of data
          we handle: these are rectangular (i.e., not hierarchical or nested) tables which are i.i.d. (i.e., having
526
          no time component or distribution shift between training data and test/inference data). iv) Unlike
527
          foundation models in other domains, we have not shown generative (Loaiza-Ganem et al., 2024)
528       capabilities. We anticipate that TabDPT could be complemented with ideas from, e.g., (Ma et al.,
529       2023), (Ma et al., 2024) or (van Breugel et al., 2024). v) Even though our runtime on new tasks
530       is extremely fast compared to the baseline models, the pre-training of TabDPT is still itself time-
531       and resource-consuming, although we will provide the weights of TabDPT to help mitigate this.
532       Furthermore, there may be specific applications or settings where TabDPT and other ICL-based
533       techniques require additional fine-tuning, which may make them slower in comparison.
534       While deep learning has traditionally struggled with tabular data, our findings demonstrate that,
535       with the appropriate architecture and data utilization, similar scaling laws to those seen in other
536       domains can emerge. This suggests that tabular data is not inherently unique, and we anticipate that
537       larger tabular models trained on increasing amounts of data will become the norm. In this paper,
538       we present TabDPT as a scalable model that achieves strong performance on the CC18 and CTR23
539       benchmarks, but we expect the field to see the development of even larger models trained on more
          expansive datasets in the future.

      A      B ITTER LESSONS
811
812
      The architecture of TabDPT is based on TabPFN with some minor modifications (see Appendix H.1).
813   Therefore, it inherits the limitations of TabPFN. To mitigate these, we introduce inference time
814   techniques (see Section 3.4), although we also attempted to overcome these shortcomings during
815   training. The following list contains some of the ideas that either hurt the performance or did not
816   lead to significant improvements. We include this list to emphasize The Bitter Lesson7 and the fact
817   that efficient use of computation and access to high-quality data are the more important factors in
818   driving performance. The list is as follows:
819
820           • Different pre-processing techniques that were more robust to outliers, or variants of soft
821
                clipping, resulted in no improvement. More advanced methods, such as Robust Scaler and
                Power Transform, only ended up slowing the training process.
822
823           • Class embeddings (either through a separate network or by using class “tokens” in the
824             transformer layer) and computing various similarity metrics between query and class em-
825
                beddings in a proto-network manner, with the aim of adapting to any number of classes,
                hurt the performance, especially on real data.
826
827           • Different embeddings for yctx , including a dense layer for regression and a dictionary of
828             Cmax × d embeddings, with the rationale of informing the model about the task, did not
829             lead to performance improvements in large models with sufficient data.
830           • Specialized tokens for NaN encoding did not improve performance compared to replacing
831             NaNs with mean values (which are zero after preprocessing). Additionally, appending bi-
832             nary features to differentiate actual zeros from NaNs (indicating that the cell was replaced),
833             effectively doubling the number of features, also failed to improve performance.
834           • Architectures encoding cells as “tokens”, with vertical and horizontal attention, similar to
835             spatial and temporal attention in videos, proved more memory intensive. While equivari-
836             ance to feature order is desirable, processing tensors of size (B, N, f, d) – where B is batch
837             size, N is the number of rows, f the number of features, and d the embedding dimension –
838             uses much more memory. The simpler architecture with tensors of size (B, N, d) permits
839
                a higher embedding dimension d.

841   B     T RAINING AND INFERENCE DETAILS
842
843   In this section we provide some additional details on some important training decisions.
844
      Preprocessing We chose to be very simple and general. All columns containing non-numerical
845
      values are mapped to integers using scikit-learn’s (Pedregosa et al., 2011) LabelEncoder
846
      function. The table is then standardized to 0 mean and unit variance, and outliers beyond 10 are
847   clipped.
848
849   After retrieval, we obtain a local context Xctx and their labels yctx . We make sure to standardize the
850
      context before the forward pass of our model to avoid distribution shifts and also standardize yctx if
      it is a regression target for the same reason.
851
852   Retrieval We use the faiss library8 for fast retrieval. All retrievals are done using the raw data
853   space after preprocessing, as in Thomas et al. (2024).
854   Missing Value Encoding We tried several strategies, including concatenating our features with a
855   mask indicating whether each value was originally a missing value or not, however we never saw
856   any performance gain from it. In the end, we simply zero out the missing values and let the model
857   learn to be robust to potentially inaccurate values in the input. Note that zeroing out is done post
858   normalization, meaning missing values are replaced with the mean.
859
      Optimizer:      We use the Schedule Free optimizer from Defazio et al. (2024) with
860   AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019). We observed significant increase in performance and op-
861   timization speed compared to a cosine scheduler.

966        D    D ETAILS ON THE RETRIEVAL
967
968        During training, it is not necessary to predict the outcome for only a single point, as the point is
969        not provided in advance. Instead, when multiple points belong to the same neighbourhood, a single
970        model call with shared context can perform prediction for all of them, optimizing both memory
971        and compute during training. Selecting a “local neighbourhood” of points and distributing them
           between context and query achieves such an arrangement. Specifically, we begin with one point per


                                                           18
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


972
       dataset (B points in the batch), each with N neighbours. We obtain the input X by either padding or
973    dimensionality reduction, resulting in a shape of (B, N, Fmax ). After constructing the random target
974    y of shape (B, N ), we randomly split these into context and query sets along the row dimension N .
975
976
       The goal is to evaluate the loss on B × Nqy points at once. If exact retrieval were performed, only
       one prediction per neighbourhood would be possible. This method creates a context that is not only
977
       local to each point but also shared across Nqy points. This approach was introduced in Thomas et al.
978
       (2024), and more details can be found there. During inference, exact retrieval is required as the query
979    points are fixed. We illustrate the treatment of samples during training and retrieval in Figure 5.

1375   I.1    D ETAILS OF PFN++
1376
1377   PFN++ In addition to our main model TabDPT, we also introduce PFN++, which is an improved
1378   version of the original TabPFN (Hollmann et al., 2023). PFN++ uses the same prior generator
1379   as Hollmann et al. (2023) but it shares the same model architecture and training procedure as Tab-
1380   DPT, detailed in Appendix H.
1381   Moreover, unlike TabPFN, PFN++ can also perform regression. In addition to TabPFN’s classifica-
1382   tion targets, we create synthetic regression targets for training PFN++ during the prior fitting stage.
1383   In the TabPFN implementation, targets are first sampled from a Structural Causal Model (SCM), then
1384   they are binned and transformed into classification targets. We slightly modify this method for re-
1385   gression purposes by taking the raw outputs from the SCM and normalizing them using the Z-score.
1386   TabPFN We use the officially released checkpoint of TabPFN9 for our experiments. In Hollmann
1387   et al. (2023), the TabPFN model is optionally ensembled by randomly shuffling features and classes.
1388   We omit feature or class ensembling in all of our experiments across all models to ensure fair
1389   comparisons.

       I.3   F EW- SHOT LEARNING RESULTS
1513
1514   We furthermore assess the performance of TabDPT on a unsupervised few shot learning setting. We
1515   consider the protocol from STUNT (Nam et al., 2023) on their 10-shot experiment and use the results
1516   for STUNT (Nam et al., 2023), CACTUs (Hsu et al., 2018), VIME+LR and ICT available in their
1517   paper. Algorithms are evaluated on seven tasks from CC18 and evaluated on accuracy. 10 labelled
1518   examples per class are available, the rest of the training is considered unlabelled. TabDPT only using
1519   the 10-shots per class performs similarly to the kNN baseline. While it is a non-trivial baseline, it is
1520
       not competitive with modern few-shot methods such as STUNT. However STUNT also uses up to
       thousands of unlabelled examples for pretraining on each dataset. Note that TabDPT is furthermore
1521
       rarely trained on small contexts (uniformly sampled between 10 and 1024 during training), so we
1522
       use a simple method to make use of the unlabelled data and use larger context. We simply predict
1523   the class probabilities for the unlabelled training set using the 10 shots as context. Then we take the
1524   top-1000 points where our certainty is highest and use them and their predicted labels and the 10
1525   shots as context. This results in TabDPT (semi), a semi-supervised technique using pseudo-labels.
1526   This method outperforms STUNT on 5 / 7 datasets and on the average accuracy (averaged over 50
1527   seeds). Furthermore, it requires only forward passes while STUNT requires pretraining for each
1528   task.
