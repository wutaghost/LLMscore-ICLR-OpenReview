009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Outliers in both covariates and output responses pose significant challenges for
012            Gaussian Process (GP) regression models. We present a novel GP regression
013            approach that effectively integrates the Huber likelihood into the GP frame-
014            work—without introducing additional parameters to infer. Specifically, we model
015            the likelihood of observed outputs using the Huber probability distribution: this
016            reduces deviations caused by output outliers. For covariate outliers, we introduce
017
               a projection pursuit weights—attenuating their influence on the model. To ad-
               dress the analytically intractable, yet unimodal, posterior distribution, we employ
018
               Laplace approximation and Gibbs sampling within a Markov Chain Monte Carlo
019
               (MCMC) framework. We simplify Gibbs sampling by expressing the likelihood
020            associated with outlying points as normally distributed through the scale mixture
021            representation of the Laplace distribution. This work is particularly important in the
022            field of transmission spectroscopy—where noisy measurements are often neglected
023            in the estimation of planet-to-star radius ratios. We demonstrate the robustness
024            and effectiveness of our method through extensive experiments on synthetic and
025            real-world datasets.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
029
      Bayesian inference which is based on Gaussian likelihood is known to be sensitive to extreme
030
      observations and gross errors, called outliers. The estimation of parameters in Gaussian processes
031
      (GPs) is affected in non-Gaussian error settings as the predictive uncertainty assigns equal confidence
032   to the measurements, regardless of whether they are outliers or not. We illustrate this problem
033
      p a numerical example. Let us consider a 2-d sinc function y(x) = sinc(x) + e, where x =
      in
034      (x21 + x22 ) with an additive error that follows the Student’s t-distribution with 2 degrees of freedom
035
      e ∼ Student’s-t(2). We add additional large outliers y l with magnitude close to 0.8. Figure 1(b)
036   shows the predicted values at test points x = [−16, 16], obtained from standard GP. We observe
037   that the mean fit deviate largely from the true values of the sinc function. This issue is amplified by
038   multiple outliers masking one another in the multivariate regression residuals.
039
      Existing studies addressing the outlier problem in GP regression use various approaches to define
040
      the likelihood. Two common strategies are: (1) using a mixture of two normal distributions or (2)
041
      employing heavy-tailed distributions. Most of these methods assume the error distribution is known
042   a priori—a condition that is often unrealistic in practical applications. Moreover, their robustness
043   is questionable when faced with extreme observations that do not correspond to the non-normal
044   distribution their heavy tailed likelihood is specified to capture. These models typically struggle to
045   handle both general noise patterns and large errors, often attempting to fit extreme values. We show
046   this shortcoming in Figure 1(a) with the sinc function data for the GP with the Student’s t-likelihood
047   and employing the MCMC integration approximation method. We notice that the model overfits to
048   the large outliers y l as it accounts for the large errors with Student’s-t likelihood.
049   In this paper, we propose a new way of handling extreme outliers in covariate space and output
050   responses that models the likelihood of the observed data using Huber density function. We signif-
051   icantly enhance downweighting of the outliers compared to the earlier work by Altamirano et al.
052   (2024), which was limited to handling outliers only in the output responses, added hyperparameters
053   (β, c), did not support maximum likelihood estimation of hyperparameters, and was incapable of
      addressing outliers occurring simultaneously in the covariate and output space.

066   2    R ELATED W ORK
067
068
      Goldberg et al. Goldberg et al. (1997) introduced a dual-model Gaussian process framework to
069   account for covariate-dependent noise. The first Gaussian process model governs the output process y,
070   while the second Gaussian process governs the noise process. West (1984) investigated heavy-tailed
071   error distributions that are constructed as scale mixtures of normal distributions, which are also
072   used for specifying a priori distribution based on the earlier ideas suggested by De Finetti (1961);
073   Ramsey & Novick (1980). By doing so, the prior distribution discounts any observations highlighting
074   inconsistency between likelihood and prior. Along the same line, Desgagné & Gagnon (2019)
075   assumed a super heavy-tailed error distribution dependent on an explanatory variable to make the
076   estimation of the population mean and ratios robust to outliers. Kuss (2006) extended a mixture
077
      of two normal distributions, one to model small errors in regular observations and a second one to
      model large errors in outlying observations. However, Naish-Guzman & Holden (2007) questioned
078
      the adequacy of the two-model approach. They proposed instead twin GP that allow us to choose
079
      between the distribution of the regular observations and that of the outliers. Kuss (2006) suggested
080   a GP with a Laplace likelihood model that utilizes a scale mixture representation of Laplace noise
081   distribution where the variance follows an exponential distribution. Vanhatalo et al. (2009) proposed
082   a GP model based on the Student’s t-likelihood function, where the noise is modeled as a scale
083   mixture of Gaussian distributions. Unfortunately with the non-Gaussian likelihood, the Bayesian
084   inference becomes analytically intractable. Consequently, various advanced approximation methods
085   were proposed Kuss (2006); Vanhatalo et al. (2009); Jylänki et al. (2011); Ranjan et al. (2016); Daemi
086   et al. (2019) to overcome the convergence failure of the classical approximation methods such as
087   expectation propagation Minka (2013), Markov Chain Monte Carlo Neal (1997), variational Bayes
088
      Ghahramani & Beal (2000), and Laplace approximation Williams (1996). More recently, Li et al.
      (2021); Andrade & Takeda (2023) presented a robust variants of GPs for datasets with substantial
089
      contamination removing the outlier data based on trimming parameters in iterative manner.
090
091   In GP regression models with Student’s t-likelihoods Kuss (2006), a scale-mixture representation
092   of the Student’s t-distribution is utilized. A variational approximation is devised presuming the
093   Gaussian likelihood whose individual variances are Gamma distributed. Combined with the Kullback-
094
      Leibler divergence, KL(q||p), between the true posterior, p, and the approximation, q, an expectation
      maximization (EM)-type algorithm is implemented. As for the models with Laplace likelihoods, the
095
      scale mixture model yields a unimodal posterior enabling the implementation of the EP approximation
096
      and the MCMC sampling. Here, a Laplace approximation is inappropriate because the discontinuous
097   derivatives of the Laplace likelihood at zero may cause the Hessian matrix to be undefined.

      Let us consider a regression setting yi = f (xi ) + ϵi , where ϵi ∼ N (0, σ 2 ) is a homoscedastic
125   i.i.d. random variable with constant variance. In GP models, the systematic dependency between
126   the covariates x and output vector y ∈ R is given by a latent function, f (x) : Rd → R. In a
127   truly non-parametric sense, the latent vector function at n covariates, f = [f (x1 ), . . . , f (xn )]⊤ , is
128   assumed to have a priori probability distribution. This distribution is a joint multivariate normal
129   distribution with zero mean vector and covariance matrix, K, that is,
130                                           f |X, θ ∼ N (f |0, K).                                          (1)
131
      The covariance matrix, K, is a positive semi-definite matrix that captures residual spatial association
132   with elements Ki,j = k(xi , xj ), i, j = 1, . . . , n. The function k(·, ·), chosen from a parametric
133   kernel family such as the Gaussian or the Matérn kernel, is characterized by hyperparameters denoted
134   by θ. The likelihood of the data is expressed as y|f , σ ∼ N (y|f , Σ), and the resulting posterior
135   distribution on f as where Σ = diag(σ12 , . . . , σn2 ).
136

      pursuit weights, and the resulting unimodal posterior distribution. Following that, we discuss the
138   hyperparametric settings of the GP-Huber.

      We can set the threshold b = 1.5 to achieve high efficiency at the Gaussian distribution (see appendix
217   A.2). This would make our model robust to 10% outliers (since fraction of contamination is ε = 0.1).
218   Note that, in the context of our work, "efficiency" refers to the estimator’s ability to achieve low
219   variance when the noise follows a Gaussian distribution. Specifically, a highly efficient estimator
220   can make the best use of data that is predominantly Gaussian, leading to more accurate parameter
221   estimation. The contamination fraction ε defines the model’s tolerance to deviations from the Gaussian
222   assumption, allowing it to handle a proportion of outlier points without being overly influenced by

224   between L2 and L1 norm treatment. By setting b = 0.45, we get ε = 0.45 for heavy-tailed and
225
      Gaussian error distributions, we aim to accommodate up to 45% outliers while maintaining reasonable

226
      the incorporation of projection pursuit weighting and the Huber likelihood does not introduce any

      extra hyperparameters.

343
344   Through our experiments, we aim to address the following questions:
345   (Q1) When is HuberLA (GP-Huber with Laplace’s method) preferable, and under which outlier
346   scenarios is HuberMCMC (GP-Huber with Gibbs sampling) more suitable?

451   6.3    T RANSMISSION S PECTROSCOPY
452
453   Transmission spectroscopy records the relative change in the stellar flux, which is the incident photons
454   per unit area, as a planet travels in front of the star. The sources of error, such as photon noise and
455   instrumental and astrophysical systematics, raise many potential challenges for precise planet’s
456
      atmosphere characterization. The goal is to infer the planet to star radius ratio ρradius from the
      observed flux as the planet passes in front of the star. The optical state parameters are metered via
457
      auxiliary measurements of the spectral trace such as position, width, angle, or other parameters,
458
      indicating the state of the detector and optics, which are thought to be the cause of instrumental
459   systematics. Instead of modeling the latter as a linear function of the optical state parameters, Gibson
460   et al. (2012) proposed a non-parametric model by leveraging GPs.
461
462
      The observation set obtained from HST-NICMOS includes the light curves for 18 wavelength channels
      extracted from n = 638 spectra of the planetary system HD-189733. The flux measurements
463
      contained in the vector, f = [f1 , f2 , . . . , fn ]T , are recorded at n time instants, {t1 , t2 , . . . , tn } and
464
      the optical state parameters xti collected in the matrix X ∈ Rn×d constitute the training dataset. We
465   extend the work of Gibson et al. (2012) by using the GP-Huber model to estimate the planet-to-star
466   radius ratio ρradius . As demonstrated earlier, the robustness to outliers of GP-Huber allows us to
467   utilize 517 measurements associated with four out-of-transit orbits, namely orbit numbers, {2, 3, 4, 5},
468   and 137 measurements associated with one in-transit orbit, namely orbit number 1. The latter was
469   excluded from the analysis performed by Gibson et al. (2012) as it constitutes much larger systematics
470   effects attributed to the spacecraft settling. The observed transit flux modeled in the GP framework

526   7       C ONCLUSIONS
527
528   The proposed GP-Huber model shows promise for handling a variety of heavy-tailed and Gaussian
529   error distributions with extreme outliers in both covariate and output dimensions. Notably, it intro-
530   duces no extra parameters to infer. The model’s unimodal posterior simplifies Gibbs sampling and
531   allows for an efficient Laplace approximation. From our experiments on the Neal and UCI datasets,
532   we observe that HuberMCMC and HuberLA offer superior robustness compared to RCGP and other
533   baselines. Additionally, the transmission spectroscopy experiment demonstrates their potential in
534   real-world applications.
535   In future work, we will examine GP-Huber’s performance with skewed error distributions and
536   investigate the use of high breakdown estimators for highly corrupted real-world datasets. Another
537   direction for future work involves extending the scalability of GP-Huber to handle large datasets by
538   implementing sparse inference techniques.

831   The sources of error, such as photon noise and instrumental and astrophysical systematics, raise many
832
      potential challenges for precise atmosphere characterization. Pointing drift or modifications in the
      telescope focus influence the spectrum position on the detector to a small degree during transit due
833
      to instrumental systematics. Note that instrumental systematics are nothing but what is popularly
834
      known as systematic errors in statistics, which are here attributed to the atmospheric effects on
835   the physical properties of an instrument. The optical state parameters are metered via auxiliary
836   measurements of the spectral trace such as position, width, angle, or other parameters, indicating
837   the state of the detector and optics, which are thought to be the cause of instrumental systematics.
838   Instead of modeling the latter as a linear function of the optical state parameters, Gibson et al. (2012)
839   proposed a non-parametric model by leveraging GPs.
840
      The observation set obtained from HST- NICMOS includes the light curves for 18 wavelength
841   channels extracted from n=638 spectra along with six optical state parameters, namely the position of
842   the spectral trace along the dispersion axis ,∆X, the average position of the spectral trace along the
843   cross-dispersion axis, ∆Y , the angle of the spectral trace with the x-axis, W , the average width of the
844   spectral trace, ψ s , the temperature, T , and the orbital phase, ψ H . The flux measurements contained
845   in the vector, f = [f1 , f2 , . . . , fn ]T , are recorded at n time instants, {t1 , t2 , . . . , tn }, contained in
846   the time vector, t, and the optical state parameters are given by xi = [∆Xi , ∆Yi , Wi , ψiH , Ti , ψis ]T
847   at time instant, ti , collected in the matrix X ∈ R6×N given by X = [x1 , . . . , xn ].
