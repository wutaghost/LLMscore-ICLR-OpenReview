                                                     A BSTRACT
008
009
010
                As the alignment training of large language models (LLMs) usually requires ex-
                pensive computational resources, exploring more efficient alignment methods to
011
                reduce training overhead has always been an important and compelling research
012
                challenge. Inspired by prior work on model interpolation, we present a sim-
013             ple method called E X PO (model extrapolation) to expedite the alignment of
014             LLMs with human preferences. Based on our observation that interpolating the
015             weights between existing DPO/RLHF models and their initial SFT checkpoints
016             usually produces new models with intermediate performance, we propose to
017             treat a partially-trained model M1 (corresponding to the intermediate-performing
018             model) as the interpolated result between the initial SFT checkpoint M0 and a
019             hypothetical better-aligned model M2 . Thus, we can obtain the hypothetical M2
020             by simply extrapolating the model weights along the direction from M0 to M1 ,
021
                which consequently saves the additional training overhead for M1 to reach bet-
                ter alignment performance. We validate our hypothesis through controlled ex-
022
                periments, demonstrating that E X PO can boost a DPO model trained with only
023
                20% steps to outperform the fully-trained one. Additionally, we show that E X PO
024             can also notably improve existing open-source LLMs (ranging from 1.8B to 70B
025             parameters), as evidenced by evaluations on the mainstream LLM benchmarks
026             AlpacalEval 2.0 and MT-Bench, which further highlights E X PO’s utility and po-
027             tential in enabling more efficient LLM alignment.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Large language models (LLMs) typically require additional fine-tuning to learn to follow human
033   instructions after unsupervised pre-training on massive textual corpora (OpenAI, 2022; 2023; Bai
034   et al., 2022). The current fine-tuning paradigm consists of two steps: supervised fine-tuning (SFT)
035   and human preference optimization. SFT employs a similar language modeling objective to pre-
036   training, where the model is trained to maximize the likelihood of responses on high-quality demon-
037   stration data. Human preference optimization, on the other hand, aims to adjust the model’s response
038   distribution to better align with human preferences. However, the alignment training1 process, as ex-
039
      emplified by the well-known Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF; Ouyang et al.
      2022; Schulman et al. 2017) and Direct Preference Optimization (DPO; Rafailov et al. 2023), still
040
      requires expensive computational resources (Ji et al., 2024a; Meng et al., 2024). This underscores
041
      the significance of exploring more efficient alignment methods to reduce the training overhead.
042
043   Our work draws inspiration from the literature on model interpolation (or model averaging). This
044   technique leverages mode connectivity of neural networks (Garipov et al., 2018; Entezari et al.,
045
      2022) and interpolates model weights between multiple fine-tuned models (e.g., trained with dif-
      ferent initializations or data subsets) to improve out-of-distribution generalization (Izmailov et al.,
046
      2018; Lin et al., 2024; Wortsman et al., 2022). It has been commonly adopted in recent LLMs like
047
      Gemma-2 (Gemma et al., 2024) and LLaMA-3 (Dubey et al., 2024). Given our interest in the align-
048   ment training, we applied model interpolation to existing open-source DPO/RLHF models (Tunstall
049   et al., 2023; Cai et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2023) and their initial SFT checkpoints. Interestingly, we
050   observed that model interpolation, while producing new models that can generate normal responses,
051   usually results in in-between performance compared to the original models, as shown in Figure 1.
052

072   Intrigued by the observation from model interpolation, we propose an interesting but unexplored
073   hypothesis: A partially-trained model M1 (e.g., with fewer training steps) may be treated as the
074   interpolated result between its initial SFT checkpoint M0 and a better-aligned model M2 that
075   requires more training steps to achieve. In other words, we assume that in the parameter space, M1
076   lies on the linear path from M0 to a hypothetically existing, better-aligned M2 . Thereby, we can
077
      simply obtain the hypothetical M2 by reversely extrapolating the model weights along the direction
      from M0 to M1 , as indicated by the gray arrow in Figure 1. If this hypothesis holds, we can bypass
078
      further training of M1 , and instead directly reach better alignment performance (corresponding to
079
      M2 ) with remarkably reduced training overhead.
080
081   We refer to the process of obtaining M2 as E X PO (model extrapolation). To empirically validate
082   the aforementioned hypothesis and E X PO’s effectiveness, we conduct controlled experiments using
083
      HuggingFace’s official checkpoints and training recipe. We first demonstrate that E X PO notably
      boosts the DPO models using fewer training steps (e.g., only 20%) to outperform the fully-trained
084
      one (§ 3.2), with the improvement of up to 8.4% on AlpacalEval 2.0 (Li et al., 2023). We then
085
      conduct ablation studies to identify key factors influencing E X PO’s efficacy, including training data
086   quality (§ 3.4) and training configurations such as training hyperparameters and optimizer (§ 3.5).
087   Furthermore, we extend E X PO’s application to twelve open-source LLMs (§ 4.1), ranging from
088   1.8B to 70B parameters, which have undergone varied alignment training such as offline DPO, iter-
089   ative DPO, or online RLHF. We show that E X PO consistently improves these LLMs, by up to 4.5%
090   on AlpacaEval 2.0 and 0.37 on MT-Bench (Zheng et al., 2023b), which suggests that E X PO can also
091   serve as a practical and efficient means to compensate for potential inadequate training of existing,
092   already-aligned LLMs. In summary, our work demonstrates the utility of model extrapolation in en-
093   abling more efficient LLM alignment, which may inspire follow-up studies and broader applications
094
      in future research.

096   2    M ETHODOLOGY
097
098   Our proposed E X PO method is inspired by the observation from model interpolation and builds upon
099   the aforementioned interpolation hypothesis. Formally, we denote the language model’s parameter
100   space as Θ and suppose that the alignment performance can be quantified by a continuous scalar
101
      function Ω : Θ → R, where higher Ω(θ) indicates better alignment with human preferences. We
      suppose that the model M1 (parameterized by θ1 ) has undergone moderate alignment training (e.g.,
102
      via DPO). We denote its SFT checkpoint as M0 (parameterized by θ0 ), which is used for initializing
103
      M1 and satisfies Ω(θ0 ) < Ω(θ1 ).
104

106   terized by θ2 ) that satisfies Ω(θ1 ) < Ω(θ2 ), and an interpolation coefficient γ ∈ [0, 1] such that:

      3.1     S ETUP AND E VALUATION P ROTOCOL
135   Model and Training Recipe To conduct controlled experiments, we follow the training recipe of
136   the zephyr-7b-dpo model that is officially released by HuggingFace2 . This open training recipe,
137   along with the resulting checkpoints, allows us to adjust the training data and configurations as
138   desired, and they are also widely used in controlled experiments in recent LLM alignment research
139   (Chen et al., 2024b; Ji et al., 2024b; Chen et al., 2024a).
140
      Specifically, we use the same UltraFeedback (Cui et al., 2023) dataset for model training, which is
141   a classical preference dataset that is popularly used for LLM alignment (Ivison et al., 2023; Tun-
142   stall et al., 2023; Zhu et al., 2023; Dong et al., 2024). UltraFeedback contains diverse instruction-
143   response pairs with GPT-4-annotated preference labels, split into 61K and 1K data as the training
144   and development sets, respectively. For DPO training, we use zephyr-7b-dpo’s SFT checkpoint,
145   zephyr-7b-sft, for model initialization and as the reference model. We adopt the same global
146   batch size of 128, the learning rate of 5e-7, the AdamW (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2019) optimizer, and
147   the DPO hyperparameter β of 0.01. While zephyr-7b-dpo is trained for one epoch (478 optimiza-
148   tion steps in total), in § 3.2 we will vary the training steps to investigate E X PO’s effectiveness.
149   To determine the optimal α value in E X PO, we use a combination of binary search and grid search
150   with manually tuned intervals. We select the α giving the highest expected reward on the UltraFeed-
151   back development set (1K instructions), as calculated by an open-source reward model3 .
152
153   Evaluation Protocol We resort to AlpacaEval 2.0 (Li et al., 2023) for model evaluation, which
154   is a leading benchmark that assesses LLMs’ instruction-following ability and their alignment with
155   human preferences. It contains a fixed set of 805 instructions chosen to be representative of real user
156   cases. For each instruction, it calculates the probability that a GPT-4 Turbo evaluator prefers the
157
      output of the evaluated model over the GPT-4 baseline, thus providing an affordable and replicable
      alternative to human annotation. The win rate over the GPT-4 baseline is computed as the expected
158
      preference probability. Recently, AlpacaEval 2.0 has introduced the new length-controlled (LC)

      3.3     W HY E X PO C AN W ORK ?
217
218   Given the surprising effectiveness of E X PO shown in § 3.2, we attempt to discuss why E X PO can
219   work before proceeding with more empirical studies. Under the formulation of § 2, E X PO can be
220   explained as a first-order approximation that implicitly optimizes the alignment objective Ω. By
221   applying a first-order Taylor Expansion of Ω at θ1 , we get:
222
                                 Ω(θ2 ) = Ω(θ1 + α∆θ) ≈ Ω(θ1 ) + α∇Ω(θ1 ) · ∆θ.                                      (3)
223
224   For this approximation to hold, the condition |α∆θ| needs to be small. Interestingly, we observe that
225   this condition aligns with the hyperparameter search results in § 3.2 (Appendix § B). Specifically,
226   for M10%
             2   , M20%
                      2   , M40%
                              2    , and M100%
                                            2    , the optimal α values found are 8.0, 2.5, 0.5, and 0.3,
227   respectively. This suggests that models trained with more steps, whose |∆θ| is usually larger, require
228
      smaller optimal α values. Based on this consistency, we can reasonably assume that the first-order
      approximation holds in the following discussion.
229
230   E X PO’s effectiveness indicates that Ω(θ2 ) > Ω(θ1 ), or equivalently, ∇Ω(θ1 ) · ∆θ > 0. In other
231   words, the gradient of Ω at θ1 has a positive component along the direction of ∆θ, which can
232   be intuitively illustrated by Figure 2. We thus speculate that for E X PO to work in practice, two
233   requirements need to be satisfied: First, θ1 should not be the local optimum or an overfitted point
234
      of Ω. Second, ∆θ should indicate a direction that genuinely improves alignment performance4 ,
      rather than exploiting spurious features (e.g., length bias; Park et al. 2024). We will discuss the two
235
      requirements more specifically in conjunction with the subsequent ablation studies in § 3.4 and 3.5.

420   4.3   D ISCUSSION ON M ODEL C HOICE
421
422   In the interpolation hypothesis in § 2 and in our experiments so far, M0 is an SFT model and M1 is
423   one that further undergoes alignment training. A natural question is whether E X PO’s interpolation
424   hypothesis can be extended to other model types, for example, where M0 is a pre-trained model
425   and M1 is an SFT one. However, based on our attempts with open-source models, we find that in
426
      this case, model extrapolation typically fails to improve alignment performance and may even lead
      to model collapse (e.g., the extrapolated model struggles to generate the EOS token or mistakenly
427
      generate special tokens). We speculate that there are two reasons for this phenomenon. First, the
428
      training from a pre-trained model to an SFT model typically employs a larger learning rate than
429   the subsequent alignment training. The resulting larger |∆θ| could invalidate E X PO’s underlying

437   5   R ELATED W ORK
438
439   LLM Alignment Modern large language models (LLMs) are first pre-trained on massive textual
440
      corpora with the unsupervised language modeling objective (Brown et al., 2020; Touvron et al.,
      2023b; Dubey et al., 2024), and then fine-tuned to learn to follow human instructions (OpenAI,
441
      2022; 2023; Ji et al., 2023). The current fine-tuning paradigm typically contains two steps: super-
442
      vised fine-tuning (SFT) and human preference optimization. Our work focuses on the later step,
443   which aims to adjust the model’s response distribution to better align with human preferences. In
444   this process, the model is usually trained on preference data (“A is better than B”; Zhao et al. 2023b;
445   Zheng et al. 2023a), thus learning to assign higher probabilities to human-preferred responses over
446   the disfavored ones. Common implementations for human preference optimization include Rein-
447   forcement Learning from Human Feedback (RLHF; Ouyang et al. 2022; Schulman et al. 2017),
448   Direct Preference Optimization (DPO; Rafailov et al. 2023), and many other DPO’s variants or
449   competitors (Azar et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024; Ethayarajh et al., 2024; Park et al., 2024; Meng et al.,
450   2024). Given LLMs’ gigantic parameters, the processes from pre-training to SFT and the alignment
451
      training still require expensive computational resources. Therefore, exploring more efficient align-
      ment methods to reduce training overhead has always been an important and compelling research
452
      challenge (Ji et al., 2024a). To address this challenge, we propose the E X PO method, which has
453
      demonstrated promising efficacy in expediting LLM alignment.
454
455   There is another line of work that attempts to bypass the expensive alignment training by blending
456   multiple models’ token predictions during the inference time (Liu et al., 2021; Lu et al., 2024; Liu
457
      et al., 2024), usually referred to as inference-time alignment methods. In comparison to E X PO,
      these inference-time methods often require more complex and varied implementations of model in-
458
      ference, which are not typically supported by existing high-performance LLM inference infrastruc-
459
      tures (e.g., vLLM). This inconvenience not only reduces the practical efficiency of model inference
460   but also significantly increases the cost of their hyperparameter search processes. In contrast, E X PO
461   only involves regular inference of a single model, which can be seamlessly supported by existing
462   infrastructures, thereby inheriting the merit in inference efficiency.
463
464   Model Interpolation Model interpolation (or model averaging) is a commonly used technique in
465   machine learning. It typically involves training multiple models with different random initializations
466   or data subsets and then interpolating the weights of these models to obtain a new model with
467   stronger out-of-distribution generalization (Izmailov et al., 2018; Lin et al., 2024; Wortsman et al.,
468   2022; Lin et al., 2023). This technique is based on the (linear) mode connectivity of neural networks
469   (Garipov et al., 2018; Entezari et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2020; Frankle et al., 2020). Specifically,
470   prior work found that multiple local optima in the parameter space can often be connected by low-
471
      loss (linear) paths, particularly for models with residual connection structures (He et al., 2016).
      This may explain why model interpolation can produce new, functional models when applied to
472
      LLMs (as we observed in § 1), as residual connection has become a dominant choice of architecture
473
      design in modern LLMs like LLaMA (Touvron et al., 2023a). We notice that recent LLMs have
474   widely adopted model interpolation, as exemplified by Gemma-2 (Gemma et al., 2024) and LLaMA-
475   3 (Dubey et al., 2024), possibly also for further enhancement in out-of-distribution generalization.
476
477
      Unlike interpolating between multiple fine-tuned M1 , our work begins with the attempt to interpo-
      late between models before and after alignment training (M0 and M1 ). The resulting observation
478
      in § 1 inspires the hypothesis and the proposal of the E X PO (model extrapolation) method.
479
480
481   6   C ONCLUSION
482
483   We demonstrate the efficacy of the E X PO (model extrapolation) method in enabling more efficient
484   LLM alignment with human preferences. E X PO is based on the hypothesis that, for a partially-
485   trained model M1 , we may obtain a better-aligned model M2 by simply extrapolating the model
      weights along the weight difference ∆θ between M1 and its initial SFT checkpoint M0 , thus


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      bypassing further training of M1 and directly reaching better alignment performance. We empiri-
487   cally validate this hypothesis through controlled experiments, where we show that the DPO model
488   trained with 20% steps can be boosted to outperform the fully-trained one. Furthermore, we extend
489   E X PO’s application to twelve existing, already-aligned LLMs, showing that E X PO consistently im-
490   proves their performance on the mainstream LLM benchmarks AlpacaEval 2.0 and MT-Bench. This
491   suggests that E X PO can also serve as a practical and efficient means to compensate for potential
492   inadequate alignment training of existing LLMs. Overall, our work highlights the utility of model
493   extrapolation in efficient LLM alignment, which may inspire future research in this direction.

      A      L IMITATIONS
757
758
      Theoretical Analysis Although we provide a preliminary discussion for why E X PO can work
759   (§ 3.3), a more rigorous theoretical analysis is necessary to fully understand the factors contribut-
760   ing to its effectiveness. Future work could establish more profound theoretical foundations for the
761   underlying mechanisms of E X PO (model extrapolation).
762
763   Hyperparameter Search The current E X PO adopts the simplest form of uniform extrapolation
764   (§ 2) and requires manual hyperparameter search for α. Future work could explore how to deter-
765   mine the optimal α automatically and adaptively (i.e., using different α values for different model
766   modules). For example, the information from optimizer states and parameter gradients during the
767   later stage of alignment training could be useful for this purpose.
768
769   Alignment Tax While E X PO makes notable improvements in instruction-following ability and
770
      alignment with human preferences, this seems not “free” and may instead incur an additional align-
      ment tax, a widely-observed issue in human preference optimization algorithms (Ouyang et al.,
771
      2022; Dong et al., 2024; Meng et al., 2024), which indicates the possible fluctuations or drops in
772
      downstream task performance after human preference optimization. We evaluate the models in § 3.2
773   and 4.1 on the six downstream tasks (Clark et al., 2018; Zellers et al., 2019; Hendrycks et al., 2021;
774   Lin et al., 2022; Sakaguchi et al., 2021; Cobbe et al., 2021) from the Open LLM Leaderboard8 (v1;
775   Beeching et al. 2023). We find that in most cases, E X PO amplifies the alignment tax introduced
776   by the alignment training (from M0 to M1 ). For example, for the partially-trained models in § 3.2
777   (Figure 3), the original DPO models (M1 ) show improvements over the initial SFT model (M0 ) on
778   TruthfulQA and declines on GSM8K, while applying E X PO (M2 ) leads to further improvements
779   or declines, respectively. For the existing, fully-trained LLMs in § 4.1, the amplification of the
780   alignment tax by E X PO is usually smaller as shown in Figure 4, suggesting a trade-off between the
781
      alignment training overhead (from M0 to M1 ) and the additional alignment tax brought by E X PO
      (from M1 to M2 ).
