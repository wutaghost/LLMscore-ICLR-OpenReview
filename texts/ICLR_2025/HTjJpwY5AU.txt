009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            This paper introduces a novel approach to improve camera position estimation
012            in global Structure-from-Motion (SfM) frameworks by filtering inaccurate pose
013            graph edges, representing relative translation estimates, before applying translation
014            averaging. In SfM, pose graph vertices represent cameras and edges relative
015            poses (rotation and translation) between cameras. We formulate the edge filtering
016            problem as a vertex filtering in the dual graph – a line graph where the vertices
017            stem from edges in the original graph and the edges from cameras. Exploiting
018
               such a representation, we frame the problem as a binary classification over nodes
               in the dual graph. To learn such a classification and find outlier edges, we employ
019
               a Transformer architecture-based technique. To address the challenge of memory
020
               overflow often caused by converting to a line graph, we introduce a clustering-based
021            graph processing approach, enabling the application of our method to arbitrarily
022            large pose graphs. The proposed method outperforms existing relative translation
023            filtering techniques in terms of final camera position accuracy and can be seamlessly
024            integrated with any other filters. The source code will be made public.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   The task of generating 3D models from large sets of unordered images poses a significant challenge
030   within computer vision and robotics, catering to a diverse range of applications, including crowd-
031   sourced mapping, among others. The leading approach for accomplishing 3D reconstruction is the
032   Structure-from-Motion (SfM) algorithm, tasked with simultaneously estimating camera parameters
033
      and generating a 3D point cloud (Ullman, 1979). This research domain is primarily divided into two
      categories: Incremental (Heinly et al., 2015; Schönberger & Frahm, 2016; Snavely et al., 2006; 2008;
034
      Wu, 2013) and Global methods (Cui & Tan, 2015; Zhu et al., 2018; Sweeney; Pan et al., 2024).
035
036   Incremental algorithms carefully integrate images into the 3D reconstruction, achieving accuracy
037   through repeated numerical optimizations. Although capable of yielding highly accurate results,
038   these approaches are computationally intensive due to the necessity for multiple bundle adjustment
039
      runs (Triggs et al., 2000), rendering them less effective for reconstructing large datasets. In contrast,
      global methods optimize the entire pose graph, contaminated by noise for all cameras, in a single run,
040
      thus providing a fast and scalable solution. Even though global methods are generally seen as slightly
041
      less accurate compared with incremental techniques (Cui et al., 2017), they offer promising directions
042   for rapid 3D model reconstruction. For example, recent advancements, such as GLOMAP (Pan et al.,
043   2024), have shown their potential for scalability and efficiency, achieving state-of-the-art run-time
044   and accuracy that is comparable to or surpassing COLMAP (Schönberger & Frahm, 2016). This
045   paper is dedicated to global strategies, specifically presenting an algorithm designed to enhance
046   camera position estimation. This is achieved using a Transformer architecture-based technique to
047   filter out outlier edges within the pose graph.
048   Global methods start by identifying image pairs that share a common field-of-view, utilizing image
049   retrieval techniques like the visual bag-of-words algorithm (Filliat, 2007) or NetVLAD (Arandjelovic
050   et al., 2016). Following this, relative pose estimation is conducted for these pairs through a robust
051   estimation method, for instance, RANSAC (Fischler & Bolles, 1981) or one of its state-of-the-art
052   variants (Barath et al.; 2022). With camera rotations established, the process estimates camera
053   translations, keeping the rotations fixed, and generates a 3D point cloud. The final step involves
      applying bundle adjustment to enhance the precision of the 3D model and camera parameters.

072   Translation averaging, alternatively known as synchronization, has a rich literature (Govindu, 2001;
073   Jiang et al., 2013; Moulon et al., 2013; Wilson & Snavely, 2014; Tron & Vidal, 2009; Ozyesil &
074
      Singer, 2015; Arrigoni et al., 2015b; Zhuang et al., 2018) and stands as one of the most complex
      challenges within global pipelines. It is typically approached as an optimization that seeks consistency
075
      between estimated relative translations and the unknown global camera positions. The core difficulty
076
      stems from the absence of known translation scales, making it hard to distinguish between short and
077   long translations based on angles alone. Noise significantly impacts the process, particularly affecting
078   short edges and often rendering them outliers. Furthermore, estimating robust relative translations
079   from view pairs is generally less stable than rotation estimation (Barath et al., 2022). These issues
080   make filtering outlier translation edges a crucial step to simplify the translation averaging problem.
081
      Translation filtering, in contrast, has not been as extensively explored as averaging. 1DSfM (Wilson
082
      & Snavely, 2014) introduced a theoretical method for discarding outlier edges, which, despite its solid
083   foundation, has been found to yield only marginal benefits in practice (Manam & Govindu, 2024).
084   Another recent notable effort (Manam & Govindu, 2024) shifts the focus from filtering outliers to
085   eliminating camera configurations detrimental to translation averaging, specifically, configurations
086   involving camera triplets representing quasi-linear motion.
087
      The main contribution of this paper is a learning-based pipeline designed to identify outlier edges
088
      within a pose graph of relative translations. Our approach (visualized in Fig. 1) transforms the pose
089   graph into a line graph, wherein cameras become edges and relative translations nodes. This setup
090   frames outlier detection as a binary classification task, where each node is assessed as either an inlier
091   or an outlier through a Transformer-based architecture. We introduce a graph clustering approach to
092   address the increase in memory associated with transforming the pose graph into a line graph. This
093   enables applying the proposed network to graphs of any size, facilitating scalability. Across a number
094   of large-scale and real-world datasets, the proposed pose graph filtering method leads to substantial
095   improvements in camera position estimation, compared to the baseline outlier filter, 1DSfM (Wilson
096   & Snavely, 2014). When our method is combined with the recent degeneracy filter (Manam &
097
      Govindu, 2024), it leads to the best results in all tested accuracy metrics across all datasets.

      Global Structure-from-Motion reconstructs the scene in 3D and obtains the global camera poses
102   by integrating estimates of relative poses between pairs. Predominantly, this integration is achieved
103   through subsequent rotation (Olsson & Enqvist, 2011; Moulon et al., 2013) and translation averag-
104   ing (Zhuang et al., 2018), though some studies have opted to perform averaging within SE(3) (Cui
105   & Tan, 2015). Following the determination of rotations, the method proceeds to ascertain trans-
106   lations and structural details that are, finally, jointly optimized by bundle adjustment. Several
107   open-source frameworks support global Structure-from-Motion, including Theia (Sweeney) and
      OpenMVG (Moulon et al., 2016). We have incorporated our relative translation filtering method into

      the Theia framework, keeping the rest of the pipeline unchanged. Nonetheless, our advancements are
109   versatile and not restricted to this particular pipeline.

111   Rotation averaging has a long history in computer vision. Govindu (Govindu, 2001) linearizes the
112
      problem using a quaternion representation, while Martinec and Pajdla (Martinec & Pajdla, 2007)
      simplify it by omitting certain non-linear constraints. Wilson et al. (Wilson et al., 2016) examine
113
      the conditions that make the problem tractable. Eriksson et al. (Eriksson et al., 2018) solve it via
114
      strong duality. Semidefinite programming-based (SDP) relaxation approaches (Arie-Nachimson
115   et al., 2012; Fredriksson & Olsson, 2012) provide optimality guarantees by minimizing the chordal
116   distance (Hartley et al., 2013). Dellaert et al. (Dellaert et al., 2020) sequentially lift the problem into
117   higher-dimensional rotations SO(n) to avoid local minima where standard numerical optimization
118   techniques may fail (Levenberg, 1944; Marquardt, 1963). To handle outliers in relative rotations,
119   various robust loss functions have been investigated (Hartley et al., 2011; Chatterjee & Govindu,
120   2013; 2017; Sidhartha & Govindu, 2021; Zhang et al., 2023).
121   The objective of translation averaging is to determine the absolute camera positions by leveraging
122   estimated pairwise unscaled relative translations. Govindu (Govindu, 2001) approaches this by
123   minimizing the cross-product of input and derived directions from absolute translations. Jiang et
124   al. (Jiang et al., 2013) utilize the geometric constraints of triangle formations among triplets of
125   nodes to approach the problem. Moulon et al. (Moulon et al., 2013) suggest a solution involving the
126   minimization of a relaxed problem via the L∞ norm. Wilson et al. (Wilson & Snavely, 2014) aim to
127   reduce the discrepancy between the input directions and those deduced from absolute translations.
128   Tron et al. (Tron & Vidal, 2009) focus on minimizing squared relative displacements in a distributed
129
      framework. Ozyesil et al. (Ozyesil & Singer, 2015) introduce the Least Unsquared Deviations (LUD)
      method to extend (Tron & Vidal, 2009), incorporating L1 loss for enhanced robustness, thus framing
130
      the problem within a convex program. Arrigoni et al. (Arrigoni et al., 2015b) aim at minimizing
131
      the squared error from the orthogonal projection of estimated relative translations onto the input
132   directions. Similarly, Goldstein et al. (Goldstein et al., 2016) minimize orthogonal projections through
133   ADMM, adopting L1 loss for robustness. Zhuang et al. (Zhuang et al., 2018) offer a relaxation of the
134   cost metrics in (Wilson & Snavely, 2014) by aligning estimated relative translations with observed
135   directions, naming it Bilinear Angle-based Translation Averaging (BATA). Additional methodologies
136   include leveraging two-view and three-view camera geometry to frame the problem (Hartley &
137   Zisserman, 2003; Arie-Nachimson et al., 2012; Moulon et al., 2013), determining edge scales
138   via cycles in a network prior to solving for absolute translations (Arrigoni et al., 2015a; 2016),
139   or employing point correspondence constraints (Cui et al., 2015; 2016), refining input directions
140
      iteratively (Manam & Govindu, 2022), averaging matrices from two-view geometries (Kasten et al.,
      2019a;b), and making use of the matrix structure resulting from pairwise displacements (Dong et al.,
141
      2020). While these algorithms are crucial to getting accurate camera positions, they often fail due to
142
      degeneracies and outliers in the estimated pose graph edges.
143
144   Pose graph filtering serves as a strategy for removing inaccurate relative poses, thereby eliminating
145   outliers to enhance the robustness of pose averaging. Given that translation averaging tends to
146
      be more prone to noise and outliers than rotation estimation in practice, most filtering methods
      assume the rotations to be pre-estimated and focus on improving translations. Zach et al. (Zach
147
      et al., 2010) introduce a method to exclude outlier edges based on loop consistency. The 1DSfM
148
      approach (Wilson & Snavely, 2014) involves projecting relative translation directions onto randomly
149   selected 3D directions and then evaluating discrepancies within this one-dimensional subspace. This
150   random projection process is iterated multiple times to accumulate instances of inconsistency for
151   each edge. Such accumulated inconsistency metrics are then employed to eliminate discordant edges
152   across multiple random projections. Shen et al. (Shen et al., 2016) focus on identifying and retaining
153   a subset of reliable edges to ensure strong connectivity among cameras. The more recent study by
154   Manam et al. (Manam & Govindu, 2024) highlights that the main issue often lies not with the outlier
155   edges themselves but with edges that contribute to forming skewed triangles, which can lead to
156   degenerate conditions in translation averaging. Our method focuses on filtering outlier edges and can
157
      be straightforwardly combined with any other filters, such as (Manam & Govindu, 2024).
158   Recently, learning-based approaches have emerged, primarily to solve the rotation averaging
159   problem. NeuRoRa (Purkait et al., 2020) introduces a dual-network system: one for cleaning the view
160   graph of relative rotations and another for fine-tuning, which optimizes an initialization of absolute
161   orientations derived from the cleaned graph in a single step. MSP (Yang et al., 2021) proposes
      an end-to-end framework that initializes and optimizes orientations using multiple measurements

192   Problem Statement. Let us assume that we are given a set of images I and a pose graph G = (V, E),
193
      with V ⊆ I denoting the images within the graph and E = {(vi , vj ) | vi , vj ∈ V} comprising the
      edges. A mapping T : E → SE(3) is defined such that T (vi , vj ) = (R, t) represents the relative
194
      transformation from view vi to vj , where (vi , vj ) ∈ E, R ∈ SO(3) is the relative rotation, and
195
      t ∈ R3 is the relative translation, initially set to be unit-scaled (tT t = 1). The construction of graph
196
      G, a preliminary step in both global and incremental SfM methods, involves image retrieval, feature
197
      detection and matching, and robust two-view geometry estimation (Schonberger & Frahm, 2016;
198   Barath et al., 2021). An overview of global pipelines is depicted in Fig. 2.
199
200   Following the construction of the initial pose graph G, the global SfM process is approximately the
201
      following. Initial steps include two-view geometry filtering, e.g., discarding edges with inlier counts
      below a threshold, such as 30 (i.e., the default in Theia (Sweeney)). Subsequently, rotation averaging,
202
      e.g., by using the robust method of Chatterjee et al. (Chatterjee & Govindu, 2013), determines the
203
      absolute camera orientations independently of positions. Rotation averaging generally achieves
204   higher accuracy than position averaging, with errors typically within a few degrees (Li & Ling,
205   2021). Therefore, we assume known camera orientations Rv for all v ∈ V in the rest of the paper,
206   concentrating on estimating the global camera translations tv . In global SfM, the step after rotation
207   averaging usually involves re-estimating translations based on these orientations, filtering outlier
208   edges to ensure consistency or eliminate degeneracy, e.g., by projecting onto 1D subspaces (Wilson
209   & Snavely, 2014) or identifying degenerate motions (Manam & Govindu, 2024). The subsequent
210   phases encompass translation averaging to derive global positions from the refined pose graph edges,
211   triangulating to obtain a 3D point cloud, and executing bundle adjustment to jointly optimize all
212
      parameters by minimizing the re-projection error in pixels.
213   Line Graph Representation. In this paper, we aim to learn a filter θ : E → {0, 1} that identifies an
214   edge (representing a relative translation estimate) as an inlier (1) or an outlier (0). To achieve this, we
215   utilize a Graph Neural Network (GNN). While the direct learning of an edge classifier is feasible (Kim
      et al., 2019), we observed it to yield unstable results, often leading to excessive edge removal from

216
      the pose graph and, consequently, significant loss of cameras crucial for the 3D reconstruction. To
217   mitigate this issue, we propose converting the pose graph G into a line graph L(G).
218
219   A line graph L(G) is constructed from the original graph G by associating each vertex in L(G) with
220
      an edge in G. Specifically, if G = (V, E), then in L(G) = (VL , EL ), each edge (vi , vj ) ∈ E becomes
      a vertex vij ∈ EL . Two vertices vij and vkl in L(G) are connected by an edge if and only if their
221
      corresponding edges in G share a common vertex, that is, vj = vk or vi = vl . Mathematically
222
      speaking, the edge set EL of L(G) is defined as follows:
223
224                     EL = {(vij , vkl ) | (vi = vk ∨ vj = vl ) ∧ (vi , vj ), (vk , vl ) ∈ E}.         (1)
225   This transformation allows us to reformulate edge classification in G as vertex classification in L(G),
226   facilitating the application of vertex-focused GNN architectures for outlier detection.
227
      Outlier Filtering with a Graph Neural Network. In the pose graph represented as a line graph
228
      L(G), we attribute to each vertex – which corresponds to an edge in the original graph G – the
229   estimated relative pose, consisting of a 3D rotation and unscaled translation. Note that these relative
230   rotations are recalculated from the global camera orientations acquired earlier by rotation averaging.
231   For each edge in L(G), linked to vertex v in G, we assign two features concatenated into a single
232   vector: (1) The 3D global orientation Rv , and (2) the image embedding dv ∈ Rd corresponding
233   to the input image Iv ∈ I. We observed that leveraging context from the underlying image helps
234   recognize outlier edges. We obtain embedding dv using DINOv2 (Oquab et al., 2023) off-the-shelf,
235   which yields a 384-dimensional image embedding. Consequently, the concatenated feature vector
236   assigned to each edge in L(G) is 393-dimensional. DINOv2 features capture the content of images,
237
      so neighboring images should have similar features, allowing the incorporation of such high-level
      information directly into the view graph. If two images are connected in the graph, we expect them to
238
      have overlapping views and, thus, similar DINOv2 features (Keetha et al., 2023).
239

      memory, depending on the connectivity of G. Precisely, the number of edges in the line graph |EL | is

276   property often leads to a memory explosion in a sufficiently large graph with well-connected images,
277
      preventing running the proposed classification network.
278   To overcome this issue, we employ a graph clustering technique (Chen et al., 2020) to reduce the
279   size of the graph G before its conversion to the line graph L(G). With this clustering, we can control
280   the maximum number of edges in the line graphs and, thus, the maximum storage complexity of the
281   proposed method. The approach consists of partitioning the graph G into k clusters, C1 , C2 , . . . , Ck ,
282   such that each cluster Ci contains a subset of vertices from V. This partitioning is designed to
283
      minimize the intra-cluster edge cut while maximizing the inter-cluster separation. Formally, the
      objective is to solve the optimization problem:
284
285                                                   k

286                                          min            cut(Ci , V \ Ci ),                              (5)

288
      where cut(Ci , V \ Ci ) denotes the total weight of edges removed to separate cluster Ci from the rest
289
      of the graph. The optimization finds a partitioning that balances the cluster sizes while minimizing
290   the edge cuts across all clusters. We set the edge weights to equal the number of pose inliers found
291   by RANSAC, which proved to be a good indicator of the edge quality. During inference, an edge
292   may be part of multiple subgraphs, thus receiving more than one inlier/outlier vote. To make a final
293   decision, majority voting is applied. The algorithm is further detailed in the Alg. 1 in the appendix.
294
      The resulting clusters are then treated as super-vertices in a reduced graph G ′ , with edges between
295
      super-vertices representing the connections between clusters in the original graph G. The weight
296   of an edge between two super-vertices in G ′ is determined by the sum of the weights of the edges
297   between vertices in the corresponding clusters in G. The reduced graph G ′ is then converted to a line
298   graph L(G ′ ), significantly reducing the memory requirements compared to directly converting the
299   original graph G to its line graph.
300
      This clustering-based approach mitigates the memory explosion problem and preserves the essential
301
      topological and connectivity information from the original graph, enabling the effective application
302   of the proposed classification network on large and densely connected graphs.

308   using the Theia library (Sweeney), using its default settings except for the deactivation of relative
309
      translation re-estimation, which was observed to impact final accuracy negatively. Relative pose

315   calculating the error metrics, the reconstructions are aligned to reference data robustly by RANSAC.

      Datasets. Evaluations were conducted on the 1DSfM (Wilson & Snavely, 2014), Photo-
420   Tourism (Snavely et al., 2006), and ScanNet (Dai et al., 2017) datasets. The 1DSfM dataset,
421   encompassing 15 landmark scenes with internet-sourced photos, includes two-view matches, epipolar
422   geometries, and a reference incremental SfM reconstruction (via Bundler (Snavely et al., 2006; 2008))
423   for error analysis. Given that Bundler is nowadays considered outdated, we generated new reference
424   reconstructions by COLMAP (Schonberger & Frahm, 2016). To ensure that the reconstruction is
425   approximately metric, we robustly align it with the provided Bundler reconstruction. The scenes used
426   for the PhotoTourism dataset are based on the CVPR Image Matching Challenge 2020. These scenes
427   are non-metric, thus, the reported position errors are not in meters. The ScanNet dataset (Dai et al.,
428
      2017) consists of 1613 monocular sequences with ground truth poses and metric depth. To evaluate
      relative translation filters, we utilize the same six sequences as what Zhu et al. (Zhu et al., 2022) use.
429
430   Training. We trained the proposed method on scene Piazza San Marco from the PhotoTourism dataset,
431   comprising 249 images and 10295 view graph edges in total. We use the COLMAP reconstruction to
      provide target inlier/outlier labels. We label a relative translation outlier if its error is higher than 20◦ ,


                                                               8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      leading to 4418 outlier edges and to good performance in our experiments. As the validation set, we
433   split scene Taj Mahal. We exclude these scenes from the main experiments. The model is trained for
434   300 epochs, with 0.009 learning rate and Binary Cross Entropy Loss, 5 ∗ 10−4 weight decay, and
435   Adam optimizer (Kingma & Ba, 2014) on 3 subgraphs, shuffled during training. We use this model
436   in all experiments and on all datasets, demonstrating the generalization capabilities of the proposed
437   method. Specifically, we train it on a single outdoor scene and test it on three large-scale datasets
438   with significant domain gaps (indoor/outdoor), showing that it performs accurately across different
439   noise and outlier distributions. For a fair comparison, we performed hyper-parameter tuning for the
440   baselines on the same training data.
441   Metrics include mean position errors, recall rates at 1, 5 and 10. We report the mean error as it clearly
442   shows large failures in the reconstruction. We include recall as a robust metric to show the accuracy
443   of the well-reconstructed cameras. Other metrics are reported in the supplementary material. To
444   make the recall rates fair across methods, we take the reconstructed cameras after applying the Oracle
445   filter and calculate the accuracy on these cameras. For each camera missing from the reconstruction
446   with a particular filter, we consider the error to be infinity for recall calculation. On 1DSfM and
447
      ScanNet, the errors are in meters, as the reference is a metric reconstruction. There, we report the
      recall thresholded at 10 and 1 meters. On PhotoTourism, the errors have no units. Thus, we report the

526
527
      This paper presents a novel filtering method that enhances camera position estimation in global SfM,
      demonstrating improved accuracy across a diverse set of datasets. By jointly addressing outliers with
528
      the proposed method and degenerate configurations by (Manam & Govindu, 2024), our approach
529
      ensures superior reconstruction quality while only being marginally slower than other alternatives.
530   The proposed method without (Manam & Govindu, 2024) is superior, in terms of accuracy, to the
531   standard outlier filtering techniques, e.g., 1DSfM (Wilson & Snavely, 2014). These results highlight
532   the critical role of advanced filtering in Structure-from-Motion. The source code will be made public.

895   D        C OMPLEXITY A NALYSIS
896
897
898   The computational complexity of the method is described below and in algorithms 1 2 3 4.
899   1. Graph clustering. The graph clustering 1 has a time complexity of O(|E|). The function for
900   computing the k-way partition is implemented in COLMAP and utilizes the METIS library (Karypis
901   & Kumar, 1997). It has a complexity of O(|E|) as discussed in (Karypis & Kumar, 1997). Thus, the
902   complexity of the graph clustering is linear in the number of edges.
903   2. Convert to line graph. The complexity of line graph conversion 2 is dominated by the nested
904   loops for adding the edges to the line graph, resulting in a total complexity of O(|V| ∗ degavg (V))2 ,
905   where degavg (V) is the average node degree. In the 1DSfM dataset, the average node degree is 45.
906   The function is implemented in the NetworkX library (Hagberg et al., 2004–2024) and can be easily
907   parallelized to convert each edge from the original graph to a node in the line graph.
908
      3. Create edge attributes. Assigning relative rotations and relative translations to every edge in the
909
      line graph 3 scales linearly with the number of edges in the line graph, implying complexity O(|E|).
910
911   4. Create node attributes. To create the node attribute 4, we stack the relative rotations and positions
912   in the linegraph L(G) into a tensor. The output is a node attribute tensor, created in O(|VL |) = O(|E|)
913   time.
914   5. Graph Neural Network Inference. We run the GNN to classify all nodes in the line graph.
915   Each layer computes the scaled dot-product attention for all nodes, where each node calculates the
916   attention from all its neighbors. Therefore, the node update has a time complexity dominated by

1006   To demonstrate the importance of global rotations, relative rotations, and relative translations as
1007   network inputs, we trained the network without each of these components individually in Ours w/o
1008   rotations, Ours w/o rel. rotations and Ours w/o rel. translations. The results are presented in table 10.
1009   Our method, using all inputs, halves the mean position error compared to Ours w/o rel. rotations
1010
       and reconstructs more cameras with higher accuracy than Ours w/o rel. translations. We framed
       the proposed method as a filtering technique for translation averaging under the assumption that
1011
       rotation averaging is generally a less complicated problem. While incorporating global rotations
1012
       into the filtering process yields the best results, we observe improvements even in the absence of
1013   rotations - Ours w/o rotations achieves good mean position error and recall, suggesting that the
1014   filter can be applied prior to rotation averaging. We would like to highlight that a poor-quality view
1015   graph negatively impacting the quality of the reconstruction is a general limitation of global SfM, not
1016   specific to translation filtering. As demonstrated in the experiments, the proposed method actually
1017   reduces the sensitivity of SfM to noisy pose graphs by removing outlier edges.
