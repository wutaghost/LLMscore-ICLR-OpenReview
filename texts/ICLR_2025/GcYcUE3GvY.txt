009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Referring Video Object Segmentation (RVOS) aims to segment the object referred
012            to by the query sentence in the video. Most existing methods require end-to-end
013            training with dense mask annotations, which could be computation-consuming
014            and less scalable. In this work, we rethink the RVOS problem and aim to inves-
015            tigate the key to this task. Based on existing foundation segmentation models,
016
               we decompose the RVOS task into referring, video, and segmentation factors,
               and propose a Temporal Prompt Generation and Selection (Tenet) framework to
017
               address the referring and video factors while leaving the segmentation problem
018
               to foundation models. To efficiently adapt image-based foundation segmentation
019            models to referring video object segmentation, we leverage off-the-shelf object
020            detectors and trackers to produce temporal prompts associated with the referring
021            sentence. While high-quality temporal prompts could be produced, they can not be
022            easily identified from confidence scores. To tackle this issue, we propose Prompt
023            Preference Learning to evaluate the quality of the produced temporal prompts. By
024            taking such prompts to instruct image-based foundation segmentation models, we
025            would be able to produce high-quality masks for the referred object, enabling ef-
026            ficient model adaptation to referring video object segmentation. Experiments on
027
               RVOS benchmarks demonstrate the effectiveness of the Tenet framework.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Segmentation, as a fundamental task in computer vision, aims to partition images into distinct visual
032   segments. With segmentation, individual image pixels would be categorized into specific regions
033   or objects of interest, making it particularly essential for visual understanding in real-world appli-
034   cations, such as autonomous driving (Zendel et al., 2022), medical imaging (Shin et al., 2023), and
035   robotics (Goyal et al., 2022). In traditional semantic segmentation (Long et al., 2015; Cheng et al.,
036   2021), models are generally trained to classify objects within a limited set of pre-defined categories
037   (e.g., bear, gold fish, zebra, etc.). While promising results have been presented, these methods could
038   not be easily applied in realistic scenarios, where the target objects are usually specified by free-form
039
      phrases or sentences (e.g., “zebra eating grass with a goose in front of it”) rather than the category
      names alone. To advance segmentation to handle such natural language descriptions, referring im-
040
      age segmentation (RIS) (Hu et al., 2016; Yang et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023b;a) emerges to learn and
041
      understand complex text queries while associating them with the input images to produce segmen-
042   tation masks for the target objects. Recent RIS works (Yang et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2023b;a) focus
043   on designing Transformer-based or cross-attention models and employ vision-language learning to
044   fuse the latent features from both modalities. Despite these advancements, when the visual input is
045   a sequence of video frames rather than a single image, the time-varying object positions and appear-
046   ance could result in inconsistent output masks from frame to frame, implying the inherent deficiency
047   of image-based segmentation approaches.
048   Referring Video Object Segmentation (RVOS), in response to this, aims to segment the object re-
049   ferred to by a text query throughout the entire video. In contrast to RIS, RVOS is particularly faced
050   with dynamic visual challenges, such as position and size variation, object occlusion or exit, pose
051   deformation, and scene variation. Moreover, the referring sentences may contain long-term motions
052   or actions (e.g., “a gold fish on the left swimming towards the top right”), which could not be eas-
053   ily recognized from a single frame. To address such a challenging task, a number of works (Seo
      et al., 2020; Wu et al., 2022; 2023a; Miao et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2024; Wu et al.,

      Recently, several foundation segmentation models (Kirillov et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023b; Zou
085   et al., 2024) have been presented. Among them, SAM (Kirillov et al., 2023) is the most prominent
086   one due to its overwhelming generalizability on various datasets. By employing large-scale model
087   architectures and leveraging numerous image data for training, SAM can produce high-quality object
088   masks according to visual prompts such as points or boxes, setting superior benchmarks for segmen-
089   tation tasks. However, as SAM is trained solely with images and their associated masks, it could
090   not properly handle natural language descriptions and video data in RVOS. Even though it is pos-
091   sible to incorporate additional grounding models (e.g., Liu et al. (2024)) to generate text-associated
092   box prompts and tracking models (e.g., Cheng et al. (2023c)) to capture object movements across
093
      video frames, such naive combinations of off-the-shelf models has shown to be suboptimal (Li et al.,
      2023c), as they are individually trained for different tasks. This therefore raises a critical question:
094
      “How to effectively and efficiently exploit foundation segmentation models for RVOS?”
095
096   In this paper, we rethink the RVOS problem and aim to investigate the key to this challenging
097   task. Based on the impressive results presented by foundation segmentation models, we decompose
098   the RVOS task into the following three factors: referring, video, and segmentation, and focus on
099
      addressing the referring and video factors while leaving the segmentation problem to foundation
      models. To achieve this goal, we propose a Temporal Prompt Generation and Selection (Tenet)
100
      framework to efficiently adapt image-based foundation segmentation models to refer and segment
101
      video objects, as shown in Figure 1. Specifically, to generate visual prompts associated with the
102   referring sentence, we leverage off-the-shelf object detectors and trackers to produce the reference
103   proposal and candidate tracks. On the one hand, we perform object detection frame-by-frame to
104   obtain box proposals indicating object positions, and the most confident (Top-1) proposals at each
105   frame are together considered as reference proposals. On the other hand, to enhance the tempo-
106   ral consistency across frames, we also apply object tracking to Top-K proposals to form multiple
107   candidate tracks. Based on our empirical analysis, a portion of these candidate tracks is shown to
      be superior to the reference proposal. However, such high-quality candidate tracks could NOT be

      2     R ELATED W ORKS
132   2.1   R EFERRING I MAGE /V IDEO S EGMENTATION
133
      Referring image segmentation (RIS) (Xu et al., 2023; Yang et al., 2022; Yu et al., 2023; Liu et al.,
134
      2023b) learns to segment the corresponding object in an image given a free-form text query. For
135
      example, PolyFormer (Liu et al., 2023b) re-formulates the RIS problem as a sequential polygon
136   generation and then converts it to a segmentation mask. Also, PolyFormer further performs zero-
137   shot transfer on the RVOS task to show its generalization ability for the video domain. However, the
138   challenging issues for RVOS such as position and size variation, pose deformation, object occlusion,
139   etc., may limit the performance of RIS methods.
140
      Referring Video Object Segmentation (RVOS) (Wu et al., 2022; Han et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023a;
141
      Miao et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2023b) strives to segment the
142   object described by a free-form sentence query across the entire video duration. Recently, Refer-
143   Former (Wu et al., 2022) views language as queries to pay attention to the referred object by adopt-
144   ing an encoder-decoder style in the transformer. However, this work only supports offline training
145   and inference, limiting its usage in real-world scenarios. More recently, OnlineRefer (Wu et al.,
146   2023a) further proposes an online RVOS setting to deal with the issues about offline limits, which
147   makes it more possible to adapt to real-world scenarios. Nevertheless, most existing methods re-
148   quire end-to-end training for vision-language models, which could be computationally expensive
149   and time-consuming. Moreover, the requirement of dense mask annotations for training impedes
150
      the scalability of those approaches. Instead, we propose to exploit foundation segmentation models
      without text- and temporal-aware prompting, which is trained without mask annotations and sup-
151
      ports online settings. Very recently, several methods (Zhu et al., 2023; Lai et al., 2024; Yan et al.,
152
      2024a; Bai et al., 2024) are proposed to leverage the knowledge learned in large language models to
153   address RVOS. Nevertheless, the results of these LLM-based methods are still inferior to traditional
154   ones.
155
156   2.2   F OUNDATION S EGMENTATION M ODELS
157   In recent years, foundation vision models have gained massive attention given their remarkable gen-
158   eralization capabilities on various downstream tasks. More recently, SAM (Kirillov et al., 2023) has
159   introduced a foundation model specifically tailored for segmentation tasks. SAM allows specific
160   position prompts (e.g., points, boxes, etc.) to demonstrate the zero-shot ability on the open vocab-
161   ulary segmentation tasks with novel image distributions. Several works have studied the versatility
      of SAM, including remote sensing images (Chen et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024), medical image


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      analysis (Ma et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023c; Cheng et al., 2023b), and adaptation
163   to video-based tracking task (Cheng et al., 2023c; Yang et al., 2023; Rajič et al., 2023), etc.
164
165   In addition to SAM, SegGPT (Wang et al., 2023b) and SEEM (Zou et al., 2024) have also emerged
166
      as generalized foundation segmentation models, showcasing comparable concepts. SegGPT ex-
      ploits the concept of an in-context learning scheme to treat the classic segmentation problems as an
167
      in-context coloring problem. With this design, SegGPT is able to focus on more contextual informa-
168
      tion when training. On the other hand, SEEM extends the versatility of a single segmentation model
169   by broadening the range of tasks. Similar to SAM, SEEM also supports various prompts including
170   points, boxes, masks, etc. Specifically, SEEM proposes to align visual-semantic space to accommo-
171   date flexible multi-prompt input. However, both SegGPT and SEEM are not directly feasible for our
172   RVOS task due to no specific adaptation to the video domain or enhancement of tracking ability.
173
      For adaptation to tracking tasks with SAM, SAM-PT (Rajič et al., 2023) designs a point-based
174
      prompt enhancement for the original SAM point prompt to support classic video object segmen-
175   tation tasks, while neglecting the importance of text prompt for advanced referring video object
176   segmentation. Another example SAM-Track (Cheng et al., 2023c) attempts to utilize SAM for seg-
177   mentation and detection of objects while the DeAOT (Yang & Yang, 2022) module captures the
178   motion across frames for tracking the objects. On the other hand, SAM 2 (Ravi et al., 2024) intro-
179   duces a memory attention mechanism on SAM to produce masklets for videos. Though it is possible
180   to combine text-grounding detection models (e.g., Grounding DINO (Liu et al., 2024)) with SAM-
181   Track to tackle RVOS, RefSAM (Li et al., 2023c) has studied the possible concerns and indicates
182   the unsatisfactory performance compared with current SOTAs in RVOS tasks. Different from the
183
      above, we propose temporal-aware prompting with foundation segmentation models (e.g., SAM) to
      tackle RVOS problems.

188   3.1   P ROBLEM D EFINITION AND M ETHOD OVERVIEW
189
190   Problem Definition. For the sake of completeness, we first define the problem setting and no-
191
      tations used in this paper. In Referring Video Object Segmentation (RVOS), we assume that the
      training data contain a set of videos, where each video V = {It }Tt=1 is a sequence of T frames and
192
      the target object is associated with a referring sentence S. The goal of RVOS is to produce seg-
193
      mentation masks M = {Mt }Tt=1 for the referred object from the video V and referring sentence S.
194   Since our goal is to prompt foundation segmentation models to achieve RVOS instead of training an
195   end-to-end segmentation network as previous works (Wu et al., 2022; 2023a; Miao et al., 2023) did,
196   we assume that we only have access to box-level annotations GT = {B̂t }Tt=1 corresponding to the
197
      referred object as the ground truth, where each bounding box B̂t = (x̂t , ŷt , ĥt , ŵt ) is represented
198   by the coordinate of the center point and the height and width. Such box-level annotations could be
199   considered as a type of weak supervision for segmentation tasks (Khoreva et al., 2017).
200
201   Method Overview. With the impressive performance of foundation segmentation models such as
202   SAM (Kirillov et al., 2023), we decompose the Referring Video Object Segmentation (RVOS) task
203   into three core components: referring, video, and segmentation. We specifically address the chal-
204   lenges of the referring and video aspects, leveraging foundation models to handle segmentation.
205   To this end, we propose a Temporal Prompt Generation and Selection (Tenet) framework to effi-
206   ciently adapt image-based foundation segmentation models to referring video object segmentation.
207
      Specifically, our approach begins by utilizing off-the-shelf detectors and trackers to generate visual
      prompts corresponding to the referred object. We perform object detection frame-by-frame to obtain
208
      box proposals indicating object positions, and the most confident (Top-1) proposals at each frame
209
      are together considered as the reference proposal. On the other hand, to enhance the temporal con-
210   sistency across frames, we also form multiple candidate tracks by applying object tracking to Top-K
211   proposals. To identify high-quality candidate tracks, we further propose Prompt Preference Learn-
212   ing, which employs a Transformer-based classifier to compare the quality of each candidate track
213   with the reference proposal. By selecting the most preferred visual prompt to instruct image-based
214   foundation segmentation models, we would be able to produce high-quality masks for the referred
215   object, enabling efficient model adaptation to referring video object segmentation. In the following
      subsections, we first introduce the generation process of temporal prompts and provide empirical

      analysis in Section 3.2, and then detail the proposed Prompt Preference Learning to select temporal

219   3.2   T EMPORAL P ROMPT G ENERATION AND A NALYSIS
220
221
      Based on foundation segmentation models, we decompose the RVOS task into the following three
      factors: referring, video, and segmentation. To focus on the referring and video factors, we first
222
      investigate how to generate high-quality temporal prompts to benefit RVOS.
223
224
      Temporal Prompt Generation. To obtain object positions, we consider Grounding DINO (Liu
225
      et al., 2024) as our detector to produce box proposals from the input video V and the referring sen-
226
      tence S. Specifically, given the box annotations GT = {B̂t }Tt=1 of the RVOS training data, we
227
      first finetune Grounding-DINO with the common regression loss and generalized IoU loss. Since
228   there is typically only one target object in referring segmentation tasks, we simply select the out-
229   put proposal with the highest confidence score at each frame to compute the loss instead of using
230   the Hungarian algorithm (Carion et al., 2020) for box matching. We then use the pretrained and
231   finetuned Grounding DINO for producing the reference proposal and candidate tracks, as described
232   below:
233
234   • Reference Proposal: With the finetuned Grounding DINO, intuitively, we can simply take the
235
        most confident (Top-1) proposal at each frame t.
236   • Candidate Tracks: The above Top-1 proposals could be sensitive to prediction error and also
237     inconsistent across frames. To achieve better temporal consistency, we consider the Top-K pro-
238     posals and take an off-the-shelf motion-based tracker, OC-SORT (Cao et al., 2023), to perform
239
        object tracking. Here, the Top-K proposals are derived from the pretrained Grounding DINO
        since the finetuned one would lose generalizability and diversity and result in K repetitive boxes.
240
        As a result, we take the Top-K proposals from the pretrained Grounding DINO plus the Top-1
241
        proposal from the finetuned one to produce candidate tracks from OC-SORT. Note that for the
242     missed frames in each track, we use the Top-1 boxes to fill those untracked frames.
243
244   Temporal Prompt Analysis. For quantitative and quantitative analysis, we consider the afore-
245   mentioned reference proposal, candidate tracks, and several of their variants and baselines. By
246   taking them as visual prompts to SAM (Kirillov et al., 2023), we present the quantitative results in
247   Table 1 and the associated visualization in Figure 2. Here, we use Refer-Youtube-VOS (Seo et al.,
248   2020) as the training dataset, and since its validation set does not provide ground truth boxes, we use
249   the Refer-DAVIS17 (Khoreva et al., 2018) dataset for evaluation instead. For evaluation metrics, we
250   consider mIoU for the box proposals, and we adopt the commonly used region similarity J (average
251
      IoU), contour accuracy F (average boundary similarity), and their mean J &F for the segmentation
      masks.
252

411   4.1      DATASETS AND I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS
412
413   Datasets. We conduct experiments on RVOS benchmark datasets: Refer-Youtube-VOS (Seo et al.,
414   2020) and Refer-DAVIS17 (Khoreva et al., 2018). Refer-Youtube-VOS is a large-scale dataset for
415   RVOS, with 3, 975 videos, 7, 451 objects, and 27, 899 expressions. Refer-DAVIS17 is augmented
416
      from the popular video object segmentation dataset, DAVIS17 (Caelles et al., 2018). It contains 90
      videos (60 for training and 30 for testing) with more than 1, 500 expressions. Since the annotations
417
      of the Ref-Youtube-VOS validation set are not publicly released, we evaluate the results on the
418
      official server. As for Ref-DAVIS17 , we use the official code for evaluation.
419
420   Implementation Details. For the image encoder, we use the CLIP image encoder pretrained
421   from Guo et al. (2024) followed by a two-layer MLP. As for the text encoder, we use the pretrained
422   CLIP text encoder with a two-layer MLP. As for the Transformer, we use a one-layer Transformer
423   encoder layer. We only train the MLP’s and Transformer’s parameters. We use the learning rate of
424
      0.0001 and train for 50 epochs with the Adam optimizer. All models are implemented in PyTorch
      and trained on NVIDIA H100 GPUs.

530   5     C ONCLUSION
531
      In this paper, we rethink the RVOS problem and decompose the RVOS task into the following three
532
      factors: referring, video, and segmentation, and propose the Tenet framework to address the referring
533
      and video factors while leave the segmentation problem to foundation models. To efficiently adapt
534   image-based foundation segmentation models to referring video object segmentation, we leverage
535   off-the-shelf object detectors and trackers to produce the reference proposal and candidate tracks,
536   which would serve as temporal prompts to instruct foundation segmentation models to produce
537   object masks. With the proposed Prompt Preference Learning, we would be able to identify temporal
538   prompts sutiable for prompting SAM, enabling efficient model adaptation to referring video object
539   segmentation. Experiments on standard RVOS benchmarks demonstrate the effectiveness of the
      proposed Tenet framework.
