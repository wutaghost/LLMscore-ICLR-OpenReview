011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            In this paper, we highlight a critical yet often overlooked factor in most 3D hu-
014            man tasks, namely modeling humans with complex garments. It is known that
015            the parameterized formulation of SMPL is able to fit human skin; while com-
016
               plex garments, e.g., hand-held objects and loose-fitting garments, are difficult to
               get modeled within the unified framework, since their movements are usually de-
017
               coupled with the human body. To enhance the capability of SMPL skeleton in
018
               response to this situation, we propose a modular growth strategy that enables the
019            joint tree of the skeleton to expand adaptively. Specifically, our method, called
020            ToMiE, consists of parent joints localization and external joints optimization. For
021            parent joints localization, we employ a gradient-based approach guided by both
022            LBS blending weights and motion kernels. Once the external joints are obtained,
023            we proceed to optimize their transformations in SE(3) across different frames,
024            enabling rendering and explicit animation. ToMiE manages to outperform other
025            methods across various cases with garments, not only in rendering quality but
026            also by offering free animation of grown joints, thereby enhancing the expressive
027
               ability of SMPL skeleton for a broader range of applications.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   3D human reconstruction endeavors to model high-fidelity digital avatars based on real-world char-
033
      acters for virtual rendering and animating, which has been of long-term research value in areas
      such as gaming, virtual reality (VR), and beyond. Traditional methods, such as SMPL (Loper et al.,
034
      2015), achieve human body parameterization by performing principal component analysis (PCA) on
035
      large sets of 3D scanned meshes, allowing for the fitting of a specified identity. Recent neural ren-
036   dering techniques have enabled implicit digital human modeling guided by Linear Blend Skinning
037   (LBS) and SMPL skeleton, realizing lifelike rendering and animating from video inputs.
038
039
      The neural-based 3D human rendering has been empowered by the cutting-edge technique, 3D
      Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) (Kerbl et al., 2023), for its real-time and high-quality novel view syn-
040
      thesis performance. By representing 3D gaussians under the canonical T-pose and utilizing the
041
      pre-extracted SMPL skeleton in the observation space, 3D human rendering results can be obtained
042   from novel views in any frame. This stream of approaches proves high quality in rendering 3D hu-
043   mans that conform to the SMPL paradigm (e.g., avatars in tight-fitting clothing). However, we raise
044   concerns regarding its ability to handle human modeling involving daily garments, such as skirts or
045   hand-held objects.
046
      In Fig. 1, we show two cases to illustrate the limitations of current 3D human methods in modeling
047
      daily garments. On the one hand, characters shot in-the-wild are dressed in garments with high
048   complexity in dynamics, rather than the tight-fitting garments configured under strict experimental
049   conditions. These loose-fitting garments break the existing methods’ paradigm by assuming that
050   garments should be bounded to the motion warping of SMPL skeleton in the same way as the human
051   body is. On the other hand, the movements of hand-held objects, e.g., mobile phones, are highly
052   decoupled from the human body and thus cannot be represented by the current SMPL. Rooted in the
053   aforementioned scenarios, SMPL is observed to suffer from appearance ambiguity when attempting
      to fit such 3D human models.

      To this end, we break through the limitations of modeling 3D human gaussians with complex gar-
071   ments by maintaining an extended joint tree from SMPL skeleton. Although existing SMPL model
072   has the potential capability to manually customize additional skeleton information, time-consuming
073   and case-by-case adjustments are necessary in this case. To overcome this issue, we extend the
074   SMPL skeleton with additional joints for each individual case adaptively. The growth is performed
075   in an explicit and modular manner, and enables the fitting of complex garments, offering high-
076   quality rendering of more complex cases compared to other methods (e.g., novel view synthesis
077   results in Fig. 1).
078   The main challenge of extending the SMPL skeleton is to determine where and how to grow ad-
079   ditional joints. To avoid the unnecessary memory consumption and potential overfitting caused by
080   an arbitrary growth, we first determine which of the joints are supposed to serve as parent joints by
081   a localization strategy. We have empirically observed that parent joints requiring growth wittness
082   larger backpropagation gradients in their associated gaussians due to underfitting. However, deter-
083   mining the association of gaussians with different joints is non-trivial, as the SMPL’s LBS blending
084   weights may not work with the human with garments. To more precisely define such association,
085
      we introduce the concept of Motion Kernels based on rigid body priors and combine them with LBS
      weights, resulting in more accurate gradient-based localization. After growth, we adaptively main-
086
      tain an extended modular joint tree and update the extra joints by optimizing two MLP decoders for
087
      joints positions and rotations. The proposed method, termed as ToMiE, allows for explicit animation
088   of complex garments represented by external joints.
089
090   By experiments on complex cases with garments of the DNA-Rendering dataset (Cheng et al., 2023),
091
      ToMiE exhibits state-of-the-art rendering quality while maintaining the animatability that is signifi-
      cant for downstream productions. To summarize, our contributions are three-fold as follows:
092
093         1) ToMiE, a method for creating an enhanced SMPL joint tree via a modular growth strategy,
094            which is able to decouple complex garments from the human body, thereby achieving state-
095            of-the-art results in both rendering and explicit animation on target cases;
096         2) a hybrid assignment strategy for gaussians utilizing LBS weights and Motion Kernels, com-
097
               bined with gradient-driven parent joints localization, to guide the growth of external joints;
            3) a joints optimization approach fitting local rotations across different frames while sharing
098
               joints positions.

103   2.1   SMPL- BASED H UMAN M ESH AVATARS
104
105   Most of the recent success in digital human modeling can be attributed to the contributions of the
106   SMPL (Loper et al., 2015) series, which parameterizes the human body as individual shape compo-
107   nents and motion-related human poses through 3D mesh scanning and PCA. The pose blend shapes
      in SMPL describe human body deformations as a linear weighted blending of different joint poses,


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      significantly improving the efficiency of editing and animating digital humans. Furthermore, it has
109   been widely adopted for human body animation, thanks to the methods (Dong et al., 2021; Shuai
110   et al., 2022) of estimating SMPL parameters from 2D inputs. Despite their wide range of applica-
111   tions, SMPL and its family still suffer from inherent limitations. Since the originally scanned 3D
112   meshes are skin-tight, according to which the pose blend shapes are learned, the model is unable to
113   handle significantly outlying meshes, such as human with garments like skirts and hand-held objects.
114
115   2.2   N EURAL R EPRESENTATION FOR 3D H UMAN
116
117   Methods based on neural representations, such as NeRF (Mildenhall et al., 2020) and 3DGS (Kerbl
118   et al., 2023), have also been playing an important part in digital human reconstruction for their high-
119   quality rendering capabilities. Early NeRF-based methods (Weng et al., 2022; Peng et al., 2021b;
120   Kwon et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2024; Goel et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2021a; 2023; 2021b; Gafni et al.,
121
      2021; Gao et al., 2023; Geng et al., 2023) aim to reconstruct human avatars by inputting monocular
      or multi-view synchronized videos. Wang et al. (2022) enforce smooth priors based on neural Signed
122
      Distance Function (SDF) to obtain more accurate human geometry. Recent breakthroughs (Li et al.,
123
      2024b; Liu et al., 2023; Zielonka et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2024b; Qian et al., 2024; Lei
124   et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2024c; Pang et al., 2024; Jung et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2024a; Li et al., 2024a;
125   Liu et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2024a; Kocabas et al., 2024; Moreau et al., 2024; Jena et al., 2023;
126   Zheng et al., 2024b) rely on 3DGS, enabling faster and more accurate rendering. All these methods
127   register the T-pose in a canonical space and use LBS weights to guide the rigid transformations.
128   The animation of human body can be achieved by warping points in the canonical space into the
129   observation space that is controlled by per-frame human poses.
130
131   2.3   G ARMENTS R ENDERING AND E DITING IN D IGITAL H UMAN
132
133   We revisit the methods that consider implicitly improving garment topology. Animatable
134   NeRF (Peng et al., 2021a) defines a per-frame latent code to capture appearance variations across
135   each frame. Lei et al. (2024); Guo et al. (2024) additionally register global latent bones to com-
136   pensate for the limitations in garments rendering. They fail to explicitly decouple the garments
137
      from human body, making precise control infeasible. Another stream (Chen et al., 2024; Hu et al.,
      2024c) leverages a human poses sequence as contexts to resolve appearance ambiguities. But cor-
138
      respondingly, they require a sequence of human poses for animation, adding to the challenges of
139
      editing. Moreover, these methods struggle to fit the object-level pose independent of the human
140   poses sequence, such as hand-held items. We also note that some works (Hu, 2024; Men et al.,
141   2024) introduce diffusion-based generative methods to enhance the realism of garment rendering,
142   but these methods are restricted by the traditional SMPL and overlook the editing of complex gar-
143   ments. Our approach enables explicit decoupling of garments and the human body by extending the
144   SMPL joint tree, allowing for high-quality rendering and explicit animating of garments, including
145   hand-held objects.

      4     M ETHODS
194

      Figure 2 illustrates ToMiE’s modular joint growth and gaussian training strategy. Our goal is to
196   extend the SMPL skeleton to handle complex human garments. However, the abuse of growth can
197   lead to unnecessary computational and memory overheads. For an efficient adaptive growth, we first
198   propose a localization strategy of parent joints to ensure that only necessary joints are grown. Fur-
199   thermore, we explicitly define the hand-held joints in the SE(3) space and optimize them end-to-end
200   through an MLP, ensuring alignment with the original skeleton of SMPL. The extended skeleton can
201   thus support rendering and explicit animation. To address the limitation of LBS in guiding gaussian
202   attributes apart from the positions, we further fine-tune the rotation and scale during training with a
203   deformation field to achieve better non-rigid warping. Next, we will elaborate on these modules and
204
      the training strategy in detail.

317   tion and set the rotation to the identity rotation. The entire parent joint localization and child joint
318   initialization process is guided by the gradients, effectively preventing unnecessary overgrowth and
319   ensuring a dense distribution of the extra joints.

342   4.4   I NFERENCE P ROCESS AND T RAINING S TRATEGY
343
344   In this subsection, we explain how our modular growth method is integrated with the training process
345   of human gaussians.
346   First, we initialize the canonical gaussians with standard SMPL vertices. At the beginning of train-
347   ing, we set up a number of warm-up iterations during which no joint growth occurs, and the gaussian
348   fitting is performed following the traditional human gaussian methods. This prevents underfitting
349   due to insufficient training, which could further affect the joint localization in Sec. 4.2.
350
      During the warm-up iterations, canonical human gaussians will be first warped to the observation
351   space according to the LBS weight in Eq. (5). To compensate for the inability of the LBS model to
352   represent gaussians rotation and scale, a deformable network Φd is employed to correct the rotation
353   and scale during the LBS process. To distinguish it from the joint rotation r of the SMPL human
354   pose, we denote the rotation of the gaussian with subscript g, and
355
                                             drg , dsg = Φd (xg , r 0 ).                                  (12)
356
357   The gaussians rotation rg and scale sg are modified with offsets drg and dsg to get the final gaus-
358
      sians in the observation space. In the observation space, we obtain the rendered images through the
      rasterisation of gaussians and compute the image loss to supervise canonical gaussians.
359
360   Once the warm-up iterations completes, we begin the modular joint growth. With the MK calculated
361   during the warm-up phase, we can locate the parent joints Js that require growth. Then we add
362   grown joints to the extra joint book B e , optimizing its positions j e and rotations r e during the
363   subsequent training. Notably, Φlbs needs to extend its output dimensions to include the blending
364
      weights for extra joints, following K = K 0 + K e . Since the extra joints do not have prior LBS
      weights, their blending weights are entirely learned through Φlbs .
365
366   Both the warm-up stage and the post-growth learning stage adopt adaptive density control to man-
367   age the updates of the gaussian points. We also dynamically adjust the threshold of gradient for
368   densification in (Kerbl et al., 2023) based on the number of gaussians, in order to balance memory
369   consumption. Please check our supplemental material for details of this design.

      6     L IMITATIONS AND C ONCLUSION
524
525
      Limitations. Although our method enhances the modeling for rigid and non-rigid garments, it can-
      not address scenarios involving drastic changes in the topology. This is because topological changes
526
      disrupt the one-to-one correspondence between frames, making the human modeling centered on the
527
      canonical space become downgraded. We notice that Park et al. (2021) address topology issues by
528   introducing high-dimensional mappings, which could be adapted to build our non-rigid deformation.
529   However, this is not the main scope of this paper and can be explored as future work.
530
531
      Conclusion. In this paper, we introduce ToMiE, a modular growth method designed to extend tra-
      ditional SMPL skeleton for better modeling of human garments. In the first stage, we assign the
532
      gradient of gaussian points to different joints by combining LBS weights with the motion kernel
533
      based on motion priors. This allows us to accurately locate the parent joints that need to grow,
534   avoiding redundant growth. In the second stage, we design an extra joint book to achieve explicit
535   joint modeling and optimize the transformation of the newly grown joints in an end-to-end manner.
536   With the improved designs mentioned above, our ToMiE stands out among numerous state-of-the-art
537   methods, achieving the best rendering quality and animatability of garments. We hope the adaptive
538   growing method will spark a renewed discussion on the current capabilities of digital human mod-
539   eling. What’s more, it is expected to offer some insights for subsequent works related to topology
      and skeleton generation.

788   A.3   C ALCULATION OF M ASKS WITH C OMPLEX G ARMENTS
789
790
      Our method focuses primarily on modeling complex garments. In Tab. 1, we further evaluate the
      model’s performance in these areas using a binary mask. To generate a mask for each scene that
791
      distinguishes regions containing complex garments, we first segment the potential complex garment
792
      points in 3D space. Specifically, points predominantly controlled by the extra joints and their parent
793   joints are identified as part of the complex garments. With the pre-trained blending weight, we can
794   easily locate these points, which are then assigned a white color, while all others are marked in black,
795   forming a 3D binary mask. Finally, we obtain a 2D binary mask representing complex garments by
796   applying Gaussian rasterization to the 3D mask. Since we rely solely on this binary mask for metrics
797   evaluation, this post-processing method for calculating masks is permissible.
