009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            C ∗ -algebra-valued kernels could pave the way for the next generation of kernel
012            machines. To further our fundamental understanding of learning with C ∗ -algebraic
013            kernels, we propose a new class of positive definite kernels based on the spectral
014            truncation. We focus on kernels whose inputs and outputs are vectors or functions
015            and generalize typical kernels by introducing the noncommutativity of the products
016            appearing in the kernels. The noncommutativity induces interactions along the
017            data function domain. We show that it is a governing factor leading to performance
018            enhancement: we can balance the representation power and the model complexity.
019
               We also propose a deep learning perspective to increase the representation capacity
               of spectral truncation kernels. The flexibility of the proposed class of kernels allows
020
               us to go beyond previous separable and commutative kernels, addressing two of
021
               the foremost issues regarding learning in vector-valued RKHSs, namely the choice
022            of the kernel and the computational cost.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Kernel methods have been one of the most fundamental tools in machine learning (Schölkopf & Smola,
028   2001; Gretton et al., 2007; Hofmann et al., 2008; Muandet et al., 2017). They have been applied,
029   for example, to ridge regression, principal component analysis, and support vector machine. Kernel
030   methods are characterized by reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces (RKHSs), which are constructed by
031   positive definite kernels. Typical positive definite kernels include the polynomial kernel, Gaussian
032   kernel, and Laplacian kernel. Product kernels, which are constructed by the product of multiple
033   kernels, have also been considered (Schölkopf & Smola, 2001; Thomas, 2008).
034   Standard positive definite kernels are scalar-valued, and are well-suited to learn scalar-valued func-
035   tions. Kernel methods for vector- and function-valued outputs have also been investigated (Álvarez
036   et al., 2012; Kadri et al., 2016). The kernels, in these cases, are instead operator-valued, and the asso-
037   ciated feature space is vector-valued RKHSs (vvRKHSs) (Kadri et al., 2012; 2016; Minh et al., 2016).
038   There are at least two challenges for vvRKHS methods: the computational cost and choice of kernels.
039   A typical kernel is the separable kernel, which is defined by the product of a scalar-valued kernel and
040   a positive semi-definite operator (Álvarez et al., 2012). Another typical kernel is the commutative
041   kernel, which is defined only with the pointwise calculation of functions or vectors (Hashimoto et al.,
042   2021). Although applying the separable and commutative kernels is computationally efficient, there
043   is a crucial shortcoming for each kernel. Separable kernels identify dependencies between input
044   and output variables separately, and cannot reflect information of input variables properly to output
045
      variables. The output is determined only by the global information of the input. On the other hand,
      commutative kernels only identify the pointwise (completely local) dependencies. Indeed, they are
046
      two extreme cases regarding the dependencies between input and output variables. Several attempts
047
      have been made to construct kernels that go beyond separable and commutative kernels. A typical
048   nonseparable kernel is the transformable kernels, which is characterized by a map that can incorporate
049   the information of input variables with the output variables. Huusari & Kadri (2021) proposed
050   entangled kernels based on concepts from quantum computing, such as partial trace and entanglement.
051   Hashimoto et al. (2023a) proposed to use the product of circulant matrices and general squared
052   matrices to construct kernels. Using this kernel, one can generalize the convolution and capture
053   the effect of interactions of different Fourier components. However, if we need an m-dimensional
      vector-valued outputs with these kernels, then we have to construct an mN by mN Gram matrix,

061   to go beyond separable and commutative kernels, the computational cost is significant.
062
      In this work, we address the two challenges of the computational cost and the choice of kernels
063   by introducing a new class of kernels based on the framework of reproducing kernel Hilbert C ∗ -
064   module (RKHM) (Hashimoto et al., 2021). RKHM is a generalization of RKHS by means of
065   C ∗ -algebra. C ∗ -algebra is a generalization of the space of complex values and has structures of the
066   norm, product, and involution (Murphy, 1990; Lance, 1995). It unifies operators and functions. In this
067   framework, kernels are generalized to C ∗ -algebra-valued kernel functions and allow us to consider
068   function-valued kernels, leading to function-valued Gram matrices. By evaluating the values of the
069   function-valued Gram matrix at m different points, we obtain m scalar-valued Gram matrices. This
070   allows us to obtain an m-dimensional vector-valued outputs with the computational cost of O(mN 3 ),
071
      which alleviates the dependency on m from cubic to linear compared to the case of vvRKHSs with
      nonseparable kernels such as transformable kernels. We summarize the difference between the
072
      proposed and existing kernels in Table 1.
073
074   To obtain kernels going beyond the separable and commutative kernels with low computational cost,
075   we propose a new class of C ∗ -algebra-valued positive definite kernels based on the spectral truncation,
076   which has been discussed in the fields of noncommutative geometry and C ∗ -algebra (D’Andrea et al.,
077
      2014; van Suijlekom, 2021; Connes & van Suijlekom, 2021). The proposed kernels are parameterized
      by a natural number n corresponding to the dimension of the truncated space. They can be applied to
078
      both vector and functional inputs. For vector inputs, we regard the elements of them as the values of
079
      functions. We approximate the input functions on the n-dimensional truncated space and obtain n by
080   n Toeplitz matrices, whose (i, j)-entry depends only on i − j. Thus, n describes the resolution of
081   the discretization, and we call it the truncation parameter. Indeed, n plays an important role from
082   at least two perspectives. First, n describes the noncommutativity of the kernel. Indeed, we show
083   that the proposed kernels converge to the commutative kernels as n goes to infinity. On the other
084   hand, if n = 1, then the proposed kernels are separable kernel. Thus, we can control local and global
085   dependencies through n. Second, the parameter n controls the tradeoff between the representation
086   power and the model complexity. We show that if n is small, then the representation power is low, and
087   the model complexity is small. On the other hand, if n is large, then the representation power is high,
088
      and the model complexity is large. By introducing the parameter n and setting n to balance them, we
      obtain higher performance compared to the separable and commutative kernels. In the sense of these
089
      two perspective, in the setting of functional data (Jim Ramsay, 2005; Wang et al., 2016; Hashimoto
090
      et al., 2021), the proposed truncation kernels shed light on the good effects of discretization (setting
091   n as a finite number) on the learning process.
092
093   Our contributions are summarized as follows:
094
      • We propose spectral truncation kernels, a new class of function-valued positive definite kernels that
        go beyond separable and commutative kernels with low computational cost. The proposed kernels
095
        are based on the spectral truncation and are indexed by a truncation parameter, which adjusts the
096
        global and local interactions along the data function domain by introducing the noncommutativity
097     into the learning process. (see Definition 3.2 and Subsection 3.1)
098   • We derive a generalization bound for the learning problem in the RKHMs associated with the
099     proposed kernels. The bound implies that the parameter n controls the tradeoff between the
100     representation power and the model complexity. (see Section 4)
101   • We propose a deep approach to gain the representation power. (see Section 6)

      8    C ONCLUSION AND L IMITATION
524
525
      In this paper, we proposed a new class of positive definite kernels for vector- or function-valued
      outputs based on the spectral truncation. The proposed kernels with the framework of RKHMs
526
      resolve two shortcomings of the framework of vvRKHSs for vector- or function-valued outputs at
527
      the same time: computational cost and choice of kernels. By considering function-valued positive
528   definite kernels, not operator-valued ones, we can alleviate the computational cost. At the same time,
529   we can introduce noncommutativity into the learning process and can induce interactions along the
530   data function domain. We also showed that we can control the tradeoff between the representation
531   power and the model complexity associated with the proposed kernels. In addition, we proposed a
532   deep approach to obtain models with higher representation powers.
533
      In the current setting, we focus on the C ∗ -algebra of continuous functions on the torus. Although
534   this setting includes many important examples, e.g., periodic time-series data, continuous functions
535   defined on a compact set in R, finite-dimensional vectors, investigations for more general C ∗ -algebras
536   allow us to apply the proposed kernel more general settings. As we discuss in Appendix H, we can
537   generalize this setting and define positive definite kernels for more general inputs and outputs. In
538   addition, as we discuss in Appendix D, there are many potential applications of the proposed kernel.
539   However, theoretical and empirical investigations for these generalized settings and applications
      remain to be investigated in future work.

      A    R EVIEW OF EXISTING OPERATOR - VALUED KERNELS
652
653
      We review existing operator-valued kernels and discuss their advantages and shortcomings in this
      section. We especially focus on the case X = Ad and the application of kernel ridge regression
654
      in this paper. For more details about the operator-valued kernels, see, for example, (Álvarez et al.,
655
      2012).
656
657   Typical existing kernels are summirized as follows: Let k̃ : X̃ × X̃ → C and p : T × T → C be
658   complex-valued positive definite kernels and let S : T × X → X̃ . Consider a kernel k : X × X →
659   B(L2 (T)) defined as
660                                          Z
661                           k(x, y)v(z) = k̃(S(z, x), S(t, y))p(z, t)v(t)dt.                      (7)
                                                  T
662
663   In general, we have to construct a Gram matrix whose elements are operators. Typically, if we need
664   an output function evaluated at m points, then we discretize the operator on O(m)-dimensional
665   space and obtain an O(mN ) by O(mN ) Gram matrix. Then, the computational cost for the kernel
666   regression task for obtaining an output function evaluated at m points is O(N 3 m3 ). Therefore, the
667   computational cost is significant. As a special case of the kernel (7), the following two kernels are
668   efficient in the sense of the computational cost.
669
670   Separable kernel If we set X̃ = X and S(t, x) = x, then the kernel k defined in Eq. (7) is called a
671   separable kernel. In Rother words, let A ∈ B(L2 (T)) be a Hermitian positive semi-definite operator
672   defined as Av(z) = T p(z, t)v(t)dt. The kernel k : X × X → B(L2 (T)) defined as
673
                                             k(x, y)v(z) = k̃(x, y)Av(z)
674
675   is called a separable kernel.
676
      For separable kernels, the computation is reduced to the computation of the Gram matrix of k̃. Thus,
677
      the computational cost for the kernel regression task for obtaining an output function evaluated at
678
      m points is O(N 3 m), where N is the sample size. However, as we can see from the definition, it
679   identifies dependencies between input and output variables separately and cannot reflect information
680   of input variables to output variables. In the framework of vvRKHSs, the output function with an
681
                                PN
      input x is in the form of i=1 k(x, xi )vi for given samples x1 , . . . , xN ∈ X , and some v1 , . . . , vN ∈
682   L2 (T). Thus, with separable kernels, we cannot specify the relationship between the value of the
683   output function at z and the value x(z) of the input x at z although they have strong connection in
684   many cases. For example, if we have a time-series input [x1 , . . . , xd ] ∈ Ad as explanatory variables
685   and try to obtain an output function as a response variable, the values of x1 (z), . . . , xd (z) at time z is
686   strongly related to the value of the output at time z. In this case, the separable kernels are not suitable
687   for extracting the relationship between x1 (z), . . . , xd (z) and the values of the output at z.
688
689   Commutative kernel        If we set X̃ = C, S(t, x) = x(t), and p(z, t) = 1 (z = t), p(z, t) = 0 (z ̸=
690   t), then we have
691
                                          k(x, y)v(z) = k̃(x(z), y(z))v(z).
692
693   We call this kernel the commutative kernel.
694
      For commutative kernels, the computation is reduced to the computation at each z. Thus, the
695
      computational cost for the kernel regression task for obtaining an output function evaluated at m
696   points is O(N 3 m). However, as we can see from the definition, it only identifies completely local
697   relationship between the input function and the output function. The value of the output function at z
698   is determined only with the value of the input function at z. In the same example as separable kernels,
699   the values of x1 (z), . . . , xd (z) at time z is strongly related to the value of the output at time z, but
700   may also be related to y(z + t) for t ∈ [−T, T ] for a small number T . In this case, the commutative
701   kernels are not suitable for extracting the relationship between x1 (z), . . . , xd (z) and the values of
      the output around z, not only exactly at z.

      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025

      As we have seen, although the separable and commutative kernels are computationally efficient,
703   they are two extreme cases regarding the description of the relationship between the input and
704   output functions; the separable kernels identify dependencies between input and output variables
705   separately (only with global information) and the commutative kernels only identify the commutative
706   (completely local) dependencies. The following existing kernels fill a gap between the above two
707   kernels, but computationally expensive.
708
709   Transformable kernel If we set p(z, t) = 1, then the kernel k defined in Eq. (7) is called the
710   transformable kernel. In this case, the kernels can identify the dependency between input and output
711   variables through the map S. However, the computational cost for the kernel regression task for
712   obtaining an output function evaluated at m points is O(N 3 m3 ) as we discussed at the beginning of
713
      this section. In addition, we have to determine the map S, but it is not easy to interpret in general.
714
715
      Combination of separable and transformable kernels Considering the sum of separable and
      transformable kernels and the product of these kernels have also been proposed. However, we have
716
      the same shortcomings of separable and transformable kernels.

      D    A PPLICATIONS : E XTRACTING LOCAL AND GLOBAL DEPENDENCIES OF
839        FUNCTIONS
840
841   In Subsection 7.2, we considered applying the proposed kernel to an image recovering task. The
842   application to image data is just an example, and there are more applications, which involves functions.
843   We list two examples here.
844
845
      Time-series data analysis We can regard a time-series as a function on a time space. In many
846
      cases, a state at a certain time z is influenced strongly by another state at the same time z, but also by
847
      the state around the time z. Since commutative kernels focus only on local information, we cannot
848   describe these two states with commutative kernels. On the other hand, since separable kernels focus
849   only on global information, we cannot describe the relationship of these two states at each time z.
850   By applying the proposed kernels, we can extract global information, but also can focus on local
851   information.
852
853
854   Operator learning In the framework of operator learning, we obtain a solution of a partial dif-
855
      ferential equation as an output from an input function (such as initial condition or parameter of the
      equation). Thus, we construct a model where both of the input and output are functions. Applying
856
      kernel methods to operator learning has been proposed (Batlle et al., 2024). We can construct the
857
      model by solving a kernel ridge regression task. Another well-known operator learning method is
858   neural operator. In the framework of neural operators, we apply integral operators to extract global
859   information and apply local linear operators and local activation functions to extract local information.
860   The proposed kernel enables us to do similar procedures for the operator learning with kernels. By
861   considering the product of multiple proposed kernels with different values of n or deep model with
862   the proposed kernels with different values of n, we can extract both global and local information; we
863   can extract global information using the kernel with small n and extract local information using the
      one with large n in the model.
