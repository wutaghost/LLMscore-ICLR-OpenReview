009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs) seek to mimic the spiking behavior of biologi-
012            cal neurons and are expected to play a key role in the advancement of neural com-
013            puting and artificial intelligence. The conversion of Artificial Neural Networks
014            (ANNs) to SNNs is the most widely used training method, which ensures that the
015            resulting SNNs perform comparably to ANNs on large-scale datasets. The effi-
016
               ciency of these conversion-based SNNs is often determined by the neural coding
               schemes. Current schemes typically use spike count or timing for encoding, which
017
               is linearly related to ANN activations and increases the required number of time
018
               steps. To address this limitation, we propose a novel Canonic Signed Spike (CSS)
019            coding scheme. This method incorporates non-linearity into the encoding process
020            by weighting spikes at each step of neural computation, thereby increasing the
021            information encoded in spikes. We identify the temporal coupling phenomenon
022            arising from weighted spikes and introduce negative spikes along with a Ternary
023            Self-Amplifying (TSA) neuron model to mitigate the issue. A one-step silent pe-
024            riod is implemented during neural computation, achieving high accuracy with low
025            latency. We apply the proposed methods to directly convert full-precision ANNs
026            and evaluate performance on CIFAR-10 and ImageNet datasets. Our experimental
027
               results demonstrate that the CSS coding scheme effectively compresses time steps
               for coding and reduces inference latency with minimal conversion loss.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Spiking Neural Networks (SNNs), recognized as the third generation of neural network models, are
033   inspired by the biological structure and functionality of the brain (Wang et al., 2020). Unlike tra-
034   ditional Artificial Neural Networks (ANNs), which rely on continuous activation functions, SNNs
035   utilize discrete spiking events. This enables SNNs to capture temporal dynamics and process infor-
036   mation in a manner that closely resembles brain activity (Taherkhani et al., 2020). The event-driven
037   nature of SNNs aligns with the brain’s energy-efficient computational paradigm, offering potential
038
      for more efficient and low-power computing systems (Yamazaki et al., 2022).
039   The two primary learning algorithms for SNNs are gradient-based optimization and ANN-SNN
040   conversion. Directly training using supervised backpropagation is challenging due to the non-
041   differentiable nature of spike generation (Lee et al., 2020; 2016). The conversion-based method,
042   however, offers a practical approach to overcome this difficulty and has produced the best-
043   performing SNNs (Deng & Gu, 2021; Bu et al., 2022; Ding et al., 2021).
044   Encoding the ANN activations into spike trains is a prerequisite for successful ANN-SNN conver-
045   sion. Various coding schemes, such as rate coding and temporal coding, have been proposed to
046   describe neural activity (Guo et al., 2021). Rate coding maps the number of spikes to the corre-
047   sponding ANN activation (Cao et al., 2015). In contrast, temporal coding focuses on the precise
048   timing or patterns of spikes (Rueckauer & Liu, 2018; Kim et al., 2018; Han & Roy, 2020). For ex-
049   ample, Time-to-First-Spike (TTFS) coding maps the the activation value to the time elapsed before
050
      the first spike (Stanojevic et al., 2022).
051   However, both the spike counts in rate coding and the spike timing in TTFS coding are linearly
052   related to the encoded activation. This necessitates a large number of time steps to provide sufficient
053   encoding granularity (Stanojevic et al., 2023; Meng et al., 2022). Recent works have proposed
      alleviating these problems by quantizing the ANN activations before conversion (Hu et al., 2023;

101   2   R ELATED W ORK
102
103   Currently, the mainstream coding schemes in converted SNNs are rate coding and TTFS coding.
104   Rate coding represents different activities with the number of spikes emitted within a specific time
105   window. Early research efforts focused on reducing conversion loss, leading to methods such as
106   weight normalization (Diehl et al., 2015), threshold rescaling (Sengupta et al., 2019), and soft-reset
107   neuron models (Han et al., 2020). More recent work has shifted towards reducing the number of
      time steps by optimizing neuron parameters. Meng et al. (2022) introduced the Threshold Tuning


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      and Residual Block Restructuring (TTRBR) method to minimize conversion error in ResNet archi-
109   tectures with fewer time steps. Bu et al. (2022) proposed optimizing the initial membrane potential
110   to reduce conversion loss when using a small number of time steps.
111
112
      Despite these optimizations, deep networks or large datasets still require hundreds of time steps to
      achieve satisfactory results. To address this, recent works in rate coding have explored quantizing
113
      the ANNs before conversion (Hao et al., 2023; Bu et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2023). This approach
114
      directly reduces the number of activations that need to be mapped, providing an alternative way to
115   minimize time steps. Notably, this approach is complementary to ours. The proposed encoding
116   scheme can also convert quantized ANNs and further reduce the required number of time steps.
117
118
      Due to the functional similarity to the biological neural network, SNNs are highly compatible with
      temporal coding. Rueckauer & Liu (2018) were the first to attempt converting an ANN to a TTFS-
119
      based SNN. While this coding method significantly increased sparsity by limiting each neuron to fire
120
      at most one spike, they observed large conversion errors, even on MNIST dataset. Stanojevic et al.
121   (2022) demonstrated that an exact mapping from ANN to TTFS-based SNN is feasible but needs
122   hundreds of time steps for accurate encoding. Yang et al. (2023) proposed a TTFS-based conversion
123   algorithm with dynamic neuron threshold and weight regularization. They completed the conversion
124   with 50 time steps per layer. Despite the reduction in the number of time steps per layer, TTFS
125   coding still suffered from high output latency in deep networks for its layerwise processing manner.
126   Han & Roy (2020) introduced the Temporal-Switch-Coding (TSC) scheme, where each input pixel
127   is represented by two spikes, and the time interval between them encodes pixel intensity. However,
128   as this time interval remains linearly related to activation, the issue of long latency persists.
129   Some recent works have also incorporated non-linearity into the coding process. Stöckl & Maass
130   (2021) and Rueckauer & Liu (2021) used spikes to encode the ”1”s in the binary representations
131   of ANN activations. However, both works did not address the temporal coupling issue caused by
132   weighted spikes. Instead, they adopted an approach similar to TTFS coding, where neurons must
133   wait for the arrival of all input spikes before firing. In contrast, our approach facilitates the greatest
134   extent of synchronous neural computation, thereby reducing both the coding time steps and output
135
      latency.

      4.2   I NCORPORATING N EGATIVE S PIKES
257
      Rueckauer et al. (2017) reported that large activation values in ANNs are rare, with most values
258   concentrated within a smaller range. This suggests that when mapped to weighted spike trains, the
259   majority of spikes will occur in the later time steps (as these spikes encode smaller values). As a
260   consequence, Eq. (9) becomes difficult to satisfy as t0 approaches T : the left-hand side contains a
261   large amount of input, while the right-hand side provides limited encoding capacity. This mismatch
262   ultimately results in an increase in uli [T ], leading to a further shift in the spike distribution.
263
      To illustrate this more clearly, we have plotted the spike distribution in the first and last layers of
264   VGG-16 (in red) in Fig. 2, alongside the distribution of the average residual membrane potential
265   across all neurons. As shown, the spike distribution in the last layer shifts significantly toward
266   later time steps compared to the first layer, a phenomenon we refer to as the temporal coupling
267   of weighted spikes. Additionally, the residual membrane potential exhibits a distribution resem-
268   bling random noise, and our experimental results indicate that this leads to nearly random classifi-
269   cation performance. Therefore, a new neuron model is needed to enable effective computation with
      weighted spikes.

404   5.2         OVERALL P ERFORMANCE
405
406   In Table 2, we compared the time steps and inference latency under different coding schemes, which
407   reflect the throughput and latency of the network, respectively. Note all SNNs are converted from
408   full-precision ANNs to ensure a fair comparison. Furthermore, the accuracy of the ANNs utilized
409   in each work is also provided.
410   Reduction in Coding Time Steps. The coding time step refers to the number of time steps required
411   to encode the activations into a spike train. This metric indicates how well the encoding scheme can
412   represent information within a given time frame and reflects the efficiency of the method.
413
      For simpler classification tasks such as CIFAR-10, CSS coding scheme demonstrated nearly loss-
414   less conversion with a significant reduction in the number of required time steps. Compared to linear
415   coding schemes like rate coding, CSS reduces time steps by more than tenfold for both VGG-16 and
416   ResNet-18, while simultaneously reducing the conversion loss. While the FS coding scheme also
417   applied weighted spikes and required fewer time steps for ResNet-20, it experienced greater con-
418   version loss compared to our method. On the more complex ImageNet dataset, the higher precision
419   demands for encoding further highlighted the benefits of spike weighting. For example, Li et al.
420   (2021) reported a conversion error exceeding 1% on ResNet-34 with 256 time steps, whereas our
421   method achieved only 0.2% conversion loss with just 14 time steps. FS-coding achieved smaller
422
      conversion loss for ResNet-50 with fewer time steps; however, this came at the cost of a latency
      eight times greater than that of ours.
423
424   Reduction in Inference Latency. Inference latency refers to the time elapsed from the beginning
425   of input encoding to the receipt of the classification result, and is also measured in time steps. It
426   indicates how efficiently the encoding scheme transmits information through neural computation
427   across the network layers.
428   In CSS coding scheme, each layer of TSA neurons incorporates a one-step silent period, making
429   the inference latency equal to the sum of layer counts and coding time steps. In contrast, both
430   TTFS coding and FS coding require each layer to wait for the arrival of all inputs. While this ap-
431   proach facilitates lossless conversion, it completely sacrifices the synchronous processing capability
      of SNNs, leading to increased output latency (i.e. the product of layer counts and coding time steps).

469
      rate coding also perform well with reduced time steps, they rely heavily on low-bit quantization.

471
      zation. Furthermore, our method can be seamlessly combined with quantized ANNs, enabling the

523    5.5                   T HE ROLE OF N EGTIVE S PIKES
524
525    In this section, we validate the role of negative spikes in achieving low-latency nonlinear encoding
526    through an ablation study. Experiments were conducted using ResNet-34 on ImageNet, where we
527    gradually increased the silent period length from zero, in the absence of negative spikes. The results,
528
       shown in Fig. 4, reveal that a silent period of at least four steps was required to match the perfor-
       mance gains introduced by negative spikes. This resulted in a nearly 100-step increase in inference
529
       latency. The introduction of negative spikes and TSA neurons in the CSS coding scheme is crucial
530
       for breaking temporal coupling, distinguishing our approach from other coding schemes that use
531    weighted spikes (Rueckauer & Liu, 2021; Stöckl & Maass, 2021; Kim et al., 2018).

535
536    In this work, we compress the coding time steps by assigning weights to spikes, enabling each spike
537
       to carry more information. We also introduce negative spikes to break temporal coupling, effectively
       reducing inference latency. The resulting CSS encoding scheme enhances the throughput, inference
538
       speed and energy efficiency of converted SNNs, while minimizing conversion loss.
