009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Multimodal contrastive learning methods (e.g., CLIP) have shown impressive zero-
012            shot classification performance due to their strong ability to joint representation
013            learning for visual and textual modalities. However, recent research revealed
014            that multimodal contrastive learning on poisoned pre-training data with a small
015            proportion of maliciously backdoored data can induce backdoored CLIP that could
016
               be attacked by inserted triggers in downstream tasks with a high success rate.
               To defend against backdoor attacks on CLIP, existing defense methods focus on
017
               either the pre-training stage or the fine-tuning stage, which would unfortunately
018
               cause high computational costs due to numerous parameter updates and are not
019            applicable in the black-box setting. In this paper, we provide the first attempt at a
020            computationally efficient backdoor detection method to defend against backdoored
021            CLIP in the inference stage. We empirically find that the visual representations
022            of backdoored images are insensitive to both benign and malignant changes in
023            class description texts. Motivated by this observation, we propose BDetCLIP,
024            a novel test-time backdoor detection method based on contrastive prompting.
025            Specifically, we first prompt the language model (e.g., GPT-4) to produce class-
026            related description texts (benign) and class-perturbed random texts (malignant)
027
               by specially designed instructions. Then, the distribution difference in cosine
               similarity between images and the two types of class description texts can be used
028
               as the criterion to detect backdoor samples. Extensive experiments validate that
029
               our proposed BDetCLIP is superior to state-of-the-art backdoor detection methods,
030            in terms of both effectiveness and efficiency.

033   1    I NTRODUCTION
034
035   Multimodal contrastive learning methods (e.g., CLIP (Radford et al., 2021)) have shown impressive
036   zero-shot classification performance in various downstream tasks and served as foundation models
037   in various vision-language fields due to their strong ability to effectively align representations from
038   different modalities, such as open-vocabulary object detection (Wu et al., 2023), text-to-image
039
      generation (Wu et al., 2023), and video understanding (Xu et al., 2021). However, recent research
      has revealed that a small proportion of backdoor samples poisoned into the pre-training data can
040
      cause a backdoored CLIP after the multimodal contrastive pre-training procedure (Carlini & Terzis,
041
      2021; Carlini et al., 2023; Bansal et al., 2023). In the inference stage, a backdoored CLIP would
042   produce tampered image representations for images with a trigger, close to the text representation of
043   the target attack class in zero-shot classification. This exposes a serious threat when deploying CLIP
044   in real-world applications.
045
      To overcome this issue, effective defense methods have been proposed recently, which can be divided
046
      into three kinds of defense paradigms, as shown in Figure 1: including (a) robust anti-backdoor
047
      contrastive learning in the pre-training stage (Yang et al., 2023b), (b) counteracting the backdoor
048   in a pre-trained CLIP in the fine-tuning stage (Bansal et al., 2023), (c) leveraging trigger inversion
049   techniques to decide if a pre-trained CLIP is backdoored (Sur et al., 2023; Feng et al., 2023a). Overall,
050   these defense methods have a high computational cost due to the need for additional learning or
051   optimization procedures. In contrast, we advocate test-time backdoor sample detection (Figure 1(d)),
052   which is a more computationally efficient defense against backdoored CLIP, as there are no parameter
053   updates in the inference stage. Intuitively, it could be feasible to directly adapt existing unimodal
      test-time detection methods (Gao et al., 2019; Guo et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023) to detect backdoored

076   In this paper, we provide the first attempt at a computationally efficient backdoor detection method
077   to defend against backdoored CLIP in the inference stage. We empirically find that the visual
078   representations of backdoored images are insensitive to both benign and malignant changes of class
079

      backdoor detection method based on contrastive prompting. Specifically, we first prompt the GPT-4
080
      model (Achiam et al., 2023) to generate class-related (or class-perturbed random) description texts by
081

082   the distribution difference in cosine similarity between images and the two types of class prompts,
083   which can be used as a good criterion to detect backdoor samples. We can see that the distribution
084   difference of backdoored images between the benign and malignant changes of class prompts is
085   smaller than that of clean images. The potential reason for the insensibility of backdoored images is
086   that their visual representations have less semantic information aligned with class description texts. In
087   this way, we can detect backdoored images in the inference stage of CLIP effectively and efficiently.
088   Extensive experiments validate that our proposed BDetCLIP is superior to state-of-the-art backdoor
089   detection methods, in terms of both effectiveness and efficiency.
090   Our main contributions can be summarized as follows:
091
      • A new backdoor detection paradigm for CLIP. We pioneer test-time backdoor detection for CLIP,
092
        which is more computationally efficient than existing defense paradigms.
093
094   • A novel backdoor detection method. We propose a novel test-time multimodal backdoor detection
095
        method based on contrastive prompting, which detects backdoor samples based on the distribution
        difference between images regarding the benign and malignant changes of class prompts.
096
097   • Strong experimental results. Our proposed method achieves superior experimental results on
098     various types of backdoored CLIP compared with state-of-the-art detection methods.

140   2.2   BACKDOOR ATTACKS AND D EFENSES
141
142   The backdoor attack is a serious security threat to machine learning systems (Li et al., 2022; Carlini
143   & Terzis, 2021; Xu et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2021). The whole process of a backdoor attack can
144   be expounded as follows. In the data collection stage of a machine learning system, a malicious
145   adversary could manufacture a part of backdoor samples with the imperceptible trigger poisoned into
146   the training dataset. After the model training stage, the hidden trigger could be implanted into the
147
      victim model without much impact on the performance of the victim model. During the inference
      stage, the adversary could manipulate the victim model to produce a specific output by adding the
148
      trigger to the clean input. Early research on backdoor attacks focuses on designing a variety of
149
      triggers that satisfy the practical scenarios mainly on image and text classification tasks including
150   invisible stealthy triggers (Chen et al., 2017; Turner et al., 2019; Li et al., 2021a; Doan et al., 2021;
151   Nguyen & Tran, 2021; Gao et al., 2023; Souri et al., 2022) and physical triggers (Chen et al., 2017;
152   Wenger et al., 2021). To defend against these attacks, many backdoor defense methods are proposed
153   which can be divided into four categories, mainly including data cleaning in the pre-processing stage
154   (Tran et al., 2018), robust anti-backdoor training (Chen et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022), mitigation,
155   detection, and inversion in the post-training stage (Min et al., 2023), and test-time detection in the
156   inference stage (Shi et al., 2023). Besides, recent research also investigates the backdoor attack on
157   other learning paradigms including self-supervised learning (Li et al., 2023) and federated learning
158
      (Nguyen et al., 2023), and other vision or language tasks including object tracking (Huang et al.,
      2023), text-to-image generation by diffusion models (Chou et al., 2023), and text generation by large
159
      language models (Xue et al., 2023).
160

261   3.2      O UR P ROPOSED BD ET CLIP
262
      Motivation. It was shown that CLIP has achieved impressive zero-shot classification performance
263
      by leveraging visual description texts (Yang et al., 2023c; Pratt et al., 2023; Maniparambil et al., 2023;
264   Yu et al., 2023; Saha et al., 2024; Feng et al., 2023b; Liu et al., 2024) generated by large language
265   models. For backdoored CLIP (i.e., CLIP corrupted by backdoor attacks), recent research (Bansal
266   et al., 2023) has revealed that implanted visual triggers in CLIP can exhibit a strong co-occurrence
267   with the target class. However, such visual triggers in CLIP are usually simple non-semantic pixel
268   patterns, which could not align well with abundant textual concepts. Therefore, backdoored images
269   with visual triggers are unable to properly capture the semantic changes of class description texts.
      This motivates us to consider whether the alignment between the visual representations of backdoored


                                                             5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      images and the class description texts would be significantly changed when there exist significant
271   changes in the class description texts. Interestingly, we empirically find that the alignment of backdoor
272   samples would not be significantly changed even given significant changes in the text description
273   texts. This observation can help us distinguish backdoor samples from clean samples because the
274   alignment of clean samples would be significantly influenced by the changes in the text description
275   texts.
276
      Contrastive prompting. Based on the above motivation, we propose BDetCLIP, a novel test-time
277   backdoor detection method based on contrastive prompting. Specifically, we prompt GPT-4 (Achiam
278   et al., 2023) to generate two types of contrastive class description texts. Firstly, based on the powerful
279   in-context learning capabilities of GPT-4, we use specially designed instructions with a demonstration
280   as shown in Appendix A. In particular, the demonstration for the class “goldfish” is associated with
281   various attributes of objects, e.g., shape, color, structure, and behavior. In this way, GPT-4 is expected
282   to output multiple fine-grained attribute-based sentences for the assigned j-th class, denoted by
283   STjk (k ∈ [m]) where m is the number of sentences. On the other hand, we also prompt GPT-4 by
284   the instruction “Please randomly generate a sentence of no more than 10 words unrelated to {Class
285
      Name}”, to generate one random sentence unrelated to the assigned j-th class. We concatenated
      the class template prompt with the obtained random sentences to generate the final class-specific
286
      malignant prompt, denoted by RTj , such as “A photo of a goldfish. The bright sun cast shadows on
287
      the bustling city street.”. In Appendix F, We also recorded the money and time costs associated with
288   the prompts generated by GPT-4, and demonstrated the feasibility of using open-source models (e.g.,
289   LLaMA3-8B (Dubey et al., 2024) and Mistral-7B-Instruct-v0.2 (Jiang et al., 2023)) as alternatives to
290   proprietary models like GPT-4.
291

345
      Compared methods. We cannot make a fair and direct comparison with other CLIP backdoor
      defense methods because our paper is the first work on backdoor detection during the inference phase
346
      for CLIP. Our method is fundamentally different from the defense methods during the fine-tuning or
347
      pre-training phases, which are designed to protect models from backdoor attacks and correct models
348   that have been compromised by such attacks, respectively. Different from them, backdoor detection
349   in the inference phase serves as a firewall to filter out malicious samples when we are unable to
350   protect or correct the model. Due to the different purposes of these methods mentioned above, their
351   evaluation metric (i.e., ASR) is completely distinct from our evaluation metric (i.e., AUROC), making
352   a direct comparison between our method and those methods impossible. This can be easily verified
353   by examining the experimental settings in many recent papers focused on (unimodal) backdoor
354   sample detection (Guo et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023). We would like to emphasize that our BDetCLIP
355   is applicable in the black-box setting (the defender only needs to access the output of the victim
356
      model instead of controlling the overall model), while other methods (Bansal et al., 2023; Yang
      et al., 2023b;a; Liang et al., 2024) have to control the whole training procedure which is infeasible in
357
      many real-world applications where only third-party models and APIs are accessible. Moreover, our
358
      defense method is much more computationally efficient, as it does not need to modify any model
359   parameters, while previous defense methods involve the update of numerous model parameters.
360   Given these distinctions, a direct comparison with other backdoor defense methods is not feasible.
361   Therefore, to provide a baseline evaluation, we compare our proposed method with three widely-used
362   unimodal test-time backdoor detection methods in conventional classification models: STRIP (Gao
363   et al., 2019), SCALE-UP (Guo et al., 2023), and TeCo (Liu et al., 2023). Further implementation
364   details can be found in Appendix D. In addition, in order to further prove the effectiveness of our
365   method, we provide a scenario for performance comparison with CleanCLIP in Appendix E.
366   Evaluation metrics. Following conventional studies on backdoor sample detection, we assess
367   defense effectiveness by using the area under the receiver operating curve (AUROC) (Fawcett, 2006).
368   Besides, we adopt the inference time as a metric to evaluate the efficiency of the detection method.
369   Generally, a higher value of AUROC indicates that the detection method is more effective and a
370   shorter inference time indicates that the detection method is more efficient. We also report additional
371   metrics such as Accuracy, Recall, and F1 in Appendix B to comprehensively evaluate the effectiveness
372   of BDetCLIP.

531
      In this paper, we provided the first attempt at a computationally efficient backdoor detection method
      to defend against backdoored CLIP in the inference stage. We empirically observed that the visual
532
      representations of backdoored images are insensitive to significant changes in class description texts.
533
      Motivated by this observation, we proposed a novel test-time backdoor detection method based on
534   contrastive prompting, which is called BDetCLIP. For our proposed BDetCLIP, we first prompted the
535   language model (e.g., GPT-4) to produce class-related description texts (benign) and class-perturbed
536   random texts (malignant) by specially designed instructions. Then, we calculated the distribution
537   difference in cosine similarity between images and the two types of class description texts and utilized
538   this distribution difference as the criterion to detect backdoor samples. Comprehensive experimental
539   results validated that our proposed BDetCLIP is more effective and more efficient than state-of-the-art
      backdoor detection methods.

      CC3M dataset. In the first case, we randomly select 500,000 image-text pairs from CC3M as the
865   fine-tuning dataset among which we also randomly select 1,500 of these pairs as target backdoor
866   samples and apply the trigger to them while simultaneously replacing their corresponding captions
867   with the class template for the target class. Then, we can fine-tune CLIP with the backdoored dataset.
868   We finetune the pretrained model for 5 epochs with an initial learning rate of 1e-6 with cosine
869   scheduling and 50 warmup steps, and use AdamW as the optimizer. In the second case, following the

872   dataset. We trained for 64 epochs with a batch size of 128, an initial learning rate of 0.0005 for cosine
873
      scheduling, and 10000 warm-up steps for the AdamW optimizer. All the experiments are conducted

875   Details of comparing methods.
876   • STRIP (Gao et al., 2019) is the first black-box TTSD method that overlays various image
877     patterns and observes the randomness of the predicted classes of the perturbed input to identify
878     poisoned samples. The official open-sourced codes for STRIP (Gao et al., 2019) can be found at:
879     https://github.com/garrisongys/STRIP. In our experiments, for each input image,
880     we use 64 clean images from the test data for superimposition.
881   • SCALE-UP (Guo et al., 2023) is also a method for black-box input-level backdoor detection that
882     assesses the maliciousness of inputs by measuring the scaled prediction consistency (SPC) of
883     labels under amplified conditions, offering effective defense in scenarios with limited data or no
884     prior information about the attack. The official open-sourced codes for SCALE-UP (Guo et al.,
885     2023) can be found at: https://github.com/JunfengGo/SCALE-UP.
886   • TeCo (Liu et al., 2023) modifies input images with common corruptions and assesses their ro-
887     bustness through hard-label outputs, ultimately determining the presence of backdoor triggers
888     based on a deviation measurement of the results. The official open-sourced codes for TeCo (Liu
889     et al., 2023) can be found at: https://github.com/CGCL-codes/TeCo. In our experi-
890
        ments, considering concerns about runtime, we selected "elastic_transform", "gaussian_noise",
        "shot_noise", "impulse_noise", "motion_blur", "snow", "frost", "fog", "brightness", "contrast",
891
        "pixelate", and "jpeg_compression" as methods for corrupting images. The maximum corruption
892
        severity was set to 6.
893
894   Details of datasets.      ImageNet-1K (Russakovsky et al., 2015) consists of 1,000 classes and
895
      over a million images, making it a challenging dataset for large-scale image classification tasks.
      Food-101 (Bossard et al., 2014), which includes 101 classes of food dishes with 1,000 images per
896
      class, and Caltech101 (Fei-Fei et al., 2004), an image dataset containing 101 object categories and
897
      1 background category with 40 to 800 images per category, are both commonly used for testing
898   model performance on fine-grained classification and image recognition tasks. In our experiment,
899   we utilized the validation set of ImageNet-1K (Russakovsky et al., 2015), along with the test sets of
900   Food-101 (Bossard et al., 2014) and Caltech101 (Fei-Fei et al., 2004). By using a fixed backdoor ratio
901   (0.3) on different downstream datasets in the evaluation, there are 15,000 (out of 50,000) backdoored
902   images on ImageNet-1K, 7,575 (out of 25,250) backdoored images on Food-101, and 740 (out of
903   2,465) backdoored images on Caltech-101. Moreover, we also use larger backdoor ratios (0.5 and
904   0.7) on ImageNet-1K, resulting in 25,000 and 35,000 backdoor samples respectively.
