009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            In dynamic domains such as autonomous robotics and video game simulations,
012            agents must continuously adapt to new tasks while retaining previously acquired
013            skills. This ongoing process, known as Continual Reinforcement Learning, presents
014            significant challenges, including the risk of forgetting past knowledge and the need
015            for scalable solutions as the number of tasks increases. To address these issues,
016            we introduce HIerarchical LOW-rank Subspaces of Policies (HILOW), a novel
017            framework designed for continual learning in offline navigation settings. HILOW
018
               leverages hierarchical policy subspaces to enable flexible and efficient adaptation
               to new tasks while preserving existing knowledge. We demonstrate, through
019
               a careful experimental study, the effectiveness of our method in both classical
020
               MuJoCo maze environments and complex video game-like simulations, showcasing
021            competitive performance and satisfying adaptability according to classical continual
022            learning metrics, in particular regarding memory usage. Our work provides a
023            promising framework for real-world applications where continuous learning from
024            pre-collected data is essential.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Humans continuously acquire new skills and knowledge, adapting to an ever-changing world while
030   retaining what they have previously learned. Designing systems capable of replicating this lifelong
031   learning ability is a key challenge in the Continual Reinforcement Learning (CRL) (Khetarpal et al.,
032   2022) community. Traditional Reinforcement Learning (RL) (Sutton & Barto, 2018), while powerful,
033
      often struggles with adaptive, cumulative learning. In CRL, a learning agent must sequentially solve
      tasks, requiring to master new skills without degrading the knowledge gained from previous tasks.
034
035   Within this framework, we focus on a specific subset of problems that combines goal-conditioned
036   learning and offline training, with a particular emphasis on navigation. Goal-Conditioned RL (GCRL)
037   (Ding et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2022a) involves learning policies that can be conditioned to reach specific
038   goal states, making it especially relevant for real-world applications in robotics and video games
039
      where navigation is crucial. The offline setting (Levine et al., 2020; Prudencio et al., 2023)], which
      relies on pre-collected datasets is particularly appealing when data collection is expensive, risky, or
040
      impractical. However, alone, this setting is not sufficient in the context of changing environments:
041
      agents need to continuously adapt to new tasks without forgetting the previous ones, while maintaining
042   scalability as the number of tasks increases (Graffieti et al., 2022; Shaheen et al., 2022).
043
044   Various CRL methods have been proposed to tackle these challenges : some use replay buffer or
045
      generative models to replicate past tasks (Rolnick et al., 2019; Huang et al., 2021) ; others involve
      architectural revisions to mitigate forgetting (Rusu et al., 2016; Veniat et al., 2020) ; and some
046
      use regularization techniques to improve scalability (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017; Kumar et al., 2023).
047
      Nevertheless, these approaches face limitations : Replay-based methods can be impractical due to
048   data storage constraints and privacy concerns, particularly in industries like video game development,
049   where data retention may be costly. Regularization techniques struggle with highly diverse changes,
050   and architecture modifications, such as expanding neural network structures, can become memory-
051   intensive thus limiting scalability. While entirely addressing all these limitations is challenging,
052   Continual Subspace of Policies (CSP) (Gaya et al., 2023) stands out within this literature as an
053   interesting balance between flexibility and efficiency. CSP introduces subspaces of neural networks
      (Wortsman et al., 2021; Gaya et al., 2022), allowing new parameters to be added when necessary,

054
      which helps adapting without forgetting previous skills. However, CSP is primarily an online method,
055   leveraging Soft Actor-Critic (Haarnoja et al., 2018) as its backbone algorithm, and remains untested
056   in offline settings where it may face new challenges.
057
058
      In this article, we propose HIerarchical LOW-Rank Subspaces of Policies (HILOW), a practical
      offline adaptation of Continual Subspace of Policies (CSP) for hierarchical architectures, which is
059
      particularly well suited for navigation tasks. HILOW relies on growing separate parameter subspaces,
060
      for a high-level path-planner policy and a low-level path-follower policy, depending on the task
061   stream (see Figure 1). To properly assess the relevance of HILOW and catalyze further research, we
062   present a comprehensive study of existing methods, introducing new environments and tasks that
063   address the lack of established benchmarks for Continual Offline Reinforcement Learning regarding
064   Goal-Conditioned navigation tasks. While Section 2 reviews the relevant and related literature,
065   Section 3 present the theoretical background that contextualizes our research. In Section 4, we detail
066   our proposed approach. Sections 5.1 and 5.2 presents our experimental methodology, comparing
067   our approach in both novel video-game-like settings with human-authored datasets and classical
068   goal-conditioned environments. Finally, Sections 5.3 to 5.5 present experimental results, evaluating
069
      performance across diverse task sequences with standard CRL metrics.
070   Our main contributions are :
071
072   • HILOW, a novel hierarchical framework for Continual Offline RL, leveraging low-rank subspaces
073     of policies for scalable low-memory adaptation for Goal-Conditioned navigation tasks.
074
      • A large panel of Goal-Conditioned navigation tasks associated with datasets, encompassing both
075
        robotics and video game scenarios with human-authored datasets. We hope this new open-source
076
        benchmark will provide a comprehensive testing ground for future research in this domain.
077
078   • A comprehensive experimental evaluation of HILOW and state-of-the-art CRL methods using our
079     proposed benchmark. Our results demonstrate competitive scalability and adaptability of HILOW,
080     showcasing its ability to handle diverse and complex task sequences across various classical metrics.

      The following section reviews related frameworks and methods to distinguish between various
111
      approaches. While these frameworks share common ground, their settings differ in essential ways.
112   By comparing them, we position our work to better highlight the unique challenges we address.
113
114   Transfer Learning (Da Silva & Costa, 2019; Zhu et al., 2023), along with Multitask Learning
115
      (Zhang & Yang, 2018; Vithayathil Varghese & Mahmoud, 2020), and Meta-Learning (Yu et al.,
      2020; Gupta et al., 2018; Beck et al., 2023) are foundational paradigms in machine learning that aim
116
      to leverage knowledge from multiple tasks to improve learning efficiency and performance. While
117
      these approaches excel at leveraging previously acquired knowledge to enhance learning on new
118   tasks, they may require simultaneous access to all tasks during training and do not inherently address
119   the sequential nature of CRL (Table 1). Thus, their use in continual learning scenarios is limited.

132   Continual Reinforcement Learning (CRL) aims to develop agents capable of learning tasks without
133   forgetting previously acquired knowledge (Khetarpal et al., 2022; Díaz-Rodríguez et al., 2018). CRL
134   methods address the challenge of sequential task learning through various strategies. Replay-Based
135
      methods mitigate forgetting by storing past experiences and replaying them (Rolnick et al., 2019;
      Huang et al., 2021). While effective, they can be impractical due to significant storage requirements
136
      and potential privacy concerns, especially in industrial applications. Regularization techniques like
137
      Elastic Weight Consolidation (EWC) (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017) and L2 regularization (Kumar et al.,
138   2023) introduce constraints on parameter updates, but they may struggle with highly diverse tasks.
139   Architectural approaches such as Progressive Neural Networks (PNNs) (Rusu et al., 2016) modify
140   a networks architecture to accommodate new tasks. Although these methods isolate task-specific
141   parameters, they can become memory-intensive and lack scalability as the number of tasks grows.
142
      Most CRL research focuses on the Online Setting, where agents learn by interacting with the
143
      environment (Wang et al., 2024). In contrast, Offline CRL involves learning solely from fixed datasets
144   (Isele & Cosgun, 2018; Liu et al., 2024). While Offline CRL is more practical when data collection
145   is demanding, it presents unique challenges due to the inability to gather new data. Benchmark
146   environments are crucial for evaluating and comparing CRL methods. Existing benchmarks like
147   Continual World (Wolczyk et al., 2021) or CORA (Powers et al., 2022) focus on the online setting
148   and do not specifically address goal-conditioned tasks. To our knowledge, there are no standardized
149   offline benchmarks for Goal-Conditioned CRL (Liu et al., 2022b; Park et al., 2024).
150   Among architectural approaches certain methods, such as Continual Subspace of Policies (CSP)
151   (Gaya et al., 2022; 2023) or Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021), are relevant for both
152   online and offline settings. CSP uses policies based on a subspace of neural networks (Wortsman
153   et al., 2021). However, it has been primarily applied in online settings, leveraging algorithms like
154   Soft Actor-Critic (Haarnoja et al., 2018), and has not been extensively explored in an offline context.
155   LoRA enhances neural network adaptability by introducing low-rank updates to weight matrices,
156   significantly reducing the number of parameters required for each new task. LoRA has been applied
157   in transfer learning (Hu et al., 2021), multitask learning (Liu et al., 2023), and continual learning for
158
      supervised tasks (Wistuba et al., 2023). Its potential for memory efficiency makes it suitable for both
      online and offline CRL applications, but its integration into offline CRL remains limited.
159
160   Hierarchical Policies (HP) structure the decision-making process into multiple levels. In CRL, HP
161   facilitate learning by allowing separate components to focus on different aspects of a task. However,
      existing approaches often rely on complex models, such as large language models (Pan et al., 2024),


                                                                     3

162
      or are tailored to meta-learning and multitask learning tasks (Shu et al., 2018; Chua et al., 2023). Our
163   approach integrates HP into an offline CRL framework, enabling lightweight flexible adaptation.
164
165   Despite the aforementioned advancements, our work stands out by specifically addressing the unique
166
      challenges of Offline CRL, without relying on data retention strategies. To the best of our knowledge,
      no benchmarks exist for Offline CRL in Goal-Conditioned navigation settings, underscoring the
167
      novelty of our framework and the importance of our introduced benchmark.

250
251   The HILOW Learning Algorithm manages hierarchical policies through distinct subspaces, each
252   specializing to different aspects of task adaptation. This specialization promotes both efficiency and
253
      scalability, allowing our framework to handle diverse and sequential tasks in an offline setting.
254   Initial Anchor Training : We begin by training the initial anchor parameters θ1h ∈ Θh and θ1l ∈ Θl
255   on the first task T1 . The anchor weights α1h ∈ ∆1 and α1l ∈ ∆1 are set to (1), indicating reliance on
256   the initial anchors. This establishes the foundational subspaces for high-level and low-level policies.
257   Training on Subsequent Tasks : For each new task Tk and for each of the two considered subspaces,
258   the algorithm performs the following steps to efficiently adapt the learning policy :
259

                                                           h          l

303   Extending a subspace involves integrating a new anchor parameter θN +1 and its corresponding weight
304
      αcurr into the existing set of anchors. This process allows the model to incorporate task-specific

305

309   control over how much each anchor contributes to the final policy.

         5.2              C ONTINUAL R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING BASELINES
382
383     We compare our method to several CRL strategies relevant to our setting, as described in Section
384     3. All baselines are built on the same Hierarchical Imitation Learning backbone and detailed in
385     Appendix A.4.
386
        The Single Naive Strategy (SC1) trains a single policy from scratch on the latest dataset and applies
387
        it to all tasks, while the Expanding Naive Strategy (SCN) trains and saves a new policy for each
388     task. The Single Finetuning Strategy (FT1) adapts a single policy across tasks but suffers from
389     catastrophic forgetting. In contrast, the Expanding Finetuning Strategy (FTN) retains a separate
390     policy for each task, preserving knowledge but increasing memory use. The Freeze Strategy (FZ)
391     trains a policy on the first task and applies it unchanged to all subsequent tasks. More advanced
392     methods include L2-Regularization (L2) (Kumar et al., 2023), which adds a penalty to the loss
393     function according to the previous weight changes between tasks, and Elastic Weight Consolidation
394     (EWC) (Kirkpatrick et al., 2017), which supposedly improves L2 by penalizing important weights
395     using the Fisher Information Matrix. Progressive Neural Networks (PNN) (Rusu et al., 2016) add
396
        new layers for each task, using lateral connections to transfer useful representations while avoiding
        interference. Finally, we adapt Continual Subspace of Policies (CSP) (Gaya et al., 2023), originally
397
        designed for online learning, for offline use while maintaining Q-function learning.

484   Adapting CSP to Offline CRL : CSP-O The original Continual Subspace of Policies (CSP) (Gaya
485   et al., 2023) leverages Soft Actor-Critic (SAC) and replay buffers to evaluate policies by learning a
      Q-function. However, in an offline setting, replay buffers are impractical. To adapt CSP for offline


                                                                 9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      CRL, we replace SAC with Hierarchical Imitation Learning (HBC) and eliminate the reliance on
487   replay buffers, creating a single subspace that encapsulates both high-level and low-level policy
488   parameters. We refer to this approach as CSP in Table 3. Despite these modifications, we observe that
489   the Q-function’s predictions become nearly independent of actions due to the optimality of expert
490   data, limiting CSP’s effectiveness in offline scenarios. To address the limitations of the adapted
491   CSP, we develop CSP-O, an improved offline adaptation that employs a loss function-based selection
492   criterion instead of a Q-function. CSP-O maintains a single subspace but enhances policy evaluation
493   by directly minimizing the loss with respect to the expert behavior, thereby improving performance
494   in offline settings.

      6   D ISCUSSION
513
514   In this work, we introduced HILOW, a framework that combines hierarchical imitation learning with
515   low-rank subspace adaptations for offline continual reinforcement learning. Our results show that
516   our framework effectively balances performance and memory usage across diverse environments,
517
      including classical mazes and complex video games. By using separate subspaces for high-level and
      low-level policies, it efficiently adapts to new tasks while mitigating forgetting. Compared to other
518
      methods, HILOW offers a strong trade-off between adaptability and resource efficiency.
519
520   Future work could study to which extent HILOW can scale to more complex CRL settings, e.g.
521   with chaotic task streams. Additionally, while HILOW performs well with expert data, scenarios
522   with imperfect expert trajectories could pose challenges. Towards such settings, improved subspace
523
      evaluations procedured could be studies, e.g. integrating Inverse Reinforcement Learning (IRL)
      (Arora & Doshi, 2021; Ho & Ermon, 2016), to refine Q-function learning, providing better scoring of
524
      sampled policies. Exploring adaptive ranks during subspace extension could enhance the framework’s
525
      flexibility and scalability with task complexity. Overall, HILOW makes a significant step toward
526   addressing offline continual learning challenges.

      A.4   TASK S TREAMS
856
857   A task stream refers to a sequence of environments and corresponding datasets that an agent learns
858   from over time. Each task in the stream introduces new environmental variations, changes in
859   dynamics, or modifications to the observation and action spaces, simulating the possible evolving
860   challenges in real-world scenarios. They may build upon previously learned skills, testing both
861   short-term adaptability and long-term memory retention. We consider several classical metrics,
862   namely : Performance (PER), Backward Transfer (BWT), Forward Transfer (FWT), and Relative
863   Memory Size (MEM). These metrics enable us to assess the agent’s continual learning capabilities by
      evaluating its ability to generalize across tasks, preserve learned knowledge, while being scalable.

       Elastic Weight Consolidation (EWC) Kirkpatrick et al. (2017). This strategy has been designed
1027   to mitigate catastrophic forgetting in continual learning. It achieves this by selectively slowing down
1028   learning on certain weights based on their importance to previously learned tasks. This importance is
1029   measured by the Fisher Information Matrix, which quantifies the sensitivity of the output function to
1030   changes in the parameters.
1031
       EWC introduces a quadratic penalty to the loss function, constraining the parameters close to their
1032
       values from previous tasks, where the strength of the penalty is proportional to each parameter’s
1033   importance. This allows the model to retain performance on previous tasks while continuing to learn
1034   new tasks effectively.
1035
1036
       However this method struggles for navigation tasks due to the penalty for updating parameters,
       making it difficult to adapt to tasks like inverse actions. This rigidity is problematic in complex
1037
       environments where different tasks demand flexibility. As a result, EWC is limited in effectively
1038
       handling tasks requiring greater adaptation.

       E.2   C OMPUTATIONAL C OMPLEXITY AND E FFICIENCY
1516
1517   Evaluating the computational efficiency of our HILOW framework is essential to demonstrate its
1518   practicality in continual offline goal-conditioned reinforcement learning. Our experiments across
1519   three sets of task streams — PointMaze, AntMaze, and Godot — show that HILOW introduces
1520   minimal additional complexity compared to baseline methods, with the main overhead coming from
1521   the evaluation of sampled anchor weights within the policy subspace.
1522   In contrast, methods like CSP and PNN incur higher computational costs. CSP slows computation by
1523   requiring the learning of a value function, while PNN’s ever-growing architecture requires learning
1524   connectors to previous layers outputs during both training and inference. These factors result in
1525   significant overhead, especially in high-dimensional environments such as Godot.
