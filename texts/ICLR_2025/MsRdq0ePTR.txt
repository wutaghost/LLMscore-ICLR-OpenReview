009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Foundation Models (FMs) are increasingly integrated with external data sources
012            and tools to handle complex tasks, forming FM-integrated systems with different
013            modalities. However, such integration introduces new security vulnerabilities, es-
014            pecially when FMs interact dynamically with the system environments. One of the
015            most critical threats is the prompt injection attack, where adversaries inject mali-
016
               cious instructions into the input environment, causing the model to deviate from
               user-intended behaviors. To advance the study of prompt injection vulnerabili-
017
               ties in FM-integrated systems, a comprehensive benchmark is essential. However,
018
               existing benchmarks fall short in two key areas: 1) they primarily focus on text-
019            based modalities, lacking thorough analysis of diverse threats and attacks across
020            more integrated modalities such as code, web pages, and vision; and 2) they rely
021            on static test suites, failing to capture the dynamic, adversarial interplay between
022            evolving attacks and defenses, as well as the interactive nature of agent-based
023            environments. To bridge this gap, we propose the Prompt Injection Benchmark
024            for FM-integrated Systems (FSPIB), which offers comprehensive coverage across
025            various dimensions, including task modalities, threat categories, various attack
026            and defense algorithms. Furthermore, FSPIB is interactive and dynamic, with
027
               evaluations conducted in interactive environments, and features a user-friendly
               front end that supports extensible attacks and defenses for ongoing research. By
028
               analyzing the performance of baseline prompt injection attacks and defenses, our
029
               benchmark highlights the prevalence of security vulnerabilities in FM-integrated
030            systems and reveals the limited effectiveness of existing defense strategies, under-
031            scoring the urgent need for further research into prompt injection mitigation.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   The rapid advancements of foundation models (FMs), including large language models (LLMs)
037   (Touvron et al., 2023; OpenAI, 2023b; Anthropic, 2024) and vision language models (VLMs) (Liu
038   et al., 2024b;a; OpenAI, 2024a), have significantly enhanced their instruction-following capabili-
039
      ties. Based on it, FMs have been integrated with external data sources and tools for more complex
      tasks and autonomous processes, leading to the development of FM-integrated systems. Different
040
      practical usage scenarios of FMs separate the systems into applications and agents. FM-integrated
041
      applications (LangChain, 2023; Weber, 2024) focus on answering specific user requests based on
042   external data collected from various sources, such as web browsing and file reading. For instance, the
043   current GPT-4 model would answer user questions with the support data acquired from web searches
044   and user-uploaded files. In contrast, FM-integrated agents (Gravitas, 2023; Yao et al., 2022b; Wang
045   et al., 2023) focus on autonomous task execution by interacting with environments using various
046   tools. For example, the WebShop agent (Yao et al., 2022a) interacts with a web HTML environment
047   to automatically complete online shopping tasks by utilizing functions such as search and click.
048   Although FM-integrated systems effectively adapt FMs to real-world scenarios, they also introduce
049   new security threats. One of the most significant threats is the prompt injection attack, (Greshake
050   et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023b; Wu et al., 2024b; Harang, 2023; Willison, 2023b;a) where malicious
051   instructions injected into external data could allow attackers to manipulate FMs for their harmful
052   purposes instead of following user instructions. Prompt injection attacks have ranked as a fore-
053   most threat for LLM-integrated applications by OWASP (OWASP, 2023). This threat becomes even
      more severe when FMs are integrated with various tools. For instance, Capitella (2024) has demon-

055   book-selling website can be compromised to automatically perform false refunds, causing signifi-

057   a comprehensive analysis of prompt injections against FM-integrated systems.

085   To advance research on prompt injections in FM-integrated systems, the development of a compre-
086   hensive benchmark is essential for evaluating robustness against various prompt injection threats.
087
      However, existing benchmarks (Liu et al., 2024d; Yi et al., 2023; Zhan et al., 2024; Debenedetti
      et al., 2024) primarily focus on single text-based modality, which limits their applicability as FM-
088
      integrated systems increasingly incorporate diverse modalities such as code, web pages, and vision.
089
      The threat levels are also limited to either specific applications or agents, lacking a unified analysis.
090   Furthermore, previous benchmarks rely on static test suites, failing to capture the dynamic, adversar-
091   ial interplay between evolving attacks and defenses, as well as the interactive nature of agent-based
092   environments. While the recently proposed AgentDojo benchmark (Debenedetti et al., 2024) makes
093   a significant contribution to offer an interactive environment and dynamic benchmarks that allow
094   for the inclusion of extensible prompt injection attacks and defenses, we found it still has certain
095   limitations (e.g., task modalities, unified analysis among different systems).
096   Given these limitations, we summarize that an ideal prompt injection benchmark should possess the
097   following properties: (1) Comprehensive coverage across various task modalities; (2) Unified anal-
098   ysis of prompt injection threats in different levels for both applications and agents; and (3) Dynamic
099   framework with evolving attack and defense algorithms in interactive environments. To achieve
100   the aforementioned ideal prompt injection benchmark for FM-integrated systems, we introduce our
101   Prompt Injection Benchmark for FM-integrated Systems (FSPIB), as illustrated in Figure 1. The
102   key contributions of FSPIB are outlined as follows:
103   Coverage of various task modalities. Our benchmark encompasses a wide range of task modal-
104   ities within FM-integrated system environments, including document reading, web browsing, code
105   interactions, operation system commands, tool usage, and multimodal applications. Specifically, we
106   assess FM-integrated applications that interact with external data sources from web pages, codes,
107   documents, and images. FSPIB also broadly evaluates FM-integrated agents and covers various
      types of them, including tool, code, web, OS, and vision agents.

      Analyze two distinct levels of prompt injection threats. Considering the variety of threats from
109   prompt injections, we categorize them into two distinct levels: the information level and the action
110   level, which are applicable to FM-integrated applications and agents, respectively. The information-
111   level threats concern injections that lead to deviated output information, with fine-grained aspects
112   including Information Leakage, Goal Hijacking, and Response Refusal. In contrast, the action-level
113   threats focus on injected prompts that cause the model to execute harmful actions, including both
114   Adversarial Action and Parameter Manipulation.
115   User-Friendly front-end for dynamic benchmark with evolving attack and defense algorithms.
116   We provide an intuitive, easy-to-use front-end interface that supports extensible attack and defense
117   methods for adversarial interplay studies. To provide an initial evaluation within our benchmark,
118   we provide five baseline prompt injection attacks and three baseline defenses. For prompt injection
119   attack analysis, we systematically categorize the methods inspired by empirical attack examples
120   from Toyer et al. (2023), offering a foundational understanding of the prompt injection properties.
121   Through the evaluation of baseline attacks and defenses, we find that FM-integrated systems remain
122   highly vulnerable to prompt injection attacks, even with the application of basic defenses. This
123   underscores the urgent need for more comprehensive research into prompt injection vulnerabilities,
124   particularly within FM-integrated systems.

127   2    R ELATED W ORK
128
129   FM-integrated Systems. To extend FMs to broader scenarios, FM-integrated systems have been
130   proposed to combine FMs with external data sources and tools. Two primary technical approaches
131   are used to implement FM-integrated systems. The first involves fine-tuning the base FMs with
132
      tool-usage examples, as demonstrated in works like Toolformer (Schick et al., 2024), Gorilla (Patil
      et al., 2023), and ToolLLM (Qin et al., 2023). While effective, this fine-tuning approach can be
133
      resource-intensive for developers. As a result, an alternative method leveraging the in-context learn-
134
      ing capabilities of FMs has gained prominence. This approach is now widely used in systems such
135   as ReAct (Yao et al., 2022b), Mind2web (Deng et al., 2024), and AutoGPT (Gravitas, 2023).
136
137   Prompt Injection Attacks and Defenses. Prompt injection attacks occur when attackers insert
138
      malicious instructions to alter FMs’ behaviors. These attacks can be direct (inserting instructions
      into the input prompt) (Perez & Ribeiro, 2022; Toyer et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023; Kang et al., 2024)
139
      or indirect (injecting instructions into environments that FMs interact with) (Greshake et al., 2023;
140
      Liu et al., 2023b; Wu et al., 2024a;b; Liu et al., 2024c). This work focuses on indirect prompt
141   injection attacks within the environments of FM-integrated systems.
142
143   Prompt injection defense strategies fall into two categories: training-time and test-time defenses. At
144
      training time, adversarial examples are integrated during fine-tuning to enhance robustness against
      prompt injection attacks (Chen et al., 2024b; Yi et al., 2023; Wallace et al., 2024). Additionally, Chen
145
      et al. (2024b) introduces special tokens to hide delimiters from attackers. For test-time defense,
146
      prompt designs are employed to separate user instructions from data and prevent responses from
147   malicious inputs (Liu et al., 2023b; Hines et al., 2024; Yi et al., 2023).
148
149   Prompt Injection Benchmarks. Several benchmarks (Liu et al., 2024d; Yi et al., 2023; Zhan et al.,
150
      2024; Debenedetti et al., 2024) have been proposed to study prompt injections, yet none has offered
      a comprehensive evaluation. Some of the benchmarks (Liu et al., 2024d; Yi et al., 2023) only focus
151
      on model-level injections, leaving the threats in interactive environments unexplored. Although two
152
      recent benchmarks Zhan et al. (2024) and Debenedetti et al. (2024) study prompt injections in LLM
153   agents, they are still restricted to single text modality with limited analysis of different threat levels.
154   Besides, only Debenedetti et al. (2024) considers a dynamic benchmark with editable tasks and
155   attacks. However, it is still far from easy-to-use for prompt injection research.

183   3.1     TASK M ODALITIES
184
185   Our FSPIB encompasses a broad spectrum of task modalities that are collected from practical inter-
186   actions with the environment, covering both FM-integrated applications and agents.
187
188   3.1.1    FM- INTEGRATED A PPLICATION
189
      FM-integrated applications involve incorporating external data sources (e.g., textual documents,
190
      code, web pages) into the input of FMs to generate more accurate responses. In this paper, we
191   implement FM-integrated applications by directly concatenating extra data sources following user
192   instructions. The prompt templates used for the Doc, Code, and Web Applications are detailed in
193   Appendix A. For the Vision Application, since images are intrinsically included by the FMs, no
194   additional prompts are required. Details for each application are outlined as follows:
195
      Doc Application leverages FMs to answer questions based on external text sources such as docu-
196
      ments, files, or articles. For the evaluation, we select examples from SQuAD (Rajpurkar et al., 2018),
197   including over 500 Wikipedia articles with over 10,000 crowd-sourced questions. The articles serve
198   as external data sources, while the questions act as user instructions.
199
200
      Code Application assists FMs in understanding the provided code. CodeQA dataset (Liu & Wan,
      2021) is applied by us with the source codes as external data and the questions as user instructions.
201
      This dataset provides 119,778 question-answer pairs for Java and 70,085 question-answer pairs for
202
      Python, including problems of functionality, purpose, properties, and workflows.
203
204   Web Application specializes in following instructions based on external information within HTML
205   pages using FMs. To evaluate in real-world scenarios, we employ the WebSRC dataset (Chen et al.,
206
      2021), which contains 0.44 million question-answer pairs from 6.5K web pages with HTML source
      code, screenshots, and metadata. In this case, the HTML source code serves as the external data,
207
      while the questions act as user instructions.
208
209   To evaluate prompt injections in the Doc, Code, and Web Applications, we randomly sampled 100
210   question-answer pairs and corresponding external data from each dataset to form the test set. To
211   ensure that the external content does not exceed the maximum text processing length of the FMs,
212
      we selected contexts with fewer than 5,000 characters during the sampling process.
213   Vision Application directly uses Vision Language Models (VLMs) as the application. To construct
214   the test set for evaluation, we choose to sample 100 different image-related question-answer pairs
215   from the mixture of several datasets including MMStar (Chen et al., 2024a), POPE (Li et al., 2023),
      TextVQA (Singh et al., 2019), and ScienceQA (Lu et al., 2022).

      3.1.2    FM- INTEGRATED AGENT
217
218   Unlike the applications, FM-integrated agents use FMs to interact with environments for au-
219   tonomously performing complex tasks such as web shopping, and code editing. In our FSPIB,
220   all agent tasks are executed using the ReAct framework (Yao et al., 2022b), which leverages the
221   in-context learning capabilities of FMs for interactive task solving. Specifically, we provide a set
222   of functions with descriptions in the system prompt, allowing the FMs to generate next-step actions
223   based on observations from environmental interactions. The prompt templates used for ReAct are
224   detailed in Appendix A. Details for each agent are shown as follows:
225   Tool Agent aims to transform FMs into agents capable of interacting with various tool function
226   APIs. Following the Tool-Operation setting in AgentBoard (Ma et al., 2024), we collect test exam-
227   ples for our tool agent in two specific environments: TODO List and Google Sheet. Tool Agent can
228   help users efficiently manage and revise their agenda or spreadsheets. Through practical interaction
229   experiments, we gathered a total of 80 test examples, 40 for each tool usage environment.
230   Code Agent focuses on using FMs to perform tasks similar to software engineers, such as writing
231   or debugging code based on user requests. For evaluation, we randomly sampled 50 test cases from
232   SWE-Bench (Jimenez et al., 2023), which contains 2,294 software engineering problems derived
233   from GitHub issues and corresponding pull requests across 12 popular Python repositories. To solve
234   these issues, the FMs are tasked with automatically identifying the correct location of the issue
235   within the repository and making the necessary code edits by interacting with a Docker environment,
236   utilizing various GitHub and Linux commands.
237   Web Agent in FSPIB is designed to assist users in finding and purchasing items on a shopping
238   website. Following the WebShop framework (Yao et al., 2022a), we construct our interactive envi-
239   ronment for the Web Agent using an HTML-based website with 1.18 million real-world products
240   and 12,087 crowd-sourced user instructions. The Web Agent leverages the structural information
241   of the processed website to make decisions on the next step, such as searching, navigating back, or
242   clicking, which are the basic interactions with a real browser. Finally, we randomly selected 78 test
243
      cases from the WebShop dataset for our evaluation.
244   OS Agent plays a crucial role as FM-integrated systems evolve to integrate with real operating sys-
245   tems for more complex tasks. Following the setup in AgentBench (Liu et al., 2023a), our OS Agent
246   interacts with the bash environment in an Ubuntu Docker container, responding to human requests
247   of running basic bash commands, managing the file system, and executing code. For evaluation in
248   FSPIB, we randomly sampled 50 user requests from the original AgentBench OS dataset.
249   Vision Agent differs significantly from other agents as it interacts with visual environments obtained
250   from user interface screenshots. Following the SeeAct (Zheng et al., 2024) framework, our Vision
251   Agent interacts with authentic webpage screenshots. To ensure the prominence of web content,
252   we dynamically inject prompts at five distinct locations on the webpage. For evaluation within the
253   FSPIB framework, we carefully selected 80 user requests from the SeeAct dataset.

      3.2     T HREAT L EVELS
256
257   To comprehensively evaluate prompt injection threats in FM-integrated systems, we categorize the
258   threats into two distinct levels based on whether the system is designed for information-augmented
259   responses or decision-making actions. We further include sub-categories for each threat level to
260   allow for a more detailed analysis.
261
262
      3.2.1    I NFORMATION - LEVEL T HREATS
263
264   Information-level threats in prompt injections primarily occur in FM-integrated applications, where
265   models return natural language responses. In these scenarios, attackers can inject malicious instruc-
266   tions, causing the FM to provide inaccurate or unintended information. Here we apply the same
267   attack goals across the Doc, Code, and Web Applications. However, due to the limited ability of
268   FMs to process text injected into images, we designed a simplified version of each threat for Vision
269   Application. Details of the attack goals for each information-level threat are shown in Appendix B.1.
      The sub-categories of information-level prompt injection threats are outlined below:


                                                        5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
      Information Leakage occurs when the attackers attempt to extract hidden information from the
271   system prompt. In our initial experiments, we observed that FMs generally refuse to leak private
272   information due to the safety alignment. Since FSPIB does not consider safety alignment during
273   prompt injections, we use privacy-unrelated information as the leakage targets.
274
      Goal Hijacking mainly explores the threats where attackers aim to divert the FMs from the users’
275
      intentions by pursuing a hijacked goal. Here we simply set the hijacking goal as repeating specific
276
      words or sentences such as “ATTACK COMPLETE” and “Hi! How are you today?”.
277
278   Response Refusal instructs FMs to refuse to execute any user instructions, effectively undermining
279   the functionality of FM-integrated applications by causing them to give abstaining responses.
280
281   3.2.2    ACTION - LEVEL T HREATS
282   Action-level threats of prompt injection are analyzed in FM-integrated agents, focusing on the ac-
283   tions performed during interactions with injected environments. Since action spaces vary across
284   agents, we design specific attack goals for each task example. Refer to Appendix B.2 for detailed
285   attack goals of action-level threats. The two sub-categories of action-level threats are outlined below:
286
      Adversarial Action directly injects the malicious actions to alter the agents’ behavior for adver-
287
      sarial purposes. For example, in the OS Agent, instead of editing a specific file following the user
288   instruction, an attacker might instruct the OS to execute harmful commands such as “rm -rf ./”.
289
290   Parameter Manipulation differs from Adversarial Action by parameter modification within the
291
      same action. This type of injection is harder to detect as it subtly alters the action outputs. For
      instance, in the Tool Agent, instead of sharing a Google Sheet with User A, a malicious injection
292
      could manipulate the parameters to share with unwanted User B within the same “share”function.

      3.3     E VALUATION P IPELINE
296   In this section, we present the evaluation pipeline for FSPIB, which covers a wide range of task
297   modalities and threat levels. To streamline the evaluation process and facilitate research into the
298   dynamic, adversarial interplay between evolving attacks and defenses in practical interactive envi-
299   ronments, our evaluation pipeline can be completed with the following steps:
300
301   3.3.1    I NTERACTIVE E NVIRONMENT WITH P OTENTIAL I NJECTIONS
302
303
      The first step in our pipeline is setting up the interactive environment for FM-integrated systems. In
      FM-integrated applications, the environment includes external data sources that interact with user
304
      requests. Applications retrieve and integrate this data into the input prompt during operation. The
305
      environments for FM-integrated agents are more complex, requiring the simulation of practical sce-
306   narios. This involves processing action outputs from the FMs, executing the corresponding actions,
307   and returning observations to the agents.
308
309
      For prompt injection evaluation, it is also essential to specify how instruction injections are intro-
      duced into the environment. The formats and positions of these prompt injections are also care-
310
      fully designed. For instance, in a code-interactive environment, injection instructions are embedded
311
      within comments rather than plain text, ensuring they do not violate code syntax. Details of the
312   injection formats and positions for each application and agent are provided in Appendix C.
313
314   3.3.2    U SER -F RIENDLY F RONT-E ND WITH BASELINE ATTACKS AND D EFENSES
315
316   The next step in our evaluation pipeline is to configure the attack or defense strategy for prompt
317   injection evaluation through our user-friendly front end. To ensure ease of use, prompt templates
318   for specific attacks or defenses can be directly set as inputs in the front end for evaluation. We also
319   provide baseline attacks and defenses to support the initial study of prompt injections within FSPIB.
320
      We present a usage example of our user-friendly front-end in Appendix F. Detailed descriptions and
      corresponding prompts of these baseline attack and defense strategies are presented in Appendix D.
321
322   Baseline Attacks: To enable a comprehensive study of prompt injection attack properties, we sys-
323   tematically analyze and categorize existing attack methods, drawing inspiration from various empiri-
      cal methods in Toyer et al. (2023). We define the following baseline prompt injection attacks: Direct


                                                         6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      Attack (DA, a straightforward approach that involves directly integrating the injection prompts into
325   the system environment without modification.); Instruction Identification Attack (IIA, strength-
326   ening injected instructions while disregarding previous ones for better identification of the user
327   instructions.); Boundary Confusion Attack (BCA, confusing the FMs by completing the response
328   to the previous instruction in the assistant role, then switching to a user role to introduce the injected
329   instructions.); Hide Instruction Attack (HIA, applying role play to let the FM act as a security sys-
330   tem to save the world by executing injected instructions.); and Combined Attack (CA, combining
331   multiple prompt injection attack methods including IIA, BCA and HIA.).
332   Baseline Defenses: Our evaluation pipeline also includes several widely adopted baseline defense
333   methods: Instructional Prevention Defense (IPD, directly incorporating defense prompts into the
334   system prompt, instructing the FM to disregard any additional instructions in the environment and
335   focus solely on the user’s input.), Sandwich Prevention Defense (SPD, inserting reminders in sys-
336   tem prompts and after input prompts to remind the FMs to focus on the user instructions.), and Data
337   Isolation Defense (DID, using the XML tags around the data as the delimiters for the isolation.).
338
339   3.3.3    I NJECTION P ERFORMANCE UNDER M ULTIPLE M ETRICS
340
341   After setting up the interactive environments and dynamic front end, the FM-integrated systems are
342   executed to evaluate injection performance. For FM-integrated applications, results are directly ob-
343   tained from the outputs of the FMs, enabling straightforward evaluation. In contrast, FM-integrated
344   agents require multiple steps of interaction with the environment, allowing for various evaluation
345
      metrics to assess injection performance. The performance of these agents can be measured through
      both output actions and interactions with the environment. As a result, our evaluation pipeline in-
346
      cludes multiple metrics, allowing us to compare the output actions with ground truth answers or
347
      check the final environment state to determine if the attack goals are achieved.

356   4.1     E XPERIMENTAL S ETTINGS
357
358
      We present our detailed experimental settings below, including the foundation models used for
      prompt injection evaluation and corresponding evaluation metrics.
359
360
361   4.1.1    F OUNDATION M ODELS
362
363
      For FSPIB, we support the evaluation of both API-based and open-source foundation models. For
      API-based models, we assess prompt injection vulnerabilities in FM-integrated systems using GPT-
364
      4o mini (OpenAI, 2024b), an optimized variant of GPT-4 designed for enhanced performance and
365
      efficiency in processing multimodal tasks. However, current open-source foundation models still
366   lack the capability to serve as the backbone for both LLM and VLM-based systems. As a result, our
367   FSPIB employs Llama 3 (Meta, 2024) as the backbone for LLMs and Qwen-VL (Bai et al., 2023)
368   for VLMs in our prompt injection evaluations.
369
370
      4.1.2    E VALUATION M ETRICS
371
372   Benign Utility evaluates the normal utility of FM-integrated systems in the absence of prompt in-
373   jection attacks. More specifically, we use the concept of LLM-as-a-Judge (Zheng et al., 2023)
374   for FM-integrated applications to assess whether the generated responses accurately respond to the
375   user’s request utilizing the additional data. We then compute the fraction of examples where this cri-
376   terion is met. Details of the LLM-as-a-Judge setting and prompts are presented in Appendix E. For
377   agents, since the direct output is an action with parameters, we can evaluate benign utility through
      exact matching, calculating the ratio of responses that exactly match the ground truth actions.


                                                          7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      Additionally, because introducing new prompting strategies may lead to trade-offs between defense
379   and normal utility of the system (Mo et al., 2024), we evaluate the Benign Utility of each defense
380   method to examine whether the benign performance is impacted by defense prompts.
381
382
      Attack Success Rate measures the fraction of test examples where the attack goals are achieved.
      For information-level threats targeting FM-integrated applications, as outlined in Section 3.2.1, these
383
      goals can be evaluated using keyword matching. On the other hand, for action-level threats in FM-
384
      integrated agents, all attack goals can be generally presented by a malicious action with parameters.
385   Exact matching is applied to assess whether the agents accurately execute the target actions.
386
387   Multi-turn Evaluation for Agents. When evaluating the Benign Utility and Attack Success Rate
388
      of FM-integrated agents, our default evaluation is to compare the agents’ immediate next-step ac-
      tions with the ground truth actions. Though this evaluation is effective, it does not account for the
389
      interactive process of FM-integrated agents. In some cases, agents may pursue action goals not in
390
      the immediate next step, but in subsequent steps. Therefore, we propose a set of more practical
391   evaluation metrics for FM-integrated agents. Given that our interactive systems can directly engage
392   with environments, we can evaluate whether the benign or attack goals are achieved by observing
393   the corresponding changes in the environment after the whole agent processes end. We refer to
394   these new metrics as Multi-turn Benign Utility and Multi-turn Attack Success Rate, which more
395   comprehensively assess the agents’ performance over multi-turn interactions.

527   5       C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this paper, we introduce a novel prompt injection benchmark for FM-integrated systems, named
530   FSPIB. Compared to previous works, FSPIB provides broader coverage across multiple dimensions:
531   (i) task modalities, encompassing both applications and agents; (ii) threat categories, including both
532   information-level and action-level threats; and (iii) multiple baseline prompt injection attacks and
533   defense strategies. Additionally, our benchmark is interactive and dynamic, featuring interactive
534   environments and an engaging front end. The evaluation of baseline methods within FSPIB reveals
535   that the risk of prompt injection is prevalent across current FM-integrated systems. Significant
536
      efforts are still required to enhance their security.
537   Limitations. Our benchmark currently focuses on a single framework for FM-integrated systems.
538   However, more advanced frameworks, such as those incorporating function-calling capabilities in
539   FMs (OpenAI, 2023a), have been proposed to enhance the performance of FM-integrated systems.
      As part of future work, we plan to expand our benchmark to include more frameworks.

849   D.1      BASELINE ATTACKS
850
851   Here, we provide details of the five baseline attacks used for evaluating the robustness of FM-
852   integrated systems in FSPIB. The full attack prompts can be found in Table 10.
853   Direct Attack (DA): A straightforward approach involves directly integrating the injection prompts
854   into the system environment without modification, which offers a plain injection performance with-
855   out employing additional strategies.
856
      Instruction Identification Attack (IIA): Failure to correctly identify user instructions is one of
857
      the fundamental causes of prompt injection attacks. While current human preference alignments
858   (Ziegler et al., 2019; Ouyang et al., 2022) significantly improve FMs’ instruction-following ability,
859   it remains challenging for the FM to determine which instructions to prioritize. Attack prompts can
860   still override the user instruction by strategies such as ignoring previous instructions, strengthening
861   injected instructions, or following both injected and user instructions (Branch et al., 2022; Perez
862   & Ribeiro, 2022; Rehberger, 2023). In FSPIB, we adopt the strategy of strengthening injected
863   instructions while disregarding previous ones, forming our baseline under Instruction Identification
      Attack.

890   Boundary Confusion Attack (BCA): This method seeks to confuse FMs regarding the boundaries
891   between instructions, external data, and responses (Willison, 2023b). Tactics include adding fake
892   answers to end the current context and simulating the delimiters for data instruction isolation. In our
