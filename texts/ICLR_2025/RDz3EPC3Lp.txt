011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Traditional autonomous driving systems often struggle to harmonize high-level
014            reasoning with low-level control, leading to suboptimal and even unsafe driv-
015            ing behaviors. The emergence of multimodal large language models (MLLMs),
016            capable of processing visual and textual data, presents an opportunity to unify
017            perception and reasoning tasks within a single framework. However, integrat-
018
               ing precise safety knowledge into MLLMs for safe autonomous driving remains
               a significant challenge. To address this, we propose SafeAuto, a novel frame-
019
               work that enhances MLLM-based autonomous driving systems by incorporating
020
               both unstructured and structured knowledge. In particular, we first propose the
021            Place-Dependent Cross-Entropy (PDCE) loss function, which is specifically de-
022            signed to enhance the accuracy of low-level control signal predictions when treat-
023            ing numerical values as text. To explicitly integrate precise safety knowledge into
024            the MLLM to enable safe autonomous driving, we build a reasoning component
025            for SafeAuto, which first parses driving safety regulations into first-order logic
026            rules (e.g., “red light =⇒ stop”) and then integrates these rules into a proba-
027            bilistic graphical model, such as a Markov Logic Network (MLN). The environ-
028            ment attributes, identified by attribute recognition models (e.g., detecting a red
029
               light), are used to form the predicates in MLN. In addition, the environmental at-
               tributes utilized for reasoning are also considered factors in retrieval to construct a
030
               Multimodal Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) model, which aims to learn
031
               from past similar driving experiences more effectively. Extensive experiments
032            demonstrate that SafeAuto significantly outperforms baselines across multiple
033            datasets. By bridging the gap between high-level reasoning and low-level control,
034            SafeAuto paves the way for more accurate, reliable, and safer autonomous driv-
035            ing, facilitating systems that learn effectively from experience, adhere to traffic
036            regulations, and execute precise control actions.

040   Autonomous Driving (AD) systems (Kim et al., 2018; Jin et al., 2023; Hu et al., 2023) have made
041
      significant strides in recent years, yet they often rely on separate modules for high-level decision-
      making (e.g., “the car should slow to a stop”) and low-level control signal prediction (e.g., providing
042
      the specific speed or steering angle for the next few moments). However, these two aspects are
043

044   design often overlooks this correlation, leading to inefficiencies and less cohesive driving behaviors.
045

046   et al., 2023) offer a promising avenue to bridge the gap between high-level reasoning and low-level
047   control in AD. These models provide a unified framework capable of processing and reasoning over
048   multiple data modalities, such as images, videos, and text. Some recent works (Wang et al., 2023;
049   Xu et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024) have begun to leverage MLLMs to generate both high-level action
050   descriptions and low-level control signals in an end-to-end manner. However, these approaches are
051   predominantly data-driven and often fail to perform at human levels due to several limitations.
052   Firstly, for low-level action prediction, current approaches in adapting MLLMs generally follow
053   two fashions. The first fashion treats the prediction of float numbers as text generation (Gruver

118   2   R ELATED W ORK
119   Advancements in autonomous driving have produced comprehensive frameworks like UniAD (Hu
120   et al., 2023), which integrates modules for tracking, mapping, motion prediction, and occupancy
121   estimation for low-level planning. However, UniAD lacks high-level action descriptions and textual
122   justifications. To address high-level explanations, Kim et al. (2018) proposed an attention-based
123   video-to-text model generating explanations of current driving actions. Similarly, ADAPT (Jin et al.,
124   2023) employs a video Swin Transformer (Liu et al., 2022) to extract video tokens for separate high-
125   level and low-level action predictions.
126   The emergence of MLLMs enables unified end-to-end generation of both high-level and low-level
127   outputs. Most of these works often treat numerical control signals as text, training models using
128
      token prediction with cross-entropy loss. For example, DriveGPT4 (Xu et al., 2024) just treats
      low-level control signals as text, fine-tuning an MLLM to sequentially predict high-level and low-
129
      level actions in a conversational manner using the BDD-X dataset. DriveLM-Agent (Sima et al.,
130
      2023), influenced by RT-2 (Brohan et al., 2023), discretizes waypoints into bins, expanding the
131   tokenizer vocabulary accordingly and fine-tuning the BLIP-2 (Li et al., 2023). While this facilitates
132   end-to-end training, it remains coarse compared to UniAD (Hu et al., 2023), which uses MSE loss.
133   Time-LLM (Jin et al., 2024) decodes numerical predictions directly from output embeddings using a
134   linear layer with MSE loss but diminishes the language model’s autoregressive capabilities, limiting
135   high-level question-answering abilities. Additionally, Tan et al. (2024) suggest that employing the
136   LLM backbone in this way does not enhance regression performance. In contrast, we propose a
137   novel PDCE loss that adapts the cross-entropy loss for numerical training to behave more like MSE
138   loss while preserving the model’s ability to perform high-level question-answering.
139   Further advancements involve integrating perception and planning tools into the MLLM context.
140   Agent-Driver (Mao et al., 2023) incorporates modules from UniAD into an MLLM framework,
141
      serving as a language agent for autonomous driving. OmniDrive (Wang et al., 2024) introduces
      a framework combining 3D perception, reasoning, and planning. However, these methods remain
142
      purely data-driven and lack explicit safety verification for generated actions. Given the safety-critical
143
      nature of autonomous driving, ensuring that output actions are safe and compliant with traffic rules
144   is essential. To address this, we incorporate extracted knowledge—specifically structured traffic
145   rules—into a probabilistic graphical model like a Markov Logic Network (MLN) for explicit post-
146   safety verification. Besides, RAGDriver (Yuan et al., 2024) further enhances reasoning by retrieving
147   similar driving experiences through triplet loss-based metric learning. We extend this approach by
148   developing a more flexible and efficient retrieval system, directly training a joint embedding based on
149   multimodal inputs to learn relative rankings from text similarity. Most importantly, we find that the
150   incorporation of binary structured environmental predicates (e.g., the presence of a stop sign) from
151   the previous reasoning components, namely MLNs, significantly improves retrieval performance.

      Motivation. Recent studies have begun to explore the integration of MLLMs into autonomous
154
      driving systems to enhance both high-level reasoning and low-level control actions. As illustrated
155
      in Figure 1, the MLLM receives a sequence of current driving images or videos, accompanied by tex-
156   tual descriptions of historical control signals, including speed, curvature, acceleration, and course,
157   as inputs. Then, during the conversation, the model is expected to answer three types of queries: (1)
158   High-Level Action Queries: These queries request a textual description of the action that the current
159   ego vehicle is performing or should perform. For example, when asked “What is the action of the
160   ego car?”, the MLLM is expected to respond with an answer like “The car is slowing down to stop”.
161   (2) High-Level Justification Queries: These queries seek an explanation for the action provided by
      the MLLM. For instance, “Why is the ego car doing this?” prompts the model to justify the action,

      such as “Because there is a red light at the upcoming intersection”. (3) Low-Level Action Queries:
163   These queries request specific control signals or trajectories that the vehicle should execute in the fu-

165   like “Speed: 2.69, Course: 0.00”, which can then be translated into actual control commands for
166   the autonomous vehicle. Typically, low-level action queries follow high-level action and justifica-
167   tion queries, ensuring that generated control signals are conditioned on prior high-level actions for

179   3.1   P LACE -D EPENDENT CE LOSS
180
      In existing approaches that utilize MLLMs for autonomous driving, the next-token prediction loss-
181
      specifically, the cross-entropy loss is commonly applied uniformly across all prediction tasks, in-
182   cluding numerical value predictions. However, for numerical regression tasks, it is standard practice
183   to use the Mean Squared Error (MSE) loss, as it directly penalizes the squared difference between
184   the predicted and true values. A fundamental difference between CE loss and MSE loss lies in how
185   they handle proximity to the target: MSE loss decreases as the prediction gets numerically closer to
186   the target value, whereas CE loss does not necessarily exhibit this property.
187   This issue is also empirically observed in the speed prediction distribution when using the original
188   CE loss to fine-tune the MLLM on the BDD-X dataset, as shown in Figure 3 (a), which displays
189   predictions over 200 samples given the same input driving context with temperature as 1.0. As we
190   can see, it reveals two distinct peaks, indicating that predictions closer to the ground truth value
191   of “12.46” do not necessarily occur with higher frequency or lower loss, contrary to the behavior
192
      expected from MSE loss. A natural solution might be to append a MLP to the MLLM to decode the
      output hidden embeddings into corresponding float values and thus use MSE loss for fine-tuning.
193
      However, currently, incorporating an MLP in this manner usually disrupts the autoregressive token
194
      generation capability of the MLLM, rendering it unable to perform high-level action queries or
195   engage in continued conversation. Essentially, the model becomes a pure transformer encoder (Tan
196   et al., 2024) used solely for regression tasks, losing its language generation functionalities critical
197   for interactive and interpretative tasks.
198   PDCE loss. To overcome these challenges, we adapt the CE loss to function more like MSE loss
199   while maintaining textual predictions. Consider the previous example of predicting the float number
200   “12.46.” Originally, the MLLM is trained to maximize the probabilities p(‘1′ ) · p(‘2′ | ‘1′ ) · p(‘.′ |
201   ‘12′ ) · p(‘4′ | ‘12.′ ) · p(‘6′ | ‘12.4′ ) by minimizing the CE loss with one-hot labels. However, as
202   we see before, this does not ensure that predictions closer to the target value—such as “11.99” have
203   a lower loss compared to more distant predictions like “2.46,” because each digit’s probability is
204   treated separately and with equal importance (with all weights set to one).
205   To make the CE loss behave more like MSE loss, we make two modifications: (1) Digit-Level
206   Loss Adjustment: Instead of using one-hot hard target labels for each digit, we employ a soft target
207
      discrete distribution D(µ, σ) centered around the target digit µ, which assigns higher probabilities to
      digits closer to the target, allowing the loss to reflect numerical proximity. Specifically, we leverage
208
      a Gaussian distribution G(µ, σ) to construct D(µ, σ) for each digit (ranging from 0 to 9), while
209
      other distribution methods are also workable. We then compute the loss for each digit as the KL
210   divergence between the target distribution D(µ, σ) and the predicted probability distribution P on
211   all digits output by the MLLM. (2) Place-Level Weighting: Instead of treating all digits equally
212   important, we apply decreasing weights from the first-place digit to the last-place digit based on
213   cumulative probabilities. For example, for float number “12.46”, the weight for the loss on digit ‘2’
214   is the probability of ‘1’ under D(1, σ), and the weight for digit ‘4’ is the cumulative probability of
215   ‘1’ multiplied by the probability of ‘2’ under D(2, σ). In this way, errors in more significant digits
      have a greater impact on the loss, while other weighting designs can also be explored.

247   3.2 K NOWLEDGE -E NHANCED P OST-S AFETY V ERIFICATION WITH S AFE AUTO - REASONING
248
      Currently, most methods for autonomous driving that utilize MLLMs are still purely data-driven.
249   While these data-driven approaches have led to significant advancements, they may not be entirely
250   suitable for safety-critical scenarios like autonomous driving, where reliability and strict adherence
251   to safety regulations are paramount. To address this concern, we propose incorporating Probabilistic
252   Graphical Models (PGMs) to verify the safety of the high-level actions suggested by the underlying
253   MLLM. Specifically, in this paper, we focus on demonstrating how to adopt Markov Logic Net-
254   works to integrate domain knowledge and traffic rules into the decision-making process, while other
255   variants are also applicable. In this section, we begin by explaining what MLNs are and how they
256   apply to our autonomous driving context.
257   Definition. Essentially, an MLN consists of a set of first-order logic formulas, each associated with
258   a weight that reflects the strength or confidence of that formula. These weights allow us to model
259
      uncertainty and handle exceptions in real-world knowledge. In our autonomous driving scenario,
      we use MLNs to model traffic rules and safety constraints. For example, a traffic rule like “If there
260
      is a stop sign, then the vehicle should stop or decelerate” can be represented as the logical formula:
261
      StopSign(x) =⇒ Stop(x) ∨ Decelerate(x), where x represents the current driving
262   scenario. Here, predicates such as StopSign(x), Stop(x), and Decelerate(x) are logical
263   functions that return true or false, indicating whether the condition holds in scenario x.
264   Formally, in MLNs, predicates are logical functions defined over a set of constants V =
265   {v1 , v2 , . . . , vN }, where each vi represents an object or concept in the domain, such as “stop
266   sign” or “red light.” A predicate takes these constants as arguments and returns a truth value:
267   k(·) : V × · · · × V → 0, 1. While formulas are logical statements composed of predicates and
268   logical connectives (e.g., =⇒ , ∧, ∨), with each formula f associated with a weight wf indicating
269   its importance. Then, an MLN defines a joint probability distribution over all possible assignments
      of truth values to the ground predicates (predicates with specific constants assigned). The prob-

278
      Main Action Predicates that encompass potential actions a vehicle might or should take, such as
      Accelerate(x), Stop(x), and TurnLeft(x), among others. While observed predicates
279
      O include: (1) MLLM Action Predicates: This set also includes the number of predicates for
280
      the main action such as MLLMAccelerate(x), MLLMStop(x), and MLLMTurnLeft(x),
281   which indicate the high-level actions suggested by the MLLM. Generally, we would prompt
282   GPT4o to map the high-level action descriptions generated by the MLLM to the corresponding
283   truth values of each predicate. Then, we introduce formulas like MLLMAccelerate(x) ⇒
284   Accelerate(x) to reflect the influence of the original high-level action decision made by the
285   MLLM. (2) Environmental Predicates: These predicates describe the surrounding environment.
286   For example, StopSign(x) indicates whether there is a stop sign in the current driving sce-
287   nario, and SolidRedLight(x) indicates whether the traffic light ahead is red. The truth val-
288   ues of these predicates can be extracted from video data using any object detector. These ob-
289
      ject predicates are combined with the main action predicates to form logical formulas based on
      traffic rules extracted from the California Driver Handbook 1 . Specifically, we first crawled the
290
      handbook and used GPT4o to map the rules into corresponding first-order logical formulas, e.g.,
291
      StopSign(x) =⇒ Stop(x) ∨ Decelerate(x) ∧ ¬PullOver(x), details are deferred
292   to Appendix A. In addition to object-related predicates, we define predicates associated with his-
293   torical control signals. For instance, the predicate HCSTurnLeft(x) determines whether the ego
294   vehicle had recently turned left, based on historical control signals. These predicates are integrated
295   with main action predicates to effectively reflect the vehicle’s inherent tendencies in its actions.
296   Inference. Our goal is to infer the most probable assignment of the unobserved main action predi-
297   cates U given the observed predicates O. To determine the safest and most appropriate action, we
298   perform inference by maximizing the conditional probability P (U|O). Specifically, we seek the as-
299   signment to the main action predicates U that maximizes this probability U ∗ = arg maxU P (U|O).
300   Since the possible worlds for U (i.e., the possible assignments to the main action predicates) are in-
301
      herently limited—a vehicle cannot simultaneously accelerate and decelerate or turn left and right—
      the inference process is thus computationally efficient. The detailed specifics of the possible worlds
302
      can be found in Appendix A.6.
303
      Training. The training of the MLN is straightforward and involves learning the weights wf of the
304
      formulas to maximize P (U|O). In our approach, we utilize a mix of real and simulated data for
305
      training. The real data serves as the ground training data, provided by datasets such as BDD-X,
306   while the simulated data allows us to model various driving conditions. This includes rare or dan-
307   gerous scenarios not present in the real data, by simulating different truth values for the predicates
308   to perform inference. Details are deferred to Appendix A.4.
309   Safety Verification. Initially, we collect observed grounded environmental predicates and the
310   MLLM action predicates from high-level actions generated by the MLLM, extracted through ob-
311   ject detector and prompting with GPT4o. These predicates undergo inference within the trained
312   MLN. If the MLN’s final main action predicate output contradicts the MLLM’s suggested action—
313   suggesting a potential safety violation or a breach of critical traffic rules, we overwrite the original
314   high-level action query based on the MLN’s output and re-prompt the MLLM to generate a new
315   high-level action, as depicted in Figure 1. Further details are available in Appendix A.5.
316   In this way, the MLN serves as a post-verification layer that can override unsafe suggestions from
317
      the MLLM, enhancing the overall reliability of the autonomous driving system.
318   3.3     S AFE AUTO -M ULTIMODAL R ETRIEVAL -AUGMENTED G ENERATION
319   In this section, we introduce a novel training method for constructing a unified embedding that
320   effectively integrates multiple modalities—current driving videos, historical control signals, and ob-
321   served environmental predicate information from Section 3.2. Specifically, we aim to train the joint
322   embedding to mirror the similarity rankings derived from the embedding of the textual descriptions
323

      for the current driving scenarios, which encapsulate the semantic information of all modalities dur-

328
      Different Modality. (1) Image/ Video Embedding: for the image or video modality, we utilize
      the pre-trained LanguageBind encoder (Zhu et al., 2024). This encoder processes an input im-
329
      age in R256×1024 , while processing video into eight frames and generates a video embedding in
330
      R2048×1024 . For simplicity and to reduce computational complexity, we apply global average
331   pooling over the first dimension for both modalities here, resulting in a compressed embedding
332   Zv ∈ R1×1024 for use in subsequent experiments. (2) Control Signal Vector: the control sig-
333   nals are numerical values representing various aspects of the ego vehicle’s historical state, such
334   as speed, curvature, acceleration, and course. In datasets like BDD-X, each of these four types of
335   control signals contains seven historical values (excluding the current frame), resulting in a total of
336   N = 4 × 7 = 28 values. We concatenate these values into a single vector Zc ∈ R1×N , which
337   serves as the initial control signal vector. (3) Environmental Predicate Vector: These environmental
338   predicates introduced in Section 3.2 are binary indicators of certain conditions or observations (e.g.,
339
      presence of a stop sign, status of a traffic light). We encode these predicates into a single binary vec-
      tor Zp ∈ {0, 1}1×M , where M is the number of the whole environmental predicates. Empirically,
340
      we found that including this explicit binary representation significantly boosts retrieval performance,
341
      as demonstrated in Section 5. This enhancement may be attributed to the reduction of noise inherent
342   in the raw video embeddings or control signals; the binary predicates provide a clearer and more
343   robust representation of essential environmental information.
344
345
      Unified Embedding Construction. The central question is: How can we train a unified embedding
      that effectively combines these different modalities for similarity computation and retrieval? A key
346
      insight is that textual descriptions of the current driving scenario typically encompass all relevant
347
      semantic information, reflecting aspects of the video, control signals, and predicates. For instance, a
348   text that concatenates action and justification—such as “The car is slowing to a stop for the red light
349   at the intersection ahead” as shown in Figure 1 captures the essence of all three modalities. This
350   comprehensive representation is particularly valuable for ranking the most similar driving scenarios.
351   However, such ground text descriptions are often not available during evaluation. Building on this
352   intuition, we propose learning a unified embedding that aligns these modalities in a shared space,
353   akin to how text embeddings represent semantic information.
354   Training. Specifically, we first utilize individual projectors to map each input vector—Zv , Zc , and
355   Zp —into aligned embeddings Zv′ , Zc′ , and Zp′ , each with the same dimension and normalized to
356   a unit ℓ2 norm. We then introduce weighting factors wv , wc , and wp to modulate the contribution
357   of each modality in the input aligned embedding. The final unified embedding is then computed
358   as Zu = Projector(wv Zv′ + wc Zc′ + wp Zp′ ) ∈ R1×H . While other design choices are also
359   feasible, we found through experimentation that this configuration provides better controllability.
360   Let Zt ∈ R1×I represent the text embeddings of scenario descriptions, e.g., the concatenation
361   of high-level actions and justifications. Our goal is for the unified embedding Zu to mirror the
362
      relational properties of text embeddings derived from scenario descriptions, particularly in terms
      of similarity rankings. Then, during training, we will first randomly sample a batch of cases with
363
      unified embeddings Zu′ ∈ RB×H and the corresponding text embeddings Zt′ ∈ RB×I with batch
364
      size B. We then minimize the KL divergence between the inter-similarity distributions derived from
365   the unified embeddings Zu′ and those from the text embeddings Zt′ . In specific, we compute the
366   similarity matrices (assuming each row in both Zu′ and Zt′ have been normalized to unit ℓ2 norm)
367   as follows: Su = Zu′ (Zu′ )⊤ and St = Zt′ (Zt′ )⊤ . Then, the loss function aims to minimize the
368   mean of the divergence between the logits Su′ and the target logits St′ /τ across each row. Here,
369   τ is a temperature parameter that adjusts the sharpness of the target probability distributions. A
370   lower τ focuses learning on the most similar (positive) examples, crucial for retrieval tasks where
371   pinpointing the closest matches is essential. By aligning the similarity distributions, we ensure the
372   unified embeddings preserve the relative rankings observed in text embeddings, enabling effective
373
      retrieval without relying on the unavailable ground textual descriptions during inference.

844
845   As outlined in Section 3.2, during safety verification, we initiate the process by extracting ob-
846   served grounded environmental predicates and MLLM action predicates using the object detector
847   and GPT4o. If the final main action predicate output of the Markov Logic Network (MLN) conflicts
848   with the suggested action from MLLM, we modify the high-level action query based on the output
849   of the MLN. In the BDD-X dataset, we replace the original high-level action queries with new ac-
850
      tions inferred from the MLN. For example, if the MLN predicts the possible world represented as
      “Stop(x) = 1” with the highest probability, we append the suggestion “The ego vehicle should
851
      stop” to the high-level action query. This approach facilitates the mapping back to the corresponding
852
      high-level action description and ensures the flow of conversation for subsequent queries.
853
854   In DriveLM, as high-level action queries are presented in a multiple-choice format, the final main
855   action predicate output from the Markov Logic Network (MLN) may not always align directly to
856
      one of the options. In such cases, we filter the available options by the probability of possible worlds.
      Given that MLLM action predicates may map to multiple high-level action descriptions, it is feasible
857
      for multiple valid options to arise simultaneously. We then overwrite the high-level action queries
858
      by removing incorrect options and prompt the MLLM to regenerate an option.
