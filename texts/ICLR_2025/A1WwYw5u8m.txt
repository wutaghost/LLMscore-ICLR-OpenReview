009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
               In this paper, we establish the global convergence of the actor-critic algorithm
012            with a significantly improved sample complexity of O(ϵ−3 ), advancing beyond
013            the existing local convergence results. Previous works provide local convergence
014            guarantees with a sample complexity of O(ϵ−2 ) for bounding the squared gradient
015            of the return, which translates to a global sample complexity of O(ϵ−4 ) using
016            the gradient domination lemma. In contrast to traditional methods that employ
017            decreasing step sizes for both the actor and critic, we demonstrate that a constant
018            step size for the critic is sufficient to ensure convergence in expectation. This key
019            insight reveals that using a decreasing step size for the actor alone is sufficient
020
               to handle the noise for both the actor and critic. Our findings provide theoretical
               support for the practical success of many algorithms that rely on constant step
021
               sizes.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Markov Decision Processes (MDPs) offer a foundational framework for addressing sequential
028   decision-making tasks, with applications spanning diverse fields such as robotics, finance, and
029   healthcare (Sutton & Barto, 2018). The objective of these tasks is to derive a policy that optimally
030   guides decision-making within an environment, relying on local reward signals to inform actions
031   (Puterman, 1994). A variety of approaches have been proposed for discovering such optimal poli-
032   cies or optimizing learning objectives like regret. Beyond value-based methods (Azar et al., 2017;
033
      Jin et al., 2018; Agrawal & Agrawal, 2024; Ji & Li, 2024), policy gradient methods have emerged
      as a prominent and widely adopted class of algorithms (Sutton et al., 2000; Schulman et al., 2017).
034
      Gradient-based methods are highly favorable to practice due to their easy adaptability to various
035
      policy parametrizations, structured state-action space, and deep learning tools. Despite their exten-
036   sive empirical success, the theoretical understanding of their convergence properties remains poorly
037   understood until recently. Recent works have explored the global convergence of policy gradient
038   methods using exact gradient oracles across various settings (Agarwal et al., 2020; Mei et al., 2022;
039   Kumar et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024a;b) with an iteration complexity of O(ϵ−1 ). However, in prac-
040   tice, the exact gradient is rarely accessible, leading to the adoption of actor-critic methods, where
041   both the actor and critic are updated concurrently using samples from trajectories (Sutton & Barto,
042   2018; Puterman, 1994).
043   The theoretical analysis of actor-critic algorithms faces significant challenges due to the strong cou-
044   pling between the actor and critic. Both components evolve together, influencing each other’s up-
045   dates, with their randomness also entangled. This interaction complicates separating and analyzing
046   them individually. Traditional approaches use two-time-scale methods Borkar (2008), where the
047   actor operates on a slower timescale, treating the critic as nearly converged, and the critic views the
048   actor as nearly stationary Konda & Tsitsiklis (1999); Bhatnagar et al. (2009a). These approaches
049   impose restrictive step sizes, leading to slow convergence.
050   More recent studies have shifted towards non-asymptotic local convergence in expectation, often
051   measured using the squared norm of the gradient of the return (Zhang et al., 2020b; Olshevsky &
052   Gharesifard, 2023; Chen et al., 2021; Chen & Zhao, 2024; Kumar et al., 2023). These studies achieve
053   a sample complexity of O(ϵ−2 ), improving the limitations of two-time-scale methods by allowing
      for less restrictive step sizes. By leveraging gradient domination lemmas, which upper bound the

054
      sub-optimality by the gradient norm, these methods also extend to global convergence, albeit with
055   slower sample complexity rates of O(ϵ−4 ).
056
057   The analysis involves formalizing and analyzing the complex interdependent system (of actor and
058
      critic). The key part is to tracking the variance of the critic’s Q-value estimation as the policy evolves,
      establishing its convergence. This simplifies the interdependent system leading to convergence of
059
      the actor as well. However, this decaying variance of critic, mandates a decaying step size for the
060
      critic, which can’t be used for constant step sizes.
061
062   To summarize, the existing methods, have huge gap between the policy gradient convergence com-
063   plexity of O(ϵ−1 ) and the slower O(ϵ−4 ) sample-based complexities of actor-critic methods. Ad-
064
      ditionally, these methods still lack a theoretical explanation for the empirical success observed in
      practical applications, leaving room for further research into bridging this gap.
065
066   n our approach, the critic views the actor’s evolving policy as adversarial but treats these changes
067   as diminishing over time, given the actor’s decreasing learning rate. Instead of tracking variance
068   as in previous methods, we focus on the critic’s bias, proving that this bias decays in line with the
069   actor’s learning rate. This allows for any step size in the critic’s updates, with constant step sizes
070
      being optimal. The key intuition is: In every iterate, the critic reduces the bias while actor increase
      it by changing the policy. But Q-evaluation being a contraction operator, so it reduces the error by a
071
      constant factor while critic ability to mess with critic decreases with time. The outcome being a very
072
      efficient tracking by the critic. On the other hand, the actor also operates independently from the
073   critic, only seeing the critic’s biased gradient, which diminishes over time with the actor’s decreasing
074   learning rate. This effectively decouples the actor and critic while retaining key information.
075
076
      We formulate a sub-optimality recursion, which is more challenging than in the exact gradient case
      found in Xiao (2022); Zhang et al. (2020b), due to the presence of a time-dependent learning rate.
077
      To address this, we develop an elegant ODE tracking methodology for solving these recursions,
078
      yielding significantly improved bounds. Additionally, when this ODE tracking method is applied
079   to the recursion for the exact gradient case, it produces better results compared to existing bounds,
080   such as those in Mei et al. (2022).
081
082   Contributions. Our contributions are threefolds.
083
084   Firstly, we establish an improved global convergence of actor-critic methods in softmax-
085
      parameterized discounted reward MDPs with sample complexity of O(ϵ−3 ), hence reducing the
      gap (see Table 1.1 for a comparison).
086

      4     C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
462
      We establish global convergence of actor-critic algorithms with significantly improved sample com-
463
      plexity of Oϵ−3 compared to existing rate of O(ϵ−4 ).The most remarkable finding of our analysis
464
      is the guaranteed convergence of the algorithm with a constant learning rate for the critic, as often
465   used in practice. Traditionally, decreasing step sizes are deemed essential for both the actor and
466   critic to reduce noise Borkar (2008); Olshevsky & Gharesifard (2023); Chen et al. (2021); Chen &
467   Zhao (2024). However, our results show that a decreasing step size for the actor alone suffices to
468   average out noise, marking a departure from the two-time-scale theory. This works reduces the gap
469   between theoretical understanding and empirical success of the algorithm.
470
      Our framework is quite general, hence the approach can be extended to other settings such as average
471   reward, function approximation setting. We leave this for the future work.
472
473   In traditional two-time-scale algorithms, the inner loop perceives the outer loop as stationary, while
474
      the outer loop regards the inner loop as converged, leading to asymptotic decoupling Borkar (2008).
475   We introduce a novel analytical framework where the inner and outer loops are ”blind” to each
476   other. The the inner loop (critic) views the the outer loop (actor) as adversarial but with diminishing
477   influence as the actor’s learning rate decreases, leading to the critic’s bias reduction over time. On
478   the other hand, the outer loop, uses this critic biased feedback (gradient) provided by the inner loop.
479   Since, the bias diminishes with time, it allows the outer loop converge.
480   This framework for resolving the two time scale coupling, combined with our novel elegant method-
481   ology to bound the recursions, can be used to analysis other two-time scale algorithms.
