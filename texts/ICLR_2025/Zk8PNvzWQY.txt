011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Reinforcement learning with offline data often suffers from Q-value extrapolation
014            errors due to limited data, which poses significant challenges and limits overall
015            performance. Existing methods such as layer normalization and reward relabeling
016            have shown promise in addressing these errors and achieving empirical improve-
017
               ments. In this paper, we extend these approaches by introducing reward scaling
               with layer normalization (RS-LN) to further mitigate extrapolation errors and en-
018
               hance performance. Furthermore, based on the insight that Q-values should be
019
               lower for infeasible action spaces—where neural networks might otherwise ex-
020            trapolate into undesirable regions—than for feasible action spaces, we propose
021            a penalization mechanism for infeasible actions (PA). By combining RS-LN and
022            PA, we develop a new algorithm called PARS. We evaluate PARS on a range
023            of tasks, demonstrating superior performance compared to state-of-the-art algo-
024            rithms in both offline training and online fine-tuning across the D4RL benchmark,
025            with notable success in the challenging AntMaze Ultra task.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      Reinforcement learning (RL) enables agents to de-
031   velop optimal decision-making strategies through
032   real-time interactions. However, these interactions
033   with real-world environments can expose the agent
034   to considerable risks. To mitigate these risks, Of-
035   fline RL, which derives optimal policies from pre-
036   collected data, has emerged as a critical area of re-
037   search (Fujimoto & Gu, 2021; Tarasov et al., 2024).
038   Additionally, agents trained with offline RL can
039
      be deployed in real-world environments to further
      acquire knowledge, leading to the development of
040
      offline-to-online RL approaches (Lee et al., 2022;
041
      Nakamoto et al., 2024; LEI et al., 2024). How-
042   ever, due to the limited coverage of offline data,
043   these methods often suffer from extrapolation error,    Figure 1: Overview of the comparison be-
044   where the Q-values of out-of-distribution (OOD) ac-     tween PARS and prior SOTA with scores
045   tions are overestimated, limiting the overall perfor-   normalized to each task’s maximum perfor-
046   mance (Kumar et al., 2020; Kostrikov et al., 2022;      mance.
047   Lyu et al., 2022; Mao et al., 2024).
048   To improve the performance of RL with offline data, several methods have been proposed. Recent
049   work by Ball et al. (2023) has demonstrated that layer normalization (LN, Ba et al. (2016)) can
050   reduce catastrophic overestimation by constraining Q-values in OOD actions, promoting more stable
051   Q-value learning and enhancing overall performance. Additionally, from a different perspective,
052   reward relabeling has been applied in various offline RL algorithms. For example, in sparse reward
053   environments like AntMaze, Kostrikov et al. (2022) modifies the rewards by subtracting 1, while
      Tarasov et al. (2024) multiplies all rewards by 100.

054
      In this work, we examined the impact of these two techniques on RL with offline data and demon-
055   strated that combining layer normalization with reward scaling (applying a constant factor to the
056   overall reward) more effectively alleviates extrapolation error. However, we observed that the Q-
057   function still tends to be overestimated beyond regions where data coverage ends. To further address
058   this, we penalize the Q-values of infeasible action regions distant from the agent’s feasible action
059   regions, which encourages a gradual reduction in Q-values beyond the data-covered regions.
060
      Building on these insights, we introduce PARS (Penalizing infeasible Actions and Reward Scaling),
061   a novel algorithm designed to mitigate Q-function extrapolation error. PARS consists of two com-
062   ponents: (1) reward scaling with layer normalization (RS-LN) and (2) penalizing infeasible
063   actions (PA). PARS is based on a minimal approach for offline RL, TD3+BC (Fujimoto & Gu,
064   2021), with RS-LN and PA being implementable through just a few lines of code. This simplicity
065   allows PARS to be easily integrated into existing off-policy algorithms with minor modifications,
066   making it highly practical for real-world use.
067   We evaluate PARS on various RL tasks during both the offline training and online fine-tuning phases.
068   Figure 1 provides an overview of the benchmark comparison, showing that the performance of PARS
069   is either close to or significantly better than the previous SOTA in all considered tasks. In particular,
070   PARS stands out as the only method to successfully learn during offline-to-online training in the
071   challenging Antmaze Ultra task, demonstrating strong performance.

      2    P RELIMINARIES
075
076
      The RL problem is formulated as a Markov Decision Process (MDP, Puterman, 1990) M =
      ⟨ρ0 , S, A, P, R, γ⟩, where ρ0 is the initial state distribution, S is the state space, A is the action
077
      space, P (st+1 |st , at ) is the transition probability, R(st , at ) is the reward function, and γ ∈ (0, 1) is
078
      the discount factor, with st ∈ S and at ∈ A denoting the state and action at timestep t, respectively.
079   In this study, we focus on a continuous action space, typically confined to a compact subset of Rn .
080   We denote the action space A, as defined in the MDP, as the feasible action region AF , and the
081   infeasible action region as AI = Rn \ AF , which consists of actions the agent cannot perform in
082   any state. We extend the action space, typically limited to feasible actions, by defining the infeasible
083   action space AI to account for potential extrapolation into infeasible regions by neural networks.
084
      Given this formulation, offline RL allows for learning a policy without real-time interaction with the
085   online environment and enables learning from a pre-collected dataset D, composed of trajectories of
                                                     −1
086   various quality, τ = {(st , at , rt , st+1 )}Tt=0 , with rt = R(st , at ) denoting the reward at timestep t
087   and T representing the episode length, without knowing the behavior policy used to collect D. The
088   goal of offline RL is to utilize the transitions in D to develop an optimal policy π that maximizes the
                                    PT −1
089   expected discounted return t=0 γ t rt .
090
091
      A policy π trained with D can be finetuned to suit online situations by interacting with the online
      environment. As new transitions are collected in a replay buffer B through online interactions, the
092
      policy can be further improved by sampling transitions from both D and B.
093
094   Layer normalization. Layer normalization (LN; Ba et al., 2016) stabilizes neural network training
095   by normalizing hidden outputs {xi } in each i-th layer. LN re-centers and re-scales them using the
096   layer’s mean µ and standard deviation σ with the transformation x̂i = xiσ−µ η + β, where η and β
097   are learnable parameters. In recent RL research, LN has been shown to improve training stability
098
      and thus lead to increased final performance (Ball et al., 2023; Nauman et al., 2024). Notably, Ball
      et al., 2023 demonstrates that applying LN can constrain Q-values for out-of-distribution actions by
099
      the weight layer norm, thereby mitigating the effects of erroneous Q-function extrapolation.

      within the feasible region. Figure 2 captures the essence of the nature of the realistic offline dataset.

121   There exists a feasible action region AF , and there exists an in-distribution (ID) region AD (⊂ AF ),
122   which may consist of multiple discontiguous subregions.
123
      First, An et al. (2021) showed that increasing the number of critics in an ensemble can provide
124
      effective critic regularization, as depicted in Figure 2 (b). Additionally, other works (Kumar et al.,
125
      2020; Lyu et al., 2022; Mao et al., 2024) introduce a penalty to reduce Q-values for OOD actions
126   deviating from the behavior policy µ within the feasible action space AF , as shown in Figure 2 (c).
127   For instance, MCQ (Lyu et al., 2022) and SVR (Mao et al., 2024) use behavior models trained with
128   either a VAE or Gaussian distribution to distinguish OOD actions and penalize them.
129
      However, both approaches have limitations. Achieving sufficient regularization using only a critic
130
      ensemble requires a large number of critics, significantly increasing training complexity. Further-
131
      more, it does not perform well on sparse reward tasks, such as AntMaze (Tarasov et al., 2022; 2024).
132   Meanwhile, methods that rely on behavior models are limited by their accuracy. Misclassifying ID
133   actions as OOD can lead to inappropriate penalties and underestimated Q-values. Additionally,
134   considering online fine-tuning, adapting the behavior model in an evolving online setting presents
135   further challenges (Nair et al., 2020). Moreover, exploring potential regions not present in the dataset
136   and adapting accordingly becomes difficult if the OOD action region within AF is fitted to a specific
137   penalty value or is overly conservative. Appendix B provides a more comprehensive comparison.
138   Therefore, we propose an alternative viewpoint on critic regularization. Unlike previous methods,
139   we leverage layer normalization and reward scaling to enhance the network’s expressivity, which
140   naturally mitigates OOD overestimation. Additionally, rather than penalizing OOD actions within
141   AF , our method imposes penalties on OOD actions outside AF to further prevent overestimation
142   in extrapolation regions, as shown in Figure 2 (d). Our goal through this is to smoothly reduce the
143   values of OOD actions outside the data coverage while seamlessly interpolating the values of OOD
144   actions within the data coverage. This allows for flexible adaptation to distribution shifts during
145   online fine-tuning (See Appendix B). Moreover, our method can be combined with a small number
146
      of ensembles to further stabilize critic regularization.

195   4.2     PARS A LGORITHM
196
197   Based on insight from Section 4.1, we present a novel algorithm that prevents Q-value extrapolation
198   error to ensure stable Q-learning across both offline and online fine-tuning phases: Penalizing in-
199   feasible actions and reward scaling (PARS). PARS is based on the minimalist offline RL algorithm,
200   TD3+BC, and is built upon two key components: (1) reward scaling combined with LN (RS-LN)
201   and (2) penalizing infeasible actions (PA).
202
203
      4.2.1    PARS C OMPONENT 1: R EWARD S CALING C OMBINED WITH LN (RS-LN)
204   For our first component, RS-LN, we scale all rewards in the offline dataset D by multiplying each
205   reward r ∈ D by a constant factor creward . During online fine-tuning, the rewards stored in the replay
206   buffer B are similarly scaled by the same constant factor creward , ensuring consistency across both
207   offline and online phases. In the previous didactic example (Figure 3), we showed the effectiveness
208   of RS-LN in mitigating OOD values, and we aim to further investigate potential underlying causes.
209
      Referring to Figure 3, RS-LN appears to foster the learning of a more intricate decision bound-
210   ary. This observation leads to the hypothesis that mitigating OOD values is linked to the network’s
211   capacity to learn more complex functions. This capacity, often referred to as neural network ex-
212   pressivity, has been extensively studied in various RL contexts (Kumar et al., 2021; Sokar et al.,
213   2023; Obando Ceron et al., 2024), and several metrics to approximate expressivity have been pro-
214   posed. Kumar et al. (2021) introduced the effective rank, Srankδ (ϕ), which provides insights into
215   how efficiently the model utilizes its capacity to represent complex data. A low Srankδ (ϕ) suggests
      the possibility of under-parameterization. Additionally, Sokar et al. (2023) introduced the concept of

      5     R ELATED W ORKS
287
288
      Offline RL methods. In addition to the offline RL methods discussed in Section 3, which focus on
289
      critic regularization, there is also ongoing research into actor regularization aimed at preventing the
290   actor from straying too far from the behavior policy. For example, TD3+BC (Fujimoto & Gu, 2021),
291   an offline variant of TD3 (Fujimoto et al., 2018), adds a behavior cloning term to the policy objective
292   to keep π close to the behavior policy. Additionally, SPOT (Wu et al., 2022) introduces a density-
293   based regularization term using a VAE to estimate the behavior policy’s density, explicitly modeling
294   the support set. Furthermore, in an approach utilizing both actor and critic regularization, ReBRAC
295   (Tarasov et al., 2024) recently enhanced TD3+BC by incorporating previously unvalidated design
296   elements, including decoupling the actor and critic penalties. ReBRAC demonstrates that significant
297   performance gains can be achieved by integrating appropriate design choices into existing methods.
298   Offline-to-online RL methods.         The policy π, trained using the offline dataset, can be further
299   fine-tuned through additional online interaction. However, this new interaction often leads to distri-
300   butional shifts between the offline and online data, resulting in performance degradation (Nair et al.,
301   2020; Lee et al., 2022; Uchendu et al., 2023). To address this issue, various studies have explored
302   effective fine-tuning methods for offline algorithms (Lee et al., 2022; Nakamoto et al., 2024; Zhang
303   et al., 2023; Beeson & Montana, 2022). For example, Off2On (Lee et al., 2022) attempts to mit-
304
      igate distribution shift issues by using an ensemble and a balanced replay strategy based on CQL
      (Kumar et al., 2020). However, traditional offline algorithms are designed with limited datasets in
305
      mind, leading to a conservative learning approach that can limit performance in online fine-tuning.
306
      To overcome this, Uni-O4 (LEI et al., 2024) proposes removing conservatism during the offline
307   phase to facilitate a smoother transition to the online phase. On the other hand, RLPD (Ball et al.,
308   2023) takes a different approach by bypassing the explicit offline phase. Instead, it leverages offline
309   data during online learning, utilizing LN and increasing the update-to-data (UTD) ratio for efficient
310   sample utilization. Recently, Zhao et al. (2024) proposed ENOTO, effectively utilizes ensembles
311   for efficient offline-to-online RL, showcasing its effectiveness across various ensemble-based ap-
312   proaches. Compared to existing methods, we do not propose new specialized techniques for online
313   fine-tuning. RS-LA and PA naturally integrate into the process with the critic ensemble, enabling
314   smooth transitions and superior performance for PARS.

515   7   C ONCLUSION
516
517   We introduce PARS, aimed at preventing critic extrapolation error and enhancing overall perfor-
518   mance in both offline training and online fine-tuning of RL with offline data. Our analysis of LN
519
      and reward scaling with offline data reveals that combining LN with reward scaling is highly effec-
      tive for mitigating OOD issues. Additionally, applying penalties to the infeasible action region re-
520
      duces OOD Q-values below ID Q-values, particularly where data coverage ends, resulting in notable
521
      performance gains. PARS has demonstrated substantial performance improvements over previous
522   SOTA algorithms across diverse RL tasks in both offline training and online fine-tuning phases. Fur-
523   thermore, PARS was the only algorithm among off-policy RL variants to achieve strong performance
524   on the notoriously challenging AntMaze Ultra task. Our findings suggest a new avenue—differing
525   from conventional views—that strong performance across a wide array of RL tasks is achievable
526   with only simple adjustments to off-policy algorithms, provided appropriate regularization for OOD
527   mitigation is applied.
528   Limitations and Future research.          Although PARS has achieved superior performance, it still
529   benefits from the support of a critic ensemble, particularly in MuJoCo and Adroit. We expect that
530   further investigation into data fitting challenges in limited datasets, coupled with potential refine-
531   ments, could lead to robust performance with just a double-critic setup. As a future research direc-
532   tion, exploring the relationship between RS-LN and neural network expressivity would be valuable.
533   Additionally, designing activation functions in combination with RS-LN or PA presents an intriguing
534   research area.

      B.2   M ORE R ELATED W ORKS
919
920   In addition to the related works covered in Section 5, we provide a more detailed comparison with
921   works that share commonalities with PARS.
922
      RLPD (Ball et al., 2023) RLPD is an algorithm that utilizes LN in the Q network to achieve high
923
      sample efficiency in online RL with offline data, notably succeeding in sparse tasks like Antmaze.
924   Unlike offline-to-online algorithms, RLPD does not include an offline pretraining phase. Conse-
925   quently, RLPD does not explicitly account for the offline phase and, on its own, does not perform
926   well offline.
927
      Given our goal of achieving robust performance both offline and during the online fine-tuning phase,
928
      we adopted the LN method from RLPD but enhanced it by incorporating reward scaling and infea-
929
      sible action penalties in addition to LN. This improvement effectively enhances OOD mitigation,
930   which was insufficient with LN alone, as seen in the ‘LN’ column with creward = 1 in Figure 3.
931
932   Consequently, as shown in the performance comparison table (Table 2) and performance graph (Fig-
933
      ure 10), PARS outperforms RLPD in more challenging tasks, such as Antmaze Ultra and Adroit,
      because it effectively leverages information learned during the offline phase in the online phase.
934
935   Uni-O4 (LEI et al., 2024) Uni-O4 is an algorithm designed to facilitate a seamless transition from
936   offline to online learning. It achieves this by eliminating excessive conservatism or regularization
937   during the offline training process. It shares a similar overarching goal with PARS, which aims to
938   smoothly reduce the Q-values of OOD actions without imposing extensive conservatism.
939   For this purpose, Uni-O4 uses an on-policy PPO-based approach for policy learning without Q-
940   function. It starts with a policy ensemble to capture behavior diversity and employs offline policy
941   evaluation (OPE) to update the policy only if it outperforms the current one. Gradual updates with
942   PPO loss and multi-step optimization ensure stability, while a unified objective integrates offline and
943   online learning. However, if the policy ensemble lacks diversity, the initial policy may be limited,
944   affecting updates. Moreover, OPE uses an additionally learned Transition Model and Monte Carlo
945
      methods to estimate rewards, but this introduces uncertainty that could impact performance.
946   Therefore, when comparing performance (Table 2 and Figure 10), PARS shows significantly su-
947   perior performance compared to Uni-O4. For smooth transition to online fine-tuning, instead of
948   focusing on multi-step policy optimization using OPE, PARS can be more efficient by utilizing a
949   simple TD3+BC-based approach while ensuring a smooth reduction in OOD values.

1193

1195   behavioral cloning term, which restricts actions to remain within the convex hull of dataset actions.

1198   teristics and limitations. Thus, the choice of implicit Q-learning method tends to impose certain
1199   constraints. Consequently, the best performance was achieved when PA and RS-LN were applied
1200   to a fundamental off-policy algorithm, such as TD3, with further explanation of the effectiveness of
1201   their combination in Appendix K. Exploring the extension of PA and RS-LN to in-sample learning,

1213   For the AntMaze domain, we leverage eight datasets: {umaze-v2, umaze-diverse-v2, medium-play-
1214   v2, medium-diverse-v2, large-play-v2, large-diverse-v2, ultra-play-v0, ultra-diverse-v0}. These
1215   datasets encompass different levels of difficulty based on the maze’s size, complexity, and the diver-
1216   sity of start and goal positions.
1217   In the Adroit domain, we utilize four tasks: {pen, door, hammer, relocate}, each associated with two
1218   dataset qualities: {cloned-v1, expert-v1}. The cloned datasets are created by training an imitation
1219   policy using demonstration data, executing the policy, and mixing the resulting data with the original
1220   demonstrations in a 50-50 ratio. In contrast, expert datasets are derived from fine-tuned RL policy.
1221   For the MuJoCo domain, we use three tasks: {halfcheetah, hopper, walker2d}, each of which has
1222   four dataset qualities: {random-v2, medium-replay-v2, medium-v2, medium-expert-v2}. The ran-
1223   dom dataset consists of data generated by randomly initialized policies, the medium dataset comes
1224   from partially trained policies, the medium-replay dataset includes data from replay buffers dur-
1225   ing training, and the expert dataset contains demonstrations from well-trained agents performing
1226   near-optimal behavior.
