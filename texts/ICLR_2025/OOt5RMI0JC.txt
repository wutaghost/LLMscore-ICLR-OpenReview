011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Humans rely on strong inductive biases to learn from few examples and abstract
014            useful information from sensory data. Instilling such biases in machine learning
015
               models has been shown to improve their performance on various benchmarks
               including few-shot learning, robustness, and alignment. However, finding effective
016
               training procedures to achieve that goal can be challenging as psychologically-
017
               rich training data such as human similarity judgments are expensive to scale, and
018            Bayesian models of human inductive biases are often intractable for complex,
019            realistic domains. Here, we address this challenge by introducing a Bayesian
020            notion of generative similarity whereby two datapoints are considered similar if
021            they are likely to have been sampled from the same distribution. This measure can
022            be applied to complex generative processes, including probabilistic programs. We
023            show that generative similarity can be used to define a contrastive learning objective
024            even when its exact form is intractable, enabling learning of spatial embeddings
025            that express specific inductive biases. We demonstrate the utility of our approach by
026
               showing that it can be used to capture human inductive biases for geometric shapes,
               distinguish different abstract drawing styles that are parameterized by probabilistic
027
               programs, and capture abstract high-level categories that enable generalization.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Human intelligence is characterized by strong inductive biases that enable humans to form meaningful
033   generalizations (Tenenbaum et al., 2011; Lake et al., 2015), learn from few examples (Lake et al.,
034   2015), and abstract useful information from sensory data (Gershman, 2017). Instilling such biases
035   into machine learning models has been at the center of numerous recent studies (Kumar et al., 2022;
036   McCoy et al., 2020; Peterson et al., 2019; Hebart et al., 2020; Sucholutsky et al., 2023a; Sucholutsky
037   & Griffiths, 2024; McCoy & Griffiths, 2023; Muttenthaler et al., 2024; Snell et al., 2023; Jha et al.,
038   2023), and has been shown to improve accuracy, few-shot learning, interpretability, and robustness
039
      (Sucholutsky et al., 2023b). Key to this effort is the ability to find effective training procedures
      to imbue neural networks with these inductive biases. Two prominent approaches for this are i)
040
      leveraging the literature on modeling human inductive biases with Bayesian models (Tenenbaum
041
      et al., 2011; Griffiths et al., 2010) to specify a computational model for the bias of interest and then
042   distilling it into the model, usually via meta-learning (Kumar et al., 2022; Binz et al., 2023; McCoy
043   et al., 2020), and ii) incorporating psychologically-rich human judgments in the training objectives of
044   models such as soft labels (Sucholutsky & Schonlau, 2021), categorization uncertainty (Collins et al.,
045   2023; Peterson et al., 2019), language descriptions (Kumar et al., 2022; Marjieh et al., 2023), and
046   similarity judgments (Muttenthaler et al., 2024; Jha et al., 2023; Esling et al., 2018).
047
      While both approaches are promising, they are not without limitations. Though Bayesian models
048   provide an effective description of human inductive biases, they are often computationally intractable
049   due to expensive Bayesian posterior computations that require summing over large hypothesis spaces.
050   This problem is particularly pronounced when considering symbolic models which are often used to
051   describe human inductive biases (Lake et al., 2019; 2017; Sablé-Meyer et al., 2021; 2022; Quilty-
052   Dunn et al., 2023). Likewise, incorporating human judgments in model objectives may be intuitive,
053   but it is often not scalable for the data needs of modern machine learning. For example, while
      incorporating human similarity judgments (i.e., judgments of how similar pairs of stimuli are) has

070   collecting them at the scale of modern datasets is challenging as the number of required judgments
071   grows quadratically in the number of stimuli (though see Marjieh et al. (2023) for proxies).
072   Here, we introduce a third approach for instilling inductive biases based on the method of contrastive
073   learning (Chen et al., 2020), a widely used training procedure in machine learning. Contrastive
074   learning uses the designation of datapoints as being the “same” or “different” to learn a representation
075   of those datapoints where the “same” datapoints are encouraged to be closer together and “differ-
076   ent” datapoints further apart. This approach provides a way to go from a similarity measure to a
077   representation. We define a principled notion of similarity based on Bayesian inference (initially
078   proposed in Kemp et al., 2005) and show that it can be naturally implemented in a contrastive
079
      learning framework even when its exact form is intractable. Specifically, given a set of samples and a
      hierarchical generative model of the data from which data distributions are first sampled and then
080
      individual samples are drawn (e.g., a Gaussian mixture), we define the generative similarity between
081
      a pair of samples to be their probability of having been sampled from the same distribution relative
082   to that of them being sampled from two independently-drawn distributions (Figure 1). By using
083   Bayesian models to define similarity within a contrastive learning framework, we provide a general
084   procedure for instilling human inductive biases in machines.
085
      To demonstrate the utility of our approach, we apply it to four domains of increasing complexity.
086
      First, we consider a Gaussian mixture example where similarity and embeddings are analytically
087
      tractable, which we then further test with simulations. Second, we consider a generative model
088   for quadrilateral shapes where generative similarity can be computed in closed form and can be
089   incorporated explicitly in a contrastive objective. By training a model with this objective, we show
090   that it acquires human-like regularity biases in a geometric reasoning task. Third, we consider
091   probabilistic programs, using two classes of probabilistic programs from DreamCoder (Ellis et al.,
092   2021; Sablé-Meyer et al., 2022). While generative similarity is not tractable in this case, we show
093   that it can be implicitly induced using a Monte Carlo approximation applied to a triplet loss function,
094   leading to a representation that better captures the structure of the programs compared to standard
095   contrastive learning. Fourth, we show the scalability of this approach by applying our method to
096
      a standard large-scale machine learning dataset, ImageNet (Deng et al., 2009), and demonstrate
      how generative similarity can allow

      2.1   R ELATED W ORK
155
      We next discuss how our framework for instilling human inductive biases connects to existing
156   contrastive techniques. First, observe that by carrying outR the integration in Equation 1 we have
157   sgen (x1 , x2 ) = p(x1 , x2 )/p(x1 )p(x2 ) where p(x1 , x2 ) = p(x1 |θ)p(x2 |θ)p(θ)dθ and p(x1,2 ) are
158   its marginals. This means that (up to a logarithm) generative similarity is a specific type of point-wise
159   mutual information (Church & Hanks, 1990). While point-wise mutual information (MI) is broadly
160   concerned with sample independence, generative similarity goes further by tying this independence
161   to a hierarchical generative structure that encapsulates an inductive bias associated with the data. This
      is in line with work suggesting that the success of MI-based contrastive learning approaches hinges


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      significantly on the specific inductive biases incorporated in the process (Tschannen et al., 2019).
163   Notable contrastive methods with losses based on MI maximization include SimCLR (Chen et al.,
164   2020) and InfoNCE (Oord et al., 2018) more broadly. SimCLR is a training framework whereby
165   a representation is learned by applying various augmentations to the training data and then using
166   those to construct positive and negative pairs that are then incorporated in a contrastive (e.g. triplet)
167   loss. Crucially, positive and negative pairs in SimCLR (and InfoNCE) are usually constructed in
168   a domain-agnostic manner by applying augmentations such as rotations and rescalings to the data.
169   While this is a good default strategy, when there is prior expectation about the structure of the
170   underlying generative process it glosses over finer within- and across-category relations that are
171
      explicitly incorporated in our generative similarity sampling scheme (Equation 5). Put differently,
      SimCLR corresponds to a default generative process in which a base image is sampled and then a
172
      random augmentation is applied, as opposed to richer generative models that group the base images
173
      themselves into meaningful hierarchies reflecting human inductive biases.
174
175   Second, it is possible to show under suitable regularization (to ensure that sgen can be treated as a
176   distribution) that generative similarity (Equation 1) can be derived as the minimizer of the functional
177
      DKL [s||psame ] − DKL [s||pdiff ] (see Appendices B-D) which is reminiscent of contrastive divergence
      learning, a method based on a contrastive objective between two Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergences
178
      (Carreira-Perpinan & Hinton, 2005). However, while the above objective is also a contrastive
179
      difference between divergences, it is different from that found in CDL in which the goal is to reduce
180   the difference between the divergences whereas in our case it is to increase their (negative) contrast.
181
182   Third, our framework is by no means the only contrastive framework that incorporates a Bayesian
183
      component. Here it is worth noting two lines of work: (i) Contrastive kernel methods such as the
      mutual information kernel (MIK) (Seeger, 2001) and the positive pair kernel (PPK) (Johnson et al.,
184
      2022). Both MIK and PPK involve a kernel object that is analogous to Equation 1 as the basis of
185
      their approach. However, they both differ from our current framework, the former being largely
186   a support vector machine, and the latter considering generative processes in the limited sense of
187   domain-agnostic augmentations (as with SimCLR) and without explicitly incorporating the kernel in
188   a contrastive loss (as with Equations 2-5). It is also unclear from these works why such contrastive
189   kernels can be an effective way of instilling human inductive biases in machines. (ii) Other hybrid
190   Bayesian contrastive learning methods (e.g., Liu & Wang (2023)) devise Bayesian computations
191   to mine hard negative examples to improve contrastive training. However, this approach is quite
192   different from the way Bayesian computations are incorporated in our framework.

      3.2   I NSTILLING H UMAN G EOMETRIC S HAPE R EGULARITY B IASES
240
241   Background: Psychological research suggests that the human species is uniquely sensitive to
242   abstract geometric regularity (Henshilwood et al., 2011; Saito et al., 2014). Sablé-Meyer et al. (2021)
243   compared diverse human groups (varying in education, cultural background, and age) to non-human
244   primates on a simple oddball discrimination task. Participants were shown a set of five reference
245   shapes and one “oddball” shape and were prompted to identify the oddball (Figure 3A). The reference
246   shapes were generated using basic geometric regularities: parallel lines, equal sides, equal angles,
247   and right angles, which can be specified by a binary vector corresponding to the presence or absence
248   of these specific geometric features. There were 11 types of quadrilateral reference shapes with
249
      varying geometric regularity, from squares (most regular) to random quadrilaterals containing no
      parallel lines, right angles, or equal angles/sides (least regular; Figure 3B). In each trial, five different
250
      versions of the same reference shape (e.g., a square) were shown in different sizes and orientations.
251
      The oddball shape was a modified version of the reference shape, in which the lower right vertex was
252   moved such that it violated the regularity of the original reference shape (e.g., moving the lower right
253   vertex of a trapezoid such that it no longer has parallel sides). Figure 3A shows an example trial.
254
255
      Sablé-Meyer et al. (2021) found that humans were sensitive to these geometric regularities (right
      angles, parallelism, symmetry, etc.) whereas non-human primates were not. Specifically, they found
256
      that human performance was best on the oddball task for the most regular shapes, and systematically
257
      decreased as shapes became more irregular. Conversely, non-human primates performed well above
258   chance, but they performed worse than humans overall and, critically, exhibited no influence of
259   geometric regularity (Figure 3B). Additionally, they tested a pretrained convolutional neural network
260   (CNN) model, CorNet (Kubilius et al., 2019), on the task. CorNet (Core Object Recognition Network)
261   is a convolutional neural network model with an architecture that explicitly models the primate ventral
262   visual stream. It is pretrained on a standard supervised object recognition objective on ImageNet
263   and is one of the top-scoring models of “brain-score”, a benchmark for testing models of the visual
264   system using both behavioral and neural data (Schrimpf et al., 2018). Like the monkeys, CorNet
265   exhibited no systematic relationship with the level of geometric regularity (Figure 3B).

507   4    D ISCUSSION
508
509
      We have introduced a new framework for learning representations that capture human inductive biases
510
      by combining a Bayesian notion of generative similarity with contrastive learning. Our framework is
511   very general and can be applied to any hierarchical generative process, even when the exact form of
512   inferences is intractable, allowing neural networks to capture domains that were previously restricted
513   to symbolic models. To demonstrate the utility and flexibility of our approach we applied it to an array
514   of domains that vary in complexity. First, we investigated an analytically tractable case that involved
515   a mixture of two Gaussians and showed that mean generative similarity monotonically decreases with
516   respect to distance in the contrastively-learned embedding space (Figure 2). Second, we examined a
517   visual perception task with quadrilaterals used in cognitive science (Sablé-Meyer et al., 2021) and
518   showed that our procedure is able to produce the human geometric regularity bias in models that were
519
      previously unable to (Figure 3). Third, we show improved representations of probabilistic programs
      for abstract human drawing styles compared to standard contrastive paradigms like SimCLR (Chen
520
      et al., 2020) (Figure 4). Fourth, we use a standard large-scale machine learning dataset, ImageNet, to
521
      show generative similarity can learn embeddings that encode abstract, high-level categories compared
522   to standard contrastive paradigms, even those not seen in training (Figure 6).
523
524   There are some limitations to our work that point towards future directions. First, there may be
525
      multiple candidates reflecting different hypotheses concerning the generative model, and more broadly
      not all domains admit a transparent hierarchical structure. Characterizing the kind of generative
526
      models underlying human judgments in domains of interest (see e.g., Destler et al. (2023) for a recent
527
      study on skeletal models of shapes) is key to instilling the right inductive biases in machine models.
528   Therefore, solving the inverse problem, i.e., inferring the generative model itself from the data in
529   tandem with generative similarity is a promising avenue. Second in our work we mainly focus on
530   domains related to vision, but our framework is general enough to be applicable for other modalities.
531   For example, Large Language Models can often produce unpredictable failures in logical (Wan et al.,
532   2024) and causal (Kıcıman et al., 2023) reasoning. Cognitive scientists have written models for
533   human logical reasoning (Piantadosi et al., 2016) or causal learning (Goodman et al., 2011) based
534   on probabilistic Bayesian inference. Potential future work may involve contrastive learning with
535   generative similarity over Bayesian models of reasoning to imbue language models with logical and
536
      causal reasoning abilities. Note that, although contrastive learning is most commonly used in vision,
      there is precedence for its use in the language domain (Gao et al., 2021; Luo et al., 2024).
537
538   Strong inductive biases are a hallmark of human intelligence. Finding ways to imbue such biases
539   in machine models is key for developing more generally intelligent AI as well as for achieving
      human-AI alignment. Our work offers a new path towards that goal.

1098   For our main experiments, we use the CorNet model which was used in the original work (variant
1099   ‘S’) that introduces the Oddball task (Sablé-Meyer et al., 2021). CorNet contains four “areas”
1100
       corresponding to the areas of the visual stream: V1, V2, V4, and IT. Each area contains convolutional
       and max pooling layers. There are also biologically plausible recurrent connections between areas
1101
       (e.g., V4 to V1). After IT, the penultimate area in the visual stream, a linear layer is used to readout
1102
       object categories. The model is pretrained on ImageNet on a standard supervised object recognition
1103   objective. The pretrained CorNet model’s performance on the Oddball task is reported in Figure 3B.
1104
1105   For finetuning the model on the supervised classification objective, we followed the protocol used in
1106
       the supplementary results of Sablé-Meyer et al. (2021). Specifically, 11 new object categories are
       added to the model’s last layer, and the model is trained to classify a quadrilateral as one of the 11
1107
       categories shown in Figure 3B. For training data, we used quadrilaterals from all 11 categories with
1108
       different scales and rotations (though the specific quadrilateral images used in the test trials were held
1109   out). We used a learning rate of 5e-6 using the Adam optimizer with a cross entropy loss. Training
1110   was conducted on an NVIDIA Quadro P6000 GPU with 25GB of memory.
1111
1112
       For finetuning the model on the generative similarity contrastive objective, we first calculated the
       Euclidean distance of the model’s final layer embedding between different quadrilateral images, then
1113
       calculated the Euclidean distance between the quadrilaterals’ respective geometry feature vectors,
1114
       and finally used the mean squared error between the embeddings’ distance and the feature vectors’
1115   distance as the loss. The geometric feature vectors were a set of 22 binary features encoding the
1116   following properties: 6 features per pair of edges encoding whether their lengths are equal or not, 6
1117   features per pair of angles coding whether their angles are equal or not, 6 features per pair of edges
1118   encoding whether they were parallel or not, and 4 features per angle encoding whether they were
1119   right angles or not. See Table 1 for a list of these values. Like the supervised model, we used training
1120   data from each category of quadrilaterals with different scales and rotations (though specific images
1121   used in the test trials were held out). We used the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 5e-4. We
1122   used the exact same training data, learning rate, and optimizer when running the control experiments
1123
       for finetuning CorNet on the SimCLR objective (Figure S2). Training was conducted on an NVIDIA
       Quadro P6000 GPU with 25GB of memory.

       Because of the similarity of the stimuli in Figure 4B to handwritten characters, we used the same
1135   CNN architecture that Snell et al. (2017) used on the Omniglot dataset (Lake et al., 2019) with six
1136   convolutional blocks consisting of 64-filter 3 × 3 convolution, a batch normalization layer, a ReLU
1137   nonlinearity, and a 2 × 2 max-pooling layer. This network outputs a 256-dimensional embedding.
1138   Our experiment was replicated with another CNN architecture (CorNet), which yielded similar results
1139   (Figure S4). We used two different training objectives: a standard contrastive learning objective from
1140   SimCLR (Chen et al., 2020) and one based on a Monte-Carlo estimate of generative similarity. For
1141   both objectives, we used the same learning rate 1e-3 and the same Adam optimizer with a batch size
1142   of 128.
1143   For the SimCLR baseline objective, images in the batch were randomly augmented. The augmenta-
1144   tions were: random resize crop, random horizontal flips, and random Gaussian blurs. The original
1145   SimCLR paper also had augmentations corresponding to color distortions which we did not use
1146   because our data were already grayscale images. Like SimCLR, we used the InfoNCE loss function
1147   (Oord et al., 2018). Let vi be the embedding of image i and vi′ be the embedding of image i’s
                                              PN            f (v ,v ′ )
1148   augmented counterpart. The loss is N1 i=1 log 1 P expi fi(v ,v′ ) where f is a similarity function
                                                           N   j       i   j
1149   between embeddings (SimCLR used cosine similarity). This effectively pushes representations of
1150   images and their augmented counterparts to be more similar while also pushing representations of
1151   images and other images’ augmented counterparts to be more dissimilar. Training was conducted
1152   with one NVIDIA Tesla P100 GPU with 16GB of memory.

       For the generative similarity objective, let image a be the representation of the anchor image that
1154
       is sampled from a random grammar c ∈ {Celtic, Greek}. Let p be the representation of a positive

1157   compute the positive Euclidean distance d(p, a) and the negative Euclidean distance d(n, a) in order
1158   to compute the loss function d(p, a) − d(n, a) (see Equation 5). Training was conducted with one
1159   NVIDIA Tesla P100 GPU with 16GB of memory.

1164   Our training data came from the tieredImageNet dataset (Ren et al., 2018). In the training set, there
1165   are 20 categories containing a total of 351 classes (in which each class can contain thousands of
1166   examples). In the test set, there are 8 categories containing a total of 151 classes. The categories
1167
       are mutually exclusive so no class belongs to more than one category. Images were resized to
       224 × 224 × 3 (for 3 RGB channels) before training. We trained a standard ResNet (He et al., 2016)
1168
       architecture on both types of losses. We used two different training objectives: a standard contrastive
1169
       learning objective from SimCLR (Chen et al., 2020) and one based on a Monte-Carlo estimate of
1170   generative similarity. For both objectives, we used the same learning rate 1e-3 and the same Adam
1171   optimizer with a batch size of 256.
1172
1173
       For the SimCLR objective, we used the same objective and image augmentations as the drawing style
       experiment, but also included random grayscaling since ImageNet images are colored. Training was
1174
       conducted with one NVIDIA Tesla P100 GPU with 16GB of memory.
1175
1176   For the generative similarity objective, we used the same Euclidean-based distance for the triplet
1177   loss as the Drawing Styles experiment. However, we included a new mechanism for identifying
1178   “high-level” and “low-level” triplets (see Figure 6B). For high-level triplets, the idea is that we are
1179
       sampling from the top-level in the hierarchical generative model (Figure 6A). The positive image
       is sampled from the same category of the anchor image, but with an independent class (so the
1180
       positive image and anchor image may have different classes). The negative image is from a category
1181
       sampled independently (so the negative image may have a different category). This corresponds to a
1182   sample from the generative model at the top of the hierarchy without consideration for the bottom
1183   level (the classes). For low-level triplets, we are sampling from the bottom-level of the hierarchical
1184   generative model, conditioned on a specific high-level category. Therefore, the positive image is
1185   sampled from the same category and same class, and the negative image is sampled from the same
1186   category but independent class. To bias the network towards learning higher-level abstract categories
1187   (as humans typically do), we sampled 80% top-level triplets and 20% bottom-level triplets during
       training. Training was conducted with one NVIDIA Tesla P100 GPU with 16GB of memory.
