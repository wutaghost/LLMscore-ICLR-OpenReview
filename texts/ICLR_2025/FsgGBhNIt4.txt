009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Facial attributes (e.g., gender, age) encompass important social cues and play a
012            pivotal role in computer vision. While supervised methods have dominated facial
013            attribute analysis, they often require large annotated datasets, which are costly and
014            time-consuming to create. In this work, we circumvent this limitation by propos-
015            ing a novel unsupervised learning framework that leverages StyleGAN to learn
016            rich and disentangled facial attribute representations. Specifically, unlike prior
017            methods that rely on labeled datasets or supervised techniques, our approach ex-
018
               ploits the unique inductive bias of StyleGAN, namely Hierarchical Feature Modu-
               lation, to automatically discover semantically meaningful representations of facial
019
               attributes. This inductive bias enables StyleGAN to generate disentangled and in-
020
               terpretable facial attribute features at different layers, benefiting a variety of down-
021            stream tasks. To leverage StyleGAN representations, we employ GAN inversion
022            methods to represent input images as StyleGAN features and propose a simple yet
023            effective feature reduction method based on mutual information to improve the
024            effectiveness and efficiency of the learned representations. Extensive experiments
025            in few-shot facial attribute analysis tasks, including clustering, classification, and
026            facial attribute annotation demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Facial attributes, such as gender, age, and the presence of accessories like glasses, are crucial social
032   cues that play a pivotal role in human perception and interaction. In computer vision, the accurate
033
      analysis and representation of these attributes have far-reaching implications for various applica-
      tions (Kortli et al., 2020; Zheng et al., 2022; Narayan et al., 2024).
034
035   Traditionally, supervised learning methods have dominated the landscape of facial attribute anal-
036   ysis, achieving remarkable performance in tasks such as attribute classification and detection (Li
037   et al., 2022; Qin et al., 2023; Kuprashevich & Tolstykh, 2023). Recently, various methods based on
038   Masked Autoencoders (MAEs) (He et al., 2022) have significantly advanced the field by introducing
039
      (unsupervised) representation learning to facial attribute analysis to improve classification accuracy,
      facilitate multi-modal learning, reduce computational costs, etc. Notable examples include ABAW5
040
      (Zhang et al., 2023), MCM (Zhang et al., 2024), MAE-DFER (Sun et al., 2023), and MARLIN (Cai
041
      et al., 2023). However, all these approaches rely on large, meticulously annotated datasets, which
042   are both costly and time-consuming to create. This dependence on labeled data presents a significant
043   bottleneck in advancing the field and limits the scalability of facial attribute analysis to new domains
044   or attributes.
045
      In this paper, we address this challenge by proposing a novel unsupervised learning framework that
046
      leverages the generative power and unique architecture of StyleGAN (Karras et al., 2020b; 2021)
047
      to learn rich and disentangled facial attribute representations. Our approach represents a paradigm
048   shift in facial attribute analysis, moving away from the reliance on labeled datasets and supervised
049   techniques towards a more flexible and scalable few-shot methodology. At the core of our framework
050   is the exploitation of StyleGAN’s distinctive inductive bias, specifically its Hierarchical Feature
051   Modulation technique. This architectural feature enables StyleGAN to generate highly realistic
052   facial images while maintaining fine-grained control over various attributes (Viazovetskyi et al.,
053   2020; Wu et al., 2021). As shown in Fig. 1, our key insight is that this inductive bias also allows
      StyleGAN to automatically discover and represent semantically meaningful facial attributes in a

087   Supervised Facial Attribute Classification. Supervised facial attribute classification has been
088   widely studied, with deep learning models like CNNs (Kalayeh et al., 2017) traditionally used
089   to identify attributes such as age, gender, and expressions. Recent advancements, particularly
090   transformer-based architectures, have further improved performance across multiple facial attribute
091   analysis tasks. For instance, the Label2Label (Li et al., 2022) framework addresses multi-attribute
092   learning as a sequence generation task, using a language modeling approach to better capture re-
093
      lationships between attributes, enhancing classification accuracy. Similarly, SwinFace (Qin et al.,
      2023), a transformer-based architecture, adopts multi-task learning to handle face recognition, ex-
094
      pression, age, and attribute estimation in one framework, leveraging transformers’ hierarchical struc-
095
      ture to improve overall performance. Meanwhile, Mivolo (Kuprashevich & Tolstykh, 2023) uses
096   a multi-input transformer to focus on age and gender classification, highlighting the value of in-
097   tegrating multiple facial cues for more accurate predictions. Nevertheless, these methods have en-
098   countered a bottleneck of facial image representations, prompting recent approaches to shift towards
099   representation learning, particularly those based on Masked Autoencoders (MAEs) (He et al., 2022).
100
      MAEs-based Facial Representation Learning. Masked Autoencoders (MAEs) (He et al., 2022)
101

105   affective analysis, showing that MAEs can efficiently learn representations from masked portions of
106   facial images. Similarly, MCM (Zhang et al., 2024) proposes a method that combines channel and
107   spatial masking to enhance facial action unit detection, enabling the network to focus on subtle fa-
      cial movements, but often requiring more complex architectures for deeper facial understanding. For

      3   FACIAL ATTRIBUTE R EPRESENTATIONS L EARNED BY S TYLE GAN

141
      Our key insight is that the unique inductive bias of StyleGAN (i.e., Hierarchical Feature Modulation)
      enables the automatic discovery of semantically meaningful representations of facial attributes.
142
143   Facial Features Learned via Hierarchical Feature Modulation. StyleGAN introduced a ground-
144   breaking approach to image generation through its Hierarchical Feature Modulation. Similar to
145   previous works, this method employs a pyramid of convolutional blocks that synthesize images in a
146   coarse-to-fine manner. However, at each layer, the feature maps are modulated via adaptive instance
147
      normalization (AdaIN), which scales and shifts them based on style information derived from a la-
      tent vector. The distinct advantage of this approach is that it directly (i.e., no need to propagate the
148
      style vector through layers) modulates each feature map independently (i.e., with unique modulation
149
      parameters) based on the input style, allowing for more disentangled and fine-grained control. In this
150   work, we observed that this strategy not only improves image generation and editing (Abdal et al.,
151   2019; 2020; Alaluf et al., 2022), but also automatically learns a semantically meaningful feature
152   representation of facial attributes.
153
      Qualitative Illustration. As an intuitive illustration, we visualize and compare selected feature
154
      maps from StyleGAN2 (SG2) (Karras et al., 2020b) and VQVAE (Rombach et al., 2021) pretrained
155
      on the FFHQ dataset using 8 input facial images obtained from the FFHQ dataset: 4 with glasses
156   and 4 without (Fig. 2). These images are fed into SG2 to obtain their representations by GAN in-

160   StyleGAN demonstrates superior disentanglement of the glasses feature, isolating it more effectively
161   from other facial attributes. In contrast, VQVAE’s feature maps exhibit lower disentanglement, mix-
      ing substantial irrelevant features such as hair, mouth, and cheeks. This observation demonstrates

188   Quantitative Justification. To quantitatively justify our observation and demonstrate its generality
189   across facial attributes, models, and layers, we propose to use the difference between i) intra-class
190   mutual information (IntraMI) and ii) inter-class mutual information (InterMI) as a metric to measure
191   the disentanglement and distinguishability of the representations learned. Specifically, given two
192   feature maps X and Y , mutual information(MI) measures how related one feature map is to the
193   other, indicating their similarity. Its formal definition is as:
194                                               XX                        p(x, y)
195                               M I(X; Y ) =               p(x, y) log                                   (1)
                                                                           p(x)p(y)
196                                               x∈X y∈Y
197
      where p(x, y) is the joint probability distribution of X and Y , and p(x) and p(y) are the marginal
198
      probability distributions of X and Y , respectively. IntraMI measures mutual information within the
199   same class, while InterMI measures it across different classes. In short, high (IntraMI - InterMI)
200   is desirable, as it indicates high similarity within a facial attribute class and low similarity between
201   different facial attribute classes (e.g., glasses vs. non-glasses), respectively. Specifically, we calcu-
202   late the average IntraMI and InterMI for each layer of 4 models (SG2, VGG16, VQVAE (van den
203   Oord et al., 2017) and Inceptionv3 (Szegedy et al., 2016)) for each of the 4 facial attributes (glasses,
204   gender, age (man) and age (woman)), respectively. For the 4 facial attributes, we use the labels from
205   the FFHQ-Features-Dataset (Karras et al., 2019) and specify i) two classes for glasses (glasses vs.
206   non-glasses); ii) two classes for gender (male vs. female); iii) three classes for age (man) and age
207
      (woman), i.e., child (age<10), adult (10≤age<60), and senior (60≤age), respectively. For each
      class, we select 4 corresponding images from the FFHQ dataset. Similarly, the input images are
208
      fed into SG2 by GAN inversion (Tov et al., 2021). Then, for IntraMI, we compute its mean by first
209
      averaging the IntraMI between feature maps of the same channel among all possible pairs of the 4
210   images in a class, and then averaging across all feature maps in a layer; for InterMI, we compute
211   its mean by first averaging the InterMI between feature maps of the same channel among all pos-
212   sible image pairs of different classes for an attribute, and then averaging across all feature maps
213   in a layer. As Fig. 4 shows, SG2 produces much higher (IntraMI - InterMI) than other models,
214   especially in low-resolution layers. This supports our observation that SG2’s Hierarchical Feature
215   Modulation technique effectively promotes the learning of disentangled and distinguishable facial
      attribute features, particularly in the low-resolution layers.

      6     C ONCLUSION
528
      In conclusion, we propose a novel unsupervised approach to learn facial attribute representations
529
      that leverages the unique capabilities of StyleGAN. By exploiting StyleGAN’s Hierarchical Feature
530
      Modulation, we have demonstrated a method to automatically discover rich, disentangled represen-
531   tations of facial attributes in an unsupervised manner. The effectiveness of our method is evidenced
532   by its strong performance across a range of unsupervised and few-shot downstream tasks, including
533   facial attribute clustering, few-shot classification, and few-shot facial attribute annotation. To the
534   best of our knowledge, ours is the first method to successfully achieve these results.
