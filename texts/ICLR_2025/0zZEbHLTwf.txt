                                                            A BSTRACT
010
011
               Solving Partial Differential Equations (PDE) has long been a critical challenge in many scientific
012
               and engineering domains. Recently, neural networks have shown great promise in solving PDEs
013            by learning solution operators from data, offering a flexible and adaptive alternative to traditional
014            numerical solvers. Despite these advancements, there is still a need for systematic benchmarking
015            of neural operator methods against conventional approaches and for the development of datasets
016            representing diverse distributions for robust evaluation.
017            In this paper, we introduce DeepFDM, a benchmark method for learning PDE solution operators
018            based on numerical PDE solvers. DeepFDM leverages the structure of the PDE, in order to achieve
019            better accuracy and generalization compared to neural solvers. It is designed as a solver for a specific
020            class of PDEs and not as a replacement for neural solvers. Moreover, because DeepFDM learns the
021            coefficients of the PDEs, it offers inherent interpretability. We also introduce a principled method
022            for generating training and test data for PDE solutions, allowing for a quantifiable measure of distri-
023
               bution shifts. This method provides a structured approach to evaluate the out-of-distribution (OOD)
               performance of neural PDE operators.
024
025            Our work sets a foundation for future comparisons of neural operator methods with traditional sci-
026
               entific computing approaches, providing a rigorous framework for performance benchmarking, at
               the level of the data and at the level of the neural solver.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031
      Solving Partial Differential Equations (PDE) has long been a critical challenge in many scientific and engineering
032
      domains. Recently, neural network methods have been applied to solve PDEs, with impressive results (Karniadakis
033   et al., 2021; Lu et al., 2021; Li et al., 2020a;b). These methods learn PDE solution operators from PDE solution data
034   sets. They bypass the strict requirements of numerical solvers by treating the problem of solving PDEs as a problem of
035   learning from data. Neural network methods offer a highly flexible and adaptive approach to solving PDEs, compared
036   to traditional numerical PDE solvers, as they can work with a wide variety of equations and input data.
037
      Although recent work has focused mainly on performance gains, with the large number of recent contributions, there
038
      is a need for (i) benchmarking neural operator methods against standard scientific computing approaches and (ii)
039   generating test and training datasets from different distributions.
040
041
      Contribution In this work, we implement a benchmark scientific computing approach to PDE operator learning,
042   DeepFDM, based on numerical PDE solvers and numerical inverse problems. In contrast to traditional inverse problem
043   methods, DeepFDM is implemented as a feedforward convolutional neural network and works with a family of PDEs.
044   This allows it to be used on benchark PDEs and trained using standard neural network methods. Because DeepFDM is
045   designed to work with a specific family of PDEs, it is expected to have better accuracy and generalization than neural
046   PDE solvers. As such, it will be useful for benchmarking the accuracy and generalization of the more flexible neural
047   PDE operator methods. See Table 1. We propose it as a benchmark method for neural solvers, since it corresponds to
048   the scientific computing ground-truth solution of a given PDE, with a given dataset.
049   DeepFDM works by learning the coefficients of the PDE from the data. DeepFDM is not a replacement for existing
050   neural operator methods, as it leverages the more structurally constrained PDE solver framework. In particular, it will
051   lose accuracy on PDEs outside the class for which it was designed. However, when applied to PDEs in the class, it is
052   both theoretically justified and empirically validated, outperforming current neural PDE solvers in both test accuracy
053   and out-of-distribution (OOD) accuracy. Furthermore, because this method is based on learning the coefficients of
      PDEs, it is inherently interpretable; see Figure 1.

      2     R ELATED WORK
109
110
      2.1   N EURAL PDE S
111
112   Early machine learning methods (Rudy et al., 2017) focused on discovering the form of a PDE from solution data,
113   without building solution operators. Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) (Karniadakis et al., 2021; Shin et al.,
114   2020) were among the first models to leverage neural networks to solve PDEs. These methods corresponded to solving
115   a single PDE from incomplete data, rather than learning PDE solution operators from a dataset.
116
117   Key Neural PDE Operator learning papers Lu et al. (2019) propose the DeepONet architecture, which learns
118   PDE solution operators. However, in this case, the PDE is fully known and the PDE residual is included in the loss.
119
      Subsequently, Li et al. (2020b) proposed a similar approach by learning Green’s function for a given PDE. This method
      gave rise to the Fourier neural operator (Li et al., 2020a), which takes advantage of certain assumptions about Green’s
120
      function to solve the problem in the Fourier space. Liu et al. (2022) build neural network models that integrate PDE
121
      operators directly in the model’s architecture while retaining the large capacity neural network architecture.
122
123   There are too many recent works on neural PDEs to mention. Zhang (2024) lists over seven hundred articles, most of
124   them from the last three years, including surveys and benchmark articles. We mention a few relevant papers: Takamoto
125
      et al. (2022), provide a benchmark dataset and an interface for learning PDEs. Solver in the loop Um et al. (2020)
      integrate NN methods with a PDE solver. ClimODE (Verma et al., 2024) solves an advection equation with source.
126
127
      Neural Networks architectures related to differential equations Several works connect neural network architec-
128
      tures and solution operators for differential equations. Chen et al. (2018) proposed a neural network architecture based
129   on ODE solvers and Haber & Ruthotto (2017) focused on the stability aspects of the architecture. Also we have neural
130   SDEs (Tzen & Raginsky, 2019). Ruthotto & Haber (2020) proposed network architectures based on discretized PDE
131   solvers; however, they do not learn PDE operators. Long et al. (2018) represents a PDE solution operator as a feed-
132   forward neural network, and learns both an approximation to the PDE coefficients and the solution operator from the
133   data, however, this early contribution had low accuracy.
134
135   2.2   I NVERSE P ROBLEMS
136
137   Neural PDE operators aim to learn to solve a given PDE from data, without assuming that the form of the PDE is
138   known. In contrast, the PDE inverse problem approach assumes that a specific form of the PDE is known, but that
139
      the coefficients are unknown. More precisely, inverse PDE problems (Taler & Duda, 2006) aim to infer unknown
      parameters of a PDE with a known form, using a dataset of PDE solutions. Although the inverse problem approach
140
      is compatible, in theory, with any numerical or neural PDE method, the drawback is the specialized nature of each
141
      solver, with custom code and custom optimization routines.
142
143   Numerical inverse problems We first discuss the approach that uses forward PDE solvers to learn the PDE coef-
144   ficients. The PDE approach uses numerical PDE solvers, such as finite element methods (FEM) and finite difference
145   methods (FDM) (Larsson & Thomée, 2009). These methods discretize the PDEs on the domain into a system of
146   equations that can be solved numerically. These solvers require knowledge of the full equation governing the process
147   of interest and operate on structured input data, which can limit their applicability and adaptability to a wider range of
148   scientific problems. For inverse problems, numerical PDE solvers are often used in combination with gradient-based
149   optimization techniques. These are implemented in packages such as (COMSOL, 2023; Logg et al., 2012; Virtanen,
150   2020; Ruthotto et al., 2017). These methods are computationally intensive and often require customized code for each
151
      problem formulation. However, when combined with proper regularization and optimization strategies, they provide
      accurate and reliable solutions.
152
153
      Neural inverse problems There are a number of works on neural inverse problems. (These works differ somewhat
154
      from ours, in that they focus on a single problem at a time, rather than developing a methodology for solving a wide
155
      class of inverse problems - they have not applied the same method to a number of benchmark problems). Zhao et al.
156   (2022) solve PDE inverse problems, such as waveform inversion. In this case, the forward solver is given by a graph
157   neural network or by a U-Net. They report faster solution times, compared to using the Finite Element Method for the
158   forward solver. Even using neural networks, their approach has the limitation that changes to the PDEs require training
159   a new forward solver. Huang et al. (2022) treat inverse problems for Darcy and Navier Stokes. Jiao et al. (2024) use
160   DeepONets as a solver in a Bayesian Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) approach to PDE inverse problems, to
161   learn from noisy solutions of a diffusion equation. Zhang et al. (2024) solves inverse problems using a PINN approach
      for the forward solver.

163   for problems with uncertainty in the model and noise in the data, and does not require PDE solvers. Cao et al. (2023)

      Generating accurate Numerical PDE solutions To generate PDE solutions from the initial data, we implemented
339
      a finite difference solver (Larsson & Thomée, 2009). The PDE solver uses the forward Euler method, with a small
340   time step, calculated from the coefficients and spatial resolution, to ensure stability and convergence. This solver can

343   PDEs. Projecting the finer grid solutions avoids biasing the solution towards the particular choice of numerical solver.

      6   R ESULTS
347
348
      In this section, we compared the performance of our proposed benchmark method, DeepFDM, again the state-of-the-
      art neural operator methods, with the primary goal of comparing the accuracy for data generated both in and out of
349
      distribution (OOD). We also report training times and show how DeepFDM is interpretable. Finally, we illustrate how
350
      the benchmark method can be used to study the bias of neural solvers.
351
352   As described above, the training procedure was the same for the neural operators and for DeepFDM, since DeepFDM
353   is implemented as a neural network. However, since DeepFDM also corresponds to an inverse problem method (3),
354
      for (PDE), we expect that it will have higher accuracy and better generalization performance. Moreover, since it has
      many fewer parameters and a simpler architecture, we expect that it will train faster.
355
356   To measure accuracy, we used the normalized mean squared error of the predicted solutions. The normalization factor
357   is designed to set the variance of the initial data (as a function of x) to one and allows for a fair comparison between
358   different distributions, which may have different coefficient norms.
359   For DeepFDM, we used the model architecture described in section 4, designed so that a forward pass corresponds
360   to a numerical solution of (PDE) with all coefficients allowed to be non-zero. The coefficients were assumed to have
361   magnitude at most 2.5. (Making the bounds on the coefficients larger did not have a significant effect since in either
362   case, the model achieved high training accuracy).
363
      For in-distribution data, we used the available solution dataset, obtained from (Liu et al., 2022). To measure the
364   performance of the models on data from a different distribution, we used the method described in section 5 to generate
365   synthetic PDE solution data, sampled from different distributions.
366
367   Considered benchmarks U-Net: We use a U-Net architecture, popular for image-to-image tasks, such as segmen-
368   tation. We consider a 2D U-Net with 4 blocks (Ronneberger et al., 2015). ResNet: We use an 18 block ResNet with
369   residual connections (He et al., 2016). FNO: We use an FNO with 12 modes for all channels and all experiments (Li
370   et al., 2020a). Our results for the ResNet and U-Net matched the results reported for these models in Li et al. (2020a).
371
372   Training dynamics All models are trained using the MSE loss function. All models were trained with the Adam
373   optimizer without weight decay. The training, validation and test data samples were split as 75%, 12.5%, and 12.5%,
374   respectively. All models were run on a Tesla T4, GPU with a batch size of 32. DeepFDM trained faster, and with a
375   smaller generalization gap than the other methods. See Figure 5.

414
415   In this work, we introduced DeepFDM, a benchmark framework for comparing neural Partial Differential Equation
416   (PDE) operators with traditional numerical solvers. Although DeepFDM is not intended as a replacement for neural
417   PDE solvers, it takes advantage of the inherent structure of PDEs to offer improved accuracy, generalization, and in-
418
      terpretability, particularly in out-of-distribution (OOD) scenarios. Furthermore, we proposed a method for generating
      and quantifying distribution shifts using the Hellinger distance, enabling robust performance evaluation across diverse
419
      PDE problems. Our results show that DeepFDM consistently outperforms neural operator methods when applied to
420
      learning PDEs from the class it was designed for. This makes it a valuable tool for benchmarking and advancing neural
421   PDE operator research.
