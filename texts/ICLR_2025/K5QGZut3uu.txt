009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            When the amount of training data is limited, augmenting it with generated data
012            from a simulator can be a beneﬁcial approach to improving prediction accuracy.
013            However, there are no clear metrics on which generated data should be added to
014            the training set and in what proportion, especially when the predictive model is
015            a Gaussian Processes (GPs) model. To address this, we propose using the log
016
               marginal likelihood as a guiding metric. The log marginal likelihood is a the-
               oretically grounded criterion for model selection when incorporating simulator-
017
               generated data into the training set. Nevertheless, computing this metric for GPs
018
               is computationally expensive. To overcome this challenge, we introduce a faster
019            method for calculating the log marginal likelihood by considering the Cholesky
020            factor and matrix element dependencies. Experimental results demonstrate that
021            metrics utilizing the log likelihood outperform basic methods in mean squared
022            error on test set.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Gaussian Processes (GPs) are widely used as predictive models due to their ﬂexibility as non-
      parametric models and their ability to output conﬁdence levels for predictions. Like other machine
028
      learning models, GPs require training data, but in real-world applications, sufﬁcient training data is
029
      often lacking.
030
031   When training data is limited, if access to a simulator capable of generating training data is avail-
032   able, incorporating the generated data can be a beneﬁcial approach to improving prediction accuracy.
033
      Typical data augmentation techniques, such as image ﬂipping and trimming, are a part of this ap-
      proach, but here we consider more complex simulators. For example, when predicting solar power
034
      generation from weather conditions, a power generation simulator can be used in addition to the
035
      previous year’s data.
036
037   However, the data generated by simulators often deviates from the true distribution. In the solar
038   power generation example, the inverters and other equipment used in the simulator tool may differ
039
      from those actually used by the company that wants to predict power generation.
040   Several studies have investigated the utilization of simulators that partially deviate from the true
041   distribution for training predictive models. These approaches can be broadly classiﬁed into two
042   categories: modifying the objective function of the predictive model or modifying the generated
043   data added to the predictive model. The former incorporates a penalty term for the degree of devia-
044   tion from the simulator into the loss function, with a representative example being physics-informed
045
      neural networks Raissi et al. (2019). However, such objective function modiﬁcation methods are dif-
      ﬁcult to apply to non-parametric models, including GPs, because non-parametric models do not have
046
      parameters to optimize with respect to the objective function (except for kernel hyperparameters).
047
      The latter approach of modifying the generated data added to the predictive model is applicable to
048   non-parametric models such as GPs, and our method belongs to this category. Within this approach,
049   existing research focuses on Auto Data Augmentation Cubuk et al. (2019; 2020); Ho et al. (2019);
050   Lim et al. (2019), which parameterizes data augmentation and optimizes those parameters (for more
051   details, see Appendix A.4). However, while Auto Data Augmentation can be applied to simulators
052   where individual operations can be parameterized, such as image ﬂipping and trimming, it is difﬁ-
053   cult to apply to off-the-shelf simulation tools where machine learning engineers cannot access the
      internal workings and thus cannot parameterize individual operations.

054
      Therefore, we adopt a framework that evaluates each generated data point and decides whether to
055   include it in the training data. This framework is applicable to non-parametric models since it does
056   not involve updating model parameters, and it is also applicable to off-the-shelf simulation tools that
057   cannot be internally modiﬁed, as it does not require parameterizing the simulator.
058
      We propose using the log marginal likelihood on training data for evaluating generated data. The
059
      negative log marginal likelihood is a metric that measures the model’s ﬁt to the training data and has
060
      a theoretical foundation that it matches, on average, the Kullback–Leibler (KL) divergence between
061   the true distribution and the model’s distribution. For more details, see Appendix F, G and Shlens
062   (2007).
063
064
      Consequently, the proposed method of evaluating generated data using the log marginal likelihood
      effectively adds the generated data to the training data if the inclusion of the generated data brings the
065
      GPs’ predictive distribution closer to the true distribution on average, and does not add it otherwise.
066
067   Furthermore, evaluating each candidate training data point using the negative log marginal like-
068   lihood can be time-consuming, so we propose a method for fast computation by considering the
069   Cholesky update and the dependencies between matrix elements. Speciﬁcally, using our method,
070
      the computational cost to select generated data to add to the training set can be reduced from
      O(M 3 N + M 2 N 2 + M N 3 ) to O(M 2 N + M N 2 ) where N is the number of true training data
071
      and M is the number of data generated by the simulator.
072
073   The contributions of this research are 2 folds. First, we proposed using the negative log marginal
074   likelihood of the GPs1 as a criterion when selectively adding simulator-generated data to the training
075   data. Second, we proposed an algorithm to efﬁciently compute the negative log marginal likelihood
076
      when selecting training data (Section 4.2).
077
078   2       R ELATED W ORKS
079
080   2.1       E FFICIENT T RAINING DATA S ELECTION M ETHODS FOR GP S
081
082   GPs have a computational complexity of O(N 3 ) during prediction, where N is the number of train-
083
      ing data points. Therefore, two approaches have been developed to reduce the amount of training
      data as much as possible. One approach is called Subset of Data (SoD), which involves retaining
084
      only the good data points from the training data and discarding the rest. The criterion for good-
085
      ness is diversity, and various ways of measuring diversity have been proposed (see Appendix A.1
086   for more details). However, selecting generated data from the simulator to increase diversity may
087   also include data points that deviate from the true distribution. We verify this experimentally. The
088   second approach is sparse Gaussian processes, which generate a small number of pseudo-training
089   data points (see Appendix A.2 for more details). However, this approach is not closely related to the
090   objective of selecting generated data from the simulator.
091
092   2.2       C OMPUTATIONAL T ECHNIQUES FOR GP S
093
094   Due to the high computational complexity of GPs, various computational techniques have been
095
      developed. After training with N data points (after computing the inverse matrix with N data), an
      additional data point can be learned with an added computational effort of O(N 2 ) using a technique
096
      called rank-one update Nguyen-Tuong et al. (2008); Seeger (2004). This technique is used in part
097
      in our method (see Appendix A.3 for more details). Additionally, methods for efﬁciently computing
098   the Cholesky factor of the kernel matrix have been proposed Osborne (2010). However, to efﬁciently
099   compute the log marginal likelihood, it is necessary to compute the Cholesky factor of the covariance
100   matrix (see footnote 5 for mathematical explanations). Section 4.2 presents our proposed method to
101   address this issue.

      3       M ETRICS
104
105
      The metric for deciding whether to add generated data from the simulator to the training data should
106   be based on whether the addition improves the prediction accuracy of the GPs. We use the log
107

      marginal likelihood of the GPs as a metric, which is the average KL divergence between the pre-
109   dictive distribution and the true distribution. (For an explanation of the approximation, refer to
110   Appendix F) Therefore, if the log marginal likelihood improves after adding the generated data, it
111   means that the predictive distribution of the GPs has moved closer to the true distribution on average,
112   and the prediction accuracy is expected to increase.
113
      The process of the proposed method is straightforward. First, the hyperparameters of the GPs are
114
      learned using training data. Next, data (x, y)2 is sampled from the simulator and added to the
115   training data of the GPs. We measure the log marginal likelihood of the GPs, and if it improves,
116   we accept the sampled data as valid training data, otherwise, we discard it. This process is repeated
117   until no continuous improvement is observed or until all generated data has been examined. The
118   GPs that possess both the original training data and the accepted generated data serve as our ﬁnal
119   prediction model.
120
      In Section 3.1, we derive the marginal likelihood when adding the training data candidates generated
121   from the simulator. In Section 4, we propose a method to quickly compute that marginal likelihood.

565
566   A.1   S UBSET OF DATA
567
568   Subset of Data (SoD) is a method that selects important data from candidate training data for GPs.
569   By generating training data candidates from simulators built using domain knowledge, the extent of
570   domain knowledge reﬂection into GPs can be adjusted by determining which data and how much
571   of it is included in the training data. SoD evaluates the training data candidates based on a metric
572
      and greedily adds the highest-ranked ones. The metric for selecting important data is the diversity
      of the training data Seeger et al. (2003); Lawrence et al. (2002); Lalchand & Faul (2018). Various
573
      methods to measure this diversity have been proposed. Lawrence et al. (2002) uses the difference in
574
      entropy between predictive distributions with and without a speciﬁc training data point as its metric.
575   Seeger et al. (2003) uses the KL divergence between predictive distributions with and without a spe-
576   ciﬁc training data point. Lalchand & Faul (2018) employs its unique diversity metric. Speciﬁcally,
577   when predicting with a GPs using the already accepted training data, they add data candidates to
578   the training set where the sum of the squared error and the prediction uncertainty is large. As the
579   training data candidates move farther from the already adopted training data, both the prediction
580   squared error and uncertainty increase. Thus, candidates that enhance the diversity of the training
581   data are chosen. These methods assume that training data candidates are sampled from the true
582   distribution and can ensure diversity within the training set. However, in the context of adjusting
583
      the amount of domain knowledge introduced, training data candidates are sampled from simulators
      using domain knowledge, which means there could be regions that deviate from the true distribu-
584
      tion. Consequently, data deviating from the true distribution might be prioritized, causing predictive
585
      distributions to diverge. Lawrence et al. (2002) employs a method using GPs classiﬁcation, while
586   Seeger et al. (2003) uses a sparse GPs method. Given this, we choose Lalchand & Faul (2018),
587   which can be directly applied to standard GPs regression, to represent the SoD method and compare
588   it with our proposed method in experiments.
589
590   A.2   S PARSE G AUSSIAN P ROCESSES
591
592   Similarly to the Subset of Data, there are methods to generate a small number of pseudo-
593   training data for reducing the training data. Some of these methods, like the proposed ap-
      proach, use the log marginal likelihood as a metric to generate pseudo-training data Titsias (2009);


                                                        11
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


594
      Snelson & Ghahramani (2005). However, none of these compute the exact log marginal likelihood.
595   For example, Titsias (2009) developed sparse GPs to reduce training data by substituting the Gaus-
596   sian process model from p(f |X) to p(f |f m ), avoiding direct dependency of function values f on the
597   training data X, rather relying of function value f m on the pseudo data. This change in the model
598   altered the formula for marginal likelihood, which could not be computed quickly, leading to the
599   use of a lower bound of marginal likelihood as the metric instead Titsias (2009). On the other hand,
600   in our intended applications, all training data are available, so there is no need to alter the marginal
601   likelihood. Our proposed acceleration method allows us to use the exact log likelihood as the metric.
602   Similarly, Snelson & Ghahramani (2005) also uses an approximation of the marginal log likelihood,
603
      not the exact value, as the metric Titsias (2009).
604
605   A.3   R ECOMPUTATION T ECHNIQUES FOR GP S W HEN DATA IS A DDED
606
607   SoD evaluates the goodness of added training data candidates by sequentially adding them to the
608   GP’s training data and measuring the predictive distribution of the model using a metric. The compu-
609   tational effort needed to compute the predictive distribution when data is added has been researched
610   in the domain of Online GPs. After training with N data points (after computing the inverse matrix
611
      with N data), an additional data point can be learned with an added computational effort of O(N 2 )
      using a technique called rank-one update Nguyen-Tuong et al. (2008); Seeger (2004). This tech-
612
      nique is used in part in our method. However, even with this technique, it’s not efﬁcient to compute
613
      the inverse of the covariance matrix for the log marginal likelihood. In this study, we propose a
614   method to efﬁciently compute it.
615
616   Since it takes O(N 3 ) computational effort to compute the predictive distribution of a GPs, meth-
617
      ods have been proposed that combine approximation techniques and online learning to reduce this.
      Among the approximation methods that use the inducing variable method and variational inference,
618
      methods to go online by mini-batch Hensman et al. (2013); Cheng & Boots (2016) and methods to
619
      go online by sequential Bayesian updates Csató & Opper (2002); Bui et al. (2017) have been pro-
620   posed. Also, an online method that used a local GPs has been proposed Nguyen-Tuong et al. (2008).
621   Although our study does not use these approximation methods to compute the log marginal likeli-
622   hood without approximation, they may be utilized in the future to reduce computational effort.
623

      A.4   AUTO -DATA AUGMENTATION
626
      When Data Augmentation rules are considered as one of the inductive biases, one can interpret it
627
      as injecting domain knowledge, such as the rules of augmentation (like an image retains its class
628
      even when ﬂipped), into the machine learning model. Notably, auto data augmentation Cubuk et al.
629   (2019; 2020); Ho et al. (2019); Lim et al. (2019) explores optimal magnitudes and application prob-
630   abilities of multiple data augmentations, like image ﬂips and rotations, based on training data. This
631   concept is similar to our proposed method. The difference lies in the fact that while auto data aug-
632   mentation employs neural network classiﬁers as the discriminative model, our approach uses a GPs
633   regression model. Moreover, auto data augmentation evaluates policies, while our method evaluates
634   individual single data. Although potentially less efﬁcient, our method offers versatility in situations
635   where deﬁning a policy is challenging. Our approach does not optimize the order of samples for
636   evaluation. This is because, compared to the vast neural networks used in auto data augmentation,
637
      the GPs evaluations can be conducted in a shorter computation time. However, considering sample
      order optimization could be a consideration for future work if it becomes crucial.
