009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Sequentially solving similar optimization problems under strict runtime con-
012            straints is essential for many applications, such as robot control, autonomous driv-
013            ing, and portfolio management. The performance of local optimization methods
014            in these settings is sensitive to the initial solution: poor initialization can lead to
015            slow convergence or suboptimal solutions. To address this challenge, we propose
016
               learning to predict multiple diverse initial solutions given parameters that define
               the problem instance. We introduce two strategies for utilizing multiple initial
017
               solutions: (i) a single-optimizer approach, where the most promising initial solu-
018
               tion is chosen using a selection function, and (ii) a multiple-optimizers approach,
019            where several optimizers, potentially run in parallel, are each initialized with a dif-
020            ferent solution, with the best solution chosen afterward. We validate our method
021            on three optimal control benchmark tasks: cart-pole, reacher, and autonomous
022            driving, using different optimizers: DDP, MPPI, and iLQR. We find significant
023            and consistent improvement with our method across all evaluation settings and
024            demonstrate that it efficiently scales with the number of initial solutions required.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028
      Many applications, ranging from trajectory optimization in robotics and autonomous driving to port-
029
      folio management in finance, require solving similar optimization problems sequentially under tight
030   runtime constraints (Paden et al., 2016; Ye et al., 2020; Mugel et al., 2022). The performance of local
031   optimizers in these contexts is often highly sensitive to the initial solution provided, where poor ini-
032   tialization can result in suboptimal solutions or failure to converge within the allowed time (Michal-
033   ska & Mayne, 1993; Scokaert et al., 1999). The ability to consistently generate high-quality initial
034   solutions is, therefore, essential for ensuring both performance and safety guarantees.
035
      Conventional methods for selecting these initial solutions typically rely on heuristics or warm-
036   starting, where the solution from a previously solved, related problem instance is reused. More
037   recently, learning-based solutions have also been proposed, where neural networks are used to pre-
038   dict an initial solution. However, in more challenging cases, where the optimization landscape is
039   highly non-convex or when consecutive problem instances rapidly change, predicting a single good
040   initial solution is inherently difficult.
041
      To this end, we propose Learning Multiple Initial Solutions (MISO) (Figure 1), in which we train
042   a neural network to predict multiple initial solutions. Our approach facilitates two key settings: (i)
043   a single-optimizer method, where a selection function leverages prior knowledge of the problem
044   instance to identify the most promising initial solution, which is then supplied to the optimizer; and
045   (ii) a multiple-optimizers method, where multiple initial solutions are generated jointly to support
046   the execution of several optimizers, potentially running in parallel, with the best solution chosen
047   afterward.
048   More specifically, our neural network receives a parameter vector that characterizes the problem
049   instance and outputs K candidate initial solutions. The network is trained on a dataset of prob-
050   lem instances paired with (near-)optimal solutions and is evaluated on previously unseen instances.
051   Crucially, the network is designed not only to predict good initial solutions—those close to the
052   optimal—but also to ensure that these solutions are sufficiently diverse, potentially spanning all un-
053   derlying modes of the problem in hand. To actively encourage this multimodality, we implement
      training strategies such as a winner-takes-all loss that penalizes only the candidate with the lowest

103    Learning for optimization. Advancements in machine learning have introduced numerous
104    learning-based approaches to optimization problems (Sun et al., 2019). Early work by Gregor &
105    LeCun (2010) replaced components of classical convex optimization algorithms with neural net-
106    works. More recent works aim to replace optimization methods entirely with end-to-end neural net-
107    works (OpenAI et al., 2020; Mirowski et al., 2017) or generate new optimization algorithms (Chen
       et al., 2022b) for specific classes of problems. Other works enhance optimization-based control

      algorithms (Sacks & Boots, 2022), learn constraints (Fajemisin et al., 2024), or learn objective func-
109   tions and system dynamics (Lenz et al., 2015; Wahlström et al., 2015; Tamar et al., 2017; Hafner
110   et al., 2019; Nagabandi et al., 2018; Xiao et al., 2022).
111
112
      Learning initial solutions. Previous studies have proposed heuristic approaches to generate ini-
      tial solutions for optimizers (Johnson et al., 2015; Marcucci & Tedrake, 2020). More recently,
113
      learning-based methods for initializing optimizers have gained attention in various fields, aiming to
114
      enhance both computational efficiency and resulting solutions quality. In mixed-integer program-
115   ming, neural networks have enhanced solver performance by predicting variable assignments (Nair
116   et al., 2020), branching decisions (Sonnerat et al., 2021), and integer variables (Bertsimas & Stel-
117   lato, 2021). Baker (2019) employed Random Forests to predict solutions for AC optimal power flow
118   problems. Kang et al. (2024) utilized nearest neighbor search to warm-start tight convex relaxations
119   in nonconvex trajectory optimization problems. In robot control, neural networks were used to pre-
120   dict initializations for trajectory optimizers or Model Predictive Control (MPC) (Chen et al., 2022a;
121   Wang & Ba, 2019; Lembono et al., 2020). An exciting line of recent work developed differentiable
122   optimization algorithms, which allow jointly learning objectives, constraints, and initializations by
123
      backpropagating through the optimization process (Amos et al., 2018; East et al., 2019; Karkus
      et al., 2022; Sambharya et al., 2023). In contrast, we learn multiple initializations instead of one,
124
      and we do so without strong assumptions about the task or the optimizer. Notably, Bouzidi et al.
125
      (2023) used multiple initializations by repurposing a motion prediction model and Bézier curve fit-
126   ting for a downstream MPC; however, this approach is specifically tailored for autonomous driving,
127   incorporating a dedicated motion prediction module.
128
129
      Parallel optimizers. Leveraging parallelism has a long history in optimization research (Betts &
      Huffman, 1991). With recent advances in parallel computing hardware, such as GPUs, methods that
130
      execute multiple optimizers in parallel have also emerged. For example, Sundaralingam et al. (2023)
131
      introduced cuRobo, a GPU-accelerated method combining L-BFGS and particle-based optimization
132   for robotic manipulators. Similarly, Huang et al. (2024) utilized massive parallel GPU computa-
133   tion for efficient inverse kinematics and trajectory optimization. de Groot et al. (2024) proposed a
134   topology-driven method that plans for multiple evasive maneuvers in parallel. Barcelos et al. (2024)
135   focused on initializing parallel optimizers through rough paths. However, these works have not uti-
136   lized learning. Lembono et al. (2020) explored learning-based strategies for initializing trajectory
137   optimizers based on a database of previous solutions and ensemble-learned models, particularly
138   in manipulation and humanoid control tasks. In contrast, we propose a single neural network to
139   generate multiple initializations, which, as shown in our experiments, significantly outperforms the
140
      ensemble-based approach.

      4     L EARNING M ULTIPLE I NITIAL S OLUTIONS (                      MISO)
163
164
      The main idea of MISO is to train a single neural network to predict multiple initial solutions to
165
      an optimization problem, such that the initial solutions cover promising regions of the optimization
166   landscape, eventually allowing a local optimizer to find a solution close to the global optimum. The
167   key questions are then how to design a multi-output predictor; how to utilize multiple initial solutions
168   in existing optimizers; and how to train the predictor to output a diverse set of initial solutions. In the
169   following, we discuss our proposed solutions to these questions, illustrate the need for multimodality
170   with a toy example, and discuss applications to optimal control.
171
172   4.1   M ULTI - OUTPUT PREDICTOR
173
174   Our multi-output predictor is a standard Transformer model (Appendix A.3) that takes the problem
175   instance, ψ, as input and outputs K initial solutions for the optimization problem,
176
177                                           {x̂init K
                                                 k }k=1 = f (ψ; θ),
178
      where θ are the learned parameters of the network. We train the network on a dataset of problem
179
      instances and their corresponding (near-)optimal solutions, {(ψi , x⋆i )}ni=1 . Such dataset can be
180
      generated offline, for example, by running a slow yet globally optimal solver, or allowing the same
181   local optimizer to run with longer time limits, potentially many times from different initial solutions.

      Our framework can be trivially generalized to allow a different number of optimizers and initial

202

203
      the considered initial solutions, which ensures that even with poor predictions, the final solution
204   quality does not degrade.

280   4.5   A PPLICATION TO OPTIMAL CONTROL
281
282   MISO is applicable to a broad class of sequential optimization problems; however, for the sake of
283   evaluation, we focus on optimal control problems. Optimal control has a wide range of applications,
284   e.g., in robotics, autonomous driving, and many other domains with strict runtime requirements, and
285   due to the complexity induced by constraints and non-convex costs, local optimization algorithms
286
      are highly sensitive to the initial solution.
287   In optimal control the optimization variable x represents a trajectory defined as a sequence of states
288   and control inputs over discrete time steps: τ = {st , ut }t=1:H . Here, st ∈ S and ut ∈ U denote
289   the state and control input at time step t ∈ Z+ , and H ∈ Z+ is the optimization horizon. The
290   constraints involve adhering to the system dynamics fd (st+1 , st , ut ) = 0, starting from an initial
291   state s0 = scurr , where scurr represents the system’s current state. The problem instance parameters
292
      ψ encompass the initial state s0 , and other domain-specific variables that parameterize the objec-
      tive function or constraints, such as target states, reference trajectories, obstacle positions, friction
293
      coefficients, temperature, etc.
294
295   A specific property of optimal control problems is that the relationship between optimization vari-
296   ables, states st and controls ut , are defined by the dynamics constraint fd ; and the initial state s0 is
297   given. Therefore, a sequence of controls uniquely defines an (initial) solution. We can leverage this
298
      property by learning to predict only a sequence of controls instead of the full optimization variable
      of state-control sequences. Further, one can define the training loss over either control, state, or
299
      state-control sequences and backpropagate gradients through the dynamics constraint as long as it
300
      is differentiable. In our experiments, we use state-control loss by default as we found it to improve
301   both our and baseline learning methods. In Appendix A.7, we show that our conclusions hold with
302   control-only loss as well.

362   model, and the optimizer’s solution is recorded but not executed. Methods are assessed by the mean
363   cost of the optimizer’s output over problem instances. (ii) Sequential, which involves solving a se-
364   ries of related optimization problems, executing each proposed solution, and starting the subsequent
365   optimization from the resulting state. This setting simulates real-world conditions where the opti-
366   mizer continuously interacts with a dynamic environment in a closed loop. We evaluate performance
367   by taking the mean cost over problems in a sequence, and then the mean over sequences.
368   To account for the additional time required to predict initial solutions, we assumed that all models
369   perform inference in under 0.85ms, which was the case for all methods on both CPU and GPU,
370   except for the ensemble (see Appendix A.6). In the autonomous driving task, we then reduced the
371   runtime allocated to the optimizer accordingly.
372   Implementation details. To generate the training data, we first create a set of problem instances by
373   sequentially executing the optimizer initialized with the default warm-start strategy. The problem
374   instances are then fed again to an “oracle” version of the optimizer with a significantly increased
375   runtime limit, and the resulting solutions are recorded. After training, evaluation is done on a sep-
376   arate unseen set of problem instances. All experiments are conducted on an Intel Core i9-13900KF
377   CPU and an NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPU. Further implementation details, including hyperparameters
      and training procedures, are in Appendix A.4 and Appendix A.3.

454   7   C ONCLUSIONS AND F UTURE W ORK
455
456
457   We introduced Learning Multiple Initial Solutions (MISO), a novel framework for learning multiple
458   diverse initial solutions that significantly enhance the reliability and efficiency of local optimiza-
459
      tion algorithms across various settings. Extensive experiments in optimal control demonstrated that
      our method consistently outperforms baseline approaches and scales efficiently with the number of
460
      initializations.
461
462   Limitations. Our approach is not without limitations. First, to train a useful model, we rely on
463   the coverage and quality of the training data, as the method does not directly interact with the op-
464   timizer or the underlying objective function. Second, the underlying assumption of our regression
465
      loss is that initial solutions closer to the global optimum increase the likelihood of successful op-
      timization may not hold in complex optimization landscapes with intricate constraints. Third, in
466
      highly complex optimization problems where each solution constitutes a high-dimensional and in-
467
      tricate structure, accurately learning initial solution candidates can become exceedingly challenging,
468   potentially diminishing the effectiveness of our approach.
469
470   Future work. There are several promising directions for future research. To address the aforemen-
471
      tioned limitations, one may simply incorporate the optimization objective into the model training
      loss, thus creating a direct link to the final optimization goal. Alternatively, using reinforcement
472
      learning (RL) to train MISO is a particularly exciting opportunity. By framing the problem in an RL
473
      context, e.g., where the reward is the negative cost of the optimizer’s final solution, models would
474   be directly trained to maximize the probability of the optimizer finding the global optima and may
475   learn to specialize to the specific optimizer. One challenge would be computational, as RL would
476   require running the optimizer numerous times during training.
477
      Other extensions of our approach include probabilistic modeling, e.g., Gaussian mixture models,
478
      variational autoencoders, or diffusion models; however, preventing mode collapse and promoting
479
      diversity would remain a challenge. Future work may explore alternative selection functions, such
480   as risk measures or criteria based on stability, robustness, exploration, or other domain-specific met-
481   rics; as well as using a heterogeneous set of parallel optimizers. Finally, we are excited about various
482   possible applications in optimal control and beyond, where sequences of similar optimization prob-
483   lems need to be solved, for example, localization and mapping in robotics, financial optimization,
484   traffic routing optimization, or even training neural networks with different initial weights, e.g., for
485   meta-learning, or scene representation learning with Neural Radiance Fields or 3D Gaussian splat-
      ting.

      A.1    D ETAILED D ESCRIPTIONS OF BASELINE O PTIMIZERS
651
652   This subsection provides detailed descriptions of the optimization algorithms used in our evalua-
653   tions: First-order Box Differential Dynamic Programming (DDP), Model Predictive Path Integral
654   (MPPI), and the Iterative Linear Quadratic Regulator (iLQR). These algorithms were selected due to
655   their widespread use and effectiveness in solving optimal control problems across various domains.
656
      First-order Box Differential Dynamic Programming (DDP). Building on the work of Tassa et al.
657
      (2014), Amos et al. (2018) introduced a simplified version of Box-DDP that utilizes first-order
658   linearization instead of second-order derivatives. This approach, termed ”first-order Box-DDP,”
659   reduces computational complexity while maintaining the ability to handle box constraints on both
660   the state and control spaces.
661
      Model Predictive Path Integral (MPPI). MPPI (Williams et al., 2015) is a sampling-based model
662
      predictive control algorithm that iteratively refines control inputs using stochastic sampling. Starting
663
      from the current state and a prior solution, it generates a set of randomly perturbed control sequences,
664   simulates their trajectories, and evaluates them using a cost function. The control inputs are then
665   updated based on a weighted average, favoring lower-cost trajectories. We use the implementation
666   from https://github.com/UM-ARM-Lab/pytorch_mppi.
667
      Iterative Linear Quadratic Regulator (iLQR). iLQR (Li & Todorov, 2004) is a trajectory opti-
668
      mization algorithm that refines control sequences iteratively by linearizing system dynamics and
669
      approximating the cost function quadratically around a nominal trajectory. It alternates between
670   a forward pass, simulating the system trajectory, and a backward pass, computing optimal control
671   updates. We use the implementation provided in the nuPlan simulator.
672

687   physics sub-steps per control step. Each episode has a maximum length of Tenv = 50 steps. Both

690

      ẋ0 ∼ U(−1, 1) m/s, θ0 ∼ U(− π2 , π2 ) rad, and θ̇0 ∼ U(− π4 , π4 ) rad/s. The goal state is defined

698

700   0.01 and motor gear ratios of 0.05. The control inputs are constrained by umin = [−1, −1] and
701   umax = [1, 1]. The wrist joint has a limited range of [−160◦ , 160◦ ]. Each episode is limited to
      Tenv = 250 steps. Both optimizers are set with a control noise covariance σ 2 = 1 × 10−3 , a

      A.4   D ESCRIPTIONS OF BASELINE M ETHODS
776
777
      This section introduces the baseline methods used in our experiments in more detail and discusses
778   their implementation specifics. These baselines serve as reference points to evaluate our proposed
779   methods’ performance and understand the benefits and limitations of different initialization strate-
780   gies in optimization algorithms.
781
      Warm-start (K = 1). A common technique involves shifting the previous solution forward by one
782
      time step and padding it with zeros. Assuming the system does not exhibit rapid changes in this
783
      interval, the previous solution should retain local, feasible information.
784
785   Oracle Proxy (K = 1). This proxy serves two purposes: (1) estimating the gap between a real-
786   time-constrained optimizer and an unrestricted one and (2) providing a proxy for a mapping worth
787
      learning. For each optimization algorithm, a suitable heuristic is defined. In DDP, the oracle is
      allowed more iterations to converge; in MPPI, it has a larger sample budget; and in iLQR, it is given
788
      more time to perform optimization iterations.
789
790   Regression (K = 1). This approach involves training a neural network to approximate the oracle’s
791   mapping. Unlike the oracle heuristic, which is impractical for real-time use, the trained neural
792   network requires only a single forward pass.
793   Warm-start with Perturbations (K >1). We utilize the warm-start technique as another baseline
794   by duplicating the proposed initial solution K times and adding Gaussian noise. While this intro-
795   duces some form of dispersion, the resulting initial solutions are neither guaranteed to be feasible
796   nor to ensure any level of optimality.
797   Regression with Perturbations (K >1). Similar to the warm-start with perturbation, after predict-
798   ing an initial solution using the neural network, we duplicate it K times and add perturbations.
799
800
      Ensemble (K >1). An ensemble of K separate neural networks leverages the idea that networks
      initialized with different weights during training will often produce different predictions. The main
801
      drawbacks of this approach are (1) the need to train K neural networks and (2) the requirement to
802
      run K forward passes, which may be impractical for real-time deployment.
803
804   Multi-Output Regression (K >1). A naive approach to predicting K initializations from a single
805   network involves calculating the loss for each prediction and summing the losses. However, since
806
      there is no explicit multimodality objective, these models are prone to mode collapse.
807   MISO Pairwise Distance (K >1). One way to mitigate mode collapse is to introduce an additional
808   term in the loss function, such as the pairwise distance between predictions. While this approach re-
809   quires weight tuning and selecting an appropriate norm, a significant challenge lies in understanding
      how effectively this term promotes multimodality in practice.


                                                          15
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


810
      MISO Winner-Takes-All (K >1). This approach updates only the best-performing mode based
811   on the loss of each prediction. Although no explicit dispersion objective is included, multimodality
812   is indirectly encouraged by maintaining multiple active modes while refining only the best one and
813   not penalizing the others.
814
      MISO Mix (K >1). Lastly, we combine the pairwise distance term with the Winner-Takes-All
815
      approach. While this allows for greater refinement, it adds complexity due to additional hyperpa-
816
      rameters and the need to apply further operations—such as clamping distances—to ensure the loss
817   won’t diverge.

1031   constraints. For example:

       Contextual Adaptation: The selection function can adapt based on the problem instance ψ. For

       A.11    S EQUENTIAL VS . C LOSED - LOOP AND O NE - OFF VS . O PEN - LOOP
1061
1062   In control settings, the terms sequential and closed-loop evaluations are often used interchangeably.
1063   However, in the context of general optimization problems, the notion of ”closed-loop” may not
1064   always be applicable, as there may be no dynamic environment or feedback mechanism involved.
1065   Therefore, we adopt more general terminology—referring to sequential evaluations—to encompass
1066   scenarios where decisions are made in a sequence but without necessarily involving feedback from
1067
       an environment.
1068   On the other hand, the distinction between one-off and open-loop evaluations is subtle yet signifi-
1069   cant. In a one-off evaluation, we assess the optimizer’s performance on individual problem instances
1070   without any interaction with an environment. This means the optimizer solves a static problem, and
1071   we can directly compare different methods on the same set of instances. In contrast, open-loop
1072   control involves sequentially executing actions in an environment without feedback.
1073

1075
1076
       In our experiments, we assess the performance of the methods using two evaluation modes:
1077   One-off Evaluation: In the one-off evaluation, problem instances are uniformly sampled from the
1078   evaluation dataset (disjoint from the training dataset). Each method is tested on the same set of
1079   independently sampled instances, ensuring a fair comparison across methods. The optimizer solves
       each problem instance independently, without any interaction with the environment or influence

1080
       from previous solutions. This evaluation mode focuses on the optimizer’s ability to find high-quality
1081   solutions for individual problems in isolation.
1082
1083   Sequential Evaluation: In sequential evaluation, the optimizer interacts with the environment
1084
       across a series of time steps. Starting from an initial state sampled from the evaluation dataset

1085

1086
       highlighting its ability to manage evolving states and adapt over time.
