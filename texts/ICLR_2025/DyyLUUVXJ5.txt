009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Generating temporally-consistent high-fidelity videos can be computationally ex-
012            pensive, especially over longer temporal spans. More-recent Diffusion Trans-
013            formers (DiTs)— despite making significant headway in this context— have only
014            heightened such challenges as they rely on larger models and heavier attention
015            mechanisms, resulting in slower inference speeds. In this paper, we introduce a
016            training-free method to accelerate video DiTs, termed Adaptive Caching (Ada-
017            Cache), which is motivated by the fact that “not all videos are created equal”:
018
               meaning, some videos require fewer denoising steps to attain a reasonable quality
               than others. Building on this, we not only cache computations through the diffu-
019
               sion process, but also devise a caching schedule tailored to each video generation,
020
               maximizing the quality-latency trade-off. We further introduce a Motion Regular-
021            ization (MoReg) scheme to utilize video information within AdaCache, essentially
022            controlling the compute allocation based on motion content. Altogether, our plug-
023            and-play contributions grant significant inference speedups (e.g. up to 4.7× on
024            Open-Sora 720p - 2s video generation) without sacrificing the generation quality,
025            across multiple video DiT baselines. Our code will be made publicly-available.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020) have become the standard for generative mod-
031   eling in recent years, arguably surpassing the quality of VAEs (Kingma, 2013; Rolfe, 2016), GANs
032   (Karras et al., 2019; Goodfellow et al., 2020) and Auto-Regressive models (Chang et al., 2022;
033
      2023). This observation holds in a wide-range of applications including image (Rombach et al.,
      2022; Saharia et al., 2022), video (Singer et al., 2022; Blattmann et al., 2023a), 3D (Poole et al.,
034
      2022; Liu et al., 2023a), and audio (Kong et al., 2020; Huang et al., 2023) generation, as well as
035
      image (Hertz et al., 2022; Avrahami et al., 2023) and video (Qi et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023) editing.
036   More recent Diffusion Transformers (DiTs) (Peebles & Xie, 2023; Ma et al., 2024a) show better
037   promise in terms of scalability and generalization compared to prior UNet-based diffusion models
038   (Rombach et al., 2022), revealing intriguing horizons in GenAI for the years to come.
039
      Despite the state-of-the-art performance, DiTs can also be computationally expensive both in terms
040
      of memory and computational requirements. This becomes especially critical when applied with a
041
      large number of input tokens (e.g. high-resolution long video generation). For instance, the reason
042   for models such as Sora (OpenAI, 2024) not being publicly-served is speculated to be the high
043   resource demands and slower inference speeds (Liu et al., 2024). To tackle these challenges and
044   reduce the footprint of diffusion models, various research directions have emerged such as latent
045   diffusion (Rombach et al., 2022), step-distillation (Sauer et al., 2023; Yin et al., 2024), caching
046   (Wimbauer et al., 2024; Ma et al., 2024c; Habibian et al., 2024), architecture-search (Zhao et al.,
047   2023b; Li et al., 2024b), token reduction (Bolya & Hoffman, 2023; Li et al., 2024a) and region-
048   based methods (Nitzan et al., 2024; Kahatapitiya et al., 2024). Fewer techniques transfer readily
049   from UNet-based pipelines to DiTs, whereas others often require novel formulations. Hence, DiT
050
      acceleration has been under-explored as of yet.
051   Moreover, we note that not all videos are created equal. Some videos contain high-frequency tex-
052   tures and significant motion content, whereas others are much simpler (e.g. with homogeneous tex-
053   tures or static regions). Having a diffusion process tailored specifically for each video generation
      can be beneficial in terms of realizing the best quality-latency trade-off. This idea has been explored

082   Motivated by the above, we introduce Adaptive Caching (AdaCache) for accelerating video dif-
083   fusion transformers. This approach requires no training and can seamlessly be integrated into a

085   cache residual computations within transformer blocks (e.g. attention or MLP outputs) in a certain
086   diffusion step, and reuse them through a number of subsequent steps, that is dependent on the video

088

089

090   We further introduce a Motion Regularization (MoReg) to allocate computations based on the mo-
091   tion content in the video being generated. This is inspired by the observation that high-moving
092   sequences require more diffusion steps to achieve a reasonable quality. Altogether, our pipeline is
093   applied on top of multiple video DiT baselines showing much-faster inference speeds without sacri-
094   ficing generation quality (see Fig. 1). Finally, we validate the effectiveness of our contributions and
095   justify our design decisions through ablations and qualitative comparisons.

      Diffusion-based Video Generation (Singer et al., 2022; Ho et al., 2022; Blattmann et al., 2023a;

      Girdhar et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024a) has surpassed the quality and diversity of GAN-based

102   et al., 2022), while also being competitive with recent Auto-Regressive models (Yan et al., 2021;
103   Hong et al., 2022; Villegas et al., 2022; Kondratyuk et al., 2023; Xie et al., 2024). They have
104   become a standard component in the pipelines for frame interpolation (Wang et al., 2024c; Feng
105   et al., 2024), video outpainting (Fan et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024e; Wang et al., 2024a), image-
106   to-video Guo et al. (2023); Blattmann et al. (2023a); Xing et al. (2023), video-to-video (i.e., video
107   editing or translation) (Yang et al., 2023a; Yatim et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2024), personalization (Wu
      et al., 2024; Men et al., 2024), motion customization (Zhao et al., 2023a; Xu et al., 2024) and

108
      compositional generation (Liu et al., 2022; Yang & Wang, 2024). The underlying architecture of
109   video diffusion models has evolved from classical UNets (Ronneberger et al., 2015; Rombach et al.,
110   2022) with additional spatio-temporal attention layers (He et al., 2022; Blattmann et al., 2023b;
111   Chen et al., 2023b; Girdhar et al., 2023), to fully-fledged transformer-based (i.e., DiT (Peebles &
112   Xie, 2023)) architectures (Lu et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2024b; Gao et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024b). In
113   the process, the latency of denoising (Song et al., 2020; Lu et al., 2022) has also scaled with larger
114   models (Podell et al., 2023; Gao et al., 2024). This becomes critical especially in applications such
115   as long-video generation (Yin et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2023a; Zhao et al., 2024a; Henschel et al.,
116   2024; Tan et al., 2024; Zhou et al., 2024), while also affecting the growth of commercially-served
117
      video models (Runway AI, 2024; OpenAI, 2024; Luma AI, 2024; Kling AI, 2024).
118   Efficiency of Diffusion models has been actively explored with respect to both training and in-
119   ference pipelines. Multi-stage training at varying resolutions (Chen et al., 2023a; 2024b; Gao
120   et al., 2024) and high-quality data curation (Ramesh et al., 2022; Ho et al., 2022; Dai et al., 2023;
121   Blattmann et al., 2023a) have cut down training costs significantly. In terms of inference accelera-
122   tion, there exist two main approaches: (1) methods that require re-training such as step-distillation
123
      (Salimans & Ho, 2022; Meng et al., 2023; Sauer et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2023b), consistency regu-
      larization (Song et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2023), quantization (Li et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024c; He
124
      et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024b; Deng et al., 2024), and architecture search/compression (Zhao et al.,
125
      2023b; Yang et al., 2023b; Li et al., 2024b), or (2) methods that require no re-training such as token
126   reduction (Bolya & Hoffman, 2023; Li et al., 2024a; Kahatapitiya et al., 2024) and caching (Ma
127   et al., 2024c; Wimbauer et al., 2024; Habibian et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024d; Zhao et al., 2024b).
128   Among these, training-free methods are more-attractive as they can be widely-adopted without any
129   additional costs. This becomes especially relevant for video diffusion models that are both expen-
130   sive to train and usually very slow at inference. In this paper, we explore a caching-based approach
131   tailored for video DiTs. Different from prior fixed caching schedules in UNet-based (Ma et al.,
132   2024c; Wimbauer et al., 2024; Habibian et al., 2024) and DiT-based (Chen et al., 2024d; Zhao et al.,
133   2024b) pipelines, we introduce a content-dependent (i.e., adaptive) caching scheme to squeeze out
134
      the best quality-latency trade-off.
135   Content-adaptive Generation may focus on improving consistency (Couairon et al., 2022; Bar-
136   Tal et al., 2022; Avrahami et al., 2022; 2023; Wang et al., 2023b; Xie et al., 2023), quality (Suin
137   et al., 2024; Abu-Hussein et al., 2022), and/or efficiency (Tang et al., 2023; Nitzan et al., 2024; Ka-
138   hatapitiya et al., 2024; Starodubcev et al., 2024). Most region-based methods (e.g. image or video
139   editing) rely on a user-provided mask to ensue consistent generations aligned with context informa-
140
      tion (Avrahami et al., 2023; Xie et al., 2023). Some others automatically detect (Suin et al., 2024)
      or retrieve (Abu-Hussein et al., 2022) useful information to improve generation quality. Among
141
      efficiency-oriented approaches, there exist proposals for selectively-processing a subset of latents
142
      (Nitzan et al., 2024; Kahatapitiya et al., 2024), switching between diffusion models with varying
143   compute budgets (Starodubcev et al., 2024), or adaptively-controlling the number of denoising steps
144   (Tang et al., 2023; Wimbauer et al., 2024). AdaDiff (Tang et al., 2023) skips all subsequent compu-
145   tations in a denoising step, if an uncertainty threshold is met at a certain layer. Block caching (Wim-
146   bauer et al., 2024) introduces a caching schedule tailored for a given pretrained diffusion model.
147   Both these handle image generation tasks. In contrast, our proposed AdaCache— which also con-
148   trols #denoising-steps adaptively— provides better flexibility, and is applied to more-challenging
149   video generation. It is flexible in the sense that (1) it can selectively-cache any layer or even just a
150   specific module within a layer, and (2) it is tailored to each video generation instead of being fixed
151
      for a given architecture. Thus, AdaCache gains more control over the diffusion process, enabling a
      better-adaptive compute allocation.

      Video Diffusion Transformers are extended from Latent Diffusion Transformers (DiTs) (Peebles
242   & Xie, 2023) introduced for image generation. DiTs provide a much-more streamlined, scalable
243   architecture compared to prior UNet-based diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022), by only hav-
244   ing transformer blocks with a homogeneous token resolution (instead of convolutional blocks with
245   up/downsampling). A simplified transformer block (i.e., w/o normalizing or timestep conditioning
246   layers) in a video DiT is shown in Fig. 4 (right)— gray block. It consists of spatial-temporal atten-
247   tion (STA), cross-attention (CA) and linear (MLP) layers. Depending on the implementation, STA
248   may be a single joint spatio-temporal attention layer, or separate spatial and temporal attention lay-
249   ers repeated within alternating blocks. Without loss of generality, let us denote a latent feature at the
250   input/output of such block by ftl and ftl+1 , respectively. Here, l represents the layer index, and t, the
251
      diffusion timestep. A simplified flow of computations within each block can be represented as,

      4.2   A DAPTIVE C ACHING
263
      In this subsection, we introduce Adaptive Caching (AdaCache), a training-free mechanism for
264   content-dependent compute allocation in video DiTs. The overview of Adaptive Caching is shown in
265   Fig. 4. Compared to a standard DiT that computes representations for all layers across all diffusion
266   steps, in AdaCache, we decide which layers or steps to compute, adaptively (i.e., dependent on each
267   video generation). This decision is based on the rate-of-change in the residual connections (e.g. plt ,
268   qtl or rtl ) across diffusion steps, which amount to all significant computations within the DiT. With-
269   out loss of generality, let us assume that the residuals in block l in current and immediately-prior
      diffusion steps t and t + k are already computed. Here, step t + k is identified as ‘immediately-prior’

297   4.3   M OTION R EGULARIZATION
298
299   To further improve Adaptive Caching by making use of video information, we introduce a Motion
300   Regularization (MoReg). This is motivated by the observation that the optimal number of denoising
301
      steps varies based on the motion content of each generated video. The core idea is to cache less
      (i.e., recompute more) if a generated video has a high motion content. To regularize our caching
302
      schedule, we estimate a latent motion-score (mlt ) based on residual frame differences. Without loss
303
      of generality, let us denote residual latent frames of plt as {plt, n | n = 0, · · · , N − 1} where N is
304   the #frames in latent space (given by the VAE encoder). We estimate the motion-score as,
305
306                                                                  m^l_t &= \|p^l_{t,\;i:N} - p^l_{t,\;0:N-i}\| \;.                                                                                                            (7)
307
      Here, i denotes the frame step-size (or, frame-rate), ∥ · ∥, the L1 distance, and i : j, all frames
308
      within the corresponding range. However, since we operate on noisy-latents, we observe that our
309   motion estimate in early diffusion steps is not reliable. Meaning, it does not provide a reasonable
310   regularization in early steps (i.e., the change in caching schedule does not correlate well with the
311   observed motion of a generated video in pixel space). To alleviate this, we also compute a motion-
312   gradient (mgtl ) across diffusion steps, which can act as a reasonable early-predictor of motion that
313   we may observe in latter diffusion steps (that also correlates with the motion in pixel space).
314
                                                                         mg^l_t &= (m^l_{t} - m^l_{t+k})\;/\; k\;.                                                                                                               (8)
315
316   Finally, we use both motion and motion-gradient as a multiplier for the distance metric (clt ) to regu-
317   larize our caching schedule.
318
319                                                                              c^l_t &= c^l_t \cdot (m^l_t + mg^l_t)\;.                                                                                                        (9)
320   This means, when we have a higher estimated motion, the distance metric will be increased and a
321   smaller basis cache-rate will be selected from the codebook. Similar to before, we enforce a common
322   motion-regularization to all DiT layers by computing a common motion score (i.e., mlt = mt ,
323   mgtl = mgt ), ensuring the stability of denoising process. We can also choose to compute motion at
      different frame-rates, which we ablate in our experiments.

      AdaCache with Motion Regularization: We compare AdaCache with different versions of mo-
460
      tion regularization in Table 2a. Both vanilla and motion-regularized versions provide significant
461
      speedups, 4.7× and 4.5× respectively, at a comparable quality with baseline Open-Sora (Zheng
462   et al., 2024). Considering motion-gradient as an early-prediction of motion at latter diffusion steps
463   helps (83.50 vs. 83.36 on VBench). We also estimate motion at different frame-rates by consider-
464   ing varying step-size in frame differences, which seems to increase the latency without improving
465   quality. Overall, we consider AdaCache + MoReg as the confifuration with best quality-latency
466   trade-off. This improvement in quality is more-prominent in qualitative examples shown in Fig. 6
467   and benchmark comparison in Table 1.
468   Speedups at different resolutions: In Table 2b, we compare the trade-offs of AdaCache at various
469   resolutions of video generations, namely, 480p - 2s, 480p - 4s and 720p - 2s, all at 100-steps.
470   AdaCache provides consistent speedups across different resolutions without affecting the quality.
471
      Cache metric and location: When adaptively deciding the caching schedule, we consider different
472
      metrics to compute the rate-of-change between representations, namely, L1/L2 distance or cosine
473
      distance. Among these, L1/L2 give an absolute measure which aligns better with the actual change.
474   In contrast, cosine computes a normalized-distance, which is not a good estimate of change (e.g. if
475   the representations differ only by a scale, the distance will be zero, even though we want to have
476   a non-zero metric). This observation is verified by the results in Table 2c. Moreover, we consider
477   computing the cache metric at various locations (i.e., layers) in the DiT. Doing so at a single layer
478   (e.g. start, mid, end) is not significantly different from computing an aggregate over multiple-layers
479   (see Table 2d). By default, we compute the cache metric in the mid-layer as a reasonable choice
480   without extra overheads.
481   AdaCache variants: To achieve a range of speedups (and quality), we consider different basis
482   cache-rates in our AdaCache implementation. For instance, we can have higher-speedup with a
483   slightly-lower quality (e.g. AdaCache-fast), a lower-speedup with a higher-quality (e.g. AdaCache-
484   slow), or balance both (e.g. AdaCache-mid). We can conveniently control this by having corre-
485   sponding basis cache-rates as shown in Table 2e. By defualt, we resort to AdaCache-fast which
      gives the best speedups.

531   6     C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this paper, we introduced Adaptive Caching (AdaCache), a plug-and-play component that im-
534   proves the the inference speed of video generation pipelines based on diffusion transformers, with-
535   out needing any re-training. It caches residual computations, while also devising the caching sched-
536   ule dependent on each video generation. We further proposed a Motion Regularization (MoReg) to
537   utilize video information and allocate computations based on motion content, improving the quality-
538   latency trade-off. We apply our contributions on multiple open-source video DiTs, showing com-
539   parable generation quality at a fraction of latency. We believe AdaCache is widely-applicable with
      minimal effort, helping democratize high-fidelity long video generation.
