009                                                    A BSTRACT
010
011            In this work, we propose a novel approach for detecting AI-generated images by leverag-
012            ing predictive uncertainty to mitigate misuse and associated risks. The motivation arises
013            from the fundamental assumption regarding the distributional discrepancy between nat-
014            ural and AI-generated images. The feasibility of distinguishing natural images from
015            AI-generated ones is grounded in the distribution discrepancy between them. Pre-
016            dictive uncertainty offers an effective approach for capturing distribution shifts, thereby
017            providing insights into detecting AI-generated images. Namely, as the distribution shift
018            between training and testing data increases, model performance typically degrades, often
019
               accompanied by increased predictive uncertainty. Therefore, we propose to employ pre-
               dictive uncertainty to reflect the discrepancies between AI-generated and natural images.
020
               In this context, the challenge lies in ensuring that the model has been trained over sufficient
021
               natural images to avoid the risk of determining the distribution of natural images as that of
022            generated images. We propose to leverage large-scale pre-trained models to calculate the
023            uncertainty as the score for detecting AI-generated images. Inspired by MC Dropout, we
024            perturb pre-trained models and find that the uncertainty can be captured by perturbing the
025            weights of pre-trained models. This leads to a simple yet effective method for detecting
026            AI-generated images using large-scale vision models: images that induce high uncertainty
027            are identified as AI-generated. Comprehensive experiments across multiple benchmarks
028            demonstrate the effectiveness of our method.

      1   INTRODUCTION
032
033
      Recent advancements in generative models have revolutionized image generation, enabling the production
      of highly realistic images (Midjourney; Wukong; Rombach et al., 2022). Despite the remarkable capabilities
034
      of these models, they pose significant challenges, particularly the rise of deepfakes and manipulated content.
035
      The high degree of realism achievable by such technologies prompts urgent discussions about their potential
036   misuse, especially in sensitive domains such as politics and economics. In response to these critical concerns,
037   a variety of methodologies for detecting generated images have emerged. A prevalent strategy treats this
038   detection task as a binary classification problem, necessitating the collection of extensive datasets comprising
039   both natural and AI-generated images to train classifiers (Wang et al., 2020).
040
      While existing detection methods have demonstrated notable successes, they typically encounter challenges
041
      in generalizing to images produced by previously unseen generative models Wang et al. (2023a). One
042   promising avenue to enhance the robustness of detection capabilities involves constructing more extensive
043   training datasets by accumulating a diverse array of natural and synthetic images. However, these attempts
044   are often computationally intensive, requiring substantial datasets for effective binary classification. Ad-
045   ditionally, maintaining robust detection necessitates continually acquiring images generated by the latest
046   models. And when the latest generative models are not open-sourced, acquiring a large number of generated

092   & Ghahramani, 2016) and Deep Ensembles (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017). However, in our attempts,

133   2   R ELATED W ORKS
134
      AI-Generated images detection. Recent advancements in generative models, such as those by (Brock et al.,
135
      2019; Ho et al., 2020), have led to the creation of highly realistic images, highlighting the urgent need for
136   effective algorithms to distinguish between natural and generated images. Prior research, including works
137   by (Frank et al., 2020; Marra et al., 2018), primarily focuses on developing specialized binary classification
138   neural networks to differentiate between natural and generated images. Notably, CNNspot (Wang et al.,
139   2020) demonstrates that a standard image classifier trained on ProGAN can generalize across various archi-
140   tectures when combined with specific data augmentation techniques. F3Net (Qian et al., 2020) distinguishes

                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


141
      real face images from fake face images with the help of frequency statistical differences. NPR (Tan et al.,
142
      2024) introduces the concept of neighboring pixel relationships to capture differences between natural and
143   generated images. Although these methods show superior performance on generators in the training set,
144   they often do not generalize well to unknown generators. In addition to this, training-based methods are
145   susceptible to small perturbations in the image. For this reason, recently, some training-free methods have
146   been proposed. AEROBLADE (Ricker et al., 2024) calculates the reconstruction error with the help of the
147   autoencoder used in latent diffusion models (Rombach et al., 2022). RIGID (He et al., 2024) finds that real
148   images are more robust to small noise perturbations than generated images in the representation space of
149   the vision foundation models and exploits this property for detection. However, these methods usually make
150   overly strong assumptions about natural or generated images, leading to insufficient generalization. In our
151
      paper, we propose a training-free detection method through uncertainty analysis. Based on the widespread
      phenomenon that generated images have greater uncertainty than real images on models trained with real
152
      images, our method achieves robust detection performance.
153
154   Uncertainty estimation. Uncertainty estimation in machine learning has seen significant advancements
155   in recent years. (Gal & Ghahramani, 2016) introduces Monte Carlo Dropout (MC Dropout), which uses
156   dropout at inference to estimate uncertainty from the variance of multiple predictions. (Lakshminarayanan
157
      et al., 2017) develops deep ensembles, demonstrating improved uncertainty estimates through training mul-
      tiple model indepently with different initializations. Recent work by (Snoek et al., 2019) analyzes the cali-
158
      bration of uncertainty in deep learning models, highlighting the importance of reliable uncertainty measures.
159
      Additionally, (Guo et al., 2017) explore the use of temperature scaling to enhance the calibration of model
160   predictions. In our paper, We measure uncertainty by perturbing the model’s weights.
161
162   Random weight perturbations. Weight perturbation, i.e. adding noise to network weights, has been exten-
163
      sively studied. Many methods (Khan et al., 2018; Wu et al., 2020) perform training regularization by adding
      noise to the weights of the neural network during training to improve the generalization of the network.
164
      Weight perturbation has also been applied in adversarial attacks to study the robustness of the network (He
165
      et al., 2019; Garg et al., 2020). In addition to this, some works also study the sensitivity of neural network
166   to weights perturbation. (Cheney et al., 2017) shows that convolutional networks are surprisingly robust to
167   a number of internal perturbations in the higher convolutional layers but the bottom convolutional layers
168   are much more fragile. (Weng et al., 2020) proposes an efficient approach to compute a certified robust-
169   ness bound of weight perturbations. In our work, we find neural networks trained on natural images exhibit
170   different robustness to weight perturbations for natural and generated images. Based on this property, we
171   propose an efficient algorithm to distinguish between natural and generated images.

      3.1   M OTIVATION
176
177   Our method is built upon a foundational assumption that natural and generated images have different distri-
178   butions. This is a reasonable assumption; otherwise, we cannot distinguish between natural and generated
179   images. Fortunately, this assumption is consistent with previous work (Tan et al., 2024) and our empirical
180   observations, as shown in Figure 1.
181
      Hence, for the large models trained merely on natural images, we can regard natural images as in-distribution
182
      (ID) data while generated images as out-of-distribution (OOD) data. This distribution discrepancy can be
183   reflected by the widely used predictive uncertainty, since neural networks typically exhibit higher uncertainty
184   for OOD samples (Snoek et al., 2019; Schwaiger et al., 2020). This leads to a simple yet novel approach
185   to determine whether a test image is generated by AI models when we can calculate its uncertainty on a
186   pre-trained large vision model. In the following, we first give a complete introduction to our method WePe,
187   then describe on our reasons for choosing the method, and finally, we explore why WePe works.

225   3.4   A N OVERVIEW OF W E P E
226
227
      As discussed above, the proposed WePe is based on a large model pre-trained on a large number of natural
      images. In this work, we chose DINOv2 (Oquab et al., 2024), a large model trained with contrastive learning
228
      on image data. In order to capture the uncertainty of the model on the test images, we extract image features
229
      using the original model and the model after adding noise to the parameters respectively. The similarity
230   between the pre-perturbation and post-perturbation feature vectors is quantified using a suitable distance
231   metric, such as cosine similarity. Images exhibiting high similarity are classified as real, while those with
232   low similarity are identified as generated. This method not only capitalizes on the characteristics of the
233   DINOv2 model but also provides a robust framework for distinguishing between real and generated images
234   based on their feature stability under model perturbation.

      3.5   D ISCUSSIONS
236
237   Why choose DINOv2? In addition to DINOv2, CLIP is a commonly used model. However, our experiments
238   show that CLIP performs sub-optimally compared to DINOv2 (see Table 4). We believe this difference
239   stems from their training strategies. Unlike DINOv2, CLIP is a multimodal model that combines image
240   and textual features from captions for contrastive learning, which may lead it to focus on broader semantic
241   features rather than fine details. In contrast, DINOv2 emphasizes contrastive learning solely on images,
242   allowing it to better capture subtle differences between natural and generated images. Therefore, we use
243
      DINOv2 in our experiments.
244   Why choose weight perturbation? Common methods for measuring uncertainty include MC Dropout
245   and Deep Ensembles. MC Dropout involves keeping dropout active during testing and performing multiple
246   forward passes on the inputs to generate outputs with different network structures. The variability among
247   these outputs serves as an estimate of the model’s uncertainty regarding the input data. However, since
248   DINOv2 does not utilize dropout during training, MC Dropout may not yield optimal results (see Table 5).
249
      Deep Ensembles, on the other hand, trains multiple networks independently and uses the differences in
      their outputs on test samples to assess uncertainty. However, training multiple DINOv2-level models is
250
      impractical. Therefore, in our study, we choose to perturb the model parameters and assess the differences
251
      in outputs from the original and perturbed models to estimate uncertainty for the test images.
252
253   Why does weight perturbation work? Incorporating weight perturbations during testing can effectively
254   simulate Bayesian inference, thereby capturing the inherent uncertainty in neural networks. From a Bayesian
255
      perspective, the weights of a neural network are not fixed but rather distributions reflecting the range of
      plausible values given the data. By introducing noise into the network weights at test time, we mimic the
256
      process of drawing samples from a posterior distribution over weights, a core concept in Bayesian inference.
257
      This technique enables the model to generate diverse predictions, reflecting its uncertainty, particularly when
258   encountering out-of-distribution samples. This approach is akin to Bayesian neural networks, where weight
259   uncertainty is explicitly modeled. For example, (Blundell et al., 2015) proposed using variational methods
260   to approximate weight distributions in Bayesian neural networks, allowing for uncertainty quantification
261   through weight perturbations. Similarly, (Gal & Ghahramani, 2016) demonstrated that introducing dropout
262   at test time serves as a Bayesian approximation, with the added noise acting as a proxy for weight sampling,
263   thus allowing for reliable uncertainty estimation. In this paper, we capture uncertainty by adding noise
264   directly to the model parameters to distinguish between natural and generated images. However, trying
265   to theoretically analyze the different sensitivities of natural and generated images to model weights in the
266
      representation space of a large model is a difficult task. Therefore, we only draw this conclusion through
      various empirical observations in this paper, and do not directly prove this theoretically. The theoretical
267
      proof will be left to our future work.

      In this work, to effectively address the challenges of detecting AI-generated images, we propose a novel

      crepancies in distribution between natural and AI-generated images, we can significantly enhance detection
462   performance. The use of large-scale pre-trained models allows for accurate computation of predictive un-

465   ing direction for improving AI-generated image detection and mitigating potential risks associated with their
466   misuse. Future work could delve deeper into refining the predictive models and exploring additional features
467   that could further enhance detection accuracy.

691   A.2   C ONCERNS ABOUT MODELS BEING CONTAMINATED BY GENERATED IMAGES
692
693   With the proliferation of generated images, future large-scale models could become contaminated by such
694   data, making it increasingly difficult to distinguish between natural and generated images. One potential
695
      solution is to employ machine unlearning (Yao et al., 2023) techniques to detect and address generated
      images. Machine unlearning focuses on removing the influence of specific data from pre-trained models,
696
      primarily due to privacy concerns. In this context, when generative images are incorporated into the training
697
      process of large-scale models, we can utilize machine unlearning techniques to eliminate the effects of these
698   images on pre-trained models. This approach would help ensure that the features of natural and generated
699   images remain distinct and separable.

732   That said, we can confirm the existence of feature distribution discrepancy of generated and natural images
733   based on an important metric for evaluating generative models, the FID score. The FID score measures the
734   feature distribution discrepancy between natural and generated images on the Inception network (Szegedy
735   et al., 2015). When the FID score is 0, it indicates that the two distributions do not differ. However, even
736   on these simple networks such as Inception v3, advanced generative models like ADM still achieve an FID
737   score of 11.84, not to mention that on powerful models such as DINOv2, we observe significant feature
738   distribution discrepancy.

      A.11    C OMPARISON WITH RIGID
847
848   The main differences between WePe and RIGID are as follows:
849
850          • The approach proposed by RIGID stems only from the phenomenon it observed: namely, that
851            natural and generated images show different sensitivities to noise in the representation space of
852
               DINOv2. Instead, WePe explicitly proposes that there is distribution discrepancy between natural
               and generated images, and utilizes the difference in uncertainty to expose distribution discrepancy
853
               between natural and generated images.
854
855          • RIGID utilizes the difference in sensitivity to noise between natural image and generated image
856
               for detection. Although the generated image is more sensitive, it is easy to think of a way to avoid
               detection, i.e., the user adds noise to the generated image and then submits it for detection.
857
               This approach easily removes the sensitivity of the generated image to noise. As shown in Table 13,
858
               after adding noise to a generated image, RIGID determines the noisy generated image as natural
859            images. This is a fatal flaw of RIGID. However, WePe is not affected by this. WePe exposes the
860            difference in distribution between the test image and the natural image through weight perturba-
861            tion. Adding noise to the generated image further increases this difference, leading to even better
862            detection performance.

864   A.12    N OTE ON THE UNBIASED ASSUMPTION
865
866   In section 3.3, we make an assumption that the expected extracted feature by noised models is unbiased for
867   that by the original model. We think this assumption is reasonable. Thanks to the over-parameterization
868   of modern neural networks and advanced optimization algorithms (e.g., AdamW), it is a well-established
869   fact that trained neural networks are usually smooth in the parameter space and show robustness to small
870   weight perturbation (Novak et al., 2018). And this robustness is used in many applications, such as quan-
871
      tization (Gholami et al., 2022) and pruning (Liu et al., 2019). And in Figure 2, we also clearly show this
      robustness: when adding tiny noise to the model weights, the features remain almost unchanged.
