011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            The data-intensive nature of Diffusion models amplifies the risks of privacy in-
014            fringements and copyright disputes, particularly when training on extensive unau-
015            thorized data scraped from the Internet. Membership Inference Attacks (MIA)
016
               aim to determine whether a data sample has been utilized by the target model dur-
               ing training, thereby serving as a pivotal tool for privacy preservation. Current
017
               MIA employs the prediction loss to distinguish between training member samples
018
               and non-members. These methods assume that, compared to non-members, mem-
019            bers, having been encountered by the model during training result in a smaller
020            prediction loss. However, this assumption proves ineffective in diffusion models
021            due to the randomly noise sampled during the training process. Rather than es-
022            timating the loss, our approach examines this random noise and reformulate the
023            MIA as a noise search problem, assuming that members are more feasible to find
024            the noise used in the training process. We formulate this noise search process as
025            an optimization problem and employ the fixed-point iteration to solve it. We an-
026            alyze current MIA methods through the lens of the noise search framework and
027
               reveal that they rely on the first residual as the discriminative metric to differenti-
               ate members and non-members. Inspired by this observation, we introduce OMS,
028
               which augments existing MIA methods by iterating One More fixed-point Step to
029
               include a further residual, i.e., the second residual. We integrate our method into
030            various MIA methods across different diffusion models. The experimental results
031            validate the efficacy of our proposed approach.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   Recently, diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020b) have been widely recognized for their
037   unparalleled capability to generate images of exceptional quality, which are increasingly becoming
038   indistinguishable from their real-world counterparts. Due to the high quality of images generated by
039   diffusion models, an increasing number of AI companies are developing generative tools predicated
040   on diffusion models for commercial art design.
041
      Nonetheless, these advancements are accompanied by inherent challenges. The data-intensive nature
042   of diffusion models has amplified the risk of privacy infringements and copyright disputes. Trained
043   on extensive unauthorized data scraped from the Internet, these methods overlook the copyrights and
044   privacy of the original owners. A case in point is the recent lawsuit filed by Getty Images against
045   Stability AI, alleging unauthorized use of 12 million of Getty’s images for model training (Brittain,
046   2023). Thus, it is imperative to develop effective tools to detect diffusion models’ privacy infringe-
047   ments.
048   To audit these privacy risks, Membership Inference Attacks (MIA) (Shokri et al., 2017) have
049   emerged as a potential solution. The objective of MIA is to ascertain whether a data sample has
050   been utilized in the training process of a machine learning model. Existing MIA methods (Sablay-
051   rolles et al., 2019; Salem et al., 2019; Song & Mittal, 2021) typically operate under the assumption
052   that member records tend to exhibit lower prediction losses compared to non-member records. Con-
053   sequently, these methodologies compute the prediction losses and utilize this metric to differentiate
      between member and non-member records.


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      Although the utilization of prediction loss to differentiate between member and non-member records
055   has been empirically validated for numerous deterministic models, such as classification models and
056   Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) (Chen et al., 2020; Hayes et al., 2019; Hilprecht et al.,
057   2019; Choquette-Choo et al., 2021; Hanzlik et al., 2021), its efficacy is diminished when applied to
058   diffusion models due to the intractability of the training loss. More precisely, during the training
059   process of the diffusion model, a random noise is sampled, serving not only as a component of
060   the model’s input but also as the target in the training loss. However, during the execution of the
061   membership inference, it is virtually impossible to replicate the exact noise sampled during the
062   training phase. The discrepancy between the noise used during training and membership inference
063
      contributes to the inaccuracy of the loss estimation.
064   To address these issues, instead of the loss assumption, we propose an alternative hypothesis: it
065   is more feasible for members to find the noise counterpart used in the training process. This
066   assumption aligns more closely with the inherent stochastic characteristics of diffusion model train-
067   ing. Based on this assumption, we introduce a membership inference framework tailored to diffusion
068   models, leveraging a noise searching mechanism. We formalize the noise searching process as an
069
      optimization problem with the training loss as the optimization objective.
070   Moreover, we propose to utilize the fixed-point iteration to solve the optimization problem. By it-
071   eratively applying a function to the initial guess, we strive to facilitate the convergence to the noise
072   encountered by the members during the training stage. We begin with an empirical analysis focuses
073   on the convergence properties of the fixed-point iteration. We discern a distinct attribute where
074   member samples exhibit faster convergence rate compared to non-member samples. This observa-
075
      tion implies that the convergence rate can be employed as a discriminative feature to differentiate
      between member and non-member samples.
076
077   This attribute provides further insights into current MIAs for diffusion models. Specifically, from
078   the perspective of the fixed-point iteration, we reinterpret current MIA methods as assessments
079   of convergence rate, primarily through the first residual. To refine this measurement and capture
080   the nuances of convergence dynamics more comprehensively, we introduce an augmentation to the
081
      iteration process, incorporating an additional step that considers the second residual. We term this
      extension the “One More Step” (OMS) approach.
082
083   We conduct experiments across various diffusion models, spanning CNN-based and Transformer-
084   based architectures, along with various datasets and MIA methods. Notably, to the best of our
085   knowledge, we are the first to evaluate MIA performance on Transformer-based diffusion models.
086   The experimental results demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed OMS approach and the noise
087
      searching-based MIA framework. In summary, our paper makes the following contributions:
088
             • We reveal the noise inconsistency issues in current MIA methods for diffusion models. To
089
               address this, we devise a novel framework in the perspective of noise searching. Formally,
090            we conceptualize the noise searching process as an optimization problem.
091
092
             • We propose the fixed-point iteration to solve the noise searching optimization problem.
               Moreover, we investigate its convergence properties in practice and find that members ex-
093
               hibit faster convergence rate compared to non-members.
094
095          • We analyze existing MIA methods through the proposed framework, revealing that the
096
               efficacy of existing methods is linked to the convergence rate, particularly as characterized
               by the first residual. Motivated by this, we introduce a refinement strategy by iterating One
097
               More Step (OMS) to include the second residual.
098
099          • We conduct experiments on various diffusion models, encompassing CNN-based and
100
               Transformer-based architectures, using various datasets. The results not only confirm the
               validity of our MIA framework but also underscore the efficacy of the OMS refinements.

103   2   R ELATED W ORK
104
105   Membership Inference Attack (MIA). The Membership Inference Attack (MIA), initially intro-
106   duced by Shokri et al. (2017), is a technique designed to extract privacy information from machine
107   learning models. Its primary objective is to predict the presence of a specific data record in the
      training set of a given model. The effectiveness of MIA fundamentally relies on the hypothesis that


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      machine learning models exhibit differential responses to member records versus unfamiliar non-
109   member records. Given the manner to exploit model’s reactions, existing methods can be divided
110   into two categories, model-based methods and metric-based methods. In the realm of model-based
111   methods (Shokri et al., 2017; Salem et al., 2019; Long et al., 2020; Chen et al., 2020; Truex et al.,
112   2019), a shadow model is trained to mimic the responses of the target machine learning model. Sub-
113   sequently, attack algorithms are formulated, predicated on the reactions of the shadow model, with
114   the ultimate objective of achieving generalization to the target model. For instance, Shokri et al.
115   (2017) employ multiple shadow models to augment the attack success rate of the MIA task. Salem
116   et al. (2019) discerns that the success of the shadow model is attributable to the transferability of
117
      the target machine learning model’s output distribution. Long et al. (2020) initially select vulnera-
      ble (outlier) samples and find that these outlier samples are subject to a heightened privacy risk by
118
      shadow model-based attacks. Despite the significant advancements in the field, model-based meth-
119
      ods are characterized by their computational intensity and exhibit susceptibility to alterations in the
120   model’s architecture.
121
122   Methods grounded in metrics (Sablayrolles et al., 2019; Yeom et al., 2018; Salem et al., 2020;
123
      Bentley et al., 2020) primarily employ a metric (typically the loss value) as a representative measure
      of the model’s response to each sample. The membership of a specific sample is subsequently
124
      determined based on the numerical values of the selected metric. For example, Sablayrolles et al.
125
      (2019) delve into the investigation of the metric within a white-box context, concluding that the most
126   effective metric is the training loss function. Yeom et al. (2018) leverage the average training loss
127   as their chosen metric and present a discussion on the interplay between model overfitting and the
128   performance of membership inference. Bentley et al. (2020) examine the relationship between the
129   generalization gap and membership inference, positing that a deficiency in generalization escalates
130   the risk of model privacy leakage.
131
      MIA for diffusion models. Given the computational intensity of training a shadow model with
132   comparable parameters, model-based methods are deemed unsuitable for diffusion models. As a
133   result, most current MIA tailored for diffusion models (Duan et al., 2023; Matsumoto et al., 2023;
134   Kong et al., 2023) are metric-based methods. These methods share a core assumption with MIAs
135   applied to other models, which assumes that the loss value for members is smaller than that for non-
136   members. A significant contribution to this field is made by Matsumoto et al. (2023), who introduce
137   a pioneering MIA method for diffusion models. This approach utilizes the training loss of the
138   model and identifies a timestep at which the divergence of loss between members and non-members
139   is maximized, thereby optimizing the performance of membership inference. SecMI (Duan et al.,
140
      2023) exploits the approximated posterior error as a proxy to estimate the training loss, which subse-
      quently serves as the membership inference metric. PIA (Kong et al., 2023) endeavors to reconstruct
141
      the training loss through the complete sampling path, thereby leveraging the training loss throughout
142
      the entire diffusion process.
143
144   Despite achieving substantial performance, these methods still suffer from the inaccuracy of loss
145   estimation. During the training phase of the diffusion model, the loss value is dictated by the training
146
      target (a random Gaussian noise). However, when executing the MIA, replicating the exact noise
      sampled during the training process is virtually unattainable. Instead, we propose a novel MIA
147
      framework predicated on noise searching. This innovative approach promises to enhance the overall
148
      performance of MIA and provide insight into the principles of MIA methods for diffusion models.

      Given a data record x0 , the goal of Membership Inference Attacks (MIA) is to identify whether x0
154
      is in the training set of the target diffusion model ϵθ . Existing MIAs (Duan et al., 2023; Matsumoto
155
      et al., 2023; Kong et al., 2023) tailored for diffusion models predominantly assume that the loss
156   values for members are lower than those for non-members. However, these loss-based approaches
157   are susceptible to inaccuracies in loss estimation, which are caused by noise inconsistency between
158   the training and inference stages (Section 3.2). In contrast, we propose a novel MIA framework that
159   employs noise searching, an approach we believe aligns more closely with the stochastic nature of
160   the model’s training process. We formulate the noise searching process as an optimization prob-
161   lem and propose to use the fixed-point iteration to solve this problem (Section 3.3). We posit that
      members can retrieve the training noise with less effort compared to non-members. Subsequently,


                                                         3

      we analyze the convergence properties of the fixed-point iteration and further validate that it is more
163   feasible for members to search the noise than non-members (Section 3.4). Motivated by this insight,
164   we reinterpret the underlying mechanisms contributing to the efficacy of existing MIA methods and
165   propose an enhancement by incorporating One More iteration Step (OMS) (Section 3.5).

192   3.2   N OISE I NCONSISTENCY BETWEEN T RAINING AND I NFERENCE
193
194   The diffusion model’s training procedure can be described by Equation 5. To elaborate, given the
195   input image x0 and a specific timestep t, a random noise ϵtrain is sampled from the standard nor-
196   mal distribution. This noise is then utilized to perturb x0 into a corrupted version xt , following the
197
      schedule predefined in Equation 2. Subsequently, the diffusion model, parameterized by θ generates
      a prediction of the noise within xt (denoted as ϵθ (x0 , t, ϵtrain )). The training loss for the diffusion
198
      model is computed as the distance between the predicted noise ϵθ (x0 , t, ϵtrain ) and the actual sam-
199
      pled noise ϵtrain . During the inference phase, due to the infeasibility of the training noise ϵtrain ,
200   an alternate noise ϵinf is sampled to estimate the loss value. However, it is important to note that
201   there exists no guarantee that ϵinf is identical or approximately similar to the noise ϵtrain . This
202   inconsistency in noise significantly impacts the accuracy of the loss values, consequently affecting
203   the effectiveness of existing Membership Inference Attacks (MIA) targeting diffusion models (Duan
204   et al., 2023; Matsumoto et al., 2023; Kong et al., 2023).

206   3.3   MIA BY N OISE S EARCHING

208   In contrast to existing MIA that rely on the randomly sampled inference noise as a surrogate for the
209
      training noise ϵtrain to approximate the loss, thereby encountering the noise inconsistency issue,
      we introduce a novel MIA framework for diffusion models, leveraging a noise search strategy. Our
210
      approach aims to reconstruct, for a given record x0 , the corresponding training noise ϵtrain that min-
211
      imizes the training loss as defined in Equation 5. We assume that it is more feasible for members
212   to obtain the training noise ϵtrain . We formulate the process of noise searching as an optimization
213   problem:

      This optimization framework directly addresses the noise inconsistency issue by focusing on iden-
217   tifying the true training noise ϵtrain . Compared with loss-based methods, the proposed approach
218   emphasizes the identification of ϵtrain . We argue this approach aligns more coherently with the

      Fixed-point iteration. We address the aforementioned optimization problem by the fixed-point
221
      iteration. For a given record x0 and timestep t, the predicted noise ϵθ (x0 , t, ϵ) is solely dependent on
222
      ϵ. This dependency can be represented as an implicit function ϵ = f (ϵ). The optimal noise, which
223   pairs with the record x0 during the training process, is also identified as the solution to the implicit
224   function f . To address this, we employ the fixed-point iteration (Smart, 1980). The iterative process
225   can be represented as follows:
226
227                                        ϵn = f (ϵn−1 ),    n = 1, 2, ...                                  (7)
228   We assume that the fixed-point iteration process essentially satisfies the constraints embedded within
229   the optimization problem, given the fact that the model ϵθ is trained to generate noises adhering to
230   the distribution. Consequently, we hypothesize that the model’s outputs also conform to the standard
231   normal distribution. Note that we do not use more advanced methods for solving implicit functions
232   such as Newton-Raphson or Conjugate Gradient (Nocedal & Wright, 1999). This is because the
233   Newton-Raphson method needs to compute gradients while the Conjugate Gradients need to search
234   high dimension gradients, both of which are computationally intensive and potentially intractable.

520   5    C ONCLUSION
521
522   In this paper, we explore the MIA for diffusion models in a novel perspective, i.e., the noise search-
523   ing. We first analyze the noise inconsistency issue between the training and membership inference
524   stage. To address this issue, we introduce a noise searching framework that formulates the search
525   for optimal training noise as an optimization problem. Utilizing the fixed-point iteration, we solve
526   the optimization problem and conduct a thorough examination of its convergence properties, reveal-
527   ing distinct convergence rates between member and non-member data. Inspired by this insight, we
528
      rethink the effectiveness of current MIA methods and propose an enhancement through one more
      iteration step, resulting in a substantial performance boost for existing MIA methods. The noise
529
      searching framework provides a unique and unified perspective for comprehending the fundamental
530
      principles of MIA tasks for diffusion models. We anticipate that our contributions will foster fur-
531   ther research into the privacy risks associated with diffusion models and contribute to the ongoing
532   research in this field.

      The Convergence Property. The theoretical foundation for fixed-point iteration is grounded in
728

729
      that if g is a contraction mapping on a complete metric space, then g possesses a unique fixed
730   point, and the sequence generated by the iteration will converge to this fixed point for any initial
731   guess. Specifically, a contraction mapping is defined as a function for which there exists a constant
732   0 ≤ k < 1 such that for any x, y in the space, d(g(x), g(y)) ≤ k · d(x, y), where d is a metric.

1004   G    L IMITATION AND F UTURE W ORK
1005
1006   Our proposed framework suffers from two primary limitations. First, taking a high-dimensional
1007   nonlinear complex neural networks (i.e., the diffusion model) as the implicit function, there exists
1008   an absence of rigorous mathematical proofs to ensure the convergence of the fixed-point iteration
1009   process. Though we validate its convergence in practical use, this gap in theoretical understanding
1010   poses a challenge for the stability of our framework. Second, while OMS shows promise in en-
1011   hancing existing MIA methods, the introduction of an additional iteration step necessitates an extra
1012
       query to the target diffusion model and an increase in computational resources. This trade-off be-
       tween performance enhancement and computational cost underscores the need for future research to
1013
       explore more efficient iteration strategies, such as Krasnoselkij, Mann and Ishikawa iterations (Babu
1014
       & Vara Prasad, 2006; Berinde, 2004a;b). We leave it for our future work.

1019
1020   This section presents a discussion on the practical applications of MIAs in the context of diffusion
1021   models. The implications of MIA vary depending on the stakeholders involved, namely individuals
1022   and organizations. For individuals, MIA poses a threat to data privacy as they allow attackers to
1023   deduce whether a particular data sample has been used to train a specific machine learning model.
1024   This can lead to the leakage or exposure of training data privacy, which is particularly alarming when
1025   the data used in model training is sensitive (e.g., medical data). Consequently, understanding MIA
       raises awareness about the protection of personal data privacy. Conversely, MIA can also serve as a


                                                                    19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
       tool for individuals to ascertain whether their data (such as their portrait or artwork) has been used
1027   without authorization. Given that diffusion models are utilized in various applications, the detection
1028   of data abuse has become an urgent necessity. By determining whether an individual’s identity
1029   is included in the training set of the target diffusion model, one can infer unauthorized usage and
1030   thereby protecting individual rights. For organizations, MIA can be leveraged to assess potential
1031   risks and serve as a privacy measurement. Additionally, MIA can be employed for auditing and
1032   regulatory purposes. For instance, designing an auditing model to detect the inclusion of a user’s
1033   personal image in the training set of the target model enables auditing and regulation of the target
1034   system model.
