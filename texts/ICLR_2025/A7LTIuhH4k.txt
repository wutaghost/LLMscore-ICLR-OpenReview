011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Robust optimization provides a principled and unified framework to model many
014            problems in modern operations research and computer science applications, such
015            as risk measures minimization and adversarially robust machine learning. To use
016            a robust solution (e.g., to implement an investment portfolio or perform robust
017            machine learning inference), the user has to a priori decide the trade-off between
018
               efficiency (nominal performance) and robustness (worst-case performance) of the
               solution by choosing the uncertainty level hyperparameters. In many applications,
019
               this amounts to solving the problem many times and comparing them, each from a
020
               different hyperparameter setting. This makes robust optimization practically cum-
021            bersome or even intractable. We present a novel procedure based on the proximal
022            point method (PPM) to approximate many Pareto-efficient robust solutions using
023            the PPM trajectory. Compared with the existing method with computation cost
024            N × TRC , the cost of our method is TRC + (N − 1) × TPPM     ] , where N is the
025            number of robust solutions to be generated, TRC is the cost of solving a single
026            robust optimization problem, and TPPM] is cost of a single step of an approximate
027            PPM. We prove exact PPM can produce exact Pareto efficient robust solutions for
028            a class of robust linear optimization problems. For robust optimization problems
029
               with nonlinear and differentiable objective functions, compared with the existing
               method, our method equipped with first-order approximate PPMs is computation-
030
               ally cheaper and generates robust solutions with comparable performance.

034   1   I NTRODUCTION
035
036   One of the main obstacles in deploying robust optimization models for real-world decision-making
037   under uncertainty is determining an appropriate trade-off between nominal performance and worst-
038   case performance before the uncertainty is realized. For example, in portfolio optimization, setting
039
      the level of risk aversion is challenging. Similarly, in adversarial machine learning, choosing the ad-
      versarial perturbation set parameters to balance average accuracy versus accuracy under adversarial
040
      attacks is a critical decision. Decision makers often need to obtain and test multiple robust solu-
041
      tions, each corresponding to a different solution on the efficiency-robustness Pareto frontier, before
042   deciding which one to deploy.
043
044   To obtain multiple robust solutions, decision makers adjust hyperparameters such as the shape and
045
      size (radius) of the uncertainty set (Soyster, 1973; Ben-Tal & Nemirovski, 1999; Bertsimas & Sim,
      2004) or by employing globalized robust optimization approaches that use penalty functions and
046
      coefficient hyperparameters (Ben-Tal et al., 2006; 2009). Consequently, generating each robust so-
047
      lution demands solving a different instance of the robust optimization problem, which is generally
048   more computationally expensive than solving their deterministic counterparts. For instance, ad-
049   versarial training is more expensive than traditional training because it involves solving an inner
050   maximization problem to find adversarial attacks. In theory, the robust counterpart of linear pro-
051   grams (LPs) with ellipsoidal uncertainty sets becomes second-order cone programs (SOCPs), and
052   the robust counterpart of uncertain SOCPs becomes semidefinite programs (SDPs). As a result, find-
053   ing the right robust solution can be prohibitively costly in practice, let alone finding many of them
      to compare.

      Contributions. To address this challenge, we propose a new way of thinking about this prob-
055   lem, along with a novel proximal point method (PPM)- based algorithm to approximate efficiency-
056   robustness Pareto efficient robust solutions. Compared with the cost of the existing approach of
057   N × TRC , the computational cost of our method is TRC + (N − 1) × TPPM    ] , where N is the number
058   of robust solutions to be generated, TRC is the cost of solving a single robust optimization problem,
059   and TPPM
            ] is the cost of a single step of an approximate PPM. Specifically, the procedure first solves
060   for the “most-robust” solution, then uses it as the starting point to perform approximate PPM up-
061   dates towards the “least-robust” (deterministic counterpart) solution. We discover, intriguingly, that
062   the proximal point trajectory approximates the set of Pareto efficient robust solutions that we want
063
      to obtain in the first place.
064   We prove that for robust LPs with uncertain objective functions under the simplex decision domain
065   and ellipsoidal uncertainty sets, the proximal point trajectory are exactly Pareto efficient robust so-
066   lutions. For robust LPs with a random polyhedron domain, we prove that with high probability, the
067   performances of the Pareto efficient robust solutions are bounded by the performances of two prox-
068   imal point trajectories. To validate the theoretical results for constrained robust LPs, we conduct
069
      numerical experiments on portfolio optimization. To demonstrate the computation efficiency of our
      method for robust optimization problems with nonlinear and differentiable objective functions, we
070
      apply our method to adversarially robust deep learning.
071
072   Related Work. Given the problem we study, our work is closely related to multiple literatures. Here
073   we review each literature and compare each with our work.
074   Proximal Point Method. In the continuous optimization literature, the interest in PPM (Martinet
075   (1970); Rockafellar (1976b)) has been predominantly in its convergence properties (Rockafellar
076   (1976a); Güler (1991); Ferris (1991); Parikh et al. (2014); Beck (2017)) and its role as a theoretical
077   framework for analyzing the convergence of other optimization algorithms (e.g., gradient descent,
078   Extra-gradient method, optimistic gradient method, and Nesterov’s Accelerated gradient method
079   can all be analyzed as approximations of PPM (Ahn & Sra (2022); Mokhtari et al. (2020b)). Our
080   work fundamentally deviates from the literature and focuses on studying the entire trajectory of
081
      (approximate) PPM as (approximate) robust optimization solutions.
082   Implicit gradient regularization. Literature has shown iterates of gradient methods when minimizing
083   a loss function alone, provide implicit regularization. Different from the literature (Ali et al. (2020);
084   Wu et al. (2020); Suggala et al. (2018); Barrett & Dherin (2020); Ji et al. (2020); Sun et al. (2023);
085   Li et al. (2021); Ji & Telgarsky (2019); Wang et al. (2023)) that tends to be descriptive and focused
086   on unconstrained problems, as an intermediate result towards our main Theorem, we give a new,
087
      direct and constructive proof for the implicit regularization of PPM under the constrained setting.
      Our work is also closely related to the literature on the equivalence between regularization/risk
088
      measure and robustness. It has been established that regularized learning is equivalent to robust
089
      optimization (El Ghaoui & Lebret (1997); Xu et al. (2009); Shafieezadeh Abadeh et al. (2015);
090   Mohajerin Esfahani & Kuhn (2018)). Moreover, risk measure minimization is equivalent to robust
091   optimization (Freund (1987); Natarajan et al. (2009b)). Our work establishes a correspondence
092   between the uncertainty set, regularization/risk measure, and PPM distance-generating function.
093
      Robust Optimization. Existing work on calibrating the uncertainty set radius typically relies on
094
      probabilistic guarantees, namely, prior bounds on solution robustness (Bertsimas et al. (2021); Mo-
095
      hajerin Esfahani & Kuhn (2018); Blanchet et al. (2019a)). However, such approaches assume prior
096   knowledge of the uncertainty distribution or observations on the uncertainty, which can be unavail-
097   able in practice. Even with distributional information or data on the uncertainty, the resulting radius
098   typically leads to overly conservative robust decisions. An alternative is to generate multiple ro-
099   bust solutions under varying radii before selecting the solution for deployment. To the best of our
100   knowledge, no methods exist for generating multiple robust solutions other than solving the robust
101   optimization problem multiple times. Our work shows for a class of robust LPs, the exact PPM iter-
102   ates are exact robust solutions; for robust optimization with nonlinear and differentiable objectives,
103   approximate PPM iterates can serve as computationally cheap approximate robust solutions.

      2.3     M AIN I DEA
163
164   If the uncertainty set radius r for Ξ is set too large, then a solution xPE (r) of (RC) tends to be
165   overly conservative and gives bad average case performance, i.e., low E(xPE (r)). If the radius is
166   set too small, then the solution may not perform reliably under large out-of-sample perturbations or
167   adversarial attacks ξ, i.e., resulting in low R(xPE (r)). What is the right r?
168   Existing literature (Mohajerin Esfahani & Kuhn (2018); Blanchet et al. (2019b;a)) points to ways
169   to choose r based on statistical theory, using observed perturbation data ξ to estimate Ξ. But in
170   practice, we may not have any data about Ξ. Even if we do, such designs are known in the robust
171   optimization community to be overly conservative. The only practical option is to solve the problem
172   (RC) many times, each with a different r, and compare the solutions in terms of their average case
173   and worst-case performances.
174   Our main contribution is proposing a completely new way of thinking about this problem. Instead of
175   solving (RC) N times where N is the number of solutions we want to compare, we can approximate
176   these Pareto efficient robust solutions within two algorithmic passes. The first pass is to obtain
177   a robust solution xPE (rmax ) for (RC) with a large value r = rmax . Then we use xPE (rmax ) as
178   the initial point of an iterative algorithm (approximate PPM), to solve for the problem (RC) with
179   r = 0 (i.e., the nominal problem (P)). The intermediate approximate PPM iterates for x provide a
180   reasonable, sometimes perfect, representation of the Pareto efficient robust solutions.

958   as projected gradient descent (PGD)-based training but with a much lower cost) and l∞ norm ball
959   perturbation sets, B∥·∥∞ (r), with r in {2, 4, 6, 8}. The Algorithm 1 is initialized with the parameter

      the full train set (ExtraFullGD). The implementation for extra gradient descent is adopted from Gidel
964

965
      ExtraSGD is a better approximation to PPM than SGD, and ExtraFullGD is a better approximation to
966   PPM than ExtraSGD. SGD and ExtraSGD have a learning rate of 5e − 4; FullGD and ExtraFullGD
967   have a learning rate of 4e − 4. The four variants of the standard training are each performed for
968   100 epochs, generating one approximate Pareto efficient robust network per epoch. The trajectories
969   of four variants of the standard training are each a set of approximate clean accuracy-adversarial
