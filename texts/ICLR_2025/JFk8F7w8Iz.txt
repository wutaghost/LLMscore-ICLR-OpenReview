011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Preference optimization is a standard approach to fine-tuning large language mod-
014            els to align with human preferences. The quantity, diversity, and representative-
015            ness of the preference dataset are critical to the effectiveness of preference opti-
016
               mization. However, obtaining a large amount of preference annotations is difficult
               in many applications. This raises the question of how to use the limited anno-
017
               tation budget to create an effective preference dataset. To this end, we propose
018
               Annotation-Efficient Preference Optimization (AEPO). Instead of exhaustively
019            annotating preference over all available response texts, AEPO selects a subset of
020            responses that maximizes diversity and representativeness from the available re-
021            sponses and then annotates preference over the selected ones. In this way, AEPO
022            focuses the annotation budget on labeling preferences over a smaller but infor-
023            mative subset of responses. We evaluate the performance of Direct Preference
024            Optimization (DPO) using AEPO and show that it outperforms models trained
025            using a standard DPO with the same annotation budget. Our code is available at
026            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/aepo-05B2.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Large Language Models (LLMs) trained on massive datasets are capable of solving a variety of tasks
032   in natural language understanding and generation (Brown et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022; Touvron
033
      et al., 2023; OpenAI et al., 2024). However, they have been shown to generate texts containing
      toxic, untruthful, biased, and harmful outputs (Bai et al., 2022; Lin et al., 2022; Touvron et al.,
034
      2023; Casper et al., 2023; Huang et al., 2024b; Guan et al., 2024). Language model alignment aims
035
      to address these issues by guiding LLMs to generate responses that aligns with human preferences,
036   steering them to generate responses that are informative, harmless, and helpful (Christiano et al.,
037   2017; Ziegler et al., 2020; Stiennon et al., 2020; Bai et al., 2022).
038
039
      The common strategies to align an LLM are Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF)
      and Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Stiennon et al., 2020; Ouyang et al., 2022; Rafailov
040
      et al., 2023). RLHF and DPO use the human preference dataset to train a reward model or a lan-
041
      guage model directly. The performance of these algorithms is highly dependent on the choice of
042   the preference dataset. However, building a human preference dataset requires human annotations,
043   which are expensive to collect. Thus, the main bottleneck in building a preference dataset is the
044   annotation cost.
045
      A large number of works have investigated the synthesis of preference data using a powerful LLM
046
      (e.g., GPT-4) to distill the knowledge of human preferences (Dubois et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2024;
047
      Ding et al., 2023; Honovich et al., 2023; Cui et al., 2023; Mukherjee et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024a;
048   Liu et al., 2024a). However, human preferences are known to be diverse and pluralistic, and they
049   are unlikely to be represented by the opinion of a single model (Qiu et al., 2022; Kirk et al., 2023;
050   Wan et al., 2023; Cao et al., 2023b; Zhou et al., 2024; Sorensen et al., 2024a; Rao et al., 2024; Xu
051   et al., 2024b; Sorensen et al., 2024b; Kirk et al., 2024; Shen et al., 2024b; Chakraborty et al., 2024).
052   Several papers have pointed out that LLMs may exhibit bias toward aligning with people from a
053   particular background (Santurkar et al., 2023; Naous et al., 2024; Adilazuarda et al., 2024). For
      example, Cao et al. (2023b) reports that ChatGPT has a strong alignment with American culture,

080   The question is how to generate an effective preference dataset with a limited annotation budget.
081   Previous work has shown that the following three features are desirable for a preference dataset to
082
      be effective (Liu et al., 2024c;a):
083
            1. Quantity and Diversity of instructions. Greater quantity and diversity are desirable for the
084            instruction set (Askell et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023; Ding et al., 2023; Honovich et al.,
085            2023; Cao et al., 2023a; Yuan et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024a; Zhang et al.,
086            2024a; Ge et al., 2024).
087         2. Diversity of responses. A set of responses with higher diversity is desirable (Cui et al.,
088            2023; Lu et al., 2024; Yuan et al., 2023; Song et al., 2024).
089
            3. Representativeness of responses. Responses that represent the behavior of the training
090
               model are more desirable (Guo et al., 2024; Tajwar et al., 2024; Tang et al., 2024a)
091
092   To achieve all three desiderata with a limited annotation budget, it is desirable to annotate prefer-
093   ence over diverse and representative responses with a minimum amount of annotation required per
094   instruction.
095
      To this end, we propose Annotation-Efficient Preference Optimization (AEPO), a preference
096   optimization with a preprocessing step on the preference dataset to reduce the required amount of
097   annotation (Figure 1). Instead of annotating the preference over all N responses, AEPO selects
098   k(< N ) responses from N responses. We deploy a sophisticated method to select a set of response
099   texts with high diversity and representativeness. It then annotates the preference for the selected k
100   responses. In this way, AEPO uses all N samples to select a subset of responses with high diversity
101   and representativeness, while requiring only an annotation over a subset of responses.
102   The strength of AEPO is threefold (Table 1). First, it is applicable to human feedback data. Com-
103   pared to Reinforcement Learning from AI Feedback (RLAIF) (Lee et al., 2024), our approach can
104   be applied to both human and AI feedback. RLAIF is a scalable approach in terms of both instruc-
105   tions and annotations, but it is known that the feedback from existing language models is biased in
106   various ways (Cao et al., 2023b; Zheng et al., 2023; Jain et al., 2024; Gudibande et al., 2024; Dubois
107   et al., 2024). Second, it is scalable with additional computational resources. By generating a larger
      amount of responses, AEPO can find more diverse and representative responses to annotate, result-

128
      shows that AEPO is a promising algorithm for efficient preference optimization, especially when
      annotation cost is the bottleneck of the alignment process.

136
      is Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2023). DPO trains the language model
      to directly align with the human preference data over the responses without using reward models.

143
144   Preference Dataset. The performance of preference optimization largely depends on the choice of
145   the preference dataset Dp . Existing approaches explore the use of high-performance models (e.g.,
146   GPT-4) to synthesize high-quality instructions, responses, and preference feedback (Ding et al.,
147   2023; Honovich et al., 2023; Cui et al., 2023; Mukherjee et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2024a; Liu et al.,
148   2024a).
149   Several papers have investigated annotation-efficient learning by reducing the number of instructions
150   rather than synthesizing more (Cohn et al., 1994; Settles, 2009). Su et al. (2023) suggested selecting
151   examples to annotate from a pool of unlabeled data to improve the efficiency of in-context learning.
152   Zhou et al. (2023) shows that fine-tuning a model with carefully selected and authored instructions
153   can improve performance. Chen et al. (2024) points out that public instruction datasets contain many
154   low-quality instances and proposes a method to filter out low-quality data, resulting in more efficient
155   fine-tuning.

179   3   A NNOTATION -E FFICIENT P REFERENCE O PTIMIZATION (AEPO)
180
181   We propose Annotation-Efficient Preference Optimization (AEPO), a method for efficiently
182   learning preferences from a large number of responses with a limited budget on preference an-
183   notations (Figure 1).
184   The procedure of AEPO is described in Algorithm 1. We assume that a set of N responses is
185   available for each instruction: D = {(x, {yi }Ni=1 )}. Instead of annotating the preference over all
186   responses in {yi }N
                        i=1 , AEPO subsamples k responses (e.g., k = 2) from the candidate set of sam-
187   ples according to the objective function (Eq. 18) that heuristically maximizes the information gain
188   (line 3). We explain the objective function later. Then, it deploys the WoN strategy (Eq. 2) on the
189   subsampled subset of responses Y ∗ instead of all N responses {yi }N   i=1 . It annotates the prefer-
190   ence over Y ∗ to select the best and the worst responses as the chosen and the rejected responses,
191   respectively (lines 4, 5). In this way, we can allocate the annotation budget only to labeling in-
192   formative responses. AEPO achieves to build a preference dataset with diverse and representative
193
      responses using a small amount of annotation effort, which is exactly the characteristics desired for
      the preference annotation methodology we discussed in Section 1.
194
195   The performance of the procedure is highly dependent on how we subsample a subset Y from the
196   candidate set of responses Ycand := {yi }N  i=1 . We propose to maximize the information gain (IG)
197   (Cover, 1999) as the criteria to select the subset Y . Let Ry be a random variable for the estimated
198   probability distribution of y’s reward value (R(x, y)) and RY be a set of random variables Ry for
199
      y ∈ Y . The information gain IG(RYcand ; RY ) measures the reduction in the entropy of the predicted
      values of RYcand when we observe the values of RY :
200
201                             IG(RYcand ; RY ) = H[RYcand ] − H[RYcand | RY ],                         (3)

202   where H is the joint entropy. Our goal is to find an informative subset Y where IG(R          ; R ) is
203   maximized.
204   Information gain is one of the primary objectives used in active learning, where the goal is to se-
205   lectively label the most informative unlabeled examples (Lewis & Gale, 1994; Engelson & Dagan,

      4   E XPERIMENTS
325
326
      Setup. We evaluate the performance of AEPO on DPO using the AlpacaFarm (Dubois et al., 2023)
327
      and Anthropic’s hh-rlhf (Bai et al., 2022) datasets. We use mistral-7b-sft-beta (Mistral) (Jiang et al.,
328   2023a; Tunstall et al., 2024) as the language model. See D.2 for the results using dolly-v2-3b
329   (Conover et al., 2023) as the language model.
330
331
      We generate up to N = 128 responses per instruction with nucleus sampling (p = 0.9) (Holtzman
      et al., 2020) to be used for the subsampling strategies. The temperature of the sampling algorithm
332
      is set to 1.0 for all experiments. All the methods use the same set of responses to ensure a fair
333
      comparison. For AEPO, the number of subsampled responses is set to k = 2 and the diversity
334   hyperparameter is set to λ ∈ {0.0, 0.5, 1.0, 2.0} for AlpacaFarm and λ ∈ {0.5, 1.0, 2.0} for the
335   rest of the datasets. We evaluate random sampling and WoN strategy as baselines. We additionally
336   evaluate a coreset-based subsampling strategy (Sener & Savarese, 2018) and a perplexity-based
337   subsampling strategy for AlpacaFarm. See Appendix B for the details of the algorithms. Since WoN
338   strategy uses N/2 times more annotations per instruction than AEPO with k = 2, we reduce the
339   number of instructions for WoN to 2/N so that the number of required annotations is the same as
340   for AEPO. Note that we assume that the cost of annotating the preference rank for N responses
341   is linear in N . This assumption favors WoN because it becomes increasingly difficult to annotate
342
      preference rank over a larger set of options (Ganzfried, 2017).
343   We use the OASST reward model (Köpf et al., 2023) to annotate the preference over the responses
344   for the training data. Although it is ideal to use human annotations to evaluate the performance of
345   the algorithms, human annotations are expensive and difficult to reproduce. To this end, we use
346   existing open source reward models as preference annotators for the experiment.
347   We train the same model that generates the responses (Mistral) using DPO with Low-Rank Adapta-
348   tion (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2022; Sidahmed et al., 2024). We set the LoRA’s r = 64 and α = r/4.
349   Other hyperparameters for the training process are described in Appendix A. For the Alpaca-
350   Farm dataset, we use the alpaca human preference subset as the training set and use the
351   alpaca farm evaluation subset as the evaluation set. For the Anthropic’s hh-rlhf datasets,
352   we use the first 5000 entries of the training set of both the helpful-base and harmless-base
353   subsets as the training set. Then we evaluate the trained model on the first 1000 entries of the test set
354
      of the helpful-base (Helpfulness) and harmless-base (Harmlessness) subsets. For WoN,
      we reduce the number of instructions evenly for the two subsets so that the dataset always has the
355
      same number of instructions from the two subsets.

512   Minimum Bayes risk decoding. Eq. 7 and 18 are largely inspired by Minimum Bayes Risk
513   (MBR) decoding (Kumar & Byrne, 2002; 2004; Eikema & Aziz, 2022). MBR decoding is a text
514   generation algorithm that selects the sequence with the highest similarity to the sequences gener-
515   ated by the probability model. As such, the objective function of MBR decoding corresponds to
516   Eq. 7. MBR decoding has been proven to produce high-quality text in many text generation tasks,
517   including machine translation, text summarization, and image captioning (Freitag et al., 2023; Suz-
518
      gun et al., 2023; Bertsch et al., 2023; Li et al., 2024a; Yang et al., 2024). In particular, Eq. 18 is
      strongly inspired by the objective function of Diverse MBR (DMBR) decoding (Jinnai et al., 2024).
519
      The novelty of our work is to introduce the objective function of DMBR as a strategy to subsample
520
      representative and diverse responses from many candidate responses so that the annotation budget
521   can be used efficiently.

527   6   C ONCLUSIONS
528
529
530   We propose Annotation-Efficient Preference Optimization (AEPO), an annotation-efficient dataset
531
      subsampling strategy for language model alignment. The subsampling strategy aims to maximize
      the information gain using two heuristics on how the preference information is propagated between
532
      samples. By focusing the annotation effort on the selected responses, AEPO achieves efficient pref-
533
      erence optimization with a limited annotation budget. We evaluate the subsampling strategy and
534   show that it successfully selects diverse and representative samples from the candidates (Figure 7).
535   Experimental results show that AEPO outperforms the baselines on AlpacaFarm, Anthropic’s hh-
536   rlhf, and JCM datasets (Figures 5, 6, and 8). Our ablation study covers various settings, including
537   GPT-4 evaluation, off-policy training, out-of-domain evaluation, and using different hyperparame-
538   ters (Appendix D). The study shows that AEPO consistently outperforms the baselines in various
539   settings. We believe that AEPO is a critical contribution to promoting preference optimization re-
      search by addressing the severe obstacle, the cost of creating better preference data.

1332   of Sener & Savarese (2018) (Algorithm 1, k-Center-Greedy). The objective function for selecting
1333   the subset Y is the following:

1337   Intuitively, Eq. 20 is similar to the representative objective (frep ; Eq. 7) but instead of minimizing
1338   the average distance of Y and Ycand , it aims to minimize the maximum distance of y ∈ Ycand and
1339   y ′ ∈ Y . Although the algorithm was originally proposed for training convolutional neural networks,
1340   its procedure applies to the response text subsampling problem. We use the cosine distance of the
1341   sentence embedding as the distance between the data points. We use the same text embedding model

       C     A DDITIONAL R ELATED W ORK
1351
1352
1353
1354
       Active learning. Annotation-efficient learning has long been a challenge in natural language pro-
1355
       cessing (Zhang et al., 2022). Active learning is an approach that aims to achieve training with fewer
1356   training labels by proactively selecting the data to be annotated and used for learning (Cohn et al.,
1357   1994; Settles, 2009; Houlsby et al., 2011). There are roughly two active learning strategies used
1358   in NLP (Zhang et al., 2022). One uses the informativeness of the data instances, such as uncer-
1359   tainty and disagreement of the models (Lewis & Gale, 1994; Engelson & Dagan, 1996; Siddhant &
1360   Lipton, 2018; Huang et al., 2024a). This approach has proven to be efficient in many text classifi-
1361   cation tasks. The other strategy is based on the representativeness of the data instances (McCallum
1362   & Nigam, 1998; Settles & Craven, 2008; Zhao et al., 2020; Chen & Wang, 2024). The strategy
1363   annotates instances with high average similarity to all the other instances so that it can cover a large
1364
       portion of the dataset with few annotations. Another approach is to select instances that maximize
       the diversity of labeled instances (Eck et al., 2005; Zeng et al., 2019; Bloodgood & Callison-Burch,
1365
       2010). Our approach is related to these approaches as our objective is a combination of represen-
1366
       tative and diversity measures designed to maximize the information gain. The novelty of our study
1367   lies in applying these ideas to the language model alignment problem to reduce the annotation cost.

       I   L IMITATIONS
2323
2324
       Although our method is motivated by the situation where the annotation is needed to align the lan-
2325   guage model, the majority of our experiments (AlpacaFarm and Anthropic’s hh-rlhf) are conducted
2326   using a proxy reward model to annotate preference on training datasets instead of using human an-
2327   notation. We use human annotation for the JCM dataset but use an LLM to automatically evaluate
2328   the agreement of the response text with the human annotation. Manual human annotation would be
2329   desirable for future work.
2330
       Our focus is on developing a method to generate a diverse and representative set of responses. The
2331   preparation of diverse and representative instructions is also an important task to generate an efficient
2332   dataset (Sanh et al., 2022; Ding et al., 2023; Cui et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024a; Xu et al., 2024a).
2333   Our method is orthogonal to methods for generating high quality instructions and can be combined.
2334   Comparing and combining AEPO with methods for generating diverse instructions is future work.
2335
       All experiments are performed using LoRA (Hu et al., 2022). The evaluation of AEPO with full
2336
       parameter fine-tuning is future work. Our experiments are limited to the evaluation on DPO. Eval-
2337   uating AEPO on variants of DPO (Amini et al., 2024; Gheshlaghi Azar et al., 2024; Tang et al.,
2338   2024b; Morimura et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024b) and other preference optimization algorithms
2339   (Ouyang et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2023; Ahmadian et al., 2024) is future work.
2340
       The performance of AEPO depends on the choice of the hyperparameter λ. We observe that λ = 1.0
2341
       is a good choice throughout the experiments, but developing a strategy to find an effective λ for a
2342
       given dataset is future work.
2343

2345   J   C OMPUTATIONAL R ESOURCES
2346
2347   Text generation and DPO training run on an instance with an NVIDIA A100 GPU with 80 GB
2348   VRAM, 16 CPU cores, and 48 GB memory. A single run of DPO takes approximately 50-55 minutes
2349
       on the A100 instance. AEPO runs on an NVIDIA A2 GPU with 8 GB VRAM, 8 CPU cores, and
       24 GB memory. AEPO takes about 49 hours on the A2 instance to run with N = 128 and k = 2 to
2350
       process all the training data in AlpacaFarm, hh-rlhf, and JCM.
2351
2352   All the experiments are run using Huggingface’s Transformers library (Wolf et al., 2020) and Trans-
2353   former Reinforcement Learning library (von Werra et al., 2020).

2356

2358   GPT-4. Our code will be available on acceptance as an open source.

       L    I MPACT S TATEMENT
2361
2362
       We believe that this work will have a positive impact by encouraging work on AI systems that work
2363
       better with a diverse set of people. LLMs would be more useful if they could adapt to the preferences
2364   of diverse groups of people, even if little preference annotation is available from their communities.
2365
2366   We foresee our method being useful for personalizing LLMs (Greene et al., 2023; Jang et al., 2023;
2367
       Kirk et al., 2023). Personalized LLMs could have far-reaching benefits, but also a number of worri-
       some risks, such as the propagation of polarized views. We refer to Kirk et al. (2023) for a discussion
2368
       of potential risks and countermeasures for personalized LLMs.
