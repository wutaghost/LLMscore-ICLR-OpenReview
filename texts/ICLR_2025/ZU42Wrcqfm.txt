011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            The significant communication overhead and client data heterogeneity have
014            posed important challenges to current federated learning (FL) paradigm. Most
015            compression-based and optimization-based FL algorithms typically focus on ad-
016
               dressing either the model compression challenge or the data heterogeneity issue
               individually, rather than tackling both of them. In this paper, we observe that
017
               by symbolizing the client model updates to be uploaded (i.e., normalizing the
018
               magnitude for each model parameter at local clients), the model heterogeneity can
019            be mitigated that is essentially stemmed from data heterogeneity, thereby helping
020            improve the overall generalization performance of the globally aggregated model
021            at the server. Inspired with this observation, and further motivated by the success of
022            Lion optimizer in achieving the optimal performance on most tasks in centralized
023            learning, we propose a new FL algorithm, called FedSMU, which simultaneously
024            reduces the communication overhead and alleviates the data heterogeneity issue.
025            Specifically, FedSMU splits the standard Lion optimizer into the local updates
026            and global execution, where only the symbol of client model updates commutes
027
               between the client and server. We theoretically prove the convergence of FedSMU
               for the general non-convex settings. Through extensive experimental evaluations
028
               on several benchmark datasets, we demonstrate that our FedSMU algorithm not
029
               only reduces the communication overhead, but also achieves a better generalization
030            performance than the other compression-based and optimization-based baselines.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
      Federated learning (FL) is a large-scale machine learning paradigm wherein a multitude of clients,
035
      under the orchestration of a central server, collaboratively learn a model without the need of sharing
036
      or exchanging any raw client data (McMahan et al., 2017). This paradigm is commonly adopted
037   in data-constrained or data-sensitive environments, such as Internet of things (IoT), healthcare, and
038   finance (Khan et al., 2021; Rieke et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2019). In essence, FL is distinguished
039   from the traditional distributed learning in the following three major challenges.
040
041          • High communication cost. During each communication round of training, the clients are
042
               required to transmit their local model parameters (or updates) to the central server for the
               global aggregation. When the number of model parameters becomes significantly large, this
043
               transmission process may result in a huge bandwidth consumption.
044
045          • Data heterogeneity. Due to the inherently private and personalized nature of federated
046
               clients, the datasets across these clients tend to exhibit distinct statistical distributions. Such
               a data heterogeneity may introduce significant biases into the globally learned model, which,
047
               in turn, can diminish the global model’s generalization capability.
048
049          • Partial client participation. In practical scenarios, clients may join or leave the FL system
050
               at random time intervals. This highly dynamic behavior results in only a small subset of
               clients being active for training during each communication round.
051
052   To address these challenges, extensive exploration has been conducted in the FL community, but
053   from different perspectives. On one hand, compression-based federated algorithms aim to reduce the
      amount of data required for model parameter (or update) transmission, including both the unbiased


                                                          1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      compression and biased compression. For instance, unbiased compression, such as SignSGD
055   (Bernstein et al., 2018a;b), QSGD (Alistarh et al., 2017) and FedPAQ (Reisizadeh et al., 2020),
056   quantize the gradient values into lower-precision integers, thereby reducing the number of transmitted
057   bits. While the common biased compression approach is to sparsify the gradient vector by setting
058   some of its elements to zero or very small values, with the aim of reducing the data transmission cost
059   (Wangni et al., 2018; Aji & Heafield, 2017; Lin et al., 2017). However, the direct use of compression
060   methods will lose a certain amount of information, resulting in the problem of decrease in the
061   accuracy (Yu et al., 2022) and slower convergence or even divergence (Beznosikov et al., 2023).
062   Some methods, such as Error Feedback (Richtárik et al., 2021), have been designed to mitigate these
063
      issues by incorporating error feedback into the optimization process.
064   Several optimization-based federated algorithms, on the other hand, have been proposed to address
065   the data heterogeneity issue. For example, SCAFFOLD (Karimireddy et al., 2020) aims to mitigate
066   the client variance by designing and iteratively updating the control variates. Though theoretically
067   effective, it incurs doubling the communication overhead. FedGen (Venkateswaran et al., 2023)
068   regulates the local training by transmitting additional generators. Most of these optimization-based FL
069
      algorithms, which mainly aim at mitigating data heterogeneity, may incur additional communication
      overhead of information exchange for performance improvement. Additionally, it remains unknown
070
      whether these optimization-based algorithms are compatible with the current compression techniques.
071
072   In this paper, we aim to design an algorithm capable of simultaneously addressing the communication
073   bottleneck and data heterogeneity, without being constrained by the partial client participation issue.
074   To achieve this goal, we first revisit the typical FedAvg algorithm and identify that heterogeneous
075
      magnitudes of model updates may result in certain clients’ updates being overlooked, thus leading
      to an unstable and sub-optimal aggregation of the global model. Building upon this observation,
076
      we then introduce the concept of “Magnitude Uniformity” index, which quantifies the clients’
077
      contribution to the global model’s update. We empirically validate that this magnitude uniformity
078   index is influenced by the degree of data heterogeneity in federated learning, indicating that a more
079   heterogeneous data distribution leads to a greater heterogeneity in the magnitudes of client model
080   updates. Furthermore, heterogeneous client updates may contribute to a decline in the global model’s
081   generalization performance. To address this issue and further reduce the communication overhead,
082   we are motivated to symbolize the model updates as an immediate solution, and propose the FedSMU
083   algorithm. Our contributions can be summarized as follows.
084
085          • We develop a compression-based FL algorithm called FedSMU. It uses the sign operation to
086
               achieve a 1-bit compression and thus greatly saves the communication cost. Simultaneously,
               we leverage the design of Lion optimizer(Chen et al., 2024) to enhance the generalization
087
               performance while maintaining the benefits of compression.
088
089          • We conduct a convergence analysis of FedSMU under the general non-convex settings, and
090
               find its convergence rate as O( √1T ), where T is the total number of communication rounds.
091            This theoretical result matches with the convergence rates of existing FL algorithms.
092          • We conduct a series of experiments to demonstrate the superiority of FedSMU. By comparing
093            FedSMU with the other compression-based and optimization-based federated algorithms,
094            we show that our FedSMU algorithm achieves a higher generalization performance while
095            greatly saving the communication overhead at most cases.

097   2   R ELATED W ORKS
098
099
      Compression-Efficient FL. Extensive studies have been dedicated to reducing the amount of data
      required for gradient transmission and thus improving the communication efficiency. Using the
100
      method called unbiased compression, QSGD (Alistarh et al., 2017), FedPAQ (Reisizadeh et al., 2020),
101
      ZipML (Zhang et al., 2017) and ECQ-SGD (Wu et al., 2018) compress the gradients uploaded to the
102   server while keeping the original data integrity and expectation unchanged to save the communication
103   cost. By leveraging the sign operation, signSGD (Bernstein et al., 2018a;b) and TernGrad (Wen
104   et al., 2017) can compress the gradients up to 1 bit. While the sparsification-based method like
105   TopK (Stich et al., 2018; Alistarh et al., 2018), which only keeps the largest K gradients, is another
106   communication-efficiently biased compression method. Some other methods, like FedEF (Li & Li,
107   2023), FedZip (Malekijoo et al., 2021) and Qsparse-local-SGD (Basu et al., 2019), incorporate both
      the quantization and sparification. While a direct application of biased compression on gradient into


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      federated learning, such as Top-k, can lead to a performance degradation and lower convergence speed
109   due to bias accumulation (Beznosikov et al., 2023). To address this, some studies have introduced
110   optimization techniques to mitigate the negative effects of bias. For example, EF21 (Richtárik et al.,
111   2021) employs the error feedback, while MARINA (Gorbunov et al., 2021) and DIANA (Mishchenko
112   et al., 2024) leverage the compression of gradient differences, both of which further enhance the
113   model performance and convergence speed. In this work, we adopt the sign operation to achieve the
114   communication efficiency, which also helps enhance the generalization capability of the globally
115   aggregated model as shown by Chen et al. (2024; 2021); Foret et al. (2020).
116   Generalization-Enhanced FL. In the advancement of FL algorithms, various techniques have
117   emerged to improve the generalization performance. By using momentum in FL, one can track the
118   historical information of gradients, suppress the noise and reduce the instability of model updates.
119   Benefiting from this, methods such as MV-sto-signSGD-SIM (Sun et al., 2023) and FedAdam (Reddi
120   et al., 2020) apply momentum instead of directly updating with gradients, while PR-SGD-Momentum
121   (Yu et al., 2019) first updates the momentum and then combines the new gradient with a weight of
122   the momentum. These methods enhance model generalization and accelerate convergence in FL. In
123
      this work, we employ two sliding average functions to update momentum after calculating the new
      gradient, a technique demonstrated by Lion (Chen et al., 2024) to effectively store more historical
124
      gradient data. Also, since weight decay regularization has been shown to outperform ℓ2 regularization
125
      in preventing overfitting and enhancing generalization (Loshchilov, 2017), we leverage a weight decay
126   strategy to mitigate the impact of data heterogeneity and further improve generalization performance.
127   Distributed Lion (Liu et al., 2024) is a distributed learning algorithm that leverages the Lion optimizer
128   (Chen et al., 2024) focusing on reducing communication overhead. It extends the Lion optimizer to
129   the distributed setting with full client participation and IID data. However, it lacks exploration of
130   partial participation and non-IID data scenarios, which are the major challenges in FL.

160   for the transmission of control variables. This observation then imposes a critical question for the
161   field of compression-efficient FL: can we design an approach that effectively mitigates both the

175   Before answering this question, we revisit the standard FL framework, i.e., FedAvg (McMahan et al.,
176   2017). With FedAvg, clients perform local training using their own datasets that are distributed over
177   clients and non-iid in nature. The server then aggregates these locally trained models to update the
178   global model, which subsequently serves as the initial model for the next round of training. However,
179   due to data heterogeneity, clients’ model updates often differ in both direction and magnitude.
180   Consequently, when model updates from different clients with large deviations are averaged, some
181
      updates with relatively small magnitudes may be overlooked. For instance, we consider three clients,
      i1 , i2 and i3 , whose model updates along one dimension are +10, −1, and −1, respectively. In this
182
      case, the updates have opposite directions, while the magnitude of client i1 ’s update is much larger
183
      than those of clients i2 and i3 . After averaging at the server (i.e., the global model’s update becoming
184   +8/3), the contribution of clients i2 and i3 to the global model’s update will be ignored, since the
185   update direction is now only dominantly determined by client i1 . Therefore, a direct averaging may
186   neglect contributions from the smaller updates and potentially compromise the fairness among clients.
187

189
      update. Through empirical analysis, we explore the relationship between this Magnitude Uniformity
190   index and the local data heterogeneity, which in turn impacts the generalization performance of the
191   globally aggregated model.

      from all the clients. Specifically, after this sign operation, the local model updates for the three
217   clients i1 , i2 and i3 in the previous example would become +1, −1, and −1, respectively. This
218   process guarantees that each client’s model update contributes equally to the globally aggregated
219   model, thereby reducing the impact of model heterogeneity and promoting fairness. By converting
220   the magnitudes of model updates into their respective signs, we actually emphasize the directions of
221   their updates rather than their magnitudes, which could help balance the contributions from different
222   clients’ model updates and lead to a more representative and informative global model. Moreover, by
223   symbolizing the updates we can reduce the communication cost to 1 bit per dimension, offering a
224   potential solution to enhancing generalization while also saving the communication.
225   In fact, numerous sign-based compression methods (Bernstein et al., 2018a;b; Wen et al., 2017)
226   have been applied in federated learning. While theoretically performant, their empirical results
227   often show only marginal improvements or comparable performance to FedAvg. Thus, effectively
228   leveraging the sign operation to simultaneously mitigate the communication overhead and enhance
229   generalization in federated learning remains a challenging and unresolved issue. On the other hand,
230   many optimization techniques have been proposed to improve generalization for the centralized
231
      learning, such as momentum, Adam, and weight decay. A brute force approach could be directly
      incorporating the sign operations with these optimization strategies in FL, formulating the algorithm
232
      design as a program search to identify federated optimization algorithms that can incorporate sign
233
      compression. However, this approach is computationally expensive.
234
235   Fortunately, in the context of centralized learning, Lion (EvoLved Sign Momentum) optimizer (Chen
236   et al., 2024) employs the sign operation to compute the updates while tracking momentum. This
237
      approach has demonstrated an overall outstanding performance across a variety of models and tasks.
      Compared to the simple SignSGD (Bernstein et al., 2018a;b), Lion leverages the dual momentum
238
      tracking and weight decay, significantly improving the generalization ability of the trained models.
239
      Inspired with our observation on the impact of Magnitude Uniformity index on the FL algorithm’s
240   generalization performance, and further motivated by the success of Lion in centralized learning, we
241   thus propose a new federated optimization algorithm aiming at both reducing the communication
242   overhead and enhancing generalization performance, through symbolizing the client model updates.

244   3.3 P ROPOSED F ED SMU
245
      To leverage the structured design of Lion optimizer and minimize the communication overhead, we
246
      propose our FedSMU algorithm for federated learning, which splits the Lion optimizer’s framework
247   of momentum tracking and weight decay to be carried out independently at the server and each client,
248   respectively, as summarized in Algorithm 1.
249
250
      Specifically, at each communication round t, FedSMU implements the following steps:
251                                                                             i
            1. Participating clients initialize their local models, denoted as yt,1 , based on the current global
252            model xt .
253         2. Each client conducts K steps of local stochastic gradient descent (SGD) to compute the
254            model update gti .
255
            3. Each client symbolically represents its model updates using momentum and sign operations.
256
257         4. The server receives and aggregates these symbolic updates, denoted as uit , to update the
258
               global model xt+1 by incorporating the weight decay.
259   Such a design offers two significant advantages for our FedSMU algorithm. First, it fully leverages
260   the structure of the Lion optimizer, thereby enhancing the generalization performance of the global
261   model. It is also worth noting that in scenarios where the number of local update steps is set to
262   K = 1, our optimizer essentially reverts to the standard Lion. Second, by transmitting only 1-bit
263   update for each dimension of the model parameters between the clients and server, we substantially
264   reduce the communication overhead in the FL systems.
265   We also notice that inspired by the advantages of the Lion optimizer in centralized learning, there has
266   been other works (e.g., FedLion (Tang & Chang, 2024)) incorporating Lion into the local updates of
267   federated learning. However, FedLion simply uses the vanilla Lion algorithm for the local updates
268   instead of SGD, resulting in a communication cost that is even significantly higher than those of
269   FedAvg, as the extra momentum terms need to be transmitted. Compared with FedLion, our FedSMU
      out-stands in the following two advantages. 1) Effective utilization of the Lion framework. Our

350   5.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
351
352   Models and Dataset. We evaluate FedSMU and the other baseline algorithms on three real-world
353   visual and language datasets: CIFAR-10, CIFAR-100 (Krizhevsky et al., 2009) and neural machine
354   translation on Shakespeare, with the same train/test splits as in (Acar et al., 2021). Each client is
355   assigned an uncertain number of classes, and the data within each class varies widely, with the labels
356   of client samples generated according to the Dirichlet distribution. For instance, using Dirichlet-0.25
357
      on CIFAR-10, there are approximately 80% of each client’s samples belonging to around three or
      four different classes. We employ CNN and RNN models similar to previous studies (McMahan
358
      et al., 2017). Furthermore, to demonstrate the applicability of our approach to other models, we also
359
      evaluate the performance of our algorithms using a larger network, ResNet18. For additional details
360   on the experimental setup, please refer to Appendix A.

      other baselines, including the optimization-based FL algorithms such as FedAvg (McMahan et al.,

      compression-based FL algorithms such as FedEF-SGD-hv-sign (Li & Li, 2023), FedEF-SGD-topk

368   compressed model updates. Consequently, the communication overhead of FedLion is higher than
369   FedAvg, even when the model updates are compressed. Additionally, SCAFFOLD needs twice of the
370   communication cost compared to FedAvg. Though SCALLION uploads the compressed incremental

511   5.2.5         A BLATION S TUDIES
512
      Indeed, while our convergence analysis and experimental results demonstrate that FedSMU’s performance is less
513
      affected by the client participation rate, the momentum of clients may still be extremely stale due to the partial
514   participation in FL. In light of this, we design the two other variants, named FedSMUMC and FedSMUM, to
515   examine the impact of this momentum staleness on the generalization performance. For FedSMUMC, clients
516   upload 1-bit model updates along with extra momentum in the full precision. The server then aggregates that
517   momentum to update the global momentum and broadcasts it at the next round as the initial momentum for the
      participating clients. For FedSMUM, every client, including the inactive clients, receives the updated global
518
      model. However, only the participating clients execute local training and upload model updates, while the other
519   clients utilize the received global model to compute the momentum for the current round. See Appendix H for
520   the detail of these two algorithms. Results in Table 6 indicate that by appropriately completing the momentum,
521   we can marginally enhance the model performance, but it necessitates additional transmission of momentum
522   with the full precision. Consequently in this sense, the local momentum staleness has a minimum impact on the
      global model’s performance.
523
524   5.2.6         L IMITATION
525
      Though our FedSMU effectively enhances the generalization performance while reducing the communication
526   overhead, it may still have some limitations. First, our compression relies on the fixed symbol quantization, which
527   might not be optimal for the adaptive scenarios. Exploring adaptive bit quantization further in our future research
528   is promising to address this limitation. Second, our FedSMU necessitates additional caching of two sets of
529   momentum, potentially resulting in a slightly increment of storage overhead. Last, from a theoretical perspective,
530
      though the generalization properties (Venkateswaran et al., 2023) of FedAvg under various assumptions have
      been extensively examined, such guarantees for the compression-based FL approaches remain an open problem.
531

533   6      C ONCLUSION
534
535   In this paper, we have proposed the FedSMU algorithm that could effectively alleviate both the communication
536
      cost and data heterogeneity issues of federated learning. The key design was the symbolization of local client
      updates which were introduced to balance the contribution of each client and avoid the dominance by some
537   relatively large update values. We carried out theoretical convergence analysis, and empirically showed that
538   FedSMU converged faster to a higher top accuracy under the same communication cost. Under the condition of
539   a very small partial client participation rate and relatively high data heterogeneity, FedSMU still demonstrated a
      better performance.

      Our FedSMU algorithm requires each client to maintain and track its momentum state. In some extreme cases,
757   where each client participates in the training only once during the entire training process, our algorithm may fail.
758
759
      We do analyze this limitation from two perspectives. First, we test our algorithm at a lower client participation
760   rate (i.e., where the momentum state of the clients is more stale). The results in Table 4 show that FedSMU
761   still achieves a good accuracy in most cases. Additionally, we introduce a variant algorithm, FedSMUMC,
762   which additionally store and communicate the complete momentum at the server at the cost of communication
763   overhead. The results in Table 6 demonstrate that appropriately completing the momentum can marginally
      improve the model performance. Overall, the experimental results indicate that, in most cases, our FedSMU
764
      algorithm remains effective and does not fail due to the stale momentum.
765
766   Additionally, FedSMU only requires uploading the 1-bit model update after symbolization for each client and
      does not need to store the momentum terms on the server side. In contrast, FedLion (Tang & Chang, 2024)
767
      requires to aggregate and store momentum on the server side. Consequently, FedLion (Tang & Chang, 2024)
768   necessitates the additional transmission of the full-precision momentum terms, along with the model update
769   after quantization.
770
771
772   B.2    D ISCUSSION ON SIGN OPERATION
773
774   The “sign operation” refers to 1 bit per dimension, not a total of 1 bit for the entire model. In practice, the
775   weights or gradients of each dimension in a model are typically stored as 32-bit floating-point values (float32).
776   This means that each dimension requires 32 bits to upload. However, when applying the sign operation, each
      dimension only requires 1 bit to represent the sign, thus significantly reducing the communication overhead
777
      compared to using 32 bits per dimension.
778
779   In terms of the physical data transfer, the actual number of bits transmitted to the server can be calculated as:
      Total bits transferred = (bits per dimension × the number of dimensions × the number of participating clients)
780
      + the header size. For example, using a small CNN model with n = 10 clients, FedSMU would transfer
781   approximately 0.95MB.
782
      Though the service information for communication includes plenty of headers, for instance: the Ethernet header
783
      has 14 bytes for the header; IPv4 has 20 bytes header; TCP has header 20 header. Given the large volume of
784   transmitted data, the header size of 54 bytes is negligible in comparison.
785
786
787   B.3    D ISCUSSION O N T HE α- BIT
788
789   First, we would like to acknowledge that for the general quantization-based compression algorithms, a higher
790   precision quantization may often lead to a faster convergence. However, this may not hold for our FedSMU.
791   This conclusion is based on the Lion optimizer (Chen et al., 2024) and our analysis, as follows.
792   1) The original Lion manuscript demonstrated that 1-bit quantization (via the sign operation) in centralized
793   learning enhances the algorithm’s convergence and generalization compared to other optimization techniques.
794   That is, the authors analyzed that the sign operation introduces noise into the updates, which serves as a form of
795
      regularization, thereby improving both convergence and generalization.
796   2) Both experimentally (Figure 1) and intuitively, the symbolic operation (i.e., 1-bit quantization) helps alleviate
797   the heterogeneity of model updates, as all updates have uniform magnitude across all dimensions for each client.
798
      Furthermore, reducing model heterogeneity should also intuitively contribute to improving model performance
      in heterogeneous federated settings.
799
800   3) However, such sign operation (e.g., QSGD) alone does not directly improve generalization in experiments.
801
      Inspired by Lion optimizer (Chen et al., 2024) that incorporates the sign operation and then enhances the
      convergence and generalization to learn in central learning, we introduce Lion’s structure into federated learning
802   and verify that this combination can indeed improve model generalization.
803
804
      Consequently, we conclude that in our optimized structure, 1-bit quantization outperforms higher-bit or even
      full-bit quantization, since multi-bit compression does not guarantee that the update amplitude of each client is
805   consistent. Experimental results in Table 8 further validate that for our designed optimization algorithm, using a
806   higher-bit compression does not enhance the algorithm’s convergence or generalization.

849

      (Sun et al., 2023; Reddi et al., 2020). Specifically, in (Sun et al., 2023), the authors demonstrate the convergence
850   of distributed SIGNSGD with momentum under Assumption 4, which is also the bounded gradient assumption.

       E.1    D ISCUSSION O N T HE L1 F ORM
1141
1142   The convergence rate in terms of L1 norm with a dependency of d is common in sign-based method, like
1143   Theorem 4 in (Sun et al., 2023), p.6, Theorem 1 in (Chen et al., 2020), P.6, and Equation 35 in (Jin et al., 2020b),
1144   P.19. Their form of expression is similar to Theory 4.4. However, it is worth noting that the value of the learning
1145   rate γ1 may be critical. In (Chen et al., 2020) and (Jin et al., 2020b), the authors use γ1 = √1T d to reduce the
                                                                                            √
1146   power of d. In that way, if we take γ1 = L√1T d , Theorem 4.4 can only depend on d.

1149   E.2    C OMPARISON WITH F ED L ION (TANG & C HANG , 2024)
1150
       In this section, we highlight some key differences between our algorithm and FedLion. Specifically, while
1151   FedLion (Tang & Chang, 2024) does not rely on the Assumption 4.3, it also introduces an additional stronger
1152   assumption (A.4 in the original manuscript of FedLion(Tang & Chang, 2024)):“ There exists a constant α ≤ 13 ,
1153   such that ∥∇f (x) − ∇fi (x)∥1 ≤ α∥∇f (x)∥1 , ∀i, x.”
1154   Additionally, FedLion (Tang & Chang, 2024) cannot be reduced to a standard Lion optimizer, because it merely
1155   parallelizes the execution of the Lion optimizer on the client side and incorporates multi-precision quantization
1156   for communication compression. Therefore, even when K = 1 and m = 1, it does not degrade to a standard
1157   Lion optimizer, and its convergence rate cannot be directly applied to the Lion optimizer.
1158   In contrast, when K = 1 and m = 1 in our algorithm, it degenerates to the standard Lion optimizer, and its
1159   convergence rate can be applied to the Lion optimizer.
1160
1161
1162   E.3    D ETERMINANTS O F T HE C ONVERGENCE B OUND
1163
       In the theoretical analysis of FedSMU, τmax and K control the client participation rate and the number of local
1164   updates, respectively. The convergence rate in Theorem 4.4 demonstrates that increasing the client participation
1165   rate and reducing the number of local updates can tighten the convergence bound.
1166
       It is intuitive that a increased participation rate can improve the model’s convergence rate. A higher client
1167   participation ratio helps the global model gather more information from the clients, which reduces the overfitting
1168   and mitigates the impact of local data heterogeneity, thereby improving the convergence bound. This conclusion
1169   is also supported by Table 1 in (McMahan et al., 2017).
1170   On the other hand, in the convergence upper bound of Theorem 4.4, the term 4Kσl2 + 4KG2 indicates that more
1171   local updates amplify both the local sampling variance σl and the gradients accumulation G, resulting in a slower
1172   convergence. This aligns with our intuition. For the SGD-based local updates, as the number of local updates
1173   increases, the cumulative sampling variance σl and gradients G grows, also further leading to more divergent
1174
       update directions caused by data heterogeneity. This negative effect is also experimentally demonstrated in
       Page 7 of (McMahan et al., 2017), where Figure 3 strongly states that “for very large numbers of local epochs,
1175   FedAvg can plateau or diverge.”

       Here, we clarify the differences and advantages of our FedSMU compared to the D-Lion (Liu et al., 2024), as
1351   follows.
1352
       1) Motivation. Our FedSMU can simultaneously mitigate data heterogeneity and reduce communication
1353
       compression through the symbolic operations. The analysis was carried out and verified by experiments (Figure
1354   1). While D-Lion only consider to compress the communication.
1355
       2) Scope of application. Our FedSMU can deal with scenarios involving the partial client participation and
1356
       multiple local updates, whereas D-Lion can not. Performing multiple local updates, in the federated settings, can
1357   effectively reduce the communication frequency and thus the overall traffic. Experimental results in Table 13
1358   and Table 14 demonstrate that D-Lion fails in such scenarios with low client participation rates and multiple
1359   local updates, whereas FedSMU remains robust and performs well under these conditions.
1360   3) Algorithms design. While both algorithms are based on the Lion optimizer, FedSMU fully leverages the
1361   structural advantages of the Lion optimizer, including weight decay in the global aggregation. In contrast,
1362   D-Lion primarily incorporates the momentum sliding averaging and symbolic operations at local update. This
1363   comprehensive utilization of the Lion optimizer structure may explain why the experimental performance of our
       FedSMU surpasses that of D-Lion.
1364
1365   4) Compatibility with majority vote. We have further extended FedSMU with majority vote, as FedSMU-MV.
1366   Experimental results show that FedSMU-MV achieves an accuracy of 47.66% on CIFAR-100, slightly lower
       than FedSMU’s 51.79% under the same settings (number of clients = 100, fraction = 0.1, Dirichlet = 0.25).
1367
       This indicates that majority vote is compatible with our algorithm. The slight accuracy drop may result from
1368   FedSMU’s symbolic model updates. Applying majority vote to the 1-bit results could further suppress some
1369   clients’ model update information due to the dominant update direction.

1401   Specifically, from the result in Table 13 and Table 14, we have following observations.
1402
1403           • With full participation and one local update (i.e., K = 1 with F), FedSMU performs slightly worse
                 than D-Lion. However, in scenarios with a partial participation, FedSMU consistently outperforms


                                                                   26
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1404
                 D-Lion. This is intuitive, as D-Lion does not maintain a complete global model at the server and only
1405             aggregates the global model updates. Thus in the partial participation settings, asynchronous clients
1406             can only save a stale global model. As a result, these clients may receive the global model updates,
1407             which, however, cannot be leveraged to recover the exact global model of the current round.
1408           • With multiple local updates (i.e., K > 1), FedSMU consistently outperforms D-Lion. This perfor-
1409             mance improvement can be attributed to the different approaches to weight decay. Specifically, the
1410             hyperparameter γ2 (denoted as λ in D-Lion) controls the weight decay (or L2 penalty) coefficient.
                 In FedSMU, the regularization is applied to the global model xt , potentially mitigating overfitting
1411
                 and thus enhancing generalization. In contrast, D-Lion applies this regularization to the local model
1412             xit−1 . As a result, when the local updates occur multiple times, D-Lion’s regularization primarily
1413             affects the local model, and does not directly improve the generalization capability of the global model.
1414             Consequently, when finally evaluating the generalization performance of the global model, FedSMU
1415             demonstrates a significant advantage over D-Lion.
1416           • In heterogeneous scenarios, the performance of both FedSMU and D-Lion is poorer than that of
1417             FedAvg, especially when k is small. This is an interesting and somewhat unexpected finding, which
                 we speculate is due to the data heterogeneity. In the heterogeneous settings, each client samples a
1418
                 mini-batch of data for training and performs only a single time of update, followed by the application
1419             of the sign operation to the model update. Since the local update occurs only once, it introduces a
1420             substantial sampling variance and inter-client variance. The sign operation, which normalizes the
1421             magnitude of updates, may inadvertently amplify this variance between clients, leading to an unstable
1422             or even divergent global model aggregation.
