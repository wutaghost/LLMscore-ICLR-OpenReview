009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Off-road autonomous driving poses significant challenges such as navigating di-
012            verse terrains, avoiding obstacles, and maneuvering through ditches. Addressing
013            these challenges requires effective planning and adaptability, making it a long-
014            horizon planning and control problem. Traditional model-based control tech-
015            niques like Model Predictive Path Integral (MPPI) require dense sampling and ac-
016            curate modeling of the vehicle-terrain interaction, both of which are computation-
017            ally expensive, making effective long-horizon planning in real-time intractable.
018
               Reinforcement learning (RL) methods operate without this limitation and are
               computationally cheaper at deployment. However, exploration in obstacle-dense
019
               and challenging terrains is difficult, and typical RL techniques struggle to navi-
020
               gate in these terrains. To alleviate the limitations of MPPI, we propose a hier-
021            archical autonomy pipeline with a low-frequency high-level MPPI planner and
022            a high-frequency low-level RL controller. To tackle RL’s exploration challenge,
023            we propose a teacher-student paradigm to learn an end-to-end RL policy, capa-
024            ble of real-time execution and traversal through challenging terrains. The teacher
025            policy is trained using dense planning information from an MPPI planner while
026            the student policy learns to navigate using visual inputs and sparse planning in-
027            formation. In this framework, we introduce a new policy gradient formulation
028            that extends Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO), leveraging off-policy trajecto-
029
               ries for teacher guidance and on-policy trajectories for student exploration. We
               demonstrate our performance in a realistic off-road simulator against various RL
030
               and imitation learning methods. Source code and videos are available at this link.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035
      Autonomous ground vehicles have advanced significantly in recent years, with applications such
036   as delivery robots and self-driving taxis. While great progress has been made in structured, ur-
037   ban environments, navigating off-road terrains remains a major challenge. Unlike on-road driving,
038   off-road driving requires effective planning to avoid obstacles, speed management to navigate ex-
039   treme slopes, and rapid adaptive maneuvers to handle varied traction levels and terrains such as
040   dirt, sand, and rocks. Hence, it requires sophisticated control techniques to traverse in these chal-
041   lenging terrains. Successfully navigating large, unstructured environments also depends on effective
042   long-distance planning, making it both a long-horizon planning and adaptive control problem.
043   Conventional control methods for off-road vehicles often depend on model-based techniques, like
044   Model Predictive Path Integral (MPPI) (Han et al. (2024); Williams et al. (2015)). Model-based
045   techniques rely on environmental details, such as segmentation maps and depth maps, to provide
046   waypoints for a low-level controller. These methods necessitate very dense sampling of waypoint
047   rollouts to effectively avoid obstacles and manage diverse terrains. However, this dense sampling
048   requirement is computationally expensive, making it impractical to run these controllers in real-time
049   for simultaneous globally optimal trajectory planning and terrain handling. Some attempts have
050
      been made to improve sampling efficiency by learning a state-dependent control action distribution
      and learning a terminal value function (Qu et al. (2024); Hansen et al. (2022)), thereby reducing
051
      required number of samples and planning horizon.
052
053   Reinforcement learning (RL) is highly effective for tackling complex, high-dimensional, and se-
      quential tasks that are often challenging for traditional control methods. RL models typically utilize

085   behavior of the other actors rather than the variability of the terrain.

087
      Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) proposed by Schulman et al. (2017) is a popular RL framework
      that allows for stable on-policy learning. Despite its advantages, PPO faces limitations in effective
088
      exploration as it relies on random actions sampled around the policy’s intended action for explo-
089
      ration. Because of this, in the proposed off-road driving problem, training a policy with PPO for
090   waypoint distances greater than tens of meters faces significant exploration challenges, and attempt-
091   ing to master multiple off-road navigation skills particularly in avoiding obstacles and navigating
092   extreme slopes leads to ineffective policy training and failure to complete the task. Therefore, our
093   goal is to distill planning information from a teacher trained with the aid of a dense planner on a
094   reduced observation space, while a student learns off-road traversal using an extended observation
095   space without access to the computationally expensive planning data.
096   Since PPO is on-policy, it can only be trained on trajectories collected from its own policy and cannot
097   incorporate external guidance. Attempts to incorporate demonstrations in PPO have been made,
098   though with notable limitations. PPO+D (Libardi & Fabritiis (2021)) extends PPO by incorporating
099   a single off-policy trajectory into the training process. This approach modifies the PPO replay buffer
100   to include three components: Dr for successful trajectories, Dv for failure trajectories and D for the
101   currently sampled trajectories. When sampling from Dv , the paper employs value-based sampling,
102   which becomes impractical for tasks that involve large replay buffers with visual inputs. In off-road
103   driving, navigating diverse terrains requires a broad range of skills, making large buffers for the
104
      teacher demonstration replay and the failure replay buffer in PPO+D crucial. This necessity renders
      PPO+D unsuitable for the task.
105
106   There have been a few attempts to learn policies from demonstrations and through a teacher-student
107   framework in autonomous vehicles. Peng et al. (2022) use off-policy methods like SAC and choose
      actions between a teacher and student policy to solve simpler tasks like lane following and obsta-

311   3.3   O FF - ROAD AUTONOMY S TACK
312
313   As shown in Figure 1, we use two subsystems to achieve off-road autonomous driving: an MPPI-
314   based high-level planner that generates waypoints towards a predefined goal using coarse, global
315   information; and a RL-based controller that learns to track sparse waypoints using local information.
316
      The MPPI planner for this problem is designed in accordance with Han et al. (2024), with the same
317
      cost function. For the teacher policy, MPPI provides dense waypoints and is referred further as
318   MPPI-d. The teacher is a PPO controller trained to track provided MPPI-d waypoints, analogous to
319   the hybrid controller for quadrupedal robots in Jenelten et al. (2024). During training and deploy-
320   ment of the student policy, MPPI provides sparse waypoints and is referred to as MPPI-s. Because
321   of the high runtime cost of the MPPI-d planner, when generating teacher demonstrations, a fixed,
322   pre-computed set of expert MPPI-d waypoints are used. By training with different waypoint dis-
323   tances, responsibility for planning at intermediate distances is shifted from the MPPI-d planner to
      the student policy controller. This allows for much faster and less frequent planning at deployment.

      4.1    E XPERIMENT S ETUP

328   Observation and Action Spaces We combine proprioceptive states with visual input for both the
329   teacher and the student policies. The proprioceptive observation includes the vehicle’s normalized
330   speed, roll, pitch, and encodings of the current and next waypoint are provided, with the teacher
331   using densely planned waypoints and the student using sparsely planned waypoints, as detailed in
332   4.1 and A.5.
333   The visual observations consist of top-down views for planning and a forward camera for obstacle
334   avoidance, with the teacher policy using a local top-down image and the student policy employing
335   a wider-area image with a lower spatial resolution. Both utilize a stack of 3 historical frames for the
336   final observation. The controller directly outputs throttle and steering commands. More information
337   about the observation and action spaces can be found in A.5 and A.6 respectively.
338
339   Reward Function The reward function we designed for off-road navigation includes five key com-
340   ponents. The progress reward encourages movement toward the goal by measuring the reduction in
341   distance between the vehicle’s current and previous positions, while collision and damage penalties
342   address vehicle safety, a jerk penalty discourages sudden acceleration changes, and a success reward
343   is granted for reaching waypoints. More details about reward functions can be found in A.9.

366   the same large map of a desert terrain. For any given start and goal position, we use a sparse global
367   map and a global MPPI planner to generate waypoints of 80 m apart. We choose a fixed set of
368   start, goal position pairs to serve as teacher training and demonstration trajectories. Using the MPPI
369   planner supplied with a detailed local semantic segmentation map and associated depth information,
370   we generate dense waypoints 6 m apart to span the intervals between the sparse 80m waypoints. A
371   similar set is generated to serve as the test trajectories on which we evaluate all learned policies. For
372   methods where expert labeling is required, we generate static, dense waypoints at the beginning of
373   each episode.

375   can qualitatively be categorized as terrains with: i) positive obstacles, ii) extreme slopes, or iii) a
376   hybrid of the preceding categories. The positive obstacles observed on the testing terrain mainly
377   consist of natural obstacles (e.g. boulders and trees) and artificial obstacles (e.g. parked trailers,
      fences, etc.). Extreme slopes include ditches and sandy cliffs.

      4.2.1    I MITATION L EARNING M ETHODS
426   DAgger DAgger Ross et al. (2011) provides a straightforward method for supervising the policy
427   by allowing queries to a teacher during training. Initially, the teacher trajectories are utilized for
428   behavior cloning (BC) on the student. Subsequently, the student policy is enhanced by penalizing
429   the discrepancy between the actions predicted by the student and those of the teacher at each state
430   encountered. In complex environments like off-road driving, DAgger fails because of compounding
431   errors. As the policy accumulates error and deviates from expert trajectories, it encounters unseen
      or irrecoverable states, thus severely degrading its performance.

      4.2.2    O N - POLICY METHODS
433
434   Vanilla PPO Vanilla PPO is trained as described in 2.1. This method does not utilize demon-
435   strations from a teacher and is trained only on sparse waypoints. As described in 2.1, Vanilla PPO
436   encounters exploration challenges in obstacle-rich terrains, which hinders its ability to learn an op-
437   timal strategy. Without guidance, the policy fails to differentiate between various types of terrains
438
      and defaults to a sub-optimal, overly-cautious strategy.
439
440
      PPO+BC A naive approach to distill teacher actions into the student is to incorporate a KL diver-
      gence loss between the predicted action distributions of the student and teacher. PPO+BC introduces
441
      a loss term that aligns the policy π with the teacher policy µ across all encountered states. The vanilla
442
      PPO loss function is modified to LKL for training.
443
444
445                                 LKL = LPPO − βKL[π(at |sπt ), µ(at |sµt )]                            (12)
446
447   While this provides strong supervision, a issue similar to DAgger arises when the student queries the
448   expert from out-of-distribution states and optimizes using sub-optimal action labeling. Additionally,
449   the updates from the KL divergence term are unconstrained, which leads to unstable training and
450   results in convergence to a sub-optimal policy.
451
452   4.2.3    O FF -P OLICY METHODS
453
454
      Vanilla SAC SAC (Haarnoja et al. (2018)) is an off-policy RL algorithm that optimizes a stochastic
      policy and value function, enabling efficient and stable learning for continuous control tasks. SAC
455
      struggles in our high-exploration, multi-task setup because its entropy maximization can lead to
456
      excessive exploration of irrelevant actions, reducing its focus on task-specific objectives. This makes
457   it less effective in environments requiring targeted exploration and adaptation to multiple tasks with
458   distinct strategies.
459
460   SAC+Teacher As an off-policy algorithm, SAC can utilize trajectories from teacher demonstra-
461   tions without requiring any modifications to the algorithm. A portion of the replay buffer is pre-
462   populated during the training. In this case, the buffer size remains consistent with TADPO, with the
463   teacher trajectory ratio set at p = 0.5. As also shown in Yu et al. (2019), SAC does not perform well
464   when it has to handle various different kinds of tasks (in this case, a very diverse set of terrains).
465
466   IQL Implicit Q-learning (Kostrikov et al. (2021)) which extends Q-learning and actor critic meth-
467   ods is an off-policy reinforcement learning method that estimates Q-values without directly opti-
468   mizing a policy, allowing the agent to implicitly select actions that maximize the value function.
469
      It is used in our teacher-student setup by following the actions suggested by the teacher’s demon-
      strations, reinforcing behavior through the learning of Q-values associated with those actions. IQL
470
      demonstrates some success in navigating extreme slope terrains, but its overall performance does
471
      not match that of TADPO. As noted in Janner et al. (2022), IQL excels in single-task scenarios but
472   faces challenges in multi-task environments, such as off-road autonomy. Given that off-road auton-
473   omy involves dynamically handling obstacle avoidance and rapid changes in how the vehicle and
474   terrain interacts, IQL struggles to perform effectively in this setup.

533
      We propose (i) a hierarchical off-road autonomy pipeline and (ii) a new hybrid policy optimization
534   method TADPO. The pipeline combines the strengths of MPPI and RL to provide a robust solution
535   for off-road autonomous driving in complex terrains. TADPO leverages a teacher-student paradigm
536   with a novel policy gradient formulation to resolve the challenges of exploration and planning. Our
537   experimental results demonstrate significant improvements in navigating challenging environments
538   compared to existing RL and imitation learning methods, validating the potential of our approach.
539   We plan to deploy this algorithm onto real vehicles in our future work. Source code for TADPO and
      videos of the pipeline in action are available at https://github.com/tadpo-iclr/tadpo.
