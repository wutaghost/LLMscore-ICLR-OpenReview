011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Restoring missing information in video frames is a challenging inverse problem,
014            particularly in applications such as autonomous driving and surveillance. This
015            paper introduces the Siamese Masked Conditional Variational Autoencoder (SM-
016
               CVAE), a novel model that utilizes a Siamese network architecture with Siamese
               Vision Transformer (SiamViT) encoders. By leveraging the inherent similarities
017
               between paired frames, SMCVAE enhances the model’s ability to accurately re-
018
               construct missing content. This approach effectively tackles the problem of miss-
019            ing patches—often resulting from camera malfunctions—through advanced vari-
020            ational inference techniques. Experimental results demonstrate SMCVAE’s supe-
021            rior performance in restoring lost information, highlighting its potential to solve
022            complex inverse problems in real-world environments.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   The rapid advancement of imaging technologies has significantly broadened the applications of
028   cameras, particularly in areas that demand high precision and reliability, such as autonomous driv-
029   ing systems (Geiger et al., 2012). In these systems, cameras serve as critical sensory components,
030   providing real-time visual data essential for navigation and environmental perception (Muhammad
031   et al., 2020; Agostinho et al., 2022). Accurate environmental perception is vital for ensuring both
032   the safety and operational efficiency of autonomous vehicles. However, missing patches in video
033
      frames—caused by camera malfunctions, occlusions, or transmission errors—pose substantial chal-
      lenges. These data gaps can severely undermine the perception systems of autonomous vehicles,
034
      leading to reduced reliability and overall performance, which may result in serious consequences
035
      in real-world scenarios (Mallozzi et al., 2019; Jebamikyous & Kashef, 2022). Despite extensive
036   research aimed at addressing these issues, accurately restoring missing information in video frames
037   remains a significant technical hurdle (Rota et al., 2023; Lamba & Mitra, 2022).
038
039
      At the same time, video inpainting techniques have seen significant progress, offering methods
      for seamlessly editing video content. These techniques allow for the removal or modification of
040
      objects within video frames while preserving the temporal continuity of the footage, resulting in
041
      smooth and visually coherent sequences (Chang et al., 2019; Wu et al., 2023). However, video
042   inpainting typically focuses on achieving aesthetic coherence rather than accurately restoring the
043   original content. This distinction is crucial because, while inpainting produces visually plausible
044   results, it often neglects the accurate recovery of missing information. In contrast, restoration tasks
045   prioritize the authenticity of the content, aiming for precise reconstruction that restores video frames
046   to their original state (Su et al., 2022).
047
      To address the pressing need for reliable restoration methods, we propose the Siamese Masked
048   Conditional Variational Autoencoder (SMCVAE), as illustrated in Figure 1. Given that adjacent
049   video frames often share similar features and structures, the use of a Siamese network—with shared
050   weights between two encoders—ensures consistent feature extraction from both masked and un-
051   masked frames. This shared-weight approach allows both inputs to be represented in a common
052   latent space, facilitating the model’s ability to learn meaningful relationships between them and ef-
053   fectively reconstruct missing information. Moreover, weight sharing reduces the overall number
      of parameters, a critical consideration in deep learning applications where minimizing complexity

      Inverse problems. Inverse problems, where one seeks to recover underlying causes from observed
      data, are central to various fields, including computer vision, medical imaging, and signal processing
087
      (Arridge et al., 2019; Hansen, 2010). These problems are typically formulated as reconstructing the
088
      original input x from an observation y, where y is often corrupted by noise or incomplete data.
089   Mathematically, the problem can be described as solving for x in the equation:
090
                                                  y = A(x) + n,                                                (1)
091
092   where A is a forward operator that maps the true signal x to the observation y, and n represents
093   noise or corruption. The goal is to invert this process and estimate x given y. However, inverse
094   problems are often ill-posed, meaning that small changes in y can lead to large variations in the
095
      estimated x, or that a unique solution may not exist (Tarantola, 2005). Traditional approaches to
      solving inverse problems rely on regularization techniques, which introduce prior knowledge about
096
      the solution to stabilize the inversion (Bertero et al., 2021). For example, one widely used method
097
      is to minimize a regularized loss function:
098
                                      x̂ = arg min ∥A(x) − y∥2 + λR(x) ,

099
                                                                                                       (2)
                                              x
100   where R(x) is a regularization term, and λ controls the regularization strength. We leverage a
101   similar deep learning-based approach to address the inverse problem of video frame restoration.
102   Our proposed SMCVAE learns a latent representation of video frames, allowing it to accurately
103   reconstruct missing or corrupted content. By incorporating variational inference and a Siamese
104   architecture, SMCVAE provides a robust and efficient solution to this inverse problem (Bora et al.,
105   2017).
106
107   Siamese networks. Siamese networks, characterized by their dual-branch architecture with shared
      weights, are highly effective for tasks involving similarity measurement and entity comparison. The


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      shared weights allow both branches to extract comparable features from different inputs, making
109   Siamese networks well-suited for applications such as signature verification (Bromley et al., 1993),
110   face verification (Chopra et al., 2005), and one-shot learning (Koch et al., 2015). Their versatility
111   extends to contrastive learning, where they help in learning discriminative features by minimizing
112   the distance between similar entities while maximizing the distance between dissimilar ones (Chen
113   et al., 2020; He et al., 2020). This architecture has been instrumental in various computer vision tasks
114   such as image classification, face recognition, and object tracking (Taigman et al., 2014; Bertinetto
115   et al., 2016). Traditionally used in discriminative tasks, our research innovatively applies Siamese
116   networks to generative modeling, opening up new possibilities in content restoration and generation.

118
      Variational inference. Variational inference is a key technique in probabilistic modeling that ap-
      proximates complex, often intractable, posterior distributions by optimizing an approximate distri-
119
      bution, q(z), to match the true posterior, p(z|x), using the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence as a
120
      measure of information loss (Blei et al., 2017; Jordan et al., 1999). This approach balances com-
121   putational efficiency with model expressiveness, making it particularly useful in scalable machine
122   learning applications. By minimizing the KL divergence, variational inference enables efficient pos-
123   terior estimation in large datasets and complex models such as Bayesian neural networks (Blundell
124   et al., 2015). Its role is central to the development of models like the Variational Autoencoder (VAE)
125   (Kingma, 2013; Rezende et al., 2014), which leverages variational inference to learn latent variable
126   models in a generative framework. The Conditional Variational Autoencoder (CVAE) extends this
127   framework to handle conditional dependencies, making it well-suited for tasks such as video frame
128   restoration, where generative models need to account for auxiliary variables (Sohn et al., 2015).
129
      Variational inference not only enhances computational tractability but also allows for more flexible
      and expressive modeling, underscoring its importance in both generative modeling and Bayesian
130
      deep learning (Hoffman et al., 2013; Tucker et al., 2017).

      Dataset. For our experiments, we utilize the BDD100K dataset, a comprehensive collection of im-
291   ages and videos captured across various driving conditions and environmental scenarios (Yu et al.,
292   2020). To streamline the training process and reduce computational overhead, we select a represen-
293   tative subset of the dataset.
294
295   Masking strategy. To replicate real-world scenarios of data loss or image corruption, we imple-
296   ment a strategic masking procedure. In each image pair, one frame is selectively occluded to simulate
297   partial data loss or corruption, as commonly observed in dynamic video feeds due to transmission
298   errors, occlusions, or sensor malfunctions (Pathak et al., 2016; Zhao et al., 2016). The unmasked
299   frame serves as a reference for restoration, enabling our model to learn how to effectively reconstruct
300   missing or corrupted content.
301
302   Baseline. Our baseline model consists of two independent ViT encoders, each tasked with sepa-
303   rately processing the masked and unmasked input frames, followed by a ViT decoder that recon-
304   structs the corrupted frames. This setup provides a strong foundation for comparison, allowing us to
305
      measure the improvements brought by our proposed model.

      5   D ISCUSSION
402
403
      The results of our experiments demonstrate the effectiveness of our SMCVAE in handling com-
404
      plex video frame restoration tasks. By incorporating a Siamese architecture with shared weights,
405   the model not only reduces the number of parameters but also maintains high reconstruction qual-
406   ity, even under severe data loss conditions. This aligns with previous findings on the benefits of
407   weight-sharing in Siamese networks for reducing model complexity while preserving performance
408   (Koch et al., 2015; Bromley et al., 1993). Our ablation studies further highlight the importance of
409   key architectural choices, such as the use of reparameterization and the fine-tuning of the Lagrange
410   multiplier, both of which significantly enhance the model’s performance. These findings are consis-
411   tent with existing literature on β-VAE, where tuning β improves the balance between reconstruction
412   fidelity and latent space regularization (Higgins et al., 2017).
413   In comparison with the baseline model, which utilizes dual independent ViT encoders, SMCVAE
414   consistently outperforms in terms of reconstruction accuracy and robustness to varying degrees of
415   corruption. The model’s ability to preserve essential content while filling in missing details with
416   high fidelity demonstrates its potential for real-world applications, particularly in domains like au-
417   tonomous driving, where data integrity is critical (Yu et al., 2020; Geiger et al., 2012). This per-
418   formance, especially in scenarios involving severe masking or large frame gaps, underscores the
419
      robustness of the SMCVAE architecture, which is capable of handling complex temporal dependen-
      cies in video data.
420
421   Despite the promising results, there remain challenges, such as further optimizing the model’s ef-
422   ficiency for real-time applications and exploring its performance in more diverse and extreme con-
423   ditions. Future work could focus on extending the model’s capabilities to multi-modal datasets or
424   integrating it with state-of-the-art video restoration techniques to further enhance its generalizability
425
      and performance.
