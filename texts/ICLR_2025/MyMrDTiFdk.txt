009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            The hidden state threat model of differential privacy (DP) assumes that the
012            adversary has access only to the final trained machine learning (ML) model,
013            without seeing intermediate states during training. Current privacy analyses under
014            this model, however, are limited to convex optimization problems, reducing their
015            applicability to multi-layer neural networks, which are essential in modern deep
016            learning applications. Additionally, the most successful applications of the hidden
017            state privacy analyses in classification tasks have been for logistic regression
018
               models. We demonstrate that it is possible to privately train convex problems
               with privacy-utility trade-offs comparable to those of one hidden-layer ReLU
019
               networks trained with DP stochastic gradient descent (DP-SGD). We achieve
020
               this through a stochastic approximation of a dual formulation of the ReLU
021            minimization problem which results in a strongly convex problem. This enables
022            the use of existing hidden state privacy analyses, providing accurate privacy
023            bounds also for the noisy cyclic mini-batch gradient descent (NoisyCGD) method
024            with fixed disjoint mini-batches. Our experiments on benchmark classification
025            tasks show that NoisyCGD can achieve privacy-utility trade-offs comparable to
026            DP-SGD applied to one-hidden-layer ReLU networks. Additionally, we provide
027            theoretical utility bounds that highlight the speed-ups gained through the convex
028            approximation.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      In differentially private (DP) machine learning (ML), the DP-SGD algorithm (see e.g., Abadi
      et al., 2016) has become a standard tool obtain ML models with strong privacy guarantees of
034
      the individuals. The (ε, δ)-guarantees for DP-SGD are obtained by clipping gradients by adding
035
      normally distributed noise scaled with the clipping constant to the randomly sampled mini-batch of
036   gradients, and by using composition DP analysis (see, e.g., Zhu et al., 2022).
037
038   One weak point of the composition analysis of DP-SGD is that it is assumes that the adversary has
039
      access to all the intermediate results of the training iteration. This assumption is often unnecessarily
      strict as in many practical scenarios only the final model is needs to be revealed. Another weakness
040
      is that it requires either full batch training or random subsampling, and, e.g., accurate privacy
041
      analyses for of many practically relevant algorithms are not available for non-convex problems such
042   as for training multi-layer neural networks. One example of such algorithms is the noisy cyclic
043   GD (NoisyCGD) with disjoint mini-batches. Having high privacy-utility ML models trained with
044   NoisyCGD would give an alternative for DP-SGD that is often difficult to implement in practical
045   settings (Chua et al., 2024a).
046
      The so called hidden state threat model of DP considers releasing only the final model of the
047
      training iteration, and existing (ε, δ)-DP analyses in the literature are only applicable for convex
048   problems such as the logistic regression which is the highest performing model considered in the
049   literature (see, e.g., Chourasia et al., 2021; Bok et al., 2024). When training models with DP-
050   SGD, however, one quickly finds that the model performance of commonly used convex models is
051   inferior compared to multi-layer neural networks. A natural question then arises whether convex
052   approximations of minimization problems for multi-layer neural networks can be made while
053   preserving model performance. In response, this work explores such an approximation for the
      two-layer ReLU minimization problem. To achieve this, we build on the findings of Pilanci &


                                                         1

054
      Ergen (2020), which demonstrate the existence of a convex dual formulation for the two-layer ReLU
055   minimization problem when the hidden layer is sufficiently wide.
056
057   The privacy amplification by iteration analysis for convex private optimization, introduced by
058
      Feldman et al. (2018), provides privacy guarantees in the hidden state threat model. However, this
      and many subsequent analyses (Sordello et al., 2021; Asoodeh et al., 2020; Chourasia et al., 2021;
059
      Altschuler & Talwar, 2022) remain challenging to apply in practice, as it typically requires a large
060
      number of training iterations for obtaining tighter DP guarantees than those of DP-SGD. Chourasia
061   et al. (2021) improved this analysis using Rényi DP for full-batch DP-GD training, while Ye &
062   Shokri (2022) offered a similar analysis for shuffled mini-batch DP-SGD. Recently, Bok et al. (2024)
063   provided an f -DP analysis for a class of algorithms, which we also leverage to analyze NoisyCGD.
064
      Also from a theoretical standpoint, convex models are advantageous over non-convex ones in

      private optimization. State-of-the-art empirical

      Our main contributions are the following:
072
073
             • By integrating two seemingly unrelated approaches, convex reformulation of ReLU
074            networks and privacy amplification by iteration DP analysis, we show that it is possible
075            to obtain similar privacy-utility trade-offs in the hidden state threat model of DP as by
076            applying DP-SGD to two-layer ReLU networks and using composition results.
077          • We carry out a number of approximations for the convex reformulation to facilitate DP
078            analysis and show that the resulting strongly convex model has the required properties for
079            hidden state analysis.
080
             • We give the first high privacy-utility trade-off results for benchmark classification tasks
081            using a hidden state DP analysis. In particular, we give the first empirical high
082            privacy-utility trade-off results for NoisyCGD with disjoint mini-batches under the hidden
083            state threat model which makes it more suitable for practical applications of DP ML.
084            The experiments also account for the privacy cost of hyperparameter tuning, and we
085            demonstrate how to conduct it effectively for NoisyCGD.
086          • We carry out a theoretical utility analysis of DP-SGD applied to the convex approximation
087            within the random data model.

380   When comparing experimentally DP optimization methods, it is crucial to take into account the
381   effect of the hyperparameter tuning on the privacy costs. The most relevant to our work are the
382   randomized tuning methods given by Papernot & Steinke (2022) and the subsequent privacy profile-
383   based analysis by Koskela et al. (2024). These results hold for a tuning algorithm that outputs
384   the best model of the K alternatives, where K is a random variable. We consider the case K

386   balance between compute cost of training, privacy and accuracy. The DP bounds in this case can be

388
      denoting the score) and A(y) the density function of the tuning algorithm that outputs the best model

390
      A′ can be bounded using the following result.

522   7                  C ONCLUSIONS AND O UTLOOK
523
524   We have shown how to privately approximate the two-layer ReLU network and we have given the
525   first high privacy-utility trade-off results using the hidden state DP analysis. In particular, we have
526   given the first high privacy-utility trade-off results for the noisy cyclic mini-batch GD which makes it
527   more suitable for practical applications of DP ML model training. As shown by our experiments on
528   benchmark image classification datasets, the results for the convex problems have similar privacy-
529   utility trade-offs as those obtained by applying DP-SGD to a one hidden-layer ReLU network and
530   using the composition analysis. Theoretically, an interesting future task is to carry out end-to-end
531
      utility analysis for private optimization of ReLU networks via the dual form. The recent results
      by Kim & Pilanci (2024) could be helpful for this as they show connections between the stochastic
532
      approximation of the dual form and the ReLU minimization problem. Also, an interesting general
533
      question is, whether it is possible to obtain still better privacy-utility trade-offs for the final model
534   in the hidden state threat model by using the privacy amplification by iteration type of analysis
535   of, for example, DP-SGD. In order to get a better understanding of this question, tighter privacy
536   amplification by iteration analysis for, e.g., DP-SGD or Noisy-CGD would be needed, as the
537   composition analysis of DP-SGD cannot likely be improved a lot. Furthermore, developing DP
538   convex models that approximate deeper neural networks (Ergen et al., 2023a), including those with
539   convolutional layers (Ergen & Pilanci, 2020) and different activation functions (Ergen et al., 2023b),
      is an intriguing direction for future research.

1014   J                   F URTHER M OTIVATION FOR N OISY CGD A NALYSIS
1015
1016   When using disjoint batches of data, currently the best option for obtaining rigorous guarantees is
1017
       to use data shuffling and shuffling amplification (Feldman et al., 2021), however it has been shown
       by Chua et al. (2024a;b) that the data-shuffling combined with disjoint batches leads to an inferior
1018
       privacy-utility trade-off compared to random mini-batch sampling. And we experimentally show
1019
       that the method we propose (strongly convex approximation of ReLU problem + NoisyCGD) has
1020   similar privacy-utility trade-off as random mini-batch sampling applied to one hidden-layer ReLU
1021   networks. Although we do not explicitly show comparisons against the shuffled DP-SGD, we believe
1022   that our approach would be better than the shuffling approach. To illustrate this, we compute the
1023   shuffling amplification bounds by Feldman et al. (2021) by considering the setting in one of our
1024   experiments, where we use noise parameter σ = 5.0. Similarly to the experiments of Chua et al.
1025   (2024b), we use the numerical method presented in Feldman et al. (2021) to accurately compute
       the shuffling upper bounds. In our experiments of Section 6, we use 50 or 60 disjoint batches per


                                                                         19
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1026
       epoch. When computing the shuffling bounds, one quickly finds that this is a too few number of
1027   batches for the conditions of the analysis of Feldman et al. (2021) to hold. The shuffling privacy
1028   guarantee clearly improves the number of batches per epoch grows (see, e.g., the comparisons of
1029   Chua et al., 2024b), and to obtain a lower bound for the upper bound, we consider 1000 bathces
1030   per epoch. The comparison to the bounds of the Gaussian mechanism (i.e., no amplification) are

1034   when using data shuffling to amplify the DP guarantees.
