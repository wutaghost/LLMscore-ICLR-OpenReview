009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011
012            The Self-Attention mechanism in Transformer models has shown great success
013            across many domains, but its effectiveness can diminish in complex settings, such
014            as multimodal tasks. This is due to the varying token granularity and the high com-
015            putational cost of processing long sequences. To overcome these limitations, we
016            propose the Learnable Context-Aware Attention Mask (LCAAM), a novel method
017
               that globally adjusts attention maps to prioritize the most important tokens in a
               sequence. Our approach integrates LCAAM into a BERT-like Transformer net-
018
               work, enhancing the Self-Attention mechanism by capturing token relationships
019
               while accounting for their contextual relevance. Additionally, we extend LCAAM
020            to a multi-layer framework, enabling it to capture diverse information across the
021            layers of the Transformer. Extensive experiments on datasets including MADv2,
022            QVHighlights, ImageNet-1K, and MSRVTT demonstrate that LCAAM improves
023            model performance while reducing redundant computations. This innovation of-
024            fers a significant improvement in tackling complex tasks, such as movie under-
025            standing.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032
033
      The evolution of deep learning has empowered us to navigate increasingly complex scenarios, many
      of which require digesting information from diverse sources, such as videos, images, audio, and
034
      text. One such scenario lies in understanding movie scenes (Soldan et al., 2022; Han et al., 2023b;c;
035
      Barrios et al., 2023; Rohrbach et al., 2015; Xiao et al., 2022; Islam et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023),
036   where models aim to extract meaningful insights from multiple modalities.
037
038   Consider a movie scene represented by video and audio tokens. While these tokens naturally align
039
      in time, each one can be associated with any other tokens presented in the scene, as shown in Fig-
      ure 1(a). While the Self-Attention module is effective for computing local associations between
040
      tokens, we have observed several drawbacks in the current attention mechanism, especially when
041
      tokens originate from diverse modalities. Firstly, different modalities introduce varying granular-
042   ities of information, leading to potential challenges. Each token in one modality may be asso-
043   ciated with multiple tokens in the other modality. Such associations can extend beyond one-to-
044   one correspondences, forming between sub-sequences of tokens in each modality. In Figure 1(a),
045   “Joanna’s shouts” might not be associated with a single video token but with several. Moreover,
046   while longer sequences of tokens generally offer richer information, the computational demands of
047   attention mechanisms increase with the input length of tokens. This constraint hinders the effective
048   processing of a higher number of tokens.
049   Our method stems from the empirical observation that not all tokens in complex input sequences
050   carry equal importance. While prior works like Fan et al. (2021); Tang et al. (2021); Lin & Joe
051   (2023); Rende et al. (2024) have demonstrated the effectiveness of dynamically updated masking
052   mechanisms, this concept remains relatively unexplored in the computer vision domain, with only
053   a few studies such as Lin et al. (2022) exploring it. This gap in vision research has motivated our
      comprehensive analysis of the impact of dynamic token masking across diverse vision tasks.

074
      To tackle the challenges posed by complex input sequences, we propose the Learnable Context-
      Aware Attention Mask (LCAAM)1 —a mechanism that dynamically generates a matrix of weights
075
      to regulate attention maps and prioritize critical tokens based on their contextual significance.
076
      LCAAM takes as input a token sequence and outputs a mask, capturing attention structures glob-
077   ally across the sequence. Here, “context” encompasses several dimensions: temporal relationships,
078   intermodal associations, and dynamic changes throughout the input sequence. By processing the en-
079   tire input sequence, LCAAM enables efficient token inspection and dynamic prioritization tailored
080   to each sequence. This adaptive masking technique integrates into existing Transformer Encoder
081   architectures, offering a flexible solution to enhance transformer-based models across diverse appli-
082   cations.
083   More formally, the LCAAM method employs linear layers that take as input a sequence of tokens
084   with shape (B, N, E), where B is the batch size, N is the number of tokens, and E is the embed-
085   ding size. The sequence can be either single-modal or multimodal. The output is a mask of size
086   (B, N, N ), where for each item in the batch, a mask is generated to capture the attention between
087   all pairs of tokens in the sequence. This generated mask can be applied either globally across all
088   transformer layers in the stack or scaled individually for each transformer layer.
089   Eventually, the resulting mask, as illustrated in Figure 1(b), captures attention structures globally.
090   This generated mask is then element-wise multiplied or added (depending on the setup) with the
091   attention scores, allowing the masking out or prioritization of specific tokens. Furthermore, our
092   observation that each layer in the Transformer Network embeds different information aspects moti-
093   vates us to install the LCAAM module per stage, leading to the extension of LCAAM to a multi-layer
094   configuration.
095   We validate the effectiveness of our approach across various experimental settings. Initially, we
096   assess the capability of the multi-layer LCAAM in multimodal settings, presenting results from
097   audio description generation experiments on the MADv2 dataset (Soldan et al., 2022; Han et al.,
098   2023b). Additionally, we apply our approach to Moment Retrieval and Highlight Detection tasks on
099   the QVHighlight dataset (Lei et al., 2021), incorporating both text and video inputs. Furthermore,
100   we demonstrate that LCAAM can enhance performance in single-modality settings, such as image
101   classification tasks in ImageNet 1K (Deng et al., 2009) and video captioning tasks in MSRVTT (Xu
102   et al., 2016), where a single modality is considered as input to the model. While the performance
103
      gain in single-modality settings is modest, we demonstrate that our multi-layer LCAAM can be
      adopted in various scenarios. Finally, we analyze how the generated mask effectively regulates
104
      attention maps.

      In summary, our contributions are three-fold:
109
110         1. We propose the Learnable Context-Aware Attention Mask (LCAAM), a mechanism that
111            dynamically generates masks to regulate attention maps and prioritize significant tokens
112            based on their contextual significance. LCAAM can be integrated into existing Transformer
113            Encoder architectures, potentially enhancing performance on complex sequence processing
114            tasks with minimal architectural modifications.
115         2. Through experiments across various benchmarks, including MAD, ImageNet-1K,
116            MSRVTT, and QVHighlights, we empirically demonstrate the effectiveness of our
117            LCAAM method, particularly when employed with multimodal encoders.
118         3. We analyze the output of LCAAM and its influence on attention weight distributions, sup-
119            plemented by qualitative analysis to provide insights into its behavior.

124   2.1   M ULTIMODAL T RANSFORMERS
125
      A predominant area of prior exploration in aligning multiple modalities centers around contrastive
126
      learning, a method extensively utilized in both image-text and video-audio alignment contexts (Chen
127
      et al., 2020; Khosla et al., 2020; Radford et al., 2021; He et al., 2019; Han et al., 2023a; Zhang et al.,
128   2023a). Recent investigations have also delved into merging diverse modalities within a unified fea-
129   ture space through the incorporation of cross-attention layers (Chen et al., 2021; Lee et al., 2021;
130   Wei et al., 2020; Moon et al., 2023). Furthermore, there is a growing trend of leveraging Transformer
131   capabilities for multimodal fusion tasks (Luo et al., 2021; Kamath et al., 2021; Han et al., 2023a;
132   Barrios et al., 2023; Lei et al., 2021). Our decision to employ a multimodal transformer in our design
133   is rooted in its unparalleled capability to integrate information across diverse modalities, thus fos-
134   tering a more comprehensive understanding of the input data. Through the utilization of this unified
135   architecture, we are enabled to effectively capture intricate interactions within the sequence, strategi-
136
      cally prioritizing relevant cues based on their significance. In contrast to conventional methodologies
      that treat modalities in isolation, the multimodal transformer facilitates the seamless integration of
137
      contextual information, thereby yielding more coherent and nuanced representations.

      2.2   L ANGUAGE M ODELS FOR V IDEO D ESCRIPTION
141
      To adapt a Large Language Model (LLM) for AD generation, we incorporate an adapter module.
142   This module processes audiovisual features and transforms them into the feature space of our LLM.
143   The concept of training an adapter module rather than finetuning the entire LLM to account for
144   a new modality has been widely explored (Yi-Lin Sung, 2022; Hu et al., 2023), but the method
145   most similar to ours is LLaMA-Adapter (Zhang et al., 2023b; Gao et al., 2023). LLaMA adapter,
146   however, does not account for audio data. Our method follows that of LLaMA-Adapter closely, but
147   changes the input feature space to include both audio and video features. The previous State-of-the-
148   Art in our specific task (generating audio descriptions of movie clips) on the MAD dataset are the
149   AutoAD models Han et al. (2023b;c). We are able to generate comparable results with significantly
150
      less fine tuning and contextual information. Recent models have also achieved significant results in
      finding important moments in longer videos, but these contributions are not particularly relevant to
151
      ours because we focus on describing shorter video segments (Lei et al., 2021; Barrios et al., 2023).
152
      Another recent result similar to ours is the Video-LLaMA model, which focuses on general purpose
153   visual question answering but uses a Q-Former instead of an adapter module to fuse the visual,
154   audio, and text modalities (Zhang et al., 2023a).

      2.3   M ASKING ATTENTION
157
158   In the field of Natural Language Processing, researchers have explored various methods of con-
159   structing attention masks, while also investigating their impact on transformer architectures (Fan
160   et al., 2021; Tang et al., 2021; Lin & Joe, 2023; Rende et al., 2024). Conversely, this exploration has
161   received limited attention in Computer Vision (Li et al., 2021; Lin et al., 2022). Motivated by this
      disparity, our objective is to investigate this phenomenon, particularly in the context of multimodal


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      data, and its implications for task performance. Unlike the approach proposed by SwinBert (Lin
163   et al., 2022), which advocates for a sparse and learnable mask, our focus aligns more closely with
164   the principles of Mask Attention Networks (Fan et al., 2021). Instead of relying on a static mask
165   matrix, which may restrict the model’s ability to capture local relationships effectively, we propose
166   employing a Learnable Context-Award Attention Mask (LCAAM). This adaptive mechanism aims
167   to prioritize and regulate attention tokens within long sequences based on their contextual signifi-
168   cance in a dynamic manner.

      3.1   D EFINITION
197
198   We aim to generate an attention mask that adeptly prioritizes and regulates tokens according to their
199   importance within a sequence. For this purpose, we develop a learnable module (denoted as F),
200   which receives a sequence of tokens X as input and returns a mask M, as shown in Equation 1. The
201   shape of the mask M depends on the sequence length and the purpose of the multi-head attention,
202   whether it is self-attention or cross-attention.
203                                                   F (X) → M                                               (1)
204   Attention. In the context of self-attention, the resulting mask output can be represented as
205   (B, N, N ), where B is the batch size and N is the number of tokens. For instance, consider a
206   multimodal sequence with 128 tokens and a batch size of 1. In this case, the output of our LCAAM
207   would have the dimensions (1, 128, 128). In the cross-attention setup, the mask shape is given by
208   (B, Nq , Nk ), where Nq represents the length of the Query tensor and Nk represents the length of
209   the Key tensor. For example, if we perform cross-attention with 75 visual features as the query, 32
210   text features as the key, and a batch size of 1, the output of LCAAM will be (1, 75, 32). To ensure
211   clarity for the reader, all content in the Section 3 refers to the self-attention scenario unless otherwise
212
      specified.
213   Scalability. The mask can be applied either globally across all transformer layers in the stack
214   (Section 3.2) or individually for each layer (Section 3.4). This flexibility allows for different masks
215   to be used at various depths, meaning that each layer can have its own set of learnable parameters,
      capturing hierarchical information at different levels.

      3.2   L EARNABLE C ONTEXT-AWARE ATTENTION M ASK M ODULE
217
218   The Learnable Context-Aware Attention Mask (LCAAM) module processes an input sequence
219   through a stack of linear layers with ReLU activations. Let X ∈ RB×N ×D denote the input to
220   the LCAAM module, where B is the batch size, N is the number of tokens, and D is the embedding
221   dimension. The module consists of L layers, where each layer i ∈ 1, 2, . . . , L is defined by a weight
222   matrix Wi ∈ RDini ×Douti and a bias vector bi ∈ RDouti . The forward pass of the LCAAM module
223   can be described as follows:
224
                                          H1 = ReLU(XW1 + b1 )                                            (2)
225                         Hi = ReLU(Hi−1 Wi + bi ), for i ∈ {2, 3, . . . , L − 1}                       (3)
226                                         M = HL−1 WL + bL                                              (4)

      Where ReLU(·) represents the rectified linear unit activation function, Hi ∈ RB×N ×Douti is the
      output of the i-th layer, and M ∈ RB×N ×N is the generated mask. Note that the input X and all
229
      intermediate outputs Hi maintain the batch and number of tokens (B, N ). For optimal implemen-
230
      tation, LCAAM’s dimensionality should align with the Transformer Layer.

249
      We extend the Learnable Context-Aware Attention Mask (LCAAM) approach to a stack of L Trans-
250   former layers, with each layer l having its own unique learnable mask, denoted as Ml . The attention
251   mechanism at each layer is computed according to Equation 5. The masks Ml are learned indepen-
252   dently for each layer, allowing each LCAAM to adapt its masking operation based on the layer’s
253   representation. As the output of one layer serves as the input to the next, this allows for hierarchical
254   representation learning across the stack.

325
326   Our comprehensive evaluation across five diverse tasks, as presented in Table 1, reveals the signifi-
327   cant impact of our proposed method. The results demonstrate a marked improvement in performance
328   when applied to multimodal encoders, in contrast to single-modality encoders. Specifically, for mul-
329   timodal tasks, we observe substantial gains: Table 1a shows a maximum improvement of 12.7 points

331   is corroborated by the results in Tables 1b and 1c, where we note maximum improvements of 2.46

478
479
      The Learnable Context-Aware Attention Mask (LCAAM) significantly enhances attention mech-
480   anisms in Transformer models, especially for multimodal tasks. By dynamically prioritizing im-
481   portant tokens, LCAAM boosts model performance in diverse tasks. However, its gains in single-
482   modality scenarios are limited. While LCAAM excels at capturing contextual relationships in mul-
483   timodal tasks, the improvements in single-modality tasks are modest due to the simpler nature of
484   processing a single input type, which underutilizes LCAAM’s dynamic masking. Future work could
485   focus on amplifying LCAAM’s impact in single-modality tasks, possibly by incorporating additional
      contextual information or hybrid strategies combining insights from multiple modalities.

      A.3.4    T RAINING P ROTOCOL
865
866   The training procedure for our Audio Description Generation model adhered closely to the method-
867   ology outlined in Gao et al. (2023). The process began with an initial alignment phase aimed at
868   ensuring robust synchronization between the audiovisual features. This phase was crucial for es-
869   tablishing coherence between the audio and visual modalities of the input data. Upon successful
870
      alignment, we resumed training with a focus on optimizing the bias and gate layers as proposed
      by Gao et al. (2023), leveraging the LLaMA Touvron et al. (2023) 7B architecture in combination
871
      with our audiovisual encoder. In this subsequent stage, we performed backpropagation exclusively
872
      on the bias, gate, and audiovisual layers to enhance the model’s capacity to generate accurate and
873   contextually relevant audio descriptions.
874
875   Training was conducted over a span of 20 epochs, with model selection based on performance on
876
      the validation subset. Hyperparameters were meticulously tuned, including a learning rate of 1e−4 ,
      weight decay of 0.05, and a batch size of 256. We employed the AdamW optimizer to ensure effi-
877
      cient parameter updates. During the audiovisual alignment phase, the adapter and audiovisual layers
878
      were trained for 2 epochs, with the rest of the model parameters held constant, facilitating stable con-
879   vergence. Importantly, the LLaMA model’s core parameters remained frozen throughout the entire
880   training process, preserving the integrity of its pre-trained features while allowing focused adapta-
881   tion of the newly introduced layers. This careful balance between alignment and fine-tuning was
882   critical for achieving high-quality audio description generation without disrupting the foundational
883   capabilities of the LLaMA architecture.
