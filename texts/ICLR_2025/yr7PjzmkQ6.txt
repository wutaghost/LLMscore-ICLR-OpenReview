011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            This paper explores the key factors that influence the performance of models work-
014            ing with point clouds, across different tasks of varying geometric complexity. In
015            this work, we explore the trade-offs between flexibility and weight-sharing intro-
016
               duced by equivariant layers, assessing when equivariance boosts or detracts from
               performance. It is often argued that providing more information as input improves
017
               a model’s performance. However, if this additional information breaks certain
018
               properties, such as SE(3) equivariance, does it remain beneficial? We identify
019            the key aspects of equivariant and non-equivariant architectures that drive success
020            in different tasks by benchmarking them on segmentation, regression, and gen-
021            eration tasks across multiple datasets with increasing complexity. We observe a
022            positive impact of equivariance, which becomes more pronounced with increasing
023            task complexity, even when strict equivariance is not required.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   The inductive bias of weight sharing in convolutions, as introduced in LeCun et al. (2010) tradi-
029   tionally refers to applying the same convolution kernel (a linear transformation) across all neigh-
030   borhoods of an image. To extend this to transformations beyond translations, Cohen & Welling
031   (2016) introduced Group Equivariant CNN (G-CNNs), adding group equivariance properties to en-
032
      compass group actions and have weight-sharing across group convolution kernels. G-CNN layers
      are explicitly designed to maintain equivariance under group transformations, allowing the model
033
      to handle transformations naturally without needing to learn invariance to changes that preserve ob-
034
      ject identity. Following this work—in the spirit of ’convolution is all you need’ (Cohen et al., 2019),
035   several works emerged like (Ravanbakhsh et al., 2017; Worrall et al., 2017; Kondor & Trivedi, 2018;
036   Bekkers et al., 2018; Weiler et al., 2018; Cohen et al., 2019; Weiler & Cesa, 2019; Bekkers, 2019;
037   Sosnovik et al., 2019; Finzi et al., 2020). Advancements in geometric deep learning have demon-
038   strated the effectiveness of incorporating geometric structure as an inductive bias, which reduces
039   model complexity while enhancing generalization and performance (Bronstein et al., 2021). Thus,
040   incorporating group structure into neural networks has become a promising area of research.
041   However, there is a growing debate as to whether group structure is overly restrictive and if similar
042   advantages could be obtained by simply adding more data. To address this question, we thoroughly
043   investigate the impact of equivariant versus non-equivariant layers in various computational tasks.
044   We explore the balance between leveraging group structures that can provide powerful inductive
045   biases and maintaining the inherent model flexibility. We implemented a convolutional architec-
046   ture in which the linear layers are either classical or group convolution layers, whilst the overall
047   architecture remains otherwise identical for fair comparison. We evaluate this model on tasks of
048   varying complexity and the extent to which equivariance is desirable under the task description. Our
049
      goal is to understand how these design choices affect performance, generalization capabilities, and
      computational efficiency.
050
051   The contributions of this paper can be summarized as follows:
052
053          • Hypothesis formulation: We present a set of hypotheses that allow us to investigate the
               different aspects of equivariant neural networks and symmetry breaking.

063   2     BACKGROUND
064
065   In this section, we begin by explaining how equivariant neural networks differ from non-equivariant
066   ones, focusing on their architectural distinctions and the impact of weight-sharing—or the lack
067
      thereof—on data efficiency. We then delve into the specifics of 3D convolutions and separable
      group convolutions, which inform the design of the architecture presented later.
068
069
070   2.1   A RGUMENTS FOR AND AGAINST EQUIVARIANCE
071
      Equivariant neural networks differ from standard networks in four key aspects:
072
073         1. High-dimensional representation spaces: They typically represent data on higher-
074            dimensional feature maps, allowing for richer internal representations.
075
            2. Constraint layers: They employ constrained linear layers that are equivariant to specific
076
               transformations, which, while less flexible, preserve important structural properties of data.
077
078         3. Weight-sharing: These constraints not only prevent overfitting to nuisance variables (e.g.
079            arbitrary rotations) but also introduce a form of weight-sharing that could benefit learning.
080         4. Data efficiency: Equivariant architectures are data efficient, as they do not need to learn
081            patterns repeatedly for different transformations (poses) under which they may appear.
082
083   When comparing equivariant networks such as group convolutional CNNs (GCNNs) to standard
084
      CNNs, we observe that: (1) the higher dimensionality could possibly be offset in a standard CNN by
      increasing hidden dimensions; (2) the equivariance constraint may disadvantage equivariant methods
085
      in settings where such constraints are not crucial; however, (3) the induced weight-sharing and (4)
086
      improved data efficiency might compensate for these limitations by enabling more effective and
087   efficient learning. So, there are arguments against (1-2) and in favor of G-CNNs (3-4).
088
089   This paper explores the scenarios in which G-CNNs outperform standard CNNs. We hypothesize
090
      that equivariant networks offer advantages not only in tasks explicitly requiring equivariance but
      also in challenging tasks where strict equivariance is not necessary.
091

      Equivariant Layer Design G-CNNs are equivariant networks that operate over higher-
109   dimensional feature maps in which an additional axis (fiber) is used to keep track of feature informa-
110   tion relative to (sub-)groups of transformations. For example, regular roto-translation equivariant
111   group convolutions for image data add an extra axis for storing the response to rotated versions of a
112   convolution kernel (Cohen & Welling, 2016). In other words, a standard 2D CNN uses linear maps
                                  ′
113   L : RX×Y ×C → RX×Y ×C , where C and C ′ are the in- and output channel dimensions, X, Y the
                                                                                                         ′
114   spatial dimensions. A G-CNN internally uses linear layers L : RX×Y ×F ×C → RX×Y ×F ×C , in
115   which F is the number of grid points (e.g. discrete rotations) on which the feature maps are sampled.
116
117   Hidden Representations and Constraints While in principle increasing the dimensionality of
118   hidden representations can enhance capacity in both standard and equivariant networks, the key
119   distinction lies in the constraints imposed on the linear layers. Equivariant layers, despite potentially
120   having similar total dimensionality, are more constrained in their operations. This constraint, while
121   limiting flexibility, introduces beneficial properties such as weight-sharing and invariance to certain
122
      transformations.
                                                                              ′
123   For instance, an unconstrained layer L : RX×Y ×F C → RX×Y ×F C may be more expressive than
                                                                  ′
124   an equivariant layer L : RX×Y ×F ×C → RX×Y ×F ×C , even when the total dimensionality of
125   the feature maps are equivalent. However, the equivariant layer’s constraints can lead to improved
126   generalization and sample efficiency in certain tasks. Our study aims to identify the conditions under
127   which the benefits of equivariance outweigh the potential limitations in expressivity, particularly in
128   tasks where strict equivariance is not explicitly required.
129   Equivariant Networks and Symmetry-Breaking Relaxing equivariant constraints to improve
130   generalization has been well studied. Finzi et al. (2021) presents RPP, which allows for relaxing
131   architectural constraints of equivariance by introducing neural network priors that allow for approx-
132   imate equivariance. Prior works like Wang et al. (2022); van der Ouderaa et al. (2022); Pertigkio-
133   zoglou et al. (2024) focus on relaxing equivariant constraints and show the benefit in performance on
134   learning approximate or partial equivariance. To understand the effects of explicit symmetry break-
135   ing while learning strict equivariance in tasks with different geometric complexity, our approach
136
      consists of breaking a) internal symmetry and b) external symmetry. See A.3 for more details.
137
      Data Efficiency and Large-Scale Learning The data efficiency provided by equivariant architec-
138
      tures is a significant advantage, particularly in scenarios with limited data. By leveraging symmetries
139
      in the data, these networks can learn patterns once and recognize them across various transforma-
140   tions, reducing the amount of data required for effective learning. However, as datasets grow larger,
141   a critical question emerges: Does the benefit of equivariance diminish when data is abundant? We
142   hypothesize that equivariant methods maintain their relevance even in large-scale learning scenarios
143   for several reasons:
144
145         1. Structured Representation Learning: The constraints imposed by equivariance guide the
146
               network to learn more structured and potentially more meaningful representations, which
               may generalize better regardless of dataset size.
147
148         2. Computational Efficiency: Equivariant networks can potentially learn from large datasets
149            more efficiently, requiring fewer parameters and computations to achieve similar or better
150
               performance.
151         3. Inductive Bias: The equivariance constraint serves as a strong inductive bias, which may
152            lead to better generalization even when data is plentiful, by focusing the model on learning
153
               relevant features rather than spurious correlations.
154   Our study aims to (empirically) investigate these hypotheses (precise formulation in Section 3.1),
155   comparing the performance and learning dynamics of equivariant networks against standard archi-
156   tectures across various dataset sizes and task complexities. By doing so, we seek to delineate the
157   conditions under which equivariance provides substantial benefits, even in the era of big data.

473
474   Hypothesis 1 is accepted: Equivariant methods are more data-efficient then their non-equivariant
475   counter part. The hypothesis is tested on Shapenet segmentation for various dataset size regimes as
476
      shown in Fig. 3 and Tab. 6 in the Appendix.
477   Hypothesis 2 is accepted: The advantage of equivariant models gets more pronounced with task
478   complexity. Although, on all experiments the equivariant models have an edge over non-equivariant
479   models, this improvement is more pronounced in complex and equivariant tasks, such as QM9 re-
480   gression and generation (Tab. 2), Shapenet generation (Tab. 1) and motion prediction (Tab. 3), in
481   which we note that both Shapenet generation and CMU motion prediction do not require equivari-
482
      ance, but are complex geometric tasks.
483   Hypothesis 3 is rejected: Increasing model capacity by increasing the number of channels does not
484   lead to a performance gain as seen by the group convolutional methods. The performance of low
485   vs high capacity (compared gray vs regular numbers in the tables) is often negligible. The results
      suggest that all of the models are already maxed-out in net capacity to reach optimal performance.

514   Hypothesis 4 is accepted: For equivariant tasks, having less constraints on the model is beneficial.
515   See, e.g., Tab. 1, comparing models 1-4 with models 16-19, the latter being less constrained, and
516   performing significantly better. Consider also the QM9 experiments (Tab. 2) which is an equivariant
517   task. All models 1,2 and 5-12 are equivariant and universal approximators and should be able to
518   solve the problem. Models 1-2 however use constrained kernels (symmetric, distance-based) while
519   the group convolutional models are not. The latter largely outperforms the former.
520   Hypothesis 5 is accepted: Providing explicit geometric information can improve performance, even
521   when breaking symmetry. This is, e.g., observed in the tables by comparing the group convolutional
522   models that take either coordinates as scalars (breaks equivariance) or as vectors (maintains equvari-
523   ance) against those models that do not take any coordinate information as input.

525   6       D ISCUSSION AND C ONCLUSION
526
527   Discussion Beyond hypothesis insights, our basic convolutional architecture (Rapidash) performs
528   exceptionally well on all considered tasks, often reaching state-of-the-art (SotA) performance, s.a.
529   on ShapeNet/QM9 generation and CMU motion prediction. Surprisingly, even non-equivariant
530   methods perform well on equivariant tasks, though not at SotA level and without equivariant gen-
531   eralization guarantees. These results support the ”convolution is all you need” idea (Cohen et al.,
532   2019; Bekkers et al., 2024), contrasting the popular ”attention-is-all-you-need” claim (Vaswani,
533   2017). The generalizability of the presented hypotheses remains to be tested outside of class of con-
534   volutional architectures, to which Rapidash belongs. Conclusion In this work, we conducted a
535
      comprehensive evaluation of equivariant and non-equivariant models across various tasks of varying
      geometric complexities. In the debate of equivariant vs non-equivariant models, we provide strong
536
      evidence in favor of introducing structure over mere scaling. Specifically, we find that equivariant
537
      models (1) are more data efficient, (2) increasingly outpace non-equivariant models when increasing
538   task geometric complexity, and (3) see performance benefits from targeted symmetry breaking.

759   A.1    M ATHEMATICAL P REREQUISITES AND N OTATIONS
760   Groups. A group is an algebraic structure defined by a set G and a binary operator · : G × G → G,
761   known as the group product. This structure (G, ·) must satisfy four axioms: (1) closure, where
762   ∀h,g∈G : h · g ∈ G; (2) the existence of an identity element e ∈ G such that ∀g∈G , e · g = g · e = g,
763   (3) the existence of an inverse element, i.e. ∀g∈G there exists a g −1 ∈ G such that g −1 · g = e; and
764   (4) associativity, where ∀g,h,p∈G : (g · h) · p=g · (h · p). Going forward, group product between two
765   elements will be denoted as g, g ′ ∈ G by juxtaposition, i.e., as g g ′ .
766   For Special Euclidean group SE(n), the group product between two roto-translations g=(x, R)
767   and g ′ =(x′ , R′ ) is given by (x, R) (x′ , R′ )=(Rx′ + x, R R′ ), and its identity element is given by
768   e=(0, I).
769
      Homogeneous Spaces. A group can act on spaces other than itself via a group action T : G × X →
770
      X, where X is the space on which G acts. For simplicity, the action of g ∈ G on x ∈ X is
771
      denoted as g x. Such a transformation is called a group action if it is homomorphic to G and its
772   group product. That is, it follows the group structure: (g g ′ ) x=g (g ′ x) ∀g, g ′ ∈ G, x ∈ X, and
773   e x=x. For example, consider the space of 3D positions X = R3 , e.g., atomic coordinates, acted
774   upon by the group G=SE(3). A position p ∈ R3 is roto-translated by the action of an element
775   (x, R) ∈ SE(3) as (x, R) p=R p + x.
776
      A group action is termed transitive if every element x ∈ X can be reached from an arbitrary origin
777
      x0 ∈ X through the action of some g ∈ G, i.e., x=gx0 . A space X equipped with a transitive action
778   of G is called a homogeneous space of G. Finally, the orbit G x := {g x | g ∈ G} of an element
779   x under the action of a group G represents the set of all possible transformations of x by G. For
780   homogeneous spaces, X=G x0 for any arbitrary origin x0 ∈ X.
781
      Quotient spaces. The aforementioned space of 3D positions X=R3 serves as a homogeneous space
782
      of G = SE(3), as every element p can be reached by a roto-translation from 0, i.e., for every p there
783
      exists a (x, R) such that p=(x, R) 0=R 0 + x=x. Note that there are several elements in SE(3)
784   that transport the origin 0 to p, as any action with a translation vector x=p suffices regardless of
785   the rotation R. This is because any rotation R′ ∈ SO(3) leaves the origin unaltered.
786
787
      We denote the set of all elements in G that leave an origin x0 ∈ X unaltered the stabilizer subgroup
      StabG (x0 ). In subsequent analyses, the symbol H is used to denote the stabilizer subgroup of a
788
      chosen origin x0 in a homogeneous space, i.e., H=StabG (x0 ). We further denote the left coset of
789
      H in G as g H := {g h | h ∈ H}. In the example of positions p ∈ X=R3 we concluded that we
790   can associate a point p with many group elements g ∈ SE(3) that satisfy p=g 0. In general, letting
791   gx be any group element s.t. x=gx x0 , then any group element in the left set gx H is also identified
792   with the point p. Hence, any x ∈ X can be identified with a left coset gx H and vice versa.
793
      Left cosets g H then establish an equivalence relation ∼ among transformations in G. We say that
794
      two elements g, g ′ ∈ G are equivalent, i.e., g ∼ g ′ , if and only if g x0 =g ′ x0 . That is, if they belong
795
      to the same coset g H. The space of left cosets is commonly referred to as the quotient space G/H.
796
797   We consider feature maps f : X → RC as multi-channel signals over homogeneous spaces X. Here,
798   we treat point clouds as sparse feature maps, e.g., sampled only at atomic positions. In the general
799
      continuous setting, we denote the space of feature maps over X with X . Such feature maps undergo
      group transformations through regular group representations ρX (g) : X → X parameterized by g,
800
      and which transform functions f ∈ X via [ρX (g)f ](x)=f (g −1 x) .

853   A.3     I NTERNAL VS E XTERNAL S YMMETRY B REAKING
854
855   In this section, we provide an analysis of symmetry breaking in equivariant neural networks, distin-
856
      guishing between what we consider to be two fundamentally different types: external and internal
      symmetry breaking.
857
858
859
      A.3.1       E XTERNAL S YMMETRY B REAKING
860
861   External symmetry breaking occurs when an inherently equivariant architecture loses its equivari-
862   ance properties due to the way inputs are provided to the network. Consider a linear layer L de-
863   signed to be G-equivariant for some group G (e.g., SE(3)). Let v be a vector that transforms under
      the group action, and define xg = g · v as its transformed version in global coordinates. When these


                                                                 16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      coordinates are provided as scalar triplets, they are processed independently with no relation to the
865   group action, thus:
866
                                       x ̸= xg      =⇒      L(xg ) ̸= L(x)                             (10)
867
868   This means the network processes transformed inputs differently, breaking equivariance. In contrast,
869
      when inputs are properly specified as vectors that transform under the group action, we maintain
      equivariance:
870
871
                                        L(g · v) = g · L(v) ∀g ∈ G                                   (11)
872   Moreover, for truly invariant features (such as one-hot encodings of atom types in QM9), the group
873   action is trivial:
874                                   g·x=x        =⇒      L(g · x) = L(x)                           (12)
875
      which guarantees invariance of the entire network to group transformations.
876
877
878
879   A.3.2    I NTERNAL S YMMETRY B REAKING
880
      Internal symmetry breaking then refers to the deliberate relaxation of equivariance constraints within
881
      the layers themselves. Recent works have explored various approaches to this, including:
882
883           • Basis decomposition methods that mix equivariant and non-equivariant components
884
              • Learnable deviation from strict equivariance through regularization
885
886           • Progressive relaxation of equivariance constraints during training
887
888   In some cases, an internally symmetry-broken layer Lbroken can be expressed as:
889                                       Lbroken = Lequiv + αLnon-equiv                               (13)
890
891   where α controls the degree of symmetry breaking, some approaches (e.g., (Wang et al., 2022))
892   implement schemes where α is annealed during training to gradually enforce stricter equivariance.
893
894
895   A.3.3    C OMBINING I NTERNAL AND E XTERNAL B REAKING
896
897   In practice, both types of symmetry breaking often occur simultaneously and can interact. For ex-
898   ample, a network may use non-stationary filters (internal breaking) while also accepting coordinate
899   inputs in a global reference frame (external breaking). The total degree of symmetry breaking then
900   depends on both mechanisms. Theoretical quantitative bounds on models with task-specific sym-
901
      metries show that aligning data symmetry with architecture leads to the best model, although having
      partial or approximate equivariance can improve generalization (Petrache & Trivedi, 2023).
902
903
904
905   A.3.4    R ELATIONSHIP TO O UR W ORK
906
907   While our study primarily focuses on external symmetry breaking (cf. the red exclamation marks in
908
      our tables), we note that the transition between translation-equivariant layers (Table 1, rows 15-18)
      and roto-translation equivariant layers (rows 1-4) could be viewed as a form of internal symmetry
909
      breaking. However, we distinguish our approach from explicit internal symmetry-breaking methods
910
      as we do not implement continuous relaxation of equivariance constraints within layers, but rather
911   compare discrete architectural choices with different symmetry properties.
912
913   The experiments demonstrate that carefully controlled symmetry breaking, whether internal or ex-
914
      ternal, can significantly improve model performance when the underlying data exhibits only ap-
      proximate symmetries. This aligns with recent findings in the field showing the benefits of relaxed
915
      equivariance constraints (van der Ouderaa et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2023; Pertigkiozoglou et al.,
916
      2024).
