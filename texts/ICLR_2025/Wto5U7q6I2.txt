011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Understanding fine-grained temporal dynamics is crucial for video understanding.
014            Yet, popular video benchmarks, such as MSRVTT and TGIF, often fail to effectively
015            evaluate AI models’ temporal reasoning abilities due to the lack of fine-grained
016            temporal annotations. As a result, text-based models, leveraging strong language
017            priors, often perform comparably to video models, and image-trained models have
018            been reported to outperform their video-trained counterparts on MSRVTT and
019            TGIF. This paper introduces a new TemporalBench benchmark for fine-grained
020
               temporal event understanding in videos. TemporalBench, sourced from a diverse
               video datasets, consists of ∼10K pairs of video description questions, derived
021
               from ∼2K high-quality human-annotated video captions. Uniquely, our benchmark
022
               provides fine-grained temporal annotations to evaluate models’ temporal reasoning
023            abilities. Our results show that state-of-the-art models like GPT-4o achieve only
024            38.0% multiple binary QA accuracy on TemporalBench, demonstrating a signif-
025            icant human-AI gap in temporal understanding. We hope that TemporalBench
026            is instrumental to fostering research on improving models’ temporal reasoning
027            capabilities.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   The ability to understand and reason about events in videos is a crucial aspect of artificial intelligence,
033   with applications ranging from activity recognition and long-term action anticipation to perception
034   for autonomous driving and robotics. Recently, there has been an emergence of highly capable
035   multimodal generative models, including proprietary ones such as GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024) and
036   Gemini (Gemini Team, 2024) as well as open-sources ones (Liu et al., 2023a; Zhu et al., 2024b;
037   Bai et al., 2023), that have demonstrated impressive results on existing video benchmarks (Xu et al.,
038   2016; Chen & Dolan, 2011; Yu et al., 2019a; Mangalam et al., 2024). However, these benchmarks
039
      often do not truly evaluate the abilities of the aforementioned models to understand video content due
      to their generally coarse-grained annotations.
040
041   The lack of fine-grained temporal details in the annotations often leads to existing video understanding
042   benchmarks suffering from a strong language prior bias. This is similar to observations in visual
043   question answering with images (Antol et al., 2015). For example, prior works (Tan et al., 2024; Li
044   et al., 2023a) show that language models such as Flan-T5 (Chung et al., 2024) and Llama-2/3 (Touvron
045
      et al., 2023) perform comparably to video models on EgoSchema (Mangalam et al., 2024) and Seed-
      Bench (Li et al., 2023a) without using any information from videos. Furthermore, the lack of
046
      fine-grained temporal details often results in the single frame bias of current video understanding
047
      benchmarks (Lei et al., 2023). These benchmarks are often biased toward spatial reasoning, where
048   static information from a single frame suffices to achieve high performance. They often fail to test a
049   model’s ability to reason about temporal sequences, leading to inflated evaluations of AI models that
050   are not genuinely capable of understanding temporal events. Specifically, vision-language models
051   (VLMs) (Liu et al., 2024a;b) that are trained on image-level datasets, including FreeVA (Wu, 2024),
052   IG-VLM (Kim et al., 2024) and M 3 (Cai et al., 2024b), often outperform their video counterparts on
053   popular video question answering benchmarks such as MSRVTT (Xu et al., 2016), MSVD (Xu et al.,
      2017), and TGIF (Jang et al., 2017).

      To address this limitation, we propose TemporalBench (Figure 1), a new video understanding
      benchmark that evaluates multimodal video models on understanding fine-grained activities, and
076
      consists of ∼10k question and answer pairs curated from ∼2k high-quality human-annotated captions
077
      with rich activity details. Unlike static image-based tasks, video understanding requires models to
078   reason effectively about both spatial and temporal information. The temporal dynamics inherent
079   in videos introduce significant complexity, as actions and events often unfold over time and cannot
080   be captured in a single frame. With this in mind, we designed our benchmark to focus on areas
081   where current models often struggle, emphasizing annotations related to long-range dependencies,
082   fine-grained visual observations, and event progression.
083
      As shown in Figure 2, we first collect video clips from existing video grounding benchmarks that span
084   diverse domains, including procedural videos (Tang et al., 2019), human activities (Krishna et al.,
085   2017; Gao et al., 2017) and ego-centric videos (Grauman et al., 2024). The positive captions include
086   rich and fine-grained details about actions and activities, which are annotated by highly qualified
087   Amazon Mechanical Turk (AMT) workers and authors of this paper. Then, we generate the negative
088   captions with respect to the actions using powerful Large Language Models (LLMs) and filter them
089   according to our defined rules. Our resulting TemporalBench contains 10K video descriptions and
090   matching questions of high quality. Furthermore, the rich temporal context of annotations in our
091   diverse corpus creates a solid foundation for the development of additional benchmarks in related
092
      tasks such as spatio-temporal localization and causal inference. We hope that our benchmark can
      pave the road for further development of multimodal video models capable of fine-grained video
093
      understanding and reasoning.

143   Large Multimodal Models. Large Language Models (LLMs) like ChatGPT (OpenAI, 2023b), GPT-
144   4 (OpenAI, 2023c), and Llama (Touvron et al., 2023) have demonstrated impressive reasoning and
145   generalization capabilities for text. The introduction of models that integrate visual data has brought
146   about a significant shift in the landscape of LLMs, such as GPT-4V(ision)(OpenAI, 2023a). Building
147
      upon open-source LLMs (Touvron et al., 2023; Chiang et al., 2023), a wide range of multimodal
      models has achieved remarkable progress, led by pioneering models such as LLaVA (Liu et al., 2023a;
148
      2024a) and MiniGPT-4 (Zhu et al., 2024b), which combine LLMs’ capabilities with a CLIP (Radford
149
      et al., 2021) based image encoder. Recently, a growing number of LMMs have been developed to
150   handle a wider range of tasks and modalities, such as region-level LMMs (Cai et al., 2024a; Zhang
151   et al., 2023c; Chen et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023b), 3D LMMs (Hong et al., 2023),
152   and video LMMs (Lin et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023a; 2024b).
153
      Multimodal Understanding Benchmarks. The recent significant advancements have resulted in
154
      more versatile multimodal models, making it imperative to thoroughly and extensively evaluate their
155
      visual understanding and reasoning abilities. Conventional multimodal benchmarks like VQA (Antol
156   et al., 2015), GQA (Hudson & Manning, 2019) and VizWiz (Gurari et al., 2018) have been revitalized
157   and used for evaluating the general visual question answering performance for LMMs. Some
158   other question answering benchmarks like TextVQA (Singh et al., 2019), DocVQA (Mathew et al.,
159   2021) and InfoVQA (Mathew et al., 2022) have also been employed to validate the text-oriented
160   understanding. Recent studies have introduced a variety of new benchmarks, such as SEED-Bench (Li
161   et al., 2023a), MMBench (Liu et al., 2023b) and MM-Vet (Yu et al., 2024b) for evaluating the models’
      integrated problem-solving capabilities, and MMMU (Yue et al., 2024a) and MathVista (Lu et al.,

      Video Understanding Benchmarks. Recently, an increasing amount of research is transitioning
      its focus from the image to the video domain. Videos differ from images in that they possess more
167
      complex content with temporal dynamics. This unique aspect calls for a different set of metrics and
168
      benchmarks. Many efforts have leveraged existing video question answering benchmarks (Xu et al.,
169   2017; Yu et al., 2019b; Xiao et al., 2021) built on top of video-text datasets (Chen & Dolan, 2011;
170   Xu et al., 2016; Zhang et al., 2019). More recently, several LMM-oriented benchmarks have been
171   proposed for different aspects such as long-form egocentric understanding with EgoSchema (Man-
172   galam et al., 2024), and temporal understanding and ordering like Tempcompass (Liu et al., 2024c).
173   MV-Bench (Li et al., 2024b) compiles existing video annotations from different disciplines into a
174   new benchmark, while Video-MME (Fu et al., 2024) and MMWorld (He et al., 2024b) claim to
175   support a comprehensive evaluation of video understanding and world modeling, respectively. Our
176   TemporalBench serves the common goal of evaluating models for video understanding but differs
177
      in several aspects. On the one hand, we exhaustively curate videos from different domains and ask
      human annotators to annotate the visual contents with as much detail as possible. On the other hand,
178
      we particularly focus on temporal dynamics such as human actions and human-object interactions that
179
      exist exclusively in videos and which are crucial for video understanding, reasoning and forecasting.
180   While the ShareGPT4Video dataset (Chen et al., 2024) also contains long captions, theirs differ from
181   ours by being entirely generated by GPT-4o instead of annotated by humans.

      3   TemporalBench
185   Compared to static images, videos inherently contain significantly more fine-grained temporal
186   information, as they capture the unfolding of actions and events over time. Existing multimodal video
187   understanding benchmarks (Xu et al., 2016) mostly evaluate models’ coarse-level understanding of
188   videos. An example from the recent Seed-Bench dataset is the question, “What action is happening
189   in the video?” with the answer, “moving something up.” However, such types of coarse-level video
190   questions have been demonstrated to be easily solved with just a single frame (Wu, 2024) or even by
191   a text-only LLM (Tan et al., 2024; Mangalam et al., 2024).
192   Such phenomena arises due to a fundamental limitation in the text descriptions in those benchmarks.
193   As a result of their coarseness, the positive and negative options for video question-answering can
194   usually be distinguished without understanding the temporal dynamics, such as the models only
195   needing to choose between “The man is cooking” and “The man is exercising”.
196
      To address this limitation, we carefully design a human annotation pipeline to curate highly detailed
197   descriptions about the activities in the videos. Given the detailed video clip descriptions, such as
198   A right hand holds a piece of peeled ginger while a knife is held in the left and makes 3 slices off
199   the ginger., the negative captions can be curated to truly reflect whether a model understands the
200   temporal dynamics, such as changing “three slices” into “two slices”. In a nutshell, such highly
201   detailed temporal annotations can be used to carefully examine whether a multimodel video model
202   truly understands the temporal state transition in videos.
203   Our benchmark enriches several fundamental video understanding tasks due to its detailed captions:
204
205
      • Fine-grained video question answering. Given a detailed positive caption, multimodal video
206     models need to distinguish it from the associated negative where a slight modification is made to
207     temporal descriptions, e.g., “push the eyeglasses up” versus “pull the eyeglasses down”, or “cut 3
208     slices off” versus “cut 2 slices off”.
209
210
      • Fine-grained video captioning. Our detailed video captions can naturally enrich the video
        captioning task, different from current video captioning tasks such as MSRVTT (Xu et al., 2016)
211
        which focus on coarse-level descriptions.
212
213   • Long video understanding and fine-grained activity inspection. Since the video clips are
214     extracted from a long source video, the respective video clip descriptions can be concatenated to
215     form a longer video description which can be pivoted to the long video understanding task, where
        we find that all current multimodal video models suffer.

216
      • Dense video-text matching and retrieval. Our detailed video captions can be naturally employed
217     to evaluate video-language embedding models such as VideoCLIP (Xu et al., 2021). Given a
218     positive caption and several negative captions, we can evaluate whether CLIP (Radford et al., 2021)
219     based video embedding models can distinguish the subtle differences in captions. In addition, given
220     a set of positive video-text pairs, video retrieval performance can be evaluated, similar to image
221     retrieval on COCO (Lin et al., 2014) and Flickr30K (Young et al., 2014).
222   • Video grounding from detailed text descriptions. Since the video clips are cropped from the
223     source video, with the documented starting and ending time, our benchmark can serve as a fine-
224     grained moment localizing benchmark from text descriptions. This is different from existing
225     video grounding datasets such as Charades-STA (Gao et al., 2017), COIN (Tang et al., 2019),
226     Ego4D (Grauman et al., 2024) where the text descriptions are usually very short, possibly resulting
227     in low temporal localization performance due to the vague and coarse descriptions.
228   • Text-to-Video (T2V) generation with detailed prompts. Given our highly detailed description, a
229     T2V generation model can be evaluated by verifying if the generated videos reflect the fine-grained
230     action details.

236   We collect video clips from a wide range of sources across diverse domains, where the majority comes

255   3.2     V IDEO C APTION A NNOTATION P ROCESS
256
      Positive Captions Annotation. We employ a two-stage human labeling process for curating video
257
      captions with fine-grained activity descriptions, where the qualified Amazon Mechanical Turk (AMT)
258
      workers are first instructed to give a detailed video caption. Then, the authors of this work refine
259   the caption by correcting the mistakes and adding missing details w.r.t. the actions. The overall
260   pipeline is shown in Figure 2. All video clips are annotated following the same pipeline except for
261   Finegym (Shao et al., 2020) as it has already provided accurate and detailed action descriptions for
262   professional gymnasium videos. Consequently, we reuse its annotations.
263
      We first use 3 probing video captioning questions with 2 in-context examples as the onboarding task
264
      for AMT master workers. We manually inspect the soundness and amount of temporal details of the
265   AMT worker captions to select high quality AMT video captioning workers. During the annotation
266   process by AMT workers, we also continue to remove the unqualified workers based on the ratio of
267   the captions that authors in this paper refined. In this way, we ensure that the AMT provides a high
268   quality initial point for positive captions.

      3.3    A P ITFALL IN M ULTI - CHOICE Q UESTION A NSWERING
294
295   A conventional approach to evaluate large multimodal models is using the multi-choice question-
296   answering format, which is adopted by the majority of current benchmarks including MMMU (Yue
297   et al., 2024a), MathVista (Lu et al., 2024), EgoSchema (Mangalam et al., 2024) etc. However,
298   indicated by recent studies by (Cai et al., 2024b) and (Yue et al., 2024b), a pure LLM can achieve
299   comparable or even stronger performance on those benchmarks without looking at the visual content
300   at all. Recent studies argue that (1) some questions are not designed well so that the question can be
301
      answered without looking at the visual content, or (2) the model memorizes the QA pairs, i.e., data
      contamination occurs.
302
303   While developing our benchmark, we notice another previously ignored but critical pitfall for multi-
304   choice QA. Specifically, if every negative answer choice is generated by changing a small part of the
305   correct answer, the LLM can detect those changes to find a “centralized” description and use that cue
306   for its prediction. To study this, given a positive caption C and its associated negative caption N (C),
307
      we intentionally derive a few negatives from N1 (C) (instead of for C), resulting in N1 (N1 (C)) and
      N2 (N1 (C)), resulting in [C, N1 (C), N1 (N1 (C)), N2 (N1 (C))] as options, so that N1 (C) becomes
308
      the “centralized” description (see Fig. 4). Surprisingly, we find that 62% of text-only GPT-4o’s
309
      predictions correspond to N (C), while only 18% of its predictions correspond to C. Our findings
310   also align with human behavior analysis from psychology (Furman & Wang, 2008), where humans
311   can achieve better than random chance performance on multi-choice QAs using similar cues.
312
313
      Motivated by this findings, we propose to decompose a single multi-choice QA into multiple binary
      QAs. In this case, we eliminate the “centralized option” due to the fact that there are only two options
314
      to choose from. As a result, given M negatives, the multiple binary QAs will query a model M times,
315
      where the random chance performance changes from M1+1 to ( 12 )M . Given that ( 21 )M > M1+1 for
316
      every M > 2, multiple binary QA is a more difficult task than multi-choice QA.

465   4.5   L ONG V IDEO U NDERSTANDING
466
467   Since our benchmark is annotated at the video clip level, we can easily extend it to long video
468   understanding by concatenating the captions of different video clips within the same original video.
469   In our study, we choose video datasets whose original length is both short (AcitivityNet and Charades,
470   average length < 3 minutes) and long (COIN and FineGym, > 20 minutes). We randomly sample
471
      video clips within the same original video, and then crop a new video segment whose starting time
      corresponds to that of the earliest sampled video clip and whose ending time corresponds to that
472
      of the latest sampled video clip. We then concatenate all the sampled video captions together to
473
      form a single long detailed description corresponding to the new video segment. Given this positive
474   caption, we generate negative captions for it by replacing the positive caption of one of the sampled
475   video clips with its negatives. The model is then tasked to choose the correct long caption out of
476   multiple choices. We set the number of negative options to be ∼4, resulting in a similar random
477   chance performance as in Sec 4.3. In this way, we investigate whether multimodal video models can
478   understand and distinguish fine details in a long video.
479
      We show in Table 7 (supplemental), that all multimodal video models show a significant performance
480   drop for this task compared to short video understanding. This is also reflected in all models
481   performing better on relatively shorter videos (ActivityNet and Charades) compared to longer videos
482   (COIN and FineGym). These results indicate that finding the subtle temporal dynamic differences
483   in a long video is indeed an extremely difficult task. It is similar in nature to the needle-in-the-sea
484   task (Kamradt, 2023) in NLP except in the temporal domain. We hope that TemporalBench for
485   long video understanding can serve as a very challenging task for future video understanding model
      development.

522   6     C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
523
524   We propose TemporalBench, a novel video understanding benchmark, to evaluate the fine-grained
525   temporal understanding abilities of multimodal video models. The video captions in our benchmark
526   are significantly denser than existing datasets such as MSRVTT and TGIF, offering detailed tem-
527   poral annotations. TemporalBench also provides a more challenging set of tasks that push current
528   multimodal models beyond coarse-level understanding. The empirical results reveal a substantial
529
      gap between human performance and current state-of-the-art models. We hope that this benchmark
      fosters further research in developing models with enhanced temporal reasoning capabilities. Our
530
      benchmark could also be easily utilized for other fundamental video tasks such as spatio-temporal
531
      localization and text-to-video generation with fine-grained prompts.
532
533   Limitations. One cannot fully analyze the behavior of proprietary models included in this paper due
534   to the lack of access to these models, which are GPT-4o, Gemini-1.5-Pro and Claude 3.5 Sonnet.
