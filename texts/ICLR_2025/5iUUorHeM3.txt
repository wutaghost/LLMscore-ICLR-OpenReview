009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The role of Large Language Models (LLMs) has not been extensively explored in
012            analog circuit design, which could benefit from a reasoning-based approach that
013            transcends traditional optimization techniques. In particular, despite their growing
014            relevance, there are no benchmarks to assess LLMs’ reasoning capability about
015            circuits. Therefore, we created the CIRCUIT dataset consisting of 510 question-
016            answer pairs spanning various levels of analog-circuit-related subjects. The best-
017            performing model on our dataset, GPT-4o, achieves 48.04% accuracy when eval-
018
               uated on the final numerical answer. To evaluate the robustness of LLMs on our
               dataset, we introduced a unique feature that enables unit-test-like evaluation by
019
               grouping questions into unit tests. In this case, GPT-4o can only pass 27.45%
020
               of the unit tests, highlighting that the most advanced LLMs still struggle with
021            understanding circuits, which requires multi-level reasoning, particularly when
022            involving circuit topologies. This circuit-specific benchmark highlights LLMs’
023            limitations, offering valuable insights for advancing their application in analog
024            integrated circuit design.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   The application of Large Language Models (LLMs) in analog integrated circuit design could pio-
030   neer a new era of AI applications in domains traditionally dominated by human expertise. Analog
031   semiconductor chips are the core building blocks in sensing and communication systems. Contrary
032   to digital chip development, where computer-aided design automation has been widely adopted for
033
      a few decades, analog design, often perceived more as a craftsmanship than a well-established engi-
      neering procedure, relies heavily on the designer’s experience and intuition to navigate in the trade
034
      space of efficiency, noise, linearity, and speed to meet certain specifications. This domain’s depth,
035
      requiring a blend of acumen and creativity, underscores the high barriers to entry and the exten-
036   sive training required to master its intricacies, which exacerbated the critical labor shortfall of the
037   semiconductor industry in this decade (Ravi, 2023).
038
039
      The advent of AI-assisted design automation in analog circuit design holds considerable promise to
      tackle the aforementioned challenge. It offers the potential to significantly streamline design cycles,
040
      enabling engineers to focus more on strategic, high-level design considerations and the exploration
041
      of novel ideas and applications. Traditional analog design automation (Wang et al., 2018; Settaluri
042   et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2022; Xue et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2019) has relied on numerical-based opti-
043   mization and machine learning techniques to train surrogate models for designing circuits with fixed
044   topologies and semiconductor processes, resulting in reduced generalization capabilities and often
045   suffering from limited interpretability. A shift towards a reasoning and knowledge-based approach,
046   facilitated by LLMs that transcend traditional optimization techniques, could leverage circuit do-
047   main expertise to innovate and refine the design of diverse analog circuits.
048   A natural starting point towards this ambitious goal is to evaluate existing LLMs’ proficiency in
049   executing various analog circuit design tasks. To that end, we introduce the CIRCUIT (Circuit
050   Interpretation and Reasoning Capabilities) dataset , which focuses on simple topology understanding
051   – a precursor to performing any complex design task. The dataset is designed to be scalable, enabling
052   a seamless incorporation of more advanced analog circuit design tasks in future iterations. We
053   evaluate leading LLMs’ performance on the dataset with a unique, template-based evaluation metric.
      Furthermore, we conduct automatic and human evaluation and error analysis of the LLM responses.

075   2       R ELATED W ORK
076
077
078   Task-specific evaluation plays a crucial role in advancing research in LLM applications by providing
079   precise insights into model capabilities and limitations within defined contexts. The scalability of
080   general-purpose models has demonstrated enhanced task performance in various domains, includ-
081   ing language (Brown et al., 2020), mathematics (Aojun Zhou, 2023; Mao et al., 2024), and code
082   generation (Chen et al., 2021)1 .
083   In the realm of digital circuit design, noteworthy progress has been made in harnessing LLMs for
084   tasks such as generating Verilog Code, as explored by Mingjie Liu (2023). Moreover, Cadence’s
085   JedAI 2 platform exemplifies the first application of LLM technology in chip design, illustrating the
086   feasibility of integrating LLMs into digital design workflows.
087
      In the realm of analog design, LLMs have already been integrated into frameworks that automate
088
      aspects of the design process (Chang et al., 2024; Lai et al., 2024). While these works focus on
089   leveraging LLMs directly for circuit design, an essential precursor is to evaluate the knowledge
090   and reasoning capabilities of LLMs on fundamental analog circuit knowledge. Without a deep
091   understanding of their foundational capabilities, the effectiveness and versatility of LLMs in real-
092   world circuit design may be limited. To address this gap, we introduce the CIRCUIT dataset, which
093   serves as a critical first step in the analog design pipeline.
094
      When reviewing existing datasets for other domains, we notice that evaluation proves difficult on
095   complex tasks. Coding tasks utilize unit testing with automatic evaluation, while other fields neces-
096   sitate human evaluation. LLMs have also been used as evaluating agents. (Mao et al., 2024; Lin
097   et al., 2021) While LLMs can evaluate large volumes of data, do not suffer from fatigue, and are
098   cheaper to utilize, our initial experiments showed that they struggle with understanding and inter-
099   preting complex reasoning about analog circuits. Inspired by unit testing, we introduce a simple
100   dataset design and evaluation metric combination that shows promise for the assessment of LLMs
101   across various fields and tasks. This framework is inherently scalable, adaptable to more complex
102   analog design tasks, and transferable to other reasoning domains.

126   3     CIRCUIT DATASET
127
128   3.1   DATASET CURATION
129
130   The CIRCUIT dataset comprises circuit problems, many of which include associated diagrams. The
131   dataset was made using templates – problems adapted from sources listed in Appendix A modified to
132   fit different numerical setups and ensure each only asks for a single numerical answer. Figure 2 is an
133   example of a dataset question. The diagram and the template are adapted so that the numerical setup
134
      can accommodate different values and ensure different answers to the template question. Therefore,
      we were able to create multiple numerical setups for each template used for the creation of the
135
      dataset. Each template question together with its numerical setups served as a single unit test in the
136
      dataset. This design enables a more nuanced evaluation of the models’ understanding of different
137   circuit topologies and provides quantifiable insights into how data homogeneity influences model
138   performance.
139
140
      Initial experiments indicated that LLMs found it challenging to interpret circuit diagrams, particu-
      larly the direction and orientation of circuit components. To aid in understanding circuit topologies,
141
      we incorporated netlists into the prompts. Netlist syntax was slightly modified to better suit our
142
      needs, detailed in Appendix B. This modification and the inclusion of a syntax explanation in the
143   prompts were aimed at enhancing LLMs’ performance on our dataset.
144
145   Figure 2 illustrates an example of a data point consisting of a template question along with its
146
      associated diagram, netlist, and a numerical setup. In this scenario, the LLM is tasked with applying
      Ohm’s law (V = IR) to calculate the current. The specific setup prompts for a calculation of I =
147   V      5V
148    R = 100Ω = 0.05A, testing the LLM’s understanding of this simple circuit topology. Our dataset
      extends this approach by using various values for V and R for numerical setups, thus methodically
149   exploring the output curve I in a unit-test-like fashion. That is, to test the understanding of this
150   topology, we create multiple data points with different numerical setups, each maintaining the same
151   structure, template question, diagram, and netlist but altering V and R values in the numerical setup
152   to produce data points with different correct answers. Providing correct answers to each numerical
153   setup strongly suggests an understanding of the topology, without requiring a detailed examination
154   of the solution methodology, much like how unit tests in programming verify that a function is
155   correctly implemented.

220   5     E XPERIMENTS
221
222   5.1     M ODELS
223
224   We evaluated gpt-4-turbo (OpenAI, 2024), gpt-4o (OpenAI, 2024) and gemini-1.5-pro
225
      (Team et al., 2024) on our dataset, setting the maximum tokens to 1,536 for each. Detailed prompt
      design is available in Appendix C. Following well-established prompting techniques (Brown et al.,
226
      2020; Schulhoff et al., 2024), four different prompts were tested for each model: zero-shot and one-
227
      shot, with and without netlists. Models were instructed to give their final numerical answers with a
228   precision of six decimal places.
229
230
      5.2     E XPERIMENTS
231
232
      In each experiment, models were provided with diagrams for questions that included them. In the
233   first 3 experiments, models received all questions from the CIRCUIT dataset with a 0-shot prompt.
234   In the next 3, models were given 395 questions that had associated netlists and the same 0-shot
235   prompt, along with netlists and customized instructions for interpreting only the elements present in
236   each netlist. In the third set of 3 experiments, models were given all questions with a 1-shot prompt.
237   In the final 3 experiments, models received all questions, a 1-shot prompt with a netlist example,
238   netlists, and the necessary netlist instructions. Details of the prompt design are in Appendix C.
239   Responses from all experiments were quantitatively analyzed, with a subset reviewed for errors and
240   qualitative insights by human evaluators.
241
242   5.3     E VALUATION
243
244   We used an automatic evaluation method to assess model responses and reported both global and
245
      template accuracies. Responses were deemed correct if the absolute difference from the ground truth
      was less than 0.001. Additionally, we conducted a human evaluation of best-accuracy responses to
246
      verify automatic evaluation results, analyze errors, and understand the qualitative aspects of the
247
      responses. Errors were categorized into mathematical, response formatting, and reasoning. The
248   models sometimes displayed clear misunderstandings of the circuit topology, which we classified as
249   topology errors, a specific type of reasoning error. A common topology error was misunderstanding
250   element orientation or direction, the rate of which we also reported. More details on error types and
251   subtypes can be found in Appendix D. Human evaluation deemed responses as correct if they were
252   devoid of errors.

260   We assessed model performance across the entire CIRCUIT dataset using automatic evaluation, with
261   results detailed in Table 1. A key observation is that the best-performing prompt varies by model and
262   by the specific accuracy metric. For instance, GPT 4-turbo achieves the highest global accuracy
263   with the 1-shot prompt, while its highest 5/5 template accuracy occurs with the 1-shot prompt with
264   a netlist example. In contrast, Gemini 1.5-pro performs best with the plain 0-shot prompt
265   across all metrics, indicating a potential struggle to integrate additional information from netlists or
266
      example-based problem-solving strategies provided in the 1-shot prompts. The most consistent and
      highest-performing model across both global and template accuracies appears to be GPT 4o, which
267
      leverages netlists effectively but does not seem to gain further advantage from the 1-shot prompt.
268
269   One important pattern we observe is that template accuracy decreases as the value of k in pass@k/n
      increases. This reflects the increasing difficulty in achieving correctness across all five numerical

287   6.1.2    H UMAN EVALUATION
288
289   Automatic evaluation predominantly assesses model outputs by comparing them to numeric ground
290   truths and typically does not penalize incorrect reasoning. Concerns about this method also include
291   mathematical errors and incorrect response formatting. GPT 4o was selected for a detailed human
292   evaluation because it demonstrated superior performance in the automatic assessment.
293   Results outlined in Table 3 affirm that the trends observed in human evaluations are consistent with
294   those from automatic evaluation. To further understand the correlation between automatic and hu-
295   man evaluations, we analyzed the occurrence of false positives—instances where responses were
296   deemed correct by automatic metrics but identified as incorrect upon human review. Approximately
297   5% of the automated evaluations resulted in false positives, impacting even the most rigorous tem-
298   plate accuracies. Despite these occasional discrepancies, automatic evaluation proves to be a de-
299
      pendable tool for understanding model performance.
300   Human evaluation involved a thorough error analysis, detailed in Table 4, with error categorization
301   methodologies explained in Appendix D. The primary error types identified were mathematical,
302   formatting, and reasoning—the latter encompassing all errors not directly related to mathematical
303   or formatting issues. Within reasoning errors, misunderstandings related to topology emerged as a
304   significant subcategory, and issues with direction or orientation of elements were recognized as a
305
      specific concern within topology errors. Our analysis indicates that mathematical and formatting
      errors constitute a minor portion of the total errors, and the predominant challenges for models
306
      stem from reasoning errors. This highlights the complexity of our dataset which requires a deep
307
      understanding of underlying concepts and their applications.
308
309   Additionally, global per-category and per-level accuracies on human-evaluated responses are sum-
310   marized in Table 5 and Table 6 respectively. These results highlight the challenges in understanding
311
      more complex topologies, as evidenced by significantly lower performance on questions with netlists
      and at higher levels. Furthermore, the consistently higher accuracy in the ’Basic’ category across
312
      configurations suggests that GPT-4o is better equipped to handle introductory-level circuits than
313
      more advanced ones.

      6.2     Q UALITATIVE ANALYSIS
316
317
      GPT 4o’s responses revealed that the model generally employed appropriate tools and formulas
318   and understood which elements were present in the given circuit. However, it struggled with com-
319   plex circuit topologies; even with netlists, higher-level reasoning remained challenging. Sometimes,
320   even when given a netlist, GPT’s response would not indicate its use. We also noticed that netlists
321   often helped GPT understand a part of or the entire given topology. Errors often stemmed from
322   misconceptions about interactions and connections between components and subcircuits. GPT also
323   struggled with directions and element orientations, such as current flow direction from a current
      source. Sometimes, GPT made minor reasoning errors which didn’t affect the correctness of the

      final solution. While GPT occasionally made mathematical errors, these were primarily confined to
325   approximation errors, often division and logarithmic and exponential calculations, and sometimes
326   careless mistakes in equation manipulation, reinforcing that the primary challenge lies in reason-

336   7     D ISCUSSION & L IMITATIONS
337
338   7.1   D ISCUSSION
339
340
      Through our experiments, we gained valuable insight into the capabilities of existing LLMs in un-
      derstanding and reasoning about various analog circuit topologies. Our quantitative and qualitative
341
      analyses indicate that these models possess reasoning abilities and relevant expert knowledge to
342
      tackle the problems in our dataset. Their understanding of circuit topologies can be improved when
343   netlists and 1-shot examples are provided, but substantial work remains to be done to improve their
344   performance further on our dataset. Addressing these basic shortcomings in topology understanding
345   is crucial before advancing to more complex analog design tasks—both of which represent exciting
346   directions for future work.
347
      Our dataset design together with the pass@k/n metric enables an automatic evaluation framework
348
      for quick, cost-effective, reliable, and comprehensive automatic evaluation of LLMs’ capabilities.
349   pass@k/n offers a more nuanced understanding of model performance than a mere global accuracy
350   score. On our dataset, it reveals that these models are proficient in only a narrow subset of topologies,
351   and a closer look found this subset focused on very simple topologies. Enhancing this pass@k/n’s
352   potential to yield deeper insights into model understanding could be explored in future work by en-
353   riching templates with more detailed annotations. Uniquely, the metric can be adjusted for different
354   levels of strictness (k), allowing researchers to evaluate model performance under varying levels of
355   precision. The unit-test-like pass@k/n metric can be beneficial in domains beyond analog circuits
356   where a deep understanding of nuanced subject matter is critical, and where datasets can be struc-
357
      tured with multiple subcomponents per main category to assess comprehensive knowledge. Future
      work could investigate applying our dataset design and metric to new domains, different unit test de-
358
      signs for distinct evaluation goals, and strategies for evaluating intermediate steps in LLM reasoning
359
      to enable a more detailed assessment.
360
361   A key aspect of the CIRCUIT dataset design is its transparency regarding data homogeneity achieved
362   through our unit test setup. When we compare global accuracy to template accuracy, we see the po-
363
      tential pitfall of relying solely on global metrics in dataset evaluation. Global accuracy provides an
      aggregate view of model performance but can mask nuanced failures that become apparent when as-
364
      sessing models on a template level. The CIRCUIT dataset’s explicit design allows us to observe this
365
      distinction more clearly, as it isolates a model’s ability to handle both the homogeneity (consistent
366   core structures) and variability (changing numerical setups) inherent in real-world problems. This
367   approach contrasts with traditional datasets, where either the homogeneity may not be explicit or the
368   variability across problem instances may not be systematically controlled. By designing datasets like
369   CIRCUIT, where the relationship between template structure and numerical variability is clear, we
370   can gain deeper insights into model robustness and generalization capabilities. Template pass@k/n
371   accuracies on our dataset show low generalization capabilities across variability in numerical se-
372   tups. This is concerning for analog circuit design because it suggests that models struggle to adapt
373   to different component values and configurations, which are critical for reliable performance in real-
374
      world circuit applications. Therefore, we encourage making homogeneity a more explicit aspect of
      dataset design and look forward to the insights future work may uncover.
375

      D    E RROR TYPES
811
812
      We categorized errors made by the model into the following types:
813
814         • Math Errors: Any type of mistake related to mathematical computation, precision, or mis-
815           understanding of mathematical concepts. This includes errors in basic arithmetic, formula
816           application, or incorrect assumptions about numerical values.
817         • Formatting Errors: These occur when the model outputs an answer in an incorrect format
818           (correct format detailed in Appendix C). For example, the model may respond with ”FNA:
819           ...” instead of using the correct label, ”Final Numerical Answer:” or might misapply other
820           required conventions.
821         • Reasoning Errors: Any mistake that is not a Math or a Formatting error. These include
822           two subcategories:
823
                 – Topology Errors: The model misunderstood the structural layout or connection of
824
                   elements in the circuit. For instance, it could misidentify an element or incorrectly
825                interpret how components are interconnected within the system.
826
                 – Direction Errors: The model misunderstood the orientation of an element, the direc-
827                tion of a current, etc. This involves confusion over how elements are aligned or how
828                power/current flows through the circuit.
829
830   The main error types are Math, Formatting, and Reasoning. That means, all direction and topology
831   errors count as reasoning errors.
