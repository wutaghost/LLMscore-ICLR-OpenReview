                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
010
               Diffusion models have risen to prominence in time series forecasting, showcas-
               ing their robust capability to model complex data distributions. However, their
011
               effectiveness in deterministic predictions is often constrained by instability aris-
012
               ing from their inherent stochasticity. In this paper, we revisit time series diffusion
013            models and present a comprehensive framework that encompasses most existing
014            diffusion-based methods. Building on this theoretical foundation, we propose a
015            novel diffusion-based time series forecasting model, the Series-to-Series Diffu-
016            sion Bridge Model (S2 DBM), which leverages the Brownian Bridge process to
017            reduce randomness in reverse estimations and improves accuracy by incorporating
018            informative priors and conditions derived from historical time series data. Exper-
019            imental results demonstrate that S2 DBM delivers superior performance in point-
020            to-point forecasting and competes effectively with other diffusion-based models
021
               in probabilistic forecasting.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020) have emerged as powerful tools for time series
027
      forecasting, offering the capability to model complex data distributions. Building on their success
      in other domains, such as computer vision (Saharia et al., 2022; Rombach et al., 2022) and natural
028
      language processing (Reid et al., 2022; Ye et al., 2023), researchers have increasingly applied dif-
029
      fusion models to time series prediction. This approach has shown promise in capturing the intricate
030   temporal dependencies and uncertainty in time series data, leading to significant advancements in
031   forecasting accuracy and reliability (Rasul et al., 2021; Tashiro et al., 2021; Alcaraz & Strodthoff,
032   2022; Li et al., 2024).
033
      However, the inherent stochasticity of diffusion models makes multivariate time series forecasting
034
      challenging. Specifically, most of these methods employ a standard forward diffusion process that
035
      gradually corrupts future time series data until it converges to a standard normal distribution. Conse-
036   quently, their predictions originate from pure noise, lacking temporal structure, with historical time
037   series data merely conditioning the reverse diffusion process and offering limited improvement. This
038   approach often results in forecasting instability and the generation of low-fidelity samples (as shown
039   in Figure 1). While diffusion-based methods perform adequately in probabilistic forecasting, their
040   point-to-point prediction accuracy lags behind that of deterministic models, e.g., Autoformer (Wu
041   et al., 2021), PatchTST (Nie et al., 2022), and DLinear (Zeng et al., 2023).
042   To improve the deterministic estimation performance of diffusion models on time series, we first
043   revisit and consolidate existing non-autoregressive diffusion-based time series forecasting models
044   under a unified framework, demonstrating that these models are fundamentally equivalent, differing
045   primarily in their choice of parameters and network architecture. Based on this framework, we
046   propose a novel diffusion-based time series forecasting model, Series-to-Series Diffusion Bridge
047   Model (S2 DBM). S2 DBM employs the diffusion bridge as its foundational architecture, which
048   proves effective for multivariate time series forecasting. Specifically, S2 DBM uses the Brownian
049   Bridge to pin down the diffusion process at both ends, reducing the instability caused by noisy input
050
      and enabling the accurate generation of future time step features from historical time series. By
      adjusting the posterior variance, S2 DBM behaves as a deterministic generative model without any
051
      Gaussian noise, thereby ensuring stability and precise point-to-point forecasting results.
052

075
076               • In this paper, we propose a comprehensive framework for non-autoregressive time series
077                 diffusion models, into which most existing diffusion-based methods can be integrated. This
078                 framework clarifies the interrelationships between these methods and highlights practical
079                 implications for diffusion models aimed at point-to-point time series forecasting.

      2       R ELATED W ORKS
091
092   Diffusion-based Time Series Forecasting. Recently, a range of diffusion-based methods are pro-
093
      posed for time series forecasting. These methods generally adhere to the framework of the standard
      diffusion model, with their primary distinctions stemming from variations in the denoising network
094
      and conditional mechanisms.
095
096   TimeGrad (Rasul et al., 2021) is the pioneer of these diffusion-based methods, integrating diffusion
097   models with an RNN-based encoder to handle historical time series. However, its reliance on autore-
098   gressive decoding can lead to error accumulation and slow inference times. To tackle this problem,
099
      CSDI (Tashiro et al., 2021) employs an entire time series as the target for diffusion and combines it
      with a binary mask (which denotes missing values) as conditional inputs into two transformers. This
100
      masking-based conditional mechanism enables CSDI to generate future time series data in a non-
101
      autoregressive fashion. SSSD (Alcaraz & Strodthoff, 2022) uses the same conditional mechanism
102   as CSDI, but replaces the transformers in CSDI with a Structured State Space Model (S4) to reduce
103   the computational complexity and is more suited to handling long-term dependencies. TMDM (Li
104   et al., 2024) integrates transformers with a conditional diffusion process to improve probabilistic
105   multivariate time series forecasting by effectively capturing covariate dependencies in both the for-
106   ward and Reverse diffusion processes. TimeDiff (Shen & Kwok, 2023) introduces two innovative
107   conditioning mechanisms specifically designed for time series analysis: future mixup and autore-
      gressive initialization, which construct effective conditional embeddings. To reduce the predictive


                                                                                   2

108
      instability arising from the stochastic nature of the diffusion models, MG-TSD (Fan et al., 2024)
109   leverages the inherent granularity levels within the data as given targets at intermediate diffusion
110   steps to guide the learning process of diffusion models. Most of the above diffusion-based meth-
111   ods emphasize their probabilistic forecasting ability; however, their performance in point-to-point
112   forecasting is suboptimal.
113
114   Diffusion Bridge. Diffusion bridges (Liu et al., 2023a; Zhou et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023a) repre-
115   sent a specific class of diffusion models designed to simulate the trajectory of a stochastic process
116   between predetermined initial and final states. They are regarded as conditioned diffusion models
117   subject to particular boundary constraints. These models, stemming from classical stochastic pro-
118
      cesses like Brownian motion or Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process, have a predetermined terminal value
      rather than being free.
119
120   DDBMs (Zhou et al., 2023) introduce diffusion bridges, stochastically interpolating between paired
121   distributions to provide smoother transitions and more flexible input handling compared to tradi-
122   tional noise-based diffusion models. Liu et al. (2023a) propose I2 SB, which constructs nonlin-
123   ear diffusion bridges between two domains, making it suitable for tasks like image restoration.
124
      BBDM (Li et al., 2023a) models image-to-image translation as a bidirectional diffusion process
      using a Brownian bridge, directly learning domain translation and achieving competitive benchmark
125
      results. GOUB (Yue et al., 2023) combines the generalized OU process with Doob’s h-transform to
126
      create precise diffusion mappings that transform low-quality images into high-quality ones. These
127   diffusion bridge models excel in image restoration by using degraded images as informative priors
128   to facilitate clean image reconstruction. Bridge-TTS (Chen et al., 2023) successfully incorporates
129   Schrödinger Bridge diffusion models into text-to-speech (TTS) synthesis task. It leverages the la-
130   tent representation obtained from text input as a prior and builds a fully tractable Schrödinger bridge
131   between it and the ground-truth mel-spectrogram. For time series data, Park et al. (2024) introduces
132   TimeBridge, a framework that utilizes diffusion bridges to model transitions between selected prior
133   and data distributions. This framework supports both data- and time-dependent priors, achieving
134   state-of-the-art performance in unconditional and conditional time series generation tasks. However,
135
      the TimeBridge uses linear spline interpolation (De Boor, 1978) to generate priors for imputation
      tasks, which is unsuitable for time series forecasting.

      Most existing diffusion-based time series forecasting methods emphasize their probabilistic fore-

189

190

246
247   As shown in Table 1, existing diffusion-based time series forecasting methods have been extensively
248   studied using various diffusion paradigms and conditional approaches in the formulation of The-
249   orem 1 and achieve promising predictive ability. However, most of these methods focus on the
250   uncertainty estimation ability and typically rely on a data-to-noise diffusion process due to current
251   conditioning mechanisms. As a result, they are often constrained by the intrinsic stochastic nature
252   and are limited in capturing the inherent complexity and dynamic nature of real-world time series
253   data, leading to suboptimal performance in point-to-point forecasting. To address this gap, we pro-
254   pose the Series-to-Series Diffusion Bridge Model (S2 DBM), which uses the Brownian Bridge to pin
255
      down the diffusion process at both ends, reducing the instability caused by noisy input and enabling
      the accurate generation of future time step features from historical time series. By adjusting the
256
      posterior variance in Theorem 1, S2DBM behaves as a deterministic generative model without any
257
      Gaussian noise, thereby ensuring stability and precise point-to-point forecasting results.
258

347
      ditioning module E(·), but also via the prior predictor F (·). In time series forecasting, the lookback
      and forecast windows often differ, and historical sequences cannot directly provide structurally in-
348
      formative priors for prediction targets as damaged images do in image restoration. Therefore, we
349
      cannot directly construct a diffusion bridge between historical time series x and future time series
350   y. Instead, we use the prior predictor F (·) to transform historical time series into a deterministic
351   conditional representation h, which serves as the endpoint of the diffusion process and provides
352   guidance at the beginning of the reverse process. Both the conditional encoder network E and the
353   prior predictor F (·) in S2 DBM employ a simple one-layer linear model, chosen for its simplicity,
354   explainability, and efficiency (Toner & Darlow, 2024).
355
356
      Label-Guided Data Estimation. The learnable transfer probability pθ (yt−1 | yt , x) is an ap-
357
      proximation of the posterior distribution q(yt−1 | yt , y0 , x) := N (yt−1 ; µ(yt , y0 , x), σ̂t2 I). In
358   our S2 DBM, the denoising network µθ is designed to estimate the data rather than the noise, as we
359   found that estimating the noise introduces more oscillations in the prediction results. Thus, µθ can
360   be expressed as:
361
362                             µθ (yt , h, c, t) = κt yt + λt yθ (yt , h, c, t) + ζt h.                    (7)
363
364   In practice, we do not directly estimate the future time series y. Instead, we utilize the labeling strat-
365   egy employed in some transformer-based time series forecasting models, such as the Informer (Zhou
366   et al., 2022). Specifically, we treat the terminal portion of the historical data, x, as the label and in-
367   tegrate it with the future time series y along the time dimension, denoted as y ∗ . Consequently,
368   the denoising network µθ is tasked not only with predicting future time steps but also with recon-
369   structing the known sequence within the label length. This methodology enables the model to more
370   effectively capture underlying patterns in the data. The training loss for S2 DBM is defined as fol-
371   lows:

405   Datasets. In this experiment, the time series forecasting benchmark datasets employed encompass
406
      several real-world datasets: Weather, Influenza-like Illness (ILI), Exchange-Rate (Lai et al., 2018),
      and four Electricity Transformer Temperature datasets (Zhou et al., 2022) (ETTh1, ETTh2, ETTm1,
407
      ETTm2). These datasets are extensively utilized for testing multivariate time-series forecasting
408
      models due to their diverse and representative nature, offering insights into the model’s performance
409   across different domains and conditions. Each dataset is normalized using the mean and standard
410   deviation of the training part.
411
412   Baselines. We compared our method with several state-of-the-art and representative baseline mod-
413   els. These include Transformer-based methods: Autoformer (Wu et al., 2021), Informer (Zhou et al.,
414   2022), and iTransformer (Liu et al., 2023b); linear models: DLinear, NLinear (Zeng et al., 2023), and
415   RLinear (Li et al., 2023b); as well as diffusion-based time series prediction methods: CSDI (Tashiro
416   et al., 2021), TMDM (Li et al., 2024), and TimeDiff (Shen & Kwok, 2023).
417
418   Evaluation metrics. To assess point-to-point forecasting performance, we employ mean squared
419   error (MSE) and mean absolute error (MAE) as primary metrics to quantify discrepancies between
420
      forecasted and actual time series values. For evaluating the quality of probabilistic forecasts, we use
      the continuous ranked probability score (CRPS) (Matheson & Winkler, 1976) across individual time
421
      series dimensions and CRPSsum for the aggregate of all dimensions.

      Implementation details. We trained our model using the ADAM optimizer, setting the initial
424   learning rate at 0.0001 and parameters β1 = 0.9 and β2 = 0.999. We configured the number of
425   time steps for the S2 DBM to be T=50 during the training and inference stages. The computational
426   environment comprised a server with an NVIDIA GeForce RTX 3090 24GB GPU.

517   5   C ONCLUSION
518
519   In this paper, we revisit non-autoregressive time series diffusion models and present a comprehen-
520   sive framework that integrates most existing diffusion-based methods. Building on this theoretical
521   framework, we propose the Series-to-Series Diffusion Bridge Model (S2 DBM). Our S2 DBM uti-
522
      lizes the Brownian Bridge diffusion process to reduce randomness in diffusion estimations, improv-
      ing forecast accuracy by effectively leveraging historical information through informative priors and
523
      conditions. Extensive experimental results demonstrate that S2 DBM achieves superior performance
524
      in point-to-point forecasting and performs competitively against other diffusion-based models in
525   probabilistic forecasting.
