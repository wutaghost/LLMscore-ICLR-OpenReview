011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            The straightforward fine-tuning of the pre-trained model for the target task, bears
014            the risk of under-utilizing the foundational knowledge accrued by the pre-trained
015            model, resulting in the sub-optimal utilization of transferable knowledge, conse-
016            quently impeding peak performance on the target task. To address this, we intro-
017            duce Divide and Conform, aimed at augmenting the transferability of pre-trained
018
               convolutional neural networks (ConvNets), in the absence of base data. This strat-
               egy exploits the mathematical equivalence of the convolution operation, concep-
019
               tualizing it as a two-step process involving spatial-only convolution and channel
020
               combination. To achieve this, we decompose (Divide) the filters of pre-trained
021            ConvNets into spatial filter atoms (responsible for spatial-only convolution) and
022            their corresponding atom-coefficients (responsible for channel combination). Our
023            observations reveal that solely fine-tuning (Conform-ing) the spatial filter atoms,
024            comprising of only a few hundred parameters, renders the transferability of the
025            model efficient, without compromising on the predictive performance. Simulta-
026            neously, the static atom-coefficients serve to retain the base (foundational) knowl-
027            edge from the pre-trained model. We rigorously assess this dual-faceted approach
028            within the demanding and practical framework of cross-domain few-shot learning,
029
               showcasing the approach’s substantial capability of transferring the knowledge in
               a parameter-efficient manner.

033   1   I NTRODUCTION
034
035   In the wake of the transformative impact of language models (Devlin et al., 2019; Raffel et al.,
036   2020; Touvron et al., 2023; Brown et al., 2020), the field of image processing is experiencing a
037   parallel evolution. Language models, often pre-trained on internet text, contrast starkly with vision
038   models pre-trained on internet images, which are typically not representative of specialized domains
039
      like medical or satellite imagery. This discrepancy in image types leads to under-performance when
      employing vision models pre-trained on generic images to specialized domains. Vision transformers
040
      (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021; Touvron et al., 2021) have marked a significant shift in vision models, yet
041
      recent findings (Smith et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2022; Woo et al., 2023) suggest that ConvNets can rival
042   or even outperform transformers at scale. Despite this, the generalization capabilities of ConvNets,
043   especially in the face of significant task discrepancies, remain a concern (Zhou et al., 2021; Bai
044   et al., 2021).
045
      Recent studies (Phoo & Hariharan, 2021; Oh et al., 2022; Li et al., 2021; Islam et al., 2021;
046
      Zhou et al., 2023; Fu et al., 2023; Li et al., 2022; Guo et al., 2020; Jiang et al., 2022)
047
      have highlighted a significant gap in the performance of pre-trained ConvNets when applied
048   to target tasks that are markedly different, especially in situations where no labeled samples
049   are available for the target task. In these scenarios, the effectiveness of pre-trained models
050   falls short compared to their supervised counterparts. This observation underscores the ne-
051   cessity for further investigation into enhancing the transferability of pre-trained models, par-
052   ticularly in contexts where the domain gap is substantial and the availability of labeled data
053   is non-trivial. This challenge, known as cross-domain few-shot learning (CD-FSL), prompted
      works by Phoo & Hariharan (2021), along with Islam et al. (2021), to utilize a small por-

080   During the Divide phase, informed by the previous works of Zhang et al. (2015) and Qiu et al.
081   (2018) showing that a convolution filter can be accurately represented as a linear combinations of a
082   predefined set of spatial filter bases1 (atoms), we exploit the inherent mathematical representation of
083   the convolution operation as a sequence of spatial-only convolution and channel combination. This
084   insight enables us to decompose the pre-trained filters into a set of spatial filter atoms (responsible
085   for spatial-only convolution) and their associated atom-coefficients (responsible for channel com-
086
      bination). Note that the channel combination is conducted as a 1 × 1 convolution operation using
      the atom-coefficients, hence, the atom-coefficients could be said to capture the spatially-invariant
087
      knowledge.
088
089   Following this decomposition, the Conform phase aims to align the pre-trained knowledge towards
090   the target task. The base pre-trained model is recalibrated by selectively fine-tuning the spatial
091   atoms while keeping the atom-coefficients static. We hypothesize that the primary constituent of
092
      the variations between the base and the target task data can essentially be construed as spatial dis-
      crepancies in the images. Thus, by exclusively adapting the spatial atoms with a few unlabeled
093
      target samples, while keeping the atom-coefficients untouched, we seek to efficaciously recalibrate
094
      the pre-trained model for the target task, positing that this selective learning mechanism for the
095   spatial atoms should suffice in aligning the pre-trained model to the target task’s characteristics.
096   This selective adaptation is conceptually similar to LoRA-style methods used in language models
097   (Hu et al., 2021), where only a small, additionally targeted subset of parameters is fine-tuned while
098   preserving the majority of the model’s parameters. By focusing on adapting spatial atoms (akin
099   to LoRA’s low-rank updates), we reduce the number of trainable parameters, achieving parameter
100   efficiency without sacrificing transferability. Moreover, this approach retains the spatially-invariant,
101   parameter-expensive atom-coefficients, which capture the channel mixing (combination) knowledge
102   inherited from the pre-trained model. In doing so, we ensure that the foundational knowledge from
103
      the base data is preserved for the target task, enhancing model transferability in constrained settings
      where comprehensive fine-tuning would be computationally prohibitive.

123   2            R ELATED W ORKS
124
125   Cross-Domain Few-Shot Learning (CD-FSL). CD-FSL addresses the challenge of transferring
126
      knowledge from a source to significantly different target domains (Guo et al., 2020; Tseng et al.,
      2020; Oh et al., 2022), where domain discrepancies hinder the direct application of learned knowl-
127
      edge (Song et al., 2023; Neyshabur et al., 2020). Recent progress emphasizes fine-tuning (tranfer-
128
      learning), outperforming traditional meta-learning based few-shot methods (Guo et al., 2020; Sung
129   et al., 2018; Snell et al., 2017; Vinyals et al., 2016; Garcia & Bruna, 2018; Tseng et al., 2020; Sun
130   et al., 2021; Wang & Deng, 2021; Hu & Ma, 2022). Notably, the prominent tranfer-learning based
131   ideas, STARTUP (Phoo & Hariharan, 2021) and Dynamic Distillation (Islam et al., 2021) leverage
132   small-scale unlabeled target data during pre-training to improve adaptability. Both methods train a
133   teacher network on labeled base data, but STARTUP also trains a student network with unsupervised
134   losses on target data, using distillation loss (Hinton et al., 2015) and SimCLR (Chen et al., 2020),
135   while Dynamic Distillation employs a KL divergence loss (Sohn et al., 2020). TUP (Li et al., 2021)
136   investigates pre-trained networks’ clustering properties, recommending a few unlabeled target sam-
137
      ples for better clustering. However, these methods necessitate access to the base data in addition to
      some unlabeled target samples during pre-training. Diverging from this, ConFeSS (Das et al., 2022)
138
      proposes a base-free adaptation strategy by learning a masking module that filters target-specific
139
      features, enhancing the model’s relevance to the target task, however, they use labeled target data to
140   align their base pre-trained model. Nonetheless, we use the CD-FSL setting as test-bed evaluating
141   representation transferability (cf . Appendix A).
142
143
      Spatial Filter Decomposition. Several studies have advanced the concept of approximating filters
      through parameter-efficient representations (Qiu et al., 2018; Li et al., 2019; Denton et al., 2014;
144
      Jaderberg et al., 2014; Zhang et al., 2015; Wang et al., 2017a). Among these, the contributions of
145
      Zhang et al. (2015) and Qiu et al. (2018) are particularly noteworthy for their proposal to repre-
146   sent filters as combinations of spatial bases (atoms). Building upon this decomposition framework,
147   subsequent research has explored various applications, including, network compression (Li et al.,
148   2019), domain adaptation (Wang et al., 2020), continual learning (Miao et al., 2021), and image
149   generation (Wang et al., 2019; Zhai et al., 2021).

187   A critical consideration in this process is the potential risk of naively fine-tuning the entire network
188   f with \protect \bm  {D}_{\text {U}} , which could inadvertently overwrite the model’s intrinsic, foundational knowledge, ac-
189   quired from the now inaccessible \protect \bm  {D}_{\text {B}} . Such an approach not only risks diluting the pre-learned
190
      knowledge but also incurs a high parameter update cost. To mitigate these concerns, we propose a
      strategy termed Divide and Conform. The initial phase, Divide, entails the decomposition of convo-
191
      lution filters into spatial filter atoms and their respective atom-coefficients (Qiu et al., 2018; Li et al.,
192
      2019; Zhang et al., 2015). Subsequently, the Conform phase involves the selective fine-tuning of
193   these spatial atoms, whilst maintaining the atom-coefficients unchanged, using unsupervised learn-
194   ing objectives (Chen et al., 2020; Grill et al., 2020). The key idea behind keeping the spatially-
195   invariant atom-coefficients static is to retain the foundational knowledge embedded within \protect \bm  {f} , and
196   advance the model’s adaptation in a parameter-efficient manner, as the fine-tuning parameters (spa-
197   tial filter atoms) account for only a few hundred parameters. This nuanced approach ensures the
198   retention of \protect \bm  {f} ’s foundational strengths while facilitating its tailored application to samples in \protect \bm  {D}_{\text {T}} ,
199   thereby striking a favorable balance between performance and efficiency. In Figure 2, we visually
200   describe the overall pipeline of the problem setup and our methodology. In the upcoming sections
201
      we discuss the Divide and the Conform steps in detail.

      Specifically, one could decompose the weight matrices of linear layers into analogous components,
342   potentially enabling similar manipulations. However, unlike ConvNets, where convolutional filters
343   possess an inherent spatial structure that allows for a clear separation between spatial atoms and
344   channel combination coefficients, linear layers lack such intrinsic spatial interpretability. The de-
345   composed components in linear layers do not correspond to distinct functional units related to spatial
346   or channel-wise operations, rendering the interpretability of such decomposition less evident.
347
      Moreover, when decomposing convolutional filters into spatial atoms and atom-coefficients within
348   ConvNets, we observe a pronounced imbalance in parameter distribution: the spatial atoms consti-
349   tute a significantly smaller subset of the total parameters compared to the atom-coefficients. This im-
350   balance is advantageous for our Conform step, as it permits fine-tuning of only the spatial atoms—a
351   relatively small number of parameters, cf . Section 3.4.
352
353   3.4      PARAMETER E FFICIENCY A NALYSIS
354
355   Furthermore, decomposing convolutional filters into spatial atoms \protect \mathbf  {D} \in \mathbb {R}^{m \times l \times l} and atom-
356   coefficients \protect \mathbf  {A} \in \mathbb {R}^{n \times c \times m} reveals a significant disparity in parameter counts between these com-
357   ponents. The spatial atoms consist of ml^2 parameters, while the atom-coefficients comprise mnc
358   parameters. Typically, the product nc vastly exceeds l^2 (i.e., nc \gg l^2 ), indicating that the spatial
359
      atoms represent a relatively small fraction of the total parameters in the filter decomposition. To
      illustrate this imbalance, consider a convolutional layer with filters of dimensions 64 \times 64 \times 3 \times 3,
360
      corresponding to 64 output channels, 64 input channels, and a kernel size of 3 \times 3. The total number
361
      of parameters in this layer is 64 \times 64 \times 3 \times 3 = 36, 864. When decomposed into m=9 spatial
362   atoms of size 3 \times 3, the spatial atoms account for only 9 \times 3 \times 3 = 81 parameters. In contrast, the
363   atom-coefficients retain 64 \times 64 \times 9 = 36, 864 parameters, matching the original filter’s parameter
364   count. This pronounced parameter imbalance is strategically leveraged in our Conform step. By
365   fine-tuning only the spatial atoms \protect \mathbf  {D}-while keeping the atom-coefficients \protect \mathbf  {A} fixed—we drastically
366   reduce the number of parameters that need updating during adaptation to the target task. Specifically,
367   we adjust a mere 81 parameters instead of the full 36, 864 parameters required when fine-tuning the
368   entire filter. This selective fine-tuning not only enhances computational efficiency but also mitigates
369   the risk of overfitting, as fewer parameters are susceptible to noise from the unlabeled data.

447   LoRA, updates to a pre-trained weight matrix  \mathbf {W} \in \mathbb {R}^{d_{\text {out}} \times d_{\text {in}}}  are constrained to a low-rank represen-
448   tation  \mathbf {W} + \mathbf {\Delta W} = \mathbf {W} + \mathbf {B \cdot C} , where  \mathbf {B} \in \mathbb {R}^{d_{\text {out}} \times r}  ,  \mathbf {C} \in \mathbb {R}^{r \times d_{\text {in}}}  , and  r \ll \min (d_{\text {out}}, d_{\text {in}}) . During
449   training,  \mathbf {W} is fixed and does not receive gradient updates, while  \mathbf {C}  and  \mathbf {B}  are adjusted as trainable
450   parameters. To adapt LoRA to convolutional filters, we interpret  \mathbf {K} \in \mathbb {R}^{n \times c \times l \times l}  as  \mathbb {R}^{n \cdot l \times c \cdot l}  , en-
451   abling the learning of a low-rank update  \mathbf {\Delta K} , thus inherently reducing the parameter complexity.
452   We conduct the LoRA experiments at rank 3, which approximately matches the parameter complex-
453   ity of our variants. The resulting superiority of our method compared to LoRA dispels our doubts

525   5    C ONCLUSION
526
527   In conclusion, our work introduces Divide and Conform a novel, efficient method for adapting pre-
528
      trained models to specialized target tasks under the constraints of limited unlabeled target data,
      without relying on extensive base datasets. By decomposing convolution filters into spatial filter
529
      atoms and their atom-coefficients, we recalibrate the pre-trained model to align with the target task
530
      through selective fine-tuning of spatial atoms. This approach allows adaptation to task-specific spa-
531   tial variances while preserving channel combination knowledge in the atom-coefficients, addressing
532   model transferability and generalization challenges, especially in scenarios with no labeled data and
533   significant domain discrepancy. Our method’s empirical evaluations in CD-FSL setting highlight
534   its effectiveness and efficiency, offering notable improvements over existing approaches with a lean
535   parameter update mechanism.

      A       CD-FSL AS A TEST- BET TO EVALUATE REPRESENTATION
757
              TRANSFERABILITY.
758
759
      We use CDFSL as a test-bed to assess model transferability that addresses substantial domain dis-
760
      crepancies between source and target in both input (diverse images domains) and label spaces (non-
761
      overlapping labels), presenting a more complex challenge than typical domain adaptation where the
762   label space is shared or FSL with meta-datasets containing images from similar domains. CD-FSL
763   ssumes scarcity of high-quality samples, underscoring the evaluation in the few-shot setting. In the
764   typical self-supervised pre-training, linear probing—applying a linear classifier to a frozen back-
765   bone—is commonly used to evaluate representation quality. CD-FSL makes this setting stringent by
766   linear-probing on a limited number of examples across 600 episodes over 8 existing, highly diverse,
767   standard benchmark datasets offering a more rigorous assessment of the representation transferred
768   from the base dataset. Therefore, we select CD-FSL as our test-bed for its comprehensive and
769   stringent evaluation protocol.
770
771   B       S IM CLR M OTVATION
772
773   The principal motivation for adopting SimCLR for fine-tuning is twofold.
774
      Firstly, SimCLR stands out as one of the most straightforward yet effective SSL methods. The
775
      fundamental concept involves positioning semantically similar examples closer together in the rep-
776   resentational space, while ensuring that dissimilar examples are kept apart. Formally, each in-
777   stance in the current batch {Ii }B   i=1 of size B undergoes augmentation to create an enhanced batch
778   {Ĩ2i−1 , Ĩ2i }B
                      i=1 , in which Ĩ2i−1 and Ĩ2i represent distinct augmentations derived from the same in-
779   put Ii . Subsequently, the transformed representations {z2i−1 = h ◦ f (Ĩ2i−1 ), z2i = h ◦ f (Ĩ2i )}B                        i=1
780   are extracted from the backbone network  \boldsymbol {f}  equipped with projection layers  \boldsymbol {h} . Utilizing these rep-
781   resentations, SimCLR conducts contrastive learning to minimize the contrastive loss given by:
782
783
                                                    \mathcal {L}_{\text {SimCLR}} = \frac {1}{2B} \sum _{i=1}^B \left [ \ell (2i - 1, 2i) + \ell (2i, 2i - 1) \right ], \label {eq:sim_clr1}                                                                                           (8)
784
785
      where
786
787                                         \ell (i, j) = - \log \left (\frac {\exp (\text {sim}(\mathbf {z}_i, \mathbf {z}_j) / \tau )}{\sum _{n=1}^{2B} \boldsymbol {1}_{[n \neq i]} \exp (\text {sim}(\mathbf {z}_i, \mathbf {z}_n) / \tau )}\right ). \label {eq:sim_clr2}         (9)
788
789                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                               ⊤
                                                                                                            u v
790   Here, \protect \bm  {1} is an indicator function, τ is a temperature hyperparameter, and sim(u, v) = ∥u∥·∥v∥ mea-
791   sures cosine similarity between two vectors u and v. The objective (9) is also know as NT-Xent loss
792   (normalized temperature-scaled cross-entropy loss) (Chen et al., 2020).
793   Secondly, Cao et al. (2021) established the following bound:
794
                                                                                                         \mathcal {L}_{\text {sup}} \leq \gamma _0 \mathcal {L}^{-}_{U} + \gamma _1 s(\boldsymbol {f}_k).\label {eq:fsl}                                                              (10)
795
796   Within this bound,  \mathcal {L}_{\text {sup}}  signifies the metric for assessing supervised learning representations. The
797   metric  \mathcal {L}^{-}_{U}  evaluates unsupervised contrastive samples, specifically those that are true negatives.
798   The function  s(\boldsymbol {f}_k)  quantifies the variation within classes as interpreted by the principal encoder
799    \boldsymbol {f}_k  (He et al., 2020). The constants  \gamma _0  and  \gamma _1  vary in accordance with class distribution. The

800
      term  \mathcal {L}_{\text {sup}}  can represent the training loss for supervised few-shot meta-learning techniques that are
801   adaptable to new classes. Given that  \mathcal {L}^{-}_{U}  sets the upper boundary for  \mathcal {L}_{\text {sup}}  , any minimization on  \mathcal {L}^{-}_{U}
802   naturally lowers  \mathcal {L}_{\text {sup}}  . It is also noteworthy that  \mathcal {L}^{-}_{U}  can be minimized without constraints since it
803   is assessed solely through true negative samples, rendering contrastive methods advantageous for
804
      learning representations conducive to efficient few-shot learning.

857   D           DC VS . L O RA

859   Our method focuses solely on fine-tuning spatial atoms. Unlike LoRA-style adaptation (Hu et al.,
860   2021), which learns a residual component \Delta \mathbf {K} across the entire filter (\protect \mathbf  {K}_t = \mathbf {K}_b + \Delta \mathbf {K}), our
861   method fine-tunes from \protect \mathbf  {K}_b = \mathbf {A}\mathbf {D}_b to \protect \mathbf  {K}_t = \mathbf {A}\mathbf {D}_t , expressed as \protect \mathbf  {K}_t = \mathbf {A}(\mathbf {D}_b + \Delta \mathbf {D}) where
862   \Delta \mathbf {D} = \mathbf {D}_t - \mathbf {D}_b . However, we avoid LoRA’s residual-style fine-tuning of atoms (\protect \mathbf  {D}), which requires
863   maintaining both \protect \mathbf  {D}_b and \Delta \mathbf {D} in memory, opting instead for a direct transition from \protect \mathbf  {D}_b to \protect \mathbf  {D}_t to
      reduce memory usage. This is how our method relates/differs from LoRA.
