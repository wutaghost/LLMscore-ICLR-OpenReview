009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Models for natural language and images benefit from data scaling behavior: the
012            more data fed into the model, the better they perform. This ’better with more’
013            phenomenon enables the effectiveness of large-scale pre-training on vast amounts
014            of data. However, current graph pre-training methods struggle to scale up data
015            due to heterogeneity across graphs. To achieve effective data scaling, we aim to
016            develop a general model that is able to capture diverse data patterns of graphs and
017            can be utilized to adaptively help the downstream tasks. To this end, we propose
018            UniAug, a universal graph structure augmentor built on a diffusion model. We
019
               first pre-train a discrete diffusion model on thousands of graphs across domains to
               learn the graph structural patterns. In the downstream phase, we provide adaptive
020
               enhancement by conducting graph structure augmentation with the help of the
021
               pre-trained diffusion model via guided generation. By leveraging the pre-trained
022            diffusion model for structure augmentation, we consistently achieve performance
023            improvements across various downstream tasks in a plug-and-play manner. To the
024            best of our knowledge, this study represents the first demonstration of a data-scaling
025            graph structure augmentor on graphs across domains.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030
      The effectiveness of existing foundation models (Radford et al., 2021; Touvron et al., 2023; Kirillov
031   et al., 2023) heavily relies on the availability of substantial amounts of data, where the relationship
032   manifests as a scaling behavior between model performance and data scale (Kaplan et al., 2020).
033   Consistent performance gain has been observed with the increasing scale of pre-training data in
034   both Natural Language Processing (Kaplan et al., 2020; Hoffmann et al., 2022) and Computer
035   Vision (Abnar et al., 2022; Zhai et al., 2022) domains. This data scaling phenomenon facilitates
036   the development of general models endowed with extensive knowledge and effective data pattern
037   recognition capabilities. In downstream applications, these models are capable of adaptively
038   achieving performance gains across different tasks.
039   In the context of graphs, the availability of large-scale graph databases (Rossi & Ahmed, 2015; Hu
040   et al., 2020; Leskovec & Krevl, 2014) enables possible data scaling across datasets and domains.
041   Existing works have demonstrated graph data scaling following two limited settings: in-domain
042   pre-training (Xia et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024c) and task-specific selection for pre-training data (Cao
043   et al., 2023). During the pre-training process, each graph in the pre-training pool must be validated
044   as in-domain or relevant to the downstream dataset. Given a specific domain or task, the crucial
045
      discriminative data patterns are likely confined to a fixed set (Mao et al., 2024c), leaving other
      potential patterns in diverse graph data distribution as noisy input. In terms of structure, graphs
046
      from different domains are particularly composed of varied patterns (Milo et al., 2002), making it
047
      hard to transfer across domains. For example, considering the building blocks of the graphs, the
048   motifs shared by the World Wide Web hyperlinks only partially align with those shared by genetic
049   networks (Milo et al., 2002). Therefore, closely aligning the characteristics of the pre-training graphs
050   and the downstream data both in feature and structure is essential for facilitating positive transfer (Cao
051   et al., 2023). As a consequence, the necessity of such meticulous data filtering restricts these methods
052   from scaling up graphs effectively, as they can only utilize a small part of the available data. Given
053   the limitation of the graph pre-training methods, a pertinent question emerges: How can we effectively
      leverage the increasing scale of graph data across domains?

054
      Rather than focusing solely on data patterns specific to particular domains, we aim to develop a
055   model that has a comprehensive understanding of data patterns inherent across various types of
056   graphs. In line with the principles of data scaling, we hypothesize that incorporating a broader
057   range of training datasets can help the model build an effective and universal graph pattern library,
058   avoiding an overemphasis on major data patterns specific to any single dataset (Mao et al., 2024a).
059   To construct such a general-purpose model, we propose to utilize a diffusion model operating only on
060   the structure as the backbone, for the following key reasons. (1) Unlike features, graph structures
061   follow a uniform construction principle, namely, the connections between nodes. This allows for
062   positive transfer across domains when the upstream and downstream data exhibit similar topological
063
      patterns (Cao et al., 2023). In particular, while the graph representations of neurons and forward
      electronic circuits are derived from distinct domains, they still share common motifs (Milo et al.,
064
      2002). (2) Current supervised and self-supervised methods tend to capture only specific patterns of
065
      graph data, with models designed for particular inductive biases (Mao et al., 2024a;c; Xu et al., 2018).
066   For instance, graph convolutional networks (GCNs) excel in node-level representation learning by
067   emphasizing homophily, whereas graph-level representation learning benefits from expressive GNNs
068   capable of distinguishing complex graph structures. (3) We opt for a structure-only model due to
069   the heterogeneous feature spaces across graphs, which often include missing features or mismatched
070   semantics (Mao et al., 2024b). For instance, node features yield completely different interpretations
071   in citation networks, where they represent keywords of documents, compared to molecular networks,
072   where they denote properties of atoms. To this end, we pre-train a structure-only diffusion model on
073   thousands of graphs, which serves as the upstream component of our framework.
074   In the downstream stage, we employ the pre-trained diffusion model as a Universal graph structure
075   Augmentor (UniAug) to enhance the dataset, where diffusion guidance (Ho & Salimans, 2022;
076   Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Gruver et al., 2024) is employed to align the generated structure with
077   the downstream requirements. Specifically, we generate synthetic structures with various guidance
078   objectives, and the resulting graphs consist of generated structures and original node features.
079   This data augmentation paradigm strategically circumvents feature heterogeneity and fully utilizes
080
      downstream inductive biases by applying carefully designed downstream models to the augmented
      graphs in a plug-and-play manner. Empirically, we apply UniAug to graphs from diverse domains
081
      and consistently observe performance improvement in node classification, link prediction, and graph
082
      property prediction. To the best of our knowledge, this study represents the first demonstration of a
083   data-scaling graph structure augmentor on graphs across domains.

      Learning from unlabeled graphs Graph self-supervised learning (SSL) methods provide examples
089
      of pre-training and fine-tuning paradigm (Hu et al., 2019; Hou et al., 2022; Kim et al., 2022; You
090   et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2021). However, these methods benefit from limited data scaling due to feature
091   heterogeneity, structural pattern differences across domains, and varying downstream inductive biases.
092   It is worth mentioning that DCT (Liu et al., 2024a) presents a pre-training and then data augmentation
093   pipeline on molecules. Despite its impressive performance improvement on graph-level tasks, DCT
094   is bounded with molecules and thus the use cases are limited.
095
      Graph data augmentation There have been many published works exploring graph data augmenta-
096   tion (GDA) since the introduction of graph neural networks (GNNs), with a focus on node-level (Park
097   et al., 2021; Liu et al., 2022b; Azabou et al., 2023), link-level (Zhao et al., 2022; Nguyen & Fang,
098   2024), and graph-level (Han et al., 2022; Ling et al., 2023; Luo et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022a; Kong
099   et al., 2022). These GDA methods have been generally designed for specific tasks or particular
100   aspects of graph data. In addition, they are often tailored for a single dataset and struggle to transfer
101   to unseen patterns, which limits their generalizability to a broader class of applications.
102   Diffusion models on graphs Diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2021; Rombach et al.,
103   2022) are latent variable models that learn data distribution by gradually adding noise into the data and
104   then recovering the clean input. Existing diffusion models on graphs can be classified into two main
105   categories depending on the type of noise injected, i.e. Gaussian or discrete. Previous works employed
106   Gaussian diffusion models both on general graphs (Niu et al., 2020; Jo et al., 2022) and molecules (Shi
107   et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2022). However, adding Gaussian noise into the adjacency matrix will destroy
      the sparsity of the graph, which hinders the scalability of the diffusion models (Haefeli et al., 2022).

      3     M ETHOD
148
149   In this section, our goal is to build UniAug to understand the diverse structure patterns of graphs and
150   perform data augmentation with a range of objectives. As illustrated in Fig.1, UniAug consists of two
151   main components: a pre-trained diffusion model and the downstream adaptation through structure
152   augmentation. We first collect thousands of graphs from varied domains with diverse patterns. To
153   construct a general model free of downstream inductive biases, we train a self-conditioned discrete
154   diffusion model on graph structures. In the downstream stage, we train an MLP guidance head on
155   top of the diffusion model with objectives across different levels of granularity. We then augment
156   the downstream dataset by generating synthetic structures through guided generation, where the
157
      augmented graph is composed of generated structures and original node features. Subsequently, we
      apply the augmented data to train a task-specific model for performing downstream tasks. Below,
158
      we elaborate on the data collection process, the architecture of the discrete diffusion model, and the
159
      guidance objectives employed.

190   3.2    P RE - TRAINING THROUGH DIFFUSION MODEL
191
192   Diffusion models have demonstrated the ability to facilitate transferability from a data augmentation
193   perspective on the images (Trabucco et al., 2024; You et al., 2024; He et al., 2023). Unlike the
194   traditional hand-crafted data augmentation methods, diffusion models can produce more diverse
195   patterns with high quality Trabucco et al. (2024). With the aid of diffusion guidance (Ho & Salimans,
196   2022; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021), these methods can achieve domain customization tailored to specific
197   semantic spaces (You et al., 2024; He et al., 2023). Despite the success of data augmentation through
198   diffusion models on images, the non-Euclidean nature of graph structures poses challenges for
199   data-centric learning on graphs. In addition, the fact that most graphs in the Network Repository are
200
      unlabeled exacerbates the challenges, as the absence of labeled data results in substantially lower
      generation quality for diffusion models (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Bao et al., 2022).
201
202   To address the aforementioned challenges, we propose to construct a self-conditioned discrete
203   diffusion model on graph structures. Unlike Gaussian-based diffusion models, discrete diffusion
204   models (Hoogeboom et al., 2021; Austin et al., 2021; Campbell et al., 2022; Vignac et al., 2023)
205   operate with discrete transition kernels between latent variables, as shown in Section 2. The key
206
      reason we opt for the discrete diffusion models lies in the sparse nature of graphs, where adding
      Gaussian noise into the adjacency matrix will result in a dense graph (Haefeli et al., 2022). On the
207
      contrary, discrete diffusion models effectively preserve the sparse structure of graphs during the
208
      diffusion process, thus maintaining the efficiency of the models on graphs.
209
210   To accommodate for unlabeled graphs, we adopt a self-supervised labeling strategy as an auxiliary
211   conditioning procedure (Gao et al., 2022; Hu et al., 2023). By leveraging the self-labeling technique,
212
      we are able to upscale the diffusion model to data with more diverse patterns (Gao et al., 2022). The
      self-labeling technique requires two components: a feature extractor and a self-supervised annotator.
213
214   Feature extractor. We extract graph-level features by calculating graph properties, including the
215   number of nodes, density, network entropy, average degree, degree variance, and scale-free exponent
      following Xu et al. (2023). The first two represent the scale of the graph corresponding to nodes and


                                                                     4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      edges, and the rest indicate the amount of information contained within a graph (Xu et al., 2023). We
217   compute the properties of one graph and concatenate them to get a graph-level representation.
218
219   Self-supervised annotator. To assign labels to graphs in a self-supervised manner, we employ
220
      clustering algorithms on the graph-level representations. The number of clusters is determined jointly
      by the silhouette score (Rousseeuw, 1987) and the separation of the graphs. The candidates of the
221
      number of clusters are chosen to ensure different clusters are well separated. Among the candidates,
222
      we select the final number of clusters by maximizing the mean Silhouette Coefficient of all samples.
223

241   The downstream phase of UniAug is to augment the graph topology through guided generation. This
242   guidance process serves to provide downstream semantics for the diffusion model, thus bridging the

253   One key question to answer is how to choose the proper guidance objectives. Our goal is to find
254   numerical characteristics that can best describe the structural properties of a graph. This includes
255   supervision signal and self-supervised information on the level of node, edge, and graph.
256
      Node level. Node labels provide the supervision signal for node classification tasks. Beyond node
257
      labels, node degrees are a fundamental factor in the evolutionary process of a graph (Liu et al., 2011).
258   From the perspective of network analysis, centrality measures indicate the importance of nodes
259   from various viewpoints (Borgatti, 2005). Empirically, we observe that utilizing different node-level
260   heuristics as guidance targets tends to yield similar outcomes. Therefore, we focus on node labels
261   and node degrees.
262
      Edge level. Edge-level heuristics can be broadly classified into two categories: local structural
263
      heuristics, such as Common Neighbor and Adamic Adar (Adamic & Adar, 2003), and global
264   structural heuristics, such as Katz (Katz, 1953) and SimRank (Jeh & Widom, 2002). Similar to
265   node-level heuristics, empirical observations suggest that different edge-level heuristics tend to yield
266   comparable guidance effects. In this work, we focus on the Common Neighbors (CN) heuristic due to
267   its efficiency. Another edge-level guidance objective is to recover the adjacency matrix from the node
268   representations in a link prediction way, similar to how we parameterize the denoising network. We
269   anticipate that such link prediction objective helps to align the generated graph with the downstream
      data on the granularity of edges.

292   3.4     C OMPARISON TO EXISTING METHODS
293
      The data augmentation paradigm of UniAug allows us to disentangle the upstream and downstream.
294
      We construct a diffusion model as the upstream component to comprehend the structural patterns
295   of graphs across various domains. In addition, we leverage downstream inductive biases with
296   downstream-specific models in a plug-and-play manner. This allows UniAug to facilitate cross-
297   domain transfer, offering a unified method that benefits graphs across different domains for various
298   downstream tasks. On the contrary, existing GDA methods are typically designed for specific tasks
299   and hard to transfer to unseen patterns. In the meantime, existing pre-training methods fail to transfer
300   across domains due to heterogeneity in features and structures. This comparison highlights the
301   success of UniAug as a data-scaling graph structure augmentor across domains. We summarize the
302   comparison between methods in Table 1.

304   4     E XPERIMENT
305
306   In this section, we conduct experiments to validate the effectiveness of UniAug. We first pre-train
307   our discrete diffusion model on thousands of graphs collected from diverse domains. For each
308   downstream task, we train an MLP guidance head with corresponding objectives on top of the
309   diffusion model. We then perform structure augmentation using UniAug and subsequently train a
310   task-specific GNN on augmented data for prediction. Through the experiments, we aim to answer the
311   following research questions:
312   • RQ1: Can UniAug benefit graphs from various domains across different downstream tasks?
313   • RQ2: What is the scaling behavior of UniAug corresponding to data scale and amount of compute?
314   • RQ3: Which components of UniAug are effective in preventing negative transfer?

479   4.2         S CALING BEHAVIOR OF UniAug
480
481   In light of the neural scaling law (Kaplan et al., 2020; Hoffmann et al., 2022; Abnar et al., 2022; Zhai
482
      et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2024b), we expect UniAug to benefit from an increased coverage of data and
      more compute budget. In this subsection, we investigate the scaling behavior of UniAug in terms of
483
      data scale and amount of compute for pre-training.
484
485   Data coverage During the data collection process, we prepare three versions of the training data
      with increasing magnitude and growing coverage on the graph distribution. We first sample 10 graphs


                                                                                          9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      per category from the Network Repository (Rossi & Ahmed, 2015) to build a SMALL collection.
487   Next, we gather all the graphs from the Network Repository and filter out large-scale graphs and
488   outliers for a FULL collection. In addition, we add a 1000 graphs subset of the GitHub Star dataset
489   from TUDataset (Morris et al., 2020) to enlarge the coverage of diverse patterns and form an EXTRA
490   collection. We pre-train three versions of UniAug respectively on the three collections and evaluate
491   them on graph classification and link prediction. As shown in Fig. 3, we observe a clear trend of
492   increase in performance as we enlarge the coverage of pre-training data. This paves the way to scale
493   up UniAug to even more pre-training graphs with an expanding distribution of graphs.
494   Amount of compute We sought to understand how effectively our diffusion model can learn data
495   patterns as we continue to train it. To this end, we checkpointed UniAug every 2,000 epochs (5 ×10−3
496   PF-days) while training on the EXTRA collection, and then applied it to graph classification and
497   link prediction tasks. The results are illustrated in Fig. 4. We observe that downstream performance
498   generally improves with prolonged training, while the trend slows down for some datasets when we
499   reach 8,000 epochs. We take the checkpoint at the 10,000th epoch for evaluations. Given the scaling
500   behavior observed, we anticipate UniAug to become even more effective with additional resources.

527   5     C ONCLUSION AND D ISCUSSION
528
529   In this work, we propose a graph structure augmentation pipeline UniAug to leverage the increasing
530   scale of graph data. We collect thousands of graphs from various domains and pre-train a self-
531   conditioned discrete diffusion model on them. In the downstream stage, we augment the graphs by
532   preserving the original node features and generating synthetic structures. We apply UniAug to node-,
533   link-, and graph-level tasks and achieve consistent performance gain. We have successfully developed
534   a showcase that benefits from cross-domain graph data scaling using diffusion models.
535   One limitation of the current analysis is the absence of an investigation into the effects of model
536   parameters due to limited resources. Given the scaling behavior of UniAug in terms of data scale
537   and amount of compute, we anticipate that a large-scale model will provide significant performance
538   improvements. One future direction is to investigate the adaptation of fast sampling methods to the
539   discrete diffusion models on graphs. This will lead to lower time complexity and enable broader
      application scenarios.

1007   In Section 4.1, we aim to benchmark the capability of cross-domain pre-training of different methods
1008   on the same set of pre-training graphs. While the pre-training graphs contain vastly different features,
1009   we have to align the feature space to allow pre-training for the baseline methods. There are two ways
1010   to tackle the feature heterogeneity issues in the existing literature. One line of them utilizes LLMs to
1011
       align text-space graphs (Chen et al., 2024), which is not applicable to broader classes of graphs. Other
       works, like GCOPE (Zhao et al., 2024), perform dimension reduction to align the feature dimension
1012
       of different graphs. We emphasize that dimension reduction methods fail to deal with extreme cases
1013
       like missing features. This phenomenon is pretty common in real life, as a large proportion of the
1014   graphs in the Network Repository do not have corresponding features. Therefore, we simply use the
1015   node degrees as the features in Section 4.1.
1016
1017
       We understand that removing the node features may result in a performance drop for the baseline
       methods. Note that most of the baselines follow the pre-training paradigm of (Hu et al., 2019) with
1018
       domain-specific model designs for chemistry and biology datasets, and thus cannot be directly applied
1019
       to the chosen graph classification datasets. Therefore, we adapt the semi-supervised (You et al.,
1020   2020) and self-supervised (Sun et al., 2020) setting for the baselines for a comprehensive benchmark.
1021   The semi-supervised setting involves pre-training with all data of that specific dataset and finetuning
1022   the training set of each split. Meanwhile, baselines of the self-supervised setting pre-train on the
1023   whole dataset and then classify the learned graph embeddings with a downstream SVM classifier.

1043
       of layers and the latent dimension of the GCN encoder are taken from (Li et al., 2024). We have
       mainly three hyperparameters for UniAug: step-size γ and regularization strength λ, and the number
1044
       of updates in (5) per each sampling step. In addition, inspired by the pseudo labeling strategy (Botao
1045
       et al., 2023), we provide an option threshold q for the sampling process of the diffusion model.

1125   In this work, we build a universal graph structure augmentor that benefits from data scaling across
1126
       domains. Given the consistent performance improvements for different tasks, we expect this work
       to contribute significantly towards the goal of building a graph foundation model. In the meantime,
1127
       we showcase the power of the deep generative models on graphs by introducing new application
1128
       scenarios. We anticipate such success will contribute to the community of generative models and
1129   graph learning.
1130
1131   It is important to mention that the model backbones of our method and baselines heavily rely on
1132
       neighboring node information as an inductive bias. However, this characteristic can result in biased
       predictions, especially when patterns in neighborhood majorities dominate, leading to potential
1133
       ethical issues in model predictions.
