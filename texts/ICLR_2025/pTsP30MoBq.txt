009                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011            Classification techniques have achieved significant success across fields such as computer
012            vision, information retrieval, and natural language processing. However, much of this
013            progress assumes input features are error-free – a condition rarely met in practice. In
014            real-world scenarios, noisy inputs caused by measurement errors are common, leading to
015            biased or suboptimal classification results. This paper presents a unified framework for bi-
016            nary classification with noisy inputs, offering a generalizable solution that applies across
017            various supervised learning algorithms and noise models. We provide a theoretical anal-
018
               ysis of the bias introduced by ignoring input noise (also referred to as feature corruption)
               and identify conditions where this bias can be safely disregarded. To address cases where
019
               noise correction is needed, we propose a novel data augmentation-based method to miti-
020
               gate input noise effects. Our approach is both comprehensive and theoretically grounded,
021            providing practical solutions for improving classification accuracy in noisy data enviro-
022            ments. Extensive experiments, including analyses of medical image datasets, demonstrate
023            the superior performance of our methods under different noise conditions.

026   1     I NTRODUCTION
027
028   With the exponential growth of data across diverse domains, classification techniques have emerged as in-
029   dispensable tools in solving complex problems in fields, such as computer vision (Krizhevsky, Sutskever,
030   and Hinton 2012), information retrieval (Pang et al. 2017), and natural language processing (Howard and
031   Ruder 2018). Widely used methods like logistic regression, support vector machines, boosting, and neural
032
      networks have demonstrated remarkable success in numerous applications (Mohri, Rostamizadeh, and Tal-
      walkar 2018), but much of their effectiveness hinges on the assumption that the input data are clean and
033
      error-free. In practice, this assumption rarely holds. Noisy, imprecise, or corrupted features are prevalent in
034
      practice, creating substantial challenges for these models and leading to suboptimal or biased results.
035
036   One of the key challenges in supervised learning is dealing with noisy input features. While research on
037   handling noisy labels has been prolific – spanning data-cleaning techniques, robust loss functions, and prob-
038
      abilistic methods (e.g., Song et al. 2022), there has been comparatively less attention given to noisy inputs
      (or corrupted features), where measurement errors affect feature values. Addressing noisy inputs remains an
039
      interesting research area, particularly as real-world data collection processes are rarely flawless.
040
041
      1.1   R ELATIVE W ORK
042
043   Research on classification with noisy inputs spans both traditional machine learning algorithms like dis-
044   criminative methods (e.g., Fidler, Skocaj, and Leonardis 2006; Adeli et al. 2018), logistic regression (e.g.,
045   Stefanski and Carroll 1985) and support vector machines (Rabaoui et al. 2008), as well as deep learning
046   techniques, particularly in the context of noisy images. In deep learning, existing methods can be broadly

                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


047
      classified into two categories. The first category involves preprocessing approaches, where denoising tech-
048
      niques are applied to reconstruct clean images from images from noisy ones before passing them through a
049   convolutional neural network (CNN) for classification (e.g., Roy, Ahmed, and Akhand 2018). However, the
050   success of this approach heavily depends on the quality of the denoising step, which introduces additional
051   complexity and uncertainty. A useful example is FROM (Face Recognition with Occlusion Masks) by Qiu et
052   al. (2021), which detects corrupted features using a CNN and dynamically cleans them with learned masks.
053   In the second approach, instead of attempting to recover the clean images, a noise-robust CNN architecture
054   is designed to directly classify the noisy images (e.g., Momeny et al. 2021). This direct approach reduces
055   the dependency on preprocessing and has shown promise in noisy image classification tasks.
056   Beyond deep learning, non-parametric approaches such as Gaussian processes (e.g., Seeger 2002; Kuss,
057   Rasmussen, and Herbrich 2005; Nickisch and Rasmussen 2008; Hernández-lobato, Hernández-lobato, and
058   Dupont 2011; Rodrigues, Pereira, and Ribeiro 2014; Zhao et al. 2021) have garnered increased attention for
059   handling noisy inputs in multi-class classification problems (Villacampa-Calvo et al. 2021). For example,
060   Hernández-lobato et al. (2014) presented a Gaussian process classification method that treats privileged
061   information as noise.
062   Another emerging area is fair classification (e.g., Donini et al. 2018; Huang and Vishnoi 2019; Zafar et
063   al. 2019; Agarwal et al. 2018; Hardt, Price, and Srebro 2016) under noisy conditions, particularly when
064   protected attributes are noisy. Lamy et al. (2019) demonstrated that fairness can still be achieved in classifiers
065   with noisy binary protected attributes, provided specific fairness measures such as the mean-difference score
066   are used. Celis et al. (2021) extended this work to the non-binary case, developing optimization frameworks
067   that enable fair classification even when the protected attributes are noisy.
068   In addition, robust machine learning has been approached by deliberately corrupting features to train models
069   (e.g., Burges and Schölkopf 1996; Globerson and Roweis 2006; Dekel and Shamir 2008; Xu, Caramanis, and
070   Mannor 2009). For example, Bahri et al. (2022) proposed SCARF, a technique for contrastive learning that
071   involves corrupting random subsets of features; Maaten et al. (2013) introduced a robust learning method
072   that corrupts features using noise sampled from known distributions and minimizes the expected loss under
073   the corrupting distribution.
074   Despite these advances, several limitations persist in the current literature. Most existing methods are tai-
075   lored to specific algorithms and often rely on simple input noise models (e.g., additive noise), commonly
076   referred to as measurement error models in the statistical literature (Yi 2017; Yi, Delaigle, and Gustafson
077   2021). Moreover, few studies offer a theoretical framework for analyzing the impact of input noise on clas-
078   sifier performance or propose generalizable correction methods that can be applied across a broad range
079   of classification problems. This leaves open questions about how to effectively handle input nose across
080   different machine learning algorithms and noise structures, especially in large-scale and high-dimensional
081
      settings.

      1.2   O UR C ONTRIBUTIONS
084
085
      In this paper, we take a significant step toward closing these gaps by focusing on binary classification with
086   noisy inputs. We present a unified framework for addressing noisy inputs, with theoretical guarantees and a
087   practical correction method that applies across a wide range of classification algorithms. Our key contribu-
088   tions are as follows:
089
090          • A General Classification Framework with Noisy Inputs: We develop a general framework for binary
091            classification that explicitly accounts for input noise. Using the commonly employed 0-1 loss
092            to evaluate classifiers, we address the computational challenges by utilizing a convex surrogate
093            loss function, denoted as ϕ(·), and use the corresponding ϕ-risk as a metric to evaluate classifier

               performance. This approach provides a flexible, robust framework that extends beyond specific
095
               algorithms or noise models.
096
097          • Theoretical Analysis of Input Noise: We provide a rigorous theoretical analysis of the bias intro-
098            duced by the naive approach that ignores input noise. Our analysis yields an informative upper
099
               bound for the disparity between the generalization error and ϕ-risk of the optimal classifier ob-
               tained from the naive procedure. Notably, this upper bound shrinks as the noise level decreases,
100
               identifying cases where ignoring input noise can be safely disregarded. This result is critical, as it
101
               offers insight into when and how noise affects classifier performance and provides guidance on the
102            conditions under which noise correction is necessary.
103
104          • A Novel Correction Method via Data Augmentation: To mitigate the effect of noisy inputs, we
105
               propose a novel correction method by augmenting the dataset with newly generated data that either
               are precisely measured or contain minimal error. This augmented dataset enables us to devise robust
106
               classifiers that mitigate the bias induced by noisy inputs. Unlike previous methods, our approach
107
               is model-agnostic and can be applied to a broad class of classification algorithms, making it highly
108            versatile for different applications.
109
110          • Extensive Empirical Evaluation: We validate the proposed method through extensive numerical
111
               experiments. We first apply our correction method to a real-world chest X-ray image dataset to
               demonstrate its effectiveness in a practical healthcare setting. We then conduct a series of synthetic
112
               experiments to assess the performance of our method under different noise levels and input distri-
113
               butions. The results consistently demonstrate the superior performance of the proposed method.
114
115   By providing a unified and generalizable approach to handling noisy inputs, this paper makes important
116   contributions to the field of classification, addressing noisy inputs in a comprehensive and theoretically
117   grounded manner.

836   Example C.1. Suppose we have Xi and Xi∗ defined by one of the following four common input noise models.
837
            (1) Additive model:
838
                                                          Xi∗ = Xi + ei ,                                  (C.1)
839
                 where ei is a noise term with zero mean and covariance matrix Σi , and is independent of Xi . In
840
                 this case, we can compute the expected squared difference between Xi and Xi∗ as:
841
842                Di = E{||Xi∗ − Xi ||22 } = E{eTi ei } = E{tr(eTi ei )} = E{tr(ei eTi )} = tr(E{ei eTi }) = tr(Σi ),
843              where tr(·) denotes the trace of a matrix, the second equality is due to the fact that eTi ei is a scalar,
844              the third equality is due to the property that tr(AB) = tr(BA) for any matrices A and B, and the
845              fourth equality is due to the fact that tr(·) is a linear operator.

                                                           18
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


846
          (2) Berkson model:
847
                                                             Xi = Xi∗ + e∗i ,                                          (C.2)
848
849            where e∗i is noise with zero mean and covariance matrix Σ∗i , and is independent of Xi∗ . Similarly,
850            the expected squared difference is given by:
851
               Di = E{||Xi∗ −Xi ||22 } = E{e∗i T e∗i } = E{tr(e∗i T e∗i )} = E{tr(e∗i e∗i T )} = tr(E{e∗i e∗i T }) = tr(Σ∗i ).
852
853
          (3) Multiplicative model: Xi∗ = Xi ei , where ei is a scalar noise term with mean 1 and variance σi2
854
              and is independent of Xi .
855
856            For i = 1, · · · , n, let µ2i = E(XiT Xi ). Then the expected squared difference is
857
                                         Di = E{||Xi∗ − Xi ||22 } = E{(ei − 1)2 XiT Xi }
858
859                                                                  = E{(ei − 1)2 }E{XiT Xi }
860                                                                  = σi2 µ2i ,
