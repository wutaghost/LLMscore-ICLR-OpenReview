009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Membership Inference Attacks (MIAs) can be used by model owners to identify
012            privacy leakage of specific points in their machine learning models. In this setting,
013            the model owner (who is playing the role of the attacker) has perfect knowledge of
014            the training data but a limited computational budget. However, current MIAs have
015            limited effectiveness in this scenario: 1) They perform poorly against the most
016            vulnerable points 2) They require training too many models. To overcome this
017            weakness, we modify two limitations, in the initial/upstream stages of the MIA
018            framework, namely sampling bias (i.e., too many points dropped during sampling)
019
               and attack aggregation (i.e., average attack results over all the data points instead
               of only the most vulnerable ones). Our improvements carryover downstream and
020
               boost attack accuracy of existing MIAs by increasing the TPR of existing attacks
021
               at incredibly low FPRs (as low as zero) while achieving a near-perfect AUC. As
022            a consequence, our modifications enable the practical and effective application of
023            MIAs for identification of data-leakage in machine learning models.

070
      The first limitation we encounter is at Step 2 of the framework (Figure 2), which we call sampling
      bias (Section 3.1). This is a result of dropping too many points during data set generation. In
071
      other words, when the drop rate is high, the attacks perform poorly for low FPRs. We find that
072
      lowering the drop rate can significantly improve attack success. For example, lowering the rate
073   from 50%, commonly used in existing works Carlini et al. (2022); Zarifzadeh et al. (2024); Ye et al.
074   (2022), to just 10% improves attack accuracy by 2×. One might be tempted to mitigate sampling
075   bias by simply reducing the drop rate during data set generation. However, this will increases the
076   number of data set partitions, and therefore, requires training more shadow models, ballooning the
077   computational costs. For example, a 50% drop rate will result in 100%/50% = 2 partitions, while
078   a drop rate of 10% results in 100%/10% = 10 partitions. In order to address this issue, we develop
079   a robust partitioning method that uses a small drop rate without increasing the number of partitions.
080   We discuss this method towards the end of this Section.
081   The second limitation we observe is at Step 4 of the framework (Figure 2), known as attack aggre-
082   gation (Section 3.2). This is a well known issue in existing MIAs (not just shadow model ones), and
083   is defined as the averaging of attack accuracy over all the points in the data set Carlini et al. (2022);
084   Steinke & Ullman; Aerni et al. (2024). Instead, MIAs should focus only on the most vulnerable
085   ones Carlini et al. (2022); Steinke & Ullman. Therefore, our contribution is to develop means to
086   identify these vulnerable points, without having to run a MIA in the first place. We find that outlier
087
      detection methods perform exceptionally by improving TPR, by as much as, 11× at zero FPR while
      achieving near perfect AUC, even for large datasets like CIFAR-100 and Tiny Imagenet.
088
089   Having described both limitations, we now combine our modifications to ensure maximal attack
090   accuracy. We devise a sampling strategy that limits the number of partitions while simultaneously
091   using a small drop rate. Specifically, instead of creating data partitions from the entire set, we only
092   sample from the most vulnerable points (i.e., the outliers) (Section 4). After doing so, we perform an
093
      extensive evaluation of our modified framework across different attacks and datasets. We find that
      our modifications not only help achieve near perfect AUC but also near perfect TPR at zero FPR,
094
      using only a single shadow model. In fact, a single shadow model with our modified framework can
095
      out perform the original framework that uses 100 shadow models. To add to that, since our approach
096   makes upstream modifications, it gives us the unique ability to easily integrate with, and boost the
097   accuracy of, existing MIAs.

159   2.2   S HADOW M ODEL F RAMEWORK
160
161   The most effective and popular means of playing the MIA game is via the use of the shadow model
      framework Salem et al. (2018); Zarifzadeh et al. (2024); Carlini et al. (2022); Watson et al. (2021);


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Long et al. (2020); Sablayrolles et al. (2019); Song & Mittal (2021); Jayaraman et al. (2020); Shokri
163   et al. (2017); Yeom et al. (2018). It involves training local (or shadow) models to mimic the target
164   model, using auxiliary data from the same distribution as the target. In doing so, an attacker can
165   understand how the target model might behave when a data point is present or absent, enabling
166   accurate membership inferences.
167
      Specifically, this framework, shown in Figure 2, involves the following steps:
168
169          • Step 1 - Collect Data Set: The first step is to collect an auxiliary data set from the same
170            distribution as the target model’s training data. This data serves as a substitute for the
171            actual training data of the target model, which the attacker typically does not have access
172            to. Auxiliary data can be obtained from public data sets, augmentation, or similar sources.
173
             • Step 2 - Generate Data Partitions: Split the data into multiple subsets or partitions. These
174            are used for training shadow models to simulate different scenarios of what the target model
175            might have been trained on. The more the partitions, the more the shadow models. In the
176            ideal world, subset generation should only remove a single point from the dataset (or run
177            a leave-one-out test). This will provide a precise understanding of how the model behaves
178            when a single point is removed. However, this setup is computationally infeasible for
179            even small datasets and models as it requires training as many models as points in the
180            set. To overcome this issue, data set generation partitions the data by dropping a specific
181            percentage of random points. As we discuss in the next Section, current MIAs drop too
182
               many points during partitioning, leading to a sampling bias, and consequently, poor MIA
               results. Therefore, we modify this step to improve MIA accuracy.
183
184          • Step 3 - Train Shadow Models: Train each shadow model on one of the partitions gen-
185            erated in the previous step. To improve the accuracy of the MIA, authors train multiple
186            shadow models per query, i.e., the number of models with a given point present/absent
187
               from the training data. The total number of shadow models that the attacker will need to
               train is partitions × models per query. The number of models per query can range from
188
               a few dozen Zarifzadeh et al. (2024) to a few hundred Carlini et al. (2022). Fortunately, we
189
               find that our proposed modifications can out perform existing the original framework while
190            requiring only a single model per query.
191
192          • Step 4 - Get Shadow Model Predictions: For each data point in the dataset, collect the
193
               models’ predictions, confidence scores, loss values, or gradient information, etc. These are
               then used by the final step to predict whether or not a given point was present in the training
194
               data. As we discuss in the next Section, current MIAs collect predictions over all the points
195
               in the set. Instead, we modify this step to focus on only the most vulnerable points, thereby
196            improving MIA accuracy.
197
198
             • Step 5 - Hypothesis Test: Finally, the attacker uses statistical tests to over model predic-
               tions to predict whether a data point was a present or absent during training. Most MIA
199
               papers focus on this step Salem et al. (2018); Zarifzadeh et al. (2024); Carlini et al. (2022);
200
               Watson et al. (2021); Long et al. (2020); Sablayrolles et al. (2019); Song & Mittal (2021);
201            Jayaraman et al. (2020); Shokri et al. (2017); Yeom et al. (2018), modifying the statistical
202            tests to scale up attack accuracy without having to train additional models.
203

      3.1   S AMPLING B IAS
232
233
      Limitation: The first limitation we identify is at Step 2 of the MIA framework (Figure 2). This
234
      step generates data partitions by dropping random points from the dataset. In most attacks, this drop
235   rate is around 50% Carlini et al. (2022); Zarifzadeh et al. (2024); Ye et al. (2022). This means,
236   for a set of 50,000 points (e.g., CIFAR-10), the MIA algorithm will drop at least 25,000 to create
237   a single partition. This leads to significant issue: The more points we drop, the further we stray
238   from the idealized leave-one-out experiment. As consequence, dropping too points can remove
239   entire sub-populations, leading to a sampling bias Abdullah (2024). If the partition does not contain
240   points from the small sub-population, the corresponding shadow models trained in the next step
241   stage (Step 3) will produce biased outputs (Step 4), resulting in poor attack accuracy (Step 5). As a
242   result, modifying this step has the potential to improve all the downstream tasks.
243   Modification: We hypothesize that these biased predictions will result in a low power at a low FPR.
244   Therefore, in order to overcome the issue, we need to drop fewer points during sampling.
245
      Setup: To evaluate this hypothesis, we follow the training parameters laid out in previous work Car-
246
      lini et al. (2022); Zarifzadeh et al. (2024). We use different drop rates (50%, 40%, 30%, 20%, 10%)
247   and record the corresponding change in attack success. Due to the computationally intensive nature
248   of this experiment (repeating it for different drop rates), we run the evaluation only over CIFAR-10,
249   a benchmark dataset commonly used for MIA Carlini et al. (2022); Zarifzadeh et al. (2024); Aerni
250   et al. (2024). While we do use more complex datasets (CIFAR-100 and Imagenet) in the rest of
251   the paper, our goal for this experiment is to reveal limitations in the existing framework. Therefore,
252   using just a single dataset is sufficient, at least for this experiment Aerni et al. (2024). Next, we train
253   RESNET-18 models on each of the partitions. We use a batch size of 512, with a triangular learning
254   rate of 0.4, and weight decay of 5e4 . We use the FFCV library Leclerc et al. (2023) to improve the
255
      training speed. Having trained the models, we now run the RMIA Zarifzadeh et al. (2024), Attack-
      R Ye et al. (2022), and LiRA Carlini et al. (2022) attack, which is the most effective one in the
256
      current literature. We use attack implementations available in the Privacy Meter repository Kumar
257
      & Shokri (2020). We use the standard metrics, notably FPR versus TPR curve, and the Area Under
258   the Curve (AUC), for analyzing attack success. Unless otherwise specified, we use these models and
259   metrics for all the experiments of this work.

      Limitation: The next limitation we identify is at Step 4 of the framework. Here, the MIA extracts
      the output statistics (such as output confidence, logit value, etc) from the shadow models. Usually,
285
      MIAs generate the output for every point in the data set. However, recent work has shown that
286
      privacy is not an average case metric and an ideal MIA should only focus on the most vulnerable
287   points Carlini et al. (2022); Steinke & Ullman; Aerni et al. (2024). While the idea itself is well
288   known, what is not clear is how to identify these vulnerable points in the first place.
289
290
      There are a few simple (yet flawed) approaches one could take here. For example, recent work has
      proposed using of artificial canaries to mimic vulnerable data Aerni et al. (2024). However, this
291
      approach is unsound for three reasons reasons. First, artificial points do not represent the actual
292
      training distribution. Therefore, findings from artificial studying artificial points might not even ap-
293   ply to real data Abdullah et al. (2023). Second, MIAs are used for auditing model privacy. Model
294   owners are more concerned with privacy exposure of existing training points instead of the artifi-
295   cial ones. Lastly, training models on artificial data can hurt generalization, thereby limiting model
296   utility Arpit et al. (2017); Raghunathan et al. (2019).
297
      Another simple approach would be to run the MIA to extract the most vulnerable points. However,
298
      this too has a number of serious limitations. First, under the current framework, MIAs require
299   training many additional models, making it infeasible for any real-world use case. Second, current
300   MIAs themselves have a number of limitations, which is the main point of this work. Therefore,
301   using MIA for outlier detection is flawed1 .
302
      Modification: To that end, we hypothesize that outliers constitute the most vulnerable points to
303
      membership inference. Therefore, we can significantly improve MIA success by first identifying
304
      and then attacking only the outlier points.
305
306   MIA and Outliers vs Inliers: Before we evaluate our hypothesis, let’s first discuss the relationship
307   between outliers, inliers, and membership inference. An outlier is a data point that significantly
308
      deviates from the other observations in a dataset. Outliers can include: rare points (e.g., a single
      pink cat in a dataset of white cats), malformed inputs (e.g., a blurry or garbled cat), or even mis-
309
      labeled points (e.g., a cat mislabeled as an ORANGE), etc. Outliers have few (if any) overlapping
310
      features with the remaining points in the label set (i.e., rare, malformed, and mislabeled inputs do
311   not look like the other images in the label set). As a result, the features learned from the rest of the
312   label distribution are not useful for classifying these points Feldman (2020); Abdullah et al. (2023);
313   Abdullah (2024). Therefore, the model ability to classify these points correctly is largely subject to
314   their inclusion in the training data. In other words, the shadow model output for outliers will differ
315   based on whether these points were present in the training data. For example, the cat mislabeled as
316   an ORANGE will likely be classified as an orange when it is present in the shadow model data but
317   might be classified as CAT when absent. As a result, membership inference of outliers is easiest
318   compared to other points in the rest of the dataset Carlini et al. (2022; 2023; 2021); Somepalli et al.
319
      (2023); Ye et al. (2022).
320   In contrast, inlier points are the least vulnerable to membership inference attacks. They are classified
321   correctly even when absent from the dataset. This is because these points have overlapping features
322   with the rest of the distribution. Features learned from remaining points in the dataset are sufficient to

393   4     C OMBINING M ODIFICATION
394
395   In the last section, we explored the limitations of the existing framework and proposed two modifi-
396   cations to mitigate them. The first involves reducing the drop rate during dataset partitioning (Step
397   2 Figure 2). The second involves running the MIA against only the outliers, instead of the entire
398   dataset (Step 4 Figure 2). In this section, we combine these modifications to achieve maximal MIA
399   performance and then compare the results against the original framework.
400
401   4.1   C OMBINING M ODIFICATIONS
402
403   The simple (yet flawed) strategy to combine the modifications would be to first generate partitions
404   using a small drop rate, train the models, and get the output statistics for only the vulnerable points.
405   For example, use a drop rate of 5% to produce 100/5 = 20 partitions. Train one shadow model per
406
      query on each partition, resulting in 20 models. Subsequently, use each models output statistics to
      execute the MIA. However, this strategy has a clear limitation as it requires training 20 models.
407
408   We propose to fix this issue by creating partitions from only vulnerable points. Specifically, 1) we
409   train a single model on all points in the dataset of size k and identify the vulnerable ones (i.e.,
410   outliers). 2) We split the dataset into two sets: vulnerable v and non-vulnerable points v ′ , where
411   k = v + v ′ . 3) During dataset generation, we sample from only the vulnerable points by randomly
412
      dropping a fraction of them. If the drop rate is d, then each partition will be of size (1 − d) · v. 4)
      We append the non-vulnerable set to the vulnerable partitions. Each data shard will contain all the
413
      non-vulnerable points but only a sub-sample of the vulnerable ones. As a result, each partition will
414
      be of the size v ′ + (1 − d) · v.
415
416   This setup will overcome the aforementioned limitations. If we select the 5,000 vulnerable points, a
417   drop rate is 50% over them will result in 100/50 = 2 partitions of 2,500 points each. This is 5% of
418
      the total dataset for CIFAR-10/CIFAR-100. This means we can use a drop rate of 5% over the total
      data but only have to train two models, instead of 20.

      Why do our improvements perform better on large datasets than the smaller ones?
491
492   One interesting observation to note is that attack accuracy for CIFAR-10 using our method is slightly
493   lower than for CIFAR-100 and Tiny-Imagenet. A natural question that emerges is, why? Popular
494   belief would indicate that larger datasets should be harder to attack Zarifzadeh et al. (2024); Bertran
495   et al. (2024). Instead, our experiments demonstrate the contrary. Fortunately, a simple explana-
496
      tion exists. Recent work from the space of memorization has shown that models memorize more
      points from larger, more complex datasets (e.g., CIFAR-100, Tiny-Imagenet) Abdullah et al. (2023).
497
      However, when the dataset is small and simple (e.g., CIFAR-10), models can generalize better, re-
498
      sulting in less memorization. Since there is more memorization in large datasets, more of these
499   points should be vulnerable to MIAs. Our results corroborate these findings by showing that the top
500   outliers are easily detectable using MIAs.
501
502

503   An astute reader will remember that our modification in Step 4 requires training one additional
504   model on the full data to identify the outlier points. Having demonstrated the overwhelming efficacy
505   of our attacks, a natural question that emerges is whether the cost is worth the improvement in attack
506   success. Therefore, we compare the modified attack with just one shadow model per query (plus one
507   extra model for outlier detection) against an original attack with 100 shadow models per query in
508
      Table 3. Even in this extreme case, where the original framework has two orders of magnitude more
      shadow models, our modified attack (with just one shadow model) wins by significant margins. In
509
      fact, the original framework fails to reach 100% accuracy across any metric. In contrast, our method
510
      achieves perfect AUC and TPR at zero FPR scores across multiple datasets.
511
512   How can the model owner identify all the leaked points?
513   While this paper only targets the top 5,000 for evaluation purposes, it is easy to see how a model
514   owner can extend it to the rest of the dataset. Table 2 shows that the stronger a point’s outlier score,
515   the more vulnerable it is to membership inference. The attacker can use this leverage this knowledge
516   to asses overall leakage: 1) Select the top N points, where N is less than dataset size, 2) partition
517   the N points, 3) train the shadow models, and 4) run the MIA. Up to now, the attacker is doing
518   exactly what we do in Section 4. However, in order to evaluate the privacy of the remaining points,
519   the attacker can now select the next N points and repeat steps 1-4. The attacker can continue to do
520
      this until they start noticing a significant reduction in attack success. At this point, the attacker has
      reached the inlier points and may decide not to proceed further. This enables the attacker to use
521
      their computational resources efficiently: Spend computation on attacking only the outliers without
522
      wasting computation on inliers.

      5   C ONCLUSION
526
527
528
      In our work, we demonstrate the effectiveness of modifying the initial stages of the shadow model
      framework to improve MIA accuracy. Despite advancements, current state-of-the-art methods do
529
      not fully leverage the complete availability present when a model owner wants to evaluate data
530
      leakage on a limited computation budget. To overcome this challenge, we address two critical issues
531   in the initial stages of the MIA process: sampling bias and attack aggregation. By resolving them,
532   we dramatically enhance the TPR of existing attacks, particularly in low false positive rate FPR,
533   achieving perfect scores across multiple metrics. As a result, our modifications make MIAs far
534   more practical and reliable for assessing privacy risks in machine learning models.
