011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Test-time adaptation (TTA) of classification models, which aims to optimize clas-
014            sifiers without labeled training samples, often employs entropy minimization as
015
               a key objective. While this approach addresses the relationship between model
               performance and prediction confidence or cluster structure, it can lead to model
016
               collapse due to the lack of ground truth labels. This work optimizes activations
017
               within batch normalization (BN) layers for TTA of graph neural networks (GNNs).
018            Our proposed method optimizes BN activations in a two-step process. First, we
019            determine weights and masks for the empirical batch mean and variance, consid-
020            ering training and test data statistics. Subsequently, we refine the scale and shift
021            parameters of the BN layers using a reformulated loss function incorporating an
022            energy-based model, aiming to enhance the model’s generalization capabilities.
023            Our approach leverages pseudo-labels derived from test samples to mitigate the
024            potential forgetting of training data. Empirical evaluation across seven challeng-
025            ing datasets demonstrates the superior performance of our approach compared to
026
               state-of-the-art TTA methods.
027
028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   We study test-time adaptation (TTA) on graph neural networks (GNN) in this paper. TTA addresses
031   the issue of the model’s performance degrading when deployed in a scenario where the target (test)
032   data differs from the training data. This discrepancy restricting the model’s generalization lies in
033   the data distribution shift between the training and the test data. TTA handles the issue by adjusting
034   or fine-tuning the model with respect to the characteristics of the test data during inference before
035   making predictions.
036   Most TTA methods are designed under the context of image classification, where the data distribution
037   shift is usually a consequence of natural variation (Koh et al., 2021) or corruption (Hendrycks
038   and Dietterich, 2019). Under the assumption that the unknown distribution shift is caused by the
039   combination of known variates or corruptions across all domains (Gao et al., 2023), these TTA
040   methods do not work well on graph data where the distribution shift is complicated. Various
041   data distribution shifts exist (Quiñonero-Candela et al., 2022) when deploying GNN models in an
042   environment inconsistent with the one during training, such as full-distribution shift, covariate shift,
043   class-prior shift, class-conditional shift, or simply noise.
044   One SOTA method that could avoid shift-type identification is entropy minimization (EM) (Wang
045   et al., 2021; Press et al., 2024). EM-based methods adapt classifiers by iteratively updating the model’s
046   parameters to minimize the entropy of the model’s predictions, i.e., to maximize the likelihood of the
047   observed data belonging to the most likely classes. However, entropy minimization-based methods
048   have limitations. They often fight hard against the catastrophic forgetting of the ground truth in
049   training data. Due to the lack of ground truth labels, they can further introduce error signals, leading
050
      to increased sensitivity to the learning rate and potential issues like model collapse.
051   The general guideline is to maintain a small divergence between the pre- and post-adaptation models,
052   thereby retaining the model’s inference capabilities acquired from the training data. Common
053   approaches to implementing the general guideline include: (1) fine-tuning only a subset of parameters,
      such as those within batch normalization (BN) layers, (2) introducing regularization based on the

      distance between pre- and post-adaptation parameter values, (3) limiting training epochs or employing
055   small learning rates during adaptation.
056
057   In this paper, our approach for GNN is somewhat similar to optimizing activations in batch normal-
058
      ization layers. A BN layer comprises four parameters: mean (µ), variance (σ 2 ), scale (γ), and shift
      (β). The statistics µ and σ 2 are derived from activations within a batch and maintained by moving
059
      averages for normalization purposes. The parameters γ and β are learned to optimally scale and shift
060
      activations, thereby enhancing the model’s expressive power. Many TTA methods focus on adjusting
061   only the statistics µ and σ 2 , leaving the parameters γ and β unchanged (You et al., 2021; Mirza et al.,
062   2022). However, it is evident that adjusting µ and σ 2 without modifying γ and β may not achieve
063   the optimal scale and shift for the updated statistics. We propose a two-step fine-tuning method to
064   address this limitation. In the first step, we fine-tune the mean (µ) and variance (σ 2 ) parameters based
065   on the activation distribution to better align training and test data. Subsequently, we fine-tune the
066   scale (γ) and shift (β) parameters based on an augmented loss function incorporating an energy-based
067   model, potentially enhancing the generalization ability of the model.
068   Regularizing the distance between pre- and post-adaptation parameter values may seem counterintu-
069   itive (Niu et al., 2022). This approach involves a trade-off: while we aim to modify parameters for
070   adaptation, we also constrain the extent of these changes. This sacrifice of potential improvement is
071   intended to preserve the ground truth from the training data, ensuring it is not entirely forgotten during
072   adaptation. It is the same reason that most methods based on entropy minimization limit the number
073   of training epochs during adaptation (Mounsaveng et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2021; Mummadi et al.,
074   2021; Zhao et al., 2023) to avoid the risk of model collapse (Press et al., 2024): EM-based adaptation
075
      is effective for a few steps but eventually deteriorates performance after prolonged adaptation. They
      appear to save the computational cost, but determining the optimal number of adaptation steps is hard.
076
      To deal with this, we propose utilizing the predictions on test data as pseudo-labels to introduce the
077
      constraint rather than directly constraining the parameters themselves. Additionally, we employ a
078   filtering and pruning mechanism to remove potentially incorrect and harmful pseudo-labels.
079
080   The contributions of this paper are summarized as follows:
081          • We introduce a data-driven, two-step TTA framework for GNNs. This approach first adapts
082            BN layer statistics to the test data distribution. Then, it refines BN layer parameters using
083            a joint energy-based model, overcoming the limitations of existing entropy minimization-
084            based methods.
085          • We propose a data-driven method for determining optimal adaptation weights, leveraging
086            non-parametric density estimation, the Jensen-Shannon divergence, and a learnable mask
087            matrix to effectively balance contributions from training and test statistics. This mask matrix
088            M allows for selective adjustment of specific dimensions within the BN layer, leading to
089            more effective adaptation. (Section 3.1)
090          • We integrate an energy-based model (EBM) into our TTA framework to enhance model
091            generalization and calibration. High-quality soft pseudo-labels are ensured through entropy-
092            based selection and confidence-based filtering. The EBM approach contributes to more
093            reliable adapted predictions and further enhances model calibration. (Section 3.2)
094
      Besides, compared with popular TTA methods, the results from extensive experiments demonstrate
095
      the proposed framework’s effectiveness. It is worth noting that TTA methods that do not require
096   access to the training data are often referred to as Fully TTA methods. While our proposed method
097   maintains a small histogram matrix to store activation distributions in BN layers, this information
098   can be obtained solely by observing the last training epoch. Inherently, BN layers also store the
099   activations’ first and second moments (mean and variance) from the training dataset.

      We evaluate test-time adaptation on the node classification task, where a distribution shift exists
109   between the training and test datasets. Let Y = {y1 , y2 , ..., yC } denote the set of class labels, where
110   yi represents one of the C possible labels. Dtr and Dts represent the training and test datasets,
111   respectively. Let θ denote the parameters of the GNN-based classification model fθ : G → Y , trained
112   on Dtr . TTA is typically performed in an online fashion. Given a graph Gi ∈ Dte , TTA fine-tunes
113   the model parameters θ before inferring the labels of nodes in Gi . The objective is to find improved
114   parameters θ∗ for the model f , such that the updated model fθ∗ can achieve enhanced generalization
115   ability and superior performance on Gi compared to the original model fθ .
116   Our approach for TTA on graph neural networks optimizes activations by fine-tuning the parameters
117   within the batch normalization layers in the GNN architecture. We do not assume a specific type of
118   data distribution shift, as our method operates on the activations within these layers rather than the
119   original data space. Notably, some existing research, such as Jin et al. (Jin et al., 2022b), explores
120   modifying the input graphs for TTA, which we will discuss further in the experimental evaluation
121   section.

151   Recall that BN layers comprise four parameters: mean (µ), variance (σ 2 ), scale (γ), and shift (β). We
152   can divide these parameters into two groups:
153
154          • Statistic group: The mean (µ) and variance (σ 2 ) are estimated from the data and capture the
155            statistical properties of the activations within a batch.
156          • Parameter group: The scale (γ) and shift (β) are learnable and optimized by the loss. They
157            allow the model to adjust the normalized activations to better suit the task at hand.
158
159   Our approach for TTA on GNNs optimizes activations within BN layers by fine-tuning the statistic
160   and parameter groups separately. The process comprises two successive steps: batch normalization
161   statistic adaptation (BNSA) and batch normalization parameter adaptation (BNPA). The overall
      process of BNSA and BNPA is presented in Fig. 1(a) and Fig. 1(b), respectively.

177   3.1     BATCH N ORMALIZATION S TATISTIC A DAPTATION
178
179   BN demonstrates strong empirical performance. However, a comprehensive theoretical understanding
180   of its underlying mechanisms is acknowledged. The original paper (Ioffe and Szegedy, 2015)
181
      proposed that BN reduces internal covariate shift, where the distribution of activations changes across
      layers during training. However, subsequent research, such as Santurkar et al. (Santurkar et al., 2018),
182
      has argued that BN works due to its ability to smooth the optimization landscape significantly.
183
184   While the theoretical foundation of BN is still under no consensus, we focus on a practical observation:
185   the scale (γ) and shift (β) are optimized based on the mean (µ) and variance (σ 2 ) during the training
186   stage. If the values of µ and σ 2 change significantly on the test data, the batch normalization process
187
      using these altered statistics, along with the unchanged γ and β parameters optimized based on the
      original µ and σ 2 , may not perform effectively.
188
189   Several works (Li et al., 2017; You et al., 2021; Lim et al., 2023) have verified that a weighted average
190   of the source (training) and target (test) data statistics can improve model inference, as shown in
191   Eq. 2:
192                                        µ = (1 − α) · µs + α · µt ,
                                                                                                             (2)

      3.2   BATCH N ORMALIZATION PARAMETER A DAPTATION
283
284   Once the optimal mean (µ) and variance (σ 2 ) are obtained through the BNSA step, we proceed to
285   optimize the parameter group: scale (γ) and shift (β). This step further enhances the model’s general-
286   ization ability and overall performance. However, TTA methods that rely on entropy minimization
287   for parameter optimization can be problematic due to the lack of ground truth labels in the test data.
288   These methods can introduce error signals, leading to increased sensitivity to the learning rate and
289   potential issues like model collapse (Choi et al., 2022; Press et al., 2024).
290   To alleviate the issue of ground truth forgetting, we propose utilizing the predictions of the pre-
291   trained model before TTA as pseudo-labels for optimizing the scale (γ) and shift (β) rather than
292   relying on entropy minimization. However, this approach does not address the challenge well, as the
293   pseudo-labels may be inaccurate and lack the guarantee of ground truth.
294
      If the pseudo-labels are largely accurate, they can be confidently used for parameter optimization. To
295
      leverage the model’s confidence across all classes, we propose utilizing soft labels, i.e., the entire
296   softmax output, rather than hard labels (the class with the highest probability). To ensure the selection
297   of high-quality soft pseudo-labels, we employ two strategies:
298
299         1. Entropy-based Selection: We select test instances with low entropy in their softmax outputs.
300            This focuses on instances where the model exhibits high confidence in its predictions.
301         2. Confidence-based Filtering: We retain only the high and low probabilities within the softmax
302            output, discarding intermediate values. This preserves the model’s discriminative ability for
303            classes in which it demonstrates confidence while filtering out less certain predictions.
304
305   However, an implicit assumption underlies these two strategies: the model should be well-calibrated.
306   A calibrated model refers to a model whose predicted probabilities align with the observed frequencies
307
      of the classes. In other words, for a well-calibrated model, if a class is predicted with a probability
      of 0.8, we would expect that class to be the true label approximately 80% of the time. Inspired
308
      by the concept of energy-based models (EBMs) applied to classifiers (Grathwohl et al., 2020), we
309
      incorporate an EBM into our TTA framework to optimize the BN layer’s parameter group (scale and
310   shift). This integration aims to enhance both the model’s generalization ability and its calibration.

454   4.2    A BLATION S TUDIES
455
456   An ablation study was conducted on GCN (see Table 2) to analyze the impact of individual components
457   in our proposed TTA method. Different settings were tested, including using only BN statistic
458   adaptation (BNSA) or BN parameter adaptation (BNPA), variations of BNSA without the adjustment
459   weight A (BNSA w/o A) or mask matrix M (BNSA w/o M ), variations of BNPA without the closest
460   sample selection (BNPA w/o MinSamp) or the entropy and confidence-based selection (BNPA w/o
461
      CEselc).
462   Results highlight the importance of both BNSA and BNPA, as well as the effectiveness of our
463   proposed weighting and selection strategies. Using a fixed weight instead of our proposed distribution
464   shift-based approach negatively impacts performance, validating our weighting strategy. Removing
465   the mask matrix M , which selectively adjusts BN statistics, also decreases performance. Minor but
466   consistent performance decreases are observed when the closest sample in SGLD is not selected, or
467
      the entropy and confidence-based selection strategy is removed. All highlight the importance of these
      components. While the overall improvement of BNSA over BNSA w/o M might seem marginal in
468
      some cases, the mask’s effectiveness extends beyond accuracy improvements. It also contributes to
469
      enhanced model calibration, as Appendix A.4.4 demonstrates. This improved calibration leads to
470   more reliable confidence estimates, which are essential for various downstream tasks.
471
472   To further understand the impact of BNSA, we visualize the activations from the last BN layer when
473
      using BNPA alone and in conjunction with BNSA (Fig. 2). The results indicate that solely utilizing
      BNPA does not yield the same level of class separation as the combined approach. Incorporating
474
      both BNSA and BNPA leads to more distinct and inherent representations for each class.

500   5                        C ONCLUSION
501
502   This work presented a novel two-step approach for test-time adaptation (TTA) of graph neural
503   networks. Our method adapts batch normalization statistics to the test data and refines model
504   parameters using an energy-based model. This approach addresses the limitations of existing methods,
505   improving model generalization and calibration. Empirical evaluation across seven diverse datasets
506
      demonstrates its superior performance compared to state-of-the-art TTA techniques.

704   A.1     R ELATED W ORK
705
706   Test-time adaptation aims to bridge the gap between a model’s training data and the test data of
707   potential data distribution shift, thereby mitigating performance degradation. Usually, in TTA, only
708   unlabeled test data and a pre-trained neural model are available. Existing approaches to fully TTA
709   can be clarified into two primary categories: batch normalization calibration and model optimization.
710
711   A.1.1    BATCH N ORMALIZATION C ALIBRATION
712   BN layers in neural networks typically use statistics of mean and variance calculated from the training
713   data to normalize activations during inference. However, this can be problematic when the test data
714   distribution differs significantly from the training data.
715
      Several methods have been proposed to address this issue: AdaBN (Li et al., 2017) replaces source
716
      statistics with estimates from the entire target domain, arguing that BN statistics can encode domain-
717
      specific information. PredBN (Nado et al., 2020) utilizes current batch statistics for normalization,
718   and PredBN+ (Schneider et al., 2020) combines source and current batch statistics based on batch size.
719   α-BN (You et al., 2021) uses a manually defined hyperparameter to blend source and target statistics.
720   DUA (Mirza et al., 2022) updates BN statistics using a decaying momentum and exponential moving
721   average on the target dataset.
722
      These methods often rely on heuristic weights for combining statistics, limiting their flexibility and
723
      stability. In contrast, our proposed method dynamically adjusts BN statistics using weights based on
724   the magnitude of the distribution shift and a learned mask matrix, enhancing performance on target
725   data with distribution shift.
726
727   A.1.2    M ODEL O PTIMIZATION
728
729   The other category of TTA techniques focuses on optimizing the pre-trained model directly on the
730
      test dataset, using an articulated-designed, unsupervised objective. Methods to optimize the whole
      model are as follows. MEMO (Zhang et al., 2022) minimizes the entropy of average predictions
731
      across augmented views of test samples. CoTTA (Wang et al., 2022) minimizes entropy based on
732
      pseudo-labels derived from weighted and augmentation averaging. AdaContrast (Chen et al., 2022a)
733   employs self-supervised contrastive learning with pseudo-labeling. GAPGC (Chen et al., 2022b)
734   adapts contrastive learning to graph neural networks.
735
736
      Methods to optimize the partial parameters or layers are as follows: TENT (Wang et al., 2021)
      minimizes entropy of predictions on test samples to optimize BN parameters. HLR (Mummadi et al.,
737
      2021) uses an unsaturated proxy loss and discrepancy regularization to optimize BN parameters. T3A
738
      (Iwasawa and Matsuo, 2021) adjusts the classifier using pseudo-prototype representations. PCL (Su
739   et al., 2023) optimizes inter-layer normalization parameters using perturbations in the feature space.
740
741   These methods may face challenges when the pre-trained model performs poorly on the target data,
742
      as unsupervised objectives like entropy minimization or contrastive learning may be less effective
      without access to ground truth labels. To address this, our proposed approach leverages an energy-
743
      based joint model training approach, exploiting the benefits of generative models to select and enhance
744
      the initial model’s predictions simultaneously. This leads to more reliable adapted predictions.
745
746
      A.2     S ETUP
747
748   To ensure a fair comparison of the effectiveness in handling the distribution shift and mitigating
749   performance degradation, we evaluate all methods following the same experimental settings in two
750   recent works, GTRANS (Jin et al., 2022b) and EERM (Wu et al., 2022). We conducted experiments
751   on seven datasets on three GNN backbone models.
752   datasets The datasets contain various distribution shift types, synthetic and natural. According to
753   different shift types, we categorize the seven datasets as follows:
754
755           • Synthetic shift. In Amazon-Photo (Shchur et al., 2018) and Cora (Yang et al., 2016), the
                synthetic shift is involved by adding artificial node features. The added node features are


                                                        14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
               different in each graph, so when the dataset is divided into training, validation, and test
757            samples, they contain different synthetic shifts, which are used to evaluate the model’s
758            generalization ability. Distribution shifts are introduced into the training and testing data
759            to evaluate the model’s ability for out-of-distribution generalization. We use the provided
760            node features for each dataset to construct node labels and spurious environment-sensitive
761            features.
762
763          • Domain shift. In Twitch-E (Rozemberczki et al., 2021), FB-100 (Traud et al., 2012), and
764            OGB-Products (Hu et al., 2020), there are domain shifts. The nodes in different graphs are
765
               from different domains. The implications of such domain shifts are profound, impacting
               the generalizability and performance of models trained on these datasets. The training,
766
               validation, and test samples are from different domains.
767
768          • Temporal shift. Dataset Elliptic (Pareja et al., 2020) and OGB-Arxiv (Hu et al., 2020) contain
769            temporal shifts; graph nodes originate from different periods. This temporal variation implies
770            that the data used for the training, validation, and testing phases are not homogenous with
771            respect to time. So, training, validation, and test samples are from different periods.
772
773   For Amazon-Photo, Cora, Elliptic, FB-100, and Twitch-E, we use the same partition ratio as the one
774   in GTRANS. Specifically, Amazon-Photo and Cora have 1/1/8 graphs for training/validation/test
775   sets. Twitch-E has 1/1/5 graphs, FB-100 has 3/2/3 graphs, and Elliptic has 5/5/33 graphs for
776   training/validation/test sets.
777
      For OGB-Arxiv, more than one-fourth class in the training samples in GTRANS contains only a small
778   number of nodes, which we consider unfair because this is dropping information by purpose. So, we
779   use the suggested partition ratio in the OGB standard for OGB-Arxiv and OGB-Product, which is
780   3/1/1 for OGB-Arxiv and OGB-Products.
