011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            In this paper, we study and analyze zeroth-order stochastic approximation algo-
014            rithms for solving black-box bilevel optimization problems, where only the upper
015
               and lower function values can be obtained. (Aghasi and Ghadimi, 2024) pro-
               posed the first full zeroth-order bilevel method that utilizes Gaussian smoothing to
016
               estimate the first- and second-order partial derivatives of functions with two inde-
017
               pendent blocks of variables. However, this method suffers from a high dimensional
018            dependency of O((d1 + d2 )4 ), where d1 and d2 are the dimensions of the outer
019            and inner problems, respectively. They left an open question: can this dimension
020            dependency be improved? To answer this question, we propose a single-loop ac-
021            celerated zeroth-order bilevel algorithm, which achieves a dimension dependency
022            of O(d1 + d2 ) by incorporating coordinate-wise smoothing gradient estimators
023            (coord). We develop a new theoretical analysis for the proposed algorithm, which
024            converges to a stationary point of Φ(x) with √ −2a complexity of O((d1 + d2 )ϵ−3 )
025            in expectation settings and O((d1 + d2 ) nϵ ) in finite sum settings. These
026
               complexities are both Best-known with respect to dimension and error ϵ. We also
               provide experiment to validate the effectiveness of the proposed algorithm.
027

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   The goal of bilevel optimization is to minimize the upper-level (UL) function f (x, y) under the con-
032   straint that y is minimized with respect to the lower-level (LL) function g(x, y). Bilevel optimization
033   has received increasing attention due to its wide applications in many machine learning problems,
034   including adversarial networks (Goodfellow et al., 2020), hyperparameter tuning (Franceschi et al.,
035   2018), neural architecture search (Liang et al., 2020), meta-learning (Rajeswaran et al., 2019),
036   reinforcement learning (Sutton and Barto, 2018). Formally, it is defined as,
037                          min Φ(x) = f (x, y ∗ (x)),       y ∗ (x) ∈ arg min g(x, y).
038                         x∈Rd1                                       y∈Rd2
039   In this work, we focus on the setting where the lower-level objective g(x, y) is strongly convex in y
040   for any x, and the UL objective f (x, y) is possibly non-convex, which is among the most common
041   in this research field. Since deterministic approaches necessitate the evaluation of the full dataset
042   at every iteration, demanding huge computational resources,so it is needed to consider stochastic
043   method. In many applications of interest, the objective functions f and g have the finite-sum form:
044                                        n                              n
                                        1X                             1X
045                        f (x, y) =         f (x, y; ζi ), g(x, y) =       g(x, y; ξi ),
046
                                        n i=1                          n i=1
047   where ζi and ξi are independent and identically distributed random variables. When dealing potentially
048   infinite number of data samples, f and g are commonly represented as the expectation form:
049
                               f (x, y) = Eζ [f (x, y; ζ)], g(x, y) = Eξ [g(x, y; ξ)].
050
051
      In strongly convex case, the LL problem has unique solution y ∗ (x), by implicit function theorem, we
052   can obtain explicit form of hypergradient ∇Φ(x)(Ghadimi and Wang, 2018),
053
               ∇Φ(x) = ∇x f (x, y ∗ (x)) − ∇2xy g(x, y ∗ (x))[∇2yy g(x, y ∗ (x))]−1 ∇y f (x, y ∗ (x)).   (1)

054
      Since the gradient expression of Φ(x) involves the inverse of the Hessian matrix, it is challenging
055   to employ gradient-based methods to solve bilevel problems. As a result, previous works often
056   required second-order oracles (Dagr’eou et al., 2022; Ji et al., 2020). Fortunately, recent advances
057   have begun addressing this limitation. For example, (Kwon et al., 2023) utilized a penalty method
058   to avoid the second-order term in the gradient, while (Yang et al., 2023; Huang, 2024) employed
059   finite differences to replace the Hessian-vector product. Despite the significant progress in first-order
060   bilevel methods, there are scenarios where zeroth-order methods are still necessary. For various
061   reasons, such as complexity, lack of access to an accurate model, or computational limitations, there
062   may be no or limited access to the objective gradient. In these situations, the common practice is
063
      to approximate the gradient using deterministic or randomized finite difference methods (Shi et al.,
      2021; Nesterov and Spokoiny, 2017). The scope of application for these methods is vast (see the
064
      remainder of this section for a comprehensive summary of applications in machine learning, and
065
      refer to (Hu et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2020) for examples in other areas of engineering). Hence, it is
066   natural to explore zeroth-order bilevel optimization, which requires only function value oracle (or
067   their stochastic samples).
068
069
      Although zeroth-order methods and bilevel optimization have many applications, only a few papers
      have considered zeroth-order bilevel optimization. In recent years, (Sow et al., 2021) explored a
070
      mixed method that utilizes both first-order and zeroth-order oracles. Additionally, (Aghasi and
071
      Ghadimi, 2024) first proposed a fully zeroth-order method via Gaussian smoothing; however, its
072   dependency on the problem dimensions can be O((d1 + d2 )4 ), making it very expensive in practice.
073   Hence, they left an open question:
074
075
076
            Another important direction of research is exploring the dependency of the sample complexity
            on the problem dimensions, as well as the existence of alternative approaches with a smaller
077
            share of dimensionality.
078
079
080   This paper aims to answer this question. We propose a zeroth-order bilevel optimization algorithm
081   called VRZSBO that can achieve O(d1 + d2 ) dependency on dimension by incorporating coordinate
082
      estimators and finite differences.
083   Before formally introducing our method, we would like to first present the core issues in bilevel
084   optimization.

      4.1   I MPROVED D IMENSION D EPENDENCE C OMPARED TO (AGHASI AND G HADIMI , 2024)
400
401   To simplify notation, let d = d1 + d2 .The improved efficiency of our method with respect to
402   dimensionality d stems from two key factors: (1) Our single-loop structure eliminates the inner loop
403   in (Aghasi and Ghadimi, 2024), saving O(d) complexity from solving inner problems. (2) We use a
404   finite-difference approximation for Hessian-vector or Jacobian-vector products, requiring only two
405   gradient evaluations, with O(d) oracle cost. This is significantly more efficient than the Gaussian
406   smoothing method in (Aghasi and Ghadimi, 2024) Proposition 2.5, where approximating the Hessian-
                                                                                      2                    2
407   vector product incurs a variance of O(d3 ) due to term (d + 4)(d + 2) ∇2xx q F = O(d3 ∇2xx q 2 ),
408   leading to O(d3 ) variance for hypergradient estimation and O(d3 ) outer loop iterations.This is slower
409   than our estimation method by a factor of d2 . Combining the two above, our complexity is d3 faster
410   than that of (Aghasi and Ghadimi, 2024).
411
412   4.2   DISCUSSION ON ϵ AND d1 , d2 DEPENDENCY
413
      We first illustrate that both single-level problems and min-max problems are special cases of bilevel
414
      problems. By setting f (x, y) = f (x), we recover a single-level problem, and by taking g(x, y) =
415
      −f (x, y), we obtain a min-max problem. This implies that the lower bounds for these problems are
416   valid for bilevel optimization under the same assumptions.
417
418         1. Our dimension dependency, O(d1 + d2 ), matches the best-known results for simpler min-
419            max problems (Wang et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023). Moreover, our ϵ dependency aligns
420            with the state-of-the-art complexity for first-order nonconvex-strongly convex (NC-SC)
421            bilevel problems (Yang et al., 2023; Chu et al., 2024; Dagréou et al., 2024), establishing our
422
               dependency on ϵ and d1 , d2 as the "best-known" results.
423         2. Since (Duchi et al., 2015) establishes a Ω(d) lower bound for single-level smooth convex
424            problems, which are simpler than NC-SC bilevel problems (e.g., by setting f (x, y) = f (x)),
425            a Ω(d1 ) dependency is inevitable.
426         3. Our assumptions align with those used to derive the lower bound
                                                                           √ in (Dagréou et al., 2024) for
427            NC-SC finite-sum bilevel problems, confirming that the O( nϵ−2 ) dependency is inevitable
428            in the finite-sum case .
429         4. The O(ϵ−3 ) lower bound for single-level problems (Arjevani et al., 2023) in the expectation
430            case is constructed under the mean-square-smooth assumption, which is slightly stronger
431            than the typical assumption of smoothness about smoothness of f (x, y, ξ) for all ξ. Thus,
               strictly speaking, O(ϵ−3 ) is not necessarily optimal in ϵ for the expectation case.

      Althoud based on existing lower bounds, the dependency on d1 is inevitable,however,the optimality
433   of the d2 dependency remains unclear. It would be particularly interesting if the exponent of d2
434   could be reduced to less than 1 for lower level strongly convex optimization, as this would imply
435   a theoretical speedup of zeroth-order algorithms compared to first-order algorithms (assuming the
436   gradient computation cost is O(d1 + d2 )).

      6     C ONCLUSION
524
525
      In this paper, we propose VRZSBO, a zeroth-order algorithm for nonconvex-strongly convex bilevel
526   optimization. Our algorithm improves the best-known complexity from O(ϵ−3 (d1 + d2 )4 ) (Aghasi
527   and Ghadimi, 2024) to O(ϵ−3 (d1 + d2 )), achieving Best-known dependence
                                                                             √         on ϵ and dimension.
528   We also analyze the finite sum case, yielding a complexity of O(ϵ−2 n(d1 + d2 )). Experiments
529   validate the effectiveness of our algorithm. Future work may investigate the use of rand zeroth-order
530   estimators for similar complexity and the extension of zeroth-order methods to additional problems,
531   such as NC-PL bilevel optimization (Kwon et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2024).
