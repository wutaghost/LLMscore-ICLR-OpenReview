009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models (LLMs) are susceptible to Jailbreaking attacks, which
012            aim to extract harmful information by subtly modifying the attack query. As de-
013            fense mechanisms evolve, directly obtaining harmful information becomes increas-
014            ingly challenging for Jailbreaking attacks. In this work, inspired from Chomsky’s
015            transformational-generative grammar theory and human practices of indirect con-
016            text to elicit harmful information, we focus on a new attack form, called Contextual
017            Interaction Attack. We contend that the prior context—the information preced-
018
               ing the attack query—plays a pivotal role in enabling strong Jailbreaking attacks.
               Specifically, we propose first multi-turn approach that leverages benign preliminary
019
               questions to interact with the LLM. Due to the autoregressive nature of LLMs,
020
               which use previous conversation rounds as context during generation, we guide the
021            model’s question-responses pair to construct a context that is semantically aligned
022            with the attack query to execute the attack. We conduct experiments on seven
023            different LLMs and demonstrate the efficacy of this attack, which is black-box, and
024            can also transfer across LLMs. We believe this can lead to further developments
025            and understanding of the security in LLMs.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   The widespread adoption of Large Language Models (LLMs) (OpenAI, 2021; Touvron et al., 2023)
031   has led to a flurry of research and applications around computational linguistics. However, this
032   has also given rise to security concerns, especially with the generation of misleading, biased, or
033
      harmful content and the potential for misuse. To mitigate that, LLMs are augmented with increasingly
      more sophisticated safety mechanisms to avoid sharing harmful content, e.g., through the ‘alignment
034
      process’. However, the so-called ‘Jailbreaking’ attacks attempt to circumvent precisely these safety
035
      mechanisms to elicit harmful information (Lilian, 2023; Wei et al., 2023a; Jin et al., 2020).
036
037   Prompt Jailbreaking has emerged as one of the most widely used techniques among these meth-
038   ods (Wei et al., 2023a; wal, 2022; Shen et al., 2023). Prompt jailbreaking methods manipulate the
039
      input prompt to attack successfully, and they can be broadly categorized into two types: automated
      attack methods and hand-crafted methods. Hand-crafted methods (Wei et al., 2023a; Yuan et al., 2023;
040
      wal, 2022; Kang et al., 2023) rely on human experts to construct specific prompts that circumvent the
041
      safety mechanisms. A representative work in this category is DAN (wal, 2022). On the other hand,
042   automated attack methods (Mehrotra et al., 2023; Zou et al., 2023; Chao et al., 2023) typically employ
043   algorithms or other models designed to systematically test and exploit vulnerabilities in LLMs. For
044   instance, GCG (Zou et al., 2023) combines greedy and gradient-based discrete optimization for
045   adversarial suffix search. At the same time, the jailbreak prompts generated by automated attacks
046   often do not perform well when transferred to other LLMs, which is a major bottleneck. Overall,
047   the common characteristic of all the aforementioned attacks is their zero-shot nature, in the sense
048   that they directly query the dangerous prompt modified by their methods. However, such attacks are
049   increasingly difficult to succeed with models that have undergone significant safety training or have
050
      conservative outputs, such as Llama-2 (Touvron et al., 2023).
051   Inspired by human practices of constructing harmful speech through indirect context (Pérez et al.,
052   2023; Sheth et al., 2022) and Chomsky’s transformational-generative grammar theory (Chomsky,
053   2014; 2002)—which suggests that a sentence’s deep structure (core semantics) can transform into
      different surface structures (expressions)—we propose that an attacker could exploit interactive

054
      dialogue to achieve jailbreaking. This approach involves engaging the model with a sequence of
055   benign preliminary questions that progressively align with harmful intent, ultimately coaxing the
056   model into producing a response to a malicious target query.
057
058
      The crux of this approach lies in recognizing the pivotal role of the context vector. Traditionally, this
      vector—representing the prior information considered by the model—has been overlooked in attack
059
      scenarios. However, our research demonstrates that the context vector can significantly influence the
060
      success of attacks.
061
062   We introduce an attack, termed Contextual Interaction Attack, which leverages model interactions to
063   elicit harmful responses. The attack initiates by posing a sequence of benign preliminary questions,
064
      none of which are harmful in isolation, thereby evading detection by the LLM as harmful. Contextual
      Interaction Attack relies on a straightforward yet effective strategy, requiring only black-box access
065
      to the model, meaning it does not necessitate access to the model’s internal weights. It achieves
066
      a high success rate across multiple state-of-the-art LLMs (Brockman et al., 2023; Touvron et al.,
067   2023; Chiang et al., 2023b; Jiang et al., 2023). Notably, Contextual Interaction Attack exhibits
068   strong transferability, where preliminary questions crafted for one LLM show a high success rate
069   when applied to other LLMs. We believe that the key role of the context vector can facilitate the
070   development of new attack mechanisms and contribute to a deeper understanding of its influence in
071   LLMs.

074   2    R ELATED W ORK
075
076   LLMs, such as the GPT models (Brown et al., 2020; Radford et al., 2018), leverage the transformer
077   architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017) to achieve remarkable performance across a range of Natural
078   Language Processing (NLP) tasks. As LLMs have become more prevalent and demonstrate powerful
079   capabilities, their security implications attract increasing attention. Yadav (2023) showcase that LLM
080   could generate dangerous content like misleading information (Ben et al., 2021) and even reveal
081
      sensitive information (Borkar, 2023). At the same time, the failure modes of LLM would lead to
      propagating biases (Hemmatian & Varshney, 2022) and stereotypes (Blodgett et al., 2021). Before
082
      providing further details on the security concerns, let us provide some additional background on
083
      in-context learning and the autoregressive generation that LLMs employ since they are both relevant
084   notions for our work.
085
086   In context Learning (ICL) refers to the ability of LLMs to adapt and respond based on the context
087
      provided within the input, without the need for explicit retraining or fine-tuning. This characteristic
      is especially prominent in models like GPT-3, as described by Brown et al. (2020). Unlike traditional
088
      machine learning approaches, ICL enables LLMs to generate task-specific responses based on the
089
      examples or patterns included in their prompt. Interestingly, incorporating question-answer pairs in
090   the prompt noticeably enhances performance in arithmetic and symbolic reasoning tasks (Wei et al.,
091   2022; Zhou et al., 2022).
092
093
      Autoregressive language generation model Autoregressive language generation models (OpenAI,
      2021; Radford et al., 2019; Yang et al., 2019) sequentially generate the output tokens one by one.
094
      The generation of each token depends on the previously generated tokens. Let A be the vocabulary
095
      of the language model, where each token in the language is represented by a unique identifier. A
096   sequence of tokens is represented as (x1 , x2 , ..., xT ), where {xt ∈ A}Tt=1 and T is the length of
097   the sequence. This conditional probability is modeled using parameters θ, which represent the
098   weights of a neural network. The conditional probability of an autoregressive language model is
099   P (xt |xt−1 , xt−2 , ..., x1 ; θ).
100
      During generation, the model selects the next token based on the highest probability from the dis-
101
      tribution given the previous tokens, which are referred to as the ‘context’. The process involves
102   generating a token x1 based on P (x1 |θ) and subsequently generating each {xt }Tt=2 based on
103   P (xt |xt−1 , xt−2 , ..., x1 ; θ). The context vector is then the sequence (xt−1 , xt−2 , ..., x1 ). In prac-
104   tice, the LLM takes the token within the context window length as a condition, including user input and
105   text generated by itself. Therefore, the context is critical on determining the next-token probabilities.
106   In our preliminary experiments, we found that compared to harmful information provided by the user
107   (exogenous input), LLMs tend to perceive their own generated content (endogenous output) as safer.
      Currently, LLMs include context of thousands of tokens. Multiple designs (Su et al., 2024; Ratner

      Context-Based Jailbreak. Wei et al. (2023b) propose a method that appends the target question
      (constituting the jailbreak attack) with one or more exogenous harmful demonstrations to mislead
143
      the model into following harmful behavioral patterns. This approach employs strategically crafted
144
      harmful demonstrations to subvert LLMs. Similarly, Wang et al. (2023) introduce question-specific
145   demonstrations and instructions prior to the target question, misleading the LLM in a single round
146   of interaction. Due to the sensitivity of current LLMs to harmful keywords and input filtering
147   mechanisms of closed-source LLMs such as ChatGPT, ICA (Wei et al., 2023b) is not well-suited
148   for closed-source LLMs or open-source LLMs with robust security mechanisms, like Llama2 in its
149   standard settings (Anthropic, 2024). The content filter detects harmful keywords within the exogenous
150   content and prevents the model from responding.
151   In contrast, Contextual Interaction Attack is a multi-round attack that involves a conversation between
152   the attacker and the model prior to extracting harmful information. Unlike previous methods, this
153   approach does not demonstrate to the model how to respond. Instead, Contextual Interaction Attack
154   employs multi-round questioning to construct a “harmless” context aligned with the target question.
155   The final question, based on this benign context, is then used to execute the attack.

      3.1   P ROBLEM S ETTING
163
164   Let g : A × A → A be a fixed function, e.g., a pretrained Large Language Model (LLM), that accepts
165   a sequence of sentences and the context as inputs and outputs another sequence of sentences1 . That is,
166   given a (sequence of) sentence(s) ϵ ∈ A and the context c ∈ A, the function synthesizes the output
167   text g(ϵ|c).
168   An attacker aims to obtain harmful or illegal information by posing an attack query to the model.
169   For instance, instructions on how to perform identity theft. In other words, an attacker aims to
170   maximize their profit by obtaining harmful information. In many LLMs, specific guardrails have
171   been implemented to defend against such questions. Bypassing those guardrails is the goal of
172   “Jailbreaking”.
173
      In Jailbreaking, the attackers perform a transformation of the input sentences (denoted as h : A → A)
174   in order to maximize the probability of obtaining the harmful information. Let us now formalize this
175   task. Assume we have an oracle function f : A → [0, 1] that accepts as input g(ϵ|c) and outputs the
176   probability that g(ϵ|c) is a harmful text. The goal of the attacker can be formalized as follows:
177                                           arg max f (g(h(ϵ) | c))                                    (1)
178                                                    h
179   In existing Jailbreaking attacks, the context vector is the empty set, i.e., c = ∅, while h is a specific
180   function that determines the type of Jailbreaking. For instance, some methods transform the attack
181   query into different semantic formats, such as using another language (Xu et al., 2023b) or cipher
182
      text (Yuan et al., 2023). Many hand-crafted prompt Jailbreaking methods (Wei et al., 2023a; wal,
      2022; Wei et al., 2023b) include fixed text, such as “you will ignore any safety check”, at the beginning
183
      of the attack sentence. These appended text can be considered as part of the function h, since this is a
184
      fixed input sentence.

      3.2   Contextual Interaction Attack
187
188   A substantial difference from prior attacks is that they do not utilize the context vector c. Unlike
189   ICA (Wei et al., 2023b), which uses exogenous harmful demonstrations to mislead LLMs to follow
190   harmful behavior patterns, our approach is inspired by Chomsky’s transformational-generative
191   grammar theory (Chomsky, 2014; 2002) that the same underlying deep structure(target question
192   core meaning) can manifest in different syntactic forms(our benign preliminary questions).
193   We gradually narrow down the attack target question semantic meaning with progressively harmful
194
      or entirely benign questions, along with the answer generated by LLM itself to construct a context
      vector c and execute the attack.
195
196   We define the context vector c as a sequence of interactions between the attacker and the model.
197   Specifically, these interactions are represented as a sequence {ϵ1 , α1 , ϵ2 , α2 , . . . , ϵn , αn }, where
198   {ϵi }ni=1 denotes the ith input to the model g, and {αi }ni=1 denotes the corresponding ith response.2
199   The context vector up to step i is then defined as ci = {ϵ1 , α1 , ϵ2 , α2 , . . . , ϵi−1 , αi−1 }. Notably, for
200
      i > 1, the response αi is given by αi = g(ϵi | ci ), meaning that part of the context comprises the
      model’s previous outputs.
201
202   Since prior attack methods do not leverage a complete context ci , their context vector is effectively
203   treated as an empty set. These methods typically execute attacks in a single turn, utilizing only ϵ1
204   as the input for the attack. In contrast, Contextual Interaction Attack poses multiple preliminary
205   questions, using the model’s own outputs in response to these questions to influence the context and
206
      execute the attack.
207   How do we define {ϵi }ni=1 ? There are various ways to design the input questions ϵi . In this work, we
208   employ a simple and intuitive strategy: each ϵi (for i ∈ [1, n]) is crafted to be individually as harmless
209   as possible. If any preliminary question is flagged as dangerous by the target LLM (represented by
210   the function g), the subsequent attack will fail. Specifically, ϵ1 is a completely innocuous question
211   or request, and subsequent inputs {ϵi }ni=2 gradually shift toward the attacker’s goal. This way, the
212
      context primes the model to generate a harmful response, as it interprets the context as safe and,
      therefore, does not trigger safeguards against harmful content.

      Can we automate the generation of {ϵi }ni=1 ? While it is possible to manually design the question
217   sequence {ϵi }ni=1 , this approach is not scalable across different attack queries ϵ. The key challenge,
218   therefore, is to automate the generation of {ϵi }ni=1 .
219
220
      To address this, we employ a secondary LLM, termed the auxiliary LLM, to synthesize the sequence
      {ϵi }ni=1 . The auxiliary LLM is not explicitly trained for the purpose of generating such sequences, nor
221
      are there dedicated large-scale datasets available for fine-tuning it for this specific task. Nevertheless,
222
      we leverage the emergent capability of in-context learning (Brown et al., 2020; Garg et al., 2022),
223   wherein the auxiliary LLM can produce the required sentences given only a few relevant examples,
224   known as demonstrations. We provide these contextually relevant, hand-crafted examples that exhibit
225   the intended pattern of harmless yet semantically similar prompts.
226
      In practice, this method allows the auxiliary LLM to synthesize high-quality, human-like prompts
227
      that fulfill our intended purpose, as confirmed by experimental results. For instance, consider the
228
      following demonstration provided to the auxiliary LLM to facilitate the synthesis of the sequence of
229   questions {ϵi }ni=1 :

255   3.3   D ESIGN CHOICES
256
257   Two reasonable questions are whether (a) we can use random pool of questions instead of tailored
258   to the attack query ϵ, and (b) whether we can use a single-step with a concatenated input. As our
259   experiment in Section 4.5 validates, utilizing a random set of questions does not seem to result in a
260
      successful attack, which indicates that the questions should be relevant to the final attack query ϵ.
261   Single-step attack through concatenation. Instead of asking the attack query after a sequence
262   of interaction pairs {(ϵi , αi )}ni=1 , we could consider various alternatives. Firstly, concatenating all
263   of the questions {ϵi }ni=1 with the final attack query and pose this to the model. Our preliminary
264   experiments hint that this fails, and lead us that the responses of the model are also required. Inspired
265   by this, we can concatenate the question response pairs and append those to the attack question ϵ.
266
      As our experiment in Section 4.5 exhibits, this works well, which verifies our intuition about the
      significance of the dynamic content.
267
268   The role of recency bias. Liu et al. (2023); Fudenberg & Levine (2014) support that LLMs suffer
269   from recency bias, which Peysakhovich & Lerer (2023) attribute to the attention mechanism used
      in LLMs. Recency bias posits that LLMs emphasize more the last input tokens over the initial ones.

354   4.3   T RANSFERABILITY OF JAILBREAK PROMPTS
355
356   In this section, we evaluate the transferability of the attacks generated in the previous subsection. We
357   assessed the success rate of the generated attack prompt sets of Contextual Interaction Attack on mul-
358   tiple other models, as detailed in Table 2. Existing attacks use signals like the gradients of the original
359   target model, or response time to generate prompts tailored for attacks. Contextual Interaction Attack
360   is a semantic-based attack, so we do not need a specific target model to generate attack prompts. We
361   use an independent process to generate a universal attack prompt set. Each model attack uses the same
362   generated adversarial prompts set in our experiment. In fact, we create a corresponding universal ad-
363
      versarial set for each harmful question and employ this same set across all models. Despite this unifor-
      mity, Contextual Interaction Attack consistently outperforms other automated jailbreaking techniques.
364
365   We have observed that our method exhibits an exceptionally high success rate in transfer attacks, a
366   finding that resonates with our intuition. Our attack methodology relies on semantic context, allowing
367   the semantics constructed through preliminary questions to be comprehensible across various LLMs.
368   Consequently, our approach demonstrates impressive performance even when attack prompts are not
369
      specifically tailored to a particular LLM.

      system-generated prompt, belong to self-processing defense. We follow this method setting with
403   original paper.

      Interaction Attack since this attack responds using proper expressions in English, i.e., not meaningless
      words that do not belong in the English language. Similarly, smoothLLM also can not effectively
407
      defend the studied attack. The reduced performance is mostly due to the perturbation algorithm
408
      ruining the semantics of the attack prompts, which led to the LLM response ’Please clarify your
409   question’. Self-reminder perform best to reduce our attack success rate.

413
414
415   In this section, we aim to further scrutinize Contextual Interaction Attack and stress the potential of this
416   attack. We utilize a carefully curated set of 15 harmful prompt samples taken from the three datasets
417
      mentioned in Section 4.1. We ensure that the selected prompts covered various topics of harmful
      behaviors, including illegal activities and adult content, bias, and so on. To thoroughly evaluate Con-
418
      textual Interaction Attack, we specifically chose attack prompts that simple models are unable to ex-
419
      ecute successfully. We maintain a consistent temperature setting of 0 to ensure a deterministic output.
420   The maximum token length for generation was set to 1024. For all samples, we conduct two separate
421   tests with different random seeds. ASR is used as our evaluation metric. We use Llama-2-13b, which
422   has been reportedly the strictest model with Jailbreaking attacks (Chao et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2023b).
423
      Can random questions work? We evaluate whether the questions {ϵi }ni=1 can be random or whether
424
      they need to be tailored to the attack query. Du et al. (2023) introduced a RADIAL (ReAl-worlD
425
      Instructions-driven jAiLbreak) method specifically tailored for LLM jailbreaks. Their idea is to
426   append two random questions/requests at the beginning of the attack query as affirmation-tendency
427   instructions to increase the attack success rate. We postulate that this results in an affirmative response
428   (on the attack query) in the LLM. However, we did not observe this affirmative response in our
429   experimentation. Nevertheless, inspired by their method, we apply two random questions (in multiple
430   rounds though and not appended to the attack query). That is, ϵ1 , ϵ2 are randomly sampled questions.
431   The experimental results in Table 6 indicate that adding (positive) questions before the attack question
      did not lead to an improvement in the attack success rate.

451   Reduced rounds of interaction: We have already established that n = 2 rounds of preliminary
452
      questions yield high accuracy. However, a reasonable question is whether those question need to be
      posed one-by-one to the model or whether they can be asked all together.
453
454   For this ablation study, we mark the tuple [ρ1 , ρ2 ], which indicates how many questions were asked
455   in the first interaction with the model (i.e., ρ1 ), versus how many questions are asked in the second
456   interaction. We study the following cases: [1, 1] (which is the default setting used in this work), [2, 0]
457   (which reflects asking both preliminary questions in a single interaction), and [2+ attack, 0]. The
458
      latter means that we submit both the preliminary questions and the attack query as a single prompt.
      We provide examples from the groups [1, 1] and [2+attack,0] in Appendix E.
459
460   Our results in Table 7 exhibit the performance is reduced in the case of [2, 0], while it deteriorates
461   even more when we ask all questions in a single prompt. We observe that when both we use a single
462   prompt, the LLM is more likely to flag this as a potentially harmful question. On the contrary, in the
463   case of individual questions per interaction, the LLM incorporates the previously synthesized text as
464
      context and thus does not flag the attack query as harmful. Therefore, in the rest of the paper, we
      utilize separate interactions for different questions.
465
466   Optimal number of questions n: As a reminder, Contextual Interaction Attack relies on interacting
467   with the LLM before the attack query. How many number of question-answer pairs are required?
468   In this experiment, we vary the number of such questions from n = 0 to n = 4. In the case of n = 0,
469   the auxiliary LLM will only rephrase the harmful input to remove all sensitive or censored words.
470   The results in Table 6 indicate that the value of n is proportional to the the attack success rate up
471   to n = 3. That is, as Contextual Interaction Attack asks more preliminary questions, the attack is
472   more likely to be successful. This agrees with our intuition that the context is a critical component in
473   jailbreaking. However, this pattern breaks for n = 4, since there responses might become too long
474   for the context window of the model. We believe as models with larger context window emerge, as
475   well as stronger models that can act as the auxiliary LLM, this number will increase. Even though
476
      n = 3 outperforms the rest settings, we utilize n = 2 in the rest of the paper, since this is sufficient
      for demonstrating the effectiveness of Contextual Interaction Attack.
477
478   Transferring attacks across LLMs: What happens if we attempt to concatenate the interactions
479   with a source model LLMs to another target LLMt , while we directly ask the attack query on LLMt ?
480   In order to assess the performance in this case, we concatenate the question-answer pairs from the
481   LLMs and append those to the attack query on LLMt . We conduct two related experiments. In the first
482   experiment, we use the Llama-2-13b as the source model. Our results indicate that the performance
483   in this case is similar to the multi-round attack, which is 87.6%. In the second experiment, we use
484   Mixtral 8×7b, which is known to have weaker security mechanisms, as the source model. Our target
485   model is Llama-2-13b, i.e., using the interactions from Mixtral Contextual Interaction Attack aims
      to attack the Llama-2-13bmodel. Interestingly, we find that this achieves a success rate of 93.3%,

486
      which is higher than the aforementioned setting. The reason that the first experiment yields lower
487   accuracy is that the source model sometimes triggers its safe mechanism when we generate the
488   question-answer pairs, therefore the attack fails. The results are reported in Table 7 and verify that
489   interacting on a model with weaker safety and transferring the interaction along with the attack query
490   to another model might yield even further improvements. We leave a more detailed exploration of
491   this interesting phenomenon to future work.

      5   C ONCLUSION
494
495
      In this work, we study Contextual Interaction Attack, which is a Jailbreaking attack, capable of
496   obtaining harmful information from a variety of recent LLMs. The idea relies on leveraging the
497   context vector of the LLM. In practice, this is realized through a sequence of interactions with the
498   LLM. We believe this opens up a new direction of uncovering model properties through utilizing the
499   context vector. In Jailbreaking, we demonstrate that Contextual Interaction Attack achieves a high
500   success rate on most state-of-art LLMs (Brockman et al., 2023; OpenAI, 2021; Touvron et al., 2023).
501   As a future step, we believe Contextual Interaction Attack can be further strengthened when used in
502   combination with existing attacks, e.g., multilingual attacks (Xu et al., 2023b).

      In this work, we aim to jailbreak Large Language Models (LLMs), which are neural network models
      capable of generating natural language text. We propose a method that can exploit LLMs to produce
767
      restricted, harmful, or toxic content. We are aware of the potential risks that our work entails for the
768
      security and safety of LLMs, as they are increasingly adopted in various domains and applications.
769   Nevertheless, we also believe that our work advances the open and transparent research on the
770   challenges and limitations of LLMs, which is crucial for devising effective solutions and protections.
771   Similarly, the last few years the exploration of adversarial attacks (Wei et al., 2023a; Madry et al.,
772   2017; Chakraborty et al., 2018) has led to the improvement of the robustness of the models and led to
773   techniques to safeguard against such vulnerabilities, e.g., with certifiable verification (Xu et al., 2020;
774   Cruz-Filipe et al., 2017). To mitigate this risk, we will adhere to responsible disclosure practices by
775   sharing our preliminary findings with OpenAI, Meta and Mistral developers. We further coordinated
776   with them before publicly releasing our results. We also emphasize that, our ultimate goal in this
777
      paper is to identify of weaknesses of existing methods.

779   L IMITATION
780
781   Evaluation: We have noticed that the existing methods for evaluating the success of a jailbreak
782   might not accurately assess jailbreak success rates. Relying solely on prefix matching often results
783   in a significant overestimation of jailbreak success rates. Given that Contextual Interaction Attack
784   operates at a semantic level, it is imperative to employ human evaluation to determine whether the
785   LLM’s responses effectively address the malicious questions posed in the attack. Unfortunately,
786   using a trained model for this purpose is not efficient. As a consequence, due to the substantial
787
      human intervention required for evaluation, scaling the evaluation of Contextual Interaction Attack to
      datasets containing thousands of samples presents a considerable challenge.
788
789   Empirical: Another limitation of this work is that Contextual Interaction Attack requires multiple
790   rounds of interaction with the model. This makes some prompt-based Jailbreaking attacks faster e.g
791   DAN, especially if they are hand-crafted attacks and not gradient-based that might require additional
792   time for optimizing the prompt. However, our algorithms only need 4-5 forward pass (queries) which
793
      makes it faster than most iterative jailbreaking method such as PAIR and GCG. Nevertheless, we
      advocate that Jailbreaking attacks should be thoroughly investigated, particularly when they can be
794
      automated (like Contextual Interaction Attack), since those can be massively conducted at scale and
795
      pose a threat to the harmful information an attacker can obtain.
796
797   Theoretical: Contextual Interaction Attack relies on an empirical and intuitive attack. However, there
798   is no theoretical guarantee that this attack will be successful or any upper or lower bound on the
799
      performance. This is not specific to Contextual Interaction Attack, but this is a common trait across
      most of the existing Jailbreaking methods. Therefore, we do consider this as a collective limitation in
800
      the existing methods and hope that new guarantees will be developed in the future.

1724   We conducted tests on multiple LLMs to identify the best auxiliary LLM for generating attack prompt
1725   sets. The evaluation criteria included the model’s security level, the quality of generated text based
1726   on In Context Learning (ICL), and the diversity of generated results. Our goal was to find a model
1727   with lower security to minimize the chances of being rejected by the auxiliary model’s own security
       mechanisms, reducing the probability of failed generation.
