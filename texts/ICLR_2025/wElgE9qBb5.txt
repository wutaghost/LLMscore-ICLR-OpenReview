010                                              A BSTRACT
011
012            The analysis of tabular data has traditionally been dominated by gradient-boosted
013            decision trees (GBDTs), known for their proficiency with mixed categorical and
014            numerical features. However, recent deep learning innovations are challenging
015            this dominance. We introduce Mambular, an adaptation of the Mamba architec-
016
               ture optimized for tabular data. We extensively benchmark Mambular against
               state-of-the-art models, including neural networks and tree-based methods, and
017
               demonstrate its competitive performance across diverse datasets. Additionally,
018
               we explore various adaptations of Mambular to understand its effectiveness for
019            tabular data. We investigate different pooling strategies, feature interaction mech-
020            anisms, and bi-directional processing. Our analysis shows that interpreting fea-
021            tures as a sequence and passing them through Mamba layers results in surpris-
022            ingly performant models. The results highlight Mambular’s potential as a ver-
023            satile and powerful architecture for tabular data analysis, expanding the scope
024            of deep learning applications in this domain. The source code is available at
025            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/mamba-tabular-485F/.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Gradient-boosted decision trees (GBDTs) have long been the dominant approach for analyzing tab-
031   ular data, due to their ability to handle the typical mix of categorical and numerical features found
032   in such datasets (Grinsztajn et al., 2022). In contrast, deep learning models have historically faced
033
      challenges with tabular data, often struggling to outperform GBDTs. The complexity and diversity
      of tabular data, including issues like missing values, varied feature types, and the need for exten-
034
      sive preprocessing, have made it difficult for deep learning to match the performance of GBDTs
035
      (Borisov et al., 2022). However, recent advancements in deep learning are gradually challenging
036   this paradigm by introducing innovative architectures that leverage advanced mechanisms to capture
037   complex feature dependencies, promising significant improvements (Popov et al., 2019; Hollmann
038   et al., 2022; Gorishniy et al., 2021).
039
      One of the most effective advancements in tabular deep learning is the application of attention mech-
040
      anisms in models like TabTransformer (Huang et al., 2020), FT-Transformer (Gorishniy et al., 2021)
041
      and many more (Wang and Sun, 2022; Thielmann et al., 2024b; Arik and Pfister, 2021). These mod-
042   els leverage the attention mechanism to capture dependencies between features, offering a signifi-
043   cant improvement over traditional approaches. FT-Transformers, in particular, have demonstrated
044   robust performance across various tabular datasets, often surpassing the accuracy of GBDTs (McEl-
045   fresh et al., 2024). Additionally, more traditional models like Multi-Layer Perceptrons (MLPs) and
046   ResNets have demonstrated improvements when well-designed and when the data undergoes thor-
047   ough preprocessing (Gorishniy et al., 2021; 2022). These models have benefited especially from
048   innovations in advanced preprocessing methods that make them more competitive.
049   More recently, the Mamba architecture (Gu and Dao, 2023) has shown promising results in textual
050   problems. Tasks previously dominated by Transformer architectures, such as DNA modeling and
051   language modeling, have seen improvements with the application of Mamba models (Gu and Dao,
052   2023; Schiff et al., 2024; Zhao et al., 2024). Several adaptations have demonstrated its versatility,
053   such as Vision Mamba for image classification (Xu et al., 2024), video analysis (Yang et al., 2024;
      Yue and Li, 2024) and point cloud analysis (Zhang et al., 2024; Liu et al., 2024). Furthermore, the

054
      architecture has been adapted for time series problems, with notable successes reported by Patro
055   and Agneeswaran (2024), Wang et al. (2024) and Ahamed and Cheng (2024b). Mamba has also
056   been integrated into graph learning (Behrouz and Hashemi, 2024) and imitation learning (Correia
057   and Alexandre, 2024). Further advancements have improved the language model, for example, by
058   incorporating attention (Lieber et al., 2024), Mixture of Experts (Pióro et al., 2024) or bi-directional
059   sequence processing (Liang et al., 2024).
060
      These advancements underscore Mamba’s broad applicability, making it a powerful and flexible
061   architecture for diverse tasks and data types. Similarly to the transformer architecture, the question
062   arises whether the Mamba architecture can also be leveraged for tabular problems, and this study is
063   focused on addressing this question.
064
      The contributions of the paper can be summarized as follows:
065
066         I. We introduce Mambular, a tabular adaptation of Mamba, demonstrating the potential of
067            sequential models in addressing tabular problems.
068        II. We conduct extensive benchmarking of Mambular against several competitive neural and
069            tree-based methods, illustrating that a standard Mambular model performs on par with or
070            better than tree-based models across a wide range of datasets.
071
          III. We examine the impact of bi-directional processing and feature interaction layers on Mam-
072            bular’s performance, and compare several pooling methods.
073
          IV. Finally, we carry out an in-depth analysis of Mambular’s sequential nature, investigating
074
               the implications of feature orderings in a sequential tabular model.

      embedded. In contrast to classical language models, each categorical feature has its own, distinct
086

087

184   In contrast to FT-Transformer (Gorishniy et al., 2021) and TabTransformer (Huang et al., 2020)
185   Mambular truly iterates through all variables as if they are a sequence; hence, feature interactions are
186   detected sequentially. The effect of feature position in a sequence, and the impact of the convolution
187
      kernel size is analyzed with respect to performance in section 4. Furthermore, it should be noted
      that in contrast to TabPFN (Hollmann et al., 2022), Mambular does not transpose dimensions and
188
      iterates over observations. Hence, training on large datasets is possible and it can scale well to any
189
      training data size, just as Mamba (Gu and Dao, 2023) does.

      3   E XPERIMENTS
248
249
      Mambular is evaluated against a range of top-performing models (McElfresh et al., 2024) across
250
      multiple datasets (Supplementary Table 8). These models include FT-Transformer (Gorishniy et al.,
251
      2021), TabTransformer (Huang et al., 2020), XGBoost (Grinsztajn et al., 2022; McElfresh et al.,
252   2024), LightGBM (Ke et al., 2017), a Random Forest, a baseline Multi-Layer Perceptron, and a
253   ResNet. TabPFN (Hollmann et al., 2022) is excluded due to its unsuitability for larger datasets.
254
255
      A 5-fold cross-validation is conducted for all datasets, with average results and standard deviations
      reported. PLE encodings (Eq. 1) with a maximum number of bins equal to the model dimension
256
      are used for all neural models (128 for most models, including MLP and ResNet). All categorical
257
      features are integer-encoded. For regression tasks, targets are normalized. Mean Squared Error
258   (MSE) and Area Under the Curve (AUC) metrics are reported for regression and classification tasks
259   respectively. TabTransformer, FT-Transformer, and Mambular employ identical architectures for
260   embeddings and task-specific heads, which includes a single output layer without activation function
261   or dropout. The [ CLS ] token embedding is utilized for final prediction in the FT-Transformer as it
262   has been shown to enhance performance (Thielmann et al., 2024b; Gorishniy et al., 2021).
263
      All neural models share several parameters: a starting learning rate of 1e-04, weight decay of 1e-06,
264   an early stopping patience of 15 epochs with respect to the validation loss, a maximum of 200 epochs
265   for training, and learning rate decay with a factor of 0.1 with a patience of 10 epochs with respect
266   to the validation loss. A universal batch size of 128 is used, and the best model with respect to the
267   validation loss is returned for testing. TabTransformer, FT-Transformer, and Mambular use the same
268   embedding functions. For the benchmarks, a basic Mambular architecture is employed, using aver-
269   age pooling, no feature interaction layer, and no bi-directional processing. The columns/sequence
      are always sorted with numerical features first, followed by categorical features. Within these two

374   the SSM. This evaluation was only implemented for the standard Average pooling technique.

402

403   quence and how their order impacts model performance. In textual data, shuffling the order of
404   words/tokens significantly affects the outcome, and even swapping single words can lead to entirely
405   different contextualized embeddings. Since these contextualized representations are pooled and fed
406   directly to Mambular’s task-specific head, this could also impact performance.

411   with a relatively small kernel size. However, this is contingent on the data. Strong interaction ef-
412   fects between features that are positioned further apart than the kernel size in the pseudo-sequence
413   can negatively impact model performance, as demonstrated by the results on the California housing

449   5   L IMITATIONS
450
451
452   The model we have presented has been tested across various datasets and compared against a range
453
      of models. However, we have not conducted hyperparameter tuning, as findings from Grinsztajn
      et al. (2022) and Gorishniy et al. (2021) suggest that most models perform adequately without
454
      tuning. These studies indicate that while hyperparameter tuning can enhance performance across
455
      all models simultaneously, it does not significantly alter the relative ranking of the models. This
456   suggests that a model that performs best or worst with default configurations will likely retain its
457   ranking even after extensive tuning. Furthermore, McElfresh et al. (2024) reported similar findings,
458   strengthening the notion that hyperparameter tuning benefits most models equally without changing
459   their comparative performance.
460
      The absence of tuning does leave potential for enhancement across all models. However, the default
461
      configurations for the comparison models have been extensively tested in numerous studies. It is
462   anticipated that if any model could gain more from hyperparameter tuning, it would be Mambu-
463   lar, due to the lack of extensive literature guiding its default settings. For the comparison models,
464   we made our selections based on literature to ensure default parameters that are meaningful and
465   high-performing. We managed to replicate average results from studies such as Gorishniy et al.
466   (2021) and Grinsztajn et al. (2022). Moreover, key hyperparameters like learning rate, patience, and
467   number of epochs are shared among all models for a more uniform approach. All hyperparameter
468   configurations can be found in Appendix E.

      6   C ONCLUSION
472
473
474   We introduce Mambular, a novel architecture for tabular deep learning. Our work demonstrates the
475   applicability of a genuinely sequential model to tabular problems, providing a unique viewpoint on
476   the interpretation and management of tabular data by treating it as a sequential problem. Our find-
477   ings indicate that a sequential model is effective for both regression and classification tasks across
478   a variety of datasets. The performance of Mambular, along with its extension to MambularLSS,
479   demonstrates its broad applicability to a wide range of tabular tasks.
480   While Mamba is still relatively new compared to architectures like the Transformer, its rapid adop-
481   tion indicates substantial potential for further enhancement. Developments such as those proposed
482   by Lieber et al. (2024) and Wang et al. (2024) could be particularly beneficial for tabular appli-
483   cations. Additionally, investigating the optimal feature ordering or integrating column-specific in-
484   formation through textual embeddings could further boost performance. Viewing tabular data as a
485   sequence offers significant benefits for feature incremental learning. New features can be directly
      appended to the sequence, eliminating the need to retrain the entire model.

      C    D ISTRIBUTIONAL R EGRESSION
757
758
      Distributional regression describes regression beyond the mean, i.e., the modeling of all distribu-
759
      tional parameters. Thus, Location Scale and Shape (LSS) models can quantify the effects of covari-
760   ates on not just the mean but also on any parameter of a potentially complex distribution assumed for
761   the responses. A major advantage of these models is their ability to identify changes in all aspects
762   of the response distribution, such as variance, skewness, and tail probabilities, enabling the model
763   to properly disentangling aleatoric uncertainty from epistemic uncertainty.
764
      This is achieved by minimizing the negative log-likelihood via optimizing the parameters θ

777   where n is the underlying number of observations and parameters y ∈ R, location µ ∈ R and scale
778   σ ∈ R+ .
779   While this has been a common standard in classical statistical approaches (Stasinopoulos and Rigby,
780   2008), it has not yet been widely adopted by the ML community. Recent interpretable approaches
781   (Thielmann et al., 2024a), however, have demonstrated the applicability of distributional regression
782   in tabular deep learning. Furthermore, approaches like XGBoostLSS (März, 2019; März and Kneib,
783   2022) demonstrate that tree-based models are capable of effectively solving such tasks. Below, we
784   show that Mambular for Location Scale and Shape (MambularLSS) outperforms XGBoostLSS in

788   CRPS Analyzing distributional regression models also requires careful consideration of the eval-
789   uation metrics. Traditionally, mean focused models are evaluated using mean-centric metrics, e.g.
790
      MSE, AUC or Accuracy. However, a model that takes all distributional parameters into account
      should be evaluated on the predictive performance for all of the distributional parameters. Follow-
791
      ing Gneiting and Raftery (2007), the evaluation metric should be proper, i.e. enforce the analyst to
792
      report their true beliefs in terms of a predictive distribution. In terms of classical mean-centric met-
793   rics, e.g. MSE is proper for the mean, however, not proper for evaluating the complete distributional
794   prediction. We therefore rely on the Continuous Ranked Probability Score (Gneiting and Raftery,
795   2007) for model evaluation, given by:

      In addition to the popular tabular models described above, we tested the architecture proposed by
813   Ahamed and Cheng (2024a). MambaTab is the first architecture to leverage Mamba blocks for
814   tabular problems. However, the authors propose using a combined linear layer to project all inputs
815   into a single feature representation, transforming the features into a pseudo-sequence of fixed length
816   1. This approach simplifies the recursive update from Eq. 2 into a matrix multiplication and makes
817   the model resemble a ResNet due to the residual connections in the final processing. Utilizing
818   a sequential model with a sequence length of 1 does not fully exploit the strengths of sequential
819   processing, as it reduces the model’s capacity to capture dependencies across multiple features.
820   We tested the architecture proposed by Ahamed and Cheng (2024a) and could achieve similar results
821   for shared datasets, but overall found MambaTab to perform similar to a ResNet, aligning with
822   expectations (see Table 2 and ??). Additionally, we experimented with transposing the axes to create
823   an input matrix of shape (1) × (BATCH S IZE ) × (E MBEDDING D IMENSION ), as outlined in their
824   implementation. While this approach draws on ideas from TabPFN (Hollmann et al., 2022), it did not
825   lead to performance improvements in our experiments. When using PLE encodings and increasing
826   the number of layers and dimensions compared to the default implementation from Ahamed and
827
      Cheng (2024a) we are able to increase performance.
828   MambaTab (Ahamed and Cheng, 2024a) significantly differs from Mambular, since it is not a se-
829   quential model. To achieve the presented results from MambaTab, we have followed the provided
830   implementation from the authors retrieved from https://github.com/Atik-Ahamed/
831   MambaTab. It is worth noting, however, that MambaTab benchmarks the model on a lot of smaller
832   datasets. 50% of the benchmarked datasets have not more than 1000 observations. Additionally, the
833
      provided implementation suggests, that MambaTab does indeed not iterate over a pseudo sequence
      length of 1, but rather over the number of observations, similar to a TabPFN (Hollmann et al., 2022).
834
      We have also tested this version, denoted as MambaTabT but did not find that it performs better than
835
      the described version. On the Adult dataset, our achieved result of 0.901 AUC on average is very
836   similar to the default results reported in Ahamed and Cheng (2024a) with 0.906. The difference
837   could be firstly due to us performing 5-fold cross validation and secondly different seeds in model
838   initialization.
