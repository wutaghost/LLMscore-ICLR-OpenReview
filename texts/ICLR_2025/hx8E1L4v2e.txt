009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Anomaly detection in dynamic graphs is a very important task that has attracted
012            a lot of attention. Many dynamic graph anomaly detection methods are al-
013            ready available, but most of these efforts are based on supervised learning. In
014            the real world, however, it is often difficult to collect large amounts of labelled
015            anomaly data, which is not conducive to the training of these supervised meth-
016            ods and severely reduces their ability to be applied in different dynamic graph
017            anomaly detection scenarios. A novel semi-supervised anomaly detection frame-
018
               work AAEDY for the detection of anomalous edges in dynamic graphs is pre-
               sented in this paper, which improves reconstruction by combining adversarial
019
               based on autoencoder, and discriminates whether an edge is anomalous by com-
020
               paring the original edge to the reconstructed edge in low-dimensional space. Ex-
021            tensive experiments have been carried out on six real-world datasets, and the ex-
022            perimental results show that AAEDY can outperform the state-of-the-art competi-
023            tors in anomaly detection significantly.

026   1   I NTRODUCTION
027
028   In real life, dynamic graph networks can be seen everywhere, such as social networks, financial
029   transaction networks, Internet of Vehicles, Internet of Things, etc. Among many dynamic graph
030   analysis tasks, dynamic graph anomaly detection is one of the most important tasks, and its focus
031   is on abnormal edge detection. Detecting abnormal edges helps to understand the state of the sys-
032   tem, protect the system and improve its robustness (Ranshous et al., 2015; Wang et al., 2022). For
033
      example, in financial transaction systems, there are often criminals who try to make profits through
      fraud, phishing, money laundering, etc., which seriously endangers the interests of ordinary users
034
      and the healthy development of the system. Detecting abnormal edges in dynamic graphs is crucial
035
      to protecting the interests of ordinary users and maintaining the healthy development of the system.
036
037   At present, most of the graph anomaly detection methods based on deep learning are aimed at
038   anomaly detection in static graphs, which are mainly divided into three methods: supervised
039
      (Chouiekh & Haj, 2018; Alsheikh et al., 2016; Perozzi et al., 2014; Grover & Leskovec, 2016;
      Tang et al., 2015), semi-supervised (Kumagai et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2017; Castellini et al., 2017;
040
      Guo et al., 2016) and unsupervised (Li et al., 2017; Ding et al., 2019; Fan et al., 2020; Ding et al.,
041
      2021). Most of the supervised methods are based on the idea of feature embedding, which uses deep
042   learning to embed nodes from high-dimensional space to low-dimensional space to obtain more
043   effective node features. Semi-supervised and unsupervised methods are mostly based on the idea
044   of residual analysis, which finds abnormal target edges or nodes by comparing the errors between
045   the original data and the reconstructed data. Compared with anomaly detection methods on static
046   graphs, there are fewer anomaly detection methods based on deep learning for dynamic graphs, and
047   most of them are supervised methods. NetWalk Yu et al. (2018) uses random walks and deep au-
048   toencoders to obtain low-dimensional embeddings of nodes and combines clustering technology to
049   detect anomaly information. StrGNN (Cai et al., 2021), H-VGRAE (Yang et al., 2020) and TADDY
050
      (Liu et al., 2021) design end-to-end supervised methods for anomaly detection. AddGraph (Zheng
      et al., 2019) designs a semi-supervised learning framework based on residual analysis.
051
052   However, dynamic graph anomaly detection based on supervised learning methods requires a large
053   amount of labeled anomaly data to train the model. In practical applications, it is difficult to collect
      a large amount of anomaly data, which seriously reduces the application ability in different dy-

076   2.1   A NOMALY D ETECTION IN S TATIC G RAPHS
077
078   The structure, attributes and other features of the graph in the static graph do not change over time.
079
      A considerable number of methods have been developed for anomaly detection in static graphs
      using deep learning, which are mainly divided into supervised methods, semi-supervised methods
080
      and unsupervised methods. Among the supervised methods, Chouiekh & Haj (2018) proposed a
081
      supervised learning method that uses convolutional neural networks to extract user attribute features
082   to distinguish normal users from abnormal users in telephone networks. Alsheikh et al. (2016)
083   proposed a method for extracting features using deep learning on Spark computing nodes, which
084   solves the problem that deep learning models contain a large number of hidden layers and a large
085   number of parameters. However, supervised methods require a certain amount of labeled data to
086   train the model, which is not suitable for real-world scenarios where anomalies are scarce.
087
      Most semi-supervised and unsupervised methods adopt the idea based on residual analysis and use
088   reconstruction errors to measure whether a data instance is abnormal. Li et al. proposed an anomaly
089   detection framework Radar in the literature (Li et al., 2017) to detect anomalies in attribution net-
090   works from the perspective of residual analysis. Ding et al. (2019) designed a novel autoencoder
091   framework and used GCN (Kipf & Welling, 2017) to capture the nonlinearity of data and the com-
092   plex interactions between nodes. Fan et al. (2020) proposed a dual autoencoder framework, using
093   structural autoencoders and attribute autoencoders to learn the structural and attribute features of
094   static graphs. Ding et al. (2021) used generative adversarial methods to enhance the reconstruc-
095   tion ability of the model, and through joint training, the model automatically generated potential
096
      abnormal data, effectively solving the problem of inductive learning. Kumagai et al. (2021) pro-
      posed a semi-supervised static graph anomaly detection method that comprehensively considers the
097
      graph structure and node attribute information, and identifies abnormal nodes by learning hyper-
098
      spheres. Zhang et al. (2017) proposed an autoencoder-based anomaly detection method for rumor
099   detection. The focus of this method is to propose several adaptive thresholds to improve rumor
100   detection. Castellini et al. (2017) proposed a semi-supervised deep learning model for anomaly de-
101   tection, mainly using an autoencoder with a denoising mechanism to detect robot followers in social
102   networks.
103
104   2.2   A NOMALY D ETECTION IN DYNAMIC G RAPHS
105
106   The structure, attributes, and other features of dynamic graphs change over time, and it is neces-
107   sary to comprehensively consider the graph information under multiple time snapshots. Miz et al.
      (2019) proposed a scalable community-based anomaly detection method that connects and aggre-

108
      gates nodes with similar behaviors to analyze abnormal behaviors that occur in collective behaviors.
109   NetWalk (Yu et al., 2018) encodes the vertices of a dynamic network into vector representations
110   through Clique embedding, jointly minimizes the pairwise distance of each vertex representation,
111   and combines clustering-based methods with low-dimensional vertex features to gradually dynam-
112   ically detect network anomaly detection. Zheng et al. (2019) proposed a semi-supervised anomaly
113   detection method for dynamic graphs, using extended time GCN to obtain the structural embedding
114   and attribute embedding of the graph, and combining attention-based GRU (Chung et al., 2014) and
115   negative sampling strategies to solve the problem of insufficient labels. Cai et al. (2021) extracts
116   subgraph features under time snapshots through GCN, and uses recurrent neural network GRU to
117
      fuse multiple subgraph features to capture the temporal relationship of dynamic graphs. Liu et al.
      (2021) uses graph diffusion technology to sample substructures in dynamic graphs, adopts three
118
      encoding methods to capture the global structural features, local structural features and temporal
119
      information of the graph, and fuses multiple features through autoencoders to achieve anomaly de-
120   tection of dynamic graphs. In order to cope with the problem of scarce abnormal data, AAEDY
121   proposed a novel semi-supervised adversarial autoencoder, which only needs to use normal data in
122   the model training stage, avoiding the problem of being unable to train the model due to lack of
123   abnormal data.

      3     F RAMEWORK
127
      A novel and effective dynamic graph anomaly detection framework is proposed. The overall struc-
128
      ture is shown in Figure 1. First, the graph diffusion technique is used to perform sub-graph sampling
129
      on the target edge to obtain the graph information of the target edge at different times, and three en-
130   coding methods are used to encode the dynamic graph information at multiple times. Secondly, a
131   new deep autoencoder based on residual analysis is designed to detect abnormal edge information
132   by comparing the errors between the original data and the reconstructed individual data, and the ad-
133   versarial idea is used to continuously optimize the reconstruction performance training of the deep
134   autoencoder.

248   3.2     A NOMANY D ETECTION
249
250   Anomalous edge detection in dynamic graphs by designing an adversarial autoencoder. This au-
251   toencoder consists of a generator G, an encoder E, and a discriminator D.
252   The generator G is a autoencoder, consisting of an encoder GE , and a decoder GD , which is mainly
253   based on transformer’s self-attention mechanism and multi-headed attention mechanism (Vaswani
254   et al., 2017) to learn how to reconstruct the original edges of the input. Unlike Transformer, we
255   add an embedding layer to embed the high-dimensional features of the original edges into the low-
256   dimensional space. This generator G works as follows: the generator G first reads an input edge x
257   before forwarding it to its encoder network GE . By adding an embedding layer to the transformer,
258   GE embeds the whole input edge x into a low-dimensional vector z, which is also referred to as the
259   bottleneck features of G, since it has the smallest dimension that contains the best representation of
260
      x. The decoder part GD of the generator network G adopts the architecture of transformer’s decoder,
      on this basis, a layer of neural network is added to restore the low-dimensional vector z to its size
261
      before embedding. This approach upscales the vector z to reconstruct the edge x as x̂.
262
263   The encoder E can take the x̂, generated by the generator G and re-embed it and generate the
264   embedding vector ẑ. The network structure of the encoder is the same as that of the encoder GE
265   in the generator, so that the generated embedding vector ẑ, has the same spatial dimension as the
266
      generator embedding vector z.
267   The discriminator D whose objective is to classify the input x and the output x̂ as real or fake,
268   respectively. In our discriminator network, a layer of self-attention network is used to capture the
269   features of the edge x or x̂, and add a layer of linear neural network to map them to a one-dimensional
      space. It is also normalized and nonlinearized by Softmax function as the activation function.

      3.2.1   M ODEL T RAINING
271
272   In the training phase of the model, trained with a large amount of normal data, the generator GD can
273   reconstruct the normal data well and then pass the encoder E. Then the embedding vector z and the
274   embedding vector ẑ have a small difference.
275   It is hypothesized that when an abnormal edge is forward-passed into the network G, GD does not
276   be able to reconstruct the abnormalities although GE can map the input x to the latent vector z. This
277   should be expected because the network is modeled exclusively by normal samples during training
278   and its parametrization makes the network unsuitable for generating abnormal samples. There is a
279   potential consequence of an output x̂ that has missed abnormalities in it, resulting in the encoder’s
280   network E mapping mapping x̂ to a vector ẑ with the same lack of representation of the abnormal
281   features,resulting in a dissimilarity between z and ẑ causing dissimilarity between z and ẑ. As result,
282   if there is some dissimilarity within latent vector space corresponding to an input edge x, the model
283
      classifies x as an anomalous edge. To validate this hypothesis, the objective function was formulated
      by combining three loss functions, each optimised for a single sub-network.
284

286   the supervision of the cross entropy loss function. The discriminatory ability of the discriminator

288   discriminatory ability of the discriminator.

      4.1     EXPERIMENT SETTING
348
349   The AAEDY framework is tested on six real-world dynamic graph benchmark datasets, including
350   UCI Messagegs (Opsahl & Panzarasa, 2009), Bitcoin-Alpha (Kumar et al., 2016), Bitcoin-OTC
351   (Kumar et al., 2018), Digg (De Choudhury et al., 2009), AS-Topology (Zhang et al., 2005), and
352   Email-DNC (Rossi & Ahmed, 2015).We compared AAEDY with seven anomaly detection meth-
353   ods, including DeepWalk (Perozzi et al., 2014) , node2vec (Grover & Leskovec, 2016) , spectral
354   clustering (Opsahl & Panzarasa, 2009) , NetWalk (Yu et al., 2018) , AddGraph (Zheng et al., 2019)
355
      , StrGNN (Cai et al., 2021) , and TADDY (Liu et al., 2021).
356   Model training requires a large amount of normal data. Therefore, in the experiment, the training set
357   and test set are divided into 80% and 20%. The training set contains all normal data, while the test
358   set is injected with 1%, 5%, and 10% of abnormal data through the anomaly injection method. The
359   test indicator uses AUC, which is insensitive to the balance of positive and negative samples and can
360   be reasonably evaluated in the case of sample imbalance. In the preprocessing stage, the number of
361
      context nodes is set to 5, the time window τ is set to 2, and the encoding dimension denc is set to
      512. The PPR (Page et al., 1999) diffusion is the same as that used in TADDY and is calculated by
362
      Equation (2). In the anomaly detection stage, in all datasets, the number of attention heads is set to
363
      1, the number of attention layers is set to 1, and the dimensions of z and ẑ are set to 10. The model
364   is trained by the Adam optimizer with a learning rate of 0.0001. On the UCI Messages, Digg, and
365   Email-DNC datasets, 0.01 is set as the anomaly threshold.
366
367
      In order to balance the performance between the generator G and the discriminator D, during the
      training process, the generator G is trained first, and then the discriminator D is trained, and the
368
      weights between the generator, encoder, and discriminator can be dynamically adjusted. Different
369
      from the above datasets, a new training strategy is used on Bitcoin-OTC, Bitcoin-Alpha, and AS-
370   Topology datasets to train the generator G and encoder E once for each discriminator D. One epoch.
371   20 epochs are trained on all datasets, and the best trained model is finally adopted. The snapshot
372   size is set to 1,000 for UCI Messages and Email-DNC, 2,000 for Bitcoin-Alpha and Bitcoin-OTC,
373   and 4,000 for AS-Topology and Digg, respectively.

      5     C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
531
      Based on the idea of esidual analysis, this paper proposes a novel semi-supervised anomaly detec-
532
      tion frameworkAAEDY, which solves the problem that the supervised model cannot be trained due
533
      to the scarcity of abnormal data. The framework mainly consists of two modules, the data prepro-
534   cessing module and the anomaly addition detection module. The data preprocessing module uses
535   three codes to obtain the spatiotemporal encoding information of the edge. The anomaly detection
536   module consists of an adversarial code autoencoder, which performs anomaly detection by compar-
537   ing the differences between edges in two low-dimensional spaces. The effectiveness of our proposed
538   framework has been confirmed by experiments on six benchmark datasets. In the next step, we will
539   combine node attribute information to jointly learn the complex cross-modal interactions between
      graph structure and node attributes.

694   A.3   BASELINES DETAILS
695
696   Seven anomaly detection methods are demonstrated as follows:
697
698
      DeepWalk Perozzi et al. (2014) is inspired by Word2vec, which acquires node sequences by random
      wandering and performs feature embedding using skip-gram models.
699
700   node2vec Grover & Leskovec (2016) is a graph embedding method that combines DFS neighbour-
701   hoods and BFS neighbourhoods. In simple terms, it can be seen as an extension of deepwalk, a
      deepwalk that combines DFS and BFS random wandering.


                                                       13
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


702
      Spectral Clustering Opsahl & Panzarasa (2009) is a technique for learning node embedding by max-
703   imizing the similarity between nodes in a region. The idea behind this strategy is to keep the local
704   connection relationship between nodes in a network intact.
705
706
      NetWalk Yu et al. (2018) obtains a sequence of nodes by designing a wander in a dynamic graph,
      embeds the nodes into a low-dimensional space using a deep self-encoder, and finally uses a tech-
707
      nique of clustering to detect anomalous information.
708
709   AddGraph Zheng et al. (2019) based on the idea of residual analysis, trains a normal pattern by
710   designing a semi-supervised learning framework that uses normal edges continuously during the
711   training process. In this framework, GCN embedding features are mainly used to extract long and
712
      short-term spatio-temporal information using GRU.
713   StrGNN Cai et al. (2021) is also a deep learning-based framework for dynamic graph anomaly
714   detection, mainly by using GCN to extract features from subgraphs under each temporal snapshot,
715   and finally using the recurrent neural network GRU to fuse features from subgraphs under multiple
716   temporal snapshots in order to capture the spatio-temporal information of the dynamic graph.
717   TADDY Liu et al. (2021) is a newly proposed anomaly detection framework for dynamic graphs.
718   And uses graph diffusion techniques to sample sub-structures in dynamic graphs, and proposes three
719   encoding methods to capture global structural information, local structural information and temporal
720   information of the graph, and finally performs information fusion learning based on autoencoders to
721   finally achieve anomaly detection on dynamic graphs.
