009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) have proven to be highly effective in various
012            graph representation learning tasks. A key characteristic is that GNNs apply a
013            fixed number of message-passing steps to all nodes in the graph, regardless of
014            the varying computational needs and characteristics of each node. Through em-
015            pirical analysis of real-world data, we show that the optimal number of message-
016            passing layers differs for nodes with different characteristics. This insight is fur-
017            ther validated with experiments on synthetic datasets. To address this, we propose
018
               Adaptive Depth Message Passing GNN (ADMP-GNN), a novel framework that
               dynamically adjusts the number of message-passing layers for each node, leading
019
               to enhanced performance. This approach is applicable to any model that follows
020
               the message-passing scheme. We evaluate ADMP-GNN on the node classification
021            task and observe performance improvements over a wide range of GNNs.
022

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   A plethora of structured data comes in the form of graphs (Bornholdt & Schuster, 2001; Cao et al.,
027   2020); this has driven the need to develop neural network models that can effectively process and
028   analyze graph-structured data, known as Graph Neural Networks (GNNs). GNNs recently gathered
029   increasing attention following their successes in learning complex node and graph representations,
030   showcasing impressive success in various applications (Corso et al., 2022; Rampášek et al., 2022).
031   Many GNNs are instances of Message Passing Neural Networks (MPNNs) (Gilmer et al., 2017a)
032   such as Graph Isomorphism Networks (GIN) (Xu et al., 2019) and Graph Convolutional Networks
033
      (GCN) (Kipf & Welling, 2017). A common characteristic of GNNs is that they typically employ
      a fixed number of message-passing steps for all nodes, determined by the number of layers in the
034
      GNN (Gilmer et al., 2017b). This static approach raises an intriguing question: Should the num-
035
      ber of message-passing steps be adapted individually for each node to better capture their unique
036   characteristics and computational needs?
037
038   Determining the optimal number of message-passing layers for each node in a Graph Neural Net-
039
      work (GNN) presents a significant challenge due to the intricate and diverse nature of graph struc-
      tures, node features, and learning tasks. While deeper GNNs are capable of capturing long-range
040
      dependencies (Liu et al., 2021), they can also encounter issues like oversmoothing, where nodes
041
      become indistinguishably similar (Luan et al., 2022). This underscores the critical importance of
042   selecting the appropriate number of layers for a GNN to effectively capture the necessary graph
043   information. In dense graphs, where information can propagate quickly, even shallow GNNs can
044   effectively capture local information (Zeng et al., 2020). Conversely, sparse graphs, particularly
045   those with isolated nodes or limited connectivity, may require additional layers to facilitate effective
046   information sharing (Zhang et al., 2021; Zhao & Akoglu, 2020). An even more compelling idea is
047   to adjust the GNN depth for each node based on its local complexity and structural properties. This
048   adaptive approach could be especially beneficial for graphs with varied local structures, ensuring
049   that each node is processed according to its unique requirements.
050   Dynamic Neural Networks, also known as Adaptive Neural Networks, represent a class of models
051   that possess the ability to adjust their architecture or parameters depending on the input. Dynamic
052   Neural Networks have gained significant popularity, especially in the field of computer vision. This
053   adaptability enables them to achieve improved performance metrics such as accuracy, computational
      efficiency, and robustness. The adaptation includes, for example, the number of layers and skip


                                                         1

054
      connections (Li et al., 2017; Huang et al., 2016; Sabour et al., 2017). However, applying these
055   adaptations to graph learning tasks presents unique challenges. While Dynamic Neural Networks
056   excel in structured and homogeneous data environments like computer vision, graph data involves
057   overcoming complexities related to the inherent complex structure of graphs. Moreover, for node
058   classification tasks, input samples are interconnected through graph edges, necessitating specialized
059   techniques to dependencies.
060
      In this work, we focus on the task of node classification by proposing ADMP-GNN, a novel approach
061   that dynamically adapts the number of layers for each node within a GNN. Our main contributions
062   are as follows:
063
064         1. Node-Specific Depth Analysis in Graph Neural Networks. We demonstrate through
065            empirical analysis that different nodes within the same graph may require varying numbers
066
               of message-passing steps to accurately predict their labels. This finding underscores the
               importance of node-specific depth in GNNs.
067
068         2. Adaptive Message-Passing Layer Integration. We present ADMP-GNN, a novel ap-
069            proach that enables any GNN to make predictions for each node at every layer. Training
070
               the GNN to predict labels across all layers is a multi-task setting, which often suffers from
               gradient conflicts leading to suboptimal performance. To address this, we propose a se-
071
               quential training methodology where layers are progressively trained and their gradients
072
               are subsequently frozen, thereby mitigating conflicts and improving overall performance.
073
            3. Adaptive Layer Policy Learning for Node Classification. We propose a heuristic method
074
               to learn a layer selection policy using a set of validation nodes. This policy is then applied
075
               to select the optimal layer for predicting the labels of test nodes, ensuring that each node
076            exits the GNN at the most appropriate layer for its specific classification task.
077
            4. Model-Agnostic Flexibility. Our approach is model-agnostic and can be integrated with
078
               any GNN architecture that employs a message-passing scheme. This flexibility enhances
079            the GNN’s performance on node classification tasks, providing a significant improvement
080            over traditional fixed-layer approaches.

142    3            ADMP-GNN: A DAPTIVE D EPTH M ESSAGE -PASSING BASED GNN
143
144    In this section, we first present an empirical analysis highlighting the necessity for node-specific
145    depth in GNNs. Then, we introduce our Adaptive Depth Message Passing-based GNN (ADMP-
146    GNN). Our study includes experiments on both synthetic and real-world datasets to illustrate the
147    importance and potential benefits of this methodology.
148
149    3.1              N ODE -S PECIFIC D EPTH A NALYSIS IN G RAPH N EURAL N ETWORKS
150
151    Analysis on Synthetic Graphs. The goal of this analysis is to motivate the need to use a vary-
152    ing number of message-passing steps based on the specific characteristics of individual nodes. As
153
       discussed in the introduction, this approach becomes especially interesting in hybrid graphs where
       nodes exhibit diverse properties, such as local structures and node features. In this experiment, we
154
       focus on analyzing the impact of the number of message-passing layers on node with varied local
155
       neighborhood sparsity. To do so, we construct a graph by merging two subgraphs extracted from a
156    real-world dataset, such as Computers and Photo (Shchur et al., 2018). Both subgraphs contain the
157    same number of nodes, exhibit nearly identical homophily, and have equally distributed node labels.
158    The main difference between these subgraphs lies in their structure as one subgraph is sparse, while
159    the other is dense. Consequently, nodes within each subgraph share similar structural characteris-
160    tics. In Appendix A, we give the construction details of these synthetic datasets, and we visualize
161    the adjacency matrix of these synthetic graphs, with additional details provided in the appendix, c.f.
       Figure 3.

      We trained a L different GCN models, with a varying number of layers ℓ ∈ [[0, L]], where L repre-
163   sents the maximum depth. For this experiment, we set L = 10. Although each GCN was trained on
164   the entire synthetic graph, which is a combination of the sparse and dense subgraphs, we evaluated
165   the performance of each model separately on the individual subgraphs. This allows us to assess the
166   impact of GNN depth on different types of local subgraph structures. The results of this analysis
167   are presented in Figure 1. As observed, in dense subgraphs, the test accuracy decreases at a faster
168   rate, while in sparse subgraphs, the drop in accuracy occurs later, typically around layers 2 or 3.
169   Moreover, the optimal number of layers differs between sparse and dense subgraphs. For instance,
170   in the Computers dataset, the highest accuracy is achieved at layer 2 for the sparse subgraph, while
171
      for the dense subgraph, the optimal performance is reached at layer 0. Additionally, in the Photo
      dataset, we observe a distinct behavior starting from layer 6, where the impact of GNN depth di-
172
      verges between sparse and dense subgraphs. This highlights the need to adapt the number of layers
173
      per node based on its characteristics.
174
175   Analysis on Real Word Graphs. Given a maximum GNN depth L, we should train L + 1 different
176   GNNs, each with a distinct number of layers ℓ, where ℓ ranges from 0 and L. Subsequently, a policy
177
      must be established to determine the optimal GNN with the appropriate number of layers for each
      individual node. However, training L + 1 GNNs separately can be computationally expensive. A
178
      more efficient approach involves designing a single GNN with L + 1 layers that provides predic-
179
      tions at each intermediate layer. To ensure that this new configuration is equivalent to the previous
180   approach (i.e., training L + 1 GNNs separately), the computational graph responsible for making
181   predictions at layer ℓ must be identical to that of a GNN with ℓ layers. Furthermore, the classifica-
182   tion performance at layer ℓ should yield results comparable to those of a conventional GNN with ℓ
183   layers. In what follows, we propose ADMP-GNN, an extension of message passing neural networks
184   that respect the aforementioned challenges.

432
      This high accuracy leads to a significant distribution shift between the predictions for train nodes
433   and those for test nodes. Conversely, validation nodes, which were not utilized during the training
434   of the ADMP-GNN, present a more suitable option for learning the policy due to their unbiased
435   predictions. We tested a variety of Deep Learning mechanisms to predict the best exit layer for
436   each node. This included generalizing the policy by training neural networks to identify layers
437   that accurately predict node outcomes or by framing the problem as an optimal stopping problem
438   following the framework of Huré et al. (2021). However, these approaches require learning the
439   policy on a set of nodes larger than the test set, which is impractical for node classification tasks
440   where the training and validation node sets, available for policy learning, are typically smaller than
441
      the test set.

443   6   C ONCLUSION
444
445   In this work, we have proposed ADMP-GNN, a novel adaption of message passing neural networks
446   that enables any message passing neural network to make predictions for each node at every layer.
447   Additionally, we have proposed a sequential training approach aimed at achieving results compara-
448   ble to training multiple GNNs separately in a single-task setting. The empirical analysis of the results
449
      demonstrates the need for a node-specific depth in GNNs to better capture the unique characteris-
      tics and computational needs of each node. Determining the optimal number of message-passing
450
      layers for each node presents a significant challenge due to several factors. Graph structures vary
451
      widely in complexity and connectivity, influencing how information propagates through the net-
452   work. Node features introduce further variability, impacting the effectiveness of message passing.
453   However, through experiments, we identified instances where node centrality can help identify the
454   optimal layer for each node. We heuristically learn a layer selection policy using a set of validation
455   nodes. This policy is then generalized on the test nodes. Extensive experiments on multiple datasets
456   demonstrate ADMP-GNN’s effectiveness in improving the prediction accuracy of GNNs.
