024                                                               A BSTRACT
025
026              Recent successes in autoregressive (AR) generation models, such as the GPT series
027              in natural language processing, have motivated efforts to replicate this success in
028              visual tasks. Some research aims to extend this approach to autonomous driving
029              by building video-based world models capable of generating realistic future video
030              sequences and predicting the ego states. However, the prior works tend to produce
031
                 unsatisfactory results, since the classic GPT framework is designed to handle 1D
                 contextual information, such as text, and lacks the inherent capability to model
032
                 the spatial and temporal dynamics necessary for video generation. In this paper,
033
                 we present DrivingWorld, a GPT-style world model for autonomous driving with
034              several spatial-temporal fusion mechanisms. This design allows for effective
035              modeling of both spatial and temporal dynamics, enabling high-fidelity, long
036              time video generation. Specifically, we first propose next-state-prediction strategy
037              to model temporal coherence between consecutive frames and then apply next-
038              token-prediction strategy to capture spatial information within a frame. To further
039              enhance generalization ability, we propose a novel masking strategy and reweight
040              strategy for token prediction to mitigate long time drifting issues and enable
041              precise control. Our work is capable of producing high-fidelity and consistent
042
                 video clips with long-time duration. Experiments demonstrate that, in contrast to
                 prior works, our method delivers superior visual quality and significantly more
043
                 accurate controllable future video generation. Visit our project page at https:

047   1    I NTRODUCTION
048
049   In recent years, autoregressive (AR) learning schemes have achieved significant success in natural
050   language processing, as demonstrated by models like the GPT series (Radford, 2018; Radford
051   et al., 2019; Brown, 2020). These models predict future text responses from past data, making AR
052   approaches as leading candidates in the pursuit of Artificial General Intelligence (AGI). Inspired by
053   these advancements, many researchers have sought to replicate this success in visual tasks, such as
      building vision-based world models for autonomous driving (Hu et al., 2023).

054
      A critical capability in autonomous driving systems is future event prediction (Guan et al., 2024).
055   However, many prediction models rely heavily on large volumes of labeled data, making them
056   vulnerable to out-of-distribution and long-tail scenarios (Santana & Hotz, 2016; Wang et al., 2023;
057   Lu et al., 2023). This is especially problematic for rare and extreme cases, such as accidents, where
058   obtaining sufficient training data is challenging. A promising solution lies in autoregressive world
059   models, which learn comprehensive information from unlabeled data like massive videos through
060   unsupervised learning. This enables more robust decision-making in driving scenarios. These
061   world models have the potential to reason under uncertainty and reduce catastrophic errors, thereby
062   improving the generalization and safety of autonomous driving systems.
063   The prior work, GAIA-1 (Hu et al., 2023), was the first to extend the GPT framework from language
064   to video, aiming to develop a video-based world model. Similar to natural language processing, GAIA
065   transforms 4D temporally correlated frames into a sequence of 1D feature tokens and employs the
066   next-token prediction strategy to generate future video clips. However, the classic GPT framework,
067   primarily designed for handling 1D contextual information, lacks the inherent capability to effectively
068   model the spatial and temporal dynamics necessary for video generation. As a result, the videos
069
      produced by GAIA-1 often suffer from low quality and noticeable artifacts, highlighting the challenge
      of achieving high fidelity and coherence within a GPT-style video generation framework.
070
071   In this paper, we introduce DrivingWorld, a driving world model built on a GPT-style video generation
072   framework. Our primary goal is to enhance the modeling of temporal coherence in an autoregressive
073   framework to create more accurate and reliable world models. To achieve this, our model incorporates
074   three key innovations: 1) Temporal-Aware Tokenization: We propose a temporal-aware tokenizer
075
      that transforms video frames into temporally coherent tokens, reformulating the task of future video
      prediction as predicting future tokens in the sequence. 2) Hybrid Token Prediction: Instead of
076
      relying solely on the next-token prediction strategy, we introduce a next-state prediction strategy to
077
      better model temporal coherence between consecutive states. Afterward, the next-token prediction
078   strategy is applied to capture spatial information within each state. 3) Long-time Controllable
079   Strategies: To improve robustness, we implement random token dropout and balanced attention
080   strategies during autoregressive training, enabling the generation of longer-duration videos with more
081   precise control.
082
      Overall, our work enhances temporal coherence in video generation using the AR framework, learning
083
      meaningful representations of future evolution. Experiments show that the proposed model achieves
084   good generalization performance, is capable of generating long-duration video sequences, and
085   provides accurate next-step trajectory predictions, maintaining a reasonable level of controllability.

090   World Model. The world model (LeCun, 2022) captures a comprehensive representation of the
091   environment and forecasts future states based on a sequence of actions. World models has been
092   extensively explored in both game (Hafner et al., 2019; 2020; 2023) and laboratory environments (Wu
093
      et al., 2023). Dreamer (Hafner et al., 2019) trains a latent dynamics model using past experiences to
      forecast state values and actions within a latent space. DreamerV2 (Hafner et al., 2020) builds upon
094
      the original Dreamer model, reaching human-level performance in Atari games. DreamerV3 Hafner
095
      et al. (2023) employs larger networks and successfully learns to acquire diamonds in Minecraft from
096   scratch. DayDreamer (Wu et al., 2023) extends Dreamer (Hafner et al., 2019) to train four robots
097   directly in the real world, successfully tackling locomotion and manipulation tasks. Recently, world
098   models for driving scenarios have garnered significant attention in both academia and industry. Most
099   previous works (Argenson & Dulac-Arnold, 2020; Diehl et al., 2021; 2023; Henaff et al., 2019) have
100   been limited to simulators or well-controlled lab environments. Drive-WM (Wang et al., 2024b)
101   explores real-world driving planners using diffusion models. GAIA-1 (Hu et al., 2023) investigates
102   real-world driving planners based on autoregressive models, but GAIA-1 has large parameters and
103   computational demands, which increase as the number of condition frames grows. In this paper, we
104
      propose an efficient world model in an autoregressive framework for autonomous driving scenarios.
105   VQVAE. VQVAE (Van Den Oord et al., 2017) learns a discrete codebook representation via vec-
106   tor quantization to model image distributions. VQGAN (Esser et al., 2021) improves realism by
107   incorporating LPIPS loss (Zhang et al., 2018) and adversarial PatchGAN loss (Isola et al., 2017).

133   Autoregressive Image Generation. Early autoregressive models for image generation operate at the
134   pixel level, using CNNs (Van den Oord et al., 2016) or RNNs (Van Den Oord et al., 2016) to predict
135   unknown pixels in a zigzag manner. Inspired by language models and enabled by VQVAE (Van
136   Den Oord et al., 2017), images are encoded into discrete tokens. This allows autoregressive models
137   to shift from operating in pixel space to working in a discrete token space. VQGAN (Esser et al.,
138
      2021), DALL-E (Ramesh et al., 2021), and Parti (Yu et al., 2022a) introduce VQVAE to encode
      continuous images to discrete tokens, and use autoregressive models to generate images in a way
139
      of next token prediction. VAR (Tian et al., 2024) decomposes an image into residuals at multiple
140
      resolutions, encoding each resolution’s residuals into a different number of discrete tokens. During
141   the next scale prediction, all tokens at a specific resolution are predicted at once, reducing the
142   number of autoregressive steps. Llamagen (Sun et al., 2024) demonstrates that vanilla autoregressive
143   models, which use the same “next-token prediction” approach as language models, can also achieve
144   state-of-the-art performance in image generation. In this paper, we take it a step further by applying
145   autoregressive models to video generation.
146
      Video Generation. Currently, there are three mainstream video generation models: GAN-based,
147   diffusion-based, and GPT-based methods. GAN-based methods (Yu et al., 2022b; Skorokhodov et al.,
148   2022; Tian et al., 2021) often face several challenges, such as mode collapse, where the diversity of
149   the videos generated by the generator becomes limited. Additionally, the adversarial learning between
150   the generator and discriminator can lead to instability during training. One major issue with diffusion-
151   based methods is their inability to generate precisely controlled videos because the stochastic nature
152   of the diffusion process introduces randomness at each step, making it difficult to enforce strict
153   control over specific attributes in the generated content. On the other hand, traditional GPT-based
154   methods (Yan et al., 2021; Han et al., 2022) allow for a certain level of control, but their computational
155
      cost grows quadratically with the sequence length, significantly impacting model efficiency. In this
      paper, we propose a decoupled spatio-temporal world model framework, which ensures precise
156
      control while significantly reducing computational cost and improving model efficiency.

178   Tokenization (Zheng et al., 2022; Van Den Oord et al., 2017) converts continuous data into discrete
179   tokens, enabling integration with language models and enhanced multimodal sequence modeling.
180   In our approach, the tokenizer maps multimodal states into a unified discrete space, which enables
181   accurate and controllable multimodal generation. To produce temporally consistent embeddings
182   for images, we propose a temporal-aware vector quantized tokenizer. Our proposed vehicle pose
183   tokenizer discretizes pose trajectories and integrates them into our DrivingWorld.

227   3.2      W ORLD M ODEL
228
      The world model aims to comprehend past inputs, mimic real-world dynamics, and predict future
229
      states. In our context, it forecasts upcoming driving scenarios and plans a feasible future trajectory.
230
      To do this, the world model concatenates historical state tokens  {\{ \left [\phi _{t}, v_{t}, \mathbf {q}_{t}\right ] \}}_{t=1}^{T}  into a long sequence,
231
      where the 2D image tokens are unfolded into a 1D form in zig-zag order. Thus the objective is to
232

234
      tokens from different modalities are mapped into a shared latent space by their respective learnable
235
      embedding layers before being fed to the world model, i.e. ht = Emb(rt ). All subsequent processes
236   are conducted within this latent space.

344
345   Token Dropout for Drifting-free Autoregression. During training, the world model uses past
346   ground-truth tokens as conditioning to predict the next tokens. However, during inference, the model
347   must rely on previously generated tokens for conditioning, which may contain imperfections. Training
348   solely with perfect ground-truth images can lead to a content drifting problem during inference,
349
      causing rapid degradation and eventual failure in the generated outputs. To address this, we propose a
      random masking strategy (RMS), where some tokens from ground-truth tokens are randomly dropped
350
      out. Each token has a 50% chance of being replaced by another random token in this frame, and this
351
      dropout is applied to the entire conditioning image sequence with a probability of 30%. As shown in
352   Figure 4, this dropout strategy significantly mitigates the drifting issue during inference.
353
354   Balanced Attention For Precise Control. The world model utilizes extensive attention operations
355
      to exchange and fuse information among tokens. However, each video frame in our experiments
      contains 512 tokens, while only 2 tokens represent the ego pose (orientation and location). This
356
      imbalance can cause the model to overlook pose signals, leading to unsatisfactory controllable
357
      generation. To address this, we propose a balanced attention operation to achieve more precise
358   control by prioritizing ego state tokens in the attention mechanism, rather than attending to all tokens
359   equally. Specifically, we manually increase the weights of the orientation and location tokens in the
360   attention map (before the softmax layer), adding constant weights of 0.4 and 0.2, respectively, to
361   these tokens. Additionally, we incorporate QK-norm (Henry et al., 2020) and 2D-rotary positional
362   encoding (Su et al., 2024) to further stabilize training and enhance performance.

520   stable computational costs and memory usage, avoiding the sharp scaling seen in GPT-2. Moreover,

524   5   C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
525
526   In conclusion, DrivingWorld addressed the limitations of previous video generation models in
527   autonomous driving by leveraging a GPT-style framework to produce longer, high-fidelity video
528   predictions with improved generalization. Unlike traditional methods that struggle with coherence
529   in long sequences or rely heavily on labeled data, DrivingWorld generated realistic, structured
530   video sequences while enabling precise action control. Compared to the classic GPT structure, our
531   proposed spatial-temporal GPT structure adopts next-state-prediction strategy to model temporal
532   coherence between consecutive frames and then apply next-token-prediction strategy to capture spatial
533   information within a frame. Looking ahead, we plan to incorporate more multimodal information
534
      and integrate multiple visual inputs. By fusing data from various modalities and viewpoints, we
      aim to improve action control and video generation accuracy, enhancing the model’s ability to
535
      understand complex driving environments and further boosting the overall performance and reliability
536
      of autonomous driving systems.
