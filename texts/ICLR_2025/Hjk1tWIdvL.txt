009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Pre-filling Large Language Models (LLMs) with long-context inputs is
012            computationally expensive due to the quadratic complexity of full attention.
013            While global attention is essential during decoding, its importance diminishes
014            during pre-filling, where the focus is on contextualizing tokens rather than
015            predicting the next one. Building on prior work, we apply diagonal block sparse
016
               attention during the pre-filling phase, reducing attention-related FLOPs by over
               90% without significant degradation in language modeling performance. To
017
               address the remaining performance gap, we propose Hierarchy-Aided Sparse
018
               Attention (HASA), which incorporates a specialized transformer branch. This
019            branch extracts global embeddings from each chunk and aligns local attention
020            with full-attention, facilitating cross-chunk interaction. HASA stabilizes sparse
021            attention computations, making the pre-filling phase highly efficient, particularly
022            in long-sequence scenarios. While HASA significantly accelerates the pre-filling
023            phase, we ensure robust language modeling performance by enabling interaction
024            between global embeddings across chunks, which prevents the performance
025            degradation typically observed in sparse attention mechanisms. Given that there
026            are limited methods specifically accelerating pre-filling, our baselines include
027
               various open-source long-context models. Across multiple benchmarks, HASA
               not only maintains performance but also outperforms baseline models in certain
028
               scenarios. We will release the models upon acceptance.

      To better understand the impact of sequence length on latency, we analyze the scaling behavior of
079   TTFT and TPOT individually. As shown in Figure 1, TTFT exhibits quadratic growth with sequence
080   length, reaching up to 80 seconds for prompts with 128K tokens. This suggests that users may
081   experience delays exceeding one minute before receiving the initial token, potentially degrading
082   their interaction experience. Conversely, TPOT scales linearly with sequence length, reaching
083   approximately 0.15 seconds per token, even with a key-value (KV) cache of 128K tokens. Thus,
084   TTFT is the sole component in the latency equation that exhibits quadratic scaling. As sequence
085   lengths increase, this quadratic growth in TTFT poses a significant challenge to the deployment
086   of long-context LLMs, leading to substantial delays in generating the first token, which must be
087
      addressed for scalability. Therefore, our work addresses the question: How can we accelerate the
      pre-filling phase to mitigate TTFT’s quadratic scaling without compromising model performance?
088
089   Building on this inquiry, we revisit pioneering research on efficient pre-filling (de Jong et al., 2023;
090   Ivgi et al., 2023), which suggests that while global attention is crucial for decoding, it is less
091   important during pre-filling. In this phase, the goal is to convert each token into its contextual
092   representation rather than predict the next token. This makes global attention less critical, especially
093
      in long-context tasks like retrieval-augmented generation (RAG), where retrieved passages are
      largely independent. Therefore, applying local attention during the pre-filling phase is highly
094
      reasonable. Inspired by this, we conducted preliminary experiments on LLaMA2-7B (Touvron et al.,
095
      2023a), applying diagonal block sparse attention during the pre-filling phase. We found that this
096   method did not result in significant degradation of language modeling performance, while reducing
097   attention-related FLOPs by over 90%. To further close the performance gap between this naive
098   method and full-attention pre-filling, we re-examined earlier hierarchical attention methods (Guo
099   et al., 2022; Zhu & Soricut, 2021). Building on these ideas, we introduce Hierarchy-Aided Sparse
100   Attention (HASA), which incorporates a specialized branch to recover the global information lost in
101   diagonal block sparse attention. Specifically, we extract a global embedding from each chunk and
102   process the sequence of global embeddings through this specialized branch, encouraging interaction
103   and fusion among them. These global embeddings are then prepended to their respective chunks
104
      and used to modulate attention scores. By calibrating the attention scores for each chunk, we align
      local attention with full-attention, stabilizing sparse attention computation and enabling the model
105
      to maintain strong language modeling performance even when processing long sequences.
106
107   We implement HASA on LLaMA2-7B (Touvron et al., 2023a) and LLaMA2-7B-32K (Together.AI,
      2023a), fine-tuning them using the LoRA method (Hu et al., 2022). Our approach is evaluated across

      Causal Attention. Causual attention is a parameterized module that takes in three inputs, Q, K, V ,
116
      each of size N × D, where N is the sequence length and D is the model’s dimension. It works as:
117
                    CausalAttention(Q, K, V ) = softmax(QWq Wk⊤ K ⊤ ) ⊙ M V Wv ,

118                                                                                                   (2)
119   where M is a matrix that only allows information to flow from earlier to later parts of the sequence
120   (like a one-way street). We skip some details for now, such as normalization after softmax, but in
121   the actual code, we do some extra steps to make sure everything works smoothly. In a typical setup,
122   if X is the input data, the output is calculated as CausalAttention(X, X, X). This process has a
123   complexity of O(N 2 D), which means it gets more demanding as the sequence gets longer.
124   There are special tools like Flash Attention (Dao, 2024) and PyTorch SDPA (Guessous, 2024) that
125   use powerful computing techniques to speed things up. These have helped a lot, but they don’t
126   change the basic problem that the complexity grows with the square of the sequence length.
127
      Efficient LLMs. Before the advent of LLMs, there were efficient transformer models that used
128
      sparse or linear attention (Wang et al., 2020; Zaheer et al., 2020) But now, training LLMs is very
129
      expense, so it’s smarter to improve existing LLMs rather than building new ones from the start.
130   LM-Infinite (Han et al., 2024) and Streaming-LLM (Xiao et al., 2024) use a lambda-shaped attention
131   that deals with long texts by keeping the time and memory needed constant. But they might skip over
132   important words. Techniques such as H2 O (Zhang et al., 2023b), Scissorhands (Liu et al., 2023),
133   and FastGen (Ge et al., 2024) make LLMs faster by reducing the size of KV cache. They do this by
134   making the attention less dense. Other innovations, such as Multi-Query Attention (Shazeer, 2019)
135   and Grouped-Query Attention (Ainslie et al., 2023), use fewer attention heads to save memory.
136   Methods like OmniQuant (Wenqi et al., 2024) and AffineQuant (Ma et al., 2024) make LLMs faster
137   by the quantization. While these methods speed up LLMs, they only reduce the complexity in certain
138
      parts, not the overall problem of handling long sequence, which remains challenging.

142   Is Full Attention Computation Essential for Pre-filling Tokens? In
143   early sparse attention models like Longformer (Beltagy et al., 2020)
144   and Big Bird (Zaheer et al., 2020), inputs are categorized into global
145   tokens and local tokens. Global tokens leverage full attention to access
146   the entire sequence, crucial for downstream tasks, while local tokens
147
      apply local attention to generate context from their surroundings. This
      methodology has proven effective for long-sequence modeling (Joshi
148
      et al., 2017; Yang et al., 2018; Tu et al., 2019), maintaining model
149
      capacity without inefficient attention. Drawing inspiration from this,
150   we question whether similar concepts could be applied to LLMs. For Figure 3: Diagonal block
151   example, during the pre-filling phase, token embeddings serve only as sparse attention with a
152   context for later decoding, not for token generation. Might we then use chunk size S = 3.
153   sparse local attention for these tokens exclusively for pre-filling tokens?
154   After pre-filling, we could revert to full attention during generation phase, as these tokens will
155   predict subsequent tokens. The success of sparse attention as a viable alternative to full attention
156   supports our preliminary investigation into applying it within LLMs.
157   One of the main challenges resulting from the sparse attention is that many implementations (Jiang
158   et al., 2024; Yao et al., 2024) require specialized kernel optimizations for efficient computation.

      4.1   H IERARCHICAL ATTENTION A PPROXIMATION
240
241   In recent years, the advent of sparse attention mechanisms has spurred the development of innovative
242   approaches to restore lost information via low-resolution or low-rank approximations. For instance,
243   the H-Transformer-1D model (Zhu & Soricut, 2021) downsamples tokens based on their proximity
244   to the sequence head, representing distant tokens at a lower resolution. Similarly, LongT5 (Guo
245   et al., 2022) pools tokens beyond the immediate local window into global tokens using average
246   pooling, then attends to these global tokens to recover omitted details.
247   Inspired by these methods, a straightforward strategy to approximate full attention is to maintain
248   high fidelity for local interactions with higher-resolution representations, while using less precise
249   approximations for distant interactions with lower-resolution representations. Specifically, for each
250   segment Xt , a summarized, low-resolution representation can be computed as follows:
251
                                              xt = AvgPool(Xt ).                                         (7)

253
      Subsequently, the missing component could be approximated by

      4.2   H IERARCHICAL T RANSFORMER
282
      To integrate hierarchical attention into a conventional attention mechanism, we developed a method
283   adhering to the original computational framework. This introduces a specialized branch to process
284   global information, forming the foundation of our hierarchical transformer.
285
286   Specifically, the specialized branch computes a global embedding representation X̃ =
287
      [x̃1 , · · · , x̃M ] ∈ RM ×D that encapsulates the global interactions of the input sequence. Here, we
      assume that each embedding representation x̃t captures the interactions between the t-th chunk and
288
289
      other chunks. The computation of X̃ will be detailed shortly. For the T -th chunk, the component
      RT is recalculated, providing an alternative to Eq. (9), as follows:
290
291                                        RT ≈ Attention(XT , x̃T , x̃T ).                             (10)
292
      Then, the precise attention computation is defined as:
293
294                   OT ≈ CausalAttention(XT , XT , XT ) + Attention(XT , x̃T , x̃T ).                    (11)
295
      This approach incorporates global information X̃ into each chunk while utilizing local attention,
296
      and facilitates information exchange among tokens within each chunk. Subsequently, OT is fed into
297
      the transformer’s following modules in the standard manner.
298
299   To ensure the dedicated branch outputs a representation X̃ that accurately captures global
300   interactions and is easily interpretable by the model, we proceed as follows. We take the embedding
301   X−1 from the previous layer Transformer−1 and compute its pooled representation X −1 as:
302                                        X −1 = [x−1,1        ···   x−1,M ] .                            (12)
303
304   Then, we directly utilize the pre-trained layer of the same transformer to compute X̃:
305
                                            X̃ = Transformer−1 (X −1 ),                                    (13)
306
307   To prevent lookahead, which refers to the leakage of information within a chunk through
308   low-resolution tokens as depicted in Figure 2, the causal attention mechanism has been subtly
309   modified. This modification is achieved by introducing a shifted causal mask, which is created
310   by removing the main diagonal from the original causal mask. We analyze that, in contrast to
311   the hard-coded attention from Eq. (9), Eq. (10) also guides XT to attend to xt with more adaptive
312   weights, since x̃T ∈ X̃ attends to xt in a manner akin to regular causal attention.
313   Following these enhancements, we conducted LoRA fine-tuning on all projection matrices within the
314   self-attention and MLP modules, ensuring the model’s performance is optimized without extensive
315   retraining. Empirical findings in Table 1 indicate that when the pretrained LLMs are augmented with
316   our method, they exhibit exceptional performance and efficiency across various tasks.
317
      The extensive preliminary research (Shen et al., 2018; Ye et al., 2019; Guo et al., 2019; Sukhbaatar
318   et al., 2019) into hierarchical attention has confirmed its effectiveness. As the importance of
319   long-context LLMs grows, the need for sub-quadratic hierarchical attention mechanisms has become
320   more critical. However, a significant limitation of these hierarchical attentions is that they are
321   not designed for LLMs, often requiring training from scratch, which is impractical in LLM
322   environments. Our research builds upon the successes of previous methods while addressing their
323   limitations, developing an innovative hierarchical attention mechanism that enables full performance
      recovery of pretrained LLMs through a few steps of LoRA fine-tuning.

473   The comprehensive benchmark results demonstrate the strong performance of HASA. In this
474   subsection, we focus on its efficiency, specifically assessing whether it achieves our goal of

476   Theoretically, HASA Should Exhibit Nearly Linear Pre-filling Latency. The low-resolution
477   branch handles fewer tokens, reducing its quadratic term by a factor of S 2 compared to the full
478   attention baseline. Consequently, the main latency arises from the high-resolution branch, where
479   latency grows linearly with sequence length.

481   with an 8×RTX3090 machine to evaluate the pre-filling latency scaling behavior.            For

508
509   First, while HASA can accelerate the pre-filling phase, this advantage becomes more evident

512   training-free approaches.
513
514   7     C ONCLUSION
515
516   In this work, we revisited efficient pre-filling strategies and demonstrated that applying diagonal
517   block sparse attention during the pre-filling phase effectively reduces computational costs by
518   over 90%, without significant degradation in language modeling performance. To further close
519   the performance gap between this naive method and full-attention pre-filling, we introduced
520   Hierarchy-Aided Sparse Attention (HASA), which leverages a specialized branch to recover global
521   information discarded by sparse attention. By processing global embeddings across chunks and
522
      calibrating attention scores, HASA stabilizes the sparse attention computation, allowing the model
      to handle long sequences while maintaining robust language modeling performance.
523
524   Our implementation of HASA on LLaMA2-7B and LLaMA2-7B-32K, fine-tuned with LoRA,
525   demonstrated improved pre-filling efficiency and significantly reduced time to first token (TTFT)
526   across a variety of benchmarks, including language modeling, few-shot NLU tasks, Needle in a
527   Haystack, and LongBench. These results confirm that HASA not only accelerates pre-filling but
528
      also sustains, and in some cases, enhances model performance.

724   Decoding Simulation. Since our focus is solely on the decoding stage—specifically on the output
725   and internal properties of tokens with global attention—applying language modeling loss to tokens
726
      from the pre-filling stage is unnecessary, as their key-value representations serve only as contextual
      embeddings, not for generating output. To improve data utilization, we aim to enable every token to
727
      function as a decoding-stage token for language modeling. Achieving this within a single forward
728
      pass is insufficient, as a token cannot simultaneously serve as both a pre-filling and decoding-stage
729   token in the same pass. Therefore, we propose a two-pass approach. In the first forward pass, all
730   tokens are treated as pre-filling-stage tokens, generating an approximate key-value (KV) cache at
731   each layer. In the second pass, every token acts as a decoding-stage token, attending to the KV
732   cache generated from the first pass. This decoding simulation approach, illustrated in Figure 8, is
733   employed in both LLaMA2-7B with HASA and LLaMA2-7B-32K with HASA architectures.
734
      Position Embedding within a Chunk. For RoPE (Su et al., 2021) within a chunk, there are two
735   encoding strategies. The first involves encoding based on the token’s position within the original
736   sequence, while the second encodes the token relative to its position within the chunk itself. In
737   post-training settings, both strategies deliver comparable performance. As a result, we adopted the
738   second approach for both LLaMA2-7B with HASA and LLaMA2-7B-32K with HASA, as it offers
739   easier implementation. However, in training-free scenarios, the choice of position encoding has a
740   more significant impact. Specifically, using the first method can lead to model instability, whereas
741   the second method maintains robust performance. This behavior is analyzed in detail by Xiao et al.
742   (2024), who propose adding a sink token to each chunk as a solution. This modification allows the
743
      first encoding method to regain its stability and match the performance of the second method in
      training-free setups, achieving near lossless results.
