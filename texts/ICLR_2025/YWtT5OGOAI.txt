010
011
               View synthesis using Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF) and Gaussian Splatting (GS)
012
               has demonstrated impressive fidelity in rendering real-world scenarios. However,
013
               practical methods for accurate and efficient epistemic Uncertainty Quantification
014            (UQ) in view synthesis are lacking. Existing approaches for NeRF either intro-
015            duce significant computational overhead (e.g., “10x increase in training time” or
016            “10x repeated training”) or are limited to specific uncertainty conditions or mod-
017            els. Notably, GS models lack any systematic approach for comprehensive epis-
018            temic UQ. This capability is crucial for improving the robustness and scalability
019            of neural view synthesis, enabling active model updates, error estimation, and
020            scalable ensemble modeling based on uncertainty. In this paper, we revisit NeRF
021            and GS-based methods from a function approximation perspective, identifying
022

               insights, we introduce PH-D ROPOUT, the first real-time and accurate method for
023

024
               GS models. Extensive evaluations validate our theoretical findings and demon-
025            strate the effectiveness of PH-D ROPOUT.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Emerging approaches in view synthesis, such as Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF) (Mildenhall et al.,
032   2021) and Gaussian Splatting (GS) (Kerbl et al., 2023b), have demonstrated remarkable advance-
033   ments in rendering quality and efficiency. These methods transcend synthetic datasets to embrace
034   real-world, unconstrained scenarios, setting new standards for generating highly realistic 3D scenes
035   that are nearly indistinguishable from reality. However, achieving high-quality results requires a
036   large number of known views for training, ensuring that the model is exposed to multiple perspec-
037   tives near any arbitrary target viewpoint. Previous works (Goli et al., 2024; Sünderhauf et al., 2023;
038   Hu et al., 2024) have highlighted that training view synthesis models from a discrete set of multi-
039
      view images is fraught with uncertainty. Even under ideal experimental conditions, occlusions and
      missing views inherently limit the epistemic knowledge that the model can acquire about the scene.
040
041   Studying epistemic uncertainty in view synthesis is crucial for understanding the limitations of algo-
042   rithms, identifying gaps in model performance, improving the reliability of predictions, and ensur-
043   ing effective generalization to unseen data. This investigation is pivotal for advancing the robustness
044   and accuracy of view synthesis methods, yet research in this area remains limited. Broadly, related
045
      work can be classified into two categories: (1) Direct estimation of overall epistemic uncertainty,
      as seen in methods such as S-NeRF (Shen et al., 2021), CF-NeRF (Shen et al., 2022), and NeRF
046
      OTG (Ren et al., 2024), which apply deep ensemble techniques to NeRF with significant computa-
047
      tional overhead. (2) Investigation of specific factors contributing to epistemic uncertainty, such as
048   Bayes Rays (Goli et al., 2024) and CG-SLAM (Hu et al., 2024), which focus primarily on spatial
049   (depth) uncertainty caused by a lack of views. In fact, epistemic uncertainty can be caused by a range
050   of factors beyond mere lack of training data, including inadequate feature representation and model
051   misspecification. Traditional methods for estimating epistemic uncertainty, such as Monte-Carlo
052   dropout (Gal & Ghahramani, 2016) and random initialization (Lee et al., 2015; Lakshminarayanan
053   et al., 2017), prove impractical for view synthesis due to conflicts with the training paradigm or the
      prohibitively high computational demands, requiring hours even for simple scenarios.

054
      Another limitation is efficiency. Ensemble-based approaches can require hours of additional train-
055   ing for a single bounded object. Furthermore, there is a noticeable gap between GS and NeRF
056   models, as existing methods lack versatility and are typically applicable to only one of these frame-
057   works. However, we observe that NeRF-based and GS-based solutions excel in different scenarios.
058   A versatile method integrating both approaches could enable the development of a new rendering
059   technique that combines their respective strengths.
060
      Driven by the goal of developing a more efficient, accurate, and versatile approach for estimating
061   epistemic uncertainty in view synthesis, we revisit current view synthesis methods through the lens
062   of function learning and approximation theory. We make the following key observations:
063   • Existing view synthesis models often exhibit substantial parameter redundancy; specifically,
064      their performance on the training set remains unaffected by the application of appropriate dropout.
065   • However, while no observable impact is seen on training views, significant performance variance
066      is evident on test views.
067
      These insights lead us to propose a relatively underexplored post hoc epistemic uncertainty estima-
068
      tion method: (1) insert dropout on trained fully connected layers (NeRF) or splats (GS); (2) increase
069   the dropout ratio as long as the performance on the training set remains unaffected; (3) measure the
070   variation after dropout on the testing set as a quantification of epistemic uncertainty (UQ).
071
072
      In summary, our contributions are:
073
      • We propose PH-D ROPOUT, the first approach, to our knowledge, that quantifies epistemic uncer-
         tainty for view synthesis in real-time, orders of magnitude faster, without additional training, and
074
         applicable to both NeRF and GS methods.
075
      • We conduct comprehensive experiments with PH-D ROPOUT on NeRF-based and GS-based meth-
076      ods, providing the first in-depth comparison of their features beyond training speed and fidelity.
077   • We evaluate the effectiveness of PH-D ROPOUT through several downstream use cases, including
078      active learning and model ensembling, where it demonstrates promising performance in support-
079      ing these applications.

082   2     BACKGROUND
083
084   2.1   E PISTEMIC UQ WITH S PECIALIZED T RAINING A PPROACH
085
086   We explore general methods for epistemic UQ and explain why they are unsuitable for typical view
087   synthesis tasks, highlighting the need for a novel approach. These traditional methods impose sig-
088   nificant computational overhead and impose strict limitations on model selection.
089   Random initialization (Lee et al., 2015; Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017): is one technique used to
090   measure epistemic uncertainty, particularly in the context of deep learning. However, it is not the
091   most comprehensive method for capturing all aspects of uncertainty for following reason.
092   • Training overhead. Repeatedly retraining the model can be impractical, especially when the
093     training process is slow or computationally expensive.
094   • Random initialization cannot address limitations inherent in the model architecture itself.
095     If the model is not capable of learning the true underlying distribution, then random initialization
096     won’t reveal this inadequacy clearly.
097   Monte Carlo Dropout (Gal & Ghahramani, 2016): Applying dropout at test time and averaging
098   predictions can be a cheaper way to estimate uncertainty without needing to train multiple models.
099   NeRF’s tendency to overfit arises from its high model capacity, the limited and specific nature of
100   its training data, and its per-scene training approach. However, dropout is a method to prevent
101   overfitting, which prevents NeRF to memorize the scene in training set. Empirically, we find that
102   training with dropout in conventional NeRF places negative effect on performance, and hence MC
103   dropout is not suitable for NeRF. Also GS models do not have a neural network, and hence hard to
104
      implement BNN based methods. Similar methods can be find in MC-batchnorm (Teye et al., 2018),
      where a deterministic network trained with batch normalization, which is also maintained during
105
      testing for UQ. Detailed discussion on applying MC-dropout on NeRF is enclosed in Appendix A.2
106
107   Deep Ensemble Methods (Lakshminarayanan et al., 2017): Training multiple models (not just with
      different initializations but potentially with different architectures or hyperparameters) and aggre-


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      gating their predictions can provide a more robust estimate of uncertainty. S-NeRF (Shen et al.,
109   2021), CF-NeRF (Shen et al., 2022), and 3D Uncertainty Field (Shen et al., 2024) are based on this
110   concept but with expensive training overhead and poor performance.
111
112   2.2   P OST HOC EPISTEMIC UQ
113
114   Post hoc epistemic UQ refers to techniques for assessing a trained model’s epistemic uncertainty
115   without altering its original training process. The most frequently used frameworks for modeling
116   uncertainty of neural networks are often not agnostic to the network architecture and task, and also
117   require modifications in the optimization and training processes, including MC-dropout and deep
118   ensembles. This is why post hoc UQ is desirable – it can be applied to already trained architectures.
119   We distinguish our study with the epistemic UQ in well pretrained models (Wang & Ji, 2024;
120   Schweighofer et al., 2023). By well pretrained, the basic assumption of this method is the model
121   is trained with sufficient in-distribution training data D, so that ∀x ∈ X → p(x ∈  / D) < ϵ, where
122   X is the set of potential inputs, ϵ is a small positive number. However, typical view synthesis tasks
123   often face missing training views, making such methods unsuitable. Therefore, we exclude them as
124   baselines in this paper.
125   Bayes Rays (Goli et al., 2024), based on Laplace approximation (Ritter et al., 2018), meets the
126   post hoc requirements for practical application, requiring only a few additional training epochs.
127   However, it solely models spatial uncertainty (depth prediction error) in NeRF and is not applicable
128   to GS-based models. Previous work (Ledda et al., 2023) has also shown that in ad network based on
129   fully connected layers, inject dropout during the inference time can achieve similar effect as MC-
130   dropout (Gal & Ghahramani, 2016) after calibration. However, it is non-trivial to apply this method
131
      to view synthesis model. NeRF does not use dropout during the training, so it is hard to justify the
      approximation of MC-dropout. GS model even does not have a typical neural network.

      3.2   C ONDITIONS OF U SING PH-D ROPOUT : F EATURES IN V IEW S YNTHESIS
178
179   The following phenomenons are integral to our reasoning and will be validated through experiments.
180
181
      Phenomenon 1: The rendering function is not stochastic since we try to render a static object.
182   Phenomenon 2: After dropout, as long as there is no change on training set performance, the
183   expectation performance on evaluation set only has negligible change as well (see §5).
184
      Besides empirical observations, we also notice a common features across all NeRF-based and GS-
185   based method: there must be redundancy in model parameters θ, which is explained in detail in
186   Theorem 3.1.
187
      Theorem 3.1. As long as the model is properly trained with overfitting (L(x) → 0 on training set),
188
      there must be significant redundancy in NeRF and GS model, i.e.,
189
190                       ∃ 0 ≪ r < 1 → ∀x ∈ Dtrain , |F (x; θ) − F (x; D(θ, r))| < ϵ
191
192   Proof. (Sketch) The rendering function is neither purely continuous nor purely discrete, but rather
193   a combination of both. Achieving fine convergence using exclusively continuous methods (e.g.,
194   NeRF) or discrete methods (e.g., 3DGS) requires an infinite number of Fourier components (as in
195   NeRF with positional encoding) or splats (as in splatting-based methods like 3DGS) to approximate
196   functions with varying degrees of continuity.
197   NeRF: Continuous function → discrete representation. For many natural signals (Oppenheim et al.,
198   1997), the amplitude of Fourier coefficients cn decay rapidly as |n| increases, especially when sig-
199   nal is “continuously differentiable”. This means that the lower-frequency components (those with
200   smaller |n|) contain most of the signal’s power, while the higher-frequency components (larger |n|)
201   contribute very little to the total power. To overfit a function with nearly discrete pattern, many low
202   power and high frequency components are introduced.
203   GS: Discrete splats → continuous representation. Similar to the Fourier transform, where fine
204   spatial details (higher frequency components) generally have lower power, the training of GS models
205   follows a similar pattern. A few large splats are used to capture the broader, background features,
206   while numerous smaller splats are introduced to capture finer details and subtle variations in the
207   rendering function.
208   As a result, in both NeRF and GS, to overfit the details of rendering function, most of the components
209   have very low power and therefore are robust to dropout.
210
211   Theorem 3.1 also highlights that NeRF-based and GS-based methods exhibit varying levels of redun-
212   dancy at different regions of an object or scene. Both experimental results and theoretical analysis
213   later demonstrate that this variation in redundancy patterns contributes to distinct robustness and
214   reliability when capturing specific features. By leveraging an ensemble approach that selects the
215   components with the lowest epistemic uncertainty, we achieve a significant improvement in view
      synthesis fidelity.

219   rameters, as this redundancy is by default required for proper convergence.

      4.1   E FFECTIVENESS A NALYSIS OF PH-D ROPOUT IN N E RF
225
      Here we discuss NeRF based methods with encoding that can reflect the spacial proximity faithfully,
226   including Positional Encoding (PE) (Tancik et al., 2020), Sinusoidal PE (SPE) (Sun et al., 2024),
227   etc. We discuss hash encoding-based methods separately in Section §4.3 due to the impact of their
228   unique probabilistic operations.
229
      Lemma 4.1. If two models a and b with same structural and number of parameter, have similar
230
      distribution of parameter, i.e., DKL (θa , θb ) < ϵ, and they both converge on the same dataset D,
231   they can be obtained via random initialization with the same setup with a or b. Meanwhile, the
232   probability density to obtain model a and b will be close, i.e., if p(a) is significant, then p(b) should
233   be significant as well.
234
235
      Proof. (Sketch). This can be proven based on the continuousity of the space of model parameter.
236   The random initialization of weights of MLP (e.g., in NeRF) from continuous distributions, which
237   influences the training dynamics Glorot & Bengio (2010). These continuous distributions provide
238   the network’s starting point for training, and as training proceeds, the weights and biases are up-
239   dated continuously by optimization algorithms (e.g., stochastic gradient descent). These updates
240   are applied to real-valued weights, thus ensuring that the neural network’s parameters remain in the
241   continuous space Rd , where d is the number of parameters Raghu et al. (2017). Because the func-
242   tion space Rd is continuous, if the two models have small KL partition, i.e., DKL (θa , θb ) < ϵ, then
243   p(a) ≈ p(b) in random initialisation because of the continuousity.
244
245   With Lemma 4.1, we establish a connection between random initialization (Lakshminarayanan et al.,
246   2017; Lee et al., 2015) and PH-D ROPOUT, where each ensemble in PH-D ROPOUT should exhibit
247   significant probability density, assuming the trained model (w/o dropout) maintains substantial prob-
248
      ability density within the function space. This forms the following Theorem.
249   Theorem 4.2. The variance after PH-D ROPOUT represents a biased epistemic uncertainty estima-
250   tion.
251
252   Proof. (Sketch) With PH-D ROPOUT, the KL divergence of the remaining parameters from the orig-
253   inal model is small. For instance, in NeRF, dropout is applied to only one hidden layer, leaving most
254   parameters unchanged. Similarly, in the GS model, most splats have low power (see Proof of Theo-
255   rem 3.1), and setting them to zero minimally impacts the overall power distribution.
256   We can further assume that the trained model is not an outlier and that the probability density of
257   obtaining F after random initialization is significant. Consequently, models after dropout represent
258   a biased subset of the ensemble, and their variation captures epistemic uncertainty (see detailed
259   discussion in Appendix A.3, Theorem A.2), as the probability density of these functions is non-
260   negligible, consistent with Lemma 4.1.
261
262   We have demonstrated that PH-D ROPOUT is an effective approach for producing ensembles in
263   NeRF, with the ensemble variation reflecting a biased estimation of epistemic uncertainty. Next,
264   we extend this reasoning to the GS model.

514
515   6.1   D IFFERENT P ERFORMANCE ON N E RF AND GS, D IFFERENT E NCODING M ETHODS
516
517
      Throughout this paper, NeRF and GS-based methods exhibit distinct patterns in redundancy and
      correlation with RMSE. As indicated by Theorem 3.1, these differences arise from the fundamental
518
      ways each method approximates the rendering function. Figure 7 illustrates that NeRF tends to
519
      show higher error and uncertainty at object edges, while GS models display increased uncertainty
520   on smooth surfaces. This behavior aligns with the theorem and is a key factor behind the distinct
521   UQ performance of each method.
522
523
      6.2   L IMITATIONS OF PH-D ROPOUT
524
525   PH-D ROPOUT struggles with UQ in the presence of input hash collisions or similar model collisions
526   in 2DGS, limiting its applicability to hash encoding-based methods (Müller et al., 2022; Tancik
527   et al., 2023). Additionally, PH-D ROPOUT is specifically designed for view synthesis tasks; further
528   research is required to adapt it for other applications.

      7     C ONCLUSION
531
532
      We present PH-D ROPOUT, an efficient and effective epistemic uncertainty quantification (UQ)
533
      method for view synthesis, designed to operate directly on trained models. PH-D ROPOUT is com-
534   patible with both NeRF and GS-based methods and can be applied to both bounded objects and
535   unbounded scenarios. By offering fast inference and easy implementation, PH-D ROPOUT makes
536   epistemic UQ practical and stands as the first training-free method for UQ in GS models. Extensive
537   evaluations across a broad range of downstream applications highlight its effectiveness. Theoretical
538   analysis of PH-D ROPOUT also uncovers fundamental differences and connections between NeRF
539   and GS rendering methods, paving the way for future research to enhance their efficiency, fidelity,
      and scalability.
