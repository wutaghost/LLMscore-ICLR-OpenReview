011                                                  A BSTRACT
012
013            In this paper, we introduce FAMMA, an open-source benchmark for financial
014            multilingual multimodal question answering (QA).1 Our benchmark aims to eval-
015            uate the abilities of multimodal large language models (MLLMs) in answering
016            questions that require advanced financial knowledge and sophisticated reason-
               ing. It includes 1,758 meticulously collected question-answer pairs from uni-
017
               versity textbooks and exams, spanning 8 major subfields in finance including
018            corporate finance, asset management, and financial engineering. Some of the
019            QA pairs are written in Chinese or French, while a majority of them are in
020            English. These questions are presented in a mixed format combining text and
021            heterogeneous image types, such as charts, tables, and diagrams. We evaluate
               a range of state-of-the-art MLLMs on our benchmark, and our analysis shows
022
               that FAMMA poses a significant challenge for these models. Even advanced
023            systems like GPT-4o and Claude-35-Sonnet achieve only 42% accuracy. Addi-
024            tionally, the open-source Qwen2-VL lags notably behind its proprietary counter-
025            parts. Lastly, we explore GPT o1-style reasoning chains to enhance the mod-
026            els’ reasoning capabilities, which significantly improve error correction. Our
               FAMMA benchmark will facilitate future research to develop expert systems in
027
               financial QA. The code and data have been anonymously released at https:
028            //github.com/random2024GO/bench-script.

031   1    I NTRODUCTION

033   Benchmarks have played a pivotal role in advancing AI research, particularly in the realm of large
034   language models (LLMs) (Brown et al., 2020; OpenAI, 2023; Touvron et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2023;
035   2024; Meta, 2024). Benchmarks have been helping researchers track the advancement of LLMs in a
036   variety of capabilities, including general language understanding and knowledge acquisition (Wang
037   et al., 2019; Hendrycks et al., 2021a; Zhou et al., 2023; Wang et al., 2024b), code generation (Chen
038   et al., 2021a; Liu et al., 2023; Jimenez et al., 2024), mathematical reasoning (Cobbe et al., 2021;
039   Hendrycks et al., 2021b), tool use (Yan et al., 2024; Srinivasan et al., 2023; Trivedi et al., 2024), and
040   legal reasoning (Guha et al., 2023). Meanwhile, we have seen a scarcity of high-quality benchmarks
041
      in financial reasoning, an area where practitioners are eager to benefit from LLMs.
042   We envision that LLMs will have a broad and significant impact in the finance industry, enabling
043   intelligent systems that can assist human experts in various tasks such as risk management and pre-
044   dictive analytics. Towards this goal, high-quality benchmarks are needed to track the capabilities of
045   LLMs in understanding financial knowledge and answering complex financial questions. Unfortu-
046   nately, existing benchmarks in this domain cannot fully reflect the nature of daily work of financial
047
      practitioners: they only have text-based questions; they are all in English; answering their questions
      only requires knowledge at a rudimentary to intermediate level (Hendrycks et al., 2021a; Chen et al.,
048
      2021b; Islam et al., 2023; Xie et al., 2024). For a detailed comparison, refer to section 2. However,
049
      financial practitioners often have to handle other data modalities, read documents in other languages,
050   and use advanced knowledge. For example, traders often rely on charts to identify trading opportu-
051   nities; financial analysts need to analyze documents that include many complicated tables; investors

080   In this paper, we present FAMMA, an open-source benchmark for financial multilingual multimodal
081   question answering (QA). Figure 2 displays three QA examples in our benchmark. Compared to
082   existing benchmarks, FAMMA has a significantly better reflection of the real problems that finan-
083
      cial practitioners address on a daily basis. This benchmark includes 1,758 meticulously collected
      question-answer pairs from university textbooks and exams, spanning 8 major subfields including
084
      corporate finance, asset management, and financial engineering. Answering these questions requires
085
      advanced knowledge such as factor models, option pricing, and asset allocation. A good portion of
086   the QA pairs are written in Chinese or French, although a majority of them are in English. These
087   questions are presented in a mixed format combining text and heterogeneous image types, such as
088   charts, tables, and diagrams.

094     In addition, there is a pronounced disparity in performance between Qwen2-VL and GPT-4o.
095

096

097     knowledge and at times generates imprecise answers despite correctly understanding the problem.

        nificantly outperforming the RAG method in correcting errors on FAMMA, particularly in the
100

200   3.1   DATASET C ONSTRUCTION
201
      Question collection. We assembled a team of seven volunteer STEM researchers to create a com-
202
      prehensive set of multimodal questions. Five are co-authors of this paper, while the other two are
203   graduates from a Chinese university. Two annotators hold finance degrees, and the others have com-
204   pleted relevant coursework. These annotators draw upon open-source textbooks, exams, and other
205   study materials (see Table 6 in Appendix A for details), and apply their expertise to rewrite or create
206   new questions when needed. The new questions are either entirely original, not present in the data
207   sources, or enhanced versions of existing questions.
208
      The annotators are tasked with selecting questions that require advanced, master-level, or profes-
209   sional knowledge to answer. This selection process is guided by aligning the questions with a min-
210   imum of CFA Level 1 difficulty (CFA Institute, 2024b), ensuring they meet industry standards of
211   complexity. Additionally, selected questions must incorporate multimodal information, such as ta-
212   bles, images, or other visual data, to enrich the input and challenge the model’s ability to process
213   diverse formats. By following these criteria, we have curated a diverse set of approximately 2,000
214   questions, drawn from a wide range of authoritative sources.
215
      Data quality control.      We follow a two-stage data cleaning process to ensure the data quality.


                                                             4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      • In the first stage, we conduct a thorough review to correct formatting errors, fix typos, remove
217     duplicate questions, and verify the accuracy of explanations. Each question is cross-verified by
218     2-3 annotators to ensure consistency and accuracy. Formatting errors and typos arise due to vari-
219     ations in the original sources, such as UTF encoding issues in Chinese and French texts, and the
220     explanations are either provided by the source materials or written by annotators.
221   • In the second stage, we classify each question into one of three difficulty levels—easy, medium,
222     or hard—and label it with the appropriate subfield.
223     The difficulty levels are aligned with the concept-specific standards of the CFA curriculum (CFA
224     Institute, 2024a). In addition, questions that require processing more complex information, such as
225     multiple tables and images, are considered more difficult. In cases where the difficulty is ambigu-
226     ous, the annotators use their judgment. Additionally, questions deemed overly simplistic—such
227     as those based purely on memorization or with answers that are obvious from the context—are
228     removed to maintain the desired level of challenge and to ensure they test knowledge and reason-
229     ing. The subfield annotation is determined by the explicit topics provided in the data source. If the
230
        subfield is not clearly specified, the annotators use their discretion to assign the most appropriate
        category based on the content of the question.
231
232   The JSON formats of the multiple-choice and open questions are illustrated in Listing 5 and Listing 6
233   in Appendix A, respectively.
234
      To conclude, the FAMMA dataset offers a diverse range of questions, enabling the evaluation of
235
      models across various scenarios and allowing for fine-grained analysis of their performance.

240   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
241
      Benchmarked MLMMs. We evaluate three cutting-edge closed-source models that are ranked
242   among the top 10 on the Multimodal Arena Leaderboard (Lmsys Org, 2024):
243
244   • GPT-4o (OpenAI, 2024a): The latest iteration in the GPT series, GPT-4o features enhanced capa-
245     bilities in language and vision understanding, as well as improved generation performance.
246   • Claude-3.5-Sonnet (Anthropic, 2024): Developed by Anthropic, Claude Sonnet introduces archi-
247     tectural innovations that improve multimodal dialogue and reduce harmful outputs.
248   • Claude-3-Sonnet (Anthropic, 2024): An earlier version of the Claude Sonnet model.
249
250   Additionally, we assess a leading open-source model: Qwen2-VL (Yang et al., 2024) that achieves
251   state-of-the-art performance on visual understanding benchmarks, including MathVista (Lu et al.,
252   2024) and DocVQA (Mathew et al., 2021).
253
254
      Generation process. MLMMs are instructed to understand the format and the structure of the
      questions, and return the response, under a zero-shot setting on our benchmark. The instruction
255
      prompts are designed to be straightforward and consistent across all models. Please refer to Listing 7
256
      and Listing 8 in Appendix B for the prompts used to to guide responses to multiple-choice and
257   open questions, respectively. During the final stage of generation for multiple-choice questions, we
258   utilize both regex and GPT-4o to extract the corresponding lettered option from the response. Any
259   discrepancies between the two methods will be manually reviewed and validated by annotators.
260
261   LM-powered evaluation. During the evaluation process, we use GPT-4o as an LM evaluator to
262   assess the accuracy of responses generated by LLMs for each question. The reported score repre-
263   sents the accuracy of these responses. Each response is categorized as either correct or incorrect,
264   and the reported score reflects the average accuracy across the entire set of questions.
265   The LM evaluator is instructed to understand the format and structure of the questions, as well as
266   to consider the key points in the ground-truth answers for evaluating the responses. Please refer
267   to Listing 9 in Appendix B for the instructions provided for evaluating the answers. Note that for
268   open-ended questions, where both gold and generated answers are provided, there is a single correct
269   answer, making the 1-0 correctness straightforward to determine. We set the temperature of the LM
      evaluator as 0 to keep the evaluation results deterministic.

      Human performance. We invite two volunteers to participate in the test to establish a human
271   benchmark. Both are experienced finance professionals: one holds a Master’s degree in Finance

285   4.2     R ESULTS AND A NALYSIS
286
287   Main results. We repeat the generation and evaluation process three times, and report the average
288
      result along with the standard error across all experiments. See the overall scores, breakdown by
      difficulty levels and languages in Table 2. We summarize the key findings as follows.
289
290   • FAMMA presents a comprehensive challenge. Human performance sets the highest benchmark
291     with an overall score of 56.96, leading across all difficulty levels. Among the models, GPT-4o
292     ranks first with a score of 42.11, followed closely by Claude-35-Sonnet at 41.87. Both models
293     fall approximately 15 points short of human performance and, based on our estimates, about 20
294     points below CFA professional levels. This substantial gap underscores the significant challenges
295     FAMMA poses for MLLMs.
296   • The open-source Qwen2-VL significantly lags behind more advanced closed-source MLLMs. Ac-
297     cording to its technical report (Wang et al., 2024a), Qwen2-VL has not been explicitly optimized
298     for financial corpora, whereas the Claude family models prioritize finance as a key domain for
299     evaluation and improvement (Claude, 2024). Interestingly, Qwen2-VL performs better on hard
300
        questions than on medium ones. A possible explanation is that hard questions often require higher
        computational complexity and advanced mathematical reasoning, areas where Qwen2-VL excels.
301
        In fact, its technical report highlights superior performance on MathVista (Lu et al., 2024), out-
302
        performing other MLLMs, including GPT-4o and the Claude models.
303
304   To conclude, the main results highlight the progress in MLMM QA in finance but also underscores
305   the challenge of surpassing human-level performance.
306
307   Analysis I: model performance across different subfields. As shown in Figure 5, GPT-4o
308   demonstrates the largest margin in economics, a social science discipline that studies the behaviour
309   and interactions of economic agents. The result indicates its rich knowledge in social domains
310   in addition to mathematics reasoning. GPT-4o also excels at financial statement analysis, whose
311
      context usually contains long tables (see Figure 2a), indicating its superior ability in table under-
      standing (Kim et al., 2024). This well-rounded performance suggests that GPT-4o possesses a broad
312
      understanding of diverse financial concepts, excelling in knowledge-based and applied assessments.
313
314   Claude-35-Sonnet leads in corporate finance, alternative investments, derivatives, and fixed income,
315   though with small margins over GPT-4o. Both Qwen2 and Claude-3-Sonnet fall significantly short
316   in most areas. The notable improvement in finance-related QA from Claude-3 to Claude-35-Sonnet
317
      is consistent with public findings, where the win rate on finance tasks improved by 27%, as reported
      in the technical report (Claude, 2024).

401   Analysis III: error characterisation. To investigate the limitations of current MLLM capabilities
402   on FAMMA, we conduct a comprehensive analysis of error types observed in our evaluation. We
403   meticulously examined 100 randomly sampled error instances from GPT-4o’s generations in a single
404
      experiment run. The sample includes 76 responses in English, 14 in Chinese, and 10 in French, with
      a distribution across difficulty levels of 30 easy, 30 medium, and 40 hard.
405
406   Through our analysis, we identified five common error types and categorized all instances accord-
407   ingly, with detailed examples provided below and in Appendix B.2:
408
      • Data misinterpretation (DM): errors where the model fails to correctly interpret the input data,
409
        whether textual, visual (e.g., charts, tables), or a combination of both. For instance, as shown
410
        in Listing 11 in Appendix B.2, GPT-4o incorrectly reads a number from a figure, where a small
411     section of the image has low resolution due to the data source.
412
      • Incomplete context understanding (ICU): errors where the model fails to understand the full con-
413
        text of the question or misses critical details. See Listing 12 in Appendix B.2 for an example that
414     GPT-4o overlooks the option and reaches to a wrong result.
415
      • Numerical inaccuracy (NI): errors involving incorrect calculations or misinterpretation of numer-
416
        ical data. Listing 13 in Appendix B.2 presents a response where GPT-4o produces incorrect deci-
417
        mal values during a square calculation.
418
      • Domain knowledge gaps (DKG): errors where the model lacks sufficient understanding of specific
419
        financial concepts or practices. See Listing 1 below for an example that GPT-4o misunderstands
420
        the nature of the high-touch agency approach in financial markets.
421
      • Ambiguous answer generation (AAG): errors where the model provides vague or incomplete re-
422
        sponses that do not fully answer the question, or fails to clearly and correctly align its internal
423
        computation with the correct final choice. Listing 2 below shows an example that GPT-4o cor-
424     rectly performs the computation but arrives at an in-correct final result.

      Analysis VI: can RAG or o1-reasoning chain help?                We explore two independent methods for
      improving GPT-4o’s performance on FAMMA:
472
473
      • Retrieval augmented generation (RAG): we augment GPT-4o with external financial knowledge
474     base by incorporating content from textbooks “CFA Level III SchweserNotes, Books 1-5, 2023”,
475     which comprehensively cover most of the topics included in FAMMA.
476
477
      • Dynamic Chain-of-Thought (COT) prompting: we implement o1-style reasoning chains (OpenAI,
        2024b), where at each step, GPT-4o can either proceed to the next reasoning step (by trying mul-
478
        tiple methods, exploring alternative answers, or questioning previous solutions) or provide a final
479
        answer. The process begins with a system prompt that includes instructions to guide a step-by-step
480     reasoning approach. Once the problem is introduced as a user message, an assistant message is
481     pre-loaded to establish a standardized starting point for the GPT-4o’s response generation.
