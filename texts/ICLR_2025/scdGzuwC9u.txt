011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Many real-world applications, such as logistics, routing, scheduling, and produc-
014            tion planning, involve dynamic systems that require continuous updates to solu-
015
               tions for new Mixed Integer Linear Programming (MILP) problems. These en-
               vironments often require rapid responses to slight changes in parameters, with
016
               time-critical demands for solutions. While reoptimization techniques have been
017
               explored for Linear Programming (LP) and specific MILP problems, their effec-
018            tiveness in general MILP is limited. In this work, we propose a two-stage re-
019            optimization framework for efficiently identifying high-quality feasible solutions.
020            Specifically, we first utilize the historical solving process information to predict
021            the high confidence solving space for modified MILPs to contain high-quality so-
022            lutions. Based on the prediction results, we fix a part of variables to apply the
023            prediction intervals and use the Thompson Sampling algorithm to determine the
024            set of variables to fix by updating the Beta distributions based on solutions ob-
025            tained from the solver. Extensive experiments across nine reoptimization datasets
026
               show that our VP-OR outperforms the state-of-the-art methods, achieving higher-
               quality feasible solutions under strict time limits and demonstrating faster conver-
027
               gence with smaller primal gaps in the early stages of solving.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Traditional combinatorial optimization problems require finding solutions for a single instance.
033   However, many real-world scenarios, such as system control (Marcucci & Tedrake, 2020), railway
034   scheduling (Zhang et al., 2020) and production planning (Dunke & Nickel, 2023; Cedillo-Robles
035   et al., 2020), involve systems that change dynamically over time. Thus, throughout the continuous
036   operation of such systems, it is required to compute solutions for new Mixed Integer Linear Program-
037   ming (MILP) problems, which are similar to the previous instances but differ in some parameters
038   in specific model elements such as objective functions, constraints, and variable bounds. Tradition-
039
      ally, each of these new MILP instances is solved from scratch, which overlooks the opportunity to
      leverage valuable information from the previously solved instances. This can be computationally
040
      expensive on resource and it is usually a challenging task to make a high-quality operation plan in a
041
      short period for time-critical applications.
042
043   Reoptimization techniques have been well-studied for the LP case (John & Yıldırım, 2008) and
044   heuristic algorithms for some special MILP problems, e.g., the railway planning problem (Blair,
045
      1998), general assignment problems (NAUSS, 1974) and other combinatorial problems (Libura,
      1996; 1991; Sotskov et al., 1995). However, the functionality of these techniques for general
046
      MILPs is very limited. The earliest reoptimization methods Ralphs & Güzelsoy (2006); Ralphs
047
      et al. (2010) were primarily based on duality theory and focused on sequences of MIPs where only
048   the right-hand side changes. These approaches leveraged dual information obtained through primal
049   algorithms to enable “warm starting”, accelerating the resolution of subsequent problems. Later
050   research (Gamrath et al., 2015) extended these methods to broader scenarios, incorporating tech-
051   niques like reusing branch-and-bound trees. In such methods, the modified problem is treated as a
052   subproblem of the base problem, or if only the objective function changes, the search can “contin-
053   ued” from the last known search boundary. Specifically, these methods use the leaf nodes of the
      base problem’s branch-and-bound tree as starting points for solving the modified problem. Build-

054
      ing on this, more recent work (Patel, 2024) addressed even more complex reoptimization scenarios,
055   where, apart from the number of variables and constraints, all other parameters can undergo upward
056   or downward perturbations, including the objective function, variable bounds, matrix coefficients,
057   and constraint right-hand side values. Their approach centers on reoptimization methods built on
058   the SCIP solver (Bestuzheva et al., 2021), where a series of past solutions is preserved, allowing the
059   method to assess whether portions of these solutions can be reused for the new problem. They also
060   explore reusing branching strategies and adjust parameters related to the invocation of cutting planes
061   and heuristic algorithms, fine-tuning the solver’s behavior to better tackle the modified problem. The
062   limitations of their approach are twofold. Firstly, the optimal solution from the original problem may
063
      no longer be valid for the new problem. This is because the range of variable values for the optimal
      solution can shift significantly, even with small modifications (Guzelsoy, 2009). Secondly, reusing
064
      branching strategies and adjusting parameters mainly saves time on selecting variables, generating
065
      heuristics and cutting planes but does not reduce the overall size or complexity of the problem.
066
067   Inspired by recent work on end-to-end problem solving (Han et al., 2023; Khalil et al., 2022; Ye
068   et al., 2023; 2024; Nair et al., 2020), we aim to leverage GNNs to predict how optimal solutions
069
      change when MIP parameters vary. However, reoptimization presents unique challenges: Firstly,
      in many real-world scenarios that rely on reoptimization techniques, integer and continuous vari-
070
      ables are common and natural representations. For example, production quantities in manufacturing
071
      are integers (Cedillo-Robles et al., 2020), while power levels in energy optimization problems are
072   continuous variables (Yokoyama et al., 2002). However, most existing end-to-end machine learning-
073   based methods primarily focus on predicting solutions for binary variables (Han et al., 2023; Khalil
074   et al., 2022) and only use the optimal solution from the previous problem without leveraging the
075   intermediate solving process. Secondly, In reoptimization scenarios, there is a pressing real-world
076   need for quickly obtaining high-quality feasible solutions (Marcucci & Tedrake, 2020; Zhang et al.,
077   2020). While current end-to-end methods handle inaccuracies in variable predictions using tech-
078   niques like Large Neighborhood Search (LNS) (Han et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2024), which explores
079   solutions near the predicted values. Although LNS intuitively narrows the search space by focus-
080
      ing on the neighborhood of predicted values, the pure LNS method without the fix strategy does
      not actually decrease the problem’s variable size. In fact, if the search range is not well-tuned, the
081
      added constraints can increase the complexity of the problem, leading to additional computational
082
      overhead (Carchrae & Beck, 2009).
083
084   In this paper, we propose a two-stage reoptimization framework designed to solve near-optimal
085   solutions for modified large-scale MILP instances in dynamic parameter scenarios. The framework
086
      consists of a Variable Prediction model and an Online Refinement module (VP-OR). The variable
      prediction model employs a Graph Neural Network (GNN) to analyze changes in problem structure
087
      and historical branch-and-bound processes. It predicts a marginal probability of each binary variable
088
      and the feasible ranges of integer and continuous variables. The online refinement module utilize
089   Thompson Sampling to iteratively select the variable to apply the prediction interval, gradually
090   improving the overall solution quality. The overall framework is outlined in Figure 1.

112
      2.1   M IXED INTEGER LINEAR PROGRAMMING (MILP)
113
114   A general MILP problem is defined by a set of decision variables, where a subset or all variables
115   are required to be integers. For simplicity, we assume that the objective of our MILP problems is to
116
      seek the minimum value, and a MILP instance can be formulated as formulated below:
117                              min c⊤ x
118                               s.t. Ax ≥ b, x ∈ {0, 1}p × Zq × Rn−p−q                               (1)
119                                    l≤x≤u
120   Here, x represents the n decision variables, where c, l, u ∈ Rn are the objective coefficients, and
121   the lower and upper bounds, respectively. The matrix A ∈ Rm×n is the coefficient matrix of the
122   constraints, and b ∈ Rm is the right-hand side vector.
123   2.2   M ODIFIED MILP P ROBLEM
124
      We consider scenarios similar to those described by Patel (2024), involving a series of MILP in-
125
      stances based on an MILP (base instance) taken from a specific application. Each subsequent in-
126
      stance (modified instance) is modified from the previous one with random perturbations and rota-
127   tions to parameters such as the objective vector, constraints, and variable bounds. The previous
128   instances has been solved to optimality. They provide not only the optimal solution but also detailed
129   records of intermediate computational steps, such as selected branches and basis variables at each
130   node’s LP relaxation. These records can be strategically leveraged in the reoptimization algorithm
131   to accelerate the solving process for the modified instances.
132
      2.3   B IPARTITE GRAPH FOR MILP
133
134   An MILP problem can be effectively represented as a weighted bipartite graph G = (V ∪C, E) (Nair
135   et al., 2020; Gasse et al., 2019). Each vertex in V corresponds to a variable of the MILP, and each
136
      vertex in C represents a constraint. An edge (vi , cj ) connects a variable vertex vi with a constraint
      vertex cj if the variable is involved in the constraint. The edge set E ∈ Rm×n×e represents the
137
      edge features, where m and n denote the number of constraints and variables, respectively, and e
138
      indicates the dimension of the edge attributes.
139
140   2.4   O NLINE C ONTEXTUAL T HOMPSON S AMPLING
141   Thompson Sampling is a heuristic strategy used in decision-making scenarios like the multi-
142   armed bandit (MAB) problem (Zhao, 2022). This method is used for choosing actions accord-
143   ing to their expected rewards, which are continuously updated using Beta probability distributions
144   Beta(α, β) (Gupta & Nadarajah, 2004). The Beta distribution forms a family of continuous proba-
145   bility distributions over the interval (0, 1). The probability density function (pdf) of a Beta(α, β)
                                                                            Γ(α+β) α−1
146   distribution, where α > 0 and β > 0, is given by: f (x; α, β) = Γ(α)Γ(β)      x    (1 − x)β−1 , where
147                                                                                α
      Γ(·) is the Gamma function. The mean of the Beta(α, β) distribution is α+β , and as the parameters
148   α and β increase, the distribution becomes more concentrated around the mean. The beta distri-
149   bution is useful for Bernoulli rewards because if the prior is a Beta(α, β) distribution, then after
150   observing a Bernoulli trial, the posterior distribution is simply Beta(α + 1, β) or Beta(α, β + 1),
151   depending on whether the trial resulted in a success or failure, respectively.

      3     I NITIAL VARIABLE P REDICTION
154
155   In this section, our goal is to train a GNN model to predict a feasible interval containing the modified
156   problem’s optimal solution by utilizing the subproblem containing the optimal solution of the base
157   problem and analyzing how the parameters of the MILP change.
158
159   3.1   G RAPH R EPRESENTATION
160
161   The feature extraction process is divided into two parts: the base instance and the modified instance.
      For the modified instance, we represent it using a classic bipartite graph structure (Gasse et al.,


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      2019). For the base instance, we aim to extract additional historical solving information to predict
163   how the optimal solution may change under small perturbations in the MILP.
164
165   In MILP, integer variables are often relaxed to continuous values to apply duality concepts. How-
166
      ever, the dual problem from the relaxed problem may not directly reflect the relationship between
      the optimal solution and constraints under integer restrictions. We address this challenge by lever-
167
      aging a key property of branch-and-bound trees: the final leaf node that yields the MILP optimal
168
      solution has the characteristic that its LP relaxation solution is also an integer solution. The leaf
169   node represents a subproblem of the original MILP, distinguished by the addition of a series of
170   branching constraints. We include the feasible basic variables and dual solutions of the leaf node as
171   features, which are commonly applied in LP sensitivity analysis (Higle & Wallace, 2003), aiming
172   to capture which variables and constraints are sensitive to parameter changes. This approach sig-
173   nificantly improves the accuracy of binary variable predictions compared to traditional end-to-end
174   solving methods, which rely solely on modeling the problem as a bipartite graph and optimal so-
175   lution values. In Appendix C.5, we present the comparison results between the reuse of historical
176   solving information and the traditional vanilla bipartite graph predictions. A list of the features used
177
      in our graph representation is detailed in Table 5 in Appendix A.
178
179   3.2   GNN-BASED I NITIAL VARIABLE P REDICTION
180
      Classic end-to-end approaches (Khalil et al., 2022; Han et al., 2023) are specifically designed for
181
      binary variables and predict a n-dimension vector (pθ (x1 = 1; M ), . . . , pθ (xp = 1; M )) to repre-
182   sent the conditional probability of p binary variables. However, these methods can not work well
183   in many real-world scenarios, which mainly contain integer and continuous variables. For instance,
184   in the dataset named “vary matrix rhs bounds” in the MIP Workshop 2023 Computational Compe-
185   tition (Bolusani et al., 2023), there are 27,710 variables but only 400 binary variables. Therefore,
186   VP-OR proposes a confidence threshold method specifically designed for integer and continuous
187   variables. Specifically, we represent these values with binary bits and hope to predict the condi-
188   tional probability of each bit, however there a new challenge arises: representing variable values
189   through high-dimensional binary bits is computationally prohibitive (Nair et al., 2020). A common
190
      technique is to decompose the high-dimensional distribution into lower-dimensional ones. This is
      suitable for our problem because we only focus on the prediction interval to reduce problem scales
191
      but do not need to predict an accurate value for continuous and integer variables.
192
193   To reduce the dimensionality of integer variables, we apply a logarithmic transformation before
194   converting the integer values into binary representations. In this process, the integer values can po-
195   tentially be negative. While two’s complement is typically used to represent negative numbers in
196
      binary form (Baugh & Wooley, 1973), it is less intuitive for tasks that involve magnitude interpreta-
      tion, such as logarithmic transformations. Instead, we introduce a sign bit s ∈ {0, 1} to separately
197
      capture the sign of the variable, making the magnitude and sign easier to handle. Specifically, we
198
      record the optimal value vi of the variable xi in the base instance, we calculate its logarithmic scale
199   and binary sign bit s as follows:
200
                                                                      0 if vi ≤ 10−5 ,

202                                                                   1 otherwise.

204
      by the sign bit s, and bin(·) denotes the binary conversion of the logarithmic value.

207   method (Yoon, 2022) to filter the predicted probabilities and distinguish between confident and
208   uncertain predictions. For the binary digits with high confidence, the binary digits are fixed to their
209   predicted values. For uncertain binary digits (i.e., those with probabilities between 0.1 and 0.9), we
210
      allow them to vary between 0 and 1. Specifically, we establish the upper and lower bounds of the
211   predicted binary encoding b̃(vi ) by setting the uncertain binary digit to its maximum value 1 for the
212
      upper bound and its minimum value 0 for the lower bound.

      4     I TERATIVE O NLINE R EFINEMENT
235   Due to the potential distance between the initial predicted solution and the optimal solution, the
236   initial prediction of variable confidence probabilities may be biased. However, by leveraging the
237   feasible range predictions for variables, as described in Sec. 3, we can significantly reduce the prob-
238   lem’s variable scale and search space, thereby substantially lowering the computation time. This
239
      reduction creates the opportunity for iterative solution refinement.
240   In this section, we first introduce our observation that only a very small number of variable predic-
241   tions are inaccurate. However, identifying these inaccurately predicted variables is challenging due
242   to their presence within a large variable space. To address this, we employ the Thompson Sampling
243   algorithm to refine the solving space by selecting the predicted variable ranges to apply and adjust-
244   ing the marginal probabilities of the binary variables. Based on the results from each iteration, we
245
      update the fixed variables for the next round of optimization, ensuring that the search focuses on the
      most promising regions of the solution space.

      In scenarios where the prediction results of certain variables may be inaccurate, existing methods
325   primarily pre-screen binary variables using metrics such as model-predicted probability scores (Han
326   et al., 2023; Khalil et al., 2022). However, as illustrated in Table 1, relying solely on machine learn-
327   ing predictions is unreliable, as even high-probability binary variables can be misclassified. This
328   misclassification can degrade the quality of the solution or, in some cases, result in an unsolvable
329   problem. Furthermore, while integer and continuous variables are represented in binary form, av-
330   eraging the confidence scores across these binary representations is not a meaningful criterion for
331   selection. This is because we are not concerned with the accuracy of individual bits within the bi-
332   nary encoding. Instead, our primary objective is to ensure that the predicted range encompasses the
333
      optimal solution.

335   bandit (MAB) problem, where an algorithm must decide which arm to play at each time step t,
336   based on the outcomes of the previous t − 1 plays. For binary variables, we select a fixed set of
337   x% × p binary variables, where p represents the total number of binary variables, and a% is a
338   predetermined probability threshold. For each binary variable in this selected set, we fix its value to

353
      We do not use the seemingly more intuitive approach of directly using the solution’s objective value
354   as the reward, as the reward could cause the model to favor solutions that are “good but not optimal”,
355   reducing the motivation for exploration. By rewarding only when the current solution is better
356   than all previous ones, we can more clearly distinguish which arms lead to true improvements.
357   Additionally, aiming for the best possible objective value in every round is unnecessary, as our main
358   concern is the overall speed of convergence.
359
      We base our approach on a simplifying assumption commonly used in prior work (Nair et al., 2020;
360   Han et al., 2023) that treats each variable as independent of others. This assumption enables us to
361   update the values of α and β separately for each variable. Inspired by Patel (2024), in the initial step,
362   we provided the solutions from previous instances as hints for the “completesol” heuristic method
363   during the presolve phase, effectively using the base solution as a warm start.
364
      Update α, β for binary variables. For binary variables xi , i ∈ {1, 2, . . . , p}, we initialize the prior
365
      distribution Beta(pi + 10−5 , 1 − pi + 10−5 ) , where pi represents the marginal predicted probability
366   of the variable being fixed to 1. The value µi , sampled from the Beta distribution, represents the
367   probability that fixing binary variable xi to 1 will lead to a better solution (i.e., obtaining r = 1).
368   We rank variables based on min(µi , 1 − µi ), selecting the lowest a%, and sample fixed values from
369   the Bernoulli distribution with probability µi . At each iteration t, the priors for unselected binary
370   variables are updated based on their observed outcomes: For unselected binary variables, we set
371   αi = αi + 1 when xi = 1, and set βi = βi + 1 when xi = 0. For selected binary variables, if the
372   current solution xt is better than the previous best x∗t−1 , compare the set of selected variables at
373   with the previously best set a∗t−1 . For variables where the current value is 1 but was 0 in a∗t−1 (or
374
      was not selected), we set αi = αi + 1. If the current value is 0 , and it was 1 in a∗t−1 (or was not
      selected), we set βi = βi + 1.
375
376   Update α, β for integer and continuous variables. For integer and continuous variables, we
377   initialize the prior distribution as Beta(1, 1), representing a uniform prior over the probability space.



                                                          7
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


378
      The sampled value µj indicates the likelihood that imposing predicted bounds on variable xj will
379   lead to a better solution. We select the top a% of these variables based on their µj values and
380   apply the predicted upper and lower bounds. The priors for continuous/integer variables are updated
381   as follows: For unselected variables, if the variable’s actual value in the solution falls within the
382   predicted bounds, we set αj = αj + 1. If it does not satisfy the predicted bounds, we set βj =
383   βj + 1. For selected variables, when the current solution xt is better than the previous best x∗t−1
384   (i.e., rt = 1), we compare the set of selected variables at and the previously best set a∗t−1 . If xj
385   was unselected in at but was selected in a∗t−1 , we set βj = βj + 1. In contrast, if a variable xj was
386   selected in at but not in a∗t−1 , no immediate conclusion about its benefit can be drawn, since the
387
      objective is to rule out incorrect bound predictions.
388   In our algorithm, we aim to avoid being trapped in local optima. To encourage exploration, no
389   penalty is given when the current solution performs worse than previous iterations. If the current
390   solution becomes infeasible, this is often due to incorrect predictions on the fixed variables. In such
391   cases, a relaxation mechanism is triggered. The variables that have been fixed are divided into 10
392   groups. We iteratively solve 10 subproblems, where in each subproblem, one group of variables is
393
      relaxed (i.e., their bounds are loosened). This process continues until a feasible solution is found
      by adjusting the bounds of variables. In our testing process, this relaxation is handled sequentially
394
      to ensure fairness and accuracy. However, under conditions allowing parallelization, each group
395
      could be processed concurrently to significantly reduce computational overhead. This would greatly
396   improve efficiency. We presents the details of our Thompson Sampling algorithm in Appendix 1.

530       6            CONCLUSION
531
532       This paper proposes VP-OR, a two-stage reoptimization framework for MILPs with dynamic param-
533       eters. VP-OR first trains a GNN model to predict the marginal probability of each binary variable
534       and the feasible ranges of integer and continuous variables in the modified MILP instance. Further,
535
          the Thompson Sampling algorithm is employed to iteratively select which variables to apply the
          predicted intervals, and adjust the marginal probability of each binary variable, ultimately solving
536
          for near-optimal solutions. Experimental evaluations conducted on 9 MILP datasets demonstrate
537
          that our framework outperforms four baselines.

747   The Parameter Update Process algorithm is designed to update the parameters of the pior distribu-
748   tions for binary and continuous/integer variables based on the outcomes of the current solution. The
749
      goal is to refine these parameters to improve the performance of the Thompson Sampling approach
      in subsequent iterations. We adjust our fixing strategy using the Beta distribution parameters, α and
750                                                      α
      β. The mean of the Beta(α, β) distribution is α+β     . As these parameters increase, the distribution
751
752
      becomes more concentrated around the mean. With a prior of Beta(α, β), the posterior updates to
      Beta(α + 1, β) or Beta(α, β + 1).
753
754   In each iteration, we fix a percentage a% of the variables. When we find a better solution, we update
755   the Beta distribution for the remaining 1-a% of unfixed variables based on this new solution. We
      also compare the current strategy to the one from the previous round that gave the best solution. If


                                                         14
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


756
      a variable was fixed before but left unfixed in the current iteration, it indicates the previous strategy
757   limited the solution quality. We update the Beta distributions for these variables to reflect this. In
758   the next round, we resample the fixing strategy using these updated Beta distributions.

798   When faced with infeasible instances, it typically indicates that some variable predictions are incor-
799   rect, resulting in conflicts with constraints. Our relaxation mechanism addresses this by dividing the
800   conflicting variables into G groups and subsequently solving each without these variable sets.
801
      When a feasible solution cannot be found, we repeatedly apply the relaxation mechanism, building
802
      upon previous relaxations. Each iteration of this mechanism reduces the number of fixed variables.
803
      Therefore, theoretically, with enough iterations, we can ensure that the variables causing conflicts
804   with the constraints are filtered out. However, in practice, it usually takes only a few iterations to
805   obtain a feasible solution. For example, in the case of bnd 1, there are errors for only 8 for 2993
806   binary variables. By splitting these 8 erroneous variables into 10 groups, at least one group will
807   inevitably exclude the erroneous variables. Of course, when the number of erroneous variables is
808   greater, this is not guaranteed, but it is important to note that some variables, even if mispredicted,
809   do not affect the ability to find a feasible solution due to their limited impact on solution sensitivity.
      We can easily filter out some variables that are highly sensitive to solution quality for each group.

904
905   All baselines that provided open-source implementations, including PS and Re Tuning, were tested
906   using their official code. Since ND did not provide open-source code, we reproduced their method
907   to the best of our ability based on their paper (Nair et al., 2020) and fine-tuned the parameters
908
      accordingly.
909   SCIP. We use SCIP 8.0.4 (Bestuzheva et al., 2021), which is the state-of-the art open source solver.
910   We keep all the other SCIP parameters to default and emphasize that all of the SCIP solver’s ad-
911   vanced features, such as presolve and heuristics, are open.
912   Re Tuning. Re Tuning is a state-of-the-art heuristic reoptimization framework (Patel, 2024), which
913   does not utilize machine learning models. This framework, developed for the MIP 2023 workshop’s
914   computational competition (Bolusani et al., 2023), earned the first prize. It is primarily based on
915   reusing historical branches and fine-tuning SCIP’s parameters for more effective reoptimization.
916   Our investigation revealed that Re Tuning adjusts its configurations based on the previous instances
917   it solves. Specifically, it may disable modules such as presolving or generating cutting planes for
      subsequent instances. While these adjustments have been shown to potentially improve overall solv-


                                                       17
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


918
      ing time on certain datasets, they inevitably make it more challenging to find high-quality feasible
919   solutions quickly in the early stages. To address this, we ensured these modules remained enabled
920   for all instances, striving to achieve the best possible results with their code.
921
922
      Predict-and-Search(PS). PS is an end-to-end machine learning-based approach (Han et al., 2023)
      which employs large neighborhood search (LNS) combined with GNN predictions. In practice, we
923
      do not know how many variables may be predicted incorrectly, and selecting an appropriate radius δ
924
      for the neighborhood in LNS can be time-consuming. To better demonstrate the performance of the
925   PS method, we select the radius δ based on the average number of binary prediction errors observed
926   during our preliminary tests, as shown in Table 1.

928
      Neural Diving(ND). Another notable method we compared against is Neural Diving (ND) frame-
      work with Selective Net (Nair et al., 2020), which is also based on a variable-fixing strategy. Since

      ND focuses on fixing variables to accelerate the solving process, it serves as a relevant baseline to

      evaluate alongside our approach.

       C.6   COMPUTATIONAL COMPLEXITY ANALYSIS
1189
1190   The primary computational complexity of VP-OR arises from the Thompson Sampling process. In
1191   the sampling phase of Thompson Sampling, we sample the probability p for binary variables and
1192   select a certain percentage (a%) of variables based on the value of min(p, 1 − p) by sorting them.
1193   For integer and continuous variables, we sample to determine whether they should be fixed and
1194   select the top a% of variables based on this criterion. This step has a time complexity of O(nlogn),
1195   where n is the number of variables.
1196   In the parameter update phase of Thompson Sampling, we update the parameters for each variable
1197   once. This step has a time complexity of O(n). We tested the sampling time and parameter update
1198   time for each dataset, as presented in Table 12.
