                                                  A BSTRACT
008
009
010            The success of iterative pruning methods in achieving state-of-the-art sparse net-
011            works has largely been attributed to improved mask identification and an implicit
012
               regularization induced by pruning. We challenge this hypothesis and instead posit
               that their increased training epochs enable improved optimization. To verify this,
013
               we show that pruning at initialization (PaI) is significantly boosted by increased
014
               training epochs with repeating (cyclic) learning rate schedules akin to iterative
015            pruning, even outperforming standard iterative pruning methods. The dominant
016            mechanism how this is achieved, as we conjecture, can be attributed to a better
017            exploration of the loss landscape leading to a lower training loss. However, at
018            high sparsity, increased training alone is not enough for competitive performance.
019            A strong coupling between learnt parameter initialization and mask seems to be
020            required. Standard methods obtain this coupling via expensive pruning-training
021            iterations, starting from a dense network. To achieve this with sparse training in-
022            stead, we propose SCULPT-ing, i.e., cyclic training of any sparse mask followed
023
               by a single pruning step to couple the parameters and the mask, which is able to
               match the performance of state-of-the-art iterative pruning methods in the high
024
               sparsity regime at reduced computational cost.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Overparameterization has been a key factor in the tremendous success of deep neural networks
031   across a variety of tasks on vision and language (Bubeck et al., 2023), among others. However,
032   the massive model sizes come with the burden of high computational and memory costs Wu et al.
033   (2022); Luccioni et al. (2023). Hence, to ensure long-term benefits of deep learning for society
034   and climate, it is imperative to improve model efficiency not only at inference time but also during
035   training Kaack et al. (2022).
036   Neural network sparsification offers a means to reduce the number of parameters of a model while
037   minimally affecting its performance. In addition to computational and memory savings, it can also
038   improve generalization Frankle & Carbin (2019); Paul et al. (2023) and interpretability Chen et al.
039   (2022); Hossain et al. (2024), perform denoising Jin et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2023), and introduce
040   verifiability Narodytska et al. (2020); Albarghouthi (2021). While state-of-the-art iterative pruning
041   methods like Learning Rate Rewinding (LRR) (Renda et al., 2020) or Iterative Magnitude Pruning
042   (IMP) (Frankle & Carbin, 2019) are able to obtain highly performant sparse networks, they require
043   training a dense network over multiple pruning and training iterations, which are computationally
044
      demanding.
045   Instead, pruning at initialization (PaI) methods find a sparse mask at initialization that defines which
046   parameters are pruned i.e. frozen to zero. It thus realizes computational and memory savings from
047   the beginning of model development. While they aim to solve one of our most pressing problems
048   by enabling sparse training from scratch, these methods struggle to keep up with the performance of
049   iterative pruning and often fall short at high sparsities, especially on more complex tasks (Frankle
050
      et al., 2021a).
051   Why is this the case? Recent work has attributed the success of iterative pruning methods to their
052   ability to find better sparse masks (Paul et al., 2023), to train flexibly by enabling more parameter
053   sign flips (Gadhikar & Burkholz, 2024; Zhou et al., 2019), and to identify better trainable parameter
      initializations of the mask (Frankle et al., 2021a; Kuznedelev et al., 2023).

054
      With the goal to fill the gap between PaI and iterative methods, we investigate to which degree we
055   can transfer successful mechanisms from LRR, regarding its training procedure and mask learning
056   ability, to achieve state-of-the-art performance with PaI methods.
057
058
      First, we study how LRR achieves peak performance in the sparse regime surpassing the perfor-
      mance of its dense counterpart (see Figure 2(a) and (b)). This typical observation has been attributed
059
      to a sparsity induced regularization effect (Frankle & Carbin, 2019; Han et al., 2015; Jin et al., 2022).
060
      We offer an alternative explanation. Instead of reaching an optimal sparsity level, we posit that the
061   peak corresponds to longer training with repeated learning rate schedules, finding well generalizing
062   parameters. In the absence of pruning, LRR follows a repeated cyclic training procedure, referred
063   to as cyclic training for the rest of the paper. Such a training procedure also boosts the performance
064   of a dense network above the peak obtained by LRR. While Jin et al. (2022) has also realized that
065   a similar training procedure like LRR, without pruning, could increase the performance of a dense
066   network, they have focused on analyzing the regularization effect of pruning and found that pruning
067   with LRR outperforms a dense network in the presence of label noise.
068   However, we focus on the optimization benefits of training longer on sparse networks in the absence
069   of label noise. We compare a two learning schedules for training longer, a repeated cyclic training
070   schedule similar to LRR and a one-cycle schedule that has a warmup phase followed by a linear de-
071   cay for the same number of steps. While both schedules are largely similar in terms of performance,
072   we find that cyclic training is better able to explore the loss landscape and hence choose to conduct
073   our experiments with this cyclic schedule, as it also mimics the LRR training procedure.
074   We find that dense networks usually outperform pruned networks with our improved cyclic training
075   schedule, highlighting the dominant role cyclic training plays to achieve state-of-the-art performance
076   with LRR. The central insight of our work is, however, that cyclic training substantially boosts the
077   performance of PaI methods like SNIP (Lee et al., 2019) and Synflow (Tanaka et al., 2020) as well as
078   random masks (Liu et al., 2021; Gadhikar et al., 2023) (see Figure 4). Even potential regularization
079   effects of sparsity that mitigate label noise can be realized on sparse masks with cyclic training.
080   These improved PaI masks not only consistently outperform or match LRR in the low sparsity
081
      regime, they also achieve state-of-the-art PaI performance in the high sparsity regime in spite of
      relying on fewer training cycles than LRR. While cyclic PaI can still not compete with LRR at high
082
      sparsities, we set out to understand its limiting factors and exploit its merits to enable sparse training
083
      even at high sparsity.
084
085   In this process, we challenge the assumption that LRR primarily excels at mask learning, as it can
086   accurately measure the importance of trained parameters. Strikingly, we find that cyclic training of
087
      a supposedly superior sparse LRR mask with a random initialization does not surpass a cyclically
      trained random mask (or other PaI masks). As we find, it can still obtain LRR performance (with
088
      cyclic training) but only by relying on a parameter initialization that is sufficiently coupled to the
089
      mask identification process. Conceptually, this is in line with insights into the lottery ticket hypoth-
090   esis that suggest, iterative pruning also serves the purpose to identify an initialization that contains
091   information about the task Paul et al. (2023) or at least parameter signs that support retraining Gad-
092   hikar & Burkholz (2024). In addition to the findings of Paul et al. (2023), we show uncover insights
093   into iterative pruning methods to find that knowledge of initial parameter signs is sufficient to train
094   a sparse mask with cyclic training and match LRR performance.
095
      These insights suggest that the primary information missing in PaI is the right coupling between
096   mask and parameter initialization. To improve this coupling, we propose SCULPT-ing (Sparse
097   Cyclic UtiLization of Pruning and Training), as illlustrated in Figure 1. It starts with a) cyclic
098   training of a (potentially random) sparse mask, which b) is pruned in a single step and c) retrained
099   with a single training cycle. This way, SCULPT-ing transfers the main benefits of iterative pruning,
100   i.e., cyclic training and parameter-mask coupling to sparse training, while requiring fewer computa-
101   tional and memory resources at high sparsity.
102   Our main contributions are as follows:
103

107            neural networks (including random ones), achieving state-of-the-art pruning at initializa-
               tion (PaI) performance.

133   2   BACKGROUND AND RELATED WORK
134
135   Iterative pruning and lottery tickets. Iterative pruning methods entail an iterative training and
136   pruning procedure to sparsify neural networks by removing parameters based on an importance
137   measure, usually parameter magnitude. Han et al. (2015) empirically showed the success of these
138   methods on CNNs. Frankle & Carbin (2019) introduced the Lottery Ticket Hypothesis (LTH) and
139   utilized Iterative Magnitude Pruning (IMP) to find sparse, trainable subnetworks of dense randomly
140   initialized source networks, i.e., lottery tickets, that can be trained from scratch to achieve a similar
141
      performance as training the dense source network. Although Frankle & Carbin (2019) show the
      existence of lottery tickets (LTs), they are only able to find them retrospectively by repeating the
142
      following steps: a) training a (dense) network, pruning usually 20% of the parameters based on
143
      lowest magnitude, c) rewinding the remaining parameters to their initial value. As this approach
144   is less successful on more complex tasks and architectures, Renda et al. (2020) proposed Weight
145   Rewinding (WR) and Learning Rate Rewinding (LRR), which obtain state-of-the-art performance
146   for sparse networks across datasets with iterative pruning. While IMP rewinds to initial weights, WR
147   rewinds to a point obtained after a few training steps, and LRR continues training from the learnt
148   weights of the previous iteration and thus never rewinds the learned neural network parameters. This
149   allows LRR to consistently outperform WR and IMP (Renda et al., 2020; Gadhikar & Burkholz,
150   2024), yet, we find that repeated cyclic retraining of the WR network is able to fill the gap between
151   WR and LRR.
152   Zimmer et al. (2023) demonstrate that the retraining duration for LRR can be reduced by adaptively
153   compressing the learning rate schedule based on perturbation induced by pruning. This adaptation
154   while reducing the length of training, does not fully match the performance of LRR, hence, we
155   choose to focus our comparisons to the original LRR method.
156   Task specificity of LT initialization. While the original LTH has given great hope that training
157   sparse neural networks from scratch might be feasible, it has become evident that the mask of sparse
158   LTs Morcos et al. (2019); Chen et al. (2020); Burkholz et al. (2022) as well as the identified initial
159   parameters contain task specific information Paul et al. (2023) that is obtained only by training the
160   dense overparameterized network and it is unclear how to identify mask and initialization otherwise.
161   Theoretical LT existence proofs Malach et al. (2020); Pensia et al. (2020); Orseau et al. (2020);
      Fischer & Burkholz; Burkolz (2022); Burkholz (2022); da Cunha et al. (2022); Gadhikar et al.


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      (2023); Ferbach et al. (2023) even suggest that just pruning the random source network can perfectly
163   couple the mask and its initial parameters so that no further training is required. Full task specific
164   information can even be contained in a subset of the parameters Frankle et al. (2021b); Giannou et al.
165   (2023); Burkholz (2024). But is a similar coupling between initialization and mask only attainable
166   by iterative pruning? We propose SCULPT-ing as an alternative.
167
      Benefits of iterative pruning. The main motivation of pruning is usually the reduction of computa-
168
      tional resources, yet, it has also been found to lead to improved generalization (LeCun et al., 1990;
169   Hassibi et al., 1993; Frankle & Carbin, 2019; Jin et al., 2022) at an optimal sparsity due to repeated
170   training cycles and due to a regularization effect in the presence of label noise. We leverage an im-
171   proved cyclic training procedure to enable sparse training from scratch. Yet, the success of iterative
172   pruning schemes has been attributed to their ability to transfer crucial information about the loss
173   landscape between consecutive pruning iterations, as they are linearly mode connected (Paul et al.,
174   2023; Du et al.; Frankle et al., 2021a), to find a performant sparse mask and initialization pair. While
175   full training is not necessary to find a good mask (You et al., 2020), training with initial overparame-
176   terization in early pruning cycles has been conjectured to improve the mask identification and enable
177
      meaningful parameter sign flips during learning (Zhou et al., 2019; Gadhikar & Burkholz, 2024). It
      is an open question whether PaI could enjoy similar advantages.
178
179   Pruning at initialization (PaI). PaI methods aim to identify a sparse mask at initialization and
180   enable sparse training from scratch. They use an importance measure like connection sensitivity
181   (SNIP) (Lee et al., 2019), gradient signal preservation (GraSP)(Wang et al., 2020) or criteria that
182   maximize the number of paths while ensuring sufficient widths (Pham et al., 2023; Patil & Dovrolis,
183
      2021; Tanaka et al., 2020) to prune weights. (Liu et al., 2021; Gadhikar et al., 2023) also showed
      that random pruning is a simple and effective pruning at initialization method which was also ear-
184
      lier verified in sanity checks of mask learning (Su et al., 2020; Ma et al., 2021). We boost their
185
      performance significantly with cyclic training.
186
187   Training schedules. LRR relies on a repeated cyclical learning rate schedule that improves perfor-
188   mance at certain sparsities as a consequence of repeated training cycles. Such cyclic training can
189
      also improve generalization of dense networks as observed by Jin et al. (2022) and confirmed in
      Figure 2. Its general benefits for dense training have also been verified by Smith (2017). Defazio
190
      et al. (2023) have also highlighted the importance of a linear warmup for improved generalization.
191
      Recent work by Kuznedelev et al. (2023) also suggests that sparse networks are under-trained and
192   proposes training them with the AC/DC (Peste et al., 2021) method for increased epochs with a lin-
193   early decaying learning rate. Evci et al. (2020) also show that their proposed method RiGL benefits
194   from longer training. However, the success of increased training is attributed to better mask explo-
195   ration for both methods, which dynamically update the mask during training, and not for a fixed PaI
196   mask which is the focus of our work. Interestingly, we find that simply training longer with cyclic
197   training helps boost the performance of PaI methods.

244   As it turns out, all three provide a partial explanation, but 2) seems to be the distinguishing factor

350   Having established that the learning rate schedule of LRR drives most, but not all of its performance,
351
      we are left to wonder what constitutes its strength in the high sparsity regime. The obvious difference
      between cyclic PaI and LRR are the masks that are optimized. As illustrated in Figure 1, iterative
352
      pruning gradually removes the parameters with smallest magnitude in every iteration, thus learning
353
      a potentially task specific sparse mask. In contrast, PaI methods identify a sparse mask in a single
354   pruning step at initialization, based on potentially less accurate information.
355
356   LRR learns more than mask structure. Investigating the sparse mask learnt by LRR, we initialize
357
      it with a new random initialization, followed by cyclic training. To our surprise, we observe that the
      mask identified by LRR with a random initialization is no better than a random mask after cyclic
358
      training, as shown in Figure 5 (LRR mask + random init). However, if we initialize the learnt
359
      LRR mask with the parameters of a dense network that was trained for a few steps, like in WR,
360   and then perform cyclic training on this combination (LRR mask + warmup init), we are able to
361   recover baseline LRR performance even at high sparsity. This suggests that along with improved
362   optimization via cyclic training, it is crucial to have an appropriate initialization for the sparse mask
363   to improve performance at high sparsity. It also implies that the mask structure learnt by LRR might
364   not be special on its own, but is in combination with the parameter initialization.
365

366   eter initialization and mask that can match LRR at high sparsity, we also look at the masks and
367   initializations of the other iterative pruning methods including WR and optimize each of these mask
368   parameter pairs with cyclic training. We find that, cyclic training of a WR mask combined with its
369   warmup initialization (WR mask + warmup init) is able to match the performance of LRR, similar
370   to LRR mask + LRR init. These results, shown in Figure 5, also confirm that when the mask and
371   parameters are coupled, for example as in case of a warmed up initialization and an iteratively learnt
372   mask, they can match the performance of LRR with cyclic training. This insight is particularly in-
373   teresting, as it suggests that lottery tickets i.e. initializations of sparse networks, might also exist

      that can achieve LRR performance.

405   possibility of lottery ticket initializations.
406
407
      Conclusion. Cyclic training alone is not sufficient to succeed at high sparsity, but requires an ini-
      tialization that is well coupled to a mask. Our analysis is inconclusive whether LRR masks alone
408
      are better aligned with a learning task than PaI masks and poses the potential universality of lottery
409
      tickets in the high sparsity regime as an open question Morcos et al. (2019); Chen et al. (2020);
410   Burkholz et al. (2022).

416
      training paradigm, yet, it lacks the right parameter initialization for a given mask and task to compete
      in the high sparsity regime. Only LRR and to some extent WR have been able to realize the benefits
417
      of both the right initialization-mask coupling and cyclic training, as they consistently find highly
418
      performant sparse networks. However, both LRR and WR are computationally demanding and
419   memory intensive as they start from a dense network. To enhance sparse training and address the
420   coupling issue, we propose SCULPT-ing, which can achieve a similar performance as LRR and WR
421   while starting sparse network and requiring fewer training cycles at high sparsity. Our experiments
422   verify that SCULPT-ing is often able to bridge the gap between cyclic PaI and LRR at high sparsity.

      7   D ISCUSSION
505
506
      We have conducted a rigorous empirical investigation into the inner mechanisms of state-of-the-art
507   iterative pruning methods Learning Rate Rewinding (LRR) and Weight Rewinding (WR) While their
508   superior performance has largely been attributed to improved mask identification and an implicit
509   sparsity regularization, we have challenged this belief and presented evidence for the insight that
510   their repeated cyclic training schedule enables improved optimization.
511
      To transfer its merits to sparse training, we have proposed to combine cyclic training with pruning
512
      at initialization (PaI), which can outperform even LRR at lower sparsity. The performance boost is
513   particularly striking, as Gadhikar & Burkholz (2024) conjectured that mainly early overparameteri-
514   zation supports LRR in learning sparse, highly performant models. As it turns out, a relevant share
515   of its performance and ability to flexibly switch signs is induced by its cyclic training procedure.
516
      Yet, cyclic PaI also faces limits in the high sparsity regime, where we find no significant perfor-
517
      mance differences between masks, including a mask that has been identified by LRR and can, in
518
      principle, achieve a higher performance. This finding identifies a remaining challenge of cyclic PaI,
519   i.e., deriving a parameter initialization that is sufficiently coupled to the mask and learning task so
520   that cyclic training can effectively learn in the high sparsity regime.
521
522
      To improve this coupling in the context of sparse training, we have proposed SCULPT-ing, which
      performs cyclic training of a sparse mask followed by a single magnitude based pruning step to
523
      induce the desired coupling. SCULPT-ing bridges the gap between sparse training and iterative
524
      pruning to save computations in comparison with LRR and improve the performance of cyclic PaI.
525
526   While SCULPT-ing can solve a trade-off between computational and performance considerations
527   by adapting its number of training cycles, efficient sparse training remains a challenge that asks for
528
      further insights into improved mask identification and effective parameter optimization.

759
760   Given the importance of sign flips for sparse training (Gadhikar & Burkholz, 2024), we investigate
761   if cyclic training is better at recovering correct signs as compared to one-cycle training for the same
762   number of epochs. We use the coupling experimental setup from Section 5 and train a learnt LRR
763   mask with the signs of the warmup initialization and randomized magnitude with both cyclic and
764   one-cycle training, as reported in Figure 10(b). We find that, with the warmup signs, cyclic training
765   is exactly able to recover LRR performance while one-cycle is worse, while at higher sparsity both
766
      cyclic and one-cycle perform identically. Similarly, perturbing 20% of the initial signs in the same
      also shows that cyclic training can recover better at lower sparsity but is identical to one-cycle at
767
      high sparsity.
768
769   To further examine the ability of sign recovery, we find that the signs learnt by cyclic training have
770   a 95.37% overlap with the signs learnt by LRR whereas one-cycle has an overlap of 93.67%. A
771   higher overlap with cyclic training suggests that it is better at being able to recover the signs given
772
      the signs at warmup.
773   However, it is also important to note that LRR is also trained cyclicly, which might be the reason
774   why cyclic training of the warmup signs is able to recover the same signs better.

847
848   Iterative Magnitude Pruning was introduced by (Frankle & Carbin, 2019) paper. The pruning method
849   can be described as follows:
850
             • Start with an initial dense network f (x; θ) where θ drawn from a distribution Do . The
851
               objective is to find a mask m, to have a network f (x; m ⊙ θ) which is sparse.
852
853
             • This model is then trained as usual, using an algorithm like stochastic gradient descent.
854          • The parameters of the trained network are then globally ranked according to their magni-
855            tude. Then x% of the lowest valued parameters are set to zero in the mask m which has the
856
               exact same size as the network. Typically, x = 20%.
857          • The parameters that have not been pruned (non-zero) after a pruning level are reset to the
858            initial random initialization θo .
859          • This model is now trained again, repeating steps 2 - 4 until a target sparsity is reached.
