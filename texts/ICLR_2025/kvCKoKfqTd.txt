011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Drug-target interactions (DTIs) are fundamental and intricate processes essential
014            for the advancement of drug discovery and design. We present a groundbreak-
015            ing unified framework for drug-target interaction (DTI) prediction that seamlessly
016            integrates advanced concepts from non-commutative geometry, optimal transport
017            theory, and quantum information science. Our approach, Non-Commutative Geo-
018            metric Adaptation for Molecular Interactions (NCGAMI), reframes the DTI pre-
019            diction problem within the context of a non-commutative pharmacological man-
020
               ifold, enabling a profound synthesis of classical and quantum perspectives. By
               leveraging the spectral action principle, we develop a novel domain adaptation
021
               technique that minimizes a geometrically motivated functional, yielding optimal
022
               transport maps between pharmacological domains. We establish a deep connec-
023            tion between our framework and non-equilibrium statistical mechanics through a
024            fluctuation theorem for domain adaptation, providing fundamental insights into
025            the thermodynamics of the adaptation process. Our unified variational objec-
026            tive, formulated using geometric quantization, incorporates quantum relative en-
027            tropy and Liouville volume forms, bridging information-theoretic and geometric
028            aspects of the problem. We introduce a quantum adiabatic optimization algo-
029            rithm for solving this objective, guaranteeing convergence to the optimal solu-
030            tion under specified conditions. Furthermore, we prove that the algebra of ob-
031
               servables generated by our model forms a hyperfinite type III1 factor, revealing
               a profound link between the algebraic structure of DTI prediction and the ge-
032
               ometry of optimal transport. This result enables us to characterize the modular
033
               automorphism group governing the evolution of adapted distributions. Exten-
034            sive numerical experiments demonstrate that NCGAMI significantly outperforms
035            existing state-of-the-art methods across a wide range of DTI prediction tasks,
036            achieving unprecedented accuracy and robustness. Our anonymous gitHub link:
037            https://anonymous.4open.science/r/NCGAMI-C19B

041   1   I NTRODUCTION
042
043   The prediction of drug-target interactions (DTIs) stands at the forefront of pharmaceutical research,
044   playing a pivotal role in drug discovery, repurposing, and the understanding of complex biological
045   systems. Despite significant advancements in computational methods, including deep learning ap-
046   proaches, current techniques often fall short in capturing the intricate, multiscale nature of molecular
047   interactions and struggle to generalize across diverse chemical and biological domains.
048   Traditional machine learning approaches to DTI prediction have primarily relied on classical statisti-
049   cal methods and, more recently, on graph neural networks (GNNs) and attention mechanisms. While
050   these methods have shown promise, they are fundamentally limited by their adherence to classical
051   probability theory and Euclidean geometry. These limitations become particularly apparent when
052   attempting to model the quantum mechanical aspects of molecular interactions or when dealing
053   with the high-dimensional, non-Euclidean spaces characteristic of chemical compound libraries and
      protein structures.

054
      In this work, we present a groundbreaking approach to DTI prediction that leverages advanced con-
055   cepts from non-commutative geometry, optimal transport theory, and quantum information science.
056   Our framework, Non-Commutative Geometric Adaptation for Molecular Interactions (NCGAMI),
057   represents a paradigm shift in how we conceptualize and model drug-target interactions. At its core,
058   NCGAMI reframes the DTI prediction problem within the context of a non-commutative pharma-
059   cological manifold, enabling a profound synthesis of classical and quantum perspectives. Central
060   to our approach is the application of the spectral action principle from non-commutative geometry
061   to the domain adaptation problem in DTI prediction. This novel formulation allows us to define a
062   geometrically motivated functional that, when minimized, yields optimal transport maps between
063
      pharmacological domains. This technique not only provides a more natural way to handle the inher-
      ent geometric structure of molecular data but also offers a direct link to fundamental physical princi-
064
      ples governing molecular interactions. We establish a deep connection between our framework and
065
      non-equilibrium statistical mechanics through a fluctuation theorem for domain adaptation. This
066   result provides fundamental insights into the thermodynamics of the adaptation process, offering a
067   new perspective on the energetics of conformational changes in drug-target binding. Furthermore,
068   we leverage concepts from geometric quantization to formulate a unified variational objective that
069   incorporates quantum relative entropy and Liouville volume forms, bridging information-theoretic
070   and geometric aspects of the problem.
071
      A key innovation in our work is the introduction of a quantum adiabatic optimization algorithm for
072   solving the proposed objective function. This algorithm, inspired by adiabatic quantum computa-
073   tion, guarantees convergence to the optimal solution under specified conditions, potentially offering
074   significant computational advantages over classical optimization techniques for high-dimensional
075   pharmacological spaces. Perhaps most profoundly, we prove that the algebra of observables gener-
076   ated by our model forms a hyperfinite type III1 factor, a result that reveals a deep connection between
077   the algebraic structure of DTI prediction and the geometry of optimal transport. This insight allows
078   us to characterize the modular automorphism group governing the evolution of adapted distribu-
079   tions, providing a powerful mathematical tool for analyzing the long-term behavior of drug-target
080
      interactions across different domains.
081   Our experimental results demonstrate that NCGAMI significantly outperforms existing state-of-the-
082   art methods across a wide range of DTI prediction tasks, achieving unprecedented accuracy and
083   robustness. Moreover, the framework provides novel interpretability mechanisms rooted in the
084   spectral properties of the Dirac operator, offering deep insights into the fundamental principles
085   governing drug-target interactions. The implications of this work extend far beyond the immedi-
086
      ate realm of DTI prediction. By bridging the gap between classical and quantum approaches to
      molecular modeling, we open up new avenues for research at the intersection of quantum comput-
087
      ing, non-commutative geometry, and computational pharmacology. The techniques developed here
088
      have potential applications in areas such as protein folding prediction, de novo drug design, and the
089   study of complex biological networks.

448   6.1   DATASET
449
450   We utilized two datasets to evaluate the classification performance of our model. We extracted
451   drug and target data from the DrugBank databaseWishart et al. (2006) to construct the experimental
452   dataset. Additionally, we applied our model to a previously established benchmark dataset, Human.
453   Specifically, the Human datasetLiu et al. (2015) consists of 6,728 positive interactions between 2,726
454
      unique compounds and 2,001 unique proteins. The datasets were randomly partitioned into source
      domain and target domain in a 6:4 ratio, followed by a further split of the target domain dataset into
455
      target train and target test datasets in a 3:1 ratio. The source domain contains all labeled samples,
456
      providing a wealth of data and corresponding labels that assist the model in learning the features
457   and patterns of the data, thereby establishing effective predictive capabilities. The samples in the
458   target train dataset are unlabeled and used for training, while the target test dataset includes labeled
459   samples for model evaluation.

462   6.2   I MPLEMENT DETAILS
463
464   In this study, the hyperparameter settings for our model on two datasets (Human and DrugBANK)
465   are as follows: the learning rate is set to 5e-4, the weight decay is 1e-5, the batch size is 256, the
466   dropout rate is 0.1, and the maximum number of training epochs is 150. Additionally, the training
467   and testing processes utilized eight A100 GPUs, each with 40GB of memory. The selection of
468   these hyperparameters aims to optimize the training effectiveness and performance of the model. To
469   evaluate the performance of our model, we employed two critical metrics: AUC (Area Under the
470   Curve) and AUPR (Area Under the Precision-Recall Curve).

474
475   In this analysis, our proposed NCGAMI model was benchmarked against several prominent models,
476   including DeepDTA Öztürk et al. (2018), DeepConv-DTI Lee et al. (2019), MolTrans Huang et al.
477
      (2021), and TransformerCPI Chen et al. (2020). The DeepDTA architecture Öztürk et al. (2018),
      which consists of two three-layer convolutional neural networks (CNNs), was initially developed for

      binding affinity predictions. In the experimental results, as shown in Figure 2, our model demon-

      strated excellent performance on the AUC and AUPR metrics, surpassing all baseline models. On

      our model across diverse datasets.
