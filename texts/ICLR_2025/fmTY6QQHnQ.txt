009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Continuous-time event sequences, in which events occur at irregular intervals, are
012            ubiquitous across a wide range of industrial and scientific domains. The contem-
013            porary modeling paradigm is to treat such data as realizations of a temporal point
014            process, and in machine learning it is common to model temporal point processes
015            in an autoregressive fashion using a neural network. While autoregressive models
016
               are successful in predicting the time of a single subsequent event, their perfor-
               mance can be unsatisfactory in forecasting longer horizons due to cascading errors.
017
               We propose EventFlow, a non-autoregressive generative model for temporal
018
               point processes. Our model builds on the flow matching framework in order to
019            directly learn joint distributions over event times, side-stepping the autoregressive
020            process. EventFlow is likelihood-free, easy to implement and sample from, and
021            either matches or surpasses the performance of state-of-the-art models in both
022            unconditional and conditional generation tasks on a set of standard benchmarks.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027
      Many stochastic processes, ranging from consumer behavior (Hernandez et al., 2017) to the occur-
      rence of earthquakes (Ogata, 1998), are best understood as a sequence of discrete events which occur
028
      at random times. Any observed event sequence, consisting of one or more event times, may be viewed
029
      as a draw from a temporal point process (TPP) (Daley & Vere-Jones, 2003) which characterizes the
030   distribution over such sequences. Given a collection of observed event sequences, faithfully modeling
031   the underlying TPP is critical in both understanding and forecasting the phenomenon of interest.
032
033
      While multiple different parametric TPP models have been proposed (Hawkes, 1971; Isham &
      Westcott, 1979), their limited flexibility limits their application when modeling complex real-world
034
      sequences. This has motivated the use of neural networks (Du et al., 2016; Mei & Eisner, 2017) in
035
      modeling TPPs. To date, most neural network based TPP models are autoregressive in nature (Shchur
036   et al., 2020a; Zhang et al., 2020), where a model is trained to predict the next event time given an
037   observed history of events. However, in many tasks, we are interested not only in the next event, but
038   in the entire sequence of events which is to follow. While these models can achieve high likelihoods,
039   their performance in many-step forecasting tasks can be unsatisfactory due to compounding errors
040   arising from the autoregressive sampling procedure (Xue et al., 2022; Lüdke et al., 2023).
041
      Moreover, existing models are typically trained via a maximum likelihood procedure (see Section
042   3) which involves computing the CDF implied by the learned model. When using a neural model,
043   computing this CDF necessitates techniques such as Monte Carlo estimation to properly compute the
044   loss (Mei & Eisner, 2017). In addition, sampling from intensity-based models (Du et al., 2016; Mei
045   & Eisner, 2017; Yang et al., 2022) is nontrivial, requiring an expensive and difficult to implement
046   approach based on the thinning algorithm (Lewis & Shedler, 1979; Ogata, 1981; Xue et al., 2024).
047
      Motivated by these limitations, we propose EventFlow, a generative model which directly learns
048   the joint event time distributions, thus allowing us to avoid autoregressive sampling altogether. Our
049   proposed model extends the flow matching framework (Lipman et al., 2023; Albergo & Vanden-
050   Eijnden, 2023; Liu et al., 2023) to the setting of TPPs, where we learn a continuous flow from
051   a reference TPP to our data TPP. At an intuitive level, samples from our model are generated by
052   drawing a collection of event times from a reference distribution and flowing these events along a
053   learned vector field. The number of events is fixed throughout this process, decoupling the event
      counts and their times, so that the distribution over event counts can be learned or otherwise specified.

      Temporal Point Processes The statistical modeling of temporal point processes (TPPs) is a classical
094
      subject with a long history (Daley & Vere-Jones, 2003; Hawkes, 1971; Isham & Westcott, 1979). The
095
      contemporary modeling paradigm, based on neural networks (Du et al., 2016), typically operates
096   by learning a history encoder and an event decoder. The history encoder seeks to learn a fixed-
097   dimensional vector representation of the history of a sequence up to some given time, and the decoder
098   seeks to model a distribution over the subsequent event(s).
099
      Numerous models have been proposed for both components. Popular choices for the history encoder
100
      include RNN-based models (Du et al., 2016; Shchur et al., 2020a; Mei et al., 2019) or attention-based
101
      models (Zhang et al., 2020; Zuo et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2022). While attention-based encoders can
102   provide longer-range contexts, this benefit typically comes at the cost of additional memory overhead.
103   Similarly, a wide range of forms for the event decoder have also been proposed. The most common
104   approach is to parametrize a conditional intensity function via a neural network. For instance, several
105   authors (Mei & Eisner, 2017; Zuo et al., 2020; Zhang et al., 2020) model the conditional intensity
106   using a parametric form inspired by the Hawkes process (Hawkes, 1971), and Du et al. (2016) model
107   the (log-)conditional intensity through an affine function of the history embedding. Similarly, Okawa
      et al. (2019) model the conditional intensity using a mixture of Gaussian kernels.

108
      Most closely related to our work are approaches which use generative models as decoders. These
109   models often do not assume a parametric form for the decoder, enhancing their flexibility. For
110   instance, Xiao et al. (2017b) propose the use of W-GANs to generate new events. Similarly, Shchur
111   et al. (2020a) learn the distribution over the next inter-arrival time via a normalizing flow. Lin et al.
112   (2022) benchmark several choices of generative models, including diffusion, GANs, and VAEs.
113   Despite the flexibility of these models, these approaches are all autoregressive in nature, making them
114   ill-suited for multi-step forecasting tasks. In contrast, Lüdke et al. (2023) propose a diffusion-style
115   model which is able to avoid autoregressive sampling via an iterative refinement procedure.
116   Our work can be viewed as a novel approach for building flexible decoders for TPPs, extending
117   flow matching to the setting of continuous-time event sequences. In contrast to prior work using
118   generative models, our model is likelihood-free and non-autoregressive, achieving strong performance
119   on long-term forecasting tasks. The work of Lüdke et al. (2023) is perhaps most closely related to
120   ours, but we emphasize that the method of Lüdke et al. (2023) requires an involved training and
121   sampling procedure. In contrast, our method is straightforward to both implement and sample from,
122   while simultaneously outperforming existing approaches.
123
124   Flow Matching The recently introduced flow matching framework (or stochastic interpolants)
125   (Lipman et al., 2023; Albergo & Vanden-Eijnden, 2023; Liu et al., 2023) describes a class of generative
126   models which are closely related to both normalizing flows (Papamakarios et al., 2021) and diffusion
127
      models (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2021). Intuitively, these models learn a path of probability
      distributions which interpolates between a fixed reference distribution and the data distribution. These
128
      models are a popular alternative to diffusion, providing greater flexibility in model design, with recent
129
      applications in image generation (Ma et al., 2024; Dao et al., 2023), DNA and protein design (Stark
130   et al., 2024; Campbell et al., 2024), and point cloud generation (Buhmann et al., 2023; Wu et al.,
131   2023). To the best of our knowledge, our work is the first to explore flow matching for TPPs.

      Sampling Autoregressive Models In many tasks, we are interested not only in an accurate model
176   of the intensity (or distribution), but also sampling new event sequences from the corresponding
177   distribution. For instance, when forecasting an event sequence, we may want to generate several
178   forecasts in order to provide uncertainty quantification over these predictions. However, sampling
179   from existing autoregressive models can be difficult.
180
      For instance, the flow-based model of Shchur et al. (2020a) requires a numerical approximation to the
181
      inverse of the model to perform sampling. Similarly, the diffusion-based approach of Lin et al. (2022)
182   can require several hundred forward passes of the model to generate a single event time, rendering
183   it costly when generating long sequences. Moreover, the predictive performance of autoregressive
184   models is often unsatisfactory on multi-step generation tasks due to the accumulation of errors over
185   many steps (Lin et al., 2021; Lüdke et al., 2023). This difficulty is particularly pronounced for
186   intensity-based models (Du et al., 2016; Mei & Eisner, 2017; Zhang et al., 2020), where naively
187   computing the implied distribution in Equation (1) is prohibitively expensive. Instead, sampling
188   from intensity-based models is typically achieved via the thinning algorithm (Ogata, 1981; Lewis
189   & Shedler, 1979). However, this algorithm has several hyperparameters to tune, is challenging to
190
      parallelize, and can be difficult for practitioners to implement (Xue et al., 2024).

192   4     E VENT F LOW
193
194   Motivated by the limitations of autoregressive models, we propose EventFlow, which has a
195   number of distinct advantages over existing approaches. First, EventFlow directly models the
196   joint distribution over event times, thereby avoiding autoregression entirely. Second, our model is
197   likelihood-free, avoiding the Monte Carlo estimates needed to estimate the likelihood in Equation
198   (2) during training. Third, sampling from our model amounts to solving an ordinary differential
199   equation. This is straightforward to implement and parallelize, allowing us to avoid the difficulties of
200   thinning-based approaches used in existing models. We build upon the flow matching (or stochastic
201
      interpolant) framework (Lipman et al., 2023; Albergo & Vanden-Eijnden, 2023; Liu et al., 2023) to
      develop our model. We begin below by focusing on the unconditional setting, and later discuss how
202
      to extend our method for conditional generation as necessary.

      4.2   BALANCED C OUPLINGS
235
236
      Our first step is to define a useful notion of couplings (Villani et al., 2009), allowing us to pair event
237   sequences drawn from µ0 with those drawn from µ1 . A coupling between two TPPs µ, ν ∈ P(Γ) is a
238   joint probability measure ρ ∈ P(Γ × Γ) over pairs of event sequences (γ0 , γ1 ) such that the marginal
239   distributions of ρ are µ and ν. We say that the coupling ρ is balanced if draws (γ0 , γ1 ) ∼ ρ are such
240   that N (γ0 ) = N (γ1 ) almost surely. In other words, balanced couplings only pair event sequences
241   with equal numbers of events. While we will later see how to interpolate between any two given
242   event sequences, this coupling will allow us to decide which sequences to interpolate. In particular, a
243   balanced coupling will allow us to pair sequences such that they always have the same number of
244   events, allowing us to avoid the addition or deletion of events during both training and sampling and
245
      thus simplifying our model. We will use Πb (µ, ν) to denote the set of balanced couplings of µ, ν,
      and the following proposition shows Πb (µ, ν) is nonempty if and only if the event count distributions
246
      of µ and ν are equal, placing a structural constraint on the suitable choices of a reference measure.
247
248   Proposition 1 (Existence of Balanced Couplings).
249   Let µ, ν ∈ P(Γ) be two TPPs. The set of balanced couplings Πb (µ, ν) is nonempty if and only if
250   µ(n) = ν(n) have the same distribution over event counts.
251
252
      We provide a proof in Appendix B. In practice, we follow a simple strategy for choosing both the
      reference TPP µ0 and the coupling ρ. Suppose q is any given density on our state space T , e.g., a
253
      uniform distribution. We take µ0 to be a mixed binomial process (Kallenberg et al., 2017, Ch. 3)
254
      whose event count distribution is given by that of the data µ1 (n) and joint event distributions given
255   by independent products of q (up to sorting). That is, to sample from µ0 , one can simply sample
256   n ∼ µ1 (n) from the empirical event count distribution followed by sampling and sorting n i.i.d.
257   points tk ∼ q. To draw a sample from our coupling ρ, we first sample a data sequence γ1 ∼ µ1 ,
258   followed by sampling N (γ1 ) events independently from q and sorting to produce a draw γ0 ∼ µ0 .
259   We call this coupling the independent balanced coupling of µ and ν.

358     conditioned on a history H, we embed H into a fixed-dimensional vector representation eH = fθ (H)
359     via a learned encoder fθ before providing this to the model vθ (γs , s, eH ) and minimizing Equation
360     (7). Note that we jointly train the encoder fθ and vector field vθ . See Algorithm 1.
361
362     Parametrization The second challenge is that we must learn a vector field vθ (γ, s) in n dimensions
363     for arbitrary values of n. In other words, vθ is a neural network which takes in a flow time s ∈ [0, 1]
364     and a sequence of events γ, and must produce N (γ) scalar outputs. We achieve this through an
365     attention-based architecture, which we detail in Appendix D. At a high level, the flow time s is
366     transformed via a learnable embedding into a fixed-dimensional vector. Similarly, each event in γ
367     is transformed into fixed-dimensional vector via a learned embedding (which is shared across the
368
        events, but not the flow time). The flow-time embedding is then added to each event embedding,
        and the resulting sequence is passed through a standard transformer architecture (Yang et al., 2022;
369
        Vaswani, 2017), resulting in a sequence of N (γ) vectors. Finally, each of these vectors is projected
370
        into one dimension via a linear layer to produce the sequence of N (γ) velocities.
371
372     For conditional tasks, we must also compute an encoding eH = fθ (H) of the history H. This is done
373     by a separate transformer encoder, which operates in the same fashion as described in the previous
374
        paragraph, but without the use of the flow-time s as an input and without the final linear projection
        layer. This embedding is fed into the intermediate layers of our velocity network via cross-attention.

413     5    E XPERIMENTS
414
415     We study our proposed EventFlow model under two settings. The first is a conditional task, where
416     we seek to forecast both the number and times of future events given a history. The second is an
417     unconditional task, where we aim to learn a representation of the underlying TPP distribution from
418     empirical observations and generate new sequences from this distribution. In a sense, this second
419     task can be viewed as a special case of the first with no observed history. Our overall experimental
420
        procedure is inspired by that of Lüdke et al. (2023). We evaluate our model across a diverse set of
        datasets encompassing a wide range of possible point process behaviors. First, we use a collection of
421
        six synthetic datasets produced by Omi et al. (2019). We additionally evaluate our model on seven
422
        real-world datasets, which are a standard benchmark for modeling unmarked TPPs (Shchur et al.,
423     2020b; Bosser & Taieb, 2023; Lüdke et al., 2023). See Appendix A.
424
425
        Baseline Models Our baselines were selected as they constitute a set of diverse and highly per-
426     formant models. For an intensity-based method, we compare against the Neural Hawkes Process
427     (NHP) (Mei & Eisner, 2017). We additionally compare against two intensity-free methods, namely
428     the flow-based IFTPP model (Shchur et al., 2020a) and the diffusion-based model of Lin et al.
429     (2022). Lastly, our strongest baseline is the recently proposed Add-and-Thin model of Lüdke et al.
430     (2023), which can be loosely viewed as a non-autoregressive diffusion model. These models use an
431     RNN-based history encoder, with the exception of Add-and-Thin which uses a CNN-based encoder.
        See Appendix E for additional details.

466   5.1    F ORECASTING E VENT S EQUENCES
467
468   We first evaluate our model on a multi-step forecasting task. We set a forecast horizon ∆T for each
469   of our real-world datasets, and generate event sequences in the range [T0 , T0 + ∆T ] for some given
470   T0 , conditioned on the history of events HT0 . Up to a shift, this means we are taking T = [0, ∆T ] as
471
      the support for our model TPP. The forecast horizon ∆T is chosen such that the window typically
      contains multiple events. At training time, we uniformly sample T0 ∈ [∆T, T − ∆T ] and split a
472
      given data sequence γ1 into a history on [0, T0 ] and a future [T0 , T0 + ∆T ]. We encode the history
473
      HT0 before training the model to fit the events occurring in the future. At testing time, we perform
474   the same splitting procedure, sampling 50 values of T0 for each test set sequence. We then forecast
475   the sequence in [T0 , T0 + ∆T ] via the model and compute the distance (9) between the ground-truth
476   and generated sequences. Importantly, we note that the distance in Equation (9) is computed using
477   T0 + ∆T rather than T as the maximum event time, as using T would result in a distance which is
478   sensitive to the location of the forecasting window within the support [0, T ]. We further normalize
479   Equation (9) by ∆T to allow for comparison across different window lengths.
480   We report the results of this experiment in Table 1. Our proposed EventFlow method obtains the
481   lowest average forecasting error on 5/7 of the datasets, and closely matches the performance of Add-
482   and-Thin on the remaining 2/7 datasets. Given that the non-autoregressive models (EventFlow,
483   Add-and-Thin) consistently outperform the autoregressive baselines, this is clear evidence that
484   autoregressive models can struggle on multi-step predictions. This is especially true on the Reddit-C
485   and Reddit-S datasets, which exhibit long sequence lengths. In Appendix C, we provide additional
      evaluations of the event count distributions and one-step prediction performance in terms of MSE.

498   Ablations We additionally perform two ablations. First, we vary the number of function evaluations
499   (NFEs) used at sampling time, i.e., steps in Equation (8). We find that 10 NFEs is sufficient, and
500   increasing the NFEs further does not result in significant gains. Interestingly, with only one step,
501   we observe only a small drop in forecasting performance. This is enabled by our carefully designed
502   interpolant construction (Equation (3)). We emphasize that Add-and-Thin uses 100 NFEs at genera-
503
      tion time and the diffusion model uses 1000 NFEs per generated event. The autoregressive baselines
      (NHP, IFTPP) require one NFE per generated event. Thus, our method is able to simultaneously
504
      obtain strong forecasting performance while only requiring a small number of model evaluations.
505
      In our second ablation, we do not sample n ∼ pϕ (n | H), but rather set n to be the true number of
506   events in the forecast window. While this is not practical, this serves to ablate the effect of errors in
507   the event counts. We see that the forecasting error improves significantly, indicating that designing
508   stronger techniques for modeling pϕ (n | H) can lead to improved forecasts.

      Next, we evaluate our model on an unconditional generation task, where we aim to generate new
513   sequences from the underlying data distribution. This task serves as a benchmark to evaluate the
514   methods in terms of how well they are able to fit the underlying TPP. Moreover, learning a general-
515   purpose TPP prior could enable downstream tasks, such as data augmentation (Graikos et al., 2022).
516   In Table 2 we report MMD values (10) for each of the synthetic and real-world datasets. Model tuning
517   and selection is based on the validation set MMD. MMDs are calculated by sampling 1,000 sequences
518   from each trained model, and estimating Equation (10) using the generated and test set samples. The
519   first row (“data”) is the MMD calculated between samples in the training and validation sets, giving
520   us a sense of the best-case performance. See Appendix C for results with standard deviations.
521   Overall, we find that our EventFlow method (mean rank: 1.8) exhibits uniformly strong perfor-
522   mance, obtaining either the best or second best MMD on 11 of the 13 datasets. This is particularly
523   pronounced on the real-world datasets, where we obtain the lowest MMD on 5 of the 7 datasets. We
524   see that IFTPP (mean rank: 2.1) is a strong baseline, obtaining results which are competitive with
525   our method. The Add-and-Thin method (mean rank: 2.4) is often similarly strong, but struggles
526   on the SC dataset. While the NHP (mean rank: 3.7) can obtain good fits, this appears to be dataset
527
      dependent, with weak results on the NSR, SC, and Reddit-S datasets. The diffusion baseline (mean
      rank: 4.8) is our weakest baseline, which is perhaps unsurprising as this model can only be trained to
528
      maximize the likelihood of a subsequent event and not the overall sequence likelihood.

531   6     C ONCLUSION
532
533
      In this work, we propose EventFlow, a non-autoregressive generative model for temporal point
534   processes. We demonstrate that EventFlow is able to achieve state-of-the-art results on a multi-step
535   forecasting task and strong performance on unconditional generation. There are several directions
536   in which our work could be extended. First, we do not explicitly enforce that the support of our
537   model TPP is [0, T ], which would necessitate moving beyond the Gaussian setting. Second, more
538   sophisticated approaches to learning the event count distribution pϕ (n | H) could lead to improved
539   performance. Our work lays a foundation for flow-based TPPs, and exploring applications in tasks
      like imputation, marked TPPs, and querying (Boyd et al., 2023) are exciting directions.

858   D      E VENT F LOW A RCHITECTURE AND T RAINING D ETAILS
859
860   Here, we provide additional details regarding the parametrization and training of our EventFlow
861   model. In general, our model is based on the transformer architecture (Vaswani, 2017; Yang et al.,
862   2022), due to its general ability to handle variable length inputs and outputs, high flexibility, and
863   ability to incorporate long-range interactions. In all settings, our reference measure µ0 is specified
      with q = N (0, I).


                                                                   16
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


864
      Model Parametrization For our unconditional model, we first embed the sequence times γs , the
865   flow-time s, and the sequence position indices using sinusoidal embeddings followed by an additional
866   linear layer. There are three linear layers in total – one for the flow time, one shared across the
867   sequence times, and one for the position indices. These embeddings are added together to create a
868   representation of the sequence, and we apply a standard transformer to this sequence to produce a
869   sequence of vectors of length N (γs ). Finally, each of these vectors is projected to one dimension via
870   a final linear layer with shared weights to produce the vector field vθ (γs , s). See Figure 2.
871   For the conditional model, we use a standard transformer encoder-decoder architecture. We first
872   embed the history sequence times H and the sequence position indices in a manner analogous to the
873   above. In addition, the model was provided the start of the prediction window T0 by concatenating it
874   as the final event in H. This yielded better results than encoding the start of the prediction window
875   separately. We feed these embeddings through the transformer encoder produce an intermediate
876   representation eH .
877   For the decoder, we provide the model with the current state γs (corresponding to the generated event
878   times at flow-time s), the flow-time s, and the corresponding positional indices. These are embedded
879   as previously described, before being passed into the transformer decoder. The history encoding eH
880   is provided to the decoder via cross-attention in the intermediate layer. This produces a sequence
881   of N (γs ) vectors, which we again pass through a final linear layer to produce the final conditional
882   vector field vθ (γs , s, eH ). See Figure 3.
883   Our architecture for predicting the number of future events given a history, i.e. p(n | H), is again
884   based on the transformer decoder, sharing the same overall architecture as our unconditional model.
885   However, the key difference is that we instead take a mean of the final sequence embeddings before
886   passing this through a small MLP to produce the final logit. See Figure 4.
887
888   Training and Tuning We normalize all sequences to the range [−1, 1], using the overall min/max
889   event time seen in the training data. All sequences are generated on this normalized scale, prior to re-
890   scaling the sequence back to the original data range before evaluation. Our model is trained with the
891   Adam (Kingma & Ba, 2015) optimizer with β1 = 0.9 and β2 = 0.999 for 30, 000 steps with a cosine
892   scheduler (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017), which cycled every 10, 000 steps. Final hyperparameters
893
      were selected by best performance on the validation dataset achieved at any point during the training,
      where models were evaluated 10 times throughout their training.
894
895   To tune our model, we performed a grid search over learning rates in {5 × 10−3 , 10−3 , 5 × 10−4 }
896   and dropout probabilities in {0, 0.1, 0.2}. Overall, we found that learning rates of 10−2 or larger
897   often caused the model to diverge, and a dropout of 0.1 yielded the best results across all settings. We
898   use 6 transformer layers, 8 attention heads, and an embedding dimension of 512 across all settings,
899
      except for the Reddit-C and Reddit-S datasets where we use 4 heads and an embedding dimension of
      128 due to the increased memory cost of these datasets.

       Diffusion Our diffusion baseline is based on the implementation of Lin et al. (2022), and our
1135   decoder model architecture is taken directly from the code of Lin et al. (2022).4 At a high level, this
1136   model is a discrete-time diffusion model (Ho et al., 2020) trained to generate a single inter-arrival
1137   time given a history embedding. Note that as the likelihood is not available in diffusion models, the
1138   CDF in the likelihood in Equation (2) is not tractable. Instead, the model is trained by maximizing an
1139   ELBO of only the subsequent inter-arrival time.
1140
       In preliminary experiments, we found that the codebase provided by Lin et al. (2022) often produced
1141   NaN values during sampling, prompting us to make several changes. First, we use the RNN encoder
1142   from Shchur et al. (2020a), i.e. the same encoder as the IFTPP baseline, to reduce the memory
1143   requirements of the model. Second, we do not log-scale the inter-arrival times as suggested by Lin
1144   et al. (2022), as we found that this often led to overflow and underflow issues at sampling time. Third,
1145   we do not normalize the data via standardization (i.e., subtracting off the mean inter-arrival time and
1146   dividing by the standard deviation), but rather, we scale the inter-arrival times so that they are in the
1147   bounded range [−1, 1]. This is aligned with standard diffusion implementations (Ho et al., 2020),
1148   and allows us to perform clipping at sampling time to avoid the accumulation of errors. With these
1149
       changes, our diffusion baseline is competitive, and able to obtain stronger results than previous work
       has reported (Lüdke et al., 2023).
1150
1151   We use 1000 diffusion steps and the cosine beta schedule (Nichol & Dhariwal, 2021), and we train
1152   the model on the simplified ϵ-prediction loss of Ho et al. (2020). We train for 1, 000 epochs with
1153   early stopping based on the validation set loss. To tune this baseline, we performed a grid search over
1154   learning rates in {10−4 , 10−3 , 10−2 }, weight decays in {0, 10−6 , 10−5 , 10−4 }, history embedding
1155
       dimensions {32, 64, 128}, and layer numbers {2, 4, 6}. Our best hyperparameters can be found in
       Table 15 and Table 16.

1161   for 1, 000 epochs with early stopping on the validation loss. Tuning is performed via a grid search
1162   over learning rates in {10−4 , 10−3 , 10−2 } and number of mixture components in {8, 16, 32, 64}. We
1163   choose to tune only these hyperparameters in order to follow the implementation provided by the
