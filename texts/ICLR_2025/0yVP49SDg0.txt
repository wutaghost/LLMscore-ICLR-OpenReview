011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Multiple instance learning (MIL) has been widely employed for gigapixel whole
014            slide image (WSI) diagnosis. Existing MIL methods, however, are found wanting
015
               to align with the clinical practice of pathologists, who typically scrutinize WSIs
               at varied scales and compare the local regions in a global perspective. Given that
016
               WSIs usually boast immense dimensions peppered with large regions not pertinent
017
               to diagnosis, we propose a novel hierarchical multiple instance learning method
018            based on the state space model (SSM) , called Mamba-HMIL, for WSI classi-
019            fication. Mamba-HMIL consists of three primary modules to enhance the per-
020            formance of MIL. First, the hierarchical feature extractor harvests features across
021            diverse scales. Second, for capturing the correlation among patches, the state
022            space model demonstrates robust modeling capabilities. A Mixture of Experts
023            (MoE) module is for stable SSM training. Third, the adaptive selection model
024            strives to reduce redundancies by focusing on disease-positive regions. We evalu-
025            ate Mamba-HMIL on two WSI subtype datasets (TCGA-NSCLC and TCGA-
026
               RCC) and two WSI survival datasets (TCGA-BRCA and TCGA-BLCA). Our
               results suggest that Mamba-HMIL outperforms existing MIL methods on both
027
               WSI tasks. Our code will be made publicly available.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Pathological image analysis serves as the gold standard for cancer diagnosis Kumar et al. (2014).
033   Rapid advancements in scanning technologies Farahani et al. (2015) have digitized pathological
034   scans into whole slide images (WSIs) of up to 100, 000×100, 000 pixels. Analyzing these WSIs can
035   be a labor-intensive and time-consuming task that demands considerable expertise and concentration
036   from pathologists Evered & Dudding (2011). Recent studies indicate that computer-aided methods
037   could alleviate these demands Tizhoosh & Pantanowitz (2018); Bera et al. (2019); Niazi et al. (2019);
038   Colling et al. (2019); Jiang et al. (2020). However, due to the immensity of WSIs, computer-aided
039
      analysis needs huge computational resources,posing a considerable challenge Evered & Dudding
      (2011). To address this, researchers have cropped each WSI into a large number of patches, which
040
      can be treated as a bag of instances. Thus, cancer diagnosis using WSIs has been formulated into a
041
      multiple instance learning (MIL) problem, where each bag (i.e., a WSI) has a label but each instance
042   (i.e., a patch) inside a bag has no label.
043
044   With the advent of convolutional neural networks (CNNs), numerous CNN-based MIL methods have
045
      been proposed for WSI diagnosis Chikontwe et al. (2020); Lerousseau et al. (2020); Xu et al. (2014);
      Feng & Zhou (2017); Ilse et al. (2018); Campanella et al. (2019); Lu et al. (2021); Li et al. (2021);
046
      Shao et al. (2021). These methods can be categorized into instance-level and embedding-level ones.
047
      Instance-level methods predict the pseudo-label of each instance based on bag-level labels, and then
048   aggregate instance-level pseudo-labels to form the bag-level prediction Chikontwe et al. (2020).
049   These methods usually have inferior performance due to their sensitivity to instance-level labels.
050   Embedding-level methods convert each instance into a feature embedding, and then feed the feature
051   embeddings from the same bag to an aggregator for bag-level prediction Xu et al. (2014); Feng &
052   Zhou (2017); Ilse et al. (2018); Campanella et al. (2019); Lu et al. (2021); Li et al. (2021); Shao et al.
053   (2021). Despite their notable success, these methods exhibit several major drawbacks. First, a WSI
      may present variable diagnostic information at different scales (Figure 1). For instance, a pathologist

075

076   features, mirroring the practice of a pathologist. Second, we employ a state space model (SSM)
077   for feature aggregation to capture long-range correlations among tissue and tumor regions across
078   thousands of patches while maintaining a manageable computational cost. Additionally, to ensure
079   stable training, we incorporate a Mixture of Experts (MoE) and sequence fusion module to balance
080   the contributions of each SSM sequence. Third, we insert an adaptive selection module to filter
081   out disease-negative patches before classification. We verify the effectiveness of each component
082   of our Mamba-HMIL and evaluate it against existing subtype classification and survival prediction
083   methods using four public datasets. The contributions of this work are two-fold.

085
               We propose a novel solution to WSI classification, which extracts multiscale features, esti-
               mates the long-range correlation among tissue/tumor regions, and utilizes sparse selection
086
               to mitigate patch redundancy.
087
088            The proposed Mamba-HMIL beats all competing methods on two public WSI classifica-
089
               tion datasets, setting the new state of the art.

192
      The feature extractor is flexible to various deep learning networks. In this paper, we choose ResNet-

193

195   ResNet-50 consists of a 7 × 7 convolutional (Conv) layer, a 3 × 3 max pooling layer, four stages of
196   residual blocks (each residual block is stacked by a fixed mode of 1 × 1, 3 × 3 and 1 × 1 Conv layers,

198

199
      model.
200
201   ViT consists of a linear projection layer followed by Transformer blocks, each containing a multi-
202   head self-attention (MHSA) mechanism, a feed-forward network (FFN), and two layer normaliza-
203   tion (LN) stages. Residual connections are applied after both the MHSA and FFN layers to improve
204
      gradient flow. We choose ViT-Large pre-trained by UNI Chen et al. (2023) as the feature extractors.

      3.4   M IXTURE OF E XPERTS M ODULE
281
282   For stable training, we use the Mixture of Experts (MoE) for multi-squence fusion. The gating
283   mechanism is a simple linear layer, which computes relevance scores for each expert. The gating
284   mechanism then activates the top-k experts based on these scores, directing the input through only
285   those experts. The sequence passes through the selected experts, the outputs from these experts are
286   combined. The aggregation is a weighted combination based on the gate’s selection scores.
287
288   3.5   A DAPTIVE SELECTION M ODULE
289
290   A adaptive selection (AS) module is used to discard redundant negative instances. It contains a
291   MLP layer and a Sigmoid function. We utilize the AS module to compute a weight score for each
292   sequence, and all sequences are then aggregated based on their respective weights. We set a temper-
293
      ature parameter P to balance the number of instances in each bag.

308   Implementation details. Experiments are implemented on the device NVIDIA GTX 3080 GPU,
309   Intel(R) Xeon(R) CPU E5-2690 v4 @ 2.60GHz, in Python 3.10 on Anaconda with CUDA 12.1 and
310   Pytorch 2.1.0. We use Adam optimizer with learning rate 2e-4 to optimize SSM+SS training. The
311   batch size is 1 and the maximum epoch is 200. In order to find the most suitable training parameters,
312   cross-validation is formed from the whole slides in all the TCGA datasets.

476   5   C ONCLUSION
477
478
      In this paper, we have proposed Mamba-HMIL to solve the WSI classification task. Mamba-
479
      HMIL consists of three stages: the hierarchical feature extractor, the state space model, and the
480   sparse selection block. We design the hierarchical feature extractor to obtain multi-scale features like
481   a pathologist. The state space model is then utilized to calculate the correlation among instances, and
482   the sparse selection module is used to select the instance embeddings with high positive probability
483   and aggregate for a WSI-level prediction. Extensive experiments have been performed on two WSI
484   classification datasets. The experimental results indicate that Mamba-HMIL can dramatically
485   improve the performance of WSI-level classification. Our future work will focus on prognostic
      analysis and validation of other external data.
