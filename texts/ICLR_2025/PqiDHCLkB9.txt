011                                                 A BSTRACT
012
013            Zero-shot neural architecture search aims to predict multiple characteristics of
014            neural architectures using proxy indicators without actual training, however, most
015
               methods focus on evaluating only a single characteristic of neural networks. Since
               the Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK) offers a promising theoretical framework for
016
               understanding the characteristics of neural networks, we propose NTK-score, a
017
               proxy indicator that includes three metrics derived from NTK’s eigenvalues and
018            kernel regression, to assess three critical characteristics: trainability, expressivity,
019            and generalization. Moreover, to exploit three metrics of our NTK-score, we em-
020            ploy the Borda Count approach on our NTK-score to rank architectures in neural
021            architecture search. Compared with state-of-the-art proxies, experimental results
022            demonstrate that the NTK-score correlates well with both the test accuracy and
023            training time of architectures, and outperforms comparison proxies across various
024            search spaces and methods, including NAS-bench-201, DARTS, and ResNet, as
025            well as pruning, reinforce, and evolutionary algorithm.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      Neural networks have brought many important technological breakthroughs and innovations to the
030   field of computer vision, promoting the rapid development and widespread application of this field.
031   However, manually designing neural network architectures is challenging and requires extensive
032   professional knowledge and experience, as well as a lot of experiments and adjustments Baker et al.
033   (2016), Rumiantsev & Coates (2023). Therefore, automatically designing neural networks, such as
034   Neural Architecture Search (NAS), has attracted increasing research interest.
035
      NAS enables automated neural network design by searching the space of possible network archi-
036   tectures and evaluating the performance of each architecture. As more and more NAS methods
037   have been proposed, the NAS methods can now be categorized into three types based on training
038   frequency: multi-shot NAS Xie & Yuille (2017), one-shot NAS Liu et al. (2018) and zero-shot
039   NAS Chen et al. (2021). This categorizing reflects the varying consumption of time and resources
040   involved in training. Due to the huge overhead of time and resources required for training architec-
041   tures, our work focuses on the zero-shot NAS.
042   Zero-shot NAS evaluates and selects potential neural network architectures based on some sophis-
043   ticated metrics, bypassing network training. The lack of training necessitates an accuracy ranking
044   agent Chen et al. (2021), Chen et al. (2023b). For instance, TEG-NAS Chen et al. (2023b) and
045   AZ-NAS Lee & Ham (2024) employ their proxies to rank the neural networks based on multiple
046   characteristics. However, most proposed agents focus solely on one characteristic, often leading to
047   results that fail to outperform certain naive agents Li et al. (2024). As Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK)
048   provides a stable mathematical framework to understand and analyze the characteristics of neural
049   networks by linearizing the training dynamics and remaining constant over time in the infinite-width
050
      limit, our designed agent utilizes NTK to evaluate neural networks across the dimensions of train-
      ability, expressivity, and generalization simultaneously.
051
052   The trainability of a neural network refers to how quickly it converges to the expected loss or accu-
053   racy during training, indicating its ability to adapt to the training data Li et al. (2024). To characterize
      the trainability, TE-NAS Chen et al. (2021) utilizes the condition number κ Xiao et al. (2020), which


                                                           1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      only focuses on the max and min eigenvalues, somewhat ignores the distribution of eigenvalues, as
055   the entire spectrum of the NTK can be a better measure Wang et al. (2023). We argue that neu-
056   ral networks with better generalization capabilities tend to exhibit more similar NTK eigenvalues.
057   Therefore, we present a metric that quantifies an architecture’s trainability by utilizing the ratio of
058   larger NTK eigenvalues.
059
      Expressivity of a neural network refers to its ability to capture and represent the number of com-
060
      plex patterns and relationships within data Raghu et al. (2017). A common expressivity measure
061   indicator is the number of parameters, which is not applicable to deep networks as networks with
062   the same number of parameters but different depths perform quite differently. Hence, the current
063   NAS approaches mostly use the Number of Linear Regions (NLR) divided by the ReLU function
064   Mellor et al. (2021), Chen et al. (2021) rather than NTK. Considering that the output of a neural
065   network can be efficiently evaluated using NTK Lee et al. (2019), and the network’s capacity to
066   map similar inputs to distinct regions can serve as a reflection of the network’s expressive ability
067   Xiong et al. (2020), we propose an expressivity metric by computing the cross-entropy of the output
068   distributions of two similar inputs, in which the output distributions are calculated through NTK.
069   Generalization of a neural network also stands as a crucial indicator for evaluating the neural net-
070   work’s capacity to operate effectively on unseen data Zhu et al. (2022). Recent research has as-
071   sociated NTK with the generalization capabilities of neural networks, such as degree k fractional
072   variance of the NTK kernel Yang & Salman (2019) and Mean Squared Error (MSE) loss in NTK
073   kernel regression Chen et al. (2023a). NTK kernel regression simply represents the network’s label
074   on the test set Lee et al. (2019), and its loss with the true label shows the network’s ability on unseen
075
      data. We also use this metric to evaluate the generalization ability of neural networks.
076   After characterizing the trainability, expressivity, and generalization of a neural network based on
077   NTK, we organize the three values of these characteristics as a proxy NTK-score for ranking neural
078   architectures in NAS. Due to the disparate nature of these three score values, direct arithmetic oper-
079   ations are challenging. To address this, we employ the Borda Count approach on the NTK-score’s
080   three characteristics, and design an NAS framework called SABoC-NAS.
081   In summary, our contributions are as follows:
082
083          • To evaluate and rank neural architectures, we introduce NTK-score, which includes three
084            training-free metrics derived from NTK’s eigenvalues and kernel regression, to assess the
085            trainability, expressivity, and generalization of a neural network respectively.
086          • We develop a NAS framework using our NTK-score, namely SABoC-NAS, managing
087            trade-offs among trainability, expressivity, and generalization by Borda Count.

091   2.1   N EURAL A RCHITECTURE S EARCH
092
093   At first, people used brute force methods to directly use the accuracy of the trained network architec-
094   ture for screening, including Genetic CNN Xie & Yuille (2017) and MetaQNN Baker et al. (2016).
095   Then, in order to reduce the overhead required for training, one-shot was proposed, that is, Supernet
096   is trained only once, and multiple different networks are obtained through weight sharing, such as
097   DARTS Liu et al. (2018), FBNet Wu et al. (2019), GreedyNAS You et al. (2020) and Single-Path
098
      One-Shot NAS Guo et al. (2020).
099   Recently, zero-shot NAS becomes the mainstream method of NAS Li et al. (2024). Gradient of
100   deep network parameters is first proposed to design agents that can rank the accuracy of candidate
101   network architectures, such as Fisher Liu et al. (2021), SNIP Lee et al. (2018), Synflow Tanaka et al.
102   (2020), GraSP Wang et al. (2020), Gradnorm Abdelfattah et al. (2021), ZiCo Bhardwaj et al. (2023).
103   TE-NAS Chen et al. (2021) ranked architectures by analyzing the spectrum of NTK and the number
104   of linear regions in the input space that respectively imply the trainability and expressivity of the
105   neural network. On the basis of TE-NAS, TEG-NAS Chen et al. (2023a) completed the training
106   indicators in generalization and promotes the visualization of the search space. KNAS Xu et al.
107   (2021) found a practical gradient kernel that exhibits strong correlations with both training loss and
      validation performance and proposed a new kernel based architecture search approach. Further-

109   to capture the extensive nonlinear characteristics present in contemporary neural architectures. Ad-
110   ditionally, Neural Network Gaussian Process (NNGP) Rumiantsev & Coates (2023) which can be
111   computed more efficiently was introduced as a kernel metric to evaluate the architectures faster.

124   2.2   N EURAL TANGENT K ERNEL
125
126
      The concept of NTK was first proposed to prove that the gradient descent of artificial neural networks
      is equivalent to kernel gradient descent. Further research shows that NTK enables the scrutiny of
127
      the network’s trainability, expressivity, and generalization.
128
129   Trainability. The condition number κ = λmax /λmin and the largest/smallest eigenvalue of the
130   NTK λmax/min Xiao et al. (2020) are used to analyze trainability. The degree k fractional variance
131   Yang & Salman (2019) is proposed as a metric to evaluate the generalization properties of neural
132
      networks. In addition, the training process of a neural network can be decomposed along differ-
      ent directions defined by the eigenfunctions of the neural tangent kernel, each direction having its
133
      own convergence rate determined by the corresponding eigenvalues Cao et al. (2019), Bowman &
134
      Montúfar (2022). Furthermore, it is borne out that larger eigenvalues express the convergence speed
135   and the learning rate is related to the eigenvalues Kopitkov & Indelman (2020).
136
137   Expressivity. The dynamics of the network function fθ aligns with kernel gradient descent in func-
138
      tion space concerning a limiting kernel during training Jacot et al. (2018). Moreover, the ODE of
      the neural network output with respect to NTK is obtained Lee et al. (2019). The result is further
139
      extended by decomposing the ODE along different eigenvectors Xiao et al. (2020). Since the out-
140
      put can be easily represented through NTK, NTK can be used to analyze the expressivity of neural
141   networks.
142
143   Generalization. NTK can provide memory, optimization and generalization guarantees in deep neu-
144
      ral networks Bombari et al. (2022). The minimum eigenvalue is used to establish the generalization
      error bound in stochastic gradient descent training Zhu et al. (2022), Zhu et al. (2023). The positive
145
      definiteness of NTK is proved by providing the lower bound of the minimum eigenvalue of NTK in
146
      deep learning theory, both in the limiting case of infinite widths and for finite widths Nguyen et al.
147   (2021), Bombari et al. (2022), Zhu et al. (2022), Zhu et al. (2023), Banerjee et al. (2023).

      4.2   SAB O C-NAS: S EARCHING A RCHITECTURE BY B ORDA C OUNT
217
218   We depict SABoC-NAS framework for selecting the target architecture with high scores across all
219   three metrics, as it is proved that there is no single architecture that is optimal in all three characteris-
220   tics given a fixed budget Chen et al. (2023b). Considering the significant disparity in the magnitudes
221   of the three metrics, simple addition or subtraction is inadequate for fusing them, we employ the
222   Borda Count approach to flexibly trade-off between trainability, expressivity, and generalization.
223   Specifically, given a set of architectures {a1 , a2 , . . . , am }, we calculate the NTK-scores of all m
224   architectures separately, denoted by Eq. (11)
225
                                     Si1:m = {Si1 , Si2 , . . . , Sin },   i = t, e, g,                       (11)
226
227   where subscript i of metric value Sik indicates different metrics, and superscript k indicates archi-
228   tecture ak .
229   Having the three metric values of all architectures St1:m , Se1:m , Sg1:m , we sort all architectures based
230   on each metric’s values separately, and calculate the Borda Count rankings, as shown by Eq. (12)
231
                               Sr1:m = rank(St1:m ) + rank(Se1:m ) + rank(Sg1:m ),                            (12)
232
233   where rank(·) sorts input values and returns a permutation of {1, 2, . . . , m} which indicates the
234   positions of all input values at the ordered array.
235   The core component of our SABoC-NAS framework is the proxy Sr1:m for ranking architectures,
236   and various search algorithms, such as pruning algorithm, reinforce algorithm and evolutionary
237   algorithm, could be easily integrated into our SABoC-NAS framework.

249   We introduce the search space, search methods, SOTA proxies, as well as parameter settings used in
250   the experiments.
251   Search Space. NAS-Bench-201 Dong & Yang (2020) search space contains 5 operations: none
252   (zero), skip connection, 1 x 1 convolution, 3 x 3 convolution, and average pooling 3 x 3.
253
254
      DARTS Liu et al. (2018) search space contains 8 operations: none (zero), skip connection, separable
      convolution 3 × 3 and 5 × 5, dilated separable convolution 3 × 3 and 5 × 5, max pooling 3 × 3, average
255
      pooling 3 × 3.
256
257   ResNet He et al. (2016) search space consists of residual blocks and bottleneck blocks. The convo-
258   lution kernel size is in the set {3,5,7}
259   Search Method. Pruning Algorithm, slimier to TE-NAS Chen et al. (2021). The neural network is
260   structured with standardized cells of parallel edges. For an operation performed on a parallel edge
261   between two cells in each iteration t, the NTK-scores of the network are calculated both before
262   and after the operation. The pruning probability is then determined by comprehensively evaluat-
263   ing the scores in three characteristics: trainability, expressivity, and generalization. The algorithm
264   iteratively prunes one of the parallel edges until only a single path remains.
265   Reinforce Algorithm, slimier to TEG-NAS Chen et al. (2023a). The action space in reinforcement
266   learning is defined as the edge operation between cells, and the reward is defined as the compre-
267   hensive score of the trainability, expressivity, and generalization of the new architecture generated
268   by the selected action. The algorithm selects actions from the action space according to probability,
269   and then updates the probability of action selection according to the reward. The above operation is
      repeated for T steps, and finally the top ranked architecture by Borda Count is selected.

272   the population size exceeds the maximum limit, the architecture with the worst score for each of
273   the three metrics is removed, resulting in the simultaneous removal of three architectures. At the
274   conclusion of iterations, we select the target architecture based on the comprehensive ranking by the

516   6      C ONCLUSION
517
518   In this work, we introduce the NTK-score, a metric that leverages NTK to evaluate neural net-
519   works across three key characteristics: trainability, expressivity, and generalization. We also present
520   the SABoC-NAS framework, which utilizes the Borda Count approach to effectively integrate the
521   diverse aspects of the NTK-score. By focusing exclusively on eigenvalues and kernel regression de-
522   rived from the NTK, our method achieves higher accuracy and lower computational costs compared
523
      with other kernel-based approaches. In the future, we will place greater emphasis on the theoretical
      analysis of NTK-based metrics, explore additional applications of NTK in NAS, and conduct more
524
      extensive experimental validations.
