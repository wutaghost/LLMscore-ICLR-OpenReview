009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            The detection of foreground targets on airport surface is the foundation of air-
012            port surveillance applications. However, effective algorithms and specialized
013            benchmarks are still lacking in this area. Based on this fact, we propose an Air-
014            port Foreground Target Detection dataset (AFTD), which contains the three most
015            important foreground targets moving on the airport surface: airplane, vehicle,
016            and person. Through self collection and collection of web images, we have ob-
017            tained a total of over 200000 images and filtered out 10050 images based on
018
               diversity principles to form the AFTD dataset, which includes a total of 26968
               airplane instances, 24759 vehicle instances, and 5064 person instances. AFTD
019
               includes a variety of changes of these targets, such as super multi-scale, multi-
020
               level occlusion and viewangle changes, etc. In addition, we further illustrate the
021            challenges posed by AFTD to existing algorithms through statistical analysis and
022            detailed experiments, and discuss how to solve these challenges in the airport
023            surveillance scenario.The AFTD dataset can be downloaded from http://www.agvs-
024            caac.com/aftd/aftd.html.And our moduel is available at https://github.com/cpc1111-
025            lab/Additional-discussion-for-ATFD.

028   1    I NTRODUCTION
029
030   In recent years, with the rapid growth of global population and economy, airports have experienced a
031   significant increase in passenger and cargo traffic. Meanwhile, there has been an upward trend in the
032   number of security incidents related to movable targets on airport surface. For example, on January 2,
033
      2024, two planes collided at Haneda Airport in Japan, resulting in five deaths, and on January 16,
      two planes collided again at New Chitose Airport in Hokkaido, Japan. Therefore, it is particularly
034
      important to develop intelligent applications to enhance the surveillance of foreground targets on the
035
      airport surface.
036
037   Object detection is widely used in airport surveillance applications. However, we found that the
038   performance of object detection algorithms has decreased to varying degrees in airport scenarios, and
039
      subsequent experiments have also proven this. This is because fundamental research emphasizes the
      generalization of algorithms, and thus classical datasets usually contain rich object categories, but
040
      without considering all states of each class. Practical applications, such as airport surveillance, do not
041
      focus on algorithm generalization, but rather on robustness to variations of specific targets. In other
042   words, the study of airport surveillance requires a dataset with rich samples for airport foreground
043   targets. Obviously, classical datasets do not meet this requirement.
044
045
      In this paper, we propose the first Airport Foreground Target Detection dataset, AFTD, for airport
      surveillance. AFTD only includes three types of movable targets on the airport surface, but strives
046
      to cover variations of each type of target. We obtained permission to collect data at multiple
047
      airports through cooperation with the institute of civil aviation, and fully utilized network resources,
048   ultimately collecting over 200000 raw images. We then rigorously filtered the data based on the
049   diversity principle. Due to the high similarity of airport surface structures, only 10050 images were
050   retained, totaling 26968 airplane instances, 24759 vehicle instances and 5064 person instances. In
051   addition, we designed more refined label format for airplane. For example, for the airplane category,
052   we designed three scale formats, three occlusion formats and eight viewangle formats. All images are
053   manually annotated following strict rules. Finally, we fully tested the AFTD dataset and analyzed the
      experimental results.

064   2     RELATED WORK
065
066   2.1   O BJECT D ETECTION DATASETS
067
068   In the past more than ten years, many classical object detection datasets have been proposed, such as
069   VOC2007Everingham et al., VOC2012Everingham & Winn (2012), ImageNetDeng et al. (2009),
070   MS-COCOLin et al. (2014), Objects365Shao et al. (2019), etc.
071   The VOC2007 dataset consists of 5k images and 12k annotated objects. The VOC2012 dataset
072   consists of 11k images and 27k annotated objects. These two datasets annotate 20 common objects in
073   daily life. The ImageNet Large Scale Visual Recognition Challenge (ILSVRC) has promoted the
074   technological level of universal object detection, with the ILSVRC detection dataset containing 200
075   categories of visual objects. The MS-COCO dataset has fewer object categories than ILSVRC, but
076   it has more object instances and contains more small objects and more densely located objects. At
077   present, the MS-COCO dataset has become the de facto standard in the field of object detection.
078   None of the above datasets specifically consider airport scenarios. The FGVC airplane datasetMaji
079   et al. (2013) is an airplane classification dataset that contains 10200 airplane images, with 100 images
080   of each of the 102 types of Boeing airplanes. The majority of the airplanes in the FGVC-airplane
081   dataset are presented in sideways view, with a very single view viewangle and no scale variation.
082
083   2.2   O BJECT D ETECTION A LGORITHMS
084
085   Since the successful application of AlexNetKrizhevsky et al. (2012) in 2012, Convolutional Neural
086   Networks (CNN) have gradually become mainstream. Object detection has been revolutionized by
087   CNN, experiencing a shift from traditional methods to deep learning methods.
088
      R-CNNGirshick et al. (2014) generates candidate frames and further refines classification and
089
      localization through a region proposition network, which marked the rise of two-phase detection.
090   SPPNetHe et al. (2015) avoids repeated calculation of convolution features by introducing a spatial
091   pyramid pooling layer that generates a fixed-length representation of a candidate region frame of
092   arbitrary size. Fast RCNNGirshick (2015) enables neural networks to train both classification and
093   regression tasks simultaneously, further reducing redundant calculations. Faster RCNNRen et al.
094   (2015) is the first near real-time object detector to replace traditional candidate region extraction
095   methods by introducing a region proposal network.
096   YOLO seriesRedmon et al. (2016)Redmon & Farhadi (2017) adopts an end-to-end approach to predict
097   both bounding box and category probabilities, pioneering the paradigm of one-stage object detection
098   algorithms. SSDLiu et al. (2016) improves the detection accuracy of one-stage detectors by detecting
099   objects at different scales on different layers of the network. RetinaNetLin et al. (2017) reshapes the
100   standard cross-entropy loss by introducing a new loss function, focal loss, which allows the detector
101   to focus more on hard, misclassified samples during training. CornerNetLaw & Deng (2018) detects
102   each bounding box as a pair of keypoints. CenterNetDuan et al. (2019) treats an object as a single
103   point and regresses all its attributes.
104   DETRCarion et al. (2020) first applies the TransformerVaswani et al. (2017) to the field of object
105   detection, directly predicting bounding boxes and categories through encoder-decoder structure,
106   eliminating anchor, NMS, and other steps. Deformable DETRZhu et al. (2020) reduces computational
107   complexity by introducing a deformable attention module, and improves performance for small objects
      by using multi-scale feature maps.

      The AFTD dataset focuses on three types of movable targets on the airport surface: airplane, vehicle,

      and person, the movements of which are the primary causes of various airport security incidents. We

      3.1   DATASET D ESIGN
116
117   The principle of dataset design is to try to cover as many variations or challenges of the target of
118   interest as possible. We will introduce the challenges for airplane, vehicle and person in turn.
119
      1) airplane are our primary target of interest. We have summarized six main challenges to airplane
120
      detection in airport scenarios, each of which is described below.
121

129   Figure 1: (a) and (b) show airplane presented at different scales on the screen. (c) and (d) show the

131   monitoring screen.
132
133   Super multi-scale. Compared with the scale change in ordinary monitoring scenes, the scale of
134   airplane in airports varies greatly on the imaging plane, as shown in Figure 1(a) and 1(b). This is due
135
      to the airport has a vast area, with monitoring distances spanning the range of ten meters to kilometers.
      When the scale of the airplane is too large, details may be magnified, resulting in the algorithm being
136
      unable to effectively capture the global features of the object. When the scale of the airplane is
137
      too small, it is difficult to effectively capture the local features of the object. In addition, there is a
138   multi-scale coexistence in the airport, as shown in Figure 1(b), where very large and small objects
139   occur simultaneously. Because the solutions to objects at different scales may be incompatible, the
140   simultaneous appearance of multiple scales poses greater challenges.
141
      Multi-level occlusion. In airport, there is a frequent occurrence of airplane being obstructed by other
142
      objects, such as bridges, adjacent airplane, etc. and it is also frequent that part of the airplane’s body
143
      is outside the screen, as shown in Figure 1(c) and (d). Occlusion often leads to incomplete feature
144   information of the object, or even results in a serious lack of key identification features.
145
146   Viewangle changes. The appearance of the airplane varies tremendously from one viewangle to
147
      another because of the wingspan shape. The shape of is completely different in the side and front and
      rear viewangle, as shown in Figure 1. The characteristics of the airplane may be more varied when
148
      the viewangle occurs in conjunction with other changes, thus increasing the difficulty in detection.

161   reduces the contrast of the image, as shown in Figure 2(b), resulting in the silhouette of the airplane
      being difficult to recognize, which may lead to missed detection.

196   2) Vehicle and person are also the main movable targets on airport surface. Some of the functions
197   they provide, such as cargo handling and runway inspection, are important part of airport production
198   activities. Although vehicle and person are common object categories, their attributes in airport surface

201
      is often poorly outlined or even difficult to recognize, as shown in Figure 3(b). Secondly, vehicles in

203   since their movement behaviors are similar, we collectively refer to such movable targets as "vehicles"
204   at present. If necessary in future research, we will differentiate different types of vehicles based on
205   specific needs.

238   2) Data screening. The purpose of data filtering is to minimize data redundancy while ensuring
239   diversity. The data collection and the data filtering of the AFTD dataset are extremely challenging.
240   Although we conducted data collection at several different airports, since the airports are very similar
241   in terms of layout, facility configuration, etc., when we analyzed the collected data, we found that the
242
      images collected from different airports were highly similar, and even unable to distinguish which
      airport they came from, as shown in Figure 5(a) and (b). In addition, due to the high standardization of
243
      airport production activities, the activities of movable targets are also very similar, so the redundancy
244
      of data is very high. After screening, we only retained 5050 out of over 100000 raw images. Opening
245   more data collection points can increase the number of effective samples. However, in addition to the
246   semi-military restrictions of airports, the number of airports is also relatively limited. There are only
247   255 civil airports in the whole of China.
248
      3) Data expansion. Due to the difficulty in opening airport data sources, we decided to enrich the
249
      AFTD dataset by collecting public images of domestic and foreign civil airports from web resources.
250
      We collected approximately 100000 images from the internet and followed similar principles as in
251   the previous section to filter the data. In addition, we especially considered the imaging angle. Web
252   images were removed if their imaging angles, especially the pitch angle, differed significantly from
253   the self-collection data. For example, satellite images viewed directly downwards were all deleted.
254   After filtering, only 5000 out of over 100000 web images were retained. The web images did enrich
255   the diversity of the dataset to some extent, as shown in Figure 5(c) and (d).
256
      In summary, the AFTD dataset currently contains 10050 image data. Although it has been possible
257   to conduct experiments on this dataset and draw the conclusions, the amount of data is still slightly
258   insufficient. We plan to enrich the data in two ways in the future. Firstly, we plan to open up more
259   data collection points. However, due to the high similarity of airport structures and the necessity of
260   obtaining permits, this work may be laborious. Secondly, we plan to collect data at the simulated
261   validation airport, such as the one to be built in Chengdu in 2025.

404
405   In this section, we select 14 object detection algorithms that perform well on MS-COCO to test our
406   dataset and analyze the results. These algorithms include two-stage, one-stage, and end-to-end object
407
      detection algorithms. Moreover, we discuss algorithm design approaches for future research.

409   4.1   E XPERIMENTAL S ETTINGS
410
      For the each algorithm we chosed, we used the source code provided in the original paper. For model
411   configuration, unless specified otherwise, we adopted the default training settings. It should be noted
412   that the three additional labels we designed for the airplane are for the need of subsequent algorithm
413   design, so they are not used in the experiments in this section.
414
415   4.2   E VALUATION M ETRICS
416
417   Average Precision (AP) is an average of the precision at different recall points, and the larger the
418   AP value indicates that the model is more effective. mAP is an average of the AP values of all the
419   categories, AP can reflect the accuracy of the prediction of each category, and mAP is used to reflect
420   the accuracy of the whole model. Since the AFTD dataset contains multiple categories, we use mAP
421
      as the evaluation metric. Here we calculate AP50 and AP75 for each algorithm on the AFTD dataset
      separately. AP50 denotes the value of mAP for the IOU threshold of 0.5, and AP75 is the same.

      4.4     A DDITIONAL D ISCUSSION
491
492   The above experiments show that object detection does face challenges in airport surveillance. Here,
493   we take one challenge as examples and propose some possible algorithm design ideas.
494
      Super multi-scale problem. Since objects at different scales differ in texture details, relationship with
495
      the context, etc., detection at different scales may not be fully compatible or have different optimal
496   solutions. Therefore, a single detection algorithm might not be effective for all scales at the same
497   time. We could use different attention mechanisms for objects at different scales. This method first
498   requires predicting the approximate locations where different scale objects will appear. Observations
499   have shown that there is a special “target/background” co-occurrence in airport scenarios, so that the
500   scale of the object can be initially predicted by dividing the background region, for example, airplane
501   appearing in the sky region generally belong to the small-scale objects.
502   Based on the above ideas, we designed a module that uses different attention mechanisms for different
503   scale features. Figure 11 shows the structure of the module.In order to prove the effectiveness of our
504   module, we conducted experiments on yolov8 with small target detection head, and the experimental
505   results are shown in Table 3.
