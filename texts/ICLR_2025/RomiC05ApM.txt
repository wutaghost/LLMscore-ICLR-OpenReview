009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Neural networks are not learning optimal decision boundaries. We show that de-
012            cision boundaries are situated in areas of low training data density. They are im-
013            pacted by few training samples which can easily lead to overfitting. We provide
014            a simple algorithm performing a weighted average of the prediction of a sam-
015            ple and its nearest neighbors’ (computed in latent space) leading to minor favor-
016            able outcomes for a variety of important measures for neural networks. In our
017            evaluation, we employ various self-trained and (state-of-the-art) pre-trained con-
018
               volutional neural networks to show that our approach improves (i) resistance to
               label noise, (ii) robustness against adversarial attacks, (iii) classification accuracy,
019
               and yields novel means for (iv) interpretability. Our interpretability analysis is of
020
               independent interest to the XAI community, as it is applicable to any network.
021            While improvements are not necessarily large in all four areas, our approach is
022            conceptually simple, i.e., improvements come without any modification to net-
023            work architecture, training procedure or dataset. Furthermore, our approach is in
024            stark contrast to prior works that often require trade-offs among the four objectives
025            combined with architectural adaptations or provide valuable, but non-actionable
026            insights. Finally, we provide a theoretical analysis.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   In the realm of machine learning, the decision boundary plays a crucial role in distinguishing be-
032   tween classes. Classes typically share certain characteristics and tend to form clusters. The decision
033
      boundary is a hypersurface that partitions the input space into regions corresponding to different
      classes. An optimal decision boundary implies an optimal classifier and vice versa. Simple classi-
034
      fiers, such as support vector machines (SVMs), logistic regression, and k-nearest neighbors, often
035
      generate simple decision boundaries (Cortes & Vapnik, 1995). On the other hand, deep neural net-
036   works (DNNs) have shown remarkable capabilities in learning feature hierarchies and capturing
037   complex, non-linear decision boundaries owing to their depth and non-linear activation functions
038   (LeCun et al., 2015). They can be said to have revolutionized the field of computer vision and oth-
039   ers, leading to astonishing improvements in accuracy on multiple benchmarks. Still, these models
040   also suffer from weaknesses such as a lack of interpretability, lack of robustness as witnessed by
041   the effectiveness of adversarial samples (Goodfellow et al., 2014). Many techniques that tackle the
042   problem of adversarial samples, interpretability, as well as improving handling of noisy labels come
043   with tradeoffs. That is, the pursuit of any of these goals often leads to lower accuracy or requires
044
      altering training schemes, datasets, and architectures.
045   In this work, we propose a technique that should tackle all of these issues as illustrated in Table 1.
046   We combine the prediction of a pre-trained neural network of a sample to predict and the prediction
047   of the k-nearest neighbors (kNNs). We compute nearest neighbors (NNs) in latent space using layer
048   activations of a (pre-trained) classifier. We calculate a weighted average of the actual prediction and
049   those of NNs as final prediction (see Figure 1).
050   This approach improves robustness against adversarial samples, interpretability, and handling noisy
051   samples without compromising performance of the classifier, i.e., we mostly improve it. It also
052   does not require altering a (pre-trained) classifier. However, obtaining kNNs is also computationally
053   expensive and our technique does also not fully address the desiderata (in Table 1), but rather it
      marks a step forward. This, is still a major achievement given that other techniques require rather

088   based on a latent representation given by layer activations rather than on input samples or final
089   outputs. Second, the prediction is a combination of the network output of the sample to predict
090   and its NNs, while for classical kNNs only the NNs are used to make a prediction. Third, the
091   combination is based on directly aggregating network outputs rather than performing a majority
092   vote of the classes of the kNNs.

      3.1   DATASETS , N ETWORKS AND S ETUP
194
195   Datasets used are CIFAR-10/100 (Krizhevsky & Hinton, 2009), scaled to 32x32, and Ima-
196   geNet (Deng et al., 2009). As networks we used VGG (Simonyan & Zisserman, 2014), Resnet (He
197   et al., 2016), MobileNetv3 (Howard et al., 2019) and ConvNext (Liu et al., 2022) networks. We used
198   pre-trained networks from the Pytorch’s torchvision library v0.15 based on ImageNet (Deng et al.,
199   2009) ‘IMAGENET1KV1’. We trained multiple models on CIFAR-10/100 on our own. Training
200   was standard, i.e., stochastic gradient descent with momentum 0.9, batchsize 128, weight decay of
201
      0.0005, no data augmentation and 80 epochs (starting from learning rate 0.11 and decaying it twice
      by 0.1). We trained five networks for each configuration, i.e. hyperparameter setting and report
202
      the mean and standard deviation of metrics. We employ two common targeted adversarial attacks
203
      using the Pytorch Advertorch library with default parameters and targets being set to “(index of
204   ground truth class +1) modulo numberOfClasses”. Specifically, we use the PGD attack (Kurakin
205   et al., 2016) and the Basic Iterative Attack(BIA) (Madry et al., 2018) which is an iterative extension
206   of FGSM (Goodfellow et al., 2014). If not stated differently, we use Algorithm 1 LaSeNN with
207   the stated parameters in the algorithm. For VGG-13, the third last convolutional layer as layer i by
208   default, while for ResNet-10 we use the output of the second last ‘BasicBlock’.

249   3.3      PARAMETER SENSITIVITY
250
      First, we assess the sensitivity to similarity metrics, i.e., we evaluate two common similarity met-
251
      rics for high dimensional vectors: the (negative) L2-norm sim(x, x′ ) = −||x − x′ ||22 and cosine
252
      similarity sim(s, s′ ) = cosine(x, x′ ). Our evaluation shows that both lead to gains when using
253   NNs but cosine leads to larger gains. This is expected since the space is relatively sparse (the Cifar
254   10/100 datasets are small with just 50k samples and the number of dimensions is large (at least 512
255   dimensions). In sparse spaces measures like cosine that neglect magnitude and are only concerned
256   with direction are more favorable. In turn, L2 is more adequate for dense spaces, i.e., large training
257   datasets – We used L2 for our benchmarks with Imagenet.
258
      The outcomes for different layers i are shown in Figure 4. Using layers further from the output
259   though not too close to the input tends to lead to best results. These layer outputs still maintain
260   a significant amount of information about the input, i.e., are not too much tuned to be discrimina-
261   tive, while still capturing task specific aspects – as can be seen when comparing reconstruction and
262   classification losses (Schneider & Prabhushankar, 2024). Given a network of sufficient capacity all
263   training samples will be perfectly classified after training, meaning they will have close to zero loss.
264   In turn, the output of the very last layer is similar for all samples of a class, e.g., samples cannot be
265   well discriminated using the final layer output.
266   In Table 5 we see that using NNs in addition to the sample to classify leads to gains from 0.1% up
267   to about 3%. Gains are largest if the weight w of the sample to predict is about 75% and that of the
268   neighbors jointly only 25% though there is no strong sensitivity of the weight w. Using only NNs

441
442   We analyze a simple scenario to illustrate the potential advantages of integrating neural network
443   predictions with a nearest neighbor approach. While both methods have been in use for decades,
444   theoretical analyses of their hybridization are notably scarce. We only outline key points here and
445   refer to the Appendix for details.
446
      In addition to assuming a one-dimensional data structure, we impose mild assumptions on the data,
447
      such as a gradual decrease in density as one moves away from a central region. Establishing results
448   for the general case poses significant challenges due to various interacting factors. These include
449   the non-linearity and high-dimensional parameter spaces inherent in neural networks, the challenge
450   of characterizing the geometry of their decision boundaries (Karimi et al., 2019), and the sensitivity
451   to the underlying data distribution (Section 5). Due to these complexities, we focus our analysis on
452   a critical region R situated near the decision boundary of the neural network, where classification
453   errors are most likely to occur. The following theorem formalizes our main result.

458   5   R ELATED WORK
459
460   Decision boundary: Studying the decision boundary of neural networks dates back multiple
461   decades (Lee & Landgrebe, 1997; Bishop, 2006). Nowadays, studying the decision boundary is
462   often motivated due to adversarial samples, which show that minor changes to a sample can result
463   in crossing the decision boundary, e.g., deep learning networks are non-robust. Commonly, de-
464   cision boundaries are also examined using measures and tools found in the context of adversarial
465   examples, e.g., Ortiz-Jimenez et al. (2020); Karimi et al. (2019); Szegedy et al. (2014). Szegedy
466
      et al. (2014) discusses adversarial examples in deep learning, illustrating the sensitivity of decision
      boundaries in neural networks to slight input perturbations. Karimi et al. (2019) generates samples
467
      near the decision boundary based on techniques from adversarial samples and in a subsequent step
468
      they analyze the generated instances. Nguyen et al. (2015) presents the existence of "fooling" im-
469   ages—unrecognizable inputs that deep neural networks classify with high confidence, highlighting
470   peculiarities in deep learning decision boundaries. Nguyen et al.’s findings shed light on the unusual
471   and unexpected shapes that decision boundaries in deep networks can take. We approach decision
472   boundaries more from the perspective that learnt representations are fixed and the task is to identify
473   an optimal boundary separating samples. Ortiz-Jimenez et al. (2020) leverages tools from adver-
474   sarial robustness to associate dataset features to the distance of samples to the decision boundary.
475   In turn, they tweak the position of the training samples and measure the resulting changes on the
476   boundaries. They show that deep learning networks exhibit a high invariance to non-discriminative
477
      features, and that the decision boundary of a neural network only exist “as long as the classifier is
      trained with some features that hold them together”(Ortiz-Jimenez et al., 2020).
478
      In addition, there are also a number of theoretical and empirical findings on decision boundaries
479
      for neural networks not relying on ideas from adversarial samples. Fawzi et al. (2017) investigates
480   topology of classification regions created by deep networks, as well as their associated decision
481   boundary. The paper claims based on empirical evidence that regions (containing samples of a
482   class) are connected and flat. Li et al. (2018) claims that the decision boundary of the last layer
483   equals that of a hard SVM. Lei et al. (2022) measures the variability of the decision boundary. They
484   show that the more variable the boundary, the less the network generalizes. Recently, Mouton et al.
485   (2023) has predicted generalization performance based on input margins. That is, they use the vari-
      ability computed based on PCA to assess generalization performance. Nar et al. (2019) argues that


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      cross-entropy loss leads to poor margins, since samples can be very close to the decision bound-
487   ary. Support vector machines lead to better margins. In fact, years earlier this has been claimed
488   empirically, i.e., Tang (2013) showed that using a margin-based loss instead of a cross-entropy loss
489   can lead to improvements. Yang et al. (2020) states that thick decision boundaries lead to increased
490   robustness. In the paper they propose training techniques to achieve this, but these techniques lead
491   to significantly worse performance on the clean test sets and only improve on adversarial and out-
492   of-distribution samples.
493   Noisy Labels: The impact of noise on decision boundaries cannot be understated. Noise in the train-
494   ing data can potentially lead to overfitting, manifesting as erratic decision boundaries (Zhang et al.,
495
      2021). Large neural networks can “memorize” arbitrary noisy training data (Zhang et al., 2021).
      However, noisy labels degenerate performance and research has investigated special techniques to
496
      deal with label noise. For example, Wu et al. (2020) constructs a topological filter to remove noisy
497
      samples. Their approach falls short, when data is non-noisy and it is only shown to yield benefits
498   if a large fraction of labels is noisy. Oyen et al. (2022) showed that label noise depends directly on
499   feature space, i.e.,“when the noise distribution targets decision boundaries, classification robustness
500   can drop off even at a small scale of noise.”
501   kNN: Early works (prior to deep learning) (Zhang et al., 2006) trained a SVM on NNs of a query
502   sample. Theoretical works, e.g., Cover (1968), studied also properties of neural networks. How-
503   ever, few theoretical and practical results are known relating deep learning and kNNs. Zhuang et al.
504   (2020) designed a network for training a neural enforcing that a sample and its kNNs all belong
505   to the same class based on a triplet loss. In contrast, we do not constrain training in any way, but
506
      rather compute NNs as they emerge by computing them based on the similarity of some layer activa-
      tion of a trained classifier. Furthermore, our objective is to improve classifiers rather than primarily
507
      enforcing that decisions are based (solely) on NNs. Khandelwal et al. (2019) used kNNs for next
508
      word prediction. They obtained the kNNs training contexts and computed a distribution of their
509   labels (i.e., words) based on the distance to the test context. Finally, they interpolate the obtained
510   distribution with that of the input sample. We differ in multiple ways: i) we mostly do not use out-
511   puts (e.g., outcomes of the softmax layer), but rely on deeper layers; ii) we do not use labels of the
512   training data. Borgeaud et al. (2022); Xu et al. (2023) enhanced LLMs by retrieving contexts based
513   on BERT embeddingsBorgeaud et al. (2022) and RAG Xu et al. (2023). Their work differ as they
514   require architectural changes and a separate embedding model.
515   Memory and attention: Our work also relates to works on including external memory in deep
516   learning (Graves et al., 2016) and to a lesser extent also attention, allowing to focus on specific (in-
517
      put) samples (Vaswani et al., 2017; Bahdanau et al., 2014). Our approach can be said to use training
      data as a read-only external memory in a static manner, in contrast to differentiable neural comput-
518
      ers (Graves et al., 2016) that allow read and write to memory and learn access. Attention allows to
519
      attend to inputs within a given sequence. Our approach attends to specific training data used already
520   for network training.
521   Explainability: Explaining using training data is common, e.g., influence functions (Koh & Liang,
522   2017) allow to explain the impact of training data on decisions. Naively, the influence of a training
523   sample is computed by removing the training sample from the training data retraining the classifier
524   on the reduced data, before assessing how the prediction for a specific sample changes. Our ap-
525   proach does not yield the influence but rather states that the output of a sample is determined by the
526   NNs (at least with fraction w). But it is then up to a human to compare the NNs and assess concepts
527   shared among them, e.g., using additional concept-based explainability methods such as TCAV and
528
      Schneider & Vlachos (2022).

533
534
535   While many approaches exist that target isolated problems such as interpretability, or robustness
536   against label noise, or adversarial robustness, or better performance in general, we achieve with
537   some extra computation“a little bit of most things” using a conceptual simple approach even on pre-
538   trained networks that highlights that “overfitting” is a concern for deep learning on large datasets in
539   areas of low data density. Thus, aside from empirical improvements our work also contributes to a
      deeper understanding of neural networks.

      of the proposed method that considers only a single nearest neighbor. Second, we assume class
672   balance and no distributional shift between the training and unseen test data, which are standard
673   assumptions in classification tasks. Third, we focus on two classes and assume that the density
674   of points decreases monotonously from a maximum when moving towards the other class, i.e., we
675   use a triangular distribution. Fourth, we assume that the neural network perfectly classifies the
676   training data, a condition that can be attained for sufficiently large networks (Goodfellow et al.,
677   2016). Finally, we assume that within the region R, the classification of a data point by the model is
678   entirely determined by the neural network’s classification of its nearest neighbor in the embedding
679   space. It can be realized or approximated in practice by selecting an appropriate weight w and
680
      considering that R is generally small and situated close to the neural network’s decision boundary.
      In such regions, predictions are typically similar and within a narrow range, which makes it easier
681
      to find a more stable and less extreme w that prioritizes the nearest neighbor’s prediction.

      7.3   A DDITIONAL CONSIDERATIONS
821
822   Note that the previous reasoning remains valid when considering any subset B ⊆ R. If we examine
823   an interval of the same width on the opposite side of the neighborhood around z ∗ , it is reason-
824   able to expect similar results, thereby suggesting that the proposed method is likely to maintain or
825   potentially enhance the classification performance of a pre-trained neural network across an entire
826   neighborhood of its decision boundary. This expectation arises from the fact that, on the right side
827   of z ∗ , blue points are likely to be classified as blue by the hybrid model, given that their nearest
828
      neighbors are generally also classified as blue in this region. Consequently, our hybrid model may
      assist in correcting the misclassification of red points within this region. A more formal analysis of
829
      this result will be addressed in future work.
