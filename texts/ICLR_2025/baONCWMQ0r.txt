011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Directly predicting labels from data inputs has been a long-standing supervised
014            learning paradigm. Its trade-off between compression and prediction is studied
015
               under the information theory framework e.g. Information Bottleneck, especially
               in the context of deep learning. It typically assumes that the information content
016
               of labels is significantly less than that of data inputs, leading to model designs
017
               that prioritize compressing and extracting features from data inputs. In fact, recent
018            supervised learning increasingly faces predicting complex labels, exacerbating the
019            challenge of learning mappings from compressed latent features to high-fidelity
020            label representations. Predictive bottlenecks emerge not only from compression
021            limitations but also from the inherent complexity of feature-to-label transforma-
022            tions. This paper proposes incorporating scheduled label information into the
023            model during training to better learn the prediction consistency mapping, which
024            stems from the consistency mapping concept from generative consistency models.
025            Unlike traditional approaches predicting labels directly from inputs, in this paper,
026
               the training of our designed conditional consistency involves predicting labels
               using inputs and noise-perturbed label hints, pursuing the predictive consistency
027
               across different noise steps. It simultaneously learns the relationship between latent
028
               features and a spectrum of label information from zero to complete, which enables
029            progressive learning for complex predictions and allows multi-step inference anal-
030            ogous to gradual denoising, thereby enhancing the prediction quality. Experiments
031            on vision, text, and graph tasks show the superiority of our consistency supervised
032            training paradigm, over conventional supervised training in complex label
033            prediction problems. Source code will be made publicly available upon acceptance.

036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Supervised learning has long been a cornerstone of machine learning, where models are trained to
039
      map input data to corresponding output labels by minimizing prediction error, which measures the
      discrepancy between the predicted labels and the ground truth labels. This direct label prediction
040
      paradigm has been widely applied across various domains, from image classification (Krizhevsky
041
      et al., 2012; He et al., 2016; Simonyan & Zisserman, 2014), natural language processing (Vaswani,
042   2017; Devlin, 2018; Radford, 2018), to structured graph learning (Kipf & Welling, 2016; Veličković
043   et al., 2017; Wu et al., 2022), due to its simplicity and effectiveness in handling large, annotated
044   datasets. In such systems, it is very typical to employ a neural network to directly map the data
045   inputs to labels, with a particular focus on the expressive capacity of (deep) models to compress
046   high-dimensional inputs into latent representations while preserving relevant information for accurate
047   predictions, viewed from an information theory perspective (Tishby et al., 2000; Tishby & Zaslavsky,
048   2015). This compression is believed to contribute to the generalization ability of deep learning
049   models, particularly in high-dimensional and noisy input scenarios.
050   This paradigm typically assumes that the labels contain a significantly lower dimensionality and less
051   information than the data inputs, thus guiding model designs toward compressing and extracting
052   relevant features from the input space for efficient prediction (Tishby & Zaslavsky, 2015). The
053   assumption further implies that transforming meaningful latent features to label outputs is relatively
      straightforward compared to the challenge of extracting expressive features. However, recent advances

066   in supervised learning have shown that many modern tasks involve much more complex labels,
067
      leading to new challenges. Examples include image prediction extending to dense, pixel-level
      outputs (Long et al., 2015; Chen et al., 2017), natural language processing tasks generating complex
068
      sentences (Brown, 2020; Touvron et al., 2023), and predicting complex structured solutions based
069
      on graph representations (Li et al., 2023; Satorras et al., 2021). These challenges reveal predictive
070   bottlenecks beyond feature extraction. To address this challenge, one approach involves learning an
071   efficient representation of the complex labels, facilitating a more effective transformation within the
072   low-dimensional feature space. Indeed, this can correspond to methods that leverage Variational Auto-
073   Encoder (VAE) (Kingma, 2013) to perform learning tasks within the latent space (Rombach et al.,
074   2022; Hottung et al., 2021). However, this approach necessitates that the transformation between
075   labels and latent features be reversible, requiring the training of two additional neural networks (an
076   encoder and a decoder) to accurately capture and reconstruct the label information.
077   In this paper, we propose an alternative approach, aiming to better capture complex label information
078   by introducing a fundamentally different supervised learning paradigm. We leverage the concept of
079   consistency mapping from generative consistency models Song et al. (2023) to frame the supervised
080   learning process as learning the prediction consistency, transitioning from noised labels to full
081   labels conditioned on the data input. Specifically, we introduce Supervised Consistency Learning
082   (SCL), which establishes trajectories from different noise levels on the target labels to the raw labels
083   conditioned on the given data inputs. This process can be interpreted as a conditional generation
084
      mechanism, where high-fidelity labels are inferred from noisy counterparts using the input data as
      a conditional guide, as shown in Fig. 1. Yet, within the supervised setup, each training instance has a
085
      reference target label, and the model learns to guide all denoising trajectories to this label by enforcing
086
      this predictive consistency across different noise timesteps, which we define as prediction consistency.
087
088   During training, unlike conventional supervised learning predicting labels directly from inputs, SCL
089   maps noisy labels at varying noise levels back to the true label conditioned on the input data and
090
      enforces different noise timesteps mapping to the same target. By enforcing predictive consistency
      across multiple noise levels, the model captures a rich spectrum of label information from entirely
091
      noisy to wholly accurate predictions, fostering a more expressive mapping between latent features
092
      and labels. During inference, the inherent multi-step denoising mechanism also facilitates progressive
093   refinement, resulting in more flexible and accurate predictions for complex labels. Intuitively, this
094   process can be seen as learning to predict with varying degrees of solution hints, which benefits
095   learning by progressively understanding the label information, especially when the labels are complex.
096
      We demonstrate the effectiveness of our approach across a range of tasks involving complex labels
097
      from diverse domains, including vision learning (e.g., semantic segmentation (Long et al., 2015;
098
      Chen et al., 2017)), graph learning (e.g., N-body simulation (Satorras et al., 2021) and combinatorial
099   optimization (Li et al., 2023)), and natural language processing (e.g., next-token prediction in large
100   language models (Brown, 2020; Touvron et al., 2023)). The empirical results highlight the superiority
101   of SCL over traditional supervised learning across various mainstream network backbones.

103   2    R ELATED W ORK AND P RELIMINARIES
104
105   Supervised Learning and Information Interpretation. In supervised learning (SL), the models
106   typically learn the direct mapping from the data inputs to labels by minimizing a loss function that
107   captures the discrepancy between predicted and ground truth labels. In particular, the Information
      Bottleneck (IB) principle (Tishby et al., 2000; Tishby & Zaslavsky, 2015) offers a theoretical


                                                                   2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      framework to analyze (deep) learning systems, indicating that models balance the trade-off between
109   compression and prediction accuracy. The IB method (Tishby et al., 2000) aims to find a compressed
110   representation of the input that retains relevant information about the target while discarding irrelevant
111   details. The IB method formulates this trade-off by minimizing the mutual information between
112   the input and a compressed representation, while maximizing the mutual information between the
113   compression and the target. By considering the relationship between the input and the label through
114   the lens of information theory, IB provides a powerful tool for understanding model generalization
115   and optimizing feature representations in supervised learning.
116   Diffusion Models and Consistency Models. Diffusion models are characterized by a forward pro-
117   cess of noise injection and a reverse process of learnable denoising, where neural networks iteratively
118   predict data distributions conditioned on increasingly noisy inputs. In continuous space, these models
119   are closely linked to Stochastic Differential Equations (SDEs), with techniques such as the Probability
120   Flow ODE offering a deterministic approximation to sample generation (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015;
121   Song & Ermon, 2019; Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020a; Song & Ermon, 2020; Nichol & Dhariwal,
122   2021; Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021). Extensions to discrete data have also been explored, with noise
123
      distributions modeled as binomial or categorical variables (Sohl-Dickstein et al., 2015; Austin et al.,
      2021; Hoogeboom et al., 2021). Building on the advancements of diffusion models, consistency mod-
124
      els (Song et al., 2023; Song & Dhariwal, 2023) have introduced an alternative paradigm to accelerate
125
      the generation process. Rather than iteratively refining noisy samples through a reverse diffusion pro-
126   cess, consistency models leverage a self-consistency mechanism across different time steps, directly
127   learning the mappings from noise to data in a single step or a small number of steps. This approach
128   has shown promise in reducing computational overhead while maintaining high sample quality.
129
130   3    S UPERVISED L EARNING TASKS WITH C OMPLEX L ABELS
131
132   Supervised learning aims to train the model to extract compressed features or representations of
133   input data x ∈ X while retaining the most relevant information about the target label y ∈ Y (Tishby
134   et al., 2000; Tishby & Zaslavsky, 2015). Note this is based on the assumption that the data provides
135   sufficient information about the labels, which means the data is abundant. From an information-
136
      theoretic perspective, the mutual information I(X, Y ) quantifies how much information X provides
      about Y . Typically, X is a high dimensional variable of a low-level representation of the data, such
137
      as pixels of an image, whereas Y has a significantly lower dimensionality of the predicted categories,
138
      which generally means that most of the entropy of X is not very informative about Y and that the
139   relevant features in X are highly distributed and difficult to extract (Tishby & Zaslavsky, 2015). In
140   deep learning (LeCun et al., 2015), deep neural networks create a compressed representation XE of
141   X through an encoder, which discards irrelevant information while preserving as much of the mutual
142   information I(XE , Y ) as possible. The compression is optimized by minimizing I(X, XE ), the
143   information between X and its compressed form XE , while maximizing I(XE , Y ), the information
144   between the compressed representation and the target. This trade-off can be formalized in the IB
145   objective: minp(XE |X) [I(X, XE ) − βI(XE , Y )], where β is a Lagrange multiplier that governs the
146   balance. In real-world scenarios, compression is often lossy, meaning that some information about the
147
      input signal X is inevitably discarded. Consequently, the challenge becomes ensuring that the model
      retains only the information about Y that is necessary for the task while minimizing redundancy.
148
149   This formulation typically assumes that Y is a low-dimensional vector (e.g., class labels) where the
150   information content is relatively limited. However, many real-world tasks, especially in structured
151   prediction (e.g., image segmentation, sequence generation), involve predicting high-dimensional
152   outputs. In these tasks, the mutual information I(XE , Y ) can be difficult to maximize because the
153
      high-dimensional labels themselves contain redundancies, and fitting a model to predict them from
      XE becomes a non-trivial task. Moreover, the space of possible outputs Y could involve complex cor-
154
      relations that are hard to capture directly. Below, we formalize such tasks with a qualitative definition.
155
      Definition 3.1. A learning task with complex labels is characterized by a label space that exhibits high
156
      complexity due to one or more of the following characteristics: (i) high dimensionality, (ii) intricate
157
      internal structure, or (iii) the presence of significant dependency patterns among label components.
158
159   In contrast to traditional tasks with simple scalar or categorical labels, complex labels encode rich,
160   multi-dimensional, or structured information. Consequently, these tasks require models to capture
161   sophisticated relationships and dependencies within the label space, transcending straightforward
      mappings from input features. The inherent complexity of the label space suggests the need for


                                                          3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      learning an effective latent representation YE of the target Y . This concept aligns with existing
163   approaches (Rombach et al., 2022; Hottung et al., 2021)that handle high-dimensional outputs in latent
164   spaces leveraging methods like Variational Autoencoders (VAE) (Kingma & Welling, 2014). However,
165   for prediction purposes, these methods rely on the invertibility of the mapping from Y to its latent
166   representation YE , requiring both an encoder to compress Y and a decoder to reconstruct YE back to
167   Y for prediction. This necessitates learning additional networks to manage latent representations. In
168   the following section, we present an alternative approach that enhances the model’s ability to capture
169   I(XE , Y ) directly by leveraging the mechanism of the learning paradigm itself, thereby avoiding the
170   need for introducing additional networks and its associated computational overhead.

182   Diffusion on Categorical Labels. For multi-dimensional categorical labels in {1, · · · , K}N where

186

187

188

227   of the label information initially and incrementally build towards mastering the full complexity of Y .

257
      estimate the consistency function from data by learning to enforce prediction consistency. To achieve
258   such consistency to learn f : x 7→ y, given that the target y is certain and explicit, we do not have to
259   rely on optimizing the expectation of the variation of the consistency mappings over two noise points

261   introduce y to optimize the triadic distance to achieve prediction consistency:

296   4.3   M ULTISTEP I NFERENCE WITH C ONSISTENCY M APPINGS
297
      With a well-trained fθ (·, ·, ·), we obtain predictions for a given x by sampling yT from the uniform
298
      distribution and then evaluate the prediction for y0 = fθ (x, yT , T ). This standard single-step
299   inference requires only one forward pass through the model, offering a fast yet approximate solution
300   akin to conventional supervised learning methods. On the other hand, accuracy tends to be higher
301   when t is small, as the label hints contain a richer amount of information. Our objective is to
302   progressively transfer this high accuracy to larger values of t through training, thereby enhancing
303   overall model performance. In the ideal case that the consistency loss converges to zero, optimal
304   results can be achieved in a single step, yet in practice, gradually decreasing t from T to 0 can lead to
305   accuracy improvements. To achieve such enhancements, a multistep inference strategy can be adopted,
306   which iteratively alternates between denoising and reintroducing noise. This approach effectively
307
      trades off runtime for enhanced prediction quality, allowing the model to refine its outputs over
      multiple inference steps and leverage increasingly rich information embedded in earlier predictions.
308
309   Given a sequence of time points τ1 > τ2 > · · · > τNτ −1 , at each step τn , the current prediction
310   yτn−1 is perturbed by a noise function to a noisier state yτn . The noise level decreases with each
311   step, meaning τn < τn−1 . The model then denoises the corrupted label by applying fθ (x, yτn , τn ),
312   producing a refined prediction. This process is repeated over successive steps, where each newly
313
      refined label incorporates progressively more accurate information from the previous step. This
      enables the model to gradually recover the whole information of y by taking the perhaps approximated
314
      prediction as the label hints and leveraging the incrementally informative hints for the final prediction.
315

      5.1   S EMANTIC S EGMENTATION
350
351   Semantic segmentation is a classic dense vision task with wide applications that involves classifying
352   each pixel of an image into a predefined category (Long et al., 2015; Zhao et al., 2017). Unlike
353   classification tasks that categorize entire images, semantic segmentation analyzes the finer granularity
354   of images to identify the boundaries and relationships between objects.
355   Dataset. We utilize the ADE20K dataset (Zhou et al., 2019), which is a commonly used large-scale
356   scene parsing dataset that contains over 20,000 images with pixel-level annotations. The dataset is
357   annotated with 150 different object classes, and we make the unannotated pixels into a new category,
358   denoted as -1, which are ignored during both training and testing. Following previous works (Zhou
359   et al., 2017; 2019), we resize the origial images during training while keeping the aspect ratio constant,
360   randomly scaling the shorter side to one of the sizes 300, 375, 450, 525, or 600.
361   Metrics. Following (Zhou et al., 2017; 2019), we adopt three evaluation metrics to measure model
362   performance: 1) Pixel Accuracy: the proportion of correctly classified pixels. 2) Mean IoU (mIoU):
363   the intersection-over-union between the predicted and ground-truth pixels, averaged over all the
364   classes. 3) Score: the average value of Pixel Accuracy and Mean IoU. During the testing phase, we
365   use Multi-Scale Test: evaluate at multiple sizes and then take the average.
366   Model Design. We generally adopt an encoder-decoder network framework. The encoder compresses
367   the input by extracting high-level features using a CNN backbone, reducing the spatial resolution while
368   capturing important semantic information. The decoder then progressively upsamples the compressed
369   features to recover the original resolution, often using skip connections to retain fine details. To
370   introduce SCL, we concatenate the image features obtained through the encoder, the timestep
371   embeddings extracted through sinusoidal position embedding, and the noised labels processed by the
372   embedding layer. Then, we feed the tensor encompassing the input, timestep, and noised label into
373   the decoder for further processing. In this task, y ∈ {−1, 0, 1, ...149}H×W where each entry will be
374
      converted into a one-hot vector of length 151, indicating the classification of the pixels. We adopt the
      categorical noising process as presented in Eq. 2 using transition matrices of Qt ∈ [0, 1]151×151 .

      The next-token prediction task is a cornerstone in natural language processing, forming the foundation
494
      for transformer-based large language models (LLMs) such as GPT (Radford, 2018), LLaMA (Touvron
495   et al., 2023). The objective of the task is to predict the next token in a sequence, given the preceding
496   context. The task’s outputs retain similar dimensionality and sequence complexity as the inputs.
497   Large language models trained on next-token prediction tasks have proven to be highly effective
498   at capturing both short-term and long-range dependencies in language, enabling them to generate
499   coherent, contextually appropriate text. This section investigates the effectiveness of SCL in the full
500   fine-tuning task on the pre-trained LLaMa-2-7B (Touvron et al., 2023) models.
501   Dataset. The Alpaca (Taori et al., 2023) dataset is based on the self-instruct method (Wang et al.,
502   2022b), utilizing the OpenAI text-davinci-003 engine to generate a collection of instructions and
503   demonstrations. These instruction data can be employed for fine-tuning language models. By filtering
504   out low-quality data, such as hallucinations, incorrect answers, and unclear instructions, we obtain the
505   Alpaca-cleaned dataset, which serves as the sole training data for all models discussed in this paper.
506
      Metrics. To evaluate the performance of models, we em- Table 6: Evaluation on LLM fine-tuning.

530   6     C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
531
      This paper has proposed a novel supervised consistency learning framework beyond the classic
532
      supervised learning paradigm, which has shown superiority in extensive experiments on various
533
      tasks with complex labels. Our approach resembles, to a certain degree, the residual learning
534   scheme by treating the noisy label as the input, which is counterpart to the raw signal of the input
535   data. By leveraging label hints perturbed by noise and progressively refining predictions through
536   multiple inference steps, our method improves predictive performance in challenging scenarios
537   where traditional direct-label prediction methods may struggle. Empirical results across vision,
538   text, and graph modalities demonstrate the superiority of the proposed paradigm. Future work will
539   explore the potential of applying the data diffusion process to other deep learning paradigms, such as
      semi-supervised and unsupervised learning.

      In our approach, we adopt the LLaMA2-7B model as the backbone, preserving the structure of the
      embedding and decoder layers. However, we modify the prediction mechanism for the next token
895
      using the hidden states. In a conventional decoder-only language model (LLM), the prediction of the
896
      next token y is achieved by leveraging the preceding context, encoded in a high-dimensional hidden
897   state h. This hidden state h is then passed through a linear layer, typically referred to as the language
898   modeling head (lm head) or unembedding layer, to yield the probability distribution P (y) over the
899   next token.
900
      In contrast, our model introduces a noise injection mechanism to perturb the token embeddings,
901
      aiming to enhance robustness and generalization.
902

904   token, instead of directly passing them through the lm head to generate logits and compute the cross-
905   entropy loss with the ground truth labels ŷ, we transform these labels back into its corresponding
906
      embedding yemb . Then add Gaussian noise ϵ ∼ N (0, σ 2 ) to yemb , resulting in a perturbed embedding:

      A.5.2    E VALUATION M ETRICS
944   The evaluation of large language model performance in this paper includes benchmarks:
945
946   • MMLU (Hendrycks et al., 2020): This benchmark assesses models’ world knowledge and rea-
947     soning abilities across 57 academic disciplines, including STEM, humanities, and social sciences.
948     Questions range in difficulty from elementary to advanced professional levels, presented in a
949     multiple-choice format. The evaluation primarily uses few-shot settings to test the models’ gen-
950     eralization capabilities. In our experiments, a 5-shot direct prompting is utilized to evaluate the
951
        model’s comprehensive performance across various dimensions.
952   • BBH (Srivastava et al., 2022): It is a subset of 23 challenging tasks from the BIG-Bench benchmark.
953     It evaluates the ability of models to handle challenging reasoning and problem-solving tasks that go
954     beyond simple language understanding. It includes complex scenarios such as navigation, logical
955
        deduction, and fallacy detection. For this work, we apply 0-shot direct prompting to measure the
        model’s capability in dealing with unseen questions without additional contextual examples.
956
957   • CRASS (Frohberg & Binder, 2022): This benchmark is designed to evaluate the model’s ability
958     to handle complex relational reasoning tasks, specifically in the context of causal structures and
959
        relationships. It includes a variety of problems that test how well the model understands and
        predicts causal relationships between different entities or events. For this evaluation, we use 3-shot
960
        direct prompting to assess model reasoning.
