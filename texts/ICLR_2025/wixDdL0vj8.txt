                                                        A BSTRACT
008
009
                   Training efficiency plays a pivotal role in deep learning. This paper begins by
010
                   analyzing current methods for enhancing efficiency, highlighting the necessity of
011                optimizing targets, a process we define as data optimization. Subsequently, we
012                reveal that current data optimization methods incur significant additional costs,
013                e.g., human resources or computational overhead, due to their inherently sequential
014                optimization process. To address these issues, we propose C O O PT, a highly
015                efficient, parallelized framework designed for collaborative data optimization.
016                C O O PT enables participants to independently optimize data subsets, ensuring that
017                the overall performance, once these subsets are collected, remains comparable
018                to the sequential optimization of the entire dataset, thus significantly reducing
019
                   optimization costs for individual participants. Extensive experiments have been
                   conducted on various real-world scenarios to demonstrate the effectiveness and
020
                   efficiency of C O O PT across various datasets and architectures 1 .

066   Self-supervised learning (Chen et al., 2020b) is proposed to exploit intrinsic relationships within
067   large volumes of unlabeled data to learn meaningful representations. While it reduces dependency on
068   labels, training on such extensive datasets demands considerable computational resources, posing
069   significant computational challenges and diminishing training efficiency (Sun et al., 2024a).
070
      To efficiently leverage unlabeled data, a straightforward but labor-intensive method is through human
071
      annotation, thereby transforming them into labeled data. To further enhance training efficiency,
072   methods such as knowledge distillation (Hinton, 2015) and dataset distillation (Wang et al., 2018)
073   have been proposed. Knowledge distillation innovatively leverages soft labels provided by a powerful
074   teacher model to improve the performance of a student model and expedite its training (Dong et al.,
075   2023). Dataset distillation focuses on compressing the original dataset into a smaller subset, enabling
076   a model trained on this distilled data to perform comparably to one trained on the full dataset, thus
077   significantly reducing computational costs.
078   The data utilization methods discussed above are summarized in Table 1 . Essentially, compared
079   to the less efficient self-supervised learning methods, methods achieve higher efficiency by either
080   optimizing targets DY through human annotation or pre-trained models (as in knowledge distillation),
081   or by optimizing both input data DX and targets DY (as in dataset distillation). Notably, all these
082   methods necessarily optimize targets DY , and we term this process as data optimization.
083
      In this paper, we identify the inefficiency of conventional data optimization methods, which suffer
084                                                                                                    2
      from a sequential optimization process with a time complexity ranging from O(|DX |) to O(|DX | ),
085
      as elaborated in Section 3.2 . As a remedy, we propose C O O PT, a highly efficient collaborative
086
      framework inspired by crowd-sourcing to achieve parallel data optimization. An overview of C O O PT
087   is depicted in Figure 1 , with detailed processes illustrated in Figure 2 .
088
089   In summary, our contributions are threefold:
090   (a) We propose C O O PT, a highly efficient and parallelized framework for collaborative data
091       optimization. This framework enables participants to independently optimize data subsets,
092       ensuring that when these subsets are collected, the overall performance is comparable
093       to sequential optimization of the entire dataset. Therefore, C O O PT significantly reduces
094       optimization costs for each participant.
095   (b) Within C O O PT, we identify a critical issue: Target Distribution Inconsistency, as defined in
096        Section 3.4 . This issue arises from the diverse prior models employed by participants, leading to
097
          heterogeneity in the target distribution spaces. To address this challenge, we propose an effective
          target alignment strategy, elaborated in Section 3.5 .
098
      (c) Extensive experiments have been conducted across a range of real-world scenarios, involving a va-
099
          riety of prior models trained on various datasets, architectures, and training paradigms. Notably,
100       special cases are explored where human or significantly weak models are employed as prior mod-
101       els, verifying the robustness and flexibility of C O O PT. These experiments consistently demon-
102       strate that C O O PT achieves superior effectiveness and efficiency across a range of scenarios.

      2.1   L OW-E FFICIENCY S ELF - SUPERVISED L EARNING FOR U NLABELED DATA
109
110   To eliminate the need for human annotation, self-supervised learning (Chen et al., 2020b) is proposed
111   to exploit the intrinsic co-occurrence relationships within large volumes of unlabeled data to learn
112   meaningful representations.
113
      Instance-instance contrastive learning has demonstrated effectiveness across various visual classi-
114   fication tasks. For example, InstDisc (Wu et al., 2018) introduces the concept of using instance
115   discrimination as a pretext task. Building on this, CMC (Tian et al., 2020) proposes to use multiple
116   views of an image as positive samples and take another one as the negative. MoCo (He et al., 2020)
117   significantly increases the number of negative samples but utilizes a relatively simplistic strategy for
118   selecting positive samples. Subsequent methods, such as PIRL (Misra & Maaten, 2020), incorporated
119   jigsaw augmentations, and SimCLR (Chen et al., 2020a) highlights the importance of hard positive
120   sample strategies by introducing data augmentation. A notable advancement is BYOL (Grill et al.,
121   2020), which discards negative sampling and surpasses the performance of SimCLR (Chen et al.,
122
      2020a). SimSiam (Chen & He, 2021) further investigates the necessity of negative sampling in
      contrastive representation learning, achieving faster convergence.
123
124   Summary. These methods, although not reliant on human annotation, often come with high
125   computational costs due to the need for large batch sizes or memory banks.
126
127   2.2   H IGH -E FFICIENCY DATA O PTIMIZATION M ETHODS FOR L ABELED DATA
128
129
      Knowledge distillation. Knowledge distillation (Hinton, 2015) innovatively employs soft labels
      generated by high-capacity teacher models to improve the performance of a student model. Many
130
      following works aim to enhance the use of soft labels for more effective knowledge transfer. WSLD
131
      (Zhou et al., 2021) analyzes soft labels and distributes different weights for them from a perspective
132   of bias-variance trade-off. DKD (Zhao et al., 2022) decouples the logits and assigns different weights
133   for the target and non-target classes. Moreover, several studies (Yim et al., 2017; Dong et al., 2023)
134   have demonstrated that knowledge distillation can accelerate the optimization process during training.
135
136   Dataset dstillation. Dataset distillation (Wang et al., 2018) aims to learn a compact distilled dataset
137
      that preserves the essential information in the large-scale original dataset, achieving comparable
      performance to the original dataset with less training time. Current solutions can be categorized
138
      based on their optimization mechanisms (Lei & Tao, 2023): meta-learning framework (Wang et al.,
139
      2018; Zhou et al., 2022), gradient matching (Zhao et al., 2020; Zhao & Bilen, 2021), distribution
140   matching (Zhao & Bilen, 2023; Yin et al., 2023), trajectory matching (Cazenavette et al., 2022; Guo
141   et al., 2024). Notably, RDED (Sun et al., 2024b) introduces an optimization-free paradigm, which
142   directly crops and selects realistic patches from the original data and then stitches the selected patches
143   into the new images as the distilled dataset.
144
      Summary. Knowledge distillation enhances model training efficiency by optimizing targets DY ,
145
      while dataset distillation optimizes both target DY and input data DX . Despite their benefits, both
146   approaches are computationally expensive as they require task-specific pre-trained models, which
147   significantly reduces overall efficiency.

159   As illustrated in Table 1 , we provide a comprehensive comparison of various unlabeled data utiliza-

161   input data (indicated by ✗ under ‘Optimize DX ’ and ‘Optimize DY ’), is effective but often suffers
      from low efficiency due to the extensive computational resources required.

      In contrast, methods that achieve higher efficiency typically involve optimizing data through different
163   strategies. For example, Human Annotation (HA) provides labeled data (indicated by ✓ under
164   ‘Optimize DY ’) and achieves high effectiveness and efficiency. However, this approach incurs
165   substantial costs in terms of human resources and time, making it impractical for large-scale datasets
166   or applications requiring rapid deployment. Knowledge distillation (KD) supplies soft labels by
167   teacher models, resulting in high effectiveness and efficiency, yet requires additional computational
168   resources to train the teacher models. Thus, the data optimization process incurs a minimum cost of c
169   when only training a teacher model with standard supervised learning, where the cost is c.
170   Dataset Distillation (DD) further extends data optimization by simultaneously optimizing the input
171   samples DX and the targets DY . While DD can significantly improve efficiency during the training
172   of new models due to the reduced dataset size, most DD methods rely on complex optimization
173   procedures, such as trajectory matching (Guo et al., 2024), which require models trained on the
174   original datasets to guide the distillation process. This reliance can offset the efficiency gains by
175   introducing additional computational overhead.

      3.3   OVERVIEW OF THE P ROPOSED F RAMEWORK C O O PT
235
236   C O O PT is a collaborative and parallelized framework that comprises an open data platform and K
237   participants, each equipped with a distinct prior model. Specifically, C O O PT operates through the
238   following four steps:
239
      Step 1 : Data distributing. The open data platform initiates the process by randomly partitioning
240   the entire set of unlabeled data D into K non-overlapping subsets. Each participant then downloads
241   one of these subsets from the platform, denoted as D(k) , where k indicates the k-th participant.
242                                                                                                        |D (k) |
243
      Step 2 : Data optimization. Participants optimize their respective datasets D(k) = {xi }i=1
244   using their local prior model ψ k . This data optimization process, detailed in Section 3.2 , yields
                              (k)            |D (k) |
245   optimized targets D′ = {xi , yi }i=1 . However, due to the heterogeneity of prior models among
246   participants, the optimized targets of all participants may exhibit significant variations, leading to
247   divergence in the distribution of the targets. This issue, referred to as target distribution inconsistency
248   (defined in Section 3.4 ), necessitates an alignment strategy to align the target distribution spaces

      Step 4 : Data merging. The platform aggregates all the optimized datasets received from the

256   the overall results are comparable to that achieved by optimizing the entire dataset as a whole.

      Consequently, this approach markedly reduces individual data optimization costs and enhances data

      3.5   A N E FFECTIVE S TRATEGY: TARGET A LIGNMENT
280
281   In the previous section, we identified that the primary challenge arises from the heterogeneity of
282   optimized target distributions across participants. To address this issue, a potential solution is to align
283   the target distributions of all participants’ prior models with that of the prior model producing the most
284   optimal target distribution space, referred to as the best prior model. Such alignment can be achieved
285   by utilizing an optimizable transformation matrix to map each participant’s target distribution to that
286   of the best prior model (Sun et al., 2024a). This alignment strategy ensures consistency across all
287
      optimized target distribution spaces.
288   In summary, it is crucial to first effectively assess each participant’s prior model quality and subse-
289   quently train the transformation matrix for alignment.
290   A metric to quantify prior model quality. Drawing inspirations from Wang & Isola (2020),
291   which proposes an optimizable metric a.k.a. uniform value loss to achieve feature uniformity on
292   the hypersphere during training, we employ this metric to evaluate the quality of prior models.
293   Specifically, each participant downloads a small shared dataset SX from the platform and computes
294   the uniformity value of their prior model on SX . They then upload this value to the platform, enabling
295   it to determine which participant possesses the best prior model. The uniform value is computed as:

525   5    C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
526
527   Conclusion. In this paper, we introduce C O O PT, an efficient and highly parallelized framework
528
      for collaborative data optimization. This framework enables participants to independently optimize
      data subsets such that, when aggregated, the overall performance is comparable to that achieved by
529
      sequentially optimizing the entire dataset. Consequently, C O O PT significantly reduces individual data
530
      optimization costs. Within C O O PT, we identify a critical issue: Target Distribution Inconsistency,
531   which arises from the diversity of prior models used in data optimization. To mitigate this, we propose
532   an effective target alignment strategy. Extensive experiments conducted across various real-world
533   scenarios demonstrate the superior effectiveness and efficiency of the C O O PT framework across
534   diverse datasets and architectures.
535
536   Future Work. In future work, we intend to (1) conduct an in-depth exploration of iterative data
537   optimization through multiple rounds, enabling more dynamic collaboration among participants.
538   (2) Furthermore, we aim to examine additional practical scenarios, such as participants optimizing
539   their local data and then uploading it to the platform. This paradigm is anticipated to broaden the
      platform’s functionalities, thereby increasing its applicability to real-world applications.
