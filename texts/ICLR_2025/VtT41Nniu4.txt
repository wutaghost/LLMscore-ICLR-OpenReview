025                                               A BSTRACT
026
027            Low-light image enhancement (LLIE) aims to improve the visual quality of im-
028            ages captured under poor lighting conditions. In supervised LLIE tasks, there
029            exists a significant yet often overlooked inconsistency between the overall bright-
030            ness of an enhanced image and its ground truth counterpart, referred to as bright-
031            ness mismatch in this study. Brightness mismatch negatively impact supervised
032            LLIE models by misleading model training. However, this issue is largely ne-
033            glected in current research. In this context, we propose the GT-mean loss, a sim-
034            ple yet effective loss function directly modeling the mean values of images from
035
               a probabilistic perspective. The GT-mean loss is flexible, as it extends existing
               supervised LLIE loss functions into the GT-mean form with minimal additional
036
               computational costs. Extensive experiments demonstrate that the incorporation of
037
               the GT-mean loss results in consistent performance improvements across various
038            methods and datasets.

041   1   INTRODUCTION
042
043   Low-light image enhancement (LLIE) is a crucial task in computer vision, aiming to improve the
044   overall quality of images captured under poor lighting conditions (Li et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2021a).
045   The primary objective of training a supervised LLIE model, denoted as f (·), is to map a low-light
046   image x to an enhanced image f (x), subjecting to the constraint that f (x) should resemble the
047   ground truth (GT) image y as much as possible. Under this paradigm, a well-trained LLIE model
048   is expected to improve brightness while suppressing other degeneration factors commonly existed
049   in low-light images, such as noise (Lu & Jung, 2022; Wei et al., 2020; Moseley et al., 2021), color
050
      distortion (Yan et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2022), and others (Zhou et al., 2022; 2021).
051   In supervised LLIE tasks, the inconsistency between f (x) and y in terms of the overall brightness
052   widely exists. In this paper, we refer to this widespread yet overlooked phenomenon as brightness
053   mismatch. It can be simply represented as the inequality between the average brightness of f (x) and
      y, i.e., E[f (x)] ̸= E[y]. We find that this phenomenon can lead to biases in computing loss values

111   The issue of model training under brightness mismatch has largely been ignored in existing super-
112
      vised LLIE research, despite some indirect solutions that do not primarily address this problem.
      For example, (Chen et al., 2018; Yang et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2022; Ma et al., 2023) designed
113
      multiple sub-networks to decouple brightness from other factors and optimized them separately.
114
      Nevertheless, the divide-and-conquer roadmap inevitably complicates the model design, as well as
115   introducing significant computational overhead.
116
117   Inspired by the GT-mean metrics, we propose a simple yet effective loss function, called GT-mean
118
      loss, through explicitly modeling brightness mismatch in its construction. The loss dynamically bal-
      ances its focus during training. For example, when f (x) and y are close, the loss function becomes
119
      unaware of brightness mismatch, and drives the model to focus more on optimizing various imaging
120
      factors except overall brightness. Therefore, the loss is able to eliminate the negative impact caused
121   by brightness mismatch during training, facilitating LLIE models to comprehensively improve vi-
122   sual quality in a more effective way. The use of this loss function is straightforward, as it directly
123   extends any existed loss function that requires the enhanced image f (x) and its GT counterpart as
124   inputs. The GT-mean loss is highlighted in the following aspects:
125
126          • Simplicity: The construction of the GT-mean loss is both theoretically and practically
127            straightforward. Its underlying mechanism is easy to understand, and its implementation is
128
               uncomplicated.
129          • Flexibility: The GT-mean loss is highly flexible. For instance, the L1 loss can be directly
130            extended into the L1 GT-mean version. This character makes adopting the GT-mean loss a
131            universal choice for supervised LLIE models to upgrade their loss functions.
132          • Low Cost: Using the GT-mean loss introduces minimal overhead during training (approx-
133            imately doubling the original loss computation). It is negligible compared to the overall
134            model optimization process.
135          • Effectiveness: Extensive experiments have demonstrated that the GT-mean loss consis-
136            tently improves model performance across a wide range of supervised LLIE methods (as
137            shown in Figure 1).

139   2     BACKGROUND
140
141   2.1   S UPERVISED LLIE F RAMEWORK
142
143   In a supervised low-light image enhancement (LLIE) framework, the objective is to learn a mapping
144   function f that transforms a low-light image x into an enhanced image f (x) that closely approxi-
145
      mates the ground truth y. This is typically achieved by minimizing a loss function that penalizes the
      differences between f (x) and y, driving the model to produce outputs that have good visual quality
146
      as in ground truth images. Various loss functions can be applied for this task. Commonly used loss
147
      functions, such as the L1 loss, are primarily adopted to minimize pixel-wise differences between the
148   input image and the ground truth, ensuring the model generates accurate reconstructions.
149
150
      2.2   L OSS F UNCTIONS FOR LLIE
151
152   Loss function is essential in LLIE tasks, as it directs model training. We categorize the loss functions
153   in LLIE into two groups based on their purposes.
154
      Fidelity Losses. These losses are designed to ensure that the enhanced image f (x) closely resembles
155
      the ground truth y. They operate across various image representations, including pixel space, color
156   space, frequency domain, and semantic space. For instance, the L1 -like loss functions directly
157   ensure the pixel-level fidelity (Li et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2021a). Loss functions focused on color
158   representation often use color histogram-based metrics (Yan et al., 2024), while others preserve
159   fidelity at the frequency domain (Wang et al., 2023a; Huang et al., 2022). Additionally, some loss
160   functions aim to maintain fidelity at higher representation levels, such as the perceptual loss (Johnson
161   et al., 2016). Recently, some novel loss functions have emerged that subtly utilize fine-grained
      semantic information (Liang et al., 2023; Wu et al., 2023). Among these, pixel-level loss functions


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      are indispensable for image reconstruction. However, their effectiveness may be compromised by
163   brightness mismatch.
164
165   Prior-Based Losses. These losses integrate domain-specific prior knowledge into LLIE models,
166
      aiming to maximize the use of available information. Typical examples are the Retinex-based meth-
      ods(Zhang et al., 2019; Wei et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2018; Yang et al., 2021; Wu et al., 2022; Ma
167
      et al., 2023; Fu et al., 2023a), which are founded on the Retinex theory that an image is composed
168
      of illumination map and reflectance map. Specific loss functions are employed to penalize the lo-
169   cally smooth properties of illumination map and the lightness-insensitive properties of reflectance
170   map. These loss functions are closely linked to specific model architectures and their underlying
171   assumptions, limiting their generalizability. Furthermore, for unsupervised LLIE models that lack
172   GT images for training, prior-based loss functions are essential for guiding model optimization.
173   However, these loss functions can be less robust. For example, ZeroDCE (Li et al., 2021) builds an
174   exposure control loss function with a hard threshold, which may result in over-exposure.
175   To pursue comprehensive visual quality enhancement, LLIE models tend to incorporate multiple loss
176   functions. While this strategy can lead to improved results, it also increases the burden of model
177   design and hyperparameter tuning. More importantly, as the existing loss functions overlook the
178   brightness mismatch factor, the fundamental challenges brought by this factor remain unaddressed.
179
      Our GT-mean loss is designed to directly address the issues caused by brightness mismatch. We
180
      argue that the primary goal of LLIE is to improve visibility while simultaneously suppressing other
181   degenerated factors. Therefore, loss functions specifically designed for supervised LLIE should be
182   sensitive to brightness mismatch and as concise as possible. To this end, we introduce brightness
183   mismatch into the construction of GT-mean loss, and design a mechanism that dynamically balances
184   the importance of optimizing for brightness and other image quality factors during training.

245   3.2.1    P ROBABILISTIC M ODELING ON AVERAGE B RIGHTNESS
246
      Instead of modeling the average brightness E[·] as a fixed value, we represent it as a random variable,
247   motivated by two key considerations. First, probabilistic modeling aligns well with the characteris-
248   tics of human brightness perception. According to the Contrast Sensitivity Function (Robson, 1966;
249   Bühren, 2018), human vision is highly sensitive to local contrasts, such as edges, textures, and inten-
250   sity changes, but much less sensitive to a minor shift of E[·]. As long as local contrast remains intact,
251   such minor shifts are unlikely to affect the human perception on visual quality, especially when E[·]
252   is relatively high. Second, probabilistic modeling enhances the control of our loss function during
253   training. It allows us to estimate W using common metrics like the Kullback-Leibler divergence or
254   the Wasserstein distance. This facilitates a smooth weighting between the two terms in Eq. 1 with-
255
      out causing abrupt changes. Consequently, the loss exhibits good continuity as parameters change
      and has flat regions around its minima.

      3.3     D ISCUSSION OF THE GT- MEAN LOSS
294
295   In application, flexibility is the primary advantage of the GT-mean loss, as it is independent of the
296   model architecture and only requires the enhanced image f (x) and the ground truth y as inputs.
297   Therefore, the GT-mean loss can be used in any supervised LLIE method by simply extending its
298   supervised loss function into the GT-mean version.
299
      The GT-mean loss is also highly efficient. Compared to the original loss, it doubles the computation,
300
      and W is estimated with an analytical solution. Despite there is some extra computation overhead,
301   the overall increase introduced by the GT-mean loss is negligible throughout the training process.

423   4.3           E FFECT OF THE PARAMETER σ
424
      To investigate the influence of σ, we conducted experiments on LOLv1 using RetinexFormer (Cai
425
      et al., 2023) trained with GT-mean L1 loss under different σ values. We selected 10 different σ
426   values, running each setting three times for consistency. Notably, σ = 0 represents a special case
427   where the GT-mean L1 loss degrades to the original L1 loss. For every 1,000 (1K) iterations in the
428   150K iterations, we calculated mean and variance of the normal PSNR and GT-mean PSNR values,
429   shown in Figure 4 for demonstrating the trend during training. In the early stages (as can be seen
430   in Figure 4 (a) and (c)), the curve tendencies under different σ settings are similar. Considering the
431   curve with σ = 0 closely resembles the original L1 loss, we can empirically verify that the GT-
      mean loss at the early stage behaves like the original L1 loss. In contrast, as shown in the zoomed-in


                                                                                                           8
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


432
      views of the last 30K iterations (Figure 4 (b) and (d)), we observe that all settings become stable,
433   and the settings with non-zero σ consistently perform better than σ = 0. This observation shows
434   that the GT-mean loss diverges significantly from the original L1 loss in the late training stage.
435   The second term in Eq.1 allows the GT-mean loss to continuously improve model performance. In
436   addition, the experiment shows that the choice of σ value is open. As σ measures the spread of the
437   random variable E[·]
                       e deviating from the observed average image brightness E[·] in our modeling, we
438   recommend using a small value, such as σ = 0.1 for real world application.

440   4.4   F URTHER ANALYSIS ON THE GT- MEAN LOSS
441
442   In this experiment, we further investigate the difference between the original loss and the GT-mean
443   loss. Specifically, we randomly selected a batch of low-light images (batchsize = 8) and their cor-
444   responding ground truth images. These images were enhanced using RetinexFormer to produce
445
      enhanced outputs f (x). To simulate the varying brightness, we introduced a unified scaling factor
      η ranging from 0 to 3, simulating the progression of the enhanced images η · f (x) from dark to
446
      bright. This experiment setting simulates how the loss value varies under different brightness levels,
447
      facilitating us to investigate the loss curve with respect to the brightness variation only.

461   Based on the above experimental design, we present the curve of the original L1 loss (Figure 5(a)),
462   and the curves of the GT-mean L1 loss under different σ values (Figure 5(b)). The difference be-
463   tween them is that the use of the GT-mean loss clearly produces basins around η = 1. In another
464   word, the GT-mean loss produces small-gradient region with regard to brightness around η = 1,
465   of which the range is controlled by σ. From an optimization perspective, since the gradients with
466   respect to brightness become smaller, the optimization along the direction of brightness adjustment
467
      is in turn slowed down. Based on this characteristic, in real-world model training, the GT-mean
468   L1 loss enables LLIE models to focus on other important degeneration factors, when p(E[y])e     and
469   q(E[f
         e (x)])] become closer. In contrast, the original L1 loss is less capable of decoupling the opti-
470   mization with respect to brightness and other visual quality factors.
471   Additionally, Figure 5(c) presents the weight curves that correspond to Figure 5(b), demonstrating
472   how the GT-mean L1 loss behaves with regard to weight variation. As η approaches 1, the weight
473   W rapidly decreases, indicating that the second term in Eq.1 begins to dominate the loss function,
474   confirming the mechanism of our loss. Notably, as σ increases, W starts to drop at smaller values of
475   η, meaning that the second term in Eq.1 takes over earlier in the optimization process. This behavior
476   aligns with the design of σ, which controls the spread of E[·].

478   5     C ONCLUSION
479
480   In this paper, we propose the GT-mean loss to advance research on supervised low-light image en-
481   hancement (LLIE) methods. The GT-mean loss enables the model training process to circumvent
482   the misleading issue caused by brightness mismatch, thereby comprehensively addressing the var-
483   ious degeneration factors in low-light images. Due to its simple construction, the GT-mean loss
484   can be easily adopted by existing supervised LLIE methods, imposing minimal additional computa-
485   tional overhead during training. Experiments across various supervised LLIE methods consistently
      demonstrate the effectiveness of the proposed loss. While the estimation of the weight W remains


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      an open problem, we plan to explore various W -estimation strategies to potentially unlock even
487   greater performance gains in LLIE models.
488
489   Additionally, we encourage the LLIE research community to adopt GT-mean metrics as a comple-
490
      ment to traditional evaluation metrics. By incorporating traditional metrics alongside their GT-mean
      extensions, researchers can gain a comprehensive perspective on assessing the visual quality, thereby
491
      facilitating the development of more effective LLIE techniques.

      B      GT- MEAN M ETRIC FOR T RAINING G UIDANCE
811
812
      In low-light image enhancement (LLIE) training, determining the optimal number of iterations is
813   challenging due to fluctuating performance and the risk of overfitting. Here, we demonstrate how
814   the GT-mean metric can assist in identifying the optimal stopping point.
815
816
      We saved RetinexFormer results every 1,000 (1K) iterations and evaluated them using normal met-
      rics (PSNR and SSIM) and GT-mean metrics. Figure (a) shows normal metrics, where the PSNR
817
      curve flattens between 60k and 100k iterations, suggesting this as the optimal range. However, in
818
      Figure (b) (GT-mean metrics), the PSNR continues improving beyond 100k iterations, indicating
819   further gains.
820
821   The GT-mean metrics provide consistent results across both PSNR and SSIM, unlike normal met-
822
      rics, which show inconsistencies. This inconsistency in normal metrics could lead to suboptimal
      decisions regarding when to stop training. Thus, the GT-mean metric offers a clearer view of model
823
      improvement, helping select better training parameters and preventing premature termination due to
824
      concerns about overfitting.

      C    EXPERIMENTAL DETAILS
865
866
      In this section, we present the experimental setup for each method. Our aim is to ensure consistency
867   with the official settings for each baseline model while introducing the GT-mean loss to demonstrate
868   its effectiveness. To ensure fair comparisons, both the baseline models and the ones using GT-mean
869   loss were trained under identical hardware and software environments, minimizing the effects of
870   randomness.
871
      Uformer. Both the baseline and the GT-mean loss variant were trained following the experimental
872
      setup for motion deblurring in (Wang et al., 2022b), selected Uformer-T as the backbone model.
873   The Charbonnier loss used in the baseline was extended to GT-mean loss for the variant.
874
875   MIRNet. Both the baseline and the GT-mean loss variant were trained according to the settings
876
      used for the denoising task in (Zamir et al., 2020). In the GT-mean loss variant, the Charbonnier
      loss was replaced with the GT-mean loss.
877
878   RetinexFormer. For both the baseline and the GT-mean loss variant, we followed the training
879   settings for LOL datasets in (Cai et al., 2023). The L1 loss used in the baseline was extended to
880   GT-mean loss in the variant.
881   Restormer. The baseline and the GT-mean loss variant were both trained following the motion
882   deblurring settings described in (Zamir et al., 2022). The L1 loss in the baseline was extended to
883   GT-mean loss in the variant.
884
      LLFormer. Both the baseline and the GT-mean loss variant were trained according to the settings
885
      for the LOLv1 dataset described in (Wang et al., 2023b). The Smooth L1 loss used in the baseline
886   was extended to GT-mean loss for the variant.
887
888   SNR-Aware. The baseline and the GT-mean loss variant were both trained using the settings for for
889
      LOL datasets outlined in (Xu et al., 2022). The Charbonnier loss and perceptual loss used in the
      baseline were extended to GT-mean loss in the variant.
890
891   CID-Net. Both the baseline and the GT-mean loss variant were trained using the LOLv1 settings
892   described in (Yan et al., 2024). In the GT-mean loss variant, the Charbonnier loss, edge loss, and
893   perceptual loss were extended to GT-mean loss.
894   In summary, for each method, the original loss functions were extended to GT-mean loss, and all
895   models were trained using consistent settings to ensure a fair comparison.
