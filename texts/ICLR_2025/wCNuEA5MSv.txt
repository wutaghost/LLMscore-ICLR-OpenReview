009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Spatio-temporal forecasting is pivotal in numerous real-world applications, includ-
012            ing transportation planning, energy management, and climate monitoring. In this
013            work, we aim to harness the reasoning and generalization abilities of Pre-trained
014            Language Models (PLMs) for more effective spatio-temporal forecasting, particu-
015            larly in data-scarce scenarios. However, recent studies uncover that PLMs, which
016            are primarily trained on textual data, often falter when tasked with modeling the
017            intricate correlations in numerical time series, thereby limiting their effectiveness
018            in comprehending spatio-temporal data. To bridge the gap, we propose R E PST, a
019
               physics-aware PLM reprogramming framework tailored for spatio-temporal fore-
               casting. Specifically, we first propose a physics-aware decomposer that adaptively
020
               disentangles spatially correlated time series into interpretable sub-components,
021
               which facilitates PLM to understand sophisticated spatio-temporal dynamics via a
022            divide-and-conquer strategy. Moreover, we propose a selective discrete reprogram-
023            ming scheme, which introduces an expanded spatio-temporal vocabulary space
024            to project spatio-temporal series into discrete representations. This scheme mini-
025            mizes the information loss during reprogramming and enriches the representations
026            derived by PLMs. Extensive experiments on real-world datasets show that the pro-
027            posed R E PST outperforms twelve state-of-the-art baseline methods, particularly in
028            data-scarce scenarios, highlighting the effectiveness and superior generalization
029            capabilities of PLMs for spatio-temporal forecasting.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Spatio-temporal forecasting aims to predict future states of real-world complex systems by simultane-
035   ously learning spatial and temporal dependencies of historical observations, which plays a pivotal
036   role in diverse real-world applications, such as traffic management (Li et al., 2018; Wu et al., 2019),
037   environmental monitoring (Han et al., 2023), and resource optimization (Geng et al., 2019). In
038   the past decade, deep learning has demonstrated great predictive power and led to a surge in deep
039
      spatio-temporal forecasting models (Xie et al., 2020; Jin et al., 2023a). For example, Recurrent
      Neural Networks (RNNs) and Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) are frequently combined to capture
040
      complex patterns for spatio-temporal forecasting (Jin et al., 2023a; Li et al., 2018; Han et al., 2020).
041
      Despite fruitful progress made so far, such approaches are typically confined to the one-task-one-
042   model setting, which lacks general-purpose utility and inevitably falls short in handling widespread
043   data-scarcity issue in real-world scenarios, e.g., newly deployed monitoring services.
044
045
      In recent years, Pre-trained Language Models (PLMs) like GPT-3 (Brown, 2020) and the LLaMA
      family (Touvron et al., 2023) have achieved groundbreaking success in the Natural Language Pro-
046
      cessing (NLP) domain. PLMs exhibit exceptional contextual understanding, reasoning, and few-shot
047
      generalization capabilities across a wide range of tasks due to their pre-training on extensive text
048   corpora. Although originally designed for textual data, the versatility and power of PLMs have
049   inspired their application to numerically correlated data (Zhou et al., 2024; Jin et al., 2023b; 2024).
050   For example, Frozen Pretrained Transformer (FPT) (Zhou et al., 2024) pioneers research in this
051   direction and showcases the promise of fine-tuning PLMs as generic time series feature extractors.
052   Besides, model reprogramming (Jin et al., 2023b; 2024) have considered the modality differences
053   between time series and natural language, solving time series forecasting tasks by learning an input
      transformation function that maps time series patches (Nie et al., 2022) to a compressed vocabulary.

      3     M ETHODOLOGY
128
129
      As illustrated in Figure 2, R E PST consists of three components: a physics-aware spatio-temporal
130
      decomposer, a selective reprogrammed language model, and a learnable mapping function. To be
131   specific, in physics-aware spatio-temporal decomposer, we first decompose the input signals into a
132   series of distinct physics-aware components. Then, we utilize an adaptive reprogramming strategy to
133   reprogram these components into textual embeddings via an expanded spatio-temporal vocabulary
134   constructing through a selective manner. After that, we employ a frozen PLM to construct spatio-
135   temporal correlations based on these textual embeddings. Finally, a learnable mapping function
136   generates future predictions based on the output of PLM.
137
138   3.1   PHYSICS - AWARE EVOLUTIONAL SPATIO - TEMPORAL DECOMPOSITION
139
140   Recent studies have revealed that PLMs possess rich spatio-temporal knowledge and reasoning
141
      capabilities (Gurnee & Tegmark, 2023; Mai et al., 2023; Jin et al., 2024). However, existing methods
      failed to fully leverage the capabilities of PLMs, which raises challenges for spatio-temporal data
142
      forecasting as well. As aforementioned, reasons for this shortcoming lies in their over simplistic
143
      encoding to time series. PLMs requires further process of spatio-temporal data to enhance their
144   comprehensibility to such complex structure. In this section, we address this shortcoming through a
145   carefully designed physics-aware spatio-temporal decomposer. Previous works (Liu et al., 2024c; Yi
146   et al., 2024; Shao et al., 2022b) decouple the time series in Fourier space and handle the decoupled
147   signals separately for better use of the hidden information of time series. Simply decomposing time
148   series solely based on frequency intensity is not interpretable and cannot effectively capture the highly
149   coupled spatio-temporal dynamics. Furthermore, this cannot be easily realized by language models
150   as well due to their limited comprehension of physical semantics.
151   To fully unlock the spatio-temporal knowledge, inspired by dynamic mode decomposition (Schmid,
152   2010; Kutz et al., 2016), we propose to capture the underlying dynamic signals in an interpretable
153   manner by leveraging the dynamic system’s evolution matrix A. To be specific, considering two
154   state observations X1:t−1 and X2:t , it satisfies X2:t = AX1:t−1 . This evolution matrix A is sought in
155   a low-rank setting to capture the modes governing the system’s dynamics. By applying a series of
156   mathematical process such as singular value decomposition (SVD) to X1:t−1 and X2:t , we obtain the
157   eigenvectors Ω = [ω1 , ω2 , ..., ωC ] and corresponding eigenvalues V = [v1 , v2 , . . . , vC ] , which can
158
      be leveraged to decompose spatio-temporal dynamic systems into different components. Each ωi ,
      referred to as a mode of the dynamical system, reflects certain physical behaviors of the system. We
159
      provide a detailed calculation process in Appendix A.5.
160
161   Specifically, we first obtain Xnorm by normalizing the input X for each node to have zero mean
      and unit standard deviation using reversible instance normalization (RevIN) (Kim et al., 2021).

222   3.2   SELECTIVE REPROGRAMMED LANGUAGE MODELS
223
224   Based on the decoupled signal patches Xenc , how to tackle the complexity of spatio-temporal
225   structures raises another question. Compared to directly handling the spatio-temporal embeddings,
226   representations in natural language space are inherently suitable for PLMs. To enrich spatio-temporal
227
      semantics and enable more comprehensive modeling of hidden spatio-temporal physical relationships,
      as well as unlocking the reasoning capabilities of PLMs, we further reprogram the components
228
      into the textual embedding place via an expanded spatio-temporal vocabulary. When handling
229
      textual-based components, the rich physical semantic information can boost the pretrained physical
230   knowledge of PLMs, resulting in an adequate modeling of the hidden physical interactions between
231   disentangled components.
232
233
      Specifically, we introduce our selective reprogramming strategy, which further constructs an expanded
      spatio-temporal vocabulary in a differentiable reparameterization process. We begin with E ∈ RV ×d ,
234
      the pretrained vocabulary of the PLMs, where V is the vocabulary size and d is the dimension of the
235
      embedding. We introduce a learnable word mask vector m ∈ RV ×1 to adaptively select the most
236   relevant words, where m[i] ∈ {0, 1}. In specific, we first obtain m through a linear layer followed by
237   a Softmax activation, denoted as m = Softmax(EW), where W is a learnable matrix. Afterward,
238   we sample Top-K word embeddings from E based on probability m[i] associated with word i for
239   reprogramming. Since the sampling process is non-differentiable, we employ Gumbel-Softmax
240   trick(Jang et al., 2016) to enable gradient calculation with back-propagation, defined as
241

      5     R ELATED W ORKS
471
472
      5.1      S PATIO -T EMPORAL F ORECASTING
473
      Spatio-temporal forecasting has been playing a critical role in various smart city services, such as
474   traffic flow prediction (Liu et al., 2023a; Shao et al., 2022a; Liu et al., 2020), air quality monitoring
475   (Han et al., 2023; 2021), and energy management (Geng et al., 2019). Unlike traditional time series
476   forecasting, the forecasting challenges associated with spatio-temporal data are often characterized
477   by the unique properties of strong correlation and heterogeneity along the spatial dimension, which
478   are inherently more complex.
479
      Early studies usually capture spatial dependencies through a predefined graph structure (Li et al.,
480   2018; Han et al., 2020; Shao et al., 2022b; Wu et al., 2020), which decribes the explicit relationships
481   among different spatial locations. In recent years, there is a growing trend toward the utilization of
482   adaptive spatio-temporal graph neural networks, which can automatically capture dynamic spatial
483   graph structures from data. For instance, Graph WaveNet (Wu et al., 2019) eliminate the need for
484   predefined graphs by learning an adaptive adjacency matrix using two embedding matrices. AGCRN
485   (Bai et al., 2020) introduces a node adaptive parameter learning layer, allowing it to learn node-
      specific spatio-temporal patterns. Besides, attention mechanism is also widely employed in existing


                                                                  9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      models, as seen in examples like GMAN (Zheng et al., 2020), ASTGNN (Guo et al., 2021), and
487   STAEformer (Liu et al., 2023a). In contrast, STID (Shao et al., 2022a), a model based on Multi-Layer
488   Perceptrons (MLPs), achieves state-of-the-art results by utilizing multiple embedding techniques to
489   memorize stable spatial and temporal patterns.
490
      More recently, inspired by the huge success of PLMs in natural language processing field, there
491
      is increasing interest in building pre-trained models for spatio-temporal forecasting tasks. Several
492
      studies (Liu et al., 2024a;b; Yan et al., 2023; Jiang et al., 2024) explore the application of PLMs
493   for handling spatio-temporal data. Among these, UrbanGPT (Li et al., 2024b) offers a promising
494   end-to-end solution by integrating spatio-temporal data with textual information, enabling accurate
495   predictions of urban dynamics. Furthermore, the strong power of Transformer offers an opportunity
496   to build spatio-temporal foundation models, such as OpenCity (Li et al., 2024c) and UniST (Yuan
497   et al., 2024). Trained on numerous spatio-temporal data, these models demonstrate strong capabilities
498   and versatility across diverse forecasting scenarios. However, due to the problems of data-sparsity
499   in multiple spatio-temporal scenarios, it is difficult for these models to gather large amount of data
500   to perform pretraining comprehensively. In addition, the PLM-based spatio-temporal forecasting
501
      approaches do not fully leverage PLM’s potential. To address these gaps, this paper introduces a
      new reprogramming framework to leverage PLM’s generative and reasoning capabilities for spatio-
502
      temporal forecasting, particularly in data-sparse scenarios.
503
504
505   5.2   P RETRAINED L ANGUAGE M ODELS FOR T IME S ERIES
506
507   In recent years, PLMs have demonstrated remarkable performance across various time series analysis
508   tasks, including forecasting (Zhou et al., 2024; Gruver et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024), classification
509
      (Sun et al., 2023), and anomaly detection (Zhou et al., 2024). A significant body of research has
      focused on leveraging PLMs to address these challenges (Cao et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2024; Gruver
510
      et al., 2023; Lai et al., 2023). However, a persistent issue in these efforts is the modality gap between
511
      time series data and natural language. To address this challenge, Time-LLM (Jin et al., 2023b)
512   develops a time series reprogramming approach (Yang et al., 2021), which can effectively bridges
513   the modality gap between time series and text data. The objective of reprogramming is to learn a
514   trainable transformation function that can be applied to the patched time series data, enabling it to be
515   mapped into the textual embedding space of the PLM.
516
      Nevertheless, (Tan et al., 2024) conducted numerous experiments showing that existing PLM-based
517
      approaches (Zhou et al., 2024; Jin et al., 2023b; Wang et al., 2024) do not fully unlock the reasoning
518   or generative capabilities of PLMs. The reason these approaches achieve high performance lies in
519   the similar sequential formulation shared by time series and natural language (Liu et al., 2024d). In
520   fact, even when PLMs are merely used as one-dimensional encoders, time series analysis tasks can
521   still benefit from the pre-trained weights of PLMs. Although PLMs can handle one-dimensional
522   sequential data like text and time series, they fall short in capturing dependencies among complex
523   spatio-temporal structure, leading to suboptimal performance for spatio-temporal forecasting tasks.
524   In this work, we propose R E PST, which enables PLMs to comprehend complex spatio-temporal
525   dynamics via a physics-aware decomposition-based reprogramming strategy.
526
527
      6     C ONCLUSION
528
529
530   In this paper, we highlight the underlying reason for the poor performance of previous PLM-based
531
      approaches in spatio-temporal forecasting, emphasizing the need for the interpretability to fully
      leverage PLM’s potential. To address this problem, we developed R E PST, a tailored spatio-temporal
532
      forecasting framework that enables PLMs to comprehend the complex spatio-temporal patterns
533
      via a physics-aware decomposition-based reprogramming strategy. We design a physics-aware
534   spatio-temporal decomposer which adaptively disentangles complex spatio-temporal dynamics into
535   components with rich physical semantics for PLM’s better comprehension. Moreover, we construct
536   an expanded spatio-temporal vocabulary by a selective approach, which enables PLMs to focus on
537   relationships among 3D geometric space. As a result, PLM’s potential is full unlocked to handle spatio-
538   temporal forecasting tasks. Extensive experiments demonstrate that our proposed framework, R E PST,
539   achieves state-of-the-art performance on real-world datasets and exhibits exceptional capabilities in
      few-shot and zero-shot scenarios.

      A.4   KOOPMAN T HEORY
875
876   Koopman Theory (Koopman, 1931) shows that any nonlinear dynamic system, including spatio-
877   temporal series, can be modeled by an infinite-dimensional linear Koopman operator acting on a
878   space of measurement functions.
879
      Koopman operator theory provides a powerful framework for analyzing nonlinear dynamical systems
880   by lifting them into a linear infinite-dimensional space. The Koopman framework has shown particular
881   utility in analyzing spatio-temporal dynamic systems, where complex, nonlinear behaviors can be
882   represented using linear superpositions of Koopman eigenfunctions. The Koopman operator, denoted
883   as K, is a linear operator that acts on observable functions of the system state, rather than directly on
884   the state space itself. In a nonlinear dynamical system described by xt+1 = f (xt ), where x ∈ Rn is
885   the state and f is a nonlinear map, the Koopman operator is defined as:
886                                            Kg(xt ) = g(f (xt )),                                        (9)
887
888   where g is an observable, a scalar-valued function that maps the system’s state to a measurable
889
      quantity. The key insight is that while the system dynamics may be nonlinear, the evolution of
      observables under the action of the Koopman operator is linear. This allows for the application
890
      of spectral analysis techniques to extract meaningful modes of the system’s dynamics. Central to
891
      Koopman analysis are the Koopman eigenfunctions, ϕ(x), which satisfy:
892
893                                              Kϕ(x) = λϕ(x),                                           (10)
894
895
      where λ is the associated Koopman eigenvalue. The eigenfunctions provide a coordinate system in
      which the dynamics of the system are fully described by linear evolution:
896
897                                            ϕ(xt+1 ) = eλt ϕ(xt ).                                     (11)
898
      In practice, the Koopman spectrum, which consists of the eigenvalues λ, determines the growth,
899
      decay, or oscillatory behavior of different dynamic modes within the system. This makes it an
900   invaluable tool for decomposing complex, high-dimensional spatio-temporal dynamics into simpler,
901   interpretable components.

       D.1   D ISCUSSIONS ON P HYSICAL K NOWLEDGE FROM DECOMPOSER
1120
1121
       There are maybe questions that “Is the information extracted from spatio-temporal decomposer truly
1122   physical knowledge or fine-grained feature?". To answer this question, we claim as follow:
1123
1124   Physics-Aware Justification. Although DMD is designed to extract dynamic modes directly from
1125
       observational data, numerous studies have demonstrated its capability to identify propagating waves,
       oscillatory behaviors, and decay patterns, which are strongly associated with dominant physical
1126
       phenomena (Rowley et al., 2009; Tu, 2013; Yu et al., 2024). For example, research has shown that
1127
       DMD can reveal vortex shedding patterns and periodic oscillations in fluid dynamics (Rowley et al.,
1128   2009). These patterns are closely aligned with the Navier-Stokes equations, reflecting the intrinsic
1129   physical dynamics of fluid motion (Tu, 2013; Yu et al., 2024). For this reason, DMD is particularly
1130   suitable for capturing physics-aware patterns from spatio-temporal data, such as traffic flow and
1131   air pollution, which exhibit similar regularities with those governing fluid dynamics. For instance,
1132   STDEN (Ji et al., 2022) and AirPhyNet (Hettige et al., 2024) model the dynamics of traffic flow and
1133   air pollution as a continuous diffusion process via differential equations inspired by fluid dynamics
       modeling.


                                                        21
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1134
       Why DMD Over PCA or Eigenvectors? Unlike DMD, PCA or eigenvector-based methods are
1135   purely statistical tools that do not inherently account for the underlying physical behavior of a
1136   system. By analyzing the dominant modes, DMD can uncover patterns that are both interpretable and
1137   consistent with the underlying physical mechanisms, providing valuable insights for PLM that can
1138   improve predictive accuracy.
1139
       To validate our choice, we first conducted experiments by replacing the physical decomposer with
1140
       PCA (see Table 5). This results in a noticeable decline in model performance, suggesting that PCA’s
1141   inability to benefit PLM in our context. Furthermore, when we randomly initialized the pretrained
1142   weights of the PLM, the impact on performance was minor, further indicating that PCA could not
1143   effectively leverage the pretrained knowledge. These findings underscore the superiority of DMD in
1144   generating physically consistent interpretations and unlocking pretrained model knowledge.
1145
       Furthermore, to verify the effectiveness of the knowledge extracted by the physical decomposer, we
1146
       conducted experiments that apply the decomposed data to two state-of-the-art spatio-temporal fore-
1147   casting models, i.e., STID and GWNet (see Table 5). The results showed only marginal improvement,
1148   indicating that even advanced spatio-temporal forecasting models struggle to effectively utilize the
1149   physical knowledge. This demonstrates that the knowledge extracted by the physical decomposer is
1150   not simply a set of fine-grained features but represents a unique form of information that cannot be
1151   easily leveraged by all models.
1152
       In summary, DMD demonstrates a clear advantage over simpler methods such as PCA or eigenvector
1153   decomposition by integrating spatial and temporal dynamics through interpretable modes aligned
1154   with physical phenomena. Our additional experimental results further emphasize its ability to enhance
1155   PLM’s performance by capturing meaningful, physics-aware patterns rather than merely extracting
1156   fine-grained statistical features.

       D.2    D ISCUSSIONS ON REASONING ABILITY OF PLM S
1177
1178   First, in our work, reasoning refers to the ability to make predictions by comprehending both spatial
1179   and temporal contexts. While zero-shot performance improvements demonstrate generalization, we
1180   argue that they also reflect enhanced reasoning capabilities. In zero-shot setting, the model encounters
1181   spatial regions or domains it has not seen during training. The model’s predictive performance solely
1182   relies on its inherent reasoning capabilities and its capacity to infer patterns from prior spatio-temporal
1183   knowledge.
1184   Second, our DMD-based decomposer inherently captures spatial correlations within spatio-temporal
1185   data by leveraging the co-evolving nature of spatial nodes. Specifically, the input matrix X, where
1186   rows represent spatial nodes and columns represent time steps, implicitly embeds spatial relationships
1187   through the correlated dynamics of the nodes. During decomposition, spatial nodes exhibiting similar
       temporal behaviors (e.g., traffic flow on adjacent roads) are naturally grouped into the same dynamic


                                                           22
       Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


1188
       mode, i.e., dominant global patterns that summarize the behavior of correlated spatial nodes. These
1189   patterns reflect how the spatio-temporal system as a whole evolves over time. Furthermore, DMD
1190   does not require prior spatial knowledge (e.g., spatial adjacency matrices), making it a flexible
1191   approach for datasets with implicit spatial relationships.
1192
       Third, we further conducted empirical studies to validate the contribution of DMD to spatial reasoning.
1193
       In specific, we decomposed each node in the dataset independently and then concatenated them
1194
       together, rather than decomposing the entire spatio-temporal system. By doing so, we can eliminate
1195   the impact of DMD on spatial dimension. We observe an obvious decrease in the model performance,
1196   indicating that the decomposer extract spatio-temporal knowledge from the system rather than simple
1197   temporal embeddings.

1223   Reconstruction plays a pivotal role in DMD-related techniques, ensuring that the extracted modes
1224   faithfully capture the system’s underlying dynamics while eliminating redundant noise. In our
1225   approach, we enhance this process by prioritizing the most dominant modes, enabling a more precise
1226   representation of key spatio-temporal patterns. We conducted experiments (Figure 9) to systematically
1227   evaluate its role and assess its influence on the overall results, following the setting of the ablation

1230   either dynamic components (Xdyn ) or reconstruction data (Xrec ). The ablation studies confirm that
1231   the reconstruction matrix is essential for maintaining high performance, alongside the dynamics
1232   component. The results also demonstrate the necessity of integrating both components to fully realize
1233   the advantages of the R E PST framework.
