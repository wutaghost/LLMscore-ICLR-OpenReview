                                                   A BSTRACT
010
011
               Mixture of Experts (MoE) enables efficient parameter scaling in large language
012
               models by dynamically activating relevant parameter subsets per input token.
013
               Compressing MoE models presents unique challenges due to their inherent spar-
014            sity. Traditional quantization techniques, which are typically effective for dense
015            models, prove inadequate when applied to MoE architectures. This paper pro-
016            poses an efficient MoE quantization algorithm. Specifically, we construct a mixed-
017            precision quantization search space encompassing different granularities from
018            expert-level to channel-level. This approach facilitates precise bit-width resource
019            allocation across model components based on their significance and activation fre-
020            quency. And then, we leverage evolutionary algorithms to efficiently navigate this
021            search space, autonomously identifying optimal quantization configurations. The
022
               synergy between adaptive granularity and automated search effectively mitigates
               the distinctive quantization challenges inherent to MoE models, culminating in a
023
               fully automated framework for efficient MoE quantization. Experimental results
024
               indicate that our method achieves significant performance improvements across
025            multiple evaluation tasks, with particularly notable results in low-bit quantization
026            scenarios. When applied to the Mixtral-8x7b-v0.1 model, our approach outper-
027            forms the current state-of-the-art by 9.24% , setting a new benchmark in MoE
028            quantization. Code is available in supplementary materials.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   In recent years, Large Language Models (LLMs) (Touvron et al. (2023a;b); Reid et al. (2024);
034   Zhang et al. (2022)) have demonstrated remarkable progress in the field of natural language pro-
035   cessing. However, the continuous expansion of model scale has concomitantly led to substantial
036   computational resource requirements. The Mixture of Experts (MoE) (Jacobs et al. (1991); Fedus
037   et al. (2022); Lepikhin et al. (2020)) architecture has emerged as an innovative paradigm in large
038   language models, offering efficient parameter scaling through dynamic activation of input-relevant
039
      parameter subsets. By partitioning the parameter space into multiple expert networks and employing
      dynamic routing, MoE models facilitate significant performance enhancements while maintaining
040
      relatively low training costs and only marginally increasing computational demands during infer-
041
      ence. (Jiang et al. (2024); Dai et al. (2024)) This approach enables the handling of increasingly
042   complex tasks while optimizing resource utilization, representing a significant advancement in scal-
043   ing model capabilities in natural language processing(Rajbhandari et al. (2022); Chen et al. (2022b)).
044
045
      As large language models continue to grow in size, the importance of model compression becomes
      increasingly evident. such as quantization (Frantar et al. (2023); Yuan et al. (2023); Lin et al. (2023);
046
      Shao et al. (2023); Ma et al. (2024)), sparsification (Frantar & Alistarh (2023); Sun et al. (2023);
047
      Zhang et al. (2023)), and knowledge distillation (Hsieh et al. (2023); Gu et al. (2024)), have demon-
048   strated significant progress in reducing model size and computational requirements while maintain-
049   ing performance. However, existing compression methods for large models do not necessarily yield
050   the same effectiveness when applied to Mixture of Experts (MoE) models. The inherent sparsity
051   characteristics of MoE models present unique challenges for compression Chowdhury et al. (2023).
052   The dynamic routing mechanism in MoE models refers to the process where input tokens are se-
053   lectively directed to specific experts based on their content. Shazeer et al. (2017) This leads to
      significant variations in the activation frequency of different experts, as some experts may be called

054
      upon more frequently than others depending on the input distribution. The heterogeneous expert
055   structure indicates that each expert within the MoE model may have distinct parameters and spe-
056   cialized functions. Unlike traditional homogeneous neural networks, this heterogeneity implies that
057   each expert may require a distinct compression strategy, further complicating the compression pro-
058   cess. Moreover, the sparse computational patterns in MoE models make it difficult to directly apply
059   conventional compression techniques without impacting model performance Li et al. (2023).
060
      While quantization has proven effective and widely adopted in dense model compression, research
061   on MoE model compression has predominantly focused on model pruning, particularly expert and
062   layer pruning techniques. Lu et al. (2024) proposed an expert-level pruning method based on router
063   norm changes, retaining experts with the largest changes in router weights. Chowdhury et al. (2024)
064   introduced a theoretically-driven approach, demonstrating that prioritizing the removal of experts
065   with smaller L2 norm changes in their routers after pre-training can guarantee test accuracy under
066   certain conditions. Some researchers have recognized the need for more comprehensive compres-
067   sion strategies. For instance, He et al. (2024) proposed a unified MoE compression framework that
068   combines expert slimming (including pruning and quantization) with expert pruning (such as layer
069
      and block deletion). This approach represents a step towards integrating multiple compression tech-
      niques. However, a common limitation of these methods is their static nature, where uniform com-
070
      pression techniques are applied across all experts. This approach fails to account for the dynamic
071
      activation patterns and heterogeneous structure inherent in MoE architectures, potentially leading
072   to suboptimal compression results. The realm of MoE quantization, in particular, remains largely
073   unexplored. Li et al. (2024) investigated the assignment of different quantization bit-widths based
074   on importance judgments at various granularities of MoE structures. Through extensive experiments
075   to select importance metrics, they pushed compression rates to higher levels. However, the field still
076   lacks a comprehensive quantization solution specifically tailored to the intricacies of MoE models.
077
      In response to the unique challenges posed by quantizing MoE models (Jiang et al. (2024); Li et al.
078   (2024)), this paper introduces an innovative and efficient automated quantization algorithm. Our
079   approach addresses this challenge by constructing a mixed-precision quantization search space that
080   encompasses a wide range of granularities, from expert-level to channel-level. This design allows
081   for dynamic and flexible quantization strategies that can adapt to the varying activation patterns
082   and heterogeneous structure of MoE models. By incorporating different levels of granularity, our
083   method can better align with the dynamic routing mechanism and diverse expert utilization inherent
084   in MoE architectures. Consequently, we extend the granularity of quantization configurations to the
085   internal structures of experts, enabling our search space to capture the dynamic characteristics of
086
      MoE models. This approach allows for efficient exploration of the vast search space, minimizing
      quantization model loss under target compression rates. Ultimately, we propose a fully automated,
087
      efficient quantization framework for MoE models that addresses their inherent dynamic nature, pro-
088
      viding a more tailored and effective approach than previous static methods. In conclusion, the key
089   contributions of this paper are as follows:
090
091          • We introduce a novel mixed-precision quantization search space that achieves fine-grained
092            quantization ranging from expert-level to neuron-level granularity. By extending quanti-
093            zation configurations to the internal structures of experts, our approach not only captures
094            the multi-layered characteristics of MoE models but also flexibly integrates with various
095            importance judgment factors. This integration enables precise allocation of quantization
096            resources across different structural levels of the model.
097          • Furthermore, to navigate the complex search space efficiently, we introduce an evolutionary
098            algorithm-based approach for identifying optimal quantization configurations. This method
099            enables effective exploration of the vast search space, automatically determining the most
100            suitable quantization strategy while minimizing model loss under specified compression
101            rates. The evolutionary algorithm’s ability to handle multi-objective optimization makes it
102            particularly well-suited for balancing the trade-offs inherent in MoE model quantization.
103          • Our novel techniques have established new benchmarks in the domain of Mixture-of-
104            Experts (MoE) quantization. In the realm of mixed-precision quantization, our proposed
105            method demonstrates remarkable superiority, surpassing existing linear-level quantization
106            approaches by a substantial 7.9% on the neuron-quantized Mixtral-8x7B-v0.1 model, while
107            maintaining identical quantization configurations. Through the innovative integration of
               evolutionary algorithms, we further enhanced our method’s efficacy, achieving an addi-

116   2.1   M IXTURE - OF -E XPERTS M ODELS
117
      Mixture-of-Experts (MoE) architectures have emerged as a significant innovation in Large Language
118
      Models (LLMs) (Jiang et al. (2024); Dai et al. (2024)), offering a balance between model capacity
119
      and computational efficiency. Originally proposed by Jacobs et al. (1991), MoE has evolved sub-
120   stantially in the context of deep learning and natural language processing. Shazeer et al. (2017)
121   pioneered the use of MoE in transformer-based models, demonstrating improved performance on
122   translation tasks. This work was extended by Lepikhin et al. (2020) with the GShard architecture,
123   scaling MoE to trillion-parameter models. Fedus et al. (2022) introduced the Switch Transformer,
124   implementing a sparse gating mechanism for more efficient routing of inputs to experts. The theo-
125   retical understanding of MoE models has also progressed.Chen et al. (2022b) provided insights into
126   the generalization capabilities of MoE models, while Chowdhery et al. (2022) offered theoretical
127   guarantees on the computational efficiency of modern MoE architectures with patch-level routing.
128
      Recently, the Mixtral model by Jiang et al. (2024) demonstrated that MoE can match the perfor-
      mance of full-parameter LLMs while using significantly fewer active parameters. This has sparked
129
      interest in MoE compression techniques. Du et al. (2019) proposed expert-choice routing to opti-
130
      mize expert utilization, while Rajbhandari et al. (2022) addressed the memory overhead associated
131   with storing multiple expert networks. These advancements highlight the potential of MoE for effi-
132   cient, large-scale language modeling and set the stage for further research in MoE compression (Li
133   et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2022a)) and optimization.

      2.2   P OST-T RAINING Q UANTIZATION
136
137   Post-training quantization (PTQ) (Wei et al. (2022b); Yao et al. (2022); Ashkboos et al. (2024); Liu
138   et al. (2024)) has emerged as an efficient technique for model compression, particularly beneficial
139   for LLMs. Unlike quantization-aware training or fine-tuning (Tailor et al. (2020); Ding et al. (2022)
140   ), PTQ operates on pre-trained models without extensive retraining (Liu et al. (2021); Fang et al.
141   (2020)). In computer vision, AdaRound Nagel et al. (2020) optimizes weight rounding strategies,
142   BRECQ Li et al. (2021) introduces block-wise reconstruction, and QDROP Wei et al. (2022a) en-
143   hances performance through activation substitution. For LLMs, GPTQFrantar et al. (2023) uses
144
      approximate second-order information for layerwise quantization, SmoothQuant Xiao et al. (2023)
      tackles activation outliers, and AWQ Lin et al. (2023) preserves critical weights’ precision. Om-
145
      niQuant Shao et al. (2023) integrates multiple strategies, combining mixed-precision quantization,
146
      outlier handling, and adaptive rounding. AffineQuant Ma et al. (2024) introduces an affine transfor-
147   mation to adjust weight distribution, effectively reducing quantization errors. These advancements
148   have significantly improved LLM deployment efficiency on resource-constrained devices (Kim et al.
149   (2023); Chen et al. (2024)).

      Mixed-precision quantization allocate different quantization bit-widths to various layers or modules
217   within a neural network. HAWQ Dong et al. (2019) is a representative method in mixed-precision
218   quantization. It utilizes the ratio of the maximum eigenvalue of the Hessian matrix to the parameter
219   count as a quantization sensitivity indicator, which guides the allocation of appropriate quantization
220   precision to different modules. Existing mixed-precision quantization methods for MoE models
221   (Li et al. (2024); He et al. (2024)) have explored strategies that allocate more bits to frequently
222   utilized experts, initial MoE blocks, or shared experts. While these approaches have demonstrated
223   some improvement in quantization efficiency, they overlook the finer-grained structural features
224   within MoE models. In the following section, we introduce a novel, more granular mixed-precision
225
      quantization method. Our approach delves into the internal expert structures, considering multiple
      levels of granularity from expert-level to neuron-level, thereby achieving more precise and efficient
226
      allocation of quantization resources.

247   coarse-grained approaches may not fully exploit the structural properties of MoE models, poten-

249   neuron-level quantization offers a more refined and flexible compression strategy. This approach
250   enables better adaptation to the inherent imbalances in MoE models by capturing the importance of

      frequently activated neurons or those with greater influence on the output, thereby improving overall

380   4.1   S ETTINGS
381
382   Implementation Details. The implementation details of our proposed method are as follows. In
383   alignment with prevailing quantization methodologies for large language models (Frantar et al.
384   (2023); Lin et al. (2023); Shao et al. (2023); Ma et al. (2024)), we employ a calibration set consist-
385   ing of 128 samples, each comprising 2048 tokens, extracted from the WikiText2 Merity et al. (2016)
386   training corpus. Our quantization strategy implements asymmetric group quantization with a group
387
      size of 128. We apply 4-bit precision quantization to all attention-related layers while maintaining
      the router in its full-precision state. The evolutionary algorithm’s hyperparameters are configured as
388
      follows: a population size of 400 individuals is maintained across a maximum of 300 generations.
389
      We implement a mutation rate of 0.07 and employ an elitism strategy, preserving the top 7 individu-
390   als in each generation. The tournament selection process utilizes a tournament size of 5. To ensure
391   consistency across all experimental conditions, we constrain the overall model bit-width to 2.54 bits
392   Li et al. (2024).
393
      Baseline. All experiments were conducted on the Mixtral-8x7B-v0.1 model Jiang et al. (2024)).
394
      Our method successfully quantizes this multi-billion parameter model, with all GPTQ Frantar et al.
395
      (2023) experiments completed on a single NVIDIA RTX 3090 GPU with 24GB memory and all
396   OmniQuant Shao et al. (2023) experiments on a single NVIDIA A800 GPU with 80GB memory,
397   demonstrating its efficiency and scalability. We compare our approach against baselines Li et al.
398   (2024) including expert-level random allocation, frequency-based allocation, and other quantization
399   parameter distribution methods.
400
      Evaluation. To assess the efficacy of our automated fine-grained quantization approach for MoE
401
      models, we conducted evaluations across five zero-shot tasks: PIQA Bisk et al. (2020), HellaSwag
402   Zellers et al. (2019), WinoGrande Sakaguchi et al. (2019), COPA Gordon et al. (2012), and Open-
403   bookQA Mihaylov et al. (2018). We utilized the lm-eval-harness Gao et al. (2021) framework to
404   obtain individual task accuracies and the overall average accuracy. This comprehensive evaluation
405   strategy enables us to gauge the impact of our quantization method on various aspects of model
406   performance, providing insights into its generalization capabilities across diverse natural language
407   understanding tasks.

      4.4     C ONCLUSION
449
450   This paper presents an automated fine-grained quantization method for Mixture-of-Experts (MoE)
451
      models, designed to overcome the limitations of current quantization techniques when applied to
      MoE architectures. As LLMs continue to grow, model compression becomes increasingly impor-
452
      tant, especially for MoE models with dynamic routing mechanisms. However, the sparsity and
453
      heterogeneity of MoE models pose challenges for traditional quantization, which is typically devel-
454   oped for dense models. Current compression methods mainly focus on pruning, such as expert and
455   layer pruning, with relatively little attention given to quantization in MoE models.
456
457
      To address these challenges, we propose a mixed-precision quantization search space that spans from
      the expert-level to the neuron-level, allowing for flexible bit-width allocation. This approach adapts
458
      to the varying activation patterns and structures in MoE models, improving quantization accuracy.
459
      Additionally, we introduce an evolutionary algorithm-based search mechanism to efficiently explore
460   quantization configurations, minimizing quantization loss while adhering to a specified compression
461   rate.
462
463
      Our experiments show that the proposed method significantly improves MoE model performance
      across various tasks, particularly in low-bit quantization scenarios. Compared to existing linear
464
      quantization techniques, our approach achieves a 7.9% performance improvement at the neuron
465
      level, with an additional 1.34% gain through evolutionary algorithms. These results highlight the
466   effectiveness of fine-grained mixed-precision quantization, particularly in optimizing resource al-
467   location for MoE models. Furthermore, our method is orthogonal to existing LLM quantization
468   techniques, enabling potential synergies with other compression methods.
469
      In summary, this work introduces an innovative, automated quantization framework for MoE models
470
      that captures their dynamic characteristics and enables precise, granular quantization configurations.
471
      By extending quantization to the neuron level, our approach offers new strategies for compressing
472   MoE models and establishes new benchmarks across multiple tasks. This framework holds promise
473   for both academic research and practical deployment of large language models, providing a novel
474   path for efficient model compression.
475
476
477   4.5     L IMITATIONS
478
479
      Despite the promising results, our work has some limitations. Due to resource constraints, we were
480   unable to conduct extensive experiments across a wide range of models. This limits the general-
481   izability of our findings, and further evaluations on different MoE models are necessary to fully
482   validate the effectiveness of our approach. Additionally, current model deployment frameworks do
483   not yet support neuron-level quantization, which restricts the practical application of our method.
484   Future research is needed to develop deployment techniques that can fully exploit the potential of
485   fine-grained, neuron-level quantization, allowing for more efficient real-world implementations of
      MoE models.
