009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Multimodal large language models (MLLMs) have exhibited remarkable capabili-
012            ties for performing complex vision-language tasks in various domains. Currently,
013            MLLMs based on urban imagery in urban studies are only developed focusing
014            on remote sensing imagery. However, except for the macroscopic information
015            from remote sensing imagery, effective urban understanding also requires de-
016
               tailed appearance information of urban zones from street-view imagery, which
               is largely overlooked by existing MLLMs. The primary challenges of develop-
017
               ing such a versatile urban MLLM are twofold. Firstly, it needs a large-scale
018
               corpus with well-organized, cross-view urban imagery paired with correspond-
019            ing text for cross-modal training. Secondly, traditional MLLMs typically learn
020            image-text pairs independently, hard to support joint modeling of cross-view ur-
021            ban imagery. To address these challenges, in this work, we propose UrbanM-
022            LLM, a novel MLLM that jointly learns from remote sensing and street-view im-
023            agery to harness their complementary information. We first collect a large-scale
024            dataset containing satellite-view and street-view imagery along with their geotags
025            and annotated texts. Technically, we propose a brand MLLM architecture with
026            a cross-view perceiver to explicitly connect visual information of cross-view ur-
027
               ban imagery. We also introduce a novel pre-training paradigm based on structural
               interleaved urban image-text documents integrating satellite-view, street-view im-
028
               agery and related textual descriptions. This approach encourages the model to
029
               implicitly learn the relationships between different types of urban imagery, en-
030            hancing the understanding in each domain. We evaluate our model on a com-
031            prehensive benchmark comprising 13 diverse urban understanding tasks across
032            satellite-view, street-view, and cross-view domains. These tasks include scene
033            classification, object reasoning, spatial relationship reasoning, geo-localization,
034            landmark reasoning, and indicator prediction, providing a robust assessment of
035            the model’s capabilities. Extensive experiments demonstrate that UrbanMLLM
036            achieves an average of 27.3% and 25.5% performance improvement compared
037            with the best open-sourced and closed-sourced MLLMs, respectively. Moreover,
038
               we thoroughly study the impact of different pre-training data choices and model
               scales on performance, offering practical insights for effective MLLM design. The
039
               proposed UrbanMLLM offers a scalable and versatile solution for understanding
040
               urban environments.

043   1   I NTRODUCTION
044
045   Urban imagery has been widely used for understanding cities in terms of urban spatial struc-
046   ture, functionality, and socio-economic status. The advancement in computer vision and multi-
047   modal learning has driven the utilization of multimodal urban data for urban understanding tasks,
048   such as scene classification (Kuckreja et al., 2024; Mall et al., 2024), scene geo-localization (Vi-
049   vanco Cepeda et al., 2024; Xu et al., 2024), urban indicator prediction (Fan et al., 2023; Hao et al.,
050
      2024), etc. More recently, benefiting from the impressive performance and generalizability of large
      language models (LLMs), multimodal large language models (MLLMs) have shown great potential
051
      for effectively solving distinct multimodal tasks in a “one-for-all” manner.
052
053   In the urban study area, there has been a line of works using MLLMs to tackle urban understanding
      tasks, while predominantly focusing on remote sensing (Kuckreja et al., 2024; Luo et al., 2024; Bazi


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      et al., 2024; Muhtar et al., 2024). Remote sensing imagery provides a macroscopic and comprehen-
055   sive overview of how the city’s functional zones are laid out while lacking some detailed contexts
056   of the urban elements. Therefore, the existing MLLMs in urban studies can only deal with high-
057   level urban understanding tasks such as land use classification and region captions. By comparison,
058   street-view imagery captures a more fine-grained appearance of urban zones, providing comple-
059   mentary information such as building facades and heights. However, no existing MLLMs in urban
060   science has explored integrating street-view imagery to enhance urban understanding. To enhance
061   MLLMs’ comprehensive understanding of urban environments, satellite imagery, which captures
062   large-scale spatial layouts, and street-view imagery, which provides ground-level details, should be
063
      integrated and jointly learned within a unified model.
064   Achieving this goal needs to address two primary challenges. The first is the lack of well-organized
065   multimodal urban data. Currently, publicly available urban datasets do not pair cross-view im-
066   agery with corresponding textual annotations, making them unable to support multimodal learning
067   of MLLMs. The second challenge comes from the joint learning paradigm of connecting remote
068   sensing and street-view imagery. In conventional MLLM frameworks (Liu et al., 2024c; Chen et al.,
069
      2024), visual features from cross-view imagery are encoded separately and aligned only with their
      respective annotated texts. These approaches fail to capture the complementary relationships be-
070
      tween satellite and street-view imagery, leaving their information isolated.
071
072   In this work, we address the above two challenges and introduce a novel urban MLLM jointly
073   learning from remote sensing and street-view imagery and associated textual data. Specifically, our
074   efforts focus on both data contribution and methodology innovation. Given that existing publicly
075
      available datasets about urban imagery (Luo et al., 2024; Astruc et al., 2024) commonly lack paired
      cross-view images and large-scale annotated text information, we first collect a large-scale multi-
076
      modal urban imagery dataset. Our dataset covers the whole United States, comprising paired-up
077
      satellite-view and street-view images, together with geotags and annotated textual descriptions. We
078   propose two key designs to break the visual knowledge isolation to facilitate the mutual learning
079   of cross-view urban imagery. The first one is about the model architecture, where we propose a
080   cross-view perceiver module that bridges the paired-up satellite-view and street-view visual features
081   through a cross-attention mechanism. This design explicitly facilitates the exchange of informa-
082   tion between the region-level context of satellite imagery and the fine-grained appearance details
083   of street-view imagery. For example, the injection of street-view information to the satellite-view
084   encoding can provide more detailed urban region context. The second part is a novel interleaved pre-
085   training paradigm to enhance the mutual learning between cross-view imagery. In detail, we design
086
      coherent image-text documents that interleave the satellite-view image with matched street-view im-
      ages and associated textual descriptions, forming a comprehensive profile of an urban region. Such
087
      interleaved training corpus helps MLLMs implicitly learn the relationship between different-view
088
      urban imagery via in-context learning. Through the explicit and implicit mutual learning between
089   cross-view urban imagery, our proposed UrbanMLLM is expected to overcome the visual isolation
090   issue, benefiting the comprehensive understanding of urban environments from diverse views.
091
092
      For a comprehensive evaluation of urban understanding abilities for MLLMs, we build Urban-
      View Benchmark which includes 13 different tasks of urban perception (scene classification, geo-
093
      localization), reasoning (object reasoning, spatial relationship reasoning, landmark reasoning) and
094
      prediction (indicator prediction) based on single-view or cross-view urban imagery. Extensive ex-
095   periments on the benchmark validate the noticeable superiority of UrbanMLLM on a wide range of
096   urban understanding tasks, achieving an average 27.3% and 25.5% performance gain compared with
097   the best open-sourced and closed-sourced MLLMs, respectively. Moreover, we study the impact of
098   different pre-training data choices and model scales on the final performance, offering practical in-
099   sights for effective MLLM design. This work serves as a foundational technique for addressing
100   a wide range of urban-related tasks requiring comprehensive visual understanding capabilities. In
101   brief, our major contributions can be summarized as follows:
102
103   • We propose a brand MLLM architecture with a designed cross-perceiver module to facilitate cross-
104     fusion of the complementary visual context from satellite-view and street-view imagery.
105
106   • We construct a novel interleaved pre-training corpus that links satellite and street-view imagery
107     through geo-location relationships, and propose a training paradigm that implicitly promotes mu-
        tual learning between cross-view imagery.

      2.1   M ULTIMODAL L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS (MLLM S )
128
      With the rapid development and significant success of large language models (LLMs), recent re-
129
      search has focused on developing multimodal large language models (MLLMs) with the ability
130   to comprehend both visual and textual knowledge, enabling them to address complex visual rea-
131   soning and understanding tasks. Existing MLLMs can be roughly divided into closed-source and
132   open-source models. Closed-source MLLMs are mostly built based on corresponding commercial
133   LLMs, such as GPT-4o (Achiam et al., 2023), Gemini (Reid et al., 2024) and Qwen-VL (Bai et al.,
134   2023). These models benefit from the large-scale and extensive training corpus, which have been
135   shown to exhibit powerful general multimodal understanding capabilities. By comparison, open-
136   source MLLMs are usually smaller-scale, established by aligning a visual encoding branch to an
137   off-the-shelf LLM. Following an earlier work LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024c), there are mainly two di-
138
      rections to advance the performance of MLLMs. The first line of works explores more advanced
      architectures such as introducing dual vision encoders (Li et al., 2024b), more sophisticated visual
139
      adapters (Cha et al., 2024) and the mixture-of-expert (MoE) strategy (Li et al., 2024c). Another line
140
      tries to uplift the performance of MLLMs with more beneficial pre-training data, such as interleaved
141   multimodal data (Lin et al., 2024) and synthetic data (McKinzie et al., 2024). By comparison,
142   our work introduces a novel MLLM architecture that integrates a cross-view perceiver module to
143   enhance cross-view information fusion and contribute a unique interleaved pre-training corpus for
144   MLLMs in urban areas.
145
146   2.2   M ULTIMODAL M ODELS F OR U RBAN U NDERSTANDING
147
148   Understanding the urban environment usually requires multimodal information from diverse
149   sources, such as satellite-view images, street-view images, POI information and geo-locations,
150   etc. Existing methods in urban study can be categorized into two types: CLIP-based methods and
151   MLLM-based methods, as shown in Table 1. From the data aspect, existing methods based urban
152   imagery in urban study all focus on satellite images while overlooking using the street-view imagery
153
      for urban understanding. CLIP-based methods are mostly developed based on the contrastive learn-
      ing strategy used in CLIP (Radford et al., 2021), such as training with satellite image-text pairs (Liu
154
      et al., 2024a; Yan et al., 2024), street-view image-text pairs (Hao et al., 2024) and satellite-view and
155
      street-view image pairs (Mall et al., 2024). These works can only deal with prediction tasks such as
156   indicator prediction via end-to-end fine-tuning, but fail to conduct perception and reasoning tasks.
157   Another line of research focuses on developing specialized MLLMs for problem-solving in the ur-
158   ban domain. Existing models, such as GeoChat (Kuckreja et al., 2024), SkysenseGPT (Luo et al.,
159   2024), H2 RSVLM Pang et al. (2024), and EarthGPT (Zhang et al., 2024) only leverage remote sens-
160   ing data including satellite images and annotated text for model learning. These models are capable
161   of handling remote sensing perception and reasoning tasks but fail to deal with prediction tasks such
      as indicator predictions. However, relying solely on region-level knowledge is insufficient to capture

      the complexities of urban environments, thereby limiting their applications for a wide range of urban
163   understanding tasks. In contrast, our work proposes a novel learning paradigm based on cross-view
164   urban image-text data which is capable of solving both remote sensing and street-view tasks.

      3.1   OVERVIEW
170   Traditional MLLMs are usually trained with paired-up separate image-text data, resulting in the
171   knowledge isolation between different images. Such limitation constrains their ability of urban un-
172   derstanding, which requires a holistic comprehension of urban imagery from diverse perspectives.
173   To overcome this issue, we propose two key designs to facilitate the comprehensive understanding
174   on cross-view urban imagery. Firstly, we introduce a cross-view perceiver module in the MLLM
175   architecture, explicitly enabling satellite-view and street-view visual contexts to complement each
176   other. Secondly, we propose a novel interleaved pre-training paradigm leveraging structurally in-
177   terleaved urban image-text contexts, integrating satellite-view and street-view imagery with corre-
178
      sponding textual descriptions. Training on such interleaved data enables MLLMs to learn relation-
      ships between cross-view urban imagery, leading to a more comprehensive understanding of urban
179
      environments. We elaborate the MLLM architecture enhanced with cross-view fusion in Section 3.2,
180
      followed by the designed pre-training paradigm based on interleaved urban contexts in Section 3.3.
181
182

185   cally developed by directly fine-tuning general-purpose MLLMs (e.g., LLaVA) on satellite image-

187   knowledge from satellite-view imagery but also detailed contexts from street-view imagery. Unfor-
188   tunately, this objective is unpromising to be achieved with the classical MLLM architecture, where

      3.3     I NTERLEAVED U RBAN C ONTEXT- BASED P RE - TRAINING

242
      Existing explorations on general MLLMs have demonstrated that training on interleaved data yields
      superior performance than the traditional image-text pairs (Lin et al., 2024; McKinzie et al., 2024).
243
      The interleaved structure fosters semantic connections between multiple images, enabling MLLMs
244
      to better capture contextual relationships across images. This advantage aligns well with our objec-
245   tive of jointly learning from cross-view urban imagery to enhance the comprehensive understanding
246   of urban visual knowledge. For instance, when predicting the geo-location of a satellite image,
247   region-level visual information alone may be insufficient. Supplementing detailed street-view infor-
248   mation can provide the necessary contextual knowledge for more accurate predictions.
249
      Motivated by this, we introduce an urban context-based interleaved training paradigm tailored for
250
      urban understanding tasks. The core of this part is the construction of multimodal interleaved ur-
251   ban data as the training corpus. We first collect a large scale satellite-view and street-view im-
252   agery individually across the United States, and perform cross-view matching based on geotags
253   (including located county, longitude and latitude), creating a paired cross-view urban imagery set

455   4.3   E VALUATION ON U RBAN V IEW B ENCHMARK
456
457   We evaluate our model with different open-source and closed-source MLLMs on our benchmark.
458   We tested various models with the same set of questions on the same dataset. Due to differences in
459   the models’ ability to follow instructions, many do not provide answers exactly matching the ground
460   truth but instead include additional explanatory text. Therefore, we consider a response correct as
461
      long as it contains the correct answer.
462   As shown in Table 2, 3 4 and Figure 4, our benchmark reveals the key challenges and limitations of
463   current MLLMs in real-world urban environments. The results show that most advanced MLLMs
464   do not perform well in satellite and street view tasks. For satellite view tasks, the top-performing
465   closed-source models, such as GPT-4o and another leading model,InternVL-2-40B, achieved only
466   52.4% and 54.2% on average across various metrics. On street view imagery, their performance is
467
      similarly limited, with average scores of 72.2% and 67.4%, respectively. This discrepancy is because
      most of our images is collected recently, while the training data for these MLLMs generally lacks
468
      similar real-world data (Wang et al., 2024). Furthermore, many current MLLMs do not yet support
469
      multi-image inputs, and those do rarely handle tasks involving joint cross-view predictions for urban
470   understanding. Consequently, this benchmark clearly highlights the limitations of advanced models,
471   showing their challenges in performing well on urban understanding tasks, especially with street
472   view and remote sensing images, and in joint cross-view prediction tasks.

535   In this paper, we propose UrbanMLLM, a novel multimodal large language model designed to jointly
536   learn from remote sensing and street-view imagery for comprehensive urban understanding. By
537   leveraging a large-scale cross-view dataset and a cross-view perceiver architecture, UrbanMLLM
538   effectively captures complementary information from satellite-view and street-view. Our model out-
539   performs existing MLLMs, achieving significant improvements across various urban understanding
      tasks.

      A.1     E THICS
705
706
      Our model uses a large amount of satellite and street view images, which poses a potential risk
707   to individual privacy. While the resolution of the satellite imagery is not high enough to identify
708   individuals, it can still detect environmental changes resulting from human activity. The street view
709   images were crawled and downloaded from the Google platform, with key information blurred,
710   ensuring that no private information is compromised.
711
      Our model is designed to better understand cities from cross views by incorporating various data
712
      types to enhance its understanding capabilities. To minimize the misuse of our model and data, we
713   will release the dataset and trained model only to those who agree to adhere to ethical guidelines.
714   By following these guidelines, users agree to comply with laws, regulations, and the ASPRS Code
715   of Ethics. It is also important to note that the data used for this training is already freely available
716   and public, so our model does not exacerbate privacy concerns.

992    A.6.2    DATASET S TRUCTURE AND C ONSTRCUTION
993
994    We first build a large-scale cross-view interleaved pretraining dataset. For each census tract, we
995    match the coordinate between satellite and street view imagery. Since we collect all satellite images
996    in the United States, each street view image can find a match. However, in order to control the size
997    of inputs, at most 5 street view images are matched with single satellite image. For the next step, we
998    use a powerful open-source MLLM, InternVL2-40B, to generate detailed descriptive captions for
999
       them. Using a similar data structure in MMC4 (Zhu et al., 2024), the county name and coordinates
       of the satellite image are also embedded to the interleaved pre-training data, together with imagery
1000
       and caption embeddings.
1001
1002   We use a Human-AI mixture method for pre-training caption quality validation. We first use two
1003   powerful open-source MLLM, VILA-1.5-40B and LLaVA-Next-34B to judge if the caption matches
1004   with the image. If either of them thinks it is not a match, we will proceed to send this case to GPT-4o,
1005
       which has state-of-the-art comprehension ability, but not quite affordable for large-scale deployment.
       If GPT-4o also thinks there is a problem with the case, graduate-level human-being will manually
1006
       check this case to give the final judgement. In order to quantify the caption quality improvement,
1007
       we further use GPT-4o to regenerate captions for excluded images with human assistance to test the
1008   quality improvement. We use CLIP-Score as the evaluation metric and calculate our original caption
1009   score and cleaned caption score of 10,000 samples, resulting 29.99535 and 29.99571 respectively.
1010   As a matter of fact, the original caption quality is good enough and only about 1.3 out of a thousand
1011   images is marked as unmatched by two-stage MLLM verification, and the regeneration process
1012   enhances the caption quality only by 0.0012%.
1013
       Then we construct the instruct tuning dataset, which is categorized into 3 major types: satellite-
1014   only, street view-only, and cross-view data. Street view images offer ground-level data of various
1015   environments, including urban and rural areas. These images provide detailed features of the envi-
1016   ronment. In contrast, satellite imagery complements this by providing top-down views, capturing a
1017   overall perception of the entire landscape. In UrbanView dataset, not only satellite and street view
1018   images linked respectively with diverse tasks such as scene classification, object reasoning, spatial
1019   relationship reasoning, cross-view combinational tasks of socioeconomic prediction and image re-
1020   trieval are delicately designed to further enhance MLLM’s comprehensive understanding of urban
1021   environment.
1022   In the construction of UrbanView dataset, a lot of street view tasks are in lack of groundtruth labels.
1023   Therefore, we use light-weight object detection specialized model to generate groundtruth bounding
1024   boxes for object reasoning tasks, and use powerful open-source MLLMs to identify the scene class
1025   and spatial relationship of the street view image. For geo-localization tasks, we simply use latitude
       and longitude of the images to match the boundaries of census tracts in the United States.

1049

1050   ing DINO (Liu et al., 2023), which has shown state-of-the-art ability on open vocabulary object
1051   detection. We further process the bounding box and object name results given by Grounding DINO
1052   to build object presence and counting dataset.
1053   Landmark Recognition (LR): Select the correct landmark name shown in the image. We use im-
1054   ages from google landmarks dataset v2, and select the street view images in the dataset via LLaVA-
1055   Next-34B. Multiple choice questions are made based on the correct landmark name and three dis-
1056   traction landmark names.
1057   Spatial Relationship Reasoning (SRR): Select the correct object relationship displayed in the im-
1058

1059
       subject name, such as ”What is the relationship between girl and computer?”. The three distrac-
1060
       tion choices are generated by InternVL2-40B based on the image provided with factually incorrect
1061   relationships. We also attempt to use other MLLM for this distractor generation task, including
1062   LLaVA-Next-34B and Vila-1.5, but InternVL2-40B is the one that generates the most reasonable
1063   distractors.
1064   Geo-Localization (GL): Select which county this image belongs to. We use the latitude and lon-
1065   gitude coordinate of each image to find the corresponding county it belongs to. Only the most
1066   populated 100 counties in the United States are taken account of. We also use multiple choices for-
1067   mat for this task type, and the distraction choices are randomly chose from the 100 counties.
1068   Indicator Prediction (IP): Predict the beautiful, wealthy and depressing level of the image from a
1069
       level of 0.0 to 9.9. We use Place Pulse 2.0 dataset, which let human to make comparison between
       two images in multiple dimensions. Then a ranking algorithm is used to assign ground-truth labels
1070
       for the images, and we ask the MLLMs to give a direct estimation in this range.
