009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Large Language Models (LLMs) have demonstrated impressive performance across
012            a wide range of applications; however, assessing their reasoning capabilities re-
013            mains a significant challenge. In this paper, we introduce a framework grounded
014            in group and symmetry principles, which have played a crucial role in fields such
015            as physics and mathematics, and offer another way to evaluate their capabilities.
016            While the proposed framework is general, to showcase the benefits of employing
017            these properties, we focus on arithmetic reasoning and investigate the performance
018
               of these models on four group properties: closure, identity, inverse, and associa-
               tivity. Our findings reveal that LLMs studied in this work struggle to preserve
019
               group properties across different test regimes. In the closure test, we observe biases
020
               towards specific outputs and an abrupt degradation in their performance from 100%
021            to 0% after a specific sequence length. They also perform poorly in the identity test,
022            which represents adding irrelevant information in the context, and show sensitivity
023            when subjected to inverse test, which examines the robustness of the model with
024            respect to negation. In addition, we demonstrate that breaking down problems
025            into smaller steps helps LLMs in the associativity test that we have conducted. To
026            support these tests we have developed a synthetic dataset which will be released.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Large Language Models (LLMs) have shown remarkable capabilities across various domains, serving
032   as the foundation for numerous applications Anil et al. (2023); Bubeck et al. (2023); OpenAI (2023);
033
      Brown et al. (2020); Kojima et al. (2022); Chowdhery et al. (2022); Saparov & He (2022).
034   Additionally, several studies have explored various aspects of the reasoning capabilities of LLMs
035   showing significant performance improvements compared to previous domain specific models Kojima
036   et al. (2022); Wang et al. (2022); Mukherjee et al. (2023); Mitra et al. (2023); Shen et al. (2023).
037   These studies have highlighted LLMs being able to understand and solve complex problems. Despite
038   comprehensive evaluation on various benchmarks, LLMs still might fail in unpredictable ways. In
039
      this work, we propose a simple yet helpful framework to study LLMs behavior from a different
      perspective grounded in group and symmetry principles.
040
041   Group and symmetry principles which made significant contributions to numerous fields, such as
042   physics, mathematics, and chemistry Sarlet & Cantrijn (1981); Jaffé & Orchin (2002); Hargittai &
043   Hargittai (2009); Fujita (2012); Gazizov & Ibragimov (1998); Weyl (2015); Polak & Trivers (1994).
044   These principles are foundation for understanding the fundamental structure of laws and the behavior
045
      of complex systems. For instance, in physics, group and symmetry principles have played a pivotal
      role in shaping modern physics by offering profound insights into the fundamental structure of
046
      physical laws and the behavior of physical systems. Noether’s theorem serves as a prime example,
047
      stating that every differentiable symmetry of the action of a physical system with conservative forces
048   has a corresponding conservation law Kosmann-Schwarzbach et al. (2011); Brading & Brown (2003).
049   As another example, temporal translation symmetry states that the laws of physics remain unchanged
050   over time. This invariance of physical laws under time translation leads to the conservation of energy.
051   This principle is essential to our understanding of various physical phenomena and is widely applied
052   across numerous disciplines, including mechanics, thermodynamics, and electromagnetism Gildener
053   (1976); Aharonov et al. (1964); Sasa & Yokokura (2016); Lewis (1930). Other group and symmetry
      principles in physics, including translation, rotation, and gauge symmetries, have contributed to the

      discovery of conservation laws and the formulation of fundamental theories like the Standard Model
055   of particle physics Weinberg (1969); Feynman & Weinberg (1999). These principles have also been
056   instrumental in understanding the behavior of complex systems, such as condensed matter systems
057   and cosmological models Zee (2010); Anderson (1972).

      Exploring the role of group and symmetry principles in LLMs can lead to:
059
060
      • Alternative Perspective to Measure LLMs’ Performance: Investigating the role of symmetry in
061
        LLMs can provide new strategies for evaluating their performance, potentially leading to better
062     learning methods. The significance of symmetry principles and group properties in these domains
063     can be exemplified by the Winoground task and dataset Thrush et al. (2022), which evaluates
064     the ability of vision and language models to conduct visio-linguistic compositional reasoning by
065     matching images with captions containing identical sets of words in different orders. For example,
066     given an image and two captions, ‘an old person helping a young person’ and ‘a young person
067     helping an old person’ a model that understands the non-symmetric nature of these sentences and
068     leverages group properties can provide the correct answer Thrush et al. (2022); Lin et al. (2023).
069     By connecting the role of symmetry and group properties in LLMs to the reasoning capabilities
070
        required for tasks like Winoground, we can develop a more comprehensive understanding of model
        performance and devise improved learning methods.
071
072   • Insights for Interpretability: Studying group properties and symmetry principles in LLMs can lead
073     to more interpretable models, similar to the insights gained from saliency maps. This method is
074     based on the idea that if a minor perturbation in the input does not alter the meaning but results in a
075     different output, the perturbed portion of the input is crucial for the model’s prediction Simonyan
076
        et al. (2013); Sundararajan et al. (2017); Zeiler & Fergus (2014). These principles help uncover
        hidden relationships and structures within the data, thereby facilitating improved decision-making
077
        and trust in the model’s predictions. For example, in an identity test, introducing various symmetries,
078
        such as inverse symmetry, to perturb the input query causes the output result to change. This is
079     similar to obtaining a saliency map by perturbing the prompt query and feeding it to the LLM.
080     While saliency maps and symmetry principles share similarities in their focus on understanding the
081     importance of different input elements, their approaches differ. Saliency maps rely on perturbations
082     to identify crucial input components, whereas symmetry principles and group properties delve into
083     the inherent structure and relationships within the data. Group properties investigate the algebraic
084     structures governing the interactions between data elements, such as the presence of an identity
085     element, the existence of inverses, and the associative property. In addition, symmetry principles
086     examine how certain aspects of the data remain unchanged or exhibit similar patterns under specific
087
        transformations, including rotations, reflections, and translations. By combining these approaches,
        a more comprehensive understanding of the data’s underlying organization can be achieved.
088
089   • Robustness, Reliability: Analyzing the influence of group properties on LLMs can aid in designing
090     ensemble strategies that combine the predictions of multiple LLM’s outputs, to enhance overall
091     robustness and generalization. Additionally, it can help to better examine the confidence of LLM’s
092
        output. For instance, using the closure, inverse, identity, and associativity tests, we observe that
        LLMs studied in this work fail for sequences longer than 15 elements, and the confidence level
093
        decreases as we increase the length of the sequence in the simple addition task.
094
095   • Generalization across Domains: Incorporating symmetry and group properties into LLMs can
096     improve their generalization capabilities across various domains such as chemistry. For example,
097     for a language model that is trained to predict the products of a reaction A + B → C + D,
098
        by understanding that the reaction B + A should have a similar prediction as A + B due to
        the symmetric nature of the reactants, the model can leverage this knowledge to improve its
099
        performance on reaction prediction tasks. The understanding of symmetry and group properties
100
        in this case allows the language model to recognize the invariance of reaction outcomes under
101     different reactant orders, leading to more consistent predictions of chemical reactions.
102
103   Our primary focus in this study is to assess performance of LLMs in arithmetic reasoning tasks using
104   these principles. Our goal is NOT to show that they have good or bad performance in arithmetic
105   operations as this has already been studied in previous studies Kojima et al. (2022); Mitra et al. (2023);
106   Wang et al. (2022), but to use them as scenarios to show the added value of using group properties for
107   analysis. We investigate the performance of GPT-4 and GPT-3.5 on four group properties: closure,
      identity, inverse, and associativity. Our main findings are:

123       The authors in Shi et al. (2023) demonstrate that even with a variety of prompting techniques on the
124       GSM-IC dataset the models are all sensitive to irrelevant information in the input. By leveraging
125       group properties, specifically identity test, we can assess the robustness of the model in a more
126       controlled and cost effective manner.
127   • In the identity test, we observed that the accuracy of models such as GPT-4 and GPT-3.5 decreases
128     when zeros are inserted into the summation of ones. The performance decline follows this order:
129     adding irrelevant information randomly, insertion in the middle, adding irrelevant information at
130     the beginning, and lastly, at the end. Our results align with Liu et al. (2023), which demonstrated
131
        that changing the position of relevant information within the input context of language models
        leads to variations in performance. This finding suggests that introducing irrelevant elements at
132
        various positions within natural language sequences can pose a significant challenge for models
133
        like GPT-4 or GPT-3.5. However, employing group properties and simple tests, such as addition,
134     can aid in validating and identifying these issues early on with less computation costs.
135
      • In our analysis of the inverse properties within group properties, we found that the LLMs inves-
136
        tigated in this work display sensitivity when subjected to inverse tests. Our inverse test consists
137
        of adding negative ones to the sum of ones, yielding a final result of zero. Inverse properties bear
138     resemblance to the introduction of negation in natural language. Similarly, the study by Truong
139     et al. (2023) highlights that LLMs exhibit multiple limitations in handling negation, including an
140     inability to reason effectively under negation. By implementing the test framework proposed in
141     this study, these issues can be identified in advance in a given LLM.
142
      • In the associativity test, we decompose each summation into smaller steps for the model to process.
143     Our findings indicate that GPT-4 demonstrates a significant improvement in performing addition
144     tasks compared to GPT-3.5 when problems are broken down into smaller components. Our results
145     highlight the importance of decomposing problems into smaller, more manageable pieces that can
146     be effectively solved by the model which is reported using more complex tasks in Kojima et al.
147     (2022). By employing group analysis, it is possible to identify and address these issues through the
148     implementation of straightforward tests proposed in this work.

150   2     S YMMETRY IN LLM S
151
152   Symmetry and invariance are fundamental concepts in understanding the behavior of systems Good-
153   man et al. (2009). An object or quantity is said to be invariant if it remains unchanged under transfor-
154   mations Kosmann-Schwarzbach et al. (2011). Consider an arbitrary quantity F = F (A, B, C, ...)
155   that depends on different quantities. If we transform A, B, C, ... to their respective primed variables
156   A′ , B ′ , C ′ , ..., and we have
157
                                        F (A′ , B ′ , C ′ , ...) = F (A, B, C, ...)                         (1)
158
159
      then F is said to be invariant under the given transformation.
160
161   Invariance can also be described through the concept of symmetry. A system is symmetric if it
      remains the same after a transformation or class of transformations. For instance, a physical system


                                                            3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      is symmetric under rotations if it can be rotated in any direction and remains unchanged. The set
163   of all transformations that leave a given object invariant is called a symmetry group Olver (1995);
164   Goodman et al. (2009).
165
166
      Throughout this paper, we will use the following symmetries to investigate the capabilities of LLMs:
167   Translation Symmetry: This type of symmetry refers to the invariance of a system or pattern under
168   a spatial transformation, such as shifting or sliding. In the context of LLMs, translation symmetry can
169   help us understand how the models respond to changes in the position of elements within a sequence
170   Weyl (2015).
171   Random Swapping Symmetry: Also known as permutation symmetry, this concept involves the
172   invariance of a system or pattern under the exchange of its elements. In our study, we will use random
173   swapping symmetry to analyze how LLMs handle rearrangements of input elements, providing
174   insights into their robustness and generalization capabilities French & Rickles (2003).
175
      Inverse Symmetry: This type of symmetry is characterized by the invariance of a system or pattern
176   under an operation that reverses its elements or their order. In the context of LLMs, we will explore
177   how the models perform when presented with inputs that have been transformed using inverse
178   symmetry, shedding light on their ability to recognize and process different representations of the
179   same information Morandi et al. (1990).
180
      To better understand the implications of these symmetries, we can examine them within the context of
181
      group theory. A group is a set G and binary operator ‘◦’ that satisfies closure, identity, inverses, and
182   associativity Aschbacher (2000); de La Harpe (2000). A group is a set G and operator ◦ such that:
183
184   • Closure: G is closed under ◦; i.e., if a, b ∈ G, then a ◦ b ∈ G.
185   • Identity: There exists an identity element e ∈ G; i.e., for all a ∈ G we have a ◦ e = e ◦ a = a.
186   • Inverses: Every element a ∈ G has an inverse in G; i.e., for all a ∈ G, there exists an element
187     a′ ∈ G such that a ◦ a′ = a′ ◦ a = e.
188   • Associativity: The operator ◦ acts associatively; i.e., for all a, b, c ∈ G, a ◦ (b ◦ c) = (a ◦ b) ◦ c.
189
      Group principles are essential in the laws of nature, as they encapsulate regularities that remain
190   consistent regardless of specific dynamics. These principles impart structure and coherence to natural
191   laws, enabling a better understanding of physical events and the discovery of the laws themselves.
192   Schwichtenberg (2018); Lax (2001); Tung (1985); Bishop (1993); Golubitsky & Stewart (2003).
193
      Our goal is to conduct a comprehensive analysis for a given LLM to determine whether they maintain
194
      group properties and symmetry principles. In this investigation, we will cover a broad spectrum
195
      of symmetries, including but not limited to rotational, translation, scaling, swapping, and inverse
196   symmetries. Examining group properties can help us understand the fundamental characteristics of
197   the LLMs and potentially improve their performance in reasoning tasks due to the following factors:
198
199   • Identifying patterns: By leveraging group properties, LLMs can effectively identify and analyze
200     underlying patterns and relationships within a problem, enhancing their problem-solving capabili-
201     ties. For instance, the set of even numbers can be considered as a group, which enables an LLM to
202
        unveil underlying connections and patterns among them. For instance, it can determine that the sum
        of two even numbers is always even. By recognizing these patterns, the LLM can make accurate
203
        predictions and solve problems related to number sets and arithmetic operations more efficiently.
204
205
      • Comprehending problem structure: Investigating group properties can potentially contribute to
        address complex reasoning tasks more efficiently. For instance, recognizing the group properties of
206
        integers under addition operator allows LLMs to devise strategic approaches to solve arithmetic
207
        tasks. When solving for the sum of a series of integers, the LLM can employ associativity to
208     rearrange terms, thereby simplifying the problem. Additionally, recognizing the role of identity
209     elements and inverses can help the LLM quickly identify shortcuts or eliminate unnecessary
210     calculations, leading to more efficient problem-solving and accurate results.
211
      • Reliable solutions: The utilization of group properties in reasoning tasks contributes to the genera-
212     tion of solutions that are both accurate and reliable, ensuring high-quality outcomes. For instance,
213     if an LLM preserves the group properties of addition on a list of integers, it suggests that the LLM
214     has a good understanding of the concept of addition operation.
215
      Our contributions in this work are:

      3.1       G ROUP OF A DDITION ON A L IST OF I NTEGERS
227
      Throughout the paper, we use the addition operator as the running example example to demonstrate
228   our framework. We evaluate whether a given LLM maintains the group properties of integer addition.
229   We propose the following experimental approach.
230
          Closure Test
231
                   1. Create a list of integers, such as [1, 2, 3].
232
                   2. Calculate the sum of the list, which in this case is 6.
233
                   3. Apply a symmetry operation to the list, such as swapping the first and last elements. This results in a new list like [3, 2, 1].
234
                   4. Request the LLM to calculate the sum of the modified list.
235
                   5. If the LLM consistently yields the same integer result as the original list (6 in this example), it implies that the LLM preserves
236
                      the closure property of the group.
237
238       Identity Test
239
                   1. Append zeroes to the list, resulting in a new list like [1, 2, 3, 0, 0].
240
                   2. Calculate the sum of the new list, which should be the same as the original list (in this case, 6).
241
                   3. Perform a symmetry operation on the new list, such as reversing it to get [0, 0, 3, 2, 1].
242
                   4. Ask the LLM to compute the sum of the modified list.
243
                   5. If the LLM consistently produces the same sum as the original list (6 in this example), it suggests that the LLM preserves the
244                   identity property of the group.
245
246       Inverse Test
247                1. Generate a new list where each integer is replaced by its negation, resulting in a list like [-1, -2, -3].
248                2. Perform a symmetry operation on the new list, such as swapping the first and last elements to get [-3, -2, -1].
249                3. Ask the LLM to compute the sum of the two lists ([1, 2, 3] and [-3, -2, -1]).
250                4. If the LLM consistently produces a sum of 0, it suggests that the LLM preserves the inverse property of the group.
251
252       Associativity Test
253
                   1. Split the list into sublists in different ways, for example, [[1], [2, 3]] and [[1, 3], [2]].
254
                   2. Perform a symmetry operation on each sublist, such as reversing each sublist. This results in new arrangements like [[1], [3,
255                   2]] and [[3, 1], [2]].
256                3. Ask the LLM to compute the sum for each arrangement.
257                4. If the LLM consistently produces the same result for all arrangements (6 in this example, which is the sum of the original list),
258                   it suggests that the LLM preserves the associativity property of the group.

259
      These tests ensure to check if a given LLM not only understands the concept of addition and the
260
      associated group properties but also the symmetries inherent in the operation.

264   We present the results of our experiments on the summation group. The summation of integer
265   values forms a group because the sum of any two integers is also an integer, thereby satisfying
266   the closure property. The identity element in this group is zero, and the inverse of a value is its
267   negative counterpart. Furthermore, the summation of integers preserves the associativity property. To
268   assess the group properties using the summation operator, we work with a set of simple arithmetic
269   expressions containing only the elements one, zero (serving as the identity element), and negative one
      (acting as the inverse element). We then proceed to investigate all the group properties within these

      3.6    A SSOCIATIVITY T EST
433
434   In this section we investigate the group’s associativity properties. This test is crucial because if LLMs
435   can understand associativity properties, it implies that they can simplify problems by decomposing
436   them down into smaller components and solving them. Another benefit of analyzing this property is
437   we can mitigate possible biases for example in a case like the summation of values near 100 where
438   LLMs produce an incorrect output of 100, the associativity properties can be used to break down the
439   problem into smaller components and mitigate the effect of biases toward these values.
440   For the associativity test, the expressions from the closure test have been broken down into smaller
441   components. We have decomposed the original expression into smaller segments with ratios of 3/8
442   and 5/8, referred to as test 1, and into segments with ratios of 1/4 and 3/4, referred to as test 2. For
443   each ratio, the query is divided into two segments and each segment has been passed to the LLM
444   to obtain the result. Then, the outputs of the two segments are provided and the LLM is queried to
445   obtain the final result. For example, for the query 1 + 1 + 1 + 1 + 1 + 1 with test 2, we break it down
446
      into two segments: 1 and 1 + 1 + 1 + 1, and pass each one to the model. Suppose the LLMs’ output
      for each query is 1 and 4; then, we will pass 1 + 4 to the LLMs one last time to obtain the final result.
447
      We will repeat each experiment 10 times and report the average accuracy. Figure 5 shows the results
448
      of the associativity test. We can derive several insights from this experiment:
449
450   • Breaking down problems into smaller sub sequences improves the accuracy of LLMs.
451
      • Segmenting and breaking down problems using test 1 yields better results for LLMs. This is
452
        reasonable since the other test creates shorter and longer segments, with the longer ones being
453
        more difficult for LLMs to solve, leading to a higher number of incorrect answers overall.
454
455   • LLMs fail to preserve associativity beyond a certain point, as they also fail the closure test.

      4    C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
513
514   In this study, we introduced a framework for testing the behavior of large language models based
515   on group and symmetry principles. Our experiments involved GPT-4 and GPT-3.5, examining their
516   performance on four group properties: closure, identity, inverse, and associativity. The results
517   indicated that they face challenges in maintaining group properties under various circumstances. We
518   also showed that similar behavior is observed for small language models like Llama2, Mistral, Vicuna
519   and Phi2. The models showed significant performance drop across all tests, which might be attributed
520   to the insufficient memory retention with respect to context. For example, after a sequence length of
521   15 for identity test and sequence length of 50 for closure, performance becomes 0%. We conducted
522
      tests with both open-weight and closed-weight models, finding that although different tokenizers
      may contribute to the problem, the root cause could extend beyond better tokenization strategies. A
523
      good direction for future work is exploring whether these tests can provide insights and be used as a
524
      predictor for model’s performance on real-world language understanding and generation tasks.

      or why they seem to favor certain numbers. Understanding these patterns is critical for making LLMs
