009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            The Transformer architecture has become widely adopted due to its demonstrated
012            success, attributed to the attention mechanism at its core. Despite these successes,
013            the attention mechanism of Transformers is associated with two well-known is-
014            sues: rank-collapse and gradient vanishing. In this paper, we present a theoret-
015            ical analysis that it is inherently difficult to address both issues simultaneously
016            in the conventional attention mechanism. To handle these issues, we introduce a
017            novel class of attention mechanism, referred to as generalized probabilistic atten-
018
               tion mechanism (GPAM), and its dual-attention implementation within the Trans-
               former architecture. Unlike conventional attention mechanisms, GPAM allows for
019
               negative attention scores while preserving a fixed total sum. We provide theoret-
020
               ical evidence that the proposed dual-attention GPAM (daGPAM) effectively mit-
021            igates both the rank-collapse and gradient vanishing issues which are difficult to
022            resolve simultaneously with the conventional attention mechanisms. Furthermore,
023            we empirically validate this theoretical evidence, demonstrating the superiority of
024            daGPAM compared to other alternative attention mechanisms that were proposed
025            to address the same issues. Additionally, we demonstrate the practical benefits
026            of GPAM in natural language processing tasks, such as language modeling and
027            neural machine translation.

      In contrast to prior approaches, we posit that the underlying issues stem from the convex combi-
084
      nation structure in the conventional attention mechanism. The normalized attention scores, Pij , as
085   defined in Eq.2, are non-negative and sum to 1 along the query axis, making the conventional atten-
086   tion mechanism a valid convex combination of input representations. Consequently, as illustrated
087   in Fig.1, the output representations, Yc , are constrained within the convex hull (yellow plane) of
088   the input value representations. This fundamental constraint limits the diversity of output represen-
089   tations, causing them to become more similar to one another than the input value representations,
090   thereby intensifying the rank-collapse problem.
091   This consideration naturally raised the question: how about making it an affine combination? The
092   affine combination is a generalization of the convex combination, allowing the normalized attention
093   scores to take negative values while still maintaining a total sum to be 1. As depicted in Fig.1,
094   the output representations, Ya , are no longer constrained to lie within the convex hull, but instead
095   within the affine hull (green plane), thereby removing the fundamental constraint that can lead to
096   rank-collapse. However, from a probabilistic perspective, the notion of negative normalized atten-
097   tion scores may seem unconventional, since negative probability is unfamiliar concept. Nevertheless,
098   the discussions by prominent physicists, such as P. Dirac (Dirac, 1942) and R. Feynman (Feynman,

      It is important to note that the first condition of generalized probability allows for the possibility of
109   negative probabilities. Building on this long-standing line of research, we introduce the attention
110   mechanism based on the affine (or scaled-affine) combination, which we refer to as the generalized
111   probabilistic attention mechanism (GPAM). As a generalized framework, GPAM encompasses the
112   conventional attention mechanism as a special case where all attention values are non-negative.
113
      In this paper, we explore GPAM in the Transformer architecture, and as a cornerstone design of
114
      GPAM, we propose a dual-attention design that facilitates GPAM with adding only a negligible
115   number of parameters. Specifically, we add an additional attention matrix computation to the orig-
116   inal scaled dot-product self-(or cross-) attention mechanism with a small additional weight matrix
117   which increases less than 1% of total parameters on average. Then, the resulting two attention matri-
118   ces are treated as positive and negative parts of the final attention score, respectively. By combining
119   these two attention scores with pre-defined or trainable scalar weights, we ensure that our proposed
120   dual-attention GPAM (daGPAM) a valid affine (or scaled-affine) combination. Fig.2 illustrates the
121   structure of this design. When we propose a GPAM method, we show theoretically that our method
122   is advantageous for mitigating not only the rank-collapse problem, but also the gradient vanishing
123
      problem. Our empirical validations give evidences for the above theories in various aspects. In
      addition, we experimentally demonstrate and explain the superiority of daGPAM compared to other
124
      alternative attention mechanisms that was proposed to address the mentioned problems. Finally, we
125
      demonstrate the benefits of daGPAM in benchmark experiments in tasks such as language modeling
126   (LM) and neural machine translation (NMT).

194

196

197

      5.3       C OMPARISON BETWEEN OTHER A LTERNATIVE ATTENTION M ECHANISMS

      In this section, we compare the performance of daGPAM with other alternative attention mecha-
444   nisms discussed in Section 2.3. We specifically focus on analyzing each mechanism’s adherence
445   to the generalized probability conditions and its corresponding performance, in order to highlight
446   the significance of adhering these conditions. For a fair comparison, we re-implemented all alterna-
447   tive attention mechanisms and applied them to the Transformer baseline, replacing the conventional
448   attention mechanism in the same manner as with daGPAM.
449
      Table 1 presents the results of various attention mechanisms trained on the PTB LM task, evaluated
450   using perplexity (PPL). Our daGPAM models resulted in approximately 1% increases in parame-
451   ters. Each model is labeled to indicate whether it adheres to the generalized probability conditions,
452   specifically finite range and a fixed total sum of normalized attention scores. The models which
453   do not adhere to the conditions exhibit significant performance degradation. Conversely, with the
454   exception of ‘Sin-Softmax,’ most models that adhered to both conditions performed similarly to the
455   baseline Transformer. Although the ‘ValueSkipInit’ and ‘Shaped’ models did not degrade perfor-
456   mance, they also failed to provide improvements, because these methods regularize the attention
457   matrix to be similar to the identity matrix, thereby weakening the contextualization effect. In con-
458
      trast, daGPAM enhances the attention mechanism itself, leading to improved performance in the
      ‘daGPAM (Trainable λs)’ model.

      7     C ONCLUSION
522
523
      In this paper, we proposed a novel class of attention mechanism, generalized probabilistic attention
524
      mechanism (GPAM), which allows the negative attention score during the information processing.
525   Also, we proposed one specific type of GPAM, dual-attention GPAM (daGPAM). While showing
526   that the rank-collapse and gradient vanishing problems in the conventional attention mechanism is
527   in trade-off relationship, we showed that daGPAM could mitigate both problems. Our empirical
528   validations provide strong evidences supporting our theories and understanding of this approach.
529   Additionally, our benchmark experiments demonstrate meaningful performance improvements with
530   only a minimal increase in the number of parameters.
531
532   8     F UTURE W ORKS
533
534   Although we proposed only one structure of GPAM (daGPAM), GPAM does not have to be lim-
535   ited to the structure. Actually, the operation of daGPAM increases computational cost due to the
536   added scaled dot-product attention process of negative part. In future, we aim to develop a more
537   efficient structure than daGPAM while preserving the feature of GPAM. Also, beyond the standard
538   Transformer architecture discussed here, GPAM can be applied to other architectures, such as graph
539   neural networks and vision Transformer, as they are also known to experience the rank-collapse
      problem.
