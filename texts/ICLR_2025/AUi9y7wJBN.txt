011                                              A BSTRACT
012
013            Recently, pre-trained models with phonetic supervision have demonstrated their
014            advantages for crosslingual speech recognition in data efficiency and informa-
015
               tion sharing across languages. The Whistle approach relaxes the requirement of
               gold-standard human-validated phonetic transcripts and adopts weakly-phonetic
016
               supervision; however, a limitation is that a pronunciation lexicon is needed for
017
               such phoneme-based crosslingual speech recognition. In this study, we aim to
018            eliminate the need for the pronunciation lexicon and propose a latent variable
019            model based method, with phonemes being treated as discrete latent variables.
020            The new method consists of a speech-to-phoneme (S2P) model and a phoneme-to-
021            grapheme (P2G) model, and a grapheme-to-phoneme (G2P) model is introduced
022            as an auxiliary inference model. To jointly train the three models, we utilize the
023            joint stochastic approximation (JSA) algorithm, which is a stochastic extension
024            of the EM (expectation-maximization) algorithm and has demonstrated superior
025            performances particularly in estimating discrete latent variable models. Based
026
               on the Whistle multilingual pre-trained S2P model, crosslingual experiments on
               Polish (130h) and Indonesian (20h) are conducted. By using only 10 minutes of
027
               phoneme supervision, the new method, called as Whistle-JSA, performs close to
028
               crosslingual fine-tuning with the full set of phoneme supervision, and on par with
029            the method of crosslingual fine-tuning with subword supervision. Furthermore, it
030            is found that in language domain adaptation (i.e., utilizing cross-domain text-only
031            data), Whistle-JSA outperforms the standard practice of language model fusion
032            via the auxiliary support of the G2P model.

035   1   I NTRODUCTION
036
037   In recent years, automatic speech recognition (ASR) systems based on deep neural networks (DNNs)
038   have made significant strides, which benefit from large amounts of transcribed speech data. Remark-
039
      ably, more than 7,000 languages are spoken worldwide (Ethnologue, 2019), and most of them are
      low-resourced languages. A pressing challenge for the speech community is to develop ASR sys-
040
      tems for new, unsupported languages rapidly and cost-effectively. Crosslingual ASR have been
041
      explored as a promising solution to bridge this gap (Schultz & Waibel, 1998; Conneau et al., 2021;
042   Babu et al., 2021; Zhu et al., 2021).
043
044   In crosslingual speech recognition, a pre-trained multilingual model is fine-tuned to recognize utter-
045
      ances from a new, target language, which is unseen in training the multilingual model. In this way,
      crosslingual speech recognition could achieve knowledge transfer from the pre-trained multilingual
046
      model to the target model, thereby reducing reliance on transcribed data and becoming one of the
047
      effective solutions for low-resource speech recognition. Most recent research on pre-training for
048   cross-lingual ASR can be classified into three categories - supervised pre-training with graphemic
049   transcription or phonetic transcription, and self-supervised pre-training. The pros and cons of the
050   three categories have recently been discussed in (Yusuyin et al., 2024). Under a common exper-
051   imental setup with respect to pre-training data size and neural architecture, it is further found in
052   (Yusuyin et al., 2024) that when crosslingual fine-tuning data is more limited, phoneme-based su-
053   pervised pre-training achieves the most competitive results and provides high data-efficiency. This
      makes sense since phonetic units such as described in International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA), are


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      exactly those sounds shared in human language throughout the world. In contrast, the methods us-
055   ing grapheme units face challenges in learning shared crosslingual representations due to a lack of
056   shared graphemes among different languages.
057
058
      A longstanding challenge in phoneme-based speech recognition is that phoneme labels are needed
      for each training utterance. Phoneme labels are usually obtained by using a manually-crafted pro-
059
      nunciation lexicon (PROLEXs), which maps every word in the vocabulary into a phoneme sequence.
060
      Grapheme-to-phoneme (G2P) tools have been developed to aid this process of labeling sentences
061   from their graphemic transcription into phonemes, but such tools are again created based on PRO-
062   LEXs. There are enduring efforts to compile PROLEXs and develop G2P tools (Novak et al., 2016;
063   Mortensen et al., 2018; Hasegawa-Johnson et al., 2020) for different languages. Overall, the exist-
064   ing approaches of phoneme-based ASR heavily depend on expert labor and are not scalable to be
065   applied to much more low-resource languages.
066
      In this paper we are interested in reducing the reliance on PROLEXs in building phoneme-based
067   crosslingual ASR systems, i.e., towards PROLEX free. In recognizing speech x into text y,
068   phonemes arise as intermediate states. So intuitively we propose to treat phonemes as hidden vari-
069   ables h, and construct a latent variable model (LVM) with pairs of speech and text (x, y) as observed
070   values. Basically, the whole model is a conditional generative model from Speech to Phonemes and
071   then to Graphemes, which is referred to as a SPG model, denoted by pθ (h, y|x). SPG consists a
072   speech-to-phoneme (S2P) model pθ (h|x) and a phoneme-to-grapheme (P2G) model pθ (y|h), and is
073   thus a two-stage model. Latent variable modeling enables us to train the SPG model, without the
074   need to knowing h, by maximizing marginal likelhood pθ (y|x). This is different from previous two-
075
      stage ASR model with phonemes as intermediate states, as reviewed later in Section 2. Learning
      latent-variable models usually involves introducing an auxiliary G2P model qϕ (h|y).
076
077   Method contribution. Note that phonemes take discrete values, and recently the joint stochastic
078   approximation (JSA) algorithm (Xu & Ou, 2016; Ou & Song, 2020) has emerged for learning dis-
079   crete latent variable models with impressive performance. In this paper we propose to apply JSA
080   to learn the SPG model, which is called the SPG-JSA approach in general. Particularly, we de-
081
      velop Whistle-JSA, a specific instantiation of SPG-JSA, where the S2P model is initialized from
      a pre-trained phoneme-based multilingual S2P backbone, called Whistle (Yusuyin et al., 2024). In
082
      practice, when viewing phonemes as labels, we combine supervised learning over 10 minutes of
083
      transcribed speech with weak phoneme labels and unsupervised learning over a much larger dataset
084   without phoneme labels. Bootstrapping from a good S2P backbone (like Whistle) and providing
085   few-shots samples of latent variables (such as 10 minutes of weak phoneme labels) is found to be
086   important to make SPG-JSA successfully work in the challenging task of crosslingual ASR.
087
      Experiment contribution. Crosslingual experiments on Polish (130h) and Indonesian (20h) are
088
      conducted. By using only 10 minutes of phoneme supervision, Whistle-JSA obtains close perfor-
089
      mance to crosslingual fine-tuning with the full set of phoneme supervision, and is on par with the
090   method of crosslingual fine-tuning with subword supervision. Furthermore, it is found that in lan-
091   guage domain adaptation (i.e., utilizing cross-domain text-only data), Whistle-JSA outperforms the
092   standard practice of language model fusion via the auxiliary support of the G2P model.

133   3   BACKGROUND
134
135   Consider a latent variable model pθ (h, y) for observation y and latent variable h, with parameter
136   θ. The joint stochastic approximation (JSA) algorithm (Xu & Ou, 2016; Ou & Song, 2020) is a
137
      stochastic extension of the EM algorithm (Dempster et al., 1977) and has demonstrated superior
      performances particularly in estimating discrete latent variable models. The annoying difficulty of
138
      propagating gradients through discrete latent variables is gracefully addressed in JSA.
139
140   Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm. The EM algorithm is an iterative method to find
141   maximum likelihood estimates of parameters for latent variable models. At iteration t, the E-step
142   calculates the Q-function Q(θ|θ(t−1) ) = Epθ(t−1) (h|y) [∇θ logpθ (h, y)] and the M-step updates θ by
143   maximizing Q(θ|θ(t−1) ) over θ or performing gradient ascent over θ when a closed-form solution
144   is not available. In the E-step, when the expectation in Q(θ|θ(t−1) ) cannot be tractably evaluated,
145   SAEM has been developed (Delyon et al., 1999).
146
      Stochastic Approximation Version of EM (SAEM). The SAEM algorithm iterates Monte Carlo
147   sampling and parameter updating. The expectation in the E-step is approximated via Monte Carlo
148   sampling h′ ∼ pθ(t−1) (h|y), where h′ looks like a stochastic pseudo label for the latent variable.
149   The parameter updating step performs gradient ascent over θ using ∇θ logpθ (h′ , y), analogous to
150   the M-step in EM.
151
      MCMC-SAEM. When exact sampling from pθ(t−1) (h|y) is difficult, an MCMC-SAEM algorithm
152
      has been developed (Kuhn & Lavielle, 2004). MCMC-SAEM draws a sample of the latent h by ap-
153   plying Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) which admits pθ(t−1) (h|y) as the invariant distribution.
154
155   Joint Stochastic Approximation (JSA). Given θ(t−1) , the MCMC step in classic MCMC-SAEM
156   is non-adaptive in the sense that the proposal of the transition kernel is fixed. In JSA, an auxiliary
157   amortized inference model qϕ (h|y) is introduced to approximate the intractable posterior pθ (h|y),
158
      which is used as the proposal for the MCMC step and adjusted from past realizations of the Markov
      chain targeting pθ(t−1) (h|y). So the JSA algorithm amounts to coupling MCMC-SAEM with an
159
      adaptive proposal.
160

195   4.1   M ODEL
196
197   Let (x, y) denote the pair of speech and text for an utterance. Specifically, x represents the speech
198   log-mel spectrogram and y the graphemic transcription of x. Let h denote the IPA phoneme se-
199   quence representing the pronunciation of x. In recognizing speech x into text y, we treat phonemes
200
      h as hidden variables, and construct a latent variable model, which can be decomposed as follows:
201                                         pθ (h, y|x) = pθ (h|x)pθ (y|h)
202   Basically, the whole model is a conditional generative model from Speech to Phonemes and then to
203   Graphemes, which is referred to as a SPG model. SPG consists a speech-to-phoneme (S2P) model
204   pθ (h|x) and a phoneme-to-grapheme (P2G) model pθ (y|h).
205
206   4.2   T RAINING
207
208   Training the SPG model from complete data, i.e., knowing h, can be easily realized by supervised
209   training. To train S2P and P2G end-to-end (i.e., conducting unsupervised training without knowing
210   h), we resort to maximizing the marginal likelihood pθ (y|x) and applying the JSA algorithm (Xu &
211   Ou, 2016; Ou & Song, 2020), which has emerged for learning discrete latent variable models with
212
      impressive performance.
213   The original JSA algorithm, described in Algorithm 1, is introduced in an unconditional form, but
214   can be straightforwardly applied in its conditional version, i.e., given x. JSA involves introducing
215   an auxiliary inference model to approximate the intractable posterior pθ (h|x, y), which, in the ASR
      task considered in this paper, is assumed to take the form of qϕ (h|y), i.e., a G2P model.

      Latent state caching. In (Ou & Song, 2020), it is found that a two-stage scheme yields fast learning
244   while ensuring convergence. For training with JSA, theoretically we need to cache a latent h-sample
245   for each training data-point to maintain a persistent Markov chain. Practically, we run a two-stage
246   scheme. In stage I, we run without caching, i.e. at each iteration, we accept the first proposed sample
247   from qϕ (y|x) as an initialization and then run MIS with multiple moves. After stage I, we switch
248   to running JSA in its standard manner. In our experiments, we carried out stage I until the model
249   converged, and then switched to stage II to continue training.
250   Whistle-JSA. The SPG-JSA algorithm is general and is in fact an unsupervised learning over (x, y)
251   without the need to know h. It is challenging to run this purely unsupervised form from scratch in
252   the ASR task considered in this paper, which involves very high-dimensional latent space. Two addi-
253   tional techniques are incorporated to add inductive bias into model training. First, the S2P model is
254   initialized from a pre-trained phoneme-based multilingual S2P backbone, called Whistle (Yusuyin
255   et al., 2024), which have been shown to have good phoneme classification ability. In our experi-
256   ments, the S2P, P2G, and G2P models are all implemented by CTC (Graves et al., 2006), which
257   will be more detailed in Section 5.2. Second, we assume that 10 minutes of transcribed speech with
258
      phoneme labels are available, which takes much less labor than compiling a complete PROLEX for
      a target language. Thus, we combine supervised learning over 10 minutes speech with phoneme
259
      labels and unsupervised learning over a much larger dataset without phoneme labels. Bootstrapping
260
      from a good S2P backbone (Whistle) and providing few-shots samples of latent variables (10 min-
261   utes of phoneme labels) is found to be important to make Whistle-JSA, as a specific instantiation of
262   the general SPG-JSA approach, successfully work in the challenging task of crosslingual ASR.

      5.2   SETUP
353
354   For phoneme-based models, both of the polish and Indonesian alphabet size of phonemes is 35. For
355   subword-based models, both of the polish and Indonesian alphabet size of subwords is 500. All text
356   normalization and phonemization strategies are consistent with the Whistle work (Yusuyin et al.,
357   2024). For each language, we use its transcripts to separately train a word-level n-gram language
358   model for WFST-based decoding.
359
      In the experiments, the S2P, P2G, and G2P models are all based on CTC. The Whistle-small 90M
360   pre-trained model 1 is used to initialize the S2P model. Both the G2P and P2G models use 8-layer
361   Transformer encoders with dimension 512. We set the self-attention layer to have 4 heads with 512-
362   dimension hidden states, and the feed-forward network (FFN) dimension to 1024. All experiment
363   are taken with the CAT toolkit (An et al., 2020). The learning rate for Whistle-JSA is set to 3e-5,
364   and when the validation loss does not decrease 10 epochs, the learning rate is multiplied by 0.5,
365   training stop until it reaches 1e-6. We extract 80-dimension FBank features from audio (resampled
366   to 16KHz) as inputs to the S2P model. A beam size of 16 is used for S2P and P2G decoding in
367   testing. We average the three best-performing checkpoints on the validation set for testing.

      6.3   A NALYSIS AND A BLATION
499
500   To provide a more intuitive description of the Whistle-JSA training process, Figure 3 shows the
501   changes in several key indicators over the number of training steps. It can be seen that the training
502   loss of all three models and validation error rates gradually decrease when using the method of
503   Algorithm 2 for parameter iteration. Through Whistle-JSA training, compared to the model fine-
504   tuned with only 10 minutes of phonetic labels, which is the initial model in this experiment, the
505
      Whistle-JSA model achieves a relative PER reduction of 45% and a WER reduction of 48% on the
      validation set. As the three models gradually converge, the diversity of samples that sampled out of
506
      the G2P begins to decrease, and the proportion of phoneme sequences accepted by the MIS sampler
507
      converges to 100% as shown in Figure 3 (f).
508
509   Table 3 shows ablation experiments with different MIS sample sizes. As the number of samples
510   increases, both PER and WER of the model significantly decrease. Compared to 10 samples, at
511
      50 samples, PER decreases by 2.5% and WER by 1.4%; at 100 samples, PER decreases by 3.1%
      and WER by 3.5%. It is worth noting that increasing the number of samples provides more diverse
512
      sampling, which benefits random approximation models in searching a wider latent space, but a
513
      balance needs to be found with computational cost. All Whistle-JSA experiments in this paper use
514   a sample size of 10, indicating that there is still have the potential for further improvement.

517   7     C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
518
519   In this paper, we achieve crosslingual speech recognition based on phonemes without a pronuncia-
520
      tion lexicon. By treating phonemes as discrete latent variables, S2P model and P2G model as latent
      variable models, and introducing CTC based G2P model as an auxiliary inference model, we utilize
521
      the JSA algorithm to jointly train these three networks. We called this approach as Whistle-JSA.
522
      This paper also proposes a MLS decoding approach that rescores each candidate hypothesis using
523   the marginal likelihood score and language model score. We also propose a P2G augmentation
524   strategy that uses the n-best results decoded by S2P to improve the robustness of the P2G model
525   towards input data. In crosslingual experiments with two languages, using only 10 minutes of data,
526   the Whistle-JSA method achieved results close to full data (130 and 20 hours) phonetic supervision,
527   and perform comparable to crosslingual subword fine-tuning. We further conduct language domain
528   adaptation experiments. The results show that the Whistle-JSA model outperform the standard lan-
529   guage model fusion approach via the auxiliary support of the G2P model. In the future, we plan to
530   train phoneme-based pre-trained models using the Whistle-JSA method on more languages.
