009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            A plethora of sentence embedding models makes it challenging to choose one,
012            especially for technical domains rich with specialized vocabulary. In this work, we
013            domain adapt embeddings using telecom, health and science datasets for question
014            answering. We evaluate embeddings obtained from publicly available models and
015            their domain-adapted variants, on both point retrieval accuracies, as well as their
016
               (95%) confidence intervals. We establish a systematic method to obtain thresholds
               for similarity scores for different embeddings. As expected, we observe that fine-
017
               tuning improves mean bootstrapped accuracies. We also observe that it results in
018
               tighter confidence intervals, which further improve when pre-training is preceded by
019            fine-tuning. We introduce metrics which measure the distributional overlaps of top-
020            K, correct and random document similarities with the question. Further, we show
021            that these metrics are correlated with retrieval accuracy and similarity thresholds.
022            Recent literature shows conflicting effects of isotropy on retrieval accuracies.
023            Our experiments establish that the isotropy of embeddings (as measured by two
024            independent state-of-the-art isotropy metric definitions) is poorly correlated with
025            retrieval performance. We show that embeddings for domain-specific sentences
026            have little overlap with those for domain-agnostic ones, and fine-tuning moves
027
               them further apart. Based on our results, we provide recommendations for use of
               our methodology and metrics by researchers and practitioners.

030   1    I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Document Question Answering (QA) methods such as Retrieval Augmented Generation (RAG)
033   typically involve retrieval of sections, paragraphs or sentences from a document corpus to accurately
034   answer user queries. Embedding models are used to map the questions or documents to a semantic
035   space. Retrieval is typically achieved by computing similarity between embeddings of questions and
036   those of documents. The most similar top-K documents are considered to be the relevant answers.
037   While many state-of-the-art (SOTA) models trained on publicly available datasets are easily accessible
038   (Reimers & Gurevych, 2019; Chen et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2023; Xiao et al., 2023), obtaining good
039   retrieval accuracies for domain specific tasks is challenging (Roychowdhury et al., 2024). It is well
040   acknowledged in literature that domain adaptation and fine-tuning can improve retrieval performance
041   (Li et al., 2020), but making an informed choice among several available models involves extensive
042   evaluation over parameters such as the number of relevant documents retrieved for a test set.
043   Some studies (Zhou et al., 2022) have identified limitations of cosine similarities to retrieve embed-
044   dings - a sample limitation is underestimation of similarity of frequent words with their homonyms. It
045   has been shown that cosine similarities can be arbitrary or dependent on regularization, making them
046   unreliable for retrieval tasks (Steck et al., 2024) - although this study was limited to linear models
047   the authors have conjectured that the same may be true for non-linear models. In fact, variations
048   in embedding space representations obtained from different architectures have been widely studied
049   (Mistry & Minai, 2023; Biś et al., 2021; Timkey & Van Schijndel, 2021). Another limitation observed
050
      is reporting of point accuracies, without any error bars, for retrieval tasks. This limits estimation of
      performance on new questions, especially when evaluated with relatively small datasets.
051
052   Recent work has explored isotropy as a measure for quantifying robust embedding space representa-
053   tions (Jung et al., 2023; Rudman & Eickhoff, 2023; Rudman et al., 2021), though it has also been
      argued otherwise (Hou et al., 2024; Ait-Saada & Nadif, 2023; Godey et al., 2023; Razzhigaev et al.,


                                                         1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      2023). In particular, Jung et al. (2023) suggests that isotropic embeddings improve retrieval whereas
055   Rudman & Eickhoff (2023) propose that reduced isotropy or anisotropy helps retrieval. Rajaee &
056   Pilehvar (2021) looks at isotropy of embeddings and show that increasing the isotropy of fine-tuned
057   models leads to poorer performance.
058
      We observe a few limitations with the current practice of measuring retrieval performance in both
059
      research and practice. First, reporting point accuracies do not provide insight into error bars (con-
060
      fidence intervals). This is especially important for relatively smaller datasets. Second, the lack of
061   confidence intervals does not allow for tests of statistical significance when comparing different
062   embedding models or domain adaptation strategies. Third, to the best of our knowledge, we have not
063   found prior work which has provided a systematic approach to choose the best threshold. In practice,
064   such thresholds are often chosen by inspection of similarity scores. Our approach of bootstrapping
065   provides the ability to perform tests for statistical significance on the results, and we choose the
066   maximum threshold such that our results are not statistically worse off. Finally, although prior work
067   Gao et al. (2021); Ethayarajh (2019) have looked at the effect of domain adaptation on embeddings,
068   the separation of domain-specific embeddings from general purpose embeddings under domain
069
      adaptation has not been studied. This does not allow a clear understanding of why performance
      changes on general purpose retrieval post domain adaptation.

072   1.1   R ESEARCH Q UESTIONS AND C ONTRIBUTIONS
073
      The primary research questions in this work are:
074
075          • RQ1: What are the confidence intervals (CI) of accuracies of SOTA retrieval models and
076            their fine-tuned versions when considering domain specific tasks?
077
             • RQ2: What facets apart from retrieval accuracies can characterise an embedding model?
078
               How does the distribution of cosine similarities vary across embeddings?
079
080
             • RQ3: Can the variation of retrieval accuracies be attributed to only the isotropy of the
               embeddings?
081
082
      Our primary contributions are:
083
084          • Demonstrate that fine-tuning improves mean accuracies as well as CI. Pre-training followed
085            by fine-tuning improves CI further.
086
             • Propose a systematic method to introduce thresholds with minimal effect on retrieval
087            accuracies.
088
             • Show that although domain adaptation via fine tuning leads to higher isotropy scores,
089
               retrieval performance across models is poorly correlated with the isotropy scores of the
090
               models; improving isotropy scores via transformations does not improve accuracies.
091
092
             • We introduce metrics which measure the distributional overlaps of top-K, correct and
               random document similarities with the question.
093
094          • Show empirically that these metrics are correlated with accuracies and similarity thresholds.
095          • Demonstrate that domain adaptation shifts the embeddings of the target domain farther away
096            from embeddings of sentences from domain agnostic datasets.
097
098   The rest of the paper is structured as follows: the methodology is detailed in Section 2. We detail the
099   domains considered along with the corresponding datasets and models in Section 3.1 and Section
100
      3.2 respectively. We report experimental results of multiple embeddings (with and without domain
      adaptation) in Section 4. We summarize our findings and discuss limitations and scope for future
101
      work in Section 5.

      and study the effects of isotropy scores. We describe each of these formally in this section. For most

111   Consider a dataset D = [s1 , s2 , . . . , sN ], where si is the ith sentence and i ∈ [1, N ]. Let D be
112

113

114

119

120
      the cardinality of the corresponding set and j ∈ [1, m]. We use these bootstrapped samples in our

140

142   can be different based on choice of the embedding model. Hence, we follow a bootstrapped analysis.

190   2.4    D OMAIN A DAPTATION
191
192   One of the key challenges in leveraging embedding models for technical domains is the lack of
193   domain specific knowledge, since the SOTA (base) models have been trained on publicly available
194   datasets which may be minimally introduced to domain specific terminology. We evaluate various
195
      domain adaptation techniques on the base models:
196
             • Pre-training Li et al. (2020): We use Masked Language Modeling (MLM) (Salazar et al.,
197            2019) approach for this. Sentences from the corpus of technical documents (of a domain)
198            are used.
199
             • Fine-tuning (Mosbach et al., 2020): We prepare triplets of the form < q, p, n > where q
200
               corresponds to the user query, p represents the correct (positive) answer and n is a list of
201
               incorrect (negative) answers. The base model is fine-tuned using these triplets. It may be
202            noted here that the fine-tuning may be performed post pre-training or independently on the
203            base model (without pre-training).
204
205   Thus, we evaluate the following variants of embedding models - base model, pre-trained only (PT),
206   fine-tuned only (FT) and pre-training followed by fine-tuning (PT-FT). As recommended2 , post
207   fine-tuning, we merge the base model with the domain adapted model.
208

      embeddings have unit-L2 norm). If the embeddings are uniformly distributed over the unit sphere i.e.

231
232   We analyze the effect of pre-training and fine-tuning base embedding models with domain-specific
233   data by comparing distribution of the resultant embeddings with that of embeddings from a domain
234   agnostic dataset.
235
      Let D represent domain-specific data, D′ represent domain-agnostic dataset. Let M be the base model,
236
      M′ be the pre-trained, fine-tuned version of the base model. Let similarity between the datasets be
237
      defined ∆M (D, D′ ) ≜ {min(||EM (d), EM (d′ )||2 ) : d ∈ D, d′ ∈ D′ }, and |∆M (D, D′ )| = |D|.
238
239   We compare the distributions of ∆M and ∆M′ . Our motivation here is to analyse the separation of
240   the distributions post domain adaptation.

423   4.2   I SOTROPY S CORE A NALYSIS
424
425   Table 5 lists the retrieval accuracies for the telecom dataset D, isotropic measures IA and IB of base
426   and adapted models for various transformations (intended to increase isotropy scores and described
427   in Section 2.5).
428   Correlation of IA and IB with accuracies across base, fine-tuned models with and without post-
429   processing using transformations described in Section 2.5 is presented in Table 3. We see that,
430   accuracy and both the isotropy scores are not correlated across datasets. Contrary to the conflicting
431   claims in Jung et al. (2023) and Rudman & Eickhoff (2023), our experiments across domains establish
      that accuracy and isotropy scores are not correlated.

      Combining these observations, we conclude that fine tuning improves the isotropy but isotropy cannot
433   be attributed to retrieval accuracies. Our studies indicate that this may be the right resolution between
434   the contradictions among studies by Jung et al. (2023) and Rudman & Eickhoff (2023) which we
435   have discussed in Section 1.

437   4.3      E MBEDDING A NALYSIS POST D OMAIN A DAPTATION
438
439   As described in Section 2.6, Figure 2 shows the distribution of distances ∆M and ∆M′ for both
440   bge_large and llm_embedder, respectively. The plots show that post domain-adaptation, the em-
441   beddings move away from the domain-agnostic embeddings. For llm_embedder, the farthest points
442   between D and D′ on public models is closer than the closest ones post domain adaptation. For
443
      bge_large, it is clear that distance between embeddings increase post domain adaptation, but this is
      less pronounced on Sci-Q and PubMedQA datasets. This is one of the effects of domain-adaption
444
      and needs further study.

447   5       R ECOMMENDATIONS AND C ONCLUSIONS
448
449   5.1      R ECOMMENDATIONS
450
      In this work, we have done a series of experiments to establish the impact of domain adaptation for
451
      embedding models. Based on this, we provide a set of recommendations to a researcher/practitioner
452
      on best using our findings. We provide anonymized code4 to perform the suggested steps, except
453   domain adaptation, below
454
455             • Use a bootstrapped approach for obtaining accuracies as this will give not only point
456               accuracies but also 95% confidence intervals.
457             • If possible, use domain adaptation - preferably pretraining followed by fine-tuning (PTFT)
458
                • Identify thresholds for the similarity scores - this will lead to bootstrapped accuracy which
459
                  is statistically same as the full dataset bootstrapped accuracy, while suppressing less relevant
460
                  documents to end-users / downstream tasks
461
462
                • We propose two new metrics COE and ROE. The observed correlations, across 3 datasets, of
                  the COE with accuracy and the ROE with thresholds indicate that they are reliable measures
463
                  for the generalisation of performance on unseen data of that domain.
464
465             • Our results establish the lack of correlation of accuracies to isotropy scores. We thus suggest
466
                  that computing isotropy scores to interpret retrieval accuracies is unlikely to be beneficial.
467
468   5.2      C ONCLUSION AND F UTURE W ORK
469
      We have reported mean bootstrapped retrieval accuracies along with confidence intervals for various
470
      SOTA embedding models with and without domain-adaptations. We observe that fine-tuning (with
471   or without pre-training) improves both mean and CI of retrieval accuracies. However, pre-training
472   followed by fine-tuning improves CI further. We propose a bootstrapped approach for choosing
473   thresholds and observe that we can significantly reduce the number of retrieved sentences without any
474   statistical deviation in retrieval performance. Our proposed cumulative distribution metrics, COE and
475   ROE, to measure overlap between distributions of cosine similarities show strong correlations with
476   retrieval performance and similarity thresholds respectively. We measure isotropy of embeddings
477   using two independent SOTA isotropy metrics. We perform extensive evaluations on embeddings
478   with and without isotropic transformations. We conclude that isotropy can be considered to be
479
      neither necessary nor sufficient from a retrieval accuracy perspective. Finally, we observe that with
      domain adaption, domain specific embedding show improved isotropy scores and move away from
480
      general domain embeddings. Our study establishes systematic methods of analysing embeddings in
481
      specialised domains. Our results hold across three different domain, which makes us believe that they
482   hold for other specialised domains too. The current work considers QA task only. Future work may
483   involve other tasks like summarization, or multimodal settings.
