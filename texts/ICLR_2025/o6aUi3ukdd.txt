013            Artiﬁcial intelligence is revolutionizing computational chemistry, bringing un-
014            precedented innovation and efﬁciency to the ﬁeld. To further advance research and
015            expedite progress, we introduce the Quantum Open Organic Molecular (QO2Mol)
016            database a large-scale quantum chemistry dataset designed for researches on or-
017            ganic molecules under an open-source license. The database comprises 120,000
018            organic molecules and more than 20 million conformers, encompassing 10 dif-
019
               ferent elements (C, H, O, N, S, P, F, Cl, Br, I), with heavy atom counts exceed-
               ing 40. Each conformation was computed at B3LYP/def2-SVP level of theory
020
               to derive quantum mechanical properties, including potential energy and forces.
021
               The molecules included in the dataset are based on fragments from compounds
022            in ChEMBL, ensuring their structural relevance to real-world compounds. The
023            extensive variety of molecular structures and elemental compositions represented
024            in the dataset can facilitate construction of potential energy surface and various
025            downstream tasks.

090   2.1   P OTENTIAL A PPLICATIONS OF O UR DATASET ON DATA -D RIVEN M ETHODS
091
      This section explores the potential impacts of our dataset on three speciﬁc areas: Potential Energy
092
      Surfaces, Force Field Models, and Conformation Generation. However, it is crucial to recognize
093
      that the scope of inﬂuence may reach well beyond these identiﬁed domains.
094
095
      Potential Energy Surface The potential energy surface (PES) of atomistic systems is the core of
096
      several aspects of physical chemistry, such as transition states, vibrational frequencies and electronic
097
      properties. Many of current methods based on deep learning mechanism focus on deploying neural
098   networks to predict QM computed properties (Qiao et al., 2020; Atz et al., 2021; Walters & Barzilay,
099   2021; Chen et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2022). These methods directly predict the QM properties
100   instead of solving the many-body Schrodinger equation numerically. All these methods require
101   high-precision QM data for training reliable models, which is what the QO2Mol dataset can provide.
102
103   Force Field Models Force ﬁelds are typically employed in downstream tasks like molecular dy-
104   namics simulations and structure optimizations (Joshi & Deshmukh, 2021; Shub et al., 2013; Suzuki
105   et al., 2022; Souza et al., 2021; Bejagam et al., 2020). They are essential for understanding and pre-
106   dicting the behavior and properties of molecular systems. Our dataset encompasses a diverse range
107   of element types and molecular structures, which is valuable for ﬁtting or validating high-accuracy
      force ﬁeld models.


                                                         2
108
      Conformation Generation The molecular conformation generation task aims to quickly obtain
109   reasonable and stable atomic 3D coordinates, which can be used for downstream tasks such as molec-
110   ular property prediction and molecular docking. Traditional methods acquire reliable 3D structures
111   through DFT calculations, but the computational costs become increasingly expensive as the number
112   of atoms increase. Recently, many studies have utilized neural network models to directly generate
113   conformations from molecular graphs (Simm & Hernandez-Lobato, 2020; Shi et al., 2021; Zhu et al.,
114   2022). Our dataset includes rotational scans of all ﬂexible bonds for each ﬂexible molecule and can
115   serve as a training set for the molecular conformation generation task.

      2.2   BASIC CONCEPTS OF COMPUTATIONAL CHEMISTRY
118
119   We introduce the necessary preliminaries of computational chemistry that will be used later.
120
121         • Density Functional Theory (DFT) (Thomas, 1927) is a popular computational method used
122           to approximately solve Schrödinger equation of molecular systems which offers energies
123           labels and possibly estimate further molecular property labels from the computed solution.
124
125
            • Force ﬁelds can be applied in various areas of computational chemistry, such as Free Energy
              Perturbation (FEP) calculations (Jiang & Roux, 2010; Wang et al., 2015).
126
127         • InChI (Heller et al., 2015) (The International Chemical Identiﬁer) is a unique representa-
128           tion of a chemical substance. InChI decomposes molecular graphs into a series of lay-
129           ered descriptive information, accurately capturing the chemical structure of the molecule.
130           InChIKey (Pletnev et al., 2012) is a compacted version of InChI with 27-character ﬁxed-
131           length. InChIKey is intended for identifying a unique molecule in database search-
132           ing/indexing (Wikipedia contributors, 2024).
133
            • SMILES (Weininger, 1988) (Simpliﬁed Molecular Input Line Entry System) is a ASCII
134
              string that represents a chemical structure in a way that can be friendly used by the com-
135           puter. It encodes molecular graph notations into compact linear strings through Depth First
136           Search (DFS) algorithm.
137
138         • Heavy atom is any atom other than hydrogen, typically used in molecular studies to fo-
139           cus on more complex atomic interactions. Heavy atoms form the structural backbone of
140           molecules, deﬁning their geometry and functional groups, while hydrogen atoms are typi-
141
              cally peripheral and less inﬂuential in determining molecular properties. Thus distinguish-
              ing heavy atoms is highly relevant to real-world applications across various ﬁelds.

      4   DATASET G ENERATION
256
257   In this section, we outline the process of data selection, processing, and preparation in QO2Mol. To
258   ensure the quality and reliablity of quantum mechanical data, the following considerations need to
259
      be taken into account :
260
            • The selected molecules should represent a chemical space that closely aligns with the distri-
261
              bution of chemical structures found in widely used compound library, such as ZINC (Irwin
262           et al., 2020), PubChem (Wang et al., 2009), and ChEMBL (Gaulton et al., 2012).
263
264         • Identify as many key conformations as possible on the potential energy surface, as these
265
              play a critical role in determining the properties of the molecules.
266         • Calculate properties using high-level quantum mechanical methods to ensure accuracy and
267           reliability.
268
269   By adhering to these guidelines, we release the QO2Mol dataset, which comprises 120,000 organic
      molecules and their corresponding 20 million conformations.

      4.1   M OLECULE F RAGMENTATION
271
272   We ﬁrst derive a set of source compounds from ChEMBL, a widely used virtual screening compound
273   database for drug design (Sadybekov & Katritch, 2023). Performing quantum mechanical calcula-
274   tions directly on these compounds is quite challenging due to the large size of these molecules. To
275   overcome the computational difﬁculties of quantum mechanical calculations, we employed a Com-
276   pound Fragmentation Process, dividing the source compounds into smaller fragments containing
277   fewer heavy atoms, as shown in Figure 4. In this way, we ensured that the basic fragment struc-
278
      tures can be found in real-world molecules and are therefore chemically meaningful. Then a total
      of 120,000 fragmented molecules were selected based on three rules: 1) with top 90% occurrence
279
      frequency over the database; 2) labeled as important phosphate groups by our chemistry expert; 3)
280
      encompassing 10 different elements(C, H, O, N, S, P, F, Cl, Br, I). We also ensured that there was no
281   fragment duplication during the generation procedure by utilizing InChIKey and canonical SMILES
282   identiﬁers.
283
284
      Our selection criteria did not impose restrictions on the number of heavy atoms. This approach
      enables us to capture a diverse range of signiﬁcant and complex chemical space that might not be
285
      adequately represented in existing databases, such as QM9 and ANI-1.

      4.2   C ONFORMATION G ENERATION
301
      The constituent atoms of a molecule exhibit dynamic motion in three-dimensional space, generating
302   the molecule’s conformational space. Each conformation has its own unique energy, collectively
303   forming the molecular potential energy surface in 3N-dimensional space. The macroscopic proper-
304   ties of a molecule are effectively described by the ensemble average of the various conformational
305   properties existing on this PES. Thus, the contributions of key conformations, such as local minima
306   or transition state structures, are considerably important, while the signiﬁcance of other conforma-
307   tions is also noteworthy. Given that, we sampled multiple conformations for each molecule within
308   the QO2Mol database.
309
310   Structure Optimization For each selected fragment molecule, an initial 3D structure is generated
311   using the RDKit package (Landrum et al., 2013) based on its SMILES (Weininger, 1988) representa-
312
      tion. Then each initial structure is optimized to a local minimum at the B3LYP/def2-SVP precision
      level. To ensure the structure reliability, during the structure optimization process, we employ four
313
      convergence criteria to ensure the resulting structures are reasonable: 1) Maximum force <0.00045;
314
      2) root-mean-square force <0.00030; 3) maximum displacement <0.00180; 4) root-mean-square dis-
315   placement <0.00120. Following each structural optimization, we perform a validation step to ensure
316   that all bond lengths fall within a deﬁned range relative to their empirical values. For example, the
317   empirical length of CC single bond is approximately 1.54 Å as widely observed (Allen et al., 1987).
318   We provide a statistic distribution of C-C bond length over the whole dataset in Figure 5.
319
320   Conformation Search Conformation search is performed on optimized structures obtained in the
321   previous step. At room temperature, the ﬂexible dihedral angles of molecules are likely to rotate.
322   Therefore, rotation is the most inﬂuential factor in constructing potential energy surfaces. Based on
323   this intuition, we perform rotational search in 30-degree increments each step on all rotatable bonds
      of each molecule. By systematically rotating the ﬂexible bonds of molecule to speciﬁc degrees,


                                                       6
324
      a series of new structures are generated. These structures are then optimized at the B3LYP/def2-
325   SVP level with ﬁxed torsions. Additionally, for speciﬁc molecules, we also perform stretching and
326   bending operations on bond lengths and bond angles, generating corresponding conformations. We
327   ensure that all bond types, such as C=C and C=O, are included in these manipulations. Moreover,
328   the database includes a collection of nearby unstable conformations for each stable conformation,
329   further enhancing the representation of the overall molecular potential energy landscape. We provide
330   a scan curve showing the potential energy changes during the ﬂexible bond rotation in Figure 5.
331   Based on the mentioned conformation generation procedure, we ﬁnally obtained a total of 20 million
332   conformers for the 120,000 molecules in our database.

348   4.3   P ROPERTIES
349
350   All conformations were analyzed to compute energy and forces at the B3LYP/def2-SVP level of
351   theory. The forces, representing the ﬁrst-order derivatives of energy with respect to coordinates,
352   were calculated for each atom in the three Cartesian directions (x, y, z). Among the 20 million con-
353
      formations, we also provide additional properties for approximately 210,000 stable conformations,
      although this is not the main focus of our contribution. For these stable conformations, we con-
354
      ducted frequency and charge population calculations. Vibrational frequencies were derived through
355
      diagonalization of the Hessian matrix, yielding 3N - 6 frequency values after excluding the three
356   translational and three rotational modes. The Hessian matrix represents the second-order derivatives
357   of energy with respect to coordinates. These frequency calculations allow for the determination of
358   thermodynamic properties, including zero-point energy, entropy, enthalpy, heat capacity, and free en-
359   ergy, utilizing both harmonic and ideal gas approximations. The charge population analysis includes
360   the calculation of electron density-derived charges such as ESP (Electrostatic Potential) charges and
361   Mülliken charges. More details are provided in Appendix B.

363   4.4   DATA S EGMENTATION
364
365   In order to support various learning tasks in this ﬁeld, we divided the data into three subsets, with
366   each subset exhibiting a different data distribution pattern serving distinct learning tasks, as depicted
367   in Figure 6.
368   The main subset, referred to as subset A, which encompasses the most extensive conformation data,
369   contains 20 million conformations from more than 110,000 molecules. Unlike previous datasets
370   that only sample equilibrium conformations at local minima, our subset A consists of equilibrium
371   conformations at local minima and near-equilibrium conformations additionally sampled around
372   local minima. These near-equilibrium conformations aid in training models and reconstructing high-
373   precision potential energy surfaces. Due to its more comprehensive conformation sampling method
374
      and broad distribution of heavy atoms, subset A can be used for various learning tasks, such as
      neural network potential (NNP) regression tasks (Kocer et al., 2022), machine learning force ﬁeld
375
      (MLFF) tasks (Fu et al., 2023), or denoising-like pretraining tasks (Zaidi et al., 2023).
376
377   To introduce a higher level of complexity and challenge, we present the second subset, referred to
      as subset B, which includes 2.4 million conformers generated from approximately 1,400 molecules.

412
413   Potential energy prediction is one of the most important benchmark tasks in the ﬁeld of compu-
414
      tational chemistry, as it serves as the foundation for numerous downstream tasks such as reaction
      simulations (Manzhos & Carrington, 2021), protein dynamics (Majewski et al., 2023), and crystal
415
      structure screening (Chen & Ong, 2022). Additionally, the potential energy prediction task is typ-
416
      ically employed to evaluate whether the model has successfully learned robust representations of
417   molecular geometries (Gasteiger et al., 2020b;a; Liao & Smidt, 2023; Liu et al., 2024). Potential
418   energy prediction task leverages the 3D structure of molecules as input to predict the potential en-
419   ergy of each conformation. In this section, we will discuss the results of benchmark models on the
420   potential energy prediction task using the QO2Mol dataset.

437   5.2   B ENCHMARK M ODELS
438
439   In this section, we mainly consider two types of benchmark models: invariant models and equivari-
440   ant models. Invariant models, such as SchNet (Schütt et al., 2017), SphereNet (Zhao et al., 2023),
441
      DimeNet++ (Gasteiger et al., 2020a), GemNet (Gasteiger et al., 2021), leverage features that remain
      unchanged under rotations and translations. These features include interatomic distances, bond an-
442
      gles, and torsion angles. By focusing on invariant features, these models can effectively capture
443
      the essential geometric relationships within molecular structures without being affected by their spa-
444   tial orientation. Equivariant models or approximately Equivariant model, such as Equiformer (Liao
445   & Smidt, 2023), EquiformerV2 (Liao et al., 2024), and eSCN (Passaro & Zitnick, 2023), utilize
446   features that transform predictably under rotations and translations. These features include the ir-

829   Table S3 summarizes the presence of ring structures in the molecules. Rings are essential com-
830
      ponents of organic molecules, and the majority of drug molecules contain ring structures. Due
      to the inﬂuence of ring strain, 5-membered and 6-membered rings are more stable compared to
831
      3-membered and 4-membered rings. It is evident from the results of the QO2Mol databases that
832
      molecules containing 5-membered and 6-membered rings are more prevalent. However, due to the
833   limitations on heavy atom counts, the QM9 database includes a greater number of molecules with
834   3-membered and 4-membered rings. Aromatic rings represent a distinct category of ring structures,
835   contrasting with aliphatic rings. Aromatic rings can be 5-membered, such as pyrrole and furan, or
836   6-membered, such as benzene and pyridine. Due to their high stability, aromatic rings are commonly
837   encountered in organic molecules. In the ChEMBL library, the majority of molecules contain aro-
838   matic rings, and a signiﬁcant proportion of molecules in the QO2Mol database also feature aromatic
839   ring. However, the QM9 database exhibits a relatively lower percentage of molecules with aromatic
840   rings, particularly 6-membered aromatic rings.

      We conducted all experiment on A100 GPU cluster. For the interpolation task, we employ a
857
      72%/18%/10% split for training, validation and testing on subset A. For the extrapolation task, we
858   use the entire subset B. In our experiments, we established the basic parameter settings as follows.
859   The cutoff radius is set to 5.0 angstrom for all models. The training process was conducted us-
860   ing the AdamW optimizer with a cosine annealing learning rate scheduler. For hyper-parameter
861   optimization, we employed a grid search strategy. Target hyper-parameters include learning rate,
862   batch size, and the weight decay, with the following ranges: learning rate {1e-3, 4e-4, 8e-4}, batch
863   size {32, 64, 128, 256}, weight decay {0, 1e-5, 1e-4}. Each combination of hyper-parameters
      was evaluated on the valid set, and the conﬁguration yielding the highest validation accuracy was
