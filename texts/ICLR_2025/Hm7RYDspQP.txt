                                                  A BSTRACT
013
014
               This paper introduces Network-based Active Inference (NetAIF), a novel ap-
015
               proach that integrates Active Inference (AIF) principles with network dynamics to
016
               enable adaptive, cost-efficient real-world applications. In benchmark tests against
017            Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL), NetAIF outperforms DRL in both compu-
018            tational efficiency and task performance. Leveraging random attractor dynamics,
019            NetAIF generates real-time trajectories, allowing robots to adapt to complex, dy-
020            namic environments without the need for extensive pre-training. We demonstrate
021            NetAIF’s superiority in industrial valve manipulation, achieving over 99% accu-
022            racy in goal position and orientation in untrained dynamic environments, with a
023            45,000-fold reduction in computational costs. NetAIF is approximately 100,000
024            times more efficient in iteration count than DRL, making it a highly robust and
025
               efficient solution for industrial applications.

      1     INTRODUCTION
029
030
      1.1   OVERCOMING AUTOMATION C HALLENGES WITH A DVANCED L EARNING M ETHODS
031
      The World Energy Employment 2023 report by the IEA highlights a significant shift towards clean
032   energy jobs, which now surpass fossil fuel employment, driven by a 40% rise in clean energy in-
033   vestment over the past two years. Despite economic and geopolitical challenges, the energy sector
034   has seen growth in employment, particularly in solar PV, wind, EVs, and battery manufacturing.
035   However, a shortage of skilled labor remains a key challenge, underscoring the need for targeted
036   training and policy support to develop a workforce suited for the energy transition (IEA, 2023).
037
      In response to these labor challenges, automation is playing an increasingly critical role in advancing
038   the clean energy sector. Robotics, in particular, offers a promising solution to enhance operational
039   efficiency and safety. However, to maximize the potential of robotics in complex and dynamic en-
040   vironments, sophisticated learning methods are required. One such approach, Deep Reinforcement
041   Learning (DRL), has emerged as a leading candidate for enabling autonomous robotic systems in
042   tasks like control, manipulation, and decision-making. Yet, despite its potential, DRL faces notable
043   barriers to widespread adoption in the energy sector.
044
045   1.2   D EEP R EINFORCEMENT L EARNING (DRL)
046
047   DRL combines the decision-making power of reinforcement learning (RL) with the pattern recogni-
048   tion capabilities of deep learning (DL). This allows robots to learn and adapt through trial and error,
049   improving performance over time. DRL is increasingly explored for enabling autonomy in control
050
      and manipulation tasks in real-world environments by training agents to recognize complex patterns
      in data and make informed decisions.
051
052   However, DRL requires large amounts of data and time for agent training, as well as expert-designed
053   reward functions to guide learning. Creating these reward functions demands substantial knowledge
      and engineering resources, as they must accurately capture desired outcomes, agent actions, and

054
      constraints. Poorly defined reward functions can lead to suboptimal or unsafe behavior (Sutton &
055   Barto, 2020). Thus, while powerful, DRL may not always be the most practical or cost-effective
056   approach for every application.
057
058
      1.3   AIF AS A N EXT G ENERATION L EARNING M ETHOD
059
060   Active Inference (AIF) is a groundbreaking framework in neuroscience, offering a unified approach
061   to understanding adaptive systems, including brain functions, and is gaining traction in fields like
062   machine learning and robotics (Friston et al., 2006; Parr, 2019; Millidge, 2020; Lanillos et al., 2021).
063   In robotics, AIF is reshaping control and learning by minimizing surprise rather than relying on
064   reward-based mechanisms like DRL. Unlike DRL, which requires fixed environments, AIF utilizes
065   a dynamic generative model, continuously adapting to changing surroundings through a feedback
066   loop of prediction, perception, and action. This approach addresses the exploration-exploitation
067   dilemma more fluidly by incorporating uncertainty directly into decision-making.
068   While AIF holds significant promise for creating adaptive robotic systems, its real-world deployment
069   faces challenges due to the complexity of model design and high computational demands (Lanillos
070   et al., 2021). Nonetheless, its potential to enhance flexibility, durability, and adaptability makes it a
071   powerful alternative to traditional DRL techniques
072
073   1.4   N ETWORK - BASED ACTIVE I NFERENCE (N ETAIF)
074
075   To overcome the limitations of both DRL and traditional AIF approaches, we propose Network-
076   based Active Inference (NetAIF), a novel framework that leverages network dynamics to simplify
077   trajectory calculations and enhance efficiency. Rooted in key AIF principles such as entropy and
078
      surprise minimization, NetAIF builds on the Free Energy Principle (FEP), which posits that systems
      self-organize by minimizing surprisal or prediction error. By harnessing the inherent dynamics of a
079
      network, NetAIF computes trajectories more efficiently than traditional AIF methods, reducing the
080
      need for complex mathematical models while enabling agents to adapt to dynamic environments in
081   real-time. This streamlined approach makes NetAIF highly suitable for real-world robotic applica-
082   tions, offering significant improvements in both speed and computational cost.

      2.2    T HE R ANDOM ATTRACTOR
200
      To represent the NESS behavior in NetAIF, Random Dynamical Systems (RDS) are employed,
201   providing a framework to understand complex systems driven by stochastic processes. In particular,
202   random pullback attractors (Caraballo & Han, 2016), also known as stochastic basins of attraction,
203   describe how NetAIF’s state evolves over time in response to environmental uncertainty. Expressed
204   as φ(t, ω, x), where t is time, ω represents randomness, and x is the state variable, these attractors
205   characterize regions in the state space where the system tends to settle. The random attractor A(ω)
206   pulls trajectories towards it, ensuring that NetAIF remains adaptive and stable within its NESS
207   framework, despite external randomness.
208   This is formalized by:
209                                        lim dist (φ(t, θ−t ω, B), A(ω)) = 0
                                           t→∞
210
211   where θ−t ω is time-shifted random noise, and B is a bounded set of initial conditions. This equation
212
      captures the system’s tendency to converge towards the random attractor, influenced by past random
      events.
213
214   This convergence can be understood as stochastic diffusion in parameter space, driven by increasing
215   random fluctuations in parameters (e.g., network connection weights) in high free energy regions. As
      the system nears free energy minima, random fluctuations decrease, stabilizing the trajectory. The

251   It is worth noting that NetAIF’s optimization process is inherently local, as free energy is an ex-
252
      tensive quantity—the system’s total free energy is the sum of the free energies of its individual
      components. The variational free energy, which approximates the true free energy, is calculated
253

254
      desired outcomes, defining the attracting set that represents the target state or desired sensor inputs.
255   By minimizing local prediction errors, the network is guided toward this attracting set.
256
257   This local approach allows the system to efficiently navigate the free energy landscape without
258
      needing global computations or information propagation across the entire network. Iterative updates
      based on local prediction errors and control laws enable the system to converge toward desired states.
259
260   The roots of this learning process trace back to early cybernetics (Ashby, 1947; 1956) and stochastic
261   thermodynamics (Ao, 2008; Seifert, 2012), where systems self-organize by reducing prediction
262   errors. In NetAIF, this drives the network towards stable, efficient behavior, enabling smooth and
263   precise robotic movements.

      This paper presents Network-based Active Inference (NetAIF), a novel approach that combines
528
      AIF principles with network dynamics to surpass Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) in real-
529
      world robotic tasks. In an industrial valve manipulation benchmark, NetAIF achieved over 99%
530   accuracy while reducing computational costs by 45,000 times and training time by 99.99%. Unlike
531   DRL, which requires extensive pre-training, NetAIF leverages random attractor dynamics for real-
532   time trajectory generation, adapting swiftly to dynamic environments with minimal overhead. Its
533   robustness across varied conditions makes it a scalable, cost-efficient solution for adaptive robotic
534   control in unpredictable settings.
