009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011
               Vision-language large models have achieved remarkable success in various multi-
012
               modal tasks, yet applying them to video understanding remains challenging due
013
               to the inherent complexity and computational demands of video data. While
014            training-based video-LLMs deliver high performance, they often require substan-
015            tial resources for training and inference. Conversely, training-free approaches of-
016            fer a more efficient alternative by adapting pre-trained image-LLMs models for
017            video tasks without additional training, but they face inference efficiency bottle-
018            necks due to the large number of visual tokens generated from video frames. In
019            this work, we present a novel prompt-guided visual perception framework (abbre-
020            viated as Free Video-LLM) for efficient inference of training-free video LLMs.
021            The proposed framework decouples spatial-temporal dimension and performs
022
               temporal frame sampling and spatial RoI cropping respectively based on task-
               specific prompts. Our method effectively reduces the number of visual tokens
023
               while maintaining high performance across multiple video question-answering
024
               benchmarks. Extensive experiments demonstrate that our approach achieves com-
025            petitive results with significantly fewer tokens, offering an optimal trade-off be-
026            tween accuracy and computational efficiency compared to state-of-the-art video
027            LLMs.

031   1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033   Recently, vision-language models (VLMs) have rapidly revolutionized multi-modal understanding
034   and generation, enabling models to comprehend and produce responses from both visual and textual
035   inputs (visual inputs include images, videos, etc). Due to the vast amount of image-text data and
036   foundational models like CLIP and large language models (LLMs) such as GPT Brown (2020) and
037   LLaMA Touvron et al. (2023), image-LLMs Zhang et al. (2024a); Liu et al. (2023); Achiam et al.
038   (2023); Chen et al. (2024) have already made significant progress. InstructBLIP Dai et al. (2023)
039
      extends the capabilities on new visual tasks by incorporating instruction-aware features. LLaVA Liu
      et al. (2023) generate multimodal language-image instruction-following data using GPT-4 Achiam
040
      et al. (2023), demonstrating impressive multimodel chat abilities. The evolution of VLMs has also
041
      given rise to video-based LLMs that are specifically tailored for video understanding. These meth-
042   ods, as discussed by Tang et al. Tang et al. (2023), can be categorized into two primary approaches
043   based on their training strategies: training-based video LLMs and training-free video LLMs.
044
045
      In the training-based video LLMs, AV-LLM Shu et al. (2023) and Vid2Seq Yang et al. (2023) trained
      all the parameters in the LLM, which can be resource-intensive. Other main stream methods in this
046
      fine-tuning category either externally(e.g., Q-former) or internally fine-tune the bridge between the
047
      video encoder and the LLM, or adopt a phased fine-tuning approach for connective adapters and
048   insertive adapters. Video-LLMs, such as Video-ChatGPT Maaz et al. (2024), Chat-UniVi Jin et al.
049   (2024), and MovieChat Song et al. (2024a), primarily focus on general question-answering (QA)
050   or captioning tasks. They utilize video encoders to produce video embeddings, which they then
051   decode into text outputs based on given prompts or instructions. Video-LLaMA Zhang et al. (2023)
052   integrates a video Q-former to learn video-language correspondence and an audio Q-Former to learn
053   audio embeddings for the LLM. Additionally, Chat-UniVi Jin et al. (2024) employs a set of dynamic
      visual tokens to uniformly represent both images and videos.

054
      Despite the remarkable success of training-based video LLMs, training-free video understanding
055   models have emerged as an attractive alternative due to the high cost of training large-scale video
056   models. Training-free methods leverage pre-trained image-based models and minimally adapt them
057   for video tasks without additional training on video data. Models such as IG-VLM Kim et al.
058   (2024) and FreeVA Wu (2024) have demonstrated the potential of transforming video frames into a
059   format compatible with high-performance VLMs. These approaches utilize parameter-free temporal
060   aggregation techniques and composite image grids, enabling robust zero-shot performance on video
061   question-answering tasks. These methods offer promising results without requiring time-consuming
062   and resource-intensive video-specific training.
063   However, existing training-free video LLMs, struggle with efficiency issues due to the large number
064   of visual tokens generated by video frames. Video data consists of multiple frames, significantly
065   increasing the number of visual tokens that need to be processed by the model. As the input sequence
066   length increases, the computational cost also scales, especially in transformer architectures where
067   self-attention and feed-forward networks dominate resource usage. This results in slow inference
068   speeds and high memory consumption, limiting the practical application of video LLMs in real-time
069
      or resource-constrained environments. Reducing token count without compromising performance is
      a key challenge in scaling video LLMs efficiently.
070
071   To address these challenges, we propose a prompt-guided visual perception framework (abbreviated
072   as Free Video-LLM) that significantly improves the efficiency of training-free video LLMs. Our
073   method introduces prompt-guided temporal and spatial sampling, which reduces the number of vi-
074   sual tokens based on the specific requirements of the input prompt. By discarding redundant frames
075
      and focusing only on the regions and time segments relevant to the prompt, our model achieves supe-
      rior performance across multiple video QA benchmarks with a significantly reduced computational
076
      burden. Table 1 compares several prominent open-source models, our proposed method uniquely
077
      possesses training-free capabilities, inference efficiency, and effective video understanding, demon-
078   strating a comprehensive advantage in the field. The proposed Free Video-LLM approach not only
079   enhances the model’s efficiency but also maintains competitive accuracy compared to existing state-
080   of-the-art video LLMs, offering a balanced solution for scalable video understanding tasks.

      2.1    I MAGE -LLM S

097   The rapid advancement of image-language models (image-LLMs) can be attributed to two key fac-
098   tors: the foundational work of CLIP Radford et al. (2021), which introduced a shared representation

101   (LLMs) like GPT Brown (2020) and LLaMA Touvron et al. (2023), which can be further enhanced
102   through instruction tuning Peng et al. (2023). Flamingo Alayrac et al. (2022) excels in few-shot
103   learning by seamlessly integrating pre-trained vision and language models and high-quality inter-
104
      leaved multimodal data. BLIP-2 Li et al. (2023a) utilizes a lightweight Querying Transformer to
      connect modalities.
105
106   Further progress on image-LLMs has been achieved through multimodal instruction tuning.
107   LLaVA Liu et al. (2023) uses GPT-4 Achiam et al. (2023) to generate robust multi-modal instruc-
      tion data, pre-training on image-text pairs and fine-tuning for end-to-end multimodal understanding.

117   Building on the foundation of LLM and image-LLMs, video-language models (video-LLMs) ia also

      rapidly developed. FrozenBiLM Yang et al. (2022) leverages frozen bidirectional language models

      (2023) both utilize dual streams to handle audio and visual signals. Specifically, Video-LLaMA in-

122   side a perception tools for captions, and introduce a video-centric multimodal instruction fine-tuning
123   dataset. Video-ChatGPT Maaz et al. (2024) utilizes a pretrained visual encoder to extract both spa-

125   projected into the input space of large language models (LLMs). Additionally, it also contributes a
126   high-quality dataset of 100,000 video-instruction question-answer pairs. Chat-UniVi Jin et al. (2024)
127   use a set of dynamic visual tokens to uniformly represent images and videos tokens. PLLaVA Xu
128   et al. (2024a) introduce a post-training weight fusion methods to alleviate forgetting phenomenon
129
      during multi-modality fine-tuning.

131   2.3   T RAINING - FREE V IDEO LLM S
132
      In contrast to these tuning methodologies for Video LLMs, there is a growing interest in exploring
133
      training-free video LLMs, specifically how existing image model architectures can be minimally
134   adapted to accommodate video inputs. IG-VLM Kim et al. (2024), as a pioneer in this exploration,
135   transforms videos into single composite image grids, enabling the direct application of a high-
136   performance VLM without the need for video-data training. FreeVA Wu (2024) also investigates
137   the potential of leveraging offline image-LLMs as training-free video assistants by simply adding
138   a parameter-free temporal aggregation, achieving strong performance in zero-shot video question-
139   answering tasks Chen & Dolan (2011); Caba Heilbron et al. (2015); Xu et al. (2016), even sur-
140   passing video instruct-tuning models. Meanwhile, SLOWFAST-LLAVA Xu et al. (2024b) utilizes
141   a dual-stream approach to efficiently capture spatial and temporal video features, demonstrating
142
      competitive performance on various video benchmarks without requiring additional training.

      3.3   P ROMPT- GUIDED V ISUAL P ERCEPTION
197
198   We introduce the prompt-guided visual perception for efficient video LLMs, by temporally and
199   spatially sampling visual information respectively, as demonstrated in Figure 1.
200
201   Prompt-guided Temporal Sampling The video is usually captured in a high frame rate, resulting
202   in large redundancy in the neighbor frames. In order to reduce the number of frames and maintain
203   the discriminative information, we propose to temporally sample the prompt-related frames. The
204   previous video-LLMs take all the visual tokens generated by the visual encoder as inputs for LLMs.
205   However, this approach does not consider the text context for different questions or tasks, which
206
      may introduce useless tokens during inference. For instance, given a video depicting two women
      shopping in a supermarket, one might ask how many apples they bought, while another person
207
      could ask whether they bought eggs. Different questions necessitate the model to focus on different
208
      periods of the video. On the other hand, different regions of the video are also required for different
209   questions. For example, one may inquire about the number of persons, while another may ask
210   about the color of the left woman’s T-shirt. Here, we introduce prompt-guided spatial and temporal
211   sampling to reduce the number of visual tokens for different input prompts.
212

213
      relevant to the prompt. In particular, We utilize the text encoder gT that is matched with the visual
214
      encoder (such as the two in CLIP) to extract prompt features:

362   Inference Speed We compare the inference speed of three representative training-free video
363   LLMs in Table 5. Two important metrics for measuring the inference speed of large models are
364   pre-filling latency and output speed. Pre-filling latency represents the time taken by the model to
365   generate the first output token after receiving the input. A lower pre-filling latency means the model
366   can begin producing results faster. Output speed refers to the rate at which the model generates
367
      subsequent tokens after the first one is produced. A higher TPS indicates that the model can output
      tokens more quickly once the generation process has started. The inference speed is evaluated on
368
      an NVIDIA V100 GPU with standard transformers framework. Our Free Video-LLM outperforms
369
      the others with fewer visual tokens (2,648), the fastest pre-filling latency (0.578 seconds), and the
370   highest output speed (20.4 TPS). In comparison, IG-VLM and SF-LLaVA have higher visual token
371   counts, slower pre-filling latency, and lower output speeds.

      5     C ONCLUSION
475
476   In conclusion, this paper introduces a novel framework for efficient inference of training-free video
477
      LLMs, named Free Video-LLM. The proposed method addresses the computational challenges as-
      sociated with video understanding by introducing a prompt-guided visual perception approach that
478
      significantly reduces the number of visual tokens processed by the model. Through temporal and
479
      spatial token sampling techniques tailored to the input prompt, Free Video-LLM achieves a remark-
480   able reduction in computational burden without compromising accuracy. Extensive experiments
481   demonstrate that our method not only matches but often exceeds the performance of current state-
482   of-the-art video LLMs, while using a fraction of the visual tokens. This represents a substantial step
483   forward in the practical application of video LLMs, offering a competitive balance between accuracy
484   and computational efficiency. The prompt-guided framework paves the way for more scalable and
485   efficient video understanding systems, potentially transforming real-time and resource-constrained
      environments.
