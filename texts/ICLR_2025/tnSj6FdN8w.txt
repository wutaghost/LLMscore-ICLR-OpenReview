                                                  A BSTRACT
010
011            Numerical simulation of dynamical systems requires time integration solvers that
012            balance accuracy and computational efficiency. Recent work indicates that neural
013            integrators, a hybrid of classical numerical integration and machine learning, can
014            achieve significant performance gains. Building upon this idea, we propose a new
015            type of neural integrator that introduces stage corrections inspired by the fact that
016            traditional time integration schemes such as Runge-Kutta exhibit different error
017            characteristics at each stage. Specifically, our method corrects numerical errors
018
               immediately after each stage evaluation by using a neural network, mitigating
               error propagation across stages. This enables the use of larger time steps while
019
               preserving stability and accuracy. We demonstrate that our approach is at least
020
               one order of magnitude more accurate than existing hybrid methods for complex
021            nonlinear dynamical systems when integrated with the same step size.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026
027   Accurate numerical simulations of dynamical systems are critical in fields ranging from computa-
028   tional fluid dynamics to computational physics and from material science to chemical engineering.
029   Traditionally, solving the governing equations for these systems relies on numerical discretization
030
      methods, which are computationally expensive and lead to trade-offs between speed and accuracy.
031         In recent years, machine learning (ML) algorithms have gained popularity due to their versa-
032   tility and potential for reducing computational demands. These methods can generally be divided
033   into two main categories. The first category is purely data-driven, where no explicit knowledge of
034   the underlying physical laws is used. Examples include Fourier Neural Operators (FNO) Li et al.
035   (2020) and DeepONets Lu et al. (2021). Some other works focused on data-driven dynamics dis-
036   covery Pan & Duraisamy (2018); Liu et al. (2022); Chen & Xiu (2021). The second category is
037   physics-informed, such as Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) Raissi et al. (2017; 2019);
038
      Rad et al. (2020), which enforce the governing equations and boundary conditions without requir-
      ing labeled data. While both approaches have successfully produced surrogate models that can
039
      replace traditional numerical solvers, they face limitations when applied to partial differential equa-
040
      tions (PDEs) involving complex geometries and boundary conditions Lu et al. (2022); McGreivy &
041   Hakim (2024), chaos Choudhary et al. (2020); Han et al. (2021); Greydanus et al. (2019), stiffness
042   Huang & Leimkuhler (1997); Liang et al. (2022), and long-term prediction Wang et al. (2022).
043
044        More recently, hybrid approaches that combine machine learning with numerical simulations
045   have emerged, offering a promising alternative for accelerating the simulation of dynamical systems.
046   These methods leverage the speed of low-fidelity simulations while improving accuracy through er-
047
      ror correction via deep neural networks (see Figure 1). For this reason, we term such methods Neu-
      ral Integrators. As a result, neural integrators are data-driven, physics-informed, and fundamentally
048
      simulation-based. The integration of simulations confers two key advantages:
049
050          • Enriched physical information and improved reliability. By combining classic time-
051            stepping methods with neural networks (NNs), these approaches retain the intrinsic physics
052            of the system. Low-fidelity simulations provide reasonable approximations of system
053            states, making it more feasible for NNs to learn and correct errors rather than to directly
               predict the solution.

073       A notable example of this neural integrator paradigm is NeurVec Huang et al. (2023) (see Fig-
074   ure 2 (a)), which has demonstrated speedups of tens to hundreds of times compared to traditional
075   solvers. However, the theory of NeurVec was developed for the Euler method, and NeurVec yields
076   suboptimal results for multistage time integration methods. In this work, we propose a new type
077   of neural time integrator based on a stage correction strategy (NeurTISC) and demonstrate its ad-
078
      vantages over NeurVec. In our method, we correct numerical errors immediately after each stage
      evaluation by using a neural network (see Figure 2 (b)). This allows us to adopt a large step size
079
      when using a classical integrator and at the same time mitigate error propagation across stages.

      setup such as training/testing data, loss function and learning rate scheduler for both models. We

374   In this paper we proposed NeurTISC, a new type of neural time integrator based on stage correc-
375   tion. NeurTISC enhances a traditional multi-stage time integrator by using a simple neural network
376   to compensate the errors at each stage; therefore, it allows the use of large step sizes to accelerate
377   the simulation of dynamical systems, including pure ODEs and ODEs converted from PDEs after
      spacial discretization. We show that the stage-by-stage correction approach can effectively account

415
