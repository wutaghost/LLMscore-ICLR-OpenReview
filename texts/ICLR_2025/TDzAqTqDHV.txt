008                                                 A BSTRACT
009
010
               In recent years, the application of language models (LMs) to retrieval tasks has
               gained significant attention. Dense retrieval methods, which represent queries and
011
               document chunks as vectors, have gained popularity, but their use at scale can be
012
               challenging. These models can under-perform traditional sparse approaches, like
013            BM25, in some demanding settings, e.g. at web-scale or out-of-domain. Moreover
014            the computational requirements, even with approximate nearest neighbour indices
015            (ANN) can be hefty. Sparse methods, remain, thanks to their efficiency, ubiqui-
016            tous in applications. In this work, we ask whether LMs can be leveraged to bridge
017            this gap. We introduce Quantised Codebooks for Retrieval (QCR): we encode
018            queries and documents as bags of latent discrete tokens, learned purely through a
019            contrastive objective. QCR’s encodings can be used as a drop-in replacement for
020            the original string in sparse retrieval indices, or can be instead used to complement
021
               the text with higher-level semantic features. Experimental results demonstrate that
               QCR outperforms BM25 with vanilla text on the challenging MSMARCO dataset.
022
               What is more, when used in conjunction with standard lexical matching, our repre-
023
               sentation yield and absolute 15.6% gain over BM25’s Success@100, highlighting
024            the complementary nature of textual and learned discrete features.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Information retrieval (IR) systems have long relied on traditional term matching methods, represent-
030   ing both queries and documents as bags of words, i.e. very high-dimensional, sparse vectors, whose
031   non-zero components are based on lexical frequencies estimated on text corpora. Approaches such
032   as BM25 (Robertson & Zaragoza, 2009; Robertson & Walker, 1997) have withstood the test of time
033
      because of their effectiveness, but also in no small part due to its simplicity and scalability. This
      scalability is a product of the sparsity of the representations, that allows the use of efficient and flex-
034
      ible data structures for search, such as the inverted index (Knuth, 1997). However, traditional sparse
035
      retrieval methods are now losing their dominance. Since they are based on exact or near-exact term
036   matching between queries and documents, they face the well-known vocabulary mismatch problem
037   (Berger et al., 2000). Vocabulary mismatch arises when concepts with same meaning are worded
038   differently in the query and the related document, causing sparse models to fail at the retrieval task,
039   — e.g. with the synonyms pants and trousers.
040
      In recent years, so-called dense retrieval models have become widely used in text applications (Wang
041
      et al., 2024; Lee et al., 2019). Embedding models typically encode queries and documents as con-
042   tinuous, fixed-length vectors, computing relevance scores with dot product. Powered by the recent
043   improvements in language model pre-training, dense approaches have shown substantial improve-
044   ments in IR tasks (Karpukhin et al., 2020; Xiong et al., 2020; Izacard et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2024),
045   particularly in scenarios where large annotated datasets are available. Dense models can capture the
046   higher-level meaning of queries and documents, enabling retrieval to go beyond simple term match-
047   ing. Despite their success, dense retrieval methods come with significant drawbacks, including high
048   computational and memory costs (Cao et al., 2021), and the need for complex approximate nearest
049   neighbor indices (Douze et al., 2024; Malkov & Yashunin, 2018). Moreover, dense retrieval systems
050
      can underperform simple term-matching where large-scale supervised data is unavailable or difficult
      to collect (Ni et al., 2022).
051
052   In this paper, we introduce a method that improves standard term-matching retrieval performance
053   by leveraging learnt, discrete embeddings of queries and documents composed of tokens from a
      latent codebook. These latent encodings can be used in place of natural language words with no

088   in scenarios where dense retrieval methods are impractical due to their resource demands.
089   Our contributions are as follows:
090
091              1. We propose a method for generating discrete representations of queries and documents,
092                 which can be used both as standalone inputs for inverted-index retrieval systems and in
093                 conjunction with traditional lexical representations.
094              2. Our approach improves retrieval performance on the challenging MSMARCO benchmark,
095                 demonstrating its effectiveness in both supervised and unsupervised settings. Notably, it
096                 outperforms BM25 on all reported metrics when using discrete representations alone and
097                 shows further gains when combining them with original text.
098              3. We highlight the practical advantages of integrating discrete representations with lexical
099                 retrieval, offering a computationally efficient alternative to dense retrieval methods while
100                 maintaining strong performance.

      2.1   C OL BERT
109
110   ColBERT (Contextualized Late Interaction over BERT) (Khattab & Zaharia, 2020) is an information
111   retrieval model that combines the strengths of both traditional sparse retrieval techniques and dense
112   vector-based retrieval. At its core, ColBERT leverages deep contextual embeddings generated by
113   BERT to represent both queries and documents. However, instead of embedding into a single fixed-
114   length vector like most dense retrieval models, ColBERT retains individual token-level embeddings,
115   thus preserving fine-grained contextual information. Like in term-based sparse retrieval approaches,
116
      in ColBERT the relevance score is computed as the max-pooling over the dot product between
      individual query and document terms.

124   2.2   FSQ
125
126   FSQ (Mentzer et al., 2023) is a technique used to discretise continuous data into a finite set of values,
127   which is particularly useful in scenarios where memory efficiency and computational simplicity are
128   priorities. FSQ maps each continuous value to the nearest point in a predefined set of quantised
129   levels, in contrast with its precursor VQ-VAE (van den Oord et al., 2018), where the codebook is
130   learnt and actively changes during training.
131   FSQ works by rounding each entry in the latent representation z to the nearest integer, usually after
132   applying a bounding function such as the hyperbolic tangent. To propagate gradients through the
133   rounding operation, it simply uses straight-through estimation (STE) (Bengio et al., 2013). FSQ
134   has been shown to be easier to optimise than other similar methods, while avoiding the issue of
135   codebook under-utilisation that VQ-VAE suffers from.
136   FSQ obtains a discrete representation of a continuous vector z by applying the following operation
137   on each one of its dimensions:
                                     zi = round(⌊L/2⌋ tanh ziprequant )

138                                                                                                  (2)
139
      Here, ziprequant represents the pre-quantized value of the i-th dimension and L is the number of dis-
140
      crete values of each dimension. Considering the same value L across all dimensions, and a total
141
      number of d dimensions (|zi | = d), the dimension of the codebook is equal to |C| = Ld .

      2.3   D ISCRETE R EPRESENTATION L EARNING
144
145   Discrete representations, particularly through methods such as VQ-VAE and FSQ, have gained trac-
146   tion in generative modeling, especially in the domain of image generation (Razavi et al., 2019;
147   Ramesh et al., 2021; Gu et al., 2022; Chang et al., 2022), but also music (Dhariwal et al., 2020) and
148   video (Rakhimov et al., 2020). These techniques enable the transformation of continuous data (e.g.,
149   pixel values) into discrete codes, which can be used to produce high-quality images. Discretisation
150   methods have also been applied to text generation (Zhang et al., 2024) to allow for better control of
151
      textual output in LLMs.
152   Perhaps most similar to our method, Sun et al. (2023) propose GENRET to learn discrete document
153   identifiers for the purpose of using them during generative retrieval. Their goal is to obtain identifiers
154   that contain semantic information about the document and are easier for the LLM to generate. They
155   use an auto-encoding framework to generate docids that are autoregressive in nature and diverse
156   enough to avoid duplicate docids across multiple documents. Similarly, Wang et al. (2023) propose
157
      a hierarchical k-means algorithm to generate docids for each documents.

166   3.1    A RCHITECTURE
167
168
      We use an encoder LLM architecture, BERT (Devlin et al., 2019) specifically, to obtain embedding
      representations for each query and document. In particular, we augment the input text, both for
169
      queries and documents, by prepending a set of special tokens to each text string. These special
170
      tokens serve as unique markers that define fixed positions for the embedding extraction.
171
172   Given a text input, we tokenize it, add special tokens, and then feed the modified text into a pre-
173   trained BERT model. In other words, for a tokenised query q = ⟨q1 , q2 , ..., qN ⟩ and document
174
      d = ⟨d1 , d2 , ..., dM ⟩ where N and M are the lengths of the query and document respectively, we
      obtain q ′ = ⟨s1 , s2 , ..., sK , q1 , q2 , ..., qN ⟩ and d′ = ⟨s1 , s2 , ..., sK , d1 , d2 , ..., dM ⟩, where K is the
175
      (same) number of special tokens prepended to both queries and documents. Instead of utilizing
176
      the entire set of token embeddings generated by BERT, we focus exclusively on the embeddings
177   corresponding to the special tokens. This step decouples size of the tokenized string from that of
178   the embedded representation. Thus, given a sequence of embeddings ⟨e1 , e2 , . . . , eK+N ⟩, obtained
179   either from q ′ or d′ , we select only the first K embeddings.
180
      Once the special token embeddings are extracted from BERT, they are passed through a single
181
      linear projection layer. This layer reduces the embedding dimensionality to a size suitable for the
182
      quantisation process, i.e. the number of levels. Following the projection, we apply FSQ to the
183   embeddings, converting the continuous embeddings into quantised codes.
184

186   3.2    C ONTRASTIVE L OSS
187
      Our goal is to obtain discrete representations where matching query-document pairs share as many
188
      codebook tokens as possible, while the distance from other “negative” documents is maximised.
189
      Contrastive losses are a popular choice in these settings, however they require big batch sizes to
190   minimise bias.
191
192   To train our model, we utilize a ColBERT-style loss function. Unlike traditional ColBERT model,
193
      where the similarity between query and document embeddings is measured using cosine similarity,

194

195
      exact matches in level space because of our use of BM25 at inference while, e.g., the codebook
196   entry (0, 0, 0, 0, 0) would return a 0 cosine-distance to all other codebook entries. Moreover, cosine
197   similarity would conflate some linearly dependant codes together. Let Zq and Zd be the sets of
198   quantized codes for the query and document, respectively. Our contrastive loss is defined as:

252   lexical and semantic retrieval signals, leading to improved results.

      4.2      P OSITIONALLY-AWARE R ETRIEVAL
298
299   During inference, we improve the matching accuracy of our discrete representations by incorporat-
300   ing positional information into each code. Specifically, we prefix each discrete code with its position
301   in the sequence; for example, we transform the codes c0 , c1 , c2 into 0 c0 , 1 c1 , 2 c2 . This modifi-
302   cation enables sparse retrieval methods like BM25 to match tokens only if they align in both their
303   semantic content (i.e., code ID) and their position in the sequence.
304   For instance, consider a query with positional codes 0 c52, 1 c113, 2 c23, . . . and a document with
305   positional codes 0 c52, 1 c45, 2 c113, . . .. In this case, the only match occurs at position 0, where
306   both the query and the document have the code c52. Although the code c113 appears in both the
307   query and the document, it is at different positions (position 1 in the query and position 2 in the
308   document). Therefore, with positional codes, these are not considered a match. We empirically
309   found that this approach leads to better results by a small margin (roughly 2% in Success100), as it
310   leverages the model’s tendency to store information at fixed positions in the code sequence.

      4.4   BASELINE
325
326   We also consider a simpler alternative method that maps sequences of text into sequences of dis-
327   crete codes using k-means. Specifically, we employ k-means clustering on token-level embeddings
328   obtained from ColBERT to assign input tokens to codes based on their cluster IDs. Note that this
329   baseline is in part an ingredient of the ColBERT recipe, in that k-means is used for ANN retrieval of
330   candidate tokens. We begin by sampling a subset of the MSMARCO dataset and encoding each doc-
331   ument into vectors. For each document we extract the sequence of encoded token embeddings and
332
      then aggregate these embeddings across all sampled documents to form a corpus of 50M token-level
      embeddings. Using this corpus we train a k-means model with k clusters, each cluster is associated
333
      with a centroid of its members. Finally, we use the clustering model to convert all documents in
334
      MSMARCO into sequences of discrete tokens: for each document, we encode it to obtaining token
335   embeddings ⟨e1 , e2 , ..., e|d| ⟩; we then map each ei to a cluster identifier ci = {1, 2, . . . , K} associ-
336   ated with its nearest centroid according to their euclidean distance. For retrieval, we consider these
337   cluster identifiers as regular tokens. We report results with the discrete tokens as well as with the
338   addition of regular words. Note that for queries and documents the transformed discrete sequences
339   have an 1-1 mapping with their respective (input) text tokens and therefore we do not consider po-
340   sitional matching for scoring. We report results with a k-means model with K = 32000, which
341   produced the best results in a small hyper-parameter tuning experiment.

344
345   The performance of QCR was evaluated on the MSMARCO dataset. The experiments were designed
346   to compare our approach against traditional BM25 and assess the benefits of incorporating discrete
347   embeddings in conjunction with textual information.
348
      In Table 1, we report several evaluation metrics, including mean reciprocal rank (MRR), normalized
349
      discounted cumulative gain (nDCG), and precision at various cutoffs for supervised QCR. Super-
350   vised QCR demonstrated superior performance over the baseline BM25 across all evaluated metrics,
351   supporting our hypothesis that QCR is able to generate semantically rich and discrete embeddings
352   that capture underlying textual semantics better than BM25. We observe similar results in the un-
353   supervised setting, although with a generally lower performance. Furthermore, the optimal results
354   were achieved through a hybrid approach that amalgamates text with discrete representations, pro-
355   viding an hybrid approach that benefits from the complementary strengths of discrete and traditional
356   text embeddings, providing a more holistic representation of text for retrieval tasks.
357   Even though supervised dense retrieval methods trained on the same architecture (BERT-large) out-
358   perform our method, this comes at the cost of high computational and memory overhead. Moreover,
359   we outperform the unsupervised version of Contriever when combining words and discrete codes
360   from QCR-unsupervised. Moreover, we surpass the unsupervised version of Contriever across all
361   reported metrics, demonstrating the potential of integrating both word-level and semantic informa-
362   tion through learned tokens. It is important to note, however, that the unsupervised Contriever was
363   trained on Wikipedia passages and CCNet (Wenzek et al., 2019) data using ICT and random crops,
364
      without any exposure to MSMARCO passages, whereas our approach leverages MSMARCO data
      and synthetic queries.

470
471   6.1                       I NFORMATION R ETRIEVAL
472
473   In recent years, dense retrieval methods have gained prominence, driven by advances in pre-trained
474   transformer-based models such as BERT (Devlin et al., 2019). These models generate dense vectors
475   representations of queries and documents in a continuous embedding space, enabling retrieval based
476
      on semantic similarity rather than lexical matching alone. Dense retrieval models have demonstrated
      strong performance on IR tasks, especially in settings with large human-annotated datasets, as seen
477
      in works like DPR (Karpukhin et al., 2020) and ColBERT (Khattab & Zaharia, 2020).
478
479   Dense retrieval methods effectively overcome vocabulary mismatch by retrieving documents with
480   semantically related terms, even if they differ lexically from the query. However, this advantage
481   comes with high computational and memory costs due to large-scale matrix multiplications and the
482
      need to store dense embeddings for both queries and documents.
483   In response to the high resource demands of dense retrieval models, hybrid approaches have been
484   proposed that combine dense and sparse signals to balance efficiency and effectiveness. Methods
485   such as DeepCT (Dai & Callan, 2019), doc2query (Gospodinov et al., 2023), SPARTA (Zhao et al.,
      2020) and SPLADE (Formal et al., 2021) enrich sparse representations with semantic information


                                                                                             9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      learned by neural networks. These hybrid models attempt to capture the strengths of both sparse
487   and dense representations, offering improved retrieval performance while mitigating some of the
488   computational costs associated with dense approaches. These methods are all orthogonal to QCR,
489   and could be used in conjuction with it to improve overall performance.
490
491
492
      6.2   D ISCRETE R EPRESENTATION L EARNING
493
      The precursor of FSQ, VQ-VAE, has shown impressive results, particularly in the image generation
494
      space, but it is notoriously challenging to optimise, with frequent codebook collapse (i.e. only a
495   small subset of the codebook vectors are utilized by the model). Several techniques have been pro-
496   posed to address the issue of codebook collapse and improve the learning of discrete representations
497   in VQ-VAE models. One approach is the use of “random restarts” (Dhariwal et al., 2020), where
498   underutilized codebook vectors are reset to encoder outputs, helping to ensure diverse usage. Other
499   work, such as (Lancucki et al., 2020), improves codebook learning by periodically reinitializing the
500   codebook using offline clustering methods.
501   However, preliminary experiments with both techniques in their formulation they did not succeed in
502   preventing codebook collapse in our retrieval setting. While the goal of learning improving discreti-
503   sation methods was not our primary objective, the proposed entropy loss demonstrated remarkable
504   stability and performance in this retrieval setting. This suggests that it could serve as an effective
505   alternative for maximizing codebook utilization in FSQ, VQ-VAE, and similar techniques. An inter-
506   esting avenue for future work could be to analyse the impact of this loss on other vector quantisation
507   methods, outside of retrieval.

      7     C ONCLUSION
511
512   In this paper, we introduce QCR, a system able to leverage LMs to generate discrete representations
513   can significantly enhance the effectiveness of sparse retrieval tasks. QCR combines the semantic
514
      richness of dense embeddings with the efficiency and scalability of sparse retrieval methods, such
      as BM25. By introducing a novel integration of FSQ with BERT embeddings, optimized using a
515
      ColBERT-style loss, our system offers a robust approach to information retrieval that significantly
516
      outperforms traditional sparse methods in both supervised and unsupervised settings.
517
518   Furthermore, the use of these discrete representations into existing sparse retrieval infrastructures
519   suggests a scalable and efficient pathway for enhancing the retrieval process without the need for ex-
520
      tensive computational resources. This characteristic is particularly valuable in resource-constrained
      environments or scenarios where rapid scaling of information retrieval systems is required.
521
522
523   Future Work Looking ahead, the potential applications of these discrete embeddings extend be-
524   yond traditional text retrieval. QCR could be adaptable to more complex retrieval scenarios, includ-
525   ing multimodal contexts where integration of different types of data is necessary. Future work could
526
      explore the application of discrete embeddings to help the performance of generative retrieval mod-
      els, where the generative capabilities of LLMs can be harnessed to create dynamic query responses
527
      based on the rich semantic understanding encapsulated within the discrete codes. Moreover, future
528
      works might be able to dynamically control the size of the query and documents, as well optimise the
529   distribution of the codebook to maximise throughput and minimise latency beyond what is possible
530   for lexical term matching, which has to adapt itself to natural language statistics. Finally, train-
531   ing on larger models and for longer periods, with increased batch sizes, could lead to considerable
532   performance gains, unlocking further potential of the method.
