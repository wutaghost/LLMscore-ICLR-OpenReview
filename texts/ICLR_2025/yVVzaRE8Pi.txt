009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            While recent advancements in large language models (LLMs) alignment have en-
012            abled the effective identification of malicious objectives involving scene nesting
013            and keyword rewriting, our study reveals that these methods remain inadequate at
014            detecting malicious objectives expressed through context within nested harmless
015            objectives. This study identifies a previously overlooked vulnerability, which we
016            term Attack via Implicit Reference (AIR). AIR decomposes a malicious objec-
017            tive into permissible objectives and links them through implicit references within
018
               the context. This method employs multiple related harmless objectives to generate
               malicious content without triggering refusal responses, thereby effectively bypass-
019
               ing most existing detection techniques. Our experiments demonstrate AIR’s effec-
020
               tiveness across state-of-the-art LLMs, achieving an attack success rate (ASR) ex-
021            ceeding 90% on most models, including GPT-4o, Claude-3.5-Sonnet, and Qwen-
022            2-72B. Notably, we observe an inverse scaling phenomenon, where larger models
023            are more vulnerable to this attack method. These findings underscore the urgent
024            need for defense mechanisms capable of understanding and preventing contextual
025            attacks. Furthermore, we introduce a cross-model conversation attack strategy
026            that leverages less secure models to generate malicious contexts, thereby further
027            increasing the ASR when targeting other models.

030   1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   Large Language Models (LLMs) have shown remarkable language understanding capabilities (Ope-
033
      nAI, 2023a; Google, 2023; Anthropic, 2023b; Touvron et al., 2023), demonstrating their effective-
      ness across various applications to interact with humans such as chatbots, code generation, and tool
034
      utilization (OpenAI, 2023b; Bubeck et al., 2023; Schick et al., 2024; Chiang & Lee, 2023; Park
035
      et al., 2023; Jiao et al., 2023). However, their widespread adoption has introduced critical secu-
036   rity vulnerabilities that pose significant societal risks, including the generation of harmful or biased
037   content, the dissemination of misinformation, and the facilitation of malicious activities such as
038   cyberattacks (Bengio et al., 2024).
039
      To address these risks, researchers have developed various security mechanisms and alignment tech-
040
      niques, including supervised fine-tuning (Ouyang et al., 2022; Bianchi et al., 2024a), reinforcement
041
      learning from human feedback (Christiano et al., 2017), and adversarial training (Perez et al., 2022;
042   Ganguli et al., 2022; OpenAI, 2023a). These approaches aim to train LLMs to reject malicious
043   queries and align their outputs with human values and ethical standards.s
044
045
      Jailbreak techniques, such as keyword replacement and scenario nesting (Liu et al., 2023b; Deng
      et al., 2024; Ding et al., 2023; Jin et al., 2024), can be effectively detected by the latest model (An-
046
      thropic, 2024). However, maintaining the models’ usefulness without imposing overly restrictive
047
      measures remains a significant challenge. It is difficult to limit every objective without hindering the
048   model’s overall functionality (Tuan et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2023a; Dai et al., 2023). Consequently,
049   while broad malicious objectives may be rejected, the model’s ability to respond to benign objectives
050   related to specific subjects in requests remains unrestricted. Previous methods typically embed the
051   malicious objective within harmless objectives (Li et al., 2023b; Zeng et al., 2024). In contrast, we
052   found that by introducing the discussion subject using a harmless objective and then incorporating
053   the malicious objective with implicit references A.2 that omit the subject—thereby making it appear
      innocuous—the model fails to identify potential malicious objectives, as illustrated in Figure 1.

054
      Based on this observation, we propose a method named Attack via Implicit Reference (AIR), which
055   comprises two stages of the conversation. In the first stage, AIR bypasses the model’s rejection
056   mechanism by breaking down malicious objectives into nested benign objectives A.2. The first
057   objective is to generate harmless content that uses the subject from the original request as the topic,
058   and the second objective is to add content about the derived behavior from the original request, using
059   implicit reference refer to the topic. In the second stage, AIR sends a follow-up rewrite request that
060   includes implicit references to the content generated for the second objective in the previous stage
061   while excluding any malicious keywords. This request prompts the model to remove unrelated parts
062   from the prior conversation and to provide more detailed information about the desired response.
063   To evaluate the efficacy of AIR, we conducted experiments on the latest LLMs using 100 malicious
064   behaviors from JailbreakBench (Chao et al., 2024). Our results demonstrated that all state-of-the-art
065   models we tested, including GPT-4o-0513 (OpenAI, 2024), Claude-3.5-sonnet (Anthropic, 2024),
066   LLaMA-3-70b (Dubey et al., 2024), and Qwen-2-72b (Yang et al., 2024a), were vulnerable to jail-
067   break attack via implicit references. Furthermore, our evaluation of existing detection methods,
068   including SmoothLLM (Robey et al., 2024), PerplexityFilter (Alon & Kamfonas, 2024), and Erase-
069
      and-Check (Kumar et al., 2024) from JailbreakBench, revealed that current detection methods were
      unable to effectively defend against this attack method.
070
071   Additionally, we observed a reverse scaling phenomenon A.2, where larger models exhibited higher
072   attack success rate (ASR). Our analysis of different model sizes within the LLaMA-3 and Qwen-2
073   series showed a significant relationship between model size and vulnerability to contextual attacks.
074   Furthermore, the probability of models refusing to answer did not vary with size, but the probability
075
      of generating desired malicious responses increased as model size became larger.
076   We also introduced a cross-model conversation attack strategy, which used less secure models as
077   the targets of the first attack stage and then targeted more secure models to continue attacks and
078   generate malicious content. This method further increased the ASR when attacking more secure
079   models. This approach demonstrated that the attack method could be transferred between models
080   and could exploit the vulnerabilities of the less secure models.
081   In summary, our key contributions are:
082
083          • We introduce implicit reference attack, a jailbreak method that leverages LLMs’ in-context
084            learning ability to enable the model to generate malicious content.
085
086          • We demonstrate a reverse scaling phenomenon in implicit reference attacks and reveal that
087
               models with better in-context learning abilities are more vulnerable to this attack method.
088          • We present a cross-model conversation attack strategy for AIR, showing that by substituting
089            less secure models during the first attack stage, the ASR increases when targeting more
090            secure models at the second stage, which also demonstrates the transferability of implicit
091            reference attack.

097   2.1   T HE S UPERFICIAL A LIGNMENT H YPOTHESIS
098
099
      Previous studies demonstrate that LLMs acquire extensive knowledge during their pre-training
      phase (Zhou et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2023). However, alignment processes—such as instruction
100
      fine-tuning and reinforcement learning from human feedback—are used to adjust how this knowl-
101
      edge is expressed, ensuring that the models meet user expectations and adhere to ethical standards.
102   These techniques primarily alter the models’ surface behaviors without fundamentally changing the
103   knowledge learned during pre-training, allowing LLMs to retain the underlying knowledge while
104   only modifying their expression. Safety alignment fine-tuning maps the knowledge triggered by
105   malicious objectives to produce refusal responses while leaving benign objectives largely unaffected
106   to prevent over-restriction. For example, a prompt such as “How to make a bomb” will be re-
107   fused, whereas a prompt like “Write an introduction about bombs” will not be refused
      by the model.

      2.2   C OMPETING O BJECTIVES
131
132   LLMs are trained with multiple goals that can sometimes conflict (Wei et al., 2023). The model
133   might prioritize fulfilling user requests over following safety rules, especially when safety mech-
134   anisms are not deeply integrated into its core reasoning. For example, a writing objective such
135   as “Write an introduction about bombs” will be fulfilled, whereas a writing objective
136   that includes malicious behavior, such as “Write an introduction about bombs and
137   how to make bombs”, will trigger the model’s internal refusal mechanism, competing with the
138   fulfillment of the user’s writing objectives.
139
140   2.3   N ESTING O BJECTIVE G ENERATION
141
142   LLMs use attention mechanisms to focus on relevant prior outputs when addressing the content of
143
      the current objective. By structuring the prompt with implicit references and nesting the outputs of
      activated objectives, we link these objectives to produce a coherent final result.
144
145   Implicit references allow the model to seamlessly connect different objectives, enabling the output
146   of one objective to serve as the context or foundation for subsequent objectives. This nesting en-
147   sures that each objective builds upon the results of the previous ones, maintaining coherence and
148   consistency in the final output.
149   Probabilistic Modeling Assumptions: The primary assumption underlying our derivation is that
150   the generation process of the model can be represented as a conditional probability distribution,
151   conditioned not only on the current objective On but also on the entire sequence of preceding objec-
152   tives O1 , O2 , . . . , On−1 . This aligns with the autoregressive nature of Transformer models, where
153   each token prediction inherently depends on the cumulative context. By modeling this conditional
154   dependency explicitly, we ensure that our formulas capture the hierarchical and temporal structure
155
      embedded within the model’s learned representations.
156   We define a series of objectives {Oi } for i = 1, 2, . . . , n, where each objective Oi generates its
157   result based on the outputs of all preceding objectives. The probability of generating the result R by
158   the model M is defined as follows:

      from the second objective, we ensure that this objective also remains benign.

      Adding More Objectives As mentioned in Section 2.2, incorporating additional objectives can ef-
217   fectively counteract the model’s refusal mechanism. To leverage this insight, we propose a method
218   to introduce more objectives into the generation process. Specifically, we add an abstract writing ob-
219   jective, Oabstract , and a background writing objective, Obackground , before the target objective, Otarget ,
220   within the same request. Additionally, we include a title objective, Otitle , to guide the generation
221   further. By introducing additional objectives that the model is unlikely to refuse, we create a conflict
222   between the model’s ability to satisfy these objectives and its inherent capacity to detect and reject
223   malicious requests from the context.

225   3.2   C ONTINUE ATTACK
226
227   The use of nested objectives often leads to the generation of irrelevant content, such as judg-
228   mental and evaluative statements, as well as the inclusion of additional objectives like abstract
229
      writing and background writing in the final output. To amplify malicious content while remov-
      ing unrelated material, we continue to send the model another benign objective. A Simplified
230
      prompt would be “Please add more details to the example in paragraph #
231
      and remove judgement”, which directs the model to add more details to specific sections of
232   its response(See Appendix A.6 for the full prompt). The prompt for this objective does not con-
233   tain any malicious elements. By prompting the model to refine specific sections of its own output
234   through the amplification task, we ensure that the generated content remains focused on the mali-
235   cious objectives and does not include unintended objective-related content.

      In this section, we conduct three experiments using the AIR framework to verify the effectiveness of
273   jailbreak attacks via implicit references. The experiments focus on the following three aspects: (1)
274   Effectiveness Verification: We verify our approach by comparing it with existing jailbreak tech-
275   niques on state-of-the-art models. (2) Model Size and ASR: We examine how changing the model
276   size affects the probability of generating malicious text by experimenting with models of different
277   sizes. (3) Number of Objectives and ASR: We explore how varying the number of objectives in
278   prompts influences the ASR by adjusting the number of abilities activated before generating mali-
279   cious content.

474   7.1   D EFENSE M ECHANISMS
475
476
      To mitigate the threat of jailbreak attacks, researchers have proposed various defense strategies.
      Many approaches enhance the security of LLMs by aligning their responses with human prefer-
477
      ences, commonly employing techniques such as Reinforcement Learning from Human Feedback
478
      (RLHF) (Ouyang et al., 2022) and Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) (Rafailov et al., 2023;
479   Liu et al., 2024b; Gallego, 2024). Additionally, adversarial training methods improve the models’
480   robustness against malicious attacks by incorporating adversarial samples during training.
481
482
      Fine-tuning on jailbreak strings is another prevalent defense strategy, where known jailbreak at-
      tack examples are added to the training data, enabling the models to recognize and resist similar
483
      attacks (Bianchi et al., 2024b; Deng et al., 2023a).
484
485   During testing, defense strategies such as SmoothLLM (Robey et al., 2024; Ji et al., 2024), Perplex-
      ity Filtering (Jain et al., 2023; Alon & Kamfonas, 2024), and Erase-and-Check add layers to detect


                                                         9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      and block jailbreak attempts in real-time by perturbing prompts and generating resistant variants, an-
487   alyzing query perplexity and token metrics to reduce false positives, and iteratively removing tokens
488   while using safety filters to ensure harmful inputs are not mistakenly approved.
489
490
      During testing, several defense methods are employed to detect and block jailbreak attempts in
      real-time. defense strategies such as SmoothLLM (Robey et al., 2024; Ji et al., 2024) enhances
491
      security by introducing character-level perturbations to prompts and generating multiple resistant
492
      variants. Perplexity Filtering (Jain et al., 2023; Alon & Kamfonas, 2024) uses query perplexity, a
493   Light-GBM model, and token length to minimize false positives and accurately identify adversarial
494   attacks. Additionally, Erase-and-Check (Kumar et al., 2024) iteratively removes tokens from inputs
495   and applies a safety filter to ensure that harmful prompts are not mistakenly approved.
496
497   7.2   JAILBREAK ATTACK M ETHODS
498
499   Despite continuous improvements in defense mechanisms, researchers have developed vari-
500   ous jailbreak attack techniques to evaluate and expose LLMs’ security vulnerabilities. These
501   methods fall into three main categories: Human-Designed, Long-Tail Encoding, and Prompt
502
      Optimization.Human-designed methods evade model restrictions by inserting malicious history con-
      text (Shayegani et al., 2023; Wei et al.; Anil et al.), generating malicious contexts through multiple
503
      rounds of conversations (Liu et al., 2024a; Jiang et al., 2024c; Gibbs et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024c;
504
      Cheng et al., 2024), and employing role-playing (Li et al., 2023a; Ma et al., 2024). These methods
505   create contextual environments that guide models to ignore safety guidelines. Long-Tail Encoding
506   strategies exploit models’ limited generalization capabilities with unseen or rare data, employing
507   techniques such as past tense rewrites, low-resource language translations, or input encryption (Deng
508   et al., 2023c; Lv et al., 2024; Yuan et al., 2023). Prompt Optimization employs automated techniques
509   to identify and exploit model weaknesses, including gradient analysis (Zou et al., 2023), genetic al-
510   gorithms (Liu et al., 2023a), prompt variations (Yu & Lin; Yao et al., 2023), and iterative prompt
511   refinement (Chao et al., 2023). These approaches may also involve building auxiliary models (Deng
512   et al., 2023b), fine-tuning with template datasets (Yang et al., 2023b), and using success rates as
513
      reward mechanisms to enhance the effectiveness of prompt generation (Lin et al., 2024).
514
515   8     L IMITATIONS AND S OCIAL I MPACT
516
517   Our approach requires models with capabilities similar to GPT-4o-0513. We found that legacy
518   models, such as GPT-3.5 Turbo, struggle to understand and effectively complete the rewriting task
519   due to their limited comprehension skills. In contrast, newer models, like GPT-4o-0806 and Claude-
520   3.5-Sonnet, often decline our rewriting requests. In addition, we only experimented with the writing
521   scenario, but this does not mean the implicit reference attack is limited to writing. It can also work
522
      in other scenarios if two objectives can be nested, such as a Python class and its functions, and table
      filling with a specific caption.
523
524   Our method does not require complex text structures or specialized encoding languages to replace
525   keywords. Human-led summarization and decomposition of harmful objectives usually would be
526   more effective. Therefore, compared to other complex rewriting methods, our findings may intro-
527   duce greater risks. However, as current LLMs only show early signs of dangerous capabilities (An-
528
      thropic, 2023a), we believe our work does not pose a significant risk to society. Instead, we see this
      study as a contribution that highlights areas that future defense efforts should focus on.
529
530
531   9     C ONCLUSION
532
533   We introduced a new jailbreak method (AIR) that decomposes malicious objectives into nested
534   harmless objectives and uses implicit references to cause LLMs to generate malicious content with-
535   out triggering existing safety mechanisms.
536   Our experiments demonstrate that implicit reference attacks represent a vulnerability in existing
537   safety alignment, and larger models with advanced in-context learning capabilities are more vulner-
538   able to them. This underscores the need for more sophisticated mechanisms to detect and mitigate
539   malicious objectives in implicit reference form. Future research should focus on enhancing models’
      ability to identify and reject malicious intent hidden in context.

913   Objective In the context of instructions given to LLMs, it is common for a single instruction to
914   involve multiple tasks. For instance, an instruction may ask the model to first write an article and
915   then translate it into English. We define such instructions as consisting of two sequential objectives.

      Nesting Objective Generation Nesting Objective Generation is a technique used by LLMs to or-
919   ganize multiple objectives in a hierarchical manner. This method ensures that each new objective
920   builds upon the results of previous ones, creating a coherent and unified final output. By utiliz-
921   ing attention mechanisms, the model focuses on relevant prior outputs when addressing each new
922   objective, maintaining consistency and logical flow throughout the generation process.
923
924   Implicit References Implicit References are indirect connections between different objectives
925   within a prompt structure that do not require explicit mentions or citations. In LLMs, implicit
926   references allow the model to integrate and build upon prior outputs naturally by using contextual
927   cues embedded in the prompt. This seamless linkage ensures that each new objective is informed
928   by the preceding ones, facilitating a smooth and coherent progression without overtly specifying
929   relationships.
930
931   Reverse Scaling Phenomenon Reverse Scaling Phenomenon refers to the situation where increas-
932   ing the size of a model does not lead to improved performance and may even result in decreased
933   effectiveness on certain tasks. Contrary to the expectation that larger models consistently enhance
934   capabilities, this phenomenon highlights instances where larger models may struggle with specific
935   objectives or exhibit unintended behaviors. Understanding reverse scaling is crucial for optimizing
936   model size and ensuring that scaling efforts contribute positively to performance outcomes.

938   A.2.1   E XTENDED D ISSCUSSION
939
940   Balancing Safety and Utility LLMs are calibrated to balance safety and utility, aiming to avoid
941
      both harmful outputs and excessive refusals of legitimate requests. Overly aggressive filtering can
      degrade user experience by rejecting valid queries. AIR leverages this calibration by presenting
942
      sensitive requests in a way that appears legitimate, thereby reducing the likelihood of triggering the
943
      model’s refusal mechanisms.

      A.2.2   C OMPARISON WITH E XISTING M ETHODS
947
      Our proposed method introduces a novel approach to jailbreak attacks on large language models
948   (LLMs), distinguishing itself from existing techniques in several key aspects. Traditional scenario
949   nesting methods, such as DeepInception (Li et al., 2023b), rely on explicitly embedding malicious
950   content within nested scenarios in the prompts. In contrast, our approach leverages implicit refer-
951   ences to subtly incorporate malicious intent, making it more challenging for defense mechanisms to
952   detect the attack due to the lack of overt malicious cues.
953
      Multi-turn jailbreak attacks (Russinovich et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024b; Zhou et al., 2024b) engage
954   the model in multiple rounds of conversation to gradually reveal malicious content. These meth-
955   ods often require evaluating each conversation turn and adjusting prompts based on the model’s
956   responses, which can be time-consuming and complex. Our method differs by eliciting the intended
957   output in the first interaction, eliminating the need for iterative evaluations or adjustments. This
958   not only enhances efficiency but also makes our approach more user-friendly, as it does not require
959   external tools or sophisticated prompt engineering.
960   Our method also sets itself apart from contextual and substitution attacks. For instance, the Past
961   Tense method (Andriushchenko & Flammarion, 2024) deceives the model by changing the tense in
962   the prompts, while ArtPrompt (Jiang et al., 2024b) relies on keyword substitution and depends on
963   external resources like ASCII art generators. These methods either achieve lower success rates or
964   require additional tools, whereas our approach is self-contained and attains higher attack success
965   rates without external dependencies.
966   Our findings suggest that only defense methods that compromise the helpfulness of the model can
967   effectively mitigate such attacks. This presents a significant trade-off between safety alignment and
968   model performance. The need to balance this trade-off is further emphasized in recent work like
969   ChatBug (Jiang et al., 2024a), which uncovers vulnerabilities induced by chat templates used in
970   instruction-following LLMs. They demonstrate that overly rigid defense mechanisms can be ex-
971   ploited by malicious users, highlighting the challenges in designing defenses that are both effective
      and maintain the utility of the model.

       A.2.3   R EFUSAL ANALYSIS
973
974    Model Limitations Some models, due to their smaller size, lack robust context learning capabili-
975    ties and are therefore unable to comprehend complex intentions. For instance, models like Qwen2-
976    0.5b tend to generate responses that are unrelated to the given context. As a result, the content
977    produced by these models cannot be recognized by evaluators as malicious content, thereby hin-
978
       dering effective assessment and control of the model’s behavior. Additionally, we observed that
       LLaMA-3-70b does not refuse to generate content such as bomb-making instructions; however, due
979
       to its lack of relevant knowledge, it fails to produce sufficiently malicious responses.
980
981
       Malicious Objectives Cannot Be Decomposed Some malicious objectives cannot be further de-
982
       composed into a nested form. For instance, the term “Pedophilia” cannot be broken down further.
983    However, Claude-3.5-sonnet clearly refuses to respond to related queries, even when asked to pro-
984    vide a neutral explanation or introduction.
985
986    Self Remind Sometimes, a model will reject nested objectives in the first attack, but may still
987    refuse to provide additional details in the second attack. However, when using historical conversa-
988    tions from other models as context, the model often doesn’t reject the query. We compared Claude-
989    3.5-sonnet’s responses to nested requests, such as bomb-making instructions, where it frequently
990    emphasizes that such activities are illegal and thus refrains from providing further details. In con-
991    trast, contexts generated by GPT-4o do not heavily emphasize the word ”illegal,” and as a result,
992    Claude-3.5-sonnet, when replying based on GPT-4o’s context, does not reject the query as firmly.
