011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Large language models (LLMs) becomes the dominant paradigm for the chal-
014            lenging task of text-to-SQL. LLM-empowered text-to-SQL methods are typically
015
               categorized into prompting-based and tuning approaches. Compared to prompting-
               based methods, benchmarking fine-tuned LLMs for text-to-SQL is important yet
016            under-explored, partially attributed to the prohibitively high computational cost. In
017            this paper, we present DB-GPT-Hub, an open benchmark suite for LLM-empowered
018            text-to-SQL, which primarily focuses on tuning LLMs at large scales. The proposed
019            benchmark consists of: 1. a standardized and comprehensive evaluation of text-
020
               to-SQL tasks by fine-tuning medium to large-sized open LLMs; 2. a modularized
               and easy-to-extend codebase with mainstream LLMs and experimental scenarios
021            supported, which prioritizes fine-tuning methods but can be easily extended to
022            prompt-based setting. Our work investigates the potential gains and the performance
023            boundaries of tuning approaches, compared to prompting approaches and explores
024            optimal solutions tailored to specific scenarios. We hope DB-GPT-Hub, along with
025
               these findings, enables further research and broad applications that would otherwise
               be difficult owing to the absence of a dedicated open benchmark. The project code
026            has been released anonymously at https://github.com/anonymity-360/DB-GPT-Hub.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
031
032   The task of text-to-SQL, which converts natural utterances into SQL queries, has emerged as a
033
      popular topic in both natural language processing and database (Yu et al., 2018b; Deng et al.,
      2022). It effectively narrows the gap between non-expert users and database systems, significantly
034
      enhancing data processing efficiency. Essentially, text-to-SQL can be characterized as a sequence-to-
035
      sequence modeling task (Sutskever et al., 2014), where the database schema and the natural language
036   question are transformed into a sequential input, while the desired SQL query serves as the sequential
037   output target. Early works focus on fine-tuning domain-specific Transformer models and developing
038   decoding techniques specifically for the task, leveraging SQL syntax, semantics, and the complex
039   interplay between questions and databases (Scholak et al., 2021; Qi et al., 2022).
040
      While recently large language models (LLMs) such as ChatGPT (Brown et al., 2020) and GPT-4 (Ope-
041
      nAI, 2023a) have showcased their remarkable capabilities in engaging in human-like communication
042   and understanding complex queries, LLMs have emerged as a new paradigm for text-to-SQL (Liu
043   et al., 2023; Trummer, 2022). Notably, since 2023, the majority of top-performing solutions on the
044   Spider leaderboard (Yale, 2018) have been methods based on LLMs.
045
      The most recent advancement in this area involves employing LLMs for generating accurate SQL
046
      queries through in-context learning (ICL) techniques, notably zero-shot and few-shot prompting
047
      (OpenAI, 2023b; Dong et al., 2023; Pourreza & Rafiei, 2023). Beyond the inherent challenge of
048   ambiguity and complexity, the laborious efforts for annotating SQL query-response exemplars by
049   domain experts hinder the process of scaling-up data hungry LLMs for text-to-SQL applications.
050   Meanwhile, another prominent approach is fine-tuning LLMs using additional task-specific training
051   data to enhance their efficacy for text-to-SQL tasks Li et al. (2023a); Sun et al. (2023). The
052   remarkable performances achieved in these works indicate the immense potential of fine-tuning.
053   However, compared to prompting approaches, fine-tuning approaches have been relatively under-
      explored, partially attributed to the prohibitively high computational cost. Recent systematic studies


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      (Gao et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024) still mainly highlight the ICL abilities of LLMs and their
055   accuracy in generating SQL queries in relevant tasks.
056
057   Up until now, there still has not been a universally acknowledged open benchmark for tuning
058
      approaches, which impedes researchers and practitioners from comparing methods and reproducing
      results, potentially slowing down advancement in this field. As a first step towards addressing these
059
      challenges, in this work, we present a holistic framework, namely DB-GPT-Hub,. Apart from existing
060
      works that mostly focus on few-shot prompting strategies or tuning relatively smaller LLMs,
061   our work focuses on tuning larger LLMs. In all, DB-GPT-Hub consolidates essential research
062   assets (e.g., data, model services, evaluation methods, documentation) with following distinct merits:
063
064   • Standardization. We establish a standardized pipeline in an open-source codebase, with uni-
065     fied experimental settings and containerized environments, to enable transparent and consistent
066     comparisons of LLM models after text-to-SQL tasks tuning.
067   • Comprehensiveness. We conduct extensive benchmarking that covers a range of medium to
068     large-sized, fine-tuned LLMs across various experimental scenarios and explore their relative
069     performance compared to prompting methods. Our work comprises one of the most pragmatic and
070     expansive sets of benchmark suites available.
071
      • Extensibility. As a rapidly evolving field, novel LLM-based methods constantly emerge, and
072     the best practice continuously evolves. Following our documentation and protocols, one could
073     effortlessly incorporate novel ingredients into our codebase: new datasets, new modules, new
074     models (or model services), and new evaluation programs. Moreover, our framework offers easy
075     compatibility with various prompting techniques. The high extensibility will eventually benefit the
076     research area of text-to-SQL.

      2.1       A G ENERALIZED S ETUP
081
082   The input of text-to-SQL task is a natural language question q and the database information D.
083   The output is the SQL s corresponding to the question. The database D = {S, Kp , Kf } includes
084   database schema S, primary keys Kp and foreign keys Kf , where S usually contains multiple tables
085   Tk : S = {T1 , T2 , ...Ts ...}. Each table Tk has table name Nk , column names cj and column data
086   types tj . Therefore, Tk = {Nk , (ck1 , tk1 ), (ck2 , tk2 )...}. Consider the queries may come from various
087   database domains, we formulate the data into a set of triples M = {(qi , si , Di )}, with i denoting the
088   index of the query, the output and source database.
089
090   2.2       P ROMPT- BASED AND F INE - TUNING S ETTINGS
091
092   Based on how LLMs are used for text-to-SQL generations, the problem settings can be categorized
093
      into two scenarios: zero-shot/few-shot prompting and fine-tuning.
094
095   Zero-shot / Few-shot Prompting. In zero-shot scenarios, no exemplar is provided while in few-
096
      shot a few input–output exemplars are provided to prompt LLMs. Formally, given a pretrained
      LLM parameterized by θ, the question qi , and k exemplars (k ≥ 0), the objective is maximize the
097
      probability of generating the correct SQL si from the LLM:
098
099                                       max PLLMθ (si |σ(qi , M)),      |M| = k                            (1)
                                           si
100
101   where Θ and σ(qi , M) 1 denotes a representation space of the target question qi by incorporating
102   relevant information from exemplars.
103
104   Fine-tuning. The fine-tuing process involves adapting the pretrained LLMθ to generate SQL from
105   the input sequences by tuning the model with text-to-SQL datasets, which contain a collection of
106   serialized inputs qi and corresponding SQL outputs si pairs. The object of fine-tuning is minimize
107

112   where L is the loss function to measure the difference between the generated SQL and the groundtruth.
113   Despite the significant advances achieved with few-shot prompting of LLMs, it remains a formidable
114   challenge for a pretrained LLM to rely solely on its parametric knowledge and prompting to accurately
115   process highly complex SQL queries.
116
117   Parameter-Efficient Fine-tuning. Medium to large-sized models with billions of parameters, are
118   prohibitively expensive to fine-tune in order to adapt them to particular tasks or domains. Parameter-
119   Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT) methods enable efficient adaptation of large pretrained models to
120   various downstream applications by only fine-tuning a small number of (extra) model parame-
121   ters instead of all the model’s parameters. Two mostly commonly used techniques are LoRA (Hu
122   et al., 2021), which proposes to freeze pretrained model weights and inject trainable layers (rank-
123
      decomposition matrices) in each transformer block, and its quantized version QLoRA (Dettmers
      et al., 2023). Throughout the benchmark, we use these two strategies consistently to tune the LLMs.
124
      See Section 3 and Section 4 for details of tuning benchmark design and experimental results.

      Datasets. We conduct experiments mainly on the following 2 well recognized public datasets:
132
      • Spider (Yu et al., 2018b). Spider is a large-scale cross-domain dataset consisting of 10,181 natural
133     language queries, 5,693 unique complex SQL queries across 200 databases, covering 138 domains.
134     The standard protocol for this dataset divides it into 8,659 training examples and a holdout of 2,147
135     test examples across 34 databases. SQL queries are categorized into four difficulty levels, i.e., easy,
136     medium, hard and extra hard.
137   • BIRD (Li et al., 2023b). It comprises an extensive dataset with 12,751 unique question-SQL pairs,
138     encompassing 95 large databases. SQL queries are categorized into three difficulty levels, i.e.,
139     simple, moderate and challenge. Notably, the SQL queries in the BIRD dataset tend to be more
140     intricate than those in the Spider dataset.
141
142   Moreover, our codebase universally supports tuning a wide range of popular dataset, such as Wik-
143   iSQL (Zhong et al., 2017), CoSQL (Yu et al., 2019), Chase (Guo et al., 2021) (see Appendix A.1

      for the detailed statistics of each dataset.) and due to the page limit, we continually post updated

190    Metrics. We use two commonly used metrics, exact-set-match accuracy (EM), execution accuracy
191    (EX) to evaluate the performance of all models. EM measures the matched SQL keywords between
192
       the predicted SQL query and its corresponding ground truth while EX compares the execution output
       of the predicted SQL query with that of the ground truth SQL query on some database instances. EX
193
       provides a more precise estimate of the model’s performance since there may be multiple valid SQL
194
       queries for a given question. For both metrics, the higher is considered the better. We mainly use EX
195    to evaluate the accuracy of SQLs in the paper. See Appendix A.2 for details.
196

223   3.2     C ODEBASE
224
225   To facilitate the innovation of the community, our DB-GPT-Hub contains a well-modularized, easy-
226   to-extend codebase for standardization of implementation, evaluation, and ablation of text-to-SQL
227
      methods.
228
      Software Architecture. Figure 1 presents the pipeline and architecture of our codebase. Pipelines
229
      are decomposed into following parts:
230
231   • Dataset Construction. Raw text-to-SQL data is processed into a suitable format (e.g., TRF shown
232     in Listing 1 ) to tune LLMs. This includes integrating the schema and database description into a
233     prompt as an instruction, along with various question representations to boost performance during
234     training and evaluation. Additionally, we will select different few-shot strategies, such as example
235     selection and organization, to construct the evaluation dataset Gao et al. (2023).
236   • Training. Our codebase supports the fine-tuning of open-source LLMs with PEFT strategies. We
237     support most of the public architecture with small to large-sized model scales, such as Qwen,
238     Llama, Baichuan, and ChatGLM.
239   • Prediction. Our codebase supports SQL query inference for open-source LLMs with its fine-tuned
240     version and closed-source LLMs as well. We support the few-shot and zero-shot method to generate
241     SQLs for specific scenarios.
242   • Evaluation. Our repository holds different metrics(EX, EM, valid efficiency score(VES)) to
243     evaluate the performance of generated SQL from different perspectives.
244
245   Implementations. The codebase is built with the PyTorch framework (Paszke et al., 2017), upon
246   the open source project DB-GPT (Xue et al., 2023a; 2024a). We release the code with Apache License
247   2.0 and we are committed to actively maintain the repository.

451
      5.1   LLM- EMPOWERED T EXT- TO -SQL M ETHODS
452
453   Driven by the considerable success of LLMs, the field of LLM-empowered text-to-SQL has captured
454   the interest of a large amount of researchers both in nature language process and database community
455   recently. The models on LLM-based text-to-SQL can be categorized into supervised fine-tuning based
456   and prompting based methods. Popular fine-tuned text-to-sql models are SQL-PaLM (Sun et al.,
457   2023), PICARD (Scholak et al., 2021) and RESDSQL (Li et al., 2023a). In contrast to supervised
458   fine-tuned models, prompting-based models do not require additional fine-tuning on task-specific
459   training data. Instead, they solely rely on the zero-shot and few-shot (Rajkumar et al., 2022; Liu et al.,
460   2023) capabilities inherent in LLMs. Within the prompting paradigm, the pivotal factor for query
461
      representation lies in the design of the prompt (Wei et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2022a).
      In particular, DIN-SQL (Pourreza & Rafiei, 2023) introduces adaptive prompt strategies via task
462
      decomposition to effectively address challenges associated with schema linking. DAIL-SQL (Gao
463
      et al., 2023) proposes a refined prompt selection and organization strategy to improve the performance.
464   In DB-GPT-Hub, we offer scripts to support researchers in fine-tuning LLMs in accordance with
465   the methodologies established in SQL-PaLM. In addition, we also integrate the popular prompt
466   techniques used in DAIL-SQL.
467
468
469   5.2   T EXT- TO -SQL B ENCHMARKS
470
471
      A pivotal factor in the progression of text-to-SQL is the establishment of high-quality benchmarks.
      Early benchmarks focus on single databases, including ATIS (Dahl et al., 1994), GeoQuery (Zelle
472
      & Mooney, 1996), Academic (Li & Jagadish, 2014), Advising (Finegan-Dollak et al., 2018), and
473
      more recent additions such as SEDE (Hazoom et al., 2021) and MIMICSQL (Wang et al., 2019).
474   These benchmarks and datasets are often adapted from real-life applications, with many containing
475   domain-specific knowledge that may not generalize effectively to unseen SQL domains. Hence,
476   large-scale cross-domain datasets featuring professional SQL queries, such as Squall (Shi et al.,
477   2020), Spider (Yu et al., 2018a), Spider-Syn (Gan et al., 2021), WikiSQL (Zhong et al., 2017), and
478   SparC (Yu et al., 2020), have been introduced to facilitate comprehensive method analyses.
479
      In retrospect, we realize two concurrent works (Gao et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2024) which perform
480   systematical benchmarking on text-to-SQL methods. Important distinctions of their work from
481   ours include: 1. comprehensiveness of benchmark settings: we evaluate both ICL and medium to
482   large-sized fine-tuning methods in an end-to-end manner while Gao et al. (2023) focus on ICL
483   methods and Zhang et al. (2024) assess various sub-tasks of the text-to-SQL process; 2. open source
484   of the codebase: we released a well-maintained open repository on Github containing all code and
485   data assets, which, to the best of knowledge, is one of the most popular text-to-SQL benchmark
      repositories (over 1k stars so far), while neither of them has achieved this.

      A.1    DATASET D ETAILS
763
      Spider (Yu et al., 2018b). It consists of 10,181 questions and 5,693 unique complex SQL queries
764
      across 200 databases, covering 138 domains, each containing multiple tables. The standard protocol
765   for this dataset divides it into 8,659 training examples across 146 databases, 1,034 development
766   examples across 20 databases, and a holdout of 2,147 test examples across 34 databases. The databases
767   used in each of these sets are nonoverlapping. SQL queries are categorized into four difficulty levels,
768   based on the number of SQL keywords used, the presence of nested subqueries, and the usage of
769   column selections and aggregations.
770
      BIRD (Li et al., 2023b). This dataset represents a pioneering, cross-domain dataset that examines
771   the impact of extensive database contents on text-to-SQL parsing. BIRD contains over 12,751
772   unique question-SQL pairs, 95 big databases with a total size of 33.4 GB. It also covers more than
773   37 professional domains, such as blockchain, hockey, healthcare and education, etc. BIRD also
774   introduces external knowledge as an additional resource to assist models in generating accurate
775   SQL queries. Specifically four sources of external knowledge were introduced: numeric reasoning
776   knowledge, domain knowledge, synonym knowledge, and value illustration. Notably, the SQL queries
777   in the BIRD dataset tend to be more intricate than those in the Spider dataset.
778   WikiSQL (Zhong et al., 2017). This dataset consists of a corpus of 80,654 natural statement expres-
779   sions and sql annotations of 24,241 tables. Each query in WikiSQL is limited to the same table and
780   does not contain complex operations such as sorting, grouping. The queries in WikiSQL are limited
781   to the same table and do not include complex operations such as sorting, grouping, subqueries, etc.
782
      CoSQL (Yu et al., 2019). This dataset is a conversational version of the Spider task. CoSQL consists of
783   30,000 rounds and 10,000 annotated SQL queries from Wizard-of-Oz’s collection of 3k conversations
784   querying 200 complex databases across 138 domains. Each conversation simulates a realistic DB
785   query scenario in which a staff member explores the database as a user and a SQL expert uses SQL
786   to retrieve answers, clarify ambiguous questions, or otherwise inform.
787
      Chase (Guo et al., 2021). This data is to date the largest Chinese dataset for the cross-database
788
      context-dependent Text-to-SQL problem. It consists of 5,459 question sequences (17,940 questions)
789   over 280 databases. Each question in Chase has rich semantic annotations, including its SQL query,
790   contextual dependency, and schema linking.

      A.2    M ETRICS D ETAILS
793
794   We clarify the properties of the two metrics in details.
795
796   Exact-set match accuracy (EM). EM treats each clause as a set and compares the prediction for
797
      each clause to its corresponding clause in the reference query. A predicted SQL query is considered
      correct only if all of its components match the ground truth. EM does not take values into account.
798
799   Execution accuracy (EX). EX compares the execution output of the predicted SQL query with that
800   of the ground truth SQL query on some database instances. Execution accuracy provides a more
801   precise estimate of the performance of the method as there may be multiple valid SQL queries for a
802   given question while EM only evaluates the predicted SQL against one of them.
