009                                                     A BSTRACT
010
011               The interaction between the T cell receptor (TCR) and peptide-human leukocyte
012               antigen complex (pHLA) is a fundamental process underlying T cell-mediated
013               immunity. Computational methods have been developed to predict TCR-pHLA
014               binding, but most existing models were trained on relatively small datasets and fo-
015               cused solely on the Complementarity Determining Region 3 (CDR3) of the TCR β
016               chain. A key barrier to developing advanced prediction models is the limited avail-
017               ability of comprehensive data containing understudied prediction components. In
018               this light, we developed the Hi-TPH dataset with more protein sequences and gene
019
                  annotations. The dataset is stratified into five hierarchical subsets at four differ-
                  ent levels, ranging from Hi-TPH level I with only the peptide sequence and TCR
020
                  CDR3 β to Hi-TPH level II, III, and IV that incorporate increasing levels of HLA
021
                  sequences, full TCR α and β chains, and gene annotations. Hi-TPH at any level
022               represents the largest dataset with corresponding prediction components to date,
023               for instance, the Hi-TPH level IV dataset is at least 5.99 times the size of existing
024               ones regarding the number of TCR-pHLA pairs. We further report benchmark
025               results on the Hi-TPH dataset, establishing valuable baselines for the TCR-pHLA
026               binding prediction task. This comprehensive dataset and associated benchmarks
027               provide a valuable resource for developing advanced TCR-pHLA binding pre-
028               diction models and exploring research directions such as understanding the con-
029               tribution of different components and enhancing model generalization to unseen
030
                  peptides, with potential applications in developing targeted therapies, including
                  personalized vaccines and immunotherapies.

      The interaction of T cell receptor, peptide, and human leukocyte antigen is a crucial process under-
055   lying T cell-mediated immunity (Davis & Bjorkman, 1988; Glanville et al., 2017b). This molec-
056   ular recognition process consists of two key steps: 1) the binding of antigenic peptides and HLA
057   molecules, forming pHLA complexes on the surface of antigen-presenting cells, and 2) the bind-
058   ing of the TCR with the pHLA complex. Identifying the specific TCR-peptide-HLA interactions
059   is of particular significance for the development of effective immunotherapies, as it enables the tar-
060   geted activation and expansion of T cells capable of recognizing and eliminating infected or cancer
061   cells (Schumacher & Schreiber, 2015; Purcell et al., 2007; Ott et al., 2017).
062   Computational methods have been developed to predict interactions between TCR, peptide, and
063   HLA molecules (Wang et al., 2023; Hudson et al., 2023; Weber et al., 2024; Meysman et al., 2023).
064   In predicting the binding of peptide and HLA, Mei et al. (Mei et al., 2021) compiled a comprehensive
065   dataset with binding peptide-HLA pairs from multiple sources, which Chu et al. (Chu et al., 2022)
066   then leveraged to develop a transformer-based method that achieved state-of-the-art pHLA binding
067   prediction performance. However, in the task of predicting TCR-pHLA binding, most available
068   computational models are trained on relatively small datasets (Springer et al., 2019; Montemurro
069
      et al., 2021; Springer et al., 2021; Xu et al., 2021; Lu et al., 2021; Gao et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2023;
      Pham et al., 2023). Moreover, the majority of these methods focus solely on the CDR3 domain of
070
      the TCR β chain. This emphasis is rooted in the understanding that the CDR3 β is the most variable
071
      segment of the TCR and directly interfaces with the bound peptide within the pHLA complex (Davis
072   & Bjorkman, 1988; Krogsgaard & Davis, 2005), as shown in Fig. 1(a), leading to the hypothesis that
073   it is the primary driving component of T cell specificity (Glanville et al., 2017a).
074
075
      Emerging evidence from recent studies suggests that other components and regions of TCRs be-
      yond the CDR3 β may also contribute significantly to the formation of the TCR-pHLA interac-
076
      tion (Springer et al., 2021; Carter et al., 2019). Specifically, the importance of the HLA molecule,
077
      the TCR α chain, and other CDR loops within the TCR β chain in mediating immunological recog-
078   nition process is highlighted (Stadinski et al., 2014; Gruta et al., 2018). Neglecting these additional
079   prediction components may limit the ability of existing methods to capture the intricate sequence
080   patterns from the full TCR chains and other relevant features, thereby constraining their prediction
081   performance (Springer et al., 2021; Fischer et al., 2019). The limited availability of comprehensive
082   data containing understudied TCR and pHLA regions is a key barrier to developing TCR-pHLA
083   binding prediction models that consider these components. Therefore, expanding the scope of col-
084   lected TCR and pHLA data will be necessary to enable a more complete understanding of the TCR-
085   pHLA interaction.
086   To address this challenge, we developed the Hi-TPH dataset, which provides data at five hierarchical
087   subsets across four levels. Each higher level contains increasing protein sequences and gene anno-
088   tations of TCR-pHLA binding components, as shown in Fig. 1(b). Notably, the level IV dataset not
089   only accounts for the TCR variable (V), joining (J), and diversity (D, β chain only) gene annota-
090   tions, but also includes the full TCR α and β chain sequences. These full sequence data are derived
091   from complex gene rearrangement and variation processes, providing a rich source of information
092
      of TCRs beyond the CDR3s. At any level, Hi-TPH represents the largest dataset to date with the
      corresponding components for TCR-pHLA binding prediction. For instance, the level IV dataset is
093
      at least 5.99 times the size of existing datasets with peptide and full TCR sequences in terms of the
094
      number of TCR-pHLA pairs.
095
096   To facilitate the development of advanced TCR-pHLA prediction models, we also introduced an
097   on-the-fly mispairing method to generate negative (i.e., non-binding) TCR-pHLA pairs, rather than
098
      using a fixed negative dataset as is typical (Gao et al., 2023; Springer et al., 2021). This on-the-fly
      mispairing approach reduces bias and exposes the model to a wider array of negative examples (Chen
099
      et al., 2020), thereby enhancing its ability to distinguish binding TCR-pHLA pairs. Furthermore, we
100
      constructed benchmark results on the Hi-TPH dataset using seven different models. These bench-
101   mark results provide useful insights in exploring the contribution of additional components and the
102   model generalization to unseen peptides.
103
104
      The main contributions of this paper are summarized as follows.
105
106
107   • We highlighted the importance of additionally considering the HLA molecule and full sequences
        of both the TCR α and β chains in TCR-pHLA binding prediction models.

117   2   R ELATED W ORK
118
119   Peptide-HLA binding Peptide binding to HLA molecules is a critical prerequisite for the forma-
120   tion of TCR-pHLA complex, as it determines the pool of peptides presented to TCRs (Chu et al.,
121   2022; Yewdell & Bennink, 1999). This peptide-HLA binding step involves the anchoring of specific
122   amino acid residues within the peptide sequence to complementary binding pockets in the HLA
123   molecule, resulting in conformational changes that facilitate the display of the pHLA complexes on
124   the surface of antigen-presenting cells. To reduce the cost of wet-lab experiments, numerous com-
125
      putational tools have been developed to predict peptide-HLA binding (Reynisson et al., 2020; Mei
      et al., 2021; Chu et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2022; Wang et al., 2023). Notable among these, Mei et
126
      al. (Mei et al., 2021) provided a comprehensive dataset containing peptide binders across different
127
      HLA class I alleles by collecting data from biological databases, prediction tools, and published
128   studies. Building upon this, Chu et al. (Chu et al., 2022) achieved state-of-the-art pHLA bind-
129   ing prediction results using a transformer-based model. In most current approaches for predicting
130   peptide-HLA binding, the primary inputs used are typically the amino acid sequences of the peptide
131   and the HLA molecule. For basic methods that only consider the protein sequence, they typically
132   only need to take these two elements into account.
133
134
      Multiple components for predicting TCR-pHLA binding Compared to the relatively straight-
135
      forward peptide-HLA binding, the binding of TCR-pHLA is a more complex process involving the
136   engagement of multiple molecules and TCR regions. Within TCR variable regions, the CDR1 and
137   CDR2 loops primarily interact with the HLA α-helices, while the hypervariable CDR3s predom-
138   inantly engage with antigenic peptides (Fig. 1(a)) (Davis & Bjorkman, 1988). The CDR3 loops
139   exhibit the highest sequence diversity and serve as the principal determinants of receptor binding
140   specificity, with CDR3 β suggested as the main driving component of T cell specificities (Glanville
141   et al., 2017b). While the basic TCR-pHLA binding prediction components are the peptide and TCR
142   CDR3 β (Gao et al., 2023; Peng et al., 2023), recent studies have indicated potential contributions
143   from the CDR3 α, HLA molecule and CDR1/2 (Carter et al., 2019; Gruta et al., 2018). Advance-
144
      ments in TCR-pHLA binding prediction models, such as NetTCR2.0 (Montemurro et al., 2021),
      TCRGP (Jokinen et al., 2021), ERGO-II (Springer et al., 2021), and TCRAI (Zhang et al., 2021a),
145
      have incorporated the CDR3 α as an additional component. ERGO-II and TCRAI also consider the
146
      V, D, and J gene annotations, as well as the HLA sequences, in their models. Furthermore, STA-
147   PLER (Kwee et al., 2023) reconstructs the full TCR α and TCR β amino acid sequences from the
148   V(D)J annotations and uses the full sequences to train transformer-based prediction models. While
149   these advancements are promising, the relative contributions of different TCR and pHLA compo-
150   nents to the binding process remain incompletely understood, warranting further investigation.

160   describes the process used to generate the Hi-TPH dataset, followed by a detailed characterization
161   of its statistical properties. Additionally, a novel solution utilizing on-the-fly mispairing to generate
      negative samples is introduced. The Hi-TPH dataset is released under a CC BY-NC 4.0 license.

      3.1     DATASET G ENERATION
217
218   3.1.1    P OSITIVE DATA COLLECTION
219
220   Building upon the wealth of publicly available biological databases and published prediction mod-
221   els, we have collected and curated a comprehensive dataset of binding TCR-pHLA pairs, as depicted
222
      in Fig. 2(a). Specifically, we accessed data from Immune Epitope Database (IEDB) (Vita et al.,
      2018), McPAS-TCR (Tickotsky et al., 2017), the VDJdb (Bagaev et al., 2019) and ImmuneCODE-
223
      MIRA (Nolan et al., 2020), all of which serve as authoritative repositories of TCR-pHLA binding
224
      information. The IEDB represents a meticulously curated and regularly updated repository, housing
225   positive TCR-peptide pairs originating from both human and non-human sources. The McPAS-
226   TCR database serves as a comprehensive compendium of disease-associated positive TCR-peptide
227   pairs, drawn from both human and murine sources. VDJdb stands as a manually curated and regu-
228   larly updated database encompassing positive TCR-peptide pairs across mouse and human origins.
229   ImmuneCODE-MIRA maps TCRs binding to SARS-Cov-2 virus epitopes. Furthermore, we in-
230   cluded TCR-pHLA binding pairs reported in published papers (Pham et al., 2023; Kwee et al.,
231   2023; Lu et al., 2021), further enriching our dataset. For each data resource, we extract prediction
232   components including two main parts: 1) protein sequences, including peptide, CDR3 β, CDR3
233
      α, HLA and the full TCR α and TCR β chains; 2) gene annotations, including V/J α genes and
      V/D/J β genes. Conflict removal is performed to ensure data quality. For ImmuneCODE-MIRA,
234
      we filtered out non-unique peptide-TCR samples. By focusing solely on positive, experimentally
235
      validated binding TCR-pHLA pairs, we have assembled a robust dataset that enables the rigorous
236   study of TCR-pHLA binding prediction and its underlying mechanisms.
237
238
      3.1.2    F ILTERING AND STRATIFYING
239
240   Recent studies suggest that, in addition to CDR3 β, other regions of the TCR can also make substan-
241   tial contributions to the formation of the TCR-pHLA complex (Springer et al., 2021; Carter et al.,
242   2019; Stadinski et al., 2014; Gruta et al., 2018). This expanding understanding highlights the need
243   for comprehensive examination of the various components involved in TCR-pHLA binding. There-
244   fore, we stratified the dataset into five subsets at four different levels with increasing information of
245
      prediction components. At the most basic level I, the data includes only the CDR3 β paired with
      the peptide sequence. Level II A incorporates the HLA allele information and the corresponding
246
      protein sequence, while level II B contains the CDR3 sequence of the TCR α chain. Advancing to
247
      level III, the dataset combines CDR3 domains of both TCR α and β chains, along with the peptide
248   and HLA sequences. Reaching the most detailed level of the Hi-TPH dataset, level IV, encompasses
249   the full TCR α and β chains, in addition to the peptide and HLA sequences. In this comprehensive
250   level, only the constant domains of the TCRs are excluded. By organizing the Hi-TPH dataset in
251   this hierarchical manner, we aim to empower researchers to systematically investigate the relative
252   contributions of diverse components in shaping TCR-pHLA binding interactions and, ultimately, T
253   cell activation and antigen recognition.
254
255   3.1.3    G ENE TO SEQUENCE MAPPING OF TCR V/J α AND V/D/J β
256
257
      As prior research has demonstrated, CDR1, CDR2, and other TCR α and TCR β regions can con-
      tribute to TCR-pHLA binding (Stadinski et al., 2014; Harris et al., 2016). While our level IV dataset
258
      includes V/J gene annotations for TCR α and V/D/J gene annotations for TCR β, the full TCR α
259
      and TCR β chain sequences are only partially available. To address this, we performed a gene-
260   to-sequence mapping procedure to generate the full TCR α and TCR β chains, thereby achieving
261   gene-sequence alignment for the Hi-TPH-level IV dataset. Following the method in previous stud-
262   ies (Kwee et al., 2023), we reconstructed the full TCR α and TCR β sequences (Fig. 2(b)). From the
263   V and J gene annotations, we conducted sequence lookup based on the IMGT/Gene-DB (Giudicelli
264   et al., 2005). The sequences were then truncated and combined with the CDR3 α or CDR3 β to
265   form the full TCR α or TCR β sequences, excluding the constant domains.

      3.3   O N - THE -F LY M ISPAIRING FOR N EGATIVE DATA G ENERATION
317
318   The variable region of TCR chains is formed by the assembly of V, D (for β chains), and J gene
319   segments, with additional nucleotide additions and deletions at the junctional regions further ex-
320   panding TCR diversity (Mora & Walczak, 2019). The diversity of pHLA complexes is facilitated
321   by the peptide-binding specificities of HLA molecules. Given the vast TCR and pHLA sequence
322   space, the typical approach to generate negative TCR-pHLA pairs is to randomly pair a TCR from
323   the repertoire with a peptide (Springer et al., 2021; Lu et al., 2021; Kwee et al., 2023; Zhang et al.,
      2021a), resulting in a low probability of generating false negatives.

      However, the use of fixed negative data has notable limitations. The predetermined set of non-
      binding TCR-pHLA pairs does not accurately reflect the true distribution of such complexes, leading
341
      to biases in the training and evaluation processes (Chen et al., 2020; Dens et al., 2023; Moris et al.,
342
      2020). Rather than fixed negatives, we opt for dynamic sampling during training (Yang et al., 2024).
343   For each positive pair, we randomly select a non-binding TCR and pair it with the peptide to gener-
344   ate a negative sample. Crucially, the negative samples differ in each training epoch, leveraging the
345   vast TCR and pHLA repertoires to efficiently generate non-binding pairs. This on-the-fly mispairing
346   approach would reduce bias and exposes the model to a wider array of negative examples, enhanc-
347   ing its ability to distinguish binding TCR-pHLA pairs (Chen et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2024). We
348   experimentally validated the efficacy of the proposed on-the-fly mispairing technique. The details
349   are listed in Appendix A.5.

351   4     B ENCHMARKS
352
353   4.1   E XPERIMENT S ETUP
354
355   To demonstrate the usage of the Hi-TPH dataset and provide comprehensive benchmark results, we
356   followed previous studies on TCR-pHLA binding prediction (Mora & Walczak, 2019; Xu et al.,
357   2021; Gao et al., 2023; Springer et al., 2021; Lu et al., 2021; Kwee et al., 2023; Montemurro et al.,
358
      2021; Zhang et al., 2021a; Meysman et al., 2023; Weber et al., 2024) and adopted a binary classifi-
      cation task to predict whether a given TCR binds to the pHLA complex. The dataset at each level
359
      was randomly split into 8:1:1 training, validation, and test sets. We evaluated different models that
360
      take various types of protein sequence information as inputs, corresponding to the different levels
361   of the Hi-TPH dataset hierarchy: level I (peptide, CDR3 β), level II A (peptide, HLA, CDR3 β),
362   level II B (peptide, CDR3 α, CDR3 β), level III (peptide, HLA, CDR3 α, CDR3 β), and level IV
363   (TCR α, TCR β, peptide, HLA). This approach enabled the evaluation of model performance with
364   increasing information depth from the Hi-TPH dataset.
365
      During training, the on-the-fly mispairing approach was employed to dynamically generate non-
366
      binding TCR-pHLA pairs. The evaluation involved binary classification on the test set, with neg-
367   ative samples randomly generated five times, and the average AUC, accuracy, and F1-score were

396   4.3   R ESULTS
397
398   Main results      The benchmark results are summarized in Table 2. Key observations are as follows:
399
400
      • The PLM-based methods outperform RF, MLP, and LSTM in most cases. This superior perfor-
        mance can be attributed to the PLMs’ pre-training on large-scale protein sequence datasets. By
401
        leveraging universal protein knowledge, the PLMs can capture more generalizable and informative
402
        features compared to models trained solely on task-specific data.
403
404
      • Most models encounter a performance drop when using a higher-level dataset compared to the
        level I dataset, possibly due to the level I dataset having at least 2 times more binding pairs than
405
        the higher-level datasets. The scale of the dataset is critical for predicting TCR-pHLA binding.
406
        For this task, methods are needed that can leverage large-scale datasets containing only basic
407     peptide and CDR3 β sequences, as well as relatively smaller datasets with additional components
408     like HLA and other TCR variable regions.
409
      • For the PLM-based methods, continuously increasing the number of model parameters does not
410
        necessarily lead to improved performance, especially given the limited data availability. For exam-
411     ple, ESM2-35M achieves higher performance than ESM2-150M in most cases. This observation
412     suggests that the larger model, ESM2-150M, may be prone to overfitting on the limited training
413     data, despite its higher parameter count.
414
415   Contribution of additional components We further investigate contributions of additional pre-
416   diction components in each subset of Hi-TPH. We select ESM2-35M and compare its performance
417   when trained solely on peptide and CDR3 β sequences, versus when trained on all available com-
418   ponents. The results are summarized in Fig. 5(a). The contribution of the HLA component appears
419   to be limited, as the model trained using only the peptide and CDR3 β has comparable performance
420   with the model trained using additional HLA sequence. This finding suggests that the peptide and
421
      CDR3 β features may be the primary drivers of TCR-pHLA binding prediction, and the HLA se-
      quence may not provide significant additional predictive capability. In contrast, the importance of
422
      the CDR3 α is particularly evident, leading to more than 15% relative improvement in performance.
423
      This suggests that the CDR3 α plays a crucial role in TCR-pHLA binding prediction, and its in-
424   clusion in the model significantly enhances the predictive capability. Further, incorporating other
425   variable TCR regions, beyond just the CDR3, provides additional gains. The largest relative im-
426   provement is observed on the Hi-TPH-level IV dataset, highlighting the value of considering the full
427   TCR sequences.

      5    D ISCUSSION
482   Protein sequence of HLA molecules The HLA molecules are comprised of α and β chains.
483   Existing works typically select the positions that directly interact with the binding peptides (i.e.,
484   the contact positions) to represent the HLA molecule (Reynisson et al., 2020; Chu et al., 2022).
485   However, in TCR-pHLA binding, the HLA molecules are hypothesized to interact with the CDR1
      and CDR2 loops in the TCR α and β chains (Carter et al., 2019). Recent studies also observe


                                                           9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      the interaction of HLA with the CDR3 (Gruta et al., 2018). Therefore, using solely the contact
487   positions to represent the HLA molecules will likely not capture the full complexity of the TCR-
488   pHLA interactions. To address this issue, we collect the full HLA protein sequence and the α1
489   and α2 domains of HLA molecules (i.e., the clipped sequence) for the further exploration of the
490   contribution of HLA to TCR-pHLA binding.
491
492
      Limitations and future works The primary limitation arises from the binary nature of the binding
      labels, which only denote whether a TCR-pHLA interaction is considered binding or non-binding.
493
      The dataset lacks a continuous confidence score that would differentiate between strongly binding
494
      and weakly binding interactions. The absence of such nuanced binding information may constrain
495   the ability of models trained on Hi-TPH to capture the full range of binding affinities. To expand
496   the utility of the Hi-TPH dataset, we plan to integrate it with additional databases in the future.
497   Furthermore, we will collect and release datasets containing 3D structural information for TCR and
498   pHLA complexes, which could facilitate the development of models capable of predicting TCR-
499   pHLA binding or docking using structural inputs (Bradley, 2022; Yin et al., 2023). Additionally,
500   we aim to complete the V(D)J gene to sequence mapping for rare genes, enhancing the dataset’s
501   representation of the TCR repertoire.
502
503   6   C ONCLUSION
504
505   The TCR-pHLA interaction is a fundamental process underlying T cell-mediated immunity, with
506   critical implications for the development of immunotherapies. While computational methods have
507   emerged to predict TCR-pHLA binding, this task has traditionally been limited by the availability of
508   comprehensive datasets considering multiple prediction components. In this work, we addressed this
509   challenge by introducing the Hi-TPH dataset. By stratifying the data into multiple levels, Hi-TPH
510   provides protein sequences and gene annotations at different levels of granularity for TCR-pHLA
511   prediction, including the HLA molecule and full sequences of both the TCR α and β chains. This
512   hierarchical structure enables more detailed and nuanced modeling of the complex TCR-pHLA in-
513
      teraction. At each level, Hi-TPH represents the largest binding TCR-pHLA dataset to date. To aid
      the development of advanced prediction models, we introduced an on-the-fly mispairing method to
514
      dynamically generate non-binding TCR-pHLA pairs during training. We further reported bench-
515
      mark results with seven models on the Hi-TPH dataset, ranging from traditional machine learning
516   models to PLM-based models. The release of the Hi-TPH dataset, along with the accompanying
517   benchmark results, offers a valuable resource for TCR-pHLA binding prediction research.

840   filters (Epitope: “Linear Sequence”, Assay: “T cell (Positive)” and Host: “Human”) and pressing
841   “Export Results” for “Receptors”. The latest complete database of the other three were downloaded:
842   VDJdb (2023-06-01 version), McPAS-TCR (2022-9-10 version), ImmuneCODE-MIRA (release-
843
      002.1 version). We only retained the HLA class I related records for subsequent data preprocessing.
      In addition, we obtained reference amino acids sequences of HLA alleles5 and Human TCR genes6
844
      in FASTA format from IMGT Lefranc (2011) for sequence lookup.
845
846   When processing ImmuneCODE-MIRA data, we implemented a rigorous filtering process to ex-
847   clude samples where the TCR exhibited binding to a group of peptides rather than to a specific
848   peptide. This step was crucial in mitigating potential data errors and ensuring the integrity of our
849
      dataset, thereby enhancing the reliability of subsequent analyses.
