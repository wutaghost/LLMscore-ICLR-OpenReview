009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Composed Image Retrieval (CIR) requires retrieving a target image based on a
012            composed query consisting of an image and accompanying text that modifies or
013            instructs changes to the visual reference. This task is particularly challenging as
014            it demands the model effectively follow modification instructions for accurate re-
015            trieval. Additionally, the difficulty in data acquisition hinders training models for
016
               specific tasks. To address these challenges, recent approaches explore Zero-Shot
               CIR (ZS-CIR), mainly leveraging CLIP-based models with tailored projections
017
               to compose images and textual modifications. However, these base models are
018
               not trained on instruction-aware data, limiting their ability to effectively combine
019            visual and textual cues. In this paper, we propose a novel embedding method uti-
020            lizing an instruction-tuned Multimodal Large Language Model (MLLM) to gener-
021            ate unified embeddings that seamlessly integrate images and modification instruc-
022            tions. Instruction-tuned MLLMs inherently align vision and text while exhibiting
023            strong instruction-following capabilities, though they are primarily used in text
024            generation. We introduce a two-stage training strategy to efficiently transform
025            the MLLM’s text generation capabilities into embedding extraction, and further
026            refining its ability to follow modification instructions in CIR. Our model demon-
027
               strates significant advancements in ZS-CIR, outperforming state-of-the-art base-
               lines across four public datasets: FashionIQ, CIRR, GeneCIS, and CIRCO. Our
028
               model highlights the potential of instruction-tuned MLLMs in capturing detailed
029
               instruction comprehension and advancing CIR systems.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   Composed Image Retrieval (CIR) is a challenging task within the field of multimodal retrieval,
035   wherein a target image is retrieved based on a composed query consisting of both an image and
036   accompanying text (Saito et al., 2023; Gu et al.; Agnolucci et al., 2024). The textual input typically
037   serves as a modification or instruction applied to the visual reference, guiding the retrieval process.
038   Such tasks are prevalent in practical applications, particularly in e-commerce scenarios (Barbany
039
      et al., 2024; Zhu et al., 2024), where users might wish to find visually similar items with slight
      modifications, such as a change in color or style. However, unlike conventional image-text retrieval
040
      tasks, CIR presents unique challenges in data acquisition, as it necessitates the creation of triplet
041
      data <source image, modifier text, target image>. This requirement significantly increases the
042   complexity and cost of data collection, as human annotators are often needed to generate appropriate
043   textual descriptions that link relevant images. Consequently, existing CIR datasets are limited in size
044   and diversity, hindering the generalization ability of current models (Bai et al., 2023; Baldrati et al.,
045   2022; Delmas et al., 2022; Ma et al., 2021; Vo et al., 2019). To address the limitations in CIR, recent
046   research (Baldrati et al., 2023b; Ventura et al., 2024) has focused on Zero-Shot Composed Image
047   Retrieval (ZS-CIR) as a scalable approach that can generalize to diverse contexts.
048   Most existing ZS-CIR models build on CLIP-based architectures (Radford et al., 2021), leveraging
049   their robust visual-text representation capabilities. For example, Pic2Word (Saito et al., 2023) and
050   SEARLE (Agnolucci et al., 2024) utilize lightweight projection modules to map visual embeddings
051   into the textual space, enhancing the interaction between visual and textual modalities within CLIP’s
052   framework. Similarly, LinCIR (Gu et al.) introduces a language-only training strategy, utilizing key-
053   words in text to represent images and circumvent the need for extensive triplet datasets. While these
      methods are effective, they are fundamentally constrained by the limitations of the CLIP model,

      Recently, the advent of Large Language Models (LLMs) (Zhao et al., 2023) has opened new
      possibilities for improving CIR by incorporating richer instruction understanding. For instance,
077
      CIReVL (Karthik et al., 2023) leverages GPT-3.5 Brown (2020) to combine detailed image captions
078
      and textual instructions, thereby enabling a more flexible retrieval process without the need for direct
079   model training. However, this method encounters high computational costs and rigidity during in-
080   ference. VDG (Jang et al., 2024) proposes generating triplet data using a trained Multimodal LLMs
081   (MLLMs) (Yin et al., 2023), but the MLLM itself remains peripheral to the retrieval process, limit-
082   ing its direct impact on model performance. Approaches such as FROMAGe (Koh et al., 2023) and
083   MCL (Li et al.) employ image captioning and contrastive learning to integrate LLMs with visual
084   encoders, yet they freeze the LLMs to function purely as static encoders. As a result, these models
085   do not fully exploit the adaptability and instruction awareness that LLMs can offer for more detailed
086   query comprehension in ZS-CIR tasks.
087   Inspired by recent advances in using LLMs for text embedding benchmarks (Wang et al., 2023;
088   Muennighoff et al., 2024), we introduce a novel embedding method based on pure instruction-tuned
089   MLLMs for CIR in this paper. MLLMs, such as LLaVA (Liu et al., 2024c), which are trained
090   through visual instruction tuning, offer two key advantages. First, they provide a solid vision-text
091   alignment, crucial for multimodal tasks like CIR. Second, they are designed to follow complex
092   instructions, a capability learned during training. However, despite their potential, MLLMs have
093
      been primarily used for text generation tasks, and their application to CIR has not been thoroughly
      explored. A related concurrent work (Jiang et al., 2024) trains such a model with pure text pair
094
      data. But their training does not introduce instruction awareness and is limited to the text modality,
095
      leading to sub-optimal solutions. To fully exploit the capability of MLLMs in ZS-CIR, we introduce
096   a two-stage training strategy to adapt MLLMs for CIR. In the first stage, we perform contrastive
097   learning (Chen et al., 2020) using pure image-text pairs to shift the MLLM’s function from text
098   generation to representation extraction, enabling it to produce multimodal embeddings suitable for
099   retrieval. In the second stage, we enhance the MLLM’s instruction-awareness by tuning it on a triplet
100   dataset derived from an existing pair-wise dataset using GPT-4o (Achiam et al., 2023). The MLLM
101   is trained to produce unified embeddings that align with the altered caption based on the composition
102   of the image and query. Our approach, called InstructCIR, enhances model performance on ZS-
103   CIR benchmarks, surpassing current state-of-the-art baselines. The differences between InstructCIR
104
      and prior methods are depicted in Figure 1.

106   propose an embedding strategy based on instruction-tuned MLLMs, providing superior instruction-
107   following capabilities over previous approaches. (2) We propose a two-stage training strategy that
      not only transforms an MLLM’s strong text generation capabilities to effective embedding extraction

132   2      M ETHODOLOGY
133
134   In this section, we first outline the preliminaries of CIR and introduce the notations used in this pa-
135   per. We then present InstructCIR, an MLLM-based embedding model capable of processing images,
136   text, or a combination of both to generate a unified embedding. This unified embedding captures the
137   composition of reference images and textual instructions, which is then used to retrieve the target
138   images. To train this model, we propose a two-stage training strategy, as shown in Figure 2. The first
139
      stage focuses on embedding alignment, where we utilize pure image-text pairs to train the MLLM
      as an effective embedding model. This step is crucial for transitioning the MLLM from a text gener-
140
      ation role to that of representation extraction, ensuring that it can generate high-quality embeddings
141
      suitable for retrieval tasks. In the second stage, we train the model to produce instruction-aware
142   embeddings. We construct a triplet dataset <source image, modifier text, target caption> from an
143   existing <image, caption> dataset by prompting GPT-4o to generate altering instructions and cor-
144   responding modified captions. The model is then trained using a contrastive learning approach, aim-
145   ing to align the image-instruction embeddings with the target caption embeddings. This two-stage
146   framework allows the MLLM to learn both modality alignment and instruction-following capabili-
147   ties, which are essential for effective CIR.
148
149   2.1      P RELIMINARY
150
151   Composed Image Retrieval (CIR) involves retrieving target images based on a combination of a
152   reference image and a modifier text which we term instruction or prompt. Formally, given a ref-
153
      erence image i ∈ I and an instruction t ∈ T that describes the desired modification, the com-
      posed query q = (i, t) is used to search for the closest target image ir within an image database
154
      D = {i1 , i2 , · · · , iN }. The primary challenge in CIR lies in generating unified embeddings that
155
      can effectively represent the composition of both visual and textual information. Existing CIR mod-
156   els (Saito et al., 2023; Gu et al.; Agnolucci et al., 2024) often address this challenge by employing
157   projection techniques on top of CLIP, where the visual representation is transformed into text tokens.
158   These tokens, along with the instruction, are encoded into a unified embedding space. However, this
159   approach can be limited by the modality gap that exists between visual and textual latent spaces in
160   the joint embedding. Furthermore, the representation learning capability of these models is funda-
161   mentally constrained by the capacity of the pretrained CLIP model, which can act as a bottleneck in
      capturing the complex semantics required for effective CIR.

      2.2   C ONSTRUCTING I NSTRUCTION -AWARE DATASET
163
164   Our training pipeline involves a two-stage strategy, where one phase is dedicated to generating
165   instruction-aware embeddings using triplet data. In this subsection, we outline the process of con-
166   structing such a dataset. Drawing inspiration from MCL (Li et al.), we induce triplet data from
167   the pair data available in the existing dataset CC3M (Sharma et al., 2018). Specifically, for each
168   image-caption pair (i, c), we utilize the caption c to represent the image i. Unlike MCL, we propose
169   leveraging GPT-4o (Achiam et al., 2023) using the Chain of Thought method (Wei et al., 2022).
170
      This involves providing GPT with the original caption c and two few-shot examples. In each exam-
      ple, GPT brainstorms an instruction by first identifying key concepts in the caption, then deriving
171
      a modified caption based on the instruction. For instance, given the caption “A husky is lying on
172
      the grass,” we identify the object husky, the action lying, and the background grass. By changing
173   the action to running, the modified caption becomes “A husky is running on the grass.” The query
174   caption c is given to GPT followed by examples, resulting in a triplet (i, t, cr ) where t is the brain-
175   stormed instruction and cr is the modified caption. Due to space limitations, figures illustrating the
176   processing pipeline and difference from the MCL data processing are included in Appendix B.
177
      Notably, acquiring the modified image ir is often more complex and costly. Therefore, we use the
178
      constructed triplet (i, t, cr ) directly for model training. Since the ultimate retrieval target in CIR is
179   an image, rather than text, we propose aligning the joint embedding space of images and text in the
180   first stage. This alignment ensures that, when the model is trained to retrieve the modified caption
181   in the second stage, the resulting embeddings are consistent with those of the modified images. This
182   approach facilitates effective training for CIR by aligning textual modifications with visual changes.

      3.2   DATASETS AND BASELINES
340
341   We evaluate our model using four well-established zero-shot CIR benchmarks: FashionIQ (Wu et al.,
342   2021), CIRR (Liu et al., 2021a), CIRCO (Baldrati et al., 2023a), and GeneCIS (Vaze et al., 2023).
343   While FashionIQ is an early benchmark for CIR, its domain is restricted to fashion e-commerce
344   images. In contrast, CIRR and CIRCO focus on more general natural images. CIRR is the first
345   CIR dataset centered on natural images, but it suffers from the limitation of having only one target
346   image per query, leading to potential false negatives. On the other hand, CIRCO improves upon this
347   by providing multiple target images per query, which reduces the likelihood of false negatives and
348   offers a more comprehensive evaluation of retrieval accuracy. GeneCIS is a dataset for conditional
349
      image retrieval. It defines four types of conditions as focusing or changing attributes or objects
      in images. In line with common practice, we report Recall@k (R@k) for FashionIQ, CIRR, and
350
      GeneCIS, with an additional subset metric for CIRR denoted as Rs @k. For CIRCO, where multiple
351
      correct images can correspond to a single query, we use mean Average Precision (mAP @k) to
352   capture both precision and recall across different retrieval positions. Importantly, although some
353   benchmarks, specifically CIRCO and GeneCIS, utilize images from MSCOCO, they are sourced
354   from different versions and dataset splits with FOIL (2017 vs. 2014, and Unlabeled/Validation

      In this section, we analyze sophisticated MLLM mechanisms with a latest MLLM microsoft/Phi-3.5-
504   vision-instruct on our training strategies. The difference between microsoft/Phi-3.5-vision-instruct
505   and xtuner/llava-phi-3-mini-hf are two folds: (1) The former is trained with three stages including
506   the feature alignment, instruction tuning, and preference optimization (Rafailov et al., 2024) while
507   the latter is only trained with the first two stages; (2) Phi-3.5-vision leverages the dynamic high

509   resized but also chunked into several parts. The resized image and image parts will be encoded by
510   the visual encoder and fed to the LLM together. While such an operation is powerful, it also suffers
511   from higher computational cost in both training and inference as more patches are fed to the LLM.

532   4   C ONCLUSION
533
534   In this paper, we present InstructCIR, a ZS-CIR model built on instruction-tuned MLLMs. Our
535   approach highlights the potential of MLLMs in CIR systems, leveraging their robust instruction-
536   following abilities and strong vision-language alignment to address the lack of instruction-awareness
537   in previous methods. The proposed two-stage training strategy effectively refines the MLLM’s text
538   generation capabilities for embedding extraction while enhancing its instruction-following within
539   the CIR context. We believe this work provides valuable insights into model selection and training
      strategies, paving the way for future advancements in ZS-CIR.

      A     R ELATED W ORKS

      A.1    I NSTRUCTION T UNING
759
760   Instruction tuning (Zhang et al., 2023; Ouyang et al., 2022; Chung et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2023)
761   is a strategy commonly adopted in modern LLM training to enhance model generalization by ex-
762   posing models to various prompts. In the realm of multimodal large language models (MLLMs),
763   visual instruction tuning (Liu et al., 2024c) has significantly improved their instruction-following
764   capabilities when processing multimodal data. This process typically involves two stages: the first
765   stage trains an adapter between the visual encoder and the LLM using image captioning data; in
766   the second stage, the LLM and the adapter are jointly trained with instruction-following data that
767   encompasses multiple tasks in a question-answer format. While previous MLLMs have primarily
768
      focused on text generation, recent research is exploring the use of LLMs for representation learning.
      Specifically, E5-Mistral (Wang et al., 2023) leverages LLMs as embedding models by training them
769
      on various retrieval tasks specified by instructions. E5-V (Jiang et al., 2024) extends this approach
770
      to multimodal domains; however, its training remains based on pure text pairs, and the full poten-
771   tial of MLLMs for multimodal embeddings is not fully realized. In this paper, we propose a novel
772   approach to train an instruction-aware model that generates multimodal embeddings through two
773   stages: embedding alignment and instruction contrastive learning.
774
775
      A.2    C OMPOSED I MAGE R ETRIEVAL
776
777   Composed Image Retrieval (CIR) involves finding images related to a source image under a spec-
778   ified condition, typically provided as a modifier text. This task has practical applications in e-
779   commerce, recommendation systems, and more. Due to the difficulty of acquiring specific datasets
780   for various CIR tasks, recent research has focused on Zero-Shot CIR (ZS-CIR). Previous methods
781   primarily represent the reference image as specific tokens and concatenate them with text tokens for
782   retrieval (Saito et al., 2023; Karthik et al., 2023; Tang et al., 2024; Suo et al., 2024; Agnolucci et al.,
783
      2024; Gu et al.). With the advent of Multimodal Large Language Models (MLLMs), researchers
      have begun incorporating LLMs into this domain. For instance, CIReVL (Karthik et al., 2023)
784
      leverages two MLLMs: one for generating image captions and another for combining captions with
785
      modifier texts for retrieval. FROMAGe (Koh et al., 2023) and MCL (Li et al.) explore using LLMs
786   for embeddings, but the LLMs are mainly used as text encoders. Despite the rapid development of
787   MLLMs exhibiting strong generalization, instruction-following, and zero-shot capabilities in multi-
788   modal data, their applications to CIR tasks are rarely explored. In this paper, we leverage MLLMs
789   as embedding models for CIR tasks, enabling direct encoding of images and modifier texts within a
790   single model.
791

      B     T RIPLET DATA G ENERATION
794
795
      B.1   DATA P ROCESSING
796
      We utilize GPT-4o (Achiam et al., 2023) to process and generate triplet data. Given an image and
797
      its caption, we use the caption as a prompt to GPT, which then derives the modifier text and the
798   modified caption. The detailed prompt structure is shown in Figure 6. Specifically, the prompt is
799   divided into three parts: task definition, requirements, and few-shot examples.
800
801
      Our data generation process differs from MCL (Li et al.) in several aspects. First, we leverage
      GPT-4o (Achiam et al., 2023) instead of LLAMA2 (Touvron et al., 2023), allowing for more gener-
802
      alizable and creative content generation. Second, GPT-4o has a larger context window, enabling us
803
      to incorporate more complex techniques within the prompt. Unlike MCL, which directly presents
804   the output modifier text and corresponding caption in few-shot examples, we divide the generation
805   process into several steps using the Chain of Thought method (Wei et al., 2022). We instruct GPT to
806   first identify key points in the example caption, then selectively alter some of them as modifications,
807   and finally derive the modified caption. This step-by-step generation ensures that the generated
808   modifier text and corresponding caption are reasonable and closely related to the original caption.
809   At the time the major work of this paper is finished, the MCL dataset has not been released. We will
      deffer the comparison between two datasets in the future work.

813   and diversity.

               1. Input: I will provide you with a source image caption.

822            This instruction should be a clear, concise description of a plausible change that can be applied
823            to the source image.

825            create a modified caption that describes the target image after the change.
               4. You should output the modification instruction and modified caption only.

829            object’s color, altering the setting, modifying an action).
830            2. The modified caption should reflect only the changes specified in the instruction while
