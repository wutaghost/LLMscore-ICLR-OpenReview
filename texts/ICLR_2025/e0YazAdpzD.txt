009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Compositional generative tasks, despite being important and having potential
012            applications, have not been thoroughly addressed due to the unclear formulation and
013            the challenges associated with selecting composition strategies. In this paper, we
014            propose a probabilistic graphical approach to tackle the problem of compositional
015            generative tasks and alleviate these challenges. Our approach formulates the
016
               problem as a Bayesian inference problem using a representative bipartite Bayesian
               network. In this network, one set of random variables represents the generation
017
               targets, while the other set represents observable variables with explicit or implicit
018
               distribution information. To solve this problem, we employ variational inference on
019            the marginal distribution of observable variables. We approximate this distribution
020            using diffusion models. We view the diffusion models as approximate Markov
021            Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) samplers for the marginals. Based on this perspective,
022            we introduce a novel MCMC-based inference algorithm that incorporates per-
023            step optimization using aggregated objectives from the diffusion models. We
024            demonstrate the generality of our method and conduct experiments to validate its
025            applicability to various compositional generation tasks.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      We understand nature in a highly compositional manner. One good example is our visual memo-
030   rization Luck & Hollingworth (2008); we decompose the visual scene into small components (e.g.,
031   to objects and background) and remember those entities with relation, where we later composite
032   them back when bringing the memory out. The compositional nature of human behavior, while it
033   does affect all aspects of parts of our lives, is especially significant in generative tasks that require
034   creativity. Most of the creative generations involve compositing immanent concepts and ideas.
035
      The need for compositional understanding applies the same to the field of generative AI. Generative
036   AI models have shown remarkable strides in recent advancements for fields requiring creativity,
037   such as text-to-image synthesis Rombach et al. (2022); Kumari et al. (2023); Kawar et al. (2023);
038   Melas-Kyriazi et al. (2023); Nichol et al. (2021), text-to-video synthesis Ho et al. (2022); Blattmann
039   et al. (2023), human motion generation Tevet et al. (2022), and so on. Such advancements are
040   driven by diffusion models: a likelihood-based method that generates plausible samples by iterative
041   denoising any random samples, which have shown astonishing improvements in terms of quality,
042   plausibility, and coherency with given input conditions.
043   However, there has not been adequate discussion under the perspective of compositionality for
044   generative models, and currently, the models cannot be actively applied to compositional generative
045   tasks as they do not guarantee synergy when composited naively. The lack of compositional ability
046   ultimately results in limitations for alternations in generative tasks, including even the slightest
047   differences. This is because when the compositional ability is not provided, generative models
048   (especially diffusion models) need to be trained on an extensive dataset of conditions and sample pairs
049   for every specific generation task. As there exists a large obstacle to training due to the exhaustive
050
      data-collection procedure and costly training procedure, only a few models can be trained under large
      capital for the limited scope of tasks. This limitation poses a significant obstacle and hinders the
051
      exploration of various potential applications.
052
053   There do exist prior works that address the compositional generation problem (Refer to Sec. 2).
      However, these works only suggest a methodology or justification for limited types of data or ways of


                                                         1

054
      composition. This is due to challenges within the problem itself: (1) the formulation for the problem
055   of compositing multiple generative models is ambiguous, unclear, and not mathematically formulated;
056   (2) for selecting optimal composition strategies, what objectives should aim for are unclear; (3) efforts
057   must be made to develop generalized formulations that are applicable to a wide range of tasks. In
058   this paper, we present a probabilistic graphical approach for formulating the problem as a Bayesian
059   inference problem. We model the situation as a bipartite Bayesian network, consisting of “control
060   variables” which we can alter directly but do not have distribution information, and “observable
061   variables” with explicit/implicit distribution information provided but mathematically dependent on
062   control variables. Under this formulation, we aim to sample the mode of the joint probability, which
063
      is an inference problem.
064   Nonetheless, there exist several obstacles when devising a composition strategy, other than the
065   challenges of defining the composition problem itself: (1) the inference problem can be notoriously
066   difficult to solve when the distribution information is not provided for generation targets (i.e., control
067   variables) or is only provided for partial observations of the targets (i.e., observable variables) as there
068   are countless ways of compositing information; (2) it is challenging to use the raw explicit/implicit
069
      distribution information directly. To mitigate these challenges, we present an optimization-based
      sampling method that resembles Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) method under the provided
070
      formulation. We suggest a method to utilize diffusion models as approximate MCMC samplers,
071
      which are used to process the information from the raw distribution of observable variables into
072   feasible score information. We then propose a method to aggregate such information from multiple
073   diffusion models, to set an optimization objective for control variables. We propose that by adopting
074   these methods, we can iteratively sample control variables via per-step optimization. This allows us
075   to sample control variables from the mode effectively.
076
      Our method is general and can be applied to arbitrary compositional generative tasks. We validate its
077
      applicability by experiments and empirically show that our method returns trustworthy generation
078   results.

      In this section, we will provide a general mathematical formulation for the compositional generation
197   problem. Consider the following Bayesian inference problem. Given the bipartite Bayesian graphical
198   model GB (V, E), where vertices can be decomposed into two mutually exclusive sets V = {X, Y }.
199   We denote X = {x1 , x2 , ..., xn } as a set of “control variables” and Y = {y1 , y2 , ..., ym } as a
200   set of “observable variables”. Refer to Fig. 1-(left) for the visualization of the graphical model.
201   Control variables are the target variables we aim to generate or possibly alter to have a higher
202   probability. Observable variables are the variables we can “observe”, which means that we can obtain
203   the distribution information p(Y ):
                                                          m
204                                                       Y
205                                              p(Y ) =     p(yi )                                     (9)
                                                         i=1

207   which suffices for each observable variable, obtaining distribution information p(yi ) either explicitly
208   or implicitly. We may consider variables “containing explicit distribution information” if the exact
209   marginal probability value p(yi ) can be evaluated and variables “containing implicit distribution
210   information” (1) if the information is obtained from the generative models for the marginal distribution
211   p(yi ); (2) if we can sample from the distribution; or so on.
212   This formulation represents the composition problem well, as we need to composite the information
213   from observable variables in order to achieve meaningful updates for the compositions (which are
214   represented as control variables). Under the intuition that the compositions we try to generate will

244   5.1   OVERVIEW
245
246   We now discuss our method under the provided formulation. Our method mitigates the aforementioned
247
      challenges by introducing various strategies. The method starts by training diffusion models on the
      marginal distribution of observable variables p(yi ) for ∀i = 1, ..., m, where the dataset is sampled
248
      from p(yi )’s. This procedure can be viewed as variational inference, and the trained diffusion model
249
      can be viewed as an approximate MCMC sampler, in which the resulting distribution information
250   (for our method, score function value) is easier to use than just direct sampling. We then aggregate
251   the score function values for yi ’s to create an optimization objective with respect to control variables
252   X, which resembles the objectives in Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm Moon (1996). We
253   optimize X according to the objective, which can be viewed as sampling from an adaptive proposal.
254   The method repeats the aforementioned process, finally giving optimal X.

506   Our method can be applied to generate a full body with detailed local parts by compositing multiple

      conditioned on body pose. Here we show a few examples of merging the full body and face as local

      which encodes 768 × 768 body image. Then set two observation variables corresponding to full body
511
      y1 identical to x1 and y2 for closed-up face area, which is a crop of x1 with latent size 48 × 48.

513   bounding box on a fixed point where the head locates. To adjust the head positioning of the generated
514   image, we used ControlNet Zhang & Agrawala (2023) to estimate the score for full body y1 with
515   OpenPose Cao et al. (2019) body keypoint conditioning as shown in Fig. 3-(right).

      To check the composition of the head and body is successful, we gave a detailed prompt only on the
517
      diffusion model for face y2 . As shown in Fig. 4-(right), our method succeed in generating images
518   consistent with both of the prompts, even though the composed result is rare in the real world. When
519   comparing the generated results to a control group, where the influence of y2 has been eliminated,
520   we can observe that the desired property of the prompt is effectively injected into the outcome in a
521   compositional manner.

524   7     C ONCLUSION
525
526   We have presented a novel approach to address the compositional generation problem by formulating
527   it as a Bayesian inference problem. Our formulation is versatile and applicable to a wide range of
528   tasks in different scenarios. Under the provided formulation, we propose an optimization-based
529   sampling method inspired by Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) techniques. The method leverages
530
      variational inference with diffusion models and aggregates information from these models to devise
      optimization-based adaptive proposals used for iterative sampling. Our method has demonstrated
531
      high performance across various tasks, as evidenced by our qualitative results.
532
533   However, it is important to acknowledge the limitations of our method. Specifically, our approach is
534   currently only justified for scenarios with zero Langevin noise. It would be valuable for future work
535   to explore and address the case with non-zero Langevin noise, as this may potentially enhance the
536
      performance of our method.
537   Furthermore, our provided formulation for the compositional generation problem offers flexibility
538   in terms of the choice of stochastic dependency (i.e., p(Y | X)) and the information types for
539   observables (i.e., p(Y )). This implies that there are numerous design choices available, which we
      leave as avenues for future research.
