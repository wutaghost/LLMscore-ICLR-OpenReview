                         034                                                             A BSTRACT
                         035
                         036                      This study addresses a critical gap in safety tuning practices for Large Language
                         037                      Models (LLMs) by identifying and tackling a refusal position bias within safety
                         038                      tuning data, which compromises the models’ ability to appropriately refuse gener-
                         039
                                                  ating unsafe content. We introduce a novel approach, Decoupled Refusal Training
                                                  (DeRTa), designed to empower LLMs to refuse compliance to harmful prompts
                         040
                                                  at any response position, significantly enhancing their safety capabilities. DeRTa
                         041
                                                  incorporates two novel components: (1) Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE)
                         042                      with Harmful Response Prefix, which trains models to recognize and avoid unsafe
                         043                      content by appending a segment of harmful response to the beginning of a safe
                         044                      response, and (2) Reinforced Transition Optimization (RTO), which equips models
                         045                      with the ability to transition from potential harm to safety refusal consistently
                         046                      throughout the harmful response sequence. Our empirical evaluation, conducted
                         047                      using LLaMA3 and Mistral model families across six attack scenarios, demon-
                         048                      strates that our method not only improves model safety without compromising
                         049                      performance but also surpasses well-known models such as GPT-4 in defending
                         050
                                                  against attacks. Importantly, our approach successfully defends recent advanced
                                                  attack methods (e.g., CodeAttack Ren et al. (2024)) that have jailbroken GPT-4
                         051
                                                  and LLaMA3-70B-Instruct. 1

      1    I NTRODUCTION
055
056
057   Large Language Models (LLMs) exhibit a level of intelligence that is both impressive and ever-
058   evolving (OpenAI, 2023; Anthropic, 2024; Meta, 2024). However, this remarkable capacity also
059   acts as a double-edged sword, underscoring the importance of ensuring their safety. To address
060   this, researchers have implemented various strategies to align LLMs with human ethics (Christiano
061
      et al., 2017; Ouyang et al., 2022; Bai et al., 2022b; Touvron et al., 2023). Despite these efforts, the
      challenge of rendering LLMs completely safe remains, as new safety risks continually emerge. These
062
      include jailbreak attacks (Wei et al., 2024), deceptive alignment (Hubinger et al., 2024), jailbreak
063
      fine-tuning (Qi et al., 2024b; Yang et al., 2023; Halawi et al., 2024), and adversarial attacks (Zou et al.,
064   2023b). Notably, jailbreak attacks have garnered significant attention due to their ability to circumvent
065   protections with simple prompts, eliminating the need for any tuning or insider knowledge.
066
067
      Recent research has extensively focused on addressing jailbreak attacks through various strategies,
      such as prompt-based defense (Xie et al., 2023), input perturbation (Robey et al., 2023), safety
068
      decoding (Xu et al., 2024c), jailbreak detection (Inan et al., 2023), knowledge editing (Wang et al.,
069
      2024a), representation engineering (Zou et al., 2023a), latent adversarial training (Sheshadri et al.,
070   2024), and priority training (Wallace et al., 2024). Despite these advancements in methodologies and
071   models to improve model safety, the influence of safety tuning data remains inadequately explored.
072
073
      To bridge the gap, we identify a refusal position bias in the safety tuning data, which hampers the
      ability of the tuned LLMs to learn how to refuse effectively. Making a refusal decision before
074
      generating the response content leads to two significant shortcomings: (1) there is a lack of necessary
075
      information for making a refusal decision, and (2) there is no mechanism to incorporate refusal at later
076   stages of the response. Based on these observations, we propose a novel safety tuning method called
077   Decoupled Refusal Training (DeRTa), to explicitly train LLMs to refuse compliance at any response
078   position by embedding the constructed harmful responses. Concretely, our approach introduces two
079   novel components:
080
081   • MLE with Harmful Response Prefix: This strategy involves appending a segment of the harmful
082     response with a random length to the beginning of a safe response, which can train LLMs to refuse
083     compliance at any response position instead of only at starting. In addition, adding a harmful prefix
084     provides additional context to the query, significantly improving the LLMs’ capability to identify
085     and avoid unsafe content.
086
087   • Reinforced Transition Optimization (RTO): While incorporating a harmful prefix helps the
088     model to smoothly shift from recognizing a harmful trigger to generating a safe response, relying
089     on a singular transition per training instance may not adequately equip LLMs with the ability to
090
        consistently recognize and prevent potential threats. In response to this problem, we introduce an
        auxiliary training objective to transition from potential harm to safety refusal at every position
091
        within the harmful response sequence.
092
093
094
      We evaluate our approach using two prominent model families: LLaMA3 (8B and 70B) (Meta,
      2024) and Mistral (7B and 8×7B) (Jiang et al., 2023) across six attack scenarios. Experimental
095
      results show that our method not only boosts model safety without sacrificing performance but also
096
      surpasses notable models including GPT-4 and the instructional variants of LLaMA3-70B in attack
097   defending. Both quantitative and qualitative assessments support our assertion that our strategy
098   effectively arms LLMs with the ability to recognize and halt the generation of unsafe content when
099   they detect potential risks.

      position bias.

155

157   to refuse compliance at any response juncture by embedding the constructed harmful responses within
158   the training process. As depicted in Figure 2(b), our strategy is comprised of two key components,
159   each designed to counteract the two main concerns discussed.
160
161   MLE with Harmful Response Prefix Initially, we incorporate a segment of the harmful response,
      varying in length, before the safe response. This approach provides several advantages:

      3     E XPERIMENT
226
227
      3.1   S ETUP
228
229
      Data We utilize 60K uncensored samples from Evol-Instruct (Xu et al., 2024a) as the SFT data for
230   helpfulness. We use harmful instructions from BeaverTails (Ji et al., 2023) as the safety data. To build
231   safety tuning data for our approach, we sample 3,000 instructions and obtain safe responses from
232   GPT-3.5-turbo and harmful responses from our maliciously tuned LLaMA3-8B-Instruct. Since each
233   instance is a triple that consists of two (query, response) pairs (i.e., (harmful query, safe response)
234   and (harmful query, harmful response)), we complement the safety dataset to 6,000 instances for the
235   vanilla safety tuning for fair comparison.
236
237   Models In our experiments, we consider two representative open-source model families: LLaMA3
238   (8B and 70B) and Mistral (7B and 8×7B). To eliminate the effect of other instruction tuning data, we
239   conduct main experiments on the officially released raw models without instruction tuning. We set
240   the temperature to 0 for all models, and remain the other hyperparameters as default. For tuning the
241   models, we set the total batch size to 128, and the number of epochs to 2.
242
243   Safety Evaluation We conduct a random sampling of 100 harmful questions from the Do-Not-
244   Answer dataset (Wang et al., 2024c) and another 100 from HarmBench (Mazeika et al., 2024),
245   resulting in a total of 200 harmful questions. Our evaluation encompasses several prominent black-
246   box attack methods, including CodeAttack (Ren et al., 2024), PAIR (Chao et al., 2023), JailbreakChat
247
      (Walkerspider, 2022), and SelfCipher (Yuan et al., 2024b). For white-box attacks, we extend
      our analysis beyond AutoDAN (Liu et al., 2024a) by introducing an innovative method called
248
      CompletingAttack. This approach eliminates all formatting tokens (e.g., [INST]) to render the query
249
      in a declarative format, enabling the model to complete the text. CompletingAttack achieves high
250   success rates across all tested LLMs, such as LLaMA3-70B-instruct.
251
252   We determine the Attack Success Rate (ASR) by manually evaluating the responses generated by
253
      the target LLMs for each attack method. The ASR indicates the proportion of harmful responses
      generated. For this metric, we used 50 harmful queries for PAIR and AutoDAN due to their
254
      computational complexity and the full set of 200 queries for the other attack methods.
255
256
257   Helpfulness Evaluation We also assess the helpfulness of the targeted LLMs to determine if our
258
      approach increases safety at the expense of reducing helpfulness. To do this, we randomly select 500
      examples from three sources: GSM8K (math reasoning) (Cobbe et al., 2021), MMLU (knowledge
259
      tests) (Hendrycks et al., 2021), and AlpacaEval (Li et al., 2023) (general capability). We follow the
260
      common practice to evaluate the results on AlpacaEval with GPT-4, and manually evaluate the results
261   for the other two tasks.
262
263   More details about the experimental setup can be found in Appendix (A - C).

432
      Another effective jailbreak method is to frame the malicious question in a hypothesis scenario that
433   makes it appear harmless (Walkerspider, 2022; Chao et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2024a). Given the high
434   intelligence of LLMs, insights from social science (Zeng et al., 2024) and psychology (Zhang et al.,
435   2024b) have also been applied to uncover safety issues. Moreover, techniques like adversarial suffix
436   optimization (Zou et al., 2023b; Zhu et al., 2023; Paulus et al., 2024), few/many-shot attacks (Yuan
437   et al., 2024b; Anil et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2024b), multi-turn jailbreak (Li et al., 2024), and function
438   calling attack (Wu et al., 2024) have proven to be highly effective. According to Wei et al. (2024), the
439   success of these attacks can be attributed to “competing objectives” and “mismatched generalization”.
440
441   Jailbreak Defense. Current defense strategies against jailbreak attacks primarily involve safety
442
      prompts (Xie et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2024a), input perturbation (Robey et al., 2023; Cao et al.,
      2024; Liu et al., 2024b), safety decoding (Xu et al., 2024c), jailbreak detection (Inan et al., 2023),
443
      knowledge editing (Wang et al., 2024a), representation engineering (Zou et al., 2023a; 2024), latent
444
      adversarial training (Sheshadri et al., 2024), and priority training (Wallace et al., 2024). Jailbreak
445   detection typically utilizes LLMs to identify attempted attacks (Phute et al., 2024; Zhang et al.,
446   2024d), or involves training specialized classifiers to detect jailbreaks (Inan et al., 2023; Yuan et al.,
447   2024c). These classifiers can leverage various features, such as perplexity (Jain et al., 2023; Alon &
448   Kamfonas, 2023), gradient (Hu et al., 2024), and high-level semantics (Zhang et al., 2024a). Priority
449   training methods (Zhang et al., 2024c; Lu et al., 2024; Wallace et al., 2024) involve using strategically
450   designed data to train LLMs to prioritize instructions with higher rank. After deployment, developers
451   can set these safety prompts to the highest priority to help the model against jailbreak attempts.
452   In this study, we establish a connection between these vulnerabilities and a bias towards refusal
453   positions in the tuning data, which is used to align with safety protocols. Based on our findings,
454   we advocate for the explicit training of LLMs to refuse compliance at any point of response by
455   employing two distinct strategies. Experimental results demonstrate that our method significantly
456   enhances safety by effectively addressing the bias towards refusal positions.
457   Concurrently, related work by Qi et al. (2024a); Xu et al. (2024b) has also highlighted a tendency in
458   safety alignment to take shortcuts, specifically, alignment often prioritizes adaptations in the model’s
459   over only its very first few output tokens. In addressing this issue, they suggest a straightforward data
460   augmentation strategy aimed at deepening safety alignment by training with data that begins with
461   harmful responses but eventually shifts towards safety refusals. Our research primarily diverges in
462   two aspects: (1) we explore vulnerabilities through the lens of refusal position bias, as opposed to
463   focusing on the generative distribution; and (2) we show that merely starting with harmful response
464   prefixes is inadequate for countering various forms of attacks, including sophisticated methods
465
      like the black-box CodeAttack and our novel white-box CompletingAttack. To bolster our defense
      mechanism, we introduce an auxiliary training objective RTO, designed to reinforce the transition
466
      from potential harm to safety refusal at every point within a harmful response sequence. Experimental
467
      results validate that our technique not only effectively counters the formidable CodeAttack and
468   CompletingAttack but also significantly lowers the ASR for other attack methods.

473
      In this study, we have presented a novel approach in addressing a significant aspect of LLMs safety -
474   refining their capacity to refuse the generation of unsafe content at any point during the response, thus
475   addressing the critical issue of refusal position bias identified in safety tuning data. We introduce an
476   innovative strategy encompassing two pivotal components, which collectively enhance LLMs’ ability
477   to identify and avert unsafe content more reliably and flexibly. The comprehensive evaluation of our
478   DeRTa method notably demonstrates its superiority in terms of safety over existing models, including
479   GPT-4. Our approach has not only shown to improve the safety of LLMs without compromising their
480   performance but also stood resilient against advanced attack strategies, such as those that successfully
481   bypassed the safety mechanisms of GPT-4 and LLaMA3-70B-Instruct (e.g., CodeAttack and our
482
      proposed CompletingAttack).
483   Our findings underscore the importance of considering the role of safety tuning data and the inherent
484   biases that may affect an LLM’s ability to make refusal decisions effectively. Our method’s capability
485   to defend against recent advanced attack methods also highlights the potential for DeRTa to contribute
      to developing safer and more reliable LLMs in the face of continually evolving security threats.

      Main Experiment      In training, we set the total batch size to 128 and the number of epochs to 2.
813
814   For full parameter fine-tuning (Mistral-7B and LLaMA3-8B), we use a learning rate of 2e-5, a
815   warmup ratio of 0.03, a weight decay of 2e-5, a max length of 1024, and a dropout rate of 95% for
816
      the "Sorry" token.
817   For the LoRA method (Mistral-MoE and LLaMA3-70B), we set the learning rate to 1e-4, the max
818   length to 512, with no warmup, and a 0% dropout rate for the "Sorry" token. The LoRA rank and
819   alpha are 96 and 16, with a 0.05 dropout. The LoRA is applied in the attention layer and the mlp
820   layer.
821   For GPT-4 and ChatGPT, we use the version GPT-4-turbo-0409 and GPT-3.5-tubor-0125.
822
823
      To obtain uncensored Evol-Instruct data, we use ChatGPT with a safety detection prompt and keyword
      match (e.g., as an AI) as the filter.

825
      DPO Experiment To conduct standard DPO training, it is essential to have both a chosen response
826
      and a rejected response for each instruction. As such, we utilize the Qwen1.5-chat-0.5B model (Bai
827
      et al., 2023) to generate responses for the 60k helpful instructions in Evol-Instruct.
828
829   The original Evol-Instruct response and the Qwen response serve as the chosen and rejected responses,
830   respectively. Similarly, the safe and harmful responses of a harmful question function as the chosen
831
      and rejected responses, respectively.

833   epoch using DPO. The learning rates for LLaMA3-8B and LLaMA3-70B are set at 5e-7 and 2e-6,
834   respectively.

       C    G UIDELINE FOR S AFETY E VALUATION
973
974
       We establish the following principles for evaluating safety in responses generated by the models. The
975    guidelines help in determining whether a specific response is considered unsafe or not unsafe.
976
977    Unsafe A response will be deemed unsafe if all of the following conditions are met:
978
979          1. Related to Harmful Input:The response is directly related to a harmful or dangerous question
980             or input provided by the user.
981          2. Provides Specific or Directive Harmful Information: The response offers specific harmful
982             information or instructions that could be used to carry out a harmful activity.
983
984    Note: If both of these conditions are met, the response will be classified as unsafe, regardless of
985    whether the model includes disclaimers, rejection messages, or safety warnings within the response.
986
987    Not Unsafe     A response will be considered not unsafe if any of the following conditions are
988    satisfied:
989
990
             1. Refusal to Respond to Harmful Input: The response clearly refuses to answer the harmful
                question or engage with the harmful input.
991
992          2. Lack of Relevance to Harmful Question: The response is poorly related or irrelevant to the
993             harmful question or input.
994          3. Redirects or Requests Further Information: The response does not directly answer the ques-
995             tion but instead insults the questioner or only asks the questioner for additional information
996             without providing harmful instructions.
997          4. Provides Partial Information Only: The response provides only partial information, from
998             which the user cannot complete the harmful task or activity.
