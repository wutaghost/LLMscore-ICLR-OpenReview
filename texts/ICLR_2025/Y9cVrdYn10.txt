009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011           Although deep reinforcement learning (DRL) has achieved great success across
012           various domains, the presence of random delays in real-world scenarios (e.g., re-
013           mote control) poses a significant challenge to its practicality. Existing delay-aware
014           DRLs mainly focus on state augmentation with historical memory, ensuring that
015           the actions taken are aligned with the true state. However, these approaches still
016           rely on the conventional expected Q value. In contrast, to model delay uncertainty,
017           we aim to go beyond the expected value and propose a distributional DRL to rep-
018
              resent the distribution of this Q value. Based on the delay distribution, we fur-
              ther propose a correction mechanism for the distributional Q value, enabling the
019
              agent to learn accurate returns in delayed environments. Finally, we apply these
020
              techniques to design the delay-aware distributional actor-critic (DADAC) DRL
021           framework, in which the critic is the corrected distributional value function. Ex-
022           perimental results demonstrate that compared to the state-of-the-art delay-aware
023           DRL methods, the proposed DADAC exhibits substantial performance advantages
024           in handling random delays in the MuJoCo continuous control tasks. The corre-
025           sponding source code is available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/DADAC.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) has demonstrated substantial success across diverse domains
031   and has become a prominent focus in AI research. Its capacity to learn optimal policies in dynamic
032   environments has catalyzed breakthroughs in areas such as gaming, robotics, autonomous systems,
033
      and finance. Noteworthy accomplishments include surpassing human expertise in games like Go
      and Atari, enhancing robotic control, and improving decision-making processes across industries.
034
035   However, applying reinforcement learning to real-world scenarios presents numerous challenges,
036   with delay being one of the most significant issues (Dulac-Arnold et al., 2021). Conventional re-
037   inforcement learning follows Markov Decision Process (MDP) and assumes that the interaction
038   between the agent and the environment is instantaneous; that is, the action taken by the agent im-
039
      mediately affects the environment, and the agent receives prompt feedback from the environment
      (e.g., observations, rewards, etc.). In practical systems, however, this ideal is often compromised
040
      by real-world delays, due to the limited communication bandwidths or constrained computational
041
      resources. For instance, self-driving cars experience computational and perceptual delays when
042   sensing the environment through various cameras and sensors (Strobel et al., 2020). Teleoperation
043   tasks are affected by network delays, while robot control tasks encounter execution delays in power
044   and mechanical systems (Kebria et al., 2019). In real-world scenarios, delay always exists and thus
045   the assumption of immediate interaction between the agent and environment does not hold, as the
046   feedback that the agent receives at any given time may correspond to a prior timestep due to de-
047   lays. This discrepancy renders conventional DRL less efficacious in environments characterized by
048   delays. The challenge is even more severe when these delays are random and unpredictable.
049   To address this issue, the most common approach is state augmentation, which incorporates histori-
050   cal action information into the original state to ensure that the MDP framework remains applicable
051   in delayed environments (Chen et al., 2021; Nath et al., 2021). However, the augmented state space
052   grows exponentially with the number of delayed timesteps, making this approach unsuitable for
053   applications involving large delays. To alleviate the curse of dimensionality associated with state
      augmentation methods, state prediction methods have been proposed. These methods typically take


                                                       1

054
      historical observations and action information as inputs, and then output predicted observations of
055   the real environment or representations of observations in the form of embeddings (Yu et al., 2023;
056   Liotet et al., 2021). However, in complex dynamic environments, the prediction error of these meth-
057   ods can increase significantly, further complicating the training process. More importantly, most
058   of the aforementioned existing studies (Kim et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2021) mainly
059   focused on environments with fixed delays, which are known in advance, but overlooked the fact
060   that, in real-world applications, delays are often random and unpredictable.
061   More recently, a few approaches were proposed to handle the random delays. Bouteiller et al. (2021)
062   proposed the Delay-Correcting Actor-Critic algorithm by designing the partial trajectory resampling
063   method, which converts off-policy sampled trajectories into on-policy ones in random delay envi-
064   ronments. However, this method relies on the observation and action delay values at each timestep
065   and suffers from training inefficiency due to the recursive nature of the partial trajectory resampling
066   process. Wang et al. (2024) recovered delay-free trajectories by time-calibrating historical data and
067   designed a series of state augmentation and prediction-based methods to address the signal delay
068   problem. However, these methods suffer from significant performance degradation in non-fixed de-
069
      lay environments, despite knowing the maximum of delay, due to limitations in the accuracy of the
      oracle state representation.
070
071   In this paper, we aim to address the challenges of deep reinforcement learning applied to the envi-
072   ronment with random delays. First, we propose a new value function correction process tailored for
073   random delay environments from the perspective of value correction. This method determines the
074   influence range and probability of an observation or action through delay distribution, thereby accu-
075
      rately reconstructing the value representation of a state-action pair in random delay environments,
      improving both performance and adaptability of reinforcement learning with random delays. Next,
076
      we use distributions rather than expectations to represent value function, allowing for a more precise
077
      expression of the value function and a more stable training process in complex, dynamical environ-
078   ments with random delays. Finally, we demonstrate that the proposed method can simultaneously
079   handle both observation delays and action delays.
080
081
      It is worth noting that our method does not modify the state space, and the agent only receives
      original observations from the delayed environment. This not only enhances the method’s adapt-
082
      ability across different scenarios but also minimizes the introduction of inaccurate information and
083
      reduces the extra computational overhead associated with state space modifications. To the best of
084   our knowledge, our proposed method is the first to use delayed original observations as state in-
085   puts without modification and to introduce the concept of distributional reinforcement learning in
086   research on addressing reinforcement learning with random delays. This combination allows for
087   more accurate modeling of uncertainty in delayed environments, offering a novel perspective on
088   effectively addressing random delays.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
092
093
      Delay-aware Reinforcement Learning. Due to the presence of delays in the environment, the
      Markov decision process (MDP) becomes inapplicable, leading to the development of variants such
094
      as delay-aware MDP and constant delayed MDP for delayed environments. To treat these variants as
095
      standard MDP, the state augmentation method is proposed and widely used for fixed delay environ-
096   ments, constructed by concatenating the last observed state with the actions taken since the last visit
097   to that observed state (Chen et al., 2021; Derman et al., 2021). While some research has proposed
098   state augmentation-based approaches for use in random-delay environments, this potential repre-
099   sentation of real-time states still faces accuracy limitations (Wang et al., 2024; Nath et al., 2021).
100   Furthermore, the size of the augmented state typically correlates with the delayed steps, causing the
101   size of augmented state space to grow exponentially with the number of delayed steps.
102   To address this issue, several state prediction-based methods have been proposed to predict the true
103   state in the current real-time environment by utilizing historical state and action information (Wang
104   et al., 2024; Yu et al., 2023; Liotet et al., 2021). However, in complex stochastic delay environments,
105   the accuracy and generality of these prediction methods significantly limit their broader application.
106   Additionally, Kim et al. (2023) proposed a novel belief projection method that tackles the state-space
107   explosion problem by projecting the augmented state space into a smaller one. However, this method
      is only applicable to fixed-delay environments, which do not reflect real-world practical features.

108
      Distributional Reinforcement Learning. Conventional reinforcement learning generally optimises
109   the expectation of the return, but the presence of randomness between the agent and the environment
110   results in the return obeying a distribution given a policy π. Building on this insight, Bellemare et al.
111   (2017) first introduced the distributional DQN, which represents a return as a discrete distribution
112   of length 51, known as C51. Subsequently, several approaches have been proposed to describe
113   distributions more accurately, providing a solid theoretical and practical foundation for the field of
114   distributional reinforcement learning (Dabney et al., 2018b;a; Rowland et al., 2019; Zhou et al.,
115   2020). Recently, the distributional perspective has also been applied to the actor-critic framework.
116   Nam et al. (2021) proposed the Gaussian Mixture Actor-Critic (GMAC), which models the return as
117
      a mixture Gaussian distributions. Specific algorithms, such as the Distributed Distributional Deep
      Deterministic Policy Gradient algorithm(D4PG) (Barth-Maron et al., 2018) and Distributed Soft
118
      Actor-Critic (DSAC) (Duan et al., 2021), have been introduced to address value estimation errors,
119
      enhancing the algorithms’ value estimation capabilities in complex scenarios. In this work, we intro-
120   duce the concept of distributional reinforcement learning to enhance the modeling of uncertainty in
121   random delay environments. In contrast to the existing methods, we make further steps in addressing
122   the issue of inaccurate return estimation in environments with random delays.

      In this study, we gain novel insights into the nature of random delays as shown below. By stepping
163   outside the local horizon of policy updates based on sampled sequential trajectories, we observe
164   that, due to random delays, when the environment feeds back an observation, the agent may receive
165   it with a certain probability at various future timesteps. Similarly, when an agent takes an action,
166   it may interact with the environment at various future timesteps with a certain probability. Consid-
167   ering the probabilistic distribution of delays, it becomes evident that both observations and actions
168   have the potential to influence a spectrum of future time steps with varying degrees of probability.
169   Consequently, the accurate computation of returns should encompass all the timesteps that might
170   be impacted by these actions and observations, rather than being confined to the immediate time
171
      step. This broader temporal consideration is crucial for a more precise and robust learning process
      in environments characterized by random delays.
172
173   Based on the above insights, in this paper, we assume we are given the probability distributions of
174   delays1 , and derive the delay-aware distributional actor-critic (DADAC), a Soft Actor-Critic-based
175   algorithm to maintain excellent performance in environments with random delays.

180   In this section, we first introduce the distributional value function that will lead to more stable learn-
181   ing in delayed environments. We then present a value correction mechanism for this distributional
182   value, which can correctly represent the state-action returns in all timesteps that might be impacted
183   due to the random delays.
184
      Distributional Value Function. In contrast to the common approach to RL which models the ex-
185
      pectations of the return, the distributional value function models the distribution of returns (Belle-
186   mare et al., 2017). Distributional perspective preserves multimodality in value distributions, which
187   improves learning stability and thus has been subordinated to specific purposes such as implement-
188   ing risk-aware behaviour Morimura et al. (2010). Being aware of the representation capability of
189   value distributions, this paper is the first to introduce the distributional value function in delayed
190   environments. Formally, in DRL, let Q(s, a) denote the expectation of the state-action value, the
191   distributional value function can be expressed as Z(s, a) whose expectation is the value Q(s, a),
192   i.e. Q (s, a) = E [Z (s, a)]. This distributional value can be also described by a recursive Bellman
193   equation, but in a distributional manner.

      6     E XPERIMENTAL R ESULTS
335
      We conducted experimental evaluations in the MuJoCo environment within Gymnasium and im-
336
      plemented the random delay settings using Wrappers. To facilitate a comprehensive evaluation of
337
      algorithm performance, we designed two random delay distributions: a gamma distribution with
338   mean of 2 and a double Gaussian distribution with mean of 5, the details of which is provided in
339   Appendix. The implementation of the distributional value function in our method draws on the
340   Distributional Soft Actor-Critic, which assumes that the random returns Z(s, a) obey a Gaussian
341   distribution (Duan et al., 2021). Given that most related work primarily considers observation de-
342   lays, the following experimental results are presented in the context of observation delays for the
343   sake of comparison.
344
345   6.1   C OMPARATIVE E VALUATION
346
347
      We compared the performance of the DADAC algorithm with the following two existing delay-aware
      DRL methods:
348
349          • State Augmentation-MLP is the SOTA method in non-fixed delay environments by state
350            augmentation and recovery of delay-free trajectories (Wang et al., 2024). This method has
351            the prior knowledge of the maximum value of the random delays.
352
             • Belief Projection-based Q-learning (BPQL) is proposed to tackle the issue of state-space
353            explosion caused by state augmentation in fixed delay environments through a novel pro-
354            jection method (Kim et al., 2023), which assumes a prior knowledge of the value of fixed
355            delay. In the experiments (i.e. environment with random delays), for fair comparison, we
356            use the expectation of the delay distribution as the fixed delay known to this method.
357
358   For each experiment, we performed eight runs. The results of the experiments in gamma delay distri-
359   bution and double Gaussian delay distribution are shown in Figure 4 and 5, respectively. Compared
360
      to the other two methods, our proposed DADAC demonstrates significant performance advantages
      in the majority of experiments. In particular, the combination of the delay-aware distributional value
361
      function and value correction enables DADAC to achieve not only superior convergence speed but
362
      also reduced performance variance across multiple runs under the same conditions, showcasing
363   strong adaptability and stability in random delay environments. The other two SOTA methods,
364   which are impacted by random delays and face challenges in accurately representing oracle states
365   based on their design, exhibit significant performance gaps compared to DADAC. Notably, BPQL
366   is almost unable to learn effective policies in certain scenarios.

473   7                         C ONCLUSIONS
474
475   In this paper, we have tackled the challenge of random delays in the environment. To effectively
476
      model the uncertainty associated with these delays, we proposed a novel method that represents the
      Q value as a distribution. Building on delay distributions, we developed a value correction method to
477
      accurately recover the true return in random delay environments. By integrating these methods into
478
      the actor-critic framework, we introduced the delay-aware distributional actor-critic (DADAC) DRL
479   method, which provides a new perspective for the field of delay-aware reinforcement learning. Our
480   experimental results demonstrate that DADAC not only significantly outperforms the state-of-the-art
481   delay-aware DRL methods, but also offers a robust solution to enhance performance in environments
482   characterized by random delays.
