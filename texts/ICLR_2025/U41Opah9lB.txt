009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Knowledge distillation (KD) aims to transfer the knowledge of a more capable
012            yet cumbersome teacher model to a lightweight student model. In recent years,
013            relation-based KD methods have fallen behind, as their instance-matching coun-
014            terparts dominate in performance. In this paper, we revive relational KD by iden-
015            tifying and tackling several key issues in relation-based methods, including their
016            susceptibility to overfitting and spurious responses. Specifically, we transfer nov-
017            elly constructed affinity graphs that compactly encapsulate a wealth of beneficial
018
               inter-sample, inter-class, and inter-view correlations by exploiting virtual views
               and relations as a new kind of knowledge. As a result, the student has access to
019
               rich guidance signals and stronger regularisation throughout the distillation pro-
020
               cess. To further mitigate the adverse impact of spurious responses, we prune the
021            affinity graphs by dynamically detaching redundant and unreliable edges. Exten-
022            sive experiments on CIFAR-100, ImageNet, and MS-COCO datasets demonstrate
023            the superior performance of the proposed virtual relation matching (VRM) method
024            over a range of tasks, architectures, and set-ups. For instance, VRM for the first
025            time hits 74.0% accuracy for ResNet50→MobileNetV2 distillation on ImageNet,
026            and improves DeiT-Ti by 14.11% on CIFAR-100 with a ResNet56 teacher. Thor-
027            ough analyses are also conducted to gauge the soundness, properties, and com-
028            plexity of our designs. Code and models will be released.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
032
033
      Deep learning is achieving incredible performance at the cost of increasing model complexity and
      overheads. As a consequence, large and cumbersome neural models struggle to work in resource-
034
      constrained environments. Knowledge distillation (KD) has been proposed by Hinton et al. (2015)
035
      to address this issue by transferring the knowledge of larger and more capable models to smaller
036   and lightweight ones that are resource-friendly. KD work by minimising the distance between com-
037   pact representations of knowledge extracted from the teacher and student models. According to
038   the type of such knowledge representations, KD methods can be broadly categorised into feature-
039   based (Romero et al., 2015), logit-based (Hinton et al., 2015), and relation-based (Park et al., 2019)
040   approaches. The former two directly match the feature maps or logit vectors produced by the teacher
041   and student models for each training sample, which is essentially instance matching (IM). By con-
042   trast, relation matching (RM) methods construct and match structured relations extracted within a
043   batch of model responses. A conceptual illustration is presented in Figs. 1a and 1b.
044   Instance matching has been the prevailing distillation approach in recent years. Popular KD bench-
045   marks see a dominance by IM-based methods such as FCFD (Liu et al., 2023a), NORM (Liu et al.,
046   2023b), and TGeoKD (Hu et al., 2024), with many different downstream tasks successfully tack-
047   led by directly adopting IM-based distillation (Wang et al., 2019; Yang et al., 2023a; Chang et al.,
048   2023; Chen et al., 2023). Yet, recent studies discovered that relational knowledge is more robust to
049   variations in neural architectures, data modalities, and tasks (Park et al., 2019; Tung & Mori, 2019).
050
      Meanwhile, methods transferring relations have also achieved promising performance for a range
      of tasks, including but not limited to segmentation (Yang et al., 2022a) and detection (Chong et al.,
051
      2022; Jang et al., 2024).
052
053   Despite growing interest, relation-based methods still fall significantly short compared to their in-
      stance matching counterparts. Even the strongest RM method has been outperformed easily by

      more challenging tasks such as object detection (Huang et al., 2022). Moreover, previous RM-based

      methods are primarily limited to matching inter-sample (Tung & Mori, 2019; Passalis & Tefas, 2018;

070
071
      This paper fills this gap with a new kind of relations for KD – inter-view relations (Fig. 1c), which
      seamlessly and compactly integrate with previous inter-sample and inter-class relations. Our designs
072
      are motivated by two important observations made about RM methods in a set of pilot experiments:
073
      1) RM methods are more susceptible to overfitting than IM methods; 2) RM methods are subject
074   to an adverse gradient propagation effect. We empirically find that incorporating richer and more
075   diverse relations into the matching objective helps mitigate both issues. To this end, we generate
076   virtual views of samples through simple transformations, followed by constructing virtual affinity
077   graphs and transferring the virtual relations between real and virtual samples along the edges. In
078   lieu of Gram matrices that suffer from significant knowledge loss (Tung & Mori, 2019; Passalis
079   & Tefas, 2018; Huang et al., 2022), we preserve the raw relations along the secondary dimension
080   as auxiliary knowledge which adds to the types and density of relational knowledge transferred.
081   Moreover, we also prune our affinity graphs by striping away both redundant and unreliable edges
082
      to further alleviate the propagating gradients of spurious samples (Fig. 1d).
083   The above insights and remedies altogether lead to a novel Virtual Relation Matching (VRM) frame-
084   work for KD. VRM is conceptually simple, easy to implement, and devoid of complicated training
085   procedures. It is capable of transferring rich, sophisticated knowledge robust to overfitting and spu-
086   rious signals. VRM sets new state-of-the-art performance on CIFAR-100 and ImageNet datasets for
087   different ConvNet and Transformer architectures. On MS-COCO object detection, VRM for the first
088
      time performs competitively to high-performance IM solutions by distilling purely relational knowl-
      edge. Perhaps more significant is that VRM makes relation-based methods regain competitiveness
089
      and back in the lead over instance matching approaches across various tasks and settings. We will
090
      release the code and models to encourage further endeavours in relation-based KD.
091
092   To summarise, the contributions of this work include:
093
              • We make an early effort to present comparative analyses of existing KD methods through
094
                the lens of training dynamics and sample-wise gradients, and identify overfitting and spu-
095
                rious gradient diffusion as two main cruxes in relational KD methods.
096
097           • We distill richer and more diverse relations by generating virtual views, constructing vir-
098
                tual affinity graphs, and matching virtual relations. We also for the first time approach
                relational KD with considerations of spurious samples and gradients by pruning redundant
099
                and unreliable edges and other designs to relax the matching criterion.
100
101           • We present the streamlined VRM framework for knowledge distillation, with extensive
102
                experimental results on a diversity of neural architectures and tasks to highlight its superior
                performance, alongside rigorous analyses on the soundness and efficiency of our designs.

105   2     R ELATED W ORK
106
107   KD via instance-wise transfer. Knowledge distillation (KD) was first proposed in Hinton et al.
      (2015), where the student is trained to match its predictions to those of the teacher for each sam-

108
      ple. Follow-up works have mostly followed such instance matching (IM) paradigm, and can be
109   categoried into logit (or prediction)-based and feature-based methods according to what is matched.
110   Since Hinton et al. (2015), logit-based KD has evolved from using adaptively-softened logits (Li
111   et al., 2023b) or an ensemble of differently-softened (Jin et al., 2023) distributions to decoupling
112   target- and non-target logits (Zhao et al., 2022; Yang et al., 2023b) and applying logit transforma-
113   tion (Sun et al., 2024; Zheng & Yang, 2024). Methods such as TAKD (Mirzadeh et al., 2020) and
114   DGKD (Son et al., 2021) set up auxiliary ad-hoc networks between teacher and student to facilitate
115   logit transfer. Early feature-based methods (Romero et al., 2015; Ahn et al., 2019; Heo et al., 2019a)
116   directly minimise the distance between the feature maps at a specified layer in both the teacher and
117
      student networks. AT (Zagoruyko & Komodakis, 2017) and CAT-KD (Guo et al., 2023) transfer the
      salient regions in features. Sophisticated distillation paths have been designed, such as “many-to-
118
      one” layer-wise matching in SemCKD (Chen et al., 2021a) and “one-to-many” patch matching in
119
      TaT (Lin et al., 2022). All these methods are based on instance-wise transfer of knowledge, either
120   logits or features, and are herein referred to as the “IM” methods, as illustrated by Fig. 1a.
121
122   KD via relation transfer. Several works attempt to transfer instead mutual relations or correlations
123
      mined amongst the network outputs extracted from a batch of training instances. These relation
      matching (RM) methods usually involve constructing network outputs into compact relation rep-
124
      resentations that encodes rich higher-order information, as depicted in Fig. 1b. Different relation
125
      encoding functions may be used, including inter-sample (Park et al., 2019; Passalis & Tefas, 2018;
126   Peng et al., 2019; Tung & Mori, 2019; Huang et al., 2022), inter-class (Huang et al., 2022), inter-
127   channel (Liu et al., 2021a), and inter-layer (Yim et al., 2017), and contrastive (Tian et al., 2020)
128   relations. To date, IM solutions have dominated KD with their superior performance, leading top-
129   performing relation-based methods by a considerable margin. While many new IM methods are
130   found within the last two years, frustratingly few new RM solutions are being proposed. In this
131   work, we strive to close this gap with a new kind of relations for KD — inter-view virtual relations,
132   and revive relation-based KD by making it overtake its IM counterparts.
133   Learning with virtual knowledge. While not a standalone research topic, learning with virtual
134   knowledge finds relevance in a variety of learning-based problems. For instance, a commonly used
135   paradigm in 3D vision tasks is to learn (or construct) from the raw data a virtual view or representa-
136   tion as auxiliary knowledge in solving the main task, which range from object reconstruction (Car-
137   reira et al., 2015) and optical flow (Aleotti et al., 2021) to 3D semantic segmentation (Kundu et al.,
138   2020), monocular 3D object detection (Chen et al., 2022a), and 3D GAN inversion (Xie et al., 2023).
139   More broadly, many data-efficient learning methods also share the spirit of utilising virtual knowl-
140
      edge. For instance, self-supervised learning methods generate virtual views of the unlabelled data
      to enable the learning of pretext tasks (Gidaris et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2020b). Another popular
141
      paradigm is transformation-invariant representation learning (Misra & Maaten, 2020; Sohn et al.,
142
      2020) in semi-supervised learning and domain adaptation. It enforces consistency between repre-
143   sentations learnt for a raw sample and a virtual view of it. The virtual view is often obtained by
144   applying semantic-preserving transformations to the raw sample (Cubuk et al., 2020; 2019). This
145   work is more related to this later paradigm, but involves learning with virtual knowledge in a differ-
146   ent context, via different approaches, and for a different problem.

182   3.2   P ILOT S TUDIES
183
184   Training dynamics of KD methods. We examine the training dynamics of different relation-
185   based methods on CIFAR-100 using ResNet32×4 → ResNet8×4 as the teacher-student pair. In
186   Figs. 2a and 2b, we immediately notice that relational methods achieve significantly higher training
187   accuracy, but they only marginally lead or even fall short in test accuracy compared to IM-based
188   KD (Hinton et al., 2015). We hypothesise that relational methods are more prone to overfitting. This
189
      is expected given that the optimality of IM matching (cond. A) implies the optimality of relation
      matching (cond. B), while the converse does not hold. Concretely, A ⇒ B ∧ ¬B ⇒ ¬A. In
190
      other words, relation matching is a weaker and less constrained objective than IM, which makes the
191
      student more readily fit the teaching signals and not generalise well. Thus, we conclude that C1:
192   relation matching methods are more prone to overfitting.
193
194   Gradient analysis of KD methods. We investigate the gradient patterns within a batch when a
195
      spurious sample produces a major misguiding signal. To this end, we first generate two random
      vectors x, y ∼ N (0, 1) for x, y ∈ RB×D , where B is the batch size and D is the dimension of
196
      per-sample predictions, x is taken as the sample-wise predictions, and y the supervision signals. We
197
      then add a noise vector ϵ = c · z to xt , where z ∼ N (0, 1) and c is a scaling factor. In this case,
198   xt + ϵ becomes a spurious prediction within our batch. We compute the loss from x and y using
199   either IM or RM objectives, and consider the change in sample-wise gradients g within the batch

      3.3   C ONSTRUCTING I NTER -S AMPLE R ELATIONS
217
218   The remaining parts of this section present step-by-step descriptions of individual design choices that
219   in tandem constitute our method. We first construct relation graph G IS that encodes inter-sample
220   affinity within a batch of sample predictions {zi }B i=1 . Different from Park et al. (2019); Tung
221   & Mori (2019), our relations are constructed from predicted logits which embed more compact
222   categorical knowledge. We use the pairwise distance between instance-wise predictions within a
223   batch as our measure of affinity. Existing methods leverage the Gram matrices (Peng et al., 2019;
224
      Passalis & Tefas, 2018; Tung & Mori, 2019; Huang et al., 2022) to encode inter-sample relations, but
      we find that this leads to collapsed inter-class knowledge via the inner product operation. Instead,
225
      our pairwise distance preserves the inter-class knowledge along the secondary dimension (i.e., the
226
      class dimension), which enables such information to be explicitly transferred as auxiliary knowledge
227   along with the matching of inter-sample relations.
228
229   Thus, we have constructed a dense relation graph G IS , which comprises B vertices and B×B edges:
230
      G IS = (V IS , E IS ). Each vertex in G IS represents the prediction vector z ∈ RC for one instance
      within a batch, and is connected to all instances within the batch including itself. The attribute of
231          IS
      edge Ei,j  connecting vertices i and j describes the class-wise relations between the predictions of
232
      instances i and j. In practice, we can organise all edges into matrix E IS ∈ RB×B×C , in which:

      3.6   P RUNING INTO S PARSE G RAPHS
296   Pruning redundant edges. The augmented E IS in Sec. 3.5 contains 2B × 2B edges in dense
297   connections, leading to quadrupled memory and computational overheads. For better efficiency,
298   we propose to prune G IS into sparse graphs. We begin by noticing that E IS is symmetric along
299   its diagonals, and prune its redundant half. This leads to up to 50% reduction in the number of
300   edges. We also remove intra-view edges as we empirically find that they are redundant and hurt
301   knowledge transfer performance. We postulate that this is because virtual views are laden with most
302   of the essential knowledge of the real views they are generated from, which makes the former also
303   redundant. This step leads to a further 2× edge reduction. For G IC , we decompose the augmented
304
      batch of predictions of size 2B into a real-view batch and a virtual-view batch, both of size B, and
      in lieu use the inter-sample batch-wise affinity vectors between them as its vertices. Compared to
305
      their original intra-view formulation of size 2B in Sec. 3.5, this new design encodes purely cross-
306
      view affinity knowledge with halved parameters. Our graphs now become sparse and the remaining
307   edges can again be rearranged into compact matrices: E ISV ∈ RB×B×C and E ICV ∈ RC×C×B ,
308   both encoding purely cross-view virtual relational knowledge. Pruning redundant edges reduces the
309   peak GPU memory usage of our VRM module from 25.1MB to 8.93MB.
310
      Pruning unreliable edges. To mitigate the diffusive effect of spurious predictions discovered in
311
      our pilot studies, we further propose to identify and prune unreliable edges. In previous graph
312
      learning works, the absolute certainty of two vertices are often used to determine the reliability of
313   an edge. For instance, REM (Chen et al., 2020a) computes the reliability of an edge as the mean
314   of the maximum predicted probabilities of two samples (i.e., two vertices). However, we argue that
315   this will cause the learning to be biased towards easy samples. Instead, we measure the discrepancy
316   between two predictions. The larger this discrepancy, the more unreliable the relation constructed
317   from them. Mathematically, the unreliable edge pruning criterion is given by:
318                                        ISV
                                         Ei,j   = ∅ if H(zsi , z˜sj ) > Pn                              (5)
319
320   where H(·) computes the joint entropy (JE) between two predictions and Pn is the n-th percentile
321   within the batch. While other measures of edge uncertainty may be used, JE suits our purpose with
322   several appealing properties: 1) higher discrepancy between two vertices leads to higher JE, which
323   is a relative measure of uncertainty; 2) as two predictions get aligned, JE approaches their individual
      uncertainty, which is an absolute measure of uncertainty. As such, our criterion takes account of both


                                                                            6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      relative and absolute edge uncertainties throughout the learning process. Also, note that the criterion
325   is enforced on student predictions, whcih results in adaptive and dynamic pruning as different edges
326   get pruned in each iteration, which improves learning.

      5    C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this paper, we have presented VRM, a novel knowledge distillation framework that constructs and
      transfers virtual relations. Our designs are motivated by a set of pilot experiments, from which we
533
      identified two main cruxes with existing relation-based KD methods: their tendency to overfit and
534
      susceptibility to adverse gradient propagation. A series of tailored designs are developed and are
535   shown to successfully mitigate these issues. We have conducted extensive experiments on different
536   tasks and multiple datasets and verified VRM’s validity and superiority in diverse settings, whereby
537   VRM consistently demonstrates state-of-the-art performance. We hope that this work could renew
538   the community’s interest in relation-based knowledge distillation, and encourage more systematic
539   reassessment of the design principles of such solutions.

907   A.5    M ORE A BLATION S TUDIES
908
909
      Effect of pruning redundant edges. We conduct ablation experiments to see the effect of pruning
      redundant edges, described in Sec. 3.6. As presented in Tab. 15, matching the raw and bulky inter-
910
      sample affinity graph with redundancy and duplication is not only less efficient but also inferior in
911
      terms of performance. We postulate that this is partially ascribed the fact that each vertex in the
912   raw graph is connected to a larger and more complex set of other vertices that involve both real
913   and virtual vertices. This complicates the learning while making each vertex more vulnerable to an
914   increased likelihood of adverse gradient propagation. Another possible reason is that matching real-
915   virtual cross-view relations are more regularised, as opposed to matching real-real or virtual-virtual

1002   Analysis of logit mean & standard deviation. In Fig. 8, we plot the histogram of the mean and
1003   standard deviation of instance-wise logit predictions given by various method. IM methods are
1004   found to produce logits closer to the teachers’ in terms of both logit mean and standard deviation
1005   distributions. Intriguingly, the proposed VRM, being a purely relation-based method that is free of
1006   any explicit instance-wise logit matching, is on par with IM methods in this regard and markedly
1007   outdoes DKD. This suggests that the proposed cross-view relational matching provides strong regu-
1008   larisation that better enables the student to learn the underlying logit distribution of the teacher. This
1009   is particularly evident given that RKD, a relation-based method which also has an IM objective, is
1010
       way further from teacher’s logit distribution.
1011
       Effect of longer training. The construction and transfer of richer and more diverse relations mean
       that VRM may more benefit from longer training. To demonstrate this, we devise a longer training
1012
       policy (denoted as “LT”) than the standard 240-epoch policy in existing KD methods. For our
1013
       LT policy, the model is trained for 360 epochs and the LR decays by a factor of 10 at the 150th,
1014   180th, 210th, and 270th epochs. All other configurations are kept the same. Tab. 13, VRM indeed
1015   benefits from longer training as a 0.21% Top-1 accuracy gain is obtained with LT. In comparison,
1016   the performance of other methods plateaued with more training epochs, which is due to overfitting
1017   to the training set and a lack of richer guidance signals from the teacher.
1018
       VRM on features. In this work, we have chosen to construct our virtual relation graphs G IS
1019
       and G IC from network prediction logits {zi }B i=1 . In this section, we conduct additional exper-
1020   iments to investigate to what extent VRM can work with features. To this end, we simply re-
1021   construct our graphs from the feature maps {fi }Bi=1 right before the final linear layer (denoted as
1022   “pooled feats” in Tab. 14) and in the mdistiller codebase. Our virtual relation graphs now be-
1023   come G IS ∈ RB×B×D and G IC ∈ RD×D×B where D is the dimension of the feature vector. Note
1024   that since we no longer work with probability distributions, we remove the Softmax operations that
1025   convert predictions to probabilities. Other operations remain unchanged. In Tab. 14, we compare
       the results of VRM trained using graphs constructed from feature maps with existing methods that

1026
       also build relations from the same features (i.e., “pooled feats”), namely RKD (Park et al., 2019),
1027   PKT (Passalis & Tefas, 2018), CRD (Tian et al., 2020), and ReviewKD (Chen et al., 2021b). It can
1028   be observed that the performance of VRM deteriorates when applied to features. The reason may be
1029   that predicted logits are more compact condensation of categorical knowledge, which is therefore
1030   more beneficial for our downstream task. This is particularly so given that VRM does not contain
1031   an IM objective that directly matches the logits. As such, VRM works best when applied to logits.
1032   Nonetheless, when applied to features, VRM still substantially outperforms all other methods that
1033   also work on the very same feature maps. This shows that VRM still encodes better and richer
1034   knowledge for distillation compared to the weaker relations transferred by RKD and PKT.
1035   Comparison to other methods. We highlight the difference between our method and two KD
1036   methods that utilise self-supervised learning, namely SSKD (Xu et al., 2020) and HSAKD (Yang
1037   et al., 2021a).
1038
       SSKD utilises self-supervision signals via image transformations and pretext tasks for KD. The
1039   proposed VRM fundamentally differs from SSKD in at least the following aspects:
1040
1041   ① Motivation: While both methods involve the utilisation of transformations to produce augmented
1042
       views of input images, SSKD is directly inspired by and leverage the pretext task in self-supervised
       learning (Chen et al., 2020b). In contrast, our method is pretext-task-free. Instead, VRM is
1043
       motivated by transformation invariance regularisation which was originally popularised in semi-
1044
       supervised learning (Bachman et al., 2014; Laine & Aila, 2017) and domain adaptation (Berthelot
1045   et al., 2021).
1046
1047   ② Training formulation: A direct consequence of ① is that SSKD’s teacher first needs to be re-
1048
       trained with additional augmentations (which also causes SSKD to use teachers of higher accuracy
       than ours), followed by a separate fine-tuning stage for the pretext task. These lead to significantly
1049
       more procedures and computations, whereas VRM is entirely free of such palaver.
1050
1051   ③ Nature of matching objectives: SSKD is essentially a hybrid method that employs both rela-
1052   tion matching and instance-to-instance matching objectives, whereas VRM is purely relation-based
1053   method. In other words, SSKD relies on IM to achieve competitive performance, while VRM in-
1054
       volves purely relation-based objectives.
1055   ④ Design choices: The designs of both methods are vastly different, including but not limited to
1056   the formulation of relations and the choices of augmentation policies, relation distance metrics, and
1057   model outputs used for computing relations.
1058   HSAKD is another method that makes use of self-supervised learning and transformed views of
1059   input images. This method is also fundamentally different from VRM from the following aspects:
1060
1061
       ① Motivation: Like SSKD, HSAKD is also directly motivated by the use of pretext task in self-
       supervised learning. HSAKD employs rotation prediction as its pretext task. The proposed VRM is
1062
       free of pretext task learning.
1063
1064   ② Training formulation: To enable pretext task learning, HSAKD appends auxiliary classifiers to the
1065   intermediate features at each stage to perform transformation classification. This means that, akin to
1066   SSKD, HSAKD also needs to re-train the teacher model with modified architecture over the pretext
1067
       task. The auxiliary classifiers also introduce extra parameters. In contrast, the proposed VRM does
       not involve these additional procedural, parameter, and computational costs.
1068
1069   ③ Nature of matching objectives: By matching the predictions made by a set of auxiliary classi-
1070   fiers between teacher and student for each sample (as well as matching the final predicted prob-
1071   ability distributions between teacher and student), HSAKD is fundamentally a instance matching
1072   approach, whereas VRM transfers purely relational knowledge. Moreover, HSAKD employs sym-
1073
       metric matching, which means the matching between teacher and student auxiliary predictions are
       for the same view of the input samples. By contrast, VRM exploits the relations across asymmetric
1074
       real and virtual views with different difficulties (i.e., cross-strength matching).
1075
1076   ④ Design choices: HSAKD also differs from the proposed VRM in terms of specific designs made.
1077   For example, HSAKD adopts rotation to construct its pretext task, whereas VRM utilises Ran-
1078   dAugment policies. HSAKD also relies on the use of the instance-wise logit matching loss from
1079
       vanilla KD (Hinton et al., 2015) to reach competitive performance, whereas VRM does not use any
       instance-matching KD objective and still achieves much more superior performance.
