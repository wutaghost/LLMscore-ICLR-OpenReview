009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Advances in Large Language Models (LLMs) have inspired various strategies for
012            integrating video modalities. A key approach is Video-LLMs, which incorporate an
013            optimizable interface linking sophisticated video encoders to LLMs. However, due
014            to computation and data limitations, existing Video-LLMs are typically pre-trained
015            to process only short videos, limiting their broader application for understanding
016            longer video content. Additionally, fine-tuning Video-LLMs to handle longer
017            videos is cost-prohibitive. Consequently, it is essential to explore the interpolation
018
               of Video-LLMs under a completely training-free setting. In this paper, we first
               identify the primary challenges in interpolating Video-LLMs: ❶ the video encoder
019
               and modality alignment projector are fixed, preventing the integration of additional
020
               frames into Video-LLMs, and ❷ the LLM backbone is limited in its content length
021            capabilities, which complicates the processing of an increased number of video
022            tokens. To address these challenges, we propose an INTerPolation method for
023            Video-LLMs (INTP-Video-LLMs). We introduce a video token rearrangement
024            technique that circumvents limitations imposed by the fixed video encoder and
025            alignment projector. Furthermore, we introduce a training-free LLM context win-
026            dow extension method to enable Video-LLMs to understand a correspondingly
027            increased number of visual tokens. We analyze the deployment costs of INTP-
028            Video-LLM, and find its efficiency bottleneck is on its KV cache cost. Accordingly,
029
               we introduce a training-free KV-cache compression mechanism that reduces mem-
               ory overhead during inference. INTP-VideoLLM not only supports the processing
030
               of longer video sequences but also optimizes memory usage during inference—all
031
               achieved without the need for additional training. In practice, whereas pre-trained
032            Video-LLaVA [Lin et al., 2024] models are configured to process just 8 frames,
033            INTP allows these models to comprehend 32 frames.

036   1   I NTRODUCTION
037
038   Large Language Models (LLMs) [OpenAI, 2023, Touvron et al., 2023a;b], have shown tremendous
039
      capabilities in question answering and reasoning. Building on this foundation, Vision LLMs extend
      these abilities to include images, employing a vision encoder and an LLM to generate text responses
040
      given an image and a related question [Liu et al., 2023a, Zhang et al., 2024]. Recent advancements
041
      aim to extend this capability from image understanding to video understanding [Lin et al., 2024,
042   Zhang et al., 2023a, Kim et al., 2024]. Video-LLMs combine video data with language models by
043   integrating learnable interfaces that capture both spatial and temporal information in video. Typically,
044   these interfaces use a projection network [Li et al., 2023b, Maaz et al., 2023, Li et al., 2023a] to
045   transform video content into video tokens that can be interpreted by LLMs, thereby bridging the gap
046   between video information and text processing capabilities of LLMs (see Fig.1).
047
      However, existing Video-LLMs [Li et al., 2023b, Lin et al., 2024, Maaz et al., 2023, Li et al., 2023a,
048   Kim et al., 2024] struggle to process long videos. In particular, Video-LLMs often lack the temporal
049   resolution necessary to precisely model temporal events, limiting its application to long video [Huang
050   et al., 2024]. For instance, Video-LLaMA [Li et al., 2023b] and Video-LLaVA [Lin et al., 2024]
051   only sample 8 frames uniformly across an entire video, which is often too short for detailed temporal
052   analysis [Huang et al., 2024]. Simply feeding more frames into these models does not resolve the
053   issue, as their architectures cannot effectively process a larger number of frames. Consequently, there
      is a pressing need for Video-LLMs that can understand larger numbers of video frames.

074   To reach this goal, one could consider training Video-LLMs from scratch to handle more extensive
075   video frames. Unfortunately, this approach can be impractical due to prohibitive training costs and
076   data accessibility issues. On the one hand, Video-LLMs require increasingly large datasets, making
077   data acquisition more challenging, especially considering copyright restrictions on video-language
078   paired data. In some cases, the training data are not publicly available. Sometimes, only the model
079   weights are released while the data is not open. On the other hand, the computation cost of training a
080   Video-LLM is expensive. For example, even the relatively less costly Video-LLaVA [Lin et al., 2024]
081   requires thousands of hours on Nvidia A100 GPUs. Therefore, retraining a Video-LLM to process
082
      more frames is not feasible in many scenarios.
083   In this paper, we aim to significantly increase the number of frames that existing Video-LLMs can use
084   to understand videos in a training-free manner. We first delineate the primary challenges associated
085   with interpolating Video-LLMs: ❶ the video encoder and modality alignment projector are fixed,
086   which prevents the integration of additional frames, and ❷ the content length capacity of the LLM
087   backbone is limited, hindering the processing of an increased number of visual tokens.
088   To overcome these obstacles, we propose an interpolation method for Video-LLMs, dubbed INTP.
089   For the ❶ challenge, we propose a video token rearrangement technique that bypasses the restrictions
090   imposed by the fixed video encoder and alignment projector. This approach allows utilizing the
091   pre-trained video encoder and alignment projector to generate an increased number of video tokens
092   for LLM reasoning while maintaining the video tokens’ temporal consistency. For the ❷ challenge,
093   derived from the positional embedding mechanism in Video-LLMs (i.e., Rotary Position Embedding,
094
      RoPE [Su et al., 2024]), we develop a training-free Video-LLM context window extension method.
      This design ensures that the interpolated Video-LLM can handle any number of video frames.
095
096   Upon developing the INTP-Video-LLM, we examine its deployment constraints and identify that the
097   handling of an increased number of video tokens incurs additional memory usage. This finding echos
098   the memory bottleneck in long-sequence LLM deployment [Yuan et al., 2023; 2024]. To address
099   this issue and enhance the deployment efficiency of INTP-Video-LLM, we introduce a training-free
100
      KV-cache compression technique that reduces memory overhead during inference. Consequently,
      INTP-Video-LLM not only facilitates the processing of longer video sequences but also optimizes
101
      memory usage during inference, all without necessitating further training.

105   2.1       V IDEO L ANGUAGE M ODELS
106
107   Prior to the emergence of LLMs, Yang et al. [2022] introduced FrozenBiLM, which combined a frozen
      vision encoder with a bidirectional language model for efficient video processing. In the LLM era,

108
      video-language models continue to evolve. For instance, Video-ChatGPT enhances video instruction
109   tuning using high-quality instructional data [Maaz et al., 2023]. Li et al. [2023a] propose VideoChat,
110   which utilizes cross-attention mechanisms to integrate video tokens with user queries and dialogue
111   context. Li et al. [2024] propose a follow-up work, VideoChat2, which advances this integration
112   with a multi-stage bootstrapping method, focusing on modality alignment and further instruction
113   tuning. Video-LLaVA [Lin et al., 2024] introduces a pre-aligned encoder that supports both image
114   and video modalities, enabling shared projections and joint training across tasks. However, handling
115   long videos is very challenging due to the high computational complexity and memory demands of
116   representing extensive video content with video tokens. To address these issues, various advanced
117
      temporal modeling techniques have been deployed. Chat-UniVi [Jin et al., 2023] proposes a unified
      model that dynamically merges spatial and temporal tokens using k-NN techniques to streamline
118
      processing. LLaMA-VID [Li et al., 2023b] uses a dual token system to effectively compress video
119
      tokens. Despite these explorations, all previous methods rely on some form of training. In contrast,
120   our work is the first to explore the extension of the Video-LLM context window in a training-free
121   manner, offering an approach to handling long video sequences without the computational expense
122   and data requirements of traditional training methods.
123
124   2.2   L ONG - CONTEXT LLM S
125
126   LLMs are generally pre-trained with a specified context length, with models like LLaMA and
127   LLaMA2 employing 2k and 4k tokens respectively [Touvron et al., 2023a;b]. Training LLMs from
128   scratch to handle extended context is often prohibitively expensive for many researchers. Thus,
129   recent innovations have focused on extending the context length through fine-tuning methods. Recent
130
      advancements focus on modifying the position embedding (PE), particularly RoPE [Su et al., 2024],
      employed in LLMs such as LLaMA and Mistral [Jiang et al., 2023]. One of the main strategies is
131
      embedding scaling [Chen et al., 2023, Liu et al., 2023b, Peng et al., 2023], which modifies position
132
      embeddings to accommodate longer sequences, ensuring they remain within the pre-training scope
133   and preventing feature extrapolation. For instance, Chen et al. [2023] condense position indices to
134   maintain alignment with the pre-training range, effectively expanding LLaMA’s context to 16,000
135   tokens with minimal fine-tuning, requiring only 1,000 steps. In a different approach, Liu et al.
136   [2023b], Roziere et al. [2023] adjust the rotary base of RoPE, known as “NTK-aware” scaling. To the
137   best of our knowledge, our work is the first exploration to extend the context window of Video-LLMs
138   in a training-free manner.
139
140   2.3   P OST- TRAINING C OMPRESSION FOR KV C ACHE
141
142   Recently, the compression of Key-Value (KV) caches has garnered significant attention due to the pro-
143
      hibitively high memory consumption associated with generating long contextual sequences in LLMs.
      Current methods can be briefly categorized into three types: Quantization-aware, eviction-based, and
144
      attention-based. Quantization-aware compression reduces KV cache size by substituting the original
145
      KV cache with lower-precision approximations. KVQuant [Hooper et al., 2024], KIVI [Liu et al.,
146   2024] and WKVQuant [Yue et al., 2024] are pioneering studies in KV cache quantization, revealing
147   that keys and values should be quantized along different dimensions. Notably, KIVI compresses the
148   KV cache to as few as 2 bits. Yang et al. [2024], Dong et al. [2024] further enhance quantization per-
149   formance by identifying and selectively preserving more significant keys and values. SKVQ [Duanmu
150   et al., 2024] advances upon KIVI using a sliding window technique, wherein quantization parameters
151   are determined on a window-wise basis. ZipCache [He et al., 2024] establishes a robust baseline
152   for KV cache quantization, achieving compression ratios as high as 5x with negligible performance
153   degradation. In this work, we analyze the development bottlenecks of long-context Video-LLMs
154
      and identify that the major constraint lies in the LLM’s KV cache. To address this computational
      overhead, we leverage quantization techniques, effectively optimizing the model’s memory usage and
155
      inference efficiency.

      3.1   P RELIMINARIES : V IDEO L ARGE L ANGUAGE M ODELS (V IDEO -LLM S )
168
169   Video-LLMs are typically composed of three core modules: a video encoder, an alignment projector
170   layer, and a fine-tuned large language model (LLM) backbone. The process begins with a video
171   encoder—often a Vision Transformer (ViT)—which converts video input Xv ∈ RN ×W ×H into a
172   series of visual tokens Zv , in which N is number of frames, W and H is the pixel width and height
173   of each frame. These tokens are processed by the input projector ΘZv →Hv , which maps the encoded
174   visual features to the text feature space H. The aligned features, along with text prompts Hq , are then
175   input into a LLM backbone, and then the language model can generate the response. The architecture
176   of a typical Video-LLMs is illustrated in Fig. 1 (left).
177   Video encoders are crucial for compressing the raw video into compact representations. These
178   encoders are often trained from scratch via multimodal alignment pretraining [Yin et al., 2023]. For
179   instance, LanguageBind [Zhu et al., 2023] employs contrastive learning during the pretraining phase
180   to gradually align video modality with language modality. Given that LLMs inherently process
181   text, bridging the modality gap between natural language and video is essential. However, training
182   a Video-LLM from scratch is prohibitively expensive. A more feasible approach involves using a
183
      learnable connector between the pre-trained visual encoder and the LLM, along with fine-tuning the
      LLM to better interpret visual tokens. Although those components can be trained via instruction
184
      tuning [Gong et al., 2023] or alignment tuning [Sun et al., 2023], their training remains costly. For
185
      example, to train the video encoder, Zhu et al. [2023] collect a large-scale video-and-text dataset,
186   VIDAL-10M. In addition, even without pre-training the video encoder, fine-tuning the Video-LLaVA
187   [Lin et al., 2024] still consumes approximately 200 hours on Nvidia A100 GPUs.
188
189
      In summary, given the significant data and computational expenses associated with training Video-
      LLMs, developing these models from scratch to process long videos is challenging. This underscores
190
      the importance of exploring methods to adapt existing Video-LLMs for extended video processing in
191
      a training-free manner.
192
193   In practice, the challenges in extending the Video-LLMs can be summarized from two key aspects:
194   ❶ the fixed nature of the video encoder and modality alignment projector, and ❷ the limited content
195
      length capacity of the LLM backbone. First, the video encoder and alignment projector are typically
      configured during initial training and remain unchanged thereafter. This prevents the system from
196
      incorporating additional frames beyond the preset limit, which limits the model’s ability to adapt
197
      to videos of different lengths or to capture more granular temporal details within prolonged video
198   sequences. Second, the LLM backbone has a predefined limit on the number of tokens it can handle.
199   This constrains the model’s capacity to interpret a larger number of visual tokens, effectively capping
200   the amount of visual information that can be processed. As the complexity and length of videos
201   increase, these constraints become significant bottlenecks, hindering the model’s ability to fully
202   understand and generate coherent responses based on longer video inputs.
203
204   3.2   A LTERNATIVE V IDEO T OKEN R EARRANGEMENT
205
206   To address challenge ❶, we enable the video encoder and alignment projector to map video inputs
207   with an increased number of frames into the text feature space. As shown in Fig. 2, the pre-trained
208   video encoder is configured to process a fixed-length video sequence, Xv , consisting of N frames.
209   The most straightforward approach is repeatedly using the encoder and projector to process m · N
210   frames by sampling and encoding multiple subsets of frames m times. Once we have collected m
211   groups of video tokens, we can concatenate them together to get the whole long video’s visual tokens.
212   However, since these video encoders and projectors are pre-trained to process only N frames
213   collectively, such repetitive usages lead to distorted temporal representations in the video token
214   sequence. The concatenating video tokens may carry the frame’s relative information—such as the
215   inconsistency between the tokens of the N -th frame and those of the (N + 1)-th frame (i.e., tokens of
      the 1-th frame and those of the (N + 1)-th frame end up sharing more similarity). This discrepancy

259      sequence based on their frame’s absolute location, H′v , as illustrated in Fig. 2.
260

261

338   3.4.1   I NFERENCE C OST A NALYSIS
339
340   To quantify the inference costs, we employ a modified roofline-based LLM-Viewer analysis, initially
341   developed in [Yuan et al., 2024]. Consider a typical scenario in which each frame is represented by
342   256 visual tokens. In standard Video-LLaVA [Lin et al., 2024] configurations, 8 frames represent
343   a video, totaling 2048 visual tokens. With the implementation of INTP, more frames can be pro-
344   cessed, escalating the number of visual tokens significantly. This increase is thoroughly analyzed in
345
      Tab. 1, which details the computational cost implications for the LLM backbone’s decoding process,
      especially focusing on the model latency and memory cost.
346
347   The analysis indicates that the primary additional cost during the inference phase for INTP-Video-
348   LLaVA is due to an increased demand for KV-cache storage, a requirement driven by the larger
349   volume of visual tokens being processed. This finding aligns with studies in long-context LLMs [Fu,
350   2024, Ashkboos et al., 2024], which highlight that the decoding phase of LLMs is predominantly
351
      memory-bounded. This underscores the critical need for effective KV-cache management strategies
      to ensure that the enhanced capabilities of INTP-Video-LLaVA can be deployed efficiently.
352
353   To address this issue, we introduce a KV-cache compression technique aimed at optimizing KV-cache
354   storage efficiency. Post-training quantization (PTQ) is utilized to convert full-precision tensor
355   inputs into quantized tensors, focusing primarily on selecting optimal quantization parameters. The
356   process requires only two parameters: the scaling factor S and the zero point Z, which are crucial for
357
      the quantization process [Shang et al., 2024b, Yuan et al., 2024]. Formally, once appropriate values
      for S and Z are determined, a full-precision key (or value) tensor can be quantized as follows:
358
359
                                                   KF
360                             KQ = S(clamp(⌊        ⌉ − Z, pmin , pmax ) + Z),                            (3.7)
361                                                S
362
      where [pmin , pmax ] is the quantization range determined by bit-width, for 2 bit integer, and the
363
      bins are {−2, −1, 0, 1}. However, direct application of this method to KV quantization is not
364
      straightforward because KV tensors require the input to be accessed. To address this, we employ a
365   calibration dataset (a significantly smaller set of input samples compared to the training dataset) to
366   collect the necessary tensors. Once the full-precision KV tensors at the k-th layer, KkF and VFk , are
367   obtained, they can be quantized using the same method to produce KkQ and VQ     k
                                                                                        based on Eqn. 3.7.
368
369
      By implementing this quantization technique, INTP-Video-LLM can manage the increased volume
      of visual tokens during inference, ensuring that the model’s enhanced capabilities are aligned with
370
      practical deployment constraints.
