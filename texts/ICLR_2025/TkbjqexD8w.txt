011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Automatic seizure type classification from electroencephalogram (EEG) data can
014            help clinicians to better diagnose epilepsy. Although many previous studies have
015            focused on the classification problem of seizure EEG data, most of these methods
016            require that there is no distribution shift between training data and test data, which
017            greatly limits the applicability in real-world scenarios. In this paper, we pro-
018            pose an invariant spatiotemporal representation learning method for cross-patient
019
               seizure classification. Specifically, we first split the spatiotemporal EEG data
               into different environments based on heterogeneous risk minimization to reflect
020
               the spurious correlations. We then learn invariant spatiotemporal representations
021
               and train the seizure classification model based on the learned representations to
022            achieve accurate seizure-type classification across various environments. The ex-
023            periments are conducted on the largest public EEG dataset, the Temple University
024            Hospital Seizure Corpus (TUSZ) dataset, and the experimental results demon-
025            strate the effectiveness of our method.

      1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Epilepsy is a pervasive neurological disease that affects individuals all over the world, with consid-
032   erable cognitive, psychological, and social ramifications (Beghi, 2020). The mainstream approach
033
      to epilepsy diagnosis relies on EEG data to classify seizures (Falco-Walter, 2020; Fisher et al.,
      2017). However, traditional methods based on human labor are not only costly but also susceptible
034
      to human uncertainty, as these methods require clinicians to meticulously review extensive EEG
035
      recordings (Jiang et al., 2017). As a result, using machine learning techniques to automatically
036   classify seizure types attracts increasing attention.
037
038   Current seizure classification scenarios can be divided into two categories, as illustrated in Figure 1.
039
      The first category is patient-specific, with a consistent distribution between the training and test
      sets (Yuan et al., 2023; Rout et al., 2022). However, a recent study (Karimi-Rouzbahani & McGoni-
040
      gal, 2024) highlights the necessity of developing cross-patient classifications due to the significant
041
      variability in EEG patterns across individuals, such as differences in epileptogenic zones and brain
042   structure. Meanwhile, another study (Jirsa et al., 2014) also demonstrates that the electrophysio-
043   logical signatures of seizures can differ significantly across patients due to individual variations in
044   brain connectivity and the mechanisms underlying seizure generation, underscoring the need for
045   developing cross-patient seizure prediction models.
046
      Several approaches have been explored in the field of EEG-based seizure classification, aiming to
047
      improve the accuracy and generalizability of identifying seizure patterns. Early machine learning
048   methods for accurately classifying EEG data included Support Vector Machines (SVM), k-Nearest
049   Neighbors (k-NN), and Bayesian methods (Lazcano-Herrera et al., 2021; Sha’Abani et al., 2020).
050   With the advancement of deep learning, further methods have been explored. Convolutional Neural
051   Networks (CNNs) (Supriya et al., 2021; Craik et al., 2019) apply convolution methods to efficiently
052   learn spatiotemporal feature representations of EEG signals. In parallel, Recurrent Neural Networks
053   (RNNs) (Huang et al., 2019; Shoeibi et al., 2021; Ma et al., 2023), were employed to leverage their
      capacity for capturing temporal dependencies and dynamics.

      a graph representation (Hajisafi et al., 2023; He et al., 2022; Klepl et al., 2024). However, these meth-
074

075
      et al., 2020) for dealing with the cross-patient problem struggle to implement effective adversarial
076   learning when applied to larger and more diverse patient groups. Consequently, these challenges in
077   efficiently addressing the cross-patient problem remain unsolved.
078
079
      In order to address previous deficiencies, we proposed a novel spatiotemporal invariant risk mini-
      mization loss to solve this problem. Specifically, we first use the invariant mask function to sepa-
080
      rate the raw EEG feature into the invariant representation and variant representation and use self-
081
      supervised learning (SSL) to guarantee the preserved invariant information is able to predict the
082   invariant feature at the next timestamp. In addition, we use the label information to generate the
083   supervised signal to ensure the preserved invariant information can predict the seizure type. Finally,
084   we use the variance of the gradient toward the mask function to control the time-varying variation
085   of our methods in different patient groups.
086
      We highlight our contributions as follows:
087
088          • We use the mask function to capture the invariant spatiotemporal information in the raw
089            EEG data and use such information for self-supervised learning.
090
             • To further control the variation of the loss of the classification model, we use the variance
091
               of the gradient as the penalty to achieve invariant learning.
092
093          • The experiments on the largest public dataset verify the effectiveness of our method.

097   2.1   EEG DATA C LASSIFICATION
098
099   Electroencephalography (EEG) data classification involves processing and analyzing EEG signals
100   to identify and distinguish different brain activity patterns or states, thereby enabling the classifica-
101   tion and diagnosis of brain functions and conditions (Rabcan et al., 2020). Early machine learning
102   methods for accurately classifying EEG data included Support Vector Machines (SVM), k-Nearest
103   Neighbors (k-NN), and Bayesian methods (Sha’Abani et al., 2020; Lazcano-Herrera et al., 2021).
104
      With the rapid development of deep learning, recent EEG classification methods can be broadly cat-
      egorized into those based on Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN), Recurrent Neural Networks
105
      (RNN), and Graph Neural Networks (GNN) (Klepl et al., 2024).
106
107   The core idea of CNN-based methods is to automatically learn spatiotemporal feature representa-
      tions of EEG signals through convolutional operations, effectively identifying and classifying brain


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      signals (Craik et al., 2019). In recent advances, EEG-DBNet decodes the temporal and spectral
109   sequences of EEG signals using two parallel network branches, each containing local and global
110   convolution blocks to extract local and global features (Lou et al., 2024). ACPA-ResNet enhances
111   the model’s ability to identify key features by introducing attention mechanisms and fully pre-
112   activated residual blocks (Yutian et al., 2024). To improve classification efficiency, EDPNet employs
113   a lightweight adaptive time-frequency fusion module to integrate time-frequency information from
114   multiple electrodes and uses a parameter-free multi-scale variance pooling module to extract more
115   comprehensive temporal features (Han et al., 2024). While CNNs have proven they outperform in
116   capturing spatiotemporal features, RNNs are also employed for their efficiency in capturing tempo-
117
      ral dependencies and dynamics over time. Ma et al. (2023) proposed a model that combines CNN
      for spatial feature extraction with a Bi-LSTM network to effectively capture the temporal dynamics
118
      of EEG signals.
119
120   The core idea of GNN-based methods is to capture non-Euclidean geometric properties by model-
121   ing the spatial relationships between EEG electrodes (Klepl et al., 2024). REST combines dynamic
122   graph neural networks and recurrent structures, achieving efficient EEG data classification through
123
      a residual state update mechanism (Afzal et al., 2024). NeuroGNN improves classification accu-
      racy by capturing the dynamic interactions between EEG electrode positions and the corresponding
124
      brain regions’ semantics within a dynamic graph neural network framework (Hajisafi et al., 2024).
125
      Tang et al. (2022) proposed a method that combines self-supervised pre-training and GNN, con-
126   structing a graph structure of spatial and dynamic brain connectivities between EEG electrodes and
127   processing spatiotemporal dependencies using a Diffusion Convolutional Recurrent Neural Network
128   (DCRNN) (Tang et al., 2022).

      2.2   E XTRAPOLATION IN M EDICAL DATA
131
132
      Extrapolation in medical data, as well as the cross-patient problem, refers to the divergence between
133
      test and training data distributions, which may be attributed to the spatial-temporal evolution of
134   patient data (Zhang et al., 2024b). This distribution shift could be partly attributed to the spatial-
135   temporal evolution of data (Zhang et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2021b). Previous studies can be broadly
136   categorized into three types to address this problem.
137
      The first category focuses on representation learning, particularly unsupervised methods that aim to
138
      generate domain-agnostic features (Zhang et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2022; 2021). By leveraging ei-
139
      ther domain generalization or expert-guided structuring of features, these approaches aim to enhance
140   capacity of the model to generalize to new distributions by capturing essential patterns in the data.
141   This ensures that the learned representations retain attributes conducive to better performance across
142   unseen domains. The second category revolves around supervised models designed to enhance gen-
143   eralization by employing techniques such as causal learning and invariant risk minimization. These
144   approaches emphasize end-to-end learning strategies, which have been shown to improve robust-
145   ness to distributional shifts (Parulekar et al., 2023; Oberst et al., 2021; Mazaheri et al., 2023). The
146   third category involves optimization-based approaches, including distributionally robust optimiza-
147   tion (DRO), which focuses on minimizing the worst-case performance under potential shifts in the
148
      data (Sagawa et al., 2019; Liu et al., 2021a).

      The primary objective of the seizure classification task is to predict the seizure type from a given
155
      EEG signal clip. These clips were sliced from seizure EEGs using non-overlapping sliding windows
156   with fixed temporal size T . For each sample, we denote X ∈ RT ×N ×M as the EEG clip feature
157   after preprocessing, where T is the temporal length of the EEG clip, N is the number of EEG chan-
158   nels/electrodes, and M is the number of features obtained through Fast Fourier Transform (FFT).
159   Meanwhile, we denote y as the seizure class label. For the independent identical distributed sce-
160   nario, different clips from the same patient may appear in both the training and test sets. However,
161   in real healthcare scenarios, patients in the test sets (a group of new patients) may completely unseen
      in the training set, leading to the cross-patient problem (Zhang et al., 2024a), which can be further

247   4   M ETHODOLOGY
248
249   In a cross-patient scenario, we propose the spatiotemporal invariant risk minimization (ST-IRM)
250   loss, making the prediction model achieves both (a) accurately predicting patient’s seizure type in
251   each patient group; (b) The variation of prediction between the different groups is small. Specifi-
252   cally, for a timestamp t, we derive an invariant mask function m(·) to separate the representations
253   of the raw EEG feature into two orthogonal components. We denote the representation of the raw
254   EEG feature as ϕ(X:,:,t ). For simplification of notations, we use Xt instead of X:,:,t . The repre-
255
      sentation in the present paper is obtained by DRCNN. Through the invariant mask function m(·),
      ϕ(Xt ) is decomposed into an invariant representation κ(Xt ) = m(ϕ(Xt )), and the variant represen-
256
      tation ψ(Xt ) = (1 − m(ϕ(Xt ))) ⊙ ϕ(Xt ), where m(Xt ) ∈ [0, 1]N ×M . For example, the invariant
257
      representation for an EEG signal data includes the key signals that determine the seizure type of
258   the patient; while the variant representation records the noise and artifact information such as the
259   blinking, muscle movement of the patient or the measure error of the signal detection machines. The
260   decomposition helps us to recognize the components which play the causal role in discriminating the
261   seizure type, and the non-causal features that would vary across patients. Utilizing the non-causal
262   features will help us get a better classification of known patients in the training set, but the unknown
263   patients in the test set would possess different features. The utilization of these features would dis-
264   turb the classifier and harm the generalization of the seizure classifier to unknown patients. Thus, it
265   is important to conduct the decomposition. Next, we introduce our method in detail step by step.
266   In time-series data, especially in the EEG data, there should be some correlation of the previous
267   representations Xt−1 with the current feature Xt (Tang et al., 2022). Unlike the previous SSL
268   approach that aims to learn a model zt (·) to ensure zt−1 (Xt−1 ) ≈ Xt , we claim that preserve
269   the relation between the variant parts, ψ(Xt−1 ) and ψ(Xt ) may not be helpful due to the spurious
      correlation. We expect only a good prediction performance between the invariant representations.

349   utilized the Temple University Hospital EEG Seizure Corpus (TUSZ) dataset, which is the largest
350   public dataset for our experiments. Specifically, we use the version v1.5.2 of the TUSZ dataset.
351   The TUSZ dataset contains 5,612 EEG signals, and 3,050 annotated seizure events from over 300
352   patients, covering eight seizure types. The EEG signal was recorded using 19 electrodes from the
353
      standard 10-20 system (Homan et al., 1987).
354   Data preprocessing and Experiment Details. Following the preprocessing approach of Tang et al.
355   (2022), we transform the raw EEG signals into the frequency domain, as seizures are associated
356   with brain electrical activity in specific frequency bands (Tzallas et al., 2009). Following prior
357   methodologies (Ahmedt-Aristizabal et al., 2020; Asif et al., 2020), EEG recordings were resampled
358   to 200Hz and segmented into non-overlapped 60-second windows (clips). For seizure classifica-
359
      tion, only clips that contain a single type of seizure are considered. If a seizure event ends and
      another begins within the same clip, it is truncated and zero-padded to preserve a 60-second dura-
360
      tion. Each 60-second clip is then segmented into 1-second intervals. The Fast Fourier Transform
361
      (FFT) algorithm is applied to each segment to obtain the logarithmic amplitudes of non-negative
362   frequency components, as is outlined in Tang et al. (2022). Consequently, each 60-second clip is
363   transformed into a sequence of 60 log-amplitude vectors. In addition, following recent studies on
364   seizure type classification Ahmedt-Aristizabal et al. (2020); Asif et al. (2020); Tang et al. (2022),
365   we use weighted F1-score as the main evaluation metric with precision and recall as well to measure
366   the classification performance. The F1-score is the harmonic mean of precision and recall, pro-
367   viding a balanced measure for evaluating models, particularly when dealing with class imbalances.
368   Precision is defined as the ratio of true positives (TP) to the sum of true positives and false pos-
369   itives (FP), expressed as: P = T PT+F P
                                              P . This reflects the model’s accuracy in predicting positive
370   instances. Recall, on the other hand, is the ratio of true positives to the sum of true positives and
371
      false negatives (FN), calculated as: R = T PT+F P
                                                         N . It indicates the model’s ability to identify all
372
      relevant positive cases. Finally, the F1-score is computed as the harmonic mean of precision and
                           ×R
      recall: F 1 = 2 × PP +R . See Appendix A for more experiment protocols and details.

      Epilepsy remains a significant global health challenge, with traditional EEG-based diagnostic meth-
      ods posing limitations due to their reliance on clinician review. With the recent advancement of deep
478
      learning, techniques such as CNNs, RNNs, and GNNs are proposed to automatically classify the
479
      seizure type. However, existing methods often lack cross-patient robustness and guarantee, which
480   is very common in practice. In addition, most of the methods addressing the cross-patient problem
481   ignore the spatiotemporal information. To bridge this gap, we propose a spatiotemporal invariant
482   risk minimization approach that addresses these challenges by adopting self-supervised learning and
483   capturing time-varying invariant features. Experimental results on the largest public dataset verify
484   the effectiveness of our approach, demonstrating its potential to advance epilepsy diagnosis in the
485   cross-patient scenario. One of the possible limitations is to investigate a more efficient way to learn
      the model parameters and reduce the complexity while maintaining the classification performance.
