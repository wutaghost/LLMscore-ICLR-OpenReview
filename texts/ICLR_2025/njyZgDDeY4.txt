011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Imperfect-information extensive-form games (IIGs) serve as a foundational model
014            for capturing interactions among multiple agents in sequential settings with hidden
015            information. A common objective of IIGs is to calculate a Nash equilibrium (NE).
016
               Counterfactual Regret Minimization (CFR) algorithms have been widely developed
               to learn an NE in two-player zero-sum IIGs. Among CFR algorithms, Predictive
017
               CFR+ (PCFR+ ) is powerful, usually achieving an extremely fast empirical con-
018
               vergence rate. However, PCFR+ suffers from the significant discrepancy between
019            strategies represented by explicit accumulated counterfactual regrets across two
020            consecutive iterations, which decreases the empirical convergence rate of PCFR+
021            in practice. To mitigate this significant discrepancy, we introduce a novel and
022            effective variant of PCFR+ , termed Pessimistic PCFR+ (P2PCFR+ ), minimizing
023            the discrepancy between strategies represented by implicit and explicit accumu-
024            lated regrets within the same iteration. We provide theoretical proof to show that
025            P2PCFR+ exhibits a faster theoretical convergence rate than PCFR+ . Experimental
026            results demonstrate that P2PCFR+ outperforms other tested CFR variants.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Imperfect-information extensive-form games (IIGs) are foundational models to capture interactions
032   among multiple agents in sequential settings with hidden information. IIGs are widely used to
033
      simulate real-world scenarios such as medical treatment (Sandholm, 2015), security games (Lisỳ
      et al., 2016), cybersecurity (Chen et al., 2017), and recreational games (Brown & Sandholm, 2018;
034
      2019b). A primary goal in addressing IIGs is to compute a Nash equilibrium (NE), which represents
035
      a rational state where no player can unilaterally improve its payoff by deviating from the equilibrium.
036
037   As with much of the literature on solving IIGs, we focus on learning an NE in two-player zero-sum
038   IIGs. The most widely used method for learning an NE in two-player zero-sum IIGs is Counterfactual
039
      Regret Minimization (CFR) (Shalev-Shwartz & Singer, 2007; Lanctot et al., 2009; Tammelin, 2014;
      Brown & Sandholm, 2019a; Farina et al., 2021; 2019; Liu et al., 2021; Pérolat et al., 2021; Liu
040
      et al., 2023; Meng et al., 2023; Farina et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2022; 2024b;a; Zhang et al., 2024), as
041
      evidenced by their success in superhuman game AIs (Bowling et al., 2015; Moravčík et al., 2017;
042   Brown & Sandholm, 2018; 2019b; Pérolat et al., 2022). CFR algorithms decompose the total regret
043   over the game into a sum of counterfactual regrets associated within information sets (infosets) and
044   employ a local regret minimizer to minimize counterfactual regrets within each infoset.
045
      Many technologies have been proposed to improve the empirical convergence rate of CFR algorithms.
046
      For instance, Counterfactual Regret Minimization+ (CFR+ ) (Tammelin, 2014) replaces the local
047
      regret minimizer—Regret Matching (RM) (Hart & Mas-Colell, 2000; Gordon, 2006)—used in
048   vanilla CFR (Zinkevich et al., 2007) with Regret Matching+ (RM+ ). RM+ improves the empirical
049   convergence rate by ensuring that the accumulated counterfactual regrets remain non-negative.
050   Subsequently, Farina et al. (2021) introduce Predictive CFR+ (PCFR+ ), an improved variant of
051   CFR+ , which leverages the key insight of looking one step ahead to improve the convergence rate.
052   Specifically, PCFR+ maintains two types of accumulated counterfactual regrets: the implicit and
053   the explicit. The phrase "looking one step ahead" refers to PCFR+ making a prediction at each
      iteration t and using this prediction to derive new explicit accumulated counterfactual regrets from the

055   by following the strategy defined by the new explicit accumulated counterfactual regret and uses it to
056   update the current implicit counterfactual regret. PCFR+ sets the instantaneous counterfactual regret
057   observed at iteration t − 1 as the prediction at iteration t. If the prediction aligns with the observed
058   instantaneous counterfactual

      Unfortunately, due to the significant discrepancy between strategies represented by explicit accumu-
061
      lated counterfactual regrets across two consecutive iterations, the alignment between the prediction
062
      and with the observed instantaneous counterfactual regret often fails, which reduces empirical con-
063   vergence rate. For instance, as illustrated in Section 4.1, within a given infoset, such strategies may
064   be [1; 0] and [0; 1], respectively. Such a significant discrepancy indicates that the prediction at t, i.e.,
065   instantaneous counterfactual regret observed at iteration t − 1, may be completely different from
066   the instantaneous counterfactual regret observed at iteration t (Farina et al., 2023). It implies that
067   this alignment fails, which undermines the empirical convergence rate of PCFR+ , as evidenced by
068   our experiments utilizing the open-source implementation of CFR algorithms (Liu et al., 2024)1 .
069   Precisely, PCFR+ converges more slowly than other classical CFR algorithms, such as CFR+ , even
070   in standard IIG benchmarks like Leduc Poker, Goofspiel, and Liar’s Dice.
071   To mitigate the discrepancy between strategies represented by explicit accumulated counterfactual
072   regrets across two consecutive iterations, we propose a novel variant of PCFR+ , named Pessimistic
073   PCFR+ (P2PCFR+ ). The key insight of P2PCFR+ is to reduce the discrepancy between strategies
074   represented by implicit and explicit accumulated regrets within the same iteration to diminish the
075   significant discrepancy between strategies represented by explicit accumulated counterfactual regrets
076   across two consecutive iterations. Specifically, from the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality (Steele, 2004),
077
      the discrepancy between strategies represented by explicit accumulated regrets at iteration t and
      t + 1 is bounded by the sum of (i) the discrepancy between strategies represented by the implicit and
078
      explicit accumulated regrets at iteration t, (ii) the discrepancy between strategies represented by the
079
      implicit and explicit accumulated regrets at iteration t + 1, and (iii) the discrepancy between strategies
080   represented by the explicit accumulated regrets at iteration t and t + 1. Obviously, the discrepancy
081   between strategies represented by the explicit accumulated counterfactual regrets at iterations t and
082   t+1 depends largely on the discrepancy between the strategies represented by the implicit and explicit
083   accumulated counterfactual regrets within the same iteration. As the latter discrepancy decreases, the
084   former discrepancy also reduces, which accelerates the empirical convergence rate.
085
      We show that P2PCFR+ enjoys a lower regret bound than PCFR+ . In other words, P2PCFR+ exhibits
086   a faster theoretical convergence rate than PCFR+ . Moreover, the implementation of P2PCFR+ is
087   also notably straightforward, requiring only a single line adjustment compared to the open-source
088   implementation of PCFR+ (Liu et al., 2024).
089
      We conduct extensive experimental evaluations of P2PCFR+ across nine instances from four standard
090
      IIG benchmarks: Kuhn Poker, Leduc Poker, Goofspiel, and Liar’s Dice, compared with previous
091
      CFR algrithms. Among the tested algorithms, P2PCFR+ achieves the fastest empirical convergence
092   rate. Furthermore, our experiments reveal that the improvement of P2PCFR+ over PCFR+ in terms
093   of empirical convergence rate is highly correlated with the reduction of the discrepancy between the
094   strategies represented by implicit and explicit accumulated counterfactual regrets, relative to PCFR+ .
095
      Concretely, we make the following contributions:
096
097            • We introduce a novel and effective variant of PCFR+ , called P2PCFR+ . P2PCFR+ mitigates
098              the discrepancy between strategies represented by explicit accumulated counterfactual regrets
099              across two consecutive iterations by reducing the discrepancy between strategies represented
100              by implicit and explicit accumulated regrets within the same iteration.
101
               • We prove that P2PCFR+ exhibits faster theoretical convergence rate than PCFR+ .
102
103            • We demonstrate that P2PCFR+ is implementable with a single-line modification to the
104              open-source PCFR+ implementation.
105            • Experimental results from four standard IIG benchmarks demonstrate that P2PCFR+ out-
106              performs other tested CFR variants.

110   We focus on CFR algorithms (Shalev-Shwartz & Singer, 2007; Lanctot et al., 2009; Tammelin, 2014;
111   Brown & Sandholm, 2019a; Farina et al., 2021; 2019; Liu et al., 2021; Pérolat et al., 2021; Liu et al.,
112   2023; Meng et al., 2023; Farina et al., 2023; Xu et al., 2022; 2024b;a; Zhang et al., 2024), the most
113   widely used method for learning an NE in two-player zero-sum IIGs, as evidenced by their success
114   in superhuman game AIs (Bowling et al., 2015; Moravčík et al., 2017; Brown & Sandholm, 2018;
115
      2019b; Pérolat et al., 2022).
116   The key insight of CFR algorithms is the decomposition of the regret over the game into the sum
117   of counterfactual regrets associated within infosets. The vanilla CFR algorithm, introduced by
118   Shalev-Shwartz & Singer (2007), employs RM (Hart & Mas-Colell, 2000; Gordon, 2006) as the
119   local regret minimizer. To improve the empirical convergence rate of CFR, it is common to design
120   more effective local regret minimizers, as the selection of the local regret minimizers has a significant
121
      impact on the overall performance of the CFR algorithm. Examples include RM+ (Bowling et al.,
      2015), Discounted RM (DRM) (Brown & Sandholm, 2019a), and PRM+ (Farina et al., 2021), which
122
      correspond to CFR+ (Bowling et al., 2015), DCFR (Brown & Sandholm, 2019a), and PCFR+ (Farina
123
      et al., 2021), respectively. PCFR+ can demonstrate faster empirical convergence than other CFR
124   variants. However, as shown in our experiments, PCFR+ is sometimes outperformed by CFR+ and
125   DCFR, even on standard IIG benchmarks.
126
127
      To accelerate the convergence rate of PCFR+ , several algorithms have been proposed (Farina et al.,
      2023; Xu et al., 2024a;b; Zhang et al., 2024). For instance, to mitigate the slow empirical convergence
128
      rate caused by the discrepancy between strategies represented by explicit accumulated counterfactual
129
      regrets, Farina et al. (2023) ensure that the lower bound of the 1-norm of implicit and explicit
130   accumulated counterfactual regrets exceeds a positive constant. However, their algorithms, Stable
131   PCFR+ and Smooth PCFR+ , forfeit a crucial property of PCFR+ —parameter-free—meaning no
132   parameters need to be tuned (Grand-Clément & Kroer, 2021) to guarantee convergence. P2PCFR+
133   offers a simpler and more effective solution to address this discrepancy while still obtaining the
134   parameter-free property. In our experiments, we observe that the empirical convergence rate of
135   P2PCFR+ consistently significantly outperforms that of Stable PCFR+ and Smooth PCFR+ .

502   6    C ONCLUSIONS
503   We propose P2PCFR+ , a novel and effective variant of PCFR+ . P2PCFR+ employs the misalignment
504   of step sizes in the updates of implicit and explicit accumulated counterfactual regrets to make a more
505   conservative prediction than PCFR+ . This conservative prediction reduces the discrepancy between
506   the strategies represented by implicit and explicit accumulated regrets within the same iteration. This
507   reduction in this discrepancy diminishes the significant discrepancy between strategies represented
508   by explicit accumulated counterfactual regrets across two consecutive iterations, which typically
509   slows down the empirical convergence rate of PCFR+ . We prove that P2PCFR+ achieves a faster
510   convergence rate compared to PCFR+ . Experimental results further validate that P2PCFR+ exhibits
511
      a faster empirical convergence rate than PCFR+ .
512   To the best of our knowledge, we are the first to propose the misalignment of step sizes in the
513   updates of implicit and explicit accumulated counterfactual regrets, a simple yet novel technique that
514   effectively improves the empirical convergence rate of PCFR+ .
515   The implementation of P2PCFR+ is straightforward, requiring only a single line modification to
516   PCFR+ . Empirically, P2PCFR+ consistently outperforms PCFR+ when α ≤ 10. This demonstrates
517   that a minor change to PCFR+ can lead to significant performance improvements.
518
      Moreover, the discounting techniques used in DCFR are compatible with P2PCFR+ . By incorporating
519
      discounting concepts from DCFR, we can assign different values of α to different iterations t, further
520
      enhancing the empirical convergence rate of P2PCFR+ . We consider this as future work.
