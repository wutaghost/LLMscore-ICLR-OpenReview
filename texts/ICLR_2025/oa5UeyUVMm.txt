009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Diffusion probabilistic models (DPMs), widely recognized for their potential to
012            generate high-quality samples, tend to go unnoticed in representation learning.
013            While recent progress has highlighted their potential for capturing visual semantics,
014            adapting DPMs to graph representation learning remains in its infancy. In this
015            paper, we introduce Graffe, a self-supervised diffusion model proposed for
016            graph representation learning. It features a graph encoder that distills a source
017            graph into a compact representation, which, in turn, serves as the condition to
018
               guide the denoising process of the diffusion decoder. To evaluate the effectiveness
               of our model, we first explore the theoretical foundations of applying diffusion
019
               models to representation learning, proving that the denoising objective implicitly
020
               maximizes the conditional mutual information between data and its representation.
021            Specifically, we prove that the negative logarithm of denoising score matching loss
022            is a tractable lower bound for the conditional mutual information. Empirically,
023            Graffe delivers competitive results under the linear probing setting on node and
024            graph classification, achieving state-of-the-art performance on 9 of the 11 real-
025            world datasets. These findings indicate that powerful generative models, especially
026            diffusion models, serve as an effective tool for graph representation learning.

029   1   I NTRODUCTION
030
031   Self-supervised learning (SSL), which enables effective data understanding without laborious human
032   annotations, is emerging as a key paradigm for addressing both generative and discriminative tasks.
033
      When we revisit the evolution of SSL across these two tasks, interestingly, a mutually reinforcing
      manner becomes evident: Progress in one aspect often stimulates progress in the other. For instance,
034
      autoencoder (Hinton & Salakhutdinov, 2006), which initially made a mark in feature extraction,
035
      laid the foundation for the success of VAEs (Kingma, 2013) for sample generation. Conversely,
036   breakthroughs in generative tasks like autoregression (Radford, 2018) and adversarial training
037   (Goodfellow et al., 2020), have deepened our understanding of representation learning, driving the
038   development of iGPT (Chen et al., 2020) and BigBiGAN (Donahue & Simonyan, 2019).
039
      Recently, diffusion models (Ho et al., 2020; Song et al., 2020) have demonstrated astonishing genera-
040
      tion quality in different domains, particularly in terms of realism, detail depiction, and distribution
041
      coverage. A natural question arises: can we draw on the successful experiences of diffusion models to
042   enhance representation learning? This issue is particularly pressing in the context of graph learning,
043   since generation—the ability to create—plays a less critical role compared to discrimination on
044   graphs, e.g., social networks, citation networks, and recommendation networks. The question seems
045   not difficult to address, as generation is considered one of the highest manifestations of learning thus
046   having powerful capability to learn high-quality representation (Krathwohl, 2002; Johnson et al.,
047   2018; Wang et al., 2023; Hudson et al., 2024); however, the reality is much more complex.
048   To generalize the representation learning power of diffusion models on graph data, two main im-
049   pediments must be addressed: ① the non-Euclidean nature of graph data, which complicates the
050   direct application of diffusion models and necessitates consideration of both structural and feature
051   information; ② the absence of an encoder component in diffusion model prevents us from obtain-
052   ing explicit data representation and finetuning encoder in downstream tasks. Motivated to overcome
053   these challenges, we investigate how to adapt diffusion models to graph representation learning and
      enhance their discrimination performance.

070   This work is particularly relevant to approaches that use diffusion models to capture high-level
071   semantics for classification tasks while enhancing representational capacity. Those approaches can be
072   broadly categorized into two main groups: (i) one treats part of the diffusion model itself as a feature
073   extractor (implicit-encoder pattern) (Xiang et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024). They
074   obtain the latent representation from a certain intermediate layer, which inevitably exposes them to
075   challenge ②. (ii) Another line of work jointly trains the diffusion model and an additional feature
076   extractor (explicit-encoder pattern) (Abstreiter et al., 2021; Wang et al., 2023; Hudson et al., 2024).
077   However, the latter pattern have struggled to surpass their contrastive and auto-encoding counterparts.
078   In this paper, we propose Graffe, which shares a philosophy similar to the explicit-encoder pattern.
079   Starting with the optimization objective for diffusion-based SSL, we analyze diffusion representation
080   learning (DRL) and show that it maximizes the mutual information lower bound between the learned
081   representation and the original input, with more informative representations leading to lower denoising
082   score matching loss, and vice versa. This suggests that DRL implicitly follows a principle akin to
083   the InfoMax principle (Linsker, 1988; Hjelm et al., 2018), which we call the Diff-InfoMax principle.
084
      Furthermore, we observe from the frequency domain of graph features that DRL excels in capturing
      high-frequency information. Inspired by our theoretical insights, we instantiate our model with a
085
      graph neural network (GNN) encoder for explicit representation extraction and a tailored diffusion
086
      decoder, both trained from scratch in tandem. The encoder transforms the graph structure and feature
087   information into a compact representation, which acts as a condition for the decoder together with
088   noisy features to guide the denoising process. The main contributions of this work are three-fold:
089
090
      ❶ We theoretically prove that the negative logarithm of the denoising score matching loss is a tractable
      lower bound for conditional mutual information. Building on this, we introduce the Diff-InfoMax
091
      principle, an extension of the standard InfoMax principle, showing that DRL implicitly follows it.
092
093   ❷ We propose an effective diffusion-based representation learning method catering to graph tasks,
094   termed as Graffe. Equipped with random node masking and customized diffusion architecture for
095   different task types, it can achieve sufficient graph understanding and obtain representations with rich
096
      semantic information.
097   ❸ We conduct extensive experiments on 11 classification tasks under the linear protocol, spanning
098   node- and graph-level tasks of diverse domains. Our method can achieve state-of-the-art or near-
099   optimal performance across all datasets. On Computer, Photo, and COLLAB datasets, our model
100   set a new accuracy record of 91.3%, 94.2% and 81.3%, respectively.

135   the joint distribution and the product of the marginal distribution

267   Frequency-aware Analysis Several works (Yang et al., 2023; Si et al., 2024; Dieleman, 2024) have
268   noted that during the noising process, the high-frequency components of the data are corrupted first,
269   followed by the low-frequency components. Conversely, in the generation process, low-frequency
      components are generated initially, with high-frequency components added later. Then the diffusion

270
      model performs a role generating high-frequency components given noisy data which mainly consists
271   of low-frequency data. From this frequency domain perspective, I(x; f (x)|xt ) guides the feature
272   extractor to focus on components with frequencies exceeding a certain threshold, with different time
273   t corresponding to different frequency thresholds.
274
275   Graph Feature BWGNN (Tang et al., 2022) defines a metric Energy Ratio to assess the concentra-
276   tion of graph features in low frequencies. They observe that perturbing graph features with random
277   noise results in a ’right-shift’ of energy, indicating a reduced concentration in low frequencies and an
278   increased concentration in high frequencies. This finding aligns with our analysis of the frequency
279   domain. Consequently, DRL operates in the spectral space of graph features, excelling at capturing
280   high-frequency information in these features."

282   4     T HE G R A F F E A PPROACH
283
284   As inspired by the above theoretical insights and to overcome the challenges mentioned in section 1,
285   the Graffe framework follows the explicit-encoder pattern and couples a graph encoder Eϕ with a
286   conditional diffusion decoder Dθ . Given an input graph G = (X, A), the encoder achieves perception
287   of both structural and feature information and extracts a compact representation z = Eϕ (G) for each
288   node. Then, the decoder receives both noisy feature xt and encoded representation z to reconstruct
289   the original feature x̃ = Dθ (xt , t, z). The overall framework is demonstrated in fig. 1. We next
290   introduce the Graffe in detail.
291
292   4.1    T HE G RAPH E NCODER
293
294
      The encoder module is the core part of our model. Since we are not concerned with generative
      capabilities, the encoder is the only parameterized module used in downstream tasks, and its capability
295
      directly impacts task performance. We consider two factors that guide the training lean toward
296
      representation learning: one is the expressive capacity of the encoder, which refers to whether it can
297   fully perceive graph data to provide strong representations. The other is the adequacy of encoder
298   training, which involves whether the optimization of the objective function can effectively coordinate
299   the optimization of both the encoder and decoder.
300
      For the first factor, we follow prior work Hou et al. (2022; 2023); Zhao et al. (2024) on the encoder
301
      selection, which adopted GAT (Velickovic et al., 2017) and GIN (Xu et al., 2018) for node and graph
302
      tasks, respectively, as both theoretical and empirical evidence demonstrate that they have strong
303   expressive capabilities for graph tasks. This also ensures fair comparison in subsequent experimental
304   analysis. Specifically, their message-passing mechanism can be expressed as:
305

      4.2   T HE D IFFUSION D ECODER
329
330   Reconstruction objective. Unlike image features, graph data incorporates feature and structural
331   information, prompting the question of which to prioritize for reconstruction. Previous work in
332   graph SSL has explored both directions: for example, GraphMAE (Hou et al., 2022) focuses only
333   on feature information, while another concurrent work, MaskGAE (Li et al., 2023), only targets
334   topological attributes. It is worth noting that in many graph learning datasets, features are often
335
      one-hot embeddings, and topology is represented by adjacency matrices—both of which are highly
      sparse, thus making it difficult to make decisions based on the nature of data. We empirically tested
336
      reconstructing features, topology, and their combination. Results in table 3 demonstrate that feature
337
      reconstruction performs best, outperforming the hybrid approach, with topology-only reconstruction
338   yielding the worst results. Therefore, we choose features x as the target for reconstruction.
339
340   Customized instantiation of decoder. In decoder design, we draw on the experience of using the
341   U-Net architecture from the visual domain as a backbone model for diffusion training. The U-Net
342   architecture Ronneberger et al. (2015) provides representations of different granularities through
343   up- and down-sampling Si et al. (2024). Additionally, it aligns well with the strict dimensional
344   requirements of diffusion models. Specifically, when handling graph-level tasks, we propose Graph-
345   UNet, which adopts GNN layers to replace the convolutional layers in the vanilla U-Net. In this
346   context, each graph in a mini-batch can be likened to an image in a visual diffusion model; by
347   uniformly sampling time step t ∼ Uniform(0, T ) within a mini-batch, we ensure that the level of
348
      feature noise within each graph remains consistent.
349   However, for node-level tasks, if we instantiate the decoder with GNNs, it becomes problematic to
350   use different time steps for different nodes, as this would lead to message passing propagating node
351   information at varying noise levels. Therefore, to enable the model to clearly perceive distinct noise
352   levels and conduct training in a principled manner, we replace the GNN layers with the MLP network.
353   Please refer to appendix B for more details of Graph-Unet.

      6     R ELATED W ORK
492
493
      6.1   S ELF - SUPERVISED L EARNING ON G RAPHS
494   Contrastive methods Being popular in SSL, contrastive methods aim to learn discriminative
495   representations by contrasting positive and negative samples. The key to obtain distinguishable
496   representations lies in the way of constructing contrastive pairs. DGI (Velickovic et al., 2019) and
497   InfoGraph (Sun et al., 2019), based on MI maximization, corrupt graph feature and topology to
498   construct negative samples. To avoid the underlying risk of semantic damage, GRACE (Zhu et al.,
499   2020), GCA (Zhu et al., 2021), and GraphCL (You et al., 2020) use other graphs within the same
500   batch as negatives. Other works, i.e., BGRL (Thakoor et al., 2021) and CCA-SSA (Zhang et al.,
501   2021), propose to achieve contrastive learning free of negatives yet demanding strong regularization
502
      or feature decorrelation. A line of works borrow from data augmentation in the field of computer
      vision (CV) to construct constrastive pairs, including feature-oriented ((Thakoor et al., 2021; You
503
      et al., 2020; Zhu et al., 2020), shuffling (Velickovic et al., 2019)), perturbation (Hu et al., 2020b; You
504
      et al., 2020)), and graph-theory-based (random walk (Hassani & Khasahmadi, 2020; Qiu et al., 2020).
505
506
      Generative methods Generative self-supervised methods aim to learn informative representations
507   using learning signals from the data itself, usually by maximizing the marginal log-likelihood of
508   the data. GPT-GNN Hu et al. (2020b), following the auto-regressive paradigm, iteratively generates
509   graph features and topology, which is unnatural as most graph data has no inherent order. GAE and
510   VGAE Kipf & Welling (2016) learn to reconstruct the adjacency matrix by using the representation
511   learned from GCN, while other graph autoencoders Salehi & Davulcu (2019); Hou et al. (2022)
512   further combine it with feature reconstruction with tailored strategies. However, these generative
513   methods are usually not principled in terms of probabilistic generative models and often prove to be
514   inferior to the contrastive ones.
515
516   6.2   D IFFUSION M ODELS FOR R EPRESENTATION L EARNING
517
      The very first attempt has combined auto-encoders with diffusion models—e.g., DiffAE (Preechakul
518
      et al., 2022), a non-probabilistic auto-encoder model that produces semantically meaningful latent.
519
      InfoDiffusion (Wang et al., 2023), as the first principled probabilistic generative model for represen-
520   tation learning, augments DiffAE with an auxiliary-variable model family and mutual information
521   maximization. Similarly, Zhang et al. (2022) uses a pre-trained diffusion decoder and designs a
522   re-weighting scheme to fill in the posterior mean gap. Targeting image classification tasks, Wei et al.
523   (2023); Gao et al. (2023); Hudson et al. (2024) combine latent diffusion with the self-supervised
524   learning objective to get meaningful representations. The decoder-only models (Xiang et al., 2023;
525   Chen et al., 2024), directly use the representations from intermediate layers without auxiliary en-
526   coders. However, the use of expressive diffusion models for graph representation learning remains
527   under-explored. DDM (Yang et al., 2024) takes an initial step, but the proposed diffusion process is
528
      not mathematically rigorous and principled.
529
530   7     C ONCLUSION
531
532   In this paper, we introduce Graffe, a self-supervised diffusion representation learning (DRL)
533   framework designed for graphs, achieving state-of-the-art performance on self-supervised graph
534   representation learning tasks. We establish the theoretical foundations of DRL and prove that the
535   denoising objective is a lower bound for the conditional mutual information between data and its
536   representations. We propose the Diff-InfoMax principle, an extension of the standard InfoMax
537
      principle, and demonstrate that DRL implicitly follows it. Based on these theoretical insights and
      customized design for graph data, Graffe excels in node and graph classification tasks. We provide
538
      discussion about limitations and future work in appendix C.

       B    A RCHITECTURE OF G RAPH -U NET
1027
1028
       As illustrated on the right side of fig. 1, our decoder adopts a UNet-like architecture, comprising a
1029
       contracting path (left side) and an expansive path (right side). However, since up-sampling and down-
1030   sampling operations cannot be directly applied to graph data, we instead represent the granularity
1031   of modeling through dimensional reduction and expansion. Specifically, due to the requirement of
1032   the diffusion model that the input and output dimensions match the original feature dimensions, we
1033   introduce additional input and output layers to perform dimensional mappings. In the contracting path,
1034   repeated dimensional reduction is performed using either GNN layers or MLP layers, depending on
1035   different task types, which halves the number of hidden dimensions at each step. In the expansive path,
1036   dimensional expansion is repeated, but before each mapping, the hidden state of the corresponding
1037   contracting path with the same dimension is added via skip connections, which differs from the
1038
       original UNet’s concatenation.
1039   It is also important to note that, in addition to the noisy data xt , the decoder also receives the condition
1040   z and time t as inputs. We encode the time information using two linear layers with SiLU activation
1041   (Elfwing et al., 2018), and employ positional encoding to enable the model to distinguish temporal
1042   order. Furthermore, a key challenge is how to fuse xt , z, and t. Based on experimental results, the
1043   optimal approach for node-level tasks is to directly sum these three components after encoding, as
1044
       shown below:
1045
                                           h(l+1) = h(l) + MLPt (t) + MLPz (z)                                  (63)
1046   where MLPt (·) and MLPz (·) are both MLP layer to achieve dimensional mapping.
1047
       For graph-level tasks, we follow the approach commonly used in the field of computer vision, utilizing
1048
       Adaptive Normalization layers (Dhariwal & Nichol, 2021; Hudson et al., 2024) to fuse the three
1049   components:
1050
1051                    h(l+1) = AdaNorm(h(l) , z, t) = zs (ts LayerNorm(h(l) ) + tb ) + zb                    (64)
1052
       where (ts , tb ) and (zs , zb ) are both obtained by linear projection.

       E    D ISCUSSIONS
1135
1136
1137
1138   Intuitive guide for choosing masking ratio m. As shown in Appendix C, although the optimal
1139   mask ratio differs across datasets, there are clear trends across different tasks. For instance, a larger
1140
       mask ratio generally yields better results in node classification, while the opposite is true for graph
       classification. We hypothesize that this may be due to the combined effect of the graph characteristics
1141
       and diffusion representation learning. Here we provide some intuitive understanding. In graph
1142
       classification tasks, where graphs are typically small and have simpler connectivity, a small mask
1143   ratio is suggested to avoid significant information loss. Conversely, in node classification tasks,
1144   where there are more nodes and more complex connections, a large mask ratio is suggested since
1145   overly detailed modeling can cause the model to become overly focused on intricate information. We
1146   suggest that when selecting the mask ratio, one should first assess the characteristics of the graph and
1147   then determine an appropriate candidate for the mask ratio accordingly.
1148
1149   Limitations and future work Despite the significant contributions of this study to the understand-
1150   ing of DRL and significant performance on graph tasks, there are certain limitations that should
1151   be acknowledged to provide a comprehensive perspective. The proposed Diff-InfoMax principle
1152   involves a weighting function over time. How to dynamically adjust the weighting function over
1153
       different data and tasks remains an unsolved problem. Additionally, methods to optimize alternative
       variational lower bounds of the Diff-InfoMax principle are left for future exploration. From an
1154
       empirical perspective, we believe that the structure, or the non-Euclidean nature of graphs, is crucial
1155
       information for graph representation learning. Therefore, an intriguing question remains regarding
1156   the deeper understanding of explicitly incorporating structural modeling into diffusion representation
1157   learning, which is a highly non-trivial task. Moreover, our method does not have an advantage in
1158   terms of time efficiency. Improvements in training speed and further refinements in model design are
1159   left as directions for future research.
