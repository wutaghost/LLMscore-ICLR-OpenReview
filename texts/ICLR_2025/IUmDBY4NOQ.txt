009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Learning in hyperbolic spaces has gained increasing attention due to the superior
012            capability of modeling hierarchical structures. Existing hyperbolic learning meth-
013            ods use a fixed distance measure that assumes a uniform hierarchical structure
014            across all data points. However, this assumption does not always hold in real-
015            world scenarios, considering the diversity of the hierarchical structures of data.
016            This work proposes to learn geometry aware distance measures that dynamically
017            adjust to accommodate diverse hierarchical structures in hyperbolic spaces. We
018
               derive geometry aware distance measures by generating projections and curvatures
               for each pair of samples, which maps each pair to a suitable hyperbolic space.
019
               We introduce a revised low-rank decomposition scheme and a hard-pair mining
020
               mechanism to reduce the computational cost incurred by the pairwise generation
021            without compromising accuracy. Moreover, we derive an upper bound of the
022            low-rank approximation error via Talagrand concentration inequality to guarantee
023            the effectiveness of our low-rank decomposition scheme. Theoretical analysis and
024            experiments on standard image classification and few-shot learning tasks affirm the
025            effectiveness of our method in refining hyperbolic learning through our geometry
026            aware distance measures.

029   1    I NTRODUCTION
030
031   The hyperbolic space is defined as a smooth Riemannian manifold with constant negative curvature.
032   A notable property of Hyperbolic spaces lies in the exponential growth of a ball’s volume relative to
033
      its radius, mirroring the exponential increase in the volume of hierarchical data with depth. Such a
      property enables the hyperbolic space to serve as a continuous analogous to trees (Sala et al., 2018;
034
      Balazevic et al., 2019), enabling to model hierarchical data with minimal distortion(Sarkar, 2011).
035
      The use of hyperbolic spaces for data embedding has shown to be superior in representing hierarchical
036   structures across various applications such as classification (Gao et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2022; Gao
037   et al., 2022), clustering (Lin et al., 2022; 2023a), retrieval (Ermolov et al., 2022), segmentation (Hsu
038   et al., 2021b; Atigh et al., 2022a; Chen et al., 2022), multi-modal (Hong et al., 2023a; Long & van
039   Noord, 2023) and 3D vision (Hsu et al., 2021a; Montanaro et al., 2022; Leng et al., 2023; Lin et al.,
040   2023b).
041
      Existing hyperbolic learning methods usually deploy a fixed distance measure, i.e., geodesic distance,
042   to assess the similarities between data points, based on the expectation that the geodesic can effectively
043   reflect the connecting paths between two nodes on the corresponding graph/tree(Behrstock et al.,
044   2019). Employing a fixed distance measure implicitly includes the assumption of having a uniform
045   hierarchical structure across all data points(Nickel & Kiela, 2018; Behrstock et al., 2017; 2019).
046   However, this assumption does not always hold in real-world scenarios, as the hierarchical structures
047   between data are diverse and complex. Thus, using a fixed distance measure in real-world scenarios
048   may cause data distortion for diverse hierarchical structures, leading to sub-optimal performance.
049   For example, as shown in Figure 1(a), the “dog-wolf” data pair has a simple semantic hierarchical
050   structure, while the “aircraft carriers-school bus” data pair has a more complex semantic hierarchical
051   structure. In this case, a fixed distance measure fails to accurately represent the two different
052   hierarchical relationships, unable to pass through the respective common ancestors, leading to a
053   misalignment between distance and hierarchical structures, as detailed in Figure 1(b). Employing
      adaptive distance measures on data with diverse hierarchical structures seems a natural choice, which

      In this paper, we propose to learn geometry-aware distance measures that automatically adapt to
078
      diverse hierarchical structures in hyperbolic spaces. Our main idea is to learn to generate adaptive
079   projection and curvature for each pair of samples in the hyperbolic spaces, conforming to the
080   hierarchical relationship between any two data points. In doing so, we design a curvature generator
081   to produce adaptive curvatures for different data pairs and a projection matrix generator to map data
082   pairs from the original hyperbolic space to an adaptive hyperbolic space with the new curvature. By
083   applying adaptive projections and curvatures to the geodesic distance function for each sample pair,
084   we obtain geometry-aware distance measures.
085   Two challenges need to be solved in learning to generate geometry-aware distance measures in the
086   hyperbolic space: (1) Given that unique projections and curvatures are required for every data pair,
087   reducing the computational cost becomes a critical consideration. (2) As a variety of hierarchical
088   structures are encountered during the optimization process, the continuity of the optimization direction
089   or trajectory for the pairwise level learning and training stability is not easily maintained. To address
090   the first challenge, we introduce a low-rank decomposition scheme and a hard-pair mining mechanism.
091   The former reduces computational complexity via low-rank approximation, and the latter eliminates
092   easy samples, i.e., we only generate adaptive distances for the remaining challenging ones. For the
093
      second challenge, we show that by incorporating residual connections into the projection matrix
      during the generation process, training stability can be well maintained. Experimental results in
094
      standard image classification and few-shot learning tasks confirm the effectiveness of our method in
095
      refining hyperbolic learning through geometry-aware distance measures.
096
097   The primary contributions of our work can be summarized as follows:
098
099               • We propose to learn to generate adaptive hyperbolic distances, enabling a more nuanced
100                 representation of diverse hierarchical structures inherent in data.
101
102
                  • We introduce a low-rank decomposition scheme and a hard-pair mining mechanism to
103
                    significantly reduce computational costs without compromising accuracy.
104
105
106               • Theoretically, we prove that with a high probability, the low-rank decomposition in hyper-
107
                    bolic spaces yields small errors relative to the original full-rank matrix.

      2.1   A DAPTIVE DEEP METRIC LEARNING
112
      Adaptive metric learning is a pivotal machine learning technique that adapts embeddings or distance
113
      measures to handle data diversity (Li et al., 2021; 2019; Yoon et al., 2020; Liu & Wang, 2021).
114
      Adaptive embeddings are crafted through flexible prototypes (Li et al., 2021), tailored discriminative
115   features (Li et al., 2019; Yoon et al., 2020), or episode-specific learning (Liu & Wang, 2021). Adaptive
116   distance measurements range from task-specific metric spaces (Oreshkin et al., 2018; Qiao et al.,
117   2019; Zhou et al., 2023) to optimized dynamic classifiers using subspaces (Simon et al., 2020). Recent
118   advances include neighborhood-adaptive metric learning (Song et al., 2022; Li et al., 2022), yet these
119   methods often rely on Euclidean space, limiting their effectiveness on data with intricate hierarchical
120   structures. Different from these Euclidean-based methods, we propose to learn geometry-aware
121   distance measures in hyperbolic spaces, which can exploit the inherent hierarchical structures of the
122   data.
123
124   2.2   H YPERBOLIC GEOMETRY
125
126   Hyperbolic geometry has shown superior performances in many applications due to their capabilities
127   in modeling data with hierarchical structures. Research on hyperbolic geometry can be divided into
128   three categories. Methods of the first category opt for modeling several applications on hyperbolic
129   spaces, such as medical image recognition (Yu et al., 2022), action recognition (Long et al., 2020),
130   audio-visual learning (Hong et al., 2023b), image segmentation (Atigh et al., 2022b), anomaly
131   detection (Li et al., 2024), and 3D Visual Grounding (Wang et al., 2024) . Methods of the second
132   category work on extending convincing neural architectures from Euclidean spaces to hyperbolic
133
      spaces, such as convolutional network (Shimizu et al., 2021) and graph network (Dai et al., 2021).
      Methods of the third category focus on extending learning paradigms from Euclidean spaces to
134
      hyperbolic manifolds, such as contrastive learning (Ge et al., 2023), self-supervised learning (Franco
135
      et al., 2023), and metric learning (Yan et al., 2021). Different from these methods, we propose to
136   learn geometry-aware hyperbolic distance measures for each data pair to match its unique hierarchical
137   structure.
138
139
140   2.3   C URVATURE LEARNING
141
142   Previous studies (Gu et al., 2019) have established that selecting the appropriate curvature is crucial for
143
      effective hyperbolic learning, as it significantly impacts the quality of the learned representations. Re-
      search on curvatures has continually advanced, encompassing the application of constant (Bachmann
144
      et al., 2020) in graph neural networks, as well as the exploration of curvature learning methods (Yang
145
      et al., 2023). Gu et al. (Gu et al., 2019) address embedding diverse hierarchical data by using a
146   product manifold that combines multiple spaces with heterogeneous curvature. Inspired by these
147   methods, our model takes it a step further by dynamically adjusting curvature for pair-wise distance
148   measures. Our method stands out by dynamically adapting curvature for each pair during inference,
149   based on their specific characteristics.

224   The goal of our method is to train the matrix generator gt and curvature generator gc , through which
225   positive pairs are closer and negative pairs are pushed farther apart. M is produced by the matrix
226   generator gt : M = gt (xi , xj ), and c is generated by the curvature generator gc : c = gc (xi , xj ).

229   mining mechanism to filter out the hard pairs H = HPM(Dtrain ), where H denotes the hard cases,
230   Dtrain is the training set and HPM(·) denotes the hard-pair mining. Our method only generates
231   projection matrices and curvatures for pairs in H. In the following section, we will introduce gt , gc ,

240   4.3.1    P ROJECTION MATRIX GENERATOR
241
242   The distance measure generator takes a pair of feature points xi and xj as inputs and provides a
243   transformation matrix M as output. In this paper, we utilize an adaptive matrix generator to produce
244   the projection matrix M . Excessive changes of M will result in instability of training. Instead of
245

      (in Eq. (2)) and the geometry aware distance measure (in Eq. (5)) to tackle this issue. Therefore,
246

247

250   M res into the product of two low-rank matrices: M res = Mares Mbres ⊤ , where Mares ∈ Bn×k and

337   4.6   C OMPLEXITY A NALYSIS
338
      Low-rank distance measures reduce computational costs in generating projection matrices. Our
339   method produces two low-rank matrices (n × k) with a cost of O(n2 k), compared to O(n3 ) for
340   directly generating an n × n matrix, where k ≪ n.
341
342
      The time complexities for our method’s components are O(pq) for hard-pair mining, O(n2 ) for
      curvature generation, and O(n2 k) for projection matrix generation. Here, p is the number of classes,
343
      q the number of queries, n the dimensionality, and k the rank. The overall time complexity is
344
      O(n2 (k + 1) + pq) = O(n2 (k + 1)), as p and q are much smaller than n.

527   6    C ONCLUSION
528
529   In this paper, we have presented geometry aware hyperbolic distance measures that accommodate
530   diverse hierarchical data structures through adaptive projection matrix and curvature. The adaptive
531   curvature endows embeddings with more flexible hyperbolic spaces that better match the inherent
532   hierarchical structures. The low-rank projection matrices bring positive pairs closer and push negative
533   pairs farther apart. Moreover, the hard-pair mining mechanism enables the efficient selection of hard
534   cases from the query set without introducing additional parameters, reducing the computational cost.
535   Theoretical analysis and experiments show the effectiveness of our method in refining hyperbolic
536
      learning through geometry aware distance measures.
537   Limitations. A primary limitation of our work is potential bias, as the distance measure generator
538   may be sensitive to specific data distributions. Additionally, while we use low-rank decomposition to
539   reduce computational costs, this is a preliminary implementation. Future work will explore other
      decomposition methods to maintain geometric consistency and enhance efficiency.

       The distance measure (determined by curvature c) and defined complexity are related in the following
973    intuitive way. We explain it in the 2-dimensional case where the hierarchical structure is represented
974    by a tree, the simplest planar graph. Now for any two vertices x1 and x2 in the tree, the complexity
975    between them is just the number of edges through which the path between them passes, or in other
976    words, the number of hierarchical levels their path runs across. For pairs of different complexity,
977    the learned curvatures and geodesics are supposed to lie in different “phase spaces”. If one insists
978    on picturing them as a common space (which might turn out to be what the neural network actually
979    configures), the geodesic edges hence the hierarchical structure would better be embedded into a
980    deformed hyperbolic surface with sufficiently large genus (to allow for enough homotopy classes of
981
       the geodesics i.e. larger fundamental group) rather than as a planar tree. Then the total complexity or
       total curvature of the hierarchical structure is afforded by the topological complexity of the deformed
982
       hyperbolic surface, i.e. its genus or equivalently its Euler characteristics. Note that for a (closed
983
       orientable) hyperbolic surface S of genus g, its Euler characteristic χ(S) = 2 − 2g. Actually, the total
984    curvature of a compact closed surface S equals to 2πχ(S) by the classical Gauss-Bonnet theorem.
985    More generally, total curvature and Euler characteristic are related by Cohn-Vossen’s inequality.

1021   Our design prioritizes computational efficiency and embeds specific inductive biases (e.g., the bilinear
1022   form and bounding the curvature) to capture the geometry of hyperbolic space effectively. We choose
1023   the factorized bilinear pooling yu2017multi to produce suitable curvature by using expressive second-
1024   order information of data. Second-order information unveils the dynamics and trends within data, akin
1025   to how curvature reflects the degree of warping in the hyperbolic space. Using this information allows
       us to comprehend the local curvature of data distributions, deepening our grasp of the geometric

1029   Details about the factorized bilinear pooling. We use a factorized bilinear pooling that produces
1030
       suitable curvature by using expressive second-order information of data, where the sum pooling
       and sigmoid function are used to reduce dimension of second-order information and constrain the
1031
       produced curvature |c| in a valid range.
1032
1033   In a non-factorized bilinear pooling method, the second-order information of a pair of data (x1 ∈
1034   Rn , x2 ∈ Rn ) is the outer products x1 xT2 , and the curvature |c| can be computed by a fully-connected
1035   layer f (·), |c| = f (x1 xT2 ). The second-order information captures expressive characterization of
1036
       data, and thus can produce suitable curvature. However, the non-factorized bilinear pooling method
       has large computation consumption, since the dimension of x1 xT2 ∈ Rn×n is high, causing a large
1037
       number of parameters in f (·). To solve this issue, the parameter of f (·) is factorized into two
1038
       parameters via matrix factorization, and the two parameters are applied to two fully-connected layers
1039   f1 (·) and f2 (·), respectively.
1040
1041   In this case, f (x1 xT2 ) can be rewritten as f (x1 xT2 ) = 1(f1 (x1 )◦f2 (x2 )), where 1 is an all one-vector,
1042
       serving as a summation function (sum pooling). The detailed derivation can be found in (Yu et al.,
       2017). The range of 1(f1 (x1 ) ◦ f2 (x2 )) is (−∞, ∞), while some hyperbolic methods (Khrulkov
1043
       et al., 2020; Ermolov et al., 2022) show that the curvature |c| in a valid range(eg. [0, 1]) is suitable
1044
       for much data. Thus, we use a sigmoid function |c| = sigmoid(sum pooling(f1 (x1 ) ◦ f2 (x2 ))) to
1045   rescale it into a valid range.

1143   Recalling that the matrix generator gt () as in (5) of section 4.1 is a local generator which appears
1144

1146

1147   enough, these samples may be effectively considered as distributed within a local mesh of data, akin
1148   to a Gaussian distribution with a small variance, as demonstrated in subsequent analyses.
1149
1150
       We first introduce the material technique of “divide and conquer” for sampling general sets through
       polynomial partitioning, developed by Larry Guth and Nets Katz when dealing with the Erdős’
1151
       distinct distances problem (L. Guth, 2015).
1152
1153   Theorem D.3 (Theorem 4.1 of L. Guth (2015)). For any set S ⊂ Rn of N points and positive
1154   integer d, there exists a hypersurface Z defined by a polynomial of degree ≤ c2d/n for some absolute
1155   constant c > 0, whose complement Rn ∖ Z is the union of 2d disjoint open cells each containing
1156   ≤ 2−d N points of S.
1157
1158   The key point here is that we need very few partitioning parameters (determining a suitable poly-
1159
       nomial) to segment big data sets into much smaller clusters with similarities as desired especially
       if the data distributes relatively continuously. Note that the hypersurface itself may contain part
1160
       of the points. If the samples distribute relatively continuously, we may see these surface points
1161
       locally as residing on a tangent space of the hypersurface, which is of dimension n − 1. Hence
1162   we may deal with the surface points inductively so that we need only concern about the points in
1163   cells. For example, ifpwe require each cell to have n points, i.e. d ∼ log(N/n), we only need
1164   O(2log(N/n)/n ) = O( n N/n) parameters. In practice, if we use training sets about size in millions
1165   and feature dimension in hundreds, the partitioning parameters are freely spared without any concern.
1166
1167
       The above explained polynomial partitioning mechanism seems to reveal at least partially the dark
       matter of miracle efficiency of even shallow-layered neural networks. From a more general theoretical
1168
       standpoint, any neural network is designed to use elementary operations to segment and model global
1169
       datasets with local subsets that align with ambient distributions in assumption, which naturally calls
1170   for and results in polynomial partitioning outcomes. This particularly facilitates the application of
1171   low-rank approximations.
1172
1173

       deal with approximation of local pieces after partitioning.

1177   can be simultaneously bisected by a single hyperplane.

1299
1300   Our distance measure cannot ensure the strict symmetry of input pair (x, y), since we do not shared
1301   the weights of the subnetworks(fa and fb in the projection matrix gt , f1 and f2 in the curvature
1302   generator gc ) within the generator, where fa and f1 are used for x, and fb and f2 are used for y. But
1303   we do not argue this is a problem. For a sample pair (x, y) in the training process, we randomly
1304   sample x and y from the dataset, and the probability of (x, y) and (y, x) are equal. Thus, if we change
1305
       (x, y) to (y, x), and send it to our distance measure, their distances will not change significantly.
1306   To demonstrate this point, we do the ablation by swapping fa and fb in the projection matrix
1307   generator(gt ) as well as f1 andf2 in the curvature generator(gc ), which is equal to swapping the inputs
1308   of gt and gc separately. As shown in Table 9, the impact of swapping the subnetworks in the projection
1309   matrix generator and the curvature generator on the performance of our method is negligible. This
1310   indicates that fa and fb , as well as f1 and f2 , have learned nearly identical knowledge. Although
1311
       our method does not ensure the symmetry, it avoids situations where swapping x and y in a pair leads

       F.1.2    M ODEL DETAILS
1679
1680   Our method regards that the features from the backbones are located in the hyperbolic space’s
1681   tangent space at the origin, and we utilize an exponential map on top of the backbone to transfer
1682   these features from the tangent space to the hyperbolic space. We pre-train the backbones on the
1683   training set using the cross-entropy loss. The backbones are fixed in our metric learning process. We
1684   calculate Einstein mid-point of all the features from the training set as the prototypes and do the
1685   classification according to the similarities between samples and prototypes. For the MNIST(LeCun &
1686   Cortes, 2010) dataset, we use a LeNet-like(LeCun et al., 1998) net as the backbone network. For
1687
       CIFAR10(Krizhevsky et al., 2009) and CIFAR100(Krizhevsky et al., 2009) we use Wide-ResNet
       28 × 2(Zagoruyko & Komodakis, 2016) as the backbone network. The Wide-ResNet 28 × 2 is trained
1688
       for 120 epochs with the SGD optimizer. The learning rate is set to 0.01 at first and decayed per 30
1689
       epochs with a decay rate of 0.1.
