009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
               In a physical system, changing parameters such as temperature can induce a phase
012
               transition: an abrupt change from one state of matter to another. Analogous phe-
013            nomena have recently been observed in large language models. Typically, the task
014            of identifying phase transitions requires human analysis and some prior understand-
015            ing of the system to narrow down which low-dimensional properties to monitor and
016            analyze. Statistical methods for the automated detection of phase transitions from
017            data have recently been proposed within the physics community. These methods
018            are largely system agnostic and, as shown here, can be adapted to study the behavior
019            of large language models. In particular, we quantify distributional changes in the
020            generated output via statistical distances, which can be efficiently estimated with
021
               access to the probability distribution over next-tokens. This versatile approach is
               capable of discovering new phases of behavior and unexplored transitions – an
022
               ability that is particularly exciting in light of the rapid development of language
023
               models and their emergent capabilities.
024
025
026
027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029
      In physics, a phase transition refers to an abrupt change in a system’s macroscopic behavior due
030   to variations in external conditions such as temperature or pressure. A familiar example is the
031   transition of water from a phase of high density (liquid) to one of low density (gas) at the boiling point.
032   Phenomena reminiscent of such classical phase transitions have also been observed in neural networks
033   (NNs), including rapid knowledge acquisition and sudden improvements in capabilities (Shwartz-Ziv
034   & Tishby, 2017; McGrath et al., 2022; Olsson et al., 2022). For instance, during training, AlphaZero
035   exhibited periods where increasingly sophisticated chess openings were suddenly favored (McGrath
036   et al., 2022), and large language models (LLMs) have shown sudden improvements linked to the
037   formation of specialized circuitry known as induction heads (Olsson et al., 2022).
038   As these phase transitions in deep learning systems often coincide with points where model capa-
039   bilities emerge, disappear, or simply fundamentally change, their systematic detection promises to
040   enable a more streamlined interrogation of the learning process, potentially leading to better training
041   methods or safer model deployment. (Ganguli et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022). For example, analyzing
042   the grokking phenomenon led to strategies for accelerating generalization (Liu et al., 2022). Existing
043   methods often require significant human input and may miss critical transitions (Zhong et al., 2023),
044
      highlighting the need for automated detection techniques.
045   Inspired by statistical methods in physics that require minimal prior knowledge (Carrasquilla &
046   Melko, 2017; Van Nieuwenburg et al., 2017), we introduce an automated approach for detecting
047   phase transitions in LLMs using statistical distances from the family of f -divergences. This method
048   provides a versatile tool for objectively mapping out the phase diagrams of generative models. Such
049   an approach has the potential to characterize unexplored transitions and discover new behavioral
050
      phases.
051   As a demonstration, we characterize transitions occurring in Pythia (Biderman et al., 2023), Mistral
052   (7B) (Jiang et al., 2023), and Llama 3 (8B) (AI@Meta) language models as a function of three control
053   parameters: an integer in the input prompt, the temperature hyperparameter for text generation, and
      the model’s training epoch. We obtain the following findings:

070   2.1   Q UANTIFYING D ISSIMILARITY BETWEEN D ISTRIBUTIONS
071
      In this work, we view phase transitions as rapid changes in the probability distribution P (·|T )
072
      governing the state of a system x ∼ P (·|T ) as its control parameter T is varied.1 That is, values
073
      of the parameter at which the distribution changes strongly are considered critical points where
074   phase transitions occur. While it is possible to generalize our approach to distributions conditioned
075   on multiple control parameters as in (Arnold et al., 2023b;a), in the following we consider the
076   one-dimensional scenario for simplicity.
077
      We quantify the rate of change using f -divergences (Liese & Vajda, 2006), as they have particularly
078
      nice properties, such as satisfying the data processing inequality. Given a convex function f : R≥0 →
079
      R with f (1) = 0, the corresponding f -divergence is a statistical distance defined as

      A natural choice for g is any linear function in x. In particular, setting g(x) = 2x − 1 results in a
163   dissimilarity measure that quantifies the ability of an optimal classifier to tell whether a sample x
164   has been drawn in the left or right sector. This measure is 0 if the two distributions are completely
165   indistinguishable and 1 if the two distributions are perfectly distinguishable. Moreover, g(x) = 2x−1
166   is bounded between 1 and -1, where the edge values are attained for the certain predictions of 0 and
167   1, and the value 0 corresponds to uncertain predictions at 0.5. This results in a low variance and
168   favorable convergence properties for D̂g(x)=2x−1 , which we will refer to as linear dissimilarity in
169   what follows.
170
      This quantity is a valid f -divergence and, as shown in Appendix B, reduces to the Fisher information
171   F (Amari & Cichocki, 2010) in lowest non-vanishing order of the distance δT between neighboring
172   parameter points on the grid. In fact, any g-dissimilarity with g(1/2) = 0 and a twice-differentiable
173   g-function can be shown to be proportional to the Fisher information in lowest order. To give a sketch
174   of the derivation, for L = 1 we can write Df (T ∗ ) = 21 f ′′ (1)F(T ∗ )δT 2 + O(δT 3 ).
175
      Having the Fisher information as a limiting case is a desirable property: It is a well-known, generic
176
      statistical measure for quantifying how sensitive parameterized probability distributions are to
177   changes in their parameters and its behavior is well-understood when used to detect phase transitions
178   in physical systems (You et al., 2007; Gu, 2010; Prokopenko et al., 2011; Arnold et al., 2023a). When
179   considering parametric distributions underlying physical systems, the Fisher information can be
180   shown to be directly related to important physical quantities, such as the heat capacity and magnetic
181   susceptibility (Arnold et al., 2023a). Physicists routinely detect phase transitions in physical systems
182   by identifying divergences (in the case of infinite-sized systems) or sharp local maxima (in the case
183   of finite-sized systems) in these quantities. While they have no direct correspondence in the more
184   abstract realm of language models, our method extends this methodology to this domain allowing for
185
      the detection of analogous “phase transitions”. The Fisher information provides a solid theoretical
      basis for applying concepts from statistical physics to LLMs, bridging the gap between physical
186
      systems and computational models.

      3.1                   T RANSITIONS AS A F UNCTION OF A VARIABLE IN THE P ROMPT
240
241
242   As an introduction, we start with the simplest case: The parameter T to be varied is a particular
243   part of the prompt, and all parameters of the language model itself are fixed. As a first such prompt,
244   consider “T is larger than 42. True or False?” with an integer T as the control parameter. An LLM
245   that understands the order of integers should output very different answers for T < 42 versus T > 42,
246   i.e., its distribution over outputs should change drastically around T = 42. Thus, in such a case we
247   expect the dissimilarities to show a clear peak around T = 42.
248   Figure 1(a) shows dissimilarities based on various g-functions for the Mistral-7B-Instruct model (Jiang
249   et al., 2023). All dissimilarities show a clear peak around T = 42, whereas they are relatively flat
250   otherwise. This is a clear example of an abrupt transition between two distinct phases of behaviors of
251   an LLM as a function of a tunable parameter. As compared to the linear dissimilarity, the logarithm-
252   based JS divergence is arguably a bit sharper in that it decays more rapidly to baseline 0. The TV
253   distance’s peak is the broadest due to the min function appearing in its g-function. In the following,
254
      we will focus on the linear dissimilarity as a compromise between sensitivity and numerical stability.
255   The transition is also clearly visible using different L settings, see Fig. 1(b). Smaller L values are
256   closer to the Fisher information limit, while larger values generally lead to higher distinguishability of
257   distributions and therefore larger peaks at transition points. As we will see in more detail in Sec. 3.3,
258   they can also be less susceptible to outliers due to the averaging over several parameter points.
259   Interestingly, when performing the same analysis on base models such as the Llama3-8B and Mistral
260   base models, as well as Pythia models (Biderman et al., 2023) of various sizes, the resulting linear
261   dissimilarity is flat, signaling the absence of any transition [see Fig. 2(a)]. In contrast to Mistral-
262   7B-Instruct and NVIDIA’s chat-tuned Llama3-8B, these models do not show a clear peak around
263   T = 42.
264   A transition of a different origin can be observed in Fig. 2(b), where the LLMs are probed using the
265   prompt “T ” with T again being an integer. Interestingly, all Pythia models show a peak between
266   T = 2020 and T = 2021. This behavioral transition may originate from a transition in the tokenizers
267   of these models, which encode numbers in a range below T = 2021 with a single token and numbers
268   in a range at and above T = 2021 with two. This explanation is corroborated by the absence of the
269   transition around T = 2021 for the Llama and Mistral models, whose tokenizers translate a number
      into tokens following rules that are independent of the number’s frequency.

448   at a single point and returns back (close) to its original behavior immediately after. As such, these
449   peaks do not mark transitions between two macroscopic phases of behavior. We further verified this
450   outlier behavior by inspecting the dissimilarity between the points directly to the left and right of the

457   progresses differently for different types of behavior. Here we have used rather generic prompts,
458   resulting in an analysis of the LLM’s general behavior during training. However, in principle,
459   conditioning on the prompt allows one to analyze whether and when specific knowledge emerges (Liu
460   et al., 2021; Gurnee & Tegmark, 2023). As an outlook, one can imagine automatically monitoring
461   changes across a multitude of prompts on different topics and testing different abilities at scale,
462   without the need to design individual metrics for each prompt.

      4    R ELATED W ORKS
465
466
      Before concluding, let us discuss how our method relates to other approaches for studying phase
467
      transitions in physics and in LLMs.
468
469   Generic performance-based analysis. Many previous works found transitions in LLM behavior by
470   locating sharp changes in generic performance measures, such as sudden drops in training loss (Olsson
471   et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023). While this may capture transitions in the overall behavior, such an
472
      approach cannot resolve transitions in specific LLM behavior. In particular, it may miss algorithmic
      transitions where the same performance is reached but by different means (Zhong et al., 2023).
473
474   Prompt-specific success metrics. Other works have found transitions by looking at success metrics
475   tailored toward specific prompts (Austin et al., 2021; Brown et al., 2020; Hendrycks et al., 2020;
476   Radford et al., 2021; Srivastava et al., 2022; Wei et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2021). Recalling the example
477   studied in Sec. 3.1, this would correspond to assigning a score of 1 if the LLM provided the correct
478
      answer to the question x < 42 and 0 otherwise. Similarly, one could compute such a score in a
      temporal analysis (Sec. 3.3) or for detecting transitions as a function of another hyperparameter
479
      (Sec. 3.2). A downside of this approach is that it is restricted to prompts that allow for a clear score
480
      to be assigned. In particular, choosing an appropriate scoring function may require lots of human
481   engineering. Moreover, discontinuous metrics can artificially induce transitions where the underlying
482   behavior varies smoothly (Schaeffer et al., 2023) 3 . Similarly, they may miss transitions where the
483   same performance is reached but by different means (Zhong et al., 2023).
484
         3
485        While spurious transitions can still occur with our method due to sampling noise, the metric we utilize is
      continuous and does not exhibit thresholding effects.


                                                            9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      Measures based on model internals. The aforementioned approaches are based on the model output.
487   Many works have also detected transitions based on changes in the internal structure of models,
488   such as its trainable parameters (Millidge; Chen et al., 2023) (similar to the weight-based analysis
489   we have performed in Sec. 3.3). However, access to model internals may not always be available.
490   Moreover, the design of measures that capture specific transitions in behavior requires lots of human
491   input (Räuker et al., 2023; Conmy et al., 2023; Zhong et al., 2023), e.g., using insights from the field
492   of mechanistic interpretability.
493
494   5    C ONCLUSION AND O UTLOOK
495
496   We have proposed a method for automating the detection of phase transitions in LLMs, and demon-
497   strated that it successfully reveals a variety of transitions. Leveraging access to the LLMs’ next-token
498   probability distributions, the proposed dissimilarity measures can efficiently quantify distribution
499   shifts without fine-tuning or adaption to the specific scenario at hand. Because the method is solely
500   based on analyzing a model’s output distribution and access to the model weights is not required, it
501   enables black-box interpretability studies.
502   The proposed method is not only applicable to language models, but can be straightforwardly
503   adapted to any generative model with an explicit, tractable density (Goodfellow, 2016; Arnold et al.,
504   2023b). If one can draw samples from the output distribution but does not have explicit access to the
505   underlying probabilities, then the dissimilarity measures can still be approximated using NN-based
506   classifiers (Menon & Ong, 2016; Arnold et al., 2023a) tailored toward the particular data type, such
507   as natural language.
508
509   Limitations. Future large-scale investigations are needed to fully understand how the uncovered
510   transitions depend on variables such as the specific prompt, the number of generated output tokens,
511   or the selected model. In particular, due to computational resource constraints, the size of the studied
512
      language models has been limited.
513
      Broader Impact. Our method has the potential to enhance the development of future AI systems
514
      due to an improved understanding of their behavior. The dual-use nature of such systems carries
515
      inherent risks, which requires one to proceed with caution and implement mechanisms to ensure they
516   are used safely and ethically.

      D.1    P HASE T RANSITIONS IN P HYSICS
926
927   The most common definition of a phase transition in physics has been given through the Ehrenfest
928   classification, which demands that the nth derivative of the free energy must have a discontinuity in
929   the thermodynamic limit of an infinitely large system size. There are, however, notable exceptions
930   to this definition, such as the Kosterlitz-Thouless transition, which is of infinite order and involves
931   topological order. Moreover, this classification requires the identification of free energy and is thus
932   restricted to the context of equilibrium systems.
933   Besides phase transitions, there are also less stringent, related phenomena like crossovers, which do
934   not require strict discontinuities. As such, even in physics, the definitions of phase transitions are
935   being debated and have to be reevaluated when venturing into novel domains.
936
      In the context of LLMs, the term “phase transition” is often used rather colloquially to draw an
937
      analogy to the physical phenomenon. For the present article, we have used the loose but pragmatic
938   definition of a phase transition referring to a rapid change of the system’s state as the tuning parameter
939   is varied.
940
941
      D.2    H EAT C APACITY
942
943   The heat capacity is the derivative of a system’s mean energy with respect to its temperature. In
944   non-physics terms, the mean energy can be understood as being proportional to the mean logarithmic
945   probability of the system’s state. Because it can be related to derivatives of the free energy, or
946   similarly, to derivatives of the underlying probability distribution, divergences in this quantity are
947   signatures of phase transitions. In a physical equilibrium setting, it is also closely related to the Fisher
948
      information.
949
950   D.3    I SING AND P OTTS M ODELS
951
      A very prominent example of a phase transition relevant to the present work is the Ising model phase
952
      transition in one dimension. The Ising model is better known as a Boltzmann machine      or Hopfield
953
                                                                                         P
      network in the machine learning community and described by the energy E = − ij wij si sj in its
954   simplest form. If the variables s have more than two possible states, this becomes a Potts model.
955
956   In one dimension and as a function of temperature, such systems can undergo phase transitions in the
957
      Ehrenfest sense involving a discontinuity only if the range of the interaction matrix w is infinite. A
      language model’s output can be viewed as a one-dimensional system of tokens whose interaction
958
      with each other is mediated by the attention mechanism. In particular, the correspondence between
959
      self-attention and Potts models has recently been formalized in (Rende et al., 2024).
960
961   Note that the interaction only occurs in the forward direction for auto-regressive models and is of
962   finite range due to the finite context length of current models. While this cannot give rise to a strict
963
      discontinuity, rapid changes qualify for our weaker definition of a phase transition (or a crossover),
      and by scaling the system size appropriately one may observe similar critical behavior as in physics.
