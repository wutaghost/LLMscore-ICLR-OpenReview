009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Diffusion models represent a powerful family of generative models widely used
012            for image and video generation. However, the time-consuming deployment, long
013            inference time, and requirements on large memory hinder their applications on
014            resource constrained devices. In this paper, we propose a method based on the
015            improved Straight-Through Estimator to improve the deployment efficiency of
016            diffusion models. Specifically, we add sparse masks to the Convolution and Linear
017            layers in a pre-trained diffusion model, then transfer learn the sparse model during
018
               the fine-tuning stage and turn on the sparse masks during inference. Experimental
               results on a Transformer and UNet-based diffusion models demonstrate that our
019
               method reduces MACs by 50% while increasing FID by only 0.44 on average.
020
               Sparse models are accelerated by approximately 1.2x on the GPU. Under other
021            MACs conditions, the FID is also lower than 1 compared to other methods.

024   1   I NTRODUCTION
025
026   Diffusion Models (DM) (Song et al., 2021; Karras et al., 2022) have been one of the core gen-
027   erative modules in various computer vision tasks. Generally, they are composed of two parts: a
028   forward/diffusion process that perturbs the data distribution to learn the time-dependent score func-
029   tions, and a reverse/sampling process that generates data samples from a prior distribution in an
030   iterative manner. Though diffusion models have advantages on both sample quality and mode cov-
031   erage over other competitors, their slow inference speed and heavy computational load during the
032   inference process inevitably restrict their applications on most mobile devices.
033   To reduce the computational load in the inference process of diffusion models, various methods have
034   been proposed to minimize the number of inference steps, such as the training-free samplers (Bao
035   et al., 2022; Zhao et al., 2023; Lu et al., 2022; 2023; Zheng et al., 2023) and the distillation methods
036   (Salimans & Ho, 2022; Luo et al., 2023b;a). However, their sample quality is still unsatisfactory
037   as a few sampling steps cannot faithfully reconstruct the high-dimensional data space, e.g., the
038   image or video samples. This problem is even more pronounced on mobile devices with limited
039
      computing capabilities. Simultaneously, a few works explore reducing the Multiple-Accumulate
      operations (MACs) at each inference step (Bolya & Hoffman, 2023; Fang et al., 2023b). However,
040
      their work cannot be accelerated on GPUs. Since DM is an intensive model parameter calculation,
041
      NVIDIA Ampere architecture GPU supports a 2:4 sparse (4-weights contain 2 non-zero values)
042   model calculation, which can achieve nearly 2 times calculation acceleration (Pool & Yu, 2021;
043   Mishra et al., 2021). Although structural pruning (Fang et al., 2023b) has been used in DM, 2:4
044   structured sparsity inference has not been implemented. Our method aims to reduce MACs at each
045   step through 2:4 and other scale sparsity.
046
      Recently, several popular computational architectures, such as NVIDIA Ampere architecture and
047
      Hopper GPUs, have developed acceleration methods for model inference and have been equipped
048   with fine-grained structured sparse capabilities. A common requirement of these acceleration tech-
049   niques is the 2:4 sparse mode, which only preserves 2 of the 4 adjacent weights of a pre-trained
050   model, i.e., requires a sparsity rate of 50%. Given this sparsity, the acceleration techniques only pro-
051   cess the non-zero values in matrix multiplications, theoretically achieving a 2x speedup. In essence,
052   a diffusion model supporting the 2:4 sparsity mode can reduce 50% computational load at each in-
053   ference step, which is valuable when considering the iterative refinement process in a generation. To
      the best of our knowledge, previous works have not explored maintaining the sample quality of dif-

094   The diffusion model is divided into a forward process and a backward process. The forward process
095   is a step-by-step process of adding noise to the original image to generate a noisy image, generally
096   formalized as a Markov chain process. The reverse process is to remove noise from a noisy image
097
      and restore the original image as much as possible. Gaussian mode was adopted to approximate
      the ground truth reverse transition of the Markov chain. The training process of diffusion models is
098
      the process of establishing noise prediction models. The loss function of DM is minimizing a noise
099
      prediction objective. The forward process is formalized as:

116   Sparse network computation is an effective method to reduce MACs in deep networks and accelerate
117   computation. Currently, one-shot sparse training and progressive sparse training are commonly used.
118   The one-shot training method is easy to use, and the steps are as follows (Mishra et al., 2021; Pool &
119   Yu, 2021): Train a regular dense model. Prune the weights on the fully connected and convolutional
120   layers in a 2:4 sparse mode. Retrain the pruned model. The one-shot pruning is expressed as:
121
                                                 W
                                                 f = W ⊙ M,                                              (3)
122
123   where, Wf is the sparse weight after the dense weight W is pruned, recorded as W
                                                                                     f ← Pruning(W),
124   M is a 0-1 mask, M is the (N :M ) sparse mask (M -weights contain N non-zero values), ⊙ repre-
125
      sents element-wise multiplication.
126   One-shot sparse pruning can cover most tasks and achieve speedup without losing accuracy. How-
127   ever, for some challenging tasks that are sensitive to changes in weight values, doing sparse training
128   for all weights at once will result in a large amount of information loss (Han et al., 2015). With the
129   same number of tuning iterations, progressive sparse training can achieve higher model accuracy
130   than one-shot sparse training. Suppose there are k masks [M1 , M2 , ..., Mk ], the progressive sparse
131
      process is formalized as:
132                                W

133   The reason why traditional progressive sparse training methods work well is to reuse the knowl-
134   edge of dense models as much as possible. However, this progressive sparse training method has
135   only proven effective when the training data distribution is stable, such as when training a CNN
136   classification model.

138   2.3   D ISTRIBUTION S HIFT ON DM T RAINING AND S PARSE P RUNING
139
140   Robust Fairness Regularization (RFR) (Jiang et al., 2024) improved that distribution shift can be
141   transformed as data perturbation, and data perturbation and model weight perturbation are equivalent
142
      for classifier models. Assuming that the perturbation of the label is not considered, the equivalent
      of data perturbation and model weight perturbation is formalized as:
143
144                   EδD (D) E(D)∼P [L(FW (D + δD (D))] = E(D)∼P [L(FW+∆W (D))],                        (5)
145    where the training dataset D with distribution P, suppose the training dataset is perturbed with data
146   perturbation δ, and the neural network is given by FW (·), for the general case, there exists model
147   weight pruned as perturbation ∆W, so that the training loss L on perturbed training dataset is the
148   same with that for model weight perturbation ∆W on training distribution.
149   The training process of DMs generally involves only the distribution shift of the noisy data. We
150   believe that DM training adds Gaussian noise to the data, which perturbs the original image data
151   distribution. Existing sparse pruning methods generally do not consider the distribution shift of
152   noisy data but only the model’s weight changes. Inspired by RFR’s conclusions, we convert the
153   distribution shifts caused by changes in model weights into distribution shifts caused by data changes
154   for the DM’s training process. The DM’s sparse ratio is fixed, and then the knowledge of the dense
155   model is transferred to the sparse model.

409   4.3          A BLATION S TUDY
410
411   Our method involves performing sparse mask fine-tuning on the existing trained model. Masks need
412   to be trained during the training process. We designed three ablation experiments to demonstrate
413   better our method design’s rationality and the necessity of each step. The first one is the STE-based
414   method. The second one is traditional progressive sparse training of DM. The last one involves
415   fixing the mask during the training process and not performing sparse training.
416   STE Sparse Training. As shown in Fig. 3(d), on datasets CIFAR10 and CelebA64, the learning
417   curve of STE converges significantly slower than our method, mainly because our sparse model is
418   trained with knowledge transfer. As shown in Fig. 3(e), STE’s results are worse than ours, mainly
419   because our method adds sparse mask information during back-propagation. At high compression
420   ratios, such as 1:32, transfer learn sparse masks also plays an important role.
421   Traditional Progressive Sparse Training. As shown in Fig. 3(b), DM is trained on dataset CI-
422   FAR10. Training after switching sparse masks for the first time is almost ineffective. Every 100,000
423   training steps, the sparsity of masks is increased for progressive sparse training. The traditional pro-
424   gressive sparse training does not work well on the diffusion model, especially when switching the
425   sparsity rate every time. It is equivalent to training stopped. However, the fixed sparse mask can be
426   continuously trained until convergence. This comparison shows that simultaneously transforming
427   the data and model distribution will fail DM training, prompting us to propose a new method for
428
      gradually sparsely training DMs.
429   Sparse Mask Inference. Unlike our trained masks, we generate untrained sparse masks for sparse
430   pruning diffusion models. Dense model weights are imported into the sparse model, and the sparse
431   model is not trained. As shown in Fig. 3(f), on dataset CIFAR10, the untrained mask’s result is
      the worst at all MACs conditions because the mask did not participate in training. The STE-trained

      5.1   N: M S PARSITY
439
440
      A neural network with N :M sparsity (M -weights contains N non-zero values) satisfies that, in
441   each group of M consecutive weights of the network, there are at most N weights have non-zero
442   values (Zhou et al., 2021; Chmiel et al., 2022). APEX’s Automatic SParsity (ASP) (Pool & Yu,
443   2021; Mishra et al., 2021)) is a 2:4 sparse tool provided by NVIDIA. This tool obtains a 2:4 sparse
444   network and can achieve nearly 2x runtime speedup on NVIDIA Ampere and Hopper architecture
445   GPUs (Nvidia, 2020). A provable and efficient method for finding N: M transposed masks for
446   accelerating sparse neural training (Hubara et al., 2021). Compared with dense networks, sparse
447   network training has gradient changes, and methods such as STE (Bengio et al., 2013) should be
448   used to improve training performance. In the forward stage, sparse weight is obtained by pruning
449
      dense weight. In the backward stage, the gradient w.r.t. sparse weight will be directly applied to
      dense weight. SR-STE (Zhang et al., 2022) enhances the backward pass information by integrating
450
      the sparse weight of the forward pass into the backward pass. DominoSearch (Sun et al., 2021)
451
      found mixed N: M sparsity schemes from pre-trained dense, deep neural networks to achieve higher
452   accuracy than the uniform-sparsity scheme with equivalent complexity constraints. The N: M sparse
453   method is also used in SparseGPT (Frantar & Alistarh, 2023) to accelerate the LLM generation
454   model GPT. Although many N: M sparse methods exist, none have been proven effective for sparse
455   DM.
456
457
458
      5.2   D IFFUSION M ODEL P RUNING
459
      Pruning is one of the most used methods to reduce the calculation time of DNN, including DM. The
460
      pruning method was divided into structured pruning and unstructured pruning. Structural pruning
461
      aims to physically remove a group of parameters, thereby reducing the size of neural networks. In
462   contrast, unstructured pruning involves zeroing out specific weights without altering the network
463   structure (Fang et al., 2023a). Sparsity can reduce the memory footprint of DM to fit mobile devices
464   and shorten training time for ever-growing networks (Hoefler et al., 2021). Pay attention to features
465   and selection of useful features for the target dataset (Wang et al., 2020), which can also reduce
466   computational complexity. The parameter-grouping patterns vary widely across different models,
467   making architecture-specific pruners, which rely on manually designed grouping schemes, non-
468   generalizable to new architectures. Depgraph (Fang et al., 2023a) can tackle general structural
469   pruning of arbitrary architecture. Structured sparsity was also used for large language models (Ma
470
      et al., 2023; Frantar & Alistarh, 2023; Guo et al., 2023). Because Transformer has been proven to
      outperform the other networks in multiple applications, including DM. ToMe (Bolya & Hoffman,
471
      2023) merge redundant tokens of Transformer. LD-Pruner (Castells et al., 2024) and P-ESD (Yang
472
      et al., 2024) prune stable diffusion for specific tasks. There are also proposals for early exiting for
473   accelerated inference in DM (Moon et al., 2023). Although there are many pruning methods for
474   DM, either the sparse model FID is poor or structured 2:4 sparsity is not implemented for general
475   base DM.

479
480   In this paper, we studied how to improve the efficiency of DM by sparse matrix for 2:4 sparse
481   acceleration GPU. The existing STE-based methods make it difficult to optimize sparse DM. To
482   address this issue, we improve the STE method and propose to gradually transfer knowledge from
483   dense models to sparse models. Our method is tested on the latest Transformer-based DM, U-ViT,
484   and UNet-based DM, DDPM. We trained the 2:4 and other sparse models to perform better than
485   other methods. Our approach also provides an effective solution for DM deployment on NVIDIA
      Ampere architecture GPUs, achieving about 1.2x acceleration.

      A.3   T HE RELATIONSHIP BETWEEN DIFFUSION TRAINING AND SPARSE TRAINING
541
542   As shown in Fig. 3(b) of this paper, from the empirical experimental results, it is observed that fixed
543   sparsity applies a consistent distribution shift for all noise levels in diffusion training, while pro-
544   gressive sparsity training gradually shifts the predefined noise level, which may hinder the diffusion
545   training process.
546   In theory, the relationship between diffusion training and sparse training is mainly explained from
547   the perspective of the difficulty of convergence of the SGD optimizer. Existing optimizers are de-
548   signed for diffusion training and sparse training, respectively, and the design of each optimizer is
549   challenging. SGD takes the current optimal gradient direction each time it descends, so when using
550   stochastic gradient descent training, usually only one distribution shift is optimized. However, if
551   there are two distribution shifts, the SGD gradient descent direction may not be the current opti-
552   mal one. Therefore, if two distribution shifts are optimized at the same time, such as switching the
553   sparsity rate when training DM, the optimization will fail.
