009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011            Solving operator eigenvalue problems helps analyze intrinsic data structures and
012            relationships, yielding substantial influence on scientific research and engineer-
013            ing applications. Recently, novel approaches based on deep learning have been
014            proposed to obtain eigenvalues and eigenfunctions from the given operator, which
015            address the efficiency challenge arising from traditional numerical methods. How-
016
               ever, when solving top-L eigenvalues problems, these learning-based methods
               ignore the information that could be inherited from other known eigenvectors,
017
               thus resulting in a less-than-ideal performance. To address the challenge, we pro-
018
               pose the Spectral Refined Network (SReNet). Our novel approach incorporates
019            the power method to approximate the top-L eigenvalues and their corresponding
020            eigenfunctions. To effectively prevent convergence to previous eigenfunctions,
021            we introduce the Deflation Projection that significantly improves the orthogonal-
022            ity of the computed eigenfunctions and enables more precise prediction of multi-
023            ple eigenfunctions simultaneously. Furthermore, we develop the adaptive filtering
024            method that dynamically leverages intermediate approximate eigenvalues to con-
025            struct rational filters that filter out predicted eigenvalues, when predicting the suc-
026            cessive eigenvalue of the given problem. During the iterative solving, the spectral
027
               transformation is performed based on the filter function, converting the original
               eigenvalue problem into an equivalent problem that is easier to converge. Exten-
028
               sive experiments demonstrate that our approach consistently outperforms existing
029
               learning-based methods, achieving state-of-the-art performance in accuracy.

032   1   I NTRODUCTION
033
034   The operator eigenvalue problem is a prominent focus in many scientific fields (Elhareef & Wu,
035   2023; Buchan et al., 2013; Cuzzocrea et al., 2020; Pfau et al., 2023) and engineering applications
036   (Diao et al., 2023; Chen & Chan, 2000), where eigenvalues are commonly used to analyze the
037   fundamental geometric structures and relationships within data (Markovsky, 2012; Blum et al.,
038   2020). Traditional methods that solve operator eigenvalue problems typically involve two steps.
039
      First, they apply numerical discretization methods to transform the operator into a matrix, such as
      Finite Element Methods (FEM) (LeVeque, 2002). Then numerical linear algebra techniques are em-
040
      ployed to solve the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of the given matrix, utilizing methods like Krylov-
041
      Shur (Watkins, 2007; Liesen & Strakos, 2013) and the Locally Optimal Block Preconditioned Con-
042   jugate Gradient (LOBPCG) algorithm (Knyazev, 2001). These methods iteratively generate a sub-
043   space that approximates the invariant subspace of the matrix, allowing the original problem to be
044   solved within this subspace. However, traditional numerical methods are constrained by the curse of
045   dimensionality, as the computational complexity increases quadratically or even cubically with the
046   size of the matrix (Watkins, 2007). Furthermore, storing the iterative subspaces incurs significant
047   memory requirements when solving high-dimensional problems (Stewart, 2002).
048   A promising alternative is using neural networks to approximate eigenfunctions (Pfau et al., 2018).
049   These approaches replace the matrix representations with parametric nonlinear representations
050   through neural networks. By designing appropriate loss functions, it updates parameters to approx-
051   imate the desired operator eigenfunctions. These methods only require sampling specific regions
052   without designing discretization grids, significantly reducing the algorithm design cost and helping
053   mitigate unwanted approximation errors (He et al., 2022). Moreover, the parametric representation
      of neural networks offers stronger expressive power than linear matrix representations, requiring

      Inspired by the power methods (Golub & Van Loan, 2013), we propose a novel method, namely
079
      Spectral Refined Network (SReNet), that uses the neural network to predict the top-L eigenvalues
080   and their eigenfunctions. SReNet takes the coordinates of sampling points as the input, while the
081   outputs of SReNet are the eigenvalues and their eigenfunctions. SReNet employs the power method
082   loss function as the guidance for iterative optimization. To prevent the predicted eigenfunctions
083   from converging towards known invariant subspaces, we introduce the deflation projection into the
084   loss function, which enhances the orthogonality and accuracy of the solved eigenfunctions. Fur-
085   thermore, we develop adaptive filter techniques that utilize intermediate approximate eigenvalues
086   to construct rational filters for transforming operator forms during the solution process, optimizing
087   their spectral distribution (eigenvalue distribution) for higher solving efficiency. Extensive exper-
088

      achieving state-of-the-art performance.

090   In summary, our contributions are as follows:
091
092          • We introduce a novel learning-based method based on power method concepts for solving
093            the top-L eigenvalues and their eigenfunctions of the differential operator.
094          • We employ the deflation projection that prevents convergence towards known invariant sub-
095            spaces, enhancing the multi-eigenvalue problem-solving. We also develop adaptive rational
096            filter techniques that utilize intermediate eigenvalues to accelerate the solution process and
097            achieve higher solving efficiency.
098

099            achieving state-of-the-art precision across different operator eigenvalue problems.

131   The power method is commonly used as the iterative algorithm for computing the largest eigen-
132   value and its corresponding eigenvector of the given matrix, particularly well-suited for large sparse

134   verge towards the largest eigenvector (Watkins, 2007). At the beginning, power method starts from a
135   random and non-zero initial vector x(0) , and update the vector through the following iterative steps:
136

141            defined number of iterations is reached.

      2.3   D EFLATION P ROJECTION
163
164   The deflation technique plays a critical role in solving eigenvalue problems, particularly when deal-
165   ing with large-scale matrices or when multiple distinct eigenvalues need to be computed. Deflation
166   projection is an effective deflation strategy that utilizes known eigenvalues and corresponding eigen-
167   functions to modify the structure of the matrix, thereby simplifying the computation of remaining
168   eigenvalues (Saad, 2011; Kressner, 2005; Watkins, 2007; Arbenz et al., 2012).
169   The essence of deflation projection lies in constructing a projection matrix P , typically v1 v1⊤ , where
170   v1 is a known eigenvector. This projection matrix allows to modify the original matrix A to a
171   new matrix B = A − λ1 P . In matrix B, the eigenvalue λ1 corresponding to eigenvector v1 is
172   effectively removed (or set to zero) from A. We provide more details about deflation projection in
173   the Appendix B.2.
174
175   2.4   F ILTERING T ECHNIQUE
176
177
      The filtering technique is employed in numerical linear algebra to accelerate the solution of eigen-
      value problems for large matrices (Saad, 2011). The core of this technique involves constructing
178
      suitable filter functions F (A) to achieve a spectral transformation of the matrix A. This spectral
179
      transformation helps optimize the spectral distribution of the matrix without altering the eigenvec-
180   tors, thereby making the target eigenvalues more prominent in the transformed spectrum and easier
181   to obtain (Watkins, 2007; Li et al., 2019).
182
183
      The spectral transformation essentially applies a function transformation to the matrix. These func-
      tions, such as polynomials or rational functions, are designed to amplify the important part of the
184
      matrix (the eigenvalues we care about) and suppress the unnecessary ones (Fang & Saad, 2012;
185
      Winkelmann et al., 2019), thus we call it ”filtering”. The filter reduces the influence of irrelevant
186   eigenvalues, making it easier to converge on the target eigenvalues (Miao, 2019; Miao & Wu, 2021).
187   We provide more details about filtering technique in the Appendix B.3.

      In this paper, we introduced SReNet, a learning-based method designed for solving operator eigen-
526
      value problems. Our experiments demonstrate that SReNet achieves the highest accuracy compared
527

528   several key areas worth mentioning: 1. Algorithm optimization for specific operator structures, such
529   as selecting more suitable deflation projections and filter functions based on the structure of the op-

531   on boundary conditions and residual distribution. 3. Integrating other computational mathematics
532   techniques to optimize the iterative process, such as incorporating matrix preconditioning technolo-
533   gies. We believe that neural network-based algorithms for solving operator eigenvalue problems
534   hold tremendous potential for real-world applications and represent a crucial direction for future
535   development.

          A     R ELATED W ORK
757
758
          Recent advancements in applying neural networks to eigenvalue problems have shown promising
759
          results. Innovations such as Spectral Inference Networks (SpIN) (Pfau et al., 2018), which models
760
          eigenvalue problems as kernel problem optimizations solved via neural networks. Neural Eigenfunc-
761       tions (NeuralEF) (Deng et al., 2022), which significantly reduces computational costs by optimizing
762       the costly orthogonalization steps, are noteworthy. Neural Singular Value Decomposition (Neu-
763       ralSVD) employs truncated singular value decomposition for low-rank approximation to enhance
764       the orthogonality required in learning functions (Ryu et al., 2024).
765
          Another class of algorithms originates from optimizing the Rayleigh quotient. The Deep Ritz
766
          Method (DRM) utilizes the Rayleigh quotient for computing the smallest eigenvalues, demonstrating
767       significant potential (Yu et al., 2018). Several studies have employed the Rayleigh quotient to con-
768       struct variation-free functions, achieved through Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) (Ben-
769       Shaul et al., 2023; 2020). Extensions of this approach include enhanced loss functions with reg-
770       ularization terms to improve the learning accuracy of the smallest eigenvalues (Jin et al., 2022).
771       Additionally, Han et al. (2020) reformulate the eigenvalue problem as a fixed-point problem of the
772       semigroup flow induced by the operator, solving it using the diffusion Monte Carlo method. The
773       Power Method Neural Network (PMNN) integrates the power method with PINNs, using an iter-
774       ative process to approximate the exact eigenvalues (Yang et al., 2023) closely. While PMNN has
775
          proven effective in solving for a single eigenvalue (Yang et al., 2023), it has yet to be developed for
          computing multiple distinct eigenvalues simultaneously.
776
777       Furthermore, in the field of computational chemistry, research on specialized model architec-
778       tures for specific operators, such as the Hamiltonian, focuses on developing novel neural network
779       ansatzes (Carleo & Troyer, 2017; Schütt et al., 2017; Choo et al., 2020; Pfau et al., 2020; Hermann
780       et al., 2020; Gerard et al., 2022; Hermann et al., 2023). These architectures are designed to embed
781
          physical inductive biases better, enhancing expressivity. Additionally, there are studies employing
          neural networks for Quantum Monte Carlo (QMC) methods to tackle related problems in quantum
782
          chemistry (Cuzzocrea et al., 2020; Entwistle et al., 2023; Pfau et al., 2023). Operators in specific
783
          scientific domains often exhibit unique structures and are associated with prior knowledge. One
784       of our future research directions is to optimize our algorithm based on this information to achieve
785       better performance in these tasks.

      B.2    D EFLATION P ROJECTION D ETAILS
835
       Consider the scenario where we have determined the largest modulus eigenvalue, λ1 , and its cor-
836   responding eigenvector, v1 , utilizing an algorithm such as the power method. These algorithms
837   consistently identify the eigenvalue of the largest modulus from the given matrix along with an as-
838   sociated eigenvector. We ensure that the vector v1 is normalized such that ∥v1 ∥2 = 1. The task
839   then becomes computing the subsequent eigenvalue, λ2 , of the matrix A. A traditional approach to
840   address this is through what is commonly known as a deflation procedure. This technique involves
841   a rank-one modification to the original matrix, aimed at shifting the eigenvalue λ1 while preserving
842   all other eigenvalues intact. The modification is designed in such a way that λ2 emerges as the
843   eigenvalue with the largest modulus in the adjusted matrix. Consequently, the power method can be
844
      reapplied to this updated matrix to extract the eigenvalue-eigenvector pair λ2 , v2 .
845   When the invariant subspace requiring deflation is one-dimensional, consider the following Propo-
846   sition 1. The propositions and proofs below are derived from Saad (2011) P90.
847   Proposition 1. Let v1 be an eigenvector of A of norm 1, associated with the eigenvalue λ1 and let
848   A1 ≡ A − σv1 v1H . Then the eigenvalues of A1 are λ̃1 = λ1 − σ and λ̃j = λj , j = 2, 3, . . . , n.
849   Moreover, the Schur vectors associated with λ̃j , j = 1, 2, 3, . . . , n are identical with those of A.
850
851   Proof. Let AV = V R be the Schur factorization of A, where R is upper triangular and V is
852   orthonormal. Then we have
853

854                                                        1 = V [R − σe1 e1 ].

855   Here, e1 is the first standard basis vector. The result follows immediately.
856
857   According to Proposition 1, once the eigenvalue λ1 and eigenvector v1 are known, we can define
858   the deflation projection matrix P1 = I − λ1 v1 v1⊤ to compute the remaining eigenvalues and eigen-
859   vectors.
860   When deflating with multiple vectors, let q1 , q2 , . . . , qj be a set of Schur vectors associated with the
861   eigenvalues λ1 , λ2 , . . . , λj . We denote by Qj the matrix of column vectors q1 , q2 , . . . , qj . Thus,
862   Qj ≡ [q1 , q2 , . . . , qj ] is an orthonormal matrix whose columns form a basis of the eigenspace
863   associated with the eigenvalues λ1 , λ2 , . . . , λj . An immediate generalization of Proposition 1 is the
      following (Saad, 2011) P94.

885   B.3    F ILTERING T ECHNIQUE
886
887   The primary objective of filtering techniques is to manipulate the eigenvalue distribution of a matrix
888   through spectral transformations (Saad, 2011). This enhances specific eigenvalues of interest, facil-
889   itating their recognition and computation by iterative solvers. Filter transformation functions, F (x),
890   typically fall into two categories:
891
            1. Polynomial Filters, expressed as P (x), such as the Chebyshev filter (Miao & Wu, 2021;
892
               Banerjee et al., 2016).
893
894         2. Rational Function Filters, often denoted as P (x)/Q(x), such as the shift-invert
895
               method (Van Beeumen, 2015; Watkins, 2007). Below we describe this strategy in detail.
896
897
      Shift-Invert Strategy The shift-invert strategy applies the transformation (A−σI)−1 to the matrix
      A, where σ is a scalar approximating a target eigenvalue, termed as shift. This operation transforms
898                                1
      each eigenvalue λ of A into λ−σ , amplifying those eigenvalues close to σ in the transformed matrix,
899
      making them larger and more distinguishable (Watkins, 2007).
900
901   For instance, consider the power method, where the convergence rate is primarily governed by the
902   ratio of the matrix’s largest modulus eigenvalue to its second largest. Suppose matrix A has three
903   principal eigenvalues: λ1 = 10, λ2 = 3, and λ3 = 2. Our objective is to compute λ1 , the largest
904
      eigenvalue. In the original matrix A, the convergence rate of the power method hinges on the spectral
      gap ratio, defined as:
