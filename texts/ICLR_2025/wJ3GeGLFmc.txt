009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Neural network training is a memory- and compute-intensive task. Quantization,
012            which enables low-bitwidth formats in training, can significantly mitigate the work-
013            load. To reduce quantization error, recent methods have developed new data formats
014            and additional pre-processing operations on quantizers. However, it remains quite
015            challenging to achieve high accuracy and efficiency simultaneously. In this paper,
016            we explore sub-8-bit integer training from its essence of gradient descent optimiza-
017            tion. Our integer training framework includes two components: ShiftQuant to
018
               realize accurate gradient estimation, and L1 normalization to smoothen the loss
               landscape. ShiftQuant attains performance that approaches the theoretical upper
019
               bound of group quantization. Furthermore, it liberates group quantization from inef-
020
               ficient memory rearrangement. The L1 normalization facilitates the implementation
021            of fully quantized normalization layers with impressive convergence accuracy. Our
022            method frees sub-8-bit integer training from pre-processing and supports general
023            devices. This framework achieves negligible accuracy loss across various neural
024            networks and tasks (0.92% on 4-bit ResNets, 0.61% on 6-bit Transformers). The
025            prototypical implementation of ShiftQuant achieves more than 1.85 × /15.3%
026            performance improvement on CPU/GPU compared to its FP16 counterparts, and
027            33.9% resource consumption reduction on FPGA than the FP16 counterparts. The
028            proposed fully-quantized L1 normalization layers achieve more than 35.54% im-
029
               provement in throughout on CPU compared to traditional L2 normalization layers.
               Moreover, theoretical analysis verifies the advancement of our method.

      1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034
      Recently, deep neural networks have made significant advancements in various tasks. However, this
035
      progress comes at the cost of high computational complexity. High energy cost and computational
036   resource consumption hamper the deployment of these deep learning applications on both edge-
037   device and high-end cloud servers. While existing studies primarily concentrate on minimizing
038   resource consumption during inference Jacob et al. (2018); Li et al. (2019); Uhlich et al. (2019);
039   Liu et al. (2022); Lin et al. (2020); He et al. (2019); Fang et al. (2023), it should be noted that the
040   computational complexity of training is approximately three times that of inference Banner et al.
041   (2018). Consequently, there is an urgent demand for efficient training methods.
042   Reducing the bitwidth of data is a reasonable approach. The resource consumption of a neural
043   network scales almost linearly with the bitwidth of data Zhou et al. (2016). However, low-precision
044   training faces two challenges. The first is the wide range of gradients. Very few outliers extremely
045   expand the data range, resulting in high quantization error Sun et al. (2020); Xi et al. (2023); Chen
046   et al. (2020). The second is the sharp loss landscape of low-precision networks Cacciola et al. (2023).
047   Sharp local minimal points disrupt optimization significantly Foret et al. (2020); Li et al. (2018).
048   To address the challenge of wide gradient range and significant quantization errors, common ap-
049   proaches include (1) employing a non-uniform quantization format Sun et al. (2020); Cambier et al.
050   (2020); Zhong et al. (2022), (2) utilizing smaller quantization granularity, or (3) suppressing outliers
051   before quantization Xi et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2020). However, all methods encounter practicality
052   issues, as they cannot be easily adapted to existing General Matrix Multiply (GEMM) software and
053   hardware implementations or extremely exacerbate the computation burden (detailed discussion
      in Sec. 2). To this end, we analyze the structure of outliers in gradients and develop an efficient

054
      and effective quantizer, ShiftQuant. With strategic and structural grouping of channels, ShiftQuant
055   achieves excellent outlier suppression at a negligible cost, and also supports GEMM.
056
057   Recent methods focus on developing new quantizers but neglect the impact of sharp loss landscape
058
      Xi et al. (2023); Chen et al. (2020); Sun et al. (2020); Fu et al. (2021); Wu et al. (2022); Das et al.
      (2018); Banner et al. (2018); Chmiel et al. (2021); Ghaffari et al. (2022). The sharp landscape of low-
059
      precision networks brings more local minimal points and leads to unstable convergence. Moreover,
060
      sharp curvature demands more bits to specify gradients Dong et al. (2019); Foret et al. (2020). In
061   this paper, we reveal that the source of the sharp landscape lies in the limited smoothening and
062   quantization-tolerated capacity of the normalization layers Ioffe & Szegedy (2015); Ba et al. (2016);
063   Ulyanov et al. (2016); Wu & He (2018). Stronger smoothening comes from stronger regularization.
064   In this paper, we introduce the stronger regularization, L1 normalization, into normalization layers,
065   which achieves clearly smooth loss landscape under less computation.
066
      Our main contributions include the following:
067
068   By analyzing the structure of outliers in gradients, we find that appropriately grouping channels can
069   effectively reduce quantization errors. Hence, we develop a new quantizer, ShiftQuant. ShiftQuant
070
      applies a smart grouping strategy that effectively approximates the optimal solution for grouping
      with minimal computational overhead. ShiftQuant utilizes the common low-bitwidth integer format
071
      and supports GEMM. Moreover, we develop a specific implementation of the new quantizer, which
072
      avoids the inefficient memory rearrangement in per-group quantization for the first time.
073
074   We experimentally analyze the source of the sharp loss landscape in low-precision networks. Quan-
075   tization weakens the smoothening effort of L2 normalization layers significantly. To this end, we
076
      develop L1 normalization layers, which achieves stronger smoothening with less computation.
077   We evaluate the proposed framework on various datasets. Our method achieves negligible perfor-
078   mance loss on ResNets, Transformers, GNN, and RNN under sub-8-bit integer training, respectively.
079   Furthermore, theoretical analysis substantiates the effectiveness of the new quantizer and normaliza-
080   tion layers.
081   Our prototypical implementation of ShiftQuant achieves more than 1.85 × /15.3% performance
082   improvement on CPU (ARMv8)/GPU (Nvidia RTX 3090) compared to Pytorch.fp16, and more
083   than 33.9% resource consumption reduction on FPGA (ZC706) compared to FP16. Moreover, 6-bit
084   ShiftQuant surpasses 4-bit integer training competitor in efficiency. The implementation of proposed
085   fully-quantized L1 normalization layers achieves more than 35.54% improvement of throughout on
086   CPU compared to traditional L2 normalization layers.

090   2.1   N EW Q UANTIZATION DATA F ORMATS
091
092   Developing new data formats with wide representing range reduces quantization loss significantly.
093   Sun et al. Sun et al. (2020) proposes a new fixed-point format using radix-4, which, unlike the
094   traditional radix-2 fixed-point format, offers a significantly larger representation range and higher
095   resolution. Fu et al. Fu et al. (2021) explores a dynamic data format with varying bit-widths to
096
      balance accuracy and efficiency by periodically adjusting the bit-width. Cambier et al. Cambier
      et al. (2020) proposes the S2FP8 format, where a vector is represented by an FP8 vector and two
097
      FP32 values, named squeeze and shift factors. Zhong et al. Zhong et al. (2022) develops the MLS
098
      tensor format, which balances the accuracy and computation efficiency in a tensor level. Although
099   the excellent accuracy, new data formats demand customer design of new hardware units, hindering
100   the deployment on contemporary devices inherently.

      3.1   S MART C HANNEL G ROUPING
163
164   As shown in Figure 2(c), the distribution of gradient varies among channel dimension extremely.
165   Channels with wide range expand the quantization range, leading to extremely fewer quantization
166   levels to represent channels with small range (as shown in Figure 2(b)). High quantization variance
167   comes from the diversity in channels’ ranges. Merging the gap between channel’s range can reduce
168   quantization error significantly. Grouping channels is a reasonable approach. Appropriately grouping
169   channels can minimize the diversity in each group. Then, per-group quantization can achieve low
170
      quantization variance and high efficiency at the same time.

      Implemented by GEMM
246
247   As illustrated in Figure 3(b), ShiftQuant divides the traditional matrix multiplication into n pairs
248   of smaller matrix multiplications. Implementing ShiftQuant through GEMM involves three steps.
249   Firstly, extracting the n pairs of smaller matrices, which requires memory rearrangement. Secondly,
250   employing GEMM to perform the n pairs of matrix multiplications. Finally, shifting and summing
251
      the results of these n multiplications. This approach achieves a good balance between efficiency and
      versatility. However, the memory rearrangement increases time consumption, and the smaller matrix
252
      multiplications do not fully capitalize on the advantages of GEMM.
253
254   Implemented without memory rearrangement
255   ShiftQuant essentially assigns different shift amounts to each index of the inner dimension. As
256   depicted in Figure 3(c), we can perform the shifting directly before the accumulation phase of the
257   vector multiplication. This technique offers two benefits. Firstly, it avoids memory rearrangement.
258   Secondly, it maintains the same level of parallelism as the original matrix multiplication. We refer to
259   this implementation method as ShiftMM. The implementation of ShiftMM just requires adding a
260   shifting operation after the multiplication step in the standard GEMM code.

      6   C ONCLUSION
523
524
      In this paper, we enhance sub-8-bit integer training from two aspects. We propose ShiftQuant to
525
      eliminate quantization noise in gradient estimation, and introduce fully-quantized L1 normalization
526
      layers to smoothen the loss landscape for stable convergence. Comprehensive experiments validate
527   the efficiency and accuracy of our method. Meanwhile, we have implemented ShiftQuant on multiple
528   types of devices and proven its applicability. The first future direction is to further improve accuracy
529   and efficiency, including developments in algorithms and implementation techniques. The second
530   future direction is to apply ShiftQuant to other tasks, such as inference acceleration of large language
531   models (LLMs). The excellent outlier suppression capacity of ShiftQuant may be useful for other
532   challenging quantization tasks.

1170   utilization of DSPs leads to the wastage of bit width (as shown in Figure 13). DSP reusing addresses
1171   this issue by packing multiple low-bitwidth data into a single data unit sized to the bit width. This
1172   approach enables the execution of multiple multiplications in a single computation cycle.
