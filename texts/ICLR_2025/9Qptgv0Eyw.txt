009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Ptychography is a computational method of microscopy that recovers high-
012            resolution transmission images of samples from a series of diffraction patterns.
013            While conventional phase retrieval algorithms can iteratively recover the images,
014            they require oversampled diffraction patterns, incur significant computational
015            costs, and struggle to recover the absolute phase of the sample’s transmission
016            function. Deep learning algorithms for ptychography are a promising approach
017            to resolving the limitations of iterative algorithms. We present PtychoFormer, a
018
               hierarchical transformer-based model for data-driven single-shot ptychographic
               phase retrieval. PtychoFormer processes subsets of diffraction patterns, generat-
019
               ing local inferences that are seamlessly stitched together to produce a high-quality
020
               reconstruction. Our model exhibits tolerance to sparsely scanned diffraction pat-
021            terns and achieves up to 3600 times faster imaging speed than the extended pty-
022            chographic iterative engine (ePIE). We also propose the extended-PtychoFormer
023            (ePF), a hybrid approach that combines the benefits of PtychoFormer with the
024            ePIE. ePF minimizes global phase shifts and significantly enhances reconstruc-
025            tion quality, achieving state-of-the-art phase retrieval in ptychography.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Microscopic and sub-microscopic imaging of tissue, cells, proteins, and crystals are a crucial tool
031   in biological and materials sciences. Optical microscopic imaging relies on expensive lenses and
032   capturing devices and is limited by the diffraction limit and lens aberrations. Furthermore, optical
033
      imaging requires high-contrast material, necessitating staining of transparent materials, making it
      impractical for live cell imaging. Coherent diffractive imaging (CDI) is a ”lensless” approach to
034
      imaging small, transparent objects by capturing downstream diffraction patterns free of lens aber-
035
      rations. Ptychography is a natural extension of CDI that captures high-resolution images by illu-
036   minating a sample at multiple scan points with a coherent light probe. The coherent source for
037   ptychography can be visible light, x-rays, or even electrons. Electron ptychography has captured
038   some of the highest-resolution atomic images ever recorded (Jiang et al., 2018).
039
      During ptychography, the sample is illuminated by the coherent probe at multiple scan points, and
040
      diffraction patterns of overlapping regions are captured. Since only the intensity of the interference
041
      can be captured downstream, losing the phase measurement, retrieving the sample’s image poses
042   a classic inverse problem. Phase retrieval algorithms, like the ptychographic iterative engine (PIE)
043   (Rodenburg & Faulkner, 2004) and its variants, use the diffraction patterns to iteratively recover the
044   sample’s image as a transmission function. This transmission function comprises of an amplitude,
045   representing the intensity distribution of the sample, and a phase, which carries information about
046   the sample’s refractive index variations and internal material properties.
047
      Conventional phase retrieval algorithms can recover the transmission function but have significant
048   drawbacks. Firstly, their slow, iterative computation, typically taking minutes to hours to converge,
049   makes real-time imaging impractical. Secondly, these algorithms require heavily dense scanning,
050   which causes numerous data collection to image a sample. Thirdly, the recovered transmission
051   function is prone to global phase shift, resulting in inaccurate phase recovery, as shown in the recov-
052   ered phase using extended-PIE (ePIE) (Maiden & Rodenburg, 2009) in Figure 1. In contrast, deep
053   learning (DL) based phase retrieval algorithms offer a significant speed advantage. These networks
      are commonly convolutional neural networks (CNNs) trained to recover the transmission function

082   is vital.

093   Section 4 details the formulation of PtychoFormer and ePF, and Section 5 provides the empirical
094   results by comparing them with other methods. Lastly, we discuss our findings and future directions
095   in Section 6 and Section 7, respectively.

      2   BACKGROUND
098
099
      In ptychography, the imaging sample is described by the complex-valued transmission function,
100
      T (x, y). At any spatial coordinate (x, y), the transmission function is defined in terms of its ampli-
101   tude, A(x, y), and phase, ϕ(x, y), as
102
103                                       T (x, y) = A(x, y) · eiϕ(x,y) .                                 (1)
104
105   The strict oversampling condition, where adjacent illumination areas must significantly overlap
106   when capturing the diffraction patterns, is essential to recovering T (x, y). This stems from a well-
107   known constraint of light detectors, like the charged-coupled devices (CCDs) or photographic plates,
      which only measure the light intensity of the exiting light and lose the phase (i.e. the phase problem).

      The measured intensity of the diffraction pattern, Ij (u, v), is defined as
134
                                                                                    2
135                             Ij (u, v) = |F {T (x, y) · P (x − Xj , y − Yj )}| ,                        (2)
136
      where F denotes the Fourier transform, P (x, y) is the complex probe function, (u, v) is the recip-
137
      rocal space coordinates, and (Xj , Yj ) are the lateral offsets at the jth scan position. Phase retrieval
138
      algorithms resolve the phase problem by numerically approximating the lost phase using the over-
139   sampled patterns. Without oversampling, the reconstruction would suffer from the ambiguities of
140   the phase problem (Shechtman et al., 2015).
141
142   The global phase shift is a trivial ambiguity in the reconstructed transmission function, T̂ (x, y), that
143   has yet to be solved. It accounts for additional constant and linear terms to the phase, as
144                T̂ (x, y) = A(x, y) · eiϕ(x,y) · ei(a+bx+cy) = A(x, y) · ei(ϕ(x,y)+a+bx+cy) ,           (3)
145
146   where a, b, and c are real-valued coefficients. The constant phase term, a, emerges from the phase
147
      problem, where the magnitude of the sample’s phase is uncertain during reconstruction, allowing a
148   to take any value in T̂ (x, y). The linear phase terms, b and c, can appear when the diffraction patterns
149
      are off-centered in the computational window in Fourier space (Guizar-Sicairos et al., 2011). Hence,
      transmission estimates from conventional phase retrieval algorithms do not contain the correct ab-
150
      solute phase values and instead provide only relative phase shifts, due to the ambiguity introduced
151
      by the global phase shift.

      terns. While several adaptations have since been developed, like mPIE (Maiden et al., 2017), MAIC-

185   Deep Learning Phase Retrieval Algorithms.               DL phase retrieval algorithms eliminated the

      4   P TYCHO F ORMER
197
198   We detail the pre and post-processing, and architectural details of PtychoFormer and extended-
199   PtychoFormer in this section. To reconstruct the phase and amplitude of a sample, multiple diffrac-
200
      tion images are pre-processed by arranging them according to relative spatial positions before in-
      putting to our transformer-based model. Local sub-regional predictions from the model are then
201
      seamlessly stitched together via feathering to minimize grid artifacts. Optionally, the predicted out-
202
      puts are iteratively refined using ePIE in the extended-PtychoFormer, our hybrid approach. Figure 2
203   provides an overview of our reconstruction process.
204
205   Input Processing. Our devised input scheme, depicted in Figure 3(a), handles up to nine spatially
206
      overlapping diffraction patterns, each separated into distinct channels. The diffraction patterns are
      arranged according to their relative scanned coordinates, maintaining the inherent overlap between
207
      adjacent patterns. This organization allows the model to focus on learning the informational and
208
      positional dependencies between the patterns. This input scheme has been tested on various scan
209   patterns and with different numbers of diffraction patterns, shown in Figure 5(b).
210
211   Model Architecture. The PtychoFormer architecture, illustrated in Figure 4 and detailed in Ap-
212
      pendix A, leverages a Mix Transformer (MiT) encoder from the SegFormer architecture (Xie et al.,
      2021), along with a convolutional decoder to achieve efficient phase retrieval. In our research, we
213
      encountered several challenges that shaped our architectural choices.
214
215   The primary challenge was devising an encoder that effectively captures long-range dependencies
      between adjacent diffraction patterns. While CNNs excel at local feature extraction, they have

      which is crucial for understanding the relationships between diffraction patterns in our research.

241   Employing standard transformers like the Vision Transformer (ViT) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) in-
242   troduces new challenges. (1) ViT extracts only a single-resolution feature map, lacking the expres-
243
      siveness to capture informative representation of the diffraction pattern to generate the transmission
      function. (2) Self-attention has quadratic time and space complexity relative to input length, creating
244
      a computational bottleneck that slows inference speed. (3) ViT relies on fixed positional encoding
245
      (PE) to retain spatial information of input patches, inhibiting innate handling of multi-resolution in-
246   puts and degrading performance with untrained input resolution—a significant limitation given the
247   varying resolutions from different scan configurations in our research.
248
249
      To address this, we adopted the MiT encoder, which offers several advantages. (1) MiT’s hierarchi-
      cal architecture and overlapping patch merging enable the extraction of multi-level feature maps at
250
      different resolutions, similar to CNNs. This allows the encoder to capture information at varying
251
      scales by combining coarse and fine-grained details. Integrating features from different layers, each
252   capturing specific aspects of the diffraction images, results in rich feature representations essential
253   for accurate image generation. (2) MiT employs Spatial Reduction Attention (Wang et al., 2021),
254   which reduces the spatial dimensions of key and value sequences before the attention operation by
255   a predefined reduction ratio. This approach has been shown to reduce computational and memory
256   costs substantially than traditional self-attention, alleviating the computational bottleneck. (3) The
257   Mix Feed Forward Network (Mix-FFN) replaces fixed PE with a zero-padded 3 × 3 convolution. As
258   demonstrated by Islam et al. (2020), zero-padded convolutions implicitly encode positional infor-
259   mation. Xie et al. (2021) empirically established that this method not only outperforms fixed PE but
260
      is also less sensitive to untrained input resolutions, aiding the handling of multi-resolution inputs in
      our experiments.
261
262   While SegFormer utilizes a multilayer perceptron (MLP) decoder designed for segmentation tasks,
263   this is not optimal for image generation. MLPs inherently lose local spatial coherence due to their
264   fully connected layers, which is detrimental when maintaining spatial relationships are crucial. On
265   the contrary, convolutions preserves locality and are translationally equivariant due to weight sharing
266
      and local connectivity. A convolutional decoder can also leverage the multi-level feature maps from
      the MiT encoder more appropriately than an MLP, effectively integrating spatially localized features.
267
      Therefore, we replaced the MLP decoder with a convolutional decoder.
268

295   Extended-PtychoFormer. The extended-PtychoFormer (ePF) is our hybrid approach that inte-
296   grates PtychoFormer and ePIE. One of the challenges in algorithms like ePIE is their susceptibility to
297   convergence on local minima, leading to inaccurate estimates of the transmission function (Maiden
298   et al., 2017). ePF leverages PtychoFormer to provide a well-informed initial estimate through the
299
      stitched prediction, serving as a robust starting point for ePIE. This strategy, similar to object ini-
      tialization (Wittwer et al., 2022a), aims to improve the convergence speed and the reconstruction
300
      accuracy.

      5.3   I MPROVEMENTS OVER E PIE
416
417
      While ePIE is the gold standard for ptychographic phase retrieval, it does have significant drawbacks.
418
      The PtychoFormer and ePF are designed to alleviate such drawbacks while achieving comparable
419
      reconstruction performance. We compare the performance of PtychoFormer and ePF against ePIE.
420   The ePIE algorithm follows the implementation from Maiden & Rodenburg (2009) and iterates until
421   its objective function, sum squared error (SSE), converges. See Appendix D for implementation
422   details of ePIE.
423
      As mentioned before, a significant drawback of ePIE and similar iterative phase retrieval algorithms
424
      is the global phase shift phenomenon. As PtychoFormer is trained in absolute phase values during
425
      training, PtychoFormer mitigates global phase shifts. As illustrated in figure 7(f) and (g), ePIE has a
426   high MAE while a comparably lower NRMSE. As the NRMSE is corrected with a global phase shift
427   factor, this shows that ePIE has converged on a shifted phase value. In contrast, PtychoFormer does
428   not suffer from a global phase shift as the MAE and NRMSE are similar. By iteratively improving

483   6   D ISCUSSION
484
485   We can see that PtychoFormer and ePF improve performance in both DL and iterative algorithms.
      By processing multiple diffraction patterns simultaneously, PtychoFormer captures their positional


                                                        9
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


486
      relationships, leading to higher-quality reconstructions than models that rely on single diffraction
487   patterns. This spatial awareness stems from our input scheme, which preserves the spatial infor-
488   mation of diffraction patterns, and the use of the MiT encoder to effectively capture the spatial
489   relationships well. Our model also generalizes well across different probe functions and scan pat-
490   terns with minimal fine-tuning and maintains its fidelity even with reduced spatial overlap. ePF uses
491   PtychoFormer, enabling iterative refinement on the data-driven predictions. Importantly, ePF not
492   only outperforms ePIE in terms of reconstruction accuracy but also significantly minimizes global
493   phase shifts, a persistent challenge in phase retrieval.
494   While we have strong performance on simulated data, transitioning from simulations to real-world
495   applications poses challenges, particularly due to the potential for global phase shifts in the training
496   data. The difficulty lies in the need to train the model on ground truth data generated by conventional
497   algorithms, which may themselves introduce global phase shifts. To address this, calibrated or
498   corrected phase measurements should be used to fine-tune PtychoFormer, allowing it to produce
499   rapid and accurate estimates without global phase shifts. This correction of phase shifts can involve
500   using objects with known phase shifts (Godden et al., 2016), employing interferometric techniques
501
      (Cai et al., 2004), or other phase calibration techniques to ensure reliable training data for real-world
      applications.

504   7   C ONCLUSION
505
506   PtychoFormer offers a new paradigm in DL phase retrieval for ptychography. It provides a ro-
507   bust and versatile solution capable of processing multiple diffraction patterns while preserving the
508   knowledge of their relative scan points. PtychoFormer enables real-time imaging, which is crucial
509   for applications requiring rapid imaging, but this speed comes at the expense of quality. The model
510   also generalizes well across different probe functions and scan patterns with minimal fine-tuning
511
      and maintains its fidelity even with reduced spatial overlap. This adaptability suggests that Pty-
      choFormer could be extended to other imaging modalities requiring phase retrieval, such as X-ray
512
      ptychography used to study molecular structures. Moreover, the integration with iterative meth-
513
      ods, as seen in ePF, underscores the potential of hybrid approaches in balancing speed and recon-
514   struction quality. ePF allows researchers to obtain a rough estimate of the transmission function in
515   real-time using PtychoFormer, followed by a more accurate refinement using ePIE. While this re-
516   search has made many significant strides in developing an improved DL phase retrieval algorithm for
517   ptychography, many potential avenues exist to explore. Future work could involve validating Pty-
518   choFormer’s performance with real ptychographic data and further refining the model to eliminate
519   the need for post hoc corrections.

      A    I MPLEMENTATION D ETAILS OF P TYCHO F ORMER
649
650
      The PtychoFormer architecture leverages a Mix Transformer (MiT) encoder with a convolutional
651   decoder to achieve efficient phase retrieval. Utilizing the MiT-B0 encoder, from the SegFormer
652   architecture (Xie et al., 2021), ensures both computational efficiency and high performance. Key
653   features such as the spatial reduction attention and elimination of fixed positional embedding within
654   the MiT contribute to the efficiency. PtychoFormer processes an input X ∈ R9×H×W , and outputs
655   Ŷ ∈ R2×H×W , where H and W are the height and width of the input image.
656
      The encoder consists of four stages, each processing the input at different spatial resolutions. These
657
      stages incorporate multiple layers of MiT encoder blocks, with 4, 6, 8, and 12 self-attention heads,
658
      respectively. In the first stage, the input is processed to H      W
                                                                    2 × 2 with 64 feature channels, by
659   performing patch embedding followed by MiT Block 1. The successive MiT stages progressively
660   decrease the resolution by a factor of 2 while increasing the feature channels to 128, 256, and 512,
661   respectively.
662
      The decoder upsamples the outputs from each stage of the encoder using bi-cubic interpolation to
663   H     W
664    2 × 2 and passes them through the convolution layer to augment the feature channels to 256. These
      outputs are concatenated to form a 1024 × H          W
                                                     2 × 2 matrix. Then a convolutional block, followed
665
      by a transposed convolutional block, reduces the feature channels and upsamples the resolution to
666
      128 × H × W . Each block is followed by batch normalization and a GELU activation function. The
667   final convolutional layer reduces the feature channels to a 2 × H × W matrix. The tanh function is
668   applied to the first channel, then multiplied by π, resulting in a range of [-π, π]. The second channel
669   uses the sigmoid function to yield a range of [0,1]. The two output channels correspond to the phase
670   and amplitude estimates, respectively.

865   datasets. As a preliminary, probes A, B, and C, from the main body of text, are relabeled as we
866   present all 12 probe functions. As visualized in Figure 10, the 11 training probes are numerically

      The Flickr30k (Young et al., 2014) is the primary dataset we used in our experiment, where we allo-
870
      cated 28,600 images for training and 3,100 images for testing. The pre-training dataset consists of
871   all training images to generate diffraction patterns using a grid scan pattern with 36 different lateral
872   offsets between adjacent scan points, ranging from 20 to 55 pixels. These offsets correspond to
873   spatial overlaps between diffraction patterns ranging from 68.7% to 20.3%. The pre-training dataset
874   consists of these 36 configurations, each containing 6,800 triplets of nine simulated diffraction pat-
875   terns along with the corresponding amplitude and phase labels, totaling 244,800 data points. We
876   only used probe 1 in this dataset.
877   The finetuning dataset uses a subset of the training images to create a smaller dataset that introduces
878   7 additional scan patterns and 9 new probe functions (i.e. probe 2 - 11). The scan patterns included
879   various configurations using probe 1, from 5 to 8 diffraction patterns and grid, diamond, parallelo-
880   gram, and random layouts using 9 diffraction patterns. Probes 2 - 11 were used in a grid scan with a
881   constant lateral offset of 20 pixels. The finetuning dataset included 2,000 samples per configuration,
882   totaling 36,000 data points.
883   We created five test datasets, out of which three were created using the allocated test images
884   from Flickr30k, to evaluate the model’s performance under various scenarios, including out-of-
885   distribution conditions. The first test dataset consists of grid scan with lateral offsets of 20, 30,
886   40, 50, and 60 pixels between adjacent scan points. These offsets correspond to spatial overlaps of
887   68.7%, 53.6%, 39.4%, 26.3%, and 14.9%, respectively. We created 100 samples for each of these
888   grid scans. The second dataset contains grid scans of 20 pixel offset using probes 2 - 11, and the test
889   probe, each with 200 samples. The third dataset includes configurations using the seven new scan
890   patterns introduced in the fine-tuning dataset. To assess the model’s ability to generalize to different
891
      domains, we further evaluated it on two new datasets: Flower102 (Nilsback & Zisserman, 2008) and
      Caltech101 (Li et al., 2022). We created 8,000 samples from each dataset.

900   We evaluated 12 distinct probe functions corresponding to different illumination patterns during
901   diffraction, detailed in Table 2. Additionally, Table 3 presents the results from eight different scan
902   configurations, demonstrating how the geometry of diffraction scans influences reconstruction accu-
903   racy. Notably, the grid formation yielded the lowest errors for both amplitude and phase reconstruc-
904   tions. This is likely because the model was primarily trained on grid configurations and only later
905   fine-tuned on other scan setups, giving it an advantage when reconstructing from grid data compared
906   to more irregular formations, such as random configurations.
