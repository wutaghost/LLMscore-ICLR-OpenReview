011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Although foundation models help increase performance on many downstream
014            tasks while reducing the amount of labeled data needed, their proliferation has
015            raised a natural question: To what extent can a model downloaded from the Inter-
016            net be trusted? We tackle this question for acoustic foundation models (AFMs)
017            and propose the Foundation Acoustic model Backdoor (FAB) attack against
018
               AFMs, showing that state-of-the-art models are susceptible to a new attack vector.
               Despite preserving model performance on benign data, FAB induces backdoors
019
               that survive fine-tuning, and, when activated, lead to a significant performance
020
               drop on various downstream tasks. Notably, backdoors created by FAB can be
021            activated in a physically realizable manner by inconspicuous, input-agnostic trig-
022            gers that do not require syncing with the acoustic input (e.g., by playing a siren
023            sound in the background). Crucially, FAB also assumes a weaker threat model
024            than past work, where the adversary has no knowledge of the pre-training data
025            and certain architectural details. We tested FAB with two leading AFMs, on nine
026            tasks, with four triggers, against two defenses, as well as in the digital and physi-
027            cal domains, and found the attack highly successful in all scenarios. Overall, our
028            work highlights the risks facing AFMs and calls for advanced defences to mitigate
029
               them.

032   1    I NTRODUCTION
033
034   The emergence of self-supervised learning (SSL) has transformed technology, enabling the rapid,
035   low-cost development of high-performing learning-based applications by fine-tuning foundation
036   models to specific downstream tasks with little effort and supervision (Misra & Maaten, 2020; Caron
037   et al., 2020; Kharitonov et al., 2021). Among others, the SSL paradigm has been particularly useful
038   in the acoustics domain, where powerful acoustic foundation models (AFMs) publicly available on
039
      the Internet can be easily acquired and fine-tuned to tackle numerous crucial tasks such as auto-
      matic speech recognition (ASR), speaker identification (SID), and speaker verification (SV) (Yang
040
      et al., 2021). Still, the proliferation of AFMs and their adoption in security- and safety-critical tasks,
041
      such as access control (Wang et al., 2015), should raise concerns about the extent they could be
042   trusted—if adversaries manipulate AFMs and ensure they receive wide adoption (e.g., by upload-
043   ing to popular public repositories (HuggingFace, 2016)), they may hinder the performance of many
044   critical systems.
045
      To help assess AFM trustworthiness, our work proposes the Foundation Acoustic model Backdoor
046
      (FAB) attack (overview in Fig. 1). FAB injects backdoors to AFMs in a manner agnostic to the
047
      downstream task. After injecting the backdoor, the adversary publishes the AFM on a third-party
048   platform where it would be fine-tuned and used in various applications. The backdoor remains
049   inactive for benign inputs and does not harm the performance of downstream tasks. However, when
050   a special adversary-chosen trigger is played alongside benign inputs, the backdoor becomes active,
051   leading to a substantial performance degradation on any downstream task, as no a priori assumptions
052   are made about the task. In particular, FAB employs inconspicuous, sync-free, input-agnostic, and
053   physically realizable triggers, allowing the adversary to mislead downstream models with little-to-no
      assumption about the attack conditions (§3).

072   While backdoor attacks in the CV and NLP domains have been extensively studied(Shen et al.,
073   2021; Zhang et al., 2023), they have been less explored in the acoustics domain. Importantly, prior
074   backdoor attacks in the acoustic domain are either task-specific (i.e., they do not target AFMs) (Cai
075
      et al., 2022a;b; Lan et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2023), fail to activate the backdoor when physically
      realizing the trigger (Koffas et al., 2022), or are not input-agnostic (i.e., they require knowledge of
076
      the input to craft the trigger) (Lee et al., 2023). FAB addresses these shortcomings.
077
078   To evaluate FAB, we conducted extensive experiments with nine downstream tasks, two AFMs,
079   four trigger sounds, and two defenses, and considered inputs passed either digitally (over-the-line)
080   or physically (over-the-air). Our results highlight that FAB is highly successful at satisfying the
081
      objectives we put forward—particularly, it preserves benign performance (i.e., performance on be-
      nign inputs) and degrades performance when introducing triggers for a wide range of downstream
082
      tasks—highlighting the risks faced by AFMs. We intend to publish our implementation hoping it
083
      would inform future work on developing more trustworthy AFMs.
084

086   technical approach of FAB (§4). Then, we present the experiment setup (§5) and results (§6) before
087
      concluding (§7).

091   2.1      P RE - TRAINED S PEECH M ODELS
092
093   Self-Supervised Learning (SSL) approaches for speech representation—where models learn by pre-
094   dicting masked frames of (unlabeled) input data— have recently demonstrated significant advance-
095   ments (Schneider et al., 2019; Baevski et al., 2020; 2022; Chen et al., 2022b; Meng et al., 2022). A
096   key motivation of SSL approaches is that effective speech representations simplify the downstream
097   tasks by reducing the amount of annotated data required for supervised fine-tuning. Particularly, SSL
098
      for speech representation results in AFMs that can be adapted for a wide range of downstream tasks
      (e.g., from automatic speech recognition (ASR) to phoneme classification) with little supervision.
099
100   Notably, HuBERT (Hsu et al., 2021) and WavLM (Chen et al., 2022b) are two performant AFMs
101   trained via SSL. While WavLM is a more recent model, in line with other AFMs (e.g., Wang et al.
102   (2022); Peng et al. (2023)), it also builds on the HuBERT architecture and training process. In turn,
103   both models attain high performance on SUPERB (Yang et al., 2021), a benchmark introduced to
104
      evaluate AFMs trained through SSL on a variety of tasks, with lightweight prediction modules that
      are fine-tuned for each specific task after freezing the AFM.
105

107   dio frames. These labels then serve as targets for computing a BERT-like loss during model training.

131   2.2   BACKDOOR ATTACKS
132
133   Backdoor attacks against deep neural networks (DNNs) pose a significant threat to their integrity
134   (Gao et al., 2020; Weber et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2021). In such attacks, adversaries induce certain
135
      malicious behaviors (such as misclassifications of certain inputs) to models that remain inactive at
      test time until a certain trigger (e.g., pattern in an image or word in a sentence) is introduced at the
136
      input. Crucially, backdooring is typically done without affecting the model performance on benign
137
      data to ensure the model remains useful and gets deployed. Prior work has primarily explored
138   backdoor attacks in computer vision (CV) and natural language processing (NLP), but some efforts
139   also studied backdoors in acoustics.
140
141
      Backdoor attack against CV and NLP model Various efforts offered backdoor attacks against CV
      models (e.g., Doan et al. (2021); Li et al. (2021b); Nguyen & Tran (2020)). Gu et al. (2017) first
142
      proposed BadNet, a backdoor attack for models trained to address specific CV tasks. Following
143
      BadNet, some work attempted to make triggers more imperceptible (Li et al., 2020; Zhong et al.,
144   2020; Salem et al., 2022) . In more recent work, researchers also proposed methods to induced back-
145   doors in task-specific NLP models that can be activated while preserving the text semantics (Chen
146   et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2021). Besides targeting models developed for specific tasks, all these
147   efforts assume the adversary has full or partial access to the training dataset.
148
      A few efforts studied backdoor attacks against foundation models (Kurita et al., 2020; Chen et al.,
149
      2022a; Guo et al., 2022). For instance, Zhang et al. (2023) studied backdoor attack methods that
150   survive fine-tuning of models while harming performance on multiple downstream tasks when acti-
151   vated. Similarly, Shen et al. (2021) used self-distillation to preserve utility of text foundation models
152   while injecting backdoor. proposed to date. Note that past work on backdooring foundation models
153   usually assumes access to the pre-training dataset (Shen et al., 2021; Zhang et al., 2023).
154
      Backdoor attacks against speech models Backdoor attacks in the speech domain can can be clas-
155
      sified into two classes, according to nature of trigger: input-specific and input-agnostic backdoors.
156   In input-specific backdoors, adversaries usually customize the trigger to the input audio or directly
157   generate malicious audio (Cai et al., 2022a;b; Koffas et al., 2023; Lee et al., 2023). These attacks
158   are impractical, as they require knowledge of the background audio or require complete control of
159   the input. Moreover, with one exception (Lee et al., 2023), prior attacks on speech models mostly
160   targeted task-specific models. Still, although Lee et al. (2023) attacked an AFM, their attack was
161   not input-agnostic, and they only tested the attack on a single downstream task (speech recognition),
      thus generalization to other tasks remains unknown.


                                                            3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Input-agnostic attacks employ a universal trigger to activate backdoors (Koffas et al., 2022; Liu
163   et al., 2022; Shi et al., 2022; Xin et al., 2022). For instance, Koffas et al. (2022) used a single
164   high-frequency audio as a trigger. To our knowledge, past input-agnostic attacks only apply to task-
165   specific models (not AFMs) and assume knowledge of the pre-training dataset. Additionally, some
166   of these attacks are not physically realizable, as they make strong assumptions about the attacker’s
167   ability to sync the trigger with the backdround audio (e.g., the trigger is played at the beginning of
168   the recording Koffas et al. (2022)).
169   Defense methods Various defenses were proposed to counter backdoor attacks. Some defenses aim
170   to detect backdoor during the training process (Tran et al., 2018; Chen et al., 2018; Shan et al.,
171   2022; Du et al., 2019; Huang et al., 2022; Hong et al., 2020; Costa et al., 2024). These defenses
172   are adequate for settings where the backdoor is injected by manipulating the training data (but not
173   the training process itself). In contrast, other defenses operate in the post-training stage to detect
174   or remove backdoors that have already been injected to a model (Guo et al., 2019; Kolouri et al.,
175   2020; Liu et al., 2018; Xiang et al., 2022; Xu et al., 2021). For instance, this may include pruning
176   the model weights to remove backdoors (Liu et al., 2018). Last, inference-time defenses aim to
177
      manipulate inputs to neutralize the backdoor, e.g., by filtering out the trigger (Carlini et al., 2016;
      Gao et al., 2019; Li et al., 2021a; Zeng et al., 2021). We show FAB remains effective when facing
178
      these defenses (see Sec. §6).

181   3     T HREAT M ODEL
182
183   We consider the following backdoor attack scenario described in Fig. 1:
184
            1. An attacker downloads the pre-trained benign weights of an AFM. It then proceeds to
185
               backdoor the model and publish its own backdoored version of the AFM weights (e.g., on
186
               some open platforms such as HuggingFace (2016)).
187
188
            2. A downstream model developer will download the backdoored AFM weights and fine-tune
               them for their specific downstream task. This downstream model is then incorporated as
189
               part of some real-world system (e.g., a system that takes a user’s audio recording and uses
190
               a downstream speech-to-text model to produce a transcript).
191
            3. When the system is used in practice, the adversary exploits the trigger to manipulate the
192
               output of the downstream model.
193
194   In this work, we show that we can backdoor a AFM and successfully exploit a trigger against a
195   downstream application, even when considering what is arguably the weakest threat model possible.
196   I.e., we consider a very constrained adversary with the following limitations:
197
198         1. The attackers only have access to the weights of the AFM. They do not have access to the
199
               original dataset used to train the AFM or auxiliary parameters used in the training process
               such as codebook and projection matrix.
200
201         2. During the backdooring process, the attacker has no knowledge about the final downstream
202            task or the dataset that will be used in the fine-tuning process.
203         3. Our attack is constrained to simple, physically realizable, input-agnostic, and sync-free
204            triggers. The attacker is not allowed to directly manipulate the recorded audio that is the
205            input to the model. Instead, the attacker can only generate an audio trigger that will be
206            recorded by the system together with the benign user’s audio. We further limit ourselves to
207
               inconspicuous triggers such as dog barking, sirens, and musical instruments.
208   We will now expend upon our threat model assumptions:
209
210
      3.1   AFM BACKDOOR I NJECTION
211
212   The goal of our attacker is to produce a backdoored AFM model. We assume that our attacker
213   does not train such a AFM model from scratch, but instead tries to inject a backdoor to pre-trained
214   state-of-the-art AFM. The goal of our attack is to backdoor a pre-trained AFM. As in prior work we
215   assume that the backdoor preserves the architecture of the AFM, and we only allow the attacker to
      modify the model’s weights (Shen et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2022a; Zhang et al., 2023). However,


                                                        4
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


216
      in contrast to previous work (Shen et al., 2021; Cui et al., 2022; Zhang et al., 2023; Lyu et al.,
217   2023), we assume that the developers of the AFM do not release their training dataset (i.e., audio
218   samples that the AFM was trained on) and thus it cannot be used by our attacker. This is, in fact, the
219   case in many published models (e.g., models trained on JFT-300M (Sun et al., 2017), Qwen2 (Yang
220   et al., 2024), and LLaMA3 (LLaMA3-Team, 2024))). Instead, we make the arguably much weaker
221   and more realistic assumption that the attacker can only access an auxiliary dataset with a similar
222   distribution to the original dataset. Moreover, we further assume that the adversary does not have
223   access to various parameters used in the training process, such as codebook and projection matrix.
224   For example, the authors of WavLM (Chen et al., 2022b) explicitly declared that they would not
225
      release these parameters as they are required only for pre-training and not for fine-tuning (Microsoft,
      2021b).

      3.2   D OWNSTREAM TASKS AND FINE - TUNING
229
      Some prior backdooring work assumed a strong threat model, where an attacker only targets a spe-
230   cific known downstream task (Gu et al., 2017; Zhang et al., 2021; Zheng et al., 2023). However, we
231   assume a much weaker “task-agnostic” threat model, where we target an AFM and the specifics of
232   the downstream tasks are unknown to the adversary.
233
      “Task-agnostic” threat model implies the following constraints on the backdoor injection process:
234
235         1. Our backdoor should be generic enough to be exploitable against a large range of different
236            downstream tasks. As the task is unknown, the goal of our backdoor is performance degra-
237            dation for trigger stamped inputs (e.g., increase the Word Error Rate (WER) for speech
238            recognition tasks). This means that the backdoor should be robust enough to survive stan-
239            dard fine-tuning techniques and work regardless of any specifics of the downstream task.
240         2. Our backdoor process should preserve benign performance for benign inputs (task perfor-
241            mance on any sample that does not contain the trigger) across the same large range of
242            downstream tasks.
243
244   We note that prior “task-agnostic” work focused on specific classes of downstream tasks such as
245   classification or speech recognition tasks (Shen et al., 2021; Chen et al., 2022a; Lee et al., 2023;
246
      Zhang et al., 2023). In contrast, to demonstrate the generality of our attack, we tested our back-
      door across a wide range of downstream task categories (e.g., categories taken from the SUPERB
247
      evaluation framework (Yang et al., 2021; Tsai et al., 2022)).
248
249
      3.3   BACKDOOR T RIGGER
250
251   Finally, While prior work assumed that the attacker has full control of the raw digital input to the
252   model (Lee et al., 2023; Ye et al., 2022), we constrain our attacker to simple and physically realizable
253   triggers. Instead of manipulating the input directly, we assume the following arguably more realistic
254   real-world scenario: The audio input to the model is recorded using a microphone, e.g., a person can
255   record a voice command to their smartphone device or to an automated teller machine (ATM). The
256   attacker cannot control or manipulate the recorded audio but only “add” their trigger to the recording
257   by generating a physical sound in the real world that will also be recorded by the microphone and
258   superimposed on the benign audio.
259   This means that in addition to being “task-agnostic” and generalizable across different downstream
260   tasks, our trigger has the added following requirements:
261
262         1. Our trigger will be physically realizable and robust, such that it will be based on sounds
263            that can be generated and recorded by off-the-shelf audio recording devices.
264         2. The trigger will be “input agnostic” — it will be effective with high probability when
265            superimposed with any input sampled from the distribution. I.e., we assume that our at-
266            tacker has no prior knowledge about the input audio and is unable to optimize the trigger
267            accordingly.
268         3. The trigger will be “sync-free” — it will be effective with high probability when superim-
269            posed at any random offset with any input sampled from the distribution. I.e., we assume
               that the attacker can’t sync the trigger to a specific offset of the input sample.


                                                         5
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


270
            4. The trigger will be “inconspicuous” — it should be based on a mundane and inconspicuous
271            sound that will not be considered out of the ordinary, e.g., a dog barking, or an ambulance
272            siren.
273
274   Finally, we want to rule out trivial triggers such as playing extremely loud music that will “drown
275   out” the benign audio. Thus, we only consider triggers that adding them will not have a significant
276   effect on the performance of downstream tasks that were fine-tuned from a benign AFM that was
277   not injected with the backdoor.

279   4     T ECHNICAL A PPROACH
280
281   We now detail how our attack, FAB, injects a backdoor into an AFM while satisfying the battery
282   of constraints described in §3. As its input, FAB receives the pre-trained AFM fθ , an auxiliary
283   dataset Xaux , and a trigger audio δ. We emphasize that, per the weak threat model we assume,
284   the auxiliary dataset used by FAB is different than the AFM’s pre-training dataset (i.e., Xaux ̸=X)
285   and fθ only carries the parameters necessary for fine-tuning on downstream tasks, thus lacking the
286   codebook and projection matrix during pre-training. As its output, FAB returns a backdoored AFM,
287   fˆθ , which preserves benign performance on downstream tasks on benign input, and whose backdoor
288   is activated by δ, leading to performance degradation on any downstream task.
289   Conceptually, FAB operates as follows to inject the backdoor while satsifying its primary objectives.
290   To hinder performance for trigger-stamped inputs on various, unknown, downstream tasks, FAB en-
291   sures the AFM produces counter-productive representations when triggers are ingested. In contrast,
292   to preserve downstream tasks’ performance for benign inputs, FAB trains the AFM to create useful
293   representation when triggers are excluded (i.e., inputs are benign), akin to standard pre-training.
294   Formally, FAB minimizes a compound loss function:
295                                       LFAB = κ · LBack + LBenign
296
297   where LBack is minimized for trigger-stamped inputs to manipulate the representations, LBenign is
298
      minimized for benign inputs to ensure model utility when the backdoor is dormant, and κ is a positive
      constant balancing the two losses. LFAB is optimized iteratively, via gradient descent, using batches
299
      containing benign and trigger-stamped samples. These batches are produced by drawing benign
300
      samples from Xaux , and creating a counterpart for each by stamping the trigger.
301
302
      4.1   M ANIPULATING R EPRESENTATIONS FOR T RIGGER - STAMPED I NPUTS
303
304   LBack ’s definition Minimizing LBack aims to ensure that trigger-stamped inputs are mapped to rep-
305   resentations unuseful for downstream tasks by detaching the representations from the input. Doing
306   so renders the attack task-agnostic, as no assumptions are made about the downstream task, and the
307   derived representations would mostly become independent of the input when the trigger is intro-
308   duced. To this end, given a representation that degrades the performance of downstream tasks, v,
309   LBack measures the distance between v and the representation, ô, pertaining to the trigger-stamped
310   input, X̂. More specifically, we define LBack = D(ô, v), where D is a distance function. In prac-
311   tice, after exploring various options for D and v (see §C.7), we find that setting v to a fixed vector,
312
      such as all ones, and the distance function to cosine distance, leads to the highest attack success.
313   A natural choice of a representation to use in LBack is the one emitted by the last layer. Selecting this
314   representation would be effective against tasks adopting the fine-tuning paradigm where the down-
315   stream model is trained only on the AFM’s last layer’s output (§2.1). However, as no constraint is
316   enforced on the representations of earlier layers, these may remain useful in the fine-tuning paradigm
317   where downstream models are trained on a weighted sum of all layers’ representations. To address
318
      this, the adversary may seek to directly manipulate some combination of all layers’ representations
      (i.e., the weighted sum), or manipulate the representations produced by a specific intermediate layer,
319
      thus cascading to all consecutive layers as well as the weighted sum. We explore both approaches
320
      and find that selecting a particular intermediate layer results in the most effective attack against both
321   common fine-tuning paradigms (see §C.7).
322
323   Producing trigger-stamped inputs We carefully create our trigger-stamped inputs, X̂s, during train-
      ing to ensure that attacks are inconspicuous, sync-free, input-agnostic, and physically realizable


                                                         6
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


324
      (see §3.3). We select the trigger, δ, as a natural, seemingly innocuous sound often encountered in
325   day-to-day interactions (e.g., siren or bark). To attain sync-free attacks, we randomly select the re-
326   gion at which we introduce δ into benign inputs (i.e., we insert δ at a random starting point), hence,
327   encouraging the model to produce the desired representation v regardless of the time the trigger is
328   played. For input-agnostic attacks, we insert δ to various benign inputs Xs, drawn at random from
329   Xaux , ensuring the δ is effective independently of X.
330
      Moreover, we adjust the δ’s length (i.e., duration) and volume to ensure that trigger stamping does
331   not have significant effect on the performance of task based on the benign AFM. Specifically, we
332   limit δ’s length and volume by a specific proportion p and scale s, respectively, w.r.t. the benign
333   input X which leads to a fixed signal-to-noise ratio (SNR). We then experimnly verified that this
334   trigger preserved the performance on various task based on benign AFM (see §6.1). Finally, we
335   empirically showed that physical realizability follows directly from the other properties, without
336   additional provisions (see §6.2).

338   4.2   P RESERVING P ERFORMANCE FOR B ENIGN I NPUTS
339
340   LBack ’s definition An intuitive means to preserve high downstream performance for benign inputs
341   is to train the backdoored AFM, fˆθ , in the same manner as the original AFM, fθ , on benign inputs,
342
      such that the representations for such inputs remain useful. Said differently, the attack could min-
      imize Eq. 2 as LBack for benign samples from Xaux to preserve benign performance, as part of a
343
      masked token prediction self-supervised task. Minimizing such a loss would be possible assuming
344
      the attack has access to (1) the AFM’s codebook and corresponding embeddings ec as well as the
345   projection matrix A, and (2) the pseudo-labels of tokens extracted from Xaux ’s samples. However,
346   as described in §3.1, we assume a weak threat model where the attacker does not have access to
347   neither the model parameters unnecessary for fine-tuning downstream models (i.e., codebook and
348   projection matrix) nor to the auxiliary dataset’s pseudo-labels, since Xaux ̸=X. Thus, we propose
349   means to produce this information to enable minimizing LBack . We find that this approach leads
350   to attack success on par with the scenario where the adversary is knowledgeable, with access to the
351   missing information (see §C.2). We also emphasize that we other means to define LBack are found
352   less effective (see §C.7).
353   Approximating the missing parameters To produce the clusters and corresponding codebook, we
354   find it effective to cluster representations emitted by the AFM’s last (encoding) layer. More specif-
355   ically, we extract representations for tokens of samples X ∈ Xaux and cluster them via k-means
356   (setting k to publicly known default values (Hsu et al., 2021). The centroids of the clusters found
357   by the k-means are treated as the codebook embeddings ec . We expect this approach yields success-
358   ful results as standard pre-training also clusters samples in a similar manner throughout pre-training,
359
      during cluster refinement (Hsu et al., 2021). We experimentally show that this is indeed true (see §6).
      Although the projection matrix A is typically used for dimensionality reduction, we find that simply
360
      treating it as the identity matrix (i.e., avoiding projection), results in performance comparable to that
361
      achieved when using the original (unknown) matrix (§6.4).
362
363   Pseudo-labeling Xaux Leveraging the reproduced parameters, we pseudo-label all tokens extracted
364   from Xaux ’s samples in advance, prior to the backdoor-injection process. Specifically, we do so by
365
      assigning each token to the closet cluster (i.e., codebook label) found by k-means. As the original
      model outputs useful representations for benign samples, this process produces high quality pseudo-
366
      labels that enables preserving performance on such samples.

376   experiments, we pre-trained HuBERT from scratch on the original LibriSpeech dataset. Pre-training
377   HuBERT from scratch provided us with the model’s codebook, corresponding embeddings, and
      pseudo-labels for the pre-training dataset. This allowed us to perform ablation tests comparing our

406

407
      all 1s vector as the target vector, v, as we found it as effective as any other choice. App. A reports
408   additional backdoor-injection parameters.

412
413   Our results show that FAB’s backdoors are task-agnostic (§6.1) and the triggers are physically re-
414   alizable (§6.2), thus satisfying the objectives we lay out (§3). Note that other objectives are either
415   satisfied by design (stealth) or are demonstrated to hold in all experiments—the triggers are intro-
416
      duced on all test samples at random time intervals, rendering them input-agnostic and sync-free.
      Our results also show that FAB withstands established defenses (§6.3). Last, we provide ablations
417
      to showcase the generality of the attack and justify different design choices (§6.4).

      7     C ONCLUSION
516
517
      In this work, we have exemplified that the wide spread use of pre-trained foundation models to fine-
518
      tune downstream task can pose a significant risk for the end users. Specifically, for the audio domain,
519   we showed a novel backdoor attack, where an attacker can inject a backdoor to a foundation model,
520   which can be activated by simple trigger and can degrade the performance of any downstream task.
521
522
      Despite preserving benign performance, our FAB attack induces backdoors that survive fine-tuning,
      and, when activated, lead to a significant performance degradation on various downstream tasks.
523
      Notably, backdoors created by FAB can be activated in a physically realizable manner by inconspic-
524
      uous, input-agnostic triggers that do not require syncing with the acoustic input (e.g., by playing a
525   siren sound in the background). FAB also assumes a weaker threat model than past work, where the
526   adversary has no knowledge of the pre-training data and certain architectural details.
527
528
      Our experiments with two leading AFMs, on nine tasks, with four triggers, against two defenses, as
      well as in the digital and physical domains, evidence that FAB is highly successful in all scenarios.
529
      As our work calls for new defenses to counter backdoor attacks against AFMs; we hope that our
530
      intention to release our code will aid in the development of such defenses.

      A    BACKDOOR - INJECTION PARAMETERS
747
748   To perform backdoor injection, we minimized LFAB per the process outlined in §3 using samples
749   from Xaux . Starting with the pre-trained AFM, fθ , we ran training for one epoch with batches con-
750   taining a mix of benign and trigger-stamped inputs, to acquire the backdoored AFM, fˆθ . Specifi-
751   cally, we used a batch size of 64, each created by drawing 32 benign samples from Xaux , introducing
752   the trigger to each (see §5), thus creating a trigger-stamped variant for each benign sample, and con-
753   catenating all benign samples and their trigger-stamped counterparts. For updating the model
754   parameters, we used the Adam optimizer (Kingma, 2015), adopting the default parameters from the
755   HuBERT work (Hsu et al., 2021) (i.e., learning rate of 1.5e-5, β1 =0.9, β2 =0.98, weight decay of
      0.01). Lastly, we set κ=1,000 in LFAB , as we found it to perform best after executing a line search.

      B     D OWNSTREAM TASKS AND M ETRICS
757
758   We considered the following nine tasks from four different categories, all taken from the SUPERB
759   benchmark (Yang et al., 2021):
760
761         1. Content: We considered three tasks from this category.
762             (a) Automatic speech recognition (ASR) aims to transcribes audio into words. It is eval-
763                 uated by word error rate (WER)—the rate of incorrectly recognized words compared
764                 to the actual words in the ground truth.
765             (b) Phoneme recognition (PR) seeks to transcribe audio into phonemes, content units
766                 smaller than words. Performance on this task is quantified by phoneme error rate,
767                 which is analogous to WER but considers phonemes, instead of words, as the units for
768                 measuring errors.
769             (c) Keyword spotting (KS) intends to classify the input audio into one of ten pre-defined
770
                    classes, each denoting a different keywords, and is evaluated by the standard accuracy
                    (ACC) metric.
771
772         2. Speaker: We used three tasks from this category.
773             (a) Speaker identification (SID) aims to classify audio samples according to the speaker’s
774                 identity and is evaluated by ACC metric.
775             (b) Automatic speaker verification (ASV) takes two audio samples as input and aims to
776                 verify whether the speaker in both samples is the same or not. Equal error rate (EER)
777                 is used to evaluate performance on this task.
778             (c) Speaker diarization (SD) aims to predict the identity of the speaker at different time
779                 intervals, given an audio recording of multiple speakers. The diarization error rate
780
                    (DER) is the metric used to evaluated performance on this task.
781         3. Semantics: We considered two tasks from this category.
782             (a) Intent classification (IC) seeks to classify audio samples to one of three categories:
783                 action, object, or location. The ACC metric is used to evaluate performance on this
784                 task.
785             (b) Speech translation (ST) translates English audio samples to German text. It is evalu-
786                 ated by the Bilingual Evaluation Understudy (BLEU) score (Papineni et al., 2002).
787         4. Paralinguistics: We considered the only task available in this category.
788             (a) Emotion recognition (ER) intends to classify each utterance by its emotional inclina-
789                 tion, into one of four classes (neutral, happy, sad, or angry). ACC is used to measure
790                 performance.
791

      The downstream models, except for ASR, were fine-tuned per the recipes published by the SUPERB
      benchmark ((Yang et al., 2021)). For ASR, we adopted the fine-tuning setup published by HuBERT’s
793
      (Hsu et al., 2021) and WavLM’s (Chen et al., 2022b) authors.
