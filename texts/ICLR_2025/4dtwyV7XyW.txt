009                                                A BSTRACT
010
011
012            Knowledge tracing aims to reason about changes in students’ knowledge and to pre-
013            dict students’ performance in educational learning settings. We propose knowledge
014            tracing set transformers (KTSTs), a straightforward model class for knowledge
015            tracing prediction tasks. This model class is conceptually simpler than previous
016            state-of-the-art approaches, which are overly complex due to domain-inspired
017
               components, and which are in part based on suboptimal design choices and flawed
               evaluation. In contrast, for KTSTs we propose principled set representations of
018
               student interactions and a simplified variant of learnable modification of attention
019
               matrices for positional information in a student’s learning history. While being
020            largely domain-agnostic, the proposed model class thus accounts for characteristic
021            traits of knowledge tracing tasks. In extensive empirical experiments on standard-
022            ized benchmark datasets, KTSTs establish new state-of-the-art performance.

027   1    I NTRODUCTION
028
029
030   The ultimate goal of knowledge tracing is to support students’ learning processes (Corbett & Anderson,
031   1994). Knowledge tracing enables the adaptation of learning materials to students’ individual needs
032   and therefore constitutes an integral component of intelligent tutoring systems. In this paper, we
033
      focus on predictive performance and study knowledge tracing as a supervised sequential learning
      task in which we aim to predict the correctness of the next response of a student, given the history of
034
      her interactions with a learning system (cf. Gervet et al., 2020).
035
036   To achieve good predictive performance, existing approaches often rely on domain knowledge either
037   regarding representations of students’ interactions (Ghosh et al., 2020; Shen et al., 2022; Yin et al.,
038   2023) or regarding model architectures (Zhang et al., 2017; Nakagawa et al., 2019; Long et al., 2021;
039
      Shen et al., 2021). So far, simpler models (Piech et al., 2015; Liu et al., 2023b) do not achieve
      comparable results on benchmark tasks and consequentially, the state-of-the-art in knowledge tracing
040
      prediction tasks consists of rather complex approaches. In addition, several recent approaches are
041
      based on suboptimal design choices and flawed evaluation. Specifically, they introduce an interaction
042   representation that is inefficient, led to label leakage in the past, and introduces a distribution shift
043   between training and evaluation (cf. Ghosh et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2022; 2023b; Yin et al., 2023).
044
045
      We propose knowledge tracing set transformers (KTSTs), a straightforward, yet principled and
      performant model class for knowledge tracing prediction tasks, based on the standard transformer
046
      architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017). To account for characteristics of knowledge tracing tasks, we
047
      propose a simplified variant of learnable modification of attention matrices (cf. Press et al., 2021), as
048   well as principled interaction representations that do not rely on domain-knowledge. Compared to
049   flawed interaction representations in related work, our representations accurately reflect the learning
050   setting and satisfy permutation invariance (cf. Zaheer et al., 2017) with regard to sets of input features.
051   We explicitly discuss limitations of prior work in the problem setting (Section 3) and in contrast to
052   KTSTs (Section 4). Empirically, we evaluate our method on eight datasets and compare it against
053   22 baseline models (Section 5), establishing new state-of-the-art performance on knowledge tracing
      benchmark tasks.

      2    R ELATED WORK
055
056   Knowledge tracing as a problem setting was established by Anderson et al. (1990). Classical machine
057   learning approaches for knowledge tracing include probabilistic graphical models (e.g. Corbett &
058   Anderson, 1994; Käser et al., 2017) and factor analysis-based approaches (e.g. Cen et al., 2006;
059   Pavlik et al., 2009; Vie & Kashima, 2019), where many contributions proposed models that explicitly
060   build upon domain knowledge regarding the educational learning setting (e.g. Pardos & Heffernan,
061   2011; Yudelson et al., 2013; Khajah et al., 2014).
062   Deep knowledge tracing, that is, the use of deep learning (LeCun et al., 2015; Schmidhuber, 2015) for
063   knowledge tracing, was introduced by Piech et al. (2015). Approaches can be differentiated by their
064   dominant modeling choices: Several methods leverage recurrent neural networks (RNNs, Hochreiter
065   & Schmidhuber, 1997) for the sequential prediction task (Piech et al., 2015; Yeung & Yeung, 2018;
066   Nagatani et al., 2019; Lee & Yeung, 2019; Sonkar et al., 2020; Long et al., 2021; Shen et al., 2021;
067   2022; Liu et al., 2023a); others include memory-augmented components (Santoro et al., 2016; Graves
068   et al., 2016) to explicitly represent students’ knowledge states (Zhang et al., 2017; Abdelrahman
069   & Wang, 2019) or utilize graph neural networks (GNNs Scarselli et al., 2009) to capture relations
070
      between students and questions (Nakagawa et al., 2019; Yang et al., 2021).
071   Transformer based knowledge tracing approaches are characterized by building upon the transformer
072   architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017). As transformers are integral components of state-of-the-art models
073   for natural language processing (NLP, e.g. Brown et al., 2020) and for structured data in general
074   (e.g. Dosovitskiy et al., 2021), they constitute a promising modeling choice for deep knowledge
075   tracing. Knowledge tracing set transformers (KTSTs), as proposed in this paper, fall into this category.
076
      Prior work investigated how changes regarding the architecture and flow of information (Pandey
      & Karypis, 2019; Choi et al., 2020; Ghosh et al., 2020; Zhan et al., 2024), regarding positional
077
      information in the attention mechanism (Ghosh et al., 2020; Im et al., 2023), and regarding the
078
      interaction and knowledge component representation (Ghosh et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2023b; Yin
079   et al., 2023) influence the predictive performance. In contrast to KTSTs, most related work include
080   domain-inspired components that increase model complexity. Approaches that combine transformer
081   based knowledge tracing architectures with other deep learning paradigms, such as hypergraph
082   convolutions and RNNs (e.g. He et al., 2024), result in even more complex models. Self-supervised
083   approaches for transformer based knowledge tracing (e.g. Lee et al., 2022; Yin et al., 2023) are
084   orthogonal to our work.
085   We continue to discuss related work throughout the remainder of this paper: We address the short-
086   comings of the prevalent, yet flawed, expanded representation for interaction sequences (Section 3).
087   We point out domain-inspired components of previously proposed architectures in comparison to
088   the straightforward architecture of KTSTs (Section 4.1). We compare attention mechanisms from
089   related work in relation to the proposed learnable modification of attention matrices (Section 4.2). We
090   contrast domain-inspired interaction representations for knowledge tracing with KTSTs’ principled
091   set representations regarding complexity and permutation invariance (Section 4.3).

139   4     K NOWLEDGE TRACING SET TRANSFORMERS
140
141   In this section, we propose sequential Knowledge Tracing Set Transformers (KTST). Our approach
142   is based on a standard transformer architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017) with adjustments for best
143   performance on knowledge tracing tasks. It features a simple, yet learnable variant of modification
144
      of attention matrices for positional information (cf. Press et al., 2021) and operates on principled
      representations of interactions that do not rely on domain-knowledge and which are permutation
145
      invariant (cf. Zaheer et al., 2017) with respect to sets of knowledge components.
146
147
148
      4.1    T RANSFORMER ARCHITECTURE FOR KNOWLEDGE TRACING
149
      The deep learning architecture for KTSTs is based on the standard transformer architecture (Vaswani
150   et al., 2017) including an encoder and a decoder, multiple layers with multi-head self-attention
151   (MHSA) and cross-attention, residual connections (He et al., 2016), dropout (Srivastava et al.,
152   2014), layer normalization (Ba et al., 2016) and feed-forward neural networks. Instead of positional
153   encodings, we propose to use a learnable modification of attention matrices to inform the model about
154   positional information (see Section 4.2); other differences are pointed out in the following. Overall,
155   the architecture allows for efficient training with a single forward and backward pass per sequence.
156   For knowledge tracing prediction tasks (see Section 3), KTSTs operate on multiple discrete tokens
157   per interaction yt : a question qt , its associated knowledge components c (qt ), and response rt .
158   We embed every token into a vector of size d. Embeddings associated with the same time step
159   are aggregated to form a joint interaction representation (respectively question representation, see
160   Section 4.3). For each interaction, KTSTs estimate the probability of a correct response at the next
161   time step, P (rt+1 = 1|xt+1 , y1:t ), where the representation of the next question xt+1 is used as
      query token in the decoder, attending to the learning history y1:t as processed by the encoder. We

197   Comparison with domain-inspired architectures in related work The architecture proposed for
198   KTSTs is technically straightforward and does not rely on domain-specific components. This is in
199   contrast to many (transformer based) deep knowledge tracing architectures, which impose a strong
200   inductive bias with respect to the task at hand and are often motivated by concepts associated with
201   human learning. Examples of inductive biases in related work include: encoding students’ knowledge
202   states by means of RNN hidden states (Long et al., 2021), explicitly modeling a student’s knowledge
203   acquisition process with memory-augmented neural networks (Zhang et al., 2017; Abdelrahman
204   & Wang, 2019), enforcing a smoothness constraint on learnable knowledge parameters which are
205
      assumed to represent knowledge components (Yin et al., 2023), explicitly including question difficulty
      estimates that guide updates within the architecture (Shen et al., 2022), and explicitly modeling the
206
      relationship between learners and questions via graph neural networks (Nakagawa et al., 2019; Yang
207
      et al., 2021). Intuitively, these components increase model complexity. Although KTSTs require only
208   small changes to the standard transformer architecture, they outperform more complicated approaches
209   empirically (see Section 5), and are conceptually simpler.

265
266   The elements of every interaction yt = (qt , c (qt ) , rt ) are embedded separately and aggregated to
267   form joint representations that are used as inputs to the transformer architecture. We require the
268   aggregation function for interaction tuples yt (and question tuples xt ) to be permutation invariant
269   (cf. Zaheer et al., 2017) to the ordering of knowledge components c (qt ) associated with question qt .
      Permutation invariance is a desirable property of functions operating on sets, whose implementations

271   knowledge components and let n = n(q) be their corresponding indices. Let π denote a permutation
272   of a k-tuple of integers 1 through k. For the aggregation function ϕ (x) to be permutation invariant
273   with respect to the ordering of knowledge components, it must hold that
274

279   satisfies permutation invariance (cf. Zaheer et al., 2017). Secondly, a function that assigns an integer

283

284

308   embeddings (1) and unique set embeddings (2) have been used in prior studies on knowledge tracing

312   Mean embeddings allow the models to attribute student performance to individual knowledge compo-
313   nents, while interaction effects appear to be more difficult to learn. Unique set embeddings naturally
314   account for interactions. However, we argue that it becomes increasingly difficult to attribute re-
315
      sponses to individual knowledge components, as the approach results in a (theoretically) exponential
      increase in the number of embeddings, each of which has only a few occurrences. In principle,
316
      MHSA embeddings involve the most general and powerful aggregation of knowledge components,
317
      accounting for both individual and interaction effects. Empirically, we observe that for knowledge
318   tracing tasks with small knowledge-component-to-question ratios, simple aggregations like mean
319   embeddings and unique set embeddings of knowledge components seem to work equally well, while
320   MHSA embeddings are difficult to optimize and result in overfitting. We conjecture, that MHSA
321   embeddings perform best in more complicated settings with large data, as we see comparatively
322   better performance for larger datasets with many knowledge components, such as the Ednet dataset
323   (see Section 5.1). This conjecture is also supported by experiments on synthetic data, where we
      experiment with varying knowledge-component-to-question ratios and dataset sizes (see Section 5.3).

      Expanded and domain-inspired interaction representations in related work The interaction
325   embeddings proposed for KTSTs build upon a principled set representation, use standard machine
326   learning paradigms for feature extraction and are domain-agnostic. This is in contrast with many
327   models proposed in related work. We highlight problems of the expanded representation of knowledge
328   tracing sequences prevalent in recent publications (e.g. Ghosh et al., 2020; Liu et al., 2023b; 2022) in
329   Section 3. Additionally, the expanded representation violates permutation invariance, as the order of
330   knowledge components influences both the transformation within the model as well as final probability
331   scores. The order of knowledge components is provided implicitly through causal masking or/and
332   explicitly in the custom attention mechanism (e.g. Ghosh et al., 2020) or via positional encodings (e.g.
333
      Liu et al., 2023b). Furthermore, interaction representations proposed in related work are often domain
      inspired and unnecessarily complex. Specifically, we consider all three interaction embeddings
334
      proposed for KTSTs conceptually simpler than domain inspired Rasch embeddings used in Ghosh
335
      et al. (2020) and other recent knowledge tracing models. Rasch embeddings prescribe, that questions
336   are represented as ex = ec ⊕(eq ·vc ), the addition of two knowledge component embeddings ec ∈ Rd
337   and vc ∈ Rd , where the latter is supposed to capture knowledge component specific variations and is
338   scaled by eq ∈ R, a scalar embedding of questions that “controls how far this question deviates from
339   the [knowledge component] it covers” (Ghosh et al., 2020). Interactions are represented analogously
340   with knowledge-component-response embeddings and knowledge-component-response variation
341   vectors. Given these design choices, Rasch embeddings require the flawed expanded representation.
342   In the next section, we provide empirical evidence, that straightforward set representations are
343   sufficient to capture relevant domain information within knowledge tracing tasks.

345   5       E XPERIMENTS
346
347   In this section, we empirically evaluate the proposed knowledge tracing set transformers on a bench-
348   mark with standardized tasks, preprocessing, data splits, and fixed test sets for various educational
349   datasets (pykt, Liu et al., 2022). We thus provide evidence that KTSTs set a new state-of-the-art.
350   Furthermore, we verify our design choices within the transformer architecture (Section 4.1) as
351   well as the proposed learnable modification of attention matrices used in KTSTs (Section 4.2) in
352   an ablation study. We additionally experiment with synthetic data, generated according to classic
353   multidimensional item response theory (MIRT, Reckase, 2009), to demonstrate the advantages of
354   the proposed MHSA aggregation (Section 4.3) in large data knowledge tracing settings with high
355
      knowledge-component-to-question-ratios.

      6   C ONCLUSION
522
523
      In this paper, we proposed knowledge tracing set transformers (KTSTs), a straightforward, yet princi-
524   pled and performant model class for knowledge tracing prediction tasks. We proposed a simplified
525   variant of learnable modification of attention matrices, as well as principled set representations of
526   student interactions that are permutation invariant with respect to sets of knowledge components and
527   that do not rely on domain knowledge. As a result, KTSTs are conceptually simpler than previous
528   state-of-the-art approaches. We proposed three different interaction representations that come with
529   different properties. The simplest representation, based on mean embeddings, empirically performed
530   best. In contrast, the representation with the highest capacity, based on MHSA embeddings, showed
531   promising results for more involved settings with large sample sizes and multiple knowledge compo-
532
      nents per question. Overall, KTSTs establish new state-of-the-art performance for knowledge tracing
      prediction tasks.
533
534   A limitation of KTSTs is that the model class does not include an interpretable internal state that
535   reflects the current knowledge state of students (cf. Gervet et al., 2020). However, a qualitative
536   inspection of the implicit knowledge representation could be insightful, for example by using post-
537   hoc model-agnostic interpretations (e.g. Rodrigues et al., 2022). This could be a valuable avenue in
538
      future work.

768   A.2    O PTIMIZATION DETAILS
769
      Learnable ALiBi ALiBi (Press et al., 2021), the attention mechanism we build upon in KTSTs,
770   was originally introduced for improved extrapolation to longer sequence lengths in language models.
771   In experiments conducted by Press et al. (2021), a learnable ALiBi variant was not found to be helpful
772   for NLP tasks. In contrast, we find that learning decay parameters θ (as introduced in Section 4.2)
773   comes with a statistically significant performance improvement for KTSTs (cf. Section 5.2). We
774   however noticed that optimization required a higher learning rate for θ compared to other model
775   parameters. Details can be found in configuration files for the benchmark experiments and ablation.
776
777   Regularization of question embeddings Empirical results for KTSTs suggest, that strong reg-
778   ularization of question embeddings eq is important for high performance on knowledge tracing
779   tasks. In practice, we propose to initialize all parameters in question embeddings to zero, by setting
780
      eq = 0. In an ablation study, we have retrained the best model configurations of KTST (mean) for
      the experiments on the benchmark datasets in Section 5.1 without this 0-init initialization. Results
781
      are provided in Tables 4 and 5, where we observe a significant drop in performance on most datasets
782
      (markers ∗ and ◦ indicate whether KTST (mean) is statistically superior or equal, respectively, using
783   a paired t-test at the 0.01 significance level). Overall, the ablation study thus supports our choice of
784   initialization. Question embedding initialization was previously identified as important for the more
785   complex Rasch embeddings (within AKT, by Ghosh et al., 2020, compare also our description of
786   Rasch embeddings in Section 4.3). Notably, we find that some recent publications do not properly
787   account for initialization within baseline models (cf. Lee et al., 2022; Im et al., 2023).
