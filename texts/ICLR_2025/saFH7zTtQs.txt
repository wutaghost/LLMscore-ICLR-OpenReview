009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Due to the tractable analysis and control, linear dynamical systems (LDSs) pro-
012            vide a fundamental mathematical tool for time-series data modeling in various
013            disciplines. Particularly, many LDSs have sparse system matrices because inter-
014            actions among variables are limited or only a few significant relationships exist.
015            However, available learning algorithms for LDSs lack the ability to learn system
016            matrices with the sparsity constraint. To address this issue, we impose sparsity-
017            promoting priors on system matrices and explore the expectation–maximization
018
               (EM) algorithm to give a maximum a posteriori (MAP) estimate of both hidden
               states and system matrices from noisy observations. In addition, we find that many
019
               learning algorithms based on the gradient descent method use an inappropriate
020
               derivative rule, because they neglect the inherent symmetry of noise covariance
021            matrices. Here, we consider the derivative rule of structured matrices during the
022            optimization process to guarantee their symmetry. Experimental results on simu-
023            lation and real-world problems illustrate that the proposed algorithm significantly
024            improves learning accuracy over classical ones.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Linear dynamical systems (LDSs) are fundamental mathematical models for analyzing time-series
030   data with application in robotics (Mamakoukas et al., 2019; 2020), systems biology (Jin et al., 2020b;
031   Pillonetto & Ljung, 2023), and natural language processing (Smith et al., 1999; Belanger & Kakade,
032   2015). Basically, LDSs consider a linear transformation between finite-dimensional hidden states
033
      disturbed by input signals and noise to capture the time evolution of systems (Hazan et al., 2017).
      In addition, LDSs are also widely used to approximate complex nonlinear systems in industrial
034
      processes given their relative simplicity (Yuan et al., 2017; Lusch et al., 2018). Due to a complete
035
      rigorous theory available on LDSs, learning LDSs from noisy observations can enable us to make
036   tractable analysis and control of systems (Chen & Poor, 2022; Bakshi et al., 2023).
037
038   In this paper, we focus on learning LDSs with sparse system matrices for two important reasons.
039
      First, the learned LDSs should include the minimally required parameters to explain time-series
      data following the Occam’s razor principle. Additionally, many real-world systems have sparse
040
      topology because each state or measurement variable only depends on a few other state variables
041
      and inputs (Efroni et al., 2022). For example, a gene only regulates the expression of a limited
042   number of other genes in gene regulatory networks (He et al., 2024). In industry, communication
043   systems usually have sparse topology to reduce energy consumption (Jin et al., 2020a;b). However,
044   available learning algorithms lack the ability to learn LDSs with the sparsity constraint on system
045   matrices. In addition, many algorithms neglect the inherent symmetry of noise covariance matrices
046   during the optimization process.
047
      To learn the LDSs with sparse system matrices, we impose the sparsity-promoting prior on them
048   to balance model complexity and modeling error in this paper (Wang et al., 2024). Subsequently,
049   we can combine the likelihood and prior functions to derive the posterior distribution of system
050   matrices following the Bayes’ rule. However, directly maximizing such a posterior distribution to
051   estimate system matrices is intractable because the states of LDSs are unknown. To address this
052   issue, we explore the expectation–maximization (EM) algorithm to give an alternate maximum a
053   posteriori (MAP) estimate of hidden states and system matrices. In the expectation step, we use
      the Rauch–Tung–Striebel (RTS) smoother, also known as the Kalman smoother, to give a closed-

      2       R ELATED WORK
100
101
      Prediction error minimization. Prediction error minimization (PEM) learns LDSs by minimiz-
102   ing one-step prediction error objective via gradient-based optimization methods (Ljung, 2002;
103   Katayama et al., 2005). Expanding LDSs into linear AutoRegressive Moving Average with eX-
104   ogenous excitation (ARMAX) or AutoRegressive Moving Average (ARMA) models, Li & Zhang
105   (2006) leverage the PEM-based method to estimate the second-order structural parameters of linear
106   structural systems. Given a symmetric transition matrix, Hazan et al. (2017) present an efficient
107   method for the online prediction of discrete-time LDSs by formulating system identification as an
      online PEM problem. In particular, Abdalmoaty & Hjalmarsson (2019) extends PEM for learning


                                                                                    2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      stochastic nonlinear models recently. However, PEM is found to be sensitive to initial values and
109   cannot characterize the sparsity of system matrices (Martens, 2010).
110
111   Subspace state-space system identification. Basically, subspace state-space system identification
112
      (4SID) algorithms project data Hankel matrices onto certain subspaces to estimate the extended
      observability matrix and hidden states using linear algebra tools (Larimore, 1990; Verahegen &
113
      Dewilde, 1992; Van Overschee & De Moor, 1994). Leveraging the least squares method, system
114
      matrices can thus be recovered from either the extended observability matrix or hidden states (Fa-
115   voreel et al., 2000). Based on principal component analysis, Wang & Qin (2002) present a new 4SID
116   algorithm to learn LDSs under the errors-in-variables situation. By choosing different weighting
117   matrices to perform the singular value decomposition, Van Overschee & De Moor (2012) provide
118   a geometric framework to unify almost all classical 4SID methods. Further, Huang et al. (2016)
119   presents the Weight-Least-Square method to learn stable LDSs by multiplying the unstable compo-
120   nent with a weight matrix. However, 4SID algorithms learn system matrices via the least squares
121   method and thus cannot produce sparse system matrices (Tibshirani, 1996). In particular, it is widely
122   recognized that such algorithms generally cannot obtain accurate system matrices as required (Qin,
123
      2006; Martens, 2010).
124   Maximum likelihood estimation. Because the joint likelihood function of LDSs involves hidden
125   states, the EM algorithm is employed to give the maximum likelihood estimation (MLE) of system
126   matrices (Shumway & Stoffer, 1982; Ghahramani & Hinton, 1996). Leveraging the EM algorithm,
127   the distribution of hidden states can be explicitly derived using the Kalman smoother based on the
128   current estimate of system matrices. Subsequently, it updates system matrices by maximizing the
129
      expected log-likelihood with respect to the hidden states. Gibson & Ninness (2005) present a robust
      MLE of LDSs by implementing the expectation and maximization steps via the LR and Cholesky
130
      factorisation respectively. To increase the efficiency of EM for learning LDSs, Martens (2010) pro-
131
      poses an approximate second-order statistics (ASOS) scheme to approximate the expectation step.
132   Combining EM and Lagrangian relaxation, Umenberger et al. (2018) use semidefinite program-
133   ming to optimize the tight bounds on the likelihood to learn LDSs with model stability constraints.
134   However, such learning algorithms lack the ability to deal with sparse system matrices. Particularly,
135   an inappropriate derivative rule is used to take the derivatives of the likelihood function with respect
136   to noise covariance matrices due to the neglect of the inherent symmetry of them.

141   Generally, LDSs describe time-series data {(ut , yt )}Tt=1 through the following stochastic difference
142   equation (Shumway et al., 2000):
143

      state, A ∈ Rn×n , B ∈ Rn×p , C ∈ Rm×n , and D ∈ Rm×p are the system matrices, and εt ∼
148
      N (0, R) and wt ∼ N (0, Q) are the process and measurement noise, respectively. In fact, LDSs are
149
      widely used to model complex systems and have been received successful applications in industrial
150   processes (Favoreel et al., 2000). To make predictions about future outputs and unknown states and
151   realize the control of systems, it is necessary to propose an algorithm to learn the model parameters
152   A, B, C, D, R, and Q from time-series data {(ut , yt )}Tt=1 . In particular, many systems have
153   sparse topology to enable efficient working mechanisms (Jin et al., 2020a). As a result, the proposed
154   algorithm needs to integrate such priori information into the learning process.
155
      Sparsity-promoting prior. Sparsity-promoting priors can enforce the sparsity of model parameters
156   by balancing model complexity and modeling error (Wang et al., 2023; Tripura & Chakraborty,

158   prior severing as its conjugate prior can be imposed on each component of the unknown system
159   matrices to promote their sparsity. Generally, the Student’s t-distribution prior is implemented in a
160   hierarchical way (Tipping, 2001). It imposes a Gaussian prior on the system matrices and then adopt
161   an Inverse-Gamma hyperprior on the unknown variance of the Gaussian distribution. For example,
      we can impose the Student’s t-distribution prior on the system matrix A to promote its sparsity as

        demonstrate that such a learning algorithm can learn LDSs with sparse system matrices accurately.

377     For LDSs, the similarity transformation is an important mathematical operation to transform them
        into different coordinate systems, making it easier to analyze system properties like controllability,

379   xt through the relation:
380

381
382   where P ∈ Rn×n is a nonsingular matrix. As such, we can derive an equivalent realization of the
383   original LDSs as follows (see Appendix C):
384                                        xt = Axt−1 + But + εt ,                                       (36)
385

388
      transformation makes it particularly difficult to accurately learn system matrices. Given the input
      signals {ut }Tt=1 , the transformed LDSs can produce the same output data {yt }Tt=1 as that of the
389
      original LDSs. Hence, classical learning algorithms for LDSs only learn the system matrices up to a
390
      similar transformation (Viberg, 1994). For LDSs with sparse system matrices, such a transformation
391   changes not only the values but, more importantly, the topological structure of the system matrices,
392   resulting in misinterpretation of intrinsic working mechanisms.
393
394   4.1   B ENEFIT OF SPARSE - PROMOTING PRIORS
395
396   Unlike classical learning algorithms, the proposed algorithm learns LDSs with sparse system matri-
397   ces by adopting a sparsity-promoting prior to balance model complexity and modeling error. Given
398   the sparsity constraint of system matrices, the similarity transformation cannot be applied using any
399   arbitrary nonsingular matrix. For the LDSs with sparse system matrices following the Occam’s ra-
400
      zor principle, the nonsingular matrix is typically restricted to be a generalized permutation matrix;
      otherwise, the transformed LDSs will include redundant parameters to describe the systems. For
401
      example, if we consider the LDSs with sparse system matrices as follows:
402
403                             0 0.9            2 0              2 0           1.5 0
                       A=                ,B =            ,C =            ,D =              ,           (38)
404
                               0.9 0             0 2              0 2            0 1.5
405   it is easy to verify that such a system follows the Occam’s razor principle because the rank of system
406   matrices is equal to the number of nonzero components. Hence, we can derive the nonsingular matrix
407   P must satisfies
408                                              a 0                0 a
                                          P =             or P =           ,                             (39)
409                                              0 b                 b 0
410   where a and b are arbitrary constants. As such, the transformed system matrices do not introduce
411   additional parameters to increase model complexity.
412
      Note that applying the similarity transformation with a generalized permutation matrix to the original
413
      state variables will scale their magnitudes and reorder them. However, it will scale the nonzero
414   components and permute the rows or columns of system matrices accordingly. Hence, an additional
415   advantage of the sparse-promoting prior is its ability to maximally preserve the inherent topological
416   structure among the variables. While the learned system matrices differ from the true ones in scale,
417   such a difference is only caused by the scaled definition of state variables. Hence, the learned LDS
418   has the same topological structure and dynamic behavior as the real one.

      6    D ISCUSSION
508
509
      To learn the LDSs with sparse system matrices, we impose sparsity-promoting priors on system ma-
510   trices to balance model complexity and modeling error in this paper. Following the MAP principle,
511   we then learn system matrices by exploring the EM algorithm to maximize the loss function com-
512   posed of the priors and likelihood function. During the optimization process, we use the derivative
513   rule of structured matrices to ensure the symmetry of noise covariance matrices. In addition, we
514   find that the sparsity-promoting prior is capable of retaining the topological structure of the LDSs,
515   as the nonsingular matrix of the similarity transformation is typically limited to be a generalized
516   permutation matrix. Hence, the proposed algorithm is more useful for us to explore the interacting
517   laws of the LDSs compared to the classical ones.
518   There still remains some potential limitations for the proposed algorithm. First, it cannot determine
519   the order n of the system from data directly. While we can compare the performance of the learned
520   systems across different orders to select the best one, such a method is quite exhaustive. The other
521   limitation is that the similarity transformation may shrink many parameters to very small values,
522   potentially leading to numerical errors. However, we believe that the proposed algorithm sheds a
523   light on the learning of LDSs with sparse system matrices. In our future work, we hope to explore
524
      how to exactly learn LDSs with additional constraints.
