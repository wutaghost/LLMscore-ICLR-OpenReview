009                                              A BSTRACT
010
011            Large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated impressive few-shot in-context
012            learning (ICL) abilities. Still, we show that they are sometimes prone to a ‘copy-
013            ing bias’, where they copy answers from provided examples instead of learning
014            the underlying patterns. In this work, we propose a novel and simple method to
015            mitigate such copying bias. First, we create a synthetic task and use the Integrated
016            Gradients method to identify neurons that prioritize copying over generalization.
017
               We demonstrate that pruning these neurons consistently improves performance
               across a diverse set of ICL tasks. We also show that our method is applicable
018
               across various LLM architectures, including Transformers and State-Space Mod-
019
               els, without requiring modifications. In our analysis, we adopt a task-recognition
020            perspective on ICL and examine task vectors (Hendel et al., 2023) induced by the
021            model. We find that pruning enhances the quality of these vectors, suggesting that
022            the pruned neurons previously hindered effective task recognition.

025   1   I NTRODUCTION
026
027   In-Context Learning (ICL) (Brown et al., 2020) has recently emerged as a powerful and simple al-
028   ternative to traditional training and fine-tuning. ICL involves presenting a Large Language Models
029   (LLM) with a “context” consisting of several example pairs, each containing an input and its cor-
030
      responding correct output, followed by a test example for prediction. For instance, consider the
      following prompt:
031
032                               Dolphin → 2, Beautiful → 5, Octopus →
033
034   In this case, the model must leverage the contextual information from the given examples to identify
035   the underlying pattern of mapping words to vowel counts; based on this, it must then predict that the
036   correct answer for “Octopus” is “3”.
037   While ICL has shown considerable effectiveness, its use in few-shot scenarios faces significant chal-
038   lenges (Zhao et al., 2021; Razeghi et al., 2022). In these settings, the inherent scarcity of labeled
039   examples becomes a critical bottleneck, as ICL often requires a substantial number of in-context
040   examples to generalize effectively. Moreover, the performance of ICL is highly sensitive to various
041   aspects of the prompt and the presentation of the examples. Factors such as the specific wording
042   of the prompt (Wang et al., 2024), the order in which the examples are presented (Lu et al., 2021),
043   and their relevance to the target example can significantly influence the outcome. Consequently, in
044
      domains where labeled data is limited, these challenges collectively hinder the reliable application
      of ICL, emphasizing the need for strategies that can mitigate sensitivity and make the most of the
045
      scarce examples available.
046
047   Recent research has primarily addressed these challenges by focusing on prompt formulation strate-
048   gies, including techniques for selecting optimal templates and examples (Zhou et al., 2023b; Hao
049   et al., 2022; Lu et al., 2022), as well as calibration methods (Han et al., 2023; Zhao et al., 2021).
050
      However, existing work has not yet explored how errors in in-context learning (ICL) relate to the
      internal processes of LLMs or how to correct them through targeted model modifications.
051
052   Our study takes a novel approach by investigating neural activation patterns related to a common
053   challenge in ICL: copying errors. Referring back to the vowel-counting example, a copying error
      would occur if the model were to output “2” or “5” for Octopus, instead of the correct answer “3”.


                                                        1
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


054
      In these cases, the model appears to directly copy an answer from the provided examples rather than
055   generating the correct, novel response based on the induced underlying pattern.
056
057   In this work, we hypothesize that there is a small subset of neurons in language models that prioritize
058
      copying behavior over task recognition. We posit that these mechanisms can be task-agnostic; that
      is, the same neurons are responsible for this reasoning shortcut across a range of tasks. We further
059
      hypothesize that deactivating these neurons will make the model less likely to follow shortcuts and
060
      encourage it to focus on recognizing underlying regularities.
061
062   To identify these neurons, we employ the vowel-counting task and apply the attribution method,
063   Integrated Gradients (IG) (Sundararajan et al., 2017), to trace the copying errors to individual neu-
064
      rons. We then select the top contributing neurons as “copying neurons.” The vowel-counting task
      is particularly interesting, as it appears challenging for a range of models and elicits the copying
065
      behavior in these models. We demonstrate that deactivating the neurons identified using this single
066
      task improves results across a diverse range of ICL problems, making our method practical – since
067   the neurons do not need to be selected for each individual task – and confirming the existence of a
068   general mechanism prioritizing the shortcut over reasoning.
069
070
      In summary, our contributions are fourfold: (1) We identify the copying bias in ICL and demonstrate
      that LLMs, particularly smaller ones, suffer from a high percentage of these errors. (2) We intro-
071
      duce a method to identify specific neurons responsible for triggering the copying behavior (copying
072
      neurons). (3) We show that pruning these identified neurons leads to out-of-the-box improvement
073   across a wide range of ICL tasks. (4) We utilize the task vectors framework introduced by (Hendel
074   et al., 2023), quantifying a model’s ability to recognize and adapt to tasks during ICL. Using this
075   framework, we provide evidence that pruning the copying neurons enhances the quality of task vec-
076   tors, indicating improved task representation. This finding explains the observed performance gains
077   across various ICL tasks.

      2   R ELATED W ORK
080
081
      ICL, first introduced by (Brown et al., 2020), has attracted significant interest in recent years due to
082
      its ability to enable large language models to perform complex downstream tasks without explicit
083
      fine-tuning. By leveraging contextual information provided within the input prompts, these mod-
084   els can dynamically adapt their behavior and generate contextually relevant outputs across a wide
085   range of tasks. While it is commonly associated with Transformer architectures, ICL has also been
086   explored in other model architectures, such as State-Space Models and the RWKV model (Grazzi
087   et al., 2024b; Park et al., 2024). Leading to a wide line of works that seek to improve the effective-
088   ness of ICL mechanisms (Zhao et al., 2021; Wei et al., 2023; Chu et al., 2023; Zhou et al., 2023a;
089   Wei et al., 2024; Li et al., 2024), as well as studies that aim to explain the underlying processes and
090   dynamics of how models internalize and utilize context (Min et al., 2022; Liu et al., 2022; Xie et al.,
091   2022; Olsson et al., 2022; Von Oswald et al., 2023; Dai et al., 2022b).
092   Works proposing methods to improve ICL have mainly focused on prompt selection and prompt for-
093   mulation strategies, Zhou et al. (2023a) propose a different prompting strategy that breaks down a
094   complex problem into a series of simpler subproblems and then solves them in sequence. Zhang et al.
095   (2022b) reformulate the example selection for ICL as a sequential decision problem, and propose a
096   reinforcement learning algorithm for identifying generalizable policies to select demonstration ex-
097   amples. Sorensen et al. (2022) introduces a method for unsupervised selection of prompt templates
098
      by maximizing the mutual information between the input and the corresponding model output. Lu
      et al. (2022) show that the order in which the samples are provided can make a significant difference
099
      and propose a method for finding the optimal permutation.
100
101   While the majority of approaches to improving ICL have focused on various methods of prompt
102   engineering, such as prompt selection and formulation strategies, our work takes a fundamentally
103   different approach. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first attempt to enhance ICL through
104
      a neuron-level analysis, specifically by identifying and pruning neurons that contribute to copying
      (which we define in Section 3.1) within large language models.
105
106   Neuron-Level analysis involves examining individual neurons within the model to determine their
107   specific roles. Relevant studies in this area have aimed to understand and categorize neurons into
      groups based on their functional roles. For example, Voita et al. (2023) show that individual neu-


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      rons in LLMs correspond to different groups such as dead neurons, positional neurons, and N-gram
109   neurons. Furthermore, Gurnee et al. (2024) discusses an additional group of categories including
110   entropy neurons, alphabet neurons, syntax neurons, and semantic neurons. Chen et al. (2024) iden-
111   tify safety neurons, which are responsible for safety behaviors. Neuron-level analysis was further
112   expanded to study multilingual LLMs. Tang et al. (2024) detect language-specific and language-
113   agnostic related neurons in multilingual language models. Neuron-Level analysis is typically per-
114   formed through activation analysis, where one examines the patterns of neuron activations across
115   various inputs (Voita et al., 2023; Gurnee et al., 2024; Stolfo et al., 2024). Attribution methods,
116   such as Integrated Gradients (Sundararajan et al., 2017), are also employed to quantify the contri-
117
      bution of individual neurons to the model’s output, thus, allowing the discovery of different neuron
      familiess (Dai et al., 2022a; Shi et al., 2024).

122   This section presents our method for detecting and mitigating copying in ICL. First, in section 3.1
123   we revisit the ICL setting, formally define what are the copying errors, and introduce the Integrated
124   Gradients attribution method. In section 3.2 we elaborate on how we create a synthetic dataset that
125   will be used as a proxy for our proposed detection method, and in section 3.3 we present our method
126   for detecting and mitigating copying neurons.
127
128   3.1   P RELIMINARIES
129
      In-Context Learning ICL enables a model f to adapt to downstream tasks without any parameter
130
      updates. This is achieved by forming a prompt p that includes concatenated training examples. In
131
      ICL, a prompt p is constructed by linking the task inputs with their corresponding labels as follows:
132
133                                  p = x1 : y1 , x2 : y2 , . . . xn : yn , xn+1 :                         (1)
134
      Using this prompt, the model f predicts the most probable label y that completes the prefix p ac-
135
      cording to the function f . In this framework, few-shot learning is characterized by the number of
136   examples in the prompt. Furthermore, we denote S = [y1 , y2 , . . . yn ] as the set of the in-context
137   example answers for prompt p, then we say p is an |S| = n-shot in-context prompt.
138
139   Copying Bias Copying bias, as we define it, refers to the phenomenon where a language model
140   f returns an incorrect answer that is one of the labels S of the in-context samples provided in the
141   prompt. In other words, given a prompt p under the n-shot ICL settings, a prediction yn+1 is called
142   a copying prediction if (1) : yn+1 is a wrong prediction and (2) : yn+1 ∈ S.
143
      We hypothesize that there exists a small number of neurons, which we call copying neurons, that
144   trigger the model to copy responses from the prompt examples S of the prompt p. The identification
145   of these neurons is, therefore, crucial for understanding how LLMs balance copying and generaliza-
146   tion, and for enhancing the reliability and interpretability of these models. We further hypothesize
147   that pruning these neurons by setting their weights to zero would encourage the model to reason
148   rather than solely rely on copying, thereby improving its ability to generalize under few-shot ICL.
149

152   its input features. It applies to any differentiable model and is computationally efficient. IG works
153   by generating attributions based on integrating the gradients as the input varies between a baseline
154
      and the final input of interest (the path):

      3.2   P ROXY ICL DATASET G ENERATION
173
174   The Integrated Gradients (IG) method operates by backpropagating the probability of the prediction.
175   To effectively utilize IG within our framework, we require a set of in-context examples with ground-
176   truth labels. We avoid relying on access to target task data for neuron selection, as this would limit
177   the practicality of our approach. Instead, we utilize a synthetic proxy dataset to identify and prune
178   neurons on evaluation tasks. This approach also aligns with our research hypothesis that copying
179   neurons are largely task-agnostic. This synthetic dataset is employed within the Integrated Gradients
180   framework to identify copying neurons.
181   The specific task we choose is that of vowel counting since the mapping from a word to such struc-
182   tural attributes requires reasoning. LLMs can occasionally make errors on this and similar tasks,1
183   potentially outputting copying responses. The synthetic samples we utilize simply map an arbitrary
184   word to its corresponding vowel counts (e.g., apple: 2). To construct a diverse set of examples, we
185   extract words from a dictionary and calculate their respective vowel counts. In our implementation,
186   we used two examples per prompt to keep the problem tractable while still allowing us to study
187
      copying behavior. To eliminate the possibility of label repetition, we ensured that each prompt’s
      examples (S) contained distinct vowel counts, with the target word’s vowel count always differing
188
      from those in S. We sampled words with vowel counts ranging from 1 to 8, providing sufficient
189
      diversity while avoiding repetition within prompts. For dataset construction, we generated 400 ICL
190   prompts, each using a 2-shot format: 300 examples were used for computing neuron importance
191   scores, and 100 were reserved for validation to determine the optimal pruning configuration. The
192   test answers were carefully designed to never appear in the prompt responses, ensuring clear evalu-
193   ation of copying behavior.

303   Our approach offers a generic method for detecting copying neurons, applicable to any language
304
      model. To demonstrate the generalizability of our method across different models and tasks, we
      conduct extensive experiments on a diverse set of LLMs. This includes the recent state-space mod-
305
      els, such as Mamba (Gu & Dao, 2023) as well as a broad spectrum of transformer-based models,

      including OPT (Zhang et al., 2022a), GPT2 (Radford et al., 2019), Bloom (Le Scao et al., 2023) and

      about the data, refer to Appendix A.

      set introduced in Section 3.2 in order to find the optimal pruning rate and the optimal block that
368
      maximizes the validation accuracy over the proxy ICL validation set, we apply multiple pruning
369   rates ranging from 1% to 10%, in 1% increments (i.e., [1%, 2%, 3%, . . . , 10%]) over each layer in
370   all of the blocks in the model, and use the best validation accuracy performing configuration to use
371   for the unseen ICL tasks. This procedure allows us to determine the optimal block and the optimal
372   number of neurons to prune for maximizing the accuracy on the validation proxy ICL set. The layers
373   we choose to apply the detection and pruning procedures are summarized in Appendix B.

503   5       C ONCLUSIONS AND D ISCUSSION
504
505   We presented a novel method to mitigate copying bias in few-shot In-Context Learning by pruning
506
      neurons that are linked to this behavior according to the integrated gradients interoperability method.
      Our approach consistently improved performance across a variety of ICL tasks and model architec-
507
      tures. These findings highlight the potential of targeted neuron pruning as an effective strategy for
508
      optimizing the capabilities of large language models.
509
510   The “out-of-the-box” improvements provided by our method, without the need for task-specific data
511   or fine-tuning, have significant practical implications for deploying more reliable few-shot learning
512
      systems. Our approach allows for the enhancement of LLM performance across a wide range of
      tasks using only a simple, synthetic dataset for neuron identification. Moreover, the consistent im-
513
      provements observed across different model architectures suggest that this method could be broadly
514
      applicable, potentially becoming a standard post-processing step in LLM deployment pipelines.
515
516   The success of our pruning method in improving performance across various tasks indicates that
517   “copying neurons” may be acting as a form of shortcut, inhibiting the model’s ability to engage
518
      in more sophisticated reasoning processes. This observation aligns with recent work on shortcut
      learning in neural networks (Yom Din et al., 2024; Belrose et al., 2023) and suggests that in-context
519
      learning quality could potentially be improved by carefully modulating the influence of different
520
      neuron groups or pathways.
521
522   Our results suggest that by pruning copying neurons, we enhance the model’s ability to distill task-
523   relevant information from demonstrations, leading to more effective task vectors. This raises in-
524
      teresting questions about the relationship between neuron-level representations and the higher-level
      task embeddings captured by task vectors. Specifically, it may be useful to consider the representa-
525
      tion in a way that disentangles multiple activation pathways.

      We believe copying errors dominate because it’s a “safer” failure mode for the model - repeating
813   a previously seen answer rather than generating a potentially incorrect novel response. This aligns
814   with the training objective of minimizing unlikely outputs. For the vowel-counting task, copying
815   errors occur at a similar rate to other tasks, suggesting that copying behavior is a general phe-
816   nomenon rather than task-specific. This consistency between in-distribution and out-of-distribution
817   tasks supports our hypothesis that we’re identifying fundamental copying mechanisms in the model

       I.1   P ROXY DATASET A BLATION
1407
1408   To investigate the generality of our copying neuron detection approach, we conducted experiments
1409   using different proxy ICL tasks beyond vowel counting. We explored tasks from both linguistic
1410   (e.g., antonyms T11) and knowledge (e.g., person-language mapping T16) domains. Our results
1411   demonstrate that neurons identified using any of these alternative proxy tasks led to improvements
1412   across our evaluations.
1413   Through these experiments, we identified three key criteria for an effective proxy task:
1414
1415          1. Clear, deterministic rules that require pattern recognition rather than memorization
1416          2. Ability to elicit copying behavior from the model
1417
              3. Sufficiently constrained output space to reliably detect copying errors
1418
1419   While vowel counting meets these criteria particularly well and enables straightforward synthetic
1420   data generation, our results show it is not uniquely special - other structured tasks with similar
1421   properties can serve as effective proxies for copying neuron detection.
1422
       We evaluate the performance using different proxy tasks for neuron detection, with results averaged
1423   across several linguistic tasks (present simple to gerund, present to past simple, present to past
1424   perfect, and singular to plural). Tables 16 and 17 show the results using linguistic antonyms and
1425   knowledge person-language mapping as proxy tasks, respectively.
