011                                               A BSTRACT
012
013            Heterogeneous multi-teacher Knowledge distillation attempt to learn a versatile
014            student neural network from multiple pre-trained heterogeneous teachers. But
015
               current methods face issues with a lack of independence and alignment in het-
               erogeneous knowledge. To address this issue, we propose a novel method called
016
               Multi-Expert Collaboration (MEC). Our approach aggregates multiple expert clas-
017
               sifiers within the student model, replacing the conventional single-head architec-
018            ture. By ensuring that each expert’s independent classifier operates without in-
019            terfering with others, we enhance the independence of heterogeneous knowledge.
020            Inspired by Helmholtz Free Energy (HFE) theory, we introduce an anchor-based
021            HFE self-normalization strategy to align the heterogeneous knowledge effectively.
022            This method ensures consistent energy levels across all classifiers, allowing the ap-
023            propriate classifier to achieve the highest confidence for in-distribution data. Ex-
024            tensive experiments on CIFAR-100 and ImageNet-100 datasets demonstrate that
025            MEC significantly outperforms existing heterogeneous multi-teacher knowledge
026
               distillation methods, achieving an average accuracy improvement of over 10%.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   Deep neural networks have achieved remarkable success across various applications, and numerous
031   deep network models optimized for different tasks and trained on diverse datasets have been made
032   publicly available, providing researchers with a rich repository of model resources. Despite this
033   abundance, effectively leveraging these heterogeneous teacher networks to train a student model
034   capable of handling multiple tasks remains a pressing and unresolved challenge in the field.
035
      Knowledge Distillation (KD) Hinton et al. (2015) methods are primarily used to transfer knowledge
036   from a single complex teacher model to a light-weight student model. The core idea is to transfer
037   knowledge by having the student model mimic the output logits or soft targets generated by the
038   teacher model, thereby capturing the teacher’s learned representations and generalization capabili-
039   tiesBeyer et al. (2022); Gong et al. (2023); Agand (2024). However, heterogeneous multi-teacher
040   models involve multiple teachers trained based on different architectures, training data, and task ob-
041   jectives. KD methods, being limited to homogeneous knowledge transfer, are unable to effectively
042   merge knowledge from multiple pre-trained heterogeneous teacher models.
043   Recently, researchers have explored a heterogeneous multi-teacher knowledge distillation approach-
044   Knowledge Amalgamation (KA) Shen (2019); Thadajarassiri et al. (2021); Xu et al. (2022); Zhang
045   et al. (2023); Gao et al. (2024), which aggregates the knowledge of multiple teachers into a student
046   model. As shown in Figure 1a, KA methods usually adopt a dual approach of aligning common
047   features and extracting soft targets.
048   However, current heterogeneous multi-teacher knowledge distillation only refines the student’s
049   knowledge by simply concatenating logits of teacher models and select the class with the highest
050   in the student’s logit. As the number of teachers increases, the mutual interference between hetero-
051   geneous knowledge in the student model will affect the performance of the model. When multiple
052   expert models’ predictions are simply integrated into a student model, the heterogeneity of knowl-
053   edge makes it difficult for the student model to determine which knowledge is more representative
      or accurate.

091   2     R ELATED WORK
092
093   2.1   K NOWLEDGE DISTILLATION
094
095   Knowledge Distillation (KD) Hinton et al. (2015) is an efficient technique for model reuse and com-
096   pression, where a smaller student model is trained to replicate the behavior of a larger, more complex
097
      teacher model. The core idea is to transfer knowledge by having the student model mimic the out-
      put logits or soft targets generated by the teacher model, thereby capturing the teacher’s learned
098
      representations and generalization capabilities. The goal is to minimize the difference in proba-
099
      bilistic outputs between the teacher and the student models. Research in this area primarily focuses
100   on exploring the potential of knowledge transfer between the teacher and student models. Vari-
101   ous approaches have been investigated, including aligning intermediate layersBeyer et al. (2022);
102   Gong et al. (2023), utilizing auxiliary teacher modelsMirzadeh et al. (2020), and selecting expert
103   modelsAgand (2024).
104
105   2.2   K NOWLEDGE AMALGAMATION
106
107   Knowledge amalgamation, as an extension of Knowledge distillation, Shen (2019) was first in-
      troduced to merge knowledge from multiple expert models into a single student model suitable


                                                          2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      for all tasks, particularly in classification tasks. Traditionally, KA methods Ye et al. (2019); Shen
109   et al. (2019); Luo et al. (2019); Xu et al. (2022) train the student model by mimicking the outputs
110   of teacher models-(classification score learning) -and/or their intermediate representations (feature
111   learning). The scenario of KA has gained attention due to the prevalence of publicly available pre-
112   trained models with different architectures. In such cases, the student model cannot directly learn
113   from the features of each block of the teacher models as in typical homogeneous settings. To bal-
114   ance this heterogeneity, most previous KA methods—such as data-free knowledge amalgamation
115   Ye et al. (2020), semi-supervised knowledge amalgamation Thadajarassiri et al. (2021), weighted
116   amalgamation strategies Luo et al. (2020), and model-heterogeneous aggregated training Xu et al.
117
      (2022); Zhang et al. (2023); You et al. (2024); Gao et al. (2024) —mainly rely on classification score
      learning to achieve heterogeneous multi-teacher knowledge distillation.

241   4.1.1   M ULTI - EXPERT REPRESENTATION LEARNING
242
      We employ multi-expert representation learning to construct a universal feature extraction backbone.
243
      This involves transforming both the experts’ features and the generalist’s features into a common
244   feature space. We then minimize two loss terms: feature loss LM , which encourages generalist’s
245   feature to align with expert’s feature in common feature space, and reconstruction loss LR , which
246   ensures that transformed expert’s feature can be remapped to the original space with minimal error.
247
      To align the output feature dimensions of the experts and generalist, we apply a 1 × 1 convolutional
248
      kernel, which standardizes the output length regardless of input sizes. Directly averaging the original
249
      features FG (from the generalist) and Fi (from expert Ei ) can introduce feature heterogeneity. To
250   address this, we introduce a small network shared by both the expert and generalist models. This
251   network converts Fi and FG into a common feature space, resulting in fi and fG , which are half the
252   size of the original features. These transformed features are then projected into a low-dimensional
253   subspace, where we constrain their variations to effectively distill the important features.

      4.1.2   H ETEROGENEOUS KNOWLEDGE INDEPENDENCE AND ALIGNMENT
271
272   Inspired by studies on Helmholtz free energy (HFE) Liu et al. (2020); Wang et al. (2023), we ob-
273   served that for a given classifier, in-stage data generally exhibits higher free energy (i.e., higher
274   confidence scores) compared to out-stage data. To tackle the challenge of heterogeneous knowledge
275   alignment, we employ an anchor-based HFE self-normalization strategy, ensuring that all classifiers
276
      operate at consistent energy levels. This approach effectively aligns the knowledge across different
      experts, ensuring that the appropriate classifier achieves the highest confidence score for in-stage
277
      data, thereby enhancing the overall alignment and integration of heterogeneous knowledge within
278
      the generalist model.
279

461   6     C ONCLUSION
462
463   In this paper, we addressed the critical challenge of utilizing heterogeneous teacher networks to train
464   a student network capable of handling multiple tasks. We proposed the Multi-Expert Collaboration
465   method, which aggregates multiple expert classifiers within the student model to ensure the inde-
466   pendence of heterogeneous knowledge. By introducing an anchor-based HFE self-normalization
467   strategy, we effectively aligned the knowledge from different experts, ensuring consistent energy
468
      levels across classifiers and enhancing the model’s ability to integrate diverse information. Exten-
      sive experiments on the CIFAR-100 and ImageNet-100 datasets validated the superiority of our
469
      approach, showing an average accuracy improvement of over 10% compared to traditional hetero-
470
      geneous multi-teacher knowledge distillation methods. The MEC framework not only improves
471   classification accuracy but also enhances scalability and generalization capabilities. Our work pro-
472   vides a scalable solution for integrating diverse expert models into a unified student network, paving
473   the way for more effective utilization of pre-trained models in multi-task learning scenarios.
