009                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011            Diffusion models have shown remarkable abilities in generating realistic and high-
012            quality images from text prompts. However, a trained model remains black-box;
013            little do we know about the role of its components in exhibiting a concept such
014            as object or style. Recent works employ causal tracing to localize layers storing
015            knowledge in generative models. In this work, we approach from a more gen-
016            eral perspective and pose a question: “How do model components work jointly to
017            demonstrate knowledge?”. We adapt component attribution to decompose diffu-
018
               sion models, unveiling how a component contributes to a concept. Our framework
               allows effective model editing, in particular, we can erase a concept from diffusion
019
               models by removing positive components while remaining knowledge of other
020
               concepts. Surprisingly, we also show that there exist components that contribute
021            negatively to a concept that has not been discovered in the knowledge localization
022            approach. Experimental results confirm the role of positive and negative com-
023            ponents pinpointed by our framework, depicting a complete view of interpreting
024            generative models.

027   1   I NTRODUCTION
028
029   Recent developments in diffusion models Ho et al. (2020); Luo (2022); Sohl-Dickstein et al. (2015);
030   Song et al. (2021) have improved significantly the synthesizing capabilities, including image quality
031   and generating a wide range of knowledge. However, these models lack interpretability; we do
032   not know how they can achieve such extraordinary performance and why they can generate images
033
      from only text prompts. To understand how generative models recall concepts, a recent line of
      works studies which components in the model store knowledge. In language models, Meng et al.
034
      (2022) propose causal tracing to locate layers storing facts and reveal that knowledge is localized
035
      in middle-layer MLP modules. This method is later transferred to diffusion models in Basu et al.
036   (2023), which shows that in contrast to language models, knowledge is distributed amongst a set
037   of components in UNet and the first self-attention layer in the text-encoder. These approaches shed
038   light on interpreting generative models and allow model editing more effectively Basu et al. (2023;
039   2024). However, they only focus on knowledge storage – modules that are responsible for generating
040   the concept – and ignore the role of other modules.
041
      In this work, we pose a more general question: How do components in diffusion models contribute
042   to the generated image? Similar to the work in Shah et al. (2024), we utilize a simple linear coun-
043   terfactual estimator and propose a framework that predicts the model behavior given the presence of
044   each component. We study how model components spark off a concept, i.e. objects, styles, explicit
045   contents, etc. In contrast to prior works focusing on layers in the model, we examine more fine-
046   grained components, which are parameters in diffusion models. Our framework called Component
047   Attribution of Diffusion Model (CAD), helps discover positive components inducing the concept,
048   which is similar to knowledge storage. Furthermore, we reveal that there are also components that
049   contribute negatively to the target concept, which is missing in previous studies. Given such under-
050
      standing, we can edit the model to remove or recall a concept by ablating corresponding components.
051   Our contributions can be listed as follows:
052
053         • We propose a comprehensive framework, called CAD, that can compute the attribution
              scores of model components efficiently.

            • We propose two algorithms to edit diffusion models to erase or amplify knowledge by
055           removing positive or negative components, respectively.
056
            • We provide extensive empirical analysis to confirm the effectiveness of CAD. The analysis

070   2   R ELATED W ORKS
071
072   Knowledge Localization. Previous works Basu et al. (2023; 2024); Hase et al. (2024); Meng et al.
073   (2022); Shah et al. (2024) utilize causal analysis to identify critical layers within models where
074   knowledge is predominantly stored. Meng et al. (2022) show that language models tend to store
075   factual information in certain causal layers, where modifying these layers improves generalization
076
      and specificity. Similarly, Basu et al. (2023; 2024) apply this technique to T2I Latent Diffusion
      variants, targeting specific text-encoder and U-Net layers to remove unwanted elements such as
077
      nudity or copyrighted styles. Extending the work on generative models, Shah et al. (2024) evaluates
078
      the impact of individual components on the model’s behavior in image classification and language
079   prediction tasks. While these methods have shown impressive results in knowledge localization
080   in the model, Hase et al. (2024) discovered that editing non-causal layers can also modify stored
081   facts in the language models. This unexpected finding indicates that causal-layer edits might not
082   consistently yield the expected model’s behavior changes.
083
      Concept Erasure. Latent diffusion models (LDMs) are susceptible to generating undesirable con-
084
      tent due to their reliance on uncontrollable large-scale datasets. These issues may include nudity,
085   outdated information, or copyrighted artistic styles. Previous works Gandikota et al. (2023); Kim
086   et al. (2023); Kumari et al. (2023); Zhang et al. (2024b); Orgad et al. (2023) fine-tune only Cross-
087   Attention layers to minimize the appropriate unlearn losses, meanwhile, studies by Arad et al.
088   (2024); Basu et al. (2023) focus solely on editing text-encoder in a closed-form. Moreover, it is
089   feasible to address the simultaneous removal of multiple concepts in real-world scenarios, as pro-
090   posed by Gandikota et al. (2024); Lu et al. (2024); Xiong et al. (2024). Specifically, Gandikota
091   et al. (2024) and Lu et al. (2024) extend the cross-attention layer fine-tuning to accommodate many
092   multiple at once through a closed-form solution, while Xiong et al. (2024) focus on editing MLP
093
      layers in diffusion text-encoder, also via closed-form update. These erasure methods enable fast,
      simultaneous edits of multiple concepts, while minimizing interference with unedited ones.
094
095   Red-Teaming Attacks and Defenses. Although model fine-tuning has successfully eliminated un-
096   desirable concepts in text-to-image models, recent studies Yang et al. (2024c); Chin et al. (2024);
097   Zhang et al. (2024c); Yang et al. (2024b); Zhang et al. (2024a); Tsai et al. (2024); Pham et al.
098   (2024) demonstrate that this approach remains unreliable against various adversarial prompt at-
099
      tacks. Black-box attacks such as SneakyPrompt Yang et al. (2024c), Ring-A-bell Tsai et al. (2024),
      MMA-Diffusion Yang et al. (2024b) can bypass many existing safety mechanisms without accessing
100
      to model’s parameters, by creating unsafe prompts with similar embeddings. Concurrently, several
101
      white-box attacks such as P4D Chin et al. (2024), UnlearnDiffAtk Zhang et al. (2024c), CCE Pham
102   et al. (2024) can also make fine-tuned models regenerate sensitive outputs, by using different tech-
103   niques to craft adversarial prompts. These attacks highlight the need for robust methods against
104   red-teaming attacks, which can remove undesirable concepts and preserve the quality of generated
105   images. Several studies have introduced defense mechanisms, including Concept-Prune Chavhan
106   et al. (2024), RECE Gong et al. (2024), RACE Kim et al. (2024), and pruning methods applied to
107   existing removal works Yang et al. (2024a). These studies mark significant progress in diffusion
      image generation security, opening the way for more reliable applications.

127   4.1   D ECOMPOSING K NOWLEDGE IN D IFFUSION

129   In this work, we consider the diffusion model as a combination of building blocks wi . We define an
130   objective function J(c, w) that measures how good the model f generates the concept c with a set of
131   components w. We can inspect the model at different levels of granularity, for example, a component
132   can be a parameter, a layer, or a module. We focus our study on the most fine-grained components,
133   which are model parameters; however, we can also extend to other types of components, such as
134   layers and modules.
135   Our goal is to interpret how each component wi contributes to a concept, quantified by J(c, w).

179
180   In this section, we investigate the application of our framework and propose two algorithms to
181   remove or amplify a concept in diffusion models.
182
      Given the attribution value of model components computed in Section 4.2, we can increase or de-
183
      crease J by ablating components with positive or negative attributions. Since J(c, w) expresses how
184
      well the model generates a concept c, this process can help us edit diffusion models.
185
186
187   5.1   L OCALIZING AND E RASING K NOWLEDGE
188
189   Previous works Meng et al. (2022); Basu et al. (2023; 2024) apply causal tracing to study in gener-
190   ative models knowledge is stored in which layers. While this approach gives some insights into the
191   model, it does not show a fine-grained understanding of parametric knowledge (weak argument). In
192   contrast, our framework allows us to focus on each parameter and examine its influence on a con-
193   cept. Formally, we define positive components for a concept c as components that when we ablate,
194   the model has a lower probability of generating c.
195   We consider positive components as knowledge storage, and by finding positive components we can
196   locate knowledge in generative models. We hypothesize that knowledge is localized, in particular,
197   there is a small subset of components that make the model not generate the concept if being ablated.
198   On the other hand, recall that our framework applies first-order expansion, thus the approximation
199   is close if the number of ablated components is small.
200
      Hypothesis 1. Knowledge is localized in a small number of components. If we remove those com-
201
      ponents of a concept c, the model will not generate c but other concepts are not affected.
202
203

225   5.2   A MPLIFYING K NOWLEDGE IN D IFFUSION M ODELS
226
227   Our attribution framework offers a complete view of interpreting the model: besides positive com-
228   ponents that are responsible for generating a concept, there also exist components with negative
229   coefficients. We hypothesize that these components suppress knowledge, decreasing the probability
230
      of inducing a concept. If we ablate negative components, the ability to generate images with the
      concept will be improved.
231
232   Hypothesis 2. Negative components exist and can amplify knowledge when it is ablated.
233

234
      modules storing knowledge. If Hypothesis 2 is true, we can also edit the model at those negative
235
      components. For instance, the attacker can remove negative components of harmful concepts to
236   make diffusion models generate those concepts more.

520   7   L IMITATIONS
521
522   In this work, we only focus on fine-grained model components that are parameters and study their
523   contribution to knowledge. We do not examine other types of components, such as layers or mod-
524   ules, which can highly influence multiple concepts at once. We leave it to future works.
525   When removing objects, we observe that CAD sacrifices some other knowledge and decreases the
526   accuracy on other classes. These results show that although knowledge is localized, some com-
527   ponents could be responsible for multiple knowledge. Studying the entanglement of parametric
528   knowledge would be an interesting direction in future study.

      8   C ONCLUSION
531
532
      In this work, we study the contribution of each component in diffusion models. We propose a
533
      framework based on first-order approximation that allows computing attribution scores efficiently,
534   and two editing algorithms that can erase or amplify knowledge in the model. Our experimental
535   results confirm the localization hypothesis, showing that knowledge is localized in a small number
536   of components. We also show the existence of negative components that suppress knowledge, and
537   ablating them increases the probability of generating the target concept. Our study provides a com-
538   plete view of interpreting diffusion models by analyzing both positive and negative components. It
539   would be interesting to study the influence of those components and utilize them for model editing
      in future works.

      A       E XPERIMENTAL S ETUP
649
650
      In our study, we compare our method with several concept erasure techniques. We provide details
651   on the hyperparameters and setups used from these methods as follows:
652
653            • ESD. We follow the setting in the original paper and fine-tune the UNet with a learning rate
654              of 1e − 5. To compute the objective, we generate images of the target class with a guidance
655              scale of 3. The scale of negative guidance in the objective is set to 1.
656            • UCE. We apply UCE across ten objects within the Imagenette class and for the artistic
657              styles of Picasso, Van Gogh, Rembrandt, Andy Warhol, and Caravaggio, including the
658              nudity concept. In artist styles, the method includes a ‘preserve‘ parameter, which retains
659              styles not targeted for erasure. We follow that setting, by erasing only one artist style at
660              each checkpoint while keeping the rest.
661            • RECE. This method continues to fine-tune models using checkpoints previously erased by
662              UCE. For artistic styles, we apply a regularization parameter (λ) of 1e−3 for all mentioned
663              styles. In contrast, for nudity content, λ is set at 1e − 1. In object removal scenarios where
664              UCE has already achieved complete erasure of five objects with an erased class accuracy of
665              0.00%, RECE is used to address the remaining objects. Specific regularization parameters
666
                 include λ = 1e − 3 for ”church” and ”garbage truck”, and λ = 1e − 1 for ”English
                 Springer”, ”golf ball”, and ”parachute”, consistent with parameters reported in their studies
667
                 for each experiment.
668
669            • Concept-Prune. We stick to the parameters specified in previous experiments. For the
670
                 nudity concept, we apply a mask at the initial denoising step with t̂ = 9 and a sparsity level
                 of k = 1%. For object removal in the Imagenette classes, we use t̂ = 10 and k = 2%. The
671
                 same parameters are applied to the erasure of artist styles. Additionally, the ’select ratio’
672
                 parameter m determines the threshold for applying the binary mask to the model weights.
673              The method prunes only those neurons that exceed m% throughout the initial time steps t̂.
674              As this parameter is not detailed in their work, we set m = 0.5.
