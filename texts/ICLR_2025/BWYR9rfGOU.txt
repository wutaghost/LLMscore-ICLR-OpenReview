009                                               A BSTRACT
010
011            Subpopulation shift refers to the difference in the distribution of subgroups be-
012            tween training and test datasets. When an underrepresented subgroup becomes
013            predominant during testing, it can lead to significant performance degradation,
014            making performance prediction prior to deployment particularly important. Ex-
015            isting performance prediction methods often fail to address this type of shift ef-
016            fectively due to their usage of unreliable model confidence and mis-specified dis-
017            tributional distances. In this paper, we propose a novel performance prediction
018
               method specifically designed to tackle subpopulation shifts, called Subpopulation-
               Aware Two-stage Estimator (SATE). Our approach first estimates the subgroup
019
               proportions in the test set by linearly expressing the test embedding with train-
020
               ing subgroup embeddings. Then, it predicts the accuracy for each subgroup using
021            the accuracy on augmented training set, aggregating them into an overall perfor-
022            mance estimate. We provide theoretical proof of our method’s unbiasedness and
023            consistency, and demonstrate that it outperforms numerous baselines across vari-
024            ous datasets, including vision, medical, and language tasks, offering a reliable tool
025            for performance prediction in scenarios involving subpopulation shifts.

028   1   I NTRODUCTION
029
030   In the training and deployment of machine learning models, it is common to encounter shifts in
031   data distribution (Shen et al., 2021). Such distributional discrepancies often result in degraded per-
032
      formance, making performance prediction prior to deployment particularly essential, especially in
      high-stakes domains like finance and medicine where the cost of errors is substantial.
033
034   A performance prediction method, also known as unsupervised accuracy estimation, typically takes
035   in labeled training data, trained model and unlabeled test data. Its goal is to produce a direct or
036   indirect measure of accuracy on test data. This serves not only as a confidence estimate but also
037   aids in discerning which models are more suitable for specific datasets or which datasets are more
038
      compatible with a given model (Yu et al., 2024). This matching capability is even more important
      with an ever-growing number of models and algorithms to date.
039
040   Previous researchers commonly evaluate their performance prediction methods using two types of
041   distribution shifts: synthetic shifts, where test datasets are generated through artificial perturba-
042   tions (Hendrycks & Dietterich, 2019), and natural shifts, such as training on ImageNet (Deng et al.,
043   2009) and testing on ImageNet-v2 (Recht et al., 2019). However, these types of distribution shifts
044
      do not encompass all scenarios encountered in real-world applications.
045   One underexplored type of shift in the field of performance prediction is the subpopulation shift.
046   It refers to the difference in the training and testing distributions in terms of how well-represented
047   each subpopulation is (Sagawa et al., 2020; Santurkar et al., 2020; Yang et al., 2023). Typically,
048   subgroups are divided by labels and attributes. Significant performance degradation may occur
049   when a subgroup that is underrepresented during training becomes prevalent while testing, making
050
      performance prediction before deployment especially necessary. Also, as highlighted in Yang et al.
      (2023), no single method remains state-of-the-art across all types and degrees of subpopulation
051
      shifts. indicating that it is improper to trust certain model without considering the test data.
052
053   In our work, we propose a performance prediction method specifically designed to address subpop-
      ulation shift called Subpopulation-Aware Two-stage Estimator (SATE). It has demonstrated superior

096   Out-of-Distribution Performance Prediction. Out-of-Distribution (OOD) Performance Predic-
097   tion is an important research theme to characterize the OOD behavior of machine learning models.
098   Its primary goal is to assess whether a machine learning model has good OOD generalization capa-
099   bilities and to determine where it can perform well only with unlabeled test data (Yu et al., 2024).
100   In this work, we focus on unsupervised performance prediction which means predicting the perfor-
101   mance without relying on prior results from other datasets. This problem is also called unsupervised
102   accuracy estimation (Diamantidis et al., 2000), since the most commonly used metric for a classi-
103   fier’s performance is accuracy.
104   Follow Yu et al. (2024), most previous performance prediction methods can be categorized into three
105   types. (1) Model Output Property-based: Methods such as ATC (Garg et al., 2022), DoC (Guillory
106   et al., 2021) and NI (Ng et al., 2022) predict performance based on the model’s output (e.g. con-
107   fidence) on the test data. (2) Distribution Discrepancy-based: Methods like Lu et al. (2023) , Yu
      et al. (2022) assess performance by evaluating some kinds of distance (e.g. Wasserstein Distance)


                                                        2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      between the training and test data. (3) Model Agreement-based: Methods such as Baek et al. (2022)
109   and Chen et al. (2021) predict performance by examining the output invariance of multiple slightly
110   varied models (e.g. trained with different random seed). Note that NAC (Liu et al., 2024) is designed
111   for the detection or evaluation of OOD models without test data and Deng & Zheng (2024) needs
112   additional data to supervise the accuracy estimator, which are inconsistent with our setting.
113
      Our algorithm differs from these approaches in several key ways. First, our approach does not rely
114
      on the model’s output on the test data. Second, rather than computing the distance between overall
115   distributions, our method focuses on linearly expressing the test set on the subgroup level. Finally,
116   in contrast to model agreement-based methods, our method does not necessitate any extra model
117   training. Recently, a method using VLM to extract priors for assisting failure detection has been
118   tested on several subpopulation shift datasets (Subramanyam et al., 2024). Our approach differs
119   by emphasizing dataset-level accuracy prediction, rather than estimating the probability of individ-
120   ual sample misclassification. Also, their method is limited to image datasets, whereas ours is not
121   restricted.
122
123   Subpopulation Shift. In the context of Subpopulation Shift, each data point contains several at-
124   tribute information a in addition to input x and label y. The entire dataset can be divided into
125
      multiple discrete subpopulations based on the combination of labels and attributes. However, the
      proportion of each subgroup may differ between the training and test datasets, causing one or more
126
      of Spurious Correlation, Attribute Imbalance, Class Imbalance, or Attribute Generalization (Yang
127
      et al., 2023). These types of subpopulation shifts will lead to performance degradation on the test
128   dataset. Various methods have been studied to address this problem, including subgroup-based
129   methods like GroupDRO (Sagawa et al., 2020) and IRM (Ahuja et al., 2020), data augmentation-
130   based methods like Mixup (Zhang et al., 2018), reweighting-based methods like Megahed et al.
131   (2021) and several two-stage methods such as JTT (Liu et al., 2021), CRT (Kang et al., 2019) and
132   DFR (Izmailov et al., 2022). These approaches aim to improve model robustness and ensure con-
133   sistent performance across different subgroups within the data. There have also been some methods
134   capable of handling subpopulation shift without attribute annotations (Hong et al., 2024; Stromberg
135   et al., 2024), but they all use some technique (optimal data partitioning, regularized annotation of
136
      domains) to perform their own subgroup partitioning, therefore they are still within our framework.
137   A model that can better handle subpopulation shift overall may exhibit a high Worst Group Accuracy
138   (WGA) (Sagawa et al., 2020) because the test data could experience unpredictable changes and
139   underrepresented groups may become major groups. Nevertheless, overall accuracy on the test
140   dataset remains crucial. For instance, in a medical diagnosis application, a model must maintain a
141   high overall accuracy to ensure reliable diagnostic results for the entire patient population. Overall
142
      accuracy is even more important in performance prediction’s context because we already know
      where the model will be deployed on (the test data).
143
144
      Data Augmentation. The goal of data augmentation is to enhance the diversity of the training set
145
      without collecting additional samples, thereby improving the model’s generalization ability. Many
146
      easy-to-use and effective data augmentation methods are popular in computer vision (CV), such as
147   cropping and flipping (Shorten & Khoshgoftaar, 2019). Thus, synthetic shifts in CV datasets are
148   well-designed, and most performance prediction papers use self-designed (Deng et al., 2021) or
149   existing synthetic datasets like ImageNet-P (Hendrycks & Dietterich, 2019) as their test sets.
150
      However, as discussed in Feng et al. (2021), Shorten et al. (2021) and Pellicer et al. (2023), in the
151
      Natural Language Processing (NLP) field, due to the discrete nature of language and the difficulty in
152
      ensuring label invariance, data augmentation methods are relatively limited. Due to the above data
153   restrictions, to the best of our knowledge, no unsupervised performance prediction method has yet
154   been tested on language datasets. Note that Xia et al. (2020) and Srinivasan et al. (2021) require
155   the performance results of a language model on several other datasets to run a regression for per-
156   formance prediction, which is inconsistent with our setting where no prior historical information is
157   available. Rychalska et al. (2019) and Talman et al. (2022) evaluated language models on corrupted
158   datasets, but neither attempted to predict their performance in advance. Therefore, we believe that
159   exploring performance prediction in the NLP domain is both novel and challenging.

205   4.1   M OTIVATIONS
206
207   Spurious correlation is a common issue in subpopulation shift datasets (Geirhos et al., 2020; Ye
208   et al., 2024) that may cause confidence-based prediction methods to fail. It refers to non-causal
209   relationship between an attribute a and the label y in the training set that does not hold in the test
210   set. Models that rely heavily on such non-causal attributes may make incorrect predictions with high
211   confidence when applied to the test set, thus results in unreliable confidence estimates.
212   Another complicating factor is that many algorithms designed to address subpopulation shifts often
213   involve different usage of training samples during optimization, examples including Sagawa et al.
214   (2020), Megahed et al. (2021), Izmailov et al. (2022). As a result, the distribution of the training
215   dataset may differ from the distribution the model actually fits. Therefore, methods based on the
      distance between training and test datasets may fail on these algorithms.

      Inspired by the work of He et al. (2024), who demonstrated that a weighted combination of source
217   domains can effectively align the target dataset, our method, SATE, takes advantage of prior domain
218   information. Instead of relying on model confidence or overall distribution distance, SATE linearly
219   expresses the test set with training subgroups, evading the limitations above, thus providing a more
220   accurate and reliable approach to handling subpopulation shifts.
221
      Furthermore, most subpopulation shift experiments assume the availability of a validation set to
222
      guide model selection and evaluation (Yang et al., 2023) (Izmailov et al., 2022). Our proposed
223   method also has the advantage of offering flexibility in using a validation set, and we show that its
224   inclusion can improve the performance predictions.

299   Augmentation on the Line. Miller et al. (2021) proposed the idea of Accuracy-on-the-Line. They

303

349   We conducted experiments on the following tasks: (1) Image Tasks: Waterbirds (Wah et al., 2011)

      For image tasks, we use two representative architectures, ResNet-50 (He et al., 2016) and Vision
353
      Transformer (ViT) (Dosovitskiy et al., 2020), supervised pretrained on ImageNet-1k (Deng et al.,
354   2009). For language task, we use BERT-base-uncased (Devlin et al., 2019) as architecture. For each
355   task-architecture combination, we use ERM (Vapnik, 1999), GroupDRO (Sagawa et al., 2020) and
356   DFR (Izmailov et al., 2022) three algorithms to train the models.
357
358   5.2   BASELINES AND M ETRICS
359
360   ATC-MC and ATC-NE (Garg et al., 2022): First, we determine a confidence threshold based on
361   overall training accuracy and then use this threshold to partition the test set. Test images with
362   confidence above the threshold will be considered correct, otherwise incorrect. The threshold can
363   also be based on negative entropy (NE). Its output is a direct estimator of test accuracy.
364   DoC and DoE (Guillory et al., 2021): Output the difference of confidence (or entropy) between
365   training and test data. It’s a indirect estimator of the accuracy gap between training and test.
366
      Neighborhood Invariance (NI) (Ng et al., 2022): Deploy different data augmentation on test
367
      set and measure the invariance of model’s output label. Here we use NI-RandAug, which uses
368
      augmentation from Cubuk et al. (2020). Its output is a indirect estimator of test accuracy.
369
370
      Datasets Design. Unlike previous experiments on covariate shifts that can easily create a diverse
371
      range of test sets using corruption and perturbation, the design of test sets with subpopulation shifts
372   is more challenging. The train-test split only provides a single test set for evaluation. This limits
373   our ability to comprehensively compare model performance across different degrees and types of
374   subpopulation shifts. Thus, to construct the test data, we designed 20 different subgroup distributions
375   to simulate a wide range of diverse subpopulation shifts. Note that we only control the number of
376   samples extracted from each subgroup, the extraction within a subgroup is still random. To construct
377   the training data, we randomly sampled from data outside the test sets, with the distribution as similar
      as possible to the distribution of the original overall dataset. Details are provided in the appendix A.

      Metrics. A good performance prediction method should give a test accuracy estimation ϕT highly
379   correlated to the groundtruth test accuracy AccT . The following metrics are used for comparison,
380   (1) coefficient of determination (R2 ): the goodness of linearly fitting AccT with ϕT . (2) Mean
381   Absolute Error (MAE): the error between AccT and ϕT if used as direct estimator, or the error
382   between AccT and fitted value if used as indirect estimator. (3) pearson’s correlation coefficient.

386   Test Sets Characteristics. We first propose a quantitative metric for subpopulation shift and
387   demonstrate that the test sets we designed effectively simulate different degrees and types of sub-
388   population shifts. Previous work utilizes entropy and mutual information to quantify the degree of
389   different subpopulation shifts (Yang et al., 2023), focusing solely on the imbalance within a single
390
      dataset. However, when considering model performance degradation, a metric that measures the dif-
      ference between two datasets is more useful. To address this, we propose a new set of quantification
391
      metrics using Jensen-Shannon (J-S) divergence. We employ the divergence in P (y), P (a), P (y|a)

      as metrics for Class Imbalance, Attribute Imbalance, and Spurious Correlation, respectively. To be
393   specific, P (y) of training set and P (y) of test set are two discrete probability distributions, their J-S
394   divergence is the metric of class imbalance. See Appendix C for detailed calculation procedure.

      7   L IMITATIONS
515
      Dependency on Attribute Annotations. Our method relies on the availability of attribute annota-
516
      tions in the training set for subgroup division. In cases where such annotations are unavailable, our
517   approach must be combined with unsupervised subgroup partitioning algorithms or require manual
518   selection of a feature from X as the attribute based on human knowledge. Furthermore, our method
519   assumes that these attributes are accurate and complete. However, in real-world scenarios, attribute
520   annotations may be noisy, incomplete, or biased, which could result in errors in both subgroup
521   proportion estimation and performance prediction.
522
523   Limited to Subpopulation Shifts. Our approach is specifically designed to handle subpopulation
524   shifts, operating under the assumption that the primary distributional changes stem from differences
525   in subgroup proportions between the training and testing data. It cannot effectively quantify or ad-
526   dress covariate shifts (changes in the overall feature distribution). Moreover, when strong covariate
527
      shifts occur together with subpopulation shifts, the test set embedding may no longer be able to rep-
      resented as a linear combination of training subgroup embeddings, leading to inaccurate proportion
528
      estimates. As a potential direction for future work, integrating our method with other performance
529
      prediction techniques could create a more robust predictor capable of managing both subpopulation
530   and covariate shifts effectively.
531
532   Challenges with Unseen Subgroups. The presence of unseen subgroups in the test set-subgroups
533   that do not exist in the training set-can further increase estimation errors. To address this issue, we
534   propose a lightweight method in appendix F for detecting the presence of unseen subgroups. This
535   enhancement improves the adaptability of our method to domain generalization settings.
536
537   Reproducibility Statement. To ensure the reproducibility of our results, we used untuned and
538   consistent random seeds during both model training and performance prediction. Our code is based
539   on the implementation from SubpopBench (Yang et al., 2023), with the majority of model training
      parameters kept at their default settings. Additionally, the sample sizes drawn from each subgroup

      C    J-S D IVERGENCE
848
849
      We use J-S Divergence as a quantitative metric for subpopulation shift. In this section, we will detail
850
      the calculation.
851
852   For example, consider the training distribution [100, 100, 100, 100] and the test distribution [200,
853   100, 50, 50]. Each number represents the number of samples in a subgroup.
854
855   J-S Divergence of P (y). (1) Combine: Merge by y, the two distributions become [200, 200] and
856   [300, 100]. (2) Normalize: After normalization, they become [0.5, 0.5] and [0.75, 0.25]. At this
857   point, both distributions are in the form of discrete probability distributions. (3) Compute: Use the
858   standard J-S divergence calculation method to compute the divergence between these two vectors,
859   which yields the final result, which is 0.221 in this example. Note that J-S Divergence of P (a) is
860
      similar to this one, only differs in that we should merge by a.
861
862   J-S Divergence of P (y|a). (1) Group by a: Calculate J-S Divergence of P (y)   P for each a ∈ A re-
863   spectively, denoted as JSya (2) Weighted sum: J-S Divergence of P (y|a) = a∈A PT (a) · JSya ,
      where PT (a) means the proportion of samples with attribute a within test set.

984    F   U NSEEN S UBGROUP D ETECTION
985
986    Here we develop a lightweight method to detect unseen subgroups after the first step of SATE. We
987    use Mean Square Error (MSE) of the linear decomposition as the indicator of the existence of unseen
988
       subgroups. Larger MSE indicates higher probability that the test set contains unseen subgroup.
989    We conducted experiments on the NICO++, a commonly used domain generalization benchmark, to
990    evaluate our detection method. The experimental setup and findings are as follows:
991
992    Benchmark Setup: We utilized the NICO++ (Zhang et al., 2022) dataset, focusing on y ∈
993    {0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5} and a ∈ {0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5}, resulting in 36 subgroups in total. The training data
994    followed the original split, where subgroup (5, 4) was absent. While all 36 subgroups were present
995
       in the original test split.
996
997
       Test Sets: To simulate various conditions, we created 50 test sets, each comprising k randomly
       selected subgroups from the original test set.
998
999
       Evaluation: We evaluate the effectiveness of detection by the Area Under the Curve (AUC) be-
1000
       tween the existence of unseen subgroup and the MSE of linear decomposition.

1011   of our method to domain generalization scenarios.
