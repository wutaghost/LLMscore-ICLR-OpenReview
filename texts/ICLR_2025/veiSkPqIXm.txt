009                                             A BSTRACT
010
011            Vision-language models (VLMs) have demonstrated impressive zero-shot ca-
012            pabilities across various image classification tasks. Their performance can be
013            further enhanced through prompt learning methods. To evaluate the effective-
014            ness of prompt learning, it is important to assess its robustness to new classes
015            and distributional shifts. However, current studies typically assume single data
016            distribution shifts and pre-known new class space, which still have gaps with
017            real-world open environments where data distributions and classes are often un-
018
               certain and subject to continuous change. To better analyze the robustness of
               prompt learning methods in more realistic scenarios, we propose a novel eval-
019
               uation benchmark called OpenPL from the following perspectives: 1) We re-
020
               construct multiple scenarios of open environments, encompassing dynamic class
021            changes, dynamic distribution shifts, and dynamic co-evolution of both distribu-
022            tion and classes; 2) We propose a series of new performance metrics for prompt
023            learning methods based on the Dynamic Robustness Curve (DRC) to better un-
024            derstand their robustness in open environments; 3) We re-implement diverse
025            prompt learning methods and evaluate their performance on the proposed OpenPL
026            benchmark. The results show that no current prompt learning method is ro-
027            bust to open environments and no meaningful performance improvement
028            is achieved compared to the zero-shot performance, designing robust prompt
029
               learning methods remains a difficult task. All re-implementations are available at
               https://anonymous.4open.science/r/OpenPL-565E.

      1   I NTRODUCTIONS
033
034
      Vision-language models (VLMs) have garnered significant attention recently(Radford et al.
035
      (2021);Yao et al. (2021);Jia et al. (2021)) because of its zero-shot prediction capabilities across
036   a wide range of visual recognition tasks. Pre-trained VLMs, such as CLIP(Radford et al. (2021)),
037   ALIGN(Jia et al. (2021)), and BLIP(Li et al. (2022)), acquire extensive vision-language knowledge
038   from a near-infinite number of text-image pairs available on the web. These models can be utilized
039   directly for downstream tasks without the need for fine-tuning.
040
      A large part of the research for VLM recently is the adaptation of pre-trained VLMs on downstream
041
      tasks(Zhang et al. (2021);Zhou et al. (2022b);Zhou et al. (2022a);Gao et al. (2024)), and prompt
042   learning(Zhou et al. (2022b);Zhou et al. (2022a)) is a newcomer to these works, with notable
043   improvements in VLM performance on downstream tasks and its simplicity and efficiency in design.
044   Unlike other transfer methods in VLM, prompt learning does not rely on adding additional network
045   layers or modifying complex network structures but rather achieves parameter-efficient VLM transfer
046   by modifying the input text or image with some learnable text or vision prompts.
047
      Although many prompt learning methods, such as MaPLe (Khattak et al. (2023a)) and PromptSRC
048   (Khattak et al. (2023b)), claim to achieve strong generalization performance on downstream tasks,
049   their evaluations often overlook more practical scenarios. Currently, most prompt learning methods
050   depend on benchmarks established by CoCoOp (Zhou et al. (2022a)), where classes are divided into
051   fixed base and new groups. These methods are trained solely on the base classes and tested on new
052   classes separately. Similarly, MaPLe (Khattak et al. (2023a)) proposes benchmarks for Cross-dataset
053   Evaluation and Domain Generalization by training on ImageNet and altering the test data distribution
      to assess performance across various ImageNet variants and other datasets. In open environments,

054
      algorithms do encounter the emergence of new classes and shifts in data distributions (Zhou (2022)),
055   but such changes are not fixed and cannot be pre-known, and sometimes both distribution and class
056   shifts could occur simultaneously. There is still a huge gap between existing evaluation settings and
057   realistic open environments.
058
      Contributions: In this paper, we propose a novel evaluation benchmark to address the existing
059
      problems above and achieve a comprehensive evaluation for prompt learning in VLM. Firstly, we
060
      focus on the dynamic class changes and propose two scenarios based on class variations. The first
061   scenario increases classification difficulty by continuously introducing new classes during testing.
062   The second scenario reduces the proportion of base classes while increasing the ratio of new classes,
063   keeping the overall label space size constant. This setup aims to measure the algorithm’s robustness
064   in an unknown and evolving label space. Secondly, we focus on dynamic distribution shifts and
065   propose a scenario in which the testing distribution continues to change, becoming increasingly
066   distant from the training data distribution. Thirdly, we focus on the dynamic co-evolution of both
067   distribution and classes and propose a scenario in which both class changes and distribution shifts
068   occur simultaneously. Moreover, we quantify the performance under these paradigms based on the
069
      Dynamic Robustness Curve (DRC), and several new performance metrics based on the DRC are
      proposed to help better analyze the robustness of prompt learning methods. We also re-implement
070
      different types of prompt learning methods under a unified standard and evaluate their robustness.
071
      We believe this has a positive impact on researchers in this field.
072
073   Observations: By researching the results of experimental evaluations on the openPL, our insights
074   can be outlined as follows:
075
            1. No single prompt learning method outperforms others in scenarios with dynamic classes
076
               changing, i.e., each method has its better case when new classes emerging.
077
078         2. No prompt learning methods currently show robustness to data distribution shifts, i.e., all
079
               methods suffer severe performance degradation with distribution shifts.
080         3. No prompt learning methods exhibit meaningful performance gains relative to the zero-shot
081            performance with compound shifts of both distribution and label space.
082         4. Enhancing the model’s capability for text feature extraction and class discriminability may
083            improve robustness since the available text and image information in downstream tasks is
084            highly imbalanced.

      2    R ELATED W ORKS
087
088
      Vision-language model Recently, researchers have demonstrated VLM (Alayrac et al. (2022);Rad-
089   ford et al. (2021)), which consists of visual and textual modalities trained on large-scale image-text
090   pairs, with strong generalization and discrimination capabilities. These VLMs like CLIP (Radford
091   et al. (2021)), ALIGN (Jia et al. (2021)), BLIP (Li et al. (2022)), FILIP (Yao et al. (2021)), LiT (Zhai
092   et al. (2022)) and Flamingo (Alayrac et al. (2022)) show exceptional performance across numerous
093   visual tasks, including few-shot and zero-shot visual recognition. For example, CLIP (Radford et al.
094   (2021)) designs objective that allows matched text representations and image representations close to
095   each other, and learns a generalized vision-language representation on about 400M text-image pairs.
096   Recent researches have focused on how such VLMs can be better adapted to downstream tasks by
097
      means of transfer learning (Zhou et al. (2022b);Zhou et al. (2022a);Zhang et al. (2021);Gao et al.
      (2024)) and knowledge distillation (Ding et al. (2022);Du et al. (2022);Gu et al. (2021)).
098
099
      Prompt learning The idea of prompt learning first originated in NLP(Liu et al. (2023)), a method
100
      of instructing language models to generate specific outputs by providing prompts without having to
101
      tune the pre-trained model’s own parameters. Because of its parsimony and efficiency, this technique
102   has attracted a lot of attention in the exploration of fine-tuning VLM pre-trained models to specific
103   visual tasks downstream. For example, at the earliest time, CoOp (Zhou et al. (2022b)) explored the
104   application of prompts to the text branch of CLIP, which was used to optimize the text embedding
105   space of the text branch so that specific categories of the downstream task could be better adapted to
106   the pre-trained model, while VPT (Jia et al. (2022)) later provided a solution for introducing prompts
107   into the visual encoder. After that CoCoOp (Zhou et al. (2022a)) explores the introduction of image
      information into text prompts to make the unified context into an instance-adaptive context. Moreover


                                                         2
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


108
      MaPLe (Khattak et al. (2023a)) experiment with the co-optimization of prompts for both the textual
109   and visual sides of the prompts. The aforementioned methods are all dedicated to improving the form
110   of prompts and enhancing the model’s ability to extract image features. Different from the above
111   methods, ProGrad (Zhu et al. (2023)) and KgCoOp (Yao et al. (2023b)) explore how to improve
112   the generalization ability of pre-trained models by better preserving their knowledge. ProGrad only
113   updates prompts whose gradients do not conflict with the knowledge of the pre-trained model to
114   prevent general knowledge from being forgotten; KgCoOp uses the gap from the fine-tuned prompts
115   to general knowledge as a regularization term for constrained models. Similarly, RPO (Lee et al.
116   (2023)) leverages masked attention to prevent the internal representation shift in the pre-trained model
117
      to reduce the decline in the generalization ability of the pre-trained model. Moreover, TCP (Yao et al.
      (2023a)) and ProDA (Lu et al. (2022)) choose to enhance the textual side of the representation, with
118
      TCP incorporating prior knowledge about classes to enhance the discriminability of classes; ProDA
119
      learns output embeddings of textual prompts rather than input embeddings. PromptSRC (Khattak
120   et al. (2023b)) achieves notable performance by simultaneously improving prompt learning from
121   three aspects: preventing pre-trained knowledge forgetting; prompt ensemble; and increasing text
122   diversity to mitigate sample diversity.
123
      In the following, we will introduce the evaluation paradigms, performance metrics, the robustness
124
      definitions of prompt learning, benchmark results, and conclusions.

127   3     E VALUATION PARADIGMS
128
129   In previous experiments on prompt learning, changes in classes and data distributions during testing
130   were fixed, lacking dynamism. To address this, our benchmark introduces dynamic scenarios where
131   new classes emerge, data distributions shift, and both distributions and classes co-evolve. This enables
132   a more comprehensive analysis of the robustness of existing prompt learning algorithms in such
133
      dynamic environments.
134
135   3.1   DYNAMIC C LASSES C HANGES
136
      In this paradigm, we introduce two scenarios in open environments where new classes continuously
137
      emerge. For each experiment, we randomly select half of the classes from a single dataset to serve
138   as base classes, while the remaining half are designated as new classes. The algorithms are trained
139   exclusively on samples from the base classes. During testing, they encounter a dynamic situation in
140   which both base and new classes co-exist, with their quantities continuously changing.
141
142   Emerging New Classes In this scenario, during testing, the base classes remain consistently present,
143   while new classes continuously emerge. As new classes are introduced, the algorithm faces a larger
144   class space, making the classification task more challenging. We define the class changing level t as
145   the ratio of new classes in the test set relative to the base classes, where a higher t signifies a more
146   complex classification task. We ensure that as t increases, the new classes from the previous level are
147   subsets of the corresponding groups for the next level.
148
149   Varying Ratio of New Classes In this scenario, during testing, the base classes do not remain
150
      constant; instead, they decrease as the number of new classes increases. The size of the class space
      remains unchanged, while the quantities of new and base classes vary synchronously. To achieve this,
151
      we define the class changing level t to represent the proportion of new classes in the test set relative
152
      to all classes. As t increases, new classes continually emerge while base classes diminish. To ensure
153   comparability of performance across different levels of inconsistency, we ensure that as t increases,
154   the new classes from the previous level are subsets of the corresponding groups for the next level,
155   and the removed base classes are subsets of their respective groups from the previous level.
156
157   3.2   DYNAMIC D ISTRIBUTION SHIFTS
158
159   Under this paradigm, we train on all classes of ImageNet and test on a mixture of ImageNet and its
160   variants with continuously changing proportions. We define a distribution change level t to represent
161   the proportion of samples from the ImageNet variants within the entire test set. As the value of t
      increases, the proportion of data from ImageNet decreases, while the proportion of data from the


                                                         3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      variants gradually increases. At the same time, we ensure that the class space of the training set
163   remains consistent with the variants. As t increases, the samples from the ImageNet variants at
164   the previous level are subsets of those at the subsequent level, with the reduced ImageNet samples
165   coming from the previous level.
166
167   3.3    DYNAMIC C O - EVOLUTION OF D ISTRIBUTION AND C LASS VARIATION
168
169   In this paradigm, we train on ImageNet and test on a mixed dataset that combines ImageNet and
170   other datasets. The number of classes and samples belonging to ImageNet in the test set is kept equal
171   to those from other datasets, with classes and samples randomly selected. We define a class and
172   distribution change level t to represent the proportion of cross-dataset samples among all test samples.
173
      As t increases, the proportion of samples from the other datasets also increases. As the value of t
      increases, the samples and classes from the cross-dataset continuously increase, while the samples
174
      and classes from ImageNet steadily decrease. We ensure that as t increases, the samples from other
175
      datasets at the previous level are subsets of those at the subsequent level, with the reduced ImageNet
176   samples coming from the previous level.

179   4     P ERFORMANCE M ETRICS
180
181
      To achieve a fair and comprehensive evaluation, for the four evaluation paradigms designed above we
      introduce a comprehensive set of evaluation metrics to analyze the robustness of prompt learning
182
      in VLM. We first define the model accuracy at inconsistency t as Acc(t). Zero-shot pre-trained
183
      CLIP also has Acczs (t) at any t, which represents only the accuracy obtained from the same test
184   set at t, for comparing the performance improvements from other prompt learning methods on the
185   pre-trained model. The accuracy of the model in changing environments is mapped to a function
186   of inconsistency t of different scenarios. In this way we construct the Dynamic Robustness Curve
187   (DRC) and propose several metrics based on it including 1) Area Under the Curve (AUC) which
188   analyzes the overall robustness of the model; 2) Worst-Case Accuracy (WA) which represents the
189   worst performance in open environments; 3) Expected Variation Magnitude (EVM) measuring the
190   overall magnitude of the change in accuracy; 4) Variation Stability (VS) quantifying the stability
191   of variation magnitude; 5) Positive Area (PA) measuring the performance gain in parts where the
192
      algorithm surpasses zero-shot performance; 6) Negative Area (NA) measuring the performance
      degradation in parts where the algorithm underperforms compared to the zero-shot performance.

      Based on the proposed performance metrics, we further define robust prompt learning in open
217   environments to enhance our understanding of the robustness gain of prompt learning methods
218   compared to zero-shot performance. This includes the concepts of performance-gain robustness and
219   decay-gain-ratio robustness.
220
221
222   Definition (Performance-Gain Robustness) We define the AUC obtained from the VLM’s zero-
223
      shot prediction as AU Czs . A prompt learning method A in VLM returns a model that can be tested
      with any class and distribution change level t. If there exists δAU C such that AU C −AU Czs ≥ δAU C
224
      holds for all t, we say A achieves δAU C -performance-gain robustness.
225
226
227   Definition (Decay-Gain-Ratio Robustness) A prompt learning method A in VLM returns a model
228   that can be tested with any class and distribution change level t. If there exists δP N such that
229   P A − N A ≤ δP N holds for all t, we say A exhibits δP N -decay-gain-ratio robustness.

234   5.1   E XPERIMENT S ETUP
235
236   Methods In our experiments, we evaluate 11 prompt learning methods based on the pre-trained
237   CLIP using a Vision Transformer (ViT). The methods are as follows: text-based prompt learning
238   methods CoOp, CoCoOp, ProGrad, KgCoOp, TCP, ProDA, RPO; the visual prompt learning VPT;
239   the text-vision prompt learning methods MaPLe and PromptSRC. We also evaluated the zero-shot
240   prediction capability of the pre-trained model CLIP as a baseline in order to compare the performance
241   of these prompt learning methods.
242
243
      Datasets Following CoOp and CoCoOp, we evaluate the performance of these prompt learning
244   methods on 11 diverse image classification datasets that cover a variety of recognition tasks. These
245   datasets include: two generic object datasets, ImageNet (Deng et al. (2009)) and Caltech101 (Fei-Fei
246   et al. (2004)); one texture dataset DTD (Cimpoi et al. (2014)); a satellite image dataset EuroSAT (Hel-
247   ber et al. (2019)); five fine-gained dataset FGVCAircraft (Maji et al. (2013)), Food101 (Bossard et al.
248   (2014)), Flowers102 (Nilsback & Zisserman (2008)), OxfordPets (Parkhi et al. (2012)), StanfordCars
249   (Krause et al. (2013)); one scene recognition dataset SUN397 (Xiao et al. (2010)), and an action
250   recognition dataset UCF101 (Soomro et al. (2012)). In the Dynamic Distribution shifts paradigm,
251   we utilize four variants of ImageNet including ImageNetV2 (Recht et al. (2019)), ImageNetSketch
252
      (Wang et al. (2019)), ImageNet-A (Hendrycks et al. (2021b)), ImageNet-R (Hendrycks et al. (2021a)).
253
254   Implementation Details For all experiments, we adopted a unified parameter setting to ensure
255   a fair comparison. In the two scenarios of Dynamic Classes Changes, we set the learning rate η
256   as 2 × 10−3 , and the total number of epochs is 50 for each dataset. And for mixed proportions of
257   ImageNet variants and the Dynamic Co-evolution of Distribution and Class Variation paradigms,
258   the training on ImageNet is set to run for 10 epochs. Additionally, to ensure a balanced mixture
259   of different datasets, we cap the maximum number of classes and the sample size for all datasets
260   during testing to maintain equivalence. We set the length of text or vision prompts in all methods
261
      as 4. We sample 16 samples per class from the training dataset and test all methods on the full
      test dataset, following the commonly used few-shot evaluation protocol as that in CLIP. We adopt
262
      ViT-Base/16 as the backbone network for all experiments. The initial setting for text prompts is
263
      fixed to “X X X X” the initialization of vision prompts follows a zero-mean Gaussian distribution
264   with a standard deviation of 0.02. For a fair comparison, the final results were averaged over three
265   rounds of experiments. To plot the curve DAC, we sampled six points for t as 0, 0.2, 0.4, 0.6, 0.8,
266   1.0. To ensure reliability, the label space is randomized for each round of training and testing, and
267   we conduct experiments three times with seed values of 1, 2, and 3. The results for each sampling
268   point were averaged over three experiments, and linear interpolation was used for the other points.
269   Our experiments are conducted on NVIDIA A800 GPUs. The complete experimental results are
      presented in the Appendix A.

      classes to base classes. For example, in the EuroSAT dataset, the performance of VPT and TCP does
371
      not always decrease with the increasing ratio of new classes to base classes; There are instances
372   where the performance actually improves with more new classes and fewer base classes. In real-world
373   scenarios where the label space is unknown, it’s not always the case that having fewer new classes
374   and more base classes will lead to better performance. Unknown mixed class proportions can
375   sometimes actually be more detrimental to the performance than having more new classes.
376   Moreover, on each dataset, there does not exist a certain method that can be optimal under
377   various ratios of new classes, e.g., on the Stanford-cars dataset, the best in terms of accuracy goes
      from MaPLe to PromptSRC to ProDA as the value of t increases.

      6      C ONCLUSION
530
531
      Research on robust prompt learning is an important step toward more practical tasks of VLM. This
      paper provides a new benchmark to evaluate the robustness of prompt learning in open environments,
532
      which includes dynamic class changes, dynamic distribution shifts, and dynamic co-evolution of
533
      both distributions and classes. We present several new performance metrics to help analyze the
534   robustness and conduct experiments on commonly adopted prompt learning methods. The results
535   reveal that current prompt learning methods in VLMs are not robust to class and data distribution
536   changes. On the contrary, they highly rely on the zero-shot ability of CLIP and show no significant
537   robust improvement compared to baseline zero-shot performance. Of course, the issues models face
538   in real-world environments may be more complex than the paradigms we have proposed. We hope
539   that our work can help promote the study of prompt learning in real-world scenarios.
