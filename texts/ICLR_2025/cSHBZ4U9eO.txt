011                                                A BSTRACT
012
013            Foundation models, such as Large language Models (LLMs), have attracted signif-
014            icant amount of interest due to their large number of applications. However, when
015            handling tasks involving repetitive sub-tasks and/or deceptive contents, such as
016
               arithmetic calculation and article-level fake news detection, simple instructional
               prompts suffer from inaccurate responses. Existing works show that more com-
017
               plicated prompting strategies, such as Chain-of-Thoughts and Least-to-Most, can
018
               unlock LLM’s powerful capacity in diverse areas. Recent researches reveal that
019            simple divide-and-conquer prompting strategy, i.e. simply dividing the input se-
020            quence to multiple sub-inputs, can also substantially improve LLM’s performance
021            in some specific tasks such as misinformation detection. In this paper, we aim
022            at examining the utility of divide-and-conquer prompting strategy and answer on
023            which kind of tasks this strategy gets advantages. Specifically, we provide a the-
024            oretic analysis to divide-and-conquer prompting strategy and help us identify the
025            specific tasks where DaC prompting can bring performance boost with theoretic
026            guarantee. We then present two cases (large integer arithmetic and fact verifi-
027
               cation) where experimental results aligns with our theoretic analysis.

031
032   Large language models (LLM) based on the Transformer architecture have led to major break-
033
      throughs in natural language processing and other related fields in artificial intelligence(Brown et al.,
      2020; Radford et al.; Touvron et al., 2023). State-of-the-art general-purpose language models have
034
      demonstrated remarkable advancements in various domains, including question answering, graph
035
      learning, reading comprehension, text generation, and machine translation (Chen et al., 2023b; Tan
036   et al., 2023; Hendy et al., 2023; Mao et al., 2023; Zong & Krishnamachari, 2023). These develop-
037   ments paves the way towards general-purpose problem solvers (Bubeck et al., 2023).
038
039
      However, as pointed out in (Wei et al., 2022), significant challenges arise when scale-up models are
      applied to tasks involved with long solution paths, such as those requiring mathematical or knowl-
040
      edge reasoning. A series theoretic works attribute this challenge to Parallelism Tradeoff (Merrill &
041
      Sabharwal, 2023a), a fundamental limitation of Transformers. Specifically, unlike Recurrent Neu-
042   ral Network whose computational depth is linear to the input sequence length (i.e., the depth is
043   O(n), where n is the input sequence length), Transformer does not contain any recurrent structure.
044   Such design, while achieving superior parallelizability than RNN, makes Transformers suffer from
045   limited expressive power. Merrill & Sabharwal proved that the expressive power of fixed-depth log-
046   precision Transformer, which is very close to the most commonly applied Transformer architecture
047   for LLMs, is bounded by constant-depth logspace-uniform threshold circuits. Thus, they fail to
048   accurately tackle the tasks requiring long solution paths.
049   To address this challenge, carefully designed prompting strategies have been developed to tackle
050   tasks that requires stronger expressive power (Feng et al., 2023). A series of works focus on prompt-
051   ing the LLM with instructions or context samples to output the intermediate steps that derive the final
052   answer in an autoregressive manner, such as Chain-of-Thoughts (CoT) (Wei et al., 2022; Wang et al.,
053   2022; Zhou et al., 2022; Chen et al., 2023a). Some works further apply programs to guide LLM to
      strictly follow designated reasoning steps (Yao et al., 2023). Theoretically, these prompting strate-

      2.1   E XPRESSIVE P OWER OF T RANSFORMER
139
140   As discussed in previous works (Merrill & Sabharwal, 2023a; Feng et al., 2023), the expressive
141   power of fixed-length log-precision transformers, which are widely applied in modern Pre-trained
142   Large Language Models, is actually much more limited than people’s expects. Merrill & Sabharwal
143   give a theoretic proof that the expressive power of fixed-length log-precision transformers is upper-
144   bounded with TC0 . Feng et al. further extend their analysis to explain that a lot of common problems
145   exceed the expressive power of fixed-length log-precision transformers. Such results explains why
146   the powerful LLM may make some ridiculous mistakes and how CoT improve the performance.
147
148   2.2   P ROMPTING S TRATEGIES OF LLM
149
150   In this sub-section, we introduce the existing prompting and discuss their limitations and drawbacks.
151   Following the notations in (Yao et al., 2023), we denote the Large Language Models with parameter
152
      θ as pθ and use lower case letters x, y, z to denote input sequence, result, and intermediate steps,
      respectively.
153
154   Input-Output (IO) Prompting is the standard prompting strategy that attach input x with instruc-
155   tions and/or few-shot in-context-learning examples to aqcuaire a prompt, denoted as prompt(x) (Yao
156   et al., 2023). The LLM takes prompt(x) as input and predict result, i.e. y ∼ pθ (y|prompt(x)).
157   Chain-of-Thought (CoT) Prompting (Wei et al., 2022) aims at simulating human’s thinking
158   process that handles complicated task (e.g. combinational reasoning and mathematical cal-
159   culation) in a step-by-step manner. More specifically, the LLM is guided to output a se-
160   ries of intermediate steps z1 , z2 , ..., zn (also known as thoughts) autoregressively, i.e. zi ∼
161   pθ (zi |prompt(x), z1 , ..., zi−1 ). Then the LLM output the prediction of result y based on the
      thoughts, i.e. y ∼ pθ (zi |prompt(x), z1 , ..., zn ).


                                                        3
      Under review as a conference paper at ICLR 2025


162
      Exploration-of-Thought (EoT) Prompting and Program-guided Prompting are two variants of
163   CoT. EoT includes a series of CoT’s variants, such as Self-consistency with CoT (CoT-SC) prompt-
164   ing (Wang et al., 2022) and Tree-of-Thoughts (ToT) prompting (Yao et al., 2023), which aim at
165   addressing the limitation of CoT in exploration. Their common central idea is to generate multiple
166   chains of thought through sampling or proposing prompting and then ensemble them to acquire a
167   final prediction. Program-guided Prompting aims at controlling the LLM’s generation process with
168   symbolic programs or pre-defined procedure (Zhu et al., 2022; Jung et al., 2022; Zhou et al., 2022;
169   Khot et al., 2022; Creswell & Shanahan, 2022; Gao et al., 2023). Among them, the Least-to-Most
170   (LtM) Prompting (Zhou et al., 2022) and Decomposed Prompting (Khot et al., 2022) are close to
171
      this work. They are the earliest attempts that explicitly prompt the LLM to decompose the task as
      a series of sub-tasks and sequentially tackle them. LtM prompt a LLM to iteratively raise sub-tasks
172
      and sequentially solve them to acquire the final resolution. Decomposed Prompting can regarded
173
      as a upgraded version of LtM. It introduces special notations into the prompt to represent program
174   states and thus can call itself (i.e., recursion) or other modules (i.e., hierarchical decomposition), en-
175   dowing it stronger expressive power. Such design increased the compositional generalization ability
176   of LLMs in different areas, such as symbolic manipulation and multi-hop QA (Khot et al., 2022).
177
      The aforementioned CoT and EoT families incorporate LLM with stronger expressive power than
178
      IO prompting. However, a critical issue of them is that, they could miss or ignore some important
179
      intermediate steps or contents (Liu et al., 2023). This problem is even worse when we are han-
180   dling tasks involved with long input (e.g. long documents and large numbers). Typical examples
181   include large number arithmetic calculation and fact verification in long documents. Compared to
182   them, Least-to-Most prompting and Decomposed Prompting introduce explicit task decomposition
183   to enumerate sub-tasks. However, their task decomposers are based on multi-round conversation or
184   question-answering, which navigate the LLM through the deceptive content’s flow sequentially, and
185   propagate the hallucination/deception in the contexts (Dziri et al., 2024; Yang & Ettinger, 2023),
186   leading to decreased performance.

188   3    P RELIMINARY OF D IVIDE - AND -C ONQUER P ROMPTING
189
190   In this section, we summarize and formalize Divide-and-Conquer prompting strategy. Divide-
191   and-Conquer prompting strategy consists of three distinct stages: task decomposition stage, sub-task
192   resolution stage, solution merge stage. In task decomposition stage, the LLM is prompted to explic-
193   itly decompose the task as a series of parallel homogeneous sub-tasks with smaller problem sizes
194   (e.g. divide a long paragraph to sentences). Such design avoids the multi-round conversation or
195   question-answering in LtM and Decomposed Prompting, making the model less prone to decep-
196
      tion. After that, in sub-task resolution stage, the LLM is prompted to provide the solutions for every
      sub-task. Finally, in the solution merge stage, the LLM is prompted to assembly the solutions of sub-
197
      tasks and acquire the final answer.To tackle tasks of different sizes, Divide-and-Conquer prompting
198
      strategy can be divided to two variants: Single-Level DaC Solver and Multi-Level DaC Solver.

238   4     T HEORETIC A NALYSIS
239
240   In this section, we provide theoretic analysis to the utility and limitations of the Divide-and-Conquer
241   prompting. In the first subsection, we provide a comparison of IO prompting (common fixed-length
242   instructional prompting) and DaC prompting in expressive power perspective. This part answers
243   the first research question: the expressive power of IO prompting is a subset of DaC prompting. In
244   the second subsection, we provide a comparison between Chain-of-Thoughts and DaC prompting in
245
      expressive power. Our comparison suggests that, although the expressive power of DaC prompting
      is a subset of Chain-of-Thoughts, for tasks satisfying specific conditions, DaC prompting can solve
246
      the problem with lower average context window length when decoding the tokens. Such property is
247
      empirically proved to be helpful for reducing the intermediate error and thus boost the performance.
248
249

      A.1    D ISCUSSIONS AND L IMITATIONS
651
652   In summary, the proposed method has following advantages:
653
654   Comparison with IO-Prompting: Superiority in Expressive Power As we proved in Sec. 4,
655
      Compared to IO-prompting, DaC has stronger expressive power and thus can solve harder problems.
656   Comparison with CoT and EoT: Disentangling the task decomposition and task resolution
657   Compared to the prompting family of CoT and EoT, DaC explicitly separate the task decomposition
658   stage and task resolution stage. Therefore, we can acquire explicit decomposed sub-task rather than
659   intermediate thoughts proposed during decoding. Consequently, we can explicitly enumerate all
660   sub-tasks output by the decomposition module and avoid the model from missing important sub-
661
      tasks.
662   Comparison with LtM and Decomposed Prompting: Parallel Sub-task Handler and Sequen-
663   tial Sub-task Handler Similar as DaC, some program-guided prompting like LtM and Decomposed
664   Prompting also explicitly separate the task decomposition stage and task resolution stage. However,
665   they are mainly designed for multi-step reasoning for complex tasks. Thus, they sequentially tackle
666   the sub-tasks and assembly the resolutions. As a result, they tend to follow the flow of the deceptive
667
      contents, leading to proneness to deceptive content.
668   Although DaC DaC surpasses the baselines on the proposed tasks, it still has some limitations.
669   The first issue is that the appliance scope of DaC is still limited. More specifically, CoT, EoT, LtM
670   and DaC are based on different algorithmic paradigms, learning to different Appliance Scopes. As
671   pointed out by Feng et al., CoT and LtM can be considered as a neural dynamic programming
672   algorithm. Thus, CoT is more suitable for tasks that can be bridged to dynamic programming, such
673
      as multi-step question answering. Differently, EoT is based on exploration and search, which is
      more suitable for planning and search, such as Game of 24 (Yao et al., 2023). DaC is based on
674
      Divide-and-Conquer algorithm. Thus, it is more suitable for tasks that can be decomposed to a
675
      series sub-tasks that are disjoint or only slightly overlapped. Our future work will focus on further
676   expand the appliance scope of DaC to more areas like question answering.

      A.2    P ROOF TO T HEOREM 4.2
680
      Before providing the proof, we first formally define how to organize the inputs (i.e., two 2-color
681
      trees) as a sequence. We assume that we acquire two trees tp of size n and tb of size m. They are
682   organized as two sequences of nodes with a random order. Each node has three variables: color, left
683   child index, and right child index. If any child is null, then the index is filled with 0. Then we can
                                                                         ′
684   organize them as as two sequences Xp ∈ Rn×3 and Xb ∈ Rn ×3 , where each item in the sequence
685   is a vector of 3 dimensions. The first dimension is the index of the left child, the second dimension
686   is the index of the right child, the third dimension is the color indicator (0 or 1). In addition, we
687   have a root vector r with three dimensions. The first dimension is the index of the root node of tp
688   (i.e., pointing to the root node of tp ) and the second is the index of the root node of tb (i.e., pointing
689   to the root node of tb ). The third dimension of r is filled with 0 to make it have same dimension as
690   the items in Xp and Xb . This expression of trees is also called as pointer list encoding according to
691
      (Jenner et al., 2003). Note that in the following proof, we assume that all indices start from 1. Thus
      0 is regarded as a NULL pointer.
692
693   Following the proof flow we provided in Sec. 4.2, we first provide the following divide-and-conquer
694   algorithm that can solve the above problem:
695   The algorithm described above is a typical divide-and-conquer algorithm for solving rooted tree

      Lemma A.1 Any fixed-size logic circuit that only contains multi-fan-in AND gates, multi-fan-in
784
      OR gates, NOT gates and has no recurrent structure can be precisely simulated by a multi-layer
785   perceptron (MLP) with ReLU activation function and a width of O(|Input| + |Circuit|) and a
786   depth of O(|Circuit|), where |Input| denotes the size of input and |Circuit| denotes the number of
787   gates in the circuit.

789   Assume that we are given a series of input pins with logic variable of 0 or 1, organized as a 0-1
790   vector x ∈ Rh . We first prove that all gates can be simulated by a two-layer perceptron. Then we
791   can serialize all gates in the circuits and stack their corresponding 2-layer simulators accordingly to
792   acquire a MLP simulator. An AND gate that take x as input can be simulated as:
793                                       AND(x) = σ(wA x − h + 1)                                         (3)
794
795
      where σ is the ReLU activation function, and wA is a weight vector with all dimensions equal to 1.
      If some dimensions of x are not the input of the gate, we can set the corresponding dimensions in
796
      the weight vector as 0 and adjust the h as the input pin number. Similarly, an OR gate that take x as
797
      input can be simulated as:
798                                      OR(x) = 1 − σ(wO x + h + 1)                                     (4)
799
      where σ is the ReLU activation function, and wO is a weight vector with all dimensions equal to -1.
800
      A NOT gate is different, since it only takes one input pin. In such a case, we denote the index of the
801
      input pin as i, then we can simulate a NOT gate as:
802
803                                          NOT(x) = σ(wN x + 1)                                          (5)
804
      where wN is a weight is a weight vector whose i-th dimension equals to -1 and all other dimensions
805   equal to 0. Also, since the x is a 0-1 vector, the activation function is equivalent to a identical
806   function to x:
807                                              x = σ(x)                                             (6)
808
809   To construct a MLP that can simulate a fixed-size logic circuit without recurrent structure, we
      apply the circuit serialization in (Merrill & Sabharwal, 2023b) which order the gates based on

      topological order. In this way, we can represent the circuit as a sequence GATE[1], GATE[2],
811   GATE[3],...,GATE[L], where each GATE[i]’s input only contains the output of the previous gates
812   and the original input x. Therefore, we can construct a 2L-layer MLP base on the above serializa-
813   tion. Specifically, the 2i-th and 2i + 1-th layers of the MLP will simulate the GAT E[i] as well as

815   by concatenate an identical matrix on the GATE’s weight vector (wA , wO or wN ). In this way, we

1068   Sequential Sub-task Tackling and Parallel Sub-task Tackling are two different paradigm in decom-
1069   posing complex tasks as sub-task to tackle. The first one decompose a complex tasks as a series of
1070   sub-tasks. In this series, each sub-task relies on the previous one’s output as input or context. The
1071   second one decompose a complex tasks as a set of sub-tasks, each of which does not rely on others.
