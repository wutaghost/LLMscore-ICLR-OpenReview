009                                                                 A BSTRACT
010
011                        Most public instruction finetuning datasets are relatively small compared to the
012                        closed source datasets used to train industry models. To study questions about
013                        finetuning at scale, such as curricula and learning rate cooldown schedules, there
014                        is a need for industrial-scale datasets. However, this scale necessitates a data
015                        generation process that is almost entirely automated. In this work, we study
016                        methods for generating large instruction datasets from a single prompt. With little
017                        human oversight, we get LLMs to write diverse sets of instruction examples ranging
018                        from simple completion tasks to complex multi-turn dialogs across a variety of
019
                           subject areas. When finetuning a Llama-3 8B base model, our dataset meets or
                           exceeds both WizardLM and Ultrachat on both knowledge-intensive leaderboard
020
                           tasks as well as conversational evaluations. We release our dataset, the “generator”
021
                           prompts that created it, and our finetuned model checkpoints.

      1              I NTRODUCTION
026
027   Datasets for language model finetuning are typically crafted by hand, crowdsourced from a pool of
028   human annotators, or built by prompting other large language models to edit and augment existing
029
      human written datasets (Ding et al., 2023; Köpf et al., 2024; Mukherjee et al., 2023; ShareGPT, 2023;
      Xu et al., 2023) The requirement of human inputs makes dataset curation an arduous and expensive
030
      process. These costs have also resulted in a split between academic and industrial finetuning
031
      practices, with academic datasets comprising hundreds or thousands of samples, and industrial
032   datasets comprising tens of millions.

      Automated dataset creation may seem challenging, as most language models suffer from a lack of
      randomness. When asked many times to write an instruction/response pair, LLMs may produce a
071
      low diversity dataset with many duplicate questions. To extract diverse questions from an LLM in an
072
      automated way, we propose generator prompts, a prompting strategy that boosts the randomness of
073   LLM outputs. By using a small number of generator prompts, we are able to create a large instruction
074   dataset containing many different question styles across a wide array of subjects.
075
076
      The GenQA dataset has a few key attributes that make it useful for research purposes. First, the
      GenQA dataset natively contains several different splits produced using different kinds of meta
077
      prompts. Second, the GenQA dataset is fairly large, and is meant to reflect the scale of the more
078
      than 10M instruction samples reportedly used to fine tune Llama 3. Finally, our empirical evaluation
079   suggests that models trained on GenQA perform well (Figure 1). In addition to demonstrating the
080   ease with which dataset creation can be automated, we hope the combination of diversity and scale in
081   the GenQA dataset will enable open research on industrial-scale finetuning practices.

      2            R ELATED W ORK
085
086   One of the earliest forms of modern data for language model finetuning was the Flan collection (Wei
087
      et al., 2021). The multi-task dataset used to train the T0 model family (Sanh et al., 2021) as well as
      the work of Chung et al. (2022) and Xu et al. (2022) expanded these data to include thousands of
088
      tasks, achieving further improvements over the original Flan. These datasets, while large and mostly
089
      factually correct, suffer from grammatical errors and other major text quality issues. Ouyang et al.
090   (2022) combined multi-task data and a reinforcement learning objective to produce InstructGPT, a
091   finetune of GPT-3 with improved controllability and utility for downstream users.
092
093
      Next, Wang et al. (2022) showed that an existing finetuned model such as InstructGPT (Text-Davinci-
      003) could be used to produce instruction-output pairs that were in turn useful to finetune other
094
      foundation models in an output based distillation process. One of the most notable demonstrations of
095
      this technique was the landmark work that turned the base Llama model (Touvron et al., 2023) into a
096   highly capable instruction-following version, Alpaca, using a dataset of just 52k outputs sampled
097   from Text-Davinci-003 (Taori et al., 2023). As distilled datasets became more popular, the community
098   began constructing others by either filtering and/or augmenting existing datasets in specific ways.
099   Xu et al. (2023) augmented the original Alpaca dataset (Taori et al., 2023) to produce Evol-Instruct,
100   sometimes referred to as WizardLM, using a pipeline that “evolves” existing instructions with five
101   types of meta prompts that constrain, deepen, concretize, increase reasoning steps, and generally
102   complicate the original input.
103   These model augmented instruction datasets were very small, often containing only tens of thousands
104   of examples. To increase the scale at which open source models could be instruction tuned, Mukherjee
105   et al. (2023) sampled the dataset proposed by Chung et al. (2022) and rewrote the responses using
106   ChatGPT and GPT-4 (Brown et al., 2020; Achiam et al., 2023). In another approach, Teknium
107   (2023) and Wang et al. (2023) methodically combined multiple sources of existing instruction tuning
      datasets to create very large singular datasets. For the domain of mathematics in particular, Yue

179
      A naive method for automating content generation is simply to choose a topic, and then construct a
180
      static prompt that asks for content on that topic. Consider the toy example of generating a long list of
181
      colors. This can be done by feeding the following static prompt to Gemini 1.0 Pro many times:
182
183   State a random color.                                Don’t output anything but the color.
184   The use of a static prompt yields low diversity. Running the above prompt through the Gemini Pro
185   1.0 language model 1000 times produces only 33 unique outputs.
186
      A generator prompt boosts diversity by asking a model to produce a long list of possible choices, and
187
      then select one of the candidates at random. For example, consider the following prompt:
188
189   First, print the heading "Colors:", followed by a numbered list of
190   100 colors. Then, print the heading "Chosen color:". Then print
191   color number {N} on a line by itself.
192   The placeholder N should be replaced with a random number each time the prompt is invoked. When
193   run 1000 times, this prompt yields 383 different colors. One can also use two nested generators as
194   follows.
195
      First, print the heading "Colors:", followed by a numbered list
196   of 100 different colors. Then, print the heading "Chosen color:".
197   Then print color number {N1} on a line by itself. Then, print
198   the heading "Color variants:". Then print a numbered list of 100
199   different color variants that look like color number {N1}, and
200   don’t appear on the original "Colors:" list. Then, print the
201   heading "Chosen variant:". Then print variant number {N2} on a
202   line by itself.
203   This prompt yields 782 unique colors from 1000 runs.
204
205
      We hypothesize that the output diversity produced when using a generator prompt comes from several
      sources. First, by explicitly creating a list, in the above example we guarantee that there will always
206
      be at least 100 candidates, and that these candidates are unique (assuming the LLM followed the
207
      provided directions). Second, the process of creating the list requires many sequential samples to be
208   drawn from the model. If the temperature is warm, this compounded randomness makes it highly
209   unlikely that the same list will be produced more than once.

212   3.2                 A S TUDY OF G ENERATOR P ROMPTS FOR DATASET C REATION
213
214   Here, we study how to apply the idea of generator prompts to create random question/answer pairs.
215   We consider several different prompt types in order of increasing complexity. Then, we rigorously
      study the level of diversity these prompts produce.

216
      Initial attempts to create the Academic split using static prompts resulted in low diversity. For
217   example, one could choose this prompt:
218
219   Static: Write a random complex question and its long answer. Begin
220
      your question with "Question:" and your answer with "Answer:
221   This results in many repeated/identical outputs, motivating us to provide additional context to our
222   prompt. We consider the following prompt conditioned on random_topic, which is randomly
223   selected from a list of topics written ahead of time by Gemini (see Appendix A.6 for full list).
224   Static-Conditional:    Write a complex question from the domain of
225   {random_topic}. Then write the long answer. Your question should
226   not contain the words "{random_topic}". Begin your question with
227   "Question:" and your answer with "Answer:"
228
      To further boost randomness and prevent the model from collapsing into a single mode around each
229
      topic, we consider the following generator prompt, also conditioned on random_topic.
230
231   Generator-Conditional:          List 40 subtopics in the domain of
232   {random_topic}. State subtopic {N}. Then write a question that
233
      is not about subtopic {N}, but can only be answered with expertise
      in subtopic {N}, and then write the answer. Both the question and
234
      answer should be long. The name of the subtopic should not appear
235
      in the question. Begin your questions with "Question:" and your
236   answer with "Answer:". Be creative.
237
238   Conditioning on a topic prevents the model from collapsing into a small number of modes, but it also
239
      constrains the range of possible topics. In the example shown in ??, we see that one can produce
      randomness using the nested generator approach. Applying this idea results in the following:
240
241   Generator-Nested: List 60 topics that you can answer questions about.
242   State topic {N1}. Then write 60 subtopics about topic {N1}. Then
243   state the subtopic {N2}. Then write a question that is not about
244   subtopic {N2}, but can only be answered with expertise in subtopic
245
      {N2}. Then write the answer. Both the question and answer should
      be long. The name of the subtopic {N2} should not appear in the
246
      question, and none of the words in subtopic {N2} should be reused
247
      in the question. Begin your questions with "Question:" and your
248   answer with "Answer:". Be creative.
249
250   This method has a potential drawback: The LLM sees the selected indices before writing the list, and
251
      this may influence the order of the listed items. For this reason, we consider the following construct:
252   Generator-Uniform:    List 60 topics that you can answer questions
253   about. Choose a topic uniformly from this list, and state it.
254   Then write 60 subtopics about the chosen topic. Then choose a
255   subtopic uniformly from this list, and state it. Then write a
256   question that is not about the subtopic, but can only be answered
257
      with expertise in the subtopic. Then write the answer. Both the
      question and answer should be long. The name of the subtopic
258
      should not appear in the question, and none of the words in
259
      subtopic should be reused in the question. Begin your questions
260   with "Question:" and your answer with "Answer:". Be creative.
261
262   In this construction, the random index is not available to the LLM when the lists are being constructed,
263
      as the index it chosen via sampling rather than appearing in the prompt.
264   Remark: The Gemini model tends to interpret instructions quite literally. If we ask for a question
265   about Cultural Anthropology, we are likely to get a question about Cultural Anthropology per se,
266   such as “Who is the father of Cultural Anthropology” or “What is that most famous textbook in
267   Cultural Anthropology.” We avoid this caveat by prompting for a question that is “not about the
268   subtopic, but can only be answered with expertise in the subtopic.”

298   To evaluate these prompting strategies, we generate a separate dataset of size 3000 for each prompt
299   type.1 To measure the level of diversity produced by each prompt, we used the all-MiniLM-L6-v2
300   retrieval model Wang et al. (2020) to encode the first 2 sentences of each question (or answer) into
301   384-dimensional dense vectors. For each question (or answer), we record its cosine similarity to its
302   nearest-neighbor. A similarity score of 1.0 indicates that a question has an exact duplicate, while
303   smaller values indicate that an input is more unique.
304   Figure 4 compares the similarity scores within each dataset, computed on either questions or answers.
305   The static prompting strategy resulted in high similarity between nearest neighbors and a higher
306   number of duplicates. The generator-conditional and generator-nested prompts, which were used to
307   create the final academic split, yield the highest diversity. Please refer to the appendix figure in A.3
308   for the full analysis of the prompting strategies evaluated in the Academic split. Similar analysis is
309   conducted for the Task split and can also be found in Appendix A.3.

311   3.4           A DDING A S UFFIX TO B OOST R ANDOMNESS
312
313   The prompts above end with the phrase “Be creative.” We call this a randomness booster. During the
314   creation of the splits, we randomly append one of the following boosters to the end of the prompt
315   every time it is invoked: “Be creative,” “Be different,” “Be smart,” “Be weird,” “Don’t ask the first
316   thing you think of,” “Be creative and don’t ask the first thing you think of,” or an empty string (no
317
      booster).
318   To assess the effect of the booster, we sample an equal number of question and answer pairs generated
319   with and without a booster from each split in our dataset (n = 200 for each type). Following the same
320   procedure in Section 3.3 to analyze diversity, we demonstrate the impact of boosters for the Academic
321   split in Figure 5. The presence of the booster improves diversity across Academic, Task, Multiple
322   Choice, MMLU and Dialogue splits. We include further analysis of boosters in Appendix A.4.

      WizardLM-Evol-Instruct-V2 (Xu et al., 2023) contains 196k single-turn instructions. The dataset is
      developed by starting with initial instructions from a base dataset such as Alpaca (Taori et al., 2023).
405
      It is then enhanced using a large language model like GPT-4, which incrementally increases the
406
      complexity of the instructions through various strategies2 .
407
408   UltraChat (Ding et al., 2023) is another synthetic instruction dataset, specifically focusing on multi-
409   turn conversational abilities. The dataset is generated by simulating conversations between two large
410
      language models on three different topics: general questions, writing, and assistance, aiming to
      ensure diversity. In this paper, we use a filtered version of UltraChat with a total of 200k multi-turn
411
      instructions 3 .

413   Evaluation To demonstrate the capabilities of our finetuned models, we evaluate them on a variety of
414   general benchmark tasks and conversational benchmarks. For general benchmark tasks, we include
415   ARC (Clark et al., 2018), BoolQ (Clark et al., 2019), HellaSwag (Zellers et al., 2019), MMLU
416
      (Hendrycks et al., 2020), OpenBookQA (Mihaylov et al., 2018), PIQA (Bisk et al., 2020), TruthfulQA
      (Lin et al., 2021), and Winogrande (Sakaguchi et al., 2019). This diverse range of benchmarks assesses
417
      the models’ reasoning, knowledge, and truthfulness. Additionally, we report scores for AlpacaEval
418
      2.0 length-control and MT-bench. These benchmarks test the models’ conversational abilities and
419   their capacity to follow instructions.

494
      GenQA is an instruction dataset written autonomously by an LLM without conditioning on human
      questions or using complex multi-stage pipelines. Beyond the obvious uses of the public GenQA
495
      samples to improve performance of open source models, we hope that the methods in this paper can
496
      serve as a Swiss army knife for easily creating datasets for other domains. Our experiments indicate
497   that prompt engineering alone can yield millions of diverse training samples with quality as good
498   as (or in some cases surpassing) high-cost human labelling. The generator prompt strategy can be
499   used to quickly generate datasets anew, or to create data to cover the blindspots of other existing data
500   sources.
